Summary
Online tracking is commonly used on a wide scale of websites. Approaches to track a
user are very diverse, from the well known cookies to the more subtle fingerprinting,
which consists in creating a unique fingerprint of a device based on its characteristics
(installed fonts, operating system, graphic rendering, browser used, etc.). In 2018, Eric
Sy and al. showed in Tracking Users across the Web via TLS Session Resumption that
a new way of tracking users could be using TLS session resumption.
This study starts with discussing the relevance of TLS session resumption as a way to
track users, by designing an extensive comparison framework of tracking mechanisms.
This framework is then used to compare TLS session resumption to other tracking mech-
anisms. Next, a tool to easily observe session resumption as it happens when browsing
the web is built, with the aim to allow interested users to display this totally transpar-
ent mechanism. Finally, using that tool, an update to the paper from Eric Sy and al.,
(which evaluated the settings of servers and clients regarding session resumption under
TLSv1.2) is provided, and evaluates how these settings evolved with the deployment of
TLSv1.3.
Though tracking through TLS session resumption has many limitations compared to
other tracking mechanisms, it is nonetheless a unique identifier which can correlate two
visits of a user. This study documents how TLSv1.3, compared to TLSv1.2, introduces
longer periods to resume the TLS sessions, which would ease the correlation between two
sessions with a server. Moreover, an inconsistent implementation of TLS session cache
clearing is observed on main browsers, and this study discusses how it could be used
by servers to recover a cookie that was deleted by the user within the same browsing
session.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Online tracking describes the action of collecting data of an individual browsing the web,
to associate this data with a particular user or its personal devices. Many studies show
how pervasive and wide-spread online tracking has become [25, 36, 39, 65]. The main
purpose of the gathered data is to display targeted ads to the user, which helps to fuel
the free Internet as we know it. Targeted advertisements are often found in services such
as news outlets, social media, streaming platforms, or blogs, which gives users free or
discounted content and services, while still generating revenues for the provider. It has
been evaluated that without these ads, regular users of a website would need to pay a
monthly fee of around $2 to replace the revenue generated by ads display [8]. But online
tracking is also used for other purposes such as price or search discrimination [43], and
poses serious privacy threats.
Most users are familiar with common tracking mechanisms such as cookies: in Europe,
following the GDPR regulations, it is highly unlikely to browse to a page without being
asked to accept third party cookies. But that is only the visible part of the iceberg.
Third party content embedded on the first party web-page visited can also deliver and
run scripts or call available APIs, and gather the right data to identify or track a user.
These embedded third parties are abundant.
The extension Lightbeams on Mozilla
[40] can help to have a better overview of the extent to which tracking is embedded in
websites.

As trackers try to build the most accurate profile for each user, the race for data

users choose to install applications and extensions to block ads and trackers, such as
Adblock, which registered 65 million users in 2020 [2]. Governments also started to issue
new regulations to protect their citizens’ data (GDPR, CCPA).
The limits of cookies [15, 35], the use of blockers, and the new regulations, pushed
trackers to use increasingly complex techniques to get through the filters and track the
user regardless [9, 16]. It has already been observed that trackers take advantage of the
implementation of communication protocols and available caches to uniquely identify a
user, such as with ETags and Last-Modified HTTP Headers, which are used to respawn1
cookies [5].
1.1
Problem statement
Transport Layer Security (TLS) is a protocol used to encrypt application data between
a client and a server. As the first handshake, which establishes the secured TLS ses-
sion, creates overheads, TLS session resumption was introduced: first in TLSv1.2 with
SessionID [19], followed with the stateless session ticket [54], then with pre-shared key
in TLSv1.3 [52]. These identifiers are stored on the client’s side and sent to the server
when resuming the TLS session. They could thus provide a new way to track users.
This concern was first raised in 2011 by the TOR community [20], and later studied in
a paper published in 2018 [63]. In that study, the authors showed that using a session
resumption lifetime of 24h, an average user could be tracked for up to 8 days.
With this paper, TLS session resumption entered the world of online tracking, and a few
concerned [46], or even alarming articles were published, such as ”Could TLS session
resumption be another ‘super cookie’?” [23] or ”Most Browsers Allow Permanent User
Tracking via TLS Sessions” [4]. The paper showed that TLS session resumption could
be used in the world of online tracking, but did not compare its benefits or limits to
other existing methods. To the best of our knowledge, none of the studies focusing on
online tracking provided an extensive comparison framework for all identified tracking
mechanisms. This framework would be useful when discovering new potential tracking
vectors such as TLS session resumption, to put it in perspective with currently identified
or implemented tracking mechanisms, and discuss its relevance for trackers.
Moreover, when the paper studying tracking through TLS session resumption was pub-
lished, TLSv1.3 was still at the draft stage and was not deployed on the web. The current

Project objectives
This paper will build on top of the previous study about tracking via TLS session re-
sumption [63] with the following contributions:
• Compare TLS session resumption based tracking to the other identified
tracking mechanisms: the first contribution of this work will propose a compar-
ison framework for tracking mechanisms, and apply it to tracking based on TLS
session resumption;
• Make session resumption visible to the user: TLS session resumption is

• Update of the study: since the paper was published, TLSv1.3 was deployed on

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
Online tracking
Online tracking can be described as all the actions performed to collect data of individ-
uals browsing the web. This can be used to display targeted online advertisement or
propose personalized content, and is described as the core business model of the free
Internet: in order to get access to free or discounted content, users accept to share their
data with the first party, or the embedded third parties. This consent is sometimes
explicitly asked for: following new regulations, it is now mandatory for websites to ob-
tain user consent before setting cookies. However, many other tracking mechanisms fall
under the radar.
Tracking mechanisms can be split into two categories: stateful mechanisms, stored on
the client’s side, and stateless mechanisms, which do not require any storage capacity
from the client. Stateful mechanisms include persistent storage, such as cookies [53], lo-
cal storage or WebSQL (HTML5) [6], or cache-related storage [5]. Stateless mechanisms
are dynamically generated (such as with session identifiers) [24] or use available APIs
like Javascript APIs to gather information on the client (device used, operating system,
browser) and correlate visits to different websites.
These tracking mechanisms are used in an increasingly complex landscape: while being
more eﬀicient to generate revenues, targeted advertisement comes with a set of actors
which allow for informed real time bidding mechanisms, by providing enough informa-
tion on each user to display the right ads to the right target group. The paper [26]
provides an overview of the attacker model and who will, in this work, be referred to by
the generic term of trackers:
• publishers are first parties which are contacted directly by the user and can then
transmit information or make calls to third party content;
• advertisers provide advertisements, and receive users’ data and can decide to bid
or not (though usually the bidding interaction is subcontracted to DSPs);
• Demand Side Platforms (DSPs) work on behalf of advertisers and selects the
best impression to bid on based on the user’s data provided, and the audience their
clients (advertisers) are targeting;
• Supply-side Platforms (SSPs) work on behalf of publishers, thus offer space
to display ads and provide information on the user;
==PAGE:13==

6
• ad exchanges act as gateways between DSPs and SSPs, can gather large amounts
of data (from several SSPs, to feed several DSPs), and represent the largest threat
to privacy.
Advertisers, DSPs, SSPs and ad exchanges are what is referred to as the third parties .
Other actors, such as Internet Service Providers (ISP) or government surveillance, will
be left out of the scope of this study, as it focuses on tracking mechanisms which can
be implemented by first and third party services (end points of the traﬀic) which do not
have extensive abilities (control over the network for ISP, extensive means and accesses
for governments).
As privacy concerns increase, so does the number of countermeasures: new regulations
introduced the choice to decline cookies and other persistent storage; users can install
extensions to block third party trackers or fingerprinting, use private browsing to avoid
any persistent storage-based tracking mechanism. Other widespread tools such as VPNs
also increase the ability to deceive trackers, by changing for example the IP address.
This tension between privacy required by users and the profits generated by data col-
lection and sale, results in trackers getting more sophisticated to implement tracking
mechanisms that can circumvent the protection mechanisms in place: the discovery of
the use of Etags being used for cookie respawning is an example that was documented
in 2011 by Ayenson, Wambach and al. [5].
2.2
TLS and TLS session resumption
TLS stands for Transport Layer Security and is a cryptographic protocol providing secure
communication on the network. It is mostly renowned for its use as a security layer in
HTTPS, and it enables a client and a server to exchange encrypted application data. It
was introduced first in 1999 with TLSv1.0, as a replacement to SSvL3.0. Both have been
deprecated since (resp. in March 2020 and June 2015). The main two supported proto-
cols are currently TLSv1.2, introduced in 2008, and TLSv1.3, introduced ten years later.
2.2.1
Initial TLS handshake
In order to establish a secure channel, the client and the server first need to perform a
full handshake, which includes at least four messages (two round trips). The first mes-
sage ClientHello provides the protocol versions, cryptographic functions, and extensions
supported by the client. The following ServerHello message will determine the proto-
col and cryptographic functions used, and is followed by the server’s certificate. Once
the certificate is validated, the client generates the session keys, either through Diﬀie-
Hellmann (providing forward secrecy) or using a PreMasterSecret (random number)
encrypted with the server’s public key. Once that data is sent to the server, both parties
can compute the session key, and the server confirms the successful handshake with one
last ”Finished” message: the application data can be sent encrypted over the network.

2.2.2
TLS session resumption
TLS session resumption is a way to decrease the overhead introduced by the TLS hand-
shake protocol: instead of performing a full-handshake every time the client reconnects to
a server (which requires two round trips), both can simply perform a session resumption
using a unique shared identifier (one round trip). As TLSv1.3 [52] has been introduced
quite recently (in August 2018), both protocols are still used: in fact, as of January
2021, a majority of websites did not have TLSv1.3 as their best available protocol yet
(57% still had only TLSv1.2, against 42% for TLSv1.3) [50]. Therefore, all three session
resumption mechanisms offered by both protocols are considered: session identifiers and
session tickets, both from TLSv1.2, and pre-shared keys from TLSv1.3.
2.2.2.1
Session identifiers
A session identifier, or SessionID, is a random value generated by a server, identifing a
particular session. During the first handshake, the client sends an empty SessionID field
to the server in the ClientHello message; the server sends back a ServerHello message
which can either contain an empty Session ID (session resumption through SessionID is
not enabled), or a random identifier that is used as the SessionID. Once the full hand-
shake is completed, the client can resume the session by sending back the SessionID. If
the server finds a match in his cache, the session can be resumed. Otherwise, a new Ses-
sionID is generated and sent by the server, and a full handshake is performed. Session
identifiers should remain valid until their lifetime expires or a fatal error is encountered.
This resumption mechanism is stateful for both the client and the server, as they both
need to cache the SessionID. It is recommended to limit the SessionID lifetime to 24
hours [19]. Figure 2.1 shows the messages exchanges during a full handshake (fig.2.1a)
and a session resumption using SessionID (fig.2.1b). The information messages in green
highlight what needs to be considered when parsing the packets to observe session re-
sumption.

2.2.2.2
Session Ticket
This standard has been introduced to avoid keeping a per-client session state: instead
of caching the session state on the server side, it is encapsulated in a session ticket and
sent to the client. It has been implemented as an extension in a later version of TLSv1.2
[54].
When the client sends the first ClientHello message, it includes an empty session_ticket
extension to notify the server that it can support that mechanism, but has no ticket yet.
The server replies with the same empty extension (if it can support it too) in the Server-
Hello message. This indicates that the server will later send a NewSessionTicket message
once the handshake is completed.
==PAGE:16==
2.2 TLS and TLS session resumption
9
The NewSessionTicket computed by the server stores the session state (such as ci-
phersuite and master secret) encrypted and integrity-protected by a key only known to
the server. It is then cached on the client’s side with the master secret and other param-
eters associated with the current session. The lifetime of the session ticket is defined by
the server as well; client should use the session ticket until it expires.
When the client wishes to resume the session, it sends the ticket using the ses-
sion_ticket extension. The ticket is decrypted and verified by the server, which can
either resume the previous session and issue a NewSessionTicket, or refuse and initiate
a full handshake (either with or without a NewSessionTicket).
The extension session_ticket should only be included by the clients if they are willing
to receive a session ticket, and by the server if a NewSessionTicket is to be sent to the
client. Its extension number is 35. The extension_data_field of session ticket extension
contains the ticket.

2.2.2.3
Interaction between SessionID and SessionTicket
According to the standard, if the server plans to send a NewSessionTicket message, then
the SessionID field should be left empty. If a session ticket is received by the client, then
it should discard any related SessionID. When both SessionID and session ticket are sent
by the client, the server should resume the session with the session ticket but answer
with the same SessionID if the session is successfully resumed, to differentiate from a
fallback into a full handshake.

2.2.2.4
Pre-shared key
This resumption mechanism has been introduced in TLSv1.3 and deprecates the two pre-
vious ones [52]. The client starts by sending a ClientHello with extensions indicating that
resumption using pre-shared key is supported (key_share and psk_key_exchange_modes).
The server answers with a key_share extension to confirm that a pre-shared key will be
later sent encrypted. Once the handshake is completed, one or eventually several pre-
shared key identities are sent as encrypted NewSessionTicket messages.
Anonymity is higher than when using Session IDs or session tickets, as the pre-shared
key can’t be observed in clear. It is also recommended to use it only once. However, the
mechanism of session resumption can still be observed in clear when a client attempts
to resume a session: the extension pre_shared_key from the ClientHello contains the
pre-shared key used for that resumption.

2.2.3
TLS session resumption and user privacy
A session token (which includes session identifiers, session tickets and pre-shared keys)
can have a lifetime hint (lifetime defined by the server) of a few minutes up to several
days. In practice, it has been observed in the study Tracking Users Across the Web via
TLS Session Resumption from Eric Sy and al. [63] that the observed lifetime (lifetime
observed when attempting to resume the session with the server) of a session token can
span up to 48 hours. Similarly to cookies, when the client attempts a session resumption,
a unique identifier initially assigned by the server is sent back to it. A prolongation attack
==PAGE:18==
2.2 TLS and TLS session resumption
11
such as the one described in [63] could also be used to extend the tracking capabilities: by
renewing the session token before its expiration, and keeping track of the tokens assigned,
a tracker can follow a user on a longer period. Based on a dataset of DNS connections of
real-world users, Eric Sy and al. determined that by combining the use of a prolongation
attack with the previously evaluated common browser settings (resumption lifetime of
up to 24 hours), an average user from that dataset could be tracked for a period of 8
days.
This mechanism can also be applied to third parties: any client contacting a third party
embedded on a website A through TLS could then resume the session with that tracker,
either from the same website A, or a new website B. This could eventually be used to
perform cross-domain tracking. The tracker would however need additional information
(such as HTTP referrer or custom URL) to trace it back to the initial first-party.

CHAPTER 3
Comparing tracking
mechanisms
There are plethora of tracking mechanisms available. As concerns surrounding online
tracking, data collection and user privacy grew, the most renowned one, tracking using
cookies, started to be regulated. Firefox even began to block some third party cookies
by default [69] in 2019. This leads to a race between user trying to keep their brows-
ing behavior private (using third party blockers and other extensions to block tracking
mechanisms such as fingerprinting, deleting browsing data, etc.) and trackers trying to
collect data, correlate visits and build up an interest profile to show targeted advertise-
ment and personalize the browsing experience. Not all trackers have the same capacities,
and providing an overview of the advantages and limits of currently known or potential
tracking mechanisms can help to understand the level of threat of a newly discovered
and potential tracking mechanism such as TLS session resumption, compared to other
identified ones.
The goal of this chapter is to provide an extensive comparison of tracking mecha-
nisms, by gathering information on trackers and defining comparison characteristics. In
Section 3.1, the research methodology is presented. Section 3.2 provides an overview
of the literature with a particular focus on comparisons that were already performed in
previous studies. Then, Section 3.3 and Section 3.4 present respectively the compared
characteristics, and the compared tracking mechanisms. Section 3.5 describes the color
scale used in the comparison table. Finally, results are presented and applied to tracking
through TLS session resumption in Section 3.6.
3.1
Research methodology
This section will detail the steps performed to identify the pool of relevant paper used to
define the considered tracking mechanisms, and how they were compared. The research
methodology follows the one proposed by Petersen and al. [49].
3.1.1
Research questions
The research questions are the following:

RQ 1.1 How would tracking through TLS session resumption compare to other

RQ 1.2 How interesting is this approach for tracking companies?
RQ 1.1 requires to identify all mechanisms available for companies to use (either

Based on the previous comparison, RQ 1.2 will then focus on the perks and drawbacks

3.1.2
Search strategy
PICOC [37] was used to identify relevant keywords.
Population: we consider papers that describe tracking mechanisms used to track a
user on the web.
Intervention: this work will focus on technologies found or used on users browsing
the web from a desktop. Techniques must be applicable by a first or third party, therefore
technologies based on accessing specific infrastructures (such as DNS servers) are not
taken into account.
Comparison: tracking mechanisms will be compared based on the characteristics
described in the Section 3.3, which include: technology, lifetime, deletion, detection,
accuracy, delay, avoidability, cross-window, cross-domain, cross-session, cross-browser,
cross-device (gathered under the name cross-instance), and storage size.
Outcomes: an overview of tracking mechanisms and a comparison to understand
their strengths and limitations, as well as an overview of detection techniques. This
comparison framework will be used to discuss the relevance of tracking using TLS session
resumption, and can be used to discuss the viability of newly discovered tracking vectors
available for first and third party trackers.
Context: papers studying potential or used tracking mechanisms have been consid-
ered. Blogs or articles referred to by selected papers have also been included during the
snowballing process.

The word ”privacy” was added as the topics of privacy and online tracking of users

3.1.4
Study selection
First, a duplicate search was performed and eliminated 33 entries with identical titles.
Three papers were not available at the DTU Finder link, nor found after a Google search,
and were also eliminated. Reading the title and abstracts, additional elimination criteria
were applied:
• focuses on localisation tracking;
• focuses on the legal or social aspect of tracking;
• only addresses tracking on mobile devices;
• proposes a solution for privacy-friendly advertising;
• studies the use of already known tracking mechanism (for example, studies on
geographical distribution of known trackers);
• solely focuses on protection and/or countermeasures of well known and identified
tracking mechanisms and trackers (obfuscation, blocking based on black-lists, etc.);
• the technique can only be applied by a tracker with capabilities out of the reach
of traditional trackers (control of a large portion of the network, nation state or
intelligence community).
After that, 72 papers were kept for further analysis, and provided a background for
the comparison. One up to two iterations of backward snowballing were performed to
gather more material going in depth with a particular tracking mechanism that was only
mentioned in the initial set of papers, which added 12 resources (including papers, blogs
and articles) to the set.
3.2
Literature review
A few studies propose surveys of available tracking mechanisms: the paper from [9],
published in 2017, is the most complete one that was found, as it describes and discusses
most of the tracking mechanisms that appear throughout the other papers from the set.
Only one tracking mechanism (QUIC session resumption, with a first documentation of
how tracers could use QUIC in 2019 [61]) can’t be found in the survey. The tracking

3.2 Literature review
15
mechanisms that are studied thus include all tracking mechanisms included in the survey
[9], and the one about tracking through QUIC session resumption [61]. All other papers
from the set are used to go more in depth with each tracking mechanism and discuss
their characteristics, as well as to define comparison characteristics, as described in the
following paragraphs.
The literature proposes several ways of classifying tracking mechanisms. These clas-
sifications highlight different categories or characteristics of tracking mechanisms, which
are gathered to provide a full comparison.
In [56], tracking methods are classified into statefull (requires to store information on
the client’s device) and stateless (no storage required). However, statefull mechanisms
focus solely on cookies and how they can be synchronized (by-passing the same-origin
policy) or respawned (re-created after deletion, from other storages), leaving cache-based
tracking out of the scope.
In [9], tracking mechanisms are classified as session-only (stops when the session
with the server or the browser is closed), storage-based (storage of the data on the
client’s device), cache-based (stored in the cache of the browser), fingerprinting
(unique identification of a user based on the characteristics of its device), and other
(which includes headers attached to outgoing HTTP requests, telephone metadata, tim-
ing attacks, collaboration of the user, clickjacking and evercookies). It also details the
technology used (HTML5, Javascript, server-side measurements, HTTP Headers, ...)
and the scope (session ID, browser instance ID, browsing history, OS instance ID, ...).
A similar exhaustive list of tracking mechanisms is provided in [42] but the authors do
not describe them in detail.
The study [26] identifies five privacy threats (first and third party tracking, cookie
matching, fingerprinting, flash cookies, canvas fingerprinting and HTML5 localStorage)
but compares only three types of cookies commonly used to track (HTTP cookies,
HTML5 cookies and Flash cookies) using the following characteristics: maximum storage
size, level of persistence, storage location, diﬀiculty to delete, usage level (whether the
tracking mechanism is widespread or not), installation (how it is installed) and access
level (whether different browsers can access it).
In [53], only persistent tracking mechanisms (cookies and other local storage mecha-
nisms such as HTML5 or Flash cookies) are classified based on their observable behavior
from the client’s side: this also highlights how the same tracking mechanism can be used
in different ways. The study focuses on mechanisms able to store a pseudonym (unique
identifier) on the user’s machine, and communicate that pseudonym, as well as visited
sites, back to the tracker’s domain. It also distinguishes between tracking within-site or
cross-site.
In [35], advertising companies point out the limits of cookies and investigate ways of
tracking users across devices. This shows that cross-device tracking can be a relevant
characteristic to compare tracking mechanisms.
In [5], the authors compare Flash cookies to HTTP cookie based on their storage
(how much data can be stored), expiration (persistence of the cookie), location and
access (who can erase it).

In [33], the authors propose a privacy scoring per visited web page to quantify the
use of tracking techniques, which introduces a quantitative comparison between online
tracking mechanisms: the higher their score, the greater the risk for the user. The scores
are between 1 to 2 per tracking mechanisms, and depend on the lifetime: the longer the
lifetime, the higher the score.
In [61], the author compares tracking using the QUIC protocol to three other track-

characteristics: tracking delay, bandwidth requirements, accuracy, coverage, survives a
change of IP address, and survives a browser restart (cross-session tracking).
3.3
Compared characteristics
Based on the previous literature review, nine characteristics are selected to compare
tracking mechanisms to each other: the technology, considered in [9], is added to un-
derstand the core needs for a tracking mechanism to work (such as Javascript enabled).
The common comparison points from [5] and [26] are bundled into three separate char-
acteristics. Persistence, or expiration, are considered in lifetime. Location, access and
diﬀiculty to delete are incorporated into the characteristic deletion, which only sees
these characteristics from a user perspective: though it does not focus on where the
tracking mechanism is stored and who can access it, it categorizes how diﬀicult it is for
a lambda user to delete manually. Storage size is also considered.
The possibility to track across instances is found in different papers but focuses on dif-
ferent levels: [53] mentions if tracking cross-site is possible or not given a technique;
[26] considers ”usage level”, which details whether a tracking mechanism can be used
across browsers; finally the Interactive Advertising Bureau [35] emphasises the need for
cross-device tracking for a more eﬀicient tracking. It is added as the comparison point
cross-instance tracking.
Many of the above mentioned papers not only discuss a tracking mechanism, but also
create tools to observe or detect them in the wild. Doing so even allows to detect new,
unknown tracking mechanisms, such as in [5] or [25]. Detection defines how easy it is
to detect a tracking mechanism.
Accuracy and delay, used in [61] are considered to define respectively how precise a
tracking mechanism is to tell users apart, and the additional operations required from
the client to use it. Finally, the diﬀiculty to opt-out of a tracking mechanism is consid-
ered in Avoidability.
The selected characteristics are further defined and discussed in the following paragraphs.
3.3.1
Technology
Technology defines the process used to track the user. Given how languages or software
can deprecate, it is relevant to consider the main technology of a tracking mechanism,
as it might be getting less popular over time. A typical example is Flash, which allowed
==PAGE:24==
3.3 Compared characteristics
17
for a wider tracking (including cross-browser), but got deprecated by other technologies
and is no longer supported by main browsers (thus all the related cells in Table 3.1 will
be grey).
3.3.2
Lifetime
Lifetime defines how long the tracking mechanism can persist if the user does not
perform any action to delete it. The longer that period last, the better for the tracker,
as it allows to follow the user’s browsing activities on a longer period and gather more
information. Four lifetimes have been identified:
• Web-session: does not exceed the session with the server;
• Brows.session: does not exceed the session with the browser (when the browser
is closed);
• Cache: is erased when the browser’s cache is cleared. It should correspond to the
browsing session, as browsers usually clear the cache when closing, but has still
been considered separately to take the cache difference into account, especially
since some browsers allow to clear the cache within the same browsing session,
without closing the browser;
• Infinite (∞): can span over multiple browsing sessions (several days, weeks, or
even years). This is the case for stateless tracking mechanisms which can’t be
deleted on the user’s side and do not rely on a specific web-server session (such
as fingerprinting, which is based on the characteristics of the device, browser or
network). It can also be applied to tracking mechanisms that have the possibility of
defining their expiration date and are not deleted by default, such as with cookies,
localStorage or IndexedDB.
3.3.3
Deletion
Deletion defines how diﬀicult it is for a user to delete the tracking mechanism. This is
considered within the lifetime of the mechanism. For example, if a tracking mechanism
is deleted after closing the browser, then deletion focuses on the ability of the user to
delete the tracker without closing the browser. It can be described as the possibility
to delete at any time without having to take measures impacting the browsing session.
The harder it is for the user, the more interesting for the tracker.
3.3.4
Detection
Detection defines how diﬀicult it is to detect the tracking mechanism. Some tracking
mechanisms display warning messages (such as cookies, due to regulation), others are
visible in the URL (session identifiers), need to do unusual calls (Javascript calls to used
==PAGE:25==
3.3 Compared characteristics
18
for fingerprinting, calls to many sub-domains of the same tracking company for HSTS)
and can be detected by extensions from the browser’s level, while others use the feature
of a lower-level protocol to track (TLS Session Resumption, IP address), and can’t be
monitored from a browser extension. The harder to detect, the better for the tracker.
3.3.5
Accuracy
Accuracy defines how easy it is for a tracker to uniquely identify a user. While some
tracking mechanisms can assign and store a unique identifier for each user, other rely on
networks’ or devices’ characteristics, which can be shared among a larger group of users.
3.3.6
Delay
Delay takes into account if the tracking mechanism requires additional actions on the
client’s side which are not part of the usual protocols or website load: these operations
can include computing operations (such as Javascript calls to determine the screen size,
the fonts present on the device or loading time) or additional round trips (such as
tracking based on HSTS cache). Mechanisms such as setting and getting stored won’t
be considered as introducing delays.
3.3.7
Avoidability
A user does not necessarily have to delete a tracking vector if its storage can be avoided in
the first place. The harder it is for a user to opt-out and not saving or running a tracking
vector on his device in the first place, the better for a tracker. Avoidability defines
how diﬀicult it is for the user to avoid the tracking mechanism. Tracking mechanism
using features on which the user does not have any control (TLS session resumption) or
can’t block without impacting the browsing experience (such as with Javascript-based
mechanisms, that would require turning off Javascript) are considered as diﬀicult to
avoid. The harder for the user to avoid it, the better for the tracker. Notably, no tracking
mechanism has been marked as ”easy” to avoid. Even persistent storage mechanisms
(cookie, local storage, etc.), which are the most regulated , can’t be avoided without
sometimes impacting the browsing experience (access is denied by some websites without
cookie consent).
3.3.8
Cross-instance tracking
Cross-instance is used as a generic term to define a tracking mechanism able to track
across two concrete occurrence of an object.
Here the object can be a window (or
browsing tab), a domain, a session (or browsing session), a browser or a device.
Cross-window: defines if the tracking mechanism can be used between two open
windows of the same website, on the same browser.
==PAGE:26==
3.4 Compared tracking mechanisms
19
Cross-domain: defines if the tracking mechanism can be shared across two opened
domains during the browsing session (also on the same browser).
Cross-session: defines if the tracking mechanism implemented by a website can be
re-used when a new browsing session is started from the same browser.
Cross-browser: defines if the tracking mechanism can be used or shared between
different browsers.
Cross-device: defines if the tracking mechanism can be used across different devices.
These tracking mechanism would be the most interesting for the tracking company and
advertisers, as highlighted by the IAB in [35].
3.3.9
Storage size
The higher the storage capacity, the more information a tracker can store on the clients’
device and retrieve afterwards. For tracking mechanisms using specific storage (cookie,
session or local storage), or a specific field in an HTTP header, this size has a maximum;
while for others such as fingerprinting, it is not limited and can’t really be defined.
3.4
Compared tracking mechanisms
The compared tracking mechanisms follow the requirements listed in 3.1.2: the tracking
mechanism must be applicable on a web-browser accessed from a desktop. Techniques
must be applicable by a first or third party hosted on a server, but which does not have
access to other infrastructures than the server hosting it (thus techniques applicable by
actors having access to other infrastructures such as a DNS server are not taken into
account. The different subsections follow the classification proposed by [9]: session-based,
storage-based, cache-based and fingerprinting.
3.4.1
Session based tracking mechanisms
3.4.1.1
Session identifiers
Session identifier is a stateless tracking mechanisms which consists in creating a unique
ID for each visitor: when a new user connects to the website, the server generates a
unique ID and appends it to all outgoing URLs available on the visited page. This way,
every time the user clicks on a link, it will send that unique ID to the server (which
can then repeat the operation for the new visited page, and so on). That identifier can
thus be passed to other websites when the user makes the request to an other domain,
allowing cross-domain tracking. Its lifetime is limited to the session with the server.
It can be detected easily by checking the URL bar or by inspecting outgoing HTTP
requests, which is often done in research when studying tracking mechanisms [36, 70,
64]. Deleting or avoiding it is also easy: a user could copy outgoing links into the URL
bar instead of clicking, and delete the ID. It introduces delays for the server as it needs
==PAGE:27==
3.4 Compared tracking mechanisms
20
to dynamically generate the URLs, but is light for the client (which just sends an ID
appended to the URL). There is no defined limit to the size that can be passed from one
website to the other.
Note: In [24], authors show that when used within e-mails, this tracking vector can
enable cross-device tracking, since a unique cross-device identifier (such as the hashed
e-mail address) can be leaked to third parties embedded in the corpus of an e-mail. This
is a very specific application which requires a user to be authenticated to its e-mail on
several devices, therefore the cross-device tracking is marked as not possible.
3.4.1.2
Window.name DOM property
Window.name DOM property is initially used to define the name of the window in the
browsing context. It can also allow cross-domain messaging, with little delay but high
accuracy, as it would consist in passing a unique identifier from a domain to an other
through the Window.name property (though it is now recommended to perform this
action by using the postMessage function [14]). This technique does not use persistent
storage, so its lifetime is limited to the web-server session. Users can’t avoid the mech-
anism, nor delete the identifier while conserving the session, but it can be read in the
source code, thus detected. It also can’t pass more than 2 MB of data [9].
3.4.1.3
Web-form authentication
Web-form authentication is the most burdensome for users as they need to authenticate,
though re-authentication on a device is eased by cookie mechanisms (users do not need
to re-authenticate each time they close the browsers, as long as they do not clear all
cookies). It works as long as the user is connected, so users can reduce the privacy risk by
disconnecting (though omnipresent players such as Facebook, Twitter or Google can still
track offline users through their widgets [51, 11]). How tracking is then performed on the
server side is obscure and can’t be detected, the data gathered during the authenticated
session can’t be deleted, but the user does not necessarily suffer any delays. It is highly
accurate due to the easy mapping between authenticated session and the user. If the
user is connected to the same account across sites, browsers or devices, it can perform
all the cross-instance tracking considered in this work.
3.4.2
Storage based tracking mechanisms
3.4.2.1
HTTP cookies
HTTP cookies are the most common and well-known tracking mechanism. It is a unique
identifier stored by the browser and sent back with outgoing requests made by the client,
which does not introduce any delays. Its lifetime is self-defined. It can be used across
windows, domains, as well as across browsing sessions, and can store up to 4KB of data.
==PAGE:28==
3.4 Compared tracking mechanisms
21
Third party calls also allow for cross domain tracking, when third party cookies are
allowed. Different way of setting third party cookies have been observed in the wild [53].
They are however more regulated and require explicit consent in Europe (though studies
showed this principle is not applied on all websites [41]), making it easier for users to
avoid them than unregulated tracking mechanisms. Cookies can also be deleted directly
from the browser’s settings, and can be easily detected as when set by the website: most
browsers allow to check the storage used by a website.
3.4.2.2
HTML5 Storage
This type of storage includes Web Storage, Web SQL Database, and Indexed Database.
Web Storage is the most used mechanism: it includes local storage (localStorage
object) and session storage (sessionStorage object), and allows web applications to cre-
ate a key-value stored in the browser, either for one browsing session (sessionStorage) or
several (localStorage). It can store up to 25MB of data [6]. Unlike localStorage, session-
Storage doesn’t even allow for cross-window tracking [31], and its tracking capabilities
are therefore extremely limited. On the other hand, it was demonstrated that the local-
Storage objects were used to rebuild deleted HTTP cookies (cookie respawning) [5].
Web SQL Database, despite being deprecated, is still supported by main browsers
(except Firefox, who pushed for its deprecation [32]) and is used to track users, but at
a much lower scale (0.76% of websites of the dataset considered in [6] used the Web
SQL API to store tracking vectors, against 57.72% for the Web Storage API). It is very
similar to IndexedDB, which is replacing it, therefore they share the same row in the
table presenting the results.
Indexed Database API is a Javascript interface used to store data in the browser,
at a bigger scale and with more advanced features than what is provided by Web Stor-
age (several databases for the same origin, one or multiple keys, object oriented). The
use of this storing mechanism is much higher in very popular websites [6]. Similarly
to localStorage, it has the same cross-instance tracking capabilities, and the study [1]
showed that it is also used for cookie respawning.
These three storing mechanisms are frequently used by trackers: when studying their
dataset, the authors from [6] concluded that 30% up to 70% of the uses are performed by
trackers. They also suggest that storage clearing mechanisms implemented by the most
common browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Safari) can put user’s privacy at risk (extra step
required in Firefox to deleted IndexedDB data, data stored in guest mode only deleted
once the browser is closed in Chrome, inconsistent disabling of APIs when going into
incognito mode). Deletion is still marked as easy for the user as they are mostly, despite
their inconsistent implementation, allowed from the browser. These storing mechanisms
can be detected by inspecting the source code or the storage tab on the developer’s

3.4 Compared tracking mechanisms
22
interface available on most browsers.
They are regulated the same way cookies are,
which could make them easier to avoid. All these storing techniques allow the tracker to
assign a unique ID to each user, and do not introduce much delay as it simply requires
to set them on and get them from the client.
3.4.2.3
Flash cookies and Flash LocalConnection Object
Flash mechanisms, including cookies and LocalConnection Objects, are deprecated since
the end of 2020, but are added to the comparison to put into perspective the higher
capabilities they offered, compared to their successor HTML5 [3]. The difference is that
Flash had a directory shared across browsers for Flash cookies, which allowed cross-
browser tracking. Moreover, cross-browser tracking was also possible between private
and non private browsing windows through LocalConnection Object [9].
This is no
longer possible using HTML5 storage.
3.4.3
Cache-based tracking mechanisms
Caching allows for better performances: instead of downloading all files every time a
user accesses a web-page, the browser can cache the resources and use the local copies
available when reloading a page.
Any tracking mechanism using that method can’t
therefore track across browsers or devices, as browser do not share these caches. Tracking
across browsing session should not be possible either, as long as the cache is erased by
the browser when closing. None of these mechanisms can be avoided without disabling
the associated cache, which isn’t an option available to use and would severely impact
performances. They should however be deleted by clearing browsing data, using the
same method as for clearing cookies.
3.4.3.1
Cached cookies
It can be implemented by embedding a unique identifier into an HTML file requested by
the user, which is cached by the user and later read from the browser cache; this file can
be shared between several websites and allows for cross domain tracking [28]. Detection
requires to check the cached file. As the value is simply read from the cached file, it
does not introduce significant delay on the client’s side, and can be used across domains.
There is no limitation to the size of the cached ID.
3.4.3.2
Loading performances test
This can be performed using Javascript calls: the website measures the loading time,
either on the client or on the server side, for a specific file, and can determine whether
the user already had uploaded it during his browsing session. The same logic can be
applied on the DNS cache, to check if a user has already loaded a given website before.
However, it is erased along with the cache, and the implementation for the tracker is a
==PAGE:30==
3.4 Compared tracking mechanisms
23
bit burdensome, as explained in [27]. Detecting or avoiding these tests is diﬀicult if done
on the server side. If implemented on the client side, they require additional operations
to calculate the loading time, which slows the client. The accuracy is limited as many
clients might have downloaded the same file.
3.4.3.3
HSTS Cache
This allows server to require a client to establish any future connections through HTTPS,
using an HTTPS address that is cached on the client’s device. Trackers could use this
feature to assign a unique identifier to the user; two different techniques have been
documented: one where the user is assigned a unique bit combination (based on whether
requests are made in HTTP or HTTPS) [60] or a unique string [17]. Once again, this
can be used across domains, but the successive calls are easy to detect when analysing
the traﬀic. Unique bit combination requires the client to perform many calls, which can
introduce delays; the higher the number of calls, the higher accuracy (more bits).
3.4.3.4
HTTP 301 redirect cache
This informs a browser that a certain resource is permanently available at a different
URL. Trackers could redirect a client from a certain URL, to the same URL but with a
unique ID attached. That second generated URL is then used by the browser when the
client requests the website again. This would allow third parties to assign a unique ID
to a client without using cookies (which is thus an alternative when third party cookies
are blocked), and can be used across domains.
It can be detected by analyzing the
HTTP traﬀic, but can’t be avoided. Deletion requires to clear the browsing cache, and
no significant delay is experienced by the client.
3.4.3.5
TLS session resumption
As explained in Section 2.2, session resumption is used to decrease the latency of per-
forming a full TLS handshake by using a session identifiers, session tickets or pre-shared
key to resume a session. These unique identifiers could be used to track a user [63].
The maximum lifetime of this mechanism is limited to the lifetime of the TLS cache,
which is cleared when the browser is closed. Detecting session resumption requires to
use an external tool such as Wireshark, or the one described in Section 4. Accuracy is
high while delays are low (session resumption is even decreasing overheads). It can’t be
avoided as browsers do not propose an option to disable session resumption. Regarding
cross-instance tracking, it can only perform both cross-window and cross-domain track-
ing, since the session is resumed with trackers across websites. In [62] and [34], the
possibility of sharing session identifiers across host names and applications is discussed;
if implemented, this would make cross-domain tracking even more likely, and not only
available for third parties embedded across these websites. As the cache is cleared when
the browser is closed, it can’t perform cross-session tracking. Its size is limited to the
==PAGE:31==
3.4 Compared tracking mechanisms
24
size of the token (usually a few hundred bytes), and it does not introduce any delays as
it is part of a protocol.
3.4.3.6
QUIC session resumption
QUIC is a protocol designed by Google aiming to improve the transport performance of
HTTPS traﬀic, comparable to TLS. Since it is implemented and used by a big player,
its use is expected to grow over the years [21]. When the client resumes a session, two
tokens are used: server config and source-address token, and can be used similarly
to TLS session tokens to correlate two visits of a user [61].
3.4.3.7
ETags and Last Modified header
They are specific fields of the HTTP Header, initially thought to check whether a cached
web-page stored on the user side is outdated. It has already been used to assign a unique
ID to a user: during the first load, the server assigns a unique identifier to the user in
the ETag or Last Modified HTTP header field of a cached file. Then, every time the user
asks for that resource, instead of downloading it from the server, it will send these field
to know whether the resource needs to be modified (thus re-sending these identifiers).
This mechanism can for example be implemented in invisible pixels (or beacons), and
planted on several websites by third parties. It has been observed that trackers use it
to respawn HTTP and HTML5 cookies [5]. It can be used across domains as the same
file needs to be loaded in separate sites, and can be detected by analyzing the traﬀic, as
performed in [5].
3.4.3.8
HTTP authentication cache
When a user lands on a page, the web-server can ask for authentication by sending a 401
Unauthorized response status. If this mechanism is coupled with a Javascript code such
as the one described [29], the web-server can force an authentication using a username
and password as a unique ID to identify the user. These credentials are cached by the
browser and re-sent every time the web-server sends the 401 Unauthorized response
status. This is done without the user’s knowledge or control, but can be detected by
checking the outgoing requests.
These identifiers are assigned for a specific domain
so can’t be used across them, and introduce a reasonable delay (one round trip to
authenticate a user).
3.4.4
Fingerprinting
Fingerprinting is a tracking mechanism aiming at uniquely identify a user based on the
network, device or applications used or present on the user’s device. Many different
fingerprinting techniques are employed in the wild [25], and can offer different char-
acteristics, based on which instance fingerprinting is applied to. They usually require
==PAGE:32==
3.4 Compared tracking mechanisms
25
Javascript calls, but can also be done using the information acquired through the HTTP
Header or on how a figure is rendered using Web Graphic Library (Web GL). Except for
cross-instance tracking, the different fingerprinting techniques display the same charac-
teristics:
• a fingerprint’s lifetimes can last for as long as the instance (device, browser) is
used;
• it can’t be deleted as it is not stored on the client;
• is fairly easy to detect when monitoring Javascript calls, as it often performs several
to get a more accurate fingerprint;
• avoiding it (by either blocking Javascript all together or using blockers) can impact,
if not block, the browsing experience, but extensions are available to block it;
• there is no defined limit to the amount of information that can be fingerprinted on
the client’s side and sent back to the tracker; but the information can be hashed
on the client’s side and sent back to the server to create a unique ID for a given
fingerprint, so storage size is marked as not applicable.
Accuracy and delays are directly linked: the more information is gathered, the higher
the accuracy, but the higher the delay, as more operations are carried out. The different
fingerprinting techniques and their ability to perform cross-instance tracking will be
further discussed in the following paragraphs.
3.4.4.1
Network and location
This type of fingerprinting is the one with the greatest potential for trackers. In particu-
lar, the study from Mishra and al. [44], the relevance of using IP addresses to track users
is discussed. Based on a data collection performed on 2230 users, the authors showed
that 93% of the users had a unique set of long-lived IP addresses (minimum retention
period of 30 days) that remain stable over time, and could thus be used as a tracking
vector. Nonetheless, [71] highlighted that ”IP addresses are a crucial feature for correlat-
ing devices” and could enable cross-device tracking once associated with other data such
as browsing history. However, it is common for a user to switch IPs (mobile devices), or
even use VPNs, so its accuracy still remains limited. It does not lead to more delays as
IPs are communicated in each packets (thus its detection can be diﬀicult).
3.4.4.2
Device fingerprinting
In a study from 2012, attributes specific to the device, such as fonts present, timezone or
screen-resolution, have already been used to create a unique fingerprint for a limited set of
users (989 users). This allows for cross-browser fingerprinting [7]. Device fingerprinting
can also include clock signals [55], or create unique fingerprint per installed hardware
through the graphic rendering capabilities [10].

3.4.4.3
OS instance fingerprinting:
Similarly, fingerprinting techniques can use the OS functionalities of a device such as
graphic rendering, architecture, and can also obtain results unique per device, thus
allowing cross-browser tracking [10].
3.4.4.4
Browser fingerprinting
Browser fingerprinting maps attributes such as user agent, language, plugins, HTTP
Headers [18], canvas, etc. to create a unique user fingerprint. This technique is already
used in the wild [25, 38]. Other research showed that browser extensions could be an
other way to uniquely identify users [30, 58]. This can be used across windows and
domains, but is limited within each browser. Fingerprinting browser extensions could
moreover offer higher accuracy.
3.5
Color-scale

3.6.1
Application of the comparison framework
Tracking using TLS session resumption, discussed in [63], has overall a poor performance
compared to other tracking mechanisms: its lifetime is only limited to the lifetime of the
TLS cache, which is cleared when the browser is closed. It can’t perform cross-session
tracking or above (cross-browser or cross-device tracking). Its size is limited to the size
of the session token, which spans from 32 bytes for SessionID, to a few hundred bytes
for session tickets and pre-shared keys.
Moreover, if properly enabled (such as with
Firefox, as highlighted in Section 5.2.3.3), deletion is easy, as the user simply needs to
clear the cache (but unfortunately, it is not currently the case for most browsers). It is
also diﬀicult to avoid (no possibility of disabling session resumption on current popular
browsers).
However, it would not be detected or blocked by the traditional tools and extensions,
which focus on the application level, and do not have access to lower level information
regarding the TLS context, as it is handled by the browser. Lower level layers are often
overlooked by research when dealing with online tracking, as pointed out in [71], and
tracking using the TLS protocol could benefit from that lack of visibility.
3.7
Conclusion
This section introduced a comparison framework for tracking mechanisms and applied
it to TLS Session Resumption.
Section 3.2 reviewed the previously published work,
Section 3.3 defines the compared characteristics, Section 3.4 described the considered
tracking mechanisms and individually outlines performance of each tracking mechanism
for a given characteristic, and Section 3.5 describes the color scale used to display the
results. Finally, the results of the comparison can be found in Section 3.6.
This table can be relevant for researchers identifying new tracking mechanisms, as
it allows to compare that new mechanism to other uncovered mechanisms (already used
or identified as potential tracking vectors). It has here been applied to tracking through
TLS session resumption, which was identified as a possible tracking mechanism in [63].
The discussion can be found in Section 3.6.1.

CHAPTER 4
Providing visibility into TLS
session resumption
As highlighted in 3.6.1, one of the strength of tracking using TLS session resumption
is the lack of visibility into that mechanism, which makes it easier for trackers to avoid
detection. The goal of this chapter is to implement a simple interface for a user to observe
when a session resumption happens, as it is now a completely transparent process. The
tool created to display session resumption as it happens will be presented. This tool
is then used in Section 5.2 to study the implementation of TLS session resumption on
browsers’ end. Since user awareness and understanding is a first step towards better
privacy and control, implementing such a tool and showing the mechanism happening
live seems to be a relevant contribution when considering concerns surrounding tracking
using TLS session resumption.
It can also be used by researchers to isolate specific
information (such as structures of the session ticket or pre-shared key used, or their
respective sizes).
4.1
Review of available tools
Tools are already available to monitor network traﬀic. This section will only focus on
tools that can be used on Windows.
Wireshark [68] is a widely-used network protocol analyzer started in 1998, which can
analyze traﬀic on all layers (from IP to application layer such as HTTP). The captured
traﬀic can be browsed live or offline via a GUI, or via the TTY-mode TShark utility.
The eﬀiciency of filters allows to only focus on the packets the user is interested in
(in our case, all packets related to a TLS handshake, and particularly ClientHello and
ServerHello).
WinPcap [67] is a Windows packet capture library which supports Windump [66], the
Windows version of tcpdump. WinPcap is however no longer maintained by developers,
and recommends to use tools that are still updated such as Npcap.
Npcap [47] is Nmap’s project for packet capture library for Windows, based on the
WinPcap library.
==PAGE:38==
4.2 Design of the tool
31
Limitations of off the shelf tools
All these tool allow to capture the traﬀic and check the different information. In Wire-
shark’s GUI, it is also possible to see the session tokens as they are exchanged, but
it requires to extend the different section until finding the right field, which can be
burdensome when several handshakes or resumptions happen in a row.
Moreover, none of these tools provide a specific warning to visualise when session
resumption happens. Our goal is to provide a very simple interface that can make session
resumption visible to a lambda user, without having to apply any filters or expand
packets, or to extensively study TLS to understand how session resumption works and
find it occurring in the captured traﬀic.
4.2
Design of the tool
4.2.1
Design process
The language selected to code the tool was Python.
The initial idea was to have a Python script running which would capture the packets,
parse them, and store the TLS session information in a CSV.
To understand what could be visualised and parsed from a capture traﬀic, a first step
is to capture the traﬀic, browse the web and trigger session resumptions on websites to
see how TLS is implemented and what information to look for in the packets. This was
done using Wireshark, as it is quite straightforward to use the GUI, filter packets, and
see the packets’ structure.
The next step is to parse the captured traﬀic offline, to extract the information to
display. The Python library Scapy was selected, as it allowed to parse the pcap files
produced by Wireshark, and it can also parse the necessary fields (sessionID, session
ticket, pre-shared key) from the TLS layer using the function load_layer('tls').
Once the packets are successfully parsed offline from a pcap file, the next step is to
have both Wireshark running and writing in a file, while the Python script continuously
reads that file and prints the right information. When these both processes worked well
together, the last step was to both capture and parse directly from the Python script.
Wireshark’s capture process was used in order to stay consistent with the working code.
The simplest way to incorporate capture into the working code was by using dumpcap
[22], which is the network traﬀic dump tool implemented for Wireshark, that captures
packets data from the network and writes them to a file. Though the wrapper Pyshark
could have been used to use dumpcap, opting to execute the command directly in the
bash was an easier solution given the simplicity of dumpcap’s command line.
Once both live capture and parsing worked well in the script, the goal was to add
more modularity without forcing an inexperienced user to dive into the code to change
parameters such as the interface of capture or the name of files. A user interface was
created, to select the network interface to capture from, start and stop the capture, or
change file names. To that interface, a logger was added to display session resumption
==PAGE:39==
4.2 Design of the tool
32
when it happens. The Python interface built with Tkinter, which is a library that
allows for minimalist but fast interface development.
The obtained interface allows a user to select the network interface to read traﬀic
from, start and stop the packet capture and parsing, save information into a file, and see
session resumption related mechanisms, such as the creation of a NewSessionTicket by
the server, or the resumption using a pre-shared key or a session ticket, when it happens
live.
4.2.2
Architecture of the tool
When the capture is started, two processes are started in cascade: the Capture process
calls dumpcap to start the capture, and calls the Reading process at the same time,
which, using Scapy, converts the pcap file to the PcapReader object. That objects then
yields packets as they are read. The packets are sent to the Parsing process. If any
interesting information is found (see Section 4.2.4 for details), the parsing process yields
it back to the Reading process to write in the CSV file, while also sending it to the
Notification process. The Notification process prints the event in the Python console,
and also sends it to the Logger. The Queue is then in charge of getting the Logger’s
messages out and into the log frame on the interface.

• only ClientHello, ServerHello and NewSessionTicket TLS messages are considered

• ClientHello and ServerHello are parsed to check the state the extensions session_id,

• in the case of a NewSessionTicket, it simply means that the server is sending a
session ticket to the client for a later resumption, and that event is notified to the
user.
Only the first and last bytes of the session tokens are displayed in the log.
TLSv1.2 - SessionID resumption
This resumption is the most complicated to detect: as explained in 2.2.2.1, both the
ClientHello and the ServerHello messages need to be considered, as well as the protocol
version used by the server: if both use TLSv1.3, the server will simply echo it. If the
server uses TLSv1.2, then it might indicate a resumption. The way to see it will be the
following:
• ClientHello which are not identified as a resumption (no session ticket or pre-shared
key non-empty extension) are logged to the log frame with their session ID through
a Client - NEW - [sessionID] entry;
• if a ServerHello does not contain any TLSv1.3 extensions, it will be flagged as a
TLSv1.2 server;
• if the sessionID from a TLSv1.2 server is not empty, it will also be logged using a
Server - SID - [sessionID] entry.
A resumption by sessionID can be observed if the ClientHello is closely followed by a
ServerHello which uses the same SessionID (see Figure 4.7 for an example).
TLSv1.2 - Session Ticket resumption
The server sends the session ticket in clear, which will appear in a Server - STK -
[sessionticket] entry. If a ClientHello message contains a non-empty session_ticket
extension, it reveals a session resumption attempt and is notified through a Client -
STK - [sessionticket] entry.
TLSv1.3 - Pre-shared key resumption
If a ClientHello message contains a non-empty pre_shared_key extension, it reveals a
session resumption attempt and is also notified with the Client - PSK - [presharedkey]
entry.

an update
When the study Tracking Users across the Web via TLS Session Resumption [63] was
published, TLSv1.3 was still a draft, so only TLSv1.2 session resumption mechanisms
(SessionID and session tickets) and their implementations were studied. The following
section will provide an update on the elements studied in [63] relevant for user’s privacy

• implementation of TLS session resumption on the server side: studies the
lifetime hints of the delivered session tickets and pre-shared keys, as well as the
observed lifetime of session resumption using a delivered token;
• implementation of TLS session resumption on the client’s side: studies
the implementation of session resumption by browsers, for both first and third
parties.
Moreover, the implementation of manual cache clearing has been studied to see if a user
can delete the TLS session cache while keeping the browser open.
The study will be limited to the desktop implementations and will focus on main
browsers available on Windows (Chrome, Firefox, Edge, Opera) and two of their privacy-

the list of Moz Top 500 websites is used instead. The study was performed from April

5.1
Evaluation of server settings
Servers have several roles when it comes to session resumption: they decide how long
a session can be resumed for, accept or not a session token sent by a client, and can
correlate two or more sessions when a client resumes the session, which is the key feature
to use TLS session resumption as a tracking mechanism. Section 5.1.1 describes the
settings evaluated, Section 5.1.2 presents how these settings were evaluated, and Section
5.1.3 discusses the results.

5.1.1.1

On the server side, it is relevant to evaluate the lifetime of sessions. Two lifetimes are

• the lifetime delivered by the server, or lifetime hint, which tells the client how

• the lifetime implemented by the server, or observed lifetime, which is the max-

the session.

• if the protocol is TLSv1.2, it can either be a SessionID or a session ticket; the
distinction is made by checking if the lifetime line is present:
– if so, it is a session ticket and the lifetime is saved;
– if not, it’s a resumption is done using a SessionID and no lifetime is recorded.
5.1.2.2
Collection of observed lifetimes
To gather the real lifetime of a session token, the session token is first saved using the
same command as described above. Then, the connection is regularly re-opened with an
attempt to resume the session using the token given during the first connection, using
the command below.
openssl s_client -connect domain.com:443 -sess_in domain_com.pem
Two methodology are applied when it comes to regular resumption:
• a first consists in resuming the session with increasing delays, to end up reaching
48 hours after the initial handshake: resumption is attempted after 5 seconds, then
30 seconds, 1 minute, 5 minutes, 10 minutes, 15 minutes, 30 minutes, 1 hour, 1
hour 30 minutes, 2 hours, 3 hours, 4 hours, 5 hours, 10 hours, 15 hours, 24 hours,
36 hours, and finally 48 hours. The increasing delay is to get a first overall idea of
the dataset’s real life resumption behavior without over-soliciting the servers with
too regular calls;
• then, a second collection of resumption data is done by attempting a session re-
sumption every 5 minutes on a 24 hour period; this research aims at getting fined-
grained results regarding SessionID, which also needs to be cached on the server
side: this can help determine if regular session resumption has an influence on the
lifetime of the SessionID lifetime on the server side.
For each reconnection, the answer is then saved and parsed to see if the resumption
was successful (Figure 5.2a) or if the server fell back into a new handshake (Figure 5.2b).
Answers are gathered on a period of 24 to 48 hours. The real lifetime is determined by
checking the last time the session token was successfully reused.

5.2.1
Settings evaluated
This section describes and motivate the settings evaluated on the browsers.
5.2.1.1
Lifetime of cached sessions
The lifetime of cached sessions are evaluated for two different resumption mechanisms
(TLSv1.2, session tickets, and TLSv1.3, pre-shared keys). The longer the lifetime, the
easier it is for a website to correlate two user visits. While session tickets can be re-used,
it is recommended not to reuse TLSv1.3 tokens, and most of the evaluated browsers
follow that recommendation. Two parameters are studied:
• lifetime of the cache of TLSv1.2 session tickets: how long a session ticket is re-used
to resume the session with a website before being discarded;
• lifetime of the cache of TLSv1.3 pre-shared keys: since some browsers discard the
pre-shared key after one use, this will evaluate the lifetime of a pre-shared key
before the first use. This test is limited to 48 hours.
5.2.1.2
Resumption with third parties
It is also evaluated if third party session resumption is enabled when the browser loads
the same third party resource from two different websites. This could pose a higher
threat to privacy: a third party could indeed establish a TLS session with a client on
one website, and resume it from an other, which can already correlate two visits; this is
however not suﬀicient in itself, as it would not give information about the websites from
which that session is resumed: as explained in [63], an HTTP referrer or a custom URL
per first party would be required to determine the first party visited by the user.
5.2.1.3
Manual deletion of TLS sessions cached
Browsers allow users to delete navigation data such as cookies or cached images, without
having to close the browsing session. The goal is to see if cached TLS sessions are also
cleared when deleting that data, which helps to define how diﬀicult it is to delete a
potential tracking mechanism.

5.2.2
Methodology
5.2.2.1
Lifetime of TLS sessions caches
For this evaluation, the server described in Section 4.3 has been used, with a consistent
redirection to https://1.tls.monokles.eu/
For session ticket (TLSv1.2):
1. the capture of the traﬀic is started
2. the browser is started and connected to https://1.tls.monokles.eu/
3. a new TLS session is established with both the first and third party and the TLS
session is closed by both client and server (but the tab stays open).
4. when the page is refreshed (after 1 minute), the client resumes the session with both
the first party https://1.tls.monokles.eu/ and with the third party present.
5. this is repeated until the initiation of a new TLS session is noticed: the client did
not resume the session, which shows that the cached TLS session was discarded.
For pre-shared keys (TLSv1.3), the methodology differed: as some browsers use the
pre-shared key only one time, the change of token observed for TLSv1.2 is no longer a
valid sign of a TLS cache being cleared.
1. the browser is started and connected to https://1.tls.monokles.eu/
2. the TLS session is established;
3. the tab is closed before a new resumption occurs: the TLS session is now cached
on the browser;
4. later, the connection is re-opened to see if the session is resumed with https:
//1.tls.monokles.eu/
5. this is repeated by increasing the time between closing and re-opening the tab
until observing a new handshake or reaching 48 hours (since servers do not resume
sessions after 48 hours).
5.2.2.2
Resumption with third parties
As stated in 4.3, sessions established with the server at https://m.tls.monokles.eu are
not resumed with https://n.tls.monokles.eu, where m ̸= n. This shows that both
are considered by the browser as separate websites. This property is used to determine
if session resumption is supported for third parties across websites following the steps
below:
1. the browser is started and connected to https://1.tls.monokles.eu/; the con-
nection to https://thirdparty.monokles.eu/ can be observed.
==PAGE:61==
5.2 Evaluation of client settings
54
2. after 30 seconds, the page is refreshed and the browser is redirected to a random
subdomain https://*.tls.monokles.eu/ which also calls the resources hosted
by https://thirdparty.monokles.eu/;
3. the tool shows if session is resumed with https://thirdparty.monokles.eu/
If the session is resumed, it is considered that the browser allows TLS session resumption
with third parties.
5.2.2.3
Clearing of the TLS cache
Browsers all propose an option to delete navigation data such as ”cookies and other site
data”, and cached content. However, it is not clear whether the TLS sessions cached are
also deleted. This is checked using the following methodology:
• TLS session resumption events are captured using the tool;
• the browser is started and a first connection to https://1.tls.monokles.eu/ is
established;
• the first page refresh is observed to see the first session resumption successfully
carried out;
• the tab is closed (but the browser stays open);
• using the Advanced Settings, all browser data is cleaned for the last hour;
• in a new tab, the browser is reconnected to https://1.tls.monokles.eu/
Using the tool, it can be seen whether the browser reconnected through a new TLS
session, or resumed the session, which shows whether the cached TLS sessions are deleted.

to resume using the same session token for an hour before initiating a full handshake,
except for Firefox, which does it every 15 minutes (instead of the previous 24h observed
in [63]).
TLSv1.3 - Pre-shared key
Only the browser Firefox reuses a the pre-shared key until reaching 15 minutes, while
all other browsers use a pre-shared key only once; on the other hand, Firefox clears
the cache after 15 minutes whether the session token has been used or not. All other
browsers did resume the session with the initially distributed pre-shared key, even after
48 hours.
5.2.3.2
Third party resumption
All browsers allowed for session resumption with third parties, even when it did not
resume the session with a first party, which happened when the browser was redirected
from one subdomain to the other.
5.2.3.3
Clearing manually the TLS cache
From the five browsers tested that support session resumption, only Firefox deleted the
TLS sessions when clearing browsing data. All the others resumed the session even after
the cached was cleared, which shows that the cached TLS sessions were not deleted.
This could actually be used by servers to correlate visits and even respawn cookies1, as
illustrated in Figure 5.11, and explained below.
Respawning cookies with TLS session resumption: Let us imagine the fol-
lowing use-case: a user visits a malicious website A: a first TLS session is established
(session I), and since session resumption is supported, the server sends back a TLS ses-
sion token (abcd) which is cached by the client’s browser. Then, the server also sends
a cookie (1234), and links it to the TLS session token of the client. When the client
browses the website (sending the cookie in each request), the server can link the cookie
to the browsing data collected. Then, the user closes the tab to that website, but needs
to keep the browser open (this could happen if a time consuming operation is happening
in an other tab, such as downloading or uploading a heavy file).
However, the user
would prefer for his visits not to be correlated, so he deletes all cached files and cook-
ies through the parameters. Later, in the same browsing session, he reconnects to the
website A (session II). But since the TLS cache was not cleared, the browser initiates

a session resumption and sends back the session token abcd. The server knows that a
session resumption happened, so it can go check its logs, and set the cookie to the same
value as before (1234). The new data collected during this session with the server can
then easily be correlated to the previous one, as the same cookie is sent in both sessions.

5.2.4
Conclusion
The implementation of TLSv1.3, both on server and client sides, has introduced a note-
worthy evolution of the configurations regarding session resumption, which tends to make
these parameters more tracker-friendly.
On the server side, the lifetime hint given when using pre-shared keys is usually higher
than the one from session ticket, which increases the likelihood of a client resuming the
session during the lifetime of the session token during a period supported by the server.
The client side implementation of TLS session resumption drastically changed since 2018.
All investigated browsers now support third party resumption. Except for Firefox, all
browsers adopt similar behaviors regarding the lifetime of tokens: it is of one hour for
session tickets, and at least 48 hours for single used pre-shared keys (as long as the
browser is not closed); clearing the cache does not delete cached TLS sessions. Firefox
stands out due to differing implementations: the lifetime of both session tickets and
pre-shared keys are of 15 minutes. Pre-shared key, on the other hand, are not single-use.
Moreover, clearing the cache deletes cached TLS sessions. This shows irregular behaviors
across browsers regarding cache and data clearing policies, that were already observed
in a previous study for HTML5 storage [6].

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
TLS session resumption, designed as a way to reduce overheads introduced by a full
TLS handshake, also requires to use a unique identifier, which is assigned by the server
and sent back by the client when resuming the TLS session. These tokens can be of
different nature: session identifiers or session tickets for TLSv1.2, and pre-shared keys
for TLSv1.3. This mechanism allows by its very nature to correlate two visits from a
user. Thus, in 2018, Eric Sy and al. highlighted how and to which extent TLS session
resumption could be used to track users, and showed that average users could be tracked
up to 8 days.
This work builds on top of the previous study, starting with a comparison framework
for tracking mechanisms. The tracking mechanisms are compared on attributes that ap-
pear throughout the literature: technology, lifetime, deletion, detection, accuracy, delay,
avoidability, possibility of cross-instance tracking (an instance being here a window, a
domain, a browsing session, a browser or a device), and finally storage size. This compar-
ison framework highlights the pros and cons of each tracking mechanism, and is applied
to TLS session resumption based tracking mechanism. TLS session resumption provides
interesting attributes, such as scarce research regarding tracking on the transport layer,
high accuracy due to the unique identifier attributed by the server to each client, no ad-
ditional delays to implement the tracking mechanism on the client side, the impossibility
for users to opt-out, and the ability to provide cross-window and cross-domain tracking.
However, closing the browser is suﬀicient to erase the session cache, which does not allow
for cross-session tracking the same way persistent storage does. TLS session resumption
based tracking has thus limited tracking capabilities.
The study continues the implementation of a tool to visualize TLS session resumption,
as no tool currently available to monitor traﬀic focuses specifically on displaying the
exchanges of session tokens, nor highlights when session resumption specific mechanisms
happen. When browsing the Internet using that tool, interesting behaviors such as TLS
session state sharing across domains can be observed and motivates its design.
The implemented tool is then used in the last contribution, which consists in updat-
ing the state of TLS session resumption implementation, now that TLSv1.3 has been
deployed. On the server side, it can be observed that compared to session tickets, re-
sumption using pre-shared key supports longer lifetimes: not only the lifetime hint given
with the pre-shared key is higher, but the lifetime observed when resuming the session
is much higher for pre-shared key than for session tickets: in the studied dataset, 77%
of TLSv1.2 sessions do not exceed 1h30, while 58% of TLSv1.3 websites do resume the
session after 1h30. This can be a higher threat for privacy, as it increases the probability
==PAGE:66==
6 Conclusion
59
of a user reconnecting to the website within the observed lifetime of the session token.
On the client side, popular browsers (Chrome, Opera, Edge) have similar behaviors of
keeping session tickets for a period of 1 hour before deleting them, while pre-shared key,
though being only used once, can be reused even 48 hours after the initial handshake.
Only Mozilla has a different behavior, with a consistent cache clearing of 15 minutes for
both session tokens and pre-shared keys, a reuse of pre-shared keys and a proper deletion
of the TLS cache when deleting browsing data from the browser. As the cache clearing
option available from the majority of major browsers does not delete TLS session cache,
we show how it could lead to cookie respawning.
This study shows that despite providing poor performances to track a user by itself,
mostly due to a lifetime limited to a browsing session, TLS session resumption could,
due to the current implementations of browsers of cache clearing, complete the arsenal
of online tracking mechanisms.
