Abstract 
Alzheimer’s Disease, AD, is one of the most common neurodegenerative diseases and may 
contribute to 60-70 % of the 55 million cases of dementia worldwide. There is no sign that the 
number of AD cases will decline, on the contrary, it is expected to more than double by 2050. 
Today most treatment for AD is only symptomatic with various off-target effects due to low 
penetrability of the blood-brain barrier, BBB, which underlines that methods to cross the brain 
barriers are deeply needed.  
Studies performed by the groups of K. Qvortrup and M. Rosenkilde have shown that the N-terminal 
of a small hydrophobic, SH, membrane protein expressed by the Mumps virus, MuV, is a ligand for 
ADGRA3. When the ligand is bound it can endocytose and thereby facilitate transport across the 
blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier, BCSFB. Thus, this peptide can function as a shuttle peptide for 
drug delivery to the brain. A linker is necessary for the conjugation of the shuttle peptide to an AD 
therapeutic. Due to increased levels of reactive oxygen species, ROS, in the brain a ROS-cleavable 
linker would selectively cleave and release the drug in the brain. 
A diazaborine, DAB, linker was synthesized as a ROS-cleavable linker but challenges in the 
synthesis led to low yields. Through an extensive series of optimization experiments of the triflation 
step of a phenol it was realized that a benzylic alcohol para to the phenol caused the low yields. 
This was overcome by TBDMS-protecting the benzylic alcohol leading to significantly better yields 
and easier purification.

de lave udbytter. Dette blev overvundet ved at TBDMS-beskytte den benzyliske alkohol, som førte 
til signifikant bedre udbytter og nemmere oprensning.

1 Introduction 
Alzheimer’s Disease, AD, is one of the most common neurodegenerative diseases and today more 
than 55 million people worldwide live with dementia, where AD may contribute to 60-70 % of the 
cases [1]. Dr. Aloysius Alzheimer was the first to describe a case of what was later known as AD in 
1907 [2] and since then the pathogenesis of AD has been widely researched. Unfortunately, no cure 
has been found yet and the medicine given to patients remains to be symptomatic therapy [3]. Some 
of these medicines such as the acetylcholinesterase, AChE, inhibitor rivastigmine have shown 
significant improvement at higher doses, but this also led to an increase in adverse effects [3]. The 
increase in adverse effects could belong to the difficulties of selectively deliver drugs to the brain 
and the challenges of crossing the brain barriers [4]. This project aims to develop a method to 
facilitate the delivery of a drug across the brain barriers and selectively deliver a drug in the brain 
for the treatment of AD. This will be done by developing a reactive oxygen species, ROS, cleavable 
linker to be inserted in a drug conjugate allowing facilitated and selective delivery of a drug to the 
brain.  
1.1 Alzheimer’s Disease 
Alzheimer´s Disease is a slow but irreversible degeneration of the brain and amongst the most 
prevalent neurodegenerative diseases in the world and the most common form of dementia. The risk 
of developing AD is influenced both by genetic and environmental factors, but the biggest risk 
factor is age [3,5]. In AD the nerve cells in the brain are destroyed leading to deterioration in 
cognitive function which in the early stage of the disease can cause short term memory loss. As the 
disease progresses and gets worse additional symptoms such as language problems, disorientation, 
mood swings and behavioral issues can be experienced. When AD progresses further it can lead to 
loss of bodily function and eventually death [5].  
Dementia is a worldwide disease and according to WHO more than 60 % of the cases live in low- 
and middle-income countries. Furthermore around 10 million new cases are observed each year and 
as the population gets older the risk of getting dementia increases. The increase is expected to be 
observed mostly in low- and middle-income countries (71 %) and in 2050 the total number of 
people with dementia is predicted to be 132 million [1]. The history and the discovery of AD can be 
seen in the timeline in figure 1 where major findings about AD pathology are listed along with the 
progress in discovery of new drugs for treatment of AD.

1.1.1 Hallmarks of Alzheimer’s Disease 
Extracellular amyloid plaques 
Amyloid beta, Aβ, protein aggregates found in the extracellular matrix in the brain is one of the 
hallmarks of AD and is used in the diagnosis of AD. The Aβ protein is a small protein made of 40 
or 42 amino acid residues, which is released from the parent amyloid precursor protein, APP. APP 
is expressed in various tissues but is predominantly found in the synapsis of nerve cells where it 
acts as an important part of synapse formation, iron export etc. [7]. Mutations in the APP, presenilin 
1, PSEN 1, or PSEN 2 genes can increase Aβ production and promote plaque formation causing 
autosomal dominant early-onset familial AD, FAD. On the other hand, both genetic (such as ApoE4 
and TREM2) and non-genetic (such as diabetes and obesity) risk factors that can cause late-onset 
sporadic Alzheimer’s disease, SAD, have also been identified to increase the generation of Aβ. 
Furthermore, risk factors can also cause an impairment in the clearance mechanisms of Aβ, which 
may also contribute to the observed accumulation of Aβ [6].  
Extensive research has been conducted to further understand the mechanism behind the formation 
of these aggregates within the brain of AD patients. This led to “the amyloid cascade hypothesis” 
made by Hardy and Higgins who proposed that the Aβ plaques in the brain were the initial event of 
the progression and development of AD [8]. This resulted in a focus in research towards targeting 
Aβ plaques in treatment of AD. However, recent clinical trials have shown little to no results in Aβ 
plaques targeting therapies and thereby casting doubt on this hypothesis [5,7,9]. The hypothesis is 
yet not disproven but researchers are beginning to investigate new approaches to treat AD. The flow 
of events during the progression of AD according to the amyloid cascade hypothesis are listed in 
figure 2.

The deposition of Aβ plaques can facilitate neurodegeneration which leads to neurotoxicity. The 
biological mechanism by this is complex and not yet fully understood and multiple mechanisms 
have been proposed as a possible explanation [11]. It is known that Aβ oligomers can decrease the 
synaptic density in AD brains as well as enhance long-term synaptic depression in a rodent 
hippocampus [10]. This has been proposed as some of the downstream consequences from the 
interconnected up and down-regulations of several kinases and phosphatases [11]. 
 
Intracellular neurofibrillary tangles 
Intracellular neurofibrillary tangles, NFTs, has been associated with the progression of AD and 
forms from precipitation of hyperphosphorylated tau proteins. Tau proteins are highly expressed in 
neurons and are important because they have the capability to stabilize the neuronal microtubules. 
Hyperphosphorylation of tau proteins leads to the instability of microtubules which causes them to 
break down and accumulate the tau proteins intracellularly [7,12,13]. The destabilization of 
microtubules is illustrated in figure 3 showing how hyperphosphorylation of tau protein leads to the 
destabilization of microtubules and ultimately the formation of NFTs.

A change in the cellular signaling can result in an imbalance in the activity of various kinases and 
phosphatases leading to the hyperphosphorylation of tau proteins. In AD this imbalance can be 
triggered by the Aβ plaques which may explain why the research has been focusing on Aβ plaques 
before focusing on tau proteins [14]. Several studies have also shown that a dysfunction in the tau 
protein alone is sufficient to cause neurodegeneration and that it can happen in the absence of Aβ 
plaques. For example, mutations within the microtubule-associated protein tau gene were found to 
cause frontotemporal dementia [15,16]. Tau pathology is known to activate microglial cells [3] 
which can phagocyte and neutralize pathogens in the brain. The numbers of microglial cells are 
seen to increase in the promotion of neuronal damage [17]. An imbalance in the signaling between 
synapses and microglial cells can lead to a dysfunction in microglial cells resulting in incorrect 
engulfment [18] and overproduction of ROS [19]. To prevent the progression of neurodegeneration 
different treatment strategies have been proposed for AD and a few drugs are already in use in the 
treatment of AD. 
1.1.2 Treatment of Alzheimer's Disease 
When neurodegeneration occurs in AD the first phase is called mild cognitive impairment, MCI. 
From here the cognitive level is readily declining and pharmacological treatment is necessary. Until 
recently there were no approved therapies that directly target the pathology of AD to reverse the 
cognitive decline and cure the disease. The existing treatment strategies are mostly symptomatic 
and works either by being an agonist of the cholinergic system or being an antagonist of the N-
methyl-D-aspartate, NMDA, receptor. The current main strategy is to increase the level of 
acetylcholine by administering compounds that inhibit acetylcholinesterase, the enzyme that 
catalyzes the degradation of acetylcholine. The three currently FDA-approved drugs to inhibit

Recently two new monoclonal antibodies, MABs, aducanumab (2021) and lecanumab (2023), have 
been approved by the FDA for treatment of AD. These work by targeting the Aβ aggregates and are 
the first disease modifying therapies for AD, seen to slow down the clinical decline. The positive 
trials of the anti-amyloid MABs support the amyloid cascade hypothesis stating amyloid as an 
important target for AD drug development. Despite the successes of the trials, amyloid-related 
imaging abnormalities, ARIA, has been observed and patients must be closely monitored during 
treatment initiation to prevent severe side effects. Currently the anti-amyloid MABs are 
administered intravenously at 2- to 4-week intervals and the development of a way to lower the 
frequency of administration and thereby decrease the likelihood of causing ARIA is an important 
goal for future work [6,20]. 
1.1.3 Future treatments 
BACE1 was in 1999 identified as the enzyme responsible to produce Aβ. Since then, a lot of 
research has been put into developing inhibitors of BACE1 to identify a new drug for the treatment 
of AD. Several drugs have made it into the clinical trials, but multiple obstacles have prevented 
these drugs from reaching the market. The structure of BACE1 is quite similar to other proteases 
making it difficult to selectively target BACE1 to avoid off-target effects. Furthermore, the active 
site of BACE1 is relatively large making it difficult for the small molecules tested to fully occupy 
and block the active site. Amongst other concerns it is important to make sure that the drug can 
penetrate the BBB to make sure that the drug reaches the brain [5,6].  
Another interesting potential therapeutic target is the γ-secretase inhibitors, GSIs, because of their 
ability to inhibit the production of Aβ. The developed GSIs which have made it to clinical trials 
have not shown too promising results due to their action being too general. This results in them 
acting on other proteins such as Notch which is involved in regulating cell-fate decisions, and 
thereby causing various off-target effects [6].  
1.2 Drug delivery to the brain 
Delivery of drugs to the brain for treatment of central nervous system, CNS, diseases is a challenge 
since the brain is well protected by multiple barriers separating CNS from the blood. These barriers 
are essential for CNS to maintain a balanced ion flux, as the neurons in the brain use various 
chemical- and electrical signals for cell:cell communication submitted via the movement of small 
ions across the membranes. Too big fluctuations will cause unprecise and unreliable communication 
between the cells. In addition to maintaining the homeostasis, the barriers are allowing the influx of 
several essential brain nutrients such as glucose and amino acids as well as efflux of potential

There are three main brain barriers: The blood-brain barrier, BBB, formed by microvascular 
endothelial cells at the cerebral capillaries, the blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier, BCSFB, composed 
of epithelial cells at the choroid plexus and the arachnoid barrier which is the middle layer of the 
meninges sealing off CNS from the extracellular fluids [21,22]. Only the BBB and the BCSFB will 
be discussed in this project. The three different brain barriers and their locations in the brain are 
illustrated in figure 7. 
 
 
1.2.1 The blood-brain barrier 
The BBB regulates the passage of water-soluble molecules between the blood and the brain. The 
structure of the BBB is composed by capillary endothelial cells, astrocytes, pericytes, and neurons. 
The endothelial cells, ECs, distinct themselves from other endothelial cells by their expression of 
tight junctions and by the absence of fenestrations, thereby ensuring maintenance of homeostasis in 
the brain. This makes out the physical barrier of the BBB. Furthermore, the endothelial cells express 
a variety of metabolizing enzymes within the barrier such as CYP450s that create a second 
enzymatic barrier ready to metabolize potential toxins [21,22,23].

parental administration of therapeutic antibodies the levels in the brain was only 0.01-0.1 % of 
those found in the plasma [24]. In some cases, this may be enough to elicit a pharmacological 
effect, but it is clear that a higher proportion of drug in the brain would be beneficial to lower the

dose needed and also limit potential side effects. There are different ways for a drug to cross the 
BBB to enter the brain from the bloodstream and the main ways are passive diffusion, active/carrier

Passive diffusion 
Many lipid-soluble molecules are able to cross the BBB by passive diffusion. To describe the 
lipophilicity of a molecule the distribution coefficient, logD, can be determined by the molecule’s 
distribution in an aqueous and a lipophilic phase, typically water and octanol, at pH 7.4. 
Furthermore, the size and weight (< 400 Da) of the molecule is crucial for its BBB permeability 
because it is believed that the diffusion happens through the formation of pores in the phospholipid 
bilayer. Rotatable bonds and the possible formation of multiple hydrogen bonds (<5 hydrogen bond 
doners and <10 hydrogen bond acceptors) can also be a factor that reduces the molecule’s 
permeability through the BBB [21,22,26].  
Active efflux 
On the luminal endothelial membrane of the BBB ATP-binding cassette, ABC, proteins are 
expressed which are ATP-driven efflux pumps. The primary efflux transporters in the BBB are the 
P-glycoprotein, Pgp, the Multidrug Resistance-associated Proteins, MRPs, and Breast Cancer 
Resistance Protein, BCRP. These pumps facilitate the efflux of various metabolites, toxins, and 
therapeutic agents thereby limiting the drugability because a therapeutic dose in the brain cannot be 
maintained due to the constant efflux [22].  
Carrier mediated transcytosis 
Water-soluble nutrients and vitamins do not significantly cross the BBB by passive diffusion and 
therefore these molecules need transport via specific carrier mediated transcytosis, CMT, systems. 
These specialized transport proteins are crucial to reach the nutrient balance needed to maintain 
normal brain function. The BBB CMT systems include the glucose transporter GLUT1 and the

Receptor mediated transcytosis 
Larger peptides and proteins, containing several peptide bonds, limits the use of CMT systems to 
cross the BBB and has to go through receptor mediated transcytosis, RMT. Besides larger peptides 
also hormones and growth factors have to enter CNS via RMT. The endocytic mechanism is called 
transcytosis and is a vesicular transport system. Here ligands bind to a specific receptor which 
triggers the endocytic event. The ligand and its receptor are internalized into a vesicle, directed 
across the cell and exocytosed at the opposite side. Lastly the ligand dissociates from the receptor to 
enter the brain [22,27,28].  
Adsorptive mediated transcytosis 
Another vesicular transport system is the adsorptive mediated transcytosis, AMT, which works 
quite similarly to RMT. Instead of having a receptor binding to its specific ligand, AMT is used to 
transport large and positively charged molecules across the BBB. This is done by binding to 
specific cell surface binding site which induce endocytosis and transcytosis to deliver the intact 
molecule to the brain. The endothelial cells in the BBB are able to direct the endosomes away from 
lysosomes during transcytosis which is important to deliver intact proteins to the brain [22,28].  
These different mechanisms for crossing the BBB have all been well researched but still more than 
98 % of small-molecule drugs are excluded by the BBB. Extensive achievements have been made 
for multiple delivery techniques, such as intranasal administration, ligand conjugation, and 
membrane coating, with several FDA-approved trials. Despite of these advances there is still a long 
way before the general application of these strategies and further studies must be made [29].  
The blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier 
Instead of entering CNS through the BBB another possible solution could be by crossing the 
BCSFB. The BCSFB is located at the choroid plexuses in the lateral, third and fourth ventricles of 
the brain [26]. Here the tight junctions between the epithelial cells protects the brain from toxins. 
The epithelial cells in the capillaries are relatively permeable to small molecules and the various 
transport mechanisms allow the transport of ions and nutrients across the BCSFB. In contrast to the 
BBB the BCSFB is way less explored, but it is known that different bacteria and viruses enter the 
CNS via the BCSFB [30,31]. Therefore, drug delivery through the BCSFB could be an attractive 
alternative for future drug delivery by mimicking the transport mechanism of the bacteria or 
viruses.

1.2.3 Shuttle peptide 
Mumps virus, MuV, is a highly neurotopic virus that is known to infect the CNS in around half of 
the cases of infection [32].  Only little research has been done and the pathology is not fully 
understood but it is suggested that MuV enters the brain through the BCSFB [31].  MuV encodes a 
small hydrophobic, SH, membrane protein and studies have showed that this protein is not essential 
for viral replication [33,34]. Studies performed by the groups of M. Rosenkilde and K. Qvortrup 
suggest that the solvent exposed N-terminal of the MuV SH is a ligand for ADGRA3, former 
GPR125, [35] which is a G protein-coupled receptor, GPCR, and highly expressed in the BCSFB 
[36]. The endogenous ligand of ADGRA3 has not yet been found but a study shows that ADGRA3 
is located on the cell surface and undergoes endocytosis [37]. The SH peptide has then been 
proposed as a possible shuttle peptide for delivery of drugs to the brain and has been patented by the 
groups of M. Rosenkilde and K. Qvortrup [35]. To avoid off-target effects a cleavable linker can be 
added between the shuttle peptide and the drug to stabilize the conjugate in the bloodstream. Thus, 
it is important that the drug is not active when bound to the linker to avoid off-target effects. When 
the shuttle peptide has delivered the drug to the brain a linker should self-immolate and release the 
drug [38]. This could be done by making the linker susceptible to ROS since an increased level of 
ROS is found in the brain as the brain has a natural increased oxygen consumption leading to a 
larger production of ROS which is further elevated in AD patients [39].  
1.2.4 ROS 
Reactive oxygen species, ROS, is a term for compounds which has been formed by incomplete 
reduction of oxygen. The main species include the superoxide anion, O2•−, hydrogen peroxide, 
H2O2, and hydroxyl radical, •OH, which are all important for living organisms. Cells can produce 
ROS at different locations, but the major site is in the mitochondria where electrons from the 
respiratory chain can react with O2 to generate ROS. Generation of ROS is crucial for the cell 
because ROS can be used as signaling molecules by i.e. reaction with cysteine residues and thereby 
regulate certain biological activities. To control the amount of ROS within the cell antioxidants are 
crucial. A dysfunction in the antioxidant system or the mitochondria can cause oxidative stress by 
the overproduction of ROS which can trigger disruption within the cell or cell death. In a number of 
neurodegenerative diseases such as AD, an excessive amount of ROS is generated leading to further 
neurodegeneration [40,41]. We suggest utilizing the increased level of ROS in AD as a trigger for 
brain specific drug release by designing a linker that is susceptible to cleavage by ROS but stable 
when circling in the bloodstream. A drug delivery system could consist of a biomolecule facilitating 
the transport, a ROS-sensitive linker, and a drug of interest which in the presence of increased ROS 
levels would release the drug as illustrated in figure 9.

1.2.5 Aryl boronates

1.2.6 Diazaborines 
Diazaborines are 6-membered heterocyclic rings containing a boron atom, two nitrogen atoms, and 
three carbon atoms. They were discovered in the 1960’s and found to have antimicrobial properties 
and in the 1980’s they were found to induce morphological changes in bacteria and having potential 
as a broad-spectrum antibiotic. Years later the mechanism was revealed as an inhibition of the last 
step of the fatty acid synthesis where the boron atom is an important factor [42]. The general 
chemical structure of a DAB can be seen in figure 11.

1.2.8 The linker 
This project aims to synthesize a DAB moiety attached to a PEG spacer with an azide handle in the 
end at one side and a nitrophenyl moiety at the other end as an analogue for a drug. The PEG spacer 
enhances the solubility of the conjugate [51] and the azide handle makes it possible to react it with 
an alkyne functionalized shuttle peptide through an azide-alkyne cycloaddition [52]. The 
nitrophenyl moiety is added as a UV-active model drug for measuring the release rate. The 
nitrophenyl moiety is attached to the DAB by a carbamate unit to increase the release rate by the 
formation of CO2. It would be interesting to see if and how the ring substituents influence the 
release rate. It was chosen to compare the reference unsubstituted DAB with a DAB containing a 
fluor atom in the ortho position and a DAB with a methyl group in ortho position to see if it is better 
to have an electron donating group or an electron withdrawing group in the ortho position.  
This conjugate should be stable in the bloodstream. Under the increased ROS level in the brain, the 
susceptible boron atom in the DAB will be attacked by ROS revealing the phenol which will 
undergo 1,6-elimination to release CO2 and the drug.

2.1 Full synthetic route 
The synthesis of the DAB linker has taken reference in the research done by António et al [47]. 
Based on the six-step synthesis of the DAB, it is relevant to synthesize different DAB analogues 
and test the release rate of the different synthesized DABs. This would allow the understanding of 
the contribution of the substituents on the release rate. The synthesis starts with an alkylation of 
salicylaldehyde for the reference DAB, which for the fluor analogue starts with 3-
fluorosalicylaldehyde and for the methyl analogue with 3-methylsalicylaldehyde. In the next step 
the phenol is converted to a triflate thus making it into a good leaving group. After this next step the 
aldehyde is protected by making it into an acetal with ethylene glycol. The following step is the 
addition of the pinacol protected boron by the elimination of the triflate group which is done with 
palladium as the catalyst. Then the benzylic alcohol is reacted to transform it into a carbamate 
containing a UV-active nitrophenyl group. The protecting groups are then removed by acid, 
revealing the boronic acid and the aldehyde which directly reacts with the hydrazine end of the 
azide handle. This provides the final DAB and the release rate can then be tested. The full synthesis 
route can be seen below in figure 13. An overview of all isolated compounds can be found in

2.2 Synthetic steps 
2.2.1 Electrophilic aromatic substitution  
The first step in the synthesis is the addition of a hydroxymethyl group in the para position to the 
phenol in an electrophilic aromatic substitution. The phenol group donates electron density into the 
aromatic ring and thereby directs the incoming group to the ortho/para in the following electrophilic 
aromatic substitution. One of the ortho positions is not available due to the aldehyde and in the 
desired product the molecule is containing a hydroxymethyl group in the para position, which is 
most likely also the most reactive position because of steric hindrance in the ortho positions. The 
electrophilic aromatic substitution happens by reacting with formaldehyde in HCl and due to the 
low boiling point of formaldehyde, this was done in a capped microwave vial to make sure that the 
formaldehyde does not evaporate from the reaction mixture [53]. The reaction goes through a 
chloromethylated intermediate, formed through the exchange of a chloride with water. This is 
converted back to the hydroxymethyl group via a following hydrolysis. A proposed mechanism for 
this reaction can be seen in figure 14. The addition of the hydroxymethyl group happens faster upon 
addition of a Lewis acid, but it is not necessary [54].  
  
Electrophilic aromatic substitution with elevated temperature 
The hydroxymethylation of salicylaldehyde was performed by the addition of salicylaldehyde to a 
suspension of paraformaldehyde in concentrated HCl in a capped microwave vial. The reaction was 
heated to 80 °C to increase the energy of the system and formation of the desired product. The 
reaction is a two-phase system and the bottom layer, which solidified when cooling down the 
reaction, contained the chloromethyl intermediate and this was then isolated, mixed with water, and 
refluxed to convert the chloromethylated intermediate into the desired hydroxylated product. LC-
MS analysis implied the formation of the dialkylated product where the addition of the 
hydroxymethyl group had occurred at both ortho and para position which was later confirmed by 
NMR. The reaction conditions are summed up in table 1.

The triflation of the phenol had previously caused problems in the group of K. Qvortrup and only a

3 Further studies 
Making a ROS-cleavable linker for specific delivery of medicine to the brain is an exciting research 
field but more research is needed to fully understand if it can work in practice. Synthesizing 
different DABs to find a structure with an optimal release rate is important and the study of the 
effect of different substituent patterns on the aromatic ring will be an important contribution to this. 
When a DAB with an optimal release rate has been discovered in vitro studies can be made 
followed by in vivo studies.  
3.1 Full synthesis with TBDMS-protected benzylic alcohol 
It would be interesting to uncover why the TBDMS-protected benzylic alcohol was partly 
deprotected in the aldehyde protection step. TLC implied no deprotection during the reaction so this 
must have happened during either the workup or the purification by flash chromatography. The 
reaction was quenched by NaHCO3 and not washed with acid making it unlikely that the 
deprotection happened during the workup. It is usually not seen that the silica in flash 
chromatography is acidic enough to deprotect TBDMS ethers, but a little base could be added to the 
eluent to catch potential acidic protons.  
If the cause of the deprotection is uncovered, good yields can be obtained which can be used for the 
last three steps of the synthesis. So far, this synthetic route suggests a higher overall yield than the 
original synthesis route thereby making the individual steps simpler due to the increased amounts of 
compound. Conducting the borylation reaction would also reveal if the TBDMS-protection have a 
positive effect on the yield or the purification or if similar results to the original synthesis are 
obtained.  
After the borylation reaction the benzylic alcohol will have to be deprotected to be able to perform 
the carbamate formation and 2-(4-nitrophenyl)ethanamine attachment. This can easily be done in 
mild acidic conditions or by using fluoride ion sources such as tetra-n-butylammonium fluoride, 
TBAF. The addition of fluoride ions leads to the coordination of F to Si which then breaks the Si-O 
bond leaving the alkoxide that can be protonated in an aqueous workup.

3.3 In vivo studies 
To show that the full conjugate made of drug, linker and shuttle peptide is active both 
pharmacodynamically and pharmacokinetically the conjugate must be fully synthesized. The DAB 
should then be synthesized with a small molecule drug that contains either a hydroxy- or a primary 
amine group. Both the AD drugs memantine and galantamine could be possible drugs that could be 
attached to the linker.  
When this has been established the linker with attached drug can be reacted with the alkyne 
functionalized shuttle peptide to yield the full conjugate. This is done by coupling the azide and 
alkyne in an azide-alkyne cycloaddition.  
The full conjugate can then be tested in vivo, possibly in a mouse model, to observe if the conjugate 
exhibits stability in the bloodstream and a controlled release in the brain when delivering the drug. 
This method of facilitated transport of an AD drug to the brain is not limited to AD drugs and can 
be broadened to other CNS active drugs for their selective delivery to the brain.

4 Conclusion 
This project aimed to develop a ROS-cleavable linker for facilitated drug delivery in the treatment 
of AD. This linker structure enables the conjugation of a shuttle peptide which selectively can cross 
the BCSFB with an amine- or hydroxy-functionalized drug. Due to the ROS susceptibility of the 
linker the conjugate is hypothesized to be stable in the bloodstream and only release the drug in the 
brain thereby decreasing the off-target effects of the drug. 
The designed ROS-cleavable linker must contain a ROS-sensitive part which triggers a release 
mechanism, a site for drug conjugation, and an azide group for conjugation of the shuttle peptide. 
The diazaborine structure was chosen due to its stability and because the boron atom is susceptible 
for attacks by ROS leading to a 1,6-elimination which releases the drug. An amine- or hydroxy-
functionalized drug can be conjugated to the DAB by a carbamate- or carbonate group respectively. 
For conjugation of the shuttle peptide an azide group can be incorporated in the formation of the 
DAB which can be used in an azide-alkyne cycloaddition with an alkyne-functionalized shuttle 
peptide.  
The diazaborine linker was synthesized, although impure, but challenges and low yields especially 
in the triflation step of the synthesis were prioritized leading to a series of experiments attempting 
different methods to optimize this reaction. Neither different triflating agents, different bases, 
activation of the triflating agent or heat seemed to affect the reaction to give higher yields. It was 
hypothesized that the benzylic alcohol was competing for the triflating agent and causing the 
formation of different polymeric unwanted biproducts. This was confirmed when triflating on a 
TBDMS-protected benzylic alcohol which was conducted in good yields (77 %). In general, 
TBDMS-protecting the benzylic alcohol also led to easier purification yielding very pure fractions 
by flash chromatography. 
These findings can be utilized by others to synthesize DABs more easily and with higher yields. 
DABs with different substituents can be synthesized to figure out the substituent contribution to the 
release rate and thereby developing an optimal linker. The linker is not restricted to the delivery of 
AD drugs but might be beneficial in the treatment of other neurological diseases.

5 Experimental 
5.1 General Methods 
All chemical reagents and solvents were obtained from commercial suppliers and stored according 
to the suppliers' recommendations and used without any further purification.  
Solvents used in reactions and during purification were of HPLC grade and dry solvents were 
obtained from a PureSolv™ MD-7 Solvent Purification System, Innovative Technology with Al2O3 
as the stationary phase. In experiments where dry solvents were necessary, glassware were flame-
dried to insure the removal of moisture.  
Solvent removal in vacuo in experiments was done by a Heidolph Laborota 4000 rotary evaporator 
which is efficient under reduced pressure at temperatures ranging from 20 °C to 50 °C. Further 
removal of solvent traces in vacuo was removed through a Schlenk line connected to a membrane 
pump.  
All reactions conducted were monitored by either TLC, UPLC-MS (ESI) or both. When using TLC 
it was conducted on Merck Aluminum TLC plates, silica gel 60 coated with fluorescent indicator 
F254. TLC plates were visualized by either UV-light or stained by KMnO4. UPLC-MS analysis was 
performed using a Waters AQUITY UPLC system equipped with PDA and SQD electrospray 
ionization, ESI, MS detector. Column: Kinetex 1.7 µm XB-C18, 2.1 x 50 mm. Column temperature 
50 °C. Flowrate: 0.6 mL/min. Eluent A: 0.1 % HCOOH in H2O, eluent B: 0.1 % HCOOH in 
MeCN. The gradient was linear (5 % B to 100 % B) in 2.4 or 4.8 min, hold 0.1 min, total run time 
2.6 or 5.0 min. 
Purification of compounds was done with flash chromatography using glass columns of various 
sizes depending on the compounds and the amount of compound. The columns were packed with 
Merck Geduran® Si 60 (40-63 µm) silica gel as the stationary phase and the eluent system is 
specified for each purification in the procedures. 
All isolated compounds were characterized by 1H NMR and 13C NMR which were recorded on a 
Brucker Ascend spectrometer with a Prodigy cryoprobe operation at 400 MHz for 1H NMR and 101 
MHz for 13C NMR. DMSO-d6 was used as the solvent. Chemical shifts, δ, are reported in parts per 
million, ppm, downfield from tetramethylsilane, TMS, (= 0 ppm) using DMSO-d6 as the internal 
standard (1H: 2.50 ppm, 13C: 39.52 ppm). Coupling constants, J, are reported in Hz and 
multiplicities are reported as singlet, s, doublet, d, triplet, t, quartet, q, doublet of doublets, dd, 
triplet of doublets, td, or multiplets, m.  
Melting points are measured by using a Stuart melting point apparatus SMP30 which is capable of 
heating from 20 °C to 400 °C and ramping from 0.5 °C/min to 3 °C/min.  
Infrared spectroscopy, IR, spectra are recorded on a Bruker Alpha P FT-IR spectrometer and 
reported in cm-1.
