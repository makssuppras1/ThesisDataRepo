Towards a More Empathetic Virtual Self
Abstract
This thesis, undertaken as part of the REHyB project, explored the potential of integrating
Human Digital Twins (HDTs) with conversational agents (CAs) to create more personalized and
empathetic virtual representations of human individuals. A novel system was developed, combining
retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) architecture with an HDT database encompassing person-
ality, preferences, memories, and physiological data.
To enhance trust, the system employs an
advanced retrieval mechanism that emphasizes recency, emotional importance, and semantic simi-
larity. The proposed system customizes responses based on the individual’s unique experiences and
communication style.
Initial accuracy evaluations demonstrate the HDT-enhanced system’s superiority in personal-
ization while minimizing hallucinations by grounding responses in the knowledge base. However,
pilot study results point to a perceptual shift: the HDT-enhanced system was seen as more person-
alized and authentic in conversations with familiar individuals, while traditional GPT-based CAs
were perceived as more specific and trustworthy. Future research should refine the experimental
design to further evaluate the system and explore the intriguing challenge of balancing human-like
qualities with perceived trustworthiness.
Beyond the specific results of this thesis, the author believes the primary contribution lies in
the proposed system design. While current AI capabilities may still exhibit artificial qualities, rapid
advancements in LLMs suggest a future where AI writing could become indistinguishable from
human-generated text. This project lays the groundwork for a unique bridge between the two: the
system’s human-centric memory modeling would shape the essence of the AI’s responses, while the
advanced language model would ensure a natural flow and style mirroring the individual.

Introduction
In recent years, the development of Human Digital Twins (HDTs) – dynamic virtual repre-
sentations that integrate an individual’s physical, biological, and behavioral characteristics – has
accelerated significantly. This advancement directly supports healthcare’s broader trend towards
personalized medicine, offering the potential to transform diagnosis, treatment strategies, and med-
ication regimens [1] [2].
HDTs possess transformative potential in rehabilitation, empowering therapists to make ev-
idence based, tailored treatment decisions through continuous patient health and behavior mon-
itoring. Additionally, the integration of HDTs with sophisticated avatars presents new frontiers
in Human-Computer Interaction (HCI). This technology could significantly enhance communica-
tion for individuals with communicative limitations, particularly in stroke rehabilitation, where
survivors often experience challenges in conveying their needs and emotions [3]. However, realiz-
ing such enhanced functionality necessitates the development of accurate avatars, language models,
and high-fidelity behavior models capable of mapping real-time behavioral and vital sign data to
authentic and responsive verbal communication.
Inspired by the work of Park et al. [4], this project, undertaken as part of the REHyB initiative
[5], seeks to advance the creation of more empathetic virtual representations of the self. This work
endeavors to expand the traditional concept of HDTs by enriching them with detailed personal
information and routine memories. We envision a system that encapsulates an individual’s core
traits, preferences, conversational patterns, smartwatch-monitored vital signs, and self-reflective
insights.
To create more advanced and human-like conversational agents (CAs), we propose a novel ap-
proach: integrating the precision and breadth of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) with the
nuanced retrieval mechanisms of human memory. This project will develop a CA agent built on a
RAG architecture and populated with HDT data, enabling it to personalize responses to user mes-
sages and foster trust. In the pursuit of empathetic and human-like communication, physiological
and psychological principles will be incorporated into the retrieval function. This hybrid approach
will ensure the system can access relevant information with selection criteria that emphasize rele-
vance, importance, and recency.
This project outlines three core goals.
Firstly, we seek to develop a comprehensive HDT
database that reflects the intricate memory architecture of the human brain. This database will
encompass core memories (personality traits, preferences) and routine episodic memories (conversa-
tions, vital signs) to maximize personalization. Self-reflection modules will be integrated within the
HDT to enable the interpretation of information – a crucial capability frequently absent in main-
stream Artificial Intelligence (AI) [6]. Neuroscience will be a crucial guide, with database design
mirroring the interplay between the hippocampus, neocortex, and amygdala for the vast storage
and retrieval of information [7] [8] [9] [10].
The second goal is developing a powerful Retrieval Function that extracts contextually relevant
memories in response to user queries. While we will leverage RAG to mitigate unsubstantiated
responses [11], we will go further by emulating the complexities of human memory processing. The
function will prioritize recent, impactful, or otherwise highly-relevant memories while selectively
attenuating older, less significant memories.
This approach ensures accuracy while retaining a
human-like understanding of memory importance.
The third goal is to seamlessly integrate the database and Retrieval Function. Our HDT will
generate responses rooted in retrieved memories and refined to reflect conversational style. Imagine

Theoretical Background
This study evaluates how the inclusion of HDT data into CAs designed to mimic specific
individuals can improve response customization and conversational style. As such, this research
integrates diverse areas, including CA, RAG, and the mechanisms of brain memory storage and
retrieval. This section provides a concise overview of these fields, introducing the reader to their
fundamental concepts, while next Section 3 will cover the latest advancements.
2.1
Conversational Agents
Conversational agents, often known as chatbots, are advanced computational systems designed
to engage in human-like conversations with users through text or voice.
Employed in various
industries, including customer service, education, and entertainment, CAs play a pioneering role
in modern digital communication. Their development relies heavily on advancements in Natural
Language Processing (NLP) and machine learning for increasingly sophisticated capabilities.
The introduction of the transformer architecture by Vaswani et al. (2017) [12] has revolution-
ized the development of CAs. Unlike prior models, transformers process words in parallel rather
than sequentially. This accelerates computation and significantly enhances the model’s capacity to
comprehend and generate natural language by understanding complex conversational contexts (see
Figure 1).
Figure 1: The Transformer - model architecture (image by Vaswani et al. 2017[12])

Two core elements define the transformer architecture: self-attention and position-wise feed-
forward networks. Self-attention allows the model to dynamically assess the importance of different
words within a sentence, improving its understanding of context and word relationships. Position-
wise feed-forward networks apply sequential transformations to refine the input for optimal task
performance.
The transformer architecture has significantly enhanced the ability of CAs to generate human-
like responses. Training these models on massive text datasets allows them to learn complex lan-
guage patterns, producing contextually relevant and linguistically fluent responses. The quality of a
CA depends heavily on the size and diversity of its training data and the complexity of its underly-
ing model. Transformers excel in handling larger datasets, enabling the development of responsive
and adaptive CAs suitable for diverse scenarios.
2.1.1
Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)
RAG is a framework designed to improve the accuracy and reliability of large language mod-
els (LLMs) by integrating them with diverse data sources. These sources can include relational
databases, unstructured document collections, streaming internet data, and various media formats.
Introduced by Lewis et al. (2020) [13], RAG operates by representing data as vectors stored within
a vector database. Upon receiving a user query, the vector database identifies and retrieves relevant
contextual information. This information is then provided to the LLM, enhancing its ability to
generate accurate and contextually appropriate responses (see Figure 2).
Figure 2: RAG integrates an embedding model to retrieve relevant data from a vector database,
augmenting a LLM to generate informed answers based on user queries.
RAG initiates a retrieval phase upon receiving a user query, sourcing relevant information from
an external database. This retrieved information augments the language model, furnishing it with
pertinent context to generate more precise and informative responses. Consequently, RAG systems
prove particularly valuable for question-answering tasks and content generation scenarios where
access to specific or up-to-date information is crucial.

2.2
How Human Memory Works
2.2.1
Types of Memory
Memory is the process of encoding, storing and retrieving experiences and knowledge, and is
the core center of our cognitive capabilities. But not all memories that we store are the same. There
are several different types of memories, some of them are transitory and others last for a lifetime.
The shortest type is known as Short-Term Working memory and can last just a few seconds.
It allows us to temporarily store information as we engage in other cognitive processes. This is the
type of memory that allows us to keep a short set of verbal directions in mind such as "turn left,
then right, then left" while driving to an unknown place. This would activate our prefrontal cortex
(PFC) in the brain (see Figure 36). There seems to be a functional separation between the left side
of the PFC, which is more involved in verbal working memory, and the right side, which is in charge
of spatial working memory.
Figure 3: Anatomical representation of the human brain highlighting regions associated with mem-
ory functions, illustrated by Levent Efe (left side). The diagram categorizes memory into distinct
types and correlates them with their respective brain areas (right side)
The other group of memories belong to the category of Long-Term Memories, and here two
main groups exists. The first one corresponds to Episodic Memories, which refer to all the different
autobiographical memories of events that happened in our live. The region in charge of this process
is the hippocampus.[14] The hippocampus plays a crucial role in the formation, organization, and
initial storage of these episodic memories, acting as a sort of intermediary for memory processing.[15]
Its significance in episodic memory formation was dramatically highlighted by the case of Henry
Molaison, who after undergoing a surgical procedure to remove portions of his hippocampus in an
attempt to cure severe epilepsy, was left with profound anterograde amnesia, unable to form new
episodic memories, though his procedural memory and aspects of his long-term memory remained
intact. [16]

2.2
How Human Memory Works
However, the hippocampus is not the final storage location for most types of long-term mem-
ories. It is believed that these memories are transferred from the hippocampus to the neocortex
as general knowledge through repeated neuronal reactivation during slow-wave sleep. [17] The neo-
cortex is the region of the brain in charge of semantic memories, which involve sensory perception,
generation of motor commands, spatial reasoning, language and general knowledge. [18][19] As an
example, we can imagine when we learn to cook a family recipe. The first time we cook a family
recipe with a parent or grandparent is a vivid episodic memory, involving specific details about the
day, the conversations, and the step-by-step guidance. All this information will be stored in the
hippocampus. Over time, the techniques, measurements, and even the flavors become part of your
general cooking knowledge, transcending the specific event to inform your overall culinary skills,
which will be stored in the neocortex.
Notably, sleep not only aids in the consolidation of memories but also plays a role in selective
forgetting, which is equally crucial for memory efficiency and management. This dual role of sleep
in memory consolidation and forgetting suggests that the same neural activities responsible for
reinforcing new memories during sleep also facilitate the organization and possible pruning of older,
less relevant memories. [20]
It is important to mention also, when talking about explicit memories the role of the amygdala,
which attaches emotional significance to memories. [21] Strong emotional memories are difficult to
forget. Overall the interactions between the amygdala with the hippocampus and the neocortex is
what will determine the stability of a memory, or in other words, how effective is retained over time.
[22] [23] Memories significantly affected by the amygdala role are for example the loss of a loved one.
The profound grief and sadness experienced are deeply encoded by the amygdala. This emotional
intensity ensures that memories related to the person, moments shared, the circumstances of their
passing, are vividly remembered and can evoke strong emotional responses years later.
The second main group in long-term memories refers to implicit memory, named sometimes
unconscious memory. The most common type of implicit memory is procedural, which involves
actions such as learning to play an instrument, and it is carried out by the basal ganglia and the
cerebellum. [24] The basal ganglia is involved in coordinating sequences of motor activity, and
in reward processing, habit formation, movement and learning. [25] In contrast the cerebellum is
mostly important in fine motor control.[26] As a comprehensive example, we can consider parking
a car. This task that initially requires conscious thought and deliberate actions. Through practice,
the basal ganglia help automate the sequence of shifting gears, turning the steering wheel at the
right moment, and judging when to stop or start moving again. Simultaneously, the cerebellum
fine-tunes the precise movements and adjustments required to maneuver the car into the parking
space without hitting other cars.
The other main subset of implicit memory is priming, which involves using stimuli to help
recognize another word or phrase in the future, such as using pictures or words to aid in recognition.
This process predominantly engages the prefrontal and parietal cortex areas of the brain.[27][28]
2.2.2
Memory Retrieval Processes
Expanding upon our understanding of memory storage, we now explore the question of how
these memories are retrieved. What makes some memories be chosen before others?
The act of getting information out of memory storage and back into conscious awareness is
known as retrieval. Psychologists distinguish information that is available in memory from that
which is accessible.[29] Available information refers to all the information stored in memory, while
accessible information is the one we can retrieve.

The effectiveness of memory retrieval is significantly influenced by cues in our environment,
underscored by the encoding specificity principle. [30] This principle posits that retrieval is most
successful when the cues at the time of retrieval closely match those at encoding. Illustrating this,
Godden and Baddeley (1975) [31] found that divers who learned and recalled words in the same en-
vironment demonstrated better memory recall than those who switched environments, highlighting
the importance of contextual cues in memory retrieval.
For a retrieval cue to be effective, a match must exist between the cue and the desired target
memory; and to produce the best retrieval, their relationship should be distinctive. This notion
is further supported by the recognition failure of recallable words, as documented by Tulving &
Thomson (1973)[30], which underscores that a cue’s effectiveness profoundly depends on how in-
formation was initially encoded. Watkins & Tulving (1975) [32] further explore retrieval nuances,
showing that a target might not be recognized immediately but can be recalled later with different
cues, emphasizing the selective efficacy of retrieval cues based on initial encoding. Moreover, the
act of retrieval itself enhances the likelihood of future retrievals of the same memory, a phenomenon
known as the testing effect or the retrieval practice effect.[33] [34]
However, memory retrieval is not a perfect replication of past events or knowledge. It is a
reconstructive process that can be influenced by current knowledge, assumptions, and the retrieval
context. Each act of retrieval can modify a memory, making it more likely to be retrieved in the
future but also potentially altering its content. This reconstructive nature of memory can lead to the
strengthening of both accurate and inaccurate memories over time. Retrieval of distant memories is
particularly reconstructive, where individuals blend concrete bits and pieces of events with assump-
tions and preferences to form a coherent story.[35] This process, however, can sometimes strengthen
errors or false memories, especially when memories are retrieved and rehearsed repeatedly.[36] More-
over, while retrieving some information can enhance memory recall, it can also cause us to forget
other information related to it, a phenomenon known as retrieval-induced forgetting.[37] Lastly,
the vividness of details remembered does not necessarily guarantee the accuracy of the memory,
as studies have shown that precise details, such as location and minor features, can be part of
manufactured memories.[38]
2.3
Human Digital Twins
HDTs are advanced digital representations that extend the concept of Digital Twins (DTs)
by specifically focusing on humans as the physical entities being modeled. Unlike traditional DTs
that represent physical systems, products, or processes, HDTs aim to replicate various aspects of
human beings, ranging from physiological to cognitive traits. This includes not only the physical
condition and health status of individuals but also encompasses their behavior, cognitive states, and
interactions with the environment and technology [39] [40].
The development of HDTs requires sophisticated integration of sensor-based data collection,
robust data processing, and feedback mechanisms [1].
They require continuous synchronization
with the human’s state to accurately reflect and predict conditions, behaviors, and potential out-
comes. HDTs find applications across diverse sectors, employing personalized medicine approaches
in healthcare, performance optimization analytics in sports, and smart home automation for en-
hanced living conditions.
The development and operation of HDTs face several challenges, including technical, ethical,
and acceptance issues. Technically, the complexity and dynamic nature of humans, combined with
the limitations of current sensor technology and data analysis methods, pose significant barriers.

Addressing these challenges requires careful design, including accurate data handling, flexibility,
interconnectivity with related systems, and a consistent focus on transparency and trust. Despite
the challenges, HDTs represent a transformative technology in personalized digital representation,
with vast potential to revolutionize healthcare, personal wellness, and many other domains.
The traditional applications of HDTs have expanded to include their role as data backbones,
powering decision-making in sophisticated cognitive information systems. This is evident in fields
like task scheduling systems [41], industrial human-robot collaboration for safety [42][43], and clinical
decision-support systems within healthcare [44][45].
Recent literature suggests that HDTs have the potential to evolve into profoundly personal

This

5.2
Retrieval Process
Section 5.1 details the composition, storage, interconnections, and generation mechanisms of
data within the HDT system. Subsequently, a Retrieval Function is defined, designed for optimal
extraction of memories relevant to user queries. In contrast to conventional RAG applications, this
function aims to emulate human memory retrieval processes rather than merely providing the most
precise answer.
The retrieval process seeks to closely mirror the mechanisms employed by the human brain when
recalling memories. Thus, the system should exhibit difficulty in accessing irrelevant memories from
the distant past due to the accumulation of subsequent experiences.
This chapter builds upon the work of Park et al. (2023) [4] on memory and retrieval, extending
it by exploring potential enhancements to result effectiveness. The central objective is to develop
a retrieval system that simulates human memory behavior. Following the cited paper, three key
factors inherent to human memory retrieval are prioritized: Recency, Relevance, and Importance.
5.2.1
Recency
Contrary to the simple decay function proposed by Park et al. (2023) [4], the retention of
past memories exhibits a greater degree of complexity [62][63]. Research demonstrates a decline in
accuracy when recalling time duration over periods of 14 to 28 days [64]. However, older adults can
still recall events, even routine ones, from a 20-year period [65].
The human memory inherently prioritizes recent experiences over those further in the past.
This bias towards recency is further reinforced by the act of recalling a past event, which strength-
ens the memory and makes it appear more recent. This process aligns with research demonstrating
that repeated information retrieval during learning solidifies long-term retention [66] [67] [68]. Fur-
thermore, the act of retrieval directly strengthens memory traces, increasing the probability of
successful future recall. This phenomenon, known as the testing effect or retrieval practice effect,
has been well-documented in research [33] [34].
To model these memory dynamics, a Recency function has been formulated as a weighted
combination of two decay functions:
Ncreation(t) = e−λct,
λc = −ln(0.9)
Naccess(t) = e−λat,
λa = −ln(0.6)
1) Creation Time Recency: This factor assesses the time elapsed since a memory’s creation.
The smaller decay constant reflects the tendency for memories to persist over extended periods.
2) Access Time Recency: This factor accounts for the time since the last interaction with
a memory. It exhibits a higher decay rate, mirroring the relatively rapid fading of recent
recollections compared to the initial memory creation.

In addition to Recency, the perceived importance of a memory plays a crucial role in its re-
trieval. As demonstrated by Park et al. (2023) [4], LLMs have inherent capabilities in discerning
core memories from routine ones. However, personal context significantly influences the subjective
assessment of a memory’s importance. Consider the following memory excerpt:

• Keywords receive an additional weight of +0.5 points in the retrieval score to emphasize
their significance.

The Retrieval Function integrates calculated values for Recency, Importance, Relevance, and
keyword matching to determine the overall significance of each memory in response to a user query.

These components are combined in a weighted sum, with scores normalized to facilitate com-
parison, where:
RetrievalScore = w1 · RecencyScore + w2 · ImportanceScore + w3 · RelevanceScore + ExtraPoints
• Weights (w) enable fine-tuning the influence of each component based on the application
domain. In this project, all weights have been initialized to 1 for equal emphasis.
• ExtraPoints provides the flexibility to boost specific keywords or memory types, if desired.
The system recalculates Retrieval Scores for each query. Memories are ranked accordingly,
with the highest scores signifying optimal alignment with the user’s prompt. For efficient execution,
the retrieval process is bounded, returning the top-10 UserProfile memories and the top-25 general
memories (refer to Section 5.3 for architectural details).

5.3.3
Prompt ii Construction
While Prompt i ensures retrieval of contextually relevant information from the HDT database
and generates a response aligned with the HDT’s persona, Prompt ii focuses on refining the response
to match the HDT’s unique conversational style. This refinement process leverages principles of
Audience Design and Speech Accommodation Theory [70], tailoring responses to resonate with the
HDT’s established communication patterns with a specific interlocutor [51]. To achieve stylistic
refinement, a structured prompt guides GPT-4 through the following process:
• Understanding HDT’s Conversational Style. The LLM analyzes a maximum of 50 past
dialogue instances between the HDT and the current Caregiver. This analysis reveals linguistic
nuances, stylistic preferences, and recurring interactional themes. If no prior conversational
data exists for this Caregiver, the LLM will examine the HDT’s 50 most recent messages to
extract general communication patterns.
• Analyzing the Ongoing Conversation. The LLM examines the current conversational
flow, identifying key characteristics, nuances, or stylistic choices that should be maintained or
avoided in the refined response.
• Providing the Intermediate Response. The unrefined response from Prompt i is pre-
sented to the LLM.

6.2.1
Experiment Design and Setup
A thorough literature review was conducted to identify key variables for CA evaluation, includ-
ing system response accuracy, user engagement, and likeability. Variables specifically focused on
personalizing feedback and conversational style were selected, following suggestions from recent CA
literature. Specifically, these variables included the Gricean Maxims (quantity, quality, relevance
and manner)[71] and additional scenario-specific variables, such as representativeness (authenticity
and anthropomorphism) and empathy, which are commonly used characteristics to evaluate CAs
[72] [73]. Evaluation methods for these variables were developed, including 5-point Likert scales
and customized qualitative assessments. This process yielded a refined set of metrics aligned with
the conceptual framework (see Figure 33) and resulted in the following hypotheses to be tested,
exploring the impact of HDT integration on the personalization and conversational style of the
developed CA:
1. H1: Integration of personal data from an HDT into a CA increases the quantity of information
in the CA’s feedback, making it more informative and specific.
2. H2: Utilizing personal data from an HDT in a CA enhances the quality of information in the
CA’s feedback, leading to more accurate and truthful responses.
3. H3: Incorporating personal data from an HDT into a CA improves the relevance of the CA’s
feedback in relation to the ongoing conversation.
4. H4: The inclusion of personal data from an HDT into a CA allows it to more accurately
mirror the conversational style of the human it represents, increasing its authenticity and
anthropomorphism.
5. H5: Incorporating personal data from an HDT into a CA enhances its empathetic response
capabilities, allowing it to adapt its answers to reflect the emotional state of the person it
represents.
In order to test these hypotheses, the experiment compared two CA systems: one enhanced with
HDT data (System A) and GPT-4 (System B) in the three different stated scenarios. Participants
were provided informed consent regarding data protection, followed by demographic data collection.
They then engaged in a structured evaluation, receiving randomized conversation snippets from both
systems and providing Likert scale ratings and qualitative feedback.

Limitations and further work
7.3
Limitations and further work
This study is subject to several limitations that warrant careful consideration when interpreting
the findings. Firstly, feedback from participants suggests that the experiment’s length may have
compromised their focus, particularly during the latter stages.
This raises concerns about the
potential for fatigue-induced degradation in response quality towards the survey’s end. In order to
mitigate this risk, a critical refinement of the experimental design is essential, ensuring a streamlined
focus on only the most crucial aspects identified within this pilot study.
Another limitation emerged during the evaluation of conversational snippets (see Appendix
9.11 ). While informed by Park et al.’s (2023) [47] framework for conversational AI abilities, the
system’s performance in combining these abilities for realistic conversation was subpar. For future
evaluations, more flexibility is needed on the interlocutor’s side, or even better, allow participants
to pose their own questions. This would showcase the system’s adaptive response capabilities, an
aspect not fully explored in the current design.
Furthermore, the reliance on synthetic data poses two challenges. Firstly, the initial narrative
generation in Catalan followed by AI-assisted translation to English may have obscured nuances
otherwise present in natural human writing. Secondly, lacking data connected to a specific individual
limited the creation of a truly realistic experimental setting. Assessing participants’ perceptions of
the CA’s responses requires contextual background, a factor that is inevitably less authentic than
the familiarity achieved with a real person.
The overall design process prioritized intuitive principles and technical methods aimed at max-
imizing human-like realism (as discussed in Section 7.1). To validate and potentially refine these
assumptions (e.g., the recency function), a systematic evaluation comparable to the importance
factor assessment (see 5.2.2)) is advisable.
Future work could, in line with understanding how
the human brain works, add weight (or a dropout mechanism) to memories associated with good
restoration sleep [61]. This and other refinements informed by physiology and psychology literature
should be implemented and evaluated to determine their effectiveness in emulating the human brain.
Importantly, the system’s performance is inherently linked to the quality and accuracy of
current LLM technology. While this limitation is mitigated by the optimism surrounding future
advancements, progress in LLMs holds direct potential for enhancing this system’s capabilities
without requiring modifications (beyond updated LLM Application Programming Interface (API)
interactions).
This could strengthen the quality of reflections, context integration, and reduce
instances of inaccurate responses.
Further refinement of the LLM’s output is possible; however, due to time constraints, human
writing style nuances were emulated through data retrieval and prompt engineering. Ideally, these
techniques would be combined with LLM fine-tuning [74], using a dataset of the specific user’s
writing for greater personalization. Additionally, LLMs do not introduce typos or errors naturally,
which becomes a consideration for future research to determine if such imperfections enhance re-
alism, or if fine-tuning with real data suffices. Furthermore, several small tasks within the system
rely on LLM performance (e.g., reflection, assigning importance to memories). Fine-tuning each
step could boost precision and long-term efficiency.
Economic constraints also played a limiting role. Development relied on the GPT3.5 API for
cost, with a last-minute switch to GPT-4 for evaluation (totaling $15.27 in API inference costs).
With more resources, prompt decomposition and chaining techniques could further optimize the
system [75] [76] [77].

Moreover, the main data types employed are raw data or LLM-generated reflections (with basic

7.4
Summary & Impact on Research Question
This study’s findings directly address the central research question, demonstrating that HDT
data integration significantly enhances CA personalization but necessitates further development to
achieve fully satisfactory results. Incorporating HDT data demonstrably improves the relevance,
authenticity, and natural flow of conversations, particularly within interactions involving individ-
uals familiar to the user. Furthermore, HDT-equipped CA s excel in informal settings, adapting
conversational style and content to align with the user’s known interests and preferences. However,
GPT-4-based models retain advantages in specific areas such as specificity, accuracy, empathy, and
information disclosure, especially in formal contexts or when interacting with unfamiliar individuals.
Further research is crucial to address several challenges, such as ensuring the user’s perception
of personalized responses as truthful. Observations suggest a potential trade-off between achieving
a more human-like and empathetic virtual self and maintaining certain aspects of conversational
quality.
A fundamental challenge lies in understanding and computationally modeling the complex
neurological processes that govern memory, retrieval, and the influence of individual identity on
expressive behavior. Overcoming this challenge would enable the generation of tailored content that
is both contextually appropriate and internally consistent across interactions. As LLM technology
continues to progress, response personalization will naturally improve. This, in conjunction with
advancements in AI avatar generation [78] [79], promises unprecedented realism in interactions
with HDTs. The potential exists for the development of highly personalized HDT that exhibit an
empathetic nature and accurately reflect the communication styles of their human counterparts.

Conclusion
This thesis explored the potential of combining HDTs with CAs to create empathetic, virtual
counterparts of human individuals. Therefore, a novel conversational system was developed, drawing
from neuroscience and cognitive psychology to model the complexities of human memory.
The
system incorporated a comprehensive HDT database, which integrated core memories (personality,
preferences), episodic memories (conversations), self-reflective insights, and real-time physiological
data. Additionally, a sophisticated retrieval mechanism emphasized contextually relevant, recent,
and impactful memories, demonstrating an understanding of memory strength similar to human
cognition.
The HDT database and advanced retrieval function were foundational to the system’s ability
to tailor responses based on the user’s unique experiences, communication style. This tailoring
surpassed conventional behavior modeling by dynamically adapting interactions based on the user’s
unique experiences, communication style, and even real-time contextual data. The ultimate goal
was to build trust and connection by creating a virtual representation that felt genuinely responsive
to the individual user.
Initial user perception studies demonstrated the HDT-enhanced system’s superiority in per-
sonalization, authenticity, and relevance when establishing conversation with known individuals.
However, a traditional GPT-4 based CA often provided more specific, informative, and truthful
responses, particularly in formal settings or when simulating unfamiliar individuals. This highlights
the challenge of balancing human-like qualities with objective accuracy and trustworthiness.
While this study offers valuable insights into the potential of HDT-based approaches, its findings
must be considered within the context of certain limitations. The use of synthetic data, the study’s
duration, and the constrained conversational format likely influenced the results. To further refine
this approach, future research should prioritize streamlined experimental design, the incorporation
of real-world user data, and more open-ended conversational settings. Additionally, neurological
insights into the role of sleep in memory consolidation could prove valuable. Real-time emotion
recognition models could enhance empathetic capabilities. Finally, before deploying HDT-driven
systems, a thorough ethical analysis is essential, ensuring responsible development that centers on
respect, empathy, and user-centric design.
Beyond the specific results of this thesis, the primary contribution lies in the proposed system
design. While current AI capabilities may still exhibit artificial qualities, rapid advancements in
LLMs suggest a future where AI writing could become indistinguishable from human-generated
text. This project lays the groundwork for combining the two: the system’s human-centric memory
modeling would guide the content of the AI’s responses, while the advanced language model would
ensure those responses sound natural and indistinguishable from the individual’s own writing.

9.3
Reflect After Dialogue
The goal of this step is to make the system reflect on a conversation that just occurred, and
has been logged in the system, both in the Dialogue and the Memory Stream Table. The prompt is
meant to be executed after the Emotional Tones and Personal Expression of each piece of Dialogue
have been extracted. See 5.1.2 for more details on that specific task. The design of the prompt
for the Digital Twin’s interaction, specifically for the entity named "Lluís", follows a meticulously
structured approach. This structure is delineated in several distinct sections, each serving a specific
purpose in the context of the interaction. Temperature value has been set to 0.5.
1. Personal Context of the Digital Twin: This section provides an introduction to the
Digital Twin’s identity, in this instance, focusing on "Lluís".
2. Information Pertaining to the Interlocutor: The subsequent part of the prompt is
dedicated to information about the person engaged in conversation with the Digital Twin.
This segment is dynamic and adaptable, constructed based on the data accumulated for each
caregiver.
3. Instructions for the Reflection Task: Following this, the prompt outlines the instructions
for the reflection task that is to be undertaken.
4. Inclusion of the Complete Dialogue: The entire dialogue is then incorporated, with each
message’s personal expression delineated within parentheses.
5. Summary of Emotional Tones: A summary is provided, highlighting the top three emo-
tional tones exhibited by each participant in the conversation.
6. Format for Reflection Outputs: Finally, the prompt concludes with a clear indication of
the expected format for the Reflections to be articulated.
This structured approach ensures a comprehensive and systematic method for analyzing and re-
flecting upon the interactions involving the Digital Twin, thereby facilitating a deeper understanding
of the conversational dynamics and emotional nuances present.

Research Question (RQ)
To what extent does integrating Human Digital Twin (HDT) data into conversational agents
(CAs) that aim to represent specific individuals enhances the personalization of feedback and con-
versational style?
Related work
Towards a tool for the Subjective Assessment of Speech System Interfaces (SASSI)
The paper focuses on developing a questionnaire measure for SASSI[80]. It aims to create a
reliable and valid tool for evaluating users’ subjective experiences with speech recognition systems,
contributing to better design and evaluation in line with human factors practices.
Variables:
• System Response Accuracy: Comprising accuracy, reliability, predictability, consistency, and
error frequency.
• Likeability: Encompassing aspects like pleasantness, friendliness, and the willingness to use
the system.
The authors began by creating a prototype questionnaire based on established measures for evalu-
ating user interfaces and a review of literature on speech system design. This resulted in a 50-item
questionnaire, that was administered to users of four different speech application, obtaining a total
of 214 completed questionnaires, rated on a 7-point Likert Scale. An Exploratory Factor Analysis
was then done, identifying the following six main factors as the ones influencing users’ perceptions
of speech systems: System Response Accuracy, Likeability, Cognitive Demand, Annoyance, Hab-
itability, and Speed. However, only System Response Accuracy and Likeability are relevant for
addressing our specific RQ.
Tell Me About Yourself: Using an AI-Powered Chatbot to Conduct Conversational Surveys with
Open-ended Questions
This paper investigates the effectiveness of AI-powered chatbots in conducting conversational
surveys compared to traditional online surveys[81]. The main focus is on user engagement and
the quality of responses to open-ended questions. However, it shows how to perform sophisticated
analysis of conversational dynamics based on Gricean’s maxims. Variables:
• Quantity: The system should be as informative as possible
– Informativeness: The response should be as informative as possible
– Specificity: The response should give as much information as needed
• Quality: The system should communicate truthfully
– Authenticity: The system should provide answers that are genuine ans sincere
– Truthfulness: The system should provide information that is not only accurate but also
believed to be true

• Relevance: The system response should be relevant to the question asked
• Manner: The system response should be clear
Even though in our scenario what our subject of study is the CA itself, this paper uses Gricean
Maxims to evaluate the quality of the response/conversation. Same methodology can be used to
address the RQ mentioned above.
The Gricean Maxims, formulated by the philosopher H. P. Grice, are intended to explain how
effective and meaningful communication is achieved in typical conversations. These maxims have
been employed to evaluate the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT in
various studies.
For instance, Di Sant Pitero, C., et al.
[82], reported that ChatGPT results
were mostly human-like but revealed weaknesses in the domains of the Gricean maxim of quantity,
text-based inferences, physical metaphors, and humor comprehension.
The influence of conversational agents’ role and communication style on user experience paper
This paper focuses on how different roles (witness or suspect) and communication styles (ag-
gressive or cooperative) of conversational agents (CAs) in a crime-solving game impact the user
experience[83].
• Perceived Intelligence: capacity to accurately simulate natural human communication with
users
• Believability: the degree to which CA are perceived by users as realistic and engaging, influ-
enced by their roles and communication styles.
• Warmth: Approachability and friendliness of the agents
In the study, participants engaged with CAs and evaluated their intelligence and credibility.
Additionally, they were tasked with identifying a culprit. The evaluation of the user experience
involved participants’ rating on items of perceived intelligence and believability.
Perceived intelligence was based on Bartneck et al., 2009[84]. This paper focuses on developing
standardized questionnaires for evaluating human-robot interaction. These questionnaires aim to
measure five key aspects: anthropomorphism (attributing human traits to robots), animacy (per-
ception of robots as lifelike), likeability, perceived intelligence, and perceived safety when interacting
with robots.
In the particular case of perceived intelligence, the evaluation included items such as "Incompe-
tent/Competent," "Ignorant/Knowledgeable," "Irresponsible/Responsible," "Unintelligent/Intelli-
gent," and "Foolish/Sensible." Participants rated their agreement with each of these descriptors
using a Likert scale.
Believability was measured as in Paulo Gomes et al. 2013. [85].This paper outlines a method
for evaluating character believability in interactive narratives. This is achieved by measuring several
dimensions of believability, which the audience can self-report. These dimensions include behav-
ior coherence, change with experience, awareness, behavior understandability, personality, visual
impact, predictability, social, and emotional expressiveness. The evaluation uses Likert scales for
each dimension, except for emotional expressiveness, which is assessed through the identification of
character emotions in specific situations. The methodology aims to provide a more objective and
detailed assessment of believability than simply asking audiences for their overall impression. With
respect to warmth, a custom 0 to 100 scale was used for participants to rate their perception (from
“0–very cold” to “100–very warm”).

Summary: Variables Relevant to Our Particular Scenario
In the context of enhancing the personalization of feedback and conversational style in Conver-
sational Agents (CAs) through the integration of Human Data Transactions (HDT) data, several
variables emerge as critical. These variables are identified based on insights from previously dis-
cussed studies to evaluate to what extent the integration of HDT data meets our objectives. Given
the innovative nature of our approach, and the lack of methods for evaluating User Experience (UX)
applications with CA, there does not exist a standard questionnaire for evaluation in our particular
scenario. Kocaballi et al., 2019 show that employing a variety of questionnaires can be beneficial in
achieving a more comprehensive evaluation of user experience. Thus, we orient our general selection
of variables based on the Gricean Maxims [71], which structure them into Quantity, Quality, Rele-
vance and Manner. However, we adapt and extend these maxims to our use case, as a combination
of existing variables in relevant literature, to assess their impact in our context. The key variables
we aim to assess are:
• Quantity:
– Informativeness: The degree to which CAs, powered by HDT data, provide comprehen-
sive and detailed information relevant to the users’ queries.
Adapted Evaluation method (1-5 Likert Scale):
∗"I found the information provided comprehensive and directly relevant to the dis-
cussed topics."
∗"The CA needed additional clarification to provide detailed and useful information."
– Specificity: The ability of CAs to tailor their responses specifically to the user’s context,
leveraging HDT data to achieve personalized communication.
Adapted Evaluation method (1-5 Likert Scale):
∗"The CA’s responses were specifically tailored to the user’s input, including questions
and requests."
∗"The CA seemed to use a generic approach rather than adapting its communication
to the specific context."
• Quality/Believability:
– Truthfulness: Ensuring that the information provided by CAs is not only accurate but
also aligns with the real-world data and experiences of the individual represented by the
HDT.
Adapted Evaluation method (1-5 Likert Scale):
∗"The information provided by the CA felt accurate and trustworthy."
∗"The CA’s responses were not reflecting accurately real-world information and knowl-
edge."
• Relevance: The extent to which CAs provide responses that are directly applicable and
pertinent to the user’s current situation or query.
Adapted Evaluation method (1-5 Likert Scale):
– "The responses from the CA were directly relevant to the questions or topics at hand."
– "The CA failed to recognize the context of the conversation."

Experiment Setup
The experiment is designed to compare the performance of two CA systems: System A (a
generic CA) and System B (enhanced with HDT data). The objective is to assess how the integration
of HDT data affects various aspects of the CA’s interaction with users.
Preparation and Consent
a. Data Protection and Consent: Participants will be fully briefed on the data collection
methods, the purposes of data utilization, and their rights regarding personal data before
giving their written consent. This ensures adherence to ethical standards and compliance with
the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), emphasizing our commitment to protecting
personal information and respecting participants’ privacy rights.
b. Demographic Information: To ensure a comprehensive understanding of the impact of
demographic variables on the interaction between users and CAs, demographic data will be
collected. This approach allows for controlling potential confounding and mediating variables,
contextualizing participant responses, and identifying any demographic trends or biases that
may influence the outcomes of the experiment. The collected demographic variables include:
(a) Age (Quantitative, Continuous): To observe any age-related differences in CA interac-
tion.
(b) Gender(Categorical, Nominal): To account gender variations in CA usage and percep-
tion.
(c) Nationality (Open-ended, Nominal): To account for cultural and linguistic variations in
CA usage and perception.
(d) Educational Background (Categorical, Ordinal): To assess the impact of education level
on CA interaction and comprehension.
As the questionnaire is intended for a Euro-
pean audience, the education level options will align with the International Standard
Classification of Education (ISCED) framework for comparability across countries.
(e) Experience with CAs and Chatbots (Categorical, Ordinal): To determine if prior exposure
to technology modifies expectations or interactions with CAs.
(f) Duration of CA or Chatbot Usage (Categorical, Ordinal): Categorized as greater than
12 months, 1-12 months, or less than 1 month, to gauge the effect of exposure time on
user experience.
(g) Frequency of CA or Chatbot Usage in the Past 7 Days (Categorical, Ordinal): Measured
to assess recent engagement levels and their impact on user experience. Categorized as
7 times, 4-7 times, 1-3 times, or 0 times, to measure recent engagement levels.
(h) Attitude towards and Experiences with CAs and Chatbots (Categorical, Ordinal): Classi-
fied as negative, neutral, or positive, to explore the subjective disposition of users towards
CAs.
Exclusion Criteria: Restrictions are placed on participation to individuals fluent in English
without medically confirmed social disorders. Thus, ensuring coherent interaction with CAs
and to prevent external factors from unduly influencing the study’s outcomes.

Not everything we remember generates the same emotion in us, nor do we explain it with the
same face, gestures, or tone of voice. Sometimes it’s not the event itself, but rather the context and
personal circumstances in which it occurred. The same event can provoke very different emotions.
We are conducting a study on the importance of the events of memories in people who are in
the process of rehabilitation from a stroke. What are the memories that leave the most emotional
impact on the patient in the recovery process? We want to know your assessment of this. Thank
you for participating in this study.
Study Description: We present to you a series of memories from a patient who suffered a
stroke and is in the recovery phase, already past the hospital stay, at home. We ask you how you
value the importance of the memory, what importance you give to the emotional impact it may
have generated. And we ask you to do this on a scale from 1 to 10, taking into account:
• The intensity of the emotion.
• The meaning in the context of recovery.
• The personal relevance in the process in which they find themselves.
• The duration of the emotional impact.
We know that we are asking for an objectively impossible quantification, but simply mark a numer-
ical value that, although totally subjective, will surely maintain a certain internal coherence within
each questionnaire.
Scale Interpretation
10 This means that it is a memory of an event that had great importance or emotional impact,
that surely allowed them to advance in their personal fight towards recovery, that meant a lot
to them, taking into account their personal, relational, or cultural factors and that they will
hardly forget during their lifetime.
1 This means that it is an insignificant memory, that surely was expressed without emotional
charge, that did not signify any step forward (or backward) in their recovery process, and that
will hardly persist over time.
