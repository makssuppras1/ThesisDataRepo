ABSTRACT
VII
Abstract
Nowadays, reducing the Cost of Energy is a main objective for wind energy enge-
nieers. There are dierent ways to achieve this goal, such as designing new blades for
existing wind turbines, allowing the reduction of the CoE by re-using other compo-
nents of the wind turbine. Therefore, the design of new blades capable of increasing
the AEP while keeping the loads within a rasonable range of value is an important
function of wind energy engineers.
There are two ways of reducing the loads: active and passive control methods.
The rst ones are able to reduce the loads due to the interaction with other com-
ponents and sensors. On the other hand, passive control methods rise up from the
idea of designing a blade capable of reducing by itself.
This thesis is focus on a passive control method: Swept blades and presents an
integrated multidisciplinary wind turbine blade design and optimization framework,
using aeroelastic and structural tool to design the geometry of the blade. The aim
of this thesis is the design and optimization of a swept blade for an existing 100 kW
wind turbine in Korea.
The development of project is divided in three parts: Parametrization of the
blade structure, formulation of the optimization problem and denition of the load
cases; parametric study of two design variables: blade length and sweep; optimiza-
tion process. At the end, an optimized swept blade design is presented.

The main objective of this thesis is to design of a swept blade for a 100 kW wind
turbine. This project is the beginning of a larger project called Development and
Demonstration of a Hybrid 100 kW Passive-Controlled Wind Turbine Smart Rotor
toward Mega-Watt Turbines, which started in 01.06.2016 and nishes in 31.05.2019.
The design will be based on a detailed numerical analysis of the passive controlled
smart rotor system to optimize the blade shape. The geometric coupled blade (i.e.
swept blade) will be designed and optimized. Designing and optimizing this type of
blades requires careful aerodynamic modeling. The eective angle of attacks along
the blade could be reduced by introducion the bend-twist coupling, therefore, the
aerodynamic loads are also reduced, resulting in an overall power outcome reduc-
tion. In order to overcome the power loss, the objective function of the aerodynamic
design must be to maximize the power output. At the same time, the material used
and the structure must not be too sti in torsion because it would be dicult to
create the bend-twist soupling eects otherwise.
As descirbed, the design of a swept blade is a multidisciplinary task where aero-
dynamics, structural and aeroelastic consderations must be taken into account si-
multaneously. To do this design, a multidisciplinary framework: HAWTOpt2 was
used. For more detailed information, refer to the ocial project proposal [1].

In windy sites, the price of the energy produce can compete with other produc-

As reducing the Cost of Energy (CoE) is a main objective for wind energy engi-
neers, there are dierent ways to achieve that goal, such as increasing blade lengths
without increasing its weight, in order to increase the capacity factors of the rotors,
or desinging new blades geometries for existing wind turbines. This allows to reduce
the CoE by re-using other components of the wind turbine. However, as the blade
length increases the loads also increase. Thus, another key point in the design of
blades is the capability of mitigating loads that appear while the wind turbine is
operating.
A good design of wind turbine rotor can improve the Annual Energy Production
(AEP) without increasing the cost of the wind turbine components. During the
last two decades, dierent methods have been developed in order to reduce loads
on wind turbines. These control methods ensures that the turbine operates within
a range of values, such as keeping the rotational speed between 20 and 50 rpm, as
this is the usual range of values for wind turbines. These methods can be classied
into two main groups [6]:
 Active control methods.
 Passive control methods.
Active control methods are those methods capable of reducing loads by actively
controlling the wind turbine, such as trailing edge aps and pitch regulation. Flaps
are one of the best devices for changing lift and load alleviation [7]. On the other

reducing the torsional stiness (GJ) the blade is able to twist more when it
bends toward the tower. With this method a reduction of loads is achieved
due to the reduction of the angle of attack during operation and a higher
tower clearance is achieved as well. However, there is a risk for aeroelastic
instabilities and buckling.
 Blade sweep: This method is based on the variation of the geometry of the
blade shape on the rotor plane.

2.1
Methodology [9] [10]
The design of a wind turbine blade is a multidisciplinary process because it has
to evaluate trade-os between discordant objectives while satisfying numerous con-
straints. Hence, this task is especially suited to the use of Multidisciplinary Design
Optimization (MDO) frameworks. The general idea is to optimized the design pa-
rameters according to a cost function, which is subjected to several constraints,
representing the dierent requirements of the wind turbine. It should be noted that
because the design is focused on the wind turbine rotor only, the set of design vari-
ables is signicantly reduced with respect a full wind turbine design and requires
the inclusion of only the internal structure and the outer shape of the blades. Thus,
MDO is an ideal tool for the design of swept-blade rotors.

Formulation of the Optimization Problem
The numerical optimization problem formulated for this thesis is dened in Equation
2.1 [10]:
minimize
xp,xs,xoper
f(xp, xs, xoper, p, w)
subject to

The cost function (f) depends on three set of variables (xp, xs, xoper), a set of
constant parameters (p) and a weighting factor (w). The design variables can be
classied in three types:
 Planform variables (xp): These variables dene the outer shape of the blade.
The chord, the twist, the relative thickness, the pitch axis aft leading edge and
rotor cone are the planform variables.
 Structural variables (xs): These variables dene the internal geometry of each
blade section. It includes the thicknesses of the material layups in dierent
regions of the blade sections and position and width of the spar caps.
 Control variables (xoper): These variables are rotor minimum and maximum
rotational speed, below rated tip speed ratio and blade pitch. Usually, the
blade pitch is controlled internally in the aerolastic solvers to optimize the
maximum power production and the minimum and maximum rotation speeds
are xed, hence, the tip speed ratio is the only control variable in those cases.

3.4
Conclusions of the Parametric Study
The objective of this parametric study was to know how two important design vari-
ables: the blade length and the sweep, aect the loads. It is known that the larger
the blade, the higher the power production, but also, the higher the loads. However,
the presence of sweep in the blade allows to build a larger blade with a lower increase
in loads.
From the point of view of the apwise bending moment, the best design would
be a blade 1.05 larger than the baseline case with a 10% swept, since with this
design the loads variations for the rated wind speed (11 m/s) are lower.
If the
equivalent fatigue loads for the edgewise bending moment are taken into account,
then, the best design would be a blade 1.05 larger and with a 5% swept. Finally,
from the point of view of the torsional bending moment, the best design is, again,
a blade 1.05 larger and with a 5% swept. The parametric study also showed that
the edgewise bending moment does not change signicantly from one case to another.
In conclusion, even when the 1.05 larger blade with 10% manage to achieve the
bigger load alleviation for the apwise bending moment, it causes also the bigger
increase in the torsional bending moment.
Therefore, the best design would be
an intermidiate point, where load alleviation for the apwise is achieved without
increasing too much the torsional loads. In other words, the blade 1.05 longer and
5% swept appears to be the best case.

Chapter 5
Conclusions
This project presents a multi-disciplinary wind turbine design framework used to
design and optimize of a backward swept blade for a 100 kW wind turbine. This
kind of design introduces the sweep of the blade as a design variable, generating
an structural coupling and forcing a re-distribution of the loads. The optimization
problem was set by maximizing the AEP and subjecting the cost function to dier-
ent constraints.
The resulting blade is 1.05 times larger, 3.08% swept and achieves an increase of
0.2% in AEP, compared with the baseline design while reducing the apwise bending
moment.
To evaluate and study the eect of the sweep and the length on power production
and the loads, a parametric study was carried out. During this parametric study,
dierent combinations of the two variables were studied using HAWC2, a useful tool
that has proven its capability to calculate the equivalen fatigue loads, among other
things. This study provides an initial hypothesis: the best design would be a blade
5% larger and 5% swept.
After the optimization process carried out by HAWTOpt2, an slender blade with
load alleviation for the apwise bending moment was the nal design. Also, this
blade is slightly tailored to increase torsion toward feather for increasing wind speed,
Carmen D. Torrente Blanco
Master Thesis
==PAGE:62==
48
CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSIONS
alleviating the loads at the tip. The results show how the initial hypothesis obtained
after the parametric study was correct.
In addition, a second optimization was carried out, maintaining every parame-
ter, design variable and constraint except the torsional bending moment constraint,
which was deleted. This study show how despite the increase of AEP and the fa-
tigue loads were slightly reduced, the torsional loads where too high and the blade
failure index was closer to its maximum. Therefore, it is convenient to constraint the
torsional bending moment, reducing the loads and increasing this way the lifetime
of the blade.
All in all, it has been proven that a passive control method as it is the sweep,
has a great importance when designing wind turbine blades, as it allows to reduce
loads and increase the AEP.
From the point of view of the design, two changes could be made in order to
investigate and improve the optimization. As it was explained in Section 2.1.1, the
blade shape is represented by dierent division points. For this project, those DPs
were not design variables of the optimization problem, however, HAWTOpt2 gives
the user the possiblity to set the DPs that dene the spar caps as design variables
and decide were to locate them. It would also be interesting to add a third shear
web, located in the trailing edge zone and study how this would aect the design
results.
