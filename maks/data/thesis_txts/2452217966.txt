Summary
Today, the global warming issue is at the centre of the energy debate. Climate change
is exacerbating building cooling requirements, reflected in the increase of cooling degree
days and high electricity peaks, especially in the summer period. This also results in
higher internal loads and urban heat island effects. Given this scenario, district cooling
can help in the decrease of the electricity consumption and associated greenhouses gas
emissions. With the support of Geographic Information Systems (GIS), cooling demand
suitable for expansion of district cooling can be located.
In this thesis, the cooling demand for Denmark has been estimated and mapped using
Geographic Information Systems. The input database is built using information from
building registries and primary energy use in industries and commercial activities. The
cooling demand is estimated from the electricity used for cooling and the space area of
the buildings. It is then divided into the main economic sectors (primary, secondary
and tertiary) to find differences in cooling requirements.
The total cooling demand for Denmark is found to be 8890 GWh. A spatial analysis with
GIS is performed to map this demand using different resolution scales. The generated
maps are then used to select a suitable area for developing a district cooling network.
After, a technical model for the selected study area is built.
The economics of this
project is then evaluated and compared with the typical individual refrigeration chillers
cooling method.
The project costs for district cooling turn out to be more favourable than individual
chillers, a total cost of 168 millions danish crowns for the first as compared to 190 for
the latter. Lastly, a one-at-a-time-sensitivity analysis of the input parameters of the
economic model is performed, which still shows positive results in implementing district
cooling for the consumers and utility companies.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Efficient planning of energy systems has a major role in urban development and can
help ensure security of supply from clean energy.
Considering the growing focus on
sustainable heating and cooling, the use of district energy can play an important role in
achieving sustainable development targets. Focusing on the cooling side, its market is
expected to notably rise over the next few years [2, 44, 21]. Hence, implementing new
cooling methodologies can play a significant role to develop sustainable solutions.
In Denmark, the Cooling Plan of 2016 [13] made by Rambøll shows significant large-scale
benefits of District Cooling (DC) in typical industry areas which reduce the overall
investment needs in cooling capacity. Storage of both heating and cooling can optimise
the co-production of cooling and heating to the fluctuating electricity prices (and keep
the cost low of the HP production). Today, district cooling in Denmark covers only 4%
of the total cooling demand, meaning it is not being fully utilised.
The use of Geographic Information Systems (GIS) can significantly improve and support
the development of new cooling plans because it is then possible to map the spatial
distribution of cooling sources and demands. The potential of geospatial analysis and
visualisation methods in decision support and building planning has been proven in
different projects, as Resch et al.
reported in [1], but a broader integration of GIS
in energy system planning is still missing. Therefore, recognising the increase in the
cooling market as well as the potential integration of a geospatial analysis system, it
seems beneficial to develop a tool that can help industries and local energy planners
make informed decisions for establishing district cooling and put more focus on cooling
at national perspective.

Project description and thesis framework

in 1945. As a company, it is committed to reducing environmental footprint, contributing
to a cohesive society with equal rights and opportunities for all. Rambøll works across the
following markets: Buildings, Transport, Planning and Urban Design, Water, Environment
and Health, Energy and Management Consulting. The company division of Energy is a

partner in this thesis project; specifically the division of Energy Planning and District
Energy. Their expertise in district energy, especially in district heating, where Denmark
has a large and established market, are proved by the great number of projects they
worked on, ranging from small villages schemes to city wide transmission networks. Some

1.2.2
Thesis statement
This study aims at developing maps using GIS to identify cooling clusters in Denmark
where district cooling networks (DCN) can be developed. General guidelines on where
and how to invest in DCN based on the model will also be presented. The starting
point will be a mapping tool already developed by Rambøll for the 2016 Cooling Plan of
Denmark [13]. After the clusters are identified, the model will be calibrated using test
cases and a feasibility study will be carried out in a chosen area. The District Cooling
Network will be designed and dimensioned for that case. The economic feasibility of
the project is also examined. The benefits of district cooling are far-reaching, it gives
better comfort in areas with a high cooling demand and increases the integration of the
electricity, heating and cooling sectors. In focus on the mapping tool there will mostly
be industrial and commercial areas. Seeing the goal of Denmark’s transition towards
100% renewable energy in 2050, district cooling can play a significant role. The research
questions that the thesis is aiming to answer are listed below:
• What is the cooling demand in Denmark divided into different sectors?
• How can GIS be used to map cooling clusters and help make more informed
decisions on potential sites for investment?
• How can we identify and study a location for the implementation of a DCN?
• What will be the costs for the designed DC system compared to conventional
refrigeration units?
1.2.3
Approach
In energy planning and in large generic energy systems, which are characterised by a
certain complexity and degree or uncertainty, a specific methodology is not present.
Yet, a multidisciplinary approach is needed to overcome the complex difficulties. The
approach used in this project takes inspiration from the one used in the article [9], where
sites of excess heat were mapped and investigated to use in DHN. The approach is here
explained with bullet points:

1.2.4
Structure of the Thesis
The thesis is organised as follow:
• Chapter 2 presents a literature review on district cooling system, and the state of
development in countries around the world. An overview on GIS technology and
its use in the energy sector is also covered.
• Chapter 3 is devoted to the database update and the methodology followed to map
the cooling demand in Denmark. The registries used in this process and the DC
mapper functions are also presented and shortly described. The obtained results
are also presented
• Chapter 4 shows the cooling demand maps. The model is then calibrated using
some reference cases.
• Chapter 5 analyse the feasibility of a DCN in an area chosen from the map.
The methodology followed for the techno-economic analysis of such network is
presented together with the results.
• Chapter 6 is an overall discussion of the efficiency of the modelling methods as
well as their limitation. Moreover, general comments regarding district cooling
systems are stated.
• Chapter 7 provides concluding remarks of this project as well as ideas and recommendations
for future work on the subject.
The thesis can be divided into two macro parts, which are the mapping of the cooling
demand and then the feasibility study. Their methodologies, results and discussions will
be presented separately in each of this part.

CHAPTER 2
Moving towards district
energy
Before developing answers to the research questions presented in 1.2.2, the framework
upon which the project is based is now presented. An overview of district energy system
is given first at global level and then focusing on Denmark. Then, more focus will be
put on district cooling, how it can be beneficial for renewable energy system and its
flexibility compared to single cooling technologies. Lastly, a brief history of geospatial
analysis will be presented, as well as its most recent application for energy planning and
future frontiers.
2.1
DC state of the art
It is hard to talk about district cooling without citing its more famous counterpart,
district heating. They can be widely seen as district energy systems. Their main goal is
to move heat and cold in an efficient manner in urban and business areas. This is done by
connecting heating and cooling customers with available heat and cold sources, usually
using less resources in comparison to conventional heat and cold supply units, such as
boilers and air conditioners. To move heat and cold, grids composed by distribution pipes
carry the hot or cold pressurised water. In order to be competitive, these distribution
pipes should be rather short and this condition is mostly fulfilled in dense urban areas.
The first district energy systems are dated back to the late 1870s, with the first systems
established in the United States. The first district cooling systems purpose was providing
refrigeration in the food supply chain [25] also established in the United States. However,
it was not until the 1930s that the first large cooling systems for air conditioning were
developed. An example being the Senate and House chambers in Washington DC, USA,
established in 1928, [25, 53].
The current status of district heating and cooling varies largely among countries, which
in [42] are classified as emerging, expanding, consolidating and refurbishing based on the
status of DHC development. An emerging market is the one of Canada, whereas China
and Italy for the EU are expanding. Lots of ex-soviet countries are now refurbishing
their district energy systems. The Nordic countries (Denmark, Sweden, Finland and
Norway) instead have a consolidate market for district energies. Most of the district
energy systems are located in the Northern hemisphere, and a majority of them in
Europe. Here, the demand for space heating is traditionally strong, leading the way to

district heating. In addition, the EU strongly supports the expansion of efficient DHC
to reduce primary energy use and emission, together with promoting the integration of
renewable energy sources (RES) in the market [30].
Even if district cooling technologies have been in the global scene for decades, their
developments are less widespread as compared to district heating. As said by Eveloy
[21] in a comprehensive review on sustainable district cooling systems, a limited numbers
of reviews about DC have been published.
Generally, books review district heating
and cooling together in terms of technologies, economic, environmental and political
frameworks, always giving larger share and attention to DH. Examples are in the books
[53] and [25].
Focusing on the market for DC today, the Americas have the 44% global installed
capacity of DC with 44.3 GWth, and the USA are leading. Then, we found the Gulf
Cooperation Council countries with 32% share, followed by the Asia-Pacic and Africa
with 19%, led by Japan. The last 5% share is in Europe, with France and Sweden leading
the way [21, 39]. By 2020, the Middle East and North African (MENA) DC market is
anticipated to overtake the American one and become the largest DC market [39].
According to [21], approximately 99% of air-conditioning and refrigeration loads worldwide
are met by electricity. The associated annual electricity demand and CO2 emissions
have been tripled between 1990 and 2016, representing 10% of global electricity use [49].
This demand is expected to triple again for the baseline International Energy Agency
scenarios [30]. In Europe the current DC market share represent only 1-2% of the total
cooling market in Europe. Euroheat and Power [19] estimates that the European cooling
market would be around 660 TWh/y in the period up to 2020.
Even if the market for DC is expected to grow mostly in hot and humid climates (see
MENA regions), a prime example of district cooling system expansion in Nordic countries
is Sweden. There, district cooling has been successfully established, supplying 3.6 PJ of
cooling [51]. Stockholm started the operation of district cooling in 1994. A reason why
large scale DC spread in northern Europe instead that in the southern part is that the
market for DH was already established for more than 50 years. Thus, property owners
were more used to buying heat from DH system and the launch of a new product (DC)
was easily accepted. In addition, one political decision that supported the development
of DC is the need of facing out CFC based refrigerants, which are extremely aggressive on
the ozone layer. The energy corporation Fortum [24] runs most of the cooling business,
accounting alone for almost half of the national energy supply. The 76 km long district

Use of GIS
A geographic information system (GIS), based on the Esri definition [18], is a framework
for gathering, managing, and analysing data.
The key word to this technology is
Geography – some portion of the data is spatial, meaning that the data are referring to
locations on the earth. GIS can be used as tool in both problem solving and decision
making processes, as well as for visualisation of data in a spatial environment. Geospatial
data can be use with different scope, that can be determining the density in a given space,
mapping the location of a given feature, its quantity, detect selected features inside a
specific area or in the nearby of that area.
GIS has become a critical planning and management tool for addressing where and
how to enable sustainable energy efforts. Seeing the attention that global warming is
gaining also at political level, a well planned energy turnaround is needed. Some of
==PAGE:25==
2.2 Use of GIS

the most pressing issue can be identified with the transition from planned production
towards renewable fluctuating production, from expensive energy carriers to cost-free
energy carriers and, as the main subject of this thesis, to centralised generation towards
decentralised generation, allowing more flexibility onto the system. Where are renewable
energy sources present and located? How many consumers will the new power plant
need to supply? And how far from them is it located? These example of questions and
many more are posed to this need of change in the energy system and are concerned by
geospatial challenges. However, the use of GIS in renewable energy system projects is still
limited to very specific sphere. According to [1], a generic methodology for integration of
energy system modelling and geospatial analysis process have not been defined yet. We
can think about the common energy system modelling as the temporal dimension and,
as obvious, the geospatial as the space dimension. Each dimension gives an important
picture of the energy system, but combined the resolution and the comprehensiveness
of the available information is much wider, giving a more complete picture. We cannot
only question the availability of intermittent RES at a certain time but also where they
are located. The same for the demand side: when and where power or heating (and
cooling) demand occurs? It is not possible to give a precise answer looking only at one
dimension of the problem.
Some examples where GIS has been used to support the planning process of energy
infrastructures are the identification of suitable places for wind and solar farms, pump
storage hydroelectricity [12, 32], as well as mapping of renewable energy sources [46].

and socio-economic aspects, as Omitaomu et al. in [43], where a multicriteria decision

applications are the ones for heat network planning [16, 41]: finding the boundaries to
witch the expansion of a DH network is economically viable is not possible without the
support of GIS technology. The same can be told for District cooling application and
this approach is sketch in this thesis. However, even if the integration of these technology
seems (and has been proven to be) promising, there are many challenges to overcome
in order to make this integration possible and smooth. For example, both approaches
require a high number of parameter to approximate the model to the real world without
much simplification. Other current challenges and future researches that can be done in
this sector will be further discussed in the final chapter.

3.1.2
Initial assumptions
Some initial remarks are here stated and will be followed in the cooling potential
estimation of this project.
1. First, only buildings with a registered commercial, industrial activity will be
counted in the study. In fact, contrary to DH, the cooling potential for expansion
of DC is only evaluated in industries and buildings with a business where cost
savings can be achieved and not for private and individual household. Thus, these
last buildings will be removed from the database.
2. Second, the cooling demand will be directly proportional to the area of the building.
When making estimation on large scale, this case on national level, going in deeper
detail can be complicated and it is not always possible due to data availability.
3. Third, only buildings with a commercial area higher than 300 m2 will be mapped.
The cooling demand for activities with smaller area is relatively lower and if such
business are present in an area of development of DC, it is assumed that their
demand will be fulfilled.

5.2.1.1
Reference year
As already specified, one key parameter that determines the thermal load is climatic
data. Different climatic parameters influence the load and energy demand such as the
temperature level. As the climate changes for each place as well as on a yearly basis, it
is a challenging task to generate a common representation of the normal climate. The
representation of weather data can be divided into yearly weather files and simplified
weather files [45].
The most important yearly representation are test reference year
TRY, design reference year DRY, typical meteorological year TMY and weather year for
energy calculations WYEC. These data represent a single year and are selected based on
weather patterns found over a multi-year period (over 30 years) which are recorded in a
database. Different names are given based on the various statistical sets these methods
satisfy.
In this study, the DRY dataset is used and extracted from the software EnergyPRO. It
contains 8760 sets of hourly weather data for a given location. The selected location is
the one for central Sjælland and Fyn and a reference outdoor temperature of 12 C◦is
set.

5.2.2
Network design and analysis
The secondary network in a DC system is the one that connects the cooling production
plant to the consumers. The primary is the one that the refrigerant is circulating. Water
is used in the secondary network, because it is cheaper and has good thermal properties.
The network layout could have a major influence to the investment cost of the pipelines,
the heat-losses as well as the required pumping power. Once the consumer nodes have
been identified, the arrangement of the pipeline net-work which will connect them with
the cooling production plant need to be design and it is done in the following sections.
At the starting point of the network construction there is the cooling plant and a suitable
location for it has to be found.
5.2.2.1
Plant location
To choose the most appropriate plant location, different factors has to be considered. The
first one is the distance between the critical consumer and the production plant. The
distance affects the pressure drop along the pipes. Longer pipes will have higher losses
due to friction. Another factor that influences the selection is the costs of the pipes,
which is proportional to their diameter.
Thus, the more consumers a pipe segment
is serving the larger its diameter and consequently its costs increase.
Based on the
number of downstream consumers, the initiatory pipes segments will undertake higher
or all the cooling load and progressively, they will become smaller until they have only
one downstream consumer. Another factor which can overcome the other is the land
availability. A suitable location where no ownership problems arise should be found and
it can go to the detriment to the other thermodynamic considerations. Here, the location
marked in red in figure 5.4 is chosen. It is an open field space close to a parking slot
and it is assumed that it is owned by NovoNordisk and available to install new cooling
units.

and the position of the sun, its spaces experience different solad gains at different times.
In addition, even similar building types can have simultaneity, like in this dimension
case.
For example, offices during summer have less staff at work as it is vacation
period. Therefore, to avoid over dimension of the pipes and save on investment costs,
the simultaneity factor SF is included in the calculations. It is expressed as follow:

5.2.2.6
Thermal Losses in Pipe Network
District cooling systems are susceptible to energy losses. In this case, they present as
thermal energy gains in the pipes carrying the cooling medium. Calance in [11] estimated
that the maximum thermal energy gain in a district cooling network would be below 2%
of the total delivered energy in the system, and in the some system configurations the
losses would be less than 1%. Given the low value from this estimation, they are not
considered in the piping network calculation.
5.2.2.7
Refrigeration plant
Different technology for producing cold and supplying it in DC system are commercially
available. One of the most common being vapor compression chillers; they are the same
type of chillers usually found in refrigerators and single unit air conditioning systems.
They use power supplied with electricity.
Another technology for producing cold is
Absorption chiller and its operation is explained in 7.0.1.
In addition, free cooling utilises cold sources in the environment to cool down water
for DC. Sources can be a lake, river, ground water or even sea water. If the source has
low and stable temperature, no extra power is needed beside the pumping power. Some
cases of free cooling can be found in Stockholm, which utilise cold water from sea and
lakes in its extend DC system [25] or Cornell University in the United States, which uses
deep-lake water and their system is described in [53].
Also in [53], future trends for cooling technology are reported and it is stated that,
giving the increasing importance of water as a natural resource, efforts to reduce use of
potable water for cooling plant are undergoing. Depending on the location of the project,
other options such as captured rain water, grey water or treated sewage effluent or sea
water can be considered. Heat pump-based cooling is also listed in the increasing and
favourable technologies for cooling production in DCN. District scale HP can generate
useful cooling and heating effect simultaneously and offers very favourable overall COP
of the system. And this last mentioned technology is the one that will be implemented

5.2.2.8
Modelling of plant operation - Heat pump, storage and chillers
Given the available installed technologies listed and explained in the previous section,
a simple operation of them including a charging and discharging of the TES is built.
Knowing exactly how much cooling (and heating) will be produced from each technology
will allow better cost and operation allocation. The modelling of the operation of the
technologies is written as algorithms.
First the the production of the HP is set as
reported in Algorithm 1:
Algorithm 1 Cooling production from Heat pump PHP and chillers Pchi
Input: HP capacity CHP, Chillers capacity Cchi, Network cooling demand Ltot
if Ltot ≤CHP then
PHP = Ltot
else Ltot > CHP:
PHP = CHP and Pchi = Ltot −PHP
end if
The storage will be charged by the excess cooling produced by the HP in the time frame
where the storage is operated which goes from April to September. We have excess
cooling all the time the heat pump is running at lower capacity than its maximum and
the cooling demand is lower than the maximum capacity. The HP will be run at higher
power to charge the storage. A binary unit of 1 and -1 indicates when the storage is
charging and discharging respectively.
It has to be noted that the storage model is only based on its capacity and the demand
of the network. It is not optimised based on electricity prices, ramp up time or other
factors. Its operation might not be optimised for the network productions. The HP will
then run when the storage is not discharging the accumulated cooling capacity and the
chillers will operate only if the demand cannot be met by both storage and HP.

Cash flows of the project vary each year throughout its lifetime. They usually come from
recurring operational expenditures (divided in fixed and variable), from specific capital
investments, or revenue stream from the cold or heat supply sales and from the salvage
value of the equipment at the end of the project lifetime. The estimation of the net
present value, NPV, accounts for these different values of money in time and it is used
in the cash flow calculations. The NPV allows for this comparison between cash flow
components in different periods by discounting the value of money along the different
years considered in the project and bringing those values back to the starting year. The
NPV can be expressed as follows:

However, the discount rate for a project used for discounting future values into present
values should reflect the cost of capital of the project in question. Usually, a project has
two sources of (costly) capital: equity and debt. They both have different characteristics
and thus different costs and they vary from project to project. Given the uncertainty
connected to this parameter, variation of it is conducted later with a sensitivity analysis.

of heat pump and storage size to evaluate the overall costs of the system over a 20 year
project lifetime. It must be noted that the term net present cost (NPC) will be also used
through the presentation of the results. The net present cost is taken as face value as the
inverse of the net present value. In other words, a positive NPC denotes outgoing money
like capital and operating expenditures. The higher the value of NPC, the more costly

5.3.4
Cost allocation
Co-generation is an effective way to reduce the use of energy inputs. The simultaneous
production of heating and cooling where heat is moved from one consumers to the
other, without rejecting it to the environment is an example of this and the flexibility
of combining district cooling and heating can help achieving energy reduction on larger
scale. As in this study both heating an cooling are supplied by the same company using
a heat pump, a pricing method that allocates the cost to each product individually
is needed.
The cost of the ownership and operation of the heat pump are included
and analysed. Given the pricing and operational parameter illustrated in Table 5.10,
investment and operational cost of the heat pump are allocated based on the heating
and cooling COP respectively. Thus, investment, fixed and variable operational cost
and electricity consumption of the heat pump are split between the cooling and heating
side on 2.5/6 and 3.5/6 respectively. With this methodology, the cost of the products
reflects only the amount of heat (or cold) transfer, as the COP reflects the efficiency of
the process. Further discussion on this and other methodologies will be presented in the
discussion in Chapter 6.

millions for the base case. Change in the COP of the individual chillers on scenario 3 is

5.3.7.1
Ownership structure
Another benefit connected to the investment of such a new project is the simultaneous
production of heating and cooling from the HP. In addition to allocate costs, this option
lead the way to different ownership structure of the plant. In one case, a district heating
company can own the plant and will sell the cooling produced to NovoNordisk or an
adequate district cooling company.
It can also be the other way around, a district
cooling or the pharmaceutical company owning and operating the plant and then selling
the heat produced to the district heating company.
As said in [53], the ownership of district heating and cooling operators and the related
legal context are straightly connected to the field of action of district energy systems
operators. Examples of companies structures can be city-owned (or municipality) ownership,
like DH in Denmark, private companies and private companies having concessions for
operating district energy systems on public ground.
These structures influences the
mission driving the operator, which can range from affordable heat delivery to profit-making
without considering any sustainability criteria. Even if the actual ownership structure
and creation and agreement of the plant operation is not analysed in this project, cost
savings connected to these kind of structure are compared. It has to be reminded that in
Denmark district heating companies are no profit, whereas it is allowed to make profits
from the selling of cooling.
If the plant is owned by a DC company (or NovoNordisk), it is assumed that all the
heat produced from the heat pump can be sold to a local DH company. It has to be
taken into account that there are taxes on EH utilisation for DH. An overview of them
is presented by Bühler et al. in [9]. These taxation could make the selling of heat less
beneficial for the company. On the other case, a DH company will own and operate
the plant, using the heat produced and selling the cooling to NovoNordisk. In both
structure, further cost savings compared to the BaU can be achieved.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
In the present work, the cooling demand over Denmark and for different sectors has been
estimated and then mapped using GIS. Then, the benefits in investing in a new district
cooling plant have been shown for a selected study area. Among the different methods
and approach used in the study, many assumptions have been made. Their effects on
the results and on the structure of the project are here discussed.
A first assumption made that influence the estimation of the cooling demand is the
input of the coefficient of performance for each business division from the DEA report.
A total demand of 8890 GWh has been estimated, but with different COP inputs it can
change. The used COP values however were the outcome of surveys and general cooling
reference from the using of energy in industries. In addition, the used values are in
the average range for cooling technologies. If specific or more accurate cooling demand
estimation are wanted, it cannot be done on national level but on smaller and/or specific
case, as it has been done with the test case comparison and in the feasibility study of a
selected area.
Using the electricity consumption as a first step to estimate the demand and then
develop a district cooling network has been done in other GIS based planning studies, for
example the one of Pampuri et al. in [44] where the area of Canton Ticino in Switzerland
is analysed. Using this input gives a good understanding and mapping of big users of
electricity for cooling. With big consumers, tertiary or industrial users are identified, as
done in the study of this thesis. This could strength the methodology followed.
The cooling demand results are based on simplifications. One is the assumption that it
cooling demand is only proportional to the area of the building. In reality, the cooling
requirements depends on the building’s construction standards and year of construction
and renovation. The type of building then determines the operating conditions, the
base load as well as internal gains and temperature set points. All these parameter were
not directly taken into account in the demand estimation and the non linearity between
the cooling demand and area has been showed with the test case comparison in Section
4.2.3. However, the division made in this work in more specific sectors compared to the
previous version of DC mapper, showed a better cooling demand estimation. If more
layers for identifying building types (even in the same sectors) can be made, the demand
estimation can be further improved.
With regards to the network planning, the layout of the pipes is based on the shortest
==PAGE:76==
60
6 Discussion
path between the nodes that connect the consumers.
The investment costs for the
distribution network is what differentiate DC to single unit cooling and reducing the
capital investment would make the first more competitive than the second traditional
options. Accounting for the shortest pipe length is the first effort made to reduce this
investment.
A model of the plant operation divided between chillers, heat pump and storage has
been developed. However, the operation is not optimised with electricity prices and
higher storage capacities only resulted in higher costs. Implementing a model based on
that, would have improve finding a better storage size with relation to costs optimisation.
In addition, other components in the systems such as substations, control system, valves
and other were not analysed and they could have affect the technical and economic
results. However in [21], it is stated that the costs for these components are the lowest in
the total cost of the network. Then, other cooling production facilities were explained but
not implemented in the technical and economic model. Giving the benefit of producing
both heat and cooling, only the HP has been chosen for the study. It has to be kept
in mind that the development of a DC system in the selected area has been done as
a feasibility study. The aim is to find benefits in transitioning to DC from individual
refrigeration units. That means that the level of technical detail and modelling does not
have to match the final system planning, when such system is implemented. Thus, the
model is satisfactory for the assumptions that were outline.
Another note that need to be done regards the combined supply of both space and
process cooling. Even if for this plant the temperature for process and space could have
been similar, as found in [35], it could not always be true. A system of micro chillers
can be added to supply cold at lower temperatures. This is usually done in DH systems
with low supply temperatures. Micro heat-booster are added to increase the final supply
temperature to the customers.
Even if the employed method of cost allocation based on coefficient of performances
allows for a fair allocation of cost between the two products and ease the expenditure
of the owning company, other methodologies are present.
Chan Nguyen et al.
in
[40] provide two thermoeconomic methods for apportioning heating and cooling cost
produced simultaneously by an ammonia heat pump. They divide the pricing methods
in non-exergy based, as done in this thesis, and exergy based methods.
These two
methods brought to two very different results, with the unit cost for heating in the
exergy based method being three times higher than the unit cost for cooling. In [6] it
is recommended that cost allocation based on exergy is the most rational way to follow
because exergy represents the truth value of a stream. The physical exergy of a stream
at state k is defined as:
ek = (hk −h0) −T0(sk −s0)
(6.1)
where T, s and h are the temperature, specific entropy and enthalpy respectively and
the subscript 0 denotes the dead state. Given this Equation 6.1, it can be noted that the

exergy content of the heating stream is higher than the cold one and thus, higher unit
cost are associated to the heat side. If such methodology would have been employed,
the unit cost of cooling can be reduce. This can make DC system even more appealing.
Energy costing prices the unit cost of heating and cooling equally independent of the
quality of heat transfer. It tends to overprice the cost of cooling in an irrational manner.
Energy costing will also not encourage rational heating and cooling temperature demand
and thus will not promote efficient use of resources. These flaws are not seen with exergy
costing, since the quality of heat transfer is taken into account.
One of the main research focus of this thesis is how Geographic Information Systems can
help in the energy planning of DC, making more informed decision for future investments.
The maps created and showed in Chapter 4 give a spatial visualisation of the total cooling
demand in Denmark, localise cooling cluster and the intensity of the demand. It is known
that district cooling, but also district heating, are more attractive in areas with high
cooling (heating) demand and proximity of lots of consumers. In fact, areas where the
demand of cooling was below a certain threshold value were not considered viable for
DCN and it is thus not suggested to investigate on those areas. The map that contains
all the mapped buildings then is used to localise and evaluate the proximity of possible
consumers.
The selected area from the maps with high cooling demand and intensity for developing
DC turned out to be a good case.
The geographical mapping thus, helped in the
identification of a feasible area. Compared to the old version of DC mapper, a mapping
giving the actual magnitude of the potential of district cooling expansion has not been
made.
Making such statement is highly uncertain and as previously stated, base it
on cost assumptions it is not performing. As a District Heating assessment tool and
more GIS based studies for the expansion of DH are available, a comparison with DC
is outlined, finding intrinsic difference that still not make possible the progress of such
mapping technology. The study done by Nielsen et al. in [41] about a GIS based analysis
to evaluate the potential of expanding DH in Denmark is taken as case for comparison.
One input of their study is the heat atlas of the Danish building stock, available from
Heat Roadmap Europe [28]. Even if the sources of their study are similar to the ones
of this thesis, meaning this atlas, based on the BBR registry, and the energy producers
account created by the Danish Energy Agency, the district heating network map was
also available.
Having a map with the existing DH areas, networks and the input energy, allowed
them to calculate the heat production costs within these areas. By adding transmission
and distribution costs, the economic feasibility of supplying new areas with DH is found.
For DC, such maps were not available.
In addition, as the market for DC is much
smaller, its historical knowledge and reference case availability is also limited. With this
thesis, it was aimed to reduce the gap in the availability and accuracy of maps of cooling
demand. With the developing of a DC network in a study area supported by GIS, a new
reference case of a positive transition to DC is shown.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this thesis, the cooling demand for Denmark has been estimated and mapped using
GIS. A total demand of 8890 GWh is found and it is divided across 11 different business
and industrial sectors. A cooling intensity coefficient for each of this sector is found and
used to allocate cooling demand onto each building based on its surface. Buildings were
divided based on their use to sort the ones with cooling demand and other with none.
A database contain all sorted buildings with their cooling demand is used as input
to create maps to have a spatial visualisation of the cooling demand and its distribution
across Denmark.
A map with cumulative cooling clusters and one with the cooling
density per km2 are the main outcome of this section. The map with all the single
building mapped is used to identify specific buildings for a case study and area for
comparison with test case. The estimated demands were then compared with test cases
and DC mapper provided by Rambøll, showing good results.
The created maps are also used to select a suitable area for developing a DC network.
The NovoNordisk campus in Kalunborg is selected as study case.
After a technical
model for the DC network is built for the selected area, the economic of the project is
evaluated and compared with the business as usual running on individual’s refrigeration
chillers. The project costs for DC turned out to be more favourable than the BaU, with
a cost of 168 million DKK compared to the 190 of the BaU. The equivalent cooling
production costs are 178 DKK/MWh-c and 203 DKK/MWh-c respectively. After the
sensitivity analysis carried out on the most affecting parameters of the cost calculations,
the results still pointed out more favourable the investment in DC.
7.0.1
Future work
The mapping of the cooling demand has been possible due to the BBR and CVR registry.
If these data were not public available, it would have not be possible to allocate the
cooling demand spatially. A wider and more accurate use of GIS needs to be supported
with more public data availability. This can improve the energy strategies and planning
process.
At the same time, the heat demand is already available in the BBR registry, and adding
estimation or real consumption data for the cooling as well, would help and facilitate
future works in the field of this project.
In addition, the database used for these kinds of large scale mapping can be pretty
==PAGE:80==
64
7 Conclusion
inaccurate. Updating information for building registries and others is not a quick tasks.
Transitioning to an online GIS platform can help the task of data-handling and to have
more up-to-date maps and information.
For a future model, in the technical analysis of the DC plant, more cooling technologies
could be implemented and automated in the model. This is possible for example in the
DH assessment tool. This feature, would give a more complete picture of the options
available and create flexibility based on the chosen location for the DC network. In
addition, an optimisation of the storage operation based on electricity prices, will help
finding the optimised capacity of it to reduce the electricity peak and decrease the
operational costs.
Another parameter that influence the technical and economic evaluation of the plant is
the meteorological data to calculate the cooling load. In this work, standard temperature
profile for the region was used.
Seeing the expected increase of climatic conditions,
cooling demand projections are needed and they could be reflected in newer standard
temperature profiles.
In the feasibility study, only private-economic indicators were used to present the benefits
of DC. However, social costs and the resulting NPV can be added to measure the positive
impact of DC on the society, for example the resulting emissions.
Overall, as said in Chapter 2, works specifically dedicated to DC are still less spread
that ones for its counterpart, DH. Thus, each new publication, research and share of
positive impact of the expansion of DC market will be beneficial.

Cooling density vs excess heat
In the paper [10], a geographical mapping of excess heat (EH) from industrial thermal
processes in Denmark is presented and for kind concession from one of the author as
well as co-supervisor, the raster map of EH is available and plotted against the one of
cooling demand in figure 2. It is interesting to note how lots of these areas overlap. As
claimed in the article and in [38], excess heat can be used as heat source exploited with
heat pumps and used for DH and industrial EH is probably the most feasible. Seeing
the spatial and magnitude coexistence of cooling and EH, one can think of both the
integration of district heating and cooling networks, using EH as source for DH, but
mostly using EH as heat source for absorption driven chillers. An absorption chiller
has the same scope as a regular chiller, with the difference that it use heat instead of
electricity as motor and the refrigerants are generally saline solutions, while electricity
is needed only for pumping the working fluid. A diagram of the schematic working of
the absorption chiller is shown in picture 1.
Figure 1: Absorption chiller
In order to be able to utilise absorption chillers, one must have steadily available streams
of waste heat at set temperature (around 90◦) as otherwise electric chillers would be more
==PAGE:86==

one unit of cooling, more heat is needed compared to electricity. Therefore, the heat

during summer periods, where the heat price is lower (if not even negative). In addition,
