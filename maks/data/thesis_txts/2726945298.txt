ABSTRACT
A hydrogen cluster, also known as a hub or valley,
represents a network that connects hydrogen producers,
potential users, and supporting infrastructure. These clusters
effectively match supply and demand within a region, often
incorporating renewable energy generation. The REPowerEU
initiative aims to double the number of hydrogen hubs by
2025 through increased investment in the Clean Hydrogen
Joint Undertaking under Horizon Europe [1]. Moreover, the
concept of hydrogen hubs has gained global traction through
the Mission Innovation (MI) Clean Hydrogen Mission,
which targets the establishment of 100 large-scale ”clean
hydrogen hubs” worldwide by 2030. Hydrogen serves not
only as an end-product but can also be converted into
renewable fuels. Methanol (MeOH) is among these fuels,
produced through hydrogenation technology utilizing CO2.
Methanol is considered a promising energy carrier due to
its higher volume-specific energy density compared to other
carriers like hydrogen and lithium batteries. Furthermore,
methanol is highly portable due to its excellent chemical
handling properties, which encompass advantageous physical
properties, stability, compatibility, safety, and the availability
of infrastructure. Consequently, global demand for methanol
is steadily increasing, particularly in the challenging-to-
decarbonize heavy transport sector. Located in Jutland,
Denmark, the Kassø PtX project acts as a hydrogen hub
with the objective of producing synthetic electrically derived
fuel (e-Methanol), adhering to the advanced sector coupling
requirements set by the European Commission. Categorized
as a multi-carrier microgrid (MCMG) system within the
broader framework of energy hubs, the thesis project focuses
on optimizing the operation of the Kassø energy hub. This
is accomplished by developing a deterministic optimization
model that considers technical constraints and technology
uncertainties. By employing a centralized approach, the
model provides valuable insights to the main regulator of
the industrial park, helping identify potential bottlenecks.
The project has several key objectives. Firstly, it aims
to simulate the operation of industrial park technologies,
effectively replicating their functionality. Additionally, it
seeks to explore methanol selling contracts during commonly
observed delivery periods and identify potential opportunities
for methanol storage. Lastly, the project investigates both
demand-driven
and
price-driven
operational
strategies,
providing a comprehensive analysis of their impacts. The
findings indicate that introducing contracts with specific
delivery periods in flexible operation hubs and following
demand-driven optimization can decrease the selling price
of renewable methanol. Additionally, it significantly affects
years with high electricity price volatility, favoring yearly
delivery contracts in terms of profitability. By increasing the
volume of renewable methanol storage before selling, the
project mitigates risk and generates additional operational
profit annually by keeping the same selling price or can
maintain a competitive selling price of 723 Eur/ton for
long-term operation, despite fluctuations in electricity prices.

1. INTRODUCTION
The European Commission has presented the REPowerEU
Plan [2], which aims to reduce the European Union’s
dependence on Russian fossil fuels and address the climate
crisis. In line with this plan, the EU has been working with
international partners to diversify energy supplies and is
considering the development of a joint purchasing mechanism
for gas and hydrogen. As part of the REPowerEU Plan,
a domestic target has been set for renewable hydrogen
production by 2030, with a focus on hydrogen clusters
or hubs. These clusters aim to match supply and demand
within the same region, often incorporating renewable energy
production. The last two years’ energy crisis has increased
both natural gas and electricity prices. As of the end of 2021,
almost 47% of the global hydrogen is produced from natural
gas, 27% by coal, 22% from oil (as a by-product), and only
around 4% comes from electrolysis, resulting in a rise in grey
hydrogen and ammonia prices [3]. While renewable hydrogen
and ammonia are becoming increasingly competitive in the
market, the prices of conventional methanol, a petroleum-
based product, remain low, posing challenges to the promotion
of renewable methanol globally.
A. Energy-hydrogen hubs
To address these challenges, the concept of Energy Hubs
(EH) has emerged as an efficient framework for modeling and
managing Multi Energy Systems (MES). EHs aim to convert,
utilize, and store various energy carriers to meet sociopolitical
and socioeconomic mandates. The planning objectives of EHs
include introducing additional resource distributions to fulfill
multiple energy demands within an EH. Renewable energies
are preferred for green hydrogen and e-fuels production within
EHs. However, implementing EH projects comes with chal-
lenges such as assessing market willingness to support inno-
vative technologies, modeling expansion uncertainty, adhering
to safety rules, estimating area requirements, and considering
water resources.
Before initiating an EH project, it is essential to deter-
mine the end product, as it influences key aspects such
as supply chains, risks, and site selection. Site selection
involves considering market conditions on an international
scale and employing a structured screening approach to ensure
an efficient selection process. Criteria for location selection
include bidding zone, existing electricity infrastructure with
connection, distance to the supplier, water availability, area
layout, proximity to residential areas, and distance to ports.
It is crucial for project owners to consider all these criteria
from the outset, including market analysis, risk assessment,
partnership formation, site selection, and regulatory planning
[4].
Governments play a crucial role in reducing risk and im-
proving the economic feasibility of low-emission hydrogen
projects in EHs by implementing policies to create demand for
low-emission hydrogen through instruments such as auctions,
mandates, quotas, and requirements in public procurement.
Additionally, innovation and demonstration efforts should fo-
cus on sectors where hydrogen can support decarbonization
and reduce dependencies on fossil fuels, such as heavy indus-
try, heavy-duty road transport, and shipping.
Improving regulatory processes, including licensing and per-
mitting, can help expedite project timelines while maintaining
environmental standards and public consultation. This also
applies to enabling infrastructure projects, including renew-
able generation capacities and carbon dioxide (CO2) transport
and storage. Therefore, governments need to coordinate their
efforts to enhance the efficiency of regulatory processes and
infrastructure projects, creating a favorable environment for
low-emission hydrogen projects and their products.
This happened with the recent update of the EU regulation
for green hydrogen. The EU regulation has raised questions
regarding its impact on hydrogen and methanol production,
particularly about the contracted volumes. In February 2023,
the EU Commission delegated regulation was issued, which
introduced rules for hydrogen labeling as originating from
”renewable” energy sources [5]. This new regulation regarding
power is built upon three main pillars: additionality, temporal
correlation, and geographical correlation.
B. The role of renewable hydrogen and methanol in the
shipping sector
Yet, the adoption of low-emission hydrogen as a clean
industrial feedstock and energy sector is at an early stage
and, as with other clean energy technologies, there is a need
to effectively track progress to assess if developments are
happening fast enough and are on a trajectory for hydrogen
to play its part in the clean energy transition and to enhance
energy security [6] , [7]. Global hydrogen demand increased
5% in 2021 (Figure 1), reflecting the recovery of economic
activity in traditional applications from the pandemic-related
curtailments. In 2021, hydrogen demand in the industry recov-
ered and slightly exceeded pre-pandemic levels. The highest
rates of economic growth ever recorded in many countries, in
part, are reflected by a surge in demand for hydrogen-derived
products: global ammonia production rose by 3%, methanol
by 12%, and Direct Reduced Iron (DRI) production by 12%
[6].
Methanol the second-largest industrial hydrogen application
is used mainly as an intermediate product to produce other
chemicals [1]. Several fuel applications are also important,
either using methanol directly or after conversion to another
compound. Recently, methanol serves as an intermediate in the
production of high-value chemicals (key chemical precursors
for making plastics) from coal, an alternative to conventional
oil-based routes. The 113 Mt methanol produced in 2021
globally led to around 15 Mt of hydrogen demand, and
virtually 100% of this production was from fossil fuels [6].
Virtually all of this electrolysis capacity is planned in Europe
and the Asia Pacific region, with one very small project
planned in the Central and South America region (Figure

Methanol has a higher technology readiness level than
hydrogen or ammonia technologies for shipping [6]. There are
56 methanol vessels in operation or on order. As a hydrocarbon
fuel, it has a high energy density that makes it well-suited
for long-distance vessels. It is actively being pursued by
companies including Maersk for large, long-distance container
ships. Maersk has plans to introduce eight large ocean-going
container vessels capable of being operated on carbon-neutral
methanol in 2024. Maersk is developing several strategic
partnerships for methanol production for their vessels (like the
European energy project in the Port of Aabenraa). Different
from hydrogen or ammonia, methanol is a liquid at room
temperature and is much less toxic than ammonia. When
made from renewable electrolytic hydrogen and sustainable
sources of CO2, referred to as synthetic methanol, it can
constitute a pathway to decarbonise shipping in a similar way
to hydrogen/ammonia. However, constraints on the availability
of biogenic or direct air capture CO2 feedstock at a reasonable
cost make the competitive edge of synthetic methanol over
low-emission ammonia uncertain.
C. Markets insights for renewable hydrogen and methanol
Consequently, this raises the question of the methanol’s
source and the methods by which a competitive market for
renewable hydrogen and methanol can be established. The
establishment of an international hydrogen and e-fuels market
is heavily dependent on the structure of transactions between
exporters and importers [6]. Currently, bilateral contracts are
the primary means of establishing this market, involving com-
panies and governments. Exporters seek long-term contracts
with clear pricing mechanisms or fixed prices to ensure a
return on their investment and encourage project development
[6]. On the other hand, some importers prefer more flexible
contracts that avoid restrictions on diversions and reselling. To
simplify negotiations, the creation of a globally standardized
template for hydrogen trade contracts is suggested, involving
consultations with both exporters and importers [6]. In order
to scale up the production of green methanol and put in
place efficient contractual arrangements and agreements, more
investigation is required in the technical and economic areas.
Another effective method of awarding contracts is through
auctions, which can be held by governments on the demand
or supply side, or both. Joint auctions for electrolyzer and
renewable electricity installations from a single developer can
be held to support the integration of the two. Auctions allow a
bidding competition for a contract, move the demand and sup-
ply price levels closer, and respond more readily to changes,
such as production cost declines or increased willingness to
pay. Auctions can be used by governments to award contracts
for differences or carbon contracts for differences, which
provide government funds to guarantee low-emission hydrogen
and hydrogen derivative products sellers a fixed price for their
product or carbon credits generated [10].
While contracts may dominate in the short term, open
market models, such as resale markets or spot markets, could
emerge in the long term. Methanol, benefiting from existing
infrastructures and market models for fossil fuel-based prod-
ucts, could be an early candidate for such markets, reducing
supply-side risks [10].
D. Research topic, objectives and organization framework
The current challenge lies in bridging the gap between
the buyer’s willingness to pay and the anticipated production
costs of electrolytic hydrogen and renewable fuels [11]. Public
funds may be necessary to cover the price difference when
intermediaries sell at a loss. This gap highlights the importance
of investigating the economic feasibility of renewable fuel
production, such as electronic methanol, in sectors like long-
haul shipping [11]. By analyzing future renewable methanol
contracts for the maritime sector and considering the operation
of hydrogen clusters, valuable insights can be gained to
promote Power-to-X (P2X) activities.
The primary objective of this study is to assess the economic
feasibility of regional energy hubs (hydrogen clusters) with
renewable fuel production in the current market landscape.
Furthermore, we propose the implementation of renewable
methanol selling contracts with various delivery periods to
mitigate risks associated with the uncertainty of renewable
power supply and delays in off-takers deliveries. In order to
do that :
• We model in detail the Kassø PtX Project operation, a
Danish case study
• We define a new methodology for quantifying renewable
methanol contracts for short or long-term
• We quantify the uncertainty of the contracted volumes
and prices over multiple weather years and electricity
prices and
• We investigate methanol storage opportunities before
selling for keeping competitive its selling price in years
with high price volatility
The remainder of this master thesis is structured as
follows: Section 2 presents related work on P2X energy hubs
depending on solar energy with main product renewable
methanol, focusing on flexible or not operation and contract
agreements. Section 3 introduces Kassø PtX Project as the
examined Danish Case study and the used data. Moreover,
Section 4 introduces the new methodology used in this master
thesis. Section 5 describes the numerical analysis, focusing
on the EnerHub2X framework and the model formulation,
while Section 6 presents the key results. Finally, Section 7
discusses the results and the conclusions.

2. LITERATURE REVIEW
Renewable methanol production has been investigated as
a promising approach for reducing carbon emissions and
mitigating climate change. Solar PV and biogenic CO2 capture
are two technologies that have been studied for their potential
to produce renewable methanol with lower carbon footprints.
These technologies are our main focus as they are used in our
case study.
A. Solar PV and the Grid as electricity sources
This literature review provides a comprehensive overview
of studies exploring the link between solar PV, grid, and re-
newable methanol production. Numerous review studies have
focused on PV power as a renewable electricity source and the
capacity of installed PV systems ( [12], [13]). Increasing the
installed PV capacity results in a rise in excess energy sent
to the grid and energy supplied to the electrolyzer, leading to
a reduction in monthly energy drawn from the grid. During
daylight hours, PV energy is utilized partially to meet the
load, especially during non-peak hours, and partially fed into
the grid, particularly during peak hours. However, the energy
drawn from the grid during nighttime remains unaffected by
the increase in installed PV power. Consequently, while the
excess energy increases proportionally with the growth of PV
power, the decrease in energy drawn from the grid is relatively
slower.
According to Herdem [14], beyond a certain threshold,
increasing the peak power capacity of solar PV systems does
not significantly enhance the Satisfied Load Fraction (SLF).
For instance, a 100 MW solar PV plant achieves a maximum
SLF of approximately 0.56 in June, while a 200 MW solar
PV plant achieves a slightly higher SLF of approximately
0.61. Hence, optimizing the capacity of the solar PV system
is crucial to increase SLF and attaining grid independence.
Recent studies recommend a PV plant size no more than
five times larger than the size of the electrolyzer to achieve
optimal results (in line with this Kassø Solar Park is five
times larger in AC capacity than the installed electrolyzer
plant). To address excess energy, Herdem [14] suggests
the utilization of battery storage systems instead of relying
on grid electricity, particularly in the case of an alkaline
electrolysis system.
B. Biogenic CO2 capture
Biogenic CO2 capture involves the capture and utilization
of carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions originating from biolog-
ical sources. These emissions result from natural biological
processes like plant respiration, organic matter decay, and
fermentation. Utilizing CO2 for methanol production, rather
than CO, offers two primary advantages [15]. These reasons
are: (i) CO emissions from the biomass power plant can be
decreased without using high-pressure CO2 compression and
sequestration process, and (ii) CO2 can be transferred to the
system from other sources when the biomass feedstock is not
sufficient for the biomass power generation plant. To reduce
CO2 emissions, converting CO2 to value-added products has
received significant attention, because CO2 is a valuable,
abundant carbon source that can be converted into fuels and
chemicals [16]. Methanol is one of the chemicals that can be
produced from CO2 via hydrogenation technology.
C. Modelling studies on renewable methanol
Studies on renewable methanol production from renewable
hydrogen and CO2 capture have been conducted using various
renewable energy sources. However, most of these studies have
focused on the levelized cost of Hydrogen (LCOH2) and the
feasibility of the project. Among the different sources of re-
newable energy, such as photovoltaic, wind, and hydroelectric,
used for hydrogen production through electrolysis to methanol
with CO2 sourced from a biogas plant or purchased, a study by
Rivarolo [17] found that the current selling price of methanol
is economically feasible relative to a hydroelectric source.
Nonetheless, future methanol selling prices of 500 and 600
Eur/tonne, photovoltaic and wind energy sources, respectively,
become economically feasible for renewable methanol produc-
tion.
To enhance the stability of hydrogen supply from variable
renewable sources, several modeling options can be explored.
Hybrid plants, which combine different renewable resources
like solar PV, wind generation, and batteries, offer an oppor-
tunity to increase annual full-load hours. Despite the poten-
tially higher electricity costs associated with such combined
systems, overall production costs can be lowered [18]. If no
other off-taker or use is available, curtailment can be consid-
ered, although it may result in cost disadvantages. However,
these drawbacks can be outweighed by better utilization of
the electrolyzer. When feasible, leveraging low-emission grid
electricity can compensate for hours with minimal renewable
generation, thus reducing the variability of the electrolyzer’s
operation. It is crucial to note that this approach is closely
linked to the renewable production capacity of the grid’s power
source [6]. While pursuing these strategies, it is essential to
prevent the energy cannibalizing effect, which could poten-
tially impact the economic viability of conventional power
generators and the overall stability of the electricity grid due to
the fluctuations associated with renewable energy sources. This
necessitates careful planning and management, particularly for
low-emission grids, to ensure a harmonious integration of
renewable sources without compromising grid stability or the
viability of conventional power generation especially with the
rapidly growing PV system [19].
Modeling studies have been instrumental in evaluating the
economic viability of PtX systems that produce methanol
from renewable electricity and carbon dioxide. These models
have considered factors such as feed-in tariffs, electricity
prices, carbon pricing, capital costs, operating costs, and
methanol prices, to calculate the LCOM. They have provided
valuable insights into the factors that affect the viability of
PtX systems, guiding policy decisions aimed at promoting
the adoption of these systems. A techno-economic analysis
of green methanol plants, focusing on optimizing the design

D. The role of power system flexibility
On the other hand, big discussions were conducted regard-
ing the flexibility of electrolysis and PtX systems. Most of
the modeling studies discussed above, highlight the positive
impact of flexible operation in terms of LCOM. The ability of
electrolyzers and methanol synthesis facilities to modify their
output power in response to shifts in the supply and demand of
the electricity grid is referred to as electrolysis and methanol
synthesis flexibility. Flexible electrolysis can also help balance
the grid by providing energy storage and balancing services.
Procedures like ramp-up and down are essential to the oper-
ation of flexible electrolysis and methanol synthesis because
they determine how long it will take to change the electrolysis-
methanol synthesis output to satisfy grid demand [22]. One
strategy to improve efficiency involves developing new designs
for electrolyzers and methanol reactors with faster ramp-up
and ramp-down times. This approach aims to optimize the
performance of the latest period, utilizing advanced proton
exchange membrane (PEM) electrolyzers that can quickly
respond to power signals. To achieve faster power adjustments,
researchers propose utilizing stacked electrolyzer modules that
can be individually activated or deactivated. Additionally, in
terms of methanol synthesis, researchers suggest employing
microreactors that can be independently activated or deacti-
vated, facilitating quicker production adjustments [23].
In the context of a fixed-bed methanol reactor, a range of
feasibility loads have been reported, with a minimum load
between 10-20% [22]. Currently, there is no literature available
regarding the ramp limit of a methanol reactor. However, in
[24] reported an hourly ramp limit of 20% (based on the
rated capacity) for ammonia reactors. In light of this, key
variables are reported for selected ramp limits up to 20%
(for both ramp-up and ramp-down) for the methanol reactor
as well. As for alkaline electrolyzers, ramping flexibility is
associated with cold or warm start time conditions. A cold
start time refers to the operation of the electrolyzer from
ambient temperature after a long shutdown, while a warm
start time indicates that the operating system is held at a
specific temperature and gas pressure (stand-by condition).
Under warm start conditions, the electrolyzer can ramp from
0% to 100% within 60 to 300 seconds. However, when starting
from a shutdown condition (cold start), the electrolyzer may
require up to 120 minutes (2 hours) to reach its maximum
capacity [25]. Those response periods are even shorter for
proton exchange membrane electrolyzers (PEMs) and for this
reason, they are preferable in similar processes.
By improving efficiency and reducing costs associated with
intermittency, it becomes possible to design and implement

based on renewable energy sources. Chen [22] conducted a

The results indicate that introducing flexibility can reduce

Overall, despite the growing interest in synergistic energy
hubs and the production of renewable methanol, there is a
lack of comprehensive analysis regarding the operation of
such hydrogen hubs considering various factors like flexibility,
operational constraints, adherence to EU regulations, and
the inclusion of renewable contracts for PtX fuels with
specific delivery periods for off-takers. This research gap

flexible formulation of EnerHub2X facilitates the modeling of
additional conversion technologies and can be easily adapted
to different cluster typologies.

3. DANISH CASE STUDY
A. Kassø PtX Project
The Danish government (2020) has committed to reducing
CO2 emissions by 70% by 2020 compared to 1990 levels and
to becoming carbon neutral by 2050. In 2021, its PtX strategy
was launched, which outlines at least 4-6 GW of electrolyzer
capacity by 2030 [26]. One of them is the Kassø PtX Project in
the Port of Aabenraa (the exact location can be seen in Figure
4), a symbiosis network using RES and PtX technologies.
European Energy’s Power-to-X-facility (PtX-facility) in
Kassø, Denmark, will be the world’s largest e-methanol fa-
cility and the largest solar park in northern Europe. The e-
methanol plant will convert hydrogen and carbon dioxide
into e-methanol while generating a lot of excess heat, which
will be supplied to 3,300 households. The innovative facility
will produce 32,000 metric tonnes of carbon-neutral fuels
annually, decarbonizing the heavy transportation sector. Sulzer
Chemtech will deliver distillation units to the project, while
Siemens Energy invests resources in electrolyzer manufactur-
ing to develop an industrial-scale plant. The Kassø project
is the first significant-scale e-methanol production facility to
address geopolitical pressures, supply chain issues, and the
need to secure energy supplies.
One of the main challenges for profitable operation in
energy hubs is the identification of off-takers the Kassø PtX
project has already contracted with four big companies to
supply them with high-purity e-methanol. More detailed,
half of the total plant output, 16,000 metric tonnes per
annum, will be delivered to danish global shipping company
A.P. Moller-Maersk to fuel the company’s first container
ship capable of operating on green methanol. In addition,
Circle K, with a 5-year fuel purchase agreement made a step
towards ensuring that danish road transportation can reduce
its carbon dioxide (CO2) footprint. Finally, European Energy
A/S partners with Novo Nordisk and Lego Group to replace
fossil-based materials in plastic production. More specifically,
Novo Nordisk has taken a step into the area of substituting
fossil-based plastic with lower-carbon sources in its plastic
injection pens. At the same time, the LEGO Group will
explore the potential of using e-methanol to make selected
elements in its portfolio.
The Kassø PtX project is a symbiosis network that takes
advantage of Kassø solar park and where the surplus energy
and resources of one plant can be utilized as an input to
the chemical process of another actor. In the case study of
the Kassø PtX Project all the topology requirements and hub
challenges are satisfied and for this reason, is a suitable and
promising case for further investigation.
The Kassø PtX project aims to utilize renewable energy
sourced from an existing solar park. Its projected maximum
annual capacity is set at 44,000 tons of green methanol. To
achieve this, the project requires a total of 380 GWh of
electricity, which will be used for various processes including
the electrolysis plant, methanol reactor, and distillation tower.

4. METHODOLOGY
This Section presents a detailed explanation of the method-
ology used to develop a linear programming model that maxi-
mizes the operational profit (revenues minus operating cost) of
the Kassø PtX project while also providing the selling price of
the hub’s product, e-methanol, for all possible contracts and
delivery periods (See Figure 8). The methodology diagram
utilizes light yellow color to represent the input data, blue color
for the initial framework and the two optimization models,
green color for the results of the model, and orange color for
the intermediate steps. The modified EnerHub2X framework
used in this study is described in detail, and the steps taken to
ensure the model is both accurate and efficient are outlined.
EnerHub2X is designed to capture the complexity and dy-
namics of P2X and provides insights into optimal dispatch and
operational bottlenecks. The model flexibly simulates different
processes and conversion technologies using accurate en-
ergy carrier transformation including technical and operational
constraints. EnerHub2X is a network and multi-commodity
flow model solved in a four-dimensional space: accounting
for spatial and temporal resolution, technology details, and
energy carriers. Its objective is to maximize the energy hub’s
profit and optimally dispatch all its conversion technologies
assuming perfect information. The planning horizon can be
from days to years using an hourly time resolution. The model
assumes a connection to the electricity grid and the day-ahead
market. It can be expanded to the ancillary service market
but this possibility is not used in a specific master thesis.
EnerHub2X is formulated as a mixed-integer linear program
in GAMS and solved using CPLEX [26].
The Kassø PtX Project mixed integer linear programming
(MILP) model is developed using the EnergyHub2X frame-
work as its foundation, incorporating all the pertinent informa-
tion and data discussed in Section 3. The model encompasses
various aspects, including the project’s assets and technologies.
Of particular significance are the modeling of 20 electrolysis
units and the integration of an intermediate storage-equipped
distillation tower. The decision to opt for 20 units is based
on findings from studies conducted by [27] and [22]. These
studies highlight the advantages of utilizing multiple smaller
units over a single large unit, emphasizing the increased flex-
ibility and adaptability offered by such an approach. Factors
considered in this decision include production scale, resource
availability, market dynamics, and technical considerations.
The individual characteristics and varied behaviors exhibited
by each unit present a distinct advantage in terms of flexibility.
Solar profile and electricity price data from Table I are also
utilized. Additionally, import and export prices for ancillaries
and by-products remained constant across all years examined
(Table XI).
As the selling price of renewable methanol varies across
different months and years [29], it is necessary to identify
a representative price to achieve the volume target (32,000
tonnes of renewable methanol) in yearly operations while
participating in the day-ahead market. To achieve the target
volume of produced renewable methanol, a price-driven op-
timization approach was implemented, which means that the
resulting volume and profit would depend on the given price
signals. By running the Kassø PtX Project MILP model with
different prices, a production evaluation was found, as shown
in Figure 11. This indicates that the production of renewable
methanol is preferable to other sources of hub profit when
the selling price is more competitive. Based on this as the
selling price is increasing so the produced volume is bigger.
Increasing the price by steps, we find the benchmark selling
price needed as the target volume produced.

Integer Linear Programming (MILP) problem to a Linear
Programming (LP) problem lies in the fact that LP models
provide well-defined dual values for their constraints, whereas
MILP models do not offer this advantage. dual values signify
the rate of change of the objective function in relation to
each constraint, furnishing valuable insights into the effect of
adjusting or tightening individual constraints on the objective
function’s value. The presence of integer constraints in a
problem transforms it into a combinatorial challenge, making
it computationally demanding to achieve an optimal solution.
By relaxing the problem to an LP, we can employ LP solvers,
which typically offer enhanced efficiency and faster solution
times [30].

the Kassø PtX model, is formulated as a MILP is:
• Binary variables which indicate the charging or discharg-
ing status of storage technologies and the online or offline
status of the technologies in the time period
• Using unit commitment constraints such as minimum
loads or ramping constraints.

B. Assumptions
Before proceeding to further analysis and implementation of
the proposed model to the study case, a critical reflection of the
limitations and assumptions of the model is performed. This
subsection outlines the assumptions made in the progression
from the EnerHub2X framework to the development of
the Kassø PtX Project optimization model. Initially, the
assumptions in the MILP Kassø PtX model are presented,
followed by the inclusion of additional assumptions for the
LP Kassø PtX model.
MILP Kassø PtX model assumptions
• The model assumes perfect information and rational
behavior of the energy hub actors. Indicating that the
model has a central structure and does not account for
the asymmetry of information.
• The model assumes solar park schedules to deliver all the
produced electricity to Kassø PtX Project.
• Both CO2 and H2O are assumed to be infinitely available.
• The reserve osmosis plant used for demineralized water
is not simulated.
• The efficiency curve of electrolyzers is nonlinear but will
not be used in this optimization model. Technologies with
no linear load-dependent production efficiencies can be
simulated in the Enerhub2X framework but are not used
in this optimization.
• The operation of each electrolyzer, methanol reactor, and
distillation tower is assumed to transition from the online
status to standby mode before going offline, and vice
versa.
• Kassø PtX Project has no access to green subsidies or
green premiums that can highly affect the operation and
profitability of the hub and at the same time decrease the
selling price of renewable methanol.
• The electricity day ahead profit estimated by the Kassø
PtX model will be fully allocated to the renewable
12
==PAGE:16==
producers.
LP Kassø PtX model assumptions
• Flexible operation is implemented for the electrolysis
plant, methanol reactor, and distillation tower. This means
that there are no minimum load fractions requirements for
these assets. As a result, there are no unit commitment
constraints imposed at this stage.
• Due to the flexible operation of the distillation tower, the
need for intermediate storage is eliminated. Previously,
intermediate storage was utilized to maintain the distilla-
tion tower’s operation within the range of 60-100%.
• In case demand delivery periods need to be considered,
demand constraints are incorporated into the model.
Both models were compared with a time horizon of 8,736
hours (364 days) per year instead of 8.760. This decision
was driven by the need to establish a mapping set for time
t (hours of a year) and time delivery periods tD (hours of
the contract period). The objective was to ensure the creation
of uniformly-sized delivery periods. To achieve this, it was
crucial for the total number of hours in a year to be divisible
by all the contract delivery periods considered, encompassing
seasons (divided by 4), months (divided by 12), weeks
(divided by 52), and days (divided by 364). It is worth noting
that this requirement would not be met if a time horizon
of 8,760 hours annually were chosen, for weekly delivery
periods (when dividing 8,760 hours by 52 weeks, the result
is a decimal value).

B. Flexible operation configuration
When integrating renewables into the chemical industry,
a new approach is being considered, where the focus is
on exploiting the flexibility of chemical processes, which
has not traditionally received much attention in steady-state
production. In a flexible chemical process, the demand
side can adjust to the variable pattern of renewable energy,
incorporating demand-side management to balance power
generation and end-use load. H2-based energy storage systems
are widely used for balancing renewable power generation due
to their flexible operation. Water electrolysis can generally run
at flexible loads to accommodate the variability of renewable
sources. However, chemical synthesis reactors and other
complex chemical processes require careful management
to achieve flexibility. For example, a fixed-bed methanol
reactor showed a wide range of feasible loads, with lower
and upper bounds of superficial velocity being ten times apart
[31]. In contrast, cryogenic distillation for air separation was
found to be less flexible, with feasible loads no lower than
60% of its full capacity. Therefore, these units may have to
operate at different load levels within their individual limits
to ensure flexibility [22]. Using this information in no flexible
operation configuration, the benchmark selling prices were
determined. This subsection attempts to answer a critical
question regarding the operation of the flexible hub and
resulting in new benchmark selling prices, particularly with
regard to adding flexibility to assets such as the methanol
reactor and distillation tower.

6. RESULTS
This section presents the key findings of the final model,
which is the quantified renewable methanol contracts by the
Kassø PtX LP model based on flexible operation configuration.
As outlined in Section 4, we followed a series of steps to
formulate an LP model. In doing so, we added a new objective
function and demand constraint to the model, with the aim of
avoiding binary variables in storage technologies and limiting
demand within specific delivery periods, respectively.
The reason for this approach was to optimize the sale of
renewable methanol while also considering the constraints that
may arise from the storage technologies and delivery period
limitations. The dual values of the new demand constraints
reveal the potential difference in price for each delivery period
when selling renewable methanol at a given price from a
flexible operation configuration. It’s worth noting that the
given price is based on our best estimate, and is consistent
with the pricing structure in flexible operation configuration
(see Table V), as the asset structure in the LP model aligns
with that of flexible operation configuration. The input data
provided included the delivery periods and the corresponding
delivery volumes for each period. The purpose of this data was
to determine the selling price for renewable methanol during
each delivery period, with the primary objective of achieving
the delivery targets while optimizing the hub’s profitability. To
achieve this objective, we apply a demand-driven optimization
approach.
In addition to conducting a detailed analysis of the bottle-
necks within the energy hub (which were found to be identical
to those in flexible operation configuration, as discussed in
Section 5.2), we also performed a potential revenue streams
analysis to gain a better understanding of the hub’s operations.
As shown in Figure 14, our analysis revealed that the main
sources of revenue across all examined years were the selling
of renewable methanol and electricity in the day-ahead market.
Conversely, as it is obvious the main source of expenses was
the consumption of electricity from the grid. Products like
excess water, oxygen, and heat are not the main focus of
the hub right now so their revenue is trivial. While we did
not conduct any further investigations into CO2 prices, it is
worth noting that they could potentially play a significant
role in renewable methanol production. However, the presence
of online CO2 storage could help to mitigate any potential
fluctuations in CO2 prices, resulting in a more constant cost
throughout the year.
Continuing our analysis, the results of the LP Kassø PtX
model are presented and discussed.

As previously mentioned, the main focus of this model is to
quantify the selling price of renewable methanol during each
delivery period. To achieve this, we took into account the most
common delivery periods observed in the real market, which
include daily, weekly, monthly, seasonal, and yearly intervals.
For each delivery period, the model requires a target volume

The impact of closing contracts on a hub’s operational
profits is presented in Table XIII. As mentioned in Section 2,
entering into a contract with an off-taker has several benefits
for mitigating the hub’s operational risks and keeping the
selling price lower. However, it may affect profits if the hub
decides to alter the operation strategy to demand-focused and
the delivery periods while communicating with the off-takers.
Using the LP Kassø PtX model, the percentage of profit loss is
computed for each of the examined years. This calculation is
based on a comparison between the base case which is a hub
price-driven operation without a contract but the same sum
volume yearly delivery (MLP model) and a hub that follows
a strategy of closing contracts (LP model) with off-takers in
the most common delivery periods (Table VII). Based on the
results of several years of examination, it can be observed that
the maximum loss incurred by following a contract strategy is
approximately 8%, keeping the same selling price. Notably, in
years such as 2019 and 2020, characterized by low electricity
prices and price volatility, the profit loss is less than 2.5%.
It is always the safest option to close a contract agreement
with a yearly delivery time with respect to profit. However,
increasing the delivery periods results in a bigger loss, yet
these approaches are more realistic as the off-takers demand
must be covered multiple times per year (please see Figure
15, 28 and 30).
Connecting these results with the Kassø PtX project case
study and finding a realistic simulation, we focus on weekly
delivery period contracts. This is due to the fact that, as
reported by [27], the current purified methanol storage at
Kassø PtX Project has a capacity of 840 tonnes of methanol,
equivalent to a maximum of 7 days of renewable methanol
production. In combination with the contract with Mærsk to
refuel its containers with this purified methanol, this means
that the Kassø PtX project needs to deliver/sell a specific
volume of renewable methanol each week. If the annual
target volume is 32,000 tonnes, then the specific volume
each week is 615.38 tonnes. It is important to note that
the demand constraint pushes the hub to sell this specific
volume, but the production is not constrained, due to the fact
that the renewable methanol storage can store the maximum
production of a week. This means that if electricity prices
are low and solar availability is high, the hub can produce
a maximum of 840 tonnes per week, but only needs to sell
615.38 tonnes. Conversely, if electricity prices are too high
and solar availability is low, the hub will produce only the
amount of renewable methanol required so that at the end of
the week, this amount, plus the amount stored in the methanol
storage for the previous week, equals the target demand of
615.38 tonnes. Similar production-selling behavior is observed
in other examined years, such as 2020 and 2021, depending
on the electricity price and solar availability profiles of each
year, as explained above. The results of 2019 are presented in
Figure 16.
To be even more realistic and sell the target volume of
615.38 tonnes of renewable methanol at a specific hour at
the end of the week, when the containers of Mærsk arrive
at the port for refueling, we need to input the selling price
only for that specific hours. As shown in Figure 17, the last
hour of each week is when the methanol storage discharges
the target volume for refueling the containers. For the same
reasons as before, the storage is not fully discharged every
week because the production volume varies depending on the
electricity prices and solar availability.
When comparing the weekly delivery period contracts,
which are the focus of our case study, with the yearly
delivery period contracts, it becomes clear which option is
more profitable for the hub. The difference in sales behavior
for renewable energy is evident. As depicted in Figure 18,
both contract options aim to sell the same volume by the
end of the year. However, in the case of weekly delivery
periods, the hub’s weekly production remains constant due
to demand constraints, disregarding solar and electricity price
profiles. Consequently, the hub ends up spending more money
purchasing electricity from the grid on a weekly basis as per
requirement. On the other hand, with yearly delivery periods,
the hub enjoys the flexibility and aligns with electricity prices
and solar profiles, generating smaller quantities of renewable
methanol in certain weeks and maximizing production in
others. The reason for opting for stricter delivery periods in
contracts is the hub’s susceptibility to losses associated with
electricity prices.
This is evident in Figure 19, which presents the electricity
consumption from the grid in the two delivery contract options
in 2019. At the beginning of the year with higher electricity
prices, the yearly delivery period contract led the hub to decide
not to consume electricity from the grid because it could cover
the loss in the following weeks when the electricity prices were
lower and solar availability was high. Meanwhile, in weekly
delivery period contracts, electricity consumption was high in
the first few weeks, despite the high electricity prices, but
during the year, it was lower or equal to the other option
because there was no need to recover the production.
Comparing the selling of electricity in the day-ahead market
for both cases, the amount sold week by week is almost the
same, as depicted in Figure 20. This is because the solar park
capacity near Kassø is very large (300 MWp in DC) and is
used for the hub’s operation. Since we do not have information
about a specific part of the capacity used in the Kassø PtX
project, we assumed that all the capacity is used and any extra
electricity is sold directly in the day-ahead market. Similar
explanations can be used for the hub’s behavior regarding
electricity consumption and shedding to the grid for the year
2020 (Figure 29) and 2021 (Figure 31).

B. Renewable methanol storage investment
During years with high electricity price volatility like 2021,
the Kassø PtX Project needs to sell renewable methanol at
extremely high prices. In order to overcome this problem,
mitigate risks, and remain competitive, alternative solutions
are investigated regarding the volume of renewable methanol
storage. More specifically, two scenarios are examined :
• Performing a sensitivity analysis on the existing storage
volume for renewable methanol and its impact on the
hub’s profit,
• Examining long-term operation without storage limita-
tions and finding a common sale price over this extended
period of time.
Methanol has similar store behavior to gasoline. Methanol
can be stored in tanks made of stainless steel, carbon steel,
or aluminum. The storage and transportation of methanol are
subject to regulations and guidelines to ensure safety and
minimize the risk of accidents or spills. Methanol can be
stored as a liquid at ambient temperature and pressure, similar
to other liquid fuels. At standard conditions (25°C and 1
atm), the boiling point of methanol is around 64.7°C. [32]. In
the case of the Kassø PtX Project, all tanks with methanol-
containing products are envisaged as a series of above-ground
tanks of 100 m3 each at a height of up to 8 m. The tanks
will be covered with earth in a mound as fire protection.

It’s important to note that the storage of methanol is done
in accordance with relevant safety regulations and guidelines
to ensure safe handling and prevent accidents or spills [27].
The cost of a methanol storage tank can vary. Factors such
as the tank’s size and capacity, the materials used, and the
location and local market conditions play a significant role
in pricing. A small methanol storage tank with a capacity
of 3 tonnes may cost between 5,000 and 10,000 Euros,
whereas larger tanks with capacities of 100 tonnes could cost
up to 125,000 Euros. However, these figures are extremely
dependent on the specific parameters stated above, and full
quotes and requirements from suppliers and manufacturers
are required for accurate cost estimates. [32]. Regarding
the above information renewable methanol storage does
not require extremely expensive storage so an investigation
into storage volume is a really interesting alternative to battery.
1) Sensitivity analysis in methanol storage volume: Having
renewable methanol storage with a maximum capacity of 7
days of production can limit the production capacity, particu-
larly in periods of high solar availability (typically weeks 16
to 30 in all examined years) and low electricity prices. When
the renewable methanol storage has a capacity of 840 tonnes,
the hub can produce a maximum of 840 tonnes of renewable
methanol in those weeks. However, even if there is stored
renewable methanol from the previous week, the hub cannot
store more, which can result in production constraints despite
the hub’s ability to produce more. A larger renewable methanol
storage capacity can avoid such constraints, allowing the hub
to produce 840 tonnes of renewable methanol in all weeks with
these characteristics. Hence, the optimal renewable methanol
storage capacity to unlock this flexibility for each year needs
to be determined.

storage volume are presented in Figure 21, which depicts the
impact of increasing methanol storage volume. By increasing

additional profit while safeguarding the hub against electricity
price volatility.

volume is not profitable economically.

A. Conclusion
Previous publications on PtX technologies in microgrid sys-
tems often overlook important aspects such as multiple market
participation, investment operation mechanisms at an hourly
level, and the analysis of stakeholder behavior associated
with hub operation. To address these issues, a deterministic
operation model of the Kassø PtX Project, based on the
EnerHub2X framework, was developed and applied to a real
Danish case study. The case study focuses on the Kassø PtX
Project industrial business park, with the ultimate goal of
producing renewable methanol while achieving a well-defined
symbiosis system and complying with all new EU regulations.
The Kassø PtX Project has already established contracts with
large off-takers to consume the produced renewable methanol.
While most publications calculate the levelized cost of re-
newable methanol, they do not take into account specific
delivery times. Projects like the Kassø PtX Project, which
are closely related to solar seasonality and periodicity, may
face significant challenges by introducing delivery periods and
targets. The master thesis aims to provide insights into the
operation of the energy hub, with a focus on adding flexibility
to the conversion and separation assets. A flexible operation
can lead to a lower selling price of renewable methanol
compared to a non-flexible operation. This proves why, the
main goals of the EU Committee regarding hubs, are flexibility
and scalability.
Relaxing the Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP)
to Linear Programming (LP) yields slightly different results,
with a deviation of approximately 2% due to the linearization
techniques used. This teaches us that these two optimization
techniques do not provide identical outcomes. Price-driven
optimization results in higher profit comparing demand-driven
optimization. However, our relaxation approach has been suc-
cessful due to the minimal impact observed in the results.
By formulating a linear programming model and incorpo-
rating specific delivery periods and annual targets, the Kassø
PtX model can calculate the precise selling prices of renewable
methanol required for the hub to achieve the given volume
target for each delivery period. The findings reveal that a
yearly delivery period contract is the most lucrative compared
to other contract options for the hub. Shorter delivery periods
result in a loss of profit, particularly in years characterized
by high electricity prices and volatility, such as 2021. The
most significant loss occurs when transitioning from a yearly
delivery period to a seasonal one due to the seasonality of solar
power. Subsequently, smaller delivery periods like months,
weeks, and days have a lesser impact on profit loss.
To address this problem of solar seasonality, we propose
two ways in which renewable methanol storage can improve
the hub’s operation and profitability. Firstly keeping the same
selling prices, by increasing the renewable methanol storage
volume by three to five times, within four years, the hub
can take back its investment and at the same time increase
its operating profit especially in years with high electricity
price volatility despite the demand-driven optimization. This
increase in storage capacity would also add more flexibility to
the production process, enabling the hub to adapt to market
conditions more effectively. Secondly, by adopting a long-
term strategy and increasing the storage volume of renewable
methanol by 22 times (18,150 tonnes of renewable methanol
storage), the hub can reduce the selling price of renewable
methanol by 45% during years with high electricity price
volatility while still keeping small delivery period contracts.
Therefore, we suggest setting the selling price at 723.4 Eur/ton
for the 3-year operation period from 2019 to 2021, which will
make it more competitive and accelerate its promotion as a
green fuel.
B. Recommendations
In this subsection, recommendations, and suggestions to the
operators of the Kassø PtX project are provided:
• Real data when the hub starts to operate must be provided
to the deterministic model so as to calibrate the efficiency
and accurate operation and can be used by the operators.
• To avoid solar curtailment, it is recommended that the
project participates in the day-ahead market with the
synthetic fuel market.
• The Kassø PtX project has two operating options: oper-
ating under an inelastic demand profile (demand-driven
optimization) or participating in markets as a flexible
asset (price-driven optimization).
• The most lucrative approach for the project is consistently
operating under a price-driven mechanism, allowing it to
optimize operational profit by leveraging price signals.
However, it is important to note that this mechanism
can lead to an increase in the selling price of renewable
methanol. In order to reduce the selling price of methanol,
the hub needs to adopt a demand-driven strategy, which
may involve accepting certain operational profit losses.
This strategy prioritizes responding to market demand
and adjusting production levels accordingly, which can
help lower the selling price of renewable methanol.
• The suggested prices for selling renewable methanol
vary significantly over the years and may introduce risks
for off-takers. For this reason, contract agreements for
long-term and demand-driven mechanism strategies are
favorable.
• By adding flexibility to the hub’s operation, the selling
price of renewable methanol can be reduced, following
the levelized cost.
• Relaxing the mixed integer model to a linear model using
linearization techniques yields similar but not exactly the
same results.
• Closing contracts is easier in years with low electricity
price volatility. Yearly delivery period contracts are the
only contract type that keeps the operational profit at
the same levels despite the volatility between price and
demand-driven operation strategy.

• Increasing the capacity of renewable methanol storage

C. Further Investigation and discussion
The thesis investigation was performed around the ini-
tial scientific question ’of how renewable methanol selling
contracts and specific delivery periods affect the operation
of the Kassø PtX Project. Following the methodology and
the assumptions significant insights are presented. During the
process of the thesis, further new topics and new points of
interest appeared. The PtX technologies and renewable fuels
are growing rapidly and are expected to evolve even further
in the next years. Here some topics for further investigation
are proposed.
In this master thesis, excess heat from both the electrolyzer
and methanol synthesis processes is sold to district heating
networks without taking into account the temperature differ-
ences between the processes. However, determining the quality
of heat produced is non-trivial. Specific heat qualities are
needed for direct use in district heating, otherwise, a heat pump
network should be installed.
An investigation into the opportunity to recycle wastewater
and reuse it is recommended. In the model, the wastewater
is sold by the hub at a very low price, so recycling it could
have significant benefits. According to [27], the wastewater
contains only 0.25% methanol and other auxiliaries in very
small quantities, so it does not require difficult treatment
before reuse. Moreover, an osmosis plant can be simulated
in further investigation.
Adding an electric storage system after Kassø solar park,
such as a battery, can provide several benefits to the Kassø
PtX project. Firstly, it can provide flexibility in electricity
consumption and help to match the electricity demand with
intermittent solar production. Secondly, it can unlock seasonal
storage opportunities by storing excess electricity produced in
the summer months for use in the winter months. However,
adding a battery also involves additional costs for the project.
The cost of the investment in the battery needs to be compared
with the additional operational profit that can be obtained
from its operation. A feasibility study should be carried out
to determine the optimal size of the battery, the charging
and discharging strategies, and the expected revenue from its
operation. This study should consider the expected electricity
prices and the expected revenue from the sale of renewable
methanol, as well as any potential subsidies or incentives for
energy storage.
The introduction of a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)
can provide a solution to the uncertainty in electricity prices,
complementing the use of batteries. Hub is directly connected
to electricity prices as half of the power needs are covered
by the grid. The most suitable PPA structure for the Kassø
PtX Project symbiosis system would be a sleeved corporate
PPA. This type of agreement, with the onsite connection of the
energy hub, can guarantee the expenses related to electricity
consumption from the grid. By using a PPA, the Kassø PtX
project can operate under a demand profile and achieve the
target production more easily, with less risk. Additionally,
introducing a PPA can help the hub follow a long-term strategy
and keep the selling price of renewable methanol stable,
without requiring a very large methanol storage volume.
In summary, this master thesis aimed to simulate the Kassø
PtX Project operation accurately, providing detailed technical
information on the project’s assets and comparing flexible ver-
sus inflexible operations. The introduction of specific delivery
period contracts and investigation into renewable methanol
storage opportunities are novel ideas in the PtX technology
modeling field. The project configuration was based solely on
the description provided by [27], with no additional changes
or information. The suggestions for further investigation can
be utilized in future studies to enhance the Kassø PtX Project’s
profitability.
