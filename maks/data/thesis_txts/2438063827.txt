Abstract
Transcranial Brain Stimulation (TBS) Methods modulate brain function by creating an
electric ﬁeld in the brain.TBS is now widely used in Neuroscience research, and has clinical
applications in the treatment of depression, migrane, and ﬁbromyalgia. Treatments for other
neurological and psychiatrical conditions are under active investigation.
To accurately apply TBS, the practitioner need to know the location of induced brain
activation, and the direction and magnitude of the electric ﬁeld in the brain. However, the
complex shape of the various head tissues such as gray matter and skull, makes estimation of
the current ﬂow a diﬃcult task. One solution to this problem is to use precise, subject-speciﬁc
head models to calculate the spatial distribution, magnitude and direction of the electric ﬁeld
in the brain. In the current work, we will advance electric ﬁeld modelling in the SimNIBS
software package by introducing novel algorithms leading to more eﬃcient calculations of
the ﬁelds generated by TBS. In addition, we will explore the geometrical requirements of
the head meshes that need to be fullﬁlled in order to have accurate simulations.
The electrical properties of head tissue are not known with high precision, which puts the
accuracy of simulation results in question. Here, we propose an uncertainty quantiﬁcation
(UQ) method to address this issue. By assuming the tissue conductivities to be stochastic
rather than deterministic, we are able to obtain a probability distribution of the electric
ﬁeld due to these uncertaintanties. This allow for a principled statistical treatment of the
simulations, thereby yielding more reliable results.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Transcranial Brain Stimulation
Non-Invasive Transcranial Brain Stimulation (TBS) methods such as Transcranial Magnetic
Stimulation (TMS) and Transcranial Electric Stimulation (TES) modulate brain activity by
generating electric ﬁelds in the cortex. These electric ﬁelds are able to cause neurons to ﬁre
(in the case of TMS [42]), or modulate excitability of the neurons (in the case of TES [32]).
Interest in both methods is increasing due to the several research applications in basic and
clinical neuroscience as well as promising clinical applications such as depression treatment.
As the electric ﬁeld is a key part of the mechanism of action of brain stimulation methods,
it is fundamental that we are able to accurately determine its strength and spatial distribu-
tion. Therefore, we need to have accurate ways to simulate the ﬁelds and we have to be able
to characterize uncertainties of the ﬁeld estimates. The latter is particularly challenged by
the lack of reliable experimental data concerning the electrical proprieties of the head tissues
at low frequency.
In chapter 2, we present a new implementation of the Finite Element Method (FEM),
which is more eﬃcient than previous implementations. After validating the implementation,
we characterize the conditions, which the FEM head model has to meet in order to obtain

Chapter 3 presents an eﬃcient way to calculate how uncertainties in the electrical pro-
prieties of the tissues aﬀect the resulting electric ﬁeld. This approach enables us to quantify

1.1.1
Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)
Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) uses a coil and a stimulator in order to deliver
a powerful magnetic pulse creating a current ﬂow in the brain. The stimulator has a high-
voltage source and a large capacitor, which when discharged delivers a current I quickly
changing in time (dI/dt > 106A/s). This current causes, by induction, a varying magnetic
ﬂux through the coil, which in turns generates an electric ﬁeld inside the subject’s brain.
There is a large number of commercially available coils, ranging from small, 60mm cir-
cular coils to larger, 96mm ﬁgure-of-eight coils.
TMS is applied in neuroscience research as a tool to investigate brain excitability and
function. Diﬀerent protocols can be used to either evoke activity in a certain region or to
inhibit it temporally. For example, TMS over the motor cortex activates the pyramidal cells
controlling the muscles and by that causes muscle twitches [42].
Repetitive TMS (rTMS), which consists of applying many TMS pulses at a frequency of
≤20Hz over time periods of several seconds to minutes is currently approved by the U.S.
Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of drug-resistant depression [8]. The
use of TMS for the treatment of other neurological conditions is currently being investigated
1.1.2
Transcranial Electric Stimulation (TES)
Transcranial Electric Stimulation (TES) refers to the application of weak direct current
(normally called transcranial Direct Current Stimulation, tDCS) or weak low-frequency al-
ternating current (transcranial Alternating Current Stimulation, tACS) to the brain through
electrodes placed on the scalp. The current applied is small (≤2mA), causing electric ﬁelds

in the cortex that are well below the threshold needed to cause neural ﬁring [33]. How-
ever, this small amount of current can facilitate or inhibit neural activity by polarizing or
depolarizing the neural membrane.
TES, especially tDCS is becoming a very popular research tool in cognitive neuroscience,
due its availability and ease of use [36]. A range of electrode shapes and montages have been
introduced that can be used to to target given brain regions. Typical electrode sizes vary
from 5cm2 to 35cm2.
Currently, tDCS has CE approval for the treatment of Major Depression Disorder, Fi-
bromyalgia and Migrane [48, 47]

TMS

1.3
Head Models
Head tissues have very diﬀerent electrical conductivities σ, resulting in a spatially varying
conductivity inside the head. For example, cerebral spinal ﬂuid (CSF) has approximately
160× higher conductivity value than skull [54]. Therefore, to accurately calculate the electric
ﬁeld in the brain, we need to have precise head models, with anatomically accurate repre-
sentations of the diﬀerent tissue compartments. The work presented on this issue was built
on top of the existing package SimNIBS [51], for which new, improved head models were
recently developed [31]. SimNIBS is able to automatically segment the major head tissues
from Magnetic Resonance (MR) images. The segmented tissues are then used to create a
tetrahedral mesh, as for example shown in Figure 1.1

2.1
Introduction
To calculate the electric ﬁelds (Equation 1.3 and Equation 1.9) in a complex geometry such as
the human head, we need a computational model to solve the partial diﬀerential equations
(PDEs) governing the TMS system (Equation 1.1) and the TES system (Equation 1.4).
Besides the Finite Element Method (FEM), there are several alternative methods, such as
the Finite Diﬀerence Method (FDM). In the current work we choose the FEM because it
works well on unstructured grids that can eﬃciently represent complex geometry such as
the head anatomy, in particular when using tetrahedra as basic elements. FEM also allows
for an easy implementation of anisotropic conductivity tensors. It has been the method of
choice in most studies working with simulations of the electric ﬁelds caused by Transcranial
Brain Stimulation, especially TES [28, 30, 6, 54].
However, most studies up until now either use commercial software, such as COMSOL,
or relatively slow generic FEM software, such as GetDP [16, 51]. As applications requiring
multiple calculations such as TES optimization [11, 43] and Uncertainty Quantiﬁcation ([53,

2.3
Implementation
In order to improve the eﬃciency of the simulations, we implemented a novel FEM system
assembly process ( subsection 2.3.1) and used advanced FEM solvers ( subsection 2.3.2)
2.3.1
System Assembly
In a classical Finite Element assembly process, we start by initializing the global stiﬀness
matrix A, and right-hand side (RHS) vector f. We then proceed to loop over all the elements,
calculate the element matrix Ae and the vector f e, and add each entry to the corresponding
entry in the global A and f.
However, as our implementation is in Python, we wanted to avoid slow loops in favor of
faster vector opetarions. Therefore, we implemented the OptVS algorithm described in [9],
with heavy usage of the vector library NumPy [34].
This algorithm, instead of looping
element wise, calculates the ij-th entry of all the element stiﬀness matrices Ae using vector
operations. Therefore, the number of external loops is reduced from the order of 106 to 10.
The implementation allows for very eﬃcient assembly of ﬁnite element matrices, as it will
be seen in section 2.5 and will be available in future versions of SimNIBS.
Implementation of Dirichlet boundary conditions is done by the method of elimina-
tion [38], whereby the rows and columns pertaining to nodes in the boundary condition
region are removed from the system. Afterwards, adjustments to both the left and right-
hand side are made to enforce the value of the nodes and keep the system’s symmetry.

2.3.2
System Solution
After the assembly process and applying the condition that the solution must be valid for
all ν ∈Vh we have a system of the type
Au = f
(2.14)
Due to the local support of the basis functions, the matrix A is sparse. However, as we have
approximately one degree of freedom per node in the mesh, the matrix is also large, typically
of the order of 105×105, as will be seen in section 2.6. As we are solving the Poisson problem
with the Galerkin method, the matrix A is also symmetric.
For the TES case, the matrix A becomes positive-deﬁnite after the introduction of the
Dirichlet boundary conditions, making it a symmetric positive-deﬁnite (SPD) matrix. In the
TMS case, however, the matrix is only positive-semideﬁnite, which limits our choice of solvers
and preconditioners. This property reﬂects the fact that constants can be freely added to the
electric potential without changing the underlying physics. Therefore, the null space of the
TMS stiﬀness matrix is one dimensional, and is the space spanned by a constant. This means
that the TMS stiﬀness matrix can be made SPD by adding a Dirichlet boundary condition
in a small region, and thereby removing the possibility of a ﬂuctuating electrical potential.
In our implementation, we have added an homogeneous Dirichlet boundary condition in a
single node, far away from the coil.
There are many methods to solve sparse systems of equations. For our applications, we
preferred iterative methods over direct ones, as they have a smaller memory requirement
and are more eﬃcient in solving for only one or a few diﬀerent right-hand sides. In the class
of iterative methods, as we have chosen to use the conjugate gradient (CG), as our systems
are SPD and we found it to be memory and time eﬃcient. The stop criteria for the iterative
solver was a relative tolerance of 10−10. In terms of eﬃciency, the choice of preconditioner is
as critical as the choice of the iterative algorithm. We found the algebraic multigrid (AMG)
preconditioner boomeramg implemented in the package hypre [12] to be very eﬃcient, as it
will be veriﬁed in section 2.5.
The interface with the preconditioner and solver is done using PETSc [4] and petsc4py [10].
Again, this implementation will be a part of future SimNIBS releases.

Calculating the sum of the gradients of the basis functions in the FEM setting corresponds
to solving one 3 × 3 system per element
As the basis functions in our case vary linearly inside each element, the gradient ∇φ is
piecewise-constant. That is, ∇φ and therefore E has a constant value within each element,
and discontinuities at the boundary with adjacent elements.
The discontinuities in E are sources of inaccuracies in interpolations of the electric ﬁelds
at a given set of points r. One approach to improve the accuracy of interpolation procedures
is to ﬁrst recover nodal values using the Superconvergent Patch Recovery (SPR) method [57]
and then calculate the electric ﬁeld E(r) by baricentric interpolation.
In our case, the
conductivity σ is discontinuous across diﬀerent tissues, and therefore the electric ﬁeld E also
presents discontinuities at tissue boundaries. So, in order to prevent mixing the values in
adjacent tissues, we perform the SPR procedure one tissue at a time.

Comparison with previous implementation
SimNIBS 2.1 [51] uses GetDP [16] to assemble the FEM matrix and solve the resulting
system. GetDP is conﬁgured to use a conjugate gradient solver and incomplete Cholesky
preconditioner with factor level 2. The stopping criteria was the same as the one used for
the new FEM procedure.
We want to validate the new FEM implementation, here called SimNIBS 2.2, and com-
pare its speed and memory consumption to the old implementation. For this purpose, we
used the TMS and TES montages shown in Figure 1.1, conductivities of 0.126S/m for white

over the entire gray matter volume, with the solution obtained with getDP as a reference.

identify a mesh resampling algorithm that can better represent the surfaces morphology with
a smaller number of nodes.
For the remaining of the current work, we selected the density of ≈0.5 Nodes/mm2, as
the errors are relatively low 6 × 10−2 and 8 × 10−2 for TMS and TES respectively, the ﬁelds
are visually hard to distinguish from the most accurate ones (Figures 2.6 and 2.8), and the
cost of the simulation is low (Figure 2.10).
As for the interpolation procedure, we can see in Figures 2.7 and 2.9 that the SPR proce-
dure signiﬁcantly improves accuracy when compared with the discontinuous FEM solution,
resulting often in almost half the original error.

2.7.2
Discussion and Conclusions
The sulcal model presented here is highly simpliﬁed, and therefore we should not over-
state the conclusions that can be drawn from it. In the sulcal model, surface density of
≈0.5 Nodes/mm2 still generates signiﬁcant errors (between 2 × 10−2 and 6 × 10−2), even
though the surface representation is accurate. As we see signiﬁcant errors stemming from
the element sizes, the results from this model appear to contradict the conclusions from sec-
==PAGE:42==
Chapter 2.
Simulating TMS and TES Electric Fields
29
tion 2.6. Those results indicated that element sizes are not of major concern, but rather
the morphology. This apparent contradiction is explained by the observation that for the
TES top/down case for the simpliﬁed sulcus, the peak ﬁelds occurred only at the sulcus
fundus (that is, the bottom of the sulcus), so that errors in that region were strongly repre-
sented in the global error metrics. On the other hand, for meshes of the complete head, high
ﬁeld strength occur at many positions in gray matter other than the sulcus fundi, so that
localized errors in these regions do not dominate the error metrics. Taken together, these
results suggest that a local reﬁnement of the mesh density around the sulcus fundi would
be desirable. This would require better mesh simpliﬁcation algorithms, that are out of the
scope of the current work.

3.4.4
Discussion and Conclusion
We showed statistical proprieties of the electric ﬁeld and other quantities of interest, given
the uncertainties in ﬁeld conductivities and were able to identify the tissues contributing
most to the uncertainties (gray matter in TMS and TES, compact bone and skin in TES).
The analysis of the dosage (98th percentile of the electric ﬁeld) and stimulated areas (RDM)
revealed that, while the spatial distribution of the electric ﬁeld in the cortex can be quite
accurately determined despite uncertainty of conductivity values, there is signiﬁcant uncer-
tainty for the dose being delivered, especially in TES. This might help us to understand the
variable outcome of TES interventions.
3.5
Convergence Analysis
Until now that we have investigated how gPC can be used to characterize uncertainties
arising in TBS problems. Here we will analyze the adaptive gPC algorithms themselves
in order to optimize their convergence.We base the analysis on two data sets for the TMS
problem and two data sets for the standard TES problem. Each data set is composed of
1000 randomly sampled conductivity combinations σ, and their corresponding electric ﬁelds
E(σ). For each TBS method, one set was assigned as a training set, and the other as a
validation set. The training set was used to draw samples for calculating the gPC coeﬃcients
s and to perform the cross-validations. The validation set was only used to compute the
error (Equation 3.33), and as it contains 1000 simulations, it was taken to yield a more
==PAGE:66==
Chapter 3.
Uncertainty Quantification
53
accurate error estimate than the ones obtained with cross-validation. This set-up allow us
to quickly draw samples and calculate error against a large set of simulations. In order to
increase the stability of the comparisons the random number generator seed was kept ﬁxed
when generating the training data.
The gPC coeﬃcients were calculated using regularized regression (Equation 3.27). The
regularization parameter λ was chosen among 9 log-spaced values between 10−5 and 103
using 10-fold CV.

obtained. On the one hand, if we have too few data points per polynomial, we might end

Chapter 4
Conclusion and Outlook
This thesis focused on numerical ﬁeld simulations for the two most important Tran-
scranial Brain Stimulation (TBS) methods: Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) and
Transcranial Electrical Stimultion (TES). We addressed the computational method employed
for the electric ﬁeld simulations, studied how to obtain accurate results with computation-
ally eﬃcient implementations of the method. In addition, we systematically characterized
the uncertaintanties in the simulated ﬁelds that arise from uncertain electrical properties in
head tissues.
In Chapter 2, we introduced the Finite Element Method (FEM), a mathematical and
computational tool able to accurately appropriate solutions for diﬀerential equations in ar-
bitrary geometries. We discussed our implementation of FEM, based on newly developed
algorithms. The new implementation was shown to be more eﬃcient and as accurate as the
previous implementation, present in the group’s software SimNIBS [51]. Finally, we explored
the conditions to have accurate TBS simulations in an anatomically realistic head model.
We reached the conclusion that an accurate model of the foldings of the cortical surfaces is
of fundamental importance, in turn requiring a relatively detailed mesh with many nodes.
Therefore simulation speed and accuracy might be improved further in the future by apply-
ing meshing procedures capable of capturing the ﬁne features of the cortical surface with
fewer mesh nodes, such as harmonic maps [41].

Chapter 3 introduces the concept of uncertainty quantiﬁcation (UQ), focusing on the
non-intrusive generalized polynomial chaos expansion (gPC) [23]. We show how the gPC
expansion helps to eﬃciently estimate the uncertainties in the electric ﬁeld, given that the
tissue conductivity values are uncertain and exhibit considerable variability across subjects.
Our results indicate that the spatial distribution of the electric ﬁeld can be determined with
relatively high certainty, while the stimulation dose is more uncertain, especially in TES.
The analysis also showed which tissues are the largest contributors to the uncertainty in the
electric ﬁeld. Additionally, we propose a new algorithm that increases the computational
eﬃciency of gPC expansions substantially. Furthermore, we demonstrate that the algorithm
can reliably estimate the electrical ﬁeld in context of TBS simulations with varying conduc-
tivities.
There are extensive possibilities of future work in this area. One immediate possibility it
to extend the UQ analysis towards uncertainties in the electrode or coil locations. There are
also opportunities to further improve the computational eﬃciency of UQ analysis by utilizing
recently developed methods. One particular example is the use of reduced basis methods
which can result in highly eﬃcient and reliable algorithms to approximate solutions in aﬃnely
parametrized PDEs [20].
Both TMS and TES problems with varying conductivities are
examples of this class of problems. There has also been advances in the ﬁeld of polynomial
chaos expansion, with the development of pseudospectral methods [7].
These oﬀer new
ways to calculate gPC coeﬃcients, requiring fewer simulations than the regression approach.
Finally, methods have been developed to measure the current-induced magnetic ﬁeld inside
the brain using MR imaging [17], opening up the possibility to infer tissue conductivity
values from non-invasive measurement data.

ABSTRACT 
Uncertainty of the ohmic tissue conductivities prevents accurate calculations of the electric 
field generated by non-invasive brain stimulation. We present an efficient and generic 
technique for uncertainty and sensitivity analysis, which quantifies the reliability of field 
estimates and identifies the most influencing parameters. For this purpose, we employ a 
non-intrusive generalized polynomial chaos expansion to compactly approximate the 
multidimensional dependency of the field on the conductivities. We demonstrate that the 
proposed pipeline yields detailed insight into the uncertainty of field estimates for 
transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) and transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS), 
identifies the most relevant tissue conductivities, and highlights characteristic differences 
between stimulation methods. Specifically, we test the influence of conductivity variations 
on (i) the magnitude of the electric field generated at each gray matter location, (ii) its 
direction relative to the cortical sheet, (iii) its overall magnitude (indexed by the 98th 
percentile), and (iv) its overall spatial distribution. We show that TMS fields are generally 
less affected by conductivity variations than tDCS fields. For both TMS and tDCS, 
conductivity uncertainty causes much higher uncertainty in the magnitude as compared to 
the direction and the overall spatial distribution of the electric field. Finally, while the TMS 
fields are mostly influenced by gray and white matter conductivity, the tDCS fields 
additionally depend on skull and scalp conductivities. The comprehensive uncertainty 
analyses of complex systems achieved by the proposed technique are not possible with 
classical methods, like Monte Carlo sampling, without extreme computational effort. In 
addition, our method has the advantages of directly yielding interpretable and intuitive 
output metrics and of being easily adaptable to new problems.

INTRODUCTION 
Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) and transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) 
are the two widely used non-invasive neurostimulation techniques [Wagner et al. (2007), 
Fregni and Pascual-Leone (2007)]. For a precise prediction of the neural effect of such 
stimulation, we need to accurately estimate the electric field generated in the brain. To 
achieve this, the complex head and brain anatomy requires numerical techniques such as 
the finite element method (FEM) [Windhoff et al. (2013), Opitz et al. (2015)]. These 
techniques rely on knowledge about the electrical conductivities of the major head tissues. 
However, reliable measurements of these conductivities are difficult to obtain and the 
results vary considerably between experimental conditions, subjects, and even repetitions 
of the same experiment [Geddes and Baker (1967), Gabriel et al. (1996)]. A recent in-vivo 
validation study by Huang et al. (2017) showed that conductivity optimization yields similar 
values compared to standard values reported in the literature. However, the optimal values 
varied considerably between subjects. For these reasons, one needs to systematically

analyze how the uncertainty in tissue conductivity translates into an uncertainty of the 
predicted electric field. Doing this in the classical way would require sampling 
computationally expensive FEM models over a large multidimensional parameter space. 
While an exhaustive sampling of the parameter space is clearly out of question, even 
established methods such as Monte Carlo (MC) sampling are not applicable due to their 
slow convergence and the need for a large number of sampling points. Here, we describe 
a principled approach based on a non-intrusive generalized polynomial chaos (gPC) 
expansion [Wiener (1938), Ghanem et al. (2016)] to bypass the aforementioned 
difficulties. We quantify the uncertainty of the electric field caused by the given 
uncertainties of the tissue conductivities, based on a precise and effective analytical 
representation of the transfer function (or functional dependence) between conductivities 
and fields [Weise et al. (2015), Codecasa et al. (2016), Schmidt et al. (2015)]. The non-
intrusive gPC approach used in the current framework is schematically illustrated in Fig. 1. 
In this report, we first describe in detail the methodology of the proposed approach. Then 
we provide and discuss three illustrative examples in order to demonstrate the usefulness 
and descriptive power of the method in characterizing the fields generated by TMS and 
tDCS. Finally, we briefly discuss possible generalizations and extensions of the techniques 
to emphasize its potential as a universal tool for model assessment and optimization.

In contrast to the Sobol indices, which are stochastic sensitivity measures, they are 
deterministic. Analyzing both at the same time may be advantageous since they feature 
different advantages and disadvantages, as illustrated in Fig. 3. The Sobol index, shown 
on the left, measures the variance of an output variable due to the variance of an input 
variable. It is thus sensitive to any sort of relationship between the two variables, which 
neither needs to be monotonous nor linear and, strictly speaking, not even deterministic 
(although, in our case, it is). On the other hand, the global derivative reflects the average 
(linear) sensitivity of an output variable towards an input variable over the range of the 
input variable. Hence, it is only sensitive to the linear part of the transfer function. A

sensitivity, whereas the Sobol indices would reveal the actual variation. This being a 
restriction, derivative-based sensitivities have the advantage that they allow for 
conclusions on the direction of the influence (proportional or inversely proportional).

cortical sheet1, as well as global parameters: (iii) the 98th percentile of the electric field

TMS, standard tDCS and focal alternative of tDCS. For TMS, the coil position is above the 
left motor cortex. A typical figure-8 coil (Magstim 70 mm) was centered above the hand

normalized rate of change of the coil current of dI/dt=1 A/µs was used. Please note that

mm gel and 4 mm rubber layers, with conductivities 1 S/m and 0.1 S/m). For each of the 
electrodes, the upper surface was set to a fixed potential corresponding to Dirichlet 
boundary conditions, and the results were later scaled to ensure a current of 1 mA. For the 
“focal” tDCS setup, a motor cortex montage was used with a central round electrode pad 
(2 cm diameter; 4 mm gel and 4 mm rubber layers, with conductivities 1 S/m and 0.1 
S/m) and a surrounding ring electrode (7.5 cm and 10 cm inner and outer diameters; 4 
mm gel and 4 mm rubber layers, with conductivities 1 S/m and 0.1 S/m). For analysis, the 
electric fields were read out in the middle of the gray matter layer, based on the 
interpolation method proposed by Bungert et al. (2016). 
 
 
RESULTS 
Overview 
We start by a general overview of the results before presenting the findings for each of the 
three examples in more detail. The gPC converged after 162 (TMS), 108 (focal tDCS), and 
77 (standard tDCS) FEM simulations. 
The expected (mean) field strengths are shown in Fig. 4a, and their uncertainty is 
characterized by the relative standard deviation (RSD) and overall variance (VAR; Fig. 4a 
& top row of 4b). The individual contributions of the conductivity uncertainties to the overall 
variance are represented by the Sobol indices (Fig. 4b). In addition, they are characterized 
by the global-derivative-based sensitivity coefficients in Fig. 5. Both measures convey 
similar information. While the Sobol indices are intuitively interpretable as portions of the 
==PAGE:94==
overall variance, the sensitivity coefficients have the advantage that they convey 
directional information. 
The 98th percentiles (Fig. 6) show how the electric field strength that is exceeded on a 
cortical area of about 40 cm² is affected by the conductivity uncertainties. It can be seen 
that the relative standard deviation for TMS is much smaller than that of tDCS.  
The effects of stimulation are not only dependent on the field strength, but also on the field 
direction. Therefore, Fig. 7 depicts the results of the gPC analysis for the field angle with 
respect to the cortex normal. Finally, the influence of conductivity variations on the overall 
field pattern is quantified by means of a gPC analysis with the RDM as quantity of interest. 
The probability density functions (pdf) of the RDM are shown in Fig. 8. In order to convey 
a visual impression of the severity of the expected field pattern changes as revealed by 
the pdfs, exemplary field patterns for the 95th percentile are also shown. In addition, the 
normalized Sobol indices are depicted in Fig. 9 to provide insight into the composition of 
the variance of the RDM. Since the conductivities of GM and WM were identified as the 
most important model parameters, the dependency of the RDM upon both of them is 
separately shown in Fig. 10. 
 
TMS 
For TMS, the mean (Fig. 4a) shows a focal distribution on the gyral crowns. RSD and VAR 
(Fig. 4a,b) reveal a much higher uncertainty in the sulcal walls (20–25%) than on the gyral 
crowns (<5%). The Sobol indices (Fig. 4b) show that the uncertainties of GM and WM 
conductivities are the main sources of the uncertainty of the electric field strength, 
explaining approximately 75% and 23% of VAR, respectively. The remaining tissues play 
a minor role. 
The results of the global-derivative-based sensitivity (Fig. 5) confirm these findings and 
additionally reveal the directionality of the effects: For example, an increased conductivity 
of WM results in an increase of the electric field in GM, which indicates a direct 
proportionality (positive derivative, red color). In contrast, an increase in GM conductivity 
reduces the field magnitude, which indicates an inverse relationship (negative derivative, 
==PAGE:95==
blue color). These effects can be explained by the law of current conservation and Ohm’s 
law. Increasing GM conductivity decreases the electric field strength in GM, indicating that 
the current density is only slightly increased. On the other hand, increasing the conductivity 
of neighboring WM generally increases the current flow in the brain, and therefore also 
increases the electric field in GM.  
For the 98th percentile of the field magnitude, the standard deviation is only about 5.6 % 
of the mean, and the Sobol indices reveal, in accordance to the location-wise results above, 
that almost all of the variance is due to GM and WM conductivity uncertainties. 
The results for the field angle relative to the cortex normal (Fig. 7) show that misestimating 
the conductivities can result in changes up to 5 degrees and more (note that the unit of 
the plotted variance is degrees²) in the sulcal walls, but it is very small on the crowns. The 
main contributors to angular deviations are again GM and WM. 
The probability density function of the RDM shown in Fig. 8 indicates that the influence of 
the conductivities on the pattern of the electric field is only small. It is very unlikely that 
the RDM exceeds a value of 0.25. In order to get a visual impression of what these values 
actually mean, the normal and tangential components of the electric field are shown for 
the reference case and for RDM=0.2. Both field maps are normalized to their respective 
maxima, since the RDM quantifies differences in field shape and not in magnitude. Their 
similarity shows that the conductivities only weakly influence the spatial pattern of the field 
generated in the cortex. 
Inspecting the Sobol indices (Fig. 9) reveals that only the GM and WM conductivities 
notably influence the RDM. Interestingly, a substantial part of the variance is explained by 
the interaction between these two.  
We also re-evalulated the model fit provided by the gPC approximation by systematically 
sampling from GM and WM while keeping the other conductivies at their mean values. This 
is illustrated in Fig. 10, showing that a low RDM occurs as long as the ratio between GM 
and WM conductivities is within a certain (quite broad) range. Moreover, we see that the 
GPC approximation of that function is very good except for some corner regions, where 
==PAGE:96==
the joint probability density function of the parameters, and therefore the impact onto the 
output distribution, is extremely low. 
 
Focal tDCS 
The mean distribution for focal tDCS resembles the one of TMS (Fig. 4), but the field 
uncertainty is generally higher. The RSD for the field magnitude is high in the sulcal fundi 
(~33%) and on the gyral crowns (~27%). The variance shows a similar pattern. The Sobol 
indices reveal that the main contributors to the variance are the uncertainties of the 
conductivities of compact bone (~40%), GM (~30%), and scalp (~25%), minor 
contributions come from WM (~3%) and CSF (~2%). Similar results are obtained for the 
global-derivative-based sensitivities shown in Fig. 5, which underline that the 
conductivities of compact bone, GM and scalp have the strongest influence on the field 
strength. Expectedly, increases in compact bone conductivity lead to increases of the 
electric field in GM, as more current passes through bone in that case. In contrast, an 
increase in scalp conductivity tends to “short circuit” or “shunt” the current along the head 
surface and in consequence decreases the electric field. These effects are mainly present 
at the gyral crowns below the central electrode.  
The standard deviation of the 98th percentile of the magnitude is about 23.5 % of the 
mean, which is much larger than the uncertainty found for TMS. The Sobol indices confirm 
the findings for the location-wise results (significant contributions by GM, compact bone 
and skin). 
The results for the field angle relative to the cortex normal (Fig. 7) show that significant 
directional uncertainty caused by the uncertainty of the conductivity values occur only 
where the field amplitude is very low (compare Fig. 4a). 
The probability density function of the RDM shows only slightly larger values compared to 
TMS (Fig. 8). Correspondingly, no strong difference in field shape can be observed when 
comparing normal and tangential component of the electric field between the reference 
and RDM=0.2. The normalized Sobol indices in Fig. 9 show that the field pattern is again 
mainly influenced by the GM and WM conductivities and their interaction, but that also 
==PAGE:97==
other tissues such as cortical bone (CB) have some influence. Figure 10 reveals a similar 
dependency of RDM upon the gray and white matter conductivities as for TMS. 
 
Standard tDCS 
As expected, standard tDCS results in a far more widespread field distribution than focal 
tDCS (Fig. 4a). Near the frontal electrode, the field peaks in the sulcal fundi, whereas the 
highest values occur on the gyral crowns for the brain regions close to the central electrode. 
RSD and VAR are consistently high in the sulcal fundi near the electrodes (up to 30%), 
with the Sobol indices identifying the conductivity uncertainties of GM (~77%), compact 
bone (~10%), and scalp (~7%) as the main contributors. These findings are confirmed by 
the derivative-based sensitivity coefficients (Fig. 5).  
The standard deviation of the 98th percentile is about 20.5 % of the mean, thus similar to 
focal tDCS and much larger than for TMS. Again, the Sobol indices reflect the findings for 
the location-wise field magnitudes. 
In contrast to TMS and focal tDCS, some regions with high field strength (Fig. 4a) have a 
high variance of the field angle relative to the cortex normal (Fig. 7), corresponding to 
angles higher than 5°. This is mainly driven by conductivity uncertainties of GM, WM and 
compact bone. 
The probability density function of the RDM (Fig. 8) is very similar to that of focal tDCS. 
The shape of the electric field differs slightly between the reference and RDM=0.2, which 
can be explained by the more widespread field distribution in case of standard tDCS 
compared to focal tDCS and TMS. The results presented in Fig. 9 and 10 are very similar 
to focal tDCS, except for a slightly lower influence of the conductivities of compact bone 
and skin. 
 
DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION  
We used a pipeline based on non-intrusive gPC in order to comprehensively characterize 
the impact of uncertain tissue conductivities on the magnitude, direction, and overall field 
pattern of the estimated electric field for three exemplary setups for non-invasive brain 
==PAGE:98==
stimulation. The uncertainty range of field strengths obtained for TDCS (Fig. 6) agrees well 
with the results presented in Opitz et al. and Huang et al., obtained by direct invasive 
measurements of the fields generated by 1 mA TDCS in patients. 
We demonstrated that our approach yields detailed insight into the spatially dependent 
impact of conductivity parameter uncertainties onto uncertainties of the electric field 
caused by TMS and tDCS. We found that for all investigated scenarios the magnitude of 
the electric field is most affected (Fig. 4-6), while the field direction and the global field 
topography are less sensitive to the conductivity uncertainties (Fig. 7-8). In other words, 
accurate knowledge of the individual conductivities is much more important for dosing than 
for targeting [Peterchev et al. (2012)]. As a consequence, interindividual conductivity 
differences could potentially contribute to the widely observed interindividual threshold 
differences in non-invasive brain stimulation (mainly TMS) experiments. We also found 
that TMS induced fields are less affected by conductivity variations than those caused by 
tDCS. TMS is primarily influenced by accuracies of the gray and white matter conductivities, 
whereas tDCS also depends on the conductivities of compact bone and scalp.  
 
In addition, we identified specific differences between the modalities with respect to the 
most severely affected locations on the cortex. For example, the magnitude of the electric 
field caused by TMS can be predicted accurately on the gyral crowns (at the field maximum) 
and less accurate in the sulcal walls, while focal tDCS is less accurate on the gyral crowns 
and in the sulcal fundi. For standard tDCS, the main inaccuracy occurs in the sulcal fundi. 
The present work is focused on conductivity variations and not on anatomical inaccuracies 
of the modeled head volume conductor. It can be clearly assumed that the latter also 
strongly contributes to uncertainties in the field estimates. Specifically, the result 
presented here point towards a low influence of the uncertainty of CSF conductivity on the 
field estimates. On the other hand, Nielsen et al. (2018) (Fig. 5b), have shown clear 
differences in the cortical electric fields for two head models that differ in the level of detail 
used for modeling the CSF-filled sulci. Taking these two findings together, it can be stated 
that varying CSF conductivity within the range reported in the literature alters the field in 
==PAGE:99==
GM only weakly, while replacing part of CSF by GM in the model clearly changes the field 
pattern. 
The employed non-intrusive gPC allows for a “black box” treatment of the system under 
investigation. This ensures maximal generalizability, because a closed analytical 
description of the problem is not needed. Large numbers of parameter combinations can 
be evaluated quickly due to the parsimonious polynomial description of the transfer 
function. In contrast, direct Monte Carlo sampling [Toschi et al. (2012)] or parameter grid 
sampling [Salvador et al. 2012] of the original transfer function would require > 105 FEM 
simulations for this number of input parameters (6 parameters) to reach the desired 
accuracy. Other approaches that seek to reduce this effort, such as worst case evaluation 
[Thielscher et al. 2011] or linearization [Santos et al. (2016)], are much more limited in 
their conclusions. In addition, the present formulation allows for a direct extraction of 
uncertainties of, for example, the electric field and sensitivities towards specific input 
parameters. These properties enable systematic investigations of systems with 
computationally expensive transfer functions (such as the FEM calculation in our case) and 
large parameter sets. An important advantage of gPC is that the expansion can performed 
with respect to quantity of interests other than the electric field itself, allowing for the 
uncertainty analysis also
for higher-level parameters such as the RDM. With the 
information provided by this and similar analyses, the relevance of precise knowledge of 
tissue conductivities for particular stimulation situations can be quantified and research 
aiming at targeted measurements of distinct tissue types can be directed.  
The gPC yields a highly efficient meta-model of the complex transfer function that can also 
be used in a variety of further applications. One further example are optimization studies.
By doing so, conductivity optimizations as they were conducted by Huang et al. (2017) 
could be solved in a very elegant and time-efficient way. 
In conclusion, the presented methodology constitutes a very flexible and efficient tool to 
assess the reliability of the predictions of brain stimulation models and to identify the 
crucial model parameters, depending on the particular stimulation configuration and region 
of interest. Even though the present demonstration is limited to an initial set of three 
==PAGE:100==
scenarios tested on a single head model, our current study already helps to reveal 
important differences between the simulated stimulation methods that likely generalize 
across individuals. Furthermore, the method offers great potential for the characterization 
of other models as well as other applications, such as model inversion, for example in 
EEG/MEG source reconstruction.
