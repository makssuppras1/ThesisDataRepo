ABSTRACT 
The purpose of this master project was to investigate if there are any conflicts be-
tween the different design requirements and the actual indoor environment in 
modern office buildings. 
When dimensioning the heating and cooling system for a building or determining 
the expected energy consumption, it is important to consider factors such as the 
construction, how the building is used and the disposition of the building layout to 
be able to make a good design. From different studies it is documented that there 
can be great challenges with operating the indoor climate in open plan offices, 
because of the great number of occupants in a limited amount of space.  
 
In this study, the indoor environments of four office buildings were investigated 
during the transitional period between winter and summer in Denmark. Long- and 
short-term measurements, Clo-value and activity level registration, interviews and 
questionnaires were carried out from March to the start of May in 2016. 
 
The clothing insulation worn by the occupants in open plan offices were registered 
in five different buildings. The result showed a great range in the clothing insula-
tion throughout the buildings. Mean Clo-values of 0.87, 0.82, 0.77, 0.83 and 0.84, 
with the respective standard deviations of 0.13, 0.12, 0.12, 0.13 and 0.14, was de-
termined for the buildings. The analysis of differences in worn clothing between 
genders did not show a clear distribution. In some of the buildings, the spread of 
Clo-values were biggest for the male occupants and in other buildings the range 
was biggest for the female occupants.  
 
The result of the short-term measurements, which in this study included vertical 
air temperature difference, air velocity and draught rate, fulfilled Category I and II 
for the thermal environment in all the buildings. Maximum velocities up to almost 
six times the mean value were measured in all of the four buildings, which indicate 
unevenness and a suddenly large delivered amount of air to the occupants.  
 
The long-term measurements conducted in this study include temperature, rela-
tive humidity and temperature drift. The result from the Predicted Mean Vote 
(PMV) and Predicted Percentage of Dissatisfied (PPD) calculations showed that two 
buildings fulfilled Category III and two other buildings were in Category IV. Tem-
perature increases showed a drift that exceeded 2°C/h in 0.8% to 1.5% of the oc-
cupied hours during the whole measurement period, which fulfil the maximum 
threshold of 5% determined for this study based on standard DS/EN 15251.  
The relative humidity was below the critical values of 15-20%, in 0-3% of the meas-
ured occupant hours in all the buildings.  
 
==PAGE:10==
 
 X
 
Questionnaires conducted in three buildings were used to evaluate the different 
measurements executed in the buildings with the occupants’ perception of the 
indoor climate. The number of responses in the buildings corresponded to respec-
tively 3%, 26% and 45% of the total number of people in the buildings. The re-
sponses from the occupants, who participated indicated problems with especially 
temperature, air quality and noise in the buildings. Lighting was the category most 
of the responders from all the three buildings were most satisfied with, but prob-
lems with solar radiation were a recurrent problem in the buildings without effec-
tive interior or exterior shading.

Introduction 
 
 
1
1. 
INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Background 
Over the last five to ten years, there has been an increased focus on indoor climate 
and productivity in schools and in workspaces. In Denmark and other places, we 
spend almost 90% of our time indoors; therefore it is very important to have good 
indoor conditions. Several different studies show that buildings with a poor indoor 
environment decrease the occupant’s productivity, learning rate, wellbeing and 
quality of life (Huizenga et al. 2006).  By improving the indoor environment in 
office buildings, productivity can be improved 4 to 10% (Clements-Croome & 
Baizhan 2000).  
 
Frank Lloyd Wright designed one of the first buildings with an open plan office 
space in 1904. The open plan office had a traditional classroom outline, with tables 
in rows, and became widely used in office buildings (Pejtersen et al. 2006). Later in 
the 1960s, the landscape office was introduced in Germany. The landscape office 
was different from the open plan office, as the floor plan was completely open, 
without single person offices for the managers and partitions to separate rooms 
into sections. 
 
The whole idea and the reason why open plan offices became so popular 
compared to cellular offices, was because of the lower cost due to less required 
square meters per person, rather than the facilitation of more transparency and 
internal communications.  
 
In the 1970s, reports about occupants’ problems and complaints stated to emerge. 
It was problems like noise and lack of privacy, which was most frequent (Pejtersen 
et al. 2006). 
For the last ten years, the tendency in Denmark has been to replace cellular offices 
with a combination of both open plan offices and cellular offices: 
 
 
==PAGE:18==
 
 2 
 
“The idea has been that the physical design should support the modern office 
work with a high degree of knowledge sharing among the employees working 
in project groups with a constant demand for flexible physical facilities.”- 
(Pejtersen et al. 2006)  
 
To be able to design a better thermal indoor environment, for more people in 
office buildings, it is important that the different factors used to determine PMV 
and PPD values, among others, is as accurate and close to the actual situations as 
possible. 
 
Standard values given for clothing insulation, and the actual clothing insulation 
value worn in office buildings were investigated to find the thermal comfort level. 
By registering worn clothing in five different open plan offices, executing ques-
tionnaires in three of these offices and measurements of the operative tempera-
ture and air velocity in four of these offices, it has been determined if there were 
any contradictions. 
Contradictions between the standard values used to determine indoor climate in 
office buildings (15251 2007)(Ansi/Ashrae 2004)(Dansk Standard 474 1995) and 
values from real case scenarios, were analysed and evaluated. A gap between the 
actual values and standard values were found for clothing insulation values, 
which means that the amount of cooling or heated needed to operate the 
building in a certain indoor climate class, is different and that the estimation of 
the energy consumption, when designing the building, is far from the actual 
conditions. 
1.2 Literature review 
1.2.1 Open plan offices 
Within the last ten years, more and more cellular offices have been replaced with 
open plan offices. The idea has mainly been to support the modern office envi-
ronment with a high degree of knowledge sharing and a demand for flexible 
facilities. A study from Denmark (Pejtersen et al. 2006), has investigated the two 
basic approaches in the behavioural literature that describe employees reaction 
to work in open plan offices: 
 
• 
A social relation approach 
• 
A sociotechnical approach 
 
 
 
==PAGE:19==
Introduction 
 
 
3
The social relation approach describes how the absence of walls in the open plan 
office will facilitate social relationships, decrease interpersonal problems, increase 
supervision and feedback as well as interactions. All in all these changes will 
increase the employee’s motivation and job satisfaction. 
The sociotechnical approach states that the absence of walls and/or partitions will 
reduce the occupant’s experience of autonomy and decrease privacy as the 
physical boundaries increase. The hypothesis in the sociotechnical approach states 
that open plan offices will decrease the occupant’s motivation, job satisfaction and 
performance.  
 
The most prevalent complaint in open plan offices is noise, which also had the 
strongest association with the size of the office according to a 2006 study 
(Pejtersen et al. 2006). In the study 60% of the occupants complained about noise 
in open plan offices compared to 6% in cellular offices. Poor air quality 
characterized by stuffy air and odor was also more mentioned in larger offices than 
in cellular offices. As concluded in the study, open plan offices may not be suitable 
for all job types, because occupants these places are more likely to perceive 
thermal discomfort, poor indoor air quality and noise than in cellular offices. 
 
A systematic review of 49 articles relating to the effect of office concepts on 
workers’ health and performance was executed by researchers in the Netherlands 
(De Croon et al. 2005). It is stated in this article that conventional and innovative 
office concepts can be described according to three dimensions: 
 
• 
Location (telework office vs. conventional office) 
• 
Layout (open plan office vs. cellular office) 
• 
Use (fixed vs. shared workplaces) 
 
The review examines how the three dimensions affect the occupant’s short- and 
long-term reactions. It is concluded that there is strong evidence that working in 
open plan offices reduces privacy and job satisfaction.  
These two studies are based on research from USA in 1979 (Oldham & Brass 1979), 
where 81 occupants participated. It was concluded that the employee’s 
satisfaction and motivation decreased significantly after the move from cellular 
offices to open plan offices.  
 
The Australian study (Kim & de Dear 2013) is based on empirical analysis of an 
occupant survey database from Centre for the built environment at University of 
California, Berkeley. It was concluded from the study that the amount of individual 
space available was the most important predictor of overall workspace satisfaction 
across all office layouts studied (enclosed private office, enclosed shared office, 
cubicles with high partitions, cubicles with low partitions, open plan office with no 
partitions).  
 
==PAGE:20==
 
 4 
Visual privacy and noise level was the most important for the people working in 
open plan offices, whereas the amount of light, ease of interaction and comfort 
of furniture where more important for people in private offices. 
The results of the study contradict the social relation approach, which says that 
employees working in open plan offices will have an increased motivation and job 
satisfaction by absence of walls. This study showed that occupants’ satisfaction 
on the interaction issue was actually higher for persons working in private offices, 
with a dissatisfaction rate of less than 5 %.  
The negative impacts of noise and privacy overruled the workspace satisfaction 
due to the positive impact of ease of interaction in open plan offices: 
 
“Even though occupants are satisfied with interactions in open plan layout, 
their overall workspace satisfaction will eventually decreased unless a certain 
level of privacy and acoustical quality are provided”- (Kim & de Dear 2013) 
1.2.2 Air turbulence and sensation of draught 
After noise, draught is one of the most common causes of complaints in build-
ings, when looking at multi-person and open plan offices. A multi-person office 
comprises of three to six occupants and open plan offices from six occupants and 
up (Pejtersen et al. 2006). The reason for draught problems among occupants is 
because of unwanted local cooling of the human body caused by high air move-
ments. For mechanical ventilated buildings the draught is most likely caused by 
the ventilation and for natural ventilated buildings, the air intake in the facade.  
A study from 1988 (Fanger et al. 1988) investigated the impact of turbulence 
intensity, Tu, in a climate chamber with 50 occupants, by performing whole body 
exposures. 25 female- and 25 male occupants, dressed to obtain a neutral 
thermal sensation, were in three experiments exposed to three different airflows 
with different Tu values.  
 
The experiment used the same protocol as in the study Perception of draught in 
ventilated spaces (Fanger & Christensen 1986), but instead of varying the air 
temperature, the turbulence intensity was varied. A predictive mathematical 
draught rate (DR) model that applies for sedentary people in air temperatures 
from 20-26°C was developed. Most people, in the experiment, complained about 
draught in the head region. Next to the head region, the arms, hands and ankles 
were regions with most complaints. With air coming from above (ventilation in 
ceiling), the velocities and turbulence intensity (Tu) was also higher in the head 
region, which can explain the outcome of complaints in the study.

Introduction 
 
 
5
Later In 1994 this model was modified by Toftum (Toftum, j., 1994) to predict the 
effects of draught on people carrying out physical work in moderately cold 
climates. Both the original- and modified model are based on experiments where 
the air velocity was increased stepwise every 15 min.  
While Fanger, Christensen and Toftum assumed that the draught induced 
annoyance reached steady state after 15 minutes, Griefahn et al. (Griefahn et al. 
1997) found that the annoyance increased with time until reaching a steady state 
after 40 to 50 min. This led to the conclusion that the DR-model might 
underestimates the effects of draught. 
By using a jack-knife technique, which is a resampling technique mainly used for 
estimation of variance, Griefahn et al. validated Fanger, Christensen and Toftum’s 
DR-models. The result showed that their model to predict draught was sufficiently 
precise. 
 
Another Danish study by Toftum & Nielsen (1996) showed that more persons 
feeling cool or cold, felt local discomfort due to draught than persons feeling 
neutral or warm, when exposing the occupants for the same air velocity. When the 
occupants are feeling warm, their tolerance for higher velocities are greater.  
The results indicated that difference in draught sensitivity between persons having 
different thermal sensations, not only depends on the extent of the difference in 
thermal sensation but also the level of thermal sensation.     
 
In all the articles regarding draught, it was concluded that an airflow with a high 
turbulence causes explains more than an airflow with low turbulence at the same 
mean velocity. For a given percentage of people feeling draught, a significantly 
higher mean velocity can be tolerated when the flow of the air has a low 
turbulence intensity. In addition to this, Fanger and Pedersen (Fanger and 
Pedersen 1977) found in their study, that humans seem to dislike velocity 
fluctuations and this fluctuation of air velocity is felt more uncomfortable than air 
at a constant velocity (Fanger and Pedersen 1977).  
 
Madsen (Madsen 1984) suggested that high turbulence felt more uncomfortable 
than low turbulence, because the heat flux sensed by the thermal receptors in the 
skin could cause the difference, since it was shown that the heat flux increased 
with growing turbulence.  
Similarly, Fanger and Pedersen (Fanger and Pedersen 1977) believed that the mean 
temperature would not change significantly during the fluctuations, but the rate of 
change of the skin temperature with time would be greater at higher turbulence.  
 
All the presented literature regarding draught is based on experiments executed in 
climate chambers. It was not possible to find literature on draught investigations 
directly related to actual workspaces and open plan offices.

1.2.3 Clothing values 
In mechanical heated and cooled buildings it is recommended that the criteria for 
thermal environment is based on the thermal comfort indices PMV & PPD.  
Instead of only using the temperature as the design criteria, the effect of the air 
velocity will also be taken into account according to DS/EN 15251 (15251 2007).  
 
In determining the acceptable range of operative temperatures in the building, a 
clothing value corresponding to the local clothing and climate habits should be 
used (ISO 2005). Even though this is specified, clothing insulation values of 0.5 clo 
in the summer and 1.0 clo in the winter are widely used as reference input values 
to the PPD equation. With these values the operative temperature can be set to 
20-24°C in the winter and 23-26°C in the summer, with the expectation that less 
than 10% of the occupants would be dissatisfied (Dansk Standard 474 1995).  
 
The Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) model of thermal comfort, created by Fanger 
(1970) is still the most widely used to determine the thermal comfort for occu-
pants. The model has been compared to other newer models, existing support 
and criticism as well as modifications has been discussed. 
It was concluded from the study that very low and high PMV values do not 
necessarily reflect discomfort for substantial numbers of people. Although many 
modifications to the original PMV model have been proposed, and tested, none 
of these have yet found widespread in practice (Van Hoof 2008).  
 
A study from Australia, conducted from August 1996 to January 1997 (Morgan & 
de Dear 2003) have examined clothing behaviour and its relationship with 
thermal environment in two different indoor settings, a shopping mall and a call-
centre office. In the call-centre office, the company had a strict business dress 
code Monday through Thursday, but on Fridays the employees were allowed to 
wear casual clothing.  
In the shopping mall, the clothing insulation worn inside, showed significant day-
to-day variations.  
The result of the study showed that clothing insulation worn inside the shopping 
mall during the summer, were more than 0.6 clo lighter than those worn in 
winter.  
Clothing insulation level worn in the call centre (strict business clothing) was 
relatively static regardless of the weather and season around 1.0 clo, which 
correspond to 0.155m2K/W.

1.3 Project description 
1.3.1 Objectives 
The hypothesis in this thesis is that a fixed clothing insulation value (Clo-value), 
given in the standards, is not consistent with the actual conditions in modern office 
buildings because of great differences in worn clothing among the occupants.  
When the thermal comfort level is determined by calculations of the PMV and PPD 
values, the Clo-value and activity level is two very important factors. Especially the 
Clo-value is very simplified to only two values as it is today, 0.5 clo in the summer 
and 1.0 clo in the winter, not depending on the context if it is a residential building 
or an office building. As a consequence of this the temperature interval operated in 
office buildings, to accommodate a certain thermal indoor climate category, may 
be different. 
 
The activity level of the occupant is not investigated in this project, so a value from 
the standards is used. 
 
This study only deals with mechanical heated and cooled buildings and not natural 
ventilated buildings, because of the great difference between these two ventilation 
methods (De Carli et al. 2007). The aim in this project is to: 
 
• 
Map the current conditions in modern office buildings in terms of thermal 
comfort, indoor air quality and user behavior related to clothing and activity 
level. It is investigated if there are any difference between the clothing worn 
by the female- and male occupants. 
 
• 
Document contradictions between design requirements, real indoor climate 
measurements, questionnaires and interviews. Analyze relationship between 
exceeded design requirements and frequency of complaints. 
 
• 
Suggest what the facility management department should work with, to 
minimize the complaint rate in an existing building, which is designed for 
different user behavior. 
 
• 
Suggest the adjustment that needs to be made in the building program, 
building regulation and in the standards to minimize the contradictions 
between design requirements and the actual real case scenario.

2.1 Statistical methods 
It has been of certain interest, as it is in many scientific studies, to check whether 
the collected data from the different buildings are comparable or not. In order to 
investigate variations, similarities, significant differences etc., different statistical 
tests were executed. 
The statistical methods used in this study were chosen based on statistical 
literature (Crawley 2005) and (Moore et al. 2009). Most often there will be 
differences between practical and theoretical measurement distributions, which 
means that conducted field measurements rarely are distributed as it would be in 
theory. 
A two-tailed test (a way to compute the statistical significance of a parameter 
inferred from a data set) was used in all the tests, because it was considered 
theoretically possible that the deviations of the parameters were in either 
direction from the benchmark.

When using a two-tailed test, the critical probability value, p-value= 0.05 is used.  
With a p-value of 0.05, the confidence interval is 95% sure, if the null hypothesis

In the following sections, the statistical tests carried out in this project are 
described.  
2.1.1 Royston Test (Shapiro-Wilk Test) 
The Shapiro-Wilk Test can be used to check if a sample size is normally distributed. 
Because this method is limited to sample sizes between 3-50 elements, an 
extended method that is built on the Shapiro-Wilk method is used; The Royston 
test. This test can handle more than 5000 samples. 
The Shapiro-Wilk Test utilizes the null hypothesis principle to check whether a 
sample came from the same population or not. If the null hypothesis are rejected 
there is statistically ground to believe that there is a relationship between the two 
compared samples. A more visual way to check for normality is by generating a 
probability plot (Q-Q plot), where the quantiles from the measured/recorded 
values are plotted against the quantiles of the expected theoretical values. An 
example can be seen on Figure 7. The less that the measured values deviates from 
the line; the more normally distributed is the sample.  
 
 
2.1.2 Wilcoxon Rank Sum Test 
The Wilcoxon Rank Sum Test is a nonparametric statistical test, which makes no 
assumptions about the probability distributions of the two tests being assessed 
and normally distributed. The values from the different data sets were sorted and 
ranked from the lowest to the highest number, and finally the dataset with the 
lowest rank-sum is compared with values from a “table of Wilcoxon Rank Sums”. 
A non-parametric test is much more appropriate than a t-test, which is used to 
determine if two populations’ means are equal.

The t-test should only be used when the two compared samples are normally 
distributed and when the errors are not normal and if the distribution is skewed. 
The Wilcoxon Rank Sum Test is more conservative than a t-test, which makes it 
less sensitive.  
2.1.3 Chi-Square Test 
The Chi-Squared Test is used to evaluate how likely it is that an observed 
deviation is actually different or a statistical uncertainty. This test is only used in 
relation to the questionnaire and not the rest of the measurements. The Chi-
Squared Test also test the null hypothesis stating that a frequently distribution of 
a chosen event, observed in a sample, is consistent with a theoretical expected 
distribution. A critical p-value of 0.05 is used in this study as the threshold.

Long term evaluation method 
The long-term measurements were evaluated according to Annex H in DS/EN 7730, 
which list five methods to evaluate the thermal conditions over a period of time 
(season, year). Method A was chosen for this project, which evaluates the 
percentage of hours, during the occupied hours, where the PMV or temperature is 
outside a specific range which is given in Table 9. To fulfill the certain categories 
given in this table, maximum 5% of the results in the occupied hours can be outside 
this range. 
 
When analyzing the temperature it should be checked, according to Figure V2.3A 
in DS 474 (Dansk Standard 474 1995), that the operative temperature during usage 
hours is between 20-24°C for a “winter situation”. If the temperature is outside this 
range, it can be assumed that the PD>15% for sedentary occupants, which can be 
categorized as not satisfying.  
 
PMV & PPD 
One of the ways to determine the thermal comfort for occupants is by calculating 
the predicted mean vote (PMV) predicted people dissatisfied (PPD), which predict 
if people are likely to feel too warm or too cold in a given thermal environment. 
The thermal balance for a person is influenced by physical clothing and activity, as 
well as the environment around them. As described earlier, a BASIC function for 
calculating single scenarios for the PMV & PPD values in excel was developed 
based on chapter 4 in DS/EN 7730. The created BASIC script has been cross 
validated with ASHRAE Standard 55, Appendix B (Ansi/Ashrae 2004), by using the 
online PMV & PPD calculator developed by Berkeley University ([1] Tyler, H. et al. 
2013).The validation and script for the BASIC function can be found in Appendix F- 
PMV & PPD .

The rate of change in temperature 
According to Chapter 2.4 in DS 474 the rate of change in temperature should not 
exceed 2°C/h. Only very short and rare exceedances should be allowed, a 
threshold of 5% is used in this study. Every result in the occupant hours between 
8 AM and 6 PM has been checked on an hourly basis.  
 
Relative humidity 
At temperatures below 26°C and an activity level less than 2, the influence of the 
humidity is rather limited. Within these boundaries, the humidity has only a 
limited impact on the thermal sensation felt by occupants. According to Annex F 
in DS/EN 7730, a 10% higher humidity will be felt like a temperature increase of 
approximately 0.3°C. 
However, high long-term humidity levels indoors will cause microbial growth and 
very low humidity levels (<15-20% RH) cause irritation of eyes and airways. The 
measured RH-values was analyzed to see if the level was below 15-20% and 
compared to the questionnaire to see if there was consistency in the results.  
Humidification and dehumidification is usually only needed in special buildings 
like some health care facilities, museums etc. (15251 2007), however these 
thresholds in Table. B.6 in DS 15251 are not used to evaluate the measured RH-
values in this project.

DISCUSSION  
In this study, the differences between design requirements and the actual indoor 
environments of modern office buildings have been investigated. This was done in 
order to evaluate the standard values with values measured in existing buildings. 
Information about contradictions between standard values and actual values can 
have enormous benefits to FM regarding improving the efficiency of heating and 
cooling and enhancing comfort of personnel. Air velocity-, temperature- and 
relative humidity measurements as well as questionnaires, registration of clothing 
and interviews have been executed in this study. 
 
4.1 Clothing insulation 
Open plan offices 
When determining the Clo-value for the occupants in Buildings A to E, standard 
Clo-values from ISO 9920 (ISO 2009) regarding garments were used. An additional 
Clo-value from chairs was added, depending on the type of chair. Without this 
chair, the clothing insulation value would have been 0.17 to 0.22 Clo lower. 
Uncertainty between the actual value of the garments insulation in the standards 
and the garments insulation worn by the occupants is assumed to be insignificant.

In Building B there is no official dress code for the employees, but managers wear

occupants.

there is a certain codex of what to wear and what not to wear.  
The supply temperature in Building C is constantly kept at 18°C in the whole 
building. If a higher temperature is desired in the room, the occupants are able to 
turn up the convectors which are located along the facades. The dead-band for the 
convectors are set to ±3°C in the BMS system, to minimize too much overheating. 
Building C is generally kept at 24±1°C to accommodate the employees different 
clothing and temperature preferences and to keep the number of dissatisfied as 
low as possible. 
 
In Building E there is no official dress code for the employees, but managers must 
wear pants and a formal shirt at a minimum. The heating in Building E is occupant 
controlled by turning up the thermostats, which means that the set point for the 
heating can be hard to determine.  
The cooling is controlled by a sensor in the exhaust diffuser, to maintain 22°C all 
year around, with a dead-band of 0.5°C compared to the supply air.

4.5.1 Suggestions to the FM department to reduce complaints 
From measurements, questionnaires and interviews suggestions for improving 
the existing conditions in open plan offices are as follows:  
 
• 
Thermal zones and temperature: To accommodate the great differ-
ences in clothing insulation worn by the occupants, a temperature inter-
val between 22-25°C was found in this study to be efficient to fulfill 
thermal indoor climate Category II with maximum 10% dissatisfied. For a 
better thermal category, the temperature interval should be narrower. 
 
• 
The interior layout: If there are departments in the office building with a 
particularly high internal heat gain caused by instruments, computers 
etc., they should, if possible, be located in the northern part of the 
building where there is no incoming heat gain from the sun. A sugges-
tion could also be to move around the occupants to specific colder or 
warmer places in the building, if they generally complain about too low 
or too high temperatures. 
 
• 
Split the office into smaller sections: From literature presented in the 
literature review and the questionnaires executed in three of the build-
ings, noise is a great problem in most of the open plan offices. By split-
ting up the open plan office into smaller sections with fewer occupants 
by partition walls or movable acoustic room dividers, noise and thermal 
comfort can be improved. 
 
• 
Altered working hours: Flexible working hours can make a difference 
especially for people working in the east and west part of the building. 
In Building B, they experience that many of the occupants working in the 
west oriented offices (where the external heat gain from the sun is 
strong in the afternoon) are meeting and leaving earlier in the after-
noon, compared to the other orientations. 
4.6 Comments on indoor climate standards 
Results from this study show that it is too simple to work with only two fixed 
Clo-values (0.5 Clo in the summer and 1.0 Clo in the winter) for open plan offic-
es, despite that it should be done according to this standard. In the transition 
period, investigated in this study, between the winter and summer season, 
mean values from 0.77 Clo to 0.87 Clo were found in all buildings. All the Clo-
values presented in this study include the insulation provided by offices chairs. 
Were these left out (as it is in most of the cases) the value would approximately 
be 0.20 Clo lower, which means that the Clo-value would be almost as low as 
0.6 for the most buildings in this study. 
To accommodate the differences in worn clothing in these workspaces, a small-
er temperature interval of 22 to 24/25°C was found for the buildings to fulfil 
Category II, see Table 33.

This temperature interval corresponds well with the feedback from the facility 
management interviewed in this study.  
From the interview in Building C, it was clear that their biggest problem regarding 
comfort temperatures during the summer, where the male occupants were 
wearing suits and the female occupants dresses and shirts with short sleeves.  
According to DS/EN 7730, the temperature interval to fulfill Category II for a winter 
situation is 22±2°C. From interviews and the questionnaires conducted in this 
study, occupants are frequently complained about very cold hands, even with a 
temperature of 22°C, which indicates that the facility management department in 
the companies may have difficulties with lowering the temperature to 20-21°C 
before getting too many complaints. Due to this, a minimum temperature below 
22°C cannot practically be allowed for most of the office buildings described in this 
study, where the occupants are sedentary.  
 
The PMV and PPD calculation showed based on the 25-75% quantile Clo-values in 
Table 27 show that the temperature should be maximum 24°C to fulfil Category II.  
This temperature corresponds very well with the standards. If a fixed Clo-value 
used, Table 32, can maximum temperatures of 25°C can be permitted to fulfil 
Category II for the investigated building in this study. Based on the questionnaire 
for Building D, 10.4% of the total number of people in the building complained 
about draught coming from the ventilation, which correspond to Category II.  
According to the standards, the threshold for categorizing draught rate to be in 
Category II is 20%, which is higher than for PMV & PPD values as well as the value 
for vertical air temperature differences (5%), warm or cold floors (10%) and radiant 
asymmetry (5%). It can be discussed if the FM departments, controlling the 
buildings, would allow 20% of their occupants to be dissatisfied, in which case 
there could be contradictions between a theoretic case and the actual case. 
 
If further studies were carried out analyzing the Clo-values in the summer, autumn 
and winter and the results of these studies showed that more heating is needed in 
the winter months and more cooling in the summer, it would be even more 
difficult to fulfill the building regulation regarding the energy consumption, than it 
is today.  
By using the temperature interval defined in the standards, which is based on the 
standard Clo-values, the results from this study shows that a calculated energy 
consumption of an open plan office, would be a lot lower than the actual 
consumption. From the measured temperatures in the long tem measurements, it 
can be seen that a lot of cooling is also needed in the spring season, to keep the 
temperatures down in the offices.

draught rate. Tests with changing the airflow direction should be executed on the

Buildings B to E when using the standard deviation values. From this study it can be 
concluded that a more narrow range of temperatures is needed to satisfy the 
majority of occupants in office buildings and to have more accurate data to 
estimate the actual energy consumption for office buildings.

6. 
PERSPECTIVE 
Due to the time limit of this project it was not possible to realize all ideas. If 
further studies were to be conducted, the following points below could be 
interesting to investigate further: 
 
• 
Clo-values conducted for a whole year: The clothing insulation values 
conducted in this study, gives a strong indication what the value is in the 
transition period between the winter and summer season. Conducted 
values for a year would give a full picture of the actual Clo-values in the 
four seasons. 
 
• 
Activity level: It was not possible to investigate the actual activity level for 
the occupants working in the measured buildings, instead standard values 
were used, from Annex B in DS 7730 (ISO 2005). In practice, the value for 
activity level (met) would be very expensive and time consuming, because 
each individual person should be tested according to DS/EN 8996 (Danish 
Standards Assosciation 2004). The mentioned standard is more that ten 
years old, so it would be interesting to investigate if the mean body 
weight has changed significantly since. 
 
• 
Velocities and draught rate: The air velocity was measured in four 
buildings in this study, but because the number of answers in the ques-
tionnaire was low compared to the total number of people working in the 
buildings, it was difficult to estimate and compare the PD-value from the 
actual conditions with the standards. It would be interesting also to 
measure the air velocity in the summer, where the velocity usually is 
higher, to see if the maximum velocities given in the standards are being 
exceeded.  
Because the occupants working in offices buildings are sedentary almost 
90-95% of their time, the questionnaire executed in this study, indicated 
that the threshold for draught rate might be different in actual situations 
than in theory. This is due to cold hands, feet and shoulders.  
 
 
==PAGE:116==
 
 
100 
 
• 
Temperature: By measuring the temperature during a whole year, further PMV 
and PPD investigations could be done to define the actual comfort temperature 
levels for the occupants. Real Clo-values conducted from the buildings would be 
necessary.  
With input values from actual conditions in the open plan offices, it would be 
easier to predict the actual energy consumption and to dimensioning the heat-
ing and cooling units for the buildings, which are capable to deliver the amount 
needed for the building. 
 
• 
Relative humidity: Even though low RH-values were measured during the occu-
pied hours, there were still a great number of people complaining about too low 
values. Further investigations could be executed to find out if the threshold for 
the critical value of 20% RH according to DS/EN 7730, should be even higher. 
 
• 
Investigate additional buildings: This study is only evaluating 
five buildings. With more time, additional buildings could have 
been found and investigated.
