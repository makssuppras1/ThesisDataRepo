Abstract 
This Thesis studies the implementation of a bidirectional soft switching DC/DC converter, 
operating at high frequency. There were looked into the disadvantages of using magnetic 
materials for inductor. As well as brief overview of the problems occurring when implementing 
a control circuit for high frequency converters. 
 
The main issue when using core materials for the configuration chosen in this thesis, is the high 
ripple current. This ripple has to have a rather high magnitude in order for the converter to 
achieve zero voltage switching. Which will results in high magnetic losses for solid cores. 
Calculation were made to estimate these losses, which shows that magnetic materials cannot be 
used optimally for this converter configuration, when operating with soft switching. 
The materials will need too many windings, than practically possible. Increasing the total 
winding losses due to the lower amount of parallel conductors or a need for a very large air gap, 
effectively removing the core. In order to maintain low core losses. 
 
The converter has been implemented with good results. Achieving operation up to 10 MHz, 
with efficiencies of up 96.6 % and achieving a peak output power of 93 W.  
Using an air core inductor, the maximum temperature rise at full power was approximately 10 
degrees Celsius. 
An additional test for the inductance was made to determine the noise emission from the 
inductor. These values were measured to be low, removing a disadvantage from using an air 
core.

Introduction 
Renewable energy is one of the foremost desired types of energy. Taking over where previously 
fossil fuels have been the major contributor.  
Power from renewable energies are uncontrollable, can be hard to predict and does not always 
have a high yield when power is demanded. 
It is therefore desirable to store the energy, when there is high production and low demand in 
order to use it when the production drops and demand rises. 
One way of storing the energy from renewable sources could be with the use of battery packs. 
In order to ensure high capacity and efficiency of these packs, it is preferable to have a low 
amount of power electronics, in order to be able to utilize most of the pack for batteries.  
To have the least amount of electronics a bidirectional converter could be implemented. This 
converter is able to perform both the charging and discharging of the batteries, when there is a 
surplus or demand of power.  
To further reduce the converter size and increase power density, the converter is to be operated 
at higher frequencies than the standard frequencies of 100 – 500 kHz. Operating from 1 MHz 
and above, will reduce component sizes for components such as inductors, capacitors and in 
some cases the semiconductors.  
There were looked into different small toroid cores suitable for high frequency operation, in 
order to reduce the inductors physical size. These cores could effectively reduce the amount of 
windings necessary for maintaining the minimum inductance.  
 
Semiconductors such as Gallium-nitride MOSFETs, are small devices made for operating at 
very high frequencies and ensuring faster switching. Which results in lower switching losses 
and an increased efficiency for the converter. There will be looked into implementing the 
LMG5200 from Texas Instruments (TI) in this topology. 
Combining the Gallium-Nitride devices, with high frequencies and smaller components, will 
ensure high power density for the converter.   
 
There could be implemented a dynamic and fast control which should offer soft switching for 
a wide load range, to further increase efficiency of the converter.

1 Converter configuration 
This chapter will explain the basic topology along with different ways of configuring it, looking 
into advantages and disadvantages for each configurations. The converter should be able to 
operate in three different modes, these modes will be documented through simulation and 
experimental results. 
There have been chosen two different configurations for analysis. There will be looked at their 
capability of performing all three modes at high operating frequencies. 
 
A disadvantages of operating at higher frequencies is that the converter becomes more 
susceptible to parasitic components, such as capacitance and leakage inductance. One of the 
methods utilize these parasitic in different ways, in order to reduce losses during operation. 
  
1.1 Standard configuration 
The topology investigated in this report is a non-isolated bidirectional DC-DC converter [2]. 
This means that the energy can flow in both directions, between V1 and V2, depending on the 
switching scheme for the switches S1 - S4. 
The configurations should furthermore be able to operate in all three different modes. These 
operational modes indicate the output voltage compared to the input.  
 
Buck mode: The output voltage will be equal or lower than the input. Operating as a 
synchronous buck converter [3]. 
Figure 1: Standard layout of the bidirectional DC-DC converter
==PAGE:17==
 
 
Page 5 of 139 
 
Boost mode: The output voltage will be equal or higher than the input. Operating as a 
synchronous Boost converter. 
Buck-Boost mode: The output voltage will be equal or almost equal to the input. Operating as 
a noninverting buck-boost converter. 
The buck-boost mode will only be in the transition between the two other modes. 
 
As the converter is bidirectional each mode can be applied in both directions. And due to the 
symmetry of the converter, the voltage and current waveforms of each mode will be equally 
independent of the direction, except for the current bias. Making only one waveform plot 
necessary for each mode. The operations in this chapter will be from V1 to V2 (left to right). 
 
The bidirectionality will be somewhat self-regulating. As an increase in potential will make the 
current travel in the opposite direction, in order to balance the circuit (increasing the reactive 
power in the circuit). This will not give the converter the soft switching capability, as the bias 
may be too high resulting in a low reverse current. 
   
The operating frequency should be variable, in order to maintain a sufficient ripple current in 
the inductor. The optimal frequency is combination of a minimum required on time to charge 
the inductor, the minimum dead time required to charge and discharge the output capacitance 
and reverse conduction time. 
 
The topology can be configured in three different ways. Conventional hard switching, resonant 
and forced zero voltage switching (ZVS). These configurations will be analyzed, to determine 
the one to be implemented.

1.2 Conventional hard switching 
The simplest configuration is the hard switched PWM converter. This does not utilize parasitic 
components as a functional part of the circuit. The control method is simple, using pulse width 
modulation, also called PWM. The PWM uses a fixed frequency and adjusts the duty cycle to 
control the power transfer of the converter. Making the converter reliable for a wide load range. 
PWM control can easily be implemented by using a microcontroller or analog circuit. 
 
1.2.1 Simplified schematic 
The configuration uses two half-bridges to a total of four switches, S1 - S4. These are controlled 
differently depending on the mode of operation. The current is sent through the inductor, either 
charging or discharging the capacitors C1 and C2. 
The simplified circuit of the hard-switched converter is shown in figure 2.

The Buck operation runs with the switch S3 always on and S4 off while S1 and S2 are 
alternating to transfer power. This means that the converter schematic can be further simplified.  
 
Buck operations is also called step-down, as this mode decreases the input voltage. Voltage 
transfer ratio [4] is determined by (1). 
V୭୳୲= D ∙V୧୬ 
(1) 
Where Vin is the input voltage, D is the duty cycle ratio for the converter (on time compared to 
the period) and Vout is the resulting voltage on the output. 
The transfer function is used for an ideal converter, meaning an efficiency of 100%, not taking 
into account any losses in the inductor.  
 
Looking at the switching waveforms of the buck operation, figure 7. It is seen that soft switching 
is possible for the low side switch, in this case S2, whereas the high side switch is hard 
switching.  
The soft switching of switch S2 is from an additional effect created by the dead time. The output 
capacitance of S2 will be discharged into the inductor during this period, where the current 
commutates from S2 to S1. S2 will have turn on losses, due to the output capacitance of the 
switch being charged when turning on.

1.3 Forced zero voltage switching  
This configuration operates much like a resonant converter, utilizing the parasitic components 
in the semiconductor. The resonant converter uses an external resonant tank to obtain zero 
voltage switching (ZVS), were this configuration will utilize a high ripple current to maintain 
ZVS.  
 
Using the energy stored in the inductor, the capacitance of the switch can be charged and 
discharged accordingly. ZVS can then be achieved for the switches, effectively increasing the 
efficiency at the cost of a more complex control. 
This control needs to be both duty cycle and frequency modulated, in order to properly control 
the power transfer and the currents in the converter. Dead time control can also be added, in 
order to ensure ZVS for the variable frequency. As a too long dead time will increase reverse 
conduction losses and a too short will remove soft switching. 
 
In order to minimize the dead time required for ZVS, the inductor needs to be properly 
dimensioned. To determine the inductance, the energy requirements for achieving soft 
switching for the device will be investigated. This will be looked at in chapter 5 calculations.

1.3.2.1 Buck 
Looking at the waveform for the buck operation, it shows that both switches are able to achieve 
soft switching in this configuration. At the cost of a very high peak to peak ripple on the current. 
This will result in low losses in the switches, but a high AC loss in the inductor. The inductor 
losses should therefore be optimized for a high current ripple, in order to maintain high 
efficiency of the converter. The switches will have an increased turn off loss, due to the higher 
current peak. These two losses should be compared to determine the benefit of ZVS. 
. 
The waveform illustrates the complementary gate signals, separated by the dead time. During 
the dead time the inductor is able to charge or discharge the output capacitance, depending on 
the switch, before turning on the semiconductor to ensuring full ZVS. Once the switches have 
turned on, the inductor current will begin to change slope. 
 
The small increase in the drain-source voltage is due to reverse conduction in the switches. This 
increase in voltage will be equal to the voltage drop of the internal body diode of the device. 
This time can be minimized or removed by optimizing the dead time set for the converter, as 
power will be dissipated during this conduction time.

1.3.3 Advantages and disadvantages 
The configurations implementation is fairly easy to implement, like the hard switched 
configuration. It is possible to achieve ZVS without the use of additional components. This will 
reduce the switching losses, but due to the high ripple current the conduction losses in the 
semiconductor and the inductor windings will increase. 
The high ripple current and low inductance may result in an inductor without magnetic material. 
Resulting in a high number of windings, higher conduction losses and noise emission, due to 
the uncontrolled magnetic fields leaking from the inductor. 
The benefit from the ZVS should therefore be compared to the increased conduction, in order 
to determine the benefits of the configuration.  
 
In order to proper control the peak currents there will have to be implemented a multiple 
feedback control loop. Controlling this current by frequency control, the power transfer via 
PWM and a dead time control in order to minimize or remove reverse conduction losses. In

order to achieve even higher efficiencies. Which makes up a rather complex control with high 
requirements for timing.

times will become comparable to the propagation delay and even the period of the conversion.

The complexity of the control loop required makes it difficult to implement with discreet 
components, and the timing requirements will make it hard to implement it in a digital control. 
The control may be able to be implemented in an integrated circuit to fulfill the requirements.

2.4 Losses 
There are different losses that needs to be determined for this project. One is the semiconductor 
losses for both the hard and soft switched converter. The difference between these will 
determine the benefit of using a converter with a complex control loop to achieve soft switching.  
Second is the inductor losses. The inductor losses will be calculated using multiple methods. 
There will be looked at the hysteresis and eddy current losses for the optimized magnetization 
variation. Afterwards there will be looked at determining the losses using the Steinmetz 
equation and optimized versions of these. These values will be compared in order to make a 
proper conclusion on the methods. 
There is a lot of methods that can be used for the inductor calculation. The equation used in this 
chapter will primarily focus on Ampere’s equations (circuital law). 
 
2.4.1 Semiconductor losses  
There are a lot of different methods to calculate semiconductor losses, each with different 
complexity and precision. These notes are written by different manufactures. The one used in 
this report is from Fairchild [7]. 
 
Power dissipation dissipated in the device is a combination of the voltage, current, frequency, 
dead time (commutation time) and parasitic components. 
The losses can be divided into multiple losses. The switching losses for a semiconductor when 
turning on and turning off. Conduction losses and reverse conduction losses. The total power 
dissipated in the semiconductor becomes the sum of these.  
There will be looked at the switching behavior for the device turning on and off, in order to 
properly understand the calculation methods used to determine the power dissipation.

2.4.2.1 Hysteresis 
When an external magnetic field affects a ferrimagnetic or mu metal core. The atomic dipoles 
in the material align, with the direction of the field. When the field is removed, part of the 
alignment remain, leaving the material magnetized. The amount of magnetization is given by 
the retentivity, also called the remanence, measured in Telsa (T). The material must then be 
forced by a reverse field in order for dipoles to realign, this is given by the coercivity factor 
also measured in T. Once the material has been reoriented, it takes some additional energy to 
turn it back on [8]. 
 
The reorientation of the dipoles are what causes the heating of the material, as some energy will 
be dissipated in the core during the reorientation [9]. The materials lack of retraceability is 
determined by the material, and is what gives the losses its name as the losses trace out a 
hysteresis loop. 
The hysteresis loop for lower magnetizations will look differently. The residual magnetization 
will not be as severe, and the force required to reach zero will therefore be lower. The exact 
value is not known, as manufactures usually state the value after saturation.  
The inner line are the starting conditions. Afterwards, the material won’t get back to that state, 
unless heated to the Curie temperature. 
The Curie temperature is a point where a material losses it magnetic capabilities. 
Figure 24: Hysteresis loop. With saturation level, residual magnetization and coercive force

Normally for ferrimagnetic materials, the magnetic interaction are kept together due to the 
polarization. This makes it so that no parallel electrons are occupying the same point. Implying 
a parallel alignment in the material. 
Once the material reaches the Curie temperature, the atoms will lose their alignment and order. 
Making the material lose its magnetic capabilities. Instead, the dipoles will just follow the field. 
If the temperature drops below the Curie point, the material will begin regaining some of its 
capabilities.

2.4.2.2 Eddy current 
Eddy current or Foucault current, are loops of electric currents generated within a conductive 
or magnetic material when affected by a perpendicular changing magnetic field. These current 
loops are perpendicular to the field generated in the conductor or material.  
The small current loops are generating an internal magnetic field, opposing the magnetization 
change caused by external field and reacting back at the source.  
 
Current flowing through the resistance of a conductive or magnetic material will dissipate 
energy as heat. The eddy currents can be looked at as energy loss, modelled as standard current 
resistance loss. The energy dissipated in the magnetic material will have to be calculated, in 
order to properly determine a temperature rise.  
 
Eddy current can be reduced by decreasing the thickness of the material. This can be done by 
cutting thin slits in the core or by making the core up of laminations. 
Laminated cores are made up of thinner sheets with insulators in between. This prevents AC 
energy from being dissipated as eddy losses in the core materials. Laminated cores for power 
converters are usually made up of powdered magnetic material.

4 Control methods 
The converter should have implemented a control loop for the converter, to ensure safe 
operating for the GaN device. 
In this chapter there will be looked at different ways of implementing a control either digital or 
analog. There will not be made a practical implementation of the control. The reason for this 
will be explained through the chapter. 
 
The different configurations requires different amount of control, in order to properly operate 
the converter. This control needs to be capable of operating in a frequency range from 1 to 10 
MHz, with high precision. 
 
The control loop need to be able to operate with different methods of regulations. 
Pulse Width Modulation will control the power transfer for both the hard switched and the 
forced ZVS configuration. The control loop should therefore be able to adjust the duty cycle 
according to the voltage levels of the converter according to the different transfer function for 
the modes of operation. 
 
Frequency control: In order to optimize efficiency and peak current, the control should be able 
to adjust the frequency up and down to compensate. This will also be part of the control 
maintaining the ZVS for the converter. 
 
Dead time control: When the frequency of the converter is varying, the requirements of an 
adaptive dead time control rises. The time control should be able to minimize the amount of 
reverse conduction through the switches, while still maintain the ZVS.  
 
A problem arises when looking over the amount of regulation. For the dead time control, the 
problem need to be measured on the occurring event, and any change to frequency or load for 
the next period, will also be affected by the dead time variation. 
The sheer complexity of establishing a control loop of this magnitude, is far beyond the scope 
of this project. Instead, there will be looked at the restriction and disadvantages by using digital 
and discreet analog control.

4.1 Microcontroller unit 
 
Microcontroller unit (MCU) is a small computer on a single integrated circuit (IC).  
This is the easiest implementation but also gives the most restrictions. For this project the TI 
C2000 Launchpad evaluation board was analyzed. This device is a 32-bit 60 MHz MCU. 
 
There will be looked into establishing PWM, frequency and dead time control on the C2000. 
Which are required for the configuration implemented in this project. 
  
Using standard libraries for this devices, help setting up the control for each operational mode. 
As there can be easily established function for adjusting duty-cycle, frequency and dead-time. 
As there are already established header files containing function parameters and registers

The C2000 has the possibility of having seven ADC’s running. Each ADC can be set up for 
separate interrupt and priority or be group. If all the values are needed simultaneously for the 
regulations.  
If the ADC’s are grouped without priority, the controller will run them in a round robin. Each 
value will be measured according to the loop with the preset number of samplings for the ADC. 
An interrupt will occur once the last ADC has completed its samplings, making the measured 
data available for the regulation and before clearing the registers for new measurements.  
Using the round-robin method reduces the regulation speed, equal to the numbers of ADC’s 
and the amount of sampling set for the system.  
The C2000 can manage up to 12.5 mega samples per second (MSPS) [13]. Meaning that each 
sample conversion takes 80 ns (for the 60 MHz MCU operating frequency). The propagation 
delay of the LMG5200 is approximately 30 ns and the total period is 100 ns, making 80 ns per 
sample way too long. To optimize this an external ADC with at least a sampling rate of 125 
MSPS could be used. This will decrease the sampling time to 8 ns, but will also require some 
discreet components to perform the fast regulation. 
 
For these configurations three to three ADC’s are required. Measuring the in- and output 
voltage and the converter current. This will ensure that the system can maintain ZVS, by 
estimating this from the current measurement Using any sampling for this amount of ADC’s 
will make for a very slow control, compared to the switching frequency. 
  
Sampling a signal is a tradeoff of speed or precision. The controller will use an average over 
multiple of cycle, in order to minimize noise that may have occurring during the measurements.  
On the other hand, not using sampling can result in faulty regulations due to measurements 
either being too high or low. 
As the input and output voltages can be assumed to be fairly stable, meaning that the regulation 
can be slow. The MCU can be used to measure these values, with a low priority and many 
samples in order to determine these and the mode of operation for the converter. 
For measuring the current and drain-source the MCU falls short. As the conversion time in 
themselves are far too long to pick up any of these values.

4.2 Analog 
 
Analog control, compared to digital control, is far more complex and due to the requirements 
on the amount of components and PCB layout, has many fall groups.  
In this chapter there will be looked into suggestions on how to implement PWM, frequency and 
dead time control using discreet components. Which would be required for the hard switched 
and forced ZVS to function properly. As with the digital control, there will also be looked in to 
problems this may cause and restriction it may set for the control loop. 
There are many ways to implement the control methods by using discreet components. This 
chapter will look into control circuit that can be set up using standard IC’s such as operational 
amplifiers (Op-Amp), semiconductors, logic circuits and passive components. 
 
The analog control will run into the same dilemma as the digital control for the fast control. 
Any time constants created by filters, rise times or propagation delays in the control loop, may 
compare to the propagation delay of the LMG5200.  
A benefit of using analog control is the high resolution for duty cycle, frequency and the dead 
time. The resolution can be determined by the number of passive component available and the 
precision of the voltage reference used. Making the resolution almost infinite.  
Component tolerances will have an effect on the precision of these. But this can easily be 
compensated for. 
 
For the dead time implementation there have been looked at an Op-Amp, exclusive or-gate 
(XOR) and a Schmitt trigger. Shown in figure 20.

There will occur different delays from the different components. These will be in the form of 
rise time and propagation delays. Propagation delay indicates the time it takes the input signal 
to reach the output of the device.  
 
Operational amplifiers are DC-coupled high gain voltage amplifiers with differential input and 
a single output. These devices usually has a very large gain, depending on the feedback of the 
circuit. 
An exclusive OR, also called XOR, gate can be utilized to make the complementary gate 
signals. A normal OR has a high output if either or both inputs are high, but an XOR gate only 
has a high output if one of the input are high. This will ensure that both gate signals won’t be 
active at the same time which will cause shoot through in the converter. 
A Schmitt trigger is an analog to digital converter with a hysteresis. Meaning that it will create 
a logic output once an analog reaches above and fall under a certain threshold. This means that 
there should not be high oscillations during the off period of the signal, as this may cause faulty 
turn on for the gates. Additionally, the charging of the capacitor will function as the dead time 
and will not be seen by the gate. 
 
Components, chosen for this analysis and for implementing dead time control are: 
LT1818 operational amplifier has a rise time of 2500 V/us or 2.5 V/ns (2 ns for a 5V step). If 
worked beyond the bandwidth of the device, there will be an additional propagation delay of 1 
ns.  
SN74AHC1G86 XOR has a propagation delay of 10 ns, while operating at five volts. 
SN74LVC1G17 Schmitt trigger, with propagation delay of 5 ns at five volt operation.

8 Conclusion 
Using magnetic material for this topology is not an option. Using materials will be a trade of 
either having high losses or applying an impossible amount of windings to the cores. In order 
to construct a functional inductor, the core material should be neglected.  
This then brings its own problems. As the leakage from the inductor requires a distance greater 
than 10 mm for any metallic components placed beside it. But due to the high operating 
frequencies, this should be avoidable. As electrolytic capacitors will now be used.  
 
The Steinmetz calculations gave a proper estimation for the constants. Furthermore, the 
optimized version showed reduction in the losses. This loss was a little high for some of 
calculations, indicating that the constant kc may be too high. 
 
Implementing zero voltage switching will increase the overall efficiency of the converter. 
Allowing for wide load range at high frequencies. 
 
The topology was proven to be functional at operating frequencies up to 10 MHz. Each mode 
of operation was verified and the converter achieved an efficiency of 96.62 % and a peak output 
power of 93.86 W.  
 
There were not implemented any control loop for the converter. By looking at the digital- and 
analog-control analysis of the controller gives a clear indication that it will be far too complex 
to implement a closed loop for this topology. 
9 Future work 
Implementing a closed loop control for the converter, in order for the converter to be able to 
operate fully autonomy’s. The control should be able to detect the direction of power flow and 
ensure a precise output voltage. Adjust the current through frequency modulation and power 
flow through duty cycle modulation. Additionally, the control loop should be able to regulate 
the dead time to ensure high efficiency and compensate for any propagation delays in the 
system, as these become close to an operation period. 
 
Verifying the core loss measurements using a sinusoidal current, in order to properly determine 
the Steinmetz constants. This will ensure a better analysis of the core losses and the necessary 
==PAGE:119==

amount of windings for each material. Encasing the air inductor in a plastic casing, to determine 
if this is the reduction in the field leaked. 
Testing different material constructions like rod cores and finding suitable materials with low

Investigate EMI and EMC compliance to determine the practical implementation usage on the 
grid.
