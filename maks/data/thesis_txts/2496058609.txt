Abstract 
Bacteria of the Vibrionaceae family are widespread in the marine environment and they are 
known producers of bioactive secondary metabolites, including antibiotics. The roles of antibiotics 
in the natural environment of their producers is still widely debated and include their potential as 
signaling molecules as well as weapons. The purpose of this project is to determine if the 
antibiotic production of one marine bacterium, Photobacterium galatheae is affected by co-
cultivation with another marine bacterium, Vibrio coralliilyticus. The aim is to explore the role of 
antibiotic mediated interactions in marine Vibrionaceae. A co-culture setup of V. coralliilyticus 
S2052 and P. galatheae S2753 in which antibiotic mediated interactions can be measured was 
achieved in this study. At the moment, the complete growth stability of bacteria the co-culture 
system come at the price of holomycin detection. Co-culturing V. coralliilyticus S2052 and P. 
galatheae S2753 has a clear effect on andrimid production in V. coralliilyticus S2052. While not 
definitively proven, results from this study indicate that both the presence of andrimid and an 
additional factor(s) in co-culturing with V. coralliilyticus S2052 induces the production of 
holomycin in P. galatheae S2753. Furthermore, these results suggest that co-culturing can be a 
way of inducing secondary metabolites in marine Vibrionaceae by allowing for microbial 
interaction, thus proving that co-culturing can provide a way of facilitating biodiscovery.  
 
 
Resumé 
Bakterier tilhørende Vibrionaceae familien er ofte forekommende i marine miljøer og er velkendte 
producenter af bioaktive sekundære metabolitter, herunder antibiotika. Antibiotikas rolle i deres 
producenters naturlige miljø er stadig under diskussion, bl.a. deres potentiale som signalstoffer så

marine Vibrionaceae. I dette kandidatspeciale er der konstrueret et eksperimentelt set-up med V. 
coralliilyticus S2052 og P. galatheae S2753 i co-kultur, hvori antibiotika-medierede interaktioner 
kan måles gennem kemiske analyser. På nuværende tidspunkt kommer en fuldstændig stabil 
vækst af begge bakterier på bekostning af manglende holomycin detektion. Dyrkning af V. 
coralliilyticus S2052 og P. galatheae S2753 i co-kultur har en tydelig effekt på produktionen af

tilstedeværelsen af andrimid samt andre faktorer i co-kultur med V. coralliilyticus S2052 inducerer

givne forsøg. Derudover, indikerer disse resultater at dyrkning i co-kultur er en metode, der 
gennem muliggørelsen for mikrobiel interaktion, kan bruges til at inducere sekundær metabolisme 
i marine Vibrionaceae, og hermed vises denne vækstmetodes validitet i søgen efter nye bioaktive 
stoffer.

1. Introduction 
In 2015, it was estimated that 5,7 million people die each year of infectious diseases because 
antimicrobials were not available to them [1]. During that same year an estimated 33110 people 
died due to infections with antibiotic resistant bacteria in Europe alone [2]. In a study by the U.S. 
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention published last year more than 35000 deaths were 
attributed to antimicrobial resistance [3]. It is numbers like these that show an urgent need for 
novel drugs to combat bacterial infections globally now and in the future.  
Scientists have approached this issue is by looking for new bioactive compounds hidden in nature, 
so-called natural product discovery. Most currently discovered bioactive compounds fall under the 
umbrella of the term secondary metabolites [4]. Secondary metabolites, whilst not believed to be 
essential for basic growth, have a broad range of bioactivities such as growth promotion, sedative, 
antimicrobial, anti- fungal, and anti-cancer activity as well as being natural pigments [5]. Thus, due 
to their many potential applications microbiologists are extensively studying secondary 
metabolites in the hope of utilization in health, agriculture and a range of other industries. 
Bioactive compounds can be isolated from organisms in every ecological niche imaginable and the 
marine environment is no exception [5–7]. Most natural product discovery efforts were originally 
centered around microorganisms from soil [5]. However, the marine environment offers a 
promising source of novel bioactive compounds for several reasons. First, the mere size of the 
oceans, covering over 70% of Earth’s surface is making room for a nearly endless abundance of 
life. Microbial life in the oceans is estimated to reach 106 cell/mL in seawater and 109 cell/ mL in 
ocean-bottom sediments [7]. Second, the many extreme conditions of the different ocean 
environments, such as high pressure, low oxygen, salinity etc. pushes microorganisms to adapt to 
specific niches and thus creating an array of biodiversity, increasing the likelihood of chemically 
unique natural products [8–10].  
Furthermore, genomic and metagenomic data confirms that there is indeed a vast genetic 
potential of marine bacteria the realization of which is still underway, as standard laboratory 
conditions are not always sufficient for the production of secondary metabolites [11,12]. As 
bacteria seldom live in isolation, but in contact with a host and/or in multi-species communities, it 
is believed that culturing microorganisms within co-culture with other microorganisms will allow 
for production and discovery of novel bioactives [13]. This has led to research into new methods 
for cultivating microorganisms and to investigating bacteria in co-cultures. This is done to mimic 
natural environments that allow for bacterial interaction. As different ecological factors including 
secondary metabolites do in fact have an impact on metabolite production, studying the natural 
role of antibiotics could give us a better understanding of when and how interesting metabolites 
are produced [13]. Additionally, information on the natural role of antibiotics as well as bacterial 
interaction and the influence of co-cultures could potentially give valuable insights useful for 
bacterial control in human health, agriculture, food production etc. [14]. 
To this end, this thesis will explore the antibiotic mediated interactions between two marine 
bacteria, Vibrio coralliilyticus S2052 and Photobacterium galatheae S2753, when grown together 
in a co-culture (for further details see section 1.7). Before doing so, relevant topics, concepts and 
methods will be introduced in the following subsections.

1.1. Orphan biosynthetic gene clusters 
Secondary metabolites are often produced by enzyme complexes such as polyketide synthases 
(PKSs) or non-ribosomal protein synthetases (NRPSs). These enzymes are encoded by genes 
clustering together in the genome of the producer aptly named biosynthetic gene clusters (BCGs). 
As DNA sequencing is becoming an increasingly common practice in many laboratories, it has 
become easier to screen the genomes of organisms as a part of biodiscovery. High quality, publicly 
available bioinformatic tools such as antiSMASH [15] have been developed to identify key 
components of different BCGs and so genomes can easily be mined for novel bioactive 
compounds. This requires that the BCGs corresponding to the novel compound has a structural 
resemblance to known BCGs. While it may be difficult to find completely unknown compounds, 
these tools are both useful for identifying the genes of already isolated compounds or give an 
indication of the bioactivity potential hidden in a genome just waiting to be discovered. In fact, 
many genomes of different origin contain BCGs for which the corresponding product is still 
undiscovered, the orphan, silent or cryptic BCGs. These orphan BCGs represent the vast potential 
of - likely to be novel - secondary metabolites. Thus, figuring out which compounds they 
correspond to and how to express them is important for future commercial use in e.g. the 
biotechnology, human health or agriculture industries.  
There are several possible strategies for unsilencing orphan BCGs including molecular and 
synthetic microbiology methods. An example is heterologous expression in a model host 
organism, where an orphan BCG is often combined with a controllable promoter and expression is 
attempted in a model organism that is easy to cultivate and control. It is also possible to rely solely 
on changing culturing conditions to change expression patterns, and such approaches are 
described in further detail in section 1.4.  
Biodiscovery is not just about how to look, but also where. While traditional natural product 
discovery was focused plants and terrestrial bacteria, scientists are now looking to the less 
explored marine environment for new bioactive compounds [16]. 
 
1.2. Bioactivity potential of marine Vibrionaceae  
Bioprospecting in the marine environment has a high potential for new compound discovery in 
several bacterial families, including the family Vibrionaceae commonly referred to as vibrios [17]. 
While members of the Vibrionaceae family include human and animal pathogens such as V. 
cholerae and V. vulnificus, symbiotic species V. fisheri and V. harveyi, most Vibrionaceae species 
are commensal or mutualistic [18]. Vibrios are associated with both biotic as well as abiotic 
surfaces, including non-nutritive surfaces, and can also occur as free-living cells [18–20]. They also 
have the ability to utilize a range of nutrients, including – in some instances – plastic films [18,21]. 
Furthermore, vibrios are found in all latitudes being, however, most abundant in tropical and 
subtropical regions [22]. 
Many bioactives have been already been isolated from vibrios, some of which are listed in table 1. 
Additionally, vibrios are considered among the most prevalent culturable marine bacteria, and so 
this family of marine bacteria are ideal candidates for bioactivity research [19].

Two such marine Vibrionaceae strains, Photobacterium galatheae S2753 and Vibrio coralliilyticus 
S2052, were isolated based on antibacterial activity during a global research expedition, Galathea 
III in 2006 [24]. P. galatheae S2753 and V. coralliilyticus S2052 each produce a known antibiotic, 
holomycin and andrimid respectively [24].  
Holomycin was originally isolated from Streptomyces griseus in 1959 and has as of January 2020 
only been isolated from other actinomycetes – with the exception of P. galatheae S2753. In 2017 
Chang et al. proved zinc chelation as the mode of action for holomycin [45]. This is thought to 
have an impact on RNA synthesis, an essential cellular process, as zinc is often associated with 
RNA polymerases, as well as other metalloprotein-dependent cellular processes [45]. Holomycin 
displays broad-spectrum antibacterial activity, inhibiting both Gram-positive and Gram-negative 
bacteria. The biosynthesis of holomycin is catalyzed by an NRPS. The biosynthetic gene cluster 
encoding this NRPS was first described in 2011 by Huang et al., when they transferred the gene 
cluster from Streptomyces clavuligerus to Streptomyces albus and managed to successfully 
produce holomycin through heterologous expression [46]. 
 
 
 
Figure 1 Chemical structure of holomycin from P. galatheae S2753 (left) and andrimid from V. coralliilyticus (right) 
  
 
Andrimid has – in addition to V. coralliilyticus S2052 – been isolated from several soil and marine 
bacteria. The antibiotic was first described in the late 1980s where the compound was isolated 
from an intracellular symbiont of a brown planthopper [47]. Andrimid is also an antibiotic with a 
broad spectrum, showing inhibition of gram-positive as well as gram-negative bacteria. The 
antibiotic activity of andrimid is attributed to its inhibition of bacterial acetyl-CoA carboxylase, an 
enzyme catalyzing the conversion of acetyl-CoA to malonyl-CoA, a primary step in fatty acid and 
polyketide biosynthesis [48,49]. The andrimid biosynthetic gene cluster was first described by Jin 
et al. in 2006, who also proposed model for the biosynthesis of andrimid catalyzed by a NRPS-
(type II)PKS hybrid enzyme [50]. Comparative analysis of sequenced andrimid biosynthetic gene 
cluster by Matilla et al. suggests that the andrimid biosynthetic gene cluster is transferred 
horizontally between bacterial strains [51].  
In addition to the BGCs for holomycin and andrimid, the genomes of both P. galatheae S2753 and 
V. coralliilyticus S2052 contain several biosynthetic gene clusters. AntiSMASH analysis revealed 13

putative BGCs in the genome of P. galatheae S2753 and 7 putative BGCs in V. coralliilyticus S2052 
[52]. Some are associated with known compounds, such as solonamides A and B in P. galatheae 
S2753, while others are still orphan, potentially encoding enzymes with the ability to produce 
novel secondary metabolites [23,53]. Uncovering the ecological roles of different secondary 
metabolites may be the key to expressing them in the lab.  
 
1.3. Beginning to explore the roles of antibiotics 
Secondary metabolites are produced by many organisms including plants, fungi and bacteria and 
are named so because their production is not considered essential for growth – as opposed to 
primary metabolites [14]. While this may be the case under standard laboratory conditions, these 
compounds have likely developed as part of an adaption to different environmental factors and 
thus confer a selective advantage in their natural setting. So, while not required for the growth of 
a single cell, secondary metabolites may be key factors for bacterial survival in an ever-changing 
environment, as they are often instrumental in cellular differentiation [54]. 
Secondary metabolites with antibiotic properties have long been of interest and they have 
classically been viewed as weapons of the producer [4,55]. However, it is also believed that 
antibiotic concentrations in soil are too low to kill or inhibit competing bacteria [56]. Thus, rather 
than exclusively being weapons, this argument led to the idea that antibiotics are cooperative 
signals [57]. That antibiotics are used for intercellular communication and collectively regulate the 
homeostasis of microbial communities [58].  
Exploring this theory, Hoffmann et al. showed that subinhibitory concentrations of aminoglycoside 
antibiotics induced biofilm formation in both Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Escherichia coli [59]. In 
2014, Seyedsayamdost demonstrated that subinhibitory concentrations of numerous antibiotics 
could induce a BCG encoding a secondary metabolite in Burkholderia thailandensis, whilst higher 
concentrations of the compounds were, as expected, lethal to B. thailandensis [60]. These dual 
effects of antibiotics could indicate that antibiotics can serve as signaling molecule, however, an 
alternative explanation could be that the ability of a bacterium to sense secondary metabolites 
produced by a different species may serve as a warning sign, giving notice of a possible competitor 
[61]. For this reason, biofilm formation and secondary metabolite production may be induced in 
the bacterium as weapons or shields in order to improve the odds of survival. Thus, antibiotics 
may not be true signals but rather be elicitors of responses in a form of competition sensing, as 
proposed by Cornforth in 2013 (this concept will be evaluated further in the discussion) [61].  
Understanding the ecological roles of antibiotics in the social context in which they are used in 
nature, will provide valuable information for the basic understanding of antibiotics and bacterial 
interactions and potentially be applicable in drug discovery and bacterial control. Investigating this 
requires experimental setups that mimic the natural environment of antibiotic producers and 
allow for microbial interactions.

1.4. Culturing conditions in natural product discovery 
As mentioned, one approach to discovery and induction of secondary metabolites is to recreate 
ecological settings and environmental factors, that mimic the natural habitat of the organism in 
question. Assuming of course that such conditions will result in expression of BGCs and production 
of the compounds. An advantage of this approach is that we may also be able to confer why a 
certain compound is important for the producing organism in a given situation, answering basic 
research questions along with finding biological solutions to our health, food and production 
challenges [13,56].  
Many adjustments can be made to standard laboratory cultivation strategies to accommodate a 
more natural growth environment for a microorganism and thus potentially promote secondary 
metabolite production. Culturing conditions to consider include nutrient sources and 
concentrations, allowing attachment and biofilm formation, temperatures, pH levels, addition of 
sublethal doses of stress-factors and co-cultivation strategies [62–65].  
The effect of a change in culturing conditions is exemplified in 2017 by Giubergia et. al 
demonstrated that growth on chitin induced the expression of genes involved in secondary 
metabolism in V. coralliilyticus and P. galatheae. Furthermore, the change was also detectable in 
the metabolome, where production of andrimid and holomycin had increased significantly. They 
proposed that both V. coralliilyticus and P. galatheae have a specific lifestyle for growth on chitin 
and that their secondary metabolites may play a crucial role during chitin colonization [52]. 
Another example from 2003, a study by Yan et al. showed that biofilm formation of Bacillus 
licheniformis, a bacterium isolated from the surface of a marine algae, resulted in production of 
secondary metabolites not detected during growth in shake flasks [66].  
Finally, as the main aim of this thesis includes setting up a co-culture system, this approach to 
inducing bioactivity deserves special attention. In nature, microbial species are connected by a 
network of complex interactions that are central to the structure and function of any community 
[67]. As such most microorganisms do not live as axenic organisms in the biosphere, but are often 
found in multispecies communities, altering the culture conditions away from monocultures 
towards co-culturing of two or more bacterial strains may provide settings more similar to nature 
[68]. The interactions of microorganisms fall along a line ranging between cooperation and 
antagonism, and the influence of these interactions between bacterial strains on the production of 
secondary metabolites can be investigated in such experimental setups [67,68]. In 2013, a study 
demonstrated that the secreted metabolome of Streptomyces coelicolor changed when it was 
pairwise co-cultured with five other actinomycete soil inhabitants. Each microbial pairing resulted 
in a unique secondary metabolite profile with a specific combination of molecules produced. This 
exemplifies the importance of interactions in secondary metabolite production and as a result 
thereof co-culturing’s potential in biodiscovery [69].

1.5. Co-culture strategies  
The basic idea of a co-culture setup is to allow cultivation of two or more cell populations with 
some degree of interaction between them (figure 2). This degree of interaction can vary greatly 
and is often chosen based on the purpose and aim of a given study as well as the methods 
available for analysis. Often, the motivations for using a co-culture setup include studying natural 
interactions, improving culturing success or cell behavior for certain populations or establishing 
synthetic interactions between populations [70]. 
Co-culturing mixed populations without separation is likely the simplest experimental setup, 
allowing for all types of interactions including exchange of molecules of all sizes as well as physical 
interaction. However, this requires that the conditions are designed so all strains can grow and co-
exist, and this can be experimentally very challenging. This kind of co-cultivation has been widely 
used and its usefulness for inducing secondary metabolites is exemplified by a study in 2012, in 
which Cho and Kim co-cultured a Streptomyces cinnabarinus strain with a marine-derived 
Alteromonas sp., and this induced the production of the antifouling compound lobocompactol in 
S. cinnabarinus [71].

However, there are some challenges for mixed co-cultures such as toxin production, differences in 
growth rates or nutrient uptake, which has led to the development of several co-culturing

experimental setups enabling that, and for some co-culture systems the defined spatial structure 
facilitates the stabilization of a co-culture [72,73]. This was the case, when Kim et al. set up a co-
culture of three soil bacteria, Azotobacter vinelandii, Bacillus licheniformis, and Paenibacillus 
curdlanolyticus. The three species were not stable in a mixed community, but when separated 
physically, but still allowing for chemical communication via a nano-porous membrane the system

populations makes it possible to view a co-culture as put together by “monoculture modules”, 
enabling the use of individualized environments for different populations or asymmetric 
separation allowing investigation of a one-way interaction.  
There are many factors to consider in order to set up and maintain a stable co-culture system, and 
it has been proposed that knowing the most about the strains to be included in co-culture 
beforehand will provide the greatest chance of getting a successful stable co-culture [70]. Thus, 
exploring growth patterns and expression profiles in monoculture – if possible – may serve well 
before initiating co-cultures.  
 
1.6. Analyzing growth and secondary metabolism in co-cultures 
Accurate measurements of the biologically relevant interactions between population in a co-
culture can be challenging, as the populations may exchange more than what the study attempts 
to investigate [70]. 
The techniques used for monitoring, characterizing and quantifying populations in co-culture are 
in large part the same as for monocultures. However, some of methods that allow for easy and 
high-throughput analysis of monitoring growth of monocultures, such as optical density (OD) 
measurements or simple plate counting are not suitable for mixed species cultures due to lack of 
differentiation options [70]. Thus, it is important to evaluate potential methods to be used as an 
initial part of co-culture set-up planning. The methods used in this study are described in the 
following subsections.  
 
1.6.1. Quantification of bacteria in a co-culture setup 
Monitoring growth is important for setting up a stable co-culture system and quantifying 
populations within the system plays a crucial role for monitoring and regulating stability. 
Counting of colony forming units (CFUs) is common practice and has been used for many years to 
quantify bacteria and fungi in co- and monocultures, as it is generally easy (for culturable bacteria) 
and cheap. A disadvantage of colony counting is that it may require selective plating to allow for 
differentiation between phenotypically alike species. This can be problematic for closely related 
strains, as they can often grow under similar conditions and finding selective agents can become a 
real challenge.  
Quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction (qPCR) is a method for quantifying the amount of DNA in 
a sample and can as such be used to quantify the number of cells in that sample. As DNA is 
amplified through PCR, a fluorescent dye binds to the DNA, and the fluorescent signal is 
continuously monitored to measure the accumulation of target DNA for each amplification cycle. 
The level of fluorescence increases proportionally to the amplification of target DNA [74]. The 
amount of DNA is measured as the number of cycles it takes to reach a given threshold, giving a Ct 
value. These values can be converted to Optical Density (OD) values or CFUs through a standard 
curve constructed by measuring the Ct values of DNA in different dilutions of a known OD of CFU 
value. One advantage of using qPCR is that specifically designed primers allow for distinct 
quantification of even closely related strains in a co-culture sample [74].

1.6.2. Analyzing the secondary metabolite profile of bacterial co-cultures 
Identification, quantification and even distribution of the compounds produced by the populations 
are interesting features to investigate when determining the effects of co-culturing on secondary 
metabolite profiles. Two methods that combined provide analysis of all these features are HPLC-
UV-MS and MALDI-TOF-IMS [75].  
HPLC-UV-MS is a method commonly used for the detection and quantification of secondary 
metabolites in environmental samples and laboratory grown cultures. In High-Pressure-Liquid-
Chromatography (HPLC) high pressure allows for a sample to be transported at a high velocity 
through the viscous environment inside a column. The column holds a stationary phase that 
causes analysts to be eluded at different times, based on their interactions with the surface of the 
stationary phase [75].  
After sample separation, a UV-detector, such as a diode array detector (DAD), exposes the eluent 
to light in the UV and visual spectrum (190-900nm) some of which is absorbed by the compounds, 
depending on their specific properties (figure 3). The unabsorbed light is detected by the DAD and 
a chromatogram is created, which holds information about retention time and at what 
wavelengths the various compounds absorb light. The non-destructive nature of DAD makes 
subsequent analysis of the compounds possible. DAD can be used for both qualitative and 
quantitative analysis. A disadvantage of the DAD is that it only detects conjugated double bonds, 
meaning that not all compounds can be detected using this method [75,76].  
 
 
Figure 3 Diode Array Detector. Light from the visible and UV-spectrum is radiated through a flow cell, in which 
molecules separated by HPLC enters at the top and exits at the bottom. Light is absorbed at different wavelengths for 
different molecules. The unabsorbed passes through an optical slit and a grating separates the light waves and the 
diode array detects the signal coming from the different wavelengths.  
 
The final step in HPLC-UV-MS is Mass Spectrometry, another analytical method that allows for 
identification as well as quantification of compounds. This method is based on the ionization of 
compounds. During ionization some compounds are fragmented and thus MS is a destructive 
means of analysis. Ionization can result in both positive and negative ions and commonly 
performed by electrospray, when in context with HPLC-UV. In positive electrospray ionization, 
each molecule will pick up one or more ions e.g. H+, Na+, NH4 and K+. Some molecules are more 
==PAGE:17==
 
17 of 63 
likely to pick up some ions than others, thus an adduct pattern is created, which can assist in the 
identification of a molecule [76]. A mass spectrometer measures the mass to charge ratio (m/z) of 
an ionized compound, e.g. by time-of-flight (TOF). In TOF, after the molecules are ionized, a 
specific energy is applied, and the velocity of the ions are then measured and correlated to the 
m/z [75]. The TOF principle is illustrated in figure 4.  
 
 
Figure 4 Simplified schematic illustration of MALDI-TOF. A matrix is applied to a sample after which laser is used to 
ionize the compounds in the sample which are then transported into a time-of-flight (TOF) field due to the charge 
applied. The time it takes for a molecule to travel through the field is measured and a m/z ratio is detected for each 
compound. Larger ions travel slower than smaller ions.  
 
MALDI-TOF-IMS is an Imaging Mass Spectrometry technique used to identify compounds as well as 
their relative abundance and distribution in tissues or plated cultures. This would allow for 
visualization of e.g. the effect of culturing two strains in close proximity on the production of 
compounds. This MS technique uses Matrix Assisted Laser Desorption Ionization (MALDI) coupled 
with TOF mass spectrometry (figure 4) [77,78]. Signal intensities for specific m/z can be measured 
and doing so for individual pixels creates an image of molecular intensity and distribution in a 
sample [77]. MALDI was originally developed for larger compounds such as proteins and lipids. 
This gives MALDI-TOF-IMS a disadvantage in bacterial secondary metabolite detection as many of 
the matrixes currently used for ionization often interferes with the detection of signals from small 
molecules, such as antibiotics. The reason behind being that matrixes often consist of molecules 
with a low molecular weight and so their m/z ratios are similar to those of the small compounds 
[79].

1.7. Purpose & Aim 
The purpose of this project is to determine if the antibiotic production of one marine bacterium, P. 
galatheae is affected by co-cultivation with another marine bacterium, V. coralliilyticus. It is 
known from work of PhD student Yannick Buijs, that pure andrimid in sub-inhibitory 
concentrations can induce holomycin production, however, this has not been demonstrated as an 
interaction between the two living organisms. Thus, the aim is to set up a stable co-culture system 
that can be used to study the antibiotic mediated effects on growth of and secondary metabolite 
production in P. galatheae S2753 and V. coralliilyticus S2052. To this end, co-culture experiments 
of both liquid and agar-plated co-cultures containing these two marine Vibrionaceae will be 
conducted. Analysis of bacterial growth and secondary metabolite production, mainly the 
antibiotics holomycin and andrimid, in the co-culture setting will be done using a range of 
chemical and biological assays, including but not limited to HPLC-UV-MS, MALDI-TOF-IMS, 
selective plating and qPCR. The experiments will include comparison to axenic cultures as well as 
non-antibiotic producing mutant versions of the two strains in order to differentiate between the 
effect of co-culturing versus the effect of antibiotic-mediated interaction.

3.4. Conjugational genes missing in V. coralliilyticus S2052∆admO 
Genome sequencing by Phd student Yannick Buijs showed that the construction of the andrimid 
knock-out mutant V. coralliilyticus S2052∆admO, had led to the loss of approx. 5% of its genetic 
material, including the entire andrimid BGC. To get an indication of what else was lost along with 
the andrimid gene cluster, all genes with a GenBank annotation were evaluated based on function. 
Hypothetical genes were excluded. This analysis resulted in the finding of two genes involved in 
conjugation, conjugational transfer protein TraA and TraE, in close proximity to the andrimid gene 
cluster. This suggest that the andrimid gene cluster is encoded on a plasmid.

3.8. Co-culture summary  
Through different inoculation ratios of S2052 and S2753 it is possible to change the stability of a 
co-culture experimental setup. In a ratio of 1:1 S2753 takes over the system somewhere between 
3h and 8h and as the ratio of S2052 to S2753 increases, S2052 is able to grow to a higher density 
within the system and the rate of cell death decreases. At a ratio of 10:1 S2052 and S2753 both 
show stable growth patterns within the system, albeit S2753 cell density is 10-fold lower than in 
monoculture.  
Results from CC3, CC4 and CC5 provide strong indication of co-culturing having an effect on the 
production of andrimid in S2052, increasing when grown in co-culture with S2753. Based on the 
results from this study it is not possible to provide reliable evidence of influence of co-culturing on 
the production of holomycin in S2753. This being due to the differences in abundance apparent 
from CC4 is not backed up by statistically significant differences in holomycin abundance by any of 
the other co-culture setups. 
 
3.9. Plated co- and monocultures chemically analyzed using MALDI-TOF-IMS 
The first MALDI-TOF-IMS setup was created to test if holomycin is even detectable. S2753 was 
grown on thin APY and LB agar before being deployed on the MALDI-PLATE and dried, see figure 
17 (left). Two different matrices, a universal matrix and DHB were used for ionization. However, 
results show no difference between holomycin producing S2753 WT and mutant strain 
S2753∆hmlE (figure 17 (right). Additionally, the pure agar controls also give a signal at same m/z 
ratio. Combined, these results suggest interference of the matrix at the same m/z as holomycin.

4. Discussion 
This study aimed at setting up a stable co-culture system enabling investigation of antibiotic 
mediated interactions between V. coralliilyticus S2052 and P. galatheae S2753. This was achieved 
by a series of five co-culture experiments in which differences in inoculation ratios between the 
two strains changed growth patterns and antibiotic production in the populations of the mixed 
cultures. The results and challenges of this study are discussed and evaluation in following 
subsections.  
 
4.1. Co-culture stability and optimization of setup 
4.1.1. Conjugational genes missing in V. coralliilyticus S2052∆admO 
Genome sequencing by Phd student Yannick Buijs showed that the construction of the andrimid 
knock-out mutant V. coralliilyticus S2052∆admO, had led to the loss of approx. 5% of its genetic 
material, including the entire andrimid BGC. To evaluate if this mutant would still be useful as an 
andrimid deficient mutant in a co-culture setup, the genes encoded on missing genetic material 
was evaluated. This resulted in the finding of two genes involved in conjugation, conjugational 
transfer protein TraA and TraE, in close proximity to the andrimid gene cluster. This suggest that 
the andrimid gene cluster is encoded on a plasmid. These results fit well with previous studies 
hypothesizing transfer of andrimid via horizontal gene transfer [51]. This may explain why approx. 
5% of genetic material was lost, as selective pressure during construction of the deletion mutant 
may have resulted in loss of the entire plasmid. In combination with the similarity in growth curves 
between WT and mutant, it was decided that the S2052∆admO mutant could be included in this 
study. 
 
4.1.2. Selective plating, qPCR and quantification 
Finding a plating option selective for growth of S2052 was not achieved in this thesis, neither 
through literature searches nor experimental screening. Using the Biolog Gen III Microplate for 
screening of possible selective agents ultimately proved unsuccessful for both S2052 and S2753. In 
both assays an addition of Sigma sea salt to a final concentration of approx. 2% in the inoculation 
fluids was done to accommodate growth of both marine strains. As this is not a part of the 
manufacturer’s protocol, the additional salt may interfere with the assay, resulting in false 
positives and negatives. The original species description of V. coralliilyticus used Biolog Gen II 
Microplates with an adjustment of the inoculation fluid to 3% NaCl [84], which may work as a 
substitute for the 2% sea salt, in future use of Biolog Gen III Microplates for screening of selective 
growth conditions for S2052 and S2753. 
Thus, further explorative work would have to be done in order to find a plating option selective for 
S2052 in co-culture, the necessity of which can be discussed as qPCR proved both very 
manageable and accurate (see comparison with CFU in section 3.7.1) for this experimental setup. 
Plating of course has the added advantage of monitoring contamination, not provided by qPCR. 
However, this would not require selective plating, on the contrary, plating for contamination 
makes the most sense on non-selective plates.  
==PAGE:43==
 
43 of 63 
Furthermore, as the qPCR method is less time-intensive during co-culturing, using only qPCR for 
quantifying would allow for more sample points to be taken and so more precise measures on 
growth and antibiotic production could be made, enabling a better understanding of antibiotic 
mediated interaction between S2052 and S2753 in co-culture. While CFU’s may provide a more 
accurate cell count, better pression is achieved with the qPCR and as the aim is to determine 
relative differences in antibiotic production, having more precision vs. accuracy is favorable, as 
accuracy in cell numbers can be obtained by conversion if necessary.  
 
4.1.3. MIC assays 
The assays exploring the minimal inhibitory concentration of sterile filtered supernatant from 
cultures of S2052, S2052∆AdmO, S2753 and S2753∆hlmE provided a somewhat unexpected result, 
as growth of S2052 and S2753 appears not be strongly influenced by the antibiotics produced by 
the opposite strain. This conclusion was drawn based on the similar OD measurements made for 
growth of S2052 and S2753 in both WT and mutant supernatant of the opposite strain. While not 
statistically validated, S2753 appears to have a higher cell density in the presence of andrimid – 
when growth is possible – compared to growth in supernatant without andrimid in the MIC assay. 
The explanation of which would be interesting to explore further.  
An explanation for the similar growth patterns in the MIC assay could be that the supernatant is 
devoid of an essential nutrient required for growth. This seems less likely as S2052 and S2753 are 
often cultured for longer periods of time, however, cell densities do not increase after approx. 8-
12 hours, so this cannot be ruled out. Another reason for this growth pattern could be that both 
strains produce additional antibacterial compounds and this theory was briefly explored by 
challenging the fish pathogen V. anguillarum in a combined well diffusion and spot assay. Results 
suggested that supernatant of S2052∆AdmO did not contain inhibitory compounds, whereas 
supernatant from S2052, S2753 and S2753∆hlmE clearly inhibited growth of V. anguillarum, and it 
was not possible to distinguish between supernatant of S2753 and S2753∆hlmE. Comparing 
inhibition zones of spotted cultures of S2753 and S2753∆hlmE showed that S2753∆hlmE still has 
the capability of inhibiting V. anguillarum. However, the zones are visibly different, as the zone 
around S2753∆hlmE is blurry at the edge, while zones surrounding S2753 have clear edges. As the 
genome of S2753 contains several BGCs, a likely explanation is that one or more compound 
biosynthesized by S2753 have antibiotic properties. Investigating this in further is outside the 
scope of this project but getting a more detailed insight into additional bioactive compounds in 
S2753 should be subject to further investigation, as also mentioned later in the discussion (section 
4.3).  
 
4.1.4. Optimizing stability while keeping ability to do chemical analysis 
In terms of sustaining growth of both S2052 and S2753 in co-culture, the most stabile co-culture 
was CC3 which was initiated by inoculation of S2052 and S2753 in a ratio of 10:1. CC3 did however 
not fulfill all qualifications for a successful co-culture as holomycin was not detectable. As the aim 
of the thesis was to evaluate the effect of co-culturing S2052 and S2753 on antibiotic mediated 
interaction, detecting holomycin is essential. Inoculation ratios of 5:1 and 7,5:1 did show an effect 
of co-culturing on antibiotic production (discussed in detail in subsection 4.3), however 
sustainable growth of S2052 was not achieved. Continuing to keep inoculation ratio as the primary 
==PAGE:44==
 
44 of 63 
variable, the stability of growth in the co-culture setup could be experimentally tested using 
inoculation ratios of 8:1 or 9:1. 
Other options that may provide stabilization at different cell densities could be looking into other 
variables such as a change in growth media. Bacteria do not always live in nutrient dense 
environments and so changing media concentration for instance by diluting complex media may 
impact the stability of a co-culture system and antibiotic production [62,91]. However, low 
amounts of nutrients could lead to low cell abundance, which would be problematic for the 
detection of holomycin using the chemical analysis protocol used in this thesis work.  
Additionally, chitin media has already proven to induce production of andrimid and holomycin in 
S2052 and S2753, respectively, and in combination with co-culturing this may provide an 
environment that is more like the bacteria’s natural ecological niche [52,92]. Thus, introducing 
chitin as the carbon source may help to induce holomycin in a co-culture – which would improve 
conditions for chemical analysis via HPLC-UV-MS and MALDI-TOF. However, inducing holomycin by 
growth on chitin may in turn destabilize the co-culture and so new optimization e.g. of inoculation 
ratios may be needed for the liquid co-cultures.  
Aside from altering growth, a way of optimizing the liquid co-culture could be to optimize the 
extraction protocol. After not detecting holomycin in CC3, the extraction protocol was changed for 
CC4 and CC5, to increase the changes of detection, by resuspending in a lower volume of 
methanol. Whether this would be enough to detect holomycin at inoculation ratios 10:1 of S2052 
to S2753 would have to be experimentally tested. An additional option for optimization of 
chemical analysis would be to make extractions from a larger volume. This solution also presents a 
scale-up challenge if a setup were to include more sample points in order obtain more information 
on the interaction throughout the duration of the experiment.  
Thus, there are many ways to optimize a stable co-culture setup, some of which may also add 
another dimension of information on behavior of S2052 and S2753 in co-culture and their 
antibiotic mediated interaction in a setting closer to their natural environment.   
 
4.2. Chemical detection and analysis challenges 
4.2.1. Normalization 
A big challenge in determining the effect of co-culturing on antibiotic production has been to 
choose a method to normalize for the cell abundance. Going through some of the literature on 
induction of secondary metabolism, it has been difficult to determine the exact normalization 
procedures used – if any. The articles of Cho et al. and Giubergia et al. have no mentioning of 
normalization [52,71]. Ariana et al. also did not describe a normalization procedure for induction 
of nisin when co-culturing the producing strain Lactococcus lactis sub sp. lactis with Yarrowia 
lipolytica [93]. While not explicitly stated, this may be because the induction per cell is not the aim 
of the study, but induction of total nisin produced is the aim. Such results would definitely be 
useful for industrial production purposes, but for basic research on antibiotic mediated 
interaction, normalization against cell number is needed. Two articles that mention normalization 
was published by Seyedsayamdost and Mao et al. who both normalizes their induction of 
secondary metabolites to OD 600, but it is not specified at which point in culture the growth phase 
[60,94]. Thus, one would assume the normalization in based on samples taken at the same time 
==PAGE:45==
 
45 of 63 
point used for quantification of secondary metabolism. However, in order to compare relative 
abundances of antibiotics in co- and monoculture one has to account for the number of cells in 
each culture responsible for production.  
When S2052 is grown in glucose/casamino acid media in monoculture, andrimid production peaks 
at about 10h and is no longer produced after 12h [92]. Comparing this with the relative abundance 
of andrimid detected in co- and monocultures at 8h and 24h in this study (section 3.7.6.) the 
pattern of production is likely somewhat similar in APY media. As the cell density of S2052 
decreases a 1000-fold in the co-cultures between 8h and 24h and using the same normalization 
for all co-cultures is preferred, normalization using the highest cell density, normally at 8h, should 
be the most accurate.  
Little is known about when production of holomycin occurs in S2753. Based on the results from 
this study, there is an increase in relative abundance between 8h and 24h in both co- and 
monoculture, but no confirmation of when production begins nor the rate of production. 
However, S2753 starts to aggregate between 8h and 24h, which could lower the cell density in 
suspension and in turn give lower OD-values from the qPCR quantification leading to a false 
indication of a lower cell number. To get the most conservative results and to unify the 
normalization strategy within and between strains, normalization was done by dividing the 
relative abundance of antibiotics with the cell density at 8h for both strains respectively.  
 
4.2.2. MALDI-TOF-IMS 
Using MALDI-TOF for small molecules is still a relatively novel practice. Inspiration for the MALDI-
TOF-IMS setup came from Yang et al. [78]. Further studies employing MALDI-TOF-IMS that relate 
to this thesis is Traxler and Shanta, in which they successfully visualized small compounds, 
including single amino acids and drugs with a low m/z ratio (Shanta) as well as secondary 
metabolites from Streptomyces coelicolor (Traxler)[69,79].  
As neither andrimid nor holomycin was detectable, the next step is to explore why this is the case 
and how to proceed. First of all, andrimid production of S2052 grown on solid APY has not been 
proven. However, as andrimid production occurs in several different media types including liquid 
APY, liquid chitin and on solid Marine Agar, there is likely a different explanation for andrimid not 
being detected using MALDI-TOF-IMS in this study. As no signal was visible at the m/z ratios of any 
andrimid adduct, there is no indication of matrix interference, which is otherwise a common issue 
with small molecules. Thus, looking into other factors in protocol before MALDI-TOF-IMS such as 
length of culturing before chemical analysis, age of media etc. may provide the answer. 
The results from using MALDI-TOF-IMS on colonies of S2753 and S2753∆ strongly indicated the 
matrix interferes with the signal obtained by the mass spectrometer during holomycin detection. 
Comparing these results with those of Yang and Traxler, compound sizes and thus m/z ratios are 
larger in both studies, in the range of 659 – 743 for Traxler and 515 – 1545 for Yang [69,78]. This 
may be why use of DHB and Universal matrixed is successful in these studies [5,78], but unsuitable 
for detection of holomycin. 
Turning to the study by Shanta, compound sizes and m/z ratios are even lower, all within the 
range of 89-447, which is similar to those of holomycin and andrimid [79]. This study aimed at 
optimizing matrixes for small molecules and in doing so came up with the combination of 3-
==PAGE:46==
 
46 of 63 
hydroxycoumarin (3-HC) and 6-aza-2-thiothymine (ATT), which resulted in identification of four 
single amino acids and the drugs gefitinib and donepezil [79]. Applying this combination of 
matrices may be an option for detection of both holomycin and andrimid in the same plated 
experiment, as they are both small amino acid-based molecules (structure in introduction).  
 
4.3. The effect of co-culturing on antibiotic production – competition sensing 
Cornforth and Foster published in 2013 a theory about competition sensing, in which they propose 
that bacteria initiates stress responses when they sense ecological competition [61]. Ecological 
competition is a combination of exploitative competition e.g. nutrient limitation due to the 
presence of a competitor and interference competition, in which an individual decreases survival 
or reproduction e.g. by production of antibiotics. This theory provides a foundation for discussing 
the effect of exploitative competition and interference competition – exemplified in this study by 
antibiotic mediated interaction – in co-culturing.  
Using this theory, the increase of holomycin production by S2753 as result from andrimid 
production by S2052 in co-culture could be a defense against interference competition of S2052. 
The fact that induction of holomycin appears lower in no-andrimid co-cultures, but still not 
comparable to monoculture, may be due to the still present exploitative competition just by being 
in co-culture. The production of additional compounds by S2052 could also be an additional factor 
sensed by S2753. It may even be that S2052 senses competition in form of holomycin, another 
compound and/or co-culturing and as a response the production of another secondary metabolite 
is induced, which in turn can be sensed by S2753. The loop of sensing and inducting secondary 
metabolism could in theory involve inducing biosynthesis of all BGCs harbored in the genomes of 
S2052 and S2753. This may not be the case, but if induction of defenses does stem from sensing 
general stress, the possibility of inducing any of the orphan or silent BGCs in S2052 and S2753 is 
present. If any of these BGCs are being activated during co-culturing, the information may already 
be in the HPLC-UV-MS data generated for this thesis. UV data from the co-culture showed a peak 
not detectable in S2052 and S2753 monocultures, suggesting not only a change not only 
abundance of known metabolites but also in metabolite diversity when in co-culture (In 
appendix/data not shown). Investigating the changes in metabolite profiles in detail is outside the 
scope of this thesis, but future research may aim at deciphering the information hidden in the 
HPLC-UV-MS data. This could provide new knowledge about the ecological role of antibiotic 
mediated interactions and furthermore lead to the discovery of new bioactive compounds.  
Taking a step back to reflect on next steps that would still be within the scope of this project, 
results from CC3, CC4 and CC5 provide a strong indication of co-culturing with S2753 having an 
inducing effect on the production of andrimid in S2052. Further co-culture experiments including a 
holomycin deficient mutant could clarify how much of this is induction is due to holomycin and 
thus the antibiotic mediated interactions and what may simply be the effect of co-culturing. 
 
It is not possible to provide strong evidence of the influence of co-culturing on the production of 
holomycin in S2753 based on the results from this is study. The differences in abundance apparent 
from CC4 is not backed up by statistically significant differences in holomycin abundance by any of 
the other co-culture setups. However, results from CC4 and CC5 suggest that there may be an 
==PAGE:47==
 
47 of 63 
inducing effect of andrimid and co-culturing in general on holomycin production in S2753. 
Therefore, additional replications are needed to determine if there is a statistically significant 
induction of holomycin production in S2753 when co-culturing and whether this increase is due to 
andrimid or the effect of co-culturing. 
 
 
 
5. Conclusion 
A co-culture setup of V. coralliilyticus S2052 and P. galatheae S2753 in which antibiotic mediated 
interactions can be measured was achieved. At the moment, the complete growth stability of both 
strains the co-culture systems comes at the price of holomycin detection. Co-culturing V. 
coralliilyticus S2052 and P. galatheae S2753 has a clear effect on andrimid production in V. 
coralliilyticus S2052. While not definitively proven, results from this study indicate that both the 
presence of andrimid and additional factors in co-culturing with V. coralliilyticus S2052 induces the 
production of holomycin in P. galatheae S2753. Furthermore, these results suggest that co-
culturing can be a way of inducing secondary metabolites in marine Vibrionaceae, by allowing for 
microbial interaction, thus showing that co-culturing is a way of facilitating biodiscovery. On a final 
note, this thesis has shown that there are studies on co-cultures inducing secondary metabolism 
and antibiotics inducing secondary metabolism. However, to the extent of the literature study 
conducted for this thesis, there are no previous papers directly describing antibiotic mediated 
interactions in a bacterial co-culture system. Thus, further exploring this area of research is not 
only needed, but very likely to provide novel insights into the magnificent world of bacterial 
interactions and the natural roles of antibiotics.

APPENDIX B: HPLC-UV-MS: holomycin and andrimid detection 
The detection of andrimid and holomycin, produced by S2052 and S2753 respectively, was 
measured using HPLC-UV-MS. The detection procedure is here exemplified by monocultures of 
S2052 and S2753 from CC2. Detection of each compound by mass spectrometry was done by 
extracting ion chromatograms for the m/z of the most abundant adduct of each antibiotic (figure 
18 and 19 top). This resulted in peaks at retention time approx. 6,45 for andrimid and 2,95 for 
holomycin. Verification of each compound was done by comparison of the m/z ratios of the 
adduct pattern (figure 18 and 19 bottom) to calculated m/z ratios (table 5 in section 2.7.2). For the 
peak on the EIC at retention time 2,95 the initial MS spectrum does not show an ion with an m/z 
ratio corresponding to holomycin. Zooming in on the m/z ratio 214,9943 shows an adduct pattern 
corresponding to holomycin.
