Abstract
This thesis presents the design, implementation, and evaluation of an Large Language
Model (LLM)-based system for retrieving and calculating sustainability information in
Alfa Laval’s decanter technology. The project aims to address fragmented access to
technical data and the growing complexity of corporate sustainability reporting, through
a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline.
The proposed system, ChatCO2 Assistant, has three functionalities: (i) Sustainability
Insights, enabling natural language queries over company sustainability documentation;
(ii) Decanter Calculations, supporting formula-based metrics such as CO2 emissions,
energy consumption, electricity cost, and return on investment; and (iii) Data Source
Search, allowing direct retrieval of relevant files from a centralized dataset originally
stored in the BUD SharePoint.
The evaluation of the ChatCO2 Assistant was structured around its three main phases.
First, Sustainability Insights was tested by measuring classification speed and accuracy,
followed by a human-rated assessment of RAG-generated responses to both theoretical
and calculation-oriented questions. Second, Decanter Calculations were evaluated through
two complementary tasks: (i) classification of user queries into predefined formula types
and (ii) extraction of the required parameters, with emphasis on both execution time and
the precision of retrieved or inferred values. Finally, Data Source Search was assessed
through cutoff threshold experiments and retrieval accuracy tests.
Overall, the results show that the assistant is feasible and effective. In Sustainability
Insights, responses were generated in under 10 seconds and achieved an average rating of
4.3 out of 5. In Decanter Calculations, query classification reached 89% accuracy with an
average time of 3.5 seconds, while parameter extraction proved more challenging, with
exact-match accuracy limited to 18–22%. Finally, in Data Source Search, the system
retrieved and displayed the correct sources within 0.04–0.13 seconds.

1. Introduction
What is the CO2 footprint of an Alfa Laval Aldec G3 centrifuge operating in Germany
in 2023? Today, finding the answer at Alfa Laval requires manually navigating through
technical specifications, emissions databases, lifecycle assessment reports, and regional
emission factor documents usually scattered across various formats and departments. In
addition, it requires workforce expertise, as someone must be familiar with this type of
data and understand where and how the relevant information can be accessed.
To address this issue, this project proposes the development of a Retrieval-Augmented
Generation (RAG) model trained specifically on internal documentation. The RAG
model enables employees to ask natural language questions and receive precise answers
based on verified company sources.
Moreover, the proposed solution includes interactive calculation tools integrated into
the interface. When a user submits a query, the model retrieves the relevant information
and displays an intuitive interface where the user can adjust parameters as needed to
perform specific calculations for a given machine.
In addition to the interactive calculators, the solution also includes a third semantic
search model designed specifically for document search. This feature allows users to input
natural language queries and receive direct links to relevant internal sources. Rather
than manually searching through folders or files, employees can quickly access the exact
document or section they need by describing what they are looking for in natural language.
All three components rely on a centralized, structured database built from trusted
internal sources. Furthermore, to ensure long-term usability, it is integrated into a
dedicated BUD Sustainability SharePoint page, which hosts documentation and calculators

in an organized, easily accessible folder structure.
Figure 1.1 illustrates the proposed solution which guides users through a data-driven
pipeline.
To start, users begin by selecting one of three core objectives: to obtain
sustainability insights, to locate specific internal data sources, or to perform a decanter-
related calculation.
In the first case, Sustainability Insights, when the user selects an option to retrieve a
sustainability insight, the system first determines whether the query refers to specific
numerical data or a conceptual inquiry. Depending on the outcome, the system uses
different sources, an API call and a prompt.
If the user opts to search for a specific data source„ Data Sources Search, the system
provides an interface to identify and return the sources most relevant to the user’s request.
Finally, for Decanter Calculations, if the user chooses to perform a decanter-related
calculation, the system classifies the task into one of four categories: sustainability metrics
(specifically CO2 emissions for the Scope 3DS Use Phase), power consumption, return
on investment or cost of electricity. The system then extracts the specified parameters
from the natural language and presents them in an interface designed for that specific
calculation.

1.1. BACKGROUND MOTIVATION
1.1. Background Motivation
As sustainability becomes more and more important to industrial operations, the ability
to retrieve and apply environmental data is essential. This section outlines the current
challenges Alfa Laval faces in obtaining sustainability information, and explains the main
background motivation behind this project.
1.1.1. Importance of sustainability in industrial processes
Integrating sustainability into business strategy not only helps mitigate environmental
risks but also offers a competitive edge. Research by McKinsey & Company shows that
organizations with strong environmental, social, and governance (ESG) practices can
increase operating profits by up to 60% [3].
European Union Initiatives
In addition to the continuous need for more environmentally friendly measures, this
shift is also driven by a series of legislative initiatives, mainly at the EU level, aimed at
promoting sustainable industrial operations. Three key directives and regulations are
shaping the near future:
The first is the European Green Deal, which sets out a roadmap to make the EU
climate-neutral by 2050. A key part of this plan is the Industrial Emissions Directive
(IED), which aims to reduce pollution from industrial activities. It requires companies
to use the Best Available Techniques (BAT) to cut down emissions and improve their
overall environmental performance [4].
In addition to the IED, the Net-Zero Industry Act aims that by 2030, at least 40% of
the EU’s annual demand for key net-zero technologies will be supplied through domestic
manufacturing rather than external imports [5].
Another significant regulation is the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive
(CSRD), which standardizes sustainability reporting across the EU. This directive requires
companies to disclose information on their environmental and social impacts, promoting

1.1.2. Role of Large Language Models (LLMs) in Sustainability Efforts
Given this context, Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as powerful tools in
the AI ecosystem, however, its growing scale has raised questions regarding environmental
sustainability. On the one hand, training and serving LLMs require vast computational
resources, which translate into immense environmental costs:
• Carbon emissions (training & inference): Training GPT-3 (~175 billion
parameters) consumed approximately 1287 MWh of electricity and emitted roughly
552 metric tons of CO2, equivalent to the annual emissions of about 110–120
gasoline-powered cars [7]. On a user-facing level, a single prompt to a GPT-3-based
service results in approximately 2.2 g CO2e per query, made up of ~1.84 g from
training (assuming monthly retraining and 10 million queries per day) and ~0.38g

• Energy usage: While training is a large one-time cost, live operation often

2.1. Architecture of RAG Systems
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) integrates two core components: an information
retriever and a sequence-to-sequence generator forming a hybrid system that produces
capable of producing high-factuality responses. Originally introduced by Lewis et al. [37],
the RAG architecture allows an LLM to condition its outputs on dynamically retrieved
chunks from an external database as it identifies the top-K most relevant documents to
a query, and these documents are concatenated alongside the query as context for the
generator. Figure 2.1 shows an schema of this architecture.
Figure 2.1.: High-level architecture of a RAG system
This structure offers several benefits over traditional language models. First, the
19
==PAGE:46==

external memory is non-parametric, meaning it can be updated without re-training
the model. Second, the retrieved documents can serve as direct evidence, enabling
explainability through citations [37], [38].
The term "RAG" was formally introduced by Lewis et al. establishing a framework
where retriever and generator are jointly optimized for open-domain question answer-
ing [37], [39]. This approach marked a shift from static knowledge in LLMs to dynamic
retrieval from external corpora, enabling up-to-date and domain-specific information
incorporation [40].
2.1.1. Open-Source Embedding Models
Modern RAG systems rely on dense vector representations of text to perform semantic re-
trieval. Popular choices include Sentence-BERT [41](including all-MiniLM-L6-v2 [42]),E5 [43],
and BGE models [44].
Furthermore, domain-specific embeddings further improve retrieval accuracy. Sev-
eral studies show that fine-tuning open-source models on project-specific corpora can
outperform off-the-shelf models [39].
For the development of this project, two open-source embedding models were evaluated
extensively: bge-large-en-v1.5 [45] and bge-small-en-v1.5 [46], both developed by
the Beijing Academy of Artificial Intelligence (BAAI). These models are part of the BGE
family.
The smaller model, bge-small-en-v1.5, generates 384-dimensional embeddings with
a maximum token length of 512, making it suitable for low-resource environments. It
offers strong retrieval effectiveness relative to its compact model size (approximately 133
MB) .
On the other hand, bge-large-en-v1.5 produces higher-dimensional representations
(1024 dimensions) but comes with greater memory and computational requirements
(approximately 1.34 GB) .
While the code and repository for this project can be used with any locally saved
embedding model, empirical evaluation showed that bge-small-en-v1.5 offered the best

trade-off between accuracy, speed, and infrastructure constraints, and bge-large-en-v1.5
provided improved retrieval precision at higher cost. These considerations are discussed

2.1.2. Making more efficient RAG Systems
Efficiency in RAG systems can be assessed at multiple levels: retrieval relevance, latency,
and environmental impact. As retrieval cost is typically the dominant factor in real-time
systems, optimizing this layer is critical for both performance and sustainability.
Common strategies to improve retrieval quality include multi-stage pipelines and
smarter document chunking to increase semantic relevance while minimizing redundant
context. While rerankers were considered, the system focused instead on optimizing the
retriever stage directly by tuning the FAISS indexing structure and embedding model
selection (see Section 8).
For this project, the FAISS index was configured to enable maximum precision with
low query latency. Chunk embeddings were computed using both bge-small-en-v1.5
and bge-large-en-v1.5, depending on the retrieval mode and multiple indexes were
generated across documents, questions, and keyword tables.
In practice, the total number of indexed chunks across all document sources reached
approximately 26,615 vectors, composed of 2,075 question chunks, 8,775 keyword-related
chunks, and 15,765 document chunks (in the case of the smaller chunking of a size of
4,000 tokens). Despite this relatively large index size, end-to-end vector search using
FAISS averaged between 5 and 8 seconds per query, including embedding the input,
retrieving top 5 results across all sources, and aggregating them for LLM generation.
Finally, environmental efficiency was also considered, particularly in minimizing the
number of unnecessary calls to the OpenAI API. By retrieving high-quality chunks from
FAISS with tuned thresholds and similarity cutoffs, the number of context tokens fed
into the LLM was also reduced.

2.1.3. What is a FAISS Index?
FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search) is a library developed by Meta AI Research for
efficient similarity search. It is widely used in natural language processing and information
retrieval tasks where the goal is to find semantically similar items by comparing their
vector representations. FAISS is optimized for large-scale use cases and offers both CPU
and GPU support, making it an ideal tool for scalable RAG systems.
A FAISS index stores the embeddings of each item in a highly optimized structure
that enables fast nearest neighbor search. When a user submits a query, the system first
embeds that query using the same model as was used to index the data. It then searches
the FAISS index to identify the closest vectors, using similarity metric distances.
In this project, the Euclidean (L2) distance was used and the faiss.IndexFlatL2
index, which performs exact (brute-force) search with efficient, low-level implementations.
Given a query vector q ∈Rd and a database vector x ∈Rd, the (squared) L2 distance
is ∥q −x∥2

i=1(qi −xi)2. The IndexFlatL2 index computes these distances for all
stored vectors, returning the k smallest values.
Furthermore, FAISS indexes require no training, only the embedding dimension d, and
results are deterministic and reproducible.
Why are FAISS Indexes so efficient?
FAISS is designed specifically for large-scale vector similarity search and therefore achieves
higher efficiency compared to general-purpose vector databases such as Chroma or other
storage solutions. Some of the key factors that led to the development of this project
with FAISS are the following:
• Low-level optimization: FAISS is implemented in optimized C++ with Python
bindings, allowing it to use multi-threading and GPU acceleration. In contrast,
Chroma is primarily Python-based and relies on external backends that are not
optimized for low-level distance computations. This was also demonstrated during
the development of this project, where initial tests using Chroma resulted in signifi-

2.2.2. Chunking Strategies
Regarding the chunking strategy, related work was also reviewed to ensure the technique
was efficient for the task at hand. Bhat et al. (2025) evaluated fixed-size chunking across
multiple datasets and embedding models, finding that smaller chunks work best for fact-
based tasks, whereas larger chunks better support broader contextual understanding [51].
Although not directly applied in this project, it is worth highlighting the work of Zhao
et al. (2024), who introduced Meta-Chunking[52]. This approach proposes a hybrid
technique that aims to balance semantic coherence with computational efficiency by
grouping sentences into logically connected clusters, referred to as “meta-chunks”. Instead
of relying solely on fixed-size windows or similarity-based segmentation, the method
detects natural boundaries in text using perplexity patterns1 and then dynamically
merges adjacent segments to form coherent units.

2.3. Challenges in Sustainability-Specific RAG Systems
Following Section 1.3.3, using RAGs in sustainability settings faces several challenges:

5.4. Step 4: Creating the Document Chunks Table
Once the full text of each document was stored in the database, the next step involved
splitting these texts into smaller segments, known as chunks.
Documents with a CalculationFile value of 0 or 2, representing descriptive or mixed
content, were processed in batches, as these files were intended to support theoretical
45
==PAGE:72==
CHAPTER 5. DATA PREPROCESSING
queries. However, documents assigned a value of 1 or 2 contained numerical or structured
data intended for "calculation" queries. These were converted into JSON format, allowing
them to be processed line by line in discrete chunks.
For the ones directed for "theoretical" queries, the script applied the Recursive
CharacterTextSplitter [58] from LangChain to split the text fields into overlapping
chunks.
RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter is a utility that breaks down large bodies of text
into smaller, semantically coherent segments while attempting to preserve meaning and
context. Unlike basic splitters that cut at fixed intervals, this splitter follows a hierarchical
approach: it first attempts to split the text at preferred boundary characters such as
paragraphs (like \n\n), then sentences (such as .), and finally at more granular levels if
needed (like spaces or individual characters).
This recursive strategy ensures that splits occur at natural language boundaries
whenever possible, reducing the risk of fragmenting critical context.
For example, consider the following excerpt from a sustainability report:
The EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) is the cornerstone of the European
Union’s drive to reduce its emissions of man-made greenhouse gases... The system
works by putting a limit on overall emissions from covered installations which is
reduced each year. Within this limit, companies can buy and sell emission allowances
as needed. This ‘cap-and-trade’ approach gives companies the flexibility they need to
cut their emissions in the most cost-effective way. The EU ETS covers approximately
11,000 power stations and manufacturing plants in the 28 EU Member States plus
Iceland, Liechtenstein and Norway...
When applying the RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter with a chunk size of 400 char-
acters and an overlap of 100, the text first splits after the description of the “cap-and-trade”
mechanism. The second chunk begins with overlapping context from the end of the first
("companies the flexibility they need...") and continues into the next paragraph.
In the case of the code, each chunk was assigned a unique Chunk_ID, such as 4_1_5
for the first chunk out of five from document ID 4.

6.1.2. Step 2 - Embedding Generation
Once the question is classified, it is transformed into a dense vector representation. The
system allows the user to select the embedding model at runtime, which determines both
the dimensionality of the vectors and the computational cost of generating them.
The chosen base (large or small) is passed as a configuration parameter. Based on
this input, the system automatically loads the correct model and paths adjusting the
embedding dimensionality to match the corresponding FAISS index. This ensures that
the generated query embedding is always compatible with the index structure used for
semantic similarity search.
6.1.3. Step 3: Semantic Retrieval
If the query is classified in Step 1 as a Theoretical question, the system performs semantic
retrieval across three distinct information sources: document chunks, Q&A pairs, and ex-
tracted keywords. This is executed through the function search_all_theory_sources().
If instead the query is classified as a Calculation request, search_calculation_sources()
is called to search only over the FAISS index dedicated to sustainability-calculation queries.

Each retrieved match is a tuple of the form:
(source_type, metadata_id, similarity_score)
Each index is searched using:
D, I = index.search(query_emb, top_k)
Here, D contains the distance values returned by FAISS, and I contains the integer
IDs of the closest vectors in the index. For this project the indexes were built with
IndexFlatL2, which means that D represents squared Euclidean (L2) distances between
the query embedding and the indexed vectors. A lower distance indicates a closer semantic
match.
These IDs are then used to fetch metadata from the corresponding id_map dictionary.
The function then aggregates results from all three sources and returns a list sorted by
similarity:
[("chunk", "chunk_42", 0.87),
("question", "q_15", 0.85),
("keyword", "k_3", 0.80), ...]
In the case of a Calculation query, the function retrieves the most relevant rows from
the dedicated calculation FAISS index and returns a list sorted by similarity:
[("row12", "C-42", 0.87),
("row11", "C-45", 0.85),
("row22", "C-42", 0.80), ...]
To avoid redundancy, for the Theoretical query, the function filters out duplicates
based on the combination of (source_type, metadata_id). This is done using Python’s
set function.

8.3.1. Cutoff Test
The first stage was to determine the cosine-similarity cutoff that best separates valid
matches from random noise. To do this the steps followed were:
1. Load the 500 test queries from words.txt.
2. For each query, generate an embedding with bge-large-en and bge-small-en-v1.5.
The FAISS index is rebuilt so that all vectors are unit-normalized and stored in an
inner-product index, meaning that search results are scored by cosine similarity.
98
==PAGE:125==
8.3. OPTION 3 – DATA SOURCES SEARCH
3. Use each embedding to query the FAISS index, retrieve the closest entry, and record
its top-1 cosine similarity score together with its expected label.
4. Sweep across all unique cosine scores as candidate cutoff thresholds between 0 and
1. For each cutoff, classify queries as “matched” if the score is at least the threshold
and “no match” otherwise, then compare these predictions with the ground truth
to compute overall accuracy.
5. Select, for each model the cutoff value.
This approach gives a data-driven threshold instead of guessing one, ensuring the
decision boundary matches the actual similarity distribution of the dataset. These are
represented in table 8.7.

9.1. Key Insights from the Results
The project delivers an internal, local ChatCO2 Assistant that shortens the path from

9.2. Limitations and Challenges
Throughout the project there are some limitations and challenges explained that are
further discussed in this section.
9.2.1. Link Change
During the final stages of the project, feedback from Hans Christian Schubell proposed
that instead of embedding full SharePoint URLs or manually written titles in the output,
the system should reference each document by its unique Document_ID. This would make
the results more stable and maintainable over time, since SharePoint links are prone
to break when documents are renamed, relocated, or access settings change. Although
the system already included a step to improve link readability by resolving titles (see
Section 6.1.6), the underlying URLs were still embedded directly.
This improvement could have been implemented by treating the links_to_files.xlsx
file as the central source. Each document’s Document_ID, URL, cleaned title (“Cleaned-
Name”), and metadata were already present in this file. At indexing time, the system
could store only the Document_ID in FAISS. Then, at render time, the output component
105
==PAGE:132==
CHAPTER 9. DISCUSSION & FUTURE WORK
would show the corresponding title and URL using this spreadsheet as a lookup table.
However, because this suggestion came only days before the submission deadline, there
was not enough time to update the indexing and rendering logic. It remains a low-effort
but high-impact improvement for future versions of the tool.
9.2.2. Change to the top N
In the proposed solution, the retrieval step returns the Top-5 most relevant chunks by
default, as defined in the configuration file (top_k_theoretical and top_k_calculation
parameters). This fixed limit works well for balancing accuracy and timing with the
current models, but the optimal number of retrieved chunks can vary depending on
factors such as embedding model size, corpus diversity, and question complexity.
A practical approach for future work would be to change N incrementally: begin with
the current value (5), increase to 6–8, and evaluate gains in retrieval recall and answer
completeness while monitoring time.
9.2.3. Adding a Follow-up Question (Theoretical Q&A)
A useful future improvement would be to support follow-up questions in the chat and
retrieval pipeline. In practice, many real user queries are initially underspecified or vague,
and the system’s first answer may not fully match the user’s actual intent.
This functionality could be implemented by adding a follow-up script that activates
when the user clicks a “Follow up” option. At that point, the API call would be modified to
include the previous question, the system’s answer, and any user feedback or clarification.
The system would then retrieve additional chunks as needed and generate a refined
response that complements the original one based on the new context.
To implement this behavior in code, a follow-up system could be integrated as a
conditional step within the existing pipeline that, after generating an initial response and
retrieving supporting chunks, the system would wait for the user to trigger a follow-up
query (by clicking a "Follow Up" button in the frontend).

9.2. LIMITATIONS AND CHALLENGES
The core change would involve appending these fields to the existing LLM prompt,
enabling the model to refine or expand the prior response.
9.2.4. Improving parameter extraction
The parameter extraction step is a key bottleneck in the current pipeline. Some of the
possible solutions for this might be:
• Confidence thresholds: Add a minimum cosine similarity threshold before
accepting a retrieved value, avoiding incorrect assumptions or hallucinations.
• Source-aware prompting: When presenting values retrieved from documents,
include source file names or document titles to improve trust and traceability.
• Source Review: The current code relies on fragmented and inconsistently struc-
tured information, which hinders reliable parameter extraction. A systematic review
of source documents is needed to ensure consistency and improve retrieval precision.
9.2.5. UI & UX Improvement
While the calculators and search views are functional, several UX gaps remain that can
slow down non-expert users or increase input errors.
To improve the user experience and reduce input errors, the interface should include
clearer guidance and built-in validation mechanisms. Inline tooltips can provide definitions,
units, and typical value ranges for each parameter, along with context-sensitive hints for
values that vary by country or year (such as emission factors). In addition, automatic unit
conversion, like, for example, between kW and W, would help ensure that the calculations
are based on consistent inputs.
9.2.6. Exploring Alternative Embedding Models
The current implementation uses two locally stored embedding models, bge-large-en
and bge-small-en-v1.5, due to Alfa Laval’s internal security restrictions, which prevent
direct access to external model repositories like importing from Hugging Face [62] .

Running these models on a larger machine would remove memory limitations and

RQ1: To what extent is an LLM-based system feasible and capable

• RQ2: What is the average response time of the system when executing
sustainability-related queries?
For routing, the binary classifier returned

(Figure 8.2). For full Theoretical RAG answers, the fastest configuration was the
Small–5000 setup with a mean of 5.90 s, while the slowest was Large–5000 at 6.75 s;

CHAPTER 10. CONCLUSION
• RQ3: How accurately can the system assist in performing numerical
calculations relevant to decanter sustainability metrics?
The system assists with numerical calculations in two stages: (i) it routes requests
to the correct calculator with 89% overall accuracy across seven labels (Table 8.4),
at a median classification time of 1.58 s (Figure 8.4); (ii) it extracts the required
inputs, which is the main limitation. Exact-match accuracy at the query level
was 18.3% for parameters explicitly present in the question and 21.7% when
allowing retrieved/inferred fields. Average extraction time was 3.5 s. In practice,
the calculators are accurate and fast when key inputs are provided or can be reliably
retrieved; otherwise, user confirmation is required to avoid gaps.
• RQ4: Can the integration of database search functionalities reduce the
reliance on the BUD SharePoint Sustainability Page as the sole access

database search meaningfully reduces reliance on the BUD SharePoint Sustainability

source in about 51% of cases (bge-large-en: 51.0%; bge-small-en-v1.5: 51.5%)
when considering what would actually be displayed to users (Table 8.9). Performance
was fast: encode and search completed in 0.037 s (small) to 0.134 s (large) on average,
well within interactive bounds (Table 8.8). In conclusion, the proposed database
search offers quick, direct linking to relevant documents avoiding having to search
through unrelated documents.
In conclusion, the proposed project shows a domain-tuned RAG assistant can make
sustainability knowledge for decanter products faster to find, easier to reuse, and more
transparent through citations. The system demonstrates how AI can actively support
corporate sustainability goals by reducing the time needed to locate and validate key
environmental data. Finally, it also establishes a unified database that not only consoli-
dates existing sustainability information but also serves as a structured foundation for
future reporting.
