SUMMARY / ABSTRACT 
Plastics are polluting the Arctic marine environment, but our understanding of their 
quantities, sources, and mitigation is still limited. The detection of plastic litter in 
remote locations and in a range of aquatic organisms has raised questions about the 
sources of entry into the marine environment. Untreated sewage discharged into the sea 
is thought to be a source of plastic litter in Greenland. The sewers chosen for sampling 
represented the same number of inhabitants. 
  
A method was developed to quantify loads of plastic entering the sea in Nuuk and 
Sisimiut, where plastics of different size fractions were extracted from the wastewater 
using sieves of different pore sizes. All plastic findings were visually and chemically 
characterized, where polymer composition was identified using FTIR spectroscopy and 
visual characterization was performed using different methods according to each size 
fraction. Plastic litter was mainly characterized in terms of abundance, morphology, 
size, and polymer type. 
 
Results showed that wastewater is a potential source of plastic waste in both cities. 
The most abundant polymer type for all size fractions was PET. In terms of 
morphology, macroplastics were mainly wet wipes, and litter with size 300 µm-25 mm 
showed mainly to be fibers. 
 
It can be concluded that wastewater is a potential source of plastic litter in Nuuk and 
Sisimiut. Recommendations on future mitigation plans have been suggested to include 
efforts from both, the Greenlandic society and municipalities.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Greenland’s Geodemographics   
Greenland is a massive island with an area of approx. 2.2 million square kilometers, and 
geologically part of North America, with about 80% of its land surface covered by an ice 
sheet that can reach up to kilometers thick. Politically, it is an autonomous Danish territory, 
and most of its 56,562 people live in communities along the often ice-free, fjord-lined cost 
[1]. The 75 communities of Greenland, consist of 13 communities with a population of more 
than 1,000; 17 communities with a population between 
200 and 1,000; 12 communities with a population 
between 100 and 200; and 21 communities with a 
population of less than 100 [2]. In southwest Greenland, 
where most of the population live, the climate is 
described as low-Arctic. This part of Greenland is 
characterized by relatively mild winters with a lot of 
snow and periods of thaw, in addition to wet summers 
with average temperatures less than 10°C in the 
warmest month.  
 
Nuuk is the capital and the largest community, and 
consequently the government center and the 
headquarters of the crown corporations that operate in 
Greenland. The population of Nuuk is 19,486 [1]. 
Sisimiut, 330 kilometers north of Nuuk, is the second-
largest community, with a population of 6163 [1] 
according to statistics from 2022.

 1.2 Plastic Litter in Greenland  
A constant increase in synthetic plastic production and poor management of plastic waste 
have led to a tremendous increase in dumping into water bodies across the world [3]. As 
much of the Arctic is sparsely inhabited, relatively low local plastic pollution inputs would be 
expected. Yet, there are widespread observations of plastics in the region [4].  
The importance of local Arctic communities as sources of plastic to the ocean despite their 
general small population was underlined in two recent studies, from Nuuk (Greenland) and 
Longyearbyen (Svalbard, Norway).  
By quantifying microplastics in the fjords of Nuuk, and considering its 19,486 inhabitants, it 
was identified as the primary important point source [5]. In Adventfjorden in Svalbard, 
untreated sewage from Longyearbyen with only 2,000 inhabitants was shown to emit 
microplastic fibers at a scale similar to a modern wastewater treatment plant serving 1.3 
million persons [6]. These findings have led to the identification of wastewater in small and 
remote Arctic communities as a primary concern regarding ocean litter contamination [7].  
 
 
1.3 Knowledge Gaps 
Although plastic pollution research in the Arctic regions has started to increase, data on 
plastic pollution in Greenland is still very limited.  
Recent studies have mainly focused on the effects of plastic litter on marine biota [8] and 
sediments [7]. In addition, some studies have highlighted pharmaceuticals and additives in 
personal care products [9] as sources of plastic litter. However, when it comes to the 
wastewater presenting a potential source of plastic litter in Greenland, no studies at all have 
been conducted addressing this issue.  
 
While sewer discharged plastic litter is evident on the coastlines on many Greenlandic 
communities, the amount of plastic has not yet been accurately accounted for or documented. 
Moreover, several studies have reported a wide range of MP concentrations for Arctic 
seawater [5].  This reveals that the estimation of the anthropogenic impact on the Arctic 
ecosystem from plastic pollution is still weak.  
 
In 2019, a relatively new secondary wastewater treatment plant was installed in the small 
settlement of Ålesund in Svalbard, Norway. A report from the Swedish Environmental 
Research Institute [10] investigated the anthropogenic microparticles in both, incoming and 
outcoming wastewater from this treatment plant and reported that the treatment plant retained 
more than 99% of the incoming microplastic particles. However, a study from 2020 [11] 
investigated the same wastewater outlet and suggested that it is still acting as a point source 
of MPs to Kongsfjorden, due to the high concentrations (>10 AMPs L-1) detected in surface 
water [11]. These findings emphasize the importance of local point- and diffuse sources of 
plastic pollution in Arctic coastal environments and the urgency of considering their 
quantification and associated environmental impact.

1.4 Goal and Scope of the Project 
The overall aim of the project is to facilitate the decision-making regarding implementation 
of solutions in small, remote, and Arctic communities to reduce the load of plastic from 
sewage to the sea. Both, a literature review, and an empirical analysis on plastic litter from 
wastewater in two major Greenlandic towns were carried out. Finally, an assessment of future 
potential wastewater treatment systems, behavioral initiatives, and specific trade regulations 
to mitigate plastic pollution were discussed.

 1.5 Research Questions 
Considering the lack of research on plastic litter in the marine environment of the Arctic 
region in general, and in Greenland in particular, it is imperative to address not only the sea-
based sources of plastic pollution, such as the fishing industry, but also the land-based 
sources. To make the assessment relevant for the community of Greenland, data collection 
took place in the two largest cities of the country: Sisimiut and Nuuk. When assessing the 
different types and mass fractions of plastic litter in both cities, it was not only the intent to 
identify the material, size, and number of plastics but also to estimate the mass fractions and 
sources of the litter found. The assessment and discussion of potential initiatives that can 
minimize future discharges of plastic litter and their impacts on the environment can be 
applied not only for those 2 cities, but in other parts of Greenland as well, where the sources 
of plastic litter could be expected to be similar.  
However, since Greenlandic wastewater is not treated at all, but rather discharged directly 
into the marine environment through different collection systems, it is clearly important to 
investigate and quantify the loads of plastic in sewage water, and consequently in the ocean.  
 
 
Research question 1:  
What types, masses, and fractions of marine litter are present in the wastewater of Nuuk and 
Sisimiut? And what are their sources?  
 
Research question 2:  
Given the practical and economic challenges in Greenland, how can the findings from this 
project help municipalities develop strategies to mitigate sewer-discharged plastic pollution 
in Greenland and minimize the impacts of plastic litter on the marine environment?

2. LITERATURE REVIEW 
2.1 Plastics  
The term “plastic” originates from Greek term plastikos, and Latin term plasticus which is 
associated with molding or shaping [13].  
Plastics are synthetic polymers that are typically produced by the polymerization of 
monomers derived from the extraction of oil or gas. Generally, dyes and plasticizers are 
added in the process of producing plastics to give them their specific properties like 
flexibility, durability, and heat resistance [8]. 
The most commonly-used polymers – and most abundant in the environment – are 
polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP), polyester (PES), polyvinyl chloride (PVC), and nylon 
[8]. Different polymer types with their common application are summarized in figure 4. 
 
 
 
 
Figure 4: Showing different polymer types with their abbreviations and common applications [12][16].

Plastics have extensively been used mainly due to their durability, low cost, and widespread 
application [14]. In the 1950’s the large-scale production of plastic materials began with the 
capacity of 1.5-2 million tons per year, and in the past 70 years it has extended to 350 million 
tons per year [13]. On a global level, it was estimated that about 4.8 to 12.7 million metric 
tons of uncontrolled plastic waste, equivalent to 1.7 to 4.6 percent of the total plastic waste 
generated in the countries under investigation, were released into the ocean in 2010 [15].  
 
In today’s world plastic has become one of the most useful materials for the society because 
of those properties that also include its lightweight, durability, inexpensive, strong, corrosion-
resistant and highly versatile nature [13]. However, these properties also give plastics a 
persistent nature in the environment. And since plastics are not bio-degradable, they are not 
only polluting land and rivers but also oceans. Plastics mainly accumulate in the natural 
environment due to their durability and low recycling volumes [16]. According to research 
from 2021, about 80% of plastic production is disposed into the marine environment [13], 
which raises even greater concern with regard to this study in particular.   
 
2.2 Classification of Plastics 
The methods used to sample and quantify plastic litter have developed over several decades. 
Consequently, the terminology used to describe physical attributes has changed, and plastics 
have been categorized using a variety of standards including particle size, shape, polymer 
composition, toxicity, etc. [17]–[19].  
 
But although size in particular is considered an important criterion for the classification of 
plastics[17], [18], there is still no global consensus among scientific communities on how to 
define and categorize plastic debris accordingly [20]. This ambiguity has created 
miscommunication among plastic pollution researchers and academics, in addition to the 
generation of incomparable data. Rubber for example is considered a major component of 
microplastic pollution in most -if not all- environmental science literature [21], [22], while 
the same polymer is not classified as plastic in some polymer chemistry definitions [20], [23].    
 
Therefore, the scientific community needs to establish standardized procedures not only for 
classifying, but also for sampling, identifying, analyzing, and reporting plastics. This will 
enable comparisons between studies and assist researchers in reducing the unpredictability in 
the type and quality of data. It will also enhance higher integration across wider marine 
ecosystems and regions.  
 
GESAMP report from 2019 for the monitoring and assessment of plastic litter in the ocean 
[24] aimed for harmonizing monitoring approaches, and  therefore the classification the 
recommendations mentioned in the report were used in this study. A size-based method was 
followed when classifying plastic litter, with four different categories of particle sizes used.

2.3 Microplastics 
2.3.1 Definition and Classification of Microplastics 
The term “microplastics” was first used in 1990 with relation to marine debris. Ryan and 
Moloney mentioned this while describing their findings of surveys of Southern African 
beaches [26].  MPs are most likely used in industrial activities and come in several shapes 
and forms including: fragments, fibers, beads, films, foams and can be made of a variety of 
polymers [13]. 
 
In most scientific fields, it is relied on the SI (Système Internationale) to provide an agreed 
set of measurement units. However, this is not the case in the field of marine litter.  
In 2003, the term “microlitter” was used to describe the fine fraction of marine plastic litter 
with sizes 63-500 µm [20], [27]. Later in 2004, the term “microplastics” became popular to 
describe truly microscopic plastic fragments with typical diameter down to ~ 20 µm [20], 
[25]. In 2008, experts attending a meeting hosted by NOAA (National Oceanic and 
Atmospheric Administration) proposed a working definition in which microplastics are 
defined as all plastic particles that have a diameter with size < 5mm [20], [28].  
However, the global assessments published by GESAMP in 2015 and 2016 mentioned again 
that there is a lack of an internationally-agreed definition [24]. And which size definition is 
most appropriate for microplastic particles has remained a contentious topic, with advocates 
of both < 1mm and < 5mm [24], [29] [30], [31]. And despite the fact that the term “Micro” 
for example is an SI descriptor (meaning a factor of 10-6), some scientists have argued that 
MPs should be defined as having a size of < 1mm [24].  
 
==PAGE:20==

In this study, the size-based classification used to for MP particles was based on the 
GESAMP report of 2019, which recommends a common definition of < 5mm diameter, in 
recognition that several national and regional monitoring programs are using this definition 
routinely. The report from 2019 adds that this classification encourages a harmonized 
approach among researchers and reduces ambiguity. It also recognizes that researchers may 
use additional size categories like < 1mm and 1-5 mm, as was followed in this study. This 
explains why this study used the terms “large-sized microplastics” for MP particles having 
size 1-5 mm and “microplastics” for those ranging from 300µm-1mm for size-based 
classification of MP particles.  
 
MP particles are also classified according to shape (sometimes called morphology or 
structure) and color, since they can help provide indication of the potential sources of MP 
litter [24]. The shape includes the length and width of the particle. 
These three aforementioned descriptors (size, shape, and color) are those used in this study to 
define detected MP particles in wastewater.   
 
From an origin-based perspective, microplastics can be classified in two categories: primary 
microplastics and secondary microplastics [28], [32], [14], [24]. However, there should be a 
clear distinction between origin-based and size-based classification of microplastics.  
 
Primary microplastics are those that are specifically manufactured to be a part of a small size 
for a specific application and serve as the feedstock in the plastic industry, in addition to 
abrasives in cosmetics [33]. For so, they are originally produced to be less than 5 mm in size, 
ranging down to 100 nm. Primary microplastics include pre-production pellets that are used 
in the plastic industry to manufacture larger plastic items and microbeads i.e. tiny spheres or 
granules added to the products such as toothpastes and face scrubs for their exfoliating 
properties, and cosmetics for their light-reflecting properties [8]. 
 
On the other hand, secondary microplastics result from the breakdown of larger items, such 
as car tires, textiles, degradation products from road-marking paints, fishing nets, household 
items, plastic containers, artificial turf, and other discarded plastic debris [8], [33], [34]. 
Synthetic fibers generated from washing clothes are also examples of secondary 
microplastics [30].   
Secondary microplastics are therefore a consequence of the degradation of manufactured 
products due to the processes such as photodegradation, and physical, chemical, and 
biological interactions [8]. They are the most type of MPs found in the environment and 
include particles with a wide range of types, shapes, colors, and sizes. 
 
Among those two kinds of microplastics, secondary microplastics are considered the greatest 
problem because they are most prevalent and will continue to be created for a long time, even 
if the input of litter to the sea was stopped [35] [36] [37].

2.3.2 Sources of Microplastics in Wastewater 
Sources of microplastics can vary and have different shapes and forms. Some sources are 
local (fisheries, landfills, wastewater, and offshore industrial activity), while others can come 
from distant regions. Specifically in wastewater, a major source of these microplastics is the 
released fibers from synthetic textiles during wear or when they are laundered or otherwise 
cleaned. Authors have suggested that primary source microplastics entering the marine 
systems include polyethylene, polypropylene, and polystyrene particles in cleaning and 
cosmetic products, which enter the aquatic system through household sewage discharge [30]. 
Other primary microplastics suggested to enter the aquatic systems include those of industrial 
origin in spillage of plastic resin powders or pellets used for air blasting, and feedstocks used 
to manufacture plastic products [30]. 
Domestic areas can also be considered as sources of microplastics, like plastic debris coming 
from bottles, containers, plastic bags and fabrics [4].  
 
In wastewater treatment plants, plastics originate from diverse primary and secondary 
sources. Primary sources include manufactured plastic microbeads, pellets and fragments 
used in cleaning agents, personal care products (e.g. facial cleansers, body washes, cosmetics, 
and toothpastes) and industrial applications [16]. Primary sources of microplastics are 
generally considered the dominant sources of plastics in wastewater treatment plants [16]. 
 
However, wastewater is clearly a significant and direct source of these components. It was 
estimated that each liter of wastewater from washing machines carries about 20,000 pieces of 
fibers from textiles to the ocean [30] [43]–[45]. 
And surprisingly, a report from 2022 by Electrolux states that a recent study discovered that 
73% of microfiber pollution in formerly pristine Arctic waters is from synthetic fiber that 
could be coming from textiles [41]. In the same context, Lassen et al. concluded that there is 
an estimate of 300/1500 mg microplastics loss per kg of synthetic textiles per wash in real life

2.4 Common Primary Sources of Plastic Litter in the Arctic Marine 
Environment 
Several authors suggest that sources of plastic litter in the marine environment can mainly be 
classified into land-based and sea-based sources with a percentage of contribution estimated 
to be 80% and 20% respectively [32], [35], [43], [44].  However, Hrissi et al. claims instead 
that 75-90% of the plastic debris in the marine environment is estimated to originate from 
land-based sources and about 10-25% from sea-based sources. 
 
Land-based sources include litter stemming from human activities such as fisheries, landfills, 
wastewater, and offshore industrial activities which usually originate from populated coastal 
areas [4]. In some Greenlandic communities like Sisimiut, open and uncontrolled dumpsites 
act as potential sources of plastic litter into the surrounding marine environment. A beach 
litter assessment from 2016 reported an input from inadequate waste facilities on the western 
shores of Greenland and a risk of waste escaping from these dump sites and thereby entering 
the sea [4], [35]. Domestic regions are also an important source of plastic litter, as evidenced 
in several studies through reporting of bottles, containers, plastic bags, and fabrics [4], [45]. 
 
On the other hand, sea-based sources include plastic litter transported from distant regions 
into the Arctic, as plastic is carried from lower latitudes to the Arctic by ocean currents, 
atmospheric transport, and rivers [4]. In addition, hydrocarbon exploration activities, 
aquaculture, ship traffic including cruise tourism also act as factors contributing to the Arctic 
marine plastic pollution issue. 
Abandoned, lost, or otherwise discarded fishing gear is considered a major source of plastic 
litter specifically in the Greenlandic, Norwegian, and Barents Seas [4], [45], [46]. For 
example, fibers or threads from fishing nets were found to be the most important sources of 
microplastics in the Barents Sea and the second most abundant type of microplastics in 
southwest Greenland [4], [47], [48]. Moreover, 80-90% of the fishing nets found in Svalbard 
had been discarded deliberately by fishers [4], [46].

2.5 Environmental Impacts of Plastic Litter in Wastewater 
Microplastics in particular raise higher concern than other particle fractions since they can be 
ingested by a much wider range of organisms, which may cause a greater health hazard on 
marine life [13]. And since Greenland is a part of the Arctic region, it is even of higher 
concern to understand how the environment is being affected by the presence of microplastics 
in the marine ecosystem. The Arctic region is a distinctive, unique, and fragile ecosystem that 
needs to be protected and preserved. 
 
==PAGE:24==

24  
Hadi Salame –February 2023 
And the reason behind the focus in recent plastic-derived pollution studies related to the 
marine environment is on microplastics in specific, is due to the fact that large plastics can be 
manually removed from the environment, in contrast to microplastics which cannot be 
removed easily.   
 
When plastics reach the oceans, plastic particles are available for ingestion by a broad range 
of organisms from lower trophic level to top predators [14]. And once they are ingested by 
aquatic animals, the plastics can cause physiological effects, including injury or clogging of 
the digestive tract and false feeling of sanitation [14]. In addition, the floating debris can also 
act as a carrier of non-indigenous marine organisms such as bacteria, algae and invertebrates 
that can be transported over long distances, thereby posing a threat to marine biodiversity and 
to the food web [14].  
2.5.1 Human Beings  
At beaches, people are at risk of physical injury from large plastic pieces that might be stuck 
in the sand for example. A litter survey conducted in Sisimiut in 2019 [50] showed that 
different types of beach litter occur, including large items like cleaning agent bottles, engine 
oil containers, jerrycans, and other types of fisheries waste that can cause injuries to 
beachgoers.  
Being a part of the food chain, humans are likely to ingest pieces of plastics when consuming 
seafood, although the physical consequences of this are not yet fully understood [30] [46]–
[48]. And despite the fact that population-level information in Greenland is still sparse [4], 
plastics smaller than 5 mm in diameter have been investigated in other parts of the world and 
detected in human placenta [53], blood [54], and lungs [55].  
 
2.5.2 Interactions with Biota 
Discharging untreated wastewater is most likely affecting the marine environment in 
Greenland. 
In the past, very few studies documented the ingestion of plastic by fish in the Arctic, the 
documentation of interactions between marine organisms and plastic marine litter has 
increased drastically over the past years.  
Plastic debris (fishing gear or line) has been found in the stomach analyses of Greenland 
sharks (Somniosus microcephalus) from south Greenland with a frequency of 8.3% [7].  
 
Ingestion: Before 2021, ingested macroplastics had never been reported in an Arctic species 
[56]. But after a study was conducted to investigate whether young Arctic marine predators 
may ingest macro-plastics or not, the first fractions of plastic debris in the stomach of a 
weaned hooded seal pup from the Greenlandic sea were recorded [56]. Two pieces of single 
use plastic were found in the stomach of a weaned hooded seal pup, and after an ATR-FTIR 
analysis was performed for both pieces, one of them showed correspondence to the spectrum 
of isotactic polypropylene (PP) with a match of 94%, while the other to polyethylene (PE) 
with a match of 90%. This indicated that these Arctic animals may also be at risk during their 
==PAGE:25==

Hadi Salame – February 2023 
  
25 
early stages of life due to human caused plastic pollution even in the remote Arctic pack ice.  
the impacts of plastic ingestion can be both: physical and chemical. The physical 
consequences of ingestion can be suffocation, starvation, and interior damage. Sea birds are 
especially known to suffer from starvation and reduced growth, due to their stomachs being 
filled up with plastics[35], [57]. Plastics can resemble natural food due to the various shapes 
and colors they have, and therefore animals can’t distinguish between plastic litter and food. 
Sea turtles for instance are known for ingesting plastic bags due to their similarity with 
jellyfish, a primary food source of sea turtles [32] [38].  The chemical consequences of 
ingestion derive from the content of plastic itself and potential adhesive chemicals in sea 
water that attach to plastic[35]. Plastics are often found to have absorbed polychlorinated 
biphenyls (PCBs), hydrocarbons, pesticides, metals, and microbial communities which all can 
have adverse impacts on the animal such as a change in the hormonal immune systems [35], 
[58]–[61].  
 
Entanglement: it is worse than ingestion since it is thought more likely to be lethal. The 
dominating marine litter material for entanglements are fishing gear, such as nets and ropes, 
and plastics, which can cause death by cuts, strangulation and drowning.  
 
2.6 Methods of Transport of Plastic Litter in Arctic Regions  
Transport of plastic pollutants to and within the Arctic is governed by various processes from 
large-scale ocean currents to small-scale phenomena, such as windrows and sea ice drift [4].   
In particular, MPs of small sizes and low densities have been noted on Arctic coastlines, 
mainly due to their transport across large distances by these currents [30]. Long distance 
transport by currents is well known and the poleward branch of the Thermohaline Circulation 
is a major pathway of plastics from the North Atlantic to the Greenland and Barents Sea [5].  
 
Rivers were also suggested as a pathway of microplastic pollution in the Arctic [4] [62].   
And although the Arctic Ocean contains only ≈1% of the global ocean volume, it receives 
>10% of the global river discharge [4]. 
 
Atmospheric deposition has recently been identified as a notable pathway for global MP 
transport [63]. This most likely explains the high concentrations of MPs detected one year 
earlier in European and Arctic snow samples [64]. However, once plastic litter is transported 
to the Arctic, it accumulates in certain areas and affect local ecosystems [4].  
 
2.7 Wastewater Legislation in Greenland

(Inatsisartutlov nr. 9 af 22. November 2011 om beskyttelse af miljøet) [65] applies to the land 
territory and land-based sea pollution [66]. The purpose of this Act is to protect the 
environment by setting rules to prevent and plan activities in the prevention of air, water, ice, 
rock and soil, and noise pollution - as well as promoting the recycling of waste disposal [67].

In Greenland, the government is responsible for the overall waste planning. However, waste 
management is a municipal matter. Which means that municipalities largely decide for 
themselves how they will design their own waste system. This is mentioned in the executive 
order from 2015 mentioned where it states: “The municipal board must draw up plans for the 
disposal of wastewater in the municipality´s towns and settlements.” This is in fact an 
advantage for both the municipality of Qeqqata and Sermersooq, where they are flexible and 
free to decide what they find would improve the overall situation in their towns and 
settlements.  
 
Wastewater may only be discharged into water courses, lakes, or the sea provided that the 
Ministry of Environment and Energy has given a permit to discharge. There are a series of 
conditions in the discharge permit that must be observed. A permit, for example contains 
conditions on the discharge quantity and composition, layout, internal control, cleaning 
measures and time limits [66].

2.8 Wastewater Management and Treatment in Greenland  
In a country with extreme weather conditions like Greenland, waste management of all types 
is quite challenging. This is due to the fact that Greenland has a scattered and small 
population, add to that the limited infrastructure [35]. When it comes to wastewater 
management, additional factors like the nature of terrain and permafrost that could be 
affected by contacting warm sewage coming from homes add up. The environmental impacts 
of discharged wastewater can also be amplified in areas with low temperature and light 
conditions like the Arctic. For example, sewage discharge in the Arctic has a slow 
decomposition rate compared to other European countries.  
 
As a result, the implementation of conventional wastewater treatment systems is not feasible, 
and therefore wastewater treatment has not been implemented in Greenland until now leading 
to discharging raw wastewater directly to nearby waterbodies. Not to mention that most 
conventional wastewater treatment systems have been developed for countries with temperate 
or hot climates and are either only applicable in the Arctic climates to a limited degree or not 
at all.

3.2.2 Chemical Analysis of Plastic Litter (ATR-FTIR) 
Several spectroscopic methods exist to distinguish plastic type, with each having some 
limitations like analysis time, physical sample size needed for analysis, and types and colors 
of plastics that are capable of being identified [79].  
 
In this study, chemical identification of polymer types of the representative items and  
particles that are larger than 1 mm (macro-plastics, meso-plastics, and large-sized  
microplastics) was performed using the ATR-FTIR (Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy- 
attenuated total reflectance) spectroscopy and relevant spectral libraries. The aim of this  
analysis was to validate the visual classification along with the polymer specific  
identification. The ATR sampling technology (Attenuated total reflection) is defined as a  
sampling technique used in conjunction with infrared spectroscopy which enables samples to  
be examined directly in the solid or liquid state without further preparation. In addition, it is a  
non-destructive method that uses infrared absorption bands to probe the stretching and  
bending patterns of polymer functional groups, enabling the identification of plastics based  
on their unique chemical composition and bonding patterns [79].   
 
The measurements were carried out using Agilent Technologies 4500 Series Portable FTIR  
Spectrometer equipped with a triple reflection diamond ATR sample interface and an in- 
depth ATR polymer library. The absorbance spectra were collected using 32 background  
scans at a 4 cm-1 resolution measuring a spectral range between 650 and 4000 cm-1. A  
background atmospheric spectrum was subtracted from all sample spectra and 8 sample scans  
were performed for each sample.  
 
The libraries used for the polymer identification were provided by professional researchers 
from the “Department of Ecoscience” at Århus Universitet.

3.3.1 Water Sample Preparation and Processing  
The first step of the processing procedure was to digest the organic matter found in the 
wastewater samples (digestion stage). For this, an experiment was performed on 50 mL of 
wastewater from one of the samples (sample 1S from Sisimiut) to ensure that the other 
samples will behave as expected when applying the same procedure on them.  
 
However, the digestion step is mainly performed to reduce the organic content found in the 
wastewater as much as possible, in addition to making sure the microplastics are not 
destroyed by the chemicals used.  
 
For this, the reagents needed were a 14% NaOCl readily available filtrated solution and a 
40% KOH prepared one. 50 mL of KOH and 50 mL of NaOCl were added to the 50 mL 
wastewater. Then the beakers were wrapped with aluminium foil and placed on a shaking 
table for 24 hours. The next day, the digested samples were filtered using a 100 µm 
zooplankton net. The net was then rinsed 3 times with filtered distilled water to remove the 
residues of the chemicals. The zooplankton nets, each placed in a separate petri dish were 
then left to dry and thereby visually analyzed using the microscope.

3.4 Data Treatment 
Microlab software automatically compares the collected spectrum with a spectral library and 
associates the best spectral match. Other relevant software can also be used to improve 
generated spectrum. In this study, the Essential FTIR software was used for the data 
processing and interpretation of the final polymer ID.  
  
Smoothing and baseline corrections are critical preprocessing techniques for improving the 
quality of raw FTIR spectra and obtaining a more precise analysis. Data smoothing mainly 
aims to remove high-frequency noises, whereas baseline correction intends to remove low-
frequency noise and hence eliminates the correlation of nearby features [80].  
As a result, all generated spectra in this study were smoothed and baseline adjusted.  
Table A2 in the appendix describes in detail the processes involved in this treatment 
procedure.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
As an example, figures 28 and 29 below illustrate how data treatment improved the quality of 
generated spectra. Both software identified the foil in figure 27 as polyethylene. However, 
Microlab software had recorded an initial HQI score of 0.84 prior to treatment, whereas this 
HQI score showed an increase reaching 0.94 after the treatment process with Essential FTIR.

5. DISCUSSION  
5.1 Observations Related to Wet Wipes 
 
Goal 1:  
In order to achieve the 1st goal of the inspection, a comparison between the results of the 
FTIR analysis of the wet wipes from the sewage were compared to the package indications. 
This comparison showed a clear correlation between the material composition of wipes found 
in the sewers and the different packages inspected at the supermarkets. Most wipes analyzed 
from both cities showed to consist of PET polymers. For example, 84% and 55% out of the 
total analyzed wipes from Sisimiut and Nuuk respectively showed to consist of PET after 
chemical characterization. In total, 27 out of 36 wipes analyzed in both cities showed to 
consist of PET. This correlates with the findings from (McCoy et al, 2020 and O Brian et al. 
2020) stating that non-woven textiles are underestimated sources of MP fibers, after they 
found fibers near a WWTP in sediments and marine organisms and identified the fibers to 
originate from commercial wet wipes (mostly PET, PS, and a combination of PET and 
cellulose) which are similar to the 75% of wipes detected in this study. 
 
 
Figure 54: Showing the percentage of wet wipes with a polymer composition of PET after chemical 
characterization with ATR-FTIR.

Goal 2:  
In order to achieve the 2nd aim of this inspection, all the packages of wet wipes from the 
aforementioned supermarkets were investigated, and the below summarized the findings. 
Results showed that all the packages, including wipes made only from natural matter (like 
bamboo, viscose, or cellulose) had “non-flushable” warning signs on the packages. This 
indicates that the material the wipes are made of has no influence on the behavior of 
flushing the wipes in toilets. The clogging issue of sewer system will remain as long as 
wipes are flushed, whether they are made of natural matter or plastic. 
Therefore, behavioral aspects need to be further understood and taken into consideration 
while mitigating the issue of plastic pollution in Greenland and cannot be ignored by the 
government while implementing solutions on the technical level.  
 
In addition, if the mechanical filter that was supposed to get tested at Nuuk the past year was 
implemented, there would still be a very high risk of affecting or blocking that system with 
the flushed wipes, and thereby cost the government lots for maintenance.  
 
Goal 3:  
According to the results of the chemical characterization in this study, a combination of 
viscose and PET fibers (75% of all wipes detected) were found to be the abundant types of 
wet wipes consumed in both cities. The presence of these specific types of wipes in 
Greenlandic local markets may be attributed to the ability of the synthetic material to provide 
wet strength to the wipes, in addition to their relatively lower prices compared to the ones 
made 100% of natural material. 
To get an estimation of the fibers shredding from wet wipes reaching the marine 
environment, a comparison between the results of this study and observations from a recent 
study [82] on microfibers shredding from non-woven materials was done. Kwon et al. 2022 
was done. 
The study showed that a wipe made of a combination between PET and viscose (which has 
the closest composition to the findings in this study) released 0.004±0.001 g of fibers per 
gram of wipes in aquatic environment.  
 
Noting that the duration of the experiment was only 16 min, the estimations here were 
calculated on a yearly base. In addition, the release conditions tested in room temperature 
(22°C), which is certainly different from temperatures in the Arctic region. Nevertheless, the 
estimation can still help get a better understanding of the quantity of MPs released to the 
marine environment only coming from wet wipes. And with all wipes from Sisimiut and 
Nuuk having a larger area than the tested specimen, the values discussed are considered best-
case scenario values.  
 
Assumptions made for calculation of masses: 
- Sewer has same discharge amount of plastic litter the whole year.  
- Each inhabitant from this sewer discharge the same quantity of plastic litter.

Due to wet wipes being the most abundant type of macro-plastic sized litter detected in both 
cities, a separate section has been written to discuss these results. From the inspection, it was 
noticed that wet wipes sold in local supermarkets of Sisimiut and Nuuk are categorized into 
two main categories: wipes made completely from natural matter (cellulose, viscose, or 
bamboo) and wipes that are a mixture of polyester and viscose (also sometimes called 
generated cellulose) with different percentages in different brands.  
From the sampling events, it was also noticed that almost all of the wipes were chemically 
identified as PET wipes, which means that they are most consumed (probably due to their 
lower prices). 
 
Looking into both, the inspection and the sampling events, and by comparing those results to 
literature on studies focused on fiber generation from wet wipes, it is thus confirmed that 
the discharge of wet wipes into the sewers of Sisimiut and Nuuk acts as a potential 
source of microplastics to the marine environment. 
  
Lee et al. (2021) used a polyester wet wipe to study the possibility of microplastics release

toilets/basins. The study also quantified the number of microfibers released in different 
scenarios. Compared to other scenarios, the highest number of microplastic fibers released 
was when the wipe was exposed to the aquatic environment. 
Since the largest number of wipes from the sampling in the current study referred to polyester 
wipes, the estimated number of microplastic fibers released per sheet will be used here to 
estimate the minimum number of possible fibers released in this case.

5.2 Observations on Size Fractions vs Masses of Plastic Litter  
For fractions with size 1-25 mm, and while comparing the particle number-based results 
(number of items) to the loads of plastic litter estimated (masses) for both Nuuk and 
Sisimiut, 3 items weighed 0.02 g and 2 items weighed 0.05 g. 
 
It was clear that the number of particles is not proportional to the masses measured. This 
stresses on the importance of the calculation of masses for a more informative input of 
plastic to the environment. Although the number of items is also significant when 
considering the biomagnification in biota. This was further validated while comparing the 
number of items to the masses for the macroplastics which showed to be proportional, due to 
the fact that the majority of the items were of the same type (wet wipes), thus having the 
approximately the same mass.    
5.3 Observations on Polymer Compositions 
Polyester showed to be the most abundant polymer ID for all plastic litter fractions. In 
regards with the 2nd abundant polymer ID, PE showed a higher percentage in the findings of 
macroplastics in Sisimiut due to the presence of a higher number of pads usually made of PE.  
For plastic litter with size 1-25 mm in Nuuk, the 2nd polymer ID detected was PUR. The 
presence of PUR indicates the potential disposal of packaging film.  
Finally, for the MP findings in Nuuk, the 2nd most abundant polymer IDs showed to be 
acrylate, acrylic, and polystyrene which are usually used in textile fabrics. 
This elucidates that the polymer composition reflects upon the source of plastic being 
disposed.  
 
5.4 Observations on the Morphology of Plastic Litter 
The results from this study showed that the abundant shape of MP particles is fibers. 
In particular in Nuuk, this correlates with the findings of Rist et al. 2020 where the abundant 
shape of MPs in 3 stations near the sampling site investigated here showed that fibers >300 
µm were the dominant MPs found. However, it is also mentioned that the concentration of 
MPs increased with decreasing size and 93% of all MP particles were found to be smaller 
than 300 µm. Therefore, it is important to quantify the smallest fractions of MPs to get a 
more accurate estimation of MP concentrations in the environment. 
5.5 Is Primary Treatment Enough in Greenland? 
During the last decades wastewater treatment has continuously been required to increase the 
quality of the final effluents. Although many authors claim that wastewater treatment 
technologies are not designed specifically to capture microplastics (Mason et al., 2016, 
Talvitie et al., 2017), literature shows that depending on the technology used, microplastic 
retention rates can exceed 90% [83].  
 
Moreover, Hrissi et al. 2020 discuss that these statements act as assertions to support the

conjectural sentiments of people who believe that WWTPs are incapable, if not completely 
ineffective, at removing microplastic. In addition, the authors of this book add that WWTPs 
were never designed to target any specific type or class of solid waste. Instead, they are 
designed to address and remove broad fractions of generic solid sharing some common 
physical properties like density, hydrophobicity, etc. 
 
Therefore, this study suggests that any treatment system that differentiates solids from 
aqueous effluents can be utilized to effect practical separation, and the current situation in 
Greenland specifically does not require any special or enhanced isolation process to 
effectively target the removal of plastics. 
 
Moreover, a study from 2020 also compared microplastic removal rates in 21 different 
studies and concluded that the majority of microplastics were removed during preliminary 
and primary treatment [84]. Furthermore, a recent report from the European Environment 
Agency released in 2022 stated that the retention efficiencies for microfibers of pre- and 
primary wastewater treatment processes are on average 72 percent [85]. In Finland for 
example, a 97 percent retention rate was achieved during pre- and primary treatment [86]. 
 
In conclusion, with this study showing that highest loads of discharged plastic waste in this 
study are macro-plastic and in specific wet wipes, it is suggested that a mechanical filter for 
the pipes or a screening system implemented in Greenland would decrease to a high extent 
the plastic discharge into the marine environment.   
 
5.6 Interviews and Mitigation Through Socio-behavioral Approach  
It is clear from the interviews that the society takes a part of responsibility in the overall 
plastic pollution issue in Greenland. The wet wipe inspection showed that all packages are 
clearly labelled as non-flushable in Sisimiut and Nuuk, which should be enough for 
consumers to understand that flushing the wipes have some negative impact (whether it is 
technical, environmental, etc.). 
This shows that people contribute to a great extent in the problem and therefore should be 
involved as a part of the solution. This was further agreed upon when conducting the 
interviews where Jakob stated that prevention through awareness campaigns would definitely 
make a difference. In addition, he added: “If you get any sort of results that we can show people 
in campaigns I believe that would be extremely helpful. People do care. I have seen that these 
campaigns work quite well and I believe in Sisimiut and here at least. There are just few challenges 
including a sense of hopelessness, and that people tend to put the responsibility on the 
municipality”.  
 
The results from this study could be used to show people how they can contribute and 
pinpoint for the municipality on where the campaigning should be focused. Specifically the 
youth in schools and universities could act as an inspiration since they have a much higher

5.7 Uncertainties and Limitations  
5.7.1 Sampling  
Due to time and weather constraints, sampling in Nuuk was limited to only one occasion for 
capturing items with size >25 mm. Repeated weekday and weekend sampling within a 
month, and over several days of the week would allow for more accurate quantification of the 
load estimations.  
In addition, blue plastic drums used for collecting wastewater are manufactured using a 
particular type of plastic, known as HDPE (high-density polyethylene). This usually results in  
a risk of contamination of the water samples with plastic fragments from the barrels. 
 
For more accurate results on the discharged masses of plastic waste, a comprehensive study 
involving all sewers with more sampling events is recommended.  
5.7.2 Results Limitations 
This study is a first step for the quantification of the loads of plastic litter being discharged 
into the marine environment of Greenland. It is not enough to rely on the results presented 
here since they are limited to the number of sewers investigated in each city, in addition to 
few sampling events. 
 
5.8 Possibilities of Improvements of Existing Implementations of 
New Wastewater Technologies  
Understanding the major sources of plastic litter entering the aquatic environment in 
Greenland is key to developing effective pollution reduction policies and regulations. 
In addition, the reduction of the plastic load being discharged into the sea would definitely 
decrease the microplastic problem since most of the macroplastics found in Nuuk and

still possible to benefit from other experiences in the Arctic region to prove that there is a 
way to success. In the Canadian Arctic for example, the WWTP in the community of 
Pangnirtung, Nunavut has been one of the most significant success stories. The facility there 
has maintained a successful operation [87]. And this facility is similar to what could be 
implemented in the future in all the wastewater facilities of Nuuk and Sisimiut, or what is 
called the chocolate factories. Since there are still many communities with no sewers, with 
the wastewater transported instead the to the chocolate factories, this makes it even cheaper 
for the municipalities since there is only one facility where the idea could be executed.

buckets, and then let the wastewater pass through a pre-screen, where large material can be

6. CONCLUSION AND 
RECOMMENDATIONS 
The research questions of this study were: 
 
Research question 1: What types, masses, and fractions of marine litter are present in the 
wastewater of Nuuk and Sisimiut? And what are their sources?  
  
Research question 2: Given the practical and economic challenges in Greenland, how can 
the findings from this project help municipalities develop strategies to mitigate sewer-
discharged plastic pollution in Greenland and minimize the impacts of plastic litter on the 
marine environment? 
 
Furthermore, the study aimed to facilitate the decision-making regarding implementation of 
solutions in small, remote, arctic communities to reduce the load of plastic from sewage to 
sea. The loads, size-fractions, morphological aspects, and polymer compositions of plastic 
litter in wastewater of both Nuuk and Sisimiut were investigated. Results showed that 
wastewater is a potential source of plastic litter to the marine environment.  
For both cities, abundant polymer was found to be PET and abundant morphology of plastic 
litter ranging from size 300 µm to 25 mm was fibers and foils/films. From a large-scale 
perspective, wastewater seems to be a significant source of plastic litter (of different size-
fractions) to the marine environment.   
 
Based on both, the quantitative and qualitative data reported, possible improvements and the 
implementation of new cost-efficient wastewater treatment systems were discussed.  
The study suggests that there is no “golden solution” to the plastic pollution issue in 
Greenland due to complex variables contributing to the problem. However, taking into 
consideration the small scattered population of Greenland, the economic situation, the 
technical and geographical challenges, the study propose that the  most practical, realistic, 
and efficient mitigation plan would involve 3 main aspects working in parallel: the technical, 
social, and legal aspects. Statements from interviewees indicated the importance of involving 
people in the solution.
