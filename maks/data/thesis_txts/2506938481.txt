Master Thesis
Abstract
P. aeruginosa is posing an increasing threat towards immunocompromised patients and in the U.S
alone 51,000 cases of P. aeruginosa induced infections are seen on a yearly basis. The treatment of
these infections is limited by an increase in antibiotic resistance of P. aeruginosa. Another factor
which is threatening the survival rate of the immunocompromised patients is the time between
infection, sampling, and diagnosis, which are limited by the current laboratory centralized health-
care system. As a consequence of the need for a more patient centralized healthcare system, the
demand for tools allowing diagnostics to be performed on the location of the patients is increasing
as well. Such devices are referred to as Point-of-Care devices and one key component of these tools
is the implemented antibodies that facilitates the detection of bacteria.
This thesis is aiming to discover single domain antibodies for the detection of P. aeruginosa based
on the virulence factor LasB. The ﬁrst part of the project has focus on the discovery of single do-
main antibodies through the phage display technique. A synthetic single domain antibody library
called ”Predator” was used for this purpose. The phage display selection showed accumulated
aﬃnity to LasB through three panning rounds. Afterward, the genes encoding the single domain
antibodies were subcloned and expressed in BL21 (DE3) E. coli and their binding abilities were
characterized through ELISAs. Based on the ELISA results, six monoclonal sdAbs were identiﬁed,
and based on the database of the U.S National Library of Medicine two monoclonal sdAbs were
deemed unique compared to already discovered single domain antibodies.
The puriﬁcation of selected monoclonal sdAbs failed and must be repeated and as a consequence,
the expression step must be repeated as well. This caused the second part of the thesis to be
conducted with alternative antibodies. These were implemented on a nitrocellulose membrane
and a detection of a target protein was performed. This was carried out late in the experimental
work, which did not allow time for validation of the recognition abilities thorugh a calibration curv.
The results in this thesis showed that phage display technology can be used to discover single
domain antibodies with the ability to bind to a chosen virulence factor expressed by P. aerugi-
nosa. Currently, the two promising monoclonal sdAbs are not available for further investigation
of the implementation and validation of nitrocellulose membranes, as they are stored in E. coli
and require expression and puriﬁcation. Moreover, a subcloning with minor alterations must be
repeated to discover the full potential of single domain antibodies displaying aﬃnity to LasB.
This project represents the ﬁrst steps toward the development of a diagnostics tool to detect P.
aeruginosa.

P.aeruginosa udgører i stigende grad en trussel mod allerede udsatte patienter med svagt immun-

I takt med, at behovet for et mere pa-

Form˚alet med dette kandidatspeciale er at identiﬁcere antistoﬀer, der kan p˚avise P.aeruginosa

identiﬁcere disse antistoﬀer baseret p˚a phag display teknologi. Phag biblioteket ”Predator” blev
brugt til dette form˚al. Baseret p˚a resultaterne fra phag display kunne en stigning i aﬃnitet ses

sekvenser subklonet ind i BL21 (DE3) E. coli. Herefter blev antistoﬀerne udtrykt og testet i ELISA

projektet, blev antistoﬀerne sporings evne ikke undersøgt.
Resultaterne af dette projekt har vist, at det er muligt at bruge phag display teknologi til at
ﬁnde antistoﬀer mod den virulente faktor LasB. P˚a nuværende tidspunkt er de to lovende anti-
stofkandidater ikke tilgængelige for viderearbejde, der inkluderer implementering og validering af
en nitrocelluose membran. Begge antistoﬀer skal udtrykkes og oprenses, da de lige nu er at ﬁnde
indeni E. coli bakterier. En ny subkloning af antistofkodende sekvenser skal laves for at sikre,
at de bedst mulige antistoﬀer mod LasB bliver fundet. Dette kandidatspeciale repræsenterer de
første skridt mod udviklingen af et patientnært udstyr til diagnostisering af P.aeruginosa.

Motivation
Wound infections are becoming a greater concern worldwide, especially wound infections con-
nected to hospitalization. Post-operation wound infections are one of the most abundant causes
for complications among hospitalized patients.[1] Many bacteria are present in a wound and all
can contribute to the loss of the integrity of the skin. The most common bacteria strains found
in wounds include, but may not be limited to: Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus), Escherichia coli
(E. coli), Streptococcus pyogenes (S. pyogenes), Klebsiella pneumonia (K. pneumonia), and Pseu-
domonas aeruginosa(P. aeruginosa).[1] If an infection is allowed to remain, caused by either an
ineﬀective immune response from the host or because it remains untreated, bioﬁlm can form. The
bioﬁlm creates a protective barrier between bacteria and host, increasing the resistance against the
defenses of the host. At this stage, the infection can develop into chronic wounds.[2] A non-healing
wound will often result in pain and discomfort for the patient. Chronic wounds can present more
critical symptoms such as systemic complications like septicemia (blood poisoning) or osteomyelitis
(infection of bones and bone marrow). These conditions can have a great eﬀect on the health and
life quality of the patients with the potential to result in death.[3, 4, 5]
The need for a device to detect P. aeruginosa is crucial. Infections caused by this bacteria strain
have been recognized as a global threat based on its increasing antibiotic resistance, fatal outcome,
and chronicity of illness.[6] Furthermore, the World Health Association has recognized P. aerug-
inosa as a threat and in 2017 P. aeruginosa was named one of ”the critical priority pathogens
posing the greatest threat to human health”.[7] To the extent of the knowledge obtained in this
thesis a commercialized monoclonal single domain antibody against P. aeruginosa is not available
on the market at present time. This causes an obstacle for the development of a paper-based
microﬂuidic Point-of-Care(PoC) diagnostic device which is aiming to target P. aeruginosa. Phage
display technology is called the golden standard of antibody discovery and allows the user to dis-
cover and develop antibodies with high precision. The phages are discovered based on a phage
library displaying a vast diversity increasing the possibility to discover antibodies against a spe-
ciﬁc taget.[8, 9] Currently, diagnostic P. aeruginosa infections are limited by traditional laboratory
methods and the laboratory centralized healthcare system.[10, 11, 12]

Project aim
The aim of this project is to develop single domain antibodies for the detection of P. aeruginosa
LasB and implement these in a lateral ﬂow device (LFD) as a PoC system. The initial step of this
thesis is to modify and optimize the usage of the Predator phage library when exposing phages
to directly coated LasB. Phage display selection will then be used to discover human monoclonal
single-domain antibodies with aﬃnity to LasB. The success of the phage display will be evaluated
by diﬀerent ELISA assays. Furthermore, antibodies will be implemented on a paper-based LDF
and a detection will be performed.
Thesis overview
The work conducted in this thesis will be illuminated through seven sections. In section one,
the motivation for the thesis is stated followed by section two, which is presenting the necessary
background information.
Next, the experimental strategy of the thesis can be seen in section
three. Section four ”Method and materials” are describes all the experimental work conducted.

in section ﬁve, while a more thorough analysis can be read in section six. Section six, which is

Finally, the thesis is ended by a conclusion and evaluation of the experimental strategy. For a

P. aeruginosa is a rod-shaped gram-negative bacteria species,
which is primarily found in water, soil, and skin ﬂora. P. aeruginosa
has a characteristic rod shape with unipolar ﬂagella for movement
which can be seen in Figure 1. P. aeruginosa is classiﬁed as an op-
portunistic pathogen and is a well-known cause of infection.[13, 14]
Moreover, these infections pose a deadly and critical issue as the
treatment is limited due to a continued increasing antimicrobial
resistance. P. aeruginosa can infect humans, animals, and plants.
However, the severe and fatal cases of P. aeruginosa infections
in humans, primarily occur in patients already suﬀering from an-
other underlying disease such as diabetes, AIDS, neoplasia, severe
burns, or cystic ﬁbrosis.[14] When infecting epithelial tissue, sev-
eral virulence factors are expressed, which includes LasB. LasB is
recognized as one of the major proteins in the secretome and it is
believed that targeting this protein could be important and beneﬁ-
cial for treating infections caused by this bacteria.[15] Therefore, the target of choice in this study
is LasB. LasB is a part of several virulence factors, which could contribute to the pathogenicity
of P. aeruginosa. The major part of virulence factors produced includes proteinases, where LasB
belongs to the thermolysin/M4 peptidase family, which contains elastolytic metalloproteinases.
In literature pseudonyms for LasB are P. aeruginosa elastase, P. aeruginosa LasB protease, and
pseudolysin.[16, 17] It has been observed that LasB plays a central role in degrading elastin in
the host tissue which could be the source of some of the tissue damaged which is often observed
during a P. aeruginosa infection. The tissue is damage due to the hydrolysis of the extracellu-
lar matrix (ECM) components, epithelial barriers, and endothelial barriers.[18] Moreover, studies
have shown that LasB tends to inhibit the activity of epithelial cells and leukocytes among other
inﬂammatory and immune cells in humans.[15, 16] It is known, that some strains have the ability
to express other varieties of proteases other than LasB. These include protease IV and LasA. To
ensure the presence of LasB in the experimental work of this thesis, the strain P. aeruginosa O1
(PAO1) will be used for representing P. aeruginosa. PAO1 is one of the most used laboratory-
adapted strains and in infection studies, the strain is used to represent lung and wound infections
caused by P. aeruginosa.[19, 20] Several studies have conﬁrmed a major production of LasB by
PAO1.[15, 16, 18]. It is believed that it might be proﬁtable to target the virulent factor LasB, as
more than 75% of clinically isolated P. aeruginosa strains are displaying the expression of LasB,

2.2
Diagnosic golden standard for Pseudomonas aeruginosa detection
In the case of the Gram-negative pathogens, physicians have aparticular d focus on P. aeruginosa,
which compromise the health of already critically ill patients. As an optimal treatment and in-
fection management tool has not been discovered yet, this presents an area of great interest for
the healthcare sector.[24] In healthcare regime P. aeruginosa causes 51,000 infections per year in
the US and in Denmark, 489 patients were reported to suﬀer from critical P. aeruginosa infec-
tions in 2018.[24, 25] The diagnosis of an infection is based on symptoms, which include fever,
fatigue, and pus-ﬁlled wounds.
Moreover, the medical history of the patient is relevant as P.
aeruginosa infections are threatening for already immunocompromised patients. In addition to
symptoms and medical history, samples of pus, tissue, blood, and/or mucus/saliva are taken based
on the location of the suspected P. aeruginosa infection and sent to a diagnostic laboratory.[10]
Often, the identiﬁcation of P. aeruginosa is based on the microbial colony growth and morphol-
ogy. The growth of P. aeruginosa does not require any strict conditions as the bacteria is not
signiﬁcantly sensitive to a wider range of temperatures and is capable of growing in a range of
diﬀerent media (both liquid and solid). P. aeruginosa is commonly recognized, when grown on
solid media plates, by the characteristic blue-green colored colonies and grape-like smell.[26] One
of the major challenges in the diagnostic laboratory is the incubation time needed to determine
and distinguish the bacteria in a sample. P. aeruginosa must grow between 16-24 hours regardless

2.4
Antibodies
An antibody is a protein which is produced by the immune system. Antibodies target molecules in
the host which are deemed foreign. These molecules can be found on the surface of pathogens, in the
ﬂuids of the host, on the surface of the hosts own cells or expressed by the infected tissue.[37, 38]
Antibodies have a characteristic Y-shape and are multi-domain proteins as they consist of two
heavy and two light chains.[38] The tip of the Y-shape consists of the ends of a light and a heavy
chain. The light chain consists of a constant domain and a variable domain. In contrast, the
heavy chain consists of three constant domains and one variable domain. The variable domain of
the light and heavy chain represents the antigen-binding-site of an antibody.[38] The region where
the binding of antigens occur is called the Fab (antigen-binding fragment) region. This region
is composed of a light and a heavy chain, where each chain has a constant and a variable part.
An scFv (single-chain variable fragment) is a monovalent complex displaying aﬃnity against one
antigen. An scFv is composed of the variable domain of the heavy and the light chain which are
linked by a single polypeptide chain.[38] The smallest function antibody fragment is called sdAb
(single domain antibody). A sdAb is composed of a light or a heavy chain and consists of three
CDRs (Complementarity determining regions). The CDR region is often deﬁned as the paratope
for an antibody which is the part of the antibody that is responsible for the binding to the the
target regions of the antigen which is denoted epitope. The three variable CDR regions, CDR1,
CDR2, and CDR3 consist of β-sheet structure creating three loops on the surface of the variable
domain.[37, 38, 39, 40] A graphic is shown in Figure 4 to obtain a ﬁgurative overview.
Figure 4: The graphic was made with inspiration from multiple articles.[37, 38, 39] Each component of
a full-size antibody (150 kilo Dalton(kDa)) is highlighted including, heavy and light chain, constant and
variable region. Moreover, Fab (55 kDa), scFv (25 kDa) and sdAb (15 kDa) are shown.

Full-size antibodies, Fabs, scFvs, and sdAbs are known for binding antigens with high aﬃnity,
however the four components are diﬀerent in size and stability.[41, 42, 43] The small size of a sdAb
does not aﬀect the aﬃnity and for some applications sdAbs might be favored caused by the size,
as sdAbs have the ability to bind to otherwise inaccessible epitopes.[44] In addition, sdAbs are
deemed more stable regarding changes in temperature and environmental factors. A sdAb tend
to be capable of refolding and still bind to an antigen after thermal or chemical denaturation.
This ability can be decreased in synthetically manufactured sdAbs, due to the introduction of
mutations at sites crucial for thermostability.[45] Fabs and scFvs tends to aggregate irreversibly, if
exposed to the same changes, however it is possible to improve the stability of Fabs and scFvs and
thereby increase their resistance abilities.[41, 42, 43] A sdAb might be assessed as the most suitable
molecule for implementation on a PoC device. This assessment is based on the presumption that
great changes in environment and temperature often occur when storing and transporting PoC
devices. Moreover, it can be assumed that PoC will be stored under varying conditions as the
device must be available in the immediate environment of the patient.[12]

2.6
Phage display technique
Phage display technology is based on libraries constructed of millions of bacteriophages, which
have been genetically engineered to present diﬀerent molecules on their surface. Phage display
technology allows the user to identify target-speciﬁc proteins without screening a vast range of
individual molecules. This technique allows for an easy identiﬁcation of the coding sequence of
the binding molecule by linking the protein of interest with its encoding gene. Diﬀerent phages
can be used for this purpose, however, ﬁlamentous bacteriophages(from this point denoted as:
phage) are the most commonly used type.[52] A phage is a bacteria infecting virus consisting of
10
==PAGE:32==
Master Thesis
a protein structure surrounding nucleic acid molecules. The phage is dependent on a host cell to
be ampliﬁed. Therefore, the phage will bind to a host cell, invade it, and force it to produce viral
components instead of bacteria components. This is performed by transduction, a process where
the phage is implementing a piece of its own DNA into the genom of the host cell by discarding
a part of the host cells’ bacterial DNA.[54] One of the commonly used phages in phage display
technique is M13, which is a virus infecting E. coli and thus, E. coli is commonly used as a host.[55]
The M13 phage is 900nm long and 6.5nm wide. A diagram of M13 can be seen in Figure 5. The
phage holds an ssDNA (single-stranded DNA) which consists of eleven diﬀerent proteins which are
encoded in nine genes. Six of these proteins play a roll in assembly and replication of the phage,
while the remaining ﬁve are coat proteins. The envelope surrounding the chromosome consists
mainly of G8P, while G3P, G6P, G7P, and G9P can be found in approximately ﬁve copies on the
surface.[56]

M13 will only infect E. coli which are expressing F-pili which are small hair-like structures
found on the surface of the cell. The formation of F-pili is temperature dependent and thereby
sensitive to change of temperature in the surrounding environment.[53] Adsorption of M13 to E.
coli is mediated by the binding between the phage coat protein and the tip of the F-pilus.[56]
The infection caused by M13 is not lethal, instead, it establishes a chronic infection-pattern in the
host inducing a continuous release of new phages. The infection begins when the tip of a F-pilus
located on the surface of E. coli and the N2 domain of the G3P found on the surface of a phage are
interacting. The F-pilus has the ability to assemble and disassemble when needed. As such, when
a phage binds to a F-pilus, the F-pilus will disassemble and thereby bring the phage closer to the
surface of E. coli. This allows a binding between the N1 domain of the G3P on the surface of a

phage and the co-receptor To1A which is found on the surface of E. coli. The binding between the
N2 domain and F-pilus causes the release of the N1 domain. The infection of E. coli is dependent
dependent on the the proteins To1Q, To1R, and To1A. The To1 proteins cause depolymerization
of the coating of the phage and transfer the ssDNA into the E. coli bacterium.[57]. Thereby, the E.
coli is infected and the phage can use the machinery of the cell for the production of a vast amount
of phages containing the same gene encoded for a surface protein which will speciﬁc for a certain
target.[56, 57] By incorporating a foreign gene into the M13 phage chromosome region that is
responsible for encoding G3P, it is possible to fuse foreign molecules on the surface of M13. When
working with antibodies, an antibody fragment is fused to the G3P. By cloning a large number
of genes encoding antibody fragments, a large phage display antibody library can be obtained.
When incorporating an antibody gene directly, a multivalent expression of antibody-G3P fusion
proteins might occur, as M13 is expressing G3P in three to ﬁve copies on each phage.[56, 57]
Instead of incorporating the gene directly, phagemids can be used. A phagemid is an engineered
vector which are containing a M13 origin and a plasmid origin. Phagemids allow another approach
of incorporating the ssDNA into a host.
A phagemid contains of the three key elements for
ampliﬁcation and production of the ssDNA, which is packed into a phage. These key elements are
1) the regions of the M13 chromosome, 2) an antibiotic marker (used in selection and propagation
of the plasmid), and 3) the gene encoding the antibody-G3P fusion protein. However, phagemids
are lacking essential elements for the assembly of a phage. Therefore, in order to produce proper
functional phage particles expressing antibody fragments, the host (E. coli) must be subsequently
infected by helper phages. The helper phage holds the complete M13 genome which encodes all the
proteins needed for chromosome replication, capsid production, and phage assembly. The helper
phages cause the conversion from phagemid to ssDNA which are then assembled into a phage that
can be secreted from E. coli.[57, 56, 58] When the helper phage is present alone in the host, it
gives rise to the replication of the helper phages. A high concentration of phagemids (with intact
M13 origin) will outcompete the helper-phage-plasmid during packaging, which results in phages
carrying the phagemid DNA. The result of this is a majority of bald phages(not displaying the
antibody-fragment).[56] Approximately 90% of the ﬁnal phage population will not display any
fusion proteins, this is due to the fact that the helper phages have a wild-type G3P which is able
to compete with the phagemid encoded G3P protein.[56]

Experimental strategy
This thesis is aiming to discover sdAbs against the virulence factor LasB from P. aeruginosa. These
sdAbs will be implemented on an LFD and the recognition abilities will be validated. If successful,
the LFD might play an important role in future infection diagnostics. To reach the objectives
stated, phage display selection will be used to identify the sdAbs against the protein LasB. The
panning round with the highest aﬃnity for the target and lowest aﬃnity for compounds such as
3%MPBS will be identiﬁed through polyclonal ELISAs. By subcloning the genes encoding LasB-
speciﬁc sdAbs into B21 (DE3) E. coli, the genes will be expressed in individual clones. Based on
monoclonal ELISAs, the monoclonal sdAbs with the highest aﬃnity against LasB will be identiﬁed.
In addition, negative control ELISAs will be performed to assess the monoclonal sdAb’s ability
to bind to other components used throughout the selection rounds. Whole bacteria ELISAs will
be performed to evaluate the monoclonal sdAb’s ability to bind to the virulence factor when it
is expressed by P. aeruginosa in a sample. The selected clone(s) will be expressed and puriﬁed,
which will lead to the possibility of implementing the sdAbs on an LFD. The experimental strategy
is illustrated in Figure 9.

Experimental purpose
The purpose of this experiment was to accumulate sdAb displaying phages which could bind
to LasB. This ability was accumulated over three rounds by exposing a range of phages to LasB
bound to the surface of a vial, wash unbound phages away, elute the bound phages, and amplify the
eluted phages in E. coli acting as the host. For each successful selection round, a phage rescue was
conducted by co-infect with helper phages and yield phages which were recombined and displayed
the ability to recognize LasB at the antigen binding size. These phages were recovered by binding
them to PEG8000 allowing an isolation of phages, which could be used in an continoued selection
round. Two individual phage display selections, denoted TPL0049 and TPL0050, were conducted.
Both phage display selections were based on the Predator library and LasB as antigen. A library
preparation was conducted before each individual phage display selection as the Predator library
was stored in an E. coli stock.

Library preparation
The library was extracted by a rescue process as the Predator library was stored in an E. coli.
This process was started 2 days before the ﬁrst selection round.
Day 1, Preparing E. coli culture
70µL of the thawed E. coli stock was added to a 50mL Falcon tube containing 10mL 2xTYAG
media to reach an OD600 = 0.1. The culture was grown at 37◦C, 280 Rounds per minute (rpm)
until OD600 = 0.5. Next, 100µL of KM13 helper phages (1/2000 dilution of 5 ∗1014 phages/mL
stock) was added and the solution was incubated for 1 hour (h) at 37◦C, 150 rpm. After 1 h, the
solution was centrifuged at 2,250 centrifugal force(xg) for 10 minutes. Afterward, the supernatant
was removed by decanting and the cell pellet was resuspended in the remaining supernatant. Then
the resuspended pellet was transferred to an Erlenmeyer ﬂask which contained 40mL 2xTY-AKG
media. The solution was incubated over-night (ON) at 25◦C, 150 rpm.
Day 2, Rescue
The ON-culture was transferred to a 50mL Falcon tube and then centrifuged for 10 minutes at
10,500 xg at room temperature. Next, the supernatant was transferred to a new 50mL Falcon tube
containing 10mL 20% PEG-8000 + 2.5 M NaCl. This solution was incubated for 1 h on ice. Next,
the solution was centrifuged at 15,000 xg for 10 minutes. The supernatant was then discarded and
the phage pellet was resuspended in 500µL PBS in an Eppendorf tube. The Eppendorf tube was
centrifuged at 10,000 xg for 10 minutes at 4◦C in a mini-centrifuge. The supernatant was then
transferred to a new Eppendorf tube. The Eppendorf tube containing the rescued phage library
was stored at 4◦C.

displaying E. coli colony. The individual sdAb-displaying E. coli was grown and the sdAb was
expressed allowing phage containing supernatant to be used in monoclonal ELISA experiments,
which used LasB-coated MaxiSorp plates. High absorbance signal was linked to high aﬃnity to
LasB. Negative and positive controls were used to validate the monoclonal ELISA experiments.

37◦C incubator and dried for 30 minutes. Next, the membranes were blocked using 1%BSA diluted
in MiliQ water. One end of each nitrocellulose membrane was deﬁned as blocking tip by marking
one end with a green marker.
The other end of each membrane was denoted testing tip and
not marked. This was done to be able to distinguish the two ends of a membrane throughout the
experiment, as test solutions should not be added to the blocking tip of a membrane. The blocking
tip of each membrane was placed in 1%BSA diluted in MiliQ water and when each membrane had
absorbed 1%BSA (could be seen with the naked eye) the membranes were submerged fully in the
1% BSA dilution and incubated at room temperature for 3 minutes on a rocker plate. Afterward,
the membranes were emerged and air dried at room temperature for 5 minutes. The test tip of
each membrane was placed downwards to ensure an even distribution of 1%BSA. Next, the dried
and blocked membranes were washed with MiliQ water. First, the blocking tip was submerged
until each membrane had absorbed MiliQ water onto the whole membrane (checked with the naked
eye) and afterward submerge totally. The blocked membranes were washed at room temperature
for 2 minutes on a rocker plate. When removing the membranes from the MiliQ water they were
ﬂicked and then placed on a Petri dish. The membranes air dried at room temperature for 15-20
minutes. Note that a membrane will be useless if dried for more than 20 minutes. 3 wells in a
MaxiSorp plate was ﬁlled with 10µL of PBP2a (1mg/ml) in deionized water, AuNps conjugated
Rabbit-anti-PBP2a antibody solution, and MiliQ water, respectively. The testing tip was ﬁrst
immersed into the PBP2a-solution (the absorption could be seen with the naked eye). When the
solution reached the absorption pad, the membrane was moved to the well containing the AuNps
conjugated Rabbit-anti-PBP2a antibody solution. When the solution reached the absorption pad
the membrane was moved to the well containing MiliQ water. When the MiliQ water reached the
absorption pad the immobilized area was examined. Ideally, a slight red-colored dot should be
visible indicating a successful detection of PBP2a.

6
Discussion
6.1
Evaluation of laboratory scale work regarding the discovery of
single-domain antibodies speciﬁc for the Pseudomonas aeruginosa
LasB protein
In general, the main part of the experimental work conducted in this thesis has been based on
protocols created for the IONTAS library which is displaying scFvs and not sdAbs as the Predator
library. On crucial areas such as plasmid cleavage sites and the use of primers the two libraries
can be treated equally. Also common for the two libraries is that E. coli plays an important role
as host for both libraries. Even though the two libraries can be treated equally to some degree,
modiﬁcations have been made for the protocols addressing the IONTAS library to ﬁt the Predator
library. In this thesis, the Predator library has displayed a general need for higher concentrations of
coating solutions, higher sensitivity towards incubation time, and decreased colony formation. The
Predator library, compared to the IONTAS library, displayed a lower CFU/mL for each panning
round. This might be caused by the diﬀerent diversities of the two libraries. The IONTAS library
has a diversity of 4 ∗1010, while Predator displays a diversity of 6.2 ∗107.[45, 73] Therefore, it
was assumed that the Predator library might display CFU/mL which were 100-1000 times lower
compared to the IONTAS library. The modiﬁcations of the original protocols have to some extent
provided results and allowed the continues work of this thesis.
6.1.1
Phages display selection
The alterations of the phage display selection were to some extent modiﬁed from IONTAS library
to the Predator library through the work with an alternative protein β-galactosidase. This was
prioritized to economize on the actual protein of interest LasB. Within 3 weeks it was deemed
that the modiﬁed protocol could be transferred from working with β-galactosidase to LasB. This
study was using direct coating of the LasB protein as it displays a size of 49kDa.[74] Due to the
size of LasB it was assessed, that the need for lifting it from the bottom of the vial when exposing
it to sdAb-displaying phages was less vital compared with smaller molecules. Furthermore, the
toxic nature of biotinylation could result in the loss of a large amount of LasB and therefore it was
decided to test direct coating and only if this failed to biotinylate the protein.[75] Even though
the used target protein was not biotinylated, it was chosen to deselect against streptavidin. It was
believed that this deselection would support the selection of target speciﬁc phages.

Evaluation of implementation of antibodies on a nitrocellulose
membrane
Even though no puriﬁed monoclonal sdAb fractions were obtained, an experiment regarding im-
mobilization of antibodies was conducted . This was done for the student to try out the technique
and allowing some experience-based reﬂection regarding the process if puriﬁed sdAbs had been
obtained. The chosen protein for detection was PBP2a which is a penicillin binding protein found
on the surface methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA).[78] A composition inspired by sandwich
ELISA was obtained by ﬁrst immobilizing the capture antigen, then blocking the membrane, next
adding the analyte, and in the end adding the detection antibody. A substrate, like TMB, was
not used to produce a color change, as the AuNP conjugated detection antibodies were already
expressing a detectable color before addition. It was the location of the color change which was of
interest. If the components in the analyte had been captured by the immobilized capture antibod-
ies the color change would occur on that location. This phenomenon can be observed in Figure
23 where a slightly red circle appears on both membranes due to the detection of PBP2a. As
mentioned in the method section 4.11 the membrane was not allowed to dry more than 20 min
after blocking with 1%BSA. This is due to the crystallization properties of BSA which can cause
the formation of too large crystals in the membrane and thereby block all ﬂow. The crystallization
process must be controlled to obtain the right amount and size of BSA crystals which will lead to
an optimal blocking of the membrane and thereby prevent unspeciﬁc binding. It is believed that
the membrane displayed to the left in Figure 23 was slightly over dried, which might have caused
a decrease in color change on the immobilization area. For now, it might be assessed that blocking
of the membrane does not provided optimal conditions for long term storage of the membrane as
it has to used for testing within 15-20 min after adding 1% BSA. A lower concentration of BSA
86
==PAGE:108==
Master Thesis
might yield an increase in drying time. The blockage of the membrane is a technique which re-
quires special care. In the present state of the blocking step, it has not been possible to submerge
the membrane in the 1%BSA dilution without ﬁrst letting the membrane absorb an amount of the
solution. This is due to the formation of dry spots. It is believed that dry spots occur on the mem-
brane due to the capture of air. When submerged in the solution the membrane will absorb the
liquid from the edges causing the liquid to move from the edges towards the middle. This ﬂow can
cause air to get captured creating dry spots in the middle of the membrane, where the immobilized
antibodies usually are located. The capturing of air bubbles in the matrix will cause low or no
absorption in that area as the sample might ﬂow around or/and under the detection area, which
will result in false negative readouts. In addition to the wetting technique of the membrane, it is
important to mark the tip of the membrane which is submerged in the solution. This tip should
not be used as the testing tip (read: the tip which will be immersed in the three solutions used for
the actual test). This is due to the increased formation of crystals at the blocking tip (see Figure
23 for an overview of the composition of the membrane). To decrease the increased formation
of crystals at the blocking tip the membrane was dried with the testing tip turned downwards.
This could to some extent even out the distribution of the 1% BSA solution. The immobiliza-
tion of capture antibodies was performed under controlled humidity conditions as low humidity
can result in nitrocellulose membranes with increase static charge. The increased static charge
would cause the membrane to attract dirt and dust which could cause irregularity of the mem-
brane and aﬀect further work. Moreover, the absorbance abilities would be aﬀected. By nature,
water droplets are electronegative which could interfere with the wetting of the membrane and the
detection of protein in sample buﬀer.[79] An experiment performed by another student prior to
this work did examined the eﬀect of biotintylated antigens. The biotintylated antigens were tried
in the same experimental conﬁguration as the one described in method section 23. The results of
the experiment yielded false-positive results, as a color change was observed every time a sample
was run through. It was impossible to know if the reaction was caused by an actual detection of
a protein or by the reaction between immobilized biotinylated antigens and streptavidin conjugates.

6.3
Future research and perspectives
In this thesis, the initial steps for discovering sdAbs for the detection of P. aeruginosa by targeting
the virulence factor LasB have been made. When looking at the experimental data as a whole it can
be assessed that two likely candidates for sdAbs against LasB have been found. TPL0049 03 D101
must be examined for phagemid insert, however the monoclonal sdAb is displaying a DNA sequence
which is similar to TPL0049 02 B7 which causes the monoclonal sdAb to be assessed as a likely
candidate. It is believed that the monoclonal sdAbs, TPL0049 03 D101 and TPL0049 02 B7 can
be expressed, puriﬁed, and implemented on a nitrocellulose membrane. However, due to time
limitations, these steps were not conducted or repeated in this thesis for TPL0049 03 D101 and
TPL0049 02 B7, respectively. Furthermore, the transformation of both TPL0049 and TPL0050
must be repeated to ensure that all monoclonal sdAbs displaying high aﬃnity against LasB are
found. A long-term perspective for this project could be the study of the possibilities for cross-
binding sdAbs against P. aeruginosa.
It has been deemed beneﬁcial to target LasB and this
approach would, in theory, allow the detection of 75% of known P. aeruginosa strains. However,
the use of sdAbs with the ability to bind to several virulence factors could cause an increase in
detectable P. aeruginosa strains. Furthermore, it could cause a more accurate determination of
the presence of P. aeruginosa in a sample as the secretome of P. aeruginosa consists of more
than one virulence factor. Moreover, it might be considered to target surface proteins found on
P. aeruginosa such as iron-regulated membrane proteins (IRMP) and maybe even try to develop
sdAbs which are displaying aﬃnity against both surface proteins and virulence factors.[80]

Format of selecting antibodies
The format of antibodies is an important perspective to include when designing PoC-devices. As
already illuminated in the introduction section 4 sdAbs seems to be the best choice due to high
stability and small size. Even though scFvs are proven to be more thermal unstable compared to
sABs they might be possible candidates, as it is possible to engineer scFVS to become more thermal
stable.[81] By using phage display selection, scFvs against P. aerugenosa has been discovered.
However, these were not discovered with the purpose of detection P. aerugenosa but for the
development of a treatment.[68] Despite this diﬀerence, it is believed that some knowledge exchange
could occur and thereby act as support in potential experiments using phage display selection for
discovering scFVs against P. aerugenosa LasB. Moreover, literature displays that scFvs have been
used for designing PoC devices.[82, 83] No deﬁnite answer exists and the diﬀerent formats and
implementation possibilities must be evaluated individually and the best solution must be found.
The use of single-domain antibodies in other applications
Another perspective which could be important to consider is whenever the sdAbs discovered in this
thesis can be used for other purposes than detection of P. aerugenosa. An eﬀective vaccine could
be a tool for ﬁghting the increased threat P. aerugenosa poses for the entire world. As mentioned
earlier several studies have been working on developing a vaccine against P. aerugenosa.[62, 63]
Despite this, no vaccines against P. aerugenosa have been licensed for clinical use.
Although
vaccines based on sdAbs does not cause permanent immunization it might be considered a powerful
tool.[85] Other vaccines, such as the ﬂu vaccine, are known to require a yearly follow-up due to
the quick adapting abilities of ﬂu.[86] If a yearly follow-up with a vaccine could protect already
immunocompromised patients against P. aerugenosa infections it might be worth considering.
Another aspect of the use of sdAbs could be the examination of neutralization abilities. This
could be an important tool for a more sustainable treatment of P. aerugenosa infections which in
the present time are displaying an increasing antibiotic resistance. In 2019, a study published work
which included the use of HuscFvs to neutralize virulence factors produced by P. aerugenosa.[68]
Based on available literature it is the impression of the student that the use of antibody fragments
as a treatment tool against bacteria infections is a fairly new, but promising ﬁeld of research. To
the extent of the knowledge obtained in this thesis no studies regarding the development of sdAb
based vaccines or neutralization treatment against P. aerugenosa infections have been published.

sdAbs against the virulence factor LasB for infection diagnostics. The development of PoC devices

Conclusion
The main object of this thesis was to discover single domain antibodies targeting P. aeruginosa
LasB for infection diagnostics.
This research could create a foundation for developing a PoC
device that would ease the diagnosis process of the pathogen P. aeruginosa. The project contained
multiple steps which to some degree were fulﬁlled. First, through the work with β-galactosidase a
phage display protocol was adjusted to accommodate the use of the Predator library. Then, all the
step involving antibody discovery was conducted. An additional step, examine and implementing
whole cell ELISA inﬂuenced the composition of conducted negative control ELISAs and added an
additional selection criteria for the ssdAbs. The experimental work resulting in the puriﬁed sdAb
fractions failed and therefore, the implementation of the sdAbs on a nitrocellular membrane could
not be completed. Consequently, a validation of the recognition abilities of the membrane was not
possible to perform. The technique of implementing antibodies was examined through the work
with antibodies targeting the protein PBP2a. Furthermore, the failed puriﬁcation caused two more
experimental steps which allowed the examination of why the puriﬁcation went wrong. Figure 24
is providing a graphical overview of all the steps carried out in this thesis.

The ﬁrst object of this thesis was the discovery of sdAbs against LasB which was performed using
on phage display selection. At ﬁrst, the phages displaying aﬃnity to LasB did not accumulate
over three selection rounds. Based on experience and better workﬂow in the laboratory a second
experiment containing three panning rounds displayed phages with accumulating aﬃnity to LasB.
It is believed, that a ﬂaw in the subsequent subcloning yielded the discovery of only six monoclonal
sdAbs. This process must be repeated to potentially discover a broader range of monoclonal sdAbs
with the ability to bind LasB. During the experimental work, an additional selection criteria for
sdAbs were implemented. Not only the phages were required to display high aﬃnity to puriﬁed
LasB and low unspeciﬁc binding, but also, they had to display a high aﬃnity to LasB when exposed
to a sample containing the P. aeruginosa bacteria itself and the related production of LasB. The
second object was to implement discovered sdAbs on a biosensor system. Due to failed puriﬁcation,
this step was not possible to conduct. The expression and later puriﬁcation were not repeated
due to the ﬁxed end time of the thesis. However, the implementation of another antibody was
conducted to allow experience-based reﬂection. As no implementation of puriﬁed monoclonal sdAb
fractions was performed, no validation of the recognition abilities of the nitrocellulose membrane
implemented sdAbs were carried out. The failed puriﬁcation lead to several experiments which were
not a part of the original experimental strategy. These experiments lead to knowledge regarding
why the puriﬁcation failed and it can be concluded that only one of the selected monoclonal sdAbs,
TPL0049 02 B7, displayed a phagemid insert which can cause the expression of sdAbs. The other
monoclonal sdAb, TPL0049 03 B53, was selected from a range of colonies based on the promising
monoclonal sdAb, B5, and therefore it was believed that the aﬃnity to LasB from the original
monoclonal sdAb existed for all the obtained colonies. Based on the examination of the plasmid
DNA insert of the monoclonal sdAb this assumption was proven to be wrong. By comparing
other obtained DNA sequences with the one for TPL0049 02 B7 another promising candidate was
located as similar tendencies in the DNA sequences were visible.
The work presented in this
thesis is to some extent successful and provides important experience and knowledge to the ﬁeld
of discovering sdAbs for diagnostic purposes. However, further optimization and work are needed
to utilize the full potential of the discovery pipeline displayed in this thesis. It is believed that the
two monoclonal sdAbs, TPL0049 02 B7 and TPL0049 03 D101, are monoclonal sdAbs displaying
high aﬃnity towards LasB and they can be expressed, puriﬁed and implemented on a nitrocellulose
membrane where they can be used for the detection of P. aeruginosa.

Thesis objective
Pseudomonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa) infections can be life threatening and cause severe com-
plications if untreated. Due to the need of rapid and aggressive treatment with antibiotics P.
aeruginosa has shown a tendency to develop antibiotic-resistance.
As infections caused by P.
aeruginosa become a bigger and bigger threat world wide fast, sensitive and speciﬁc Point-Of-
Care (PoC) diagnostic devices are more needed than ever. POC diagnostic devices are achievable
through the use of paper-based lateral ﬂow devices (LDF) among other techniques. Paper-based
LDF provides the opportunity to implement antigen-speciﬁc antibodies on a low cost, easy man-
ufacturing device, which provide a fast output. POC diagnostic devices provide a fast and cheap
supplement to laboratory-based diagnostic techniques such as ELISA, which is a relative time-
consuming and expensive process. This master thesis aims to identify, characterize, and produce
at least two single-domain antibodies sdAbs speciﬁc for the P. aeruginosa LasB-protein(in lab
scale). This work will include phage display experimentation, ELISA assays, subcloning, PCR,
DNA sequencing, and characterization of single-domain antibodies targeting the Las-B protein.
More over the project will include implementing the developed antibodies in a paper-based lateral
ﬂow assay and qualitatively evaluate the recognition ability of this system.
Necessary knowl-
edge about phage display technology, protein science, and antibody discovery techniques will be
acquired.
Moreover an understanding regarding diﬀerent discovery methodologies for eﬀective
selection and screening of target-binding proteins will obtained through out the work with this
master thesis.

– Handling and grow bacteria, infecting them with virus and isolating hits
– Performing molecular cloning in bacteria
– Amplifying and sequencing DNA
– Screening antibodies for binding to a speciﬁc protein target
– Characterizing antibody binders and/or inhibitors
