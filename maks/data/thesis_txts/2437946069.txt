Preface
This master thesis investigates the possibility to increase the eﬃciency of wall el-
ements by utilization of the super-light principle invented by prof. Kristian Dahl
Hertz from Technical University of Denmark. The ambition is to reach a signif-
icant decrease in the self-weight and necessary reinforcement of supporting walls
while matching the structural capacities of existing solutions.
The proposed designs are analysed with the Rankine formula, FEM-analyses,
stringer method and strut and tie to deliver the most favourable proposal while
satisfying requirements and recommendations prescribed by the Eurocodes, Dan-
ish Standard and the Danish Building Regulations, BR18.
Special thanks to my supervisor Prof. Kristian Dahl Hertz for thorough and bright
advise throughout the project, both in regard to practical solutions and technical
approaches as well as providing me with a wide range of articles, reports and doc-
uments on ﬁre and acoustic conditions among others.
I also want to thank Abeo A/S for assistance in regard to granting me permission
to reproduce technical drawings of their super-light deck elements.

Abstract
Combining light aggregate concrete with concrete of higher strength in a single structural element is
not a new principle. Utilising the capacities of each material in super-light structures, as the concept is
denoted, has existed for more than ten years and has generated lightweight elements with remarkable
structural capacities in addition to providing environmental and economical beneﬁts, e.g. the super-
light deck (SL-Deck) and pearl chain bridges.
This project investigates and evaluates on the possibility to give shape to a precast wall element
with signiﬁcant reduction in self-weight and reinforcement requirements while matching the structural
capacities of existing walls by exploiting the advantages of the super-light principle.
A theoretical case study is conducted with reapplication of materials applied in the SL-Decks for
computational analyses with stringer method, Rankine formula, strut and tie as well as FEM-analysis
for a wall element measuring three meters in height. The structural capacities were compared for a
wide range of material composition possibilities, where it was found, that a sandwich arrangement with
lightweight concrete ﬂanges and a high strength concrete core provided the most advantages in regard
to utilization of the material capacities, ﬁre resistance, sound insulation, application ﬂexibility and
production complexity.
The resulting estimated environmental and economical potentials are compelling for the proposed
super-light wall element (SL-Wall) compared to a solid reference wall. The 200 mm SL-Wall conﬁgu-
ration would reduce the self-weight by up to 28%, provided that sound insulation is the dimensioning
factor, it would provide approximately similar structural capacity, the required volume of bearing con-
crete is reduced by more than 50%, the lower limit to required reinforcement is reduced by more than
50%, the self-load induced by the wall is signiﬁcantly reduced resulting in the possibility to transport
more elements simultaneously. The 200 mm wall is approximated to be able to sustain a standard ﬁre
for 90 minutes and, ﬁnally, initializing the production of the SL-Wall is not associated with a signiﬁcant
investment due to its simple composition of materials and reinforcement.

Introduction
According to the worldwide consultancy agency, McKinsey, not a single other industry has experi-
enced a similarly weak growth in productivity over the past 20 years as the construction industry.
It is estimated that both clients and construction ﬁrms will beneﬁt greatly from lower bills and faster
completions, but the implementation of prefabrication is missing encouragement to spread globally. [33]
One initiative to convince the construction industry across the globe to realise the long term beneﬁts of
pre-cast elements is by fully utilizing the endless possibilities and the added ﬂexibility associated with
working with pre-cast concrete.
With the salaries of construction workers in Denmark being relatively high compared to many other
countries, prefabrication has gained a solid footing in the Danish construction industry. This is due to
the mitigated completion time from the ﬁrst shovel hits the ground to completion of the building.
Today, most greenﬁeld building projects in Denmark, both solid wall panels supported and column
frame structures, consist of elements prefabricated far from the construction site. While this develop-
ment, along with the increasing application of digitalization processes, has a positive impact on the
productivity, many of the possible advantages are often missing.
In addition to reinforcement, solid wall elements in Denmark are homogeneous in the nature that they
commonly consist of only one material; concrete with densities of 1900-2200 kg/m3 and compressive
strengths of 25-65 MPa. This despite the possibilities of producing concrete with compressive strengths
of more than 250 MPa, and of making a composition of several types of concrete and other materials
within a single element to place compressive forces in optimal paths and applying light material where
high strength is not needed. [20]
Inspired by ancient structures and the human body with compressive resistant bones and stabilizing
soft tissue, Prof. Kristian Dahl Hertz of the Technical University of Denmark has re-invented the ap-
proach on concrete structures. [20]
The conceptualisation is called super-light and is based on a composition of strong and lightweight
aggregate located eﬀectively in the structure. It is intended to challenge the common impression that
steel structures are lighter than concrete structures. Full-scale tests and actual applications, however,
have proved that if steel structures are light, then super-light structures are truly... super-light. [20]
While the super-light concept has resulted in the popular super-light decks (SL-Decks) and the pearl-
chain principle by post-tensioned pre-cast beams, a thorough investigation is yet to be made into the
possibilities of reinventing the wall via the super-light concept.
Today, most wall panels erected in Denmark have bearing capacities that far exceed the design load.
This is due to national requirements on sound transmittance through structural elements between ad-
jacent rooms and buildings. Where homogeneous concrete wall panels are advised to attain a surface
weight of 440 kg/m2, acoustic tests of SL-Decks have shown, that the necessary surface weight can be
reduced for elements composed by diﬀerent concrete types are able to reach the same threshold sound
insulation value. [4] [30]
Where the SL-Decks and pearl-chains have pushed the limits of the range of spans and bearing capac-
ities, the goal with this project is to achieve similarly beneﬁting properties for structural walls, which
will hopefully lead to a decrease in need for reinforcement and supports while increasing the usability
and architectural opportunities while keeping costs and environmental impact at a minimum.

With inspiration from the SL-Deck, this project investigates the outer dimensions of a standard concrete
wall element for various partial replacements of bearing concrete with light aggregate. The resulting
diﬀerent cross sections will be subjected to static analyses in regard to realistic application cases for
which the necessary reinforcement is assessed and compared to threshold values. Furthermore, the

As the Eurocodes and Danish standards and building regulations in the end dictate what structural
elements can be erected in Denmark for most applications, the SL-Wall will be proposed in compliance
with these.

Central aspects and material assessment
2.1
Environmental impact
The construction industry is estimated to account for around 5% of the global CO2 emissions. With a
production of around 3 tons of concrete for every human on the planet annually, concrete is the second
most expended substance after water. [31]
With the cement production growing by ∼2.5% annually and with nearly 1 ton of CO2 emitted for
every ton of cement, the impact from optimizing the application of cement is considerable. [31]
While the type of cement applied in a concrete has a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the structural behaviour,
the compressive strength of the resulting concrete is largely inﬂuenced by the choice and amount of
aggregates as well as the water/cement ratio. Often adding less water in the cement paste will result
in stronger concrete. Too little water in the mixture, however, will make it become too dry which gives
a porous and weak concrete due to a lacking cohesion within the concrete. With use of plasticizers
and other additives in the concrete mix, it has proved possible to mitigate the porous behaviour and
manufacture compact concrete with much higher compressive strengths than in the past. [1]
Besides increasing the eﬃciency of the cement in the concrete, it is possible to limit the total CO2
emissions by switching to sustainable fuel for the cement kilns, which currently represents ∼40% of the
total cement emissions. [31]
The SL-Deck in production consists partly of lightweight expanded clay aggregate, which is produced
under heat from burning waste with additional heat being distributed as district heating.
Along with the more sustainable energy consumption, the lower self-weight of super-light structures
and more eﬀective application of cement in diﬀerent types of concrete, total energy and CO2 savings
of more than 50% can be achieved. [20]
If it is possible to expand the super-light concept to more types of structural elements with success,
the environment will beneﬁt greatly.
2.2
Monetary beneﬁt
With lower self-weight and more eﬀective application of materials, the gravitational load of the struc-
tural elements themselves is also reduced which leads to a reduction in design load.
Ultimately, material saving of more than 50% is deemed realistic in certain design cases. [20]
It is estimated that a super-light column with light aggregate material exterior and strong concrete
center can cost around 1/6 of a steel column with equal load bearing capacity. Choosing a super-light
arch over a welded steel beam can even result in a price 47 times lower due to the shape and special
moment distribution of the arch. [20]
In some cases it is also possible to beneﬁt from the insulating material properties of applied lightweight
materials when dimensioning the necessary heat insulation on facades, ﬂoors and roofs.
Furthermore, transportation costs are often directly proportional with the total weight of the elements.
By making the elements lighter, it is possible to transport a larger number on e.g. each truck to the

2.3
Sound conditions
One of the major factors preventing current wall panels in Denmark from utilizing the bearing capacity
of the applied materials most eﬃciently is the national regulations on noise transmittance and sound
insulation.
Acoustic Design of Super-light Structures, Christensen, J. E. (2013) [4] provides insight to the sound
transmittance properties of super-light structures.
The SL22-Deck with a thickness of 22 cm has been subjected to acoustic tests and compared with the
results of a standard hollow core slab with similar element thickness. The tests concluded that the
airborne sound insulation was ∼3.2 times better for the super-light deck than for the hollow core slab
in terms of reducing airborne sound transmittance. Where the hollow core slab consists of nothing
but homogeneous concrete with occasional pipe-shaped air voids, the SL-Deck is a composition of two
types of materials; one strong concrete and with ﬁlling made of lightweight expanded clay aggregate.
The sound transmittance through an element is heavily inﬂuenced by the material dependent eigenfre-
quency of the element, which is largely associated with the material stiﬀness. The two constituents of
SL-Decks have a stiﬀness ratio of ∼1:10. This mitigates the risk of resonance for impact noise, and the
diﬀerent dynamic eigenperiods of the materials will turn more of the sound pressure waves into heat
between the materials. [4] [8] [17]
The Danish Building Regulations as of 2018 (BR18) require, that ”buildings must have satisfactory
noise conditions in terms of health and comfort in consideration of their use” (§ 368 [5]), with demand
for consideration of sound transmission between rooms/adjacent buildings. Furthermore, noise from
particular acoustic sources e.g. technical installations, roads and railways must be appraised. [5]
The Building Regulations on sound conditions are largely referring to diﬀerent limit values deﬁned
and listed in Danish Standard 490:2007: Sound classiﬁcations of dwellings (DS 490) [7] for various
construction classes and applications which are diﬀerentiated in the BR18.
While the required Danish threshold values for sound conditions of structures are derived from an
aﬃliation of the Building Regulations and DS 490, there exists no comprehensive approved deﬁnitive
relation between material properties and sound absorption abilities for any existing construction ma-
terial.
Ultimately, whether the sound absorption/transmission properties of any room-encircling structural el-
ement are in compliance with the national noise insulation requirements ultimately depend on physical
sound measurements.
When the noise properties of a ﬁnished structure are sought to be compared with the requirements,
the key values are determined through thorough ﬁeld measurements in accordance with international
standards; DS/EN ISO 10052 - Acoustics – Field measurements of airborne and impact sound insula-
tion and of service equipment sound – Survey method [15].
While they are deﬁnitive, relying completely on ﬁeld measurements is not desired as it can result in
legally forced alterations or additions to the structure in order to achieve the required sound insulation.
This is associated with signiﬁcant economical risk. Furthermore, ﬁeld measurement tests are likely
to provide undesirable results as the sound is able to travel along several paths from the source to
the receiver, see Figure 1. This is associated with the risk of unwanted sound bridges and weak links
occurring, which can not always be easily determined and mitigated before the producton of untested

2.4
Material parameters
This theoretical project applies a total of four diﬀerent types of concrete for investigation; one refer-
ence normal strength concrete (fck = 35 MPa), a lightweight expanded clay aggregate (fck = 3 MPa),
a strong concrete (fck = 55 MPa), and lastly a high strength concrete (fck = 250 MPa).
Normal strength concrete with compressive strengths around 35 MPa is widely applied in the construc-
tion industry for homogeneous structural elements. This is often due to the sound insulation properties
which are mostly proportional with the surface weights of the respective elements, why concrete with
a higher strength is regularly unnecessary. Furthermore, this concrete type is chosen as the reference
material due to it matching the lower limit to characteristic compressive concrete strength of an ag-
gressive environmental class in accordance with Eurocode 2. [14]
The lightweight expanded clay aggregate will in this project be denoted lightweight concrete and matches
the material properties of the light material applied in the SL-Deck. [4]
Eurocode 2 does not contain advisories on light weight concrete with a compressive strength fck < 12
MPa. A realistic tensile strength is approximated, however, by following the development of strength
classes for heavier types of light weight concrete (Table 11.3.1 [14]): For an estimated mean compressive
strength of the light material of fcm = 5 MPa (fck = 3 MPa) the design tensile strength is found to
be 0.17 MPa. This small addition to the total tensile strength is deemed negligible when compared to
tensile strength of stronger concrete. The tensile strength of light concrete is therefore not included in
further calculations. [14]
The strong concrete has material parameters closely related to the SL-Deck but with a small increase
in compressive strength and stiﬀness and is advised for in Eurocode 2.
The high strength concrete with a compressive strength of 250 MPa is only rarely applied due to its
explosive spalling behaviour when subjected to heat, i.e. ﬁre. When the brittle concrete is applied
within a protective shell, however, high strength concrete can be fully utilized while safeguarding its
unstable ﬁre resistance. [21]

2.5
Super-light wall compositions
The desired wall element is a wall that excels in regard to all of the following; minimum reinforcement
requirement, ﬂexible cover layer, ﬁre resistance, sound insulation, production complexity, self-weight,
application ﬂexibility and material utilization.
Only the creativity is the limit to the manner in which lightweight aggregate and load bearing concrete
can be composed. The unlimited amount of composition approaches, however, can be divided into two
main approaches, which, depending on the outcomes of the investigations, are possible to combine in
order to fully utilize the beneﬁts of each behavioural aspect if so desired:
One with a homogeneous material layout along the height and length of the wall element, the sandwich
wall, and another with a homogeneous material along the depth or thickness of the wall, the cavitated
wall, see Figure 9.
Figure 9: Illustrated examples of a) sandwhich wall, and b) cavitated wall.
The cavitated wall acts as an obvious noise bridge compared to the sandwich wall elements and their
expected sound insulating behaviour, but as any combination of the two layout approaches is possible,
it is found worth investigating the structural behaviour of these as well.
While the sandwich wall does not necessarily have to be symmetric around the center of the wall
thickness, this is chosen as most beneﬁts follows from either the bearing core being fully shielded from
potential ﬁre or from achieving a high structural capacity with load bearing shells, here denoted ﬂanges.
The sandwich wall with structural concrete ﬂanges will need additional editing to be able to transfer
shear between the two ﬂanges. This can be executed in many ways; with stirrups or Carbon Fibre
Reinforced Polymer (CFRP) rods, or with solid concrete bridges in an unlimited range of diﬀerent
conﬁgurations.

3.2
Approach on structural capacities
Evaluating the theoretical structural capacities of hollowed/cavitated wall elements demands other cal-
culation methods than stated in the Eurocodes and Danish Standards.
Therefore, computational structural engineering is applied in the form of Finite Element Method
(FEM). Each wall element constituent is divided into a ﬁnite number of elements organised in a mesh.
The overall stress distribution is determined from local force transfer between adjacent key-points on
each element in the mesh.
The accuracy of the FEM-analysis largely depends on the mesh size when it comes to e.g. elements
with voids leading to peaking stress accumulations at internal corners. A solid and square element,
however, is less inﬂuenced by the mesh size when it comes to approximating the stress distribution. [27]
The commercial computer software Abaqus FEA is utilized for this purpose under license of Technical
University of Denmark.
As a ﬁne mesh demands more computational power and is more time consuming, the lowest necessary
mesh size is applied which provides results within the tolerance margin of a very ﬁne mesh.
Cavitated wall Type 1 is the subject to the mesh investigation for an arbitrary vertical compressive
load (Figure 18), where the convergence of the determined maximum induced equivalent stress at the
corner is the decisive parameter for a series of mesh partitions. The total wall element size is 4m/3m
(length/height) as visualized in Figure 17.

3.5
Assessment of utility
In contradiction to the expected behaviour of the sandwich wall layout, the applied concrete is not
utilized to a satisfying degree in terms of bearing capacity.
By combining the composition principles and achieving a partly cavitated sandwich wall, the weight
can potentially be reduced signiﬁcantly to sustain an external load that is not extreme.
If the walls are to be subjected to sound insulation requirements, the purely cavitated composition
conﬁguration is expected to demand a thick wall, as the materials are constant trough the thickness
of the wall. Alternatively, additional noise insulating surfacing materials should be provided on the
cavitated walls to satisfy the airborne sound insulation criteria. [18]
For bearing walls elements not circumferring any inhabited rooms, however, the cavitated walls are
deemed applicable if mitigating the self-weight is prioritized and if a higher wall thickness is demanded
to comply with surrounding elements instead of being dependent on bearing capacity.
With risk of tension occurring remaining around the outer corners and the inner horizontal edges of

==PAGE:40==

Technical University of Denmark
the cavitations, there is likely to be needed rebar reinforcement purely for the wall elements to sustain
a vertical compressive load.
Where the cavitated wall can prove beneﬁcial, however, is when it comes to the minimum reinforcement
requirements prescribed by Danish Standards and Eurocode. It states that the minimum reinforcement
relies on the cross section area of concrete (0.2% of Ac in the vertical direction and 0.1% of Ac in the

When it comes to the production of the cavitated walls, the costs associated with investing in produc-

types.

4.3
Assessment of utility
As anticipated, the investigation into the sandwich wall as SL-Wall indicated that a constant thickness
of bearing concrete along the height of the wall results in a better utilization of the the materials than
for the cavitated wall types.
In regard to production, the sandwich walls with bearing concrete in ﬂanges will likely experience an
increase in layout complexity, as the ﬂanges will require the possibility to transfer shear between one
another. If this is not performed with the application of an extensive amount of steel stirrups or other
materials, this could be accomplished with bearing concrete connections. These are presumed to behave
as noise bridges, and it is likely that the conﬁguration will require an increased surface weight compared
to the SL-Deck to satisfy the sound insulation.
Additionally, having only bearing concrete at the ﬂanges of the sandwich wall limits the ﬂexibility in
regard to environmental class as well as limiting the possibility to apply pretension strands if such are
desired.
Furthermore, the conﬁguration with lightweight concrete core is likely to require solid bearing concrete
at the edges to be able to sustain imposed loads such as horizontal ties to decks and vertical to adjacent
wall elements.
Whether the bearing concrete is located as ﬂanges or cores, the minimum reinforcement required by
the Danish Standards and Eurocode is consistent. This means that a sandwich wall conﬁguration with
bearing concrete in ﬂanges is subject to reinforcement in both ﬂanges. This indirectly increases the
minimum reinforcement requirement, as both rebar diameter and interdistances should comply with
prescribed limits stated in the standards. [13] [14]
Where the SL-Decks consists of only two layers of constituents, the sandwich wall contains three why
the sound insulating properties can be presumed to match or even exceed the threshold surface weight
found for the SL-Deck. This, provided that each layer is represented to a signiﬁcant degree.
Where a solid 35 MPa reference wall element demands a thickness of 205 mm to satisfy the recom-
mended surface weight for sound insulation, a high strength sandwich wall is presumed to reach similar
insulating capabilities at a total thickness of 150 mm of which 40 mm is lightweight concrete. [18]
The wall type with the most potential is therefore deemed the sandwich wall with high strength con-
crete core, from hereafter denoted the SL-Wall.

load cases.

5.1
Stringer method
The stringer method is applicable for structures subjected to plane stresses and is based on the lower
bound theorem of plastic theory. The reinforcement requirement found with the stringer method is
therefore designed for concrete experiencing cracking. This aﬀects the calculative structural capacities
of concrete which are signiﬁcantly reduced in the calculations and a lower bound theorem is achieved.
[28]
The approach is to carry normal stresses in internal, predeﬁned located concentrated stringers in the
structure. Distributed loads are transformed into equivalent nodal forces located at predeﬁned stringers.
Whereas compressive stringer forces occasionally can be carried by the concrete, all tensile stringer
forces should be taken by reinforcement bars.
Between each pair of stringers a shear panel is located which carries the induced shear stresses. The
determined shear reinforcement requirement is formulated as tensile capacity pr width/length of the
shear panel. While shear reinforcement generally is applied to sustain the determined shear stresses,
it should be investigated whether the concrete can sustain the shear alone to avoid applying unneeded
steel.
Prior to evaluating the necessity for reinforcement, the stringer method is applied for normalised loads,
i.e. load sizes of 1 for the given unit (N or N/m). This provides enhanced insight to the resulting forces
and stresses, were the element to be tested for a range of diﬀerent load sizes. Furthermore, the stringer
method is conducted for a variable thickness t, allowing for applying the results on all investigated
SL-Wall thicknesses with ease.
5.1.1
Pure shear
The ﬁrst load case is the SL-Wall exposed to external shear as depicted on Figure 54.
With asymmetric load applications around the centre axes, it is not possible to simplify the structure
or exploit symmetry for this load case.
Locating stringers from edge to edge of the wall adjacent to the boundaries of the void and the external
boundaries leaves a total of 8 shear panels. See App. B.1: Stringer method - illustrated - Load case 1:
Pure shear
This results in more shear panels than equilibrium equations. Therefore, the shear stresses in three
zones can be chosen freely, provided that they are not the only constituents in any equilibrium, e.g. all
panels along any edge. [28]
The shear stresses should not be chosen without thought, however. This is a system where all the
shear panels subjected to external loads will experience shear with the same direction. If the statically
undetermined system is assigned arbitrary entrance parameters, which are necessary to estimate the
distribution, one or more remaining shear panels may experience shear in the opposite direction than
the rest in order to satisfy equilibrium. In this case the resulting internal stringer forces have the
possibility to exceed that of the external force, which is directly delivered at one end of the string. This
would lead to an excessive application of reinforcement and should be avoided.
When the shear stresses of the panels are to be chosen, it is of beneﬁt to the further stringer force
calculations, and realistic accuracy, that the shear stresses of adjacent panels are as similar as possible.
This will mitigate the risk of needing additional shear reinforcement through distributing the shear

Whereas solid walls are presumed to provide spacing for most reinforcement applications eﬀortlessly,
the ﬂexibility is reduced signiﬁcantly when it comes to sandwich walls with surface weights of ∼315

7.1
Fire resistance
The SL-Wall is exposed to a simulated standard ﬁre from both sides of the element, see Figure 89.
Figure 89: Sandwich wall element with high strength concrete core and ﬁre exposure on both sides of

7.2
Producing the SL-Wall
When the SL-Deck is produced, ﬁrst specially designed moulds are applied to shape the lightweight
concrete into blocks on the casting bed. While still in the moulds, the lightweight concrete is subjected
to vibrations before the moulds are lifted. When the reinforcement has been secured in place between
the lightweight blocks, the strong concrete is cast. After a ﬁnal vibration, the SL-Deck is ready for
setting. A ﬁnished SL-Deck is shown in Figure 92.
Figure 92: SL-Deck post casting, Christensen, J. E. (2013) [4].
Producing the proposed SL-Wall is a more simple matter. Where the SL-Deck requires special moulds,
any established producer of concrete elements is expected to be able to produce the SL-Wall with little
to no eﬀort as no special moulds are required.
Casting the SL-Wall follows the same basic procedure as for The SL-Deck but with an added step due
to an additional layer of lightweight concrete.

7.3
Application opportunities
Doubling the frequency and increasing the diameters of the reinforcement mesh, the investigated 200
mm thick SL-Wall could eﬀortlessly sustain the compressive self-load from 20-30 walls - even with a
window void. This is, however, provided that the concrete can not sustain any shear. If the shear
capacity of the concrete is accounted for, then by solely increasing the capacities of the horizontal
stringer bars over and under the window void, at least 60 walls could be sustained.
Were all walls to be replaced by the investigated solid reference wall, then the horizontal stringers
around the window void would demand 40% more tensile capacity.
The real advantage of the SL-Wall, however, is to be found where moments are induced by wall ele-
ments that are not being directly supported in a structure. Exchanging the solid wall elements with
the proposed SL-Wall would reduce the self-weight of the wall panels with ∼28%, provided that the
walls are dimensioned in respect to required sound insulation criteria.
Designing a structure solely with SL-Walls and SL-Decks, it is possible to create more daring and ﬂexible
architectural solutions than seen today. An example is shown where buildings can be constructed with
large parts of structure overhanging empty space, see example in Figure 96.

Conclusion
The investigation into cavitated walls as super-light walls clariﬁed that the chosen constituent materials
were not utilized to a satisfying degree for any of the three load cases. For the compressive load case
simulated with a FEM-analysis, the results showed that none of the 17 examined designs, with 50-20%
lightweight concrete, managed to keep the maximum induced stresses below 2.8 times that of a 0%
lightweight concrete reference wall. This, despite that a signiﬁcant stabilizing eﬀect was concluded for
the application of lightweight concrete in voids.
Besides the concluded lack of capacity exploitation for the materials, the cavitated wall principle is not
presumed to provide an enhanced noise insulation capability compared to existing, solid wall panels,
which is a central incentive behind this project.
The sandwich wall principle was analysed with the Rankine formula for various compositions of light-
weight concrete as stabilizer with either strong concrete (fck = 55 MPa) or high strength concrete
(fck = 250 MPa) as the bearing component. With uniform compositions along the height of the wall,
the sandwich wall proved to utilize the material capacities to a greater degree than the cavitated walls,
as was anticipated.
The conﬁgurations were evaluated for the lower threshold value that the surface weight should attain
to satisfy the presumed sound insulating capabilities required by Danish Building Regulations for walls
surrounding inhabitable spaces. Acoustic tests on a deck element (SL-Deck), with similar material
composition, concluded that the necessary surface weight could be lowered by 28% compared to oﬃcial
advisories for solid elements.
High strength concrete is vulnerable to ﬁre and should not be applied at outer edges of concrete ele-
ments. Even though a conﬁguration with strong concrete ﬂanges provided a small advantage to a high
strength core conﬁguration with a matching surface weight, several complications arise when looking
into the practicality of the bearing ﬂanges. Solid cross sections are necessary at wall edges to transfer
shear end tension to adjacent elements, transferring shear between the ﬂanges is associated with in-
creased complexity in regard to both design and production, and the limited space for reinforcement in
the thin ﬂanges led to the abandonment of the strong concrete ﬂanges conﬁguration as a super-light wall.
Due to the signiﬁcantly greater compressive capacity of high-strength concrete than strong concrete,
the sandwich wall with a high strength concrete core and lightweight concrete ﬂanges was deemed the
most advantageous and righteous for the Super-Light title, SL-Wall.
A welded reinforcement mesh was applied to satisfy the lower limits prescribed in Eurocode 2. The
resulting structural capacities of the SL-Wall, which was conﬁgured with a hollowed window void, were
analysed for three simulated load cases using the stringer method. The minimum reinforcement proved
to be suﬃcient in resisting pure shear induced by wind forces at a height of ∼30 m and in being
subjected to lifting with the self-weight as tensile load. For a compressive load case where the subject
was supporting nine similar three meter tall SL-Walls, however, an additional tensile capacity in the
bars should be provided around the window void to sustain the load. The compressive load case was
compared to a solid reference wall (fck = 35 MPa) subjected to the same scenario. While the reference
wall required a reinforcement mesh of more than twice the capacity of the SL-Wall, the horizontal bars
around the window void needed an additional 40% capacity due to the increased load size. The Rankine
lineload capacity of the reference wall demonstrated that the SL-Wall attains only a minor reduction
in bearing capacity.
For total thicknesses ranging from 200 to 240 mm of the SL-Wall, all but extra aggressive environ-
mental classes could be satisﬁed in regard to cover thickness. To satisfy this, either the total thickness
78 of 82
==PAGE:88==
Super-light wall elements

should be smaller or the surface weight should be increased by adding additional high strength concrete.
Connection details were analyses for horizontal ties to decks, continuous vertical ties along the height
as well as shear connection to adjacent walls. The investigation concluded that the SL-Wall can exceed
the requirements eﬀortlessly - even for a high consequence class.
The SL-Wall was subjected to a standard ﬁre from both sides with the ﬁre simulation software Con-
Fire.exe. The ﬁre resistance was taken as the time duration until the high strength concrete would
experience explosive spalling. As anticipated, the analysis demonstrated that the lightweight concrete
ﬂange thickness has a large inﬂuence on the heat development at the edge of the high strength concrete
core. The 200 mm thick SL-Wall, however, can sustain a stand ﬁre for 90 minutes.
Producing the SL-Wall should be signiﬁcantly less complex than both the SL-Deck, which is already in
production, as well as all cavitated wall elements. No special moulds or equipment should be required
to initiate the production.
The environmental and economical potentials are compelling for the 200 mm SL-Wall compared to the
reference element; the self-weight can be reduced by up to 28%, provided that sound insulation is the
dimensioning factor, the volume of bearing concrete is reduced by more than 50%, the lower limit to
required reinforcement is reduced by the same percentage, loads induced from walls can be signiﬁcantly
reduced and more elements can be transported simultaneously due to the reduced self-weight. Finally,
initializing the production of the SL-Wall is not associated with a signiﬁcant investment.

Future work
Several presumptions have been made when evaluating the capacities, ﬁre resistances and acoustic
properties. Therefore, in order to verify the design, full scale testing should conducted in regard to
validate the structural capacities. More importantly, lab tests should be performed to verify the sound
insulation properties and ﬁre resistance as these factors decide the applicability of the SL-Wall.
Further investigation should made into how the prescribed lower limit reinforcement, which is prescribed
in the Eurocodes and Danish Standards, could be lowered. The concrete alone is in most analysed cases
able to carry the induced shear without the addition of reinforcement.
In regard to achieving even further advantage to a traditional reference wall, it should be investigated
whether Carbon Fibre Reinforced Polymer (CFRP) rods, wood or similar can be applied to lower the
environmental footprint even further, when measures are taken to sustain tensile forces.

function upperlowerboundcorner(l x,l y,q,d r,R)
%%
UPPER-LOWER BOUND METHOD typ1, LC1 (corner analysis)
% l x:
Element width [m]
% l y:
Element height [m]
% q:
load [N/mˆ2]
%
% d r:
Minimum x-directional thickness of strong concrete in cross [m]
% R:
Radii [m]
% I y = 1/12*d rˆ3; %moment of inertia in regard to moment capacity
% sigma x
= 0;
% %upper bound
% for i = 1:size(R)
%
d(i,:)
= 0.25;
%
I y(i,:)
= 1/12*d(i,:)ˆ3;
%
moment up(i,:)
= 1/12*q*0.9*(l x-2*(d(i,:)+R(i)));
%moment [Nm]
%
sigma m up(i,:)
= moment up(i,:)/I y(i,:) *d(i,:)/2;
%stress [N/mˆ2]
%
sigma R up(i,:)
= 1+(q*0.9*l x/2-d r)/d r;
%
sigma up(i,:)
= sigma m up(i,:)+sigma R up(i,:);
% end
%
% %lower bound
% for i = 1:size(R)
%
d(i,:)
= 0.5;
%
I y(i,:)
= 1/12*d(i,:)ˆ3;
%
moment low(i,:)
= 1/24*q*0.9*(l x-2*(d(i,:)+R(i)));
%moment [Nm]
%
sigma m low(i,:)
= moment low(i,:)/I y(i,:) *d(i,:)/2 ;
%stress [N/mˆ2]
%
sigma R low(i,:)
= 1+(q*0.9*l x/2-d r)/d r;
%Vertical load.
%
sigma low(i,:)
= sigma m low(i,:)+sigma R low(i,:);
% end
%
% [sigma up, sigma low]
%% Moment distribution (TEKNISK STAABI, PG 83)
- Moments positive for tension inside frame
q
= 1;
%load [N/mˆ2]
R test = R(5);
%Minimum x-directional thickness of strong concrete in cross.
I y = 1/12*d rˆ3; %moment of inertia in regard to moment capacity
l x = 4;
%Element width [m]
l y = 3;
k = l y/l x;
%Stiffness coefficient
M supports = q*l xˆ2/(12*(k+2));
M corners = -q*l xˆ2/(6*(k+2));
M midt
= 1/8*q*l xˆ2;
[M midt, M supports, M corners]
d temp = sqrt(2)*d r+R test*(sqrt(2)-1);
I temp =
1/12*d tempˆ3;
M corners/I temp*d temp/2;
end
