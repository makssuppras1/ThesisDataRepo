Abstract
This project showcases a unique approach to developing and deploying a fungi classifi­
cation solution for the DTU Bioengineering department. It leverages state­of­the­art deep
learning models, including ResNet, DenseNet, and Vision Transformers (ViT), to address
the challenge of accurate fungal identification. The research also explores the potential
of more sophisticated data augmentation and texture analysis techniques.
The models were trained and validated on datasets of over 26 thousand images, showing
35 different genera and 110 different species, resulting in 110 unique classes. All photos
were taken in the laboratory every 30 minutes for each species, spanning between 4 and
7 days.
The project faced several challenges: limited training data, high computational demands
due to large image sizes, and the complexity of integrating texture analysis algorithms.
Nevertheless, the methodologies chosen and extensive testing allowed the ViT­16 to cor­
rectly classify the genus and species with over 90% accuracy while only being trained on
the first three days of observations.
The deployment of the model can be tested here: https://moldai.dkenez.dk/.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Fungal species identification is paramount in various fields, including medicine, agricul­
ture, and environmental science [1]. Traditionally, the identification process relies heavily
on manual observation and culturing techniques, which are labour­intensive and time­
consuming. These conventional methods often require several days for fungi to grow,
significantly delaying the analysis and decision­making processes. Such delays can hin­
der timely interventions, impacting the effectiveness of responses to fungal­related issues.
In recent years, advancements in Deep Learning (DL) and Computer Vision (CV) have
presented new opportunities to streamline and accelerate fungal identification. By lever­
aging these technologies, automated systems can recognise and classify fungal species
based on time­lapse images captured during their growth phases. This approach promises
to enhance the speed and accuracy of fungal identification, reducing human error and
improving scalability. Automated systems can handle large amounts of data efficiently,
provide consistent and reproducible results, and facilitate rapid processing of complex
datasets, ultimately leading to quicker and more reliable interventions and research out­
comes.
1.2
Domain knowledge
The following sections detail the traditional methods used for fungal identification, includ­
ing observation and culturing techniques, and highlight their inherent limitations.
1.2.1
Observation techniques
Microscopy
Microscopy is a manual observation technique that examines fungal structures under a
microscope to identify morphological characteristics such as spore shape, size, arrange­
ment, hyphal structures, and reproductive features [2]. This technique requires significant
expertise and experience to differentiate between species accurately, as many fungi have
similar morphological features.
Staining
Another example is staining, where special stains such as lactophenol cotton blue, cal­
cofluor white, and potassium hydroxide (KOH) preparations enhance the visibility of fungal
structures under the microscopes [3]. These stains help differentiate fungal cells from host
tissues and other microorganisms.
1.2.2
Culturing techniques
Agar plate
One of the culturing techniques involves isolating fungi by placing samples on nutrient­
rich agar plates and incubating them under specific conditions conducive to fungal growth.
Typical media include Sabouraud dextrose agar, potato dextrose agar, and malt extract
agar. The fungi are then identified based on colony morphology, colour, texture, and
growth rate [4].
Slide culture
A different method involves growing fungi on a small block of agar placed on a microscope
slide, which allows for direct microscopic observation of fungal growth and development
without disturbing the fungal structures [5].

1.3
Summary
These traditional methods have significant limitations. They are time­consuming, often
requiring several days to weeks for fungi to produce identifiable structures. This delay
is particularly problematic in clinical settings where timely diagnosis and treatment are
critical. Additionally, these techniques are labour­intensive, require skilled mycologists,
and are costly due to the need for specialised personnel and equipment.
Also, the accuracy of manual identification depends heavily on the expertise of the my­
cologist, and human error and subjectivity can lead to misidentification. These methods
are also not scalable for more extensive studies or high­throughput screening due to the
time and labour required for each sample, creating a significant bottleneck in research
and industrial applications.
Therefore, computer scientists and data analysts are exploring how DL and CV can rev­
olutionise this process. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and visual transformers
have great potential in image recognition tasks. These models can be trained to extract
and learn features from images, making them ideal for analysing fungal growth’s complex
and evolving structures.
Integrating these domains involves several challenges, including handling variations in
fungal growth patterns, ensuring the robustness of the models across different media,
and generalising the models to work with diverse fungal species.
1.4
Objectives
This project aims to develop an automated system for fungal species identification utilising
deep learning techniques. The specific objectives of the project are as follows:
1. Literature Review: Conduct a comprehensive literature review to understand the
current state of DL applications in biological sciences and identify critical insights
that can inform the research methodology.
2. Data Acquisition Exploration: Investigate the data acquisition process in laboratory
settings to understand the crucial aspects involved in capturing time­lapse images
of fungal growth.
3. Architecture Exploration: Research and evaluate diverse DL architectures, including
CNNs and ViT, to determine the most effective models for fungal identification.
4. Model Training: Train DL models capable of accurately identifying fungal species
from time­lapse images, leveraging selected architectures.
5. Model Robustness: Investigate strategies to handle variations in fungal growth pat­
terns across different media, enhancing the robustness and generalizability of the
developed models.
6. Performance Evaluation: Evaluate the performance of the trained models in pre­
dicting fungal species identity at various time points, assessing their accuracy and
reliability.
7. Web Application: Develop a user­friendly web application to deploy the trained mod­
els, allowing scientists to upload images and quickly receive rapid fungal species
predictions.
An end­to­end solution for automatic fungal identification is created by achieving these
objectives, which are explored more in the following chapters:

Research & Solution Proposal
2.1
Literature Review
The integration of ML, DL and CV techniques into fungal research represents a trans­
formative advancement in various fields, including healthcare, agriculture, and food pro­
cessing. This review explores multiple applications of these technologies in biological
research, highlighting fundamental studies and their contributions.
2.1.1
Machine Learning algorithms
The application of advanced ML algorithms for image­based analysis has demonstrated
significant potential. Przybył explored the use of Multi­Layer Perceptron (MLP), Radial
Basis Function (RBF), and CNNs to detect fungal contamination in rapeseed. Their study
revealed that CNNs achieved the highest classification accuracy of 89%, followed by MLP
with 90% and RBF with 85%. The study utilised a dataset of 520 images with a resolution
of 2572 × 1936 and 32­bit colour depth rapeseed images collected by stereoscopy micro­
scope. This research underscores the effectiveness of CNNs in accurately detecting fun­
gal contamination, showcasing the potential of advanced DL techniques in non­invasive
detection methods in agricultural products [6].
Jones developed a supervised ML approach using the GentleBoost algorithm to auto­
matically score complex and subtle cellular phenotypes in high­throughput image­based
screens. By extracting numerical descriptors for each cell using the CellProfiler software
and employing iterative feedback from researchers, they trained the GentleBoost algo­
rithm to classify and score diverse cellular morphologies in RNA interference screens. The
dataset of 40,000 fluorescence images from RNAi­treated human cells was used, and the
performance was assessed using enrichment scores and cross­validation. This method
demonstrated improved efficiency and accuracy in identifying rare and subtle cellular phe­
notypes, even in the absence of positive control samples, highlighting the potential of ML
in high­content screening [7]
2.1.2
Deep Learning Applications
DL has remarkably performed in various image classification tasks, particularly medical
imaging. Tania used pre­trained CNNs models such as AlexNet and Inception v3 to de­
tect assay types, specifically enzyme­linked immunosorbent (ELISA) and Lateral Flow
Assay (LFA). By leveraging transfer learning, they adapted these models to classify the
assay types with high accuracy, achieving 100% accuracy in visual recognition. The LFA
dataset consisting of 1600 RGB images was used. This study highlights the potential of
DL models to assist users in performing colourimetric tests using personal digital devices
and calibrating image­based colourimetric classifications [8].
In another study, Kuo applied the DenseNet architecture to diagnose fungal keratitis using
corneal photographs. The DenseNet model, known for its efficiency in parameter usage
and ability to handle complex image data, achieved a sensitivity of 71% and a specificity
of 68%, with an overall diagnostic accuracy of approximately 70%. This performance
was comparable to that of corneal specialists but significantly better than non­corneal
ophthalmologists. 288 eye RGB photos were collected from 288 laboratory­confirmed
patients. As for evaluation metrics, sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, and
negative predictive value were used. This study emphasises the promise of DL in enhanc­
ing diagnostic accuracy and assisting clinicians in managing fungal keratitis effectively,
particularly in primary care and rural settings [9].

Zieliński employed deep neural networks such as AlexNet, DenseNet169, InceptionV3,
and ResNet to classify fungi species from microscopic images. These models, pre­trained
on the ImageNet database and fine­tuned for the specific task of fungi classification, sig­
nificantly reduced the time and cost of fungal species identification. The models were
trained on 180 microscopy images (9 strains × 2 preparations × 10 images) of resolution
3600 × 5760 × 3 with a 16­bit intensity range in every pixel, and the evaluation metrics
included accuracy, precision and recall. This research also introduced a deep bag­of­
words approach combined with Support Vector Machines (SVM) to improve classification
accuracy, demonstrating the benefits of hybrid models in enhancing DL performance [10].
Vu investigated the application of deep learning techniques for fungal sequence classi­
fication. The study compared CNNs and Deep Belief Networks (DBNs) with traditional
methods like BLAST and the Ribosomal Database Project classifier (RDP) classifier. The
CNNs and DBNs models, trained on barcode datasets, outperformed BLAST and RDP
regarding speed and accuracy for taxonomic assignments. The CNNs model, in particu­
lar, demonstrated superior performance in classifying fungal sequences, achieving higher
Matthews correlation coefficients across various taxonomic levels. The yeast dataset con­
sisted of 3,784 ITS sequences of 1,035 species, 138 genera, 45 families, 18 orders, and 9
classes. This study highlights the potential of DL to enhance the efficiency and accuracy
of fungal classification in metagenomics [11].
2.1.3
Computer Vision Techniques
CV techniques have been extensively applied in agricultural and industrial settings, demon­
strating significant improvements in efficiency and accuracy. Yin reviewed the use of CV
and ML technologies in the mushroom industry, focusing on species identification, quality
classification, and automated harvesting. Essential methods included using CNNs for im­
age recognition and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) to create synthetic mush­
room images to enhance training datasets. These technologies addressed challenges
such as labour shortages and the need for high­throughput phenotyping, underscoring
the potential of integrating Artificial Intelligence (AI)­driven approaches to advance mush­
room production and quality control [12].
Evangelisti developed AMFinder, a tool utilising CNNs to identify and quantify arbuscular
mycorrhizal fungal colonisation in plant roots. By analysing images of ink­stained roots,
AMFinder accurately detected colonised root sections and intraradical hyphal structures,
offering a robust and reproducible method for phenotyping arbuscular mycorrhizal fungal
colonisation dynamics. The tool demonstrated high­confidence predictions across multi­
ple plant hosts and arbuscular mycorrhizal fungal species, supporting the documentation
of mycorrhizal interactions in various experimental conditions [13].
A review of actual implementations of CV techniques in the agriculture industry can be
found in the article by Ghazal [14]. The authors show that classical CV algorithms have
been effectively utilised in crop yield estimation alongside more advanced ML and DL
techniques.
Traditional methods such as image segmentation and thresholding have
been employed to extract key features from agricultural imagery, aiding in the identification
and quantification of crops. For example, the Partial Least Squares algorithm was used
to generate yield models for winter wheat based on various vegetation indices, achiev­
ing root mean square errors of 693.9 kg/ha and 786.5 kg/ha for predicted and measured
yields, respectively. In another application, connected component detection was used to
count mangoes in images, contributing to their yield estimation approach. Despite the
broad use of DL, classical CV techniques remain valuable for their computational effi­
ciency and ability to handle more straightforward tasks.

2.1.4
Integrated Approaches
Combining traditional DL architectures with innovative data augmentation techniques has
shown promising results in improving classification accuracy. The study on diabetic foot
thermograph classification introduced a novel data augmentation technique using the
Fourier transform to address the limited availability of training data. This technique in­
volved modifying the amplitude of the Fourier transform of the images while preserving the
spatial distribution, effectively generating new training samples. The ResNet50v2 model,
trained with this augmented data, achieved perfect classification accuracy, demonstrating
the effectiveness of this approach.[15].
Zieliński also explored integrating deep bag­of­words approaches with SVM to enhance
fungi classification accuracy from microscopic images. By leveraging pre­trained CNNs
architectures and fine­tuning them for specific classification tasks, the study demonstrated
the benefits of hybrid models in improving DL performance, reducing the time and cost of
species identification and potentially enhancing clinical diagnostics [10].
2.1.5
Challenges
Despite the successes, several challenges remain in applying ML and DL techniques.
One significant challenge is the limited availability of high­quality training data. Tech­
niques such as data augmentation, as seen in the study on diabetic foot thermograph
classification [15], can help mitigate this issue by generating additional training samples.
However, acquiring diverse and representative datasets remains crucial for training robust
models.
Another challenge is requiring specialised hardware and computational resources to train
complex DL models. The computational demands of training and fine­tuning deep neural
networks can be substantial, necessitating access to high­performance computing infras­
tructure.
The variability in image quality and noise can also affect model performance. Pre­processing
steps such as normalisation, noise reduction, and data augmentation are essential to
enhance model accuracy. Kuo demonstrated the importance of pre­processing corneal
photographs to improve the diagnostic accuracy of their DL model for fungal keratitis di­
agnosis [9].
Lastly, integrating these technologies in real­world applications requires overcoming prac­
tical issues such as user acceptance, regulatory compliance, and continuous model up­
dates to maintain accuracy over time. Ensuring that AI­driven tools are user­friendly and
comply with relevant regulations is critical for their successful adoption in various indus­
tries.
2.2
Method proposal
This research aims to develop an effective fungi classification system using advanced DL
architectures and sophisticated data augmentation techniques. Based on the extensive
literature review, ResNet, DenseNet, and ViT are state­of­the­art models with significant
promise in various image classification tasks. Additionally, integrating multiple data aug­
mentation techniques and texture analysis algorithms will enhance model robustness and
accuracy.
2.2.1
Choice of Models
ResNet
ResNet is known for its ability to train deep networks by mitigating the vanishing gradient

problem through residual connections [16]. This architecture performs superiorly in nu­
merous image classification tasks due to its depth and ability to capture complex patterns.
Vu demonstrated the effectiveness of ResNet in fungal sequence classification, achieving
higher accuracy and efficiency compared to traditional methods [11]. Given its robustness
and proven performance in related tasks, ResNet is a suitable choice.
DenseNet
DenseNet is selected for its unique architecture, where each layer is connected to every
other layer, facilitating better feature propagation and reducing the number of parameters
required. Kuo applied DenseNet to diagnose fungal keratitis, achieving commendable
diagnostic accuracy. This indicates that DenseNet can handle complex image data effec­
tively and provide high classification performance [9].
Vision Transformers
ViT leverages the transformer architecture for image classification tasks, showing excep­
tional performance in natural language processing. ViT uses self­attention mechanisms
to capture global dependencies in the input data, offering a different approach than tradi­
tional CNNs. The potential of transformers in medical image classification, as shown in
the study on diabetic foot thermograph classification using a Transformer model, suggests
that ViT could be highly effective for the image classification task as well [15].
2.2.2
Data Augmentation Techniques
Basic Techniques
Horizontal and Vertical Flips: These simple augmentation techniques are widely used
to increase the diversity of the training dataset, preventing overfitting and improving the
model’s ability to generalise. Studies such as Tania’s and Kuo’s have successfully em­
ployed these techniques to enhance model performance [8][9]. Color Jitter: Introducing
random changes in brightness, contrast, saturation, and hue can help the model become
invariant to colour variations in the images. This technique has been used effectively in
the literature to improve classification accuracy [10].
Advanced augmentation
Fourier Transform: Using the Fourier transform for data augmentation involves modifying
the amplitude of the Fourier­transformed images while preserving the spatial distribution.
This approach has been shown to generate adequate augmented data and improve clas­
sification accuracy significantly [15]. Applying this technique can introduce a novel form
of variability in the training data, potentially leading to better generalisation.
2.2.3
Texture Analysis Algorithms
Sobel
The Sobel operator is a simple and effective edge detection method that calculates the
image intensity gradient. By highlighting the edges, the Sobel operator can help the model
focus on the structural features of fungi, which are crucial for accurate classification. This
method is a fundamental tool in image processing and has been successfully used in
various image analysis tasks.
Local Binary Patterns (LBP)
LBP is a robust texture descriptor that labels an image’s pixels by thresholding each pixel’s

3.3.1
Texture analysis
Due to Fungi’s unique morphological characteristics, such as the shape and arrangement
of spores, hyphae, and fruiting bodies, the texture analysis algorithms can enhance the
visibility of these structures, providing valuable insights into the fungal growth patterns
and structural arrangements that are often key identifiers in mycology.
Sobel
The Sobel operator is widely used to highlight images’ structural features in edge detec­
tion. It calculates the image intensity gradient and provides information about the edges
by identifying regions of high spatial frequency.
The Sobel operator computes the gradient in horizontal (X) and vertical (Y) directions.
(Figure 3.6 These gradients are combined to produce a gradient magnitude image that
highlights the edges in the original image. The following steps outline the implemented
approach:
Figure 3.6: Sobel operator kernels [17].
The process begins with transforming the input image to a gray­scale format. The Sobel
gradient is then computed separately in the X and Y directions. The X­gradient captures
the horizontal edges, while the Y­gradient captures the vertical edges. These gradients
are calculated using the OpenCV Sobel function with a kernel size of 3 [18].
Next, the combined gradient magnitude is calculated by taking the square root of the sum
of the squares of the X and Y gradients. This combined gradient image represents the
overall edge strength in the image. To enhance the visibility of the edges, a scale factor
of 2 is applied, which makes the edges stand out more prominently.
The resulting image is then normalised to the range [0, 255] to ensure it has the appropri­
ate format for visualisation and further processing. The resulting image can be observed
in Figure 3.7.

Local Binary Patterns
LBP is a powerful method for texture description that labels an image’s pixels by thresh­
olding each pixel’s neighbourhood and considering the result as a binary number. This
technique effectively captures the texture details, which is beneficial for differentiating
various fungi species.
Generally, LBP works by comparing each pixel with its neighbours in a defined radius. If
a neighbouring pixel’s intensity is greater than or equal to the central pixel, it is assigned
a value of 1; otherwise, it is assigned a value of 0. These binary values are combined to
form a binary number representing the local texture around the pixel (Figure 3.8).
Figure 3.8: LBP Example [19].
In this implementation, a radius of 1 and 8 points (calculated as 8 * radius) are used.
The image is first smoothed using a Gaussian filter with a sigma value 0.5 to enhance tex­

accuracy.
The LBP transformation is then applied to the smoothed image using the local_binary_pattern
function [20] with the ’uniform’ method. The ’uniform’ method ensures that patterns with at
most two transitions (0 to 1 or 1 to 0) are encoded with unique binary patterns, enhancing
the texture representation’s robustness.

3.3.2
Fourier transform
The core idea behind the Fourier transform augmentation is to introduce random uniform
noise into the image’s frequency domain, thereby generating new and diverse training
samples. The process begins by developing a noise image using a uniform random dis­
tribution, which is the basis for introducing variability into the original image.
Next, the image is split into its constituent channels—Red, Green, and Blue. Each chan­
nel undergoes a Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) to convert it into the frequency domain.
The complex values obtained from the DFT are then split into magnitude and phase com­
ponents. The magnitude component is multiplied by the generated random uniform noise
image to introduce noise. The modified magnitude and the original phase are combined
to reconstruct the complex values.
An inverse DFT is performed on these modified complex values to convert the data from
the frequency to the spatial domain. This results in a modified version of each channel
merged into a single image.
In the context of this project, where images consisted of three channels, the results of ap­
plying the Fourier transform­based augmentation needed to be more satisfactory. Unlike
the single­channel heat maps used in the referenced paper [15], the multi­channel im­
ages in this study did not benefit significantly from this method. Consequently, the Fourier

3.5
Models
3.5.1
ResNet
The necessity for a model like ResNet emerged from various challenges modern neural
networks face. Training deep neural networks is challenging due to the exponential pa­
rameter increase as layers are added. Each convolutional layer’s parameter count grows
significantly.
ResNet addresses these challenges by introducing residual connections. These connec­
tions allow gradients to flow directly through the network, mitigating the vanishing gradient
problem, a common issue in deep networks where gradients diminish as they propagate
backwards through the layers. By adding a layer’s input directly to its output, the network
ensures that it can learn identity mappings, allowing for the construction of much deeper
networks without loss of performance.
Figure 3.11: ResNet50 architecture [16].
ResNet­50 architecture was chosen and implemented from scratch, allowing users to
easily specify the size and number of channels for the input, which results in an automated
adjustment in network structure. This choice in this project is due to its balance between
depth and computational efficiency and promising performance in reviewed articles [11].
The architecture comprises multiple residual blocks, each containing convolutional layers
with batch normalisation and ReLU activation functions (Figure 3.12).
3.5.2
DenseNet
DenseNet differs from other architectures, such as ResNet, in that it uses a unique con­
nectivity pattern. In DenseNet architecture, the layers are organised into dense blocks.
Each layer is connected to every other layer within each dense block, resulting in a total
of L(L+1)/2 connections for L layers. This approach significantly increases the network’s
parameter efficiency, as each layer only introduces a limited number of additional pa­
rameters, typically around 12 kernels per layer. Transition layers follow dense blocks to

Figure 3.12: ResNet50 architecture parameters [16].
manage the growth of feature maps and prevent computational inefficiency. These tran­
sition layers, which include a combination of 1x1 convolutions and pooling layers, reduce
the dimensionality of the feature maps.
Initially, DenseNet­121 architecture (Figure 3.15 was selected and implemented from
scratch as being ResNet50 equivalent, but due to relatively fast training times and promis­
ing results, a few other architectures were tested. The choice was justified because this
architecture has been shown to outperform traditional CNNs and even other advanced
architectures like ResNet in various benchmarks due to its ability to maintain rich feature
hierarchies and promote gradient flow.
The implementation was set up to easily accommodate different architectures (such as
DenseNet121, DenseNet169, and DenseNet264) by specifying the number of layers in
each dense block. This modular structure allows straightforward network architecture
customisation by simply changing parameters. This results in the automated construc­
tion of a convolutional layer followed by dense blocks, transition layers, and a final fully
connected layer. This approach allowed easy change in the model based on the size and
number of inputs (3,4 or 5 channels).
3.5.3
ViT
The ViT represent a significant departure from traditional CNNs architectures by employ­
ing the transformer encoder model, initially designed for natural language processing
tasks. ViT applies this architecture to image data, leveraging transformers’ powerful ca­
pabilities in handling sequences and capturing long­range dependencies.
The ViT processes images by dividing them into fixed­size patches of 16x16 pixels. These
patches are then linearly embedded to form a sequence analogous to the token sequence
in natural language processing. An additional () token is prepended to this sequence to
facilitate classification tasks, and absolute position embeddings are added to retain the
positional information of each patch within the image.
The embedded sequence, along with the position embeddings, is then fed into the trans­
former encoder layers. The model learns intricate patterns and representations within the
image data. The final hidden state of the token is used as a comprehensive representa­
tion of the entire image, which can be further utilised for downstream tasks. In practical
applications, a standard classifier is trained by placing a linear layer on the pre­trained

5.1
Challenges
One of the foremost challenges was the limited availability of training data. With less
than 100 images per class for training, the dataset’s high variability made it challenging to
train DL models effectively. This scarcity necessitated extensive data augmentation tech­
niques to increase the dataset size and improve model robustness artificially. However,
finding suitable augmentation techniques that enhanced rather than degraded model per­
formance proved a significant challenge. Techniques such as vertical and horizontal flips
unexpectedly decreased accuracy, like the texture analysis algorithm, which needed to be
clarified for the model more than they helped. Fortunately, models were capable of rea­
sonably good predictions with a limited amount of data, and the decision to utilise transfer
learning for ViT resulted in this architecture being the most prominent.
Given the images’ original resolution (4149x6225 pixels, another challenge arose. The
high resolution created a bottleneck in the training loop. The preprocessing step of rescal­
ing photos to a manageable size of 224x224 pixels, even though it was computationally
intensive (24 hours on a performance computing cluster), paid off by allowing for rapid
parameter tuning and testing.
Another significant challenge was integrating texture and edge information, using and
Sobel operator passed as additional channels to enrich the feature set available to the
models. In reality, they introduced noise and complexity to the model, resulting in signifi­
cantly lower performance metrics than models trained on plain images.
These methods were chosen due to their effectiveness in enhancing texture and edge
detection[19][17]. On top of that, both methods are simple to implement and efficient,
which makes them practical choices before trying more advanced techniques.
Alternative data augmentation techniques and feature extraction methods could be con­
sidered for future exploration. GANs could be used to generate synthetic training images,
providing a more diverse and robust dataset. Additionally, more sophisticated texture
analysis methods, such as wavelet transforms or advanced filtering techniques, could be
explored to enhance the feature extraction process without introducing significant noise.
The web deployment was also quite challenging. While it was an extensive task, the
attention had to span several platforms. Also, enabling anyone to test the model through
an HTTPS connection required a few extra steps, such as certificate acquisition.
Overall, the project had a few challenges that required innovative solutions and meticulous
planning. From data preprocessing and augmentation to model training and deployment,
each phase presented unique obstacles that were overcome.
5.2
Methodology assessment
5.2.1
Pros
1. Advanced Model Selection
ResNet, DenseNet, and ViT, being state­of­the­art architectures for image classification
tasks, proved to be good choices that showed satisfactory results. While other archi­
tectures like Inception, VGG, or traditional CNNs could have been considered, previously
mentioned architectures were chosen based on the literature review, proving their superior
Automatic Fungal Identification
33
==PAGE:44==
in­depth performance, efficiency, and global feature extraction capabilities in classification
tasks.
2.Transfer learning
Transfer learning was crucial in the ViT case. Using models pre­trained on datasets like
ImageNet, ViT could effectively learn and generalise from limited training data and outper­
form other models. This approach for this particular architecture was dictated by literature
review and model characteristics [8].
3. Preprocessing and use of a subset of dataset
Taking the image resizing out of the training loop and starting with just a subset of a dataset
allowed for extensive parameters and architecture testing, which wouldn’t be possible
otherwise.
4. Web deployment
It is creating a web application that anyone can access, which has proved to be the optimal
solution. While allowing the model prediction in a rapid and automated manner, it is also
a solution that does not require coding knowledge.
5.2.2
Cons
1. Computational Complexity
Training deep models requires significant computational resources and time, which was
additionally amplified by the high resolution of images. While the preprocessing allowed
to avoid the bottleneck, in much research, it may not be possible to resize the photos
before training or at all.
2. Fourier transform and texture analysis
Both texture analysis and inverse Fourier transform augmentation techniques could have
yielded more satisfactory results. Methods that were supposed to fix the data limitation
issue ultimately had to be discarded, leaving the issue unsolved.
3. Resizing the images
The choice to resize the images to 224x224 pixels can be considered arbitrary; lowering
the training time significantly simplifies model input, which could influence the final results.
4. Image specific solution
The model developed in this project is highly tailored to the specific characteristics and
features of the fungal images used in the study. This specialisation may limit the model’s
applicability to other datasets or different types of images. However, since the project is
assumed to provide a closed solution for the DTU Bioengineering department, this does
not diminish the outcome.
5.3
Summary and future directions
The project objectives were fully met, and the results are considered highly satisfactory.
The model achieved accuracy rates above the 90% baseline, demonstrating its effective­
ness in fungal species identification. A comprehensive literature review was conducted,
which provided critical insights into the application of deep learning in biological sciences.
This review informed the selection of state­of­the­art architectures.
The data acquisition process was explored, focusing on capturing high­quality time­lapse
images of fungal growth. This exploration was essential for understanding the variability
and challenges associated with fungal image datasets.
Different architectures were trained and evaluated to achieve the best possible classifica­
tion performance, and strategies to enhance model robustness, such as texture analysis
and data augmentation, were explored.

• Enhanced Data Augmentation Techniques: Further research into and implement­

or feature extractions could provide better results.

tion framework would allow the model to be retrained automatically as new data
