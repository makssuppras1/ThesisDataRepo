Title: Comparative study of rotor blade induction using
full-scale measurements and CFD
Department: DTU Wind Energy
Abstract
The purpose of this study is to investigate the ﬂow ﬁeld
in the rotor of a turbine, since such a study can be
used for improving and validating diﬀerent models. The
current thesis has the advantage of using the ﬁrst full scale
measurement of the ﬂow in the rotor plane.
A comprehensive data collection from the full scale
measurements performed in 2009 in the Danish Tjæreborg
wind farm, were used. The database includes two Light
Detection and Ranging systems which were the foundation
of this thesis.
In addition, one set of full 3D CFD
simulation and an Actuator Line model were used during
this project.
The induction ﬁeld was visualized using the 2D LiDAR
data in ﬁxed and rotating frame of references. The ﬂow
ﬁeld was simulated in the Actuator Line model and the
results were in convincing agreement with measurements.
These results were used to improve the regularization
parameter (ϵ) of the Actuator Line model.
It was seen
that the optimum ϵ in the range from 15 m to 30 m
from root should be higher than 3 times the grid spacing
(investigation in rotating frame of reference).
The axial induction, normal forces and power of the
Actuator Line model were compared to the full 3D CFD
simulations to optimize ϵ.
It was concluded that for
the middle part of the blade ϵ larger that 3 times the
grid spacing is most consistent between the Actuator
Line model and the full 3D CFD simulations, however
the relative error in this region does not get lower than
5%.
Moreover, it was observed that ϵ is a function of
chord length. A variable ϵ was used and some improve-
ments could be observed. It was seen that, to minimize
the relative error of power, ϵ should be 2.2 the grid spacing.
The uncertainty from the main sources of uncertainty were
quantiﬁed. It was seen that the main source of uncertainty
for each observation is the estimation of the axial velocity
based on the line of sight measured which can be between
less than 1% up until 13% in the measurement area. The
relative error of the mean of the velocity time series were
calculated and it was seen that the region from 15 m to 20
m from the root is the most reliable part in case of number
of observations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In this chapter the motivation and objectives of the undertaken project are explained.
An
overview of the report is presented in the end of this chapter.
1.1
Motivation
As the prediction of the power and the loads on a wind turbine requires a thorough understanding
of the ﬂow around and before the rotor of a turbine, the motivation to investigate the induction
ﬁeld upstream and in the rotor plane of a wind turbine arises. Such a study can be used for
improving and validating diﬀerent models. For example aero-elastic models like Horizontal Axis
Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd generation (HAWC2) [15] or computational models suchlike
Actuator Line (AL),Large Eddy Simulations (LES).
1.2
Literature review
Several experiments have been conducted to investigate the eﬀects of the rotor plane on the ﬂow.
F. Massouh et al. [17] used Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) and hot wire technique to visualize
the ﬂow close to the rotor plane. A small wind turbine in a wind tunnel was used to perform
the experiments on. Measurements were performed in planes parallel and perpendicular to the
rotor plane. W. Haans et al. [4] used hot wire phase locked measurements to obtain detailed
understanding of near wake development. The experiment were conducted in a wind tunnel with
a down scaled wind turbine. G. Kocer et al. [11] conducted the ﬁrst ever measurement of the
wake of a full scale wind turbine using an instrumented uninhabited aerial vehicle. The sensing
elements were miniature silicon piezo-resistive chips. These diﬀerential pressure sensors were
embedded within a 20 mm hemispherical probe head and used to measure the wind velocity
in comparison to the vehicle velocity. B. Noura et al. [24] used PIV to measure velocity in a
plane perpendicular to the rotor plane of a down scaled wind turbine. G. V. Iungo et al. [7,8]
did some full scale measurements using 2 Light Detection And Ranging (LiDAR) systems to
obtain information about the velocity and wakes down and upstream the rotor plane.
The
measurements were presented in planes parallel and perpendicular to the rotor plane. Volumetric
scans also were performed and development of wakes was investigated. V. del Campo et al. [3]
did experiments on the blades of a down scaled wind turbine using stereo PIV to investigate 3D
loads on the blades. K. Nilsson et al. [23] used PIV to measure velocity distribution close to the
rotor plane on a plane perpendicular to the rotor plane to validate the actuator line method.
The measurements were performed as part of the MEXICO project. Larsen and Hansen [14]
used LiDAR to measure velocity distribution in the rotor plane of a turbine with 80 m rotor
diameter. In their work the measured axial induction was compared to HAWC2 predictions. It
is worth mentioning that the current thesis used the same data as Larsen and Hansen [14].

1.3
Objectives
The objective of the current masters thesis can be summarized in ﬁve items:
1. To analyse the data from the three comprehensive measurement campaigns and establish
whether they could be used to characterize the induction ﬁeld of the investigated turbine.
2. To characterize the induction ﬁeld in the rotor plane of the turbine in a ﬁxed and a rotating
frame of reference.
3. To investigate whether the ﬂow ﬁeld measured in the ﬁeld can be reproduced using CFD
and the AL method.
4. Identify reasons for possible diﬀerences between measurements and CFD.
5. Improvement of the AL model, based on the above studies.
6. Fine tune ϵ and deeply investigate the ﬂow structure and the force distribution around the
blades using comparative study between AL and full 3D simulations.

2.2
Actuator Line method
The AL method for simulating the wind turbine rotor in the ﬂow was introduced by Sørensen
and Shen [28, 31, 38]. In this method blades are replaced by forces located on rotating lines.
The 3D forces are calculated using Blade Element Theory (BEM) theory knowing the relative
velocity in each element of the blade and the tabulated airfoil data. The initial version of the
method uses vorticity-velocity metrics but, Mikkelsen replaced it with pressure-velocity metrics
to integrate this model with EllipSys3D [31]. The basis of the model is incompressible Navier
Stokes equations:

As the

element of the blade is located in the space and put into the Navier-Stokes equations. The mesh
will be updated subsequently, and new velocities will be assigned to all the cells. Thus the forces
are recalculated. This process is repeated several times, and then the simulation will proceed to
the next time step.

using a regularization function in space. In the original AL method this regularization function
is a 3D Gaussian distribution.

radial eﬀect of the blades on the ﬂow at tips [20].

2.3
Optimization of AL regularization parameter
Some studies were performed regarding the inﬂuence of the regularization parameter imple-
mented in diﬀerent solvers. J. Sørensen et al. [27] investigated the inﬂuence of ϵ on the axial
induction distribution in the radial direction for three diﬀerent values equal to respectively 1,
2 and 4 × ∆grid in the used meshgrid. It should be mentioned that this article investigates
the eﬀect of the regularization parameter on the Actuator Disk method. In the Actuator disk
method the rotor is modelled by replacing it with thrust forces calculated by BEM method in
the mesh grid. The work concludes that there is not a big diﬀerence in choosing diﬀerent ϵ so
1 × ∆grid is chosen through out the work.
R. Mikkelsen et al. [20] also studied the inﬂuence of the regularization factor on the axial in-
duction as a function of radial coordinate considering the eﬀect of tip loss correction and tip
speed ratio. In addition, the diﬀerence of 2D and 3D regularization functions was compared in
this Ph.D. thesis. The work suﬃces to mention the sensitivity of the model to ϵ and does not
conclude any speciﬁc value.
N. Trodlborg et al. [37] studied the inﬂuence of the regularization factor in a 3D Gaussian dis-
tribution on the axial interference in radial direction. In this study the values of 1.5, 2, 2.5 and
3 × ∆grid on the blades were chosen for the regularization parameter. The report concludes
that for the speciﬁc studied characteristics a regularization size of 2 × ∆grid is suitable while
values smaller than 1 introduce ﬂuctuations at the tip of the blades.
Ivanell et al. [9] used a 2d Gaussian distribution to regularize the forces over the actuator lines.
The regularization parameter was set to 1×∆grid and the phase diﬀerence between the tip and
root vortices was calculated.
W. Jin [10] compared the 2d and 3d distributions for regularization of forces. Also the inﬂuence
of regularization parameter on axial induction and the position and size of tip vortices was in-
vestigated for ϵ = 1.5 × ∆grid, ϵ = 2 × ∆grid, ϵ = 2.5 × ∆grid. Finally the value of 2 × ∆grid
was concluded to be suitable for the speciﬁc conditions.
L. Bernini [2] studied the inﬂuence of the parameter on velocity distribution. The report con-
cludes that a ϵ = 1 × ∆grid would be the optimum value which was the smallest value that did
not cause singularities in the results.
Shives and Crawford [25] investigated the inﬂuence of the grid size and the mesh and the
regularization parameter in both ﬁnite and inﬁnite length non rotating wings on the velocity
distribution in the ﬂow direction. The results were compared to theoretical solution of lifting
line theory. They have concluded that the regularization parameter should be a scale of chord
length by a ratio of 1

3.2
Numerical Approach
In the current project the in-house developed CFD code EllipSys3D was used for all numerical
simulations. In the current section, ﬁrstly, this code is described brieﬂy. The implementation of
the two approaches -i.e. the full 3D modelled turbine CFD simulations and the AL method - in
the solver are explained afterwards.
EllipSys3D is a Navier-Stokes equations solver for incompressible ﬂows using ﬁnite vol-
umes discretized in a multi-block grid mesh [18,19,29,30]. Ellipsys3D uses primitive variables
(pressure-velocity) on a collocated grid to solve Reynolds Average Navier Stokes (RANS), or in
combination with LES method (so called Detached Eddy Simulations (DES)).
The solver was used for three sets of simulations. A full 3D modelled turbine RANS simu-
lation of the rotor was performed prior to the beginning of the masters thesis by N. Trodlborg
during the DANAERO project [36]. In addition the AL method was previously implemented in
the solver which was used to perform two sets of simulations (LES) during the current master
thesis. One set of the AL method simulations was then used for comparison with the measure-
ments, and the other one was used to investigate the ﬂow in comparison with full 3D simulations.
In the previously performed full 3D CFD model the shape of the rotor of the turbine was
modelled and put into the grid. Particularly, the blades were modelled in detail. However the
nacelle and the tower were not modelled. Since the main interest in this thesis was the eﬀect of
the blades themselves, neglecting of the nacelle and the tower was acceptable.
The grid was from o-o type while the radius of domain was equal to 10 rotor diameter. There
were 256×256×256 cells on each blade, and the thickness of the cells on the blades was set so
that ∆grid < y+ = 1. The grid consisted of 432 blocks of each 323 cells.
In the full 3D CFD model, the pitch angle and yaw angle were set to zero, and the rotational
speed of the rotor was set to 16.234 rpm. A uniform and steady ﬂow was used as the inﬂow
with constant 8 m s−1 wind speed. Neither external turbulence was put into the ﬂow nor wind
shear and wind veer. The turbulence created inside the domain by the walls of the blades was
resolved directly in the numerical simulation.
In the performed simulations, implementing the AL method, decoupling between pressure
and velocity grids were avoided by Rhie-chow interpolation technique.
A correlation based
transition model was used. The diﬀerencing scheme used in these simulations was a hybrid com-
bination of third-order Quadratic Upstream Interpolation for Convective Kinematics (QUICK)
and fourth-order Central Diﬀerencing Scheme by 10 and 90 percent respectively, while the Semi-
Implicit Method for Pressure-Linked Equations (SIMPLE) was used for pressure correction. In
the simulations the air density was set to 1.225 kg m−3 and viscosity to 1.7840 m2 s−1.

Time series selection
From the available 10 min times series four time series were trimmed based on the following

1. The time series should be as long as possible.
2. No yaw activity of the turbine.
3. No pitch activity of the turbine.
4. The turbine should be facing free stream (not in the wake of other turbines).

ones).

time series from which a ﬁt close to real conﬁguration could be reached were selected. In some of

4.1.3
Filters
Since the LiDAR system works based on the Doppler eﬀect it will detect not only movements of
aerosols in the ﬂow but also the blades. A band pass ﬁlter from 0.1 m s−1 to 20 m s−1 was used
to remove the outliers caused by the moving blades or falling aerosols in the time series.
However, even after using this ﬁlter there were many ﬂuctuations in the LiDAR measure-
ments compared to pitot tube measurements and power time series. The reason could be the
fact that the pitot tube is measuring constantly in the same relative location in relation to the
blade while the 2D LiDAR measures every where around the blades. This introduces a higher
variability to the 2D LiDAR measurements. Another reason was the sampling frequency of the
2D LiDAR system, which was much higher than the one of pitot tubes and the power signals.
The ﬂuctuations of time series could be problematic in the process of investigating of the syn-
chrony between time series. Hence, a moving window averaging technique was used to ﬁlter the
time series [42].
In the current thesis a window size of one tenth of the length of the time series was selected
for all the time series which were supposed to be smoothed. This length was selected to keep
consistency for all time series while the large scale ﬂuctuations in a time series were preserved.
A velocity time series measured by the 2D LiDAR is presented in ﬁg. 4.3 for the time series
from 16:30 to 16:40 together with several smoothed versions of it as a sample to illustrate the
eﬀect of smoothing on a 10 min time series. In this plot n denotes the window size in which the
averaging is performed while N is the length of the time series. It can be seen that using a time
window of one tenth of the time series length only leaves the large scale ﬂuctuations.

4.1.10
Interpolations
To calculate the synchrony between diﬀerent time series they had to have the same length.
However the sampling frequency of the diﬀerent databases were diﬀerent so they did not have
the same number of data point in the same period of time. In addition, in many cases the
data points were selected based on a ﬁxed phase of the blades and in certain distances from
the blades. This caused even more diverse number of data points from diﬀerent databases. To
overcome this problem linear interpolations were used to increase the number of data points up
to the length of the longest time series.
In extracting the velocities from the full 3D simulation results, several planes on both sides
of the rotor plane was extracted and then results were interpolated using a spline function on
exactly in the rotor plane.
4.1.11
Calculating time lags between time series
The time series for which the synchrony was investigated were velocity and power.
These

Synchrony of 2D LiDAR and DANAERO databases using power measurements:

periods to avoid ﬂuctuations.

4.3.2
Comparative study of velocity distribution in a rotating frame of ref-

To investigate the ﬂow inside the rotor plane and look into the eﬀect of the blades, a rotating
frame of reference rotating with the rotor was used. To produce contour plots illustrating the
eﬀect of each blade using measurements, the coordinates of the points were rotated based on
the position of one blade for each time step and then the data was divided into square tiles of
4 m2 in the y −z plane. Afterwards the data was averaged over time in each tile. These tiles
then had one set of y and z coordinates and a mean velocity which could be used to plot the
contours.
To take advantage of symmetry around the nacelle in a rotating frame of reference (since
the veer and shear are already averaged out), the position of small tiles of averaged velocities
where rotated once by 120◦and once by 240◦. Then the average of three values of velocity were
assigned to each tile. Using this process the length of the time series were virtually increased
by a factor of three.
To produce the contour plots for the numerical simulations the same process was used except
that in each time step one set of extracted points were rotated based on the position of the blades.
Then again the data was divided into tiles of 2 m×2 m and averaged over time.
In some cases, when the turbulence was not present in the simulations and contour plots were
not of interest, the coordinates of the extracted data from the simulations for the last time step
were converted from Cartesian to polar frame of reference, and proper data for several radius
was extracted. This method was used when the velocity distribution was compared between
the AL simulations and 3D CFD simulations or measurements on azimuthal paths around the
nacelle (on circles in the rotor plane).

In this ﬁgure the relative velocity calculated by the pitot tube measurement is always lower
than the relative velocity calculated based on LiDAR measurements. The reason is that the
pitot tube measures less than the vector summation of axial velocity and the rotational speed of
the rotor, because the ﬂow slows down in front of the walls (blockage by the blades). It should
be kept in mind that the rotation is added, because the pitot tube measures in a rotational
frame of reference. The equation used to calculate the relative velocity using the 2D LiDAR
measurements is shown in eq. (5.1).
V reltrue =
q
(rω)2 + U2
(5.1)
where V reltrue is the LiDAR based relative velocity, r = 20 m is the radius of the pitot tube,
ω = 16.2 rpm is the rotational speed of the rotor and U is the axial wind velocity calculated
using eq. (4.2). The 2D LiDAR measures the ﬂow in the rotor plane in a ﬁxed frame of reference,
which does not include the eﬀect of the rotation of the blades. In fact the ﬂow behind the rotor
does rotate but not nearly as fast as the rotor and in the opposite direction. Hence to calculate
the relative velocity based on the LiDAR measurement the tangential velocity of the ﬂow should
be added to rω. Since the velocity measured by the 2D LiDAR contains some eﬀect of the
transversal velocity components, thus by using eq. (5.1) the eﬀect of the tangential velocity is
partly taken into account.
Also the deviation of the LiDAR based relative velocity time series is lower than the deviation
of the relative velocity calculated by the pitot tube, as it is visible in ﬁg. 5.9. The reason is the
neglecting of the changing eﬀect of the rotational term in eq. (5.1).

two time series of relative wind speed from 16:00 to 16:10 on one segment of the pitot tube path

consistency is more visible in the general trend of the time series in this plot. However the minor
ﬂuctuations are not observed in the pitot tube measurements since the sampling frequency of

It should be noticed that the energy extracted from the wind passing the rotor eﬀects the
high frequency low energy level eddies more than the low-frequency high-energy-level eddies.
Hence the inconsistency of the small ﬂuctuations in the wind velocity time series before and
after the rotor is expected. In addition, because of the mentioned pauses in the 2D LiDAR data
acquisition system the position of the blades were not known precisely. It can be concluded that
the major ﬂuctuations which are consistent are caused by large eddies which cover the rotor
area.

Chapter 6
Uncertainty analysis
In the current chapter the sources of uncertainty in measurements, numerical simulations and
data analysis are investigated. The study of the uncertainties reveals the most important lim-
itations of the methods and the data used in the current thesis. The key data used in this
thesis were pitot tubes measurements, LiDAR measurements, power signal data and azimuthal
position measurements. Velocities measured by the 2D LiDAR were used for investigation of
the axial induction in the rotor plane, and the measurements from Spinner LiDAR were used
for determining the incoming wind characteristics. Hence only the two LiDAR databases were
investigated for uncertainties in the measurement or data analysis process.
6.1
Uncertainties in measurement process
The main sources of uncertainty in the LiDAR measurement process and instruments are de-
scribed in this section.
6.1.1
Precision of LiDAR instruments and inherited uncertainties
Using the continuous wave laser LiDAR the line of sight velocity is given [1] by:
Ulos = νD × λ/2
(6.1)
where νD is the measured Doppler frequency and λ is the laser wavelength. Equation (6.1)
shows that the accuracy of Ulos depends on the two parameters of Doppler frequency and laser
wavelength. It is shown by Rutherford et. al. that the combination of the two uncertainties is
less than 0.1% for wind speed [33].
As explained in chapter 2 the velocity measured using a continuous wave LiDAR is an
integration over the laser beam length with a Lorentzian weighting function. This introduces
another source of uncertainty in the measurement of velocity. In practice this weighting function
poses a low pass ﬁlter with a limit, which is a function of the focal distance. In the case of the
2D LiDAR, used in this thesis, the low pass ﬁlter have averaged over the ﬂuctuations with a
frequency higher than 0.08 m−1 [26]. Also in the case of the Spinner LiDAR the low pass ﬁlter
have smeared out the ﬂuctuations with a frequency higher than 0.03 m−1 [26].
In addition Rutherford et. al. showed that the uncertainty in focus length about 100 m is of
order 70 cm [33], which are further considered with other location uncertainties in next section.
6.1.2
The uncertainties in estimation of turbulence intensity
Because of the volume averaging of velocity in the line of sight direction, the measurement of the
turbulence intensity using the Spinner LiDAR is uncertain. In addition, the method used in the
current thesis to estimate the turbulence intensity is similar to the method used in conical wind
72
==PAGE:75==
CHAPTER 6. UNCERTAINTY ANALYSIS
proﬁling, in which the velocities in each circular path are averaged. The eﬀect of Lorentzian
weighting and the averaging of velocity measured on each cycle is thoroughly investigated by
R. Wagner et. al. [40]. As explained in this article the turbulence intensity measured by the
technique used in the current thesis, is 20 % lower than the true turbulence intensity. However,
since the diﬀerence of turbulence intensity considering this 20 %, is about 2 % to 4 %, and all the
comparisons between the measurements and the AL simulations are made using time averaged
data, the eﬀect of it can be neglected.
6.1.3
Position and relative direction of the 2D LiDAR
The 2D LiDAR was located on top of a basket crane as mentioned before. The distance of the
truck carrying the crane from the tower was measured using a laser meter. Hence the position of
the 2D LiDAR was not precisely known throughout the test. In addition the truck was parked
in an approximate manner down wind the turbine. This means that the relative angle of the
axis of the 2D LiDAR to North was not precisely known either. Thus the diﬀerent geometrical
uncertainties include the following items: 1) The stream wise distance between the truck and
the tower was assumed to be 56 m, which based on experts’ opinion, can have about ±2 m uncer-
tainty [5]. 2) The uncertainty of the position of the focal point was considered to be on this axis
too, thus the total uncertainty of ±2.1 m. 3) The other two position (y and z axis) coordinates
were estimated using the average contour plots. Since the contour plots were made using spatial
averaging over small 1 m2 areas, these components also have a ±0.5 m uncertainty. However, an
uncertainty of ±1 m was assigned to them conservatively. 4) Using the standing still period in
the beginning of the ﬁrst time series the initial relative angle between the nacelle axis and the
2D LiDAR axis was estimated. The uncertainty of this angle is estimated by Kragh et. al. to
be about 2◦for a ﬂow similar to the current cases [12].
Using Monte Carlo method [41] the position and angle uncertainties were used to create 100
random samples of position and angles. The velocities over the measurement area was calculated
by each sample set. Then the standard deviation of the calculated velocities for each position in
the measurement area is deﬁned as the combined position source uncertainties. It was observed
that the uncertainty caused by the position errors is not a function of radial position or of height,
so the results are presented as one value for each time series in table 6.1.

6.1.4
Precision of the moving parts of 2D LiDAR system
The moving parts of the 2D LiDAR included a rotating wedge and a periodically left-right
movement system for horizontal movement.
The tolerance of the angle of the wedge is 0.1◦, which contributes less than 0.5 % to the
uncertainty in axial wind speed as Rutherford et. al. mentioned [33], when the angle between
the instantaneous wind direction and the laser beam is around 20◦. The tolerance of rotating
mechanism of the wedge was not known.
The horizontal position movement was not precise and had an oﬀset deviation as a function
of vertical distance from the central plane. The horizontal component of the coordinates were
corrected manually using the time averaged contour plot.

6.2
Uncertainties in the simulations
The uncertainties associated the numerical simulations, AL method, are discussed in this section.
The model limitations mainly include:
• The deviation caused by the diﬀerences between the simulations input and the measure-
ments.
Although it was tried to mimic exactly the same inﬂow characteristics, some
diﬀerences were observed in the measured and simulated inﬂow.
• The inﬂuence of atmospheric stability which was not considered in the current thesis.
• The uncertainty of the airfoil data which was measured and estimated using wind tunnel
experiments and corrections.
• The solver limitations like the k-ω-SST turbulence closer model.
However, quantiﬁcation of these uncertainties are out of scope of this thesis, and the models
were trusted as they were, since they were used in other projects like DANAERO project [36].
Other sources of uncertainty in the simulations are explained in the following subsections.

6.3.3
Interpolations
Through out the data analysis process several interpolations are used. However, it has been
tried to use as few interpolations as possible. In addition it was tried to keep, for example, the
data extraction planes or averaging tiles in the contour plots as small as possible, so the points
are close together, and the error introduced by the interpolation is minimized. The magnitude
of this error is assumed to be negligible compared to other sources of uncertainty.

7.1
Analysing the full-scale measurements:
By analysing the full-scale measurements the inﬂow wind characteristics of a NM80 2MW tur-
bine deﬁned using the Spinner LiDAR measurements. Four time series were selected which had
constant yaw and pitch angle and did not contain any long pauses in the 2D LiDAR data col-
lection. The mean wind speed for these time series was ranging from 8.2 m s−1 to 9 m s−1 and
turbulence intensity was ranging from 8 % to 12 %. The yaw misalignment angle, wind shear
and veer were estimated from Spinner LiDAR measurements for all time series. However two of
the four time series investigated in this thesis were too short, between 2 min to 3 min, and the
results from these time series were often unreliable. Hence more investigation for ﬁnding longer
times series is needed. Merging time series with similar yaw angles and yaw misalignments can
be an alternative way to increase the length of these time series.
The three databases, including Spinner LiDAR, 2D LiDAR and DANAERO database, were
synchronized, or the synchrony between them was conﬁrmed. It was concluded that the main
problems in the synchrony of the databases were partly asynchrony between the Spinner LiDAR
and the other databases, and partly these measurement pauses appearing in the 2D LiDAR
database. These pauses were recorded for later reference. It is suggested to investigate the
reasons for all the pauses in future works.
In addition the average induction ﬁeld was illustrated over the measurement areas in both
a ﬁxed and a rotating frame of reference. In the contour plots referring the rotating frame of
reference some high velocity regions were found close to the tip of the blades for two of the
selected time series, where the measurement height was about 69 m for which the reason is un-
known. The probable causes such as stall were investigated by calculating the angle of attack
distribution over the blade length. Also the blade vibrations were investigated for which a cor-
relation was not observed too. It was observed that these high velocity regions appear when
the axial induction is higher than approximately 0.6. Nevertheless, the further investigation of
these high velocity regions is recommended because of the huge eﬀect that they seem to have
on the velocity distribution. A starting point could be investigation of the pressure distribution
in such cases and comparing them with other cases where these high velocity regions are not
observed.
The turbulence intensity was measured by the Spinner LiDAR measurements in this thesis,
which is uncertain because of the volume averaging of velocity over the laser beam and the
averaging of data for each cycle. A correction for the measured turbulence intensity should be

considered to take these uncertainties into account.
7.2
AL simulations, modelling and comparative studies:
The measured ﬂow ﬁelds were modelled with the AL method simulations, and the agreement
of wind shear and wind veer between the simulations and measurements were shown. The time
averaged induction was compared between the measurements and the AL simulations; a con-
vincing consistency was observed for most of the time series. The best agreements were observed
in the longer time series, for which the error did not exceed 7 %, while in the shorter time series
the relative error was up to 15 %. These results were used to tune the regularization parameter
in the AL model. It was seen that ϵ should be higher than 2×∆grid at least to have the smallest
error between the simulations and the measurements.
It should be mentioned that the implementation of wind veer did not increase the agreement
between the simulations and the measurements. Nevertheless, the eﬀect was estimated and be-
cause it was negligible the implementation was neglected during the investigation.
The agreement of the velocity ﬁeld in the rotor plane before and after the blades passing
between AL simulations and the experimental results were illustrated in contour plots. It was
concluded that the ﬂow around the blade also can be visualized and compared with the AL
simulations. Hence the velocity distribution was investigated in a rotating frame of reference
both for the AL simulations and experimental measurements. The consistency between them
was shown especially for radii from 15 m to 25 m. The relative error of velocity distribution in
this range of radii does not exceed 12 % for all times series. The main challenge in this step
was the ﬂow structure around the body of the blades. Either the high velocity regions at higher
radii or the velocity drop on the blades at lower radii.
It was seen that ϵ changes the mean and amplitude of the velocity distribution in the rotor
plane. So the optimization of ϵ was investigated according to both metrics. Based on the mean
velocity metric ϵ should be higher than 3 × ∆grid for investigated radii. The use of amplitude
metric as an optimization factor was not successful, because of the eﬀects of the blades, which
are not modelled in AL simulations, and the high velocity regions. It was seen that the AL
method, over predicts the velocity in the rotor plane in the region of interest between 15 to 30
meters from root.
7.3
Comparative study of full 3D and AL simulations:
Velocity distribution based on full 3D CFD simulations were used to ﬁne tune the ϵ. It was
seen that for ϵ = 1 × ∆grid, the velocity distribution in the rotor plane contains numerically
caused ﬂuctuations. However, it could be seen that the exact limit might be a function of radius.
Both mean and amplitude of the velocity distribution in full 3D simulations were used to
optimize ϵ of AL method. It was concluded that based on mean velocity distribution ϵ should be
higher than 3 × ∆grid as it was seen in the comparison with measurements. For ϵ > 3 × ∆grid,
the mean velocity does not change signiﬁcantly. However, a more detailed investigation was
performed using the mean velocity distribution metric, and exact optimum values of ϵ were
found and presented as a function of radius and chord length.

It should be emphasized that the investigation of the induction ﬁeld in a rotating frame of
reference, in the measurements and the full 3D CFD simulations led to the conclusion that in the
radii from 15 m to 30 m the optimum ϵ is higher than 3×∆grid. The lower limit was recognized
as ϵ = 1 × ∆grid in both studies. The comparative study between the measurements and the
AL simulations was conducted using 1 × ∆grid < ϵ < 5 × ∆grid while the comparison between
AL simulations and full 3D CFD simulations was extended to 10 × ∆grid.
Based on the amplitude of the velocity distribution ϵ should be as small as possible, while
it should be kept in mind that at some point, close to ϵ = 1 × ∆grid, the numerical singularity
will be introduced to the solution.
It is recommended that the sensitivity of the optimum ϵ, and the lower limit of it, to grid
resolution should be investigated in depth. Several studies are conducted with diﬀerent grid
types and resolutions, which seem to have diﬀerent conclusions for the optimum and limit of ϵ.
An in depth study investigating the eﬀect of grid would probably solve the paradoxes between
the conclusions.
In addition to the grid spacing, the sensitivity of the optimum and lower limit of ϵ in AL simu-
lations to the number of posing points on the blades should be investigated. In the current study
this number was kept constant and equal to 33 points along the blade length. The optimum and
the lower limit of ϵ might be more sensitive to this factor than to grid resolution. The variabil-
ity of the optimum ϵ to wind speed and thrust coeﬃcient is recommended to be investigated too.
The axial induction factor over the blade length was compared between the full 3D CFD
and AL simulations results with diﬀerent ϵ values. It was seen that for ϵ ≥3 × ∆grid the
induction does not change so much over the blade length. However, for 8 × ∆grid the error of
AL simulations was increased compared to the full 3D simulations. The closest distribution of
axial induction to the full 3D distribution is for ϵ ≥3 × ∆grid and ϵ ≤5 × ∆grid keeping in
mind that the optimum choice of ϵ is a function of radius. In addition, one set of simulations was
performed using a variable ϵ scaling with the chord length. The axial induction of the variable
ϵ is generally not better than the simulations results with ϵ ≥3 × ∆grid, except for the tip of
the blade.
To have a better agreement between the AL simulations and the measured data, a more
sophisticated dispersion for variable ϵ as a function of chord length or radius could be suggested.
Such a function can be used for other rotor designs too.
Moreover, a 2D Gaussian distribution of the forces in AL simulations instead of a 3D distri-
bution can be more realistic since the eﬀect of lift and drag forces of a section of a blade should
not be observed in the other sections. This can achieve with a 2D distribution in which the
forces are distributed in planes perpendicular to the blade lines in AL method.
Normal force distribution was investigated over the blade length and a comparison study
was conducted between full 3D CFD and AL simulations with diﬀerent ϵ values. This study was
also used to optimize ϵ and similar to the method using the mean velocity distribution, it was
seen that ϵ should be bigger than 3 × ∆grid.
Changes in generated power was studied in the AL simulations for diﬀerent values of ϵ and
this was used to optimize ϵ to get the closest power to the reference power from the full 3D CFD
simulations. The optimum value of ϵ = 2.2 × ∆grid was found.

7.4
Uncertainty analysis:
The uncertainties of measurements, simulations and data analysis were mentioned and when
possible estimated. The combined error was presented as an example for one radial position
for one of the short times series.
It was shown that the highest errors are posed into the
investigations at higher radial positions. This is consistent with the comparisons between the

The eﬀect of Lorentzian weighting extraction of data was investigated, and it was shown to
be very modest and negligible in comparison with other uncertainties sources.
7.5
More suggestions for future works:
Finally, based on the uncertainties in the current thesis some suggestions are made to reduce
the uncertainties for the future works and measurement campaigns:
1. It is suggested that the position and elevation of all the turbines and instruments including
the 2D LIDAR and the met mast should be recorded by a global positioning system. The
position of the 2D LiDAR can be set beforehand on map based on the focal point and free
wind direction and then put exactly on the desired point.
2. In the beginning of measurement in one position several minutes of measurement should
be collected, while the turbine is standing still. This would help to ﬁnd the initial angles
between the turbine and the 2D LiDAR for all cases.
3. More modern and sophisticated equipments such as WindScanner [22] can be used to have
velocity vectors of ﬂow in the rotor plane. This would reduce the uncertainties drastically,
since a major part of the uncertainties in the current thesis were caused by the calculation
of axial velocity based on the los velocity.
4. To have longer time series, which would reduce the uncertainties considerably, phase locked
measurement for certain yaw angle in a long period of time would be very beneﬁcial.
5. The synchrony of the databases can be assured using the same on-line clock on diﬀerent
machines or using the same machine if possible.
6. A faster and more powerful system for recording the measurements which would avoid
the disruptive pauses is strongly recommended for the next measurement campaigns, since
these pauses reduced the length of the time series and increased the deviation gradually.
It is assumed that using these suggestions would increase the validity of the hypothesis
promised in the current thesis and in other projects with similar subject signiﬁcantly.

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and 
public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new opportunities and technology for the global and Danish 
exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind 
energy and provides the basis for advanced education at the education. 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes 
organized into three main topics: Wind energy systems, Wind turbine technology and Basics for wind energy.
