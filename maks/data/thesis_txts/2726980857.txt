Abstract
This thesis explores the application of diffusion models in interactive image segmen-
tation, driven by the success of generative models in computer vision tasks. Gener-
ative models, especially diffusion models, have demonstrated the ability to generate
high-quality samples from complex distributions. The increasing demand for labeled
image data motivates the investigation of diffusion models for interactive image seg-
mentation. The proposed method utilizes diffusion models to generate high-quality
segmentation masks guided by the images of objects. Two processes, an offline and
an online process, are employed to find the optimal segmentation masks or refine the
generated masks. In the offline process, an iterative user-guided selection mechanism
filters out poor-quality masks based on user input. The online process focuses on
refining the sampled masks using user feedback, enhancing their quality and preci-
sion. The objective of this research is to accelerate the image segmentation process
and facilitate faster and easier dataset creation. Manual annotation is a costly and
time-consuming task, and by leveraging diffusion models for interactive image seg-
mentation, the thesis aims to alleviate this burden.
The results demonstrate the
effectiveness of the proposed method in producing high-quality segmentation masks,
contributing to the scalability and eﬀiciency of computer vision tasks.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In recent years, generative models have emerged as powerful tools in the field of com-
puter vision, offering valuable contributions to various image-related tasks. Notably,
generative models such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [15], Variational
Autoencoders (VAEs) [21] and Transformers [39] have revolutionized image synthe-
sis, manipulation, and understanding. These models have demonstrated exceptional
capabilities in generating realistic images [12], in painting missing regions [44], and
even creating entirely new images based on given prompts [32]. In addition, diffusion
models have gained significant attention due to their ability to generate high-quality
samples from complex data distributions [34]. Diffusion models have shown great
potential in the quality of the results they are able to produce.
In the field of computer vision, one of the main challenges that need to be faced is
the need for labeled images. More and more sectors of industry are using computer
vision applications to solve problems and simplify processes. Usually, however, for the
optimal training of these models in each problem, a customized dataset that covers
the problem and which will be used for training is also required. The creation of a la-
beled dataset with annotated images from scratch is a time-consuming and expensive
process, as it requires a lot of hours of work. Interactive image segmentation plays a
fundamental role in computer vision and image processing applications, enabling eﬀi-
cient and accurate extraction of objects from images. It finds extensive use in various
domains such as medical imaging, object recognition, video editing, and augmented
reality. The goal of interactive image segmentation is to separate foreground objects
from the background, a task that remains challenging due to the complex and diverse
nature of images. Traditional manual segmentation methods require extensive user
intervention, making the process time-consuming and tedious. Therefore, there is a
pressing need for advanced techniques that can make interactive image segmentation
faster and more user-friendly.
The evolution of diffusion models has paved the way for their application in various
image-related tasks, including interactive image segmentation. This thesis aims to
address the aforementioned challenges by leveraging diffusion models for interactive
image segmentation. Driven by diffusion models’ capability to produce high-quality
data from complex distributions, in our method, we propose an offline and an online
process to generate high-quality segmentation masks. In the offline process, a diffu-
sion model is employed to produce a set of N-sampled masks from the input image,
guided by the image representation of the target object. These sampled masks cap-

ture a range of potential object boundaries, allowing for the exploration of different
segmentation possibilities. However, not all sampled masks will accurately represent
the object, necessitating a means of discarding poor-quality masks. To achieve this,
an iterative user-guided selection mechanism is employed, where the user provides
click inputs to indicate the desired regions or boundaries. Through this iterative pro-
cess, the algorithm progressively filters out the masks that do not align well with the

the click inputs provided by the user, the algorithm aims to regenerate masks with
higher quality and precision. By integrating the user’s feedback, the system adapts its
segmentation approach to align more closely with the user’s expectations. Through
this thesis, we aim to accelerate the process of image segmentation and thus make
the creation of datasets faster and easier. The existence of labeled datasets in the
last few years has been a catalyst in the evolution of deep learning models and tasks
and has improved dramatically their performance. By reducing the effort required by
the annotators, we can achieve faster results and scale our datasets and our projects.
In summary, this thesis addresses the need for faster and more eﬀicient interactive
image segmentation by proposing an approach based on diffusion models. By deploy-
ing the offline and online processes, the algorithm generates a set of sampled masks
and progressively refines them based on user input, resulting in high-quality segmen-
tation masks. The subsequent sections of this thesis will delve into the methodology,
experiments, and results, demonstrating the effectiveness and potential of diffusion
models in the realm of interactive image segmentation.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
Diffusion models
Diffusion models were first introduced by Sohl-Dickstein et al.[35] Diffusion models
come under the broader category of generative models such as GANs, VAEs, etc.,
and are inspired by the non-equilibrium thermodynamics and most specifically by
the random movement of particles through a medium. Thanks to their remarkable
performance, diffusion models have gained a lot of attention over the last few years.
[11]. A lot of research has been conducted around them leading to improvements
compared to the initial model. Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models [36] introduced an
alternative sampling scheme that enables deterministic sampling by skipping multiple
steps. Changes have been made to enhance DDPMs [28],[37]. These improvements
encompass modifications to the loss objective, architectural enhancements, and the
incorporation of classifier guidance during sampling, ultimately leading to improved
image quality. These advancements highlight the evolution of diffusion models, from
their initial formulation to the development of more eﬀicient and effective training
procedures.
The whole model is based on the idea of adding noise repetitively to the original
data distribution until the distribution is transformed into pure Gaussian noise. Then
a model can be trained to recover the original distribution by going backward in this
chain of steps. Diffusion models are trying to learn the process of recovering the image
from the pure noise distribution. The process consists of 2 steps, the forward and the
backward process. Below, we define the mathematical formulation and the purpose
of each process to get a deeper understanding of the functionality of the model.

formalization introduced by [17]. Suppose that we have a data distribution denoted
by q(x0) where x0 signifies that the data is not altered, and it is the original. The
diffusion model’s architecture is based on the Markov chain and the forward process

Convolutional Neural Networks
Convolutional Neural Network also known as CNN is another deep learning archi-
tecture that is mainly used with image and video data. A simple CNN architecture
consists of three layers 2.1: a convolutional layer, a pooling layer, and a fully con-
nected layer[19].
Figure 2.1: Convolutional Neural Network architecture.
This is the basic
architecture behind convolutional neural networks with a convolutional
layer, a pooling layer, and a fully-connected layer.
The convolutional layer is the core of CNNs as this is where the convolution
operation is performed. By convolution operation, we define the process where we
take the dot product of the image and a filter or kernel to extract features from the
original image. The filter/kernel slides over the input image and produces a feature
map which is the output of the convolution layer. In figure 2.2, we see a 2d kernel
that is multiplied with the input, and the result is the output of this multiplication.
Depending on the filter which is being applied, the convolutional layer allows the
model to catch contextual information such as geometric features or textures.
Figure 2.2: Convolution operation.
Dot product operation between the filter
and input matrix.
The pooling layer is placed after the convolution layer. Its role is to retain all
the useful information which was produced from the convolutional layer as output
but to decrease its spatial dimensionality. Consequently, pooling layers reduce the
==PAGE:18==
2.3 U-Net architecture
6
parameters inside the network thus they save time and further preprocessing during
the training phase. There are a lot of pooling methods such as max pooling, average
pooling, and sum pooling. The most widely used pooling method is max pooling. The
operational procedure of the pooling layer involves sliding a kernel over the feature
map and selecting the highest value of the feature map inside this window 2.3. This
value is then used as a representation of this part of the original map and all the
other values inside the window are ignored. By doing this process through the whole
feature map we end up with a new feature map representation that is smaller than
the original. Pooling layers must be used with caution and depending on the original
data as there is the danger of losing important information.
Figure 2.3: Max pooling shows the input and the output matrix of the max pool-
ing operation with a 2D kernel.
The final component of CNNs is the fully-connected layer which takes the previous
layers’ outputs to produce a classification decision. The neurons in the fully-connected
layer are linked directly to the neurons in the previous and next layers. Unlike other
layers in the network, there are no intermediate connections between these neurons.
Usually, the number of output neurons is equal to the number of different classes and
the fully connected component is calculating the different scores of each class for a
specific input - making a classification decision.

U-Net architecture
Since we are using Ho et al.[17] architecture for our diffusion model, we need to
analyze the U-net network which is used for the denoising of the image in the reversed
process. The U-net architecture was introduced by Ronneberger et al [33] where at
that time it achieved very promising results in medical image segmentation. The
U-net architecture is a type of convolutional neural network which is structured in 2
parts: a contracting path and an expanding path. This enables the network to learn
high-level features and spatial information, which is essential for image segmentation.
It has a similar structure to the autoencoder where the contracting path (encoder)
is used to decrease the resolution whereas the expanding path (decoder) is doing the
opposite to retrieve the original data shape. The different layers of the contracting

Let’s now define the relevant terms that were outlined above:
• Encoder: The role of the encoder is the extraction of the relevant features of
the input image. To do so, the encoder must learn how to make an abstract
representation of the given image. To achieve this, the encoder is made up of
convolutions that are followed by a ReLU activation function. This is a neces-
sary step as it allows us to introduce nonlinearity to the network. Afterward,
there is a pooling layer, which allows for reducing the dimensionality.
This

• Decoder: On the other hand, the decoder does the reverse process of the
encoder. This means that the decoder is responsible for taking abstract repre-

transpose convolutions. Then, it follows up with skip connections. Skip connec-

layers. The feature maps which are being produced in the encoder blocks are

a ReLU activation function.

In terms of classification, the way the SVM algorithm works is by placing an
optimal hyper-plane between the data such that the distance between the closest
points will be maximized. The goal of the SVM algorithm is to find a hyper-plane in
an N-dimensional space, where N is the number of classes, to classify the data points
(depending on the class they belong to). For instance, if we have a binary classification

are several possible hyper-planes that could be chosen to separate the data points.
The objective is to find the hyperplane which offers the maximum margin/maximum
distance between the points of two classes. By maximizing the margin between the
classes, we increase the probability and the confidence level that the next data points
after the training will be put on the correct side of the hyperplane.

2.5
Principal Component Analysis
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a widely used method in machine learning
for analyzing multidimensional data. It is a mathematical procedure that transforms
a set of correlated variables into a new set of uncorrelated variables, called principal
components. PCA is used to identify the underlying structure in the data, reduce the
dimensionality of the dataset, simplify it, and extract the most important features.
The basic idea of PCA is to find a new set of variables that capture the maximum
amount of variation in the original data. These new variables are linear combinations
of the original variables and are chosen in such a way that the first variable captures
the most variation, the second variable captures the most remaining variation, and so
on. The number of principal components is equal to the number of original variables.
PCA can be perceived as a process of transforming the data from the original
coordinate system to a new coordinate system, where the axes are aligned with the
directions of maximum variation in the data. The first principal component is the
direction of maximum variation, the second principal component is the direction of
maximum remaining variation, and so on.
Let X be an m x n matrix of m data points, each with n features. The goal of PCA
is to find a set of k orthogonal vectors, called principal components, that best explain
the variance in the data. The first principal component is defined as the direction
that maximizes the variance of the projected data. The second principal component
is defined as the direction that maximizes the variance of the projected data, subject

component is defined as the direction that maximizes the variance of the projected
data, subject to being orthogonal to the first (k-1) principal components.

C of the data matrix X. The covariance matrix is an n x n matrix that describes the

3.4
Point selection
The point selection is set up so that we can simulate the human annotator input.
As we can see in Figure 3.3 the way human annotation works is the following. The
annotator is presented with an object image and gives a positive or a negative click
based on whether the pixel belongs or not in the object we want to segment. From
this click, we extract the coordinates of the pixel and also the pixel value. To do this
without human presence we deploy different methods for the click selection and then
we extract the value of the pixel by using the ground truth mask as the reference.
Figure 3.3: Point selection. A representation of the user making a positive click
(selecting a pixel that belongs to the object we want to segment (the
value is 1). By clicking on the image we are able to extract the coordi-
nates x,y of the click as well as its value.
3.4.1
Point selection with maximum uncertainty
The initial algorithm implemented for click selection aims to identify the pixel with
the maximum uncertainty. In this context, maximum uncertainty refers to a pixel that
exhibits an equal probability (0.5) of being included or excluded from the produced
masks. To accomplish this selection, we consider the set of sampled masks, denoted
as Si, and aggregate them at the pixel level. Each mask assigns a value of 0 if the
pixel is not part of the produced mask, and 1 if it is included. With a total of n
masks, summing the masks at each pixel results in values ranging from 0 to n. A
pixel attains a value of 0 if it is consistently absent across all produced masks, while
==PAGE:34==
3.4 Point selection
22
a value of n indicates its presence in every mask. To identify the most uncertain
pixels, we calculate the absolute distance of each pixel from the midpoint n/2, which
corresponds to half the number of produced masks. Pixels exhibiting a distance of
zero represent the highest uncertainty. From this set of uncertain pixels, we select a
target pixel that will serve as an informative signal for our next steps. The objective
behind selecting the most uncertain pixel is to identify areas within the object where
our model’s predictions exhibit the greatest uncertainty. This algorithm facilitates
the exploration of regions where the model may lack confidence, allowing for targeted
refinement or guidance in subsequent processes.
3.4.2
Uncertainty-driven point selection algorithm with maximum
distance constraint
The algorithm leverages again the concept of uncertainty in point selection to identify
pixels with maximum uncertainty. At first, it selects one random point among those
exhibiting maximum uncertainty. For subsequent clicks, the algorithm again identifies
pixels with maximum uncertainty and selects the pixel with the maximum distance
from the previously selected points. This iterative point selection component ensures
that each click provides distinct information about the object, compared to previous
selections.
3.4.3
Point selection with standard deviation
Building upon the maximum uncertainty approach, our method incorporates a pre-
processing step where a Gaussian filter is applied to the segmentation masks. This
filtering operation aims to smooth the masks and eliminate potential outliers. Subse-
quently, the most uncertain pixel is determined by calculating the standard deviation
per pixel across all masks and selecting the pixel exhibiting the highest standard de-
viation value. We demonstrate that the standard deviation, serving as a measure of
uncertainty, approximates the distance from the mean of the masks, with a higher
standard deviation indicating greater uncertainty. Our proposed method contributes
to refining the object annotation process by improving the robustness and reliability
of point selection.

This chapter presents the evaluation of our methodology for interactive image segmen-
tation with diffusion models. The primary objective of the evaluation is to assess the
effectiveness of our approach in producing high-quality segmentation masks. Thus
we analyze and discuss the different experiments employed to select the best masks
from the generated sequence. In section 4.1 we present the datasets we used during

down the selection until a single mask remains as the outcome. In the offline process
section, we present all the experiments which were conducted regarding this method

approach for refining the segmentation masks and offers insights into the potential
improvements that can be achieved through automated refinement. Throughout this

experiments are further discussed later in chapter 5.

Preprossesing
The preprocessing procedure involves several steps for both the image and the segmen-
tation mask. Starting with the image, it begins by loading the image and converting
it to the RGB color space. The next step involves cropping the image based on the
bounding box coordinates. The bounding box specifies the region of interest within
the image that contains the object of interest. By cropping the image, we isolate the
object and remove any unnecessary background. After cropping, the image is trans-
formed to the desired resolution of 128x128. Scaling is applied to ensure that objects
have a consistent size, regardless of their original dimensions. Additionally, the center
of the bounding box of the object becomes the center of the new image. Positional
noise is also incorporated to slightly displace the transformed image. Finally, the
pixel values of the image are normalized to a standardized range, typically between 0
and 1, to facilitate further processing and analysis. Similarly, the segmentation mask
undergoes preprocessing steps. Initially, the mask is loaded in its original format. To
focus on the specific object of interest, the mask is cropped based on the bounding box

4.4.4
Testing different probabilities for the selected points
As we mentioned earlier when we add the 256 masks on the pixel level, each pixel
of the new image which is produced after the summary of all the masks can take
values from 0 to 256. If a pixel has a value of zero then it means that all the masks
had a value of zero at this coordinates and thus this pixel was not part of any of
the produced masks. On the other hand, if a pixel has a value of 256, then this
means that this pixel was part of all of the masks. In our subsequent experiment, we
extend the selection criterion beyond identifying the most uncertain pixels and aim to
identify pixels present in a range of 25% to 75% of the masks. Instead of selecting the
pixel with the closest distance to zero, we calculate the distances for all pixels, sort
them in ascending order, and retain the first d distances based on a predetermined
threshold. For example, if the threshold is set to 4, we keep the first 256/4 = 64 pixels
with the lowest distances. From this subset, a random point is selected and used as
the selection point. The purpose of this experiment is to assess the performance of
alternative selection points apart from the most uncertain pixels, as these uncertain
pixels often reside on the boundaries of the object. Our aim is to investigate if clicks
closer to the center of the object yield better results.

4.5
Online process
The online process is investigated as a means to refine the generated masks and
enhance their quality. To evaluate the eﬀicacy of this method, three distinct experi-
ments are conducted. In the first experiment(see figure 4.21), the selection of a click is
guided by identifying the pixel with maximum uncertainty, akin to the offline process.
Utilizing this click, the masks are partitioned into positive and negative categories.
After, principal component analysis (PCA) is employed, aiming to identify a reduced
set of components that collectively account for 95% of the variance. Following the
determination of the principal components, an SVM algorithm is trained to estimate
a linear hyperplane capable of effectively separating the masks based on the PCA
results. Based on the perpendicular to the hyperplane vector w, the PCA of each
negative mask is shifted via PCA′
i = kw+PCAi towards the direction of the positive
hyperplane. The outcomes of this manipulation are then reconstructed returning the
data to their original dimensions resulting in new refined masks.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis delved into the exploration of diffusion models for interactive image seg-
mentation. Our objective was to train a model capable of generating high-quality
segmentation masks guided by the images of objects.
We began by training our
model using a diverse range of datasets and their corresponding ground truth masks.
The training process enabled the model to learn the intricacies of image segmenta-
tion and improve its performance over time. Through extensive experimentation, we
found that the model achieved its maximum potential in terms of Intersection over
Union (IoU) with approximately 100 sampled masks. This indicates that our model
possesses satisfactory sampling eﬀiciency, providing a reasonable balance between
accuracy and speed. We evaluated the performance of the model by comparing the
quality of the generated masks. Our findings revealed that the model consistently
produced masks with high IoU values, indicating a strong alignment with the ground
truth masks. This demonstrates the effectiveness of our proposed method in leverag-
ing diffusion models for interactive image segmentation. Furthermore, we introduced
various sorting processes to filter and refine the generated masks. Among the different
point selection methods we explored, the approach utilizing standard deviation exhib-
ited superior performance. This method allowed for more precise and accurate mask
selection, resulting in improved segmentation outcomes. The conclusion we can draw
from the superiority of this method is that having an effective way of dealing with
outliers can increase eﬀiciency, since through this method outliers were dealt with,
using Gaussian filtering. In conclusion, this thesis has successfully demonstrated the
potential of diffusion models for interactive image segmentation. The model’s ability
to generate high-quality segmentation masks has the potential to significantly accel-
erate the image annotation process and facilitate faster and easier dataset creation.
By leveraging the strengths of diffusion models, we have contributed to the scalability
and eﬀiciency of computer vision tasks.
The outcomes of the evaluation play a crucial role in validating the effectiveness
of our methodology for interactive image segmentation. The insights gained from
this evaluation will inform the refinement of our approach and guide future research
directions in this field. By improving the accuracy and eﬀiciency of interactive image
segmentation, our methodology holds the potential to benefit various applications,
such as object recognition, image editing, and computer vision tasks.

CHAPTER 6
Future work
Our proposed method has shown promising results in terms of image segmentation.
However, there are several areas that can be further improved and explored to enhance
its eﬀiciency and performance.
One aspect that can be improved is the time required for producing the segmenta-
tion masks. One potential approach is to replace the Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic
Models with latent diffusion models. By operating in the latent space, we can reduce
the dimensionality of the dataset and sample images faster. This change would allow
us to work with a feature space of the input, resulting in quicker sampling. Addition-
ally, working in the latent space may lead to better refinement in the online process
when using the SVM method. With fewer parameters to consider, it becomes easier
to separate the masks and find a more suitable hyperplane for segmentation.
Another area for improvement is the exploration of better click methods to opti-
mize the segmentation algorithms. In the offline process, we observed that it takes
approximately 8 to 10 clicks to find a good-quality mask from a dataset of 256 sam-
pled masks. By investigating techniques that minimize the number of clicks while
maximizing the quality of the final segmentation, such as active learning or rein-
forcement learning-based approaches, we can further enhance the performance of our
model. This not only can improve the accuracy and eﬀiciency of the segmentation
process but also reduce human involvement, aligning with the motivation behind this
thesis.
Our proposed method can be synergistically combined with other existing im-
age segmentation methods discussed in the literature. By integrating our approach,
which generates a variety of masks, with their refinement techniques, we can poten-
tially achieve highly promising results. Exploring combinations with state-of-the-art
segmentation methods may lead to further improvements in accuracy.
In conclusion, future research directions should focus on enhancing the eﬀiciency
of the segmentation process, exploring advanced click methods, and investigating the
integration of our method with existing segmentation approaches. These endeavors
would not only reduce the time required for generating masks but also improve the
overall segmentation quality and reduce human involvement. By addressing these
aspects, we can advance the field of interactive image segmentation and facilitate
faster and more accurate object segmentation in various applications.
