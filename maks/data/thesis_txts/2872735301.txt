Abstract
This thesis investigates the use of pseudo-residuals as a method for modelling correlation
structures.
The primary goal was to model the correlation structure of wind power-production fore-
casts for four different sources in Denmark.
The goal was accomplished, with an improvement in the relevant score of 17%-19% over
the base marginal forecast, or up to 33%-51% improvement over the raw forecast used.
The conclusion of the project is that pseudo-residuals provides a simple and fast way of
modelling correlation structures, that would otherwise be difficult to model.

Introduction
The general electricity systems of the world have evolved significantly in the last 20 years.
Liberalisation of electricity markets, as well as the development of renewable energy so-
lutions, has significantly impacted the operations of national energy grids. [1][2]
Figure 1.1
Percentage of renewable energy in the EU. Graph Taken directly from Energy Dashboard - Euro-
stat [3]
Renewable energy sources, such as solar, wind, and hydropower, tend to exhibit much
higher volatility compared to traditional power sources [1].
This higher volatility increases the difficulty operating, planning, and balancing the energy
production in the grid, and increases the demand for good forecasts. Good forecasts
helps in planning production, reducing the need for expensive regulation, and reducing
the risk of brown or blackouts.[1]
Electricity in particular has had a dramatic increase in usage of renewable sources, ac-
counting for 45% or all electricity in 2023[2], see fig. 1.1

1.1
Electricity Markets
A very brief overview of electricity markets is presented here.
In general, the electricity market is split into 3 markets: The day ahead market, intra-day
market, and an imbalance market for ancillary services.
Along the three markets, there also exist markets for forwards and futures, these are
beyond the scope of this thesis.
TSO & DSO
The energy grid generally consists of a high voltage transmission net, transporting the bulk
energy from power plants to areas where it is needed, and a lower voltage distribution net,
distributing it to end consumer in useful form.
These entities are known as Transmission System Operator (TSO). These companies are
often state-owned. The entities operating the distribution net are known as Distribution
System Operator (DSO)[1]
In Denmark, the sole TSO is Energinet[4]. There are several independent DSO, as can
be seen in fig. 1.3
Producers and Consumers
The electricity in the grid has to be generated and used for some purpose. Producers
would be power plants that can generate electricity. Consumers would be either large
companies that need power for production or companies that buy power for resale to
retail-consumers. [1]
Day-ahead Market
The day ahead market opens, as the name suggests, the day before. Here energy can
be bought and sold for predefined blocks for the next day.
At a certain point, the market closes, bids and offers are matched to find the market price.
After market prices are determined, a production schedule is published obligating all buy-
ers and sellers to the scheduled production/consumption. [1]
The Danish day-ahead electricity market opens the day before and buy and sell orders
can be made until 12:00 the day before. At 13:00, buy and sell orders are matched and
the final production schedule is published [5]
Intra-day Market
After the day-ahead market closes, the intra-day market opens. Here, it is possible to
trade electricity up to just before the respective hours begin.
This ensures that parties can react to changing circumstances, Like unexpected produc-
tion stops, changing forecasts, etc. [1]
In Denmark, the intra-day market for a given hour opens at 15:00 the day before and
closes one hour before the given hour [5]
Ancillary/Imbalance market
In the ancillary market, balancing services are usually traded, e.g. reserve capacity and
frequency stabilisation services. These ancillary services are not scheduled to be used
but bought to activate should the need arise.[1]
Ancillary services is defined by the expect operating properties, with a major factor being
response time. In Denmark is divided into six ancillary services: FFR, FCR, FCR-D, FCR-
N, aFRR, mFRR. With each zone only trading a subset of these:

1.2
Perspective
Motivation
The main motivation for this thesis is to improve the forecasting of power production. and
builds on the previous thesis by JГёrgensen [10], and subsequent paper JГёrgensen, MГёller,
Nystrup, et al. [11].
The method, Neural Adaptive Basis for Quantile Regression (NABQR), employed by JГёr-
gensen, MГёller, Nystrup, et al. [11] showed (substantial) improvements in marginal fore-
casts when compared to the base forecast. the method was also purely mathematical
requiring no knowledge of the underlying system.
The resulting forecast only gave marginal distributions, the full correlation structure was
not determined.
Previous Work
As mentioned above, this thesis builds on the work of JГёrgensen, MГёller, Nystrup, et al.
[11]. Where a Neural-Net (NN) was used to construct an basis for regression to improve
forecasts.
This work in turn used the work of MГёller, Nielsen, and Madsen [12], who created Time
Adaptive Quantile Regression (TAQR). Other works with similar purpose include MГёller,
Zugno, and Madsen [13], using stochastic differential equations to directly estimate the
full distributional model of the forecast.
Since 2020 huge advances have been made in the development of deep learning models,
especially transformer models. These models seem to perform well, at the cost of requir-
ing more data and computational resources. An overview of recent advances was made
by Li and Law [14]
Problem Statement, and work done
The main question of the thesis is formulated as
Problem Statement:
Can the correlation structure of a time-varying process be deter-
mined through the pseudo-residuals of a marginal forecast.
This problem is investigated in chapter 7.
To properly answer this question, methods of evaluation are needed. For one score, the
Variogram Score (VarS), work was done to investigate whether the standard setup was
optimal, done in section 3.5.
During the course of the project, problems was found in the implementation. These prob-
lems had to be investigated and fixed for ensuring accurate results, chapter 6.
Finally methods of monotone interpolation was needed to convert a set of estimated quan-
tiles into a distribution, a short review was done in chapter 4.

This thesis builds on the research of JГёrgensen, MГёller, Nystrup, et al. [11], using the
same data set.
The entire data set spans an approximately three-year period from January 2022 to mid
October 2024 with the specific dates given in table 2.1

The data set consists of hourly observations of wind power production for each of the four
zones, detailed in section 2.1.
In addition to observation data, the dataset also includes hourly production forecasts,
again in each of the four zones. Forecast data is described in section 2.2.
All data, both production and forecast, are given in MWh.
In the thesis by JГёrgensen [10] a larger effort was made to deal with two things in the
dataset.
Firstly, due to agreements between Denmark and other countries, Danish producers are
sometime paid to shutdown production, known as curtailment.
As changes the agreements this is curtailment is expected to be less of a problem in the
future. Periods with curtailment was therefore removed as these periods would not be
comparable with future behaviour.
Secondly, The forecast for DK2-offshore had some obvious failure modes, described in
section 2.2, These periods was also removed.
For this project it was decided not to remove these periods, Firstly, Removing the data
would mean there would be jumps in observations which would interfere with learning the
correlations structure.
Secondly it was decided that removing the failures in DK2-offshore would artificially im-
prove the performance of the base forecast. The forecasts should be evaluated on the
actual performance, not the ideal performance.
Data Alignment
One small detail is the alignment of the data. The forecast data were provided with time

Training and Evaluation
To avoid biases and detect overfitting, it is essential to train and evaluate models on sep-
arate datasets. Ensuring that no direct information is shared between the training and
evaluation phases, other than the underlying system of interest, is crucial[18].
This separation helps prevent models from overfitting, similar to memorising an answer
sheet for a test, which can lead to artificially inflated performance metrics. [18].
A simple and common way of handling this is the k-fold cross-validation. Where the data
are (randomly) divided into k blocks, the models are trained in k вҲ’1 blocks and evaluated
on the last block, one fold. Then repeated for each block[18].
Because of the high temporal correlation that exists in the data, this approach does not
work. Data leaks can occur as future data correlate with current.[19].
Ideally, more advanced strategies are used as described in Roberts, Bahn, Ciuti, et al.

4
Distributions
For analysing autocorrelation it is necessary to have a full continuous probability distribu-
tion available. The scoring rules described in sections 3.2 and 3.5 require the complete
distributions to be evaluated or at least the ability to simulate from the distribution
For most of the methods used in this project the output is not a complete distribution but
point estimates corresponding to quantiles of the estimated distribution.
To remedy this, methods are needed to interpolate a distribution from quantiles.
Approaches
Most of the literature seems to focus on fitting parametric distributions to empirical dates
using least squares or maximum likelihood methods[27], or kernel methods to estimate a
Probability Density Function (PDF) directly from data[28]
Both of these approaches require samples directly from the underlying distribution and
are therefore not applicable to this project.
For fitting a parametric distribution to quantiles directly, there are fewer resources and
most seem to rely on exactly matching quantiles, not always possible if number of quan-
tiles > number of parameters[29]. Or using a weighted least squares fit [30].
During the course of this project one other method was found, which seems promising
Gabachov [31], there is however a mistake in the method described making it unsuitable.
For non-parametric interpolation, spline models seem the most popular. An additional
challenge compared to normal interpolation is that and interpolated cdf has to be mono-
tone.

6.1
NABQR
NABQR is a method to improve the reliability of the forecast introduced by JГёrgensen,
MГёller, Nystrup, et al. [11]. The main motivation was that ECMWF-based forecasts, relying
on simulating a physical system, provided good point estimates but did not always give
good risk estimates. [11]
The idea was therefore to use improve the forecast using purely numerical methods. The
method implemented had two steps:
First, the forecast was fed into a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) translating the forecast
into a new (reduced) basis.
Secondly, the transformed ensembles was then run through the TAQR, which gave the
new improved forecast. [11]

Time Adaptation
For adding time adaptivity. the size of the design matrix X was limited. When a new
observation Xt, yt was added to the data, the oldest observation in X, Y was dropped,
and the optimisation algorithm was run again to find new parameters ОІП„.

mate would most likely be close to the new optimal point, and the simplex algorithm can
converge quickly[12].
An additional detail is how the oldest observation was dropped. To ensure that all situa-
tions are adequately described, the design matrix X and observations Y were divided into
bins. When a new observation was introduced, the corresponding bin was determined,
and the oldest observation from the corresponding bin dropped.

Problems in TAQR
During the course of the project, some critical problems were found in the python imple-
mentation of TAQR.
Some of these affected the reliability of the code, these problems were fixed, while another
affected the usability of the implementation, due to this problem the useability of TAQR
was very limited and it was decided to include only as a reference point.
Misalignment of output
The first problem was the alignment of the output.
For a given data input, the implementation TAQR returned three outputs: Estimated quan-
tiles, actual observations, and regression parameters at each time point.
All three for only the test data.
There was a misalignment between these outputs, defining t1 as the time of the first test
data point, and tn for the last. The returned parameters spanned t2 to tnвҲ’1, the returned
quantiles spanned t3 to tn and the return observations spanned t1 to tnвҲ’2.
However, the pipeline of NABQR used the alignment of the parameters for all the outputs
returned, t1 to tnвҲ’1. the result of which can be seen in fig. 6.2
Missing Initialisation
The second problem found was that of initialisation.
It was found that no matter the amount of initialisation data, the estimates were always
more noisy in the beginning of the test data compared to the end of the test data.
This was weird when the algorith was supposed to have вҲј16000 data points after initiali-
sation.
It turns out that the TAQR implementation only used the first 500 data points for initialisa-
tion and discarded the rest. This can be seen in fig. 6.3.
The initial estimates are always the same once there are more than 500 data points of
initialisation.
The estimates have a rapid change in value after roughly 500 new data points, when the
new data begin to outnumber the initial data.
The root of the problem was found in the R code used to provide an initial point for the sim-
plex algorithm. The code accidentally discarded everything but the first 500 data points.

Problems in NABQR Architecture
Some problems in relating to the architecture of the NN in NABQR was also found during
the course of the project.
Dead Outputs
One problem with the NN used in the original implementation of NABQR was that of dead
outputs. That is, outputs which only estimate zeros no matter the input. [10]

During the course of this project the cause of this problem was discovered. As negative
power production, in the context of the data, was not possible, it was decided to use a
ReLU activation on the output layer of NN.
This ensured that the output was never negative but erased the gradient of any output
neurons that happened to be zero.
Training by backpropagation propagates the loss gradient back through the network, but
with ReLU activation, one can quickly determine that

As neural networks are initialised at random, some outputs will be negative by chance.
During training, some of these dead outputs will, also by chance, suddenly вҖқresurrectвҖқ
and become trainable.
This can be seen in the sudden drops in the loss curves reported by JГёrgensen [10].
To fix the problem, the ReLU activation of the output was replaced by a leaky ReLU.
Overfitting
Another problem with the NN basis model of NABQR found during this project was that of
overfitting.
The models used by JГёrgensen, MГёller, Nystrup, et al. [11] did not attempt to control
for over-fitting. It was probably not needed, as the loss was often dominated by dead
neurons.
After fixing dead neurones, it was noticed that the original NABQR model had a tendency
to overfit, as can be seen in fig. 6.4
To address the problem of overfitting, an early stopping criterion was implemented. The
validation loss was tracked, and if there had been no improvements in validation loss
for 20 epochs, the training would be stopped and the model from 20 epochs earlier was
used.

7.3
Pipeline
With all theory of preparatory work in place, the final method for modelling correlation
structure can be detailed.
The ensemble forecast, described in chapter 2, is given as input to the marginal model,
the feature model from chapter 6, giving estimated quantiles.
These quantiles are then interpolated into a full distribution using a monotone spline of 3.
order, from chapter 4.
The interpolated distribution is then used to transform the observed power production,
chapter 2, into pseudo-residuals, chapter 5.
The pseudo-residuals are used to make a correlation corrected forecast using the SARMA
model, found in chapter 7,
Finally, the corrected forecast is translated back into the original space. Here to be eval-
uated using the methods in chapter 3
The whole process is illustrated in fig. 7.4.
Modularity
As was the intention of the original NABQR method, the whole process is very modular[11].
Each part of the method can be changed without altering the other parts.
For example, it is very possible that better model architectures can be found to estimate
marginal quantiles. This part of the model could then be changed while the rest is kept.
Likewise, a better way of interpolating/estimating the full distribution is possible, for ex-
ample, using higher-order polynomials, which can then be implemented in the pipeline
without changing anything else.

8
Summary
VarS
In chapter 3 the example presented showed that the standard method of calculating VarS,
using the inverse distance as a weight, is not ideal.
In the example the score went from being able to chose the correct model in 82% of the
time to 77%. A 7% reduction in performance.
More work should be done to figure out if a good general weighting exist. What weights
would be good to use in different situations.
NABQR & TAQR
In chapter 6 problems in the current implementation of TAQR and NABQR was investi-
gated and fixed. Resulting in better, and more reliable forecast. A problem affecting the
run time of the code was however not fixed.
The large improvements in scores, as found by JГёrgensen, MГёller, Nystrup, et al. [11] was
also found chapter 6. The large improvements seemed to only be in DK2, where the base
forecasts were less reliable and had clear failure modes.
TAQR in general perform equivalently or better than both the ensembles and the NN
models in all cases. This could indicate there is some shift in the underlying system
which the other models do not capture.
The good performance of the simple model would also indicate that the forecast do not
require a complex setup to model. A simpler method than a NN could used, for example
a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or Independent Component Analysis (ICA).
Correlation Structure
In chapter 7 the new method of modelling autocorrelation was presented. The Results
presented in table 7.2 suggests the method is very capable of capturing the correlation
structure of the system of improvements of 17% вҲ’19% in VarS score compared to the
base marignal forecast.
The results also shows there is some improvements to be made just by incorporating
previous observations, this could be done in the marginal forecast.
Taking the results chapters 6 and 7 together also imply that the NABQR method can be a
very simple, fast and powerful method of modelling time-series. Though some fine-tuning
are still needed.
Final Remarks
An original idea of the thesis was to use Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) to model
the correlation structure in original space, but due to the good results found using the
simple SARMA method this was abandoned.
The advantage of SDEs would be that can model the correlation in original space allowing
for easy interpretation. The obvious interpretation of the SARMA parameters are hard to
find.
To conclude on the on the main problem as presented in section 1.2. Modelling correlation
structures through pseudo-residuals seem very promising and allows leveraging simple
well-known methods in more complex situations.

Further Work
As mentioned in section 6.2 sproblems still exists in the TAQR Which were not fixed. As
the results in table 6.4 indicate, the algorithm has the potential to improve the forecast
further.
The complex NN basis for regression is possibly not necessary as indicated by the simple
model.
If the basis was replaces be a simpler method, it would result in a complete method con-
sisting of simple basis + TAQR + spline interpolation + SARMA. A potentially very fast and
general method of modelling time-series.
Other NN architectures exists for forecasting time series, which were not explored in this
thesis. It would be prudent to compare the method presented here to the state of the art
models.
More work could be put into finding a distribution from the estimated quantiles. Higher
order monotone splines could be implemented, or parametric distributions could be used
that could alleviate some the the problems in the tail distributions.
Finally the methods here only looked at each zone in isolation. As noted in section 2.1
there is a definition correlation between zones. Modelling all zones together could easily
give better results.
