Adhesion strength of 2k over-moulded plastic materials
Abstract
Two component injection moulding is not a new technology, but there is still lack of in-
formation needed to ensure a safe and mechanically robust part design.
Today, when
designing two component parts there are no material data from suppliers regarding the
adhesion strength of 2k components.
The design of 2k parts is based on assumptions
that combine the qualitative input from the material suppliers and the individual material
data. In this project it has been proven that these assumptions yield to over-optimistic
results because the phenomena occurring during and after the 2k over-moulding process
of polymers are complex and require significant attention.
Having acknowledge about the lack of missing information for the adhesion strength of 2k
parts, the designers cannot ensure the strength of the bond based solely on the chemical
bond created during the remelting of the two materials and they are forced to introduce
mechanical locks that they have severe influence in the mould-ability of the components.
Therefore, Novo Nordisk’s R&D department created this a project with a view to establish
a method for the characterisation of the adhesion strength.
The project starts from the selection of the most relevant testing method available, con-
sidering the loads are more prone to be applied in NN medical devices. Subsequently,
a test specimen and its mould that is suitable for characterisation of adhesion strength
of 2k parts were designed taking into account shrinkage, NN parts dimensions, adhesion
strength factors etc. Finally, a robust fixture with main driver the even stress distribu-
tion on the interface of the two components was designed. The characterisation process
has been verified with structural analysis simulation software where the adhesion strength
obtained from the torsion testing was used as an input and the simulation yielded results
sufficiently similar to the actual torsion test..
Among numerous factors identified from the literature that affect the adhesion strength
of 2k parts, two of them were further investigated experimentally. The first was the sur-
face roughness of the interface of the two components, with results showing that it has
a considerable impact on the adhesion strength. In addition, the adhesion strength was
measured as a function of post-processing time showing that there is no concern for re-
duction of adhesion strength with time.
Finally, a further study of the adhesion strength took place using the means of computed
tomography (CT) and optical microscopy to obtain more information about the adhesion
of polymers in micro scale and create a deeper understanding of the phenomena that
take place and influence the obtained results. CT by recognising the differences in the
material density could identify gaps in between the 2K part components that compromise
the bonding strength. Transmitted optical microscopy using polarised light through thin
sections of the 2k component, can illustrate the micro-structure including crystals, weld
line of the bond and skin layer of semi-crystalline materials.

Introduction
The integration of secondary operations in the injection moulding process has been pro-
posed to meet the needs of the industry, particularly when high volume production is
the goal. The advantages of injection moulding in terms of productivity and automation,
which allows the combination of technologies, make its use even more interesting [22]. It
is one of the main goals of the cycle to join two or more components together, and for that

1.4
Introduction of thermoplastic polymer adhesion
The quality and the mechanical robustness of a part made from 2k injection moulding is
very much defined from the quality of the adhesion between the two polymer materials.
The establishment of a bond between two thermoplastic materials is characterized by two
main phenomena that define the degree of bonding: (1) development of intimate contact
between the insert and injected polymer, and (2) interdiffusion of polymer chains across
the interface. The latter is also known as healing or autohesion and can only occur once
intimate contact has been achieved [28].
Weld lines, which are typical for injected moulded parts, could be thought of as intimate
contacts that occur almost instantaneously as the molten flow fronts merge, where bonding
takes place at temperatures close to melting point of materials. To achieve full intimate
contact, the surface roughness, the applied pressure and the viscosity of the materials,
which is temperature dependent, are crucial [29]. Therefore, during the over-moulding
process the melt temperature at which the second polymer melt meet the first moulded
polymer and the injection and packing pressure are critical for establishing an intimate
contact.
Upon achieving intimate contact between the surfaces, thermal motion allows the polymer
chains to diffuse across the interface.
The polymer chains entangle with those of the
adjacent surface, contributing to the actual interface strength development. As long as
the polymer mobility is high enough, diffusion and entanglement will occur continuously,
and the interface will fade until the material cannot be distinguished from the bulk polymer
[29].

3.1
Parameters affecting adhesion strength
Generally, the adhesion between two polymers is formed through inter-molecular attraction
between molecules in intimate contact. The bonds are formed due to :
• Van der Waals attractive forces
• Polar interactions and hydrogen bonds
• Covalent, chemical bonds
The key mechanisms involved in the development of an efficient bond are:
Mechanical adhesion: Associated with the degree of roughness and thus the friction
of the surface of the bonding materials. A certain amount of bonding can be expected
solely from the mechanical interlocking of the two surfaces in contact, increasing the total
surface area available for chemical bonding and creating a convoluted failure path where
the plastic melt penetrates into cracks in the surface of the second plastic.
Although
the tensile strength of the weld may be dependent on the re-entrant angles at the sur-
face of the adherent, the shear strength increases significantly with increased roughness [4].
Adsorption and wetting: The formation of a physical bond resulting from highly lo-
calised inter-molecular forces. If sufficiently close contact is achieved between the adherent
plastics , a physical interaction between the atoms of the two surfaces develops, leading
to wetting. Wetting may be due to acid-base interactions, weak hydrogen bonds or Van
der Waals forces (dipole-dipole and dispersion forces). The extent of wetting depends on
the differences in the surface free energies of the plastics and subsequent interface.
Chemical bonding: The formation of a stable bond by the reaction between a functional
group on the surface of the one plastic and a compatible group in the other. Adherent
surfaces are usually subjected to surface treatments (e.g., chemical etching, corona dis-
charge and plasma treatments) to create compatible groups. These treatments serve to
increase the concentration of oxygen and nitrogen-containing oxygen functional groups
on the surface of the adherents, since these species are thought to enhance adhesion by
encouraging the formation of strong covalent or hydrogen bonds [4].
These mechanisms act simultaneously, but the degree of their impact on the resulting
bonding strength depends on the nature of the paired materials and the processing pa-
rameters of joining.

3.1.1
Manufacturing process, surface temperature and roughness
Mould quality
The precision manufacturing of a high quality mould is a pivotal factor
to succeed a strong bond in 2k parts. The following factors are particularly critical for
the adhesion strength of 2k parts.
• Dimensioning and positioning of injection gates
• Mould halves alignment
• Cooling system
• Ejection system
• Air venting
More specifically, the injection gates are a critical aspect of the mould design. Incorrect
placement of a gate affects the way the plastic melt flows and solidifies in the cavity.
Uneven filling of the cavity of the second component during over-moulding process can
affect the bonding of the two components, as the melt temperature will not be uniform,
unevenly remelting the component of the first shot and thus affecting their bonding. It is
very important to maintain the designed gate size after mould manufacturing to maintain
acceptable shear rates, avoid pressure drop and control the packing phase [33]. Alignment
of the mould halves is also important for the quality of the part and also the life of the
mould. Excessive mould misalignment can compromise the quality of the part and the
way the melt flows into the cavity, again resulting in uneven temperature distribution in
the cavity and poor bonding of the two moulded parts. An uneven cooling system leads to
unevenly cooled parts and creates differential shrinkage in the part causing sink marks and
negatively impacts on the bonding [34]. In addition, the adhesion of the two components
can be significantly affected by the demoulding process. If the ejectors hit the part in a
sensitive area or if the part does not have big enough draft angles to be ejected easily,
the forces during ejection can compromise the strength of the weld. Finally, air venting
must always be included in the design of the mould, because if air has nowhere to escape
from the cavity it will become trapped between the plastic parts, creating gaps at their
interface and weakening their weld.
Process parameters
As inferred from [24, 6, 7] injection moulding process parameters
such as melt and mould temperature, injection speed, injection pressure, packing pres-
sure, packing time, cooling time, have a significant effect on the adhesion strength of 2k
over-moulded parts. Therefore the appropriate choice of the process parameters can lead
to a stronger adhesion between two plastics or even make movable parts with no adhesion
when normally they would have been bonded.
The melting temperature of the first component is less important because often, the first
component must be intensively cooled down to achieve stability in the moulded part or
dimensional precision. Insufficient cooling of the first component may lead to the contact
area deformation due to the second component’s melt pressure. If the first component is
cooled excessively, even zero adhesion to compatible materials can occur if the thermal
input of the second component is too low [8].
The melt temperature of the second component should be chosen in relation to its ability
to melt the boundary layer of the first component. However, if the selected temperature

Interface temperature
For amorphous plastics, a temperature higher than the glass
transition temperature is recommended at the interface, for long period of time. A tem-
perature higher than the crystalline melting temperature at the interface is recommended
for semi-crystalline plastics. Regarding the thickness of the layer to be melted to achieve
good adhesion of 2k parts, the literature suggests values between 10 nm and 10 µm [8].
This wide range can be explained by the fact that the amount of influence strongly de-
pends on the materials and material combination [6].
In injection moulding of 2k polymer materials, the interface temperature is influenced by
several parameters. In addition to the component temperatures, thermal diffusivity plays
an essential role. It is defined by density, specific heat capacity and thermal conductivity.
The extent to which the melting temperature of the second component can be influenced
is limited because it depends on the material and cannot be changed to any desired degree.
If the first component comprises a semi-crystalline plastic with a temperature that is lower

Surface roughness
Surface roughness is a possible modification of the mould and sub-
sequently the surface of the first shot plastic. This essentially enhances adhesion due to
the larger contact surface but is also associated with negative influencing factors. High
levels of roughness can inhibit wettability and allow air or dirt to enter the contact area

a larger 2k part surface area can lead to greater heat dissipation, leading to a lower heat
level in the contact region. However, in most cases, the influence of roughness is less than
the influence of process control and should be evaluated depending on the material. In
this project, surface roughness was studied, as it is a type of surface treatment that can
be easily applied during the design of the mould of the component. Such an addition to
the mould design has insignificant cost and no other impact on the injection moulding

Creep and stress relaxation
In order to understand creep failure and stress relaxation
of plastics, there must be an appreciation of viscoelasticity. Viscoelasticity is the property
of plastics in which they exhibit both viscous and elastic characteristics when placed under
stress [36]. Viscoelasticity is unique to thermoplastics due to the structure of the polymer
molecules. Polymer chains are mobile and can slide next to each other because they do
not share chemical bonds with the other chains around them.
Viscoelasticity has a key role to the performance of plastic materials. It is a fundamental
property of the behaviour of plastics and due to viscoelasticity:

• thermoplastic materials have both long-term and short-term properties (they flow
due to the application of stress over time)
• most mechanical testing of plastic materials is actually evaluating the material’s
viscoelasticity and how the plastic flows when different stresses are applied
• plastics are temperature- and strain rate-sensitive.
Creep is the tendency of a solid material to deform permanently under the influence of
constant stress: tensile, compressive, shear, or flexural. It occurs as a function of time
through extended exposure to stress levels that are below the yield strength of the mate-
rial. Stress relaxation depicts how viscoelastic materials, especially polymeric materials,
relieve stress under constant strain. Both of them are a result of the inherent viscoelastic
nature of polymers causing time dependence.
Two major factors driving the viscoelastic behaviour of polymers and, consequently, the
creep and relaxation behaviour of a plastic part are: temperature and strain rate. As
temperature increases, polymer chains move further apart, there is more free volume and
kinetic energy and they can slide past each other to detach more easily. As the strain
rate increases, the polymer chains do not have enough time to undergo yielding, and
disengagement is favoured over yielding.

obtained from material suppliers, but also perform even more material characterizations
to obtain even more representative properties for the specific conditions of interest for Novo
Nordisk medical devices, such as the temperature range around the world. In particular,
creep and stress relaxation tests are carried out for materials used in the production
line, such as PP 58MNK10.

Surface and interface tension
Surface tension refers to the forces acting at the molec-
ular level to keep a liquid’s surface area small. Surface energy determines the amount of
energy required to break chemical bonds in forming a new surface. In liquids, surface ten-
sion equals surface energy. In solid materials, this value can only be measured indirectly.
The energy, or tension, determines the wettability and adhesion ability and is determined
by the chemical structure of the materials [37]. It is based on intermolecular interactions
and different types of bonds. The surface tension consists of a non-polar, dispersed por-
tion and a polar portion. The strength of the 2k part depends on both segments[37]. The
smaller the difference between the surface stresses of the two components, i.e., the closer
the surface stress ratio is to 1, the better the strength of the over-moulded part.
The surface tension is also influenced by the processing temperatures and the process-
induced degree of crystallinity. As the temperature increases, the surface tension reduces.
Thus, during processing at high temperatures, lower stress values are on hand in a mate-
rial than for room temperature [8]. Typically, the reduction in surface tension does not
occur equally in both components, so the ratio of surface tension between the materials
also varies during processing [8].
Crystalline phases generally exhibit higher surface tension than amorphous phases. In
plastic-plastic 2k parts, this can lead to different values for identical components because
the second component is in a molten (and therefore amorphous) state at the time of con-
tact. During the injection moulding process, in this context, it is essential to consider that
the first component has an amorphous surface layer due to the rapid cooling conditions in
these regions [8].
The term interface tension denotes the forces between two phases in contact and is essen-
tially defined by the surface tensions of the individual phases. As far as plastic-plastic 2k
parts are concerned, the lower the interface tension, the more the adhesion and diffusion
processes are promoted [38]. The interface tension is zero when the same components
are in contact. The surface tension indirectly influences the interface tension due to the
material and the processing parameters.
Thus, additives, molecular weight, degree of
crystallinity and processing temperatures affect the interface tension [38].
Crystallinity
The crystallinity of materials strongly influences the molecular mobility
and, in the case of semi-crystalline materials, has a substantial effect on the strength
of the over-moulded part. The mobility of molecular chains is limited in the crystalline
regions. As mentioned previously, high molecular mobility facilitates diffusion processes.
An increasing degree of crystallinity, whether induced by the material or the process,
compensates for the diffusion tendency and thus the strength of the over-moulded part
[8].
During injection molding of semi-crystalline plastics, a surface layer with a low degree of
crystallinity, small crystallization nuclei and spherulites, is developed due to rapid mold
cooling. The reheating initiated by contact with the second-shot plastic may cause the
crystallization nuclei and spherulites to function as crystal nucleus and may lead to post-
crystallization of the surface area[8].
In the case of reheating, diffusion processes can

Polarity
Polarity has a significant influence on wetting. Good wetting is given if the

in turn, harms the strength of the 2k part.

Shrinkage
As a result of the injection moulding process, the liquid plastic melt is con-
verted into a solid, which results in a density change that reduces the overall volume.
There are three types: demoulding shrinkage, processing shrinkage and post-shrinkage.
Demoulding shrinkage refers to the deviations of the moulded parts that occur imme-
diately after the demoulding process. Processing shrinkage is defined as the shrinkage
phenomenon that occurs 16 hours after manufacture. All subsequent shrinkage phenom-
ena (i.e., relaxation of internal stresses, reorientation processes, and post-crystallization)
are called post-shrinkage and are not categorized into a specific time period (Figure 16).
Shrinkage can be affected by numerous processing parameters, particularly in the case
of semi-crystalline plastics. In plastic-plastic bonded parts, all shrinkage ratios can be
affected depending on the type of process control [8].

automatically by transferring the components manually from one cavity to the other. This
had the effect of increasing the production time of the over-moulding specimens and ad-
ditionally a risk of contamination of the interface of the two plastic components. For this
reason gloves were used during the manual transfer of the specimen from one cavity to the
other to eliminate this problem. In addition, the manufacturing of the first component
is not done in parallel with the over-moulding process, and this means that the tempera-
ture of the first shot during over-molding is the same as the ambient temperature, which
reduces the interface temperature and adhesion strength of the 2k components. Knowing
the consequences of producing the parts on a conventional injection moulding machine
instead of a 2k machine is still a significant improvement for Novo Nordisk who until to-
day had limited information about the bonding strength of 2k plastics during the design
phase and assumed their bonding strength from the material properties of the individually
manufactured materials.
More specifically, the manufacturing of the two component parts was carried out in three
stages.
• Drying of hygroscopic polymers (PC, ABS, PC/ABS)
• Manufacturing of first shot component
• Over-moulding of hexagonal ring

is 168°C. Furthermore, during the flow of the plastic melt in the mould’s cavity, the
molecules align themselves to a limited extent in the direction of flow and expand to
an extent proportional to the shear rate. In the cooling phase, partial recovery of this
expansion leads to a higher shrinkage value.
Most of the overall shrinkage is evident
almost immediately after ejection from the mold but shrinkage will continue for more
than 24 hours [55]. During this time, further crystallization and relaxation of internal
stresses result in minor dimensional changes. Figure 35b shows how the post-moulding
shrinkage of POM progresses over time at ambient temperature. Considering all these
phenomena, the adhesion strength during the first hours after moulding is expected to
yield large deviation.
The blue region of figure 33 depicts the time frame after the over-moulding process that
the specimen goes to a higher dimensional stability as the the phenomena referred to above
have essentially been completed [55], [16].

The adhesion strength values obtained from the specimens with a rough interface led this

As it has been described in section 5.2.1, an increase in the interface temperature can lead

surface roughness increasing the interface area and creating micro mechanical inter-locks
[53]. Furthermore, the increase of the interface temperature is likely to increase the bond-

temperatures the semi-crystalline plastics have more time to develop molecular entangle-
ments. In addition, the experimental study on interface temperature was conducted on
semi-crystalline materials in order to examine whether remelting of their crystalline region
can be succeeded leading to a higher adhesive strength.

Conclusion
With the completion of this project, a method was established to characterize the adhe-
sion strength of 2k over-moulded plastics. The chosen test method, designed specimen
and designed fixtures gave high fidelity results. The torsion test results were verified by
the structural simulation, where the rotation angle and torque required to break the bond
were quite similar to the actual test results.
The study of the adhesion strength as a function of time after over-moulding showed that
there is no risk of relaxation of the bond strength and moreover the bond seems to become
stronger and stronger with the passage of time, which is quite positive for Novo Nordisk’s
medical devices. However, long-term testing methods are needed to further improve the
characterisation of the bonding properties.
Furthermore, the roughness of the interface of the over–moulded plastics was proven to
influence the adhesion strength substantially. A rough interface can improve the adhesion
strength of 2k material combinations where the bond is less strong than the shear strength
of the weaker material. However, the interface temperature is of critical importance. The
cavity transfer process with the conventional injection moulding machine produces speci-
mens with lower adhesion strength when the interface is rough, as the plastic melt cannot
flow through the grooves of the rough surface which results to a smaller interface area and
many stress concentration points at the edges of the rough surface. By adding a a delay
time where the first shot is allowed to increase its temperature simulating the actual 2k in-
jection moulding process, the rough surfaces yield better results than the polished surfaces.
Last but not least, the additional characterisation of adhesion strength by CT-scanning
and transmitted light microscopy revealed many valuable results for deeper evaluation and
understanding of the bonding of 2k parts. With a computed tomography scan, voids and
air gaps at the interface of the two components can be identified and their volume and
dimensions can be measured. The resolution of the scan is critical, as features smaller than
the voxel size of the scan cannot be detected. The transmitted light microscopy yielded
important information for the structure of the crystals, the quality of the welding line and
the stresses applied during the torsion testing of the semi-crystalline materials studied.
Novo Nordisk’s R&D engineers have been equipped with new tools for designing 2k com-
ponents. The structural analysis of 2k components can now be supported by high quality
internal data for every combination of materials that can be derived, contributing to the
overall robustness of Novo Nordisk medical devices.

Future work
Long term testing
The creep behaviour of thermoplastics plays an important role in
engineering design and especially the failure and lifetime prediction of structural compo-
nents in medical devices of Novo Nordisk. The properties of the polymers are changing
along the time and the results of the report shows that the properties of the 2k bonds are
time-sensitive too. Thus, a creep test would give very important information and empower
the results for the adhesion strength of 2k parts
Design of fixtures for thermoplastic elastomers
The design of the fixtures in this
project yielded high-fidelity results, ensuring uniform stress distribution. However, the soft
thermoplastic elastomers cannot be gripped tightly with the existing fixture. Therefore,
a new fixture for the thermoplastic elastomers needs to be designed, which can grip them
tightly and lead to successful detachment during the torsion test.
Design of 2k parts
After this project’s study on the adhesion strength of two com-
ponent parts and the design of the test specimens, several factors that can affect the
adhesion strength in terms of component design were identified, such as the ratio of over-
all dimension and wall thickness, roughness, interface area, etc. According to the CT
results, the differential shrinkage resulting from differential cooling in 2k components acts
against bonding, but would it be the same if the component dimensions were in a different
ratio or a thinner wall was designed? Both this and other questions could be answered in
a study of 2k part design.
