ABSTRACT 
Within process manufacturing it is common practice to design the process first, test the main 
principles in the lab and then scale up production gradually, at each step verifying the 
performance of the production plant and the quality of the output. Occasionally scaleup projects 
fail for various reasons, a significant one being a lack of understanding of the process and the 
factors that influence output quality, leading to a variation of quality between plant scales. There 
exist guidelines from industry experts on handling these issues, but only a few research 
grounded methods address them directly. There are examples of experiences with robust design 
in this regard, but while many concrete tools have been developed for robustness quantification 
and visualisation in discrete manufacturing (e.g. the Variation Management Framework, VMF) 
the robust design methods related to process manufacturing are not focused on scaling, although 
scaling is an integral part of any process design. 
In this thesis, these issues are investigated and subsequently addressed by the development and 
introduction of the new Design for Robust Scaling Technique (DEFROST). The method 
provides an easy-to-use 7 step method that is generalized across industries and incorporates 
well-renowned robust design techniques such as axiomatic design and the VMF, adapting them 
to the context of robust scaling within process manufacturing. The method includes the 
introduction of a new framework for visualisation of robust scaling, the Robust Scaling 
Management Framework (RSMF), which is a derivative work of the VMF. 
The usefulness of the method is verified by means of a case study, ‘Robust Scaling of an 
Infrared Pyrolysis Reactor’, in which a novel pyrolysis reactor concept under development by 
DTU spinout MASH Biotech is taken from a lab-scale prototype to a pilot and demonstration 
reactor level. The case study is used to demonstrate the power of the new method which allows 
process and design engineers to design two scales of production plants simultaneously while 
keeping track of the impact of the scaling on output quality, thereby ensuring that the plants 
perform exactly alike and decreasing the risk of scale-up failure. The case study succeeds with 
demonstrating the main aspects of the method. Finally, a discussion of the method is presented 
along with suggestions for further research.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Motivation and Problem Description 
When designing a new product that is to be manufactured via process manufacturing (e.g. food 
and chemicals) it is common practice to develop a pilot plant prior to constructing a full-scale 
production plant. The pilot plant is a pre-commercial production system that employs the same 
processing technology but in smaller volumes, and it is typically used to verify working 
principles and learning about the technology [1]. The idea of using models to test assumptions 
about the large-scale design has been known for centuries with the purpose to “commit your 
blunders on a small-scale and make your profits on a large-scale,” to quote “The Father of the 
Plastics Industry” L. H. Baekeland [2]. However, even when using lab scale plants and pilot 
plants, companies often fail to reach expected production capacity when scaling up industrial 
processes [3], [4]. 
In a study by McNulty of 41 companies handling process manufacturing within the inorganic 
chemistry and mining industries, it was found that approximately half of the companies did not 
reach their expected production capacity within two years and hence went over budget [5]. 
Some of the key factors causing the companies to fall behind schedule were that the process 
technology was novel and the process chemistry was poorly understood, that insufficient pilot 
testing was done before scaling, or that the pilot testing was carried out in a way that did not 
yield representative results for the scaled design [6]. An example of one such company that has 
experienced problems with scale-up of process manufacturing is MASH Biotech, the company 
treated in the case study in this report (see section 4.1.2). After successfully building and 
running a pilot-scale batch reactor that produced bio-oil from sewage sludge, a commercial 
scale version with a larger reaction chamber was built. However, when running the reactor it 
became clear that the output was not similar to the output produced on the pilot scale. The bio-
oil quality was lower and the oil consisted mostly of water. This made the company identify

their need for a design method that would allow them to predict the effects of scaling on the 
final output of their reactors. 
As it is, we therefore believe that there is a need for a method to tackle variation in process 
performance resulting from scaling. In relation of this, we find the principles of robust design to 
be potentially useful. The variation management framework (VMF) is a recently introduced tool 
to increase robustness within discrete manufacturing systems [7] but it has no direct counterpart 
within process manufacturing. Considering the above-mentioned challenges, the VMF and 
robust design principles in general could potentially be used for managing variation induced by 
process manufacturing scale-ups. 
1.2 Research Goals and Objectives 
The goal of this project is to come up with a method to decrease risk and avoid failure when 
scaling process plants (see A1). This is done by investigating the domain of process 
manufacturing and the current situation to obtain an understanding of the extent and nature of 
the problem. Thereby the need for and desirable features of the new method should be clarified. 
It will be investigated which strategies and methods exist and are currently employed when 
scaling up process plants. Furthermore, scale-up methods and robust design theory used in 
discrete manufacturing should be investigated to work as inspiration. This is based on our 
assumption that some of the principles or methods used for managing discrete manufacturing 
variation and scaling might be usable for managing process manufacturing scaling as well. The 
overall goals of this project are therefore to answer the following research questions: 
RQ1. Why do process manufacturing projects fail in the scaling up process? 
RQ2. What design methods exist for scaling of process plants today? 
RQ3. Is it possible to minimize the risk of process scale-up failure by introducing a 
systematic method based on robust design principles? 
The scope of the project is limited to explore the research questions described above. Hence, the 
thesis will not go in depth with specific process technologies, chemical engineering, economic 
aspects etc. unless areas within these are found to be particularly relevant from the literature or 
case study.

that many of these terms are mental constructs [10]. DRM was introduced by L. Blessing and A.

1.4 Definition of Robust Design in Relation to Scaling 
The term ‘robustness’ can be defined in many ways. In this thesis we will use the definition 
presented in the DTU course 41632 Robust Design of Products and Mechanisms: 
Robust Design is the designing of products, devices & production equipment so 
that their performance is insensitive to different sources of variation, e.g. 
manufacturing variation, external noise factors, degradation, etc. 
The particular source of variation of interest in this study is scaling, both by itself and effects 
resulting from it.

Process manufacturing exploits physical, compositional changes or chemical reactions that 
change raw material or feedstock into a product of higher value. Under this branch lie

power generation, refinement of water and others [11]. Process manufacturing includes both 
continuous and batch production processes. In comparison to discrete manufacturing there are 
distinct differences as shown in Table 2.1.

The design process for processes and productions plants is an iterative process that comprises 
customer involvement, requirements specification, concept generation, detailed design and 
ongoing R&D and testing activities [13]. As such, the process is similar to many other 
engineering disciplines. Within chemical process engineering specifically the design work is 
frequently divided in two phases: 
1. Process design, which covers specification of the process, selection of unit operations, 
and creation of flowsheets. 
2. Plant design, which covers detailed mechanical, electrical and control engineering 
design of the production equipment. 
Traditionally, process plants are designed and scaled using a stage-gate approach consisting of 
bench (aka laboratory) scale systems, pilot-scale plants, demonstration plants, and finally 
commercial plants. The bench scale tests are typically done in batches and the pilot plant is the 
first continuous plant with which the feasibility of a continuous production process is tested 
(Figure 2.1). Demonstration plants are not always designed; sometimes the commercial plant 
will be developed directly after the pilot plant, especially for batch processes. However, for 
continuous processes a demonstration plant is normally built to test the feasibility of the 
production process over longer time periods and with equipment that more closely resembles 
that of the commercial or full-scale plant. [14]

2.2 Robust Design 
Robust design can be defined as the designing of products, mechanisms and systems so that 
their performance is insensitive to variation, which can include manufacturing variation, 
variation from ambient noise etc. Robust design can be considered as a sub-branch within 
quality and reliability engineering. [16]

2.2.1 Quality Loss and Transfer Functions 
At the heart of robust design lies the idea of quality loss which originates from the teachings of 
Dr. Genichi Taguchi [17]. In his understanding, the quality loss of a product increases with 
increased functional performance variation. This is in contrast to the existing production 
paradigm where parts are manufactured to perform within upper and lower specification limits. 
The difference between the two quality paradigms is illustrated in Figure 2.2. 
 
Figure 2.2: Left: The stepwise quality loss function. Right: The quadratic loss function. 
The main objective of Taguchi methods is therefore, by using transfer functions that describe 
the translation between functional requirements and design parameters, to optimize design 
parameters so that their variation will have the least possible influence on the performance of 
the system, hence increasing its robustness (Figure 2.3). 
 
Figure 2.3: Using the Taguchi Method, design parameters are optimized to limit the sensitivities of the 
functional performance on parameter variation 
2.2.2 Axiomatic Design 
Another important contribution to robust design is axiomatic design, first described by Nam P. 
Suh [18]. This theory is based on two proposed axioms: The independence axiom, which states 
that the design couplings should be minimized, and the information axiom, which states that the 
information content of the design should be minimized. In axiomatic design theory, product 
development contains of four domains: customer attributes (CA), functional requirements (FR), 
design parameters (DP) and process variables (PV); these domains have a hierarchical structure. 
==PAGE:20==

The goal of the designer is to make as few functional parameters as possible depend on the same 
design parameter, and to make as few design parameters as possible have an influence on the 
same functional parameter. 
2.2.3 The Variation Management Framework (VMF) 
The VMF was introduced as a means of visualizing how variation can be mapped from 
manufacturing to design, from design to functional performance, and from functional 
performance to customer satisfaction [7]. This shows the amount of quality loss perceived by 
the customer for a single product. The VMF incorporates the hierarchical product development 
structure of axiomatic design with the quality loss transfer functions from Taguchi, thus 
combining some of the most prominent robust design tools in a unified, graphical framework to 
support product development in discrete manufacturing (Figure 2.4). 
 
Figure 2.4: VMF modelling an example of a pen cap removal force. [7] 
2.3 Process Scaling and Robust Design 
In this section, different process scaleup methods are presented as well as an example of how 
robust design has been used in process manufacturing. 
2.3.1 Industrial Process Scale-Up Guidelines 
The book “Industrial Process Scale-up” [19] provides guidelines on how to scale up industrial 
processes from lab scale to commercial application, focusing on risk mitigation. The author, Jan 
Harmsen, identifies two main reasons for commercial failure: lack of technical understanding 
and organizational preparation. A lack of technical understanding is often a result of a high 
number of process parameters with many main and interaction effects on the outcome. The 
==PAGE:21==
Chapter 2: Literature Study 
Mathias Lind Neuenschwander & Martin Damgaard - June 2018 
 
 
9 
proposed guidelines focus on risk incl. identification, assessment, and reduction. The major risk 
factors are the number of new process steps (incl. new applications of existing technologies), 
lack of knowledge of streams (i.e. mass and heat transfer), and solids processing. The book 
describes three major scale-up strategies: 
• 
Brute force scale-up: The pilot plant is a scaled-down version of the commercial plant, 
and all critical process parameters (mixing velocity, volatile residence times etc.) are 
kept the same. This method is often applied when detailed kinetic models of the process 
are unavailable but scaling effects must be predictable. 
• 
Model based scale-up: The effects of scale-up of the individual processing unit on the 
critical process parameters are predicted by mathematical models. The model is 
validated using experimental results. 
• 
Empirical scale-up: Several (typically 3-4) processing plants of various sizes are built 
and the process parameters are measured and adjusted at each stage to achieve the 
desired output. The changes are plotted, and a trend can be observed. 
Of the three strategies described, the empirical scale-up is the least reliable. However, when 
detailed models are missing, the model-based strategy cannot be used, and the brute force 
strategy is suitable mainly for unit operations. Harmsen advises the designer to redesign the full-
scale plant if possible to make it suitable for a brute force scale-up. This strategy is usable for 
most process designs since detailed kinetic models cannot always be defined. 
2.3.2 Pharmaceutical Product Manufacturing Scaling Guidelines 
In a blog post published by Wellspring Pharma Services [20] they provide guidance on how to 
approach process scaling within the pharmaceutical industry [21]. The blog post focuses on 
batch production of chemical compounds and it presents some important considerations for the 
scaling process. 
Regarding feedstock supply, the article suggests investigating whether the supply chain can 
support the wanted change in scale. Ideally, raw materials for the different plant scales should 
come from the same supplier to rule out material differences. It is also important to identify 
critical process steps and parameters. Note that critical process steps found when creating the 
small-scale reactor might change when scaling; for instance, a larger blender will have a 
different rotational mixing speed than a smaller blender which can affect the output. Therefore, 
it is important to know all the equipment used in the plant to determine the factors and their 
correlations when scaling to a different size. These correlations enable the designers to identify 
target process parameters which are essential to control for the large plant to achieve the same 
output.

2.3.3 Dimensional Analysis 
Dimensionless numbers have been used for modelling and scale-up of chemical processes for 
several years [22]. This method, known as dimensional analysis, can be applied even when the 
equations that determine the process are not known. It basically includes expressing the process 
at hand by a set of dimensionless numbers and their relationships to each other. In essence, two 
processes at different scales will be the same if the dimensionless numbers have the same 
values; in this case, the processes are both geometrically, kinematically and dynamically similar 
[23]. The book “Scale-Up in Chemical Engineering” provides guidelines on how to approach a 
scale-up using this method, stating that “dimensional analysis [is] the only secure foundation for 
scale-up” [24]. 
2.3.4 Process Manufacturing Concept Robustness Estimation 
Although current robust design literature focuses on discrete manufacturing products and 
processes, one article proposes to use robust design methods for process manufacturing design 
[12]. This article proposes a new method for quantifying the robustness of process 
manufacturing concepts. The motivation for this study was that there exist many robust design 
methods and theories that can help achieve higher product robustness in discrete manufacturing, 
but few in process manufacturing. In discrete manufacturing components are manufactured first 
and assembled afterwards, allowing for part variation to be absorbed during assembly. In 
process manufacturing, the author proposes instead to calculate the expected functional 
performance variation induced by each process step, thereby giving an overall quantification of 
the robustness of the process manufacturing concept. This allows the process design engineer to 
optimize the process design, e.g. by enabling some stations to absorb variation, or to pick a 
more robust process concept. 
2.4 Scaling Methods in Discrete Manufacturing 
In this section, a few examples of scaling methods used in discrete manufacturing are presented. 
2.4.1 Design for Scalability Using Modular Components 
A design methodology named ‘design for scalability’ for industrial processes has been 
developed by Voorthuysen et al. [25]. The motivation behind it was to enable small and medium 
sized enterprises (SME) to scale according to the availability of commercial-off-the-shelf 
(COTS) components in order to cope with increasing manufacturing costs, enabling them to 
compete in a global market. The article lists robust design methods (axiomatic design and 
Taguchi methods) as a potential way to solve complexity issues with modular designs. The 
method proposed by the authors uses some domains from axiomatic design (FR and DP).

11 
2.4.2 Scaling Under Dynamic Uncertainty Using Laws of Growth 
Lotz and Kloberdanz have proposed a new way of handling size range development of physical 
products based on laws of growth. The method incorporates dynamic, size-dependent product 
behaviour which allows the designer to quantify scaling effects on time-dependent variation in 
product behaviour, thus getting a quantification of the performance uncertainty. Eventually, by 
adjusting certain factors (e.g. safety factors) the required dimensioning of the scaled product can 
be calculated. This allows companies to save significant amounts of time and money when up or 
downscaling. [26], [27] 
The method works by relating time-dependent variation (e.g corrosion, wear) directly to design 
parameters to find the impact of scaling on the time-dependent variation. Thus, relationships 
between variation factors and the dimensions of the design are established and it is thereby 
possible to predict how these factors change when the dimensions are increased or decreased. 
For example, a pushrod is constantly being exposed to movement, torque, wear or corrosion. As 
the corrosion rate of the pushrod is a constant factor dependent on the exposed area, the 
degradation rate is slower for a bigger rod than a smaller. 
2.5 Description of the Current Situation 
In this section, a description of the factors that can influence scale-up risk is presented followed 
by an account of the current landscape of scale-up methods. 
2.5.1 Factors that Influence the Chance of Scale-up Failure 
From the literature studied, several factors play a role in determining whether a process 
manufacturing scale-up will be successful. As discussed by Gertenbach and Cooper problems 
often arise when taking results from a lab scale process (batch) and using them to build the pilot 
plant (continuous) [6]. They argue that assumptions are sometimes made from the lab scale test 
that make the pilot and full-scale plant differ in fundamental ways, specifically with regards to 
material handling. We find this indicative that the importance of developing a pilot-scale plant 
that is representable for the full-scale plant is not always recognized. 
Harmsen found that one of the primary reasons for scale-up issues is an unsound technical base 
(see section 2.3.1). For instance, companies sometimes do not have an overview of the (often 
large number of) parameters that influence the process. Combined with the effects of scaling, 
this makes it hard to predict the output quality from the pilot and commercial scale plants. This 
problem typically arises when the company believes they are using a conventional process while 
it in fact includes novel elements. 
Finally, McNulty found that several of the failed scale-up projects had in common that they did 
not address product quality during process development [5]. Furthermore, pilot testing was 
==PAGE:24==
The Design for Robust Scaling Technique (DEFROST) 
12  
Mathias Lind Neuenschwander & Martin Damgaard - June 2018 
often not done to confirm relevant process parameters. Lastly, design criteria were sometimes 
made less conservative to save costs. McNulty’s findings support Harmsen’s in that the 
parameters that govern product quality are often not mapped and controlled. 
2.5.2 The Current Landscape of Methods 
In the current landscape of process manufacturing and plant design, there exist only a few 
general methods to ensure a robust scale-up. For the most part, the identified guidelines focus 
on specific industries like production of pharmaceuticals and biomass processing. This might be 
because some universities have neither the interest in nor the capacity to conduct research in 
process scaling (as argued in [24]). Several of the identified methods and guidelines address 
scale-up in somewhat similar ways, in that they focus on identifying critical process parameters 
and maintaining these. This is the case for Harmsen’s ‘brute force’ scale-up method (see section 
2.3.1), the guidelines from WellSpring Pharma Services (see section 2.3.2), and the dimensional 
analysis approach (see section 2.3.3). 
Regarding the use of robust design principles in industrial process scaling, a few studies focus 
on considering the availability of feedstock and COTS to ensure robustness and economic 
feasibility (see section 2.3.2 and section 2.4.1). Design parameters will be defined by COTS and 
are likely to influence the output, hence an iterative approach between designing the process and 
evaluating its sensitivity to scaling based on relationships between the process parameters and 
the design parameters is suggested. On the same token, Harmsen describes an incidence where 
he used the Taguchi method to improve the quality of the output from a scaled process plant 
(page 99-102 of [19]), and Boorla uses robust design principles to quantify the robustness of 
process plants (see section 2.3.4). 
2.6 Goal Setting for the New Method 
Based on current situation described in the previous section we can establish the following: 
• 
Process scale-ups often fail because of unexpected scaling influences on output quality, 
resulting from a lack of technical overview. 
• 
There is a limited amount of available design methods for robust scale-up in process 
manufacturing. 
• 
There are indications that robust design principles are useful for decreasing process 
scale-up risks. 
In light of the above points we believe that the current situation could be greatly improved if it 
were easier for plant designers to get an understanding of the impact their plant design has on 
product quality. This would enable them to scale up their plant in a way that accommodates 
increased throughput without negatively affecting product quality. Ideally, plant designers

should be able to design pilot-scale and demonstration-scale plants concurrently to make sure 
learnings from the pilot-scale apply to the large-scale reactor. In other words it should be more

C2. The method should enable process plant designers to gain knowledge from the small-

3 METHOD SYNTHESIS 
This chapter presents a new design method for robust scaling in the domain of process 
manufacturing. The method is named DEFROST. It was developed by an iterative process of 
working with the case study (see chapter 4) and finding inspiration in literature. The purpose of 
the method is to enable process and plant designers to design pilot and large-scale plants 
concurrently while ensuring a robust scalability and predictable outcomes on the large-scale.  
3.1 Key Concepts 
In this section, the key concepts of the method will be introduced including the scaling approach 
and frameworks used to support the robust scaling. 
3.1.1 Scaling Approach 
The method uses the domains from axiomatic design, although they are altered slightly. Inspired 
by the idea of ensuring robust product quality by maintaining the governing parameters of the 
process (see section 2.5.2) a domain is introduced that includes all parameters that have an 
influence on the quality of the output, but which are generalized so that they are irrespective of 
different scales of the process plant. The parameters in this domain are named process 
parameters (PP). The scaling approach taken is then inspired by Harmsen’s ‘brute force’ method 
(see section 2.3.1). The idea of being able to discuss scaling robustness by a tool similar to the 
VMF was found useful, and the VMF was therefore adapted to the context (see section 2.2.3). 
The method is furthermore based on the ideas of E. Tjalve in that we distinguish between the 
principle structure and the quantitative structure of the design [28]. 
The approach taken to ensure a robust scaling is that the small-scale plant and large-scale plant 
are designed concurrently with a focus on output quality robustness (see section 2.6). This way 
==PAGE:27==

we believe results from the small-scale plant will be representable for results from the large-
scale plant to the greatest extent possible. 
3.1.2 Introducing the Robust Scaling Management Framework 
The VMF has been adapted to a process manufacturing context and for scaling rather than 
product robustness. This adaptation is named the Robust Scaling Management Framework 
(RSMF). In Figure 3.1 the domain flowdown of the VMF and the RSMF are juxtaposed. 
 
Figure 3.1: Parameter flowdown of the VMF compared to the parameter flowdown of the RSMF. 
A description of the domains of the RSMF follows below: 
• 
Input Parameters (IP): These parameters are related to all inputs to the process. This 
includes parameters from the input material as well as information and power input. 
Some IP’s are the adjustable parameters of the process that can be changed to optimize 
the process system for different feedstocks or to achieve different output characteristics. 
Design Parameters (DP): The design parameters are defined by the design and cannot 
be changed after building the plant. They include part dimensions and placements, 
ratios between input parameters and design constraints, material properties etc. 
• 
Process Parameters (PP): The process parameters ensure that a consistent output is 
achieved with the different production scales. This is achieved by designing the process 
manufacturing plant in such a way that the process parameters remain the same for 
different scales. 
• 
Customer Attributes (CA): The commercial attributes are attributes of the product 
produced by the manufacturing plant. They are parameters of the reactor output (e.g. 
uniformity of taste for food products, viscosity of paints). They are defined by 
consideration of customer needs. 
Designing the process plant in a robustly scalable way means that the CA’s (parameters of the 
output) should be as little sensitive as possible to scaled versions of the design (variations in the 
DP’s), which is achieved by maintaining the values of the PP’s. Following the above definitions 
of the domains and their flowdown structure (Figure 3.1), the RSMF for the process 
manufacturing context can be defined as in Figure 3.2, where it is juxtaposed with the VMF 
from the discrete manufacturing domain. When using DEFROST the content of the RSMF is

3.1.3 Sensitivity Analysis and Robustness Quantification 
The content of the right section of the RSMF described above is a matrix of sensitivity indices 
between the process parameters, design parameters and input parameters. These indices 
represent quantifications of the scaling’s robustness. They are calculated by means of one or 
more sensitivity analyses of the following definition [29]: 
Sensitivity analysis is the study of how the variation in the output of a model 
(numerical or otherwise) can be apportioned, qualitatively or quantitatively, to 
different sources of variation, and of how the given model depends upon the 
information fed into it. [30], [31] 
As part of DEFROST, sensitivity analyses are conducted to identify how scaling the design 
parameters affects critical process parameters. Depending on the available information, several 
types of sensitivity analyses can be carried out. Three strategies commonly used in robustness 
quantification are:1 [32], [33] 
• 
Analytic: Well-known equations about the relationships between DP’s and PP’s are 
used to establish models and thereby transfer functions. The robustness indices can then 
be determined by taking the partial derivative of the transfer function. 
• 
Simulation: Simulation tools like FEM (Finite Element Methods) and CFD 
(Computational Fluid Dynamics) can be used to determine the system response (PP) to 
varying factors (DP). Robustness indices can then be determined, e.g. using a 
regression-based model or a nominal-range-based approach.

• 
Experiments: Physical experiments can be used in the same manner as with

Depending on the case, different strategies for mapping sensitivities can be used in combination 
or alone. Models can be established based on well-known equations if they exist. If a model 
cannot be established and the process cannot be simulated, experiments can be run on 
(adjustable) prototypes that represent critical parts of the design. 
3.1.4 Handling Other Sources of Variation 
Besides scaling, other sources of variation are likely during design and manufacture of process 
plants. Also, when the plant is scaled it is typically used in a different environment and by 
different people, which can cause variations to the plant performance either directly or through 
DP variation. These additional sources of variation are not treated directly by the new method 
since various robust design methods already exist that can be employed to handle these [34]. 
One way to get an overview of the system to visualize potential robustness issues is through a P-
Diagram [35]. 
3.2 Outline of the Method 
In this section, a walkthrough of DEFROST is given. The method is presented in a sequential 
manner, although we advise that it is followed in a less rigid way, since following design 
methods strictly sequentially in general leads to designs of lower quality compared to design 
processes with more iterations [36]. The starting point for using the method may vary between 
projects. In this section the method is described as it would be usable for new process plant 
designs where no existing process or plant is used. 
The method consists of seven steps that can be divided in three stages (Figure 3.3): 
1. The first stage (steps 1-3) focuses on process design and making the principle structure 
of the plant designs. 
2. The second stage (steps 4-6) focuses on scaling by making the quantitative structures of 
the plant designs. Robustness is ensured by the groundwork performed in the previous 
steps. 
3. Finally, the scaling robustness is quantified and evaluated (step 7).

3.2.2 Step 2: Defining the Process Parameters and Their Criticality 
The second step includes expanding the knowledge about the process and defining the 
governing process parameters (PP’s). The PP’s determine the values of the CA’s of the output 
and they must therefore be the same for the small and large-scale process and plant design. They 
are crucial to ensure a robust scaling of the production plant. Depending on the project, 
knowledge can be obtained internally or externally from the organisation, e.g. through literature 
studies and by asking experts within the field. The purpose of identifying the PP’s is twofold: 
• 
To find optimal values or goals for the PP’s to achieve a good output (measured by the 
CA’s). 
• 
To enable a robust scaling of the process plants. 
The influence of each PP should be investigated, and set-point values be set if applicable. To 
achieve the best process output possible, some PP’s can be of the larger-the-better kind (e.g. 
temperature homogeneity), some can be smaller-the-better (e.g. heat loss to surroundings) and 
some can be nominal-the-best (e.g. specific reaction temperatures). 
Although all process parameters influence the customer attributes, the influence of some are 
greater than of others. Therefore, they should be ranked according to their influence or 
importance, 𝐼𝑃𝑃. If possible, these ‘criticality scores’ should represent the customer attributes’

Depending on the choices made by the designer, each functional requirement can be fulfilled by

3.2.4 Step 4: Making the Small-Scale Design 
As the principal structure of the design is outlined in the previous step, it is now possible to 
create a quantitative structure of the small-scale design. Here the design team defines the 
components’ relative placement to each other, the numbers of components, and their dimensions 
and shape. The quantitative structure is designed based on the design goals previously specified. 
The choices made in this step determine the design parameters (to be identified in the next step), 
hence it is important to make some initial considerations regarding scaling. 
If the design team wants to use commercial-off-the-shelf-components (COTS), e.g. for a small 
of medium sized enterprise (SME), said components must be available for both scales of the 
plant. From a robustness perspective, while COTS of different sizes might fulfil the same 
function, they might affect the PP’s differently (i.e. some governing design parameters are 
determined by the COTS and vary between sizes) [25]. Therefore, special considerations must 
be made when using commercially available components from a sub supplier.

3.2.7 Step 7: Evaluating the Robustness of Scaling and Process Design 
In this step the robustness of the scaling between the small and large-scale plant is validated. In 
accordance with the definition of robustness used in this study, the robustness of the scaling is 
determined by the extent to which the scaling has an impact on the output quality, which is 
governed by the process parameters and indirectly by the design parameters. The robustness of 
the scaling is quantified by means of a sensitivity analysis as described in section 3.1.3. The 
output from this is the RSMF which can then be used as a visual tool for discussion about the 
designs. 
Three types of parameters should be included in the RSMF: 
1. The most important design parameters are the ones that should be maintained between 
scales (the identified DP’s) but are not. The higher their 𝐼𝐷𝑃, the more important it is to 
investigate them. High sensitivity values for them should prompt a discussion about 
redesigning the plants. 
2. Furthermore, design parameters that have not been identified as relevant for the process 
parameters and that are thus scaled should be investigated to validate the robustness. 
These design parameters might not have been identified previously. 
3. Finally, design parameters that are frozen can be investigated as well to validate the 
assumptions made in the weighted coupling matrix (step 5). 
When conducting the sensitivity analysis, the design team might find that the process 
parameters have different values for the two designs due to unexpected design parameters or 
interaction effects having an influence. In this case they should revisit some of the earlier steps 
and update the designs. 
Along with evaluating the robustness of the scaling, we advise that the design team takes this 
step as an opportunity to evaluate the overall process design, especially if it contains novel 
operations (or well-known operations in a new context).

3.3.1 Limitations 
The method has some limitations. First, one can imagine that it might be possible to improve the 
quality of the output upon scaling in some cases which might be desirable, but the method 
focuses on robustness of scaling (i.e. making the large-scale and small-scale plant perform 
exactly alike), not the performance of the large-scale design. Regarding this, during step 7 one 
might find that the design could be improved. In this case the design team would need to redo 
several steps, potentially having to build more prototypes. Related to this is also the weak point 
that to get a better robustness quantification, a better model or several prototypes are needed, 
which cost money and can be time consuming. 
Regarding the viability of the approach to lock certain parameters, the method does not take 
interaction effects into account, although they can be investigated in step 7. Also, if all DP’s 
influencing the PP’s are locked, it is not always possible to maintain the same principle 
structure while scaling and therefore the RSMF cannot be specified (with the introduction of 
different DP’s for the two scales). 
Finally, the usefulness of the method is limited by its scope. The method only focuses on the 
robustness of the output quality, while many other factors can cause scaleup failure, including 
(but not limited to): 
• 
(Lack of) previous experiences with equipment of the same size [5] 
• 
Maturity of the technology [5] 
• 
Mechanical complexity of the unit operations [19] 
• 
Whether solids or liquids are processed [6], [19]  
• 
Lack of organizational preparation [3], [4]  
• 
Economic feasibility in relation to manufacturability [25] 
As argued in section 2.6 we believe the robustness aspect to be one of the more important issues 
to address during scaleup. It is possible that focusing on robustness can potentially prevent 
failure resulting from some of the other factors mentioned. In any case, it might be relevant to 
use other guidelines or DfX methods during scaleup to address issues that are specific to the 
project and not covered by DEFROST [10]. 
3.3.2 Benefits 
The method uses terminology that is understandable for engineers of many fields, making the 
RSMF and the domains in question serve well as a basis for communication between 
departments. Combining this with the fact that many steps can be conducted in different ways 
makes the method suitable for many industries and projects within process manufacturing, 
regardless of whether models can be established. This indicates that the method satisfies the first 
success criteria C1, although this needs further investigation. 
==PAGE:36==
The Design for Robust Scaling Technique (DEFROST) 
24  
Mathias Lind Neuenschwander & Martin Damgaard - June 2018 
The method provides a framework for handling the robustness aspect of process manufacturing 
scale-ups which we believe will lower the chances of failure. An important feature contributing 
to this is the structured approach throughout which the output quality is in focus. An overview 
of the quality in relation to the process and plant scales is maintained throughout the project and 
the design team knows when new information must be obtained. Thereby a robust scaling is 
ensured, which makes the small-scale reactor representable of the large-scale reactor. This 
satisfies C2, and by using the RSMF to quantify the robustness, C3 should be satisfied as well. 
As a result, no significant amount of money is spent on development activities before the 
designs have been justified.

4.1.1 Bio-Oil Production and Pyrolysis 
Pyrolysis (originally from the Greek words ‘pyro’ meaning fire and ‘lysis’ meaning separating 
or the breaking down of parts) is a process in which materials are thermally decomposed in the 
absence of oxygen [37]. By heating heavier molecules in an anaerobic (low oxygen) 
environment, they are essentially disintegrated into lighter molecules, some of which can be 
condensed into bio-oil. Pyrolysis is not new and has historically been used for production of 
==PAGE:38==
The Design for Robust Scaling Technique (DEFROST) 
26  
Mathias Lind Neuenschwander & Martin Damgaard - June 2018 
charcoal, which is the solid remains of a pyrolysis process. The technology has attracted 
increasing interest in recent years due to its perceived potential for production of bio-fuels 
which are expected to play a more essential part of the sustainable energy paradigm in the future 
[38]–[40], and many reactor principles have been designed (see A2). Industrial concerns include 
predictability and energy balance (see A3).  
Pyrolysis yields three outputs: bio-oil, bio-char and so-called syngas (short for synthetic gas). 
The relative fraction and properties of each output vary based on the reactor design, pyrolysis 
operating parameters, and the nature and preparation of the feedstock: for instance, high heating 
rates lead to higher bio-oil yields, while lower heating rates lead to higher bio-char yields [41]. 
4.1.1.1 Bio-Oil 
Bio-oil (aka pyrolysis oil or biocrude) is a dark, tarry substance that consists of a variety of 
compounds which result from depolymerization of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin. These 
compounds are mostly organic and consist mainly of carbon, hydrogen and oxygen, but bio-oil 
may also contain inorganic compounds from the biomass (known to cause stability and fouling 
problems). Bio-oil contains water and is non-polar, and bio-oils and fossil fuels are thus 
immiscible. The water content of bio-oil contributes to a high oxygen content, accounting for a 
lower calorific value: bio-oil typically has a calorific value of 30-50% of that of hydrocarbon 
fuels [42], [43]. Furthermore, bio-oil is highly corrosive and by nature an unstable substance. 
Significant effort has been spent on developing ways to refine bio-oil through upgrading [44]. 
4.1.1.2 Bio-Char 
Bio-char (also known as charcoal) consists mostly of the black lumps of carbon left after 
volatiles have been removed during pyrolysis [45]. It also contains hydrogen and a number of 
inorganic species, and depending on the pyrolysis process, the feedstock properties etc., bio-
char can be used for gasification, it can be combusted for heat and power generation, and it can 
be used for soil amendment among other applications [46]. 
4.1.1.3 Syngas 
Syngas consists mainly of hydrogen and carbon monoxide, but it also contains small amounts of 
carbon dioxide and other compounds and molecules, some of which are not combustible [47]. It 
is typically produced by gasification and combusted to produce electricity. Syngas is produced 
at high pyrolysis temperatures above 650ºC where the condensable volatiles that have 
evaporated from the biomass are further thermally decomposed along with the bio-char, 
decreasing the bio-oil yield.

4.1.2 About MASH Biotech 
MASH Biotech is a Denmark based company that specialises in creating value from waste 
products by gasification and bio-oil production. The company was founded as a spin-out from 
the course 41633 HardTech Entrepreneurship at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU)

4.1.3 MASH Biotech’s Scale-Up Experiences 
As mentioned above, MASH Biotech’s initial vision was to commercialise a DTU patent for a 
new pyrolysis reactor concept first described by N. Trinh et al. [49], [50]. However, after testing

4.1.5 Justification of the Case Study 
DEFROST was developed for process manufacturing. Even though the industries that employ 
process manufacturing are different, they face many of the same challenges and use a common 
language to describe terms such as ‘process unit’, ‘pilot-scale’ and ‘feedstock’. We believe 
justifying the validity of DEFROST is possible due to this defined boundary of the method. 
If the method can be proven useful for complex processes, it is likely useful for more well-
defined processes as well. Bio-oil production by pyrolysis is a complex field, and many meta-
studies conclude that pyrolysis is difficult to control [47], [53]. As a result, it is prone to scale-
up failure [6]. In our opinion it is therefore an ideal field to work with that enables us to 
generalize results from the case study on the usefulness of the method. MASH Biotech’s 
infrared reactor is a novel piece of technology, which further supports the appropriateness of 
this particular case study.

4.3.1 The Impact of Secondary Effects on Oil Quality 
It is currently considered challenging to fully describe the processes that happen during 
pyrolysis and predict their outcome on the oil quality. In a literature study on this topic by 
Neves et al. [53], it is stated as follows: 
The description of the pyrolysis process is particularly challenging because it 
evolves a great deal of physical and chemical transformations and produces a 
large number of product species. As a result, existing models aiming to predict the 
rates or yields of the released pyrolytic volatiles are still supported by empirical 
data.

4.3.1.1 Secondary Pyrolysis 1 (SP1) 
As the volatiles travel inside the feedstock particles through their capillaries, they can interact 
with the feedstock and generate char and heat. This is undesirable because it decreases the oil 
yield and the homogeneity of the feedstock temperature, which can lead to other negative 
effects like combustion. 
==PAGE:44==
The Design for Robust Scaling Technique (DEFROST) 
32  
Mathias Lind Neuenschwander & Martin Damgaard - June 2018 
4.3.1.2 Secondary Pyrolysis 2 (SP2) 
After the volatiles are released from the solids, they are present in a turbulent state within the 
reactor until vented out of the chamber. In this state they can react with both aerosol particles4 
and the solid grains (feedstock and char). During this interaction the volatiles can be 
decomposed into non-combustible gases and char, which decreases the oil yield. 
4.3.1.3 Dehydration Reaction 1 (D1) 
The volatiles released at high temperatures undergo dehydration reactions that make them split 
into new fragments of non-condensable or non-combustible gases, which is not desirable. 
4.3.1.4 Dehydration Reaction 2 (D2) 
When the volatiles have moved outside the reaction chamber and begin to cool off in the 
condensing unit, liquid is formed. The newly formed liquid can react with the oxygenated gases 
(formed from the previous secondary effects and dehydration) and produce water, polar acids, 
and different types of gases. This has a negative impact on the calorific value of the bio-oil. 
4.3.1.5 Gasification (G) 
When the feedstock is heated above a certain level, typically around 650°C, its constituents are 
broken down into lighter molecules that are released as non-condensable gases, decreasing the 
bio-oil yield. 
4.3.1.6 Char Overheating (CO) 
Char overheating is more likely to occur during slow pyrolysis. It happens when the feedstock 
has already released the desired vapours at lower temperatures and then releases undesirable 
vapours (e.g. SOx, CO, H2S) at higher temperatures. These can interact with the desired 
volatiles in the chamber and cause SP2. 
4.3.1.7 Combustion (C) 
Combustion effects can occur when the feedstock starts to release volatiles at high temperatures. 
This leaves the hot charcoal, which can interact with the remaining feedstock that has a lower 
temperature and contains a more significant amount of oxygen which in combustion of the 
feedstock. Losing volatiles in this reaction is undesirable. Combustion can furthermore be a 
catalyst for gasification, as it promotes an uneven temperature distribution in the feedstock.

4.3.2 Mapping the Pyrolysis Process 
Considering the goal of optimizing pyrolysis for production of bio-oil, it is clear that the yield 
and quality of the oil output are negatively influenced by secondary effects that occur after and 
simultaneously with the primary pyrolysis. These effects must therefore be limited to ensure the 
best possible output. An overview of the effects and their parameters of influence is presented in 
A12 (based on company data from MASH Biotech). Furthermore, a cause-effect chain has been 
used as a visual tool to map the relations between primary and secondary pyrolysis processes 
and effects to relevant parameters throughout the process (see A13). 
To obtain a high calorific value of the bio-oil (CA1) and a high bio-oil yield (CA2), it is 
important for the reactor to support a fast and homogeneous heating of the feedstock and 
support a flow which moves the volatiles out of the reaction chamber as fast as possible without 
dragging too many aerosols with them in the process. After the volatiles have been moved out 
of the heated area the condensing should happen as fast as possible.

4.4.3 Inspiration from Researchers 
As described in section 4.3.4, pyrolysis researchers were involved in determining the criticality 
of the process parameters. As mentioned, a few of the correspondences yielded more interesting 
results that expanded our knowledge and inspired us to consider specific improvements to the 
MASH Biotech’s infrared pyrolysis reactor prototype. The learnings from these meetings are 
described in A19. The primary take-aways were as follows: 
• 
The infrared reactor design by G. Xu was quite similar to MASH Biotech’s prototype, 
but the reactor has been improved since their article was published. The carrier gas now 
has a sweeping flow over the feedstock tray which they claim suppresses secondary 
reactions. 
• 
P. Jensen found the idea of placing infrared heaters directly above the feedstock and 
quenching the volatiles right after they evaporate quite promising. Still, he had concerns 
about the scalability due to potential difficulties with collecting all the bio-oil. Also, he 
suggested a partial condensing system to separate water and improve the calorific value 
(CA1) although this would decrease the yield (CA2). 
• 
M. Dicko’s reactor also uses infrared heating, but the design is different. Due to the low 
penetration depth of infrared heating in wood, around 0.3 mm [63], the heating was 
very inhomogeneous. This resulted in a high water concentration in the bio-oil. This 
suggests that it might be difficult to achieve the necessary heating homogeneity in an 
infrared pyrolysis reactor. 
4.4.4 Analysis and Evaluation of the Infrared Pyrolysis Reactor Prototype 
After improving our knowledge on pyrolysis reactors by involving experts in the field, we 
returned to the prototype (described in section 4.1.4) to determine how well suited its working

done by consideration of the learnings described in the previous section, our understanding of 
the process parameters (section 4.3.3) and their relationship with the pyrolysis process (see 
A12), as well as practical challenges expected when turning the prototype into a continuous 
reactor. Several improvement areas were identified (see A20); these were handled in subsequent 
steps. 
One big drawback of the prototype is that it does not represent all the functional requirements 
for the pilot plant which is known to cause scale-up problems [15]. However, considering its 
similarities with the reactor design by G. Xu and P. Jensen’s positive response to this, it was 
decided that the basic principle is usable for the reactor. The design of the pilot and 
demonstration-scale reactors was therefore chosen to be based on the prototype, and the initial

Three types of scaling are used: multiplication, scaling of (one or more) dimensions and

4.8 Step 7: Evaluating the Robustness of Scaling and Process 
Design 
In this step, the robustness of the scaling was quantified by means of experimentation and 
simulation. Furthermore, the overall concept was evaluated based on the experiments. In section 
4.3.1 it was established that existing pyrolysis models are still supported by empirical data due 
to the highly complex nature of the pyrolysis process [53]. This is further supported by Sharma 
et al., who in a review of the current state of pyrolysis modelling and process characterization 
write: 
There are a number of uncertainties in current biomass pyrolysis models, 
especially in their ability to optimize process conditions to achieve desired product 
yields and distribution. [68] 
As a result, and due to our limited background in chemical process engineering, it was not an 
option to establish an accurate model of the system. We therefore decided to collect the 
necessary data for the sensitivity analysis from experiments. This was possible with the reactor 
prototype which we modified to be able to adjust the most critical design parameters (see A25). 
The experiments were planned using Design of Experiments (DOE) tools and by use of JMP, a 
statistical analysis program [69]. 
Considering the three types of design parameters to be included in the RSMF (described in 
section 3.2.7), none of the critical design parameters (Table 4.7) have different values in the 
large-scale reactor compared to the small-scale reactor. However, we decided to investigate

4.8.2.4 Learnings from the Experimentation 
Looking back at the experiments we did not acquire the information we set out to. 
Unfortunately, the reactor broke down before we even got to start conducting the screening 
experiment (see section 4.8.1). Needless to say, we did not get enough information to be able to 
use it for the sensitivity analysis and thereby the RSMF which is a critical part of the DEFROST 
method. From the perspective of using the method the experimentation was therefore a failure. 
Despite this, a lot of valuable learnings were gained from the experimentation. Most importantly 
we found that the layer thickness (DP7) and particle size and shape have a significant impact on 
the homogeneity of the heating. This puts into question the viability of heating from one side 
without some stirring of the particles if a decent amount of biomass needs to be processed. We 
also found that the gas flow (DP3) had a significant impact on the heating rate of the biomass 
(PP6); it took several seconds longer for the biomass to reach the desired temperature with a 
volume flow of 30 l/min than 25 l/min. The same was true for the distance between the 
feedstock and the heaters (DP5). This validates our results from the coupling matrix (Table 4.8) 
that these three design parameters (DP3, DP5 and DP7) are critical to maintain from the small to 
the large-scale reactor.

4.8.5 Other Sources of Variation upon Scaling 
The direct influences of scaling were mapped out in the RSMF, but the influence from noise 
factors might affect the performance of the reactor as well when it is used in a new environment. 
As stated in section 4.2.1, MASH Biotech wants to implement the large-scale reactor in Kenya 
where the environment is warmer and the amount of annual precipitation is higher than in 
Denmark [71], [72]. The ambient temperature might influence the process parameters related to 
pyrolysis temperature and condensing (PP3, PP6, PP7 and PP8) and the change in humidity 
might have an impact on corrosion rates of plant elements [73], [74]. The output might not be 
entirely the same for the two reactors which is worth keeping in mind, although having a higher 
heating rate is desirable for the ‘good pyrolysis process’ (see Table 4.3). 
4.9 Conclusions on the Case Study 
In this case project, DEFROST was tested on a novel infrared pyrolysis reactor concept. It was 
found that pyrolysis is a complex process with many influencing parameters and thus it is 
difficult to control, making it suitable for testing the method (see section 4.1.5). Most steps of 
the method were carried out as intended, although the sensitivity analysis could not be 
completed and the robustness of the scaling could not be quantified in the RSMF. However, 
even though the DP-PP sensitivities could not be quantified, some of the most important 
relationships were verified from the experimentation. 
The infrared heating had the potential to provide high heating rates, but whether or not it is 
feasible to use the principle for bio-oil production remains an open question. From our 
experiences, the feedstock parameters would need to be carefully controlled and the reactor 
design needs significant fine-tuning. Regarding the feasibility of making a continuous reactor

5 CONCLUSIONS AND CLOSING 
REMARKS 
In this thesis we wanted to investigate what determines the success of scaleup of process 
manufacturing plants and to what extent robust design methods have been used in this context. 
The underlying assumption, which was verified in chapters 1 and 2, was that even though robust 
design has potential to remedy some of the problems that occur when scaling, a structured 
framework for using it in this context is lacking. It was found that designing process plants that 
are robust to scaling is an important aspect of scaleup success, and based on this a robust scaling 
method was developed. 
In this chapter, a discussion on the project approach and methodology is given along with a 
reflection of the process we have had through the project. This is followed by a description of 
the core contributions of this thesis and their potential academic and industrial impact. Lastly, a 
selection of suggestions for further research is presented. 
5.1 Discussion and Reflection 
In this section a discussion and reflection on the project and findings is given. 
5.1.1 Research Clarification and Literature Study 
The goal of this project was formulated in three research questions: 
RQ1. Why do process manufacturing projects fail in the scaling up process? 
RQ2. What design methods exist for scaling of process plants today? 
RQ3. Is it possible to minimize the risk of process scale-up failure by introducing a 
systematic method based on robust design principles? 
==PAGE:67==
Chapter 5: Conclusions and Closing Remarks 
Mathias Lind Neuenschwander & Martin Damgaard - June 2018 
 
 
55 
In order to answer RQ1 and RQ2, a literature study was conducted (chapter 2). Here it was 
found that several of the reasons behind process scaleup failure were related to the quality of the 
output produced, in the sense that scaleups often fail because process engineers are either (1) 
unaware of the impact scaleup will have on their output quality or (2) unable to achieve output 
quality on a large-scale within the same margins as on a pilot scale. Of the methods identified, 
only the dimensional analysis approach is grounded in academia and can be used across 
industries, while the rest were developed for specific industries based on insight and 
experiences (e.g. Harmsen’s and Wellspring Pharma Services’ guidelines). It was also found 
that robust design methodology focuses exactly on addressing these kinds of problems (inability 
to maintain or unawareness of effects on desired quality due to variation), although some of the 
methods focus specifically on discrete manufacturing. It seems like there is awareness of 
Taguchi methods within process manufacturing, although few methods using these principles 
have been developed for scaling purposes. 
All in all, these findings sufficiently answer RQ1 and RQ2, and to a small extent, RQ3. We 
could thereby affirm our initial assumption that there was a gap between the need for method to 
tackle scaling robustness and the current landscape of robust design methods. 
5.1.2 Development and Validation of the New Method 
With these initial findings in mind, a new method for managing quality variation deriving from 
scaling was developed: DEFROST. It was expected that fulfilling three criteria would help 
solve the identified issues and reduce the chance of scaleup failure: 
C1. The method should be defined on such a level of abstraction that it can be specified and 
adapted to a specific industry within process manufacturing. 
C2. The method should enable process plant designers to gain knowledge from the small-
scale plant that is directly applicable to the large-scale plant. 
C3. The method should enable process plant designers to quantify the impacts of scaling on 
the output quality. 
In order to achieve these criteria, it was decided to adapt the domains of axiomatic design and 
the VMF to a process manufacturing context in order to bridge the gap between process 
engineering and robust design. The method should focus on quality by identifying critical 
parameters and freezing them, an approach which is known from other process scaleup methods 
[19], [21], [22]. 
Validating research in engineering design is not trivial as no benchmarked methods and 
common terminology currently exist [75]. We decided to test the usefulness of DEFROST and 
the RSMF by means of a case study, an approach commonly taken within (especially design) 
research. The case study consisted of a design project, ‘Robust Scaling of an Infrared Pyrolysis 
==PAGE:68==
The Design for Robust Scaling Technique (DEFROST) 
56  
Mathias Lind Neuenschwander & Martin Damgaard - June 2018 
Reactor’, where DEFROST was used for designing two reactors: a pilot-scale (test) reactor and 
a full (demonstration) scale reactor. Because the method was developed simultaneously with the 
case study, the effects of using it vs not using it were immediately noticeable for us. Without the 
method, the idea of scaling the reactor was quite intangible, but getting a structured approach to 
follow made the areas we needed to investigate and concentrate on more evident for us. In 
particular, the method was useful for the following reasons: 
1. The terminology used throughout DEFROST was general enough to allow us to 
describe a highly complex field (pyrolysis) and formulate knowledge about facilitation 
of the ‘good pyrolysis process’, even with our limited knowledge within chemical 
engineering. 
2. Going through the steps, we became increasingly aware of the relationships and 
differences between the small-scale and large-scale design and how they would perform 
differently, allowing us to continuously improve both designs to optimize the scaling 
robustness. This is evident from the several design iterations that occured around step 4-
6 (see section 4.5-4.7). As seen, the designs would have looked significantly different 
without using DEFROST. This would have caused scaleup issues and require additional 
prototype iterations. 
3. Going through step 7 of the case study some of the previous findings about the 
significance of the design parameters were verified. This indicates that the approach 
proposed in step 3-5 where process parameters are identified and related to design 
parameters works as intended and increases the scaling robustness. 
4. The parameter flowdown structure (Figure 3.1) provided an intuitive overview of 
propagation of scaling effects on output quality, similar to what has been the case for 
the VMF [33]. 
The first criterion is sufficiently achieved by point (1) above. We also believe the method 
satisfies the second criterion, since it ensures a robust scaling as described in (2) and (3). Lastly, 
the third criterion is answered by help of the RSMF as described in (4). 
By answering the success criteria for the method, we believe that RQ3 has been sufficiently 
answered. The method has some limitations, some of which were covered in section 3.3.1. We 
became aware of other limitations and areas of improvement during the case study; these are 
mentioned with suggestions for further research in section 5.4. 
5.1.3 Generalization of Results 
A common objection against the use of case studies is that they are biased due to the 
subjectivity of the researcher [75]. Our background as design and innovation engineers has 
likely played a significant role in our approach to the research and the case study; process and 
plant engineers, who are the intended end users of DEFROST, would perhaps have taken a 
==PAGE:69==
Chapter 5: Conclusions and Closing Remarks 
Mathias Lind Neuenschwander & Martin Damgaard - June 2018 
 
 
57 
different approach. Even so, we believe it is possible to infer general learnings from case studies 
in general, as argued in [8] and [75]. 
Regarding the choice of case study, it is likely that a different case would have prompted other 
considerations that could potentially have altered our way of thinking about process scaling, 
causing the method to end up different. This brings into question the validity of DEFROST for 
other types of projects compare to bio-oil production. In this regard, the language and terms 
used in the method come from axiomatic design and are well-known among practitioners of 
reliability and quality engineering who would likely be in charge of ensuring product robustness 
during a scaleup. The method works on a general level of abstraction, so even though the 
characteristics of the process parameters might differ between industries, we believe this 
domain is broad enough to be useful for many. Hence, we are confident that DEFROST will be 
applicable to other industries and technologies. 
5.2 Reflections on the Project Process 
The research methodology used in this project was DRM and the idea was to spend significant 
time on the case study and conduct is concurrently with the literature studies. This was mainly 
because of practical considerations: As seen by comparison of the original and updated project 
plans (see A1 and A10) the original plan was to design a test reactor based on the prototype (it 
only briefly mentions the scaling aspect), while the revised plan was to focus on robust scaling 
in general and transform the design project of the new test reactor to a case study. 
Even though we believe we have taken a sound approach to the project, it would have been 
beneficial to clarify the new (research-oriented) scope significantly faster. Substantial amounts 
of time were spent in the first half of the project on pyrolysis research and reactor 
conceptualization that was not as necessary after the change of scope. It would also have been 
beneficial to clarify what was possible to achieve within the time limits of the project sooner, 
especially on the practical side regarding the prototype. As described in section 4.8, the scaling 
robustness quantification (step 7 of DEFROST) could not be completed due to technical issues 
and time constraints. Lastly, it would have been beneficial to involve process engineers and 
researchers in the development of DEFROST, especially for an initial evaluation of the method. 
The external researchers that have been involved work with mechanical and chemical 
engineering, and while they have played an important role for us to understand pyrolysis and 
design a good reactor as well as for coming up with ideas for the sensitivity analysis, we believe 
input from process engineers would have allowed us to create a more usable method and 
formulate criteria for the good method sooner. 
5.3 Conclusion and Core Contributions 
Summing up, the core contributions of this thesis are as follows:

• 
An investigation of the reasons behind process scaleup failure and the current methods 
used to address these issues, incl. existing experiences with robust design methods in 
process manufacturing. 
• 
A demonstration of the usefulness of robust design methods in process manufacturing 
scaleup. 
• 
A development and case study verification of a design method that facilitates robust 
scale-up of process manufacturing (DEFROST). 
• 
A reframing of the axes of the VMF and an adaptation of this tool and the axiomatic 
design domain to the context of process scaling through the RSMF. 
The primary academic impact of these contributions is to prompt further research in how robust 
design methods can be used in process manufacturing and for scaling. On an industrial level, 
DEFROST and the RSMF provide an easy-to-use method and a visual framework for 
systematic, concurrent design of process plants on various scales, allowing companies to ensure 
robustness of scaling and continuously evaluate the robustness of the scaling to support concept 
selection. The language used in the method is easy to understand for engineers and non-
engineers alike, making it suitable for internal communication among stakeholders, engineers 
and managers. 
5.4 Limitations and Suggestions for Further Research 
This project was carried out over a limited time period and there are therefore areas which 
should be further investigated. An overview of suggestions for further research is given in this 
section. 
As the method DEFROST was developed through this project, one of the criteria (C1) defined 
in DS1 stated that the method should be adoptable within various industries of process 
manufacturing. Therefore, having different companies apply the method would provide essential 
data for refining it; hence, more empirical studies should be conducted to validate the usefulness 
of the method. Also having companies actively working with the method would provide more 
knowledge about whether the method is intuitive to use or not. Furthermore, the usefulness of 
the method should be investigated by researchers within process engineering in order to 
compare it to existing methods, identify deficiencies and make it more usable within various 
industries. It would be interesting to see if DEFROST could be used for scale-up of products 
within discrete manufacturing. 
Regarding the RSMF, defining the parameters was a continuous process and we have had many 
discussions about how to define the parameters and their flowdown structure. During the case 
study it was found that some critical design parameters were relations between design 
dimensions and input parameters, blurring the line between the DP’s (fixed control factors) and 
==PAGE:71==
Chapter 5: Conclusions and Closing Remarks 
Mathias Lind Neuenschwander & Martin Damgaard - June 2018 
 
 
59 
the IP’s (adjustable control factors). Related to this is the fact that interaction effects are not 
considered in the proposed method; we would suggest further research to investigate whether 
this can be justified and if needed redefine the method to take these into account. 
The process parameter domain proposed in this thesis is arguably similar to the functional 
requirement domain commonly used in robust design. The translations between FR’s and DP’s 
have been investigated by Göhler et al. [67] who found that it is often beneficial to define a ‘sub 
functional requirement’ domain instead of linking the functional and design parameters directly. 
This could be useful in the RSMF as well, to make clear distinctions between parameter 
domains and perhaps allow for conversion between domains (e.g. from DP to IP) through 
design choices in a systematic way. 
Related to this, the concept of Key Characteristics (introduced and by Thornton et al. [76]) 
could be useful in process manufacturing and the RSMF, although this will likely depend on the 
type of process (as demonstrated in the case study, pyrolysis is an example of a process where 
not just a few critical parameters influence the quality of the output greatly). It might be relevant 
to investigate how well the parameter flowdown presented in this thesis is suited for actual use, 
by researching existing terminology and experiences from process manufacturing.

Purpose of the project 
The objective of this MSc project is to design a method for robust scaling of process 
manufacturing equipment to avoid failure when scaling process plants. The method needs to be 
applied to a case provided by the DTU spin-out company MASH Biotech where an infrared 
pyrolysis reactor will be used.

process manufacturing and scale-up. Thereby an understanding of the extent and nature of the 
problem is obtained and the need for and desirable features of the new method should be 
clarified. It will be investigated which strategies and methods exist and are currently employed 
when scaling up process plants. Furthermore, scale-up methods and robust design theory used 
in discrete manufacturing should be investigated to work as inspiration.

variation of interest is scaling. It should be investigated whether the VMF is suitable for

(1) Fixed Bed Reactor 
 
The fixed bed reactor is one of the most commonly used reactor designs for lab testing, however it 
is rarely used for commercial processing of biomass due to its inefficiency. This design is also 
known as the frying pan principle. It is typically used to process one batch at a time.

heat is transferred from the heater to the container and afterwards to the feedstock through 
conduction, hence the feedstock is not heated simultaneously as conduction between feedstock 
particles plays an important role.

A3 Industrial Concerns About Pyrolysis Scaling 
One of the main industrial concerns about scaling pyrolysis reactors is that the rig will not be 
efficient enough. The efficiency is based on the following equation: 
𝜂𝑒𝑛𝑒𝑟𝑔𝑦=
𝐸𝑏𝑖𝑜𝑜𝑖𝑙
𝐸𝑝𝑟𝑒𝑝𝑎𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 𝑜𝑓 𝑓𝑒𝑒𝑑𝑠𝑡𝑜𝑐𝑘 + 𝐸𝑝𝑦𝑟𝑜𝑙𝑦𝑠𝑖𝑠
 
Where 𝜂𝑒𝑛𝑒𝑟𝑔𝑦is the energy recovered from the process and 𝐸𝑏𝑖𝑜𝑜𝑖𝑙 is the energy in the bio oil. 
The latter is divided with the amount of energy it takes to create the bio-oil which includes  
preparation of feedstock and the energy it takes to run the pyrolysis reactor. Therefore when 
scaling a pyrolysis reactor it is crucial for its success to improve the energy balance or at least 
keep the same relations between energy output from the oil and the energy used to run the 
system. There is only a very limited research and knowledge about this topic. A small 
experiment was conducted by Stals et al. where he states that on a lab scale reactor using flash 
pyrolysis, an energy recovery of 35-39 percent was obtained, performed on different types of 
hardwood [78]. However, this is not enough data conclude anything about an industrial scaled 
up version, as the energy is takes for preparation and running the system needs to controlled to 
the output. Therefore, to make pyrolysis more interesting for companies or other institutions, it is 
important to ensure a predictable output for a industrial scaled version of a pyrolysis rig.

A6 Prosopis Juliflora 
The prosopis family consists of approximately 45 different species categorized as spiny trees 
or shrubs. Prosopis trees and shrubs are found in areas that have a subtropical and tropical 
climate. A commonality for the trees and shrubs are that they both thrive in arid soil, this by 
extracting water from a deep rooting depth, which means that they are very resilience to 
drought. [79] 
Description and Application Areas 
Prosopis Juliflora (P. Juliflora), is categorized as a hardwood tree that can grow to a height of 
17 meters and a trunk width up to 1.2 meters [80]. P. Juliflora has a recorded maximum root 
depth of 53 meters which places it among deepest recorded root depths of all trees. In warm 
remote areas, water is normally a scarce resource, and having a significant amount of P. 
Juliflora trees can drain the groundwater supply and dry out soil to be used for agriculture [81], 
[82]. Thus, getting rid of P. Juliflora in dry climate areas can be a way of securing a supply of 
water. [83] 
The bark and the leaves of the prosopis tree have been used for the antifungal properties [84] 
and in various products through history [85]. The spores and pods can be used as a food 
supplement as they contain sugar and fatty acids. Historically wood from the P. Juliflora has 
been used as timber for both building houses and as a heat source for cooking food or heating.  
In the more modern age the prosopis tree have been used to make ornaments or as general 
wood work, due to the fact that the prosopis thee have a very dense core (also called 
heartwood) which is ideal for processes like spinning. 
In a study by Basavaraja et al. [86] that was carried out over a decade, it was found that 
plantation of P. Juliflora trees significantly improved the soil quality of a central dry zone in 
Karnataka, India by decreasing the pH levels of the soil etc. The study concluded that prosopis 
juliflora is effective for reclamation of salt affected wastelands. In the 1970’s, P. Juliflora was 
introduced in Kenya in order to improve biomass cover in the country and remedy 
environmental problems; however the growth has since gone out of control [83]. By 
investigation of the wood properties, Kenyan researchers have suggested using the wood to 
solve the problem of wood shortage in the country. 
Biochemical Composition and Its Impact on Pyrolysis Products 
The chemical composition of the P. Juliflora can be divided into four compounds: cellulose, 
hemicellulose, lignin and extractives [87]. The estimated amounts of compounds are roughly 
25%-30% hemicellulose, 40%-45% cellulose, 11%-28% lignin and lastly 3%-15% extractives 
(includes sugar, resins,volatile oils, fatty acids, tannis, and more). As all other biomaterials, 
prosopis wood also consists of ash and water. 
In 2017 a study of the attainable quality of bio-oil made from prosopis juliflora was carried out 
by Mythili et al. [54]. In this study, a fluidized bed reactor was used, and several experiments 
==PAGE:93==

A generic pyrolysis process is visualised in Figure 7.1. The feedstock is heated up in a oxygen 
free environment until the volatiles are released from the feedstock. Some volatiles are then 
condensed into bio-oil by a cooling system while the remaining syngas is collected or released

1. Feed preparation 
2. Biomass heating 
3. Char separation 
4. Liquids collection

Feedstock Preparation 
Feed preparation is the process of preparing the feedstock for the pyrolysis process, which 
consists of drying the feedstock and grinding it to the desired particle size. It has a heavy 
influence on the quality and yield fractions of each output. Research has shown that after drying 
wood biomass at a high temperature (~240 C), significantly less energy is required to extract 
==PAGE:95==
Chapter 7: Appendices 
Mathias Lind Neuenschwander & Martin Damgaard - June 2018 
 
 
83 
bio-oil from the feedstock [89]. It also gives the bio-oil a higher calorific value, i.e. the oil quality 
is higher. 
Biomass Heating 
There are many ways of heating biomass and many attempts have been made to optimize the 
heating process for pyrolysis. Two main principles are commonly used: a heated bed transfers 
heat to the biomass through conduction, or a hot gas is pumped through the biomass, but many 
additional principles exist (see A2) [77]. Different heating methods are used for different desired 
outputs. 
MASH Biotech’s prototype uses infrared heating which is not commonly used for pyrolysis. It 
offers high potential heating rates, although wood’s penetration depth of mid-infrared radiation is 
only around 0.3 mm [63]. Therefore, thicker biomass layers are partly heated by conduction like 
the heated-bed principle. 
Char Separation 
Char separation is important when designing a pyrolysis reactor that has a continuous flow of 
biomass going through the system. A common principle is to simply drop the char out of the 
chamber when the volatiles are released. Char can also be removed from the gasses before 
condensing; particles like aerosols will decrease the oil quality and are therefore in great 
interest to get removed. This can be done by filtering the gasses or by using a cyclone. 
Liquids Collection

Purpose of the project 
The objective of this MSc project is to design a pyrolysis test reactor and map the relationships 
between secondary pyrolysis effects, the bio-oil quality and the process variables, design

A11 Secondary Effects and How to Avoid Them 
This appendix includes and elaborates on the basic information on secondary pyrolysis effects 
given in secion 4.3.1. 
Secondary pyrolysis effects and dehydration reactions can occur throughout the pyrolysis 
process (Figure 4.3: Overview of secondary reactions in relation to the pyrolysis process). 
The information in this document is primarily based on the following sources: [41], [47], [53], 
[90]–[95] 
Secondary Pyrolysis 1 (SP1) 
When the biomass undergoes the primary pyrolysis process volatiles are released. The volatiles 
travel inside the feedstock particles through the feedstock capillaries, during which period they 
can interact with the feedstock and generate char and heat. This is undesirable because it 
decreases the oil yield and the homogeneity of the feedstock temperate, which again can lead 
to other negative effects like combustion. 
To mitigate the chance of secondary pyrolysis 1 one needs to consider the time it takes the 
volatiles to leave the solids, which is based on the time it takes to heat up the feedstock and the 
size of the feedstock particles. Therefore the feedstock should be as homogeneous as possible, 
meaning it should consists of only one material and the chip sizes should be controlled, to 
obtain a homogeneous heating of the feedstock. 
Secondary pyrolysis 2 (SP2) 
After the volatiles are released from the solids, they are present in a turbulent state within the 
reactor until vented out of the chamber. In this turbulent state the volatiles can react with both 
aerosol particles  and the solid grains (both feedstock and char). During this interaction the 
volatiles can be decomposed into non-combustible gases and char, which limits the oil yield. 
Typically this can be controlled by having the vents close to where the reaction happens in the 
chamber and how fast the volatiles are being moved from the heated chamber to 
cooling/condensing; this can be controlled by a suction pump or having the a continuous flow of 
inert gas through the system. When designing a flow system one needs to keep in mind that 
with a system with a high flow, the aerosols will more likely move with the volatiles into the 
condensing unit and result in a decrease of oil quality. Therefore the venting system needs to 
be balanced to get the desired effect. 
Dehydration reaction 1 (D1) 
The volatiles released at high temperatures undergo a dehydration reaction that makes them 
split into new fragments of CO2, CO, H2O, CH4, C3H8 HCHO C2H2, NOx, SOx, and more. 
These fragments of the volatiles are non-condensable or non-combustible, which is not 
desirable. 
To control this reaction the total vapour residence time should be as low as possible. To 
achieve this one needs to consider, as in secondary pyrolysis, the total distance from where the 
volatiles are released to the condensing area. As D1 is triggered by high temperatures it is 
crucial that the volatiles are being moved as fast as possible to minimize dehydration effects on 
the volatiles. 
Dehydration 2 (D2) 
When the volatiles have moved outside the reaction chamber and begin to cool off in the 
condensing unit, liquid is formed. The newly formed liquid can react with the oxygenated gases 
(formed from the previous secondary effects and dehydration) and produce water, polar acids, 
and different types of gases. This has a negative impact on the calorific value of the bio-oil.

The dehydration 2 reaction can only be mechanical controlled by cooling rate of the volatiles 
passing the condensing unit. The rig design also plays a part in this matter: to get a faster 
cooling of the volatiles one could consider shielding the condensing unit from the hot reaction 
chamber without increasing the distance from the chamber to avoid other unwanted effects. 
Gasification (G) 
When the feedstock is heated above a certain level, typically around 650°C, its constituents are 
broken down into lighter molecules (e.g. CH4, CO2, C2H6) that are released as non-
condensable gases. This process is known as gasification, and if the purpose of the reactor is to 
generate gas, this process is actively supported in the design (e.g. in micro-gasifiers and MASH 
Biotech’s gasification systems). However, since the scope of this project is production of oil and 
gasification promotes generation of gas at the expense of oil, this process should be avoided. It 
is therefore important that the feedstock temperature is kept below 650°C at all times. 
Char Overheating (CO) 
Char overheating is another negative effects that can happen as a result of heating of feedstock 
above the desired temperature. However in this case, which is more likely to happen during 
slow pyrolysis, the feedstock has already released the desired vapours at lower temperatures, 
and it then releases undesirable vapours (e.g. SOx, CO, H2S) at higher temperatures. These 
can interact with the desired volatiles in the chamber and cause secondary pyrolysis reactions 
(cf. Secondary Pyrolysis, SP2). 
Combustion (C) 
Combustion effects can occur when the feedstock starts to release volatiles at high 
temperatures. This leaves the hot charcoal, which can interact with the remaining feedstock that 
has a lower temperature and contains a more significant amount of oxygen; this can result in 
combustion of the feedstock within the meeting of the tree elements. Losing volatiles in this 
reaction is undesirable. In order to avoid combustion, the compounds of equal weight in the 
feedstock should crack as simultaneously as possible, which is achieved by a high homogeneity 
of heating. Therefore, reactor designs that use (turbulently flowing) gas as a heat transfer 
medium, e.g. fluidized bed reactors, are more likely to prevent combustion. Another approach to 
avoid combustion could be to separate the charcoal from the feedstock not yet cracked, but we 
have not been able to identify reactors that use this principle. Combustion can furthermore be a 
catalyst for gasification, as it promotes an uneven temperature distribution of the feedstock. 
Initiatives to avoid combustion, gasification, and char overheating 
Combustion and gasification can be avoided by controlling the heating rate and max 
temperature of the feedstock and ensuring a homogeneous heating. A homogeneous heating 
can be supported by using a suitable heating technique (e.g. radiation) and limiting the particle 
and feedstock batch/group size. Keeping the max temperature below the gasification threshold 
is vital, hence reactors that use metal plates etc. for conduction of heat should be avoided, 
since they promote a lower heat homogeneity. Having a lower heating rate to ensure that the 
volatiles have time to leave the feedstock can also be a way of limiting gasification; however, 
this will also decrease the overall oil yield .

Guangwen Xu, Professor at Institute of Process Engineering, Chinese Academy of

As part of a research project on pyrolysis of oil shale, Prof. G. Xu has developed a pyrolysis

Peter Arendt Jensen, Senior Researcher at DTU Chemical Engineering 
A meeting with Peter Arendt Jensen was held at DTU Chemical Engineering. From this we 
learned that in order to reduce the secondary effects, the volatiles need only be cooled to a 
temperature below 300ºC. Below this temperature, secondary effects are highly suppressed. 
Combined with the fact that the solid particles are much easier to separate from the vapours 
before they condense than after, P. Jensen recommended having a cooling device in the 
reactor followed by at least one filtration unit before the condenser. In order to increase oil 
quality further, he proposed using a partial condenser system to separate the output into two 
portions with and without water. However, this will decrease the oil yield since some 
combustible elements would still wind up in the watery output portion. 
When presented with the IR reactor design created by Guangwen Xu & Co. (described above), 
P. Jensen noted that although the cooling ribs directly beneath the feedstock decrease the 
volatile residence time (which is desirable), they will also capture some oil, making it difficult to 
calculate the mass balance when the reactor does not work continuously. Finally, we discussed 
purging the reactor with other gases (e.g. hydrogen) to which P. Jensen pointed out the risk of 
combustion and explosion. Even if we could make it work it might pose a significant challenge to 
make the reactor safe enough for use in e.g. a refugee camp.

A21 CFD New Tray 
Setting up the simulations – CAD 
In order to shorten simulation times, a simplified CAD model of the old reactor design was 
created in Autodesk Fusion 360 (see figure below). The model consists of the reactor exterior, a 
part for the distribution of nitrogen (in real life composed of the rock wool and the reflector), nine 
heaters, and two trays, one for each design. 
 
Figure: Cross-sectional view of the simplified reactor CAD model used for CFD simulation 
The old tray design was modelled as a solid box that should simulate the packed feedstock bed. 
The box height was set to 3 mm, slightly lower than the nominal feedstock layer thickness of 4 
mm. Modelling the steel mesh below the feedstock as a separate part was omitted to decrease 
simulation time. 
The new tray design was modelled as a single box with holes to simulate the cylinders. The 
height was set to 5.7 mm to include the nominal feedstock layer thickness of 4 mm plus the 
mesh thickness of 1.7 mm. The box had 133 holes to simulate the cylinders mounted on the 
tray. 
Setting up the simulations – Autodesk CFD 
The packed feedstock bed was modelled as a porous material in Autodesk CFD using a 
distributed resistance. The flow resistance was modelled by specifying the permeability of the 
feedstock bed, which is calculated using the Darcy equation 
𝑄= 𝜅𝐴Δ𝑃
𝜇𝐿 
where the permeability 𝜅 is a function of the dynamic viscosity 𝜇 of the surrounding fluid, the 
total volume discharge 𝑄, the total pressure drop Δ𝑃, the length over which the pressure drop 
takes place 𝐿 (the height of the feedstock bed), and the cross-sectional flow area 𝐴. Since 
finding the right permeability value for the feedstock bed would require extensive 
experimentation, it was approximated to 220 mm2 based on a study on the permeability of sand 
particles [106]. The particle size in this study was 0.223 mm on average which is close to the 
desired feedstock particle size (although it is in input parameter IP that might vary).

In both simulations, the internal volume was assigned the material nitrogen; this simulates the 
inert gas supplied to the reactor. All other parts (except for the feedstock bed in the old reactor) 
were modelled as stainless steel. Two boundary conditions were assigned: A volume flow rate 
of 20 l/min (equal to the nominal value) at the inlet and a pressure of 0.6 atm at the outlet (see 
figure below). The environment temperature was set to 293 K (19.85ºC) and the static pressure 
was set to 1 atm (1.01 bar). Even though the current prototype is a batch reactor, both 
simulations were run as steady-state since this is more accurate when the reactor is operated in 
a continuous configuration. 
 
 
Figure: Assigning boundary conditions to the CFD models 
CFD results 
To get a visual comparison of the results of the simulations, massless particle traces were used 
(see figures below). This allows us to get a view of the nitrogen flow through the model. The 
traces are colored by velocity magnitude. As seen in the figure, there is a slight change of hue 
in the color of the gas at the outlet of the reactor, from a more blue color using the old tray to a 
more green color using the new tray. This gives an indication that the packed feedstock bed 
slows down the gas flow, while the new tray allows for a faster flow as expected. 
 
Figure: (Left) Particle trace of the nitrogen stream through a packed feedstock bed (the old design). (Right) 
Particle trace of the nitrogen stream through the new tray. 
To better see the difference in vapour residence times between the two reactor designs, the 
residence times of the particles were exported to Excel. The mean for the old design was  
𝜇𝑜𝑙𝑑=25.24 s, while the mean for the new design was 𝜇𝑛𝑒𝑤=20.34 s. This confirms that the 
average residence time using the new tray design was reduced by 4.0 s. When running the

assembly. The two models differed only by their length (and thereby volume) and the number of 
inlets and outlets on each.

in the CFD program. Outliers were removed to give a more accurate result.
