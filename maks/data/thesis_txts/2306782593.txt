Abstract
This thesis is a study in how a system capable of performing LTE compliant baseband
processing can be designed, implemented and placed in an evolved Remote Radio Head
in order to improve the eﬃciency and combat the limitations of current CPRI-based
C-RAN fronthaul solutions.
The fronthaul architecture used as reference in this thesis is the one proposed in the
IEEE 1914 Next Generation Fronthaul Interface work group where the stated goal
is to deﬁne a standard for packet-based fronthaul transport networks including rec-
ommendations for functional partitioning schemes between Remote Radio Heads and
centralized Baseband Units. A trade-oﬀanalysis is made between diﬀerent functional
partitioning schemes where a functional split, where the baseband modulation/demod-
ulation, resource mapping/demapping and generation of static LTE physical signals
are oﬄoaded from the Baseband Unit to the Remote Radio Head, is proposed. IQ
data rate analysis shows that a pure fronthaul data rate reduction of at least 50% can
be achieved under full load and up to 97% for low load by using the proposed split
compared to current fronthaul solutions.
The design and implementation in digital hardware of the oﬄoaded baseband process-
ing is based on 33 identiﬁed requirements regarding functionality, performance and
interfacing. During veriﬁcation of the implemented system it is found that the system
complies with 28 out of the 33 set requirements. The thesis is completed with eﬀorts
that can be made in order to obtain a fully compliant system together with a series of
improvements and extensions.

Preface
This master’s thesis was prepared at the department of Photonics at the Technical
University of Denmark in fulﬁllment of the requirements for acquiring a master of sci-
ence degree in engineering.
The project was designed and completed in collaboration with the company Comcores
together with founder Thomas Gerner Nørgaard with Søren Forchhammer as the DTU
appointed supervisor.
The majority of the work done towards completing this project was made at Comcores
in Hørsholm, Denmark.
As requested by Comcores implementation speciﬁc details and developed VHDL code
are only disclosed to Søren Forchhammer and the external censor of this project. All
sensitive implementation details have been moved out to an appendix which is only
disclosed in the conﬁdential version. The main report will be identical for the conﬁ-
dential and the open version.
This is the open version.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The annual report on global mobile traﬃc forecast from the American multinational
technology company Cisco Systems forecasts the growth trends for mobile data traﬃc.
The 2016 report lists a 5 year forecasts using data collected by the company from 2015
on several areas within global mobile data traﬃc.
The ﬁgures for 2015 concerning the total amount of mobile traﬃc generated on a global
basis is clear; the amount of traﬃc grew with 74% compared to 2014 from 2.1 exabytes
to 3.7 exabytes. The predictions made by Cisco Systems for the upcoming years also
show that the amount of mobile traﬃc increases approximately 50% on an annual basis
as seen on Figure 1.1 [1].
Figure 1.1: Cisco forecast on mobile traﬃc growth taken from [1].
The continuing increase of mobile traﬃc on a global scale puts the operators of
mobile networks under pressure since they have to meet the demands for both mobile
coverage and capacity. In order to meet these demands new standards for mobile com-
munication are speciﬁed by large standardization organizations such as 3GPP. One of
the most promising 3GPP standard, which is currently being deployed on a large scale
by mobile operators, is referred to as the fourth generation (4G) of mobile communi-
cation also called Long Term Evolution (LTE) (3GPP release 8). The intention from
3GPPs side is to deﬁne a framework and system in LTE which can easily be extended
and build upon when evolving into next generations of mobile communication.
One of the major changes made from 3G to 4G was the simpliﬁed network architec-
ture, both for the core network and the Radio Access Network (RAN), which was a
==PAGE:26==
2
1 Introduction
result of LTE only being capable of handling packet switched services using Internet
Protocol (IP) as the base. By removing circuit switched services from the network a
great simpliﬁcation could be made in the network architecture. The simpliﬁcation in
the RAN was achieved by centralizing all processing to the base station, referred to
as the eNodeB (eNB), thereby eliminating interfacing, communication and controlling
to and by other RAN nodes. The eNB in LTE is therefore responsible for making all
resource allocation, user scheduling and physical layer processing decisions on its own.
For eNBs to cooperate and communicate a new eNB-to-eNB interface called X2 is
deﬁned. This interface can be used for inter-cell interference control and cancellation
since it allows for cooperation between the base stations [3].
The latest completed 3GPP standards, release 10-12, also referred to as LTE-Advanced
(LTE-A) deﬁnes and sets the evolution from 4G to 5G. One of the key concepts regard-
ing the network architecture for enabling 5G mobile networks is the use of small cells
which form so-called Ultra Dense Networks (UDN) in order to increase coverage and
capacity. UDNs suﬀers from increased inter-cell interference because many small and
densely placed cells use the same spectral resources for the wireless communication. To
address the problems with interference 3GPP has standardized a series of techniques
for dealing with interference in LTE/LTE-A such as Inter-Cell Interference Coordina-
tion (ICIC) and Cooperative Multi Point (CoMP) by the use of the X2 interface [4]. In
order to eﬃciently use interference cancellation techniques tight synchronization and
low latency between the diﬀerent eNBs are required. These requirements have been
proven hard to meet with the traditional LTE RAN architecture, which has given birth
to the concept of Cloud-RAN (C-RAN). In C-RAN the processing of several eNBs are
further centralized to the same physical area called the Baseband Unit (BBU) where
it can be implemented in cloud computing environments. The only processing left at
the original base station sites, named the Remote Radio Head (RRH), is the Radio
Frequency (RF) and antenna processing.
Picture 1.2 shows the diﬀerence between traditional RAN and C-RAN architectures.
As illustrated, centralizing all eNB processing gives rise to a new internal RAN
network called the Fronthaul which covers the networking and interfacing between
RRHs and BBUs.
The advantages of introducing a centralization of base station
processing in C-RAN is clear; timing and synchronization requirements between base
stations can now be met. This comes from the fact that all the processing made for
base station in a certain physical area is centralized and controlled by the same BBU
controller using a pool of BBU resources. The controller can thereby control resource
allocation and user scheduling on all the diﬀerent access points connected to the BBU
pool, allowing for eﬀective interference control and cancellation.
This is especially
rewarding with UDN deployments where the capacity is signiﬁcantly increased because
of the inter-cell coordination as evaluated in [5].
Furthermore C-RAN can oﬀer a pooling gain since the BBU resources are shared in a
pool among several base station sites. The processing required in the BBU pool would
in general be lower compared to a traditional RAN since the load on the network
changes with time. A pooling gain is a desired eﬀect of C-RAN since this can reduce

With the introduction of C-RAN focus has been shifted to the new fronthaul net-
work and standardization of the interface between the BBU and the RRH. At the time
of writing the most dominant fronthaul transportation protocol is Common Public
Radio Interface (CPRI) where the latest speciﬁcation is the CPRI version 7.0.
CPRI oﬀers a communication link with Constant Bit Rate (CBR) with rates ranging
from 614.4 Mbit/s (CPRI line option 1) up to 24330.24 Mbit/s (CPRI line option 10).
CPRI transports complex valued baseband carrier samples, referred to as antenna car-
riers (AxC), characterized by an In-phase (I) and an Quadrature-phase (Q) component.
One CPRI link can carry several AxCs on the BBU-RRH interface [7].
However scalability and ﬂexibility issues with CPRI and the high requirements for low
latency, low jitter and high bandwidth eﬃciency on the fronthaul link has hindered
the deployment of C-RAN on a large scale. CPRI oﬀers a circuit switched connection
with CBR and is therefore incapable of gaining any statistical multiplexing gain when
increasing the number of cells in the network. Once the capacity of a certain CPRI
line option has been reached either the CPRI link must be upgraded or a new CPRI
link must be established, requiring more physical resources. This makes a CPRI based
fronthaul expensive, inﬂexible and with very poor scalability regarding RAN expan-
sion.
The limitations of CPRI as a fronthaul transport protocol is slowing down the evo-
lution into 5G. Fronthaul networking and dimensioning is currently one of the most
challenging problem for next-generations mobile networks, being a major research topic
in both the industry and the academia.
In order to achieve a more eﬃcient fronthaul it is desirable to move from a circuit-
switched network to a packet-switched network. Packet-switched networks can achieve
==PAGE:28==
4
1 Introduction
a statistical multiplexing gain when the load on the network varies over time. The
move however requires a revision of the current C-RAN architecture including the pro-
cessing made at the RRH and the BBU.
To address this challenge a working group called Next Generation Fronthaul Interface
(NGFI, IEEE 1914) has been formed by the industry with support from IEEE. The
stated scopes of IEEE 1914 NGFI are the following [8]:
• Architecture for the transport of mobile fronthaul traﬃc, user data traﬃc, and
management and control plane traﬃc.
• Requirements and deﬁnitions for the fronthaul link, including data rates, timing
and synchronization, and quality of service.
• Recommendation on diﬀerent functional partitioning between RRHs and BBUs
that will improve fronthaul link eﬃciency and interoperability among vendors.
This thesis will investigate the possible solutions for next-generation fronthaul outlined
by the NGFI work group with focus being on the functional partitioning between the
BBU and RRH.
The goal of this project is to design, implement and evaluate baseband processing
for the RRH that enables next-generation fronthaul, based on a proposed BBU-RRH
processing split. The design and implementation will be in digital hardware and the
evaluation will then be on verifying implemented functionality and to estimate the
implementation loss. The evaluation will be used to make an assessment of the im-
plemented system and its usage in an actual deployment of a NGFI enabled next-
generation fronthaul.
First a project deﬁnition is given which states the goals of the project and deﬁnes

CHAPTER 3
LTE overview
This chapter will give an overview of some of the most fundamental concepts for the
LTE system, which are essential to known in order to fully understand the analysis
made on the NGFI and the BBU-RRH functional processing splits presented in the
following chapter. Appendix A has been appended to this thesis where several of the
concepts outlined in this chapter are described in detail. The concepts covered in the
appendix are used heavily in both the NGFI analysis and in the design description but
are not essential to understand the work that has been conducted in this thesis.
The appendix will be referenced throughout this chapter with references to where in
depth descriptions of the concepts introduced can be found.
As mentioned the LTE system for mobile communication was deﬁned and speciﬁed
by 3GPP in their release 8 in 2006 and has since then been further speciﬁed and build
upon in the following releases. The overall goal with LTE in release 8 was to deﬁne a
new, simpliﬁed and generic framework for both radio access and mobile network archi-
tecture that could be used as a base for the future releases to increase coverage and
capacity. This has led to the standardization of LTE-A in release 10 together with
LTE-Licensed Assisted Access (LTE-LAA) in the, at the time of writing, on-going
speciﬁcation release 13.
The single most important simpliﬁcation to network architecture was the decision to
only support packet-based services resulting in a purely packet-switched network. This
was a major simpliﬁcation since legacy standards such as UMTS (3G) or GSM/EDGE
(2G) supported both circuit-based and packet-based services resulting in a more com-
plex network architecture. As a result of this simpliﬁcation it was also decided to
centralize all radio related processing at the eNB in order to further simplify network
architecture. Section A.1 in Appendix A presents the LTE network in more detail.
In order not to loose generality it was decided from 3GPP’s side to support both
Frequency Division Duplex (FDD) and Time Division Duplex (TDD) for duplexed
communication in LTE. For FDD paired spectrum is used, i.e. one part of the allocated
spectrum is reserved for the DL and another part reserved for UL. The division between
DL and UL is hence made in frequency. In TDD unpaired spectrum is used, i.e. the
same part of the spectrum is reserved for both DL and UL. The division between DL
and UL is then made in time, where at some predeﬁned time interval the spectrum is
used for DL transmission and in other time intervals for UL.
Since many radio related conﬁgurations in LTE depends on the duplexing mode a radio

3.2
OFDMA modulation for DL
For DL Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiple Access (OFDMA) modulation is used
for multiple access to the resources and for modulation of the DL carrier. The multiple
access is achieved by allocating diﬀerent PRBs to users scheduled for access. 1 PRB
in the duration of a 1 ms (i.e. 1 subframe) is the smallest assignable piece of physical
resources that can be allocated to a user. The PRB allocation for a single user does not
have to be contiguous meaning that PRBs that are not located adjacently in frequency
can be allocated to the same user in the same transmission. The maximum number of
PRBs that can be allocated to a UE depends on the capabilities of the given device
[3].
The data that is to be transmitted in LTE is mapped to subcarriers in the fre-
quency domain, however in order to physically transmit the carrier a conversion to the
time-domain must be made. This conversion is in general made with a Discrete Fourier
Transformation (DFT) in discrete-time using a speciﬁc transformation size. The data
that is to be transmitted is then mapped on to all the orthogonal subcarriers which
form the input to the DFT, the transformed output will then be the time-domain
OFDM symbol samples.
Since the DFT is in general a very resource demanding processing function to imple-
ment in real-time simpliﬁcations to the function can be made when using radix-2 sizes
of the transformation. Fourier transformations with radix-2 sizes implemented with
the simpliﬁed algorithm are in general referred to as Fast Fourier Transformations
(FFT) and are used in the LTE system to relax processing requirements on both re-
ceiver (Rx) and transmitter (Tx). Figure 3.6 shows the principle of OFDMA used for
DL transmission in LTE, in a highly simpliﬁed LTE system.

• Physical layer (PHY): responsible for performing all LTE related physical

modulation in UL.

3.4.1
HARQ
For HARQ retransmissions decoded blocks are acknowledged by the receptor by send-
ing an ACK for successful decoding or a NACK otherwise back to the transmitter.
An ACK/NACK in a given subframe must be sent after 4 subframes, this allows the
receiver to perform the decoding. Seen from the network side, the data REs received
at the antenna in UL subframe 1 the HARQ ACK/NACK should be transmitted in
DL subframe 5. Figure 3.10 illustrates the required HARQ timing.
As seen the delay from the point where the last row of REs in UL subframe 1 is
received to the point where the HARQ ACK/NACKs should be transmitted in the
ﬁrst row of REs in DL subframe 5 is 3 ms. During these 3 ms all transmission and
reception processing must be carried out in the network including all LTE PHY and
MAC, fronthaul transportation and RF processing.

4.1
NGFI deﬁnitions and design principles
NGFI is the fronthaul interface between the baseband processing center and the RRH.
The baseband processing center contains resources capable of performing baseband
processing of several cell sites and for several channels. Furthermore the processing
center contains all logical decisions regarding resource allocation and user scheduling
across all sites while also consisting of the backhaul interface to the core network.
NGFI is described as an open interface that should support several NGFI designs in-
cluding diﬀerent functional partitions between the centralized processing center and
the RRHs. This has lead to a redeﬁnition of the classical C-RAN RRH-BBU architec-
ture and interface since now some baseband processing functionality is shifted out to
the RRH. The BBU is redeﬁned as the Radio Cloud Center (RCC) while the RRH is
redeﬁned as the Remote Radio System (RRS). Furthermore the redeﬁnition involves
changing the view of the fronthaul network and interface as a point-to-point circuit-
switched connection with constant bit rate to a point-to-multipoint or many-to-many
packet-based fronthaul network. This also involves changing current C-RAN fronthaul
transport protocols as e.g. CPRI with transport and exchange protocols suitable for
==PAGE:46==
22

packet-based networks carrying time-sensitive radio data as e.g. RoE using TSN.

The RRS consists of one or more RRHs (RRU on ﬁgure) together with a Radio
Aggregation Unit (RAU). The RAU will implement the shifted baseband processing
according to the functional partitioning scheme used for the speciﬁc RRS-RCC con-
nection.
For the design and standardization of NGFI and the RRC-RSS interface six (6)
design principles have been listed which serves as a guideline during the design process.
The design principles are:
1. Statistical multiplexing: The ability of utilizing the fact that the load on the
network and on an interface connection varies over time. It is a common beneﬁt
experienced on packet-based networks. Statistical multiplexing should be used
on the RRC-RSS interface in order to reduce required bandwidth on the link and
to allow better cost control.
2. Adaptive bandwidth modulation in response to payload: The NGFI
bandwidth should vary according to the level of traﬃc from the layer above.
During low levels of traﬃc the NGFI bandwidth should be also be low and vice
versa. The ability of being bandwidth adaptive to traﬃc is a required condition
for enabling a statical multiplexing gain.
==PAGE:47==
4.2 NGFI designs
23
3. Maximum support for high-gain coordinated algorithms: The design
process should seek to favor NGFI design maximizing support for high-gain co-
ordinated algorithms. This includes interference cancellation algortihms such as
ICIC, enhanced ICIC (eICIC) and further enhanced ICIC (FeICIC) and coordi-
nation algorithms such as CoMP.
This is important since for 5G technologies coordination algorithms plays a sig-
niﬁcant role in order to increase capacity and limit inter-cell interference.
4. Decoupling of interface traﬃc from number of antennas at RRH: An-
tenna related processing and processing involving direct contact with antenna
arrays should be kept remote at the RRS. This will maximize the adaptability
of the NGFI design to future 5G technologies where especially the concept of
Massive MIMO where large antenna arrays are used will limit the demand for
NGFI bandwidth on the fronthaul link.
5. Neutrality with respect to air interface technology: The proposed NGFI
design should be capable of supporting not only 4G mobile communication sys-
tems (LTE) but also support future 5G technologies such as LTE-A and LTE-
LAA.
6. Optimization of RRS-RCC connections: Means that a given RRS-RCC
conncetion should be optimized in terms of functional partitioning so a best
ﬁt is achieved for a given deployment scenario. This also means that there is
not an one-ﬁt-all solution but rather the choice of functional partitioning is a
optimization made over several parameters such as cost, delay, bandwidth and
RRS/RRC complexity.

Division 1: internal division of MAC
The ﬁrst division seeks to make an internal division of the MAC layer in the LTE
protocol stack where the higher processing steps covering mainly scheduling, reporting
and priority handling are located in the RCC and the lower processing steps covering
mainly the HARQ processing is relocated to the RRS.

• By making the internal division of the MAC sublayer the most time critical
processing which puts the most pressure on the RRS-RCC link, i.e. the HARQ
retransmissions, are moved close to the antennas and are freed from fronthaul
transport delay which results in drastic delay relaxations on the interface link.
• The RRS-RCC link capacity will depend on the cell load.

• Since the user scheduling is centralized at the RCC the internal division also

algorithms for Joint Transmission and Reception (JT/JR) can not be achieved

and network upgrade.

Division 2: PHY/MAC division
The second division is a division of the MAC and PHY layers in the LTE protocol where
all PHY related processing is relocated to the RRS and the MAC related processing
is kept at the RCC.
Advantages:
• Shares the same possibilities for statistical multiplexing as for the division 1
solution, and the required minimal NGFI fronthaul bandwidth requirement is to
a large extend the same.
Disadvantages:
• The HARQ retransmission processes are kept centralized at the RCC which puts
a strict delay requirement on the NGFI fronthaul link.
• Shares the same possibilities for only limited coordinated scheduling together as
for the division 1 solution.
• Has a slightly better pooling gain at the RCC since the HARQ processes can be
shared among all the cells controlled by the RCC, but is still very limited.

• The complexity of the RAU is quite high since all the PHY processing is dis-

RAN fronthaul solution to a NGFI using a division 2 fronthaul solution quite
demanding in terms of cost and network upgrade.
4.2.3
Division 3: Bit-level/symbol-level division
For the third division the functional partitioning is made at a bit-to-symbol level,
meaning that all bit-level processing covering mainly channel coding (described in
Appendix A, section A.5.1) is kept at the RCC while all processing made on a symbol-
level covering mainly modulation/de-modulation and channel estimation/equalization
(described in Appendix A, section A.5.2) is relocated to the RRS.
Advantages:
• The processing made on a bit-level depends on the current network load which
enables the possibility of a statistical multiplexing gain on the fronthaul link.
• Having the channel coding at the RCC enables a bigger pooling gain since e.g. the
channel decoding processing engines can be shared between all the cells covered
by the RCC.
• Since the channel decoding is one of the most resource consuming processing steps
in the LTE PHY the complexity of the RAU is lowered signiﬁcantly compared
to division 1 and 2 solutions making the upgrade easier.
Disadvantages:
• Has strict fronthaul link delay requirements due to the HARQ processes being
centralized at the RCC.
• The fronthaul bandwidth requirements for a division 3 solution is increased com-
pared to division 2 since now the information exchanged between the RRS and
the RCC contains redundancy introduced by the channel encoders.
• Making the division between the demodulation and the channel decoding steps
imposes some serious interface challenges especially for realizing advanced de-
modulation and decoding schemes. This can be e.g. with the use of iterative
demodulation where the result of the channel decoding can be used to improve
the demodulation in an iterative manner. This requires an additional exchange of
information over the fronthaul link which increases both bandwidth requirements
and interface complexity.

4.2.4
Division 4: Symbol-level/sample-level division
For the fourth division the functional partitioning is made on a symbol-to-sample level
where all symbol and bit-level processing is located at the RCC while all sample-level
processing covering baseband modulation/demodulation and resource mapping/demap-
ping is relocated to the RRS.
Advantages:
• The processing made on a symbol-level depends on the current network load
which enables the possibility of a statistical multiplexing gain on the fronthaul
link.
• Since all symbol-level processing is centralized at the RCC a high pooling gain
can be achieved. The increased gain is achieved since sharing especially DFT
and demodulation resources in UL is possible for this division.
• Since almost all PHY processing is kept at the RCC the complexity of the RAU
in the RRS is signiﬁcantly decreased. This makes an upgrade to a fronthaul with
NGFI division solution 4 much more feasible compared to the other divisions.
Disadvantages:
• Has strict fronthaul link delay requirements due to the HARQ processes being
centralized at the RCC.
• The fronthaul bandwidth requirements is increased compared to division 3. This
increase is mainly seen in UL since now the information exchanged is the mod-
ulated subcarrier symbols containing unequalized noise. This requires a larger
sample width to contain all the information thereby increasing the required NGFI
bandwidth. Furthermore the bandwidth will, for this split, dependent on the
number of antennas used and will therefore suﬀer from poor scalability since the
required fronthaul bandwidth increases proportional to the number of antennas
used. For 5G technologies such as Massive MIMO this is an undesired eﬀect.
4.2.5
Division 5: Baseband/RF division
The ﬁfth and ﬁnal division uses the classical C-RAN functional partitioning where all
baseband processing is centralized in the RCC while only RF processing is relocated
to the RRS.
Advantages:
• Locating all baseband processing at the RCC enables the use of high-gain coordi-
nation algorithms since every processing step is controlled with a single central-
==PAGE:52==
28

ized controller. This enables both CS algortihms but also more advanced JT/JR
algorithms that can increase the total throughput signiﬁcantly.
• Has the lowest RAU complexity since no baseband processing is required re-
motely.
• Possibility for the highest pooling gain in the RCC.
Disadvantages:
• Has strict fronthaul link delay requirements due to the HARQ processes being
centralized at the RCC.
• This solution requires maximum NGFI bandwidth and since the bandwidth is
constant not depending on current network load achieving a multiplexing gain
is in general not possible. The solution relies on the use of a transport protocol
with constant bit rate such as e.g. CPRI.
• Suﬀers from poor fronthaul bandwidth scalability since the interface traﬃc is
directly coupled to the number of antennas at the RRH.

In DL the split includes moving the resource element mapper together with the
OFDM baseband modulation out to the remote radiohead. Furthermore it is decided
to also move the generation of LTE physical signals (reference and synchronization
signals) out to the RAU processor. Since the physical signals solely exist in the LTE
PHY layer and contain static information only depending on predeﬁned cell conﬁgura-
tion, generating them remotely saves additional bandwidth on the fronthaul link. In
order to achieve the load depending fronthaul bandwidth only active REs, i.e. REs
in the resource grid where data has been scheduled, are sent from the RCC to the
RAU/RRS over the fronthaul link and then mapped onto the resource grid by the
resource mapper.
In UL the functional partitioning chosen includes moving the SC-FDMA baseband de-
modulation out to the RAU in the RCC together with a resource element de-mapper.
In order to achieve a traﬃc dependent fronthaul bandwidth in UL as well, the RAU
must get UL scheduling information from the RCC in order to only forward active
REs. The scheduling information for the UL resources must be received by the RAU
in advance in order for the system to perform the extraction of only active resources.
The proposed NGFI functional partitioning implies that during low cell load the
required fronthaul bandwidth will be low since many REs will not be scheduled in
both DL and UL.

5.3.1
Common (UL and DL) requirements
LTE duplex mode and frame structure
The L1 oﬄoad core shall support LTE FDD mode only using LTE frame structure 1
since this is the only frame structure supported for FDD. This is a hardcoded conﬁg-
uration.
Since the frame structures for FDD and TDD are fundamentally diﬀerent separate
systems must be designed and implemented for support of both duplex modes. FDD
and frame structure 1 are selected since it is the most widely used mode in Europe
including Denmark [12].
LTE bandwidths
The core must support oﬄoading for the following LTE bandwidths; 1.4, 3, 5, 10 and
20 MHz channels. This essentially means that the cores should be able to process
channels with diﬀerent physical sizes measured in PRBs. The only conﬁguration that
has been left out is the 15 MHz carrier since it requires an odd FFT/IFFT size for
the OFDMA modulation and SC-FDMA demodulation. Only allowing LTE channels
that uses radix-2 FFT sizes signiﬁcantly simpliﬁes the design.
The conﬁguration of bandwidth should be available as a design generic.
FFT/IFFT
In order to generate OFDM symbols and demodulate SC-FDMA symbols the core

Unwanted emissions
TS 36.104 also lists requirements for unwanted emissions which consists of out-of-band
emissions and spurious emissions which arise from the baseband modulation process
(OFDM modulation) or from non-linearities in the transmitter. The following three
requirements are applicable for the L1 oﬄoad system:
• Occupied bandwidth: sets the requirements for the bandwidth occupied by
the generated carrier, which must be lower than the assigned channel bandwidth.
This minimum requirement is listed in clause 6.6.1.1 in TS 36.104.
• Adjacent Channel Leakage Power Ratio (ACLR): sets requirements for the
power ratio between the generated channel and adjacent channels. The minimum
requirements for ACLR depends on the size and category of the base station
and whether the spectrum is paired (FDD) or unpaired (TDD). The minimum
requirements are listed in clause 6.6.2.1 in TS 36.104.
• Operating band unwanted emissions: sets the limits of the unwanted emis-
sions outside the assigned operating band. The minimum requirements again
depends on the size and category of the base station. The minimum require-
ments are listed in clause 6.6.3.1 to 6.6.3.3 in TS 36.104.
Setting up direct requirements for ACLR and operating band unwanted emissions
for the implemented L1 oﬄoad system can not be straight forwardly made since it
to a large extent also depends on the transmitter circuits used.
The requirements
and measurements are typically set for the entire system including both baseband
modulators and RF circuits (converters, ﬁlters and PA).
Therefore only soft requirements are set for the L1 oﬄoad system which requires that
there should not be any indication that the requirements for ACLR and operating
band unwanted emissions cannot be met in a realistic base station setup.
The requirements for Occupied bandwidth shall be met by the system according
to the minimum requirements set in TS 36.104.

CHAPTER 6
Design description
This chapter outlines the design of the L1 oﬄoad system based on the requirement
speciﬁcation given.
The design covers deﬁning and describing all blocks required for the core in both UL
and DL. Algorithms and procedures used are also presented and the reasoning behind
is given. The design is based on an implementation in digital hardware targeting a
Virex-7 FPGA which is limited in terms of resources and speed. Especially the limited
DSP resources on FPGAs is a key factor for the choice of design given in the following.
The design description is split into the following ﬁve (5) main sections:
• FFT/IFFT: Design of FFT and IFFT
• DL: Design of DL subsystem designed to process a single antenna port
• UL: Design of UL subsystem designed to process a single antenna port
• Interfaces: Design of external interfaces
• LTE timing module: design of LTE timing module used across all antenna
ports in both DL and UL
Figure 6.1 shows the top-level design of the L1 oﬄoad system. The overall system
contains subsystems for both UL and DL L1 oﬄoading each having their own dedicated
external R-L1, T-L1 and RF-L1 interfaces.
The number of DL and UL subsystems to be used depends on the number of
antenna layers used for the conﬁguration. For this core a maximum of two UL and DL
subsystems can be used as required from requirement 1.2.1 and 1.3.1.
A LTE timing module is generating timing information used by both the DL and
UL subsystems. The timing information contains information on start and status of
OFDM symbols, slot and subframes. Using the same LTE timing module for both UL
and DL ensures that the two subsystems are frame aligned. This is a necessity since
the base station requires that the frame alignment between UL and DL frames is valid.

the order of the inputs to the IFFT.
The resource mapper is provided with REs for reference and synchronization signals

the R-L1 interface. For all REs provided by the interface an active indicator is given
that indicate whether the subcarrier placed on the RE is active or inactive. Depending
on current LTE symbol and subframe number the mapper should be able to perform
the mapping as deﬁned in TS 36.211.
Since the resource mapper is not able to distinct the underlying channel structure in
the REs received from the R-L1 interface the mapper must be able to map according
to three (3) diﬀerent cases. These are:

LTE timing module

The platform consists of both a RCC and a RRS/RAU entity containing the func-

On the RCC side a BBU DL generator is used to generate realistic LTE DL data
including most of the LTE MAC and PHY processing except for the PHY processing
oﬄoaded to the RAU. The generator is a static block that will only be responsible for
delivering LTE DL data generated statically and stored on the platform to the RoE
framer in the format that is expected. A static BBU generator is used since it allows
for easy integration with the platform. Software capable of generating realistic LTE
DL data is used for generating the data and made available for the generator.
The BBU generated LTE DL data is framed into RoE frames which are then deliv-
ered to a TSN MAC which will control the transmission of the Ethernet frames over
a fronthaul transmission link. The TSN MAC will accept both RoE frames and nor-
mal Ethernet frames generated randomly with random time intervals by an Ethernet

The BBU generated LTE DL data extracted by the RoE de-framer is ﬁnally processed

TAE
Since it is required from the R-L1 interface that subcarrier data across all conﬁgured
antenna ports are presented (validated) at the same time by the RoE de-framer the
resource mapping and OFDM modulation at all layers will be initiated at the same time.

layers. This ultimately means that the generated and delivered OFDM modulated
blocks are fully aligned in time on all the layers resulting in no time alignment errors.
The resulting TAE of zero will naturally meet the requirement for TAE stated in 2.2.3.2
and since it is a direct consequence of the design and no further veriﬁcation will be
made.

9.1.1
Reducing unwanted emissions
For the DL performance veriﬁcation it was concluded that the level of unwanted emis-
sion and spectral leakage caused by the implemented system was too high. It was
shown that the system did not meet minimum requirements for ACLR and operating
band unwanted emissions, which must be met before the system can be used in a real
fronthaul solution.
This subsection will state the main contributor to the unwanted emission in the gen-
erated LTE channel and also present a method for reducing it.
The main reason for the high level of unwanted emission can be found in the
generation of the OFDM modulated signal. As described in chapter 3 the time domain
signal is generated and transmitted one OFDM symbol at the time.
Since guard
spaces at both channel edges are included in the OFDM modulation a certain degree
of oversampling is found in the generated symbols, which essentially results in a rather
smooth time domain waveform. Since the waveform samples are generated on a symbol-
basis the samples within a symbol will form the smooth waveform but at the points
where there is a transition from one OFDM symbol to another discontinuities may
be observed which causes unwanted emissions. An illustration of this phenomena is
shown in Figure 9.1, where the transition between two adjacent OFDM symbols in
time is shown.
Here the discontinuity is observed since the last sample of the blue symbol and the
ﬁrst sample of the red symbol are not connected resulting in a sharp symbol transition.
This sharp transition will result in spectral leakage of the bandlimited channel.
The sharp transitions between two adjacent symbols can be made more smooth by
applying a windowing function applied over the symbol transition. Several windowing
approaches exist where the most simple approach is to have a window where the
coeﬃcients gradually go towards zero at the symbol edges which when multiplied to

116

9.2.1

For the implementation of the L1 oﬄoad system in UL it was assumed throughout
the project that the input samples were aligned in time meaning that the ﬁrst sample
received was the ﬁrst sample of the ﬁrst SC-FDMA symbol. As also mentioned this
assumption is rather coarse and in a full L1 oﬄoad system time synchronization of the
samples received from the RF processing unit would be required. This subsection will
present a method for performing time synchronization which can be included in the
future work of the L1 oﬄoad core.
Achieving synchronization in time typically involves searching for recognizable pat-
terns within the received signal. In LTE CP is inserted as a guard space between
generated OFDM and SC-FDMA symbols in order to avoid ISI and consists of merely
copying a part of the end of the symbol and inserting it at the front. This redundancy
can essentially be used for time synchronization since it is known from the symbol and
CP sizes how far the two matching parts are from each other. A correlation between

9.2.2
PRACH detection
Initially, during the requirement speciﬁcation, it was decided not to include the pro-
cessing of the PRACH LTE channel in the ﬁrst version of the L1 oﬄoad system in
order to safe system development time.
For a full L1 oﬄoad system it would be required to include PRACH processing ca-
pabilities since this is not a part of the SC-FDMA demodulation of the PUSCH and
PUCCH channels and the DRS signal. Therefore implementing a PRACH detector
that is able to decode and deliver the RACH requests generated by the UEs will be a
natural improvement of the implemented system.
The UE requests made on the RACH are performed by sending uncoordinated
PRACH preambles on predetermined allocation in frequency and time.
A total of
64 PRACH preambles have been deﬁned, where the preamble used is coded into a
Zadoﬀ-Chu sequence of length 839 and then modulated and transmitted by the UE.
==PAGE:146==
122

The PRACH allocation in frequency and time is broadcast by the base station on the
PBCH [3].
The base station can then based on the received PRACH signal determine which
preambles have been sent by performing correlation among all known preambles. The
correlation info can then be sent over the fronthaul to the RRS/BBU which can further
decode the UE RACH requests.
The generation of the PRACH signal is quite similar to the SC-FDMA modulation
process however a much smaller subcarrier spacing is used (1.25 kHz instead of 15
kHz). Therefore in order extract the 839 subcarriers carrying the PRACH preambles
a certain amount of down-sampling is required before performing the FFT. Figure 9.9

1024 the subcarrier spacing of 1.25 kHz is achieved. Due to centralization in frequency
the position of the PRACH subcarriers in the FFT output is known and they can be
extracted and correlated.

conﬁguration mode essentially means that 504 diﬀerent FPGA/chip implementations

the start-up routine has ﬁnished the full system is ready for operation and can be
enabled by the processor.
Not only the cell ID can be conﬁgured dynamically but many of the other conﬁgu-

be a desired change since it would result in only a single chip implementation of the
full L1 oﬄoad system that can be set up to run any conﬁguration.

A.1
LTE network architecture
One of the major simpliﬁcations made in LTE compared to its predecessors is the
support of only packet switched services and not a mix of both circuit and packet
switched services as was the case for both 2G (e.g. GSM using EDGE) and 3G (e.g.
WCDMA) mobile communication generations. The choice of only supporting packet
switched services was based on an assessment that these services would have a greater
impact on the total amount of user traﬃc generated in the future with the Internet
Protocol (IP) being the most dominant protocol for packet-based communication. Also
many IP applications require much higher bandwidth, e.g. video streaming, compared
to classical circuit-switched services, e.g. voice calls, increasing the capacity require-
ments for packet-based services.
By only supporting packet-switched services a great simpliﬁcation was made on the
network architecture compared to GSM/EDGE and WCDMA architectures since the
network entities dealing with the circuit-switched traﬃc could be completely removed.
Figure A.1 gives an overview of the LTE network architecture.
The network is split into three sections; the UE section covering all UE related pro-
cessing, i.e. reception and transmission, the evolved UMTS Terrestrial Radio Access
Network (eUTRAN) covering all RAN and base station processing and the Evolved
Packet Core (EPC) covering all core network related processing. Since the LTE net-
work is only packet-based the network and interconnections are based on IP.
The user and control plane has been separated in the architecture where interfaces
between network entities for both planes have been deﬁned. Figure A.1 lists the user
plane (UP) and control plan (CP) interfaces for the eUTRAN.

A.4.1
RRC
The Radio Resource Control (RRC) protocol speciﬁcation is speciﬁed in TS 36.331
[20] and covers the signaling functions between the UE and the eNbs.
The most important functions encapsulated in the RRC protocol is the broadcast of
system information to UEs and to control RRC connections. In LTE an UE can be
in two diﬀerent modes: idle or connected mode. An UE can only have an active data
transfer with the network once it is in connected mode.
A connection is initiated
through use of a random access channel in UL and once granted by the network an
UE can switch from idle to connected and be allocated resources. An UE can roam
in a cell in idle mode and listen to broadcast information sent with the RRC protocol.
Paging is also carried out with the RRC protocol when a network wishes to establish
a connection to an idle UE.
A.4.2
PDCP
The Packet Data Convergence Protocol (PDCP) sublayer is speciﬁed in TS 36.323 [21]
and covers a series of functions that performs the convergence from the IP domain to
the LTE air interface domain.
The PDCP sublayer is responsible for ciphering user and signaling traﬃc over the air
interface. Several ciphering algorithms have been deﬁned for LTE and it is chosen
during the bearer establishment procedure where a RRC connection is established.
PDCP also does IP header compression (conﬁgurable) to minimize the bandwidth
used by the IP layer, and it transfers relevant control data during handovers to the
other eNbs.
One PDCP entity is associated with one radio bearer and one PDCP is associated with
one or two (one for each direction UL, DL) Radio Link Control (RLC) entity depending
on the RB characteristics and RLC mode. The PDCP sublayer is conﬁgured by the
RRC sublayer.
A.4.3
RLC
The Radio Link Control (RLC) protocol is speciﬁed in TS 36.322 [22]. It speciﬁes
how to control the radio link between the eNb and the UE. An RLC entity exchanges
RLC Service Data Units (SDUs) with the PDCP sublayer and RLC Packet Data Units
(PDUs) with its peer RLC entity through lower layers (MAC and PHY).
Three RLC modes have been deﬁned for the RLC data transfer; Transparent Mode
(TM), Unacknowledged Mode (UM) and Acknowledged Mode (AM). The TM and UM
RLC can be either a transmitting (Tx) RLC or a receiving (Rx) RLC while an AM
RLC is covering both. Only AM RLC supports retransmissions of RLC PDUs with the
use of an Automatic Repeat reQuest (ARQ) scheme. The Rx AM RLC shall detect
the order of the received RLC PDUs and if a PDU is missing an ARQ is issued to the
peer AM RLC.
==PAGE:160==
136
A The LTE system - Background theory
A.4.4
MAC
The Medium Access Control (MAC) protocol is speciﬁed in TS 36.321 [23] and handles
all the deﬁned transport channels for both UL and DL. The functionality performed
by a MAC entity is diﬀerent from a MAC eNb entity to a MAC UE entity.
A MAC entity receives/delivers MAC SDUs from/to the RLC sublayer and sends/re-
ceive MAC PDUs to/from its peer MAC entity through lower layers (PHY).
To higher layers MAC oﬀers data transfer and radio resource allocation services while
it expects from lower layers; signaling of Hybrid ARQ (HARQ) feedback, signaling of
Scheduling Request and measurements (e.g. Channel Quality Indicator CQI) together
with data transfer services. The data transfer to/from lower layers are carried out
with the use of deﬁned transport channels.
The MAC sublayer supports several functionalities including:
• Mapping between logical and transport channels (both UL and DL)
• Multiplexing and de-multiplexing of MAC SDUs
• Scheduling information reporting
• Error-correction using HARQ retransmissions
• Priority handling between UEs using dynamic scheduling
The received MAC SDUs from higher levels are multiplexed onto Transport Blocks
(TBs) sent over the transport channels while it uses logical channels for data transfer
to higher layers. Mapping from logical layers to transport layers are performed in the
MAC sublayer depending on the multiplexing used (conﬁgured by the RRC).
The MAC sublayer is responsible for HARQ packet retransmission functionality and
the MAC header inserted during encapsulation contains ﬁelds for the addressing of
individual UEs, for bandwidth request (UL) and grants (DL) functionalities and for
power management and timing advance control.
HARQ
A HARQ entity inside the MAC sublayer controls the HARQ processes. Several HARQ
processes can run in parallel each associated with a unique HARQ process identiﬁer.
This reduces data transmission blocking on an active UE to base station connection.
The HARQ entity expects an acknowledgment (ACK) from the UE if the TB has been
decoded correctly and a negative-acknowledgment (NACK) otherwise.
If an ACK
is received the TB is removed from the transmission buﬀer inside the HARQ entity
allowing transmission of other TBs. If a NACK is received the TB is retransmitted
and additional redundancy can be added to the block if required. The redundancy

ﬁrst row of REs in DL subframe 5 is 3 ms. During these 3 ms all transmission and
reception processing must be carried out in the network including all LTE PHY and
MAC, fronthaul transportation and RF processing.
The number of retransmission of a TB depends on the network, when all attempts have
been used the TB can be discarded and the correction and detection of the discarded
block is left for higher layers to detect. The HARQ entity must be able to process
up to 8 HARQ processes in parallel to utilize the time between the acknowledgments.
All details on the HARQ procedure, i.e. coding and modulation scheme, transmission
type (new or retransmission) and HARQ process number is signaled when the scheduler
grants access to the user.

Code block segmentation
The code block segmentation will segment the input IBs to CBs to a maximum size
of 6144 bit per block. If the IBs are larger than this size segmentation is performed
and an additional CRC of length 24 bit is appended to each of the segmented blocks.
If an IB is less than 40 bit ﬁller bits are added to the front of the block to reach the
minimum block size. The procedure is described in section 5.1.2 in TS 36.212.
At the receiver the IBs are reassembled from the decoded CBs.
Channel coding
The channel coding block contains functions for adding redundancy to data for error
correcting purposes. The channel coding scheme used depends on the channel type
that is encoded. A series of diﬀerent channel encoders have been deﬁned including
(the complete list can be found in Tables 5.1.3-1 and 5.1.3-2 in [24]):
• Tail biting convolutional code: used for Broadcast Channel (BCH) and some
control information
• Turbo code: Used for most of the transport channels e.g. Uplink Shared Channel
(UL-SCH) and Downlink Shared Channel (DL-SCH)
• Repetition code: Used only for the HARQ Indicator (HI) control information
• Block code: Used for the Control Format Indicator (CFI) control information
and for some of the Uplink Control Information (UCI)
The diﬀerent channel codes varies in code rate, complexity and robustness from
the eﬃcient but rather complex turbo codes used for coding the user data contained
in the DL-SCH and UL-SCH with a code rate of 1/3 to the more simple but robust
block codes used for the CFI with a code rate of 1/16.
The reason why several channel coding schemes have been deﬁned is that the priority
of the information contained in the physical channels are diﬀerent. Some control in-
formation like the CFI is extremely important since it indicates to the receiving UEs
how many of the symbols in a subframe are reserved for control data. If the CFI
is not decoded correctly by the UEs the entire frame structure becomes invalid and
therefore a channel code with rate 1/16 is used. However for the user data if a channel
decoding is failed, indicated by the CRC, a retransmission can take place. Therefore
==PAGE:164==
140
A The LTE system - Background theory
a turbo code which has a very good code rate and performance is used to maximize
throughput.
Only the encoding used for the diﬀerent kind of transport channels and control infor-
mation is speciﬁed in the standard, the channel decoding is implementation speciﬁc
and is thus not mentioned.
Rate matching
In the rate matching block each of the sub-blocks produced by the encoders are ﬁrst
interleaved and then they are serialized using a circular buﬀer. Finally the serialized bit
stream is rate matched so it matches the requirements and capabilities of the receiving
UE. Rate matching in accordance with section 5.1.4 in TS 36.212 and is required since
diﬀerent UE categories with diﬀerent receiving and decoding capabilities exists.
Channel interleaver
Finally a channel interleaver is used to combine the encoded control information and
CBs into a single stream of codewords passed on to the next sublayer within the LTE
PHY.

it is one of the ﬁrst steps in distributing the data to the diﬀerent antenna ports.
After layer mapping the generated layers are precoded which generates the data
that will be mapped to each antenna port. Precoding is used in LTE to enhance the
transmission and reception when using multiple antennas. The eﬀect of precoding is
that the generated layers are combined using a precoding matrix from a codebook of
matrices. Diﬀerent matrices are deﬁned depending on the number of layers and antenna
ports used. The receiving part, i.e. the UEs, can for some transmission modes control
the use of precoding matrices by providing the base station with channel state reports.
The number of layers are not necessarily equal to the number of antenna ports used.
However the number of layers cannot exceed the number of antenna ports, but the data
from the same layer can be precoded out to two (2) antenna ports. This transmission
mode is known as transmit diversity (TxDiversity), and is in general used to strengthen
the quality of the signal since the same data in some sense is transmitted on both
antenna ports.
MIMO is then the concept of mapping multiple layers to multiple
antenna ports which increases the overall throughput to the MIMO users.
The antenna port layers are then mixed with physical signals for the respective
antenna port and then mapped onto physical resources by a resource element mapper.
There will then naturally be a resource element mapper for each antenna port used.
Finally the OFDMA signal is generated from the resource mapped antenna layers using
the principle described in section 3.2. The signal generation also includes the insertion
of CP.

APPENDIX C
Fronthaul networking
The introduction of C-RAN and centralization of baseband processing in order to
achieve a more energy-eﬃcient utilization of base station processing resources have
given birth to the concepts of fronthaul and fronthaul networks. Since the fronthaul
is responsible for carrying time sensitive radio data much attention has been on how
to perform the encapsulation and transportation of the data in order to meet strict
timing requirements.
For LTE networks the strict timing requirements originates from the required HARQ
response time which limits the overall delay imposed by the entire RAN network to 3
ms. This includes both baseband processing, RF processing and fronthaul transporta-
tion. In addition to the 3 ms maximum delay requirement also the amount of jitter
introduced by the fronthaul network must be kept low in order ensure the required
tight synchronization between the diﬀerent RRHs connected to the centralized unit.
The following sections will give a brief overview of some of the most common
and promising technologies and protocols that are used and/or proposed for fronthaul
networking.
C.1
CPRI
Common Public Radio Interface (CPRI) is the currently most common and used fron-
thaul interface protocol used for C-RAN deployments. The CPRI link rate is constant
irrespective of the active user traﬃc and the intention with CPRI is to connect the
BBU and the RRH with a bi-directional point-to-point topology supporting distances
up to 20 km with a maximum transmission delay of 200 µs. CPRI encapsulates mod-
ulated radio IQ data into CPRI frames together with synchronization and Operation,
Administration and Management (OAM) information. The CPRI protocol speciﬁca-
tion speciﬁes both L2 and L1 functionalities and requires a dedicated transmission link
for the connection [7].
CPRI suﬀers from poor scalability and an ineﬃcient use of transmission resources
which hinders large scale deployments of C-RAN using CPRI. The poor scalability
comes from the constant bit rate independent of active user traﬃc while the use of
dedicated transmission links makes the deployment of CPRI quite costly.

150

C.2
Radio over Ethernet
When looking for a suitable transportation technology for transporting radio data in
next-generation fronthaul networks focus was immediately shifted towards Ethernet.
Ethernet was introduced by the IEEE 802.3 working group in 1973 and commercially
oﬀers, at the time of writing, up to 100 Gbps connections over optical ﬁbers (802.3bm)
with 400 Gpbs being standardized (802.3bs). Ethernet is thus a mature technology for
packet-based transportation and has demonstrated cost eﬃcient speed and capacity
growth throughout its lifetime, making it a dominant and widely used technology.
For these reasons attention in the industry and within IEEE has been on how to
encapsulate radio data within Ethernet frames in order to enable transportation on
existing Ethernet core networks. This has lead to the creation of the task force within
the IEEE 1904 Access Networks Working Group called ”IEEE 1904.3 Standard for
Radio Over Ethernet (RoE) Encapsulations and Mappings1”. The scope of the task
force is the following [26]:
• Encapsulation of digital radio IQ data and control data into the Ethernet frame
payload ﬁeld.
• A header format for both structure-aware and structure-agnostic encapsulation of
existing digital radio transport formats. The structure-aware encapsulation has
detailed knowledge of the digital radio transport format content. The structure-
agnostic encapsulation is merely a container for the encapsulated digital radio
transport frames.
• A structure-aware mapper for CPRI frames to/from Ethernet encapsulated frames.
As noted a special focus within the task force is on how to map CPRI frames and
CPRI IQ payload into RoE frames. This is important since it allows for an easier
evolution from currently deployed CPRI-based fronthaul solutions into a Ethernet
based fronthaul by the use of a structure-aware mapper.
While IEEE 1914 NGFI conducts its work on standardizing a packet-based fronthaul
and analyzing RRH-BBU functional partitioning splits new RoE encapsulations and
mappings will be deﬁned in IEEE 1904.3 in parallel and in close collaboration with
IEEE 1914.

to an exclusive-or operation where 1 + 1 = 0, making a hardware implementation of
such a sequence generator very simple.
D.2
Gold-sequences
Gold-sequences are a class of sequence that have good periodic cross-correlation prop-
erties. The sequences are named after Robert Gold who ﬁrst introduced them in [28].
Gold sequence are made from a pair of two m-sequences with period N = 2m −1 care-
fully chosen so they form a preferred pair. The Gold sequence is then created by XOR
the output of the m-sequences as seen on Figure D.2. To generate the next value the
m-sequences are shifted through a single time and the result is XOR again.
A Gold-sequence has a bounded cross-correlation between any other Gold-sequence
or a shifted version of itself which is a useful property in many communication systems.

Zadoﬀ-Chu sequences is the common name for complex sequences with good periodic
correlation properties ﬁrst characterized by Frank, Zadoﬀand Heimiller in 1962 in
[29] and later by Chu in 1972 in [30]. Apart from having good periodic correlation
properties the sequences are also known for, when applied to radio signals, to give rise
to a constant magnitude signal.

generated and are known at the receiver, which is a useful property when performing
e.g. channel estimation. Furthermore the constant magnitude can be used for easier
estimation of the magnitude impact of the channel on the received signal.

APPENDIX F
FFT configuration
In order to improve the performance of the implemented FFT/IFFT module it is chosen
to use a larger I and Q sample width size of 16 bits internally in the core compared
to the smaller 8-16 bit per I/Q delivered as data input to the L1-oﬄoad system. By
using a larger internal register size for the I and Q data registers the dynamic range
of the signal is expanded from a range covering [2−9 : 29 −1] up to [2−15 : 215 −1].
The increased dynamic range makes the FFT computations more precise and limits
the quantization error made when rounding and scaling at the end of each butterﬂy
calculation.
To illustrate the eﬀect of using a higher internal data register width the implemented
FFT/IFFT module was simulated in ModelSim using diﬀerent register sizes.
The
following 3 cases were tested:
• Test Case 1: Internal register width: 10 bits, Output sample width: 10 bits
• Test Case 2: Internal register width: 16 bits, Output sample width: 10 bits
• Test Case 3: Internal register width: 16 bits, Output sample width: 16 bits
For all cases the input to the IFFT was set to 10 bits per I/Q. In order to match
the increased data width in test case 2 and 3 the input samples were bit-shifted 6
times to the left, and similarly the output for test case 3 was bit shifted 6 times to the
right to match the expected output width. Figure F.1 shows the result for the 3 cases,
here the output of the IFFT is collected and passed through a FFT in Matlab in or-
der to see the eﬀect on the constellation diagram for an input modulated with 16QAM.
The simulation clearly shows the eﬀect of using an increased internal data register
width compared to just using the same as the input sample width. The small dynamic
range when only using 10 bit internal registers results in increased noise as seen on
the constellation diagrams. A much better performance, in terms of reduced noise, is
achieved when using 16 bit internal registers and then removing LSBs on the output
to get to the 10 bit target output I/Q sample width.
The noise added on the OFDM-baseband signal by the L1-oﬄoading core should ob-
viously be kept low since this in the end will lead to a channel decoding performance

G.1
Platform setup
The evaluation board used for the PoC platform is a Xilinx VC709 featuring a Virtex-7

daughter board to the motherboard.
The PoC hardware platform setup can be seen in Figure G.1 together with the used
FPGA clocking architecture. The blocks with black frames on the FPGA have been

frame have been generated from the Xilinx IP catalog to accelerate platform estab-

transceivers signiﬁcantly. In order to create a fronthaul transmission link a back-to-

the evaluation board. Since only DL is showcased on the platform the SFPs will only
be operating in single mode, i.e. one of them will be the transmitter and the other
the receiver. The Ethernet MAC used for the transmission link will be the developed
TSN MACs while the Ethernet Physical Coding Sublayer (PCS) will be a 10G PCS
(10GBASE-R) generated from the Xilinx IP catalog.

G.2
AD9361
The AD9361 chip is used for realizing the RF processing required for the hardware
platform. The functionality mainly used on the chip are up-conversion to a carrier
frequency together with digital to analog conversion. In the following a functional
overview of the AD9361 chip is given together with the timing of the data interface
that is used to deliver Tx samples to the chip.
The AD9361 is a RF agile transceiver with the functional block diagram as shown
in Figure G.2 [31].
The chip features 2 Rx and 2 Tx chains each having its own dedicated digital and
analog processing chain. Each chain uses complex IQ data format on the digital data
interface and performs the mixing of the two internally. The mixing and up-conversion
is carried out in the analog domain, the carrier frequency is generated by a Local
Oscillator (LO) which is mixed on to the In-phase and Quadrature-phase components.
The frequency generated by the LO can be controlled through the control interface.
On the digital side each Tx chain consists of a series of concatenated Finite Im-
pulse Response (FIR) ﬁlters used for up-sampling the input IQ data stream to the set
sampling frequency of the DAC. Figure G.3 shows the digital ﬁlter structure for a Tx
chain.
