Abstract
The increase in the percentage of power generated from renewable sources especially from
wind power in the power system and the high number of High Voltage DC (HVDC) trans-
mission lines between interconnected synchronous areas are considered the main eﬀects for
decreasing the inertia of the power system. This decrease have a direct eﬀect on the behavior
of the system during frequency disturbances which cause the frequency to drop under the
minimum limits and thus activating the breakers in the system resulting in load shedding
and in worst cases blackouts.
Accordingly, the Transmission system operators (TSOs) are now imposing requirements on
the wind power plants to contribute during the frequency disturbance and provide synthetic
inertia.This support scheme has many forms and it can be supplied by wind turbines in the
form of fast frequency reserve (FFR) that acts as an initial fast power injection in the system
that has a direct eﬀect on the initial rate of change of the frequency and thus aﬀecting the
instantaneous minimum value of the frequency.
This thesis works was done via a detailed model of the Nordic Synchronous Power System
keeping attention to the frequency reserves requirements and limits. Also a model for the
wind turbine including converters, active power and DC voltage controls.
Afterwards a
model for the frequency control was developed for the FFR support from the wind turbine
to the power system in the case of frequency disturbance. An aggregated model of wind
farms connected to the power system is developed and connected to the power system. The
models are developed in MATLAB/SIMULINK, where each model was tested and veriﬁed.
Following the developing of the models, test cases are conducted for diﬀerent inertia levels
in the power system to represent future inertia forecast reports along with the eﬀects of
the diﬀerent gains in the FFR controller and its eﬀect on the system frequency. The case of
occurrence of the second dip in frequency after the FFR support is observed and a mitigation
solution via diﬀerent delays from wind power plants (WPPs) contributing in the support is
applied and its eﬀect on the frequency response and the second dip is discussed.
The results showed that the high gains and diﬀerent delay times aﬀect mainly the ﬁrst nadir
of the frequency with a little eﬀect on the second dip due to the high KE supplied by the
wind turbines (WTs) for a long period. The low gain values had a negative eﬀect on the
ﬁrst nadir but a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the second dip and by applying a smaller gain and a
small delay the second dip was eliminated and a frequency within the limits of operation was
achieved. This mitigation solution had a positive eﬀect on the frequency response at diﬀerent
inertia levels of the power system and also at diﬀerent power disturbance occasions.

Chapter 1

Introduction
In recent years, power systems all over the world and especially in the Nordic Synchronous
power system area are experiencing big changes in the nature of power generation and
transmission. This is due to the high penetration level of renewable sources into the power
systems and the increasing number of high voltage DC (HVDC) transmission lines that are
being used to interconnect diﬀerent synchronous areas. An example of such big share of
renewable sources in generation was shown in renewable energy policy network for the 21st
century [1] as illustrated in Figure 1 which shows the total share of renewable sources in
electricity generation for the top 10 countries in 2017. In ﬁrst place comes Denmark with a
percentage of 55% of electricity production from renewable sources consisting of almost 53%
wind power.
Figure 1: Generation from renewable sources for top 10 countries in 2017 [1]
According to [2], in order to maintain an operational security for the frequency in the system,
the frequency should be within predeﬁned limits and it mustn’t deviate too far from these
limits. If the frequency deviates too much, protection devices in the system are activated
in order to protect the machines and keep the power system in operation. The deviation
can be large when a large production unit is disconnected or some big transmission links are
disconnected. When this occurs, balancing between generation and consumption in active
power is disturbed.
This imbalances results in the drop of frequency in the system.
If
1
==PAGE:12==
this drop reaches an unacceptable level, it results in more generation units and loads being
disconnected in a cascade eﬀect that can lead to a blackout in the power system.
The synchronous generators in the power system are the main responsible in resisting changes
that occur in the frequency.
This resistance comes from the fact that these generators
are synchronously rotating machines connected to the power system and thus have stored
kinetic energy in their rotating masses that are released to the power system as active
power to the system when frequency deviations occur.
This support of active power at
disturbances is deﬁned as reserves in the system that is released when needed. Typically,
the frequency control reserves includes 3 diﬀerent control actions: primary, secondary and
tertiary control [3].
Therefore, more renewable sources connected to the system that replaces these synchronous
generators, the less is the resistance to frequency changes. The new technology in the wind
and solar power plants includes power electronics frequency converters. These converters
are controlled to decouple the frequency at the sides of generation from the power system
frequency allowing a wider range of operation at diﬀerent wind speeds. The power system
inertia is deﬁned as the ability of the power system to oppose the changes in the frequency
of the system due to the resistance supplied by the rotating masses in the system. Thus
inertia is directly proportional to the amount of kinetic energy stored in the rotating masses
in the system in form of generators or motor loads [4]. Based on these decoupling eﬀects
from the renewable sources and replacing the conventional power plants with renewable
sources, transmission systems operators (TSO) are currently imposing new requirements
on these renewable sources to contribute during frequency disturbances by supplying fast
active power injections emulating synthetic inertia behaviour to help keeping the frequency
within the acceptable limits. In [5] synthetic inertia is deﬁned as a controlled contribution of
electrical torque from a unit that is proportional to the rate of change of frequency measured
at the terminal of the unit. These new requirements that come in form of fast reserves are
going to be a complement and not a replacement for the existing frequency control measures
taking place at frequency disturbances. This thesis focuses on investigating the eﬀects of the
increased number of the wind power in the Nordic power system and its eﬀect on the future
inertia behaviour during the frequency disturbances.
In addition, the new requirements
imposed by the TSOs mentioned before which comes in the form of fast frequency response
(FFR) from wind turbines and its control gains eﬀect on the system are investigated. Wind
turbines, aggregated wind farms, the Nordic power system and FFR controller are modelled
in MATLAB/SIMULINK. The results are investigated and analyzed to show the eﬀect on
the diﬀerent frequency control strategies and approaches by the added fast support reserve
from the wind turbines. Also the eﬀect of this FFR can cause a second dip in frequency
during energy recovery periods.
Accordingly an approach for mitigating this problem is
modeled and tested in this thesis aiming to reduce its eﬀect or eliminate it.
1.1
Motivation and problem deﬁnition
The frequency in the power system is deﬁned as a balance between generation and consump-
tion in electrical power. Therefore the frequency is determined based on the rotational speed
of the synchronous generators and it is deﬁned as 50 Hz in the Nordic synchronous area. For
a ﬁxed frequency in the system, the generation must equal to the consumption required. Any

Since the inertia is a constant value based on the generator mass and size, for a given

systems mentioned in [2, 5]. It can clearly be concluded that the high penetration level of
the inverter-based generation sources such as that in wind turbines which gives a bigger range
of operation over a big range of wind speeds and also used in solar power will decouple the
frequency of the system from the rotational speed of the turbine. When these technologies
replace the conventional units, it will reduce the inertia of the system at disturbances.
This new situation of more renewable resources and less conventional thermal and hydro
generation units introduced the need for a new solution to counter this decrease in the
inertia of the system that will increase over time according to forecast studies carried out
in [2] and [5] reaching minimum levels of 120 GWs and 134 GWs in 2020 and 2025 respectively.
These values decrease to a minimum value of around 55 GWs for about 4800 hours in 2040 [6].
FFR is one of the several mitigation solutions to the problem of the decreasing inertia level
introduced by the Regional Group Nordic based on previous analysis done by the Nordic
Analysis Group (NAG) [4]. It should be kept in mind that in the Nordic synchronous area the
main concern is the instantaneous minimum frequency value and not the RoCof [6]. FFR
acts as a fast active power injection supplied as a complement to the primary frequency
control and not as a replacement.
According to [5], FFR is deﬁned as "system service that delivers a fast power change to mit-
igate the eﬀect of reduced inertial response, so that frequency stability can be maintained".
Thus, emulation of inertial behaviour from wind turbines is needed via a controlled gain
proportional to the rate of change of frequency and its equivalent inertial constant to give an
additional power boost added to the produced wind turbine power according to the following

1.2
Objectives
This thesis’ objectives are to investigate and analyze the eﬀects of the added fast frequency
response from the wind turbines to the Nordic power system and how it performs along
the existing frequency control measures during the reference disturbance incident deﬁned by
the TSOs in the Nordic synchronous area. This analysis also takes into consideration the
future conditions of the Nordic system by using diﬀerent values for system inertia that are
forecasted in the near and far future along with some worst case values for inertia. Also, it
analyzes a mitigation measure for the problem of the 2nd frequency dip that occurs in the
system during the recovery period of the wind turbines after supporting power during the
incident at the worst possible values of inertia. To be able to do such analysis and tests, the
following tasks were targeted throughout the period of the thesis work:
1. Overview and understand of the state of the art concerning the future inertia and its
behavior at disturbances in the Nordic power system. Mainly due to the replacement
of the conventional units by renewable sources. Moreover, forming an understanding
about the state of the art and the technical requirements of the new fast frequency
reserve (FFR) imposed by the Nordic TSOs.
2. Modelling of the wind turbine and its controls including machine side converter (MSC),
grid side converter (GSC) and aggregated wind farms model
3. Modelling of the Nordic synchronous area power system
4. Validating the individual models via test cases and integrating them to have a complete
system.
5. Developing the fast frequency reserve (FFR) control and tuning it in the wind turbine
model
6. Performance analyses of the Nordic power system without FFR at diﬀerent future
inertia levels
7. Performance analyses of the Nordic power system with diﬀerent FFR gain values at
future inertia value

Assumptions and Limitations
The list below includes the assumptions that were considered during the thesis work:
1. The wind turbine operates at a constant wind speed throughout the whole simulation
time which is based on the fact that the scope of the investigation is short in time
which is in range of seconds up to 1 minute. In reality the speed of the wind changes
due to ﬂuctuations and gusts but they are considered small in the range of 1 minute
which is the main concern during the frequency disturbance and the support needed
from the turbine during that time.
2. The wind farms are developed as an aggregated connection of several wind turbines
and all operating at a constant wind speed.
3. Grid voltage in the study at the terminals of the turbine is considered constant and
accordingly the reactive power set point for the MSC is considered zero. As the fo-
cus of the thesis is investigating behaviour and support for frequency response, this
assumption was made to focus on the main objective.
4. The Nordic Powers system model is implemented using the single bus (copper-plate)
approach. This approach ignores the tie-lines between control areas.
5. Power system inertia is considered constant during each simulation time and only
includes thermal and hydro generation units.
6. The provided power system assumes that frequency support comes from thermal and
hydro power plants only before introducing the wind power under investigation.
7. Manual Frequency Restoration Reserve (m-FRR) is not modelled and activated man-
ually by the TSO.

2.1
Wind Turbines
In the last years, the high demand on switching to more clean sustainable renewable energy
sources and replacing the conventional power stations as thermal and nuclear power plants
has lead to a development rate in the wind turbines. The diﬀerent types of wind turbines is
categorized according to their speed and power control technologies [7].
Wind turbines are classiﬁed into ﬁxed speed and variable speed. The ability of variable
speed generators to change the rotational speed and operate over a big range of wind speeds
happens due to the usage of power electronics converters. The big range of speeds of op-
eration has given the beneﬁts of maximizing the aerodynamic eﬃciency over a big range of
wind speeds. Therefore, the variable speed wind turbine was used in this thesis work. A
comparison between the ﬁxed and variable speed turbines is presented in [8].
From [7], the most common WT concepts are :
• Fixed speed concept (Type 1)
• Variable speed win turbines (VSWT) with rotor resistance (Type 2)
• VSWT with partial scale frequency converter (Type 3)
• VSWT with full scale frequency converter (Type 4)
In the past years type 3 was the most dominant type, but in large scale wind power plants
(WPPs) type 4 is becoming more popular [7]. Since type 1 and 2 WT are directly connected
to the grid, they inherit an inertial response to the system. However the range of operation
over big range of wind speeds is not big which limits the capability of extracting power
from the turbine. Moreover, no power electronics frequency are used in these types thus the
inertial response is not controllable. Therefore those 2 types rarely contribute to the system
synthetic inertia. On the other hand, in types 3 and 4 with the help of pitch control of the
turbine blades plus the power electronics converters have introduced controllable behaviour
during normal operation by supplying constant amount of power at maximum aerodynamic
eﬃciency and ability of supplying synthetic inertia during frequency disturbances.
The
ability to supply synthetic inertia comes from the decoupling of the WT from the grid by
the power electronics converters. Therefore the kinetic energy is stored in the rotor is isolated
from the grid. But the power converters can be controlled to release the kinetic energy when
needed so that the WT can contribute during disturbances [7] [8].

2.2
Nordic Power system
In the past the Nordic power system was to a high level similar to the other power systems
around the world in terms of technologies of control and generation. But during the 1950’s
and 1960’s Hydro power plants were built followed by building Nuclear power plants in the
1970’s and 1980’s.
In 1980’s, digital PI controllers were used instead of the mechanical
governors. The 1990’s introduced the separation of electricity transmission and production
resulting in the market deregulation [3]. These reasons resulted in the major developments
in the Nordic power system which made it diﬀerent from the other power systems around
the world [11].
Due to the market deregulation, the frequency in the Nordic synchronous power system has
worsen resulting in changing the regulations for the frequency normal band to be between
9
==PAGE:20==
49.9 Hz and 50.1 Hz over an average of 100 minutes in 1995 and increased the average time
in 2011 to 1100 minutes [12]. This is due to hourly blocks design for selling and buying
the electricity thus after clearing the market, production will follow load changes each hour.
This can introduce sudden changes during the cases of low or high power diﬀerence at the
hourly intervals leading to large power imbalances [11].
Moreover, the development and integration of more Renewable Energy Resources has aﬀected
the market in addition to the new technologies and methods used in planning and operation
of the electricity market whose eﬀects would be more noticeable in the future. As in the
Nordic area the wind power will be tripled between 2010 and 2025. Also the interconnections
would increase by 50% by 2025. Keeping in mind that the common parameter within the
whole Nordic power system is the frequency, this means that any imbalance in any part of
the system will aﬀect the rest and reﬂects the power exchange between the interconnectors.
A fact as this shows that dimensioning and design of the reserves is highly dependant on the
frequency quality in the system which is directly coupled with the security of the supply. As
it was mentioned in Chapter 1 and shown in Figure 1, the wind power reached almost 53%
in Denmark and will increase in the future in the Nordic power system to more than 50% in
2025 [3]. In 2017, the existing interconnectors in the Nordic power system were 9740 MW
with 3900 MW under construction that would be ﬁnished by 2021 and 8000 MW planned
for 2025 [13].
According to the new Nordic power system operation regulations in 2019 [14], the frequency
operation limits are shown if Figure 4 below. Normal operation values for frequency is in

is activated after a certain time depending on the deviation value. However, after the range
of 49.5 Hz or 50.5 Hz an emergency state is active and measures such as load shedding can
be activated to protect the system.

2.3.4
Manual frequency restoration reserve (m-FRR)
The main source of frequency restoration reserve in the Nordic power system is the m-FRR.
A reason for this comes from the limited value of the a-FRR and the common congestion on
the transmission lines. In the regulation power market m-FRR is required to be activated
within 15 minutes. A fact that must be taken into consideration is the diﬀerent generation
sources in the system which have diﬀerent response times. Therefore, the activation time,
which is the total time between receiving the activation command to delivering the power,
should be taken into account. Ideally, a-FRR should restore the frequency to 50 Hz within 15
minutes [3]. As a result, m-FRR includes many diﬀerent forms such as voluntary bids in the
regulating power market, peak load reserves and capacity reserves payed by the TSOs. Peak
load reserves are put in the Nordic operational information system while capacity reserves
are there to be a security for the state of suﬃcient reserves in case of disturbances. Moreover,
m-FRR can be exchangeable between diﬀerent synchronous areas [15].
2.3.5
Additional measures in frequency control
Load shedding is an option for stopping any further decrease in frequency below 49.5 Hz
in case of insuﬃcient FCR-D reserves. Another special measure is the usage of the HVDC
tie lines that can supply emergency power. Additionally, disconnection of transmission lines
and generation start up to increase generation in the system are options to achieve frequency
control [20].

2.4
Inertia in Nordic Power system and Fast frequency reserves
(FFR)
As stated earlier, the power systems around the world are going through many structural
changes which introduces new challenges for the operation and control of the system. The
main concern is what is called the inertia problem. This problem cause is not only the
low inertia value due to the replacement of conventional thermal and hydro units by more
renewable sources but also it has other factors as momentary power imbalance and activation
reserves speed [6].
A combination of these factors can challenge the frequency stability
margins and control based on the fact that they aﬀect the initial rate of change of frequency
(RoCoF) or the maximum instantaneous frequency deviation which was discussed thoroughly
in [5] and [6]. The challenges varies signiﬁcantly depending on the power system nature.
In the Nordic synchronous area the frequency stability is referred to the ability to maintain
the frequency above the 49 Hz limit to avoid load shedding for the reference DI. Currently
the primary frequency reserves in the Nordic synchronous area don’t guarantee frequency
stability at the low inertia levels given the reference DI [6].
2.4.1
Future inertia in the Nordic power system
To accurately study the frequency related issues in the power system, knowledge about the
amount of kinetic energy in the system is a necessity to determine the inertia value. In [2]
and [5] the Nordic analysis group has started a project named "Future system inertia" in
2013. This projects tasks were to establish a systematic process to study the inertia and
frequency disturbances, harmonising inertia calculation method, implementation of inertia
real-time estimation and study on impact of future production and consumption changes on
inertia [2]. For the scope of the thesis, the main outputs to be used from this study are the
future values of the inertia and worst case values in the near and far future to be used in the
investigation process carried out.
The estimation of the kinetic energy in the system were done using an empirical analysis
by looking into historical disturbances. The change in power for theses disturbances and
its eﬀect on the change in frequency was compared and a relation was to be found between
them. This relation will then be used for estimating the maximum instantaneous frequency
deviation [5].
This empirical study is carried out by studying the simulations to better
understand the factors that aﬀect the inertia estimation, the models used are based on
previous studies and work done and developed over the three last decades by the Nordic
TSOs and was used for the grid and operation planning purposes [2].
Six diﬀerent simulation cases were created to study the frequency behaviour in diﬀerent
operational scenarios anlong with diﬀerent frequency disturbances in each simulation. The
disturbances could either be tripping of large generators or HVDC links connected to other
synchronous areas [2]. The conclusion conducted from these cases was that the estimation
of inertia during the disturbance is proved hard due to the operational scenarios and fault
locations. Therefore, this method for inertia estimation using only frequency measurement
was proved unreliable. An alternative to this method was found to be an implementation

of inertia online estimation based on the fact that TSOs have information about production
in each area and then combining the results from these diﬀerent countries to get an overall
estimation. Using the SCADA systems in each TSO in the Nordic countries, an estimation

showed that the inertia reduction results in a higher change in frequency that is not a linear

2.4.3
FFR deﬁnition and its technical requirements
FFR is considered the most promising mitigation solution for the low inertia scenarios as
fast active power response at low socio-economic costs [4]. FFR acts as a complement to
FCR-D and not as a replacement [21].
Fast frequency reserve is deﬁned as a system service that delivers a fast power change to
mitigate the eﬀect of reduced inertial support to maintain frequency stability [5]. So it is a
faster response than the FCR-D.
Reference [21] introduces the main technical requirements for the fast frequency reserve.
The resources to participate in FFR market must pass a pre-qualiﬁcation test deﬁned by the
TSOs. These sources should meet the main design FFR aspects which are:
• Full activation time (Faster activation time improves system frequency response)
• Activation frequency (activation at smaller frequency improves the frequency response
but increases activation occurrence)
• Duration (longer duration extracts more energy from the supplying sources)
• Deactivation (Combination of fast deactivation and short duration may cause a second
dip in the frequency which can be overcomed by extending duration time or smother
deactivation)

3.1.1
Pitch control
At the moment when the speed of the turbine and the output power reach their nominal
values the turbine starts to operate at the power limitation region. This output power limit
is controlled via pitching the blades of the turbine. The control system of this pitch system
can be observed in Figure 13 below. It can be seen that the pitching control system consists
of 2 main parts, a PI controller and an actuator. For the PI controller part, the error in
speed between the the generator nominal speed and the actual speed is passed to the PI
controller where the reference pitch angle is calculated. At low wind speeds the optimum
pitch angle for the provided MPPT table is zero and the optimum Cp depends only on the
optimum tip speed ratio λ which depends on the wind speed and rotor rotational speed itself.
On the other hand, at high wind speeds λ is not the optimum anymore, thus as wind speed
increases the rotational speed has already reached its nominal value. When this occurs, the
PI controller starts having a positive magnitude and therefore starts adjusting the pitch
angle to limit the power output and keep the rotational speed at its nominal value.
For the actuator part, the reference pitch angle is then compared to the actual pitch angle
of the turbine to account for any errors that might arise and then the actuator will correct
it. It is worth to consider here that some rate limitations, maximum and minimum limits
are accounted for in the model of the actuator to act as a realistic pitch servo.

3.1.4
MPPT lookup table
The MPPT table is used to give the optimum value for the generator active power with
respect to the rotational speed of the generator. MPPT tables diﬀer from one turbine to
another depending on the rating and capabilities of the turbine. For low wind speeds the
increase in power is small with the increase in the rotational speed. On the other hand, at
higher speeds the generator speed is ﬁxed at the rated value and thus the power is also the
rated power. In Figure 17 the control strategy shows that once the rated speed is reached,
a step in power reference is happening. However, for the implementation part, this sudden
change in the reference can cause simulation errors due to computational issues. For that
reason the increase in the power is done gradually with a very steep slope to avoid the

4.1
Future inertia simulations
This thesis objective that was deﬁned in Chapter 1 shows that the goal of the work done is to
analyze and investigate the diﬀerent eﬀects support from wind power at power disturbances
on the frequency response in the Nordic power system at futuristic conditions where they
replace conventional power plants. This mainly reﬂects on the decreasing value of the inertia
of the system year after year. Therefore the extra new requirements to support the existing
frequency control in form of FFR from diﬀerent sources. For this thesis, FFR control from
wind turbines is to be studied and tested in simulations to determine the eﬀects of this
added support. The main side eﬀect as speciﬁed is the 2nd dip in the frequency during the
energy recovery period of the WT. To test all these situations and eﬀects, the following main
approaches are to be followed and tested:
• Frequency response of the system at diﬀerent values of system inertia to represent
future cases without FFR
• Testing diﬀerent FFR gains eﬀects on one of the future values of the power system
inertia (with the worst frequency response)
• Validating that the measures and solutions chosen will work at diﬀerent cases of the
system other than the worst case scenario
After testing the diﬀerent FFR gain values, the 2nd dip of the frequency would appear.
Diﬀerent methodologies to mitigate the eﬀect this problem are mentioned in [4, 5]. One
of these methods is to ramp up the the FFR support from the turbines thus shaping the
activation ramp (time and value). This can be achieved by applying delays between the wind
farm contributing to the FFR support. Another eﬀect of this delays as well is decreasing the
high slope of deactivation which helps as well in decreasing the 2nd dip or eliminating it.
The WPPs to be tested are 500 turbines each rated at 2 MW, they were divided into 10
WPPs each consisting of 50 turbines. Fixed wind speed of 8 m/s is assumed for the study.
Diﬀerent test cases are then applied to mitigate the eﬀect of the 2nd dip and trying to achieve
the main goal of keeping the frequency over 49 Hz to avoid load shedding with a minimum
2nd dip (which shouldn’t have a lower value than the ﬁrst frequency nadir) or removing it
at all if possible.
Success criteria for these tests is that the frequency is over the 49 Hz limit deﬁned by the
TSOs. For the 2nd dip eﬀect, the success criteria is that its eﬀect and signiﬁcance is decreased
or removed while keeping a smooth frequency response without oscillations that my appear
when the dip occur.

5
Conclusion and future work
The investigations carried out in this thesis had the objectives of testing the eﬀect of the
fast frequency reserve from wind turbines in the Nordic power system and its eﬀect on the
existing primary frequency control measures at diﬀerent power disturbances. These tests
were done considering the future conditions of the Nordic power system where the inertia
value of the system decreases due to the high penetration percentage of the renewable sources
and replacing the conventional power plants. Moreover a special care was directed towards
the problem of the 2nd dip that appears in the frequency response during the energy recovery
period of the wind turbines after the FFR support. Based on models for the type 4 WT and
the Nordic power system developed before in previous theses and reports and provided by
Wind Energy Department at DTU, an integration between them to have a representation
of aggregated WPPs connected to the Nordic power system was achieved with some work
done in re-tuning the active power and DC voltage controllers in the WT model. Then a
FFR controller was developed and the test cases were carried out investigating the eﬀect
of diﬀerent FFR gains on the Nordic power system conditions in the future. A mitigation
solution for the 2nd dip was tested in form of delays applied between the WPPs and its
eﬀects were shown and discussed. The eﬀect of this mitigation solution was also tested for
the diﬀerent inertia values of the system other than the worst case scenarios and for diﬀerent
disturbances.
The ﬁrst test carried out was the eﬀect of the decreasing inertia level of the Nordic system
based on future inertia estimations done by the Nordic TSOs without any FFR support. The
test showed that the ability of the Nordic to keep the frequency over the minimum frequency
limit of 49 Hz (which is the limit before applying load shedding procedures) will decrease to
the point that in 2025 the frequency will barely cross the limit with a value of 49.03 Hz and
decreasing far below it at 48.76 Hz in 2040. Therefore a justiﬁcation for the need of a new
mitigation solution if form of FFR was shown.
A worst case scenario was chosen to carry further studies on it was selected at an inertia
value of 21 GWs. Diﬀerent FFR gain values increasing in magnitude were applied to the
system and the results showed a better behavior in the frequency nadir. However, the 2nd
dip started to appear at high gains due to the large KE extracted form the WTs. Therefore
this test case with the 2nd dip was selected for applying the selected solution for mitigating
the 2nd dip problem via applying delays.
The ﬁrst mitigation tests were carried out at a FFR gain of 15 with the same number of WTs
and same wind speed at inertia value of 21 GWs. 3 time delays of 1.3, 1, 0.5 seconds were
applied and tested based on the technical requirements from the FFR supplying units. The
high delay value of 1.3 seconds resulted in the frequency to fall below the 49 Hz limit but it
had a better eﬀect on the 2nd dip value. The 1 and 0.5 second delays had eﬀects on the ﬁrst
nadir magnitude and it was over the limit but since the gain was constant the eﬀect on the
2nd dip was negligible and had a similar behavior as the 1.3 seconds delay but better than
50
==PAGE:61==
the base case. However, oscillations appeared in the response and a non smooth frequency
response was observed.
The second approach was to try the same test at a more realistic inertia value representing
the far future cases. This approach goal was to try reaching a middle solution between
keeping the frequency ﬁrst nadir over the 49 Hz limit and decreasing the 2nd dip and having
a smoother frequency response without oscillations.
After applying the delays, a better
response was achieved at all the delays values. Some oscillations were observed at higher
delay time of 1.3 second but with a better magnitude of the nadirs values. The other 2
delays values gave a much smoother response and an acceptable frequency levels.
The frequency response for the case of the futuristic inertia value was accepted and followed
the success criteria for all delays values. However a small oscillations was observed for the
longest delay but not as signiﬁcant as the ﬁrst test case. For the worst case inertia level
the only acceptable response was the one for the very fast response (small delay). As the
longer delays values made the frequency nadir go outside the limits and high oscillations
were present in the response.
Testing the mitigation solution of delay 1 seconds and gain of 15 at diﬀerent inertia levels
other than the worst case value for the inertia and diﬀerent disturbance values showed an
improvement in the frequency response. This improvement was more obvious in the low
inertia conditions.
A conclusion derived from these tests is that the high values of FFR gains can results in
an extra injection of KE in the system than needed. This would cause an overload in the
supplied power from the wind turbines extracting a high percentage of KE that results in a
2nd dip with diﬀerent magnitudes during the recovery periods. Given that the high gain has
a positive eﬀect on the ﬁrst nadir magnitude. Moreover, applying delays at high gains at
extremely low inertia level mainly aﬀect the ﬁrst nadir magnitude and not the 2nd dip due
to the constant KE injected to the system. Alternatively, at larger inertia level where the
WTs are not overloaded for a long time has a bigger eﬀect on the 2nd dip and the ﬁrst nadir
magnitude. Oscillations tend to appear in the frequency response at very low inertia levels
and high gains and its severance tends to decrease as the inertia value increases and at faster
response. Tuning the delay values and the FFR gain proves to be a complicated operation
to reach the main goal of keeping the frequency over the limit and eliminating the 2nd dip
without injecting extra or low KE to the system. But an overall statement would be that the
need of FFR to support the existing FCR-D during disturbances given the future conditions
of the Nordic system inertia is a must to avoid load shedding and risks of blackouts and
instabilities in the system.
Given the time limit for carrying out the thesis, extra work and investigation tests could
be carried out in the future to further understands the eﬀects of the FFR and solutions to
the 2nd dip. One of the tests that could be done is applying diﬀerent gains from each WPP
contributing in the FFR support instead of a ﬁxed gain from the WPPs to shape more the
FFR support curve and its deactivation shape. Moreover, the eﬀect of diﬀerent wind speeds
experienced by each WPP would be a more realistic approach for the operation conditions
of the WPPs. In addition, eﬀects of adding other sources of FFR support as disconnecting
big loads (market regulated to participate in FFR) along with the support from wind power
51
==PAGE:62==
can give more ﬂexibility in shaping the overall support to better meet the requirements as
FFR from wind power can’t have a long recovery period which is speciﬁed in the technical
requirements. An optimization approach for gains from WPPs would also be needed given
the large amount of variables that could have eﬀect on the amount of power supplied (as
diﬀerent wind speeds and disturbance size) which would shape the frequency response, this
is also given the big size and complexity of the control systems in the wind turbines and the
power system.
