Abstract
Plastic waste and significantly microplastics have become a larger pollutant globally, to
such a degree that plastic is found in every marine environment in the world. The extent
and the dangers of plastic waste to marine ecosystems, have led to a significant amount of
research on the transport of microplastic waste in the ocean. However, not much research
has gone into the behaviour and transport of plastics in nearshore regions, which often are
a hotspot for plastic waste. This thesis aims to change this by experimenting with three
types of plastics with different materials and shapes but the same overall size, 5 mm. The
experiment was performed by inserting particles into a flume with an active wave field to
then measure their beaching times for different positions. The experiment was able to
characterise their velocities and how they behaved. The results of the experiment show
that particles in deep to intermediate water move and behave as predicted by Stokes Drift
theory. However, when reaching the breaking zone, lighter particles are faster to reach the
shore and become beached. At the same time, heavier particles moved slower, as they
are often submerged below the water, causing them to move slowly towards the beach.
Therefore, the heavier the particle is, the longer it will take to reach the shore. This thesis
has provided a first step into this area of microplastic transport, but more research should
be done.

Introduction
Plastic waste has over the years become a larger and larger pollutant, significantly so
in the marine environment (Sebille et al. 2020). Furthermore, the pollution of the marine
environment is not slowing down, but it is instead increasing with each year (H. Zhang
2017, Kooi et al. 2017), as more plastic products are being produced to cover the world’s
growing need for consumer goods (Sebille et al. 2020). Therefore, plastic waste has
become a concern for both governments and the scientific community as the scope of
the plastic pollution has become clear along with its dangers to the environments. Plastic
waste has been spotted in almost all types of marine environments, even in areas where
humans have very little presence, such as the polar regions and below the ice caps (H.
Zhang 2017, Sebille et al. 2020). The widespread distribution of microplastics is caused
by the fact that plastics degrade very slowly, as they are designed to be resilient(Sebille
et al. 2020).
1.1
Introduction to microplastics
Out of all the plastic waste, microplastics are the most dangerous to marine life and
ecosystems (H. Zhang 2017, Z. Zhang et al. 2020). This is because microplastics can
be ingested by marine life, and can cause significant harm (Kooi et al. 2017). The mi-
croplastics ingested by filter feeders including whales, can cause blockages of the ani-
mals’ digestive tracts or even damaging it (Kooi et al. 2017), which can lead to the animals
not being able to eat and end up staving to death. The microplastics also pose a differ-
ent danger to animal lives, as some microplastics can contain toxic chemicals (Z. Zhang
et al. 2020), that leak from the particles into the animals and cause poisoning. And if the
poisoning does not kill the animal, the plastics can be incorporated into the food webs
(Forsberg et al. 2020, as larger animals can eat the animals that have ingested the mi-
croplastics. This can cause the toxic chemicals from the plastics to accumulate to the
point where animals die or they survive but can become toxic to consume by humans
damaging fisheries in the affected area (Lamb et al. 2018).
Most plastic waste in the ocean is not large pieces of plastic but rather small fragments
of the larger pieces of plastic or small particles used in consumer products (Kooi et al.
2017, H. Zhang 2017). These particles are usually termed microplastics. Microplastics
can have a wide range of shapes; cylinders beads, spheres, fibers or irregular shapes,
(H. Zhang 2017). Furthermore, microplastics particles can have different densities or
sizes, both having an impact on how the particle behaves in the water. Density is a big
factor in determining the position of a particle in the water column, be it on the surface,
the bottom or somewhere in between (Alsina, Jongedijk, and Sebille 2020). There is not
a universal definition for the size of these microplastics. However, there is a common
European categorisation, which defines the size of microplastics as being between 1 - 5
mm size. A number of articles and papers in regards to microplastics likewise define them
as being between 1 - 5 mm in size (Kerpen et al. 2020, Forsberg et al. 2020, Kooi et al.
2017), and that definition will be used in this thesis.
The reason why there is such a large amount of microplastics is due to larger pieces of
plastics being degraded into smaller pieces (Kerpen et al. 2020, Kooi et al. 2017). There
are several processes that can cause the plastic to break apart. One of the processes
is photodegradation, where the particles exposed to UV light degrade and break up, bro-
ken apart by breaking waves and currents or degraded by suspended sand, grinding the

particles into smaller fragments (Kerpen et al. 2020). These processes do however not
stop after the formation of microplastics as they will continue degrading the particles from
smaller sizes, 1-5 mm, (Kooi et al. 2017) to immeasurable sizes, < 1 mm.
Depending on the position of the particle in the ocean, different forces will influence the
transport of microplastics (Sebille et al. 2020, H. Zhang 2017). The transport of the mi-
croplastics in the ocean is dominated by currents (Sebille et al. 2020, Forsberg et al. 2020).
These currents along with the high resilience of microplastics allow them to be transported
far way from their sources (Sebille et al. 2020), allowing hotspots for microplastic pollution
to affect much wider than the local area. Hotspots for plastic waste near coastal regions
are usually near population centres or river outlets (H. Zhang 2017). Discharge of mi-
croplastic can also come from maritime activities (H. Zhang 2017, Sebille et al. 2020), but
does not necessarily the same intensity as land-based sources (Sebille et al. 2020).
Research has focused on describing the behaviour of the microplastics in the ocean,
but few have looked at the transport of particles in coastal regions (Alsina, Jongedijk,
and Sebille 2020, Kerpen et al. 2020, H. Zhang 2017). Therefore, there is a significant
amount of knowledge regarding to how the particles are moved in the ocean, (Forsberg
et al. 2020), but little is known about the behaviour of microplastics in the nearshore and
the hydrodynamics of the particles in this region. The hydrodynamics of the shoaling
and breaking zones are rather complex, making it difficult to know exactly what occurs
in this zone, (Forsberg et al. 2020). It is however known that the transport of particles in
nearshore areas are driven by nonlinear coastal waves, but it is not known how (Sebille et
al. 2020). The beach is however seen as a sink for plastics (Forsberg et al. 2020, Sebille
et al. 2020, H. Zhang 2017) and it is therefore of interest to see how the particles behave
in this area. The goal of this thesis is to try and expand on the limited knowledge about
this area of the nearshore microplastic transport.
1.2
Previous experiments
As stated earlier, numerous papers and research have focused on the movement of mi-
croplastics in the oceans or the chemical properties of the material and the danger they
pose. There are, however, not a lot of research performed regarding the behaviour of
microplastics nearshore and in the surf zone. However, there are some experiments that
have looked into this. The experiments will be presented along with the conclusions they
drew and how they can be applicable to this thesis and experiments
1.2.1
Forsberg et al. 2020
The experiments performed in Forsberg et al. 2020, were done to investigate the distri-
bution of microplastics in nearshore water. The particles used were made of 4 different
types of plastic, LDPE (low density polyethylene 0.92 g/cm3), PP (polypropylene 0.95
g/cm3)), PA (poly-amide 1.15 g/cm3) and PVC (polyvinyl chloride 1.38 g/cm3), where 3
different shapes were used, pellet, fibers and sheets. The experiment was performed in
a 6 m long and 0.5 m wide test flume. Wind could be simulated and a wind speed of 6
m/s was used. 4 cases were performed:
1. reference case with a wave height of 9.2 cm and a wave period of 1.2 s.
2. same waves as reference case with a wind flowing offshore.
3. same waves as reference case but with an onshore wind.
4. case with 8.0 cm wave height and a 0.7 s wave period.

Each experiment ran for at least 5 min until a stationary state was achieved. For the
experiment, the beaching time of the particles was visually tracked. The PP pellets used
were translucent and could not be observed. When the experiment was over, the flume
was compartmentalised into five zones and the water was drained. This was done to
separate the particles and get a clear picture of the beaching of the particles.
There were several points that could be learned from the experiment:
1) Heavy plastic particles or non-buoyant particles, can be expected to behave similarly
to natural sediment and accumulate near the breaking zone. This was the case in all 4
cases.
2) For the light (buoyant) particle, the wind and waves both control the distribution of the
light. If there is no wind or an onshore flowing wind along with the reference case waves,
then the particles will be pushed onshore, while with an offshore going wind, the particles
will move offshore. Furthermore, the shape of the particle can impact how fast they beach.
The experiment, however, had some issues. One of the issues was that it did not take
the salinity of the water into account, as the salinity can significantly change the density
of water, and change the density boundary for buoyancy. The bed used was a plane
rigid bed and could not model the effect that a sandy bed could have on the particles.
Lastly, the scaling and dimension of the experiments to a full size beach. Due to the size
of the microplastics, it would be very difficult to do a scaled experiment for the beaching
particles.
This thesis will try to work around some of these issues. One workaround is to use a
sandy bed to observe the effects on beaching. Furthermore, the experiment in the thesis
will use visible particles, so it is possible to get data on all the particles used. It would be
difficult to make a scaled experiment, but the experiment will yield data that can give an
insight to how the particles would behave.
1.2.2
Alsina, Jongedijk, and Sebille 2020
The experiment performed in Alsina, Jongedijk, and Sebille 2020, was done to investigate
the wave-induced motion of inertial particles in intermediate water depth. The experiment
used spherical particles with different densities and sizes. The experiment was performed
with 4 different wave cases. The waves for each case had a different wave steepness,
wave height and period. The experiment was performed in a 16 m long flume that is 0.4
m wide and used a water depth of 0.3 m. At the end of the flume a dissipative beach is
placed to absorb the waves. The interest was to look at the motion of the particles with
intermediate water depth.
Several things became clear from the experiments performed, Alsina, Jongedijk, and Se-
bille 2020:
1) The net drift of floating particles came from wave orbital motion and followed Stokes
drift theory. While the movement of non-floating particles was dependent on the wave
orbital motion that occurs at the bottom of the flume.
2) Floating particles net drift fitted Stokes theory and the net drift for a particle increased
with the wave steepness. A higher wave steepness would cause higher net drift of the
particles. It also showed that the density and size only has an impact on the buoyancy of
the particle and that drift of the particles would be the same regardless of the properties
of the particle.
3) Non-floating particles move along the bottom velocity and the velocity of the particle

non-floating particles to speed up as wave-number increases as a wave gets closer to

1.3
Thesis goals
The focus of this thesis is to examine the behaviour of floating microplastics as they ap-
proach a shoreline. There have been studies regarding the behaviour of microplastics,
floating and none-floating, in nearshore conditions (Kerpen et al. 2020, Alsina, Jongedijk,
and Sebille 2020, Forsberg et al. 2020). However, these experiments have been con-
ducted using regular waves along with other parameters, such as wind (Forsberg et al.
2020) or waves with different wave steepness’s, (Alsina, Jongedijk, and Sebille 2020).
None of the mentioned studies have investigated the behaviour of microplastics in irreg-
ular wave conditions.
Therefore, this thesis will focus on examining the behaviour of floating microplastics in
irregular wave conditions. Determining the beaching times for floating particles is a step
towards understanding the behaviour of microplastics in irregular wave conditions. The
beaching time is the time it takes for a particle dropped at a specific position to reach
a shore and become beached. A particle becomes beached when it has been washed
up, and not returned to the ocean as the wave withdraws. The goal of determining these
beaching times is to determine the velocity of the particles and provide insight into how
the particles behave and travel onshore.
1.4
Outline of thesis
This thesis is composed of 4 chapters: Methodology, Results and discussion, Conclusion
and additional work and an appendix.
Chapter 2 will present the methodology used for the main experiment which includes the
setup used, what assumptions and definitions had to be made for the experiment. It will
also include a presentation of the chosen plastics used for the experiment along with how

In Chapter 3 the results will be presented and discussed. The results are separated into
three parts. Part one is a confirmation of the assumption that 1-hour experimental runs

be extracted from the results. Part 3 will be a comparison of the particles along with a
possible physical explanation for the behaviour of the particles based on the results and
video recordings.
Chapter 4 will conclude based on the results and whether the goals of the thesis have
been achieved along with the main conclusions based on Chapter 3. This chapter will also
contain some proposals for further investigation of the nearshore transport of microplastics
using the developed setup and methodology.

2.1
Setup for experiments
The experiments to determine the initial beaching time for microplastics (MP) were per-
formed in one of the wave flumes at the Hydraulic Engineering Laboratory of the Technical
University of Denmark. The used flume had a length of 28 m, with a width of 0.6 m and
a height of 0.8 m. Inside the flume, a water depth of 0.5 m was used, where the water
depth is written as h = 0.5 m.
Inside the flume, a live sandy beach bed profile with a slope of 1:25 is placed. This was
done to provide an idealised bed profile to determine the beaching time of the MP. Figure
2.1, shows a sketch of the inside of the flume, with the placement of the beach profile,
measured in meters from the start point, x = 0 m. The paddle of the wave maker is placed
at x = 0 m. In Figure 2.1 the free surface is marked by the dotted line, at z = 0 m. The
free surface at z = 0 is also the still water level before any wave action occurs.
Figure 2.1: Beach profile located in test flume. x = 0 m is placed at the wave paddle.
The wave maker used in this flume is a hydraulic piston wave maker, that uses a hydraulic
piston to move a paddle to create the necessary waves. The wave-maker is issued with
an Active Absorption Control System, which absorbs the reflected waves from the beach.
The wave maker is used to create a 30 min long irregular wave series, based on the
JONSWAP spectrum, using a peak enhancement factor γ = 3.3. The waves have a
significant wave height (Hm0) of 0.152 m and a peak period Tp at Tp = 1.6 s. The waves
are intermediate waves , as kh = 1. k is the wavenumber and h is the water depth on
h = 0.5 m. This is calculated using the the significant wave height and peak period
The experiments were recorded using three cameras to record the areas of interest. Two
of the cameras, Camera 1 and 2, were used to record the waves as they reached the
shore, and the microplastics became beached. The last camera, Camera 3, is used to
record when the microplastics entered the flume, which is used to determine how long

shapes and materials. At the start of the experiments, some of the material properties
of the plastics were not known. These properties were the rising velocity, which is the
velocity a particle has as it rises to the surface after being submerged, and the particles’

foam. The used particles can be seen in Figure 2.5. The three shapes are:
• Hollow cubes composed of ABS, also referred to as ABS cubes.
• Hollow cylinders composed of PE also referred to as PE cylinders.

dimensions used for calculations are based on an average of 10 particles. The cubes

dimensions. The EPS spheres were not uniform, likely due to the manufacturing process.
In this case, it is assumed that all particles are spheres with a diameter of 5 mm even if
most particles are not completely spherical.

2.3
Procedure
For the experiment to determine the beaching times for the microplastics, a mobile sandy
beach profile was used. The sandy bed was used as it represents more realistic beach
conditions. Additionally this was done see if the sandy bed had any influence on the run-
up/drawdown of the particles in the swash zone of the beach. A disadvantage of a sandy
bed is the mobility of the sand and how it way’ change throughout the experiment. In the
experiment irregular waves were used. This was done as most experiments and papers
focusing on the nearshore transport of microplastic particles, have chosen to focus on
regular waves (Kerpen et al. 2020, Forsberg et al. 2020, Alsina, Jongedijk, and Sebille
2020), and not irregular waves. This means there is no data on how microplastics be-
have during irregular wave conditions. Furthermore, it is known that nonlinear waves that
drive the transport of microplastics in nearshore regions (Sebille et al. 2020). The use of
irregular waves may be observed to change the length of the beaching time and how the
particles behave on the journey to the beach.
Due to the use of a sandy bed and irregular waves, some considerations have to be made.
The use of irregular waves makes it necessary to define a definition for beaching. This
definition is described in Section 2.3.1. In the case of the sandy bed, it is necessary to
determine how long the experiments can last before the changes in the sand bed becomes
significant. Changes in the bed can impact the beaching time for the particles, making it
harder or easier for the beaching to occur. This issues is discussed in section 2.3.2.
2.3.1
Definition of initial beaching
By using irregular waves, the breaking zone of the individual wave will vary, causing the
breaking zone to be much wider than normal. Furthermore, this also impacts how far up
the beach the waves will reach and therefore how far the floating particles will be moved
up the beach. This is because floating particles will move with the surface of a wave, even
as it breaks.
Therefore, it is necessary to record the beaching of the microplastics via video, as particles
that is beached by a small wave, can either be washed offshore by a larger wave or pushed
further up the beach. Recording the beaching of the particles, makes it easier to count
the beached particles, if a larger amount beaches at the same time.
The behaviour of irregular waves makes it necessary to set out a definition for the beaching
time for this thesis. The beaching time of the microplastics is, for this experiment, defined
as the time at which the MP has been washed up and there is no clear movement of water
or MP in the view of cameras 1 and 2. As mentioned earlier, it is cameras 1 and 2 whose
field of view covers the entire beach as seen on Figure 2.6.

2.3.3
Procedure of Experiment
Procedure during experimentation run
The beaching time for the particles is the time it takes the particles to move from their start
position to the position at which they become beached. This is determined by measuring
how long it takes for a group of particles to be dropped into the flume at specific positions
to becoming beached. The particles are considered beached based on the definition in
section 2.3.1. The particles will be dropped from a container, into the flume, at 4 positions:
x = 11 m, x = 13 m, x = 15 m and x = 17 m, see Figure 2.1, in relation to the wave paddle,
being x = 0 m.
The waves are allowed to propagate for 30 seconds before the first drop will happen. This
is to allow the wave field to be fully developed, as the influence of the reflected waves
have been included into the shoreward waves. This time was calculated in Appendix A.1.
Once the waves have fully developed, the particles will be dropped into the flume, using
the bucket at the positions mentioned previously.
The dropping of the particles will be recorded via camera 3, and the drop is marked by a
red light, that is manually activated, and it will be done simultaneously with the drop. The
beaching of the particles is then recorded using Cameras 1 and 2. Using the timestamps
from the three cameras, marking the drop time and the beaching time, it is possible to
determine a particles beaching time.
At the start of the experiment, the camera setup will be set to record and then the wave
maker will be activated and instructed to start producing the wave series. In Section 2.3.2,
it was discussed that the experiment can only last 2-hours before it has to be stopped.
Therefore, the wave maker is instructed to repeat the 30 min wave series four times to
cover the 2-hours run time, leading to four wave repetitions.
It was judged, based on early data for the PE cylinders, that dropping particles for 1-hour
which corresponds to ruining the wave series twice, would get sufficient data to estimate
the beaching time. This would also allow drops of particles to occur in parts over the
wave series not covered by the first wave series run. This was done as early data for the
PE cylinder showed a minor change in the statistics of the results between 1 hour or 1.5
hours. Further reasoning for this choice is in Section 3.1.
The particles will then travel down the flume towards the beach, where they will beach.
As the particles beach, they will be picked up and removed from the beach. This is done
to decrease the amount of time spent between each drop. When the all the particles have
been beached and picked up, they will be dropped at the same position. The goal is to
drop as many particles into the flume, before the experiment has to be paused to gather
as much data as possible. The waves run continuously to make the drop of the particles
random and not at a predetermined section of the wave series.
The number of particles used for the experiment was: 50 ABS cubes, 50 PE cylinders

container, to allow a similar amount of particles to be dropped into the flume as for the

When the 2 hours are done, the flume is drained of water and left to dry for some time.
This makes it practical to move the sand and easier to shape into the correct slope. The

the beach profile is scanned with a wheeled laser rig, see Figure 2.9. The laser rig is
composed of two lasers: a vertical laser measuring the height of the beach profile and
a horizontal laser, that is used to determine where the measured height is located in the
flume. The vertical laser is mounted on top of a small cart allowing it to move up and down
the flume, while the horizontal laser is stationary at the end of the flume, opposite of the
wave paddle. The horizontal laser shines on a piece of wood attached to the rig allowing
it to measure the distance from the laser to the rig.

time stamp. The beaching time is determined for each drop of particles, which are spread
over the entire wave series. With this spread it will be possible to get an mean beaching
time for the particles.

Results and discussion
By using the methods that were described in the previous section, it is possible to de-
termine the beaching times, along with the distribution of the particles. In this section,
these results will be presented along with other observations which can explain why the
particles behave as they do and what can cause this behaviour.
The results of the experiment are presented using two types of figures; an S-curve, shown
on figures 3.3a, 3.4a, 3.5a and a PDF, shown on figures 3.1, 3.2 ,3.3b, 3.4b, 3.5b. The S-
curve shows the percentage of particles that have been beached within a certain amount
of time. One line in the figure is based on an average for all the drops performed at one
position. The lines in the S-curve figures can be compared with each other, by looking at
the differences between the medians, as some S-curves have a steep slope or relative
shallow slope. PDF stands for the probability density function and it shows the probability
for a particles to become beached for at given time. Each line is the probability density
function for beaching times at a specific position.

4
Conclusion and additional work
The goal to determine the beaching time for a series of different microplastic particles is
achieved. With the experiments conducted, it was possible to observe that different types
of plastics will have different beaching times in an irregular wave series.
The results show if drop of particles is located far away from the beach, the beaching
times will become more spread out than if drop of particles occurred close to the beach.
It was observed that when the particles were in intermediate waver that they moved with
the velocity gained from Stokes drift, regardless of the density and shape of the particles.
This corresponds well with the results gained from Alsina, Jongedijk, and Sebille 2020.
Furthermore, as the particles get closer to the shore, they sped up from the velocity gained
from Stokes drift. It was possible to see that the particles gained significant velocity from
the breaking of waves as they neared the shore.
The increase in speed for the microplastics was, however, not uniform. The EPS spheres
had the greatest change in velocity. At x = 17 m, the EPS particle moved five times the
velocity they gained from Stokes drift. ABS cubes at x = 17 m moved with a velocity 3.5
times greater than its Stokes drift. The PE cylinders, however, only moved twice as fast.
This speed difference was due to the behaviour of the particles as they got submerged
by a breaking wave, either in its initial breaking or the wave roller. The EPS spheres rose
straight up to the surface if they managed to get submerged, while the ABS cubes spent
a relatively short amount of time submerged. On the other hand, the PE cylinders spent
a long time below the water surface and would, in some cases, be pulled by offshore
currents. Spending time below the surface would cause the particles to travel with a
reduced velocity resulting in a longer beaching time.
The main difference between these particles is their shape and density, which impacts
rising velocity. However, the experiment conducted did look into the effects of the shape
of the particles, as there were not particles of different shapes but same material. The
experiment showed that the density of the different particles had an impact on if they could
be submerged and how long they were submerged. However, as the density decreases,
its impact on the rising velocity decreases. For example, the ABS cubes and PE cylinders
have a difference in density of 138 kg/m3, but ABS has twice the rising velocity of the PE.
While the EPS spheres have a velocity twice that of the ABS while the density difference
is 735 kg/m3.
This experiment is only a first step towards understanding how floating particles behave
as they enter nearshore. Still, it has provided an insight into an area that impact the
beaching time of microplastics, being the density. These results can then be used to
help verify numerical models to try and predict the movement of particles in nearshore
areas. They can like wise help provide the insight on the behaviour of microplastics as
they become beached and deposited on the beach, which is an known sink for plastics.
Additional work can be done to further improve the knowledge within this area by experi-
menting with particles of the same material or density but with different shapes to explore
how big an impact the shape of the particles have on their travel to the shore and sub-
sequently their beaching times. Different sizes of particles could also be introduced to
investigate see how size influences the rising velocities and beaching times.
Changing the type of bed used in the experiment could also be an option, as it was noticed

Rising velocity
For all the 3 plastics used for the main experiment, ABS, PE and PS, none of their rising
velocities are known. The rising velocity is the velocity at which floating particles will rise
to the surface if submerged. This is an important material property, as it can have an
influence on how the particles behave if they are submerged in the test. The setup to
determine the rising velocity is visible in figure A.5. The test setup is composed of a tall
cylindrical container, (3), filled with distilled water, as not to have the density of the water
affected by other minerals. The water has a depth of 49 cm which, based on previous
experiments, is sufficient for determining the rising velocity. A long stick is used to place
the particles on the bottom of the cylinder 2). A camera is placed to record the bath of the
particle (1). It is set up such that it can record the particle from the bottom to the top of
the water column.
Figure A.5: Experimental setup for rising velocities of particles
The experiment starts by turning on the video recording. A particle is then placed on the
water surface of the cylinder. The stick is then used to push the particle down the cylinder,
at the same time raising the water level. When the particles have reached the bottom, the
stick is slowly removed from the top of the particle, allowing it to freely rise to the surface.
When the stick is removed it will be held in place in the water, as to not impact the upward
motion of the particle. The stick inside the cylinder, the water level rose to 51 cm, which
is the distance on which the rising velocity is calculated. This can be seen in figure A.6
