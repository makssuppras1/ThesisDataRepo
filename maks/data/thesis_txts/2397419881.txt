Abstract
The motive of this thesis is to scrutinise the factors, that are contributing to harmonic
amplitudes and to ﬁnd the techniques to reduce harmonics, that are emerging from
voltage source converters. By using modulation method PWM (Pulse Width Modula-
tion), the inﬂuence of deadtime, carrier symmetry and semiconductor voltage drops
in the voltage spectrum of two-level converter are experimented. These factors limit
the power quality of signal by introducing harmonics.
Above limitation factors are investigated in laboratory hardware, and also inspected
in the developed VSI simulation model in Simscape. The single-phase half-bridge,
full-bridge and three-phase inverter topologies have been analysed. Finally, both sim-
ulation and laboratory results are compared to acquire practical value of harmonics
in inverter voltage and current signals. FFT of output voltage and current have been
computed in Matlab to observe the amplitude and phase of low-order harmonics.
The feasibility of minimising harmonics, that are originated by semiconductor volt-
age drops and deadtime have been illustrated throughout this work. This thesis pro-
poses a new compensation technique to decrease dynamic voltage contribution in the
harmonic spectrum, that is described in the chapter 6 - "Compensation". The com-
pensation technique provide satisfactory results in both laboratory and simulation
experiments. The harmonics at DC side are analysed in the chapter 7 and the strat-
egy to deduct the inﬂuence of DC harmonics in inverter voltage spectrum has been
researched.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter discusses the general issues due to harmonics and the factors, that are
causing harmonics. It provides brieﬁng about the method, that are used for harmonics
ﬁltering originated from VSC. It also presents the procedure used for computing
results from laboratory and simulation experiments throughout the thesis.
1.1
Harmonics and it’s consequence
Generally, both voltage and current signals at "n" number of frequencies are multi-
plied with fundamental frequency which are deﬁned as "Harmonics". Harmonics
are signiﬁcant problem that reduces power quality of signal by distorting waveform.
The power converter generating harmonic characteristics result in production of peak
currents during short circuit and increase switching losses as a consequence, that de-
crease the life expectancy of electrical components used in the power converter appli-
cations. These characteristics of harmonics are not only highly related to switching
frequencies but also due to several other factors.
It is believed that, non-linear loads and switching semiconductors are resources of
harmonics, these contributes to decrease the fundamental frequency amplitude at con-
verter output voltage. An efﬁcient power quality means steady voltage and frequency
within prescribed range, that should resembles smooth voltage waveform. Increased
use of power converters have brought a demanding challenge of decreasing power
quality to grid-connected applications which have uncontrollable production (i.e., re-
newable energies). The future electrical systems could have several sensitive power
electronics, this escalate harmonics effect further in the VSI spectrum. Hence, it is
==PAGE:28==
2
1 Introduction
predominant to conﬁne the harmonics in cost effective ways.
Effects of harmonics in power system utilities and consumers leads to prefer signif-
icant solutions such as advanced current controllers, higher-order active and passive
ﬁltering. These ﬁltering techniques enhances power quality meanwhile, it elevates
the losses and cost. According to IEEE standards for power quality maintenance, the
total harmonic distortion and harmonic limits have to be ensured, especially while de-
signing an optimum ﬁlter for grid power converter. This process is time consuming
and increase the complexities in designing an optimal electrical system.
1.2
Factors Causing Harmonics
The amplifying factors of harmonic levels are carrier symmetry, deadtime, semicon-
ductor voltage drops, DC-link capacitors and, phase changes in reference and carrier
signals. Other factors which are related to switching components degrades power
quality and results in distorted signals.
The detailed fourier spectrum analysis of output PWM signal with dead time for var-
ious sampling methods of a inverter are discussed in the paper [6]. The effects of
deadtime are further shown in the research paper [7] that describes THD calculation,
distortion level due to deadtime and spectral information of PWM signal with dead-
time for arbitrary modulating signals.
So far, the expensive current sensors and diode conduction device (DCD) in [12] are
used to compensate the effect of deadtime in the harmonic spectrum. Diode Conduc-
tion Device is a device i.e., installed in an inverter to estimate the direction of load
current ﬂows via phase legs and compensate deadtime effect. This can be studied
in detail in the paper [12]. A recent paper [5] that understandably, scrutinise the
inﬂuence of harmonics due to semiconductor voltage drops in the spectra of output
converter voltage.

1.3 Thesis Aim
3
These ﬁndings are limited by analysing the spectral inﬂuences of semiconductor volt-
age drops and deadtime. However, the literature’s that educates speciﬁcally, about
compensation of harmonic magnitudes which are provoked by both deadtime and
semiconductor voltage drops are only few. This whole work represents the proba-
bility of developing power converter in the way to convert the voltage and current
waveform with acceptable harmonic contents.
The precise analytical approach of pulse width modulation for inﬁnite series of si-
nusoidal voltage including semiconductor voltage drop and deadtime is not derived
yet. Deriving mathematical equation which is relevant to pulse width modulation is
strenuous work. Hence, this thesis focus on the simulation and practical results of
PWM signal including deadtime and semiconductor voltage drop.
1.3
Thesis Aim
The vital aim of thesis is to suppress the low-order harmonic magnitudes without
using ﬁlters. It presents the compensating solution to attenuate semiconductor volt-
age drop effects by alleviating the dominant contribution of dynamic voltage (Ud) of
semiconducting components and it also illustrate a method to eliminate the deadtime
effect by introducing the error signal i.e., originated due to blanking time. This work
also reveals the inﬂuence of capacitor size in DC-Side harmonics by comparing sim-
ulation and laboratory results. The report consists of following main sections such
as:
 Hardware and Simulation Models
 Pulse Width Modulation Process
 Single-phase Half-bridge Inverter
 Single-phase H-bridge Inverter
 Mitigation of Harmonics
 DC-side Harmonics Investigation

Chapter 3
Pulse Width Modulation
This chapter describes an effective technique to modulate signals with different pulse
widths i.e., called "Pulse Width Modulation (PWM)". It can be used for both low
and high switching frequencies. It is a beneﬁcial method, due to the maximum utili-
sation of DC voltage. The proposed changes in reference signal (in chapter 6) used
for pulse width modulation reduces total harmonic distortion than traditional carrier
based pulse width modulation techniques, it is applicable to variable frequency drive
and grid applications. In this work, the double-side modulation is performed by com-
paring triangular carrier with continuous reference.
3.1
PWM Process
The pulse width modulated signal is the combination of square shape pulses, which
are constructed by comparing the low frequency sinusoidal reference with high fre-
quency triangular carrier. This is known as "traditional natural sampling method of
PWM". PWM pulse switches between the values zero and one. Each pulse width
waveform consists of carrier frequency, sideband and its harmonics. The duration of
each pulse at output voltage depends on the period of PWM signal.
The harmonic amplitude at the sideband frequency fs1 = m(fc) + n(f1) has same ampli-
tude of an another sideband frequency fs2 = m(fc) - n(f1) for any integer m,n. Where,
(fc) is carrier frequency, (f1) is fundamental and fs1, fs2 are sideband frequencies. In
other words, the sidebands shows the symmetry with carrier frequency. When "m+n"
term is even, the frequency "f = m(fc) - n(f1)" is zero. Thus, the term sin(m + n)π/2
becomes zero in equation (3.14) which eliminates even order harmonics.

3.1.1
Sampling and Modulation Type
Pulse width modulation can follow different sampling methods such as asymmetri-
cal uniform sampling, symmetrical uniform sampling and natural sampling methods.
Natural sampling is different from other sampling techniques which is consistently
based on the intersection moments of carrier and reference signals. Types of general
modulation techniques are trailing-edge, leading-edge and double-edge modulation.
Figure 2.2 displays the triangular carrier (1kHz) and reference signal (50Hz).
The triangular waveform in ﬁgure (2.2) is double-side modulated so that, the orig-
inated even-order harmonics will be lower than single-sideband modulation. DC
component will not be depicted during double-side modulation as anticipated. The
switching frequency relies on carrier signal and the frequency of output voltage is
based on the reference signal. The reference signal is deﬁned in equation (3.1).
Uref (t) = A·sin(ωft +θf )
(3.1)
Where, "A" refers to amplitude. When triangular carrier is integer multiple of refer-
ence signal, the PWM technique is known as synchronous PWM. It generates iden-
tical pulse width modulated signal for every cycle of reference, which has constant
frequency value and provides stable output voltage. It is used in high power applica-
tions, where low frequency modulated signal is required. Whereas, the asynchronous
PWM produces pulses in different voltage forms for every cycle. It is used in both
low power and medium power applications. Both, modulation techniques permits to
vary reference signal amplitude to control inverter voltage.

3.4
Deadtime Effect
Inverter switches will not be turned on simultaneously. In order to avoid shoot-
through during switching, the semiconductor switches have remarkable turn on and
off times, it means including short deadtime in positive and negative edges of gate
signals. The length of deadtime is depend on characteristic of switching components
which distorts PWM voltages and current.
Adding deadtime in modulated signal affects power quality by increasing amplitude
of lower-order harmonics and also it decreases the fundamental value in the spec-
trum of voltage. Meanwhile, incorporating deadtime has vital advantage i.e., saving
switching components from short circuit. The delay time between "ON" and "OFF"
function of IGBT’s avoids unnecessary current spikes appeared during sudden switch-
ing. The major disadvantage of deadtime is widening of both "ON" and "OFF" pulses,
that deviates voltage spectrum.
It is salient to consider the rise of distortion level in voltage waveform and the re-
duction of RMS value of voltage due to deadtime. The control signals are delayed
at the time of turn-on state of one switch and let another switch to turn-off completely.
Consider an inverter leg, since all power switches are "OFF" during dead time, the
voltage (Uo) depends on the direction of current (Io). The ﬁgure 3.6 (at next page)
explains the changes emerged due to deadtime in the inverter voltage (∆U) which is
proportional to deadtime (td) and switching frequency ( fc). The two dotted lines in
ﬁgure 3.6 denotes deviations in output voltage due to increase and decrease of current
(Io). The ﬁgure 3.6 is referred from [3].

From DC midpoint to the midpoint of inverter leg, series "R-L" load is connected.
The combination of unidirectional switch and inversely conducting diode acts as an
inverter switch. This leads current to ﬂow in a direction i.e., based only on one polar-

current ﬂows through switch "S2" is negative and positive current ﬂows via diode
"D2".

in order to avoid short circuit while switching both power switches at same time. In
case of shoot through, the switching components will be degraded and decreases it’s
life expectancy.

The operation of H-bridge inverter are stated below:
1. When reference signal "Uref " is higher than carrier signal "Ucar", "S1" and

positive. While, the current ﬂows via freewheeling diodes "D1 and "D3".

"S4" will be turned "ON". After, complete switch off of "S1" and "S3".
3. The current will not passes through "S2" and S4, unless the current via load
becomes negative. It happens only, when reference signal is lesser than carrier
signal, when lower DC rail is switched "ON". Now, the current ﬂows via
protecting diodes "D2" and "D4" sets the load voltage to negative.
In simple words, "S1" is open while "S2" is closed. The same phenomenon applies to
"S3" and "S4". In order to prevent short circuit of power supply, the opening/closing
of four switches will not occur at same time. The output voltage from the H-bridge
inverter will be twice of half bridge inverter value i.e., 2Udc. Though, the H-bridge

Harmonic Mitigation
This chapter presents a technique to control semiconductor voltage drops and dead-
time effects in the harmonic spectrum of inverter voltage. By including compensating

This compensation technique provides satisfactory results by decreasing the harmon-

6.1
Strategies for Harmonic Reduction
From chapters 4 and 5, it is understood that with odd carrier frequency 1.05kHz, the
inverter voltage spectra has lower even-order harmonics whereas, the odd harmonics
amplitudes are identical for both carriers 1kHz and 1.05kHz. In case of 1kHz, the
amplitude of both even and odd-order harmonics are increasing due to the cross cou-
pling effect of deadtime and semiconductor voltage drops with fundamental and with
carrier frequency.
The other consequences such as., having higher dead time (for instance, 5µs) in-
crease the amplitude of harmonics further and it is previously seen that deadtime
effect contributes towards odd-order harmonics shown in ﬁgure 3.5. The phase an-
gles of inverter voltage with semiconductor voltage drop effect are similar with the

Future work
The positive reaction for implementing square wave compensation for semiconductor
voltage drops are depicted in laboratory results. Due to some hardware limitations,
the identical compensation result from laboratory and simulation tests are challeng-
ing to achieve, it can be achieved accurately by further optimisation of offsets from
the instrument used in laboratory hardware. It is essential to add an extra heatsink to
avoid excess heating while performing experiments for a long time period.
As a step forward, it is possible to test the square wave compensation method in
three-phase inverter or three-level H-bridge inverter practically. It will obviously
provide satisﬁed results than H-bridge inverter. To implement this technique in the
three-phase or three-level inverter hardware need some more time. According to the
timeline and objective of thesis, both laboratory and simulation work have been done
to produce the results showing that, the low-order harmonics can be controlled using
compensation test in H-bridge inverter.
The present control board have correction only for dynamic voltage contribution, the
dynamic resistance (Rd) contribution in the voltage drop of converter spectrum is
smaller than Ud contribution. Hence, it is not implemented yet for hardware investi-
gation. There is an aim to develop the three-phase inverter hardware in near future,
that compensates higher Rd effect which contributes for larger voltage drops, and
also it compensate deadtime by generating error pulse practically. This deadtime
compensation method implementation will be more effective for higher DC voltage
and high-level inverters. Hence, this work will be continued further to present an op-
timised results from laboratory measurements at an international conference paper.

Three Phase Inverters
This chapter presents the three phase construction of power inverter in the simulation
model and also shows the implementation of semiconductor voltage drop compen-
sation. Three phase inverters are normally used for high power applications. The
advantages of using a three phase inverter are:
• The frequency of the output voltage waveform depends on the switching rate
of the switches hence, it can be varied over a wide range
• The direction of rotation of the motor can be reversed by changing the output
phase sequence of the inverter
• The ac output voltage can be controlled by varying the dc link voltage

Operation of Three phase Inverter
The simulation model in ﬁgure E.1 comprises the three-phase two-level inverter
which has same DC power supply that has three inverter legs with switches S1, S2,
S3 are upper switches and S4, S5, S6 are bottom switches. The switches S1, S3 and
S5 are connected to positive voltage. Therefore, the switches S2, S3 and S6 are con-
nected to negative voltage. When switch S3 is open, the switch S4 is closed and vice
versa. The same phenomenon applies to switches S1 and S2 and also to S5 and S6.
It produces three voltage outputs which are 120◦out of phase from each other. The
output of three inverter legs are connected across the balanced three phase load volt-
age where, the three phase load voltage can be measured. This circuit can be iden-
tiﬁed as three single-phase half-bridge inverter circuits put across the same dc bus.

Effect in Three Phase Inverter
The experiments done for three phase inverters having voltage drop and dead time
effect. This experiment is analysed in different view point by varying the carrier with
phase changes of half of the total period [i.e., T = 0.02s] and also for carrier signal
the time value equal to the quarter of the total period [i.e., T/4] for three different
frequencies such as 950Hz, 1kHz and, 1.05kHz. The carrier frequencies 1.05kHz
and 950Hz can suppress the odd and even harmonics comparing to 1kHz switching
frequency in H-bridge inverter. Due to the emergence of zero order frequency har-
monics in three phase two level inverter, this test is further continued with 900Hz,
so that zero order harmonics are damped in three phase inverter harmonic voltage.
Even with the optimal carrier frequency, the semiconductor voltage drops are not at
the negligible magnitude level in three phase inverter.
