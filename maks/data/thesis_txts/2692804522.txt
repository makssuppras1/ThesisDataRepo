Abstract
In the ﬁeld of renewable energies, wind energy is one of the leading sectors, with a continuous
growth of installed GW per year. However, a not optimal layout of the wind turbines in a
wind farm may lead to losses in terms of energy production, due to the presence of wakes.
Multiple wind turbine types might overcome these problems, as diﬀerent hub heights and
rotor diameters can mitigate the wake eﬀect. This thesis investigates, through an optimiza-
tion procedure, the beneﬁts that wind farms with non homogeneous wind turbines have in
comparison to wind farms with uniform ones, from an energetic and an economic perspec-
tive. Three test cases have been developed and studied by performing a wind shear and a
wind direction sensitivity analysis, before applying an optimization process on a real wind
farm, namely the Lillgund wind park. The optimization mechanism has been performed
with DTU’s wind farm layout optimization software – TOPFARM, together with the Py-
Wake package – by using a gradient search method.
The outcome of the performed optimizations has shown the higher beneﬁt of having multiple
wind turbine hub heights than multiple rotor diameters, from the Annual Energy Production
(AEP) as well as the Internal Rate of Return (IRR) perspective.

Introduction
1.1
Premise
Since the Industrial Revolution, the combustion of fossil fuels has been the dominant
way of generating energy, with the production of huge amounts of ’green house gasses’(GHG)
which have consequently contributed to environmental pollution and climate change [1].
Because of this, and since fossil fuels are going to be exhausted in the near future, a transition
to less energy–related CO2 sources in a world where the energy demand is rising (Figure 1a)
can be achieved by the employment of renewable technologies. This objective is politically
supported by deals like the "Paris Agreement"(2015), where 196 nations signed a legally
binding international treaty on climate change with several goals, including the limitation
of global warming to well below 2 ◦C by reaching the net–zero green house gas emissions by
2025 and by increasing the amount of renewable energy sources in the energy supply mix. [2].
(a) Energy consumption in the world
(b) Installed capacity per renewable technology
Figure 1: Primary energy consumption and renewable energy generation per technology
Figure 1a and Figure 1b show that the energy produced from renewable sources is still
far from covering the world’s energy demand, even though their share is rising, especially
where the wind energy branch is one of the leading sectors [3]. This means displaying an
increase of the yearly installed capacity and providing a worldwide rising annual energy
contribution second only to Hydropower (Figure 1b), due to a Levelized Cost of Energy
(LCOE1) reduction in comparison to the conventional energy generation sources, i.e. Coal,
Nuclear and Gas [4]. To achieve the goals set by the "Paris Agreement" great innovation is
needed, and the deployment of the wind energy sector has to increase further, with an LCOE
decrease in the near future, to boost its rate of penetration also in more diﬃcult markets.
Nowadays, the most common technology to extract energy from the wind is the hori-
zontal axis wind turbine (HAWT), generally characterized by a three–blade rotor located at

a speciﬁc height, the hub height, which converts the wind’s kinetic energy into electric by
slowing down the wind speed.
As the LCOE of one single wind turbine wouldn’t be competitive in the energy market
– due to land, grid, installation, and maintenance costs –, to increase the produced energy
a set of wind turbines, i.e. a wind farm, has to be connected. The wind turbines are usually
characterized by the same rotor diameter located at the same hub height.
When a wind farm is installed, several trade–oﬀs have to be taken into account. One has
to do with the best placement of the wind turbines, because when the wind speed is slowed
down by the rotor, an area characterized by reduced wind speed and higher turbulence is
generated behind. This is the wake, which is responsible for power losses of the wind turbines
located downstream as well as in the whole wind farm.
On one side, as the wake recovers after a certain distance, locating the wind turbines
further apart leads to a wake eﬀect reduction, but at the same time to an increase of the
land and cabling costs, which rise with space and represent 15% to 30% [5] of the wind farm
capital costs. On the other side, placing the wind turbines too close to one another will
reduce the aforementioned costs, but amplify the wake eﬀect and consequently lower the
power production.
Diﬀerent techniques have been applied to bypass this trade–oﬀ, as shown in section 2.
One of these techniques consists in locating wind turbines with diﬀerent design parameters,
i.e. hub heights and rotor diameters. This is the topic investigated in this thesis, focused on
the beneﬁts of that method in terms of Annual Energy Production (AEP) and Internal Rate
of Return (IRR), in comparison to the outcomes of a traditional wind farm characterized by
uniform design parameters.
1.2
Research Questions, Objectives and Project Description
This master project is intended to answer the following research question :
• ’ Is a wind farm made of wind turbines with diﬀerent hub heights and rotor diameters
more beneﬁcial than one with uniform hub heights and diameters, from the energetic
and the economic perspective ?’
– ’ How are the results of the optimizations aﬀected by diﬀerent wind shear values
? ’
– ’ How are the results of the the optimizations aﬀected by diﬀerent sites, charac-
terized by diﬀerent wind directions ? ’
The results are obtained by performing two types of optimizations independently, i.e. max-
imizing the AEP in one case and the IRR in the other. For each optimization, the rotor
diameter(Drotor) and the hub height have been used as design variables, separately. More-
over, a combined sensitivity analysis for diﬀerent wind shear values α and wind directions has
been carried out to understand the inﬂuence that these parameters have on the outcomes.

Chapter 2:
A literature review presents the state of the art of wind farm optimization by highlighting

2
Methodology
2.1
Introduction to wind farm optimization
As the contribution of wind energy becomes more and more important in the electricity
market[3], the performance of its components has to improve and the costs must decrease in
order for the wind industry to become an up–to–date stable and competitive energy resource.
Since wind energy systems involve many elements from diﬀerent disciplines, a system
engineering approach is necessary in order to perform improvements. System engineering is
a branch of engineering that has been already used in complex ﬁelds such as aerospace, and
can be deﬁned as "the art and science of developing an operable system capable of meeting
requirements within often opposed constraints"[6]. It is a holistic and integrative discipline
where the contribution of many ﬁelds is taken into account and evaluated one against the
other in order to achieve the best possible solution among all the solutions proposed from
the point of view of the individual disciplines.
In other words, System Engineering related to wind energy can be understood as the science
that brings together all the single sub–disciplines that are involved in the wind industry to
achieve an acceptable solution from all points of view.
The desired improvements can be performed trough some optimization techniques,
among which the Multidisciplinary Design Optimization and the Multi Objective Optimiza-
tion analyzed in the works reviewed in the following section.
2.2
Literature Review
Studies of wind farms optimization aiming to reduce the LCOE by increasing the wind
farm’s AEP and decrease their costs have focused and are currently focusing on three diﬀerent
techniques, as pointed out by Stanley and Ning [7]. The main technique consists in optimizing
the wind farm’s layout to reduce the wake eﬀect and increase therefore the energy production.
This has been proven to be successful for the ﬁrst time by Mosetti et al.[8] in 1993, where
a genetic algorithm approach has been used to improve the wind farm’s energy output and
decrease its costs.
Since then, many other publications using this technique appeared, so that the wind
farm optimization process has been deﬁned "as the process of ﬁnding the positions of the
wind turbines that maximize the value of some objective function”"[9].
But because the spatial extension of the wind farm is constrained, which implies that some
wind turbines will be located in the wake of others, and because of the stochastic nature
of the wind, which means that the optimal wind farm’s layout one year might not be the
same during all the operational period, two other methods with the same purpose have been
developed, i.e. the active yaw control optimization and the wind turbine design parameter
optimization.
The active yaw control optimization technique consists of yawing the wind turbines to mit-
igate the wakes within the wind park, as described by Gebraad et al. [10] and Fleming et
al.[11], and is only mentioned for the sake of completeness as further description is out of
the scope of this project.
4
==PAGE:14==
2.3
Introduction to TopFarm
On the contrary, the following works regarding the wind turbine design parameter op-
timization have been reviewed, as this is precisely the subject of this thesis.
P.Graf et al. presented the results for the "next generation" wind farm optimization
problems in [12], where they added to a layout optimization the complexity of using two
diﬀerent wind turbines, one for lower and the other for higher wind speeds. The optimizations
have been performed at ﬁrst in a simpliﬁed site with one wind direction and then in a complex
site, where the objective function was set to maximize the AEP by using an ad hoc developed
hierarchical parallel algorithm. The ﬁnal results show a large number of conﬁgurations with
diﬀerent combinations of turbines with similar AEP estimation, where a 20% increase from
the starting condition has been achieved.
Stanley, Ning, and Dykes[13][14] presented in their work the results of a wind farm
optimization with an exact analytical gradient approach where the "Cost of Energy"(CoE)
has been minimized by allowing the wind turbines’ hub heights to vary within two height
classes.
The particularity of their work was that a three–dimensional modiﬁed FLORIS
wake model has been used and that the hub height classes have been limited by structural
constraints for the simulations to be more realistic. They obtained a 4.9% reduction in CoE
compared to a wind farm with uniform hub heights, which increased further to 11.2% when
the distance between the wind turbines was set to three rotor diameters.
The same authors investigated furthermore in[15] the beneﬁt, from the economic per-
spective (CoE), of having wind turbines with multiple hub heights in combination with
multiple rotor diameters. They found that when the rotor diameters and the hub heights
are optimized at the same time, a reduction of the CoE in the range of 4% to 13% compared
to the baseline case was achieved, which increased furthermore to 22%-41% by optimizing
the rotor diameters and the hub heights independently. Moreover, they simulated a more
realistic case, by allowing a uniform and a non–uniform variation of the design variables
with two rotor diameter and hub height classes optimized for two wind roses, achieving in
all optimizations a COE reduction.
Chen et al.[16] investigated the eﬀects of using wind turbines with diﬀerent hub heights
classes by using a Genetic Algorithm approach through which they obtained an increase of the
wind farm’s energy output and an improvement of the cost per unit power. Furthermore, they
applied their research on a real wind farm case to have results for more realistic conditions.
This thesis project diﬀers from the reviewed works, where the optimizations of the wind
turbine design parameters were performed between predeﬁned classes, by investigating the
optimum value across a range of parameters limited by lower and upper boundary values.
The optimizer is therefore able to choose the best value for the optimal design parameter as
long as it is in the aforementioned range.

2.4
PyWake
PyWake is an open–source Python implemented wind farm simulation tool, able to
calculate the ﬂow ﬁelds, power production, and, as mentioned before, the Annual Energy
Production (AEP) of wind farms, given a site, a wake model and a wind turbine model.
The wind farm simulation is set up by initializing the wind farm model with the site of the
wind farm and the selected wind turbine type.
Given a certain position, a reference wind speed (WSref) and a wind direction (WDref)
the site object provides the local wind speed(WS), wind direction(WD), turbulence inten-
sity(TI), and the combination of wind direction and speed, and is responsible for the calcu-
lation of the down-wind, cross-wind ,and vertical distance between the wind turbines.[18]
The wind turbine then returns the power and thrust coeﬃcients (Cp and Ct respectively)
for a given eﬀective wind speed. Once the site and the wind turbine objects are initialized
in the wind farm model, meaning that the wind turbines positions, types, and wind speeds
are connected together, the wind farm model calculates how the wake propagates within the
wind farm, returning the calculated eﬀective wind speed, power production, thrust coeﬃ-
cients, and the AEP.
The way of how the wind turbines and the wind speeds in the site interact together is deﬁned
in the engineering wind farm model, composed of one of two wind farm models, where the
procedure of how the wake propagates within the wind farm is determined, a wake deﬁcit
model, which calculates the wake deﬁcit from one wind turbine to downstream wind turbines,
and a superposition model, which deﬁnes how the wakes from diﬀerent sources are summed
up. Optionally also a blockage deﬁcit and a turbulence model can be included.
In this speciﬁc project, the used wind farm model is PropagateDownwind which is faster
compared to the All2Alliterative but the Blockage eﬀect is neglected. Regarding the Deﬁcit-
Model, the Bastankhah Gaussian model[19] has been used where the superposition model

2.4.1
The Wake Model
As already mentioned the used DeﬁcitModel for this project is the Bastankhah Gaus-
sian Deﬁcit analytical model, which is implemented in PyWake according to the work of
M.Bastankhah and F.Porte’-Angel [19].
In their work, they have proposed a new wake model starting from the work of N.O.Jensen[20]
and Frandsen et al.[21] and changed the wake shape from "top hat" to "gaussian". They
included furthermore the conservation of momentum to derive the wake equations which is
proven not to be taken into account in N.O.Jensen’s work and which has been applied to
the control volume around the turbine by Frandsen et al.
With those improvements this wake model is accurate enough for power prediction whereas
"top hat" models generally underestimate the velocity deﬁcit, leading to a less accurate
estimation of the power within a wind farm.

CASE 2: AEP Maximization with only Diameter Variation
By considering a single wind turbine, Equation 4 shows that the power extracted from
the wind is directly proportional to the rotor area which is, in turn, proportional to the
square of the diameter, therefore it is understandable that wind turbines with bigger rotors
produce more power and consequently more energy. It must be stated that for the following
optimizations the rated power has been constrained to 2MW and therefore wind turbines
with bigger rotors can produce more power at lower wind speeds as shown in Figure 27 but
are not able to exceed the rated power.
When wind turbines with bigger rotors are located within a wind farm, they will generate
larger wakes which consequently reduce the wind speed seen by wind turbines located down-
stream. However, at the same time, they are also able to extract more energy from lower
wind speeds and thus can win back some energy lost due to the wake eﬀect of bigger rotors
located upstream. The trade–oﬀthat the optimizer has to handle therefore is between set-
ting bigger rotor diameters to win energy from lower wind speeds–but generate larger wakes–
and reducing the wake eﬀect by installing smaller rotors but lose some energy for lower wind
speeds.

In this project, TopFarm’s DTU cost model is responsible for calculating the IRR and
is capable of breaking up the project costs in the four aforementioned expenditure classes
which in turn depend also on the following parameters, that have been kept at the default
value in the performed optimizations:
• distance from shore = 10 km
• energy price = 0.13 e/kWh
• project duration, T = 25 years
• rated rotor speed = 8 [rpm]
• water depth = 10 m
The TopFarm’s DTU cost model working process can be summed up as follows:
• t = 0:
The total initial investment of a wind farm set in Equation 26 as C0 is related to the
CAPEX and to the DEVEX, where the ﬁrst is connected to the wind turbine structure
itself, as it takes into consideration the cost of the components of the wind turbine as
well as the foundation, whereas the second to the cost of environmental and seabed
surveys as well as the met mast and some development services. It has to be mentioned
that at t = 0 no energy is produced yet and consequently no cash inﬂow is present as
it represents the starting point of the project.
• 0<t<T:
The net cash inﬂow of the project shown in Equation 26 as Ct, covers the duration
period T of the project and is the diﬀerence between the energy sold and the expendi-
tures, where the latter is represented now entirely by the OPEX of each period t, which
include the expenditures related to the personnel, harbor fees, failed components and
oﬀshore operations.
• t = T:
at the end of the project, set by default to T = 25 years, no energy will be produced
anymore and Ct will be characterized only by the decommissioning costs, better known
as ABEX, which are set to be a fraction of the CAPEX.

5
Conclusion and Future Work
5.1
Conclusion
The performed optimizations have demonstrated that a wind farm characterized by mul-
tiple hub heights and rotor diameters can perform better, from the energetic and economic
perspective, when compared to a wind farm with uniform design parameters, due to a wake
loss reduction.
Furthermore, higher beneﬁts from having multiple hub heights rather than multiple
diameters have been obtained in comparison to the baseline cases, for both objective func-
tions.The outcomes relative to the hub height optimization of the performed test cases have
shown that, for high shear values, it is more beneﬁcial to set wind turbines partially in the
wakes of other turbines, but at bigger elevation, so to extract more energy from higher wind
speeds, rather than avoiding the wakes at lower elevation and consequently extract less en-
ergy at lower wind speeds. Regarding the results which involve the rotor diameter, the test
cases have shown that smaller rotor diameters for speciﬁc wind turbines produce smaller
wakes, leading in turn to an increase of the wind farm’s AEP and IRR.
Finally, the optimizations performed on the Lillgrund wind farm have shown AEP and
IRR increments for both used design variables.
5.2
Future Work
• The type of rotor average model used in the optimization inﬂuences the optimization
outcome and therefore a deeper investigation by performing a sensitivity analysis with
diﬀerent rotor average models could be helpful to conﬁrm or deny the results obtained
in this project
• An optimization where the hub heights and the rotor diameters can vary independently
but at the same time could also be performed in order to understand whether any
60
==PAGE:70==
5.2
Future Work
improvement could be achieved.
• Due to the fact that for the wind turbine manufacturer it would be more expensive to
build and install heterogeneous wind turbines within a wind farm, it could be inter-
esting to perform an optimization by implementing a penalty function which leads to
a cost increase whenever the optimizer generates a result which is out of the range of
the building and installing capacity of the wind turbine manufacturer.
• Future work could include optimizations with load and eigen frequency constraints, in
order to have more reliable results.
• The optimizations in this project were performed with a gradient–based method but
a comparison with other optimization approaches could also be relevant.
