Abstract
Within the field of Structural Engineering, Finite Element Analysis is ubiquitous, however discrepancies
between numerical models and the actual structure in question will always exist to some extent. To
remedy the inaccurate parameterization inherent to some of the discrepancy using experimental data,
the Finite Element Model Updating Problem has to be solved. In this thesis the use of Bayesian
Optimization as applied to Finite Element Model Updating problems will be investigated and compared
with the state of the art Black Box Optimizer: Covariance Matrix Adaptation-Evolutionary Strategy.
Various acquisition functions will be examined and a framework to extend Bayesian Optimization to
high-dimensional problems will be implemented: Trust Region Boundary Optimization. Finally Finite
Element Model Updating will be used within the context of fault detection in structures, to predict the
location and weight of a point mass placed on the structure in question.

1
Introduction
Finite element modelling of structures has become ubiquitous within the field of structural engineering
and vibration analysis. However when modelling the properties and responses of structures using Finite
element Analysis, inevitably inaccuracies are going to arise due to incorrect assumptions regarding
material parameters and boundary conditions as well as the discretization error inherent to FE modelling
that arises when modelling a continuous body as a discrete system of masses, springs and dampers.
To remedy these errors comparisons can be made using experimental data collected from the structure
in question. However experimental data contains errors of its own, most notably relating to noise and
resolution. Thus in order to obtain a valid model of the structure in question, both the experimental
and analytical data should be reconciled. This is usually done by solving the so-called iterative Finite
Element Model Updating (FEMU) problem - an inverse problem in which a set of parameters relating
to the analytical FE Model have to be adjusted using the experimentally measured responses of the
structure in question. FEMU however is not guaranteed to result in a perfect representation of the
structure being analyzed due to the presence of numerous sources of error which aren’t being factored
into the FEMU problem formulation (see figure 1.1 for a listing of analytical and experimental errors). In
particular FEMU is only ever good for remedying incorrect parameterization of analytical models. The
issue of modelling the structure correctly as well as setting the right boundary conditions have to be
made by the analyst in advance based on intuition. Sensor-Placement and resolution meanwhile have
to be decided based on the specific modes to be captured. Signal-Processing Errors, Discretization
errors as well as Noise cannot be fully removed, but remedied based on the choice of proper sampling
rates and filters as well as by making the analytical model sufficiently complex (but not too complex so
as to avoid overfitting) to capture the intrinsic properties of the structure in question.

Evolutionary Stratagems like CMA-ES [2] as well as
Bayesian Optimization Techniques [3]. In this thesis
Bayesian Optimization will be investigated for use in
high-dimensional FEMU problems and its performance
will be compared with a state-of-the-art optimizer, CMA-

Focus will be given on the exploration-exploitation

Vibrations and Dynamics
In this chapter the background theory behind the basics of vibrations and dynamics of Linear systems
is presented, culminating in the specific theory pertaining to the vibrations of Linear Isotropic Plates. In
addition, the processing of experimental data as well as its use in FEMU will be illustrated as well.
2.1
Dynamics and Vibrations of LTI SDOF systems
Vibration can be defined as motion that is periodic (i.e., repeats itself). In a physical system this is
realized through a means of storing potential energy as well as releasing it in the form of kinetic energy.
The former is done by an elastic (spring-like) force which in the linear case is described by Hooke’s
law, whilst the latter is done by the Mass of the system being accelerated by the stored energy as per
Newton’s second law. Denoting the displacement from equilibrium position (or position for short) by x
as well as its derivatives with respect to time of velocity by ˙x and acceleration by ¨x, we have following
relations describing the force stored by the system as well as the force released by the system:
Fpot = k · x
Fkin = m · ¨x

If the acting force on the system is 0, the system is said to be in free vibration. Above idealization

be adjusted by a (viscous) damping term, such that the modified equation of motion for the Single
Degree of Freedom System becomes (note that the effects of hysteretic damping have been ignored in
below equation):

2.4
Vibrations of Plates
For the special case of continuous bodies that can be modelled as plates i.e., a body which is significantly
longer in 2 of the dimensions that span it compared to the third and whose mid-surface (called the
neutral plane analogously to the neutral axis in beams) lies in a flat plane (if curvature in the mid-plane
exists, the body in question is called a shell), a special set of rules applies when modelling the dynamics
of aforementioned bodies. Analogous to the theory regarding beams which essentially can be regarded
as 1D specializations of plates, 2 main theories have been postulated regarding the dynamics of plates:
the Kirchhoff theory (which is analogous to the Euler-Bernoulli theory for beams) and the Mindlin theory
(which is analogous to the Timoshenko theory for beams). The main postulates of the Kirchhoff theory
for plates can be stated as follows [7]:
1. Normals to the undeformed Neutral Plane remain straight and normal to the deformed Neutral
Plane and unstretched in length
2. The effect of Rotary Inertia is negligible and thus neglected
3. No deformation occurs in the midplane of the plate
4. Transverse normal stress is not allowed

Above assumptions are approximately valid for plates in which the thickness is of order one tenth of
the planar dimensions. For plates which possess a larger thickness than this approximative cutoff
value, Transverse shear deformation as well as the effects of Rotary Inertia will be noticeable. Thus,
the Mindlin theory for plates relaxes the first 2 of the assumptions of the Kirchhoff theory [7]:
1. Normals to the undeformed midplane remain straight and unstretched in length, though not
necessarily normal to the deformed midplane
2. The effect of Rotary Inertia is included
In the following chapters both theories will be explained further.

2.5
Experimental Modal Analysis
Experimental Modal Analysis refers to a framework used to experimentally determine the dynamic
characteristics of a given structural component. It is very important within the topics of engineering
analysis due to the fact that it can be used to determine the reaction of a body to a range of excitation
frequencies. This proves particularly useful for a range of applications in mechanical & aerospace
engineering (a prominent example would be the design of Rotating Machinery and Accoustical devices),
electrical & control engineering (design of Electromagnetic motors as well as system identification as
related to control theory) as well as Civil engineering (design of bridges and buildings and damage
assessment). The basic concept behind Experimental Modal Analysis is to excite the body using
an excitation force (common ways to do this include by using an instrumented Impulse Hammer or
an Electromagnetic Shaker) and measure the resulting vibrations using a series of accelerometers
mounted on the component being tested. Special care has to be taken before testing to determine the
desired frequency range to be measured, the support conditions to which the component is subject
to (i.e. clamped, simply supported, free-free) as well as the placement of sensors and the position
of the excitation points. The sampling rate of the ADC (analog to digital converter) has to be at least
twice the highest desired measured frequency as per the Nyquist theorem. Once these preliminary
considerations relating to the physical setup have been completed, the choice on how to process
the data also has to be made. In order to determine the Modal characteristics of the system in the
frequency domain, Frequency Response Function (FRF) are used. FRF’s essentially are just the
output (accelerometer recordings) of the system divided by the input (excitation force) and can be used
to determine the reaction of a system to frequency-dependent excitation.

2.6
Finite Element Model Updating
Oftentimes when comparing the modal data obtained through experimental tests with the analytical
modal data obtained by solving the generalized eigenvalue problem 2.65, certain discrepancies can be
noticed between the experimental and analytical data. To reconcile the 2 models and thereby reduce
the discrepancy between them, so called Finite Element Model Updating techniques are applied. These
techniques are somewhat similar to system identification techniques in use for control theory, with the
main difference between them being that FEMU techniques do require an initial FEM model of the
structure in question and operate by varying the parameters of the structure (thus it is classed as an
inverse problem), whilst system identification models work by fitting a model (ARMA or polynomial)
to the data (thus being classed as regression). Generally we distinguish between 2 types of FEM
Updating techniques [13]:
• Direct Matrix Methods: For these types of FEM Updating techniques, individual elements in
the system matrices are adjusted directly from comparison between initial analytical model
predictions and test data. These methods require complete mode shape vectors as input (i.e.
experimental mode shape vectors should have the same dimension as analytical ones) and
are computationally very efficient, however they produce models that do not obey the FEM
formulations. [16] Examples of methods include Direct Matrix updating (DMU) and Error Matrix
Method (EMM).
• Indirect Physical Property Adjustment Methods (Iterative methods): The types of FEM Up-
dating techniques work by changing the physical or elemental properties of certain parts in the
model (an example would be to change the Young’s modulus/Stiffness of the Shaft in a FEM
of a Jet Engine). Methods in this group are iterative and require more computational power in
comparison to the direct Matrix methods. However their major advantage is their ability to work
with incomplete mode shape vectors (i.e. if experimental mode shape vector is lower in order
to the analytical ones) as well as returning models that obey FEM formulations. Examples of
methods include Eigendynamic Constraint (ECM) and Inverse Eigensensitivity (IES) (essentially
just least squares) methods and Response Function Methods (RFM). Most black box optimization
based FEM Updating Algorithms also fall in this category.
Due to Experimental Mode Shapes having the same dimension as the number of Sensors mounted
on the component, whilst Analytical Mode Shapes have the same dimension as the DOFs included
in the FE Model (which often number in the thousands), experimental and analytical mode shapes
almost never have the same dimension. Due to this Direct Matrix Methods work by either expanding
the experimental models using data from the analytical ones, or reducing the analytical models down to
the size of the experimental ones using for example SEREP (System Equivalent Reduction Expansion
Process) or Guyan decomposition. Due to their property of obeying FEM Formulations, Iterative
methods are often preferred among Modal Analysts. In addition to the distinction between Direct matrix
methods and iterative methods, one can furthermore classify FEM Updating algorithms based on
which data is used to encapsulate the discrepancy between the analytical and experimental models.
Methods that directly apply Frequency Response Functions are known as FRF based methods, whereas
methods that use quantities derived from FRF’s (for example natural frequencies, mode shapes or
pseudomodal energies) are known as Modal data based methods. Modal data based methods have
the disadvantage of discarding data containing information about the phase as well as damping ratios.
Due to this they are more sensitive to noise. When using FRF based methods (or methods using less
derived quantities like pseudomodal energies), damping ratios have to be factored in due to damping
affecting the shape of a given FRF (the more damping a given resonance peak has the wider it is).
The complex, non-isotropic and often-times non-linear nature of damping however means that FRF
based FEMU methods are associated with difficulties of their own. In this thesis only iterative modal
data based methods will thus be considered. Iterative methods rely on minimizing cost functions which
encapsulate the discrepancy between Analytical and Experimental models. Discrepancies in natural
frequencies are pretty straightforward to compute:

3.1
Introduction to Bayesian Optimization
Bayesian Optimization refers to a class of algorithms used for solving Black Box Optimization problems.
The Black Box part refers to the fact that we do not explicitly know the function f but only have access
to evaluations f(x) of it at certain samples x. Given the allowed sample space A = {x ∈RD : lbi <
x < ubi} of dimension Ndims and bounded by the lower bounds lbi and the upper bounds ubi. Thus
we can formulate the objective of the Black Box optimization setup by:
min
x∈A f(x)
(3.1)
The three main assumptions when working with Bayesian Optimization can be stated as follows [18]:
1. The Black Box function and its associated action space are continuous
2. The Black Box function is derivative free (i.e. when observing f we do not observe any derivatives
but only function evaluations)
3. f(x) is observed without any noise (this assumptions is relaxed for certain implementations of BO)
The second assumption means that gradient based optimizers cannot be used. However the lack of
gradient based information might be useful when optimizing certain non-convex functions (specifically
ones with several ”peaks” and ”valleys”) since it means that the optimizer is not as prone in falling
into local maxima as gradient based optimizers are. Bayesian Optimizers work by assuming that

3.1.1
Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS)
In order to initialize a Gaussian Process and establish a prior on f, we need to populate our model with
sample function evaluations (analogous to securing a training data set in machine learning applications).
To do this there exist various strategies, the most common being Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS). The
basic premise behind LHS is that samples obtained through random sampling may be clustered around
certain points depending on the seed used for the generator. To prevent this and secure an even
spread of initial data, LHS attempts to divide the data into a grid (as shown on figure 3.1), whereupon it
makes sure to sample once per row and column of the grid. LHS can be generalized to any number of
dimensions, making it useful for high dimensional optimization problems.

3.1.3
Acquisition functions
Many different acquisition function have been put forward over the years. In this section, 3 will
specifically be explained further: Thompson Sampling (TS), Upper Confidence Bound (LCB) as well as
Expected Improvement (EI).
Thompson Sampling (TS)
Thompson Sampling is the most straightforward way to implement a acquisition function. Given that
Gaussian process regression returns a surrogate function (Our current guess about how f behaves
based on the already sampled function evaluations f(x)) with mean µ and covariance σ, we can
generate a given number of randomly generated Sample points within the confidence interval as
suggested by our current model of f. Thompson sampling then works by taking the n best sample
functions as suggested by our model and taking the maxima of these functions as the next sample
points. It is important to note that if we assume no noise, the confidence interval at already sampled
observations will have converged (this won’t be the case for situations in which noise is assumed,
since the confidence interval in this scenario is merely reduced) and that these points thus never can
be picked again. The terminology for Thompson Sampling was originally derived from the Multi-Armed
Bandit Problems (MABs), however Thompson sampling for MABs is a radically different process than
for Bayesian Optimization since Thompson sampling for MABs means adjusting the probability of
choosing an option out of the set of possible actions by adjusting the parameters of a beta distribution.

Trust Region Bayesian Optimization (TuRBO)
Bayesian Optimization traditionally is reserved for problems which have low dimensions as well as
sample budgets (maximum number of samples allowable). This is due to the fact that the search
space grows exponentially with the dimension and as local optima become more plentiful, global
optimization becomes increasingly difficult. The most common approach used to modify Bayesian
optimization algorithms to high dimensional - high sample budget situations is by modifying them for
local optimization, this is done by partitioning the search space (dividing it into trust regions). Trust
Region Bayesian Optimization or TuRBO for short is one such algorithm, which divides the search
space into n trust regions. [24] Here each trust region is defined as a hyper rectangle of length
L = {l ∈RD : lbT R < l < ubT R} centered around the n best solutions x∗
n. The length in each
dimension is obtained by re-scaling the base Length with the length-scales λl from the GP model (thus
elongating the search in the direction of the maximum sensitivity) while maintaining the volume LD.
For nsucc consecutive successes (we define a success as finding a solution better than the current
best f(xn) > f(x∗)) we widen the dimension of the hyper-rectangle along the length-scale (up to the
maximum allowable length Lmax) whereas for nfail consecutive failures (we define a failure as failing
to find a solution better than the current best) we shrink the dimension of the hyper rectangle. If the
edge-length reaches a certain threshold Lmin, we say that the current run has converged, whereupon
we restart the process by again obtaining random samples (for example using latin hypercube sampling)
while retaining the previously obtained function evaluations of f. Note that the Gaussian Process doesn’t
use all of the samples obtained for training but only the ones in the current run (using all of the samples
would make training it a cumbersome and lengthy process). For the case that of more than one trust
region, TuRBO chooses in which trust region to sample next based on the current best theoretical
optimum proposed by each trust region. TuRBO has been proven to be able to compete with other
state-of-the-art algorithms for high dimensional high sample budget optimization including (CMA-ES).
[24] A further advantage of TuRBO is that its performance is invariant of the batch size unlike CMA-ES.

3.1.5
Sparse Bayesian Optimization (SEBO)
In many applications within the context of Black Box Optimization, in addition to minimizing the cost
function it is important the solution returned by the optimizer be as sparse as possible, that is as many
parameters are set equal to their nominal values as possible. The reason for this is to prevent over-
fitting, which could translate to minimal improvements in cost function in return for adding significant
modifications to the solution. This could have adverse effects when applying the optimizer to drug-
design or metallurgical problems, however in this thesis the concept of sparsity inducing optimization
will be explored within the context of fault detection and identification, due to the basic assumption
made in these fields that the initial FE model is generally descriptive of the structure in question and that
most discrepancies between the nominal model and the actual test structure in question are caused by
the presence of defects and faults. Within this context the usage of Sparse Bayesian Optimization
Sparse Bayesian Optimization or SEBO [26] will be investigated in this thesis.

or Strategies. These methods take inspiration from evolutionary processes occurring in nature, and
subject their population of candidates to crossover (combining the aspects of the candidates), mutation
(randomly introducing changes to the pool of potential candidate solutions) as well as selection
operations (choosing the best solutions for further processing) in order to reach the optimum. Due

The first of the 2 benchmark cases considered, involved the model updating of a Steel Pedestrian
Bridge based on an actual bridge located on the campus of the Georgia Institute of Technology. The

formulations using TuRBO-1 using Thompson Sampling and CMA-ES. After which they were compared
in order to conclude whether there a statistically noticeable difference in their performance when
compared to each other.

3.3.2
Simulated Fault Detection using FEMU
A major application of Finite Element Model Updating is to the topic of Fault Detection and Localization.
Here the purpose is to detect a decrease in stiffness or an increase in mass, due to either cracks
(resulting in a decrease of stiffness) or accumulation of residue (resulting in an increase of mass). It is
also important that the optimizer be able to localize the fault in order for it to be remediated. In general
damage detection can be quantified into 3 seperate levels [15]:
1. Determine the Presence of Damage
2. Determine the Location of Damage
3. Quantify the extent of Damage
Per definition FEMU attempts to perform level 3 damage detection, that is the localization and quantifi-
cation of damage. The ability of FEMU to perform fault detection depends significantly on the sensitivtity
of the faulty element(s) in question. [15] Before applying FE Model Updating to an experimental Fault
Detection scenario, it was decided to test it on purely simulated formulations in order to ascertain its
effectiveness. For this purpose 2 separate scenarios where formulated: A Crack Detection scenario and
a Point Mass Detection scenario. They constitute high-dimensional problems, where TuRBO should
have a clear advantage over standard Bayesian Optimization. [24]. Thus to ascertain the effectiveness
of TuRBO over regular Bayesian optimization and CMA-ES as well as to compare the performance
based of TuRBO based on the acquisition function applied, 5 test runs were made in each case using:
TuRBO-1 with Thompson Sampling, TuRBO-1 using Expected Improvement, TuRBO-1 using Lower
Confidence Bound (using β = 1), TuRBO-1 using Randomized Lower Confidence Bound (using θn = 6)
, CMA-ES, as well as Regular Bayesian Optimization with Randomized Lower Confidence Bound
to serve as a benchmark. In particular the point mass detection scenario was formulated in order
to ascertain the effectiveness of the listed optimizers when applied to an actual experimental fault
detection & localization scheme as discussed in subsection 4.4.2.

in both scenarios). The initial parameter guess, parameter values obtained for the crack detection

presence of fatigue related damage taken at face value. Furthermore, the mass of the sensor too was
inaccurately estimated, most likely due to the inherent ill-posedness of FEMU problems where mass

location 10 it was initially able to detect a point mass at location 11 without any regularization, however
the addition of even a minor regularization factor of 0.01 reduced the sensitivity significantly, leading
to the model becoming unresponsive to point mass faults. It was thus decided to use the material
parameters and spring stiffnesses obtained from the model refinement study in subsection 4.4.1, such

significantly and the parameters listed in table D.1 and D.2 were obtained. This in a sense can be said
to validate the model obtained in 4.4.1 and the theory that the plate was imparted a distributional mass
& stiffness through the bungee cords as well as point masses.

Based on the results obtained, it can be concluded that in none of the scenarios was the optimizer
able to exactly quantify the faults to the same extent as in the simulation-based studies in subsection
3.3.2. Based on the penalties & cost function evaluations obtained when placing the analytical point
masses at the same placement as their experimental counterparts for both regularization weights, it can
however be concluded that in none of the presented cases was this due to the optimizer failing to reach
the global minimum since the true penalties were higher than the optimized ones. It is thus likely that
this discrepancy most likely was caused by innaccurate modelling of boundary conditions(ie. neglecting
inertia of accelerometers, not accurately modelling the bungee cord), as well as experimental noise
and experimental errors when calculating the PSDs and FRFs to a lesser degree and any derived
quantities hereoff to a lesser extent). For all cases considered except for when the point mass was
located at location 9, the true cost function evaluations and penalties were lower than if no mass was
added at all, meaning that the model is sensitive to actual point mass type defects in the structure.
When initially having weighted the regularization term by 0.05, the optimizer was able to correctly
localize the point mass placements at Locations 1, 6 & 10, either detecting their exact position or the
vicinity of where they were located as well as approximate their magnitudes to a good extent. For all
the tested locations, pseudo point masses were detected in locations where no physical point masses
were placed (ie. at position 14 & 15 for the point mass placement at location 6 and at position 4, 14, 17
& 18 for point mass placement 1). For Point Mass Location 2 & 9 the optimizer was unable to detect
the true location and quantify the magnitude of the point masses to any meaningful extent (the largest
detected pseudo point mass being placed at location 15 for point mass 2 and location 11 for location
9). In order to minimize the instances of pseudo-point masses a second set of results were obtained,
where the regularization penalty was weighted by 0.1. Doing so seemed to increase the ability of the
optimizer to isolate faults substantially. For location 1 only one other pseudo-point mass at location 4
was detected, whilst the magnitude of the point mass at location 1 was increased so as to match the
actual one to a greater extent (117.11-129.54 g vs 181 g). Similarly for location 6, the pseudo point
mass at location 14 was reduced substantially (164.30 g vs 50.25 g), whilst increasing the magnitude
of the point mass located at the true placement (115.6 g vs 151.23 g). Even for locations in which
the optimizer had been unable to detect the precise location of the point mass (Location 2 & 9), the
magnitude of the actual point masses increased substantially whilst those of the pseudo point masses
shrank. Only for location 10 did increased regularization seem to have an adverse effect, such that the
true point mass nor its vicinity could not be detected at all. This can be explained given the fact that
location 10 is far less sensitive than the other locations tested so far (see B for sensitivities obtained for
the simulation based study), having a ARD length-scale more than twice (28.05 vs 13.83) that of the
next least sensitive location (Location 9) (it is important to note however that the sensitivities obtained
in the simulation-based case aren’t directly comparable to the experimental study). An interesting
trade-off thus can be observed between the ability to properly isolate faults (achieved by weighting the
regularization term higher) as well as detecting faults in less sensitive locations (achieved by weighting
the regularization term lower). Thus it can be concluded that in practical setting, when applying FEMU
to fault identification and detection, the sensitivity of the of the various parameters have to be taken
into account when determining the weighting on the regularization term. Furthermore, FEMU can at
most provide a guideline in determining (localization & quantification) any faults that the system might
be experiencing, subject to the quality of the FE model used to represent the experimental structure in
question, though in general the more sparse a solution is (how many of the parameters have been set
to their nominal values) the better it corresponds to physical reality within the context of fault detection.

Conclusion and Further Work
In this thesis the use of a high-dimensional Black Box Optimizer based on Bayesian Optimization
(TuRBO) was investigated and applied to the Finite Element Model Updating Problem with the stated
goal of reconciling analytical and experimental data. In benchmark problems consisting of a steel
pedestrian bridge as well a steel plate used for fault detection and identification it has successfully been
proven that TuRBO outperformed CMA-ES (another state-of-the-art optimizer for high-dimensional
optimization). For the simulation-based fault identification problem it was proven that TuRBO-1 using
LCB/RP-LCB was able to outperform TuRBO-1 using TS. The most likely reason for this is that LCB is
sparsity inducing being an analytical acquisition function and thus is particularly well-suited for fault
identification and quantification tasks, compared to TS which relies on random perturbations. For the
model refinement problem all the optimizers proved comparable in their ability, though CMA-ES did
(somewhat) outperform TuRBO due to the low-dimensional nature of the problem. The parameters
obtained from the model refinement study were for the most part within the realistic bounds for structural
steel, though Young’s modulus was set rather high (higher than even most forms of high-tensile steel),
leading to the conclusion that the Bungee Cord as well as the accelerometers imparted a certain
distributed stiffness and mass to the plate. When tested for its performance as applied to fault detection
and localization, TuRBO managed to obtain solutions within a good tolerance of the global optimum in
the theoretical study (within 0.01-0.02). When applied to fault detection using actual experimental data
TuRBO was able to locate and quantify the point masses to a satisfactory degree for locations 1 & 6,
while it failed at properly isolating the point masses at locations 2 & 9. Even for the cases where the
point masses had been successfully localized, pseudo masses were added to the analytical model by
the optimizer. To reduce the instances of pseudo point masses an increased regularization penalty
was applied. While this was associated with a better ability to isolate and quantify faults, it lead to the
inability of the optimizer to detect point masses at less sensitive locations (location 10). When explicitly
optimizing for sparsity using SEBO with L0 regularization, it was observed that when applying SEBO
to the case of the experimental point mass at location 6, the optimizer failed to detect the location of
the actual point mass. It can thus be concluded that while sparsity is a desirable trait in fault detection
and identification, care should be taken when explicitly optimizing with respect to sparsity. The inability
of the optimizer to correctly isolate and quantify the point masses at location 2 & 9 almost certainly be
explained as being caused by incorrect modelling of the boundary conditions of the plate, due to the
fact that the optimized configurations resulted in a lower cost function evaluation than the analytical
configuration corresponding to the true experimental configuration.

In a practical setting, FEMU thus can be used as a guideline when detecting and remedying faults,
subject to the level of correspondence between the initial FE model and the experimental setup. Further
work could include applying the developed Bayesian Optimizers to more complex structures as well as
include the effects of (proportional) damping when solving the FEMU problem (for damping estimation
FRFs or less derived quantities like pseudomodal energies have to be used).[15] TuRBO based FEMU
could also be used to perform FE model selection in order to find the best model to represent a
particular structure. In particular Jin et al. used mixed integer non-linear programming to perform model
selection using FEMU, where each model was associated with a categorical value in addition to the
updating parameters. [35] Since TuRBO already has been adapted to work using mixed continuous and
categorical variables, it could easily be adapted for model selection. [36] The use of regularization within
the context of Bayesian Optimization as well as how to optimally set the weighting on the regularization
term in order to ensure desired levels of sparsity need to be explored further. Based on the conclusion
reached in 3.3.1 that under-determined FEMU problems are inherently ill-posed, TuRBO could be
modified so as to include uncertainty quantification, making it more applicable to under-determined
problems. [29]

1.0 INTRODUCTION 
Congratulations on the purchase of a quality ICP® 
industrial sensor. In order to ensure the highest level of 
performance for this product, it is imperative that you 
properly familiarize yourself with the correct mounting 
and installation techniques before attempting to 
operate this device.  If, after reading this manual, you 
have any additional questions concerning this sensor or 
its application, feel free to call an Application Engineer 
at 716-684-0003 or 800-959-4464. 
 
Proper sensor selection requires special attention to 
three 
main 
areas: 
sensor 
design, 
dynamic 
expectations, and application environment. 
 
Sensor design encompasses the actual sensing 
element, the physical material, and component 
selection for the sensor. Preferred industrial 
accelerometers employ a shear sensing element 
with either a quartz or ceramic crystal. 
 
Quartz sensing elements are typically used when 
long-term stability and minimum output shifts due 
to temperature changes are desired. Ceramic 
sensing elements provide excellent resolution and 
durability in noisy environments, and can be 
designed to supply low-frequency and high-
frequency measurements. Shear-design sensors are 
preferred because of their inherent insensitivity to 
adverse environmental influences, such as case or 
base strain and thermal transients. Internal case 
isolation and shielding is important in avoiding 
erroneous signals resulting from ground loops 
and pick-up of electromagnetic and radio 
frequency interference. Other critical material 
selection criteria include non-magnetic stainless 
steel housing, hermetic sealing, and industrial 
military connectors. See Figure 1. 
 
 
 
 
Dynamic expectations are application-specific and 
refer to the frequency range of measurement and the 
anticipated amplitudes of vibration. After careful 
review of the machinery to be monitored, minimum 
and maximum measurement frequency ranges may 
be 
established. 
The 
minimum 
measurement 
frequency is normally related to any sub-harmonics of 
running speed or any lower frequencies where 
vibration data is to be collected. The maximum 
measurement frequency of interest is determined by 
the maximum number of harmonics of an event like 
running speed, bearing frequencies, or gear mesh. 
This measurement frequency range should be well 
within the specified frequency range of the sensor. 
 
Amplitude range refers to the anticipated levels of 
vibration to be measured. These values are related 
to the alarm levels set for the machine. By carefully 
evaluating the idiosyncrasies of the machinery, the 
predictive maintenance engineer can estimate the 
minimum expected vibration levels and ensure that 
the electrical noise floor of the accelerometer is less 
than those levels.

2.0 ICP® ACCELEROMETERS 
Enclosed in the back of this guide is a Specification 
Sheet, which lists the complete performance 
characteristics of the particular sensor. 
 
All ICP® sensors require a constant current power 
source for proper operation. A typical sensing 
system includes an ICP® sensor, ordinary two 
conductor cable and a basic constant current power 
supply (as shown in Figure 2). 
 
 
The signal conditioner consists of a well-regulated 18 
to 30 VDC source (battery or line-powered), a 
current-regulating diode (or equivalent constant 
current circuit), and a capacitor for decoupling 
(removing the bias voltage) the signal. 
 
The current-regulating device is used in place of a 
resistor for several reasons. The very high dynamic 
resistance of the diode yields a source follower gain 
which is extremely close to unity and independent of 
input voltage. Also, the diode can be changed to supply 
higher currents for driving long cable lengths. Constant 
current diodes, as shown in Figure 3, should be used in 
ICP® signal conditioners. (The correct orientation of the 
diode within the circuit is critical for proper operation.) 
Except for special models, standard ICP® sensors require 
a minimum of 2 mA for proper operation.  
 
 
The typical limits for this type of diode are to a 4 mA 
maximum rating; however, several diodes can be placed in 
parallel for higher current levels. All line-powered signal 
conditioners should use higher capacity (up to 20 mA) 
constant current circuits in place of the diodes, particularly 
when driving long signal cables (see Section 5). 
 
Decoupling of the data signal occurs at the output stage 
of the signal conditioner. A 10 to 30 µF capacitor coupled 
with a resistor shifts the signal level to essentially 
eliminate the sensor bias voltage. The result is a drift-free 
AC mode of operation.

5.0 CABLING 
The selection of connectors and cables has a direct 
impact on the ruggedness and reliability of the sensor 
installation. A consideration when dealing with cables is 
the way in which the cable conductors are terminated.  
ICP® accelerometers are internally amplified, two-wire 
accelerometers. Connections to the sensor require two 
leads: one for the power and signal, and the other for the 
common and signal return. Often, coaxial cables are used 
since only two conductors are needed. Coaxial cables are 
less expensive. With coaxial cables, however, erroneous 
signals can be introduced into sensor systems through 
ground loops, electromagnetic interference, or radio 
frequency interference (EMI or RFI).  To avoid ground 
loops, there should only be one ground in the system.  
 
It is recommended for permanent installations that the 
sensor be case-isolated and internally shielded with a dual-
case design and use a two-conductor shielded cable to 
insure clean vibration signal transmission. Two-conductor 
shielded cables allow the signal and the signal return 
(common) to be fully shielded from the sensor to the 
readout equipment. To insure that ground loop signals are 
not induced, the shield should only be terminated at one 
end. Typically, the shield of a two-conductor shielded 
cable is left open or not connected at the sensor end and 
is tied to earth ground at the instrumentation end. 
 
Operation over long cables may affect the frequency 
response of ICP® accelerometers, and introduce low 
frequency noise and high frequency distortion when an 
insufficient current is available to drive cable capacitance.  
 
Unlike charge mode systems, where the system 
noise is a function of cable length, ICP® sensors 
provide a high voltage, low impedance output well-
suited for driving long cables through harsh 
environments. While there is virtually no increase in 
noise with ICP® sensors, the capacitive loading of the 
longer cable may be distort or filter higher frequency 
signals depending on the supply current and the 
output impedance of the sensor.  
 
Generally, this signal distortion is not a problem with 
lower frequency testing within a range up to 1,000 
Hz. However, when monitoring higher frequency 
vibrations traveling over cables longer than 500ft, 
the possibility of signal distortion exists.

reason, do not supply excessive current over short cable runs 
or when testing at elevated temperatures.  
 
5.1 CABLE CONNECTOR PROCEDURE 
Care and attention to installation is essential, as the 
reliability and accuracy of your system is no better than that 
of the output cable.  
 
STEP 1: Ascertain that you have ordered the correct cable 
type.  
 
As with sensors, no cable can satisfy all applications. Special 
low-noise cabling should be used with high impedance, 
charge-output devices. ICP® sensors usually operate with 
any ordinary tow-wire cable. Industrial applications often 
require twisted/shielded cables to reduce the effects of EMI 
and RFI. PTFE-jacketed cabling may be necessary to 
withstand corrosive environments.  
 
STEP 2: Connect the cable to the accelerometer. A small 
amount of thread-locking compound placed on the 
connector prior to attachment helps secure the cable during 
testing. In harsh environments, the connection can be 
sealed with silicone rubber, O-rings, and flexible heat-shrink 
tubing. 
 
STEP 3: Plug the connector of the cable into the mating 
sensor connector. Then, holding the sensor stationary, 
secure the connector in place by tightening down the 
attached threaded cable sleeve.  
 
STEP 4: Route the cable to the signal conditioner, making 
certain to stress-relieve the sensor/cable connection and 
minimize motion by clamping the cable at regular intervals.  
 
Common sense should be used to avoid physical damage 
and minimize electrical noise. For instance, avoid routing 
cables near high-voltage wires. Do not route cables along 
floors or walkways where they be stepped on or become 
contaminated. Shielded cables should have the shield 
grounded at one end only, normally at the instrumentation 
end.  
 
STEP 5: Finally, connect the remaining cable end to the 
signal conditioner or readout device. To dissipate charge 
that may have accumulated in the cable short the signal to 
ground prior to attachment.

9.0 TROUBLESHOOTING 
Piezoelectric sensors are dynamic measuring equipment. 
They use piezoelectric sensing elements to convert or 
transduce the mechanical phenomena to an electrical 
signal. The mechanical parameter may be force, pressure 
or vibration. The raw electrical signal from a piezoelectric 
charge element is a high impedance signal. This charge 
signal is normally converted to a low impedance voltage 
signal by either an external charge amplifier or an external 
voltage amplifier. The cables between the charge sensor 
and the amplifier must be high quality, low noise cable 
and must be kept as short as possible. Figure 15 shows a 
typical high impedance system.  
 
Figure 15. High Impedance System Connection 
 
Internally amplified sensors employ miniature 
amplifiers to convert the high impedance charge 
signal into a low impedance voltage signal. These 
amplifiers are internal to the sensor, and therefore, 
do not require low noise cables or external 
amplifiers. These amplifiers have set gain so that 
output sensitivities are standardized.  
 
Figure 16. Sensor with Integral Electronics System 
Connector 
ICP® sensors are two-wire sensors. They are 
powered with a constant current DC source. The 
power supply is typically 18 to 30 volts DC current 
limited via a constant current between 2 and 20 mA. 
Typical battery operated supplies offer 2mA of 
constant current to extend battery life while 
continuous monitoring systems offer more current in 
order to drive longer cables. Figure 16 shows a 
typical system for a sensor with integral electronics. 
 
The signal output of a sensor with integral 
electronics is a low impedance voltage signal 
proportional to the dynamic measurement such as 
force, pressure, or vibration. This voltage signal is 
carried on a DC bias voltage. The AC dynamic signal 
is superimposed on the DC bias voltage and is 
allowed to swing between the supply voltage and 
ground as shown in Figure 17. Unlike an operational 
amplifier that requires a plus and minus supply and 
allows the signal to “ride” on ground and “swing” 
between the plus and minus “rails,” the sensor with 
integral electronics requires the output signal to be 
DC biased.  
 
Figure 17. Sensor DC Bias Voltage 
 
This DC bias voltage is an excellent diagnostic tool. 
The voltage provides a means of verifying that the 
amplifier is “turned on.” Typical input/output power 
supplies will block this DC bias voltage at the output 
via a blocking capacitor in order to AC couple the 
signal to readout devices. By “teeing” off the input 
into a DC volt meter, as shown in Figure 18, the bias 
voltage can be measured.  
 
While measuring the supply voltage, the bias voltage 
can be measured agter the sensor is plugged in. If 
the meter stays at supply, something in the system is 
open or not connected. If the meter reads “0,” 
something in the system is shorted. If the meter 
reads within the bias voltage range of the sensor, 
then the sensor and cabling are functioning properly.
