Abstract
Passenger satisfaction within the air transport business is highly influenced by the quality of the
baggage handling. Further, the mishandling of baggage comes at a great expense for an airport, as
it must compensate for every bag lost to the passenger. In 2021, transfer mishandling accounted for
25% of all mishandled baggage worldwide. Optimising the transfer baggage handling process has
the potential to decrease compensation expenses and increase passenger satisfaction. The process is,
however, highly complex as it includes many uncertainties in the different processes that should be
aligned and with an increasing number of passengers every year, this only becomes more complicated.
Currently, the research within the area is very scarce and only few model solutions have been proposed
considering the uncertainties of the environment to this day, all which have in common that the models
are computationally heavy and thus time consuming to run.
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate how optimisation and simulation can be used to optimise
the transfer baggage operations in hub-airports. The models are developed with focus on improving
the daily transfer baggage operations, enabling the application on all hub-airports, producing robust
results in a system influenced by uncertainty and ensuring fast running time. The transfer baggage
problem (TBP) is formulated based on the generalised assignment problem, and since the TBP exists
within a dynamic environment influenced by uncertainties, both a deterministic and stochastic solution
approach is used. The developed models are able to find optimal solutions with an efficient running
time. This is possible due to an extensive amount of data preprocessing which have removed the time
variable from the model formulations. The two solution methods are compared to each other based
on real-life data from Copenhagen Airport. It is concluded that the expansion of the deterministic
model through stochastic simulation performs better than the deterministic model and the stochastic
simulation is, therefore, used to analyse the sensitivity in the system within Copenhagen Airport.
The solution is further developed so it can be used as a strategic tool to identify critical transfer
connections and thus, when mitigating actions should be performed and further, how specific facility
alterations can help improve the robustness of the system.

Introduction
The air traffic industry plays an important role in today’s life and global economy. The number of
passengers have increased in the last decades with 2020 as an exception due to COVID-19 that rapidly
disrupted the growth [13, 5]. It is, however, expected that the air traffic will experience a significant
growth by 2035 where the volume of passengers will be doubled [3] compared to 2018, according to
the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO). The main reason for this development is the
economic and demographic growth that has enabled the growing middle-class to travel more. Another
factor is the emergence of low-cost airlines offering competitive prices on popular destinations.
The tendency of the increasing number of passengers also leads to an increase in the number of transit
passengers which puts higher pressure on hub-airports - large airports used for transit by hundreds of
millions of passengers every year, world wide. Hub-airports are competing on the minimum connection
time (MCT) which is the amount of time needed to send both passengers and their luggage with the
departing transfer connection. The MCT in many hub-airports is currently 45 minutes and, if the
airport is not able to complete the individual transits within this time frame, the airport is required
to pay compensation to the airline and to the affected passenger. Therefore, there are potentially
high costs connected to the transfer baggage operations and as delayed or lost baggage can also lead
to highly dissatisfied passengers, it is crucial to plan these operations meticulously. The Baggage IT
Insights 2021 provided by SITA [32] shows that 69% of mishandled bags are caused by delayed bags
and 37% of these are due to transfer mishandling. This highlights the room for improvement within
this area. Planning transfer operations, however, is extremely complex as many processes, resources
and schedules need to be aligned, which all include different levels of uncertainties. With the increased
number of passengers, this only becomes more complicated, as the utilisation of all resources also
increases, which results in even higher uncertainties. This forces hub-airports to continuously improve
their transfer baggage operation processes, in order to respond positively to the increasing passenger
development and to remain competitive and attractive for both airlines and passengers.
The above mentioned need for optimisation establishes the foundation of this thesis, where it will be
investigated how the transfer baggage operations can be formulated as a transfer baggage problem
(TBP) and solved using optimisation and simulation methods. This thesis is written in cooperation
with Copenhagen Airports A/S (CPH) who has provided the data instances used to solve the TBP.
CPH has agreed to share their data as they are interested in knowing how their new baggage handling
facility [2] can be used in the most optimal way in the existing system in terms of distributing flights
in a way that minimises the number of missed bags. The models developed in this thesis are, however,
generic and can be used within any hub-airport.
1.1
Scope of the Thesis
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate and develop an efficient solution to the TBP that is
applicable in the real-world and flexible enough to be applied in different hub-airport layouts. The
developed solutions are based on the generalised assignment problem (GAP), and since the thesis
considers a dynamic environment influenced by high uncertainty, both a deterministic and stochastic
1
==PAGE:10==
solution approach has been used. To test the applicability of the solutions to the TBP and whether
the solutions fulfil practical requirements of the real-world, the solutions were tested on data from
CPH. Thus, all interpretations of the results are specific to the instance of CPH. The field of research
for this thesis is a combination of operations research (OR) and operations management (OM). This
thesis applies the Cross Industry Standard Process for Data Mining (CRISP-DM) methodology as
a framework to ensure that the implemented models is well aligned with the business objectives of
CPH. Therefore, the thesis does not focus solely on providing a solution which can improve the daily
transfer baggage operations in a hub-airport, but also on providing a solution that can be applied as
a strategical decision tool. The scope, therefore, includes implementing a tool that highlights when
CPH should perform mitigating actions within the transfer baggage handling process. Moreover, the
scope includes performing scenario analysis to demonstrate how the model can be used, to investigate
the sensitivity of the infrastructure in CPH, and which actions would help increase the robustness of
the infrastructure.
Though the focus of this thesis is to optimise the transfer baggage operations by solving the TBP, the
solutions found can be utilised in similar assignment problems that exist at airports where uncertainty
likewise is an important factor. The model solutions presented in this thesis do, if deemed valuable,
have the potential to optimise several of the operations within airports.
1.2
Outline of the Thesis
The remainder of the thesis is structured as follows; Chapter 2 provides an introduction to CPH,
including an introduction of their expansion project. Chapter 3 briefly introduces the operations
conducted in an airport and provides insides into the terminology applied within an airport. Chapter
4 reviews the existing literature on optimisation in airports with a focus on optimisation of baggage
handling processes and optimisation within CPH. Chapter 5 describes the methods and theories
applied in the thesis and chapter 6 describes the transfer baggage handling process in detail and
introduces the general TBP. In chapter 7, the precalculations and structure of the data that are
necessary for simplifying the model solution for the TBP are introduced. This is followed by the
formulation of the deterministic model in chapter 8 and the deterministic model with stochastic
simulation in chapter 9. After the general TBP solution is introduced an explanation of the metrics
used to evaluate the results of both the general TBP and for the instance of CPH are introduced. The
specific instance of CPH are introduced in chapter 11 and includes a general business understanding of
the airport, a presentation of the provided data, estimations of parameters and necessary prepossessing
to run the deterministic model. In chapter 12, a computational experiment is performed based on the
data from CPH which presents the results of the deterministic model and the stochastic simulation.
In chapter 13, a benchmarking comparing the results of the two solution approaches is conducted.
Chapter 16 discusses the findings of the deterministic model and the stochastic simulation and their
advantages and limitations as well as key findings for the daily function and infrastructure in CPH.
Finally, chapter 17 concludes on the most important findings of the project and chapter 18 highlights
various suggestions for future work to improve the solution to the TBP.

Copenhagen Airports A/S
Copenhagen airport was founded in 1925 and was one of the first airports to manage civilians in the
world [6]. The airport is situated eight kilometres southeast of Copenhagen’s centre and 24 kilometres
from the Swedish city Malmö. The airport is an essential part of the general infrastructure and
transport system in Denmark and southern Sweden. 20% of the airport’s passengers are transfer
passengers and the airport is considered to be the main transfer airport in Scandinavia [22].
Before the COVID-19 pandemic in 2019, the airport serviced 30 million passengers annually. Due to
Covid-19, the air traffic industry has been in recession all over the world, however, CPH expects to
reach 30 million passengers again in 2024, and a further growth of 33.3% by 2030 which corresponds
to 40 million passengers.
As Copenhagen airport is positioned in the outer area of Copenhagen city centre the available land is
limited thus making it difficult for the airport to expand in order to meet the increasing passenger
demand. Due to this constraint, it is of great economic importance for CPH to use their existing
capacity and resources as efficiently as possible. In their constant strive for this, CPH has managed
to become a leading airport when it comes to the use of operation research optimisation techniques
and, combined with extensive peer network and knowledge sharing, CPH has been able to use state-
of-the-art methodologies for strategic planning and in their daily operations [22].
The tendency of the increasing number of passengers and competition forces CPH to continuously
improve their processes and remain competitive and attractive for both airlines and passengers [4].
CPH has focused their strategy to obtain this goal partly through the T3 Airside Project [2] that
will build a new baggage handling facility by expanding Terminal 3 with 80.000 m2. More specifically,
the project includes an expansion of the baggage hall, a new and bigger baggage handling system
for arriving baggage, better passenger flow and new entertainment, more space for passengers and a
bigger area for passport control. Today, CPH has the capacity to handle 30 million passengers on a
yearly basis and with the new facility they should be able to handle 40 million passengers. First part
of the project will be implemented by 2023 and the entire construction is estimated to be completed
by 2028/29. CPH has invested approximately 4 billion DKK to date and a total investment of 20
billion DKK is expected to complete the project, which makes it the biggest in CPH’s history.
CPH will face new challenges when launching the facility and it is essential for the business that it is
utilised in the most optimal way. This is a complex task, firstly, because the new baggage handling
system needs to be incorporated with the existing one and, second, as baggage handling in general
is complicated with the many different sub-processes that need to be aligned. From an economic
perspective, baggage handling is a large cost driver due to the labour intense processes and, therefore,
efficiency within this area plays an important role [9]. Moreover, the quality of baggage handling is
critical as it often affects the passenger directly and because it is one of the biggest influence factors
for passenger satisfaction. Poor baggage handling will be connected directly with the airport even
if it is caused by the airline or ground handlers. Lastly, baggage handling influences the subsequent
events in the baggage flow and delays can lead to delayed flights, terminal and roadway congestion
and blockage of parking positions or handling equipment for longer duration.

In order to ensure that CPH can meet the increasing demand and that passengers will choose CPH
as preferred gateway as well as airlines choosing CPH as a preferred transfer airport, it is highly
relevant to investigate how the new facility can be utilised in the most optimal way in the existing
setting.

Airport Operations
Airports are complex systems that interacts with both airlines and passengers. These should be taken
into account when planning and performing airport operations in order to function successfully. In
general, the processes of airport operations can be divided into two categories; airside and landside
operations [10, 7]. Airside operations concerns air traffic control processes such as routing, departure
and arrival planning, runway scheduling and staffing. The landside operations are connected to the
terminal such as staff management at check-in counters, baggage handling, security, maintenance
of facilities and information management. Furthermore, airport operations include non-aeronautical
activities such as food and beverage sales, duty free shops etc.
Baggage handling constitutes one of the most important services at airports both in terms of op-
erational efficiency, safety and passenger satisfaction [24]. Baggage handling consists of three main
processes: Outbound, inbound and transfer baggage. Outbound baggage consists of entering baggage
from passengers that are departing from the airport. Inbound baggage are baggage belonging to
passengers arriving at their final destination and needs to be reclaimed at baggage carousels. Transfer
baggage are baggage that arrives with inbound flights and leaves with outbound flights [9].
Most of the processes related to the different baggage types, are handled in the baggage handling
system (BHS). The BHS is an essential part of the baggage handling infrastructure and has a high
influence on the quality and time efficiency of the airport operations [9]. More concrete, the BHS is
a network connected through conveyor belts that scan, store, sort and distribute bags to the correct
container or carousel, depending on the baggage type. There are different entry points, based on the
baggage location; if the baggage arrive with an incoming flight it is loaded into the BHS through
infeed stations close to the parking stands and if the baggage is checked-in by the passenger at the
terminal counters, it is loaded into the BHS from conveyor belts at the check-in counter.
There are two big factors that influence the efficiency and the outcome of the baggage handling
processes: the infrastructure and the apron [9]. The infrastructure concerns the operations of the BHS
and the check-in facilities in the terminal which are controlled by the airport operators. This process
is supplemented by the apron processes which refers to the transport and loading of baggage which
is performed by ground handlers and is usually in the responsibility of third party companies. The
handling of baggage is thus influenced by operations that are both within and outside of the airport’s
responsibility. The operations the airport controls are; flight to gate assignment, baggage security,
baggage handling infrastructure, sorting of baggage and baggage transfer inside the airport building.
The operations that are not controlled by the airport are the allocation and resource scheduling of
ground handlers together with the container handling outside the airport building.
4
==PAGE:13==
This project focuses on the transfer baggage operations and the assignment problem that occurs when
incoming flights are assigned to different infeed areas connected to the BHS. The assignments should
minimise the time it takes for the transfer baggage to reach the connecting flight in order to minimise
the number of total missed bags. The BHS transports and sorts both baggage from the check-in
counters and transfer baggage to containers from which the baggage is transported to the outgoing
flights. If the baggage do not have time to reach the connecting flight through BHS, the baggage is
often labelled as "short transfer" and loaded onto a vehicle that will drive it directly to the departing
flight. Many unforeseen events can influence the transfer baggage operations which makes the planning
complex. An example is the uncertainty within the scheduled arrival times (sta) which can arise due
to, for instance, bad weather conditions which will cause the flight to be delayed. This highlight the
relevance in investigating a solution to the assignment problem that takes potential uncertainties into
account. This is a highly complex task and even though many of the airport expenses are related
to missed bags, there is limited literature on solving the TBP. However, research on optimisation of
other airport operations can be found in the literature and since a resemblance exists between some
of these processes and the transfer baggage operation, these are relevant to study.

Literature Review
In the following section, the results of a systematic literature review is presented to firstly highlight
the existing research within optimisation of airport processes with focus on baggage handling. Fur-
thermore, the existing research on optimisation of transfer baggage handling is highlighted and finally,
optimisation projects in general performed at CPH is presented.
4.1
State of the Art
In general the research on optimisation of airport operations is limited, especially within baggage
handling. The most common fields in OR at airports are related to ground handling work tasks as
well as stand and gate allocation for aircrafts.
The problems related to ground staff covers both staff scheduling, work demand modelling and trans-
portation problems. Both Clausen [16] and Herbers [21] studied how these problems could be solved
with various optimisation models. Clausen has studied the dynamic shift design problem through two
different approaches; a construction heuristic and a local search metaheuristic based on simulated
annealing. The objective for both models focused on finding the best sequence of shifts by considering
several objectives; overstaffing, understaffing and general shift cost that was weighted differently in
the objective function. Furthermore, Clausen studied dynamic route planning for airport personnel
transporting passenger with reduced mobility through a simulated annealing based heuristic. The
objective was to maximise the quality of service by scheduling as many of the passengers as possible,
while ensuring a smooth transport with short waiting times.
Herbers used the vehicle routing problem with time windows and a large neighbourhood search
heuristic to study the workload levelling for ground staff in airports. The objective aimed at avoiding
sharp demand peaks, which were difficult to cover by staff, by minimising the sum of the squared
5
==PAGE:14==
resource requirements. Herbers also studied the shift planning problem through a constraint based
method for local re-optimisation of the shift plans. The objective was to minimise shift cost and
qualification penalties with different weights.
Another aspect of the ground handling work tasks covers managing the check-in counters. Parlar and
Sharafali [26] studied the allocation of airline check-in counters with focus on a queuing optimisation
approach. The study used a stochastic dynamic programming model to determine the optimal numbers
of counters to open over a specified time window. The objective aimed to minimise the check-in counter
cost. Other optimisation methods such as simulation using C++ and Python can also be found in
the existing research on optimisation of check-in allocations and early baggage storage [20].
All above mentioned research focuses on optimising ground handling work tasks. An aspect that
influences the demand for ground staff resources, is the scheduling of landing aircrafts. Boysen and
Fliedner [14] focuses on balancing ground staff resources with the aircraft landings. An exact dynamic
programming approach and two heuristic start procedures was developed with objective functions
aimed at evenly distributing the number of landed passengers over time. Furthermore, a product
rate variation problem was utilised to balance landings per airline where the objective function
minimised the maximum absolute difference between the actual number of landings per airline and
the ideal number. Lastly a dynamic programming approach was used to balance the number of
landed passengers per airline. Besides the scheduling of aircrafts, research has also been conducted on
optimising the stand and gate allocation of aircrafts. Ding et al [18], for instance, modelled the airport
gate assignment problem as a quadratic assignment problem where the objectives are to minimise the
number of un-gated flights and the total passenger walking distance.
Research on the handling of in- and outbound baggage is limited, as previously mentioned, but few
researchers have looked into how the related processes can be optimised. Frey [19] used a guided
fast local search in a greedy randomised adaptive search (GRASP) procedure framework to minimise
baggage carousel’s utilisation as well as passengers’ walking distance to carousels and waiting time
for inbound baggage. Barth [10] optimised the outbound baggage handling using a GRASP approach
and decomposition approach which included splitting the problem into subproblems which could be
modelled as mixed integer programming problems. More specifically, the research focused on allocating
each flight with outbound baggage to a handling facility and assigning them a handling start time.
The objective function consisted of several criteria; minimise transportation time, maximize stability
of solution, maximize robustness of schedule, balanced distribution of flights to handling facilities,
balanced distribution of flights to regions, minimize excess usage of sorting space at a facility and
minimize excess flights at facilities. The models were tested on real world data from Frankfurt Airport.
Barth further made a static model for optimising the assignment of baggage carousels using an extended
assignment problem approach with a multi-criteria objective. Some of the model criteria concerned
the minimisation of the direct cost, balancing the use of baggage carousels and baggage claim halls,
as well as maximising customer satisfaction with regards to e.g. waiting time.
The literature available on OR optimisation of transfer baggage handling is even more scarce than
the literature on in- and outbound baggage handling. Barth is one of the few who have made research
within this area and has in several papers [11, 12] developed models that optimises the handling of
6
==PAGE:15==
transfer baggage. The first paper focuses on assigning transfer bags from each arriving aircraft to an
infeed area into the airport infrastructure. The objective functions minimises the number of missed
bags and transportation time, while ensuring a fair distribution of the workload and robustness. The
problem is solved using a commercial MIP-solver and the use of the model in the dynamic environment
during daily operations is introduced. The second paper is an extension of the first paper as stochastic
and a semi-stochastic models are introduced and optimised over a finite set of scenarios. In the semi-
stochastic model all short term decisions are stochastic while long term decisions are deterministic.
Both studies are build on data from Frankfurt airport.
Additionally, Clausen [17] has investigated the TBP through a vehicle routing problem approach and
weighted greedy algorithm that works in an dynamic context with a focus on optimising the baggage
truck routes. The objective function minimises the number of undelivered bags. All transfer baggage
handling models mentioned above are generic and can thus be applied on different airports.
When focusing the research on Copenhagen Airport specifically the amount of literature is very sparse.
Justesen [22] has studied some of the problems mentioned above in Copenhagen Airport such as the
check-in counter allocation problem, stand and gate allocation problem and basic resource allocation
problem. Furthermore, Pisinger and Rude [28] have studied the optimisation of in- and outbound
baggage in CPH using advanced scheduling algorithms to improve chute allocations and support arrival
processes. Currently, few researchers have focused on the generic optimisation of transfer baggage
handling processes at airports but none has focused on CPH specifically. Therefore, there is a solid
foundation for investigating how OR can be used within this field.

Method and Approach
In the following sections, the methods applied to solve the TBP are described in detail. The generalised
assignment problem is presented as it lays the foundation for how the deterministic model is formulated.
Furthermore, the solution approach to modelling uncertainty is presented with focus on the Monte
Carlo sampling approach to stochastic optimisation. The Rossetti method used to determine the
sample size and tune the stochastic simulation is presented and finally, the framework, CRISP-DM,
used to have a structured solution approach to the problem is described.
5.1
The Assignment Problem
The assignment problem is a fundamental combinatorial optimisation problem, in which the objective
is to assign a number of agents to a number of tasks, such that the cost is minimised or the total profit
of the allocation is maximised. Within the assignment problem many different extensions exists.
In this thesis the generalised assignment problem (GAP) is applied. GAP includes a capacity constraint
in which the agents have a size and the tasks have a capacity restriction, and for each assignment of an
agent it must apply that the capacity of the task is not exceeded. GAP appears in many problems such
as vehicle routing, facility location, resource scheduling and flexible manufacturing systems [1].

the problems of a reasonable size to optimality. In this thesis the modified GAP is solved utilising a
MIP solver.
5.2
Modelling Uncertainty
The modified GAP is solved through a deterministic model thus using a static input. However, as
the airport’s daily operations include high uncertainties due to unforeseen events such as delays
and disruptions, the results of the deterministic model does not represent a system influenced by
uncertainties. Such problems is categorised under the realm of stochastic optimisation, that comprises
modelling and methodology for optimising models of systems influenced by uncertainties. Stochastic
optimisation allows the inclusion of uncertainties in the formulation of optimisation models, by
representing these as random variables.
One approach to solve the stochastic optimisation problem is through the two-stage stochastic pro-
gramming approach, in which a number of scenarios are established with a corresponding probability
for the event occurring. Each scenario is optimised and the best solution is found by considering the risk
and result of all scenarios. However, in this case, of modelling uncertainty through random variables,
it would require a significant amount of scenarios, which would be computationally inefficient.
8
==PAGE:17==
Instead, this thesis applies a heuristic approach to solve the stochastic problem using Monte Carlo
Simulation. Monte Carlo Simulation enables one to apply randomness to a system through random
variables that are modelled on the basis of a probability distribution. Moreover, the Monte Carlo
Simulation provides a probabilistic estimate of the uncertainty in a model. The sampling of the
random variables can be performed in different ways, as there exists many types of Monte Carlo
sample methods. In this thesis the sample average approximation (SAA) approach [27] is used to
model the uncertainty of the system.
The SAA is an approach used to solve stochastic optimisation problems by utilising Monte Carlo
Simulation. In this approach the expected objective function of the stochastic problem is approximated
by a sample average estimate derived from random samples. The resulting sample is then solved using
a deterministic optimisation algorithm. To obtain good candidate solutions this process is repeated.

solution is found within a reasonable time frame. To determine the sample size of the number of

5.4
CRISP-DM
This thesis concerns the transfer baggage handling operations in airports using data from CPH. To
ensure that the solution process is reliable and can be efficiently repeated, it is important to have
a structured approach to the problem. The CRISP-DM (Cross industry standard process for data
mining) is a methodology that is used as a framework for data mining projects. This method ensures
that the process is standardised when conducting a data science project and moreover that important
aspects of the project is considered. Utilising this framework, it is ensured that the project is structured
and that all aspects are reevaluated thoroughly for each time new information has been discovered.
The method is based on six phases and an iterative approach that ensures frequent evaluations of the
model findings against the business objective(s) - this ensures that the analysis is kept on track and
solves the business problem of interest. Moreover, this ensures contact with relevant stakeholders, and
gives both parties the opportunity to adapt and change the objectives, according to the new findings.
An overview of the iteration phases included in the method can be seen in Figure 2
The six phases of the method seen in Figure 2 are Business Understanding, Data Understanding, Data
Preparation, Modelling, Evaluation and Deployment. Business Understanding is the initial phase and
includes a thorough assessment of the current business situation. Within this phase, the exact business
objectives and data science goals are determined which considers the requirements and restrictions
related to the problem. Furthermore, a preliminary plan for the project is developed. The Business
Understanding phase is connected to the Data Understanding phase which is the second phase. Several
iterations between these two phases often exists, as it requires a familiarity with the available data
in order to properly formulate the data science problem. In this phase, it is essential to identify data
quality problems and discover the first insights that can help navigate the scope of the project, through
the data possibilities.

The next step is the Data Preparation phase which covers all activities connected to preparing the data
such that it can be used as an input for the model. It is also in this phase, relevant features are selected
and where data is transformed, structured and cleaned. The Modelling phase is where appropriate
modelling techniques are selected and applied. In some instances, it is essential to continuously revisit

requirements, defined by the business objectives.
When such a model has been obtained the last step can begin, which is the Deployment phase. This
phase consist of deploying the code and the model into the operating business system, as well as

document the solution and a detailed description of the data preparation steps that are essential for

The Transfer Baggage Problem
To improve the general business understanding, the transfer baggage operations processes within an
airport will be elaborated. Furthermore, the TBP will be explained with focus on aligning the problem
with essential business objectives. Additionally, important factors that influences the problem will be
described.
6.1
The Transfer Baggage Handling Process
Transfer baggage processes concerns the handling of baggage from passengers that are in transit
and are merely changing aircrafts at an airport. Transfer passengers accounts for a large part of the
airports overall revenue, thus, it is important for hub-airports to be favoured by both passengers and
airlines. In order to be the preferred airport, the quality of the transfer baggage operations must be
high in terms of minimising the number of missed bags and keeping the MCT low. These are, thus,
the two most important competing factors between hub-airports.
The transfer baggage process includes various activities between the arrival of an incoming aircraft
and the departure of the connecting flight. The process of the transfer baggage handling can be seen
in Figure 3.
When the aircraft touch ground, it drives to its assigned parking stand where it is put ’on blocks’,
locking the aircraft’s position. When the aircraft is parked, the ground handlers work begin. First
an unloading of the bags is initiated followed by transport to the infeed areas. The aircraft can have
the baggage stored in one of two ways, depending on the aircraft type. Either the baggage is bulk,
meaning the bags are packed as individually lose items, or else the baggage are packed in containers.
During the unloading, if the baggage is bulk, the baggage is transported individually via a conveyor
belt onto a vehicle. Transfer bags and inbound bags are usually separated manually at the aircraft
side, and if necessary put into different vehicles and transported to different infeed areas. In case that
the baggage is stored in containers, it will be unloaded onto the vehicles in containers as well. In this
case inbound and transfer bags are transported in one trip and are not separated until it reaches an

infeed area. Ultimately, it is the ground handlers responsibility to ensure that inbound and transfer

When the vehicle arrives at the infeed area, the baggage are loaded into the BHS. However, the
vehicles operate after the first in first out (FIFO) principle, which means that the vehicle that arrives
first at the infeed area will also unload the baggage into the BHS first, regardless of the urgency of
the baggage it is handling. It is thus not possible to prioritise baggage at the infeed station, and if
the loading spots are occupied the vehicle will have to wait until a spot is available.
Once a spot is available at the infeed area the baggage are loaded into the BHS. The BHS is an
essential part of the baggage handling infrastructure, as it is the cross point of the three baggage
handling procedures related to inbound, outbound and transfer baggage. Therefore, the BHS has
a high influence on the quality and time efficiency of the airport operations. The ground handlers
manually load the baggage onto one of the BHS’ transfer conveyor belts, that will then transport
the baggage to the sorting system belts of the BHS, where it will be merged with outbound baggage
coming from the check-in counters. On the transfer belts, before entering the sorting system, the
baggage is scanned and data is collected to ensure that the baggage is distributed correctly. The data
includes information about the number of bags on each flight and the departing flight’s appertaining
storage box. All bags in the sorting system, both transfer and outbound baggage, are transported by
the sorting conveyor belt until they reach their assigned storage box. When the conveyor belt position
matches the storage box, the baggage will be pushed down a chute and into its assigned storage box
where outbound and transfer baggage belonging to the same flight will be mixed.
If the connecting aircraft has not yet started its handling process, the baggage from the arrived
aircraft is placed in early baggage storage (EBS) within the BHS, until a storage box is opened for its
departing flight. At last the ground handlers pick up the storage box and the bags are transported to
the departing flight where it is loaded, either bulk or in a container, onto the departing flight where
it will be stored until take-off. In some cases a short connection time may necessitate that baggage
are transferred directly to connecting flights (tail-to-tail).

The transfer baggage process and the complexity is significantly influenced by the airport layout, as
the layout affect the process time of several procedures and can thus be a major cost driver. This will
vary for the different hub-airports around the world and have an influence of how relevant the model
will be for the given airport. The stated transfer baggage handling process are however almost similar
for all hub-airports in Europe, with only small variations.
6.2
Problem Description
The transfer baggage problem concerns the assignment problem that occurs when incoming flights
are assigned to the different infeed areas. The assignments should minimise the time it takes for the
transfer baggage to reach the connecting flight, in order to minimise the total number of missed bags
which is the most important business objective of hub-airports.
The choice of assignment influences different processes that are a part of determining the total time
taken, for the transfer baggage to reach the departing flight:
• Transport time from aircraft to infeed area; If the assigned infeed area is close to the parking
position of the aircraft, the transport time will be shorter compared to parking positions further
away.
• Queue at infeed area; If the infeed area is already working at full capacity, waiting time will
occur before new baggage can be loaded into the BHS.
• Manual handling at infeed area; The time it takes for the baggage to be loaded onto the conveyor
belts depends on the number of ground handlers available at the infeed area and how fast the
they can process the baggage.
• Transport time through BHS; This concerns the transport time for baggage travelling from
an infeed area to a storage box. This transport varies dependent on the distance between the
assigned infeed area and the location of the storage box.
All of the above mentioned processes are impacted by the choice of infeed area and have limited
capacities which, therefore, influences the choice of the best assignment. The transport time is limited
by the speed of vehicles transporting the baggage from the aircraft to the infeed areas. The manual
handling at the infeed areas are limited by the speed of the baggage handling and the amount of
ground handling resources available. Furthermore, the time through BHS is limited by the number
and speed of conveyor belts at the assigned infeed area. The queue at infeed area is dependent on
both the manual handling capacity and conveyor capacity.
The considered objective is to utilise the existing capacity to minimise the multi-criteria objective
which includes the number of missed bags.

Data Structure and Precalculations
The following sections will introduce how certain data structures and precalculations have been
managed in order to simplify the deterministic model presented in chapter 8 as much as possible. This
first section describe how the capacity of the infeed areas are defined and structured according to
predefined time-intervals. The next section describes how the arriving flights are connected to the

the model can manage the critical time periods through postponement options is presented. Finally,
the precalculation of the number of missed bags for each arriving flight is explained.
7.1
Manual Handling and Conveyor Capacity
Time is an essential factor in the TBP and has a large influence on the decision making when assigning
the baggage of an aircraft to an infeed area. A factor that has a high influence on the baggage handling
time, is the potential creation of queues which occur when there are more baggage incoming than
the available capacity can handle. The risk of queue creation is highest during peak hours, and could
result in a delay of the baggage transferred to the departing flight. This potential congestion, therefore,
needs to be managed appropriately in the model to minimise the risk of queue at the infeed stations
and thereby minimise the number of missed bags.
As mentioned, the queues occur when there are more baggage incoming than the available capacity can
handle. Therefore, it is essential to track how much capacity is available in predefined time intervals in
order to capture potential conflicts. To incorporate both time and capacity constraints in the model it
is firstly necessary to define the different types of capacity limitations that are present at each infeed
station.
The capacity at the infeed stations can be divided into two different types; manual handling and
conveyor speed. The manual handling concerns the ground handlers ability to transfer the bags from
the container onto the conveyor belt and is expressed as the number of bags transferred per minute.
The conveyor capacity is related to the speed of the conveyor belt and is likewise expressed as the
number of bags transferred per minute. To ensure that the model respects these capacity types in a
predefined time interval, time needs to be included in the model. To accommodate for the time aspect
in the model one could create a 3-dimensional decision variable that keeps track of the time for each
arriving flight. This enables the model to continuously track queue and keep track of time. However
this structure will induce a long running time when testing all possible assignments. Therefore, in this
project, time is handled outside of the model in order to get a simplified mathematical formulation
and shorter running time. This does however require heavier data preprocessing, in terms of creating
a relation between time and flights.
7.2
Identification of Critical Time Intervals
A critical time interval, is an interval in which the number of arriving flights exceeds the capacity
available within the given time interval. When including only these time intervals in the model, a long
running time is avoided. In order to create a relation between arriving flights and time, all arriving

aircrafts have been grouped into both 10 and 30 minute intervals, based on the baggage time of arrival
at the infeed station. The 10 minute intervals represents the capacity for the manual handling and
the 30 minute intervals represent the conveyor belt capacity at the infeed areas. The manual capacity
is based on the 10 minute intervals to ensure that if a conflict arise within the work of the employees,
it is captured fast. The conveyor belt capacity is set to 30 minutes to capture if potential congestion
arise in the system.
The following sets are introduced: FA is the set of all arriving transfer flights fa ∈FA connected to
a departing flight fd ∈FD for a specific day. Set S is the set of all parking stands s ∈S to which
an arriving flight can be allocated and set I is set of all infeed areas i ∈I. The baggage arrival time
at the infeed station is based on the aircraft’s scheduled time of arrival (stafa), the unloading time
(u) and the time it takes for the ground handlers to transport (ds,i) the baggage from the arriving
aircraft (fa) at parking stand (s) to one of the infeed areas (i).
taifa,i = stafa + u + ds,i
(10)
The first time interval begins at 00:00 and ends at 23:59 which results in 144 intervals in the 10
minute grouping and 48 intervals in the 30 minute grouping. For more information about the grouping
and data structure, see Appendix A.1. This grouping enables the determination of potential capacity
conflicts within each time interval, and introduces a relation between flights and time without the use
of a 3-dimensional decision variable.
Assuming that the conveyor belt capacity is lower than the manual handling capacity, it will be possible
to have conflicts within the manual capacity which does not exists within the conveyor capacity and
vice versa. Assuming that the conveyor belt capacity is higher than the manual handling capacity,
conflicts will always occur within the manual handling capacity when conflicts arise in the conveyor
capacity.
An airport normally has more than one infeed area, located in different places which will result in
different transportation times for the baggage and thus different arrival times at the infeed areas.
Therefore, the flights involved in a conflict is not necessarily the same flights for the different infeed
areas when considering the same time interval. Thus, the conflicts at each infeed area should determined
separately.
To describe the conflicting time periods, the following set is introduced; Ci
α which is the conflicting
time periods at infeed area i using capacity type α where α = 1 corresponds to manual handling
capacity and α = 2 corresponds to conveyor capacity. The set is used in the model such that the
model only considers time periods where a potential conflicts may arise, and are thus used as conflict
constraints in the model. As mentioned prior, this is implemented in order to reduce the running
time.

7.3
Postponement Options
The identification of conflicting time intervals are based on the assumption that all flights are send to
the same infeed area. However, in reality some of these conflicts will be avoided by one of the following
options: 1) assign some flights to another infeed area or 2) wait at the parking stand until capacity
is available at the infeed area. The first option is already taken into account as the model is based
on the GAP. In order to accommodate for the second option, additional prepossessing is necessary to
allow a postponement of transferring baggage to infeed area. In this thesis, these the postponement
options are set to 0, 10 and 20 minutes and are defined by the set p ∈P. Incorporating the possibility
of postponement in the model, will remove some of the limitations that FIFO gives. The model will
be able to prioritise according to urgency instead of arrivals, where urgency refers to the flight with
the highest risk of missed bags. This approach allows the model to control the queues and potentially
avoid congestion and long unexpected waiting time during peak hours. The model can then determine
which assignment is most profitable, in terms of minimising the total number of missed bags, based
on the different postponement options.
7.4
Calculation of Missed Bags
The number of missed bags is denoted missfa,i and is the primary parameter in the model, as the
model makes its decision based on minimising the number of missed bags.
In practice, a bag is missed when it is not loaded onto its departing flight. This is mainly dependent on
the time it takes to transfer the bag from its inbound flight to its outbound flight, which is described
in section 6.1. However, a study [11] in Frankfurt Airport, showed that a more realistic estimate of
the number of missed bags can be formulated as a function of its buffer time.
The buffer time is determined by the time between the scheduled time of the arriving and departing
flight subtracted by the transport to infeed area, unloading and the time through BHS:
btfa,fd,i = stdfd −stafa −ds,i −u −BHSi −k
(11)
Where stdfd is the scheduled time of departure for flight fd, stafa is the scheduled time of arrival for
flight fa, u is the time taken (in minutes) to unload baggage from the aircraft, ds,i is the time taken
(in minutes) for ground handlers to drive from the parking stand of the arriving flight s to infeed
area i, BHSi is time taken (minutes) through BHS dependent on infeed area i and k is the time the
storage box closes. In the original paper [11] k is not a constant but the time it takes to drive from
the storage box location to the departing flight.

9
Stochastic Simulation
In the model from section 8 most factors such as the arrival and departure times of the flights
are predefined and assumed to be deterministic. The model only considers uncertainty in terms
of calculating the number of missed bags based on a probability. In other words the deterministic
model reflects the results of an almost perfect world, where everything runs according to schedule
and all labour resources work at full capacity at all times and at a constant pace. However, airports
are highly influenced by both external and internal factors disrupting the system that are out of
their control. Severe weather conditions, mechanical failures, heavy traffic at the airport and labour
resources are typical causes of such disruptions. The deterministic approach does not consider these
potential disruptions, thus making the model less robust when deviations from the estimated plan
occur. Therefore, these uncertainties should be taken into account in the model in order to make it
more realistic and to generate more reliable results when making day-to-day planning.
To address disruptions and uncertainties that are present at airports and moreover, to improve the
robustness of the model, the deterministic model is expanded with a stochastic simulation using the
method described in section 5.2. The developed heuristic is from now on referred to as stochastic
simulation. In this thesis, focus is restricted to disruptions in the scheduled arrival and departure
times and resource related deviations in terms of unloading and transportation time. Besides not
addressing the uncertainties, the deterministic model does not consider the potential queue that may
arise at the infeed areas when several trucks arrive in the same time interval. Further it does not
address the time it takes to load the baggage onto the conveyor belts at the infeed areas. This also
contributes to the generation of unreliable results from the model. The waiting time within these
potential queues and the load time are thus also included in the stochastic simulation in order to
make it more realistic.
In the stochastic setup it is expected that the deterministic assignment will result in a significantly
larger number of missed bags when introducing stochastic data. On the contrary, it is expected that
the stochastic simulation, through numerous iterations, is able to adapt its assignment such that the
number of missed bags is minimised as much as possible when including uncertainties. Normally, the
stochastic simulation is expected to converge towards the output of the deterministic model but since
the load time and time in queue is added, it is expected that the results will converge towards a higher
value.
9.1
Queue handling
As described in chapter 7, the deterministic model handles conflicting time periods by including the
option to postpone the time at infeed area by 0, 10 or 20 minutes. This gives the model the opportunity
to avoid capacity violations and at the same time work around certain FIFO limitations. However, the
deterministic model work with capacity in a fixed manner, meaning that a certain amount of capacity
is set for a given time interval. Therefore, the model does not take notice of whether the demand
for capacity occurs at the exact same time or if it is spread out over the corresponding time interval.
However, this is an important factor as if all baggage arrives at the same time, conveyor belt and

9.2
Stochastic Simulation Description
In the stochastic simulation, the TBP is considered under uncertainty. As previously mentioned, the
incorporation of random disruptions is crucial in constructing effective flight-to-infeed assignments in
terms of minimising the number of missed bags.
The stochastic simulation is based on the method described in section 5.2 using the same structure
and elements as presented in Figure 1. The assignments from the deterministic model (section 8) is
used as input for the stochastic simulation. Within the stochastic simulation, a predefined number of
simulations of how the baggage handling process is carried out with the specific assignment and random
disruptions is performed. The disruptions considered are within the flight arrival and departures times,
unloading and transportation time. The number of missed bags for each simulation is computed and
the average number of missed bags across all simulations is used to update the expected number of
missed bags, for the specific assignment, in the objective function in the deterministic model. The
deterministic model is run again with the new number of missed bags and the entire process is repeated
a predefined number of times with a fixed number of simulations for each iteration. It is expected that
the stochastic simulation will adapt its assignment such that the number of missed bags is minimised
as much as possible when including random disruptions. The model adapts by using realistic estimates
as input in the deterministic model which contains information that enables better decision-making
for the deterministic model after each iteration.

11.3
Parameter Estimations
As mentioned in chapter 6.1 the transfer baggage handling process consist of several steps. The time
spent on each step can vary dependent on the infeed area and therefore, these have an influence on
which infeed area is chosen in the TBP. Since the times of the process steps, for each infeed area,
are not given directly in the data provided by CPH, it is necessary to estimate them using other
information and assumptions. In the following sections the driving time, unloading time, time through
BHS and capacity will be estimated.
11.3.1
Estimation of Driving Time
The time it takes to drive from the parking stand of the arriving flight to the infeed areas is estimated
by the distance driven and the speed of the vehicle. In Figure 7, the location of the parking stands
at CPH are illustrated, as well as the anticipated legal driving routes at the site, that are marked
with a red stippled line. The distance between the parking stands and the two respective infeed
areas, East and West, have been measured on the map in cm with the scale ratio being 1cm:95m.
From an interview with CPH [29], it was found that the allowed speed for the airport vehicles are 15
km/hour. With this information the driving time from each parking stand to both infeed areas could
be estimated. The estimated driving times can be seen in Appendix A.4. It can be assumed that the
estimations contain some uncertainty, as the measurements may not be precise.
11.3.2
Estimation of Unloading Time
As some of the necessary data for calculating the unloading time contain large uncertainties, the
unloading time is instead defined through CPH’s estimates, that were obtained in an interview [29]. In
the interview it was found that it takes 3 minutes to open the doors that gives access to the baggage
on the aircraft. The baggage are, as mentioned in section 6.1, stored in the aircraft as either bulk
baggage or in a container. If the baggage is bulk, the ground handlers can unload 12.5 bags per minute.
If the baggage comes in containers, it takes the ground handlers 3 minutes to unload 2 containers,
with a maximum capacity of 80 pieces of baggage per container. As the total number of bags on the
arriving flights (inbound and transfer), as well as the ratio of bulk baggage contra container baggage,
are unknown, the baggage is assumed to always be in containers with a maximum of 160 pieces of
baggage on average. The total unloading time will hence be 3 minutes for opening the door to the
baggage compartment and 3 minutes to unload two containers onto vehicles, summing up to a total
unloading time of 6 minutes. This estimate contains uncertainty as the unloading times varies a lot
depending on the number of inbound and transfer bags in each arriving flight.

11.3.4
Estimation of Capacity
The manual handling capacity is estimated to 15 bags per minute per transfer belt, based on an
interview with CPH [29]. This means that for infeed area East with two conveyor belts, the capacity
corresponds to 30 bags per minute. Infeed area West has five conveyor belts. However, since the belts
in West are used for both inbound and transfer baggage the capacity needs to be reduced to reflect
the capacity allocated to transfer baggage exclusively. CPH’s key facts and figures [8] shows that
around 20% of the annual passengers are transfer passengers whereas approximately 40% accounts for
inbound passengers, and 40% for outbound passengers. It is assumed that the number of passengers
reflects the number of baggage. Therefore, the capacity of the belts in West are reduced to 33% of
the full capacity, resulting in a total capacity in West of 25 bags per minute.
The conveyor capacity is estimated to 20 bags per minute per conveyor belt [29]. However, as the
conveyor belts are not self-contained, but connected to the sorting system, the speed will also be
influenced by the sorting system’s capacity. A constant flow of transfer and outbound baggage into
the sorting system would create a congestion at the merging point and cause an interruption in the
conveyor belts’ flow. Therefore, the sorting system’s capacity is important to consider when estimating
the conveyor capacity. As mentioned, 40% of passengers are outbound passengers and 20% transfer
passengers. Therefore, it is assumed that only a third of the baggage entering the sorting system will
be transfer baggage. The capacity of the transfer conveyor belts is thus reduced to 33% of the 20 bags
per minute per conveyor belt. Further, it is assumed that the sorting system’s conveyor belt runs at a
slower pace than the transfer conveyor belts as it needs to be able to tip the baggage into the storage
boxes. In order to compensate for this reduction in speed, the capacity of the transfer conveyor belts
is reduced by an additional 30%. This results in a capacity estimation of 4.6 bags per minute per
conveyor belt. This means that for infeed area West with five conveyor belts, the capacity corresponds
to 23 bags per minute and for infeed area East with two conveyor belts the capacity corresponds to 9
bags per minute.

minimisation of the number of missed bags is the most important business objective for CPH [29].

model, it does make sense to perform a tuning. A beta value of β = 1 would naturally give the lowest
number of missed bags, however, this would make the capacity constraints irrelevant. This will also
eliminate the impact of the capacity breach part of the objective function completely and thus the
indication of when a queue will arise in the deterministic model. Therefore, it is chosen to set the
beta value of β = 0.9 for the computational experiments.

12.2
Stochastic Simulation
In this section the uncertainties applied in the stochastic simulation will be explained, as well as
a tuning to determine the number of simulations and iterations that should be used when running
the stochastic simulation. Finally, the results for the computational experiment of the stochastic
simulation will be presented.
12.2.1
Uncertainty Functions
In the instance of CPH, the biggest uncertainties within the scope of this thesis and the provided
data from CPH are found to be scheduled time of arrival and departure, unloading time and driving
time, thus the uncertainties for these four parameters will be presented in the following.
Uncertainty on Scheduled Time of Arrival and Departure
The deterministic model uses the sta and std when calculating the number of missed bags for each
flight. However, the ata and atd of each flight often differs from the sta and std and by assuming
that these parameters are deterministic, a lot of uncertainty is put into the model. Therefore, the
deviations related to sta and std are investigated and modelled in order to create simulations that are
based on realistic disruptions in the stochastic simulation.
The delays for the arriving and departing flights can be described as: Delayarriving = ata −sta and
Delaydeparting = atd −std. In Figure 12 and 13 the density function for the delay of the arriving and
departing flights from the data provided by CPH is plotted together with a fitted normal distribution
density function. The normal distributions are based on a paper that investigates uncertainties on sta
and std within the 10 largest airports in USA [25].
From Figure 12 and 13, it is observed that the raw data density functions follows the normal distri-
butions nicely. The mean of the fitted normal distribution for the delays of arriving flights is -2.14
minutes with a standard deviation of 9.84 minutes. For the delays of departing flights, the mean of the
fitted normal distribution is -1.54 minutes with a standard deviation of 10.49 minutes. This indicates
that only a few flights arrive and depart exactly on-time and that many flights are either delayed or
early with around 10 minutes. The mean indicates that most flights arrive and depart a bit earlier
than estimated. If the departing flight leaves earlier, then the probability of missed bags increases as
the buffer time between the arriving and departing flight decreases. For arriving flight the probability
of missed bags increases, if the flight arrives late.
The normal distributions will be used to model the uncertainty within sta and std in the stochastic
simulation. The large variance within the normal distributions and the fact that the mean is not zero,
will have an influence on the value that the stochastic simulation will converge towards.

Uncertainty on Unloading Time
In the deterministic model, unloading was set to a fixed time of six minutes, due to the data needed
to calculate the actual time being unreliable. Further the data was missing information on whether
the arriving flight contained bulk baggage or container stored luggage as well as the total amount
of baggage on the flight. Therefore the unloading time estimate contributes to a big uncertainty
in the model. In order to make better estimates, a sampling from historical data could be made.
First a grouping of historical unloading times should be made dependent on the amount of baggage
present on the flights. Then each flight should replace their current unloading time with an unloading
time sampled from the group that they fit into. Unfortunately it was not possible to obtain this
historical data. Therefore, it was agreed through correspondence with CPH [30] to make use of a
normal distribution with a small variance. The uncertainty for unloading time is therefore chosen to
have a mean of 0 minutes with a standard deviation of 0.5 minutes.
Uncertainty on Driving Time
Driving time is set to a fixed time, dependent on the parking stand of the flight and the chosen infeed
area. The time is calculated based on a measurement made on paper and adjusted according to a given
ratio. Uncertainties appear in this estimate both due to the difference in the distance calculated and
the actual distance at the airport, and due to the speed of the vehicle being fixed, which will rarely
happen since the vehicle both carries different weights at each trip and since the speed is controlled
manually by an employee at the airport. Preferably a data collection of the driving times between
the various parking stands to the two infeed areas should be made, with enough data to make an
analysis of how it differs from the fixed time. However, due to limited time it was chosen not to request
permission for such data collections. Instead it was agreed in a correspondence with CPH [30] to use a
normal distribution with a small variance. The uncertainty for driving time will thus follow a normal
distribution with a mean of 0 minutes and a standard deviation of 1 minute.
12.2.2

To ensure that the stochastic simulation runs enough simulations S and iterationsH to find an optimal
solution but also no more than necessary due to computational reasons, a tuning of the two parameters
are performed. The Rossetti half-width method described in section 5.3 is used for the tuning of S
as each simulation is independent. The desired half-width is set to 2% of the mean, meaning that a
variation of ±2% from the mean of the number of missed bags is accepted, as this would be reasonable
from a business perspective. Furthermore, the risk is set to 5% meaning that a 95% confidence interval
is used. The results from the applied method on S simulations for each day type, can be seen in Table

practical approach where a steady-state plot is made to visually determine the number of simulations.
In Appendix A.7 a steady-state plot for each of the three day types can be found. Comparing the two
methods, it can be concluded that the steady-state approach for all three instances results in a lower
number of needed simulations. Since the theoretical approach is considered the more accurate one,
the numbers are higher than the steady-state approach and the running time is fast for the model, it

12.3
Conclusion of Computational Experiments
The deterministic model and the stochastic simulation has been used on the three data instances
provided by CPH, representing a slow, normal and busy day in terms of transfer baggage.
Overall, the results of the deterministic model and stochastic simulation both showed the tendency
that an increase in the number transfer bags resulted in an increase in the number of missed bags.
Further, the results showed that East is the preferred infeed area and that it is more profitable to
violate the capacity and thus send flights into a queue in East than to send them to West. This is
due to the chosen weights of the beta value. Had the beta value been smaller, the importance of
the capacity breach would have been higher for the model. Additionally, the results showed that the
postponement options were not used in the stochastic simulation. This can be explained by the queue
in East resulting in fewer missed bags than if the flights were postponed. It can, however, not be ruled
out that postponements would be valuable to include if longer postponement options were introduced.
The deterministic model did not show any flagged transfer connections, however, this was not the case
for the stochastic simulation where flagged transfer connections were present in all instances. When
stochastic data is introduced to the model, CPH is no longer able to avoid having flagged transfer
connections. This indicates that the additional capacity that comes along with the T3 Airside Project
is not enough to handle the current demand of a slow, normal and busy day instance, without some
of the baggage are guaranteed to be lost.

Figure 16 clearly shows that the stochastic simulation experiences a significant increase from the
deterministic model in both the objective value and the number of missed bags. With the step-wise
elimination of the different elements the value decreases. Overall it is observed that the stochastic
simulation without any of the added elements decreases and converges towards the same value as the
deterministic model. This confirms that the stochastic simulation computes the number of missed
bags correctly. When investigating the individual impact of the elements in the simulation step-wise,
it is observed that the queue has no remarkable impact on the busy day instance, as both metrics only
decrease slightly. However, when eliminating the load time from the simulation, both metrics decrease
approximately 36%. This indicates that the load time, has a bigger impact on the objective value
than the time spent in queue. It makes sense that the load time has a big impact, as large batches of
baggage can extend the transfer process time in up to 15 minutes, which has a significant impact on
the buffer time. From the queue having a smaller impact, it can be interpreted that the flights are
more evenly spread out in the data instances of CPH.
When observing the uncertainties’ impact, it is observed that the transport and unloading uncertainties
have almost no impact. This can be explained by their distributions which both have a mean of zero
and a small variance. This means that the added stochastic data will have a regression towards zero and
only have a small impact in the objective value when running a sufficient amount of simulations.
On the contrary, it is observed that the uncertainty added to the sta and std impacts the stochastic
simulation notably, as both metrics decline when the uncertainty is removed from the model. This
especially concerns the uncertainty for std, that has a decrease of 43%, while the uncertainty for sta
has a decrease of 28%. These large changes relates to the distribution explained in section 12.2.1 and
also seen in Figure 15. For both std and sta the variance is large, which naturally will result in large
fluctuations in terms of delays and too early arrival/departure. If the variance is reduced for these
distributions, the impact would also be smaller. Furthermore, the mean for sta is negative, which is
in favour of the number of missed bags as more flights will arrive before schedule resulting in a longer
transfer time. However, the mean of std is also negative which is not in favour of the number of missed
bags, as it indicates that more flights will depart before time. This explains the larger decrease when
the uncertainty for std is removed.
This investigation has elucidated how the different elements impacts the stochastic simulation. It
showed that the load time and the uncertainties for sta and std account for the majority of the reasons
as to why the results of the stochastic simulation has such a significant increment. To reduce this
difference in the deterministic and stochastic simulation, it would be necessary to include queue and
load time in the deterministic model. Moreover, to reduce the difference further the estimates of sta
and std has to be more precise, such that the variance was smaller and mean centred around zero
which would make the model become less sensitive towards these uncertainties. These changes would
result in a convergence value closer to the deterministic model in the stochastic simulation.

14.2
Conveyor Belt Breakdowns
In the scenario regarding an increase in demand, it was seen that the capacity breaches were one
of the most sensitive metrics, with large spikes for different instances. It is therefore interesting to
investigate the capacity and its impact upon the model.
Airport operations are highly influenced by unforeseen events such as a malfunction in a conveyor
belt. These events reduces the available capacity to execute a work task, making it difficult to manage
the baggage handling process within the expected time interval. This can potentially lead to large
bottlenecks and influence the remaining operations on the given day. It is, therefore, essential to
investigate scenarios in which the capacity of the conveyor belts are reduced.
This scenario investigates how sensitive the infrastructure is in the event of capacity loss due to an
unexpected breakdown in the conveyor belts. Furthermore, the scenario investigates which facility
that has the largest impact on the number of missed bags and thus creates the largest disruptions
in the transfer baggage operations, if a conveyor belt breaks down. This can give an indication if
the results of the model is highly dependent on a specific facility and moreover give an indication on
which facilities the airport should be more aware of, and potentially be prepared to mitigate in case
the event occurs.
The model is run on the busy day data instance and to examine the impact of one or more breakdowns
in both facilities 6 different scenarios are created. Each of these scenarios is compared with the baseline,
being the assumption that all conveyor belts function perfectly.

14.3

two prior scenario investigations in section 14.1 and 14.2. The strong preference to infeed area East

are a necessity to sustain a smooth operation. Since the transfer belts are used more at infeed area

the need for maintenance at infeed area East and make the infrastructure less sensitive towards
potential breakdowns. Therefore, this scenario investigates the impact on the model’s solution, if a
balance constraint is added in the stochastic simulation that ensures a balanced distribution of the

Discussion
In the following sections, the key findings from the analysis will be discussed. Firstly, important
decisions for the model implementation will be discussed in terms of their impact and alternative
solutions. Subsequently, the advantages and limitations of the deterministic model and stochastic
simulation will be highlighted and the reliability of the results will be discussed. The sensitivity of the
system is discussed based on the findings in the scenario analysis where both the scenario setup and
results are evaluated. Finally, important trade-offs identified from the analysis is presented in order
to create a foundation for the decision making made by CPH management.
16.1
Evaluation of the Models
In the implementation of the deterministic model and stochastic simulation, it has been necessary
to make certain decisions that effect the efficiency and the end-results of the models. In this section,
the most important decisions for the model implementations are discussed, followed by a thorough
evaluation of the deterministic model and stochastic simulation with focus on advantages and limita-
tions.
16.1.1
Decisions in the Model Implementations
Some of the most essential decisions, that had a big impact on the model implementations and the
end-results of the models, are the estimation of conveyor and manual capacity and the computation
of missed bags. Another interesting aspect in the model implementation is the postponement option
which, as opposed to the capacity and missed bags, had an insignificant impact on the results.
Estimation of Capacity
The estimation of capacities has a high impact on the solution, as it is one of the main elements
restricting the assignment of the model. In this section, the accuracy of the capacity estimations and
how they are utilised in the model is evaluated.
81
==PAGE:90==
In this thesis, the capacity has been divided into two types; manual capacity and conveyor capacity, as
described in chapter 7.1. The manual capacity accounts for the number of bags put onto the conveyor
belt per minute and the conveyor capacity reflects the speed for a conveyor belt and is estimated
as bags per minute per conveyor belt. The capacity was estimated to 15 bags per minute for the
manual capacity and 20 bags per minute for the conveyor capacity. To compensate for the inbound
and outbound baggage that are processed in the same system, some additional adjustments were made
to the capacities.
The conveyor speed was reduced with 66%, due to the assumption that a congestion would occur
at the merging point with the storage system, where the transfer bags and the flow of outbound
bags meets. This estimate is based on the fact that the transfer passengers accounts for 1
3 of the
total number of outbound and transfer passengers. However, it can be discussed how representative
this capacity reduction is, as it assumes that the flow of both transfer and outbound baggage on the
conveyor belts is constant and with the same ratio. The same reduction of 66% has been made for the
manual capacity in West, due to the assumption that the flow of both transfer and inbound baggage
into the infeed area is constant and with the same ratio.
Both scenarios are rather unlikely and it could, thus, be argued that the capacity should have been
higher or more optimally time dependent by determining the capacities through predefined ratios
provided by the airport. However, since it is known that transfer baggage often occurs together with
inbound baggage, this estimate is deemed less coarse. These estimations have a large impact on the
model’s determination of the optimal solution and by improving the accuracy of these, the results of
the model would also become more reliable.
The Computation of Missed Bags
In this thesis, missed bags has been computed based on the assumption that a bag is defined as missed,
if the bag does not manage to reach the storage box 20 minutes before the connecting flight departs.
However, through the interviews, it was established that other mitigation plans were available in the
airport to make the baggage reach the departing flight in time even if it did not manage to reach
the storage box. If a bag does not reach the storage box, it will foremost be categorised as too late,
and sorted to a too late box. The bag could, therefore, potentially still be loaded onto the connecting
flight before it departs.
It can, therefore, be discussed if the assumption that defines whether a bag is missed or not is a bit
coarse. However, by utilising Torben Barth’s missed probability function[10] the probability of the
missed bag still reaching the departing flight is taken into account. Torben Barth computes the buffer
time as the available time minus the total time used to transfer the bag from the inbound flight to
the outbound flight. In this thesis the buffer time is, however, computed with a cut off 20 minutes
before the departing flight, as it has not been possible to model the process from the storage box to
the outbound flight with the available data. Because of this difference in the calculation, the results
of missed bags is potentially higher, as it is not certain that the process from the storage box to
the departing flight would take 20 minutes. The statement that the bag is missed can, therefore, be
misleading and it might be more accurate to define the bag as mishandled.
82
==PAGE:91==
Postponement Options
When constructing the model, a postponement option was implemented so that a flight’s baggage
handling process could be postponed 10 or 20 minutes. This was implemented in order to give the
model the option to prioritise flights with smaller buffer times than the remaining incoming flights in
the concerned time period, confer the FIFO problematic mentioned in section 6.1.
However, when evaluating the results for the models, it was observed that this option was rarely used.
Only in a few cases 1-2% of the flights were postponed. Therefore, it was decided not to include this
metric when evaluating the results for the bench marking in section 13 and for the scenario analysis
in section 14. It can be discussed whether the postponement option is unnecessary in the model, if the
system has not been stressed enough to exploit the option, or if the postponement options have been
too small. Outside peak hours, where capacity is not pressured, it will rarely be preferred to utilise
the postponement options, as it has no beneficial effect on the number of missed bags. During peak
hours, the capacity is typically pressured in multiple consecutive time intervals, thus, making the 10
and 20 minute postponement irrelevant. Therefore, it might be valuable to postpone the handling of
a non-urgent flight up to a couple of hours so that these flights would avoid taking away capacity for
more urgent flights, but instead be handled in the BHS during less stressed periods.
16.1.2
Deterministic model
In the following section, the deterministic model is discussed with focus on its limitations and the impact
of these. Furthermore, the advantages and disadvantages of the solution approach is considered.
In the construction of the model, focus was to obtain a model that could solve the problem for a full
day at a fast running time. This was achieved as the deterministic model was able to find an optimal
solution within seconds. The fast running time was achieved by removing the time dimension from
the decision variable and instead manage the time by using the conflicting constraints, as described in
section 11.4. Furthermore, the model assigns arriving flights as opposed to baggage which simplifies
the model in terms of the size of the data instance. Additionally, it is simplified in terms of avoiding
the additional constraints needed to ensure that baggage belonging to the same transfer connection
are kept together when following the transfer baggage handling process, as described in section 6.1.
However, this has entailed in extensive data preparation and creation of new data structures to ensure
that all calculations forming the information needed to solve the assignment problem were performed
outside the model and given as input instead. Further, it has compromised the ease of the interpretation
of the model, as it is necessary to trace back through the complex data structure. It can, therefore, be
discussed if it is most valuable to provide efficient results or an easier interpretation. However, some
reconsideration related to the data structure and implementation approach could potentially lead to
a more elegant solution and, thus, more accessible interpretation.
A limitation in the deterministic model, is that the model is missing essential elements in the transfer
baggage handling process such as queue and load time. In the deterministic model, the queue is not
managed, as the time aspect is eliminated from the model and only considered in 10 and 30 minute
conflicting time intervals. The queue is, therefore, only expressed in terms of capacity breaches at a
facility, but the effect on the remaining flights’ transfer process and missed bags probability of such
83
==PAGE:92==
a breach, is not considered in the model. Moreover, the deterministic model does not consider the
time it takes to load a bag onto a conveyor belt, which also impacts the results as for some flights
the additional time added to the transfer time is up to 10 minutes. To neglect these elements could
impose a big impact on the results and the general assignment, as the assignment for a flight could
potentially change if the queue were too long and would affect the output. This was also seen in the
step-wise evaluation where the queue and especially the load time had a big impact on the results. In
retrospect, the load time could have been included in the prepossessing of the data and added to the
buffer time of the arriving flight.
Another aspect that was neglected in the deterministic model is the lost capacity problem. In theory,
the capacity at a conveyor belt is time dependent, which means that for each second gone by some
of the capacity disappears, if it is not used. This thesis manages the manual and conveyor capacity
in 10 and 30 minute intervals, respectively. This also means that the capacity is fixed for a specific
time period, which means that if a flight arrives in the last minute of a 10 minute time period, the
capacity is the same. Since this does not consider lost capacity, it makes the estimate less accurate,
and, thus, the results from the deterministic model less reliable. A better way to manage this type of
lost capacity is to reduce the capacity each minute during a 10 or 30 minute interval, so that when
five minutes are left in a 10 minute interval, the capacity is reduced by half. However, it was decided
not to implement this in the deterministic model, as the stochastic simulation takes lost capacity into
account through the queue model described in section 9.1.
16.1.3
Stochastic Simulation Model
In the following section, the stochastic simulation is discussed with focus on its limitations and the
impact of these. Furthermore, the advantages of the solution approach is considered.
As the primary analysis and conclusions drawn are based on the results of the stochastic simulation, it is
essential to evaluate the credibility of the model and its results in terms of functionality, limitations and
advantages. The primary purpose of the stochastic simulation was to implement an optimisation model
using stochastic techniques characterised by randomness and uncertainties, as airports experience
high uncertainty in many of their daily operations. Moreover, it was essential to cover the elements
that the deterministic model were lacking such as load time, queue and lost capacity in the stochastic
simulation. This has ensured that the results in the scenario analysis are more credible, and that the
end-results could provide value for CPH.
As the stochastic simulation uses the deterministic model to provide an optimal assignment based
on the updated estimates of missed bags, it can be discussed if better results could be obtained if
the deterministic model did not lack important elements such as load time and queue. On one hand,
the deterministic model creates the foundation for the stochastic simulation and since it is lacking
essential elements, the basis for the assignment is less accurate. On the other hand, the stochastic
simulation does take these elements into account and should, therefore, guide the assignments made
by the deterministic model through the update of the number of missed bags. It could be interesting
to investigate if the results of the stochastic simulation would improve by adding queue and load time
to the deterministic model so that the stochastic simulation would have a better input.
84
==PAGE:93==
An advantage of the stochastic simulation is that the model is capable of simulating a relatively
realistic scenario of how the baggage handling process is carried out in the airport. This is done
through including uncertainties within unloading, transportation time from parking stand to infeed
area and scheduled time of arrival and departure. The accuracy of the uncertainties is important to
consider as some were determined without a thorough uncertainty analysis. Furthermore, additional
uncertainties could potentially have been included in the model. As mentioned, in the estimation in
chapter 11.3 the unloading time data has been inaccessible, which has resulted in a quite coarse estimate
in regards to the time it takes to unload the flight. In the stochastic simulation, the variance of the
uncertainty of unloading is rather small and, therefore, it can be argued that the applied uncertainty
to this estimate does not represent the true uncertainty. In order to make the uncertainty more
representative, it would be necessary to conduct a thorough uncertainty analysis for the distribution,
based on on-block time data. Furthermore, uncertainty on the BHS times should perhaps have been
included, as this estimate is fixed to the median, even though the actual BHS time varies for each bag.
These uncertainties impact the credibility of the model and, thus, accuracy of the final results.
Another advantage of the stochastic simulation is the simulated queue as described in chapter 9.1.
Through the queue, the stochastic simulation considers the lost capacity that is neglected in the
deterministic model. Furthermore, it manages the capacity breaches by generating a queue that as
a consequence increases the overall transfer process time for the respective bags. This increase in
process time leads to an update in the missed bags, which ensures that the deterministic model in it’s
next iteration can re-evaluate the assignment and provide a better solution. However, a disadvantage
of the queue is that it is assumed that the second the belt is available, the loading can begin. This is
quite unrealistic, as other factors such as shifting time between vehicles and human errors is neglected.
Furthermore, the West facility also handles inbound baggage at its transfer conveyor belts. At times,
it will be experienced that flights with inbound baggage will exclusively occupy a transfer belt. This
has not been simulated in the queue in West. Further, the load time that is part of the queue model
is only considered for the transfer baggage in West and not for the inbound baggage arriving from
the same flight. Therefore, it can be argued that the time spent in queue at the conveyor belts for the
transfer bags is slightly optimistic.
Despite the limitations of the stochastic simulation, it has been confirmed through conversations with
the stakeholders from CPH that the results of the stochastic simulation aligns well with the reality
of the airport. CPH’s current number of mishandled bags, defined as baggage that arrives too late
to its storage box, is around 4%. The results from the stochastic simulation lie around 5% missed
bags. It was expected that the results from the model would be lower, as more capacity was added
through the West facility. However, this can be explained by the capacities of the model being rather
pessimistic and the East facility being more favourable than the West facility, as the analysis shows.
It can, therefore, be concluded that the stochastic simulation provides realistic results and that it
creates a good foundation for the scenario analysis.

16.2
Scenarios
In this section, the comprehensiveness of the performed scenarios which evaluate the sensitivity and
robustness of the baggage handling system in CPH is discussed. Further, the sensitivity and robustness
of the system itself is evaluated and the results from the scenarios are discussed with the focus on
identifying potential improvements for the stochastic simulation.
16.2.1
Scenario Setup Evaluation
In this thesis, five different scenarios for the airport were investigated. The scenarios were split into two
different types, respectively events affecting the airport operation performance and facility expansions,
that would provide the airport with additional capacity powers to manage the incoming baggage.
It is unsure whether the chosen scenarios give a comprehensive image of the of the airport’s strengths
and weaknesses and investigates enough potential reconstruction possibilities. It is, moreover, be
discussed if the investigated scenarios creates a valid and adequate foundation in the decision making
process for CPH management. The facility expansion scenarios could potentially have benefited from
investigating cross-scenarios such as the conveyor break down scenario with additional capacity. This
would enable an analysis of how much the expansion would help improve the results when breakdowns
occur in the system. However, it is comprehensive enough to see the results compared to a baseline, as
it can be assumed that the trends will be the same although with an improvement in the performance
metric results.
The individual instances in each of the scenarios have been assessed with regards to how encompassing
they are for revealing trends and shift in tendencies for the airport. The combination of conveyor
belt breakdowns could have been more extensive and provided all combinations of breakdowns, or
the percentage steps in the distribution requirement scenario could have been smaller, to follow the
development more closely. However, it is presumed that the scenario instances are extensive enough
to show the general and most important tendencies in the system, and comprehensive enough to
help reveal the strengths and weaknesses of the system. Lastly, it is relevant to consider whether it
is comprehensive to evaluate the scenarios using the busy day instance as the baseline. For the first
scenario investigating an increase in the number of passengers, and thereby the amount of transfer
baggage, it makes sense to build on top of the busy day instance, to foresee how the airport operations
will perform in the future. When investigating the breakdown of conveyor belts and distribution
requirements, as well as the facility alterations scenarios with additional transfer conveyor belts or
sorting systems, it is uncertain how well the analysis reflects how the airport will perform in the future
under these scenarios, as it is not cross-tested with an increase in demand.
The airport expects to have an increase in the annual number of passenger of 33%. If assumed that
the ratio between the outbound, inbound and transfer passengers remains the same, the number of
transfer passengers will likewise increase with 33%. It is further assumed that the airport will have
a slow, normal and busy days in the future as they have now, but where all instances are increased
with regards to the number of bags. If the normal day, which is assumed to be the instance occurring
most often for the airport, increases with 33%, it would result in 4763 · 1.33 = 6334.80 transfer bags.
86
==PAGE:95==
This is lower than the amount of bags occurring on the current busy day instance. Therefore, it seems
reasonable to use the busy day as a baseline and still obtain valid results for how the airport will
perform in the future under a normal to a semi-busy day. The fact that this thesis does not investigate
all scenarios with increased demand will limit the findings for how the airport reacts to a busy day
with increased demand. However, it is not an unrealistic assumption that the trends will follow the
tendencies found in the scenarios, just with increased numbers in the performance metric results, as
demonstrated in the demand increase scenario.
16.2.2
Scenario Results Evaluation
It was stated in the discussion of the stochastic simulation in section 16.1.3, that the results align well
with the reality of CPH. Therefore, it can be argued that the results from the scenario analysis will
also be aligned with the reality, if these scenarios occurred in the airport. The sensitive elements and
robustness improvements of the system will be evaluated and potential improvements within selected
scenarios will be discussed in the follwoing sections.
Sensitivity in the System
In general, the scenario analysis has highlighted the baggage handling system’s high sensitivity towards
changes in capacity. Especially disruptions in the East facility caused a significantly higher amount of
missed baggage in the system, as seen in section 14.2. The system’s high sensitivity towards capacity
changes in East makes it vulnerable in the events of disruptions such as conveyor maintenance or
breakdowns. The scenario analysis further revealed that additional capacity in the form of extra
conveyor belts or a new sorting system, as seen in the respective sections 14.4 and 14.5, would increase
the robustness of the system. This is due the extra capacity providing the system with a buffer
that makes the system less vulnerable towards disruptions. In addition, the conveyor belt scenario
reconfirmed the sensitivity towards the capacity in East, as additional belts in East provides higher
value than additional belts in West. The implementation of a new sorting system is, however, the
alteration that makes the system most robust, according to the model. This is due to the more
balanced facility distribution that makes the system more sustainable as it relies more equally on
both facilities. Moreover, it is interesting that the preferred facility becomes West where the manual
capacity is the restricting capacity. Therefore, it would be interesting to investigate how sensitive the
system is towards disruptions in West with two sorting systems and if the results could be improved
even more when adding extra manual capacity in the West distribution. Due to time limitations,
however, this is something that could be included in future work.
Improvements in the Demand Increase Scenario
In the demand increase scenario, large fluctuations were found in the results for the flagged bags ratio,
measured as the number of flagged bags out of the total number of missed bags. Large fluctuations were
also found in the conveyor capacity breach for infeed area East. Both metrics relate to the assignment
of the incoming flights and how that impact the probability of missed bags as well as the amount of
baggage on board the flight. The capacity breach is further dependent on the capacity of the infeed
areas. Thus, the fluctuations can partially be explained by the model choosing different compromises
between missed bags and capacity breaches in the assignments for the different instances. However,
87
==PAGE:96==
the way the synthesised data is made also has an affect on these fluctuations. The synthesised data
has been generated at random for each instance. This means that for the 5% increase instance, bags
corresponding to a 5% increase were drawn at random from the normal day instance. For the 10%
increase instance, completely new data was drawn. For a demand increase scenario with most bags
added outside of the peak hours of the busy day instance, the effect might be smaller on flagged
baggage than for a scenario with most bags added within the existing peak hours. Thus, it can be
discussed whether it had been more favourable to fix the additionally added synthetic data and only
add on top of it. This would make it easier to make a direct comparison of the demand increase, as
the distribution of the fixed added data would be the same. However, the way the synthesised data is
produced in the scenario gives a more realistic picture of the variation the airport can experience and
will still present the major trends the airport should be aware of.
Improvements in the Facility Distribution Scenario
In the conveyor belt breakdown scenario, it was found that the airport is highly sensitive towards
breakdowns in the East facility. Therefore, it is valuable to consider if it would be favourable to force
a more even distribution, to make the airport performance more stable and to lower the abrasion and
frequency of breakdowns at the East facility. This was investigated in the distribution requirement
scenario, where it was found that up to 50% of the flights could be sent to the West facility without
any noticeable increases in the number of missed bags. The value of this finding for the airport would,
however, be dependent on the trade-off between the cost of the additional missed bags occurring in
the equal distribution instance and the savings obtained by a decrease in abrasion, and breakdowns,
of the East facility. Since this is outside the scope for this thesis, a conclusion can not be drawn on
these trade-offs but merely be mentioned as a point of interest for the airport to investigate further
in the future.
In the scenario, it was further interpreted that the West facility had the capacity to manage 75%
of the flights without being pressured on its capacity, but merely resulted in more missed bags.
However, the accuracy of this assumption is questioned. The distribution requirement is based on
the percentage-wise distribution of assigned flights. This means the distribution indicates how many
flights are assigned to the two infeed areas. However, the abrasion of the transfer belts will be affected
by the number of bags they transport and not the number of assigned flights. The number of flights
will to some extend give an indication of the number of bags, but not as accurate as if the number
of bags were used to generate the distribution. Stating that sending 50% of the flights to the West
facility would reduce the abrasion on the East facility should thus be taken with slight caution, as
spreading the distribution of flights does not have a linear relationship with spreading the distribution
of baggage. Likewise, the assumption that 75% of the flights can be handled at the West facility
without pressuring the capacity should be taken with caution, as the 75% distribution is not equal
to 75% of the baggage, which the capacity breaches are measured by. The reason it was chosen to
go with the flight distribution and not the baggage distribution was due to the knowledge it would
provide the airport with on how their resources were spent and which facility that was used by most of
the arriving flights. In retrospect, it could have been valuable to measure distribution on both flights
and baggage.

Overall, it is found that most metrics used to analyse the model performance are stable and shows
the expected tendencies of the different scenarios of the thesis. Flagged bags and flagged transfer
connections have, however, shown to be highly sensitive towards changes in the system and show
no clear tendencies through the scenarios. This can be explained by the model choosing different
compromises between missed bags and capacity breaches in the assignments for the different instances
according to the scenario it is put in. Furthermore, it can be explained by the fact that flagged bags

16.3
Trade-offs for CPH
In the model developing phase, certain decision have been made that all have an impact on the final
solution delivered to CPH. Within these decisions, certain trade-offs exist that should be considered
by upper management in order to decide which decisions would create most value for CPH as an
organisation. The most important trade-offs are highlighted and discussed in detail in the following
sections.
16.3.1
Missed Bags and Capacity Breach
The multi-criteria objective function in the deterministic model consists of two parts, as explained in
section 8. The first part consists of the number of missed bags which is weighted β and the second part
is the number of violated bags in conveyor and manual capacity and is weighted by β −1. Therefore,
β determines the importance of each element and how the model defines the optimal solution. It was
decided to set β = 0.9, as CPH is mostly interested in minimising the number of missed bags, as
these lead to high costs connected to extra resources spend on managing the mishandled baggage
and compensation to the customer. By setting β = 0.9 the model prioritises the minimisation of the
number of missed bags, but still allows the capacity constraints and thus the capacity breach to have
a small relevance.
An investigation of the trade-off between large capacity breaches and thus queues at the infeed areas
and the number of missed bags was performed by analysing the stochastic simulation’s performance
with beta values ranging from 0.1 to 1 with an increment of 0.1 (section 15.1). The analysis investigated
if it was possible for the model to find a solution with smaller capacity breaches without compromising
on the number of missed bags. It was deduced that β < 0.9 resulted in an increase in the number of
missed bags which suggests that the performance of the model is worse for the airport.
Despite the capacity breach diminishing when β < 0.9, it is not preferred over β = 0.9. Thus, the
analysis showed that it was not possible to find a solution with smaller capacity breaches without
compromising on the number of missed bags. However, as the capacity breaches first become remarkably
larger with a demand increase at 45%, it is not relevant for CPH to look into alternative solutions
with lower capacity breaches until this demand has been reached.
In the analysis, it was further investigated whether ignoring the capacity breaches entirely by setting
β = 1 would produce more favourable results for the airport. The results showed that the minimum
number of missed bags was obtained with this adjustment as well as fewer flagged transfer connections.
89
==PAGE:98==
However, by focusing solely on missed bags in the objective function, the model created much higher
capacity breaches which would result in long queues at the infeed areas - in this case in East, as it
is often more favourable to assign flights to East and let them wait in queue rather than sending
them to West as mentioned in 16.2. Having employees spending their time in queues is not a valuable
utilisation of resources for the airport. Additionally, long queues will make the system more vulnerable
towards congestion as small errors will have larger impacts. The high utilisation of East will also entail
high abrasion on the East facility which, as found in the breakdown scenario in section 14.2, makes
the system more vulnerable for breakdowns and maintenance stops.
In the end, one could argue that the high capacity breaches would make CPH’s ability to perform a
steady low number of missed bags more unstable when setting β = 1. Therefore, it is recommended
to weight the multi criteria objective function with the current value of β = 0.9 as this would produce
better missed bags results for the airport. However, more information is required about the risk and
cost connected to large capacity breaches compared to the cost and number of missed bags, in order
to conclude what would be the best solution for the airport in the long run.
16.3.2
Flagging Transfer Connections
In order to not only plan the daily flight assignments for CPH in a way that minimises the number
of missed bags, the flagging method was also implemented, in order to warn the airport when critical
transfer connections were identified. This enables the model to also be utilised as a strategic decision
tool for highlighting when mitigating actions should be taken, in order to reduce cost connected to
missed bags as much as possible.
In the scenario analysis in chapter 14, it was discovered that the number of flagged transfer connections
and the number of missed bags fluctuated independently of the objective value. This can be explained
by the model finding equally good solutions with different numbers of flagged transfer connections
and bags as these are not elements that are being minimised in the objective function. As the number
of flagged transfer connections are potentially connected to high costs, it could be interesting to
include this in the model as a criterion in the objective function. This would enable the model to
minimise the number of transfer connections that unconditionally would be in need of mitigating
actions and thus potentially spare the airport for wasted resources and cost. This would, however,
result in higher probabilities spread out on all transfer connections which could cause confusion on
where extra resources should be allocated in order to make mitigating actions. Therefore, a trade-off
exists between minimising the flagged transfer connections and minimising the number of missed bags,
exclusively.
Another important aspect when discussing the trade-off is the threshold that determines when a
transfer connection should be flagged. It was decided to only flag transfer connections with 100%
probability of loosing baggage as this would give the airport a list of the flights where mitigating actions
were required unconditionally, according to the model. However, it would potentially be favourable
for the airport to set the threshold lower in order to be more prepared for other flights potentially
in need of mitigating actions. As the amount of flagged transfer connections increases rapidly when
decreasing the threshold to 90%, 80% and 70%, it would not be useful to gather all the flagged transfer
90
==PAGE:99==
connections in one list. Instead, the flagged transfer connections should be split according to criticality
based on both the probability and number of missed bags. This way, the airport would know exactly
which transfer connection that for sure would need mitigating actions, which would only potentially
need them and which should be prioritised first.
16.3.3
Investment in New Sorting System
A factor that influences the baggage handling process is the strategic and tactical decisions that
determines the operational efficiencies and trade-offs between cost and quality that is decided by
upper management.
As previously mentioned, there is a trade-off between the number of missed bags and the facility
distribution. With the baggage handling system using two infeed areas, the model tends to send most
of the baggage to infeed area East when trying to minimise the number of missed bags. The high
utilisation in one infeed area, makes the system more vulnerable for breakdowns and maintenance stops
which in the end can result in large costs for the airport. When adding a new sorting system in West
so it runs independently of the sorting system in East, the results showed that the facility distribution
became inverse. West became the preferred infeed station, however, the facility distribution did also
become more even. That West is preferred when the process time through BHS is no longer 3.5 times
as large as in East can be explained by its location which is more centred and, thus, closer to most
parking stands. When looking into the model performance, it was revealed that the number of missed
bags was reduced remarkably. The results without two sorting systems showed that approximately
5% of the total number of transfer bags were missed bags and when utilising two sorting systems,
this was reduced to 3.6%. It is, therefore, recommended that CPH looks into a potential expansion of
the Airside T3 project to include a separate sorting system in the West infeed area, as this will not
only reduce the number of missed bags but also more naturally even out the facility distribution. The
true benefits of this investment should, however, be analysed more in-depth through cost/benefits and
return of investment analysis before any further conclusions can be made.

Conclusion
In this thesis, the transfer baggage operations in hub-airports has been formulated as a transfer
baggage problem (TBP) and solved using the general assignment problem with both a deterministic
and stochastic solution approach. The models were developed with focus on improving the daily
transfer baggage operations, enabling the application on all hub-airports, producing robust results in
a system influenced by uncertainty and ensuring fast running time.
This thesis is conducted in cooperation with Copenhagen Airports A/S (CPH) who are currently
expanding a terminal by building a new baggage handling facility. CPH provided three data instances
representing a slow, normal and busy day in terms of the number of transfer bags to provide a
foundation for analysing the models and determining the optimal use of their new facility. When
running all data instances on the deterministic model, the results showed that the objective value
increased for each day type and so did the number of missed bags, though with a constant ratio of
around 1%. Furthermore, the results showed that only the busy day had a capacity breach, which
indicated that the system is only pressured on a busy day, according to the deterministic model. As the
deterministic model does not take uncertainties within the transfer baggage operations into account,
the stochastic simulation was developed. In order to compare the deterministic model to the stochastic
simulation, a benchmarking was performed between the deterministic model under uncertainty and the
stochastic simulation on the same three instances. The results showed that the number of missed bags
decreased respectively, 2.5%, 3% and 3.5% for a slow, normal and busy day when running the instances
using the stochastic simulation. The tendency revealed that the stochastic simulation was able to
provide better results when the pressure on the system increased. The results showed that the system
was still only pressured on the busy day but more pressured than the deterministic model indicated
which was revealed by an increase in capacity breach. Through the results, it can be concluded that
the stochastic simulation is more robust towards the dynamic environment that exists within the
airport and that it provides better and more realistic solutions than the deterministic model.
In the discussion, several improvement points for the models were highlighted. The deterministic model
does not include queue and load time which instead is expressed as capacity breaches. This is handled
in the stochastic simulation, but to establish a better input to stochastic simulation, these should have
been incorporated in the deterministic model. The stochastic simulation could be improved by adding
shift time between vehicles at the infeed areas and including inbound baggage in the queue. The queue
and load time together with the uncertainties for the scheduled time of arrival and scheduled time of
departure entailed that the stochastic simulation converged towards a higher value than the optimal
objective value from the deterministic model.
As the field of this project is a combination of operations research and operations management,
the data provided by CPH has not only been used to solve the TBP but also to provide results
specifically valuable for CPH. Therefore, several scenario analysis were conducted to highlight the
sensitivity of the infrastructure in CPH. The results showed that the baggage handling system is highly
sensitive towards changes in capacity and that especially disruptions in the East facility resulted in a
significantly higher number of missed bags. This is due to East being the preferred facility which was
92
==PAGE:101==
the general tendency throughout the analysis. The imbalanced facility distribution indicates that the
new facility in West currently provides little value in terms of transfer baggage which also makes the
system more vulnerable in the events of breakdowns or maintenance stops in the East facility. The
results further showed that additional capacity in the form of extra conveyor belts, especially in East,
or adding a new sorting system in West, would increase the robustness of the system. When adding
a new sorting system in West, the results showed that the percentage of the number of missed bags
was reduced to 3.6% from 5% and that the facility distribution became more balanced. It can, thus,
be concluded that the amount of value that the new facility provides CPH will increase significantly
if a new sorting system is added.
An important metric for CPH is the flagged transfer connection that highlights critical transfer
connections and when mitigating actions are required in order to avoid missed bags. The analysis
indicated that the number of flagged transfer connections fluctuated independently of the objective
value, as it is not minimised in the multi-objective criteria function. The flagging method can be used
in several ways, for instance, by minimising it in the objective function or setting various thresholds
for flagging. The value that this method should bring and how it should be used, should be decided
by upper management.
In the discussion, several improvement points for the estimations for CPH were highlighted. It was
discovered that coarse assumptions were made for the manual and conveyor capacity used in the
models and that a thorough analysis of these could help setting more proper estimates. Furthermore,
the random variable for unloading contains a lot of uncertainty and a thorough uncertainty analysis
could provide a better estimate. These improvements would all contribute to making the models more
realistic and thus create more reliable results.
Overall, it can be concluded that the stochastic simulation is able to produce robust results in an
environment influenced by uncertainty even though some improvements could be made. The model
can improve the daily transfer baggage operations by planning the flight-to-infeed assignment for
all arriving flights on a daily basis while minimising the number of missed bags. Additionally, the
model can be used as a strategic decision tool that highlights critical transfer connections in need of
mitigating actions, which in the end can result in large cost reductions. The model can also be used to
analyse the effect of potential disruptions and alterations in the facilities, which can help highlight the
sensitivity and increase the robustness of the infrastructure. It can, therefore, be concluded that the
implemented model aligns well with the business objectives defined by CPH and using the CRISP-DM
method.Moreover, it can be stated that both models are able to find optimal solutions with an efficient
running time. This was possible due to an extensive amount of data preprocessing which have, however,
made the interpretation less accessible. Finally, the models can be applied in several hub-airports
with different amount of infeed areas and capacity by adjusting the models to the corresponding
system, as seen for the instance of CPH. Since the stochastic simulation can be deemed valuable, it
can be concluded that it can be utilised to solve similar assignment problems within airports where
uncertainty is an important factor.

Future work
In the following section different areas that would be interesting to include in future work are high-
lighted and described in detail.
18.1
Adjusting the model
The discussion in section 16 highlighted how specific decisions in the modelling phase resulted in
certain limitations in the models. If more time was available, it would be interesting to look into
potential improvements of the models in terms of making them more realistic.
The decision regarding unloading times are one of the things that could be improved in the model.
This could be done in one of two ways depending on the data the airport would be able to acquire from
their arriving flights. Optimally the airport should acquire information on whether the arriving flight
contains bulk or container baggage, as well as how much baggage the flights carried in total, including
inbound. Alternatively a data collection on the ratio between bulk and container baggage as well as
the ratio between inbound and transfer baggage could be obtained, to produce some distributions the
model could draw from at random. Another improvement of the model would be to obtain some more
accurate uncertainties for the driving times. This could be done by collecting data on the different
vehicle routes and make a distribution based on the deviations from the calculated driving times, to
use for the uncertainty instead. In the discussion it was highlighted that the capacity estimation was
coarse. Therefore, it could be interesting to adjust the capacity, as described in section 16.1.1 and
16.1.2. This includes making the capacity time dependent through predefined ratios and taking lost
capacity into account.
Through the result analysis the postponement options were found to be a rather useless element in the
model that rarely were used. In the discussion it was highlighted that the addition of longer postpone-
ment times potentially could be of value, as it gives the airport the opportunity to withhold baggage,
with long connection time, from entering the BHS during peak hours. The change of postponement
times in the model would be rater simple, however it would require a rework of the data preprocessing.
For a more simple implementation of postponement times the current postponement options could
simply be changed in the model to other time options, such as 60, 90 and 120 minutes. Furthermore,
it would be interesting to look into implementing the load time in the deterministic model, to create a
better input result for the stochastic simulation and perhaps improve its search conditions. This would
be done by subtracting the load time in the buffer time equation for each transfer connection.
Capacity Utilisation Constraint
Another aspect that contributes to making the model unrealistic, is the capacity utilisation. Currently,
it is assumed that the capacity can be utilised 100% at any time which is not realistic in a functioning
organisation. Usually the utilisation is set to 80% to ensure the well being of employees as well as
avoiding overloading systems. The capacity breaches revealed in the analysis indicates that the manual
and conveyor capacity is currently utilised 100% during peak hours. Therefore, it would be relevant to
add a capacity constraint that would ensure, that both conveyor and manual capacity is not utilised
more than 80%. The change would perhaps lead to worse results in terms of missed bags as the
capacity becomes more limited, however, the results will be more realistic.

==PAGE:103==
18.2
Inclusion of Inbound and Outbound Baggage
An interesting extension of the model would be to include both inbound and outbound baggage
in the model. This would entail a more realistic model, as much of the uncertainty related to the
assumptions connected to capacity would be removed and replaced by real data. Furthermore, the
queue generation would be represented more accurately as it would take both inbound and transfer
load times into account. However, this model would require comprehensive extensions to include
inbound and outbound processes.
18.3
Rolling intervals
An interesting project for future work, could be to adjust the model such that it was updated con-
tinuously as the flights arrived during the day. This would make the model more useful in the daily
operations at the airport.
This could be implemented by including a rolling time horizon which is a more extensive improvement
than the ones mentioned above. A model with a rolling time horizon would run the model, for instance,
every other minute and update the assignments based on new information of arrival times. The new
information that should be given to the model would be the estimated on block times (EON), that
indicate when the aircraft is expected to be parked at the parking stands. The EON is regularly
updated during the flights time in the air and reflects possible delays or time gain due to weather,
air traffic or conditions at the departure airport. The implementation of this improvement would
thus require extra data from CPH. With the EON estimations it would be possible to make more
realistic calculations for each connecting flight’s buffer times and thus the number of missed bags, as
the sta would be replaced with the EON which are a more realistic estimate. Assuming that the model
plans 2 hours ahead, it would be necessary to fix previous assignments, for instance, assignments for
flights arriving 30 minutes prior to this point in time. By fixing these assignments, the model receives
information about which capacities that are available at the current point in time. This is necessary to
ensure that no more capacity is used than available and avoid flights being assigned to an infeed area
that are stressed. The output of the model should be new assignments for the flights only within the
2 hours time frame and these should be based on the updated estimates based on the latest reported
EON. The more accurate estimates on the number of missed bags together with the information of
available capacity, will allow the model to produce better assignments, as it is given better information
to base its decisions on.
With the updates described above, the same framework as for the stochastic simulation, can be used
to implement the rolling time horizon model. This includes the preprocessing of the updated data in
terms of pre-calculations and structuring of data before it is given to the model as input. Additional
preprocessing should be made in order to have an indication of which time interval is in scope and
which flights the model should fix and assign, respectively.

Dictionary Creation
To keep track of the number of flights arriving at an infeed area in a given time interval, several
dictionaries are created. These dictionaries contains data in regards to which flights arrive in a given
10- or 30 minute time interval along with the number of transfer baggage from the respective flight
and the infeed index(Explained later..). Figure 40 explains the structure of such a dictionary, with
the keys being the time interval and for each key information about the flights is accessible. This
dictionary provides an easy lookup overview of arriving flights...
Figure 40: Data structure of time interval dictionary. The keys

about which aircrafts are in the corresponding time interval
along with the number of transfer baggage and what delay

This type of dictionary is created for both time intervals and for both infeed areas, and is used to
determine what time intervals in which a potential capacity conflict may occur.
Figure 40 illustrates a potential conflict in time period 2 and 3 with their respective flights. These
periods will be determined as conflicts that the model needs to handle appropriately such that the
choice of assignment ensures that the number of missed bags are minimised.
Figure 40 illustrates this data structure where infeed index refers to the postponement, meaning that
if infeed index is 1 for an aircraft the time interval is the first possible time interval. Is infeed index
2 for an aircraft it means that for the aircraft to arrive in this time interval the aircraft is delayed
10 minutes. The purpose of this data structure is to give the model some slack in terms of delaying
aircraft that may not be influence by a 10 or 20 minutes delay in order to avoid congestion and allow
more urgent air crafts to be prioritised first.

A.2
Pseudo Code for Queue
The pseudo code for how the queue has been handled. The pseudo code is based on Figure 6 with
two conveyor belts, however, the queue handling approach can be used with an arbitrary amount of
conveyor belts, as the concept will remain the same.
The pseudo code for the queue handling solution approach is presented. The time at the two conveyor
belts are initialised to zero and are updated for each incoming arriving flight, fa. The time it takes
to load each arriving flight is added to it’s arrival time at the infeed station, in order to determine
how long the next flight will have to wait in queue. The three possible scenarios described beneath
Figure 6 is represented by four if-statements. If conveyor belt one is free, then the ground handler
will start unloading at this belt and the timestamp for the belt is updated. If conveyor belt one is
occupied, conveyor belt two is chosen. If none of the belts are available the conveyor belt with the
smallest queue time is chosen and the timestamp for the conveyor is updated by adding the current
flight’s load time.

Estimating S and H for the Stochastic Simulation
Steady State Plots for Estimating Number of Simulations S
When trying to determine the number of simulations S necessary to obtain stable results in the
stochastic simulation different approaches where used. From a theoretical perspective the Rossetti
half-width method was applied. Further, a practical approach, in the form of a steady-state plot was
used to estimate the number of required simulations per iteration for a slow, normal and busy day.
The theoretical estimations can be found in table 5. In figure 44, when interpreting the graph, it is
found that the steady-state occurs around 200 simulations. In figure 45 it is estimated to around 120
and in figure 46 to 155. All steady-state plots gives a lower amount of replications necessary to obtain
an optimal solution for the model, than the theoretical approach.

Graphs for Estimating Number of Iterations H
When trying to determine the number of iterations H necessary to obtain stable results in the
stochastic simulation the theoretical Rossetti half-width method can no longer be applied as the
iterations H are not independent. Therefore a visualisation was made, showing the number of missed
bags per iteration for each day type. From the visualisations an assessment can be made of the number
of iterations necessary to run in the stochastic simulation. In general for the three visualisations it
can be seen that the number of missed bags quickly reaches a steady level of missed bags. From figure
47 it is estimated that 60 iterations should be performed, from figure 48 40 iterations and from figure
49 70 iterations. The estimated number of iterations are gathered in table 6.
