Abstract
Natural pigments have become crucial in modern manufacturing, with fungi, particularly
those producing orsellinic acid­derived compounds, gaining prominence as significant
contributors to this field due to their rapid growth and cost­effectiveness. Among these
functional pigments, oosporein, an aromatic fungal polyketide synthesized by Beauve­
ria.bassiana, stands out for its industrial potential. The aim of this project was to explore
the flexibility of oosporein production in B.bassiana across varying media and environ­
mental conditions, with a focus on maximizing yields. This is due to its structural similarity
to phenicin, aiming for potential use in redox flow batteries.
To accomplish these objectives, the fungal strain IBT 36140 of B.bassiana was utilized,
obtained from the IBT culture collection at DTU Bioengineering. The cultivation involved
growing the strain on various solid media, including PDA (Potato Dextrose Agar), OAT
(Oatmeal Agar), YES (Yeast Extract Sucrose Agar), and CYA (Czapek Yeast Autolysate
Agar), as well as liquid media, including CY (Yeast Autolysate), CY90 (Czapek Yeast
Autolysate Agar sucrose 90), YES (Yeast extract sucrose), and PDB (Potato Dextrose
broth) under different conditions. The study spans 13 days for solid media and 7 and 12
days for liquid media under static and shaking conditions in darkness. The subsequent
analysis includes chemical extraction and comprehensive UHPLC­TOF­MS analysis.
Considering the oosporein biosynthetic pathway outlined in the literature [1], the derepli­
cation of known compounds was performed to uncover potential associations between
these compounds and the oosporein biosynthetic pathway, facilitating the proposal of a
potential extended pathway for oosporein. This pathway involves crucial metabolites,
such as orsellinic acid (compound 2), serving as precursors. Orsellinic acid 2 undergoes
a series of transformations, including decarboxylation and simultaneous hydroxylation,
resulting in the formation of an intermediate identified as compound 3, also known as
6­methyl­1,2,4­benzenetriol and then oxidized form of compound 4 (2­hydroxy­6­methyl­
2,5­cyclohexadiene­1,4­dione), producing phenicin (compound 5). Following this, com­
pound 3 undergoes monooxygenation, leading to the generation of another pivotal inter­
mediate denoted as compound 7, specifically known as 6­methyl­1,2,4,5­benzenetetrol
and its oxidized form compound 6 which is named 2,5­dihydroxy­6­methylbenzoquinone.
This, in turn, plays a critical role in the synthesis of oosporein 1.
The study also revealed potential associations between new metabolites (9, 10, its iso­
mer 10*, 11 and its isomer 11*) and the oosporein 1 biosynthetic pathway, shedding light
on their roles in the intricate enzymatic processes involved. Through the dereplication
of known and unknown compounds, compound 10 was tentatively identified as a shunt
product intricately linked with the pathway, along with compounds 11 and 11* postulated
as tetrameric forms of compounds 10 and 10*, respectively.
One significant finding was the high adaptability of B.bassiana to produce oosporein in
various media, with or without agar, under different conditions. Another finding was the
introduction of different sucrose levels to CY, CY90, and YES media under static and
shaking conditions to optimize oosporein production, based on the literature [2]. Unex­
pectedly, higher concentrations of sucrose produced orsellinic acid favorably compared
to oosporein in shaking media, suggesting that high concentrations of sucrose may not
be effective for oosporein and phenicin production by B.bassiana.
The results from this work suggest that among all solid and liquid media, CY static liquid
media on the 7th day is the preferred medium for large­scale optimization of oosporein
production due to its ability to produce highly crystallized oosporein, despite its lower con­

Aim of project
The primary objective of this study is to optimize the production of oosporein, a pivotal
final product within a biosynthetic pathway shared with phenicin, both originating from
orsellinic acid. This investigation will delve deeply into oosporein biosynthesis, with a
particular focus on its unique pathway featuring a monooxygenated intermediate distinct
from phenicin, as documented in the literature [1]. The motivation behind this research
stems from the industrial promise of oosporein, coupled with its structural resemblance
to phenicin, a deep violet dibenzoquinone synthesized by select Penicillium species,
renowned for its utility in redox flow batteries, as outlined in the literature [2].
Specifically, this study will concentrate on elucidating the biosynthesis of oosporein and
its comprehensive chemical profile by B.bassiana. By pinpointing optimal conditions for
the efficient production of oosporein, the aim is to make significant strides toward the de­
velopment of economically viable, environmentally sustainable energy storage solutions.
Additionally, the potential application of oosporein as an electrolyte in quinone redox flow
batteries will be explored, presenting an alternative energy storage approach aimed at ad­
dressing the global imperative of achieving a more resource­efficient and environmentally
conscious energy landscape.

1.1
Fungi and pigments
Fungi, in their singular form as a fungus, constitute a diverse kingdom of eukaryotic or­
ganisms [3][4]. These organisms share fundamental cellular features with both plants
and animals, including possessing a nucleus and organelles. However, their distinctive­
ness arises from their chitin­based cell walls, a rigid substance also found in arthropod
exoskeletons [5].
Fungi have both beneficial and detrimental effects within ecosystems. On one hand, they
play vital roles in nutrient cycling by decomposing organic matter due to heterotrophic [6].
On the other hand, fungi can also have detrimental effects, causing diseases in plants
and animals [7].
One notable genus among filamentous fungi is Beauveria, which belongs to Deuteromycetes.
It comprises 23 anamorph species [8][9]. Notable species within this genus include B.bassiana,
B.brongniartii, B.amorpha, and B.caledonica [10]. B.bassiana, a globally distributed soil­
dwelling fungus, is remarkable for its ability to produce various toxic secondary metabo­
lites for pest control [11]. This fungus acts as an entomopathogen, infecting many insect
species and playing a crucial role in controlling insect populations, particularly in veteri­
nary, agricultural, and forestry environments [10][7].

Pigments
Natural color has become a significant focus of modern manufacturing, particularly in the
food industry [12]. Natural pigments are derived from various sources, including plants,
animals, and microorganisms [13][14].
Among these different microorganisms, fungi have emerged as significant contributors
to natural pigment production. Their unique attributes make them an attractive choice.
Fungi can be cultivated on a large scale and grow rapidly and cost­effectively [13]. These
combined features have established fungi as a compelling option for pigment production,
with applications spanning various industries [13][14].
In the fungal world, filamentous species are notably recognized for their ability to produce
diverse pigments responsible for the array of colors and hues found in fungi and their re­
productive structures. Some of these pigments include melanins, quinones, azaphilones,
and carotenoids [12].
One notable example of these pigments is oosporein, a red 1,4­bibenzoquinone deriva­
tive produced by B.bassiana [15]. Oosporein is a multifaceted compound with biocontrol
agent properties and pigment­producing abilities. Recent research has unveiled a detailed
biosynthesis pathway for oosporein, driven by a polyketide synthase, shedding new light
on this versatile compound’s production [1].

1.2
Natural products
Natural products encompass various organic compounds from living organisms, including
plants, animals, and microorganisms [16][17]. These compounds are synthesized through
various biosynthetic pathways and exhibit remarkable structural diversity of secondary
metabolites [17]. They can be categorized into the following major classes: [18][19][16]
• terpenes
• Alkaloids
• Non­ribosomal peptides (NRPs) and ribosomally synthesized peptides (RIPPs)
• polyketides
• shikimic acid derivatives
Terpenes
Terpenes, as the largest group of secondary metabolites in fungi, play a crucial role due
to their diverse functions, including medicinal properties, agricultural applications, and
anti­inflammatory and antimicrobial effects [20]. Additionally, they are primarily lipophilic
compounds [21][22].

terpenes contain atoms other than carbon and hydrogen, such as oxygen, they are re­

alkaloids
Alkaloids are diverse natural molecules derived from amino acids, containing nitrogen
atoms. They are classified based on nitrogen atom presence and amino acid­derived
skeletons, and this diversity allows for various classification systems considering botani­
cal, biochemical, structural, and pharmacological aspects [26][27][28].
These compounds are further grouped based on their heterocyclic ring systems and
biosynthetic precursors, such as pyrrolidine, pyrrolizidine, indole, quinazoline, quinoline,
diketopiperazine, and purine alkaloids [29]. Alkaloids exhibit diverse properties, including
antibacterial, antimicrobial, antiproliferative, and antioxidant effects, making them poten­
tial candidates for drug development. For example, antimicrobial quinoline derivatives like
7­methoxycyclopeptin highlight their pharmacological relevance [29]. One of the unique
subgroups of nitrogenous fungal metabolites is ergot alkaloids, derived from L­tryptophan
primarily found in Claviceps species [30]. An example of an ergot alkaloid is clavine, illus­
trated in Figure 1.4. For a more detailed description and classification of fungal alkaloids,

Non­ribosomal peptide (NRPs)
Non­ribosomal peptides, crucial in microbial secondary metabolites, are formed through a
distinctive process. These peptides consist of amino acid building units linked together by
peptide bonds. A group of mega enzymes known as non­ribosomal peptide synthetases
(NRPs) oversees their assembly. NRPs consists of multiple modules, with each module
taking charge of incorporating one amino acid unit. These modules host a set of critical
domains designed for specific functions [32].

Ribosomally synthesized peptides(RIPPs)
RiPPs are characterized by their structural complexity, arising from a dual process involv­
ing ribosomal synthesis and subsequent post­translational modifications (PTMs). The
majority of RiPPs are derived from precursor peptides encoded by structural genes, typi­
cally ranging from 20 to 110 amino acid residues [34].
Despite utilizing the 20 proteinogenic amino acids during ribosomal synthesis, RiPPs
achieve enhanced structural diversity through extensive PTMs. These modifications not
only enrich the complexity of RiPP structures but also contribute to their diverse biologi­
cal activities. This category of peptides has gained recognition relatively recently, span­
ning all three domains of life—prokaryotes (bacteria and archaea), eukaryotes (including
fungi), and archaea. Fungi, as part of the eukaryotic domain, also produce a variety of
RiPPs [34]. One example of a ribosomal peptide in fungi is ustiloxin B, which was ini­
tially believed to be synthesized by an NRPS due to the presence of non­proteinogenic
amino acids. However, further research revealed that ustiloxin B follows a RiPS pathway,
confirming its synthesis as a ribosomal peptide in fungi, as shown in Figure 1.6 [35].

Polyketides biosynthesis
The polyketide biosynthesis pathway begins with the selection of a starter molecule, typi­
cally an acyl­CoA. This acyl­CoA is predominantly derived from acetyl­CoA, which is gen­
erated through processes like glycolysis or beta­oxidation [41].
The conversion of Acetyl­CoA to Malonyl­CoA is a critical step in this process. It com­
mences with ATP (adenosine triphosphate) and HCO3‾ (bicarbonate ion) interacting to
form a high­energy mixed anhydride (a). This mixed anhydride serves as an essential
intermediate for activating biotin, a coenzyme vital for carboxylation reactions. Initially
inactive, biotin is activated through its binding to the mixed anhydride via a nucleophilic
attack (b), leading to covalent attachment and enabling its role in subsequent carboxyla­
tion reactions.
In the subsequent stage, acetyl­CoA, a two­carbon molecule, becomes the target for car­
boxylation. The enzyme carboxylase catalyzes this process, resulting in the addition of a
CO2 molecule to acetyl­CoA (c). The activated biotin acts as a carrier for CO2, culminating
in the formation of Malonyl­CoA, a three­carbon molecule. In this step, the biotin­enzyme
complex is released as a leaving group (d), as shown in Figure 1.8 [18].
Figure 1.8: Conversion of Acetyl­CoA to Malonyl­CoA. Based on the reference [18].

an acyl carrier protein (ACP). The polyketide chain’s growth occurs through repetitive
cycles, with the addition of new building blocks (extender units), typically malonyl­CoA, to
the extending chain. The ketosynthase domain of a polyketide synthase (PKS) enzyme

Polyketide synthases (PKSs) are typically classified into three main classes: type I, II,
and III [36]. Type I PKSs are commonly found in bacteria and fungi, while type II PKSs
are predominantly observed in bacteria. In contrast, type III PKSs can be found in various
organisms, including plants, bacteria, and fungi [36][18].
Fungi employ iterative type I polyketide synthases (PKSs), notably multifunctional en­
zymes responsible for the stepwise assembly of polyketides. These PKSs rely on es­
sential domains, including ketosynthase (KS), acetyltransferase (AT), and an acyl carrier
protein (ACP), to carry out their functions [18].
The classification of iterative type I polyketide synthases (PKSs) is based on the extent
of reduction they undergo during the synthesis of the polyketide chain. These categories
include highly reducing (HR­PKSs) [42], partially reducing (PR­PKSs), and non­reducing
(NR­PKSs) [43], as shown in Figure 1.10.
• Highly Reducing (HR­PKSs): HR­PKSs possess the full complement of reducing do­

and double bond functional groups, exemplified by compounds like orsellinic acid, one of
the simplest known fungal aromatic polyketides [43].
Aromatic polyketides biosynthetic pathways
Orsellinic acid biosynthesis pathway
Orsellinic acid (compound 2) biosynthesis is initiated with two essential building blocks,
acetyl­CoA, and malonyl­CoA, as extensively discussed in the earlier section. These

molecules are used to construct a poly­β­keto ester through the action of enzymes known
as polyketide synthases (PKSs). Key enzymes involved in this process include ketosyn­
thase (KS), acyl transferase (AT), dehydratase (DH), acyl carrier protein (ACP), and thioesterase
(TE). This assembly involves repetitive addition of malonyl­CoA units to extend the polyke­
tide chain [18].
As the polyketide chain elongates, it adopts a more stable conformation. This folding
is facilitated by the ionization of the α­methylene group and allows for an aldol addition
to occur, specifically onto a carbonyl group located six carbon atoms away along the
chain. This addition results in the formation of tertiary alcohol. Subsequently, dehydra­
tion is favored due to the creation of a conjugated system. Following dehydration, eno­
lization takes place. Enolization involves the rearrangement of a hydrogen atom and a
double bond within the molecule, ultimately forming an aromatic ring. In the final step, the
enzyme­thioester(TE) linkage, which has been temporarily holding the growing molecule,
is hydrolyzed. This enzymatic process releases the fully formed orsellinic acid, as shown
in Figure 1.11 [18].

Shikimic acid
The shikimate pathway, also known as the chorismate biosynthesis pathway [19], is a
metabolic pathway found in bacteria, fungi, plants, and some protozoa [45]. It is respon­
sible for the biosynthesis of aromatic compounds, including essential amino acids such
as phenylalanine, tyrosine, and tryptophan, as shown in Figure (a) 1.13 [18][19][46]. The

verse biological processes. Phenylalanine, for example, serves as a foundational build­
ing block for proteins and acts as a precursor for the synthesis of essential molecules,
including neurotransmitters and hormones. Likewise, tyrosine and tryptophan also play
significant roles, serving both as building blocks for proteins and as precursors for the syn­
thesis of various bioactive compounds [19]. An illustration of fungal shikimic acid­derived

pathway reactions results in the formation of a seven­carbon molecule called 3­deoxy­D­
arabino­heptulosonate 7­phosphate (DAHP) [18][19][46]. This odd­carbon nature sets
the shikimic acid pathway apart from other metabolic pathways, especially polyketides
that primarily involve even­carbon molecules, allowing the production of unique aromatic
compounds in subsequent steps [18]. It’s worth noting that the shikimic acid pathway is
absent in animals and in other mammals [45][48][49].

Analytical chemistry
Analytical chemistry’s significance shines in its capacity to improve the quality and safety
of natural products, foster precision in scientific research, enhance reliability in diverse

Analytical chemistry is an indispensable tool in scientific research focused on natural prod­

empower researchers to delve deeply into the intricate molecular structures and chemical
properties of these compounds. This, in turn, leads to breakthroughs in understanding,

The role of analytical chemistry doesn’t stop there. Its impact extends across diverse
applications, including agriculture, cosmetics, and environmental monitoring. In each of

Moreover, as industries increasingly turn to using natural products in their processes, an­
alytical chemistry enhances the efficiency and sustainability of these practices. Whether
it’s optimizing the extraction of bioactive compounds from microorganisms, plants, and

ceuticals, or monitoring the environmental impact of natural product production, analytical
chemistry is an invaluable ally in driving progress [52][53].
1.3.1
Chromatography
Chromatography is a technique used to separate complex mixtures, and in this case,
fungal secondary metabolites, for analysis and identification. This procedure relies on a
straightforward principle, employing two crucial phases, namely the stationary phase and
the mobile phase [54]. Chromatography starts by introducing the mixture into a stationary
phase. This phase, along with the mobile phase, is essential for the separation. As
this unfolds, the components within the mixture move at different speeds because they
interact differently with the stationary and mobile phases. This difference in interaction
is what causes the effective separation of the components. Chromatography is typically
divided into two types based on the nature of the mobile phase: Liquid Chromatography
(LC) and Gas Chromatography (GC). Furthermore, chromatography can be classified into
different types, including ion exchange, size­exclusion, and adsorption chromatography,
each aimed at separating compounds according to their unique properties [54][55].
Ion Exchange
Ion exchange chromatography is a versatile technique used to separate and analyze ions
and charged molecules in a sample. It operates based on electrostatic interactions, uti­
lizing a charged resin in the stationary phase to attract and retain oppositely charged an­
alytes from the sample. The strength of this attraction depends on the nature and density
of charges present on both the stationary phase and the analytes [54][55]. This method
is invaluable in various scientific and analytical applications, including natural product
Thesis title
15
==PAGE:23==
analysis. It facilitates the differentiation of chemical components, such as enols, phe­
nols, carboxylic acids, and amines within fungal metabolites. Strong anion­ and cation­
exchangers (SAX and SCX) are employed to target carboxylic acids and amines, offering
crucial insights into the presence of these specific chemical groups. Additionally, it pro­
vides insights into the relative polarity of these compounds, reducing the need for extra
chromatographic steps and fractions in the analytical process [56].
Size Exclusion
Size Exclusion Chromatography (SEC), also known as Gel Permeation Chromatography
(GPC), is a chromatographic technique that separates molecules based on their size.
SEC utilizes a stationary phase, typically a porous gel matrix, through which molecules
traverse. Larger molecules are excluded from the pores, unable to penetrate and thus
move more rapidly through the column, while smaller molecules enter the pores, taking
a more extended route. Consequently, larger molecules elute first, followed by smaller
ones [57][58].
This technique is universally applicable, as it does not rely on specific interactions between
the analyte and the stationary phase. SEC finds diverse applications, including biochem­
istry for protein separation, polymer chemistry for determining molecular weight distribu­
tion, and quality control processes to assess the purity and integrity of biomolecules. Ad­
ditionally, This method is precious for scientists studying the composition and properties
of complex natural product mixtures [57][58].
Adsorption Chromatography
This technique includes both normal phase and reverse phase chromatography methods
to achieve separations based on the polarity differences [55].
Reverse Phase Chromatography (RPC)
Reversed­phase chromatography (RPC) is a versatile chromatographic technique for sep­
arating various compounds. In RPC, the mobile phase comprises polar organic solvents
like methanol, acetonitrile, water, or their mixtures, distinguishing it from normal phase
chromatography. The stationary phase typically involves nonpolar hydrocarbons, often
long carbon chains attached to silica beads. Common RPC columns include octyldecyl
(C18), octyl (C8), phenyl­hexyl (C6­Ph), and pentafluorophenyl (PFP).
For aromatic polyketides, phenyl­hexyl is the preferred choice due to its ability to capitalize
on π­electron cloud interactions, retaining these aromatic compounds efficiently. Con­
versely, aliphatic stationary phases are ideal for retaining linear non­polar compounds,
such as reduced polyketides, through van der Waals forces.
In RPC, the elution sequence is determined primarily by the hydrophobicity of compounds.
More polar compounds elute first with shorter retention times, as they exhibit a stronger
affinity for the polar mobile phase and form hydrogen bonds with H2O, interacting less
with the nonpolar stationary phase. In contrast, less polar compounds elute later due to
their strong interaction with the nonpolar stationary phase.
Other factors, including pH adjustment (often buffered with organic acids like trifluoroacetic
acid, formic acid, or bases like ammonium formate), pKa (the acid dissociation constant),
and Log D (the distribution coefficient), can also influence elution. Modifying the mo­
bile phase’s pH affects analyte ionization.
Depending on their acidic or basic forms,
compounds with different pKa values may behave differently in RPC. Log D, reflecting
the partitioning between the water and nonpolar phases, can also impact elution times
[54][55].

High­performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)
HPLC is an advanced separation method that employs high pressure, primarily due to
small­sized particles in the column. The elevated pressure is necessary to drive a liq­
uid mobile phase through a specialized separation column. Through this high­pressure
method, HPLC achieves efficient and rapid separation and analysis of complex mixtures
[54]. A typical HPLC system consists of several essential components, each with a dis­
tinct role in the separation process. These components encompass solvent reservoirs for
holding the mobile phase. This autosampler facilitates automated sample introduction,
a solvent delivery system (typically a high­pressure pump) for precise control of mobile
phase flow, a sample injection valve for accurate sample introduction, a high­pressure
chromatography column where the actual separation occurs, and a detector for captur­
ing and quantifying the separated compounds, converting the analog signal into a digital
chromatogram. This chromatogram is key for analyzing, quantifying, and reporting re­
sults. Refer to Figure 1.14 for a visual representation. In HPLC, there are two elution
methods: isocratic and gradient. Isocratic keeps the mobile phase constant, which is
ideal for simpler separations with similar compounds. Gradient elution changes the mo­
bile phase composition, which is suitable for complex mixtures with different retention
times. The synergy of these components and the choice between isocratic and gradient
elution make HPLC an indispensable and versatile tool in a wide range of scientific and
analytical applications [54][55].

1.3.2
Spectroscopy
Detectors
UV­Vis spectroscopy is an essential analytical method that relies on a UV­Vis detector,
a key component in the process. At its core, UV­Vis spectroscopy lies in the interaction
with light. It utilizes the output of two different light sources: Deuterium lamps, which
emit UV light in the range of 190–375 nm, and Tungsten lamps, covering the visible light
range from 200–640 nm [54][55]. Additionally, xenon pulse lamp sources offer an alter­
native to the traditional deuterium/tungsten dual lamp system [55]. A monochromator is
employed to select specific wavelengths within the UV­Vis range precisely. This straight­
forward method is widely used in UV­Vis spectrophotometers, where it plays a pivotal role
in measuring compound absorbance [60].
Diode Array Detector (DAD)
The DAD is an advanced detector used in UV­Vis spectroscopy [54]. The Diode Array
Detector (DAD) operates by first passing light from a deuterium lamp through a sample in
a flow cell. This light is then separated into its individual wavelengths by a fixed grating,
and these different wavelengths are detected by an array of photodiodes, each sensitive
to a specific wavelength. The DAD captures the entire UV­Vis spectrum of a sample as
it flows through the cell. This data is continuously collected and processed by a com­
puter, as shown in Figure 1.15. The UV­Vis DAD is often combined with HPLC to study
the light­absorbing properties of natural products, focusing on their chromophores. Nat­
ural products often feature unique chromophores that result in characteristic UV spectra,
making it easier to identify known compounds. Moreover, these spectra are valuable
for identifying shared chromophores among compounds of the same biosynthetic group,
aiding in the recognition of potential new analogs within these groups [61][62].

Mass Spectrometry is a versatile analytical method for identifying and quantifying sub­
stances by their mass­to­charge ratios. It involves ionizing a sample, separating the ions
according to their mass and charge, and then detecting and measuring them. Some com­
mon ionization techniques include Electrospray Ionization (ESI), Matrix­Assisted Laser

Electrospray Ionization (ESI)
ESI is an atmospheric pressure ionization technique with the unique advantage of serving
as both a sample inlet and an ionization source. It is a soft ionization method widely
used for coupling with liquid chromatography­mass spectrometry (LC­MS), allowing for
transferring ions from a liquid solution into the gas phase for mass spectrometry analysis
[61][54].
In ESI, compounds in a liquid sample are transformed into ions. This process begins by
pushing the liquid through a charged capillary tube, where a high voltage, typically ranging
from 2 to 6 kV, is applied at the capillary tip. This voltage causes the liquid to break
into tiny, charged droplets. As these charged droplets move, the liquid simultaneously
evaporates, leaving charged molecules behind. These charged molecules become ions
and are directed into a mass spectrometer for analysis. Nitrogen gas, often used as
a drying gas, assists in nebulization and directs the spray emerging from the capillary
tip toward the mass spectrometer, aiding in determining the size and structure of the
sample’s molecules. ESI is particularly beneficial for analyzing large, delicate molecules
like proteins and nucleic acids [61][63]. An ESI ion source schematic is illustrated in Figure

Quadrupole­Time of Flight Mass Spectrometry (QTOF)
After ESI, the ions enter a device known as a quadrupole, often referred to as Q1. This
essential component consists of four parallel metal rods. The quadrupole acts as a mass
filter by applying specific radio frequency (RF) and direct current (DC) voltages to these
rods. It permits ions with detailed mass­to­charge ratios (m/z) to pass through while ex­
cluding others. This selective filtering is crucial for isolating ions of interest from the com­
plex ion mixture generated during electrospray ionization (ESI).
Following Q1, the selected ions progress to Q2, the second quadrupole, which operates
as a collision cell, often equipped with a hexapole in my case. Here, ions undergo con­
trolled collisions with neutral gas molecules, often nitrogen or argon, initiating a process
known as collision­induced dissociation (CID). This process results in the fragmentation
of ions into smaller pieces [65].
Subsequently, these ions enter a flight tube. They travel a fixed distance within this tube,
and their precise arrival time at the detector is recorded. All ions are accelerated to the
same kinetic energy (Ekin = 1/2 mv²), meaning lighter ions move more rapidly, while heav­
ier ions move more slowly. This time­of­flight analysis allows for the accurate determina­

Data analysis
In LC­DAD­MS analysis, the dereplication process for discovering novel natural prod­
ucts involves distinguishing between adducts, fragments, and isotopic patterns [62][64].
Here’s how it unfolds: During both positive and negative Electrospray Ionization (ESI)
modes, analytes can generate ions by associating with elements such as hydrogen (H),
sodium (Na), and potassium (K), resulting in the formation of adducts. Common posi­
tive adducts include [M+H]+, [M+Na]+, [M+K]+, and [M+NH4]+, while common negative
adducts encompass [M­H]‾ [61].

In addition, adducts and fragments can occur during the ionization process. These frag­
ments involve the loss of specific groups or atoms, such as CO2 or H2O, which further
contributes to the complexity of the mass spectra [61].
Recognizing common adducts like [M­H]‾ or [M + H]+ is pivotal for accurate compound
identification. These adducts can induce variations in the observed mass within the mass

Additionally, Isotopic patterns are crucial for characterizing compounds, with a focus on el­
ements like carbon (C), oxygen (O), nitrogen (N), hydrogen (H), chlorine (Cl), and bromine
(Br). The isotopes of these elements determine these patterns and are vital for accurate
compound characterization [54].
The monoisotopic mass calculation, which is fundamental in compound identification, re­
lies on the most abundant isotope of each element. For instance, carbon­12 (12C)is the
monoisotopic isotope of carbon and is used as a reference for calculating the monoiso­
topic mass. In nature, various isotopes are often present. For example, carbon­13 (13C)
is another isotope of carbon, contributing to the isotopic pattern due to its natural abun­
dance of 1.1%. This contribution results in the appearance of a small M+1 peak in the
isotopic pattern, which provides valuable information about the molecular composition.
To calculate the number of carbon atoms based on the ratio between 12C and 13C, you
can use the following formula [54]:
n = (relative height % of M+1) / (relative abundance % of (13C))
Furthermore, specific elements in a molecule result in distinctive isotopic patterns. Iodine
(I) introduces a gap in the isotopic pattern, typically at mass 127. Chlorine (Cl) causes the
M+2 peak to be one­third of the M+ peak. Bromine (Br) can lead to an M+2 peak equal
to M+ in some cases. Nitrogen (N) leads to an odd­numbered M+ peak, while sulfur (S)
results in the M+2 peak being approximately 4.5% of the M+ peak, as shown in Figure
1.18 [54][61]. Understanding these isotopic patterns and the influence of various isotopes
is essential for accurate molecular composition determination during compound analysis
and characterization. This knowledge is applied when identifying known and unknown
compounds in LC­DAD­MS analysis.
When analyzing known compounds, the process involves targetted extraction in an ex­
tracted ion chromatogram (EIC) for the positive or negative molecular ions. This is fol­
lowed by comparing the EIC with a reference standard to confirm the match, which in­
cludes comparing the retention time. Additionally, the UV spectra are examined, and the
MS/MS spectrum is extracted to compare with established databases such as Reaxys,
SciFinder and Pubchem. When identifying unknown compounds, the process begins by
extracting the base peak chromatogram using negative mode ESI. After that, specific
peaks within the chromatogram are chosen for further analysis, focusing on identifying
the [M­H]‾ ion. The major mass spectrum peaks are particularly important in this context.
By comparing them, it is possible to determine whether they belong to the same or dif­
ferent compounds. Deconvolution techniques are employed, which is accomplished by
aligning the extracted ion chromatograms of these peaks. After this, it’s time to venture
into comprehensive databases like Antibase. The search is based on the estimated exact
mass of the molecular ion, other adducts and fragments, and hypothetical formulas. Fur­
ther information retrieval, including UV data, significantly aids identification. Once all the
data have been gathered, the results are evaluated to determine whether the compound
is already known or a likely new discovery. This process is named dereplication.

Global Natural Products Social Molecular Networking (GNPS)
GNPS, the Global Natural Products Social Molecular Networking platform, is an essential
mass spectrometry­based compound analysis tool. It empowers researchers to explore
and understand complex compound mixtures, from natural sources like fungi, and plants.
At the heart of GNPS lies the principle of spectral networking, a fundamental concept
in mass spectrometry data analysis. This principle allows for the efficient organization,
identification, and discovery of compounds within these intricate mixtures. Let’s explore
the key steps and significance of spectral networking within the GNPS platform [66].
First, MS/MS spectra are generated, serving as detailed fingerprints of the compounds
within the mixture. As explained above, during data collection, observing similarities in
these spectra, such as common peaks, peak intensities, and fragmentation patterns, indi­

computational algorithms are employed to compare and identify spectra that share resem­
blances. These similar spectra are then grouped, forming clusters. These clusters can be
associated with reference spectral libraries, which contain spectra of known compounds.
This linkage facilitates the identification of biosynthetical related compounds within the
mixture. When a spectrum in the sample aligns with a cluster of reference spectra, it sug­
gests that the compound in the sample is related to those represented in the reference
spectra. Referring to the Figure 1.19. If a cluster in the desired sample matches a cluster
of reference spectra, it allows for the confident identification of known compounds, en­
hancing our understanding of the sample’s composition. However, clusters in the desired
sample that do not match with reference spectra suggest the existence of unknown or
novel compounds [66].

Convert tool (version 3.0.23233­c72ce16) [70]. Subsequently, peak picking was con­

In the data preprocessing phase using MZMine, a meticulously designed workflow was

Initial screening
The primary objective of the initial screening was to cultivate B.bassiana to produce oosporein

The investigation involved cultivating a single strain of B.bassiana, IBT 36140, in both

Dextrose Agar (PDA), additional media formulations included Czapek Yeast Autolysate

under consistent conditions at a temperature of 25°C in darkness for 13 days to assess
fungal growth and metabolite production.
In addition to solid media, three distinct liquid media formulations were explored: CY,
PDB, and CY90. Both CY and CY90 media contain sucrose, with CY90 having a higher
concentration compared to CY. A nutrient­rich CY and CY90 media supplemented with
sucrose was chosen based on study findings and existing literature [2]. Cultivation was
conducted under static and shaking conditions at 25°C in darkness for 7 days to assess
fungal growth and metabolite production under varying agitation levels.
Unlike literature [1], this study refrained from utilizing bacterial strains, yeast, or other
species of Beauveria, and avoided heterologous expression. This approach was aimed
at understanding the behavior of B.bassiana in the selected media formulations without
the influence of external organisms. By combining insights from the study with existing
literature, the aim was to gain a comprehensive understanding of B.bassiana’s adapt­
ability in various media under different conditions and its biosynthesis of oosporein. The
findings contribute valuable insights into cultivation strategies and biosynthetic pathways
underlying this fungal secondary metabolite.

The second screening aims to further evaluate the most promising candidates or con­

their suitability to identify the most suitable media for optimizing oosporein production

untargeted compounds. This investigation evaluated the same solid media employed in
the initial screening by cultivating fungi under the same media and conditions. Addition­
ally, various liquid growth media without agar, namely CY, CY90, PDB, were evaluated,
along with the additional media YES, under both static and shaking conditions. The evalu­
ation was conducted over both the standard 7­day period and an additional 12­day period.
This approach aimed to comprehensively understand the impact of the different growth
conditions on pigment production over an extended duration.
By the 7th day, a noticeable difference in fungal morphology became apparent between
static and shaking cultures. In static cultures, B.bassiana formed a surface layer, while in
shaking cultures, it exhibited a denser, gel­like appearance, consistent with observations
from the first screening. The color of the static liquid CY, CY90, and YES media, at both
7 and 12­day intervals, resembled red wine. Shaking cultures also displayed a red wine
color on day 7, transitioning to a lighter hue on day 12.
Additionally, a notable disparity was observed in the static YES, CY, and CY90 media.
On the 9th day, B.bassiana covered the surface of the media and became thicker with
a rough surface after 12 days of incubation. Growth of B.bassiana in most liquid media,
while evident, was less pronounced in the PDB media over a 12­day period, revealing
limited granulated biomass compared to the other three media. This suggests that the time
period does not significantly affect the formation of mycelium in PDB media, as illustrated
in Figure 3.23. The discernible reduction in mycelium growth in Potato Dextrose Broth
(PDB) during the 12­day shaking period, in contrast to other media such as YES, CY, and
CY90, prompts considerations about the media’s appropriateness. Despite PDB being a
nutrient­rich media, the fungus may exhibit specific nutrient preferences better addressed
by alternative media. YES, CY, and CY90 could potentially provide a more favorable
nutrient profile conducive to enhanced mycelium growth.

3.3.3.1 The proposed oosporein (compound 1) biosynthesis pathway
In the study, the aim was to propose a potential extended pathway for oosporein biosyn­

of oosporein closely parallels that of phenicin up to a critical stage. Both phenicin and
oosporein undergo a similar process to produce compound 3. However, beyond this

radical at carbon position 6. This transformation is a significant step in the pathway. The
proposed pathway for oosporein biosynthesis suggests a potential route in which the rad­
ical form of compound 6 undergoes dimerization to yield oosporein as the final product,

3.3.3.2 The proposed phenicin (compound 5) biosynthesis pathway

the oosporein biosynthesis pathway, it yielded distinct new pigments compared to the
other three media. This difference may suggest a competition between oosporein pro­
duction and the formation of these new pigments, implying that the YES media may be
less conducive to oosporein production. As a result, further testing in the YES media was
discontinued.
Between CY and CY90, these two media exhibited similar LC profiles. The unexpected
absence of phenicin in CY90, compared to its presence in CY media, challenges pre­
vious assumptions regarding the effect of nutrient conditions on phenicin production, as
reported in the literature [2]. This suggests that higher concentrations of sucrose might
not effectively stimulate phenicin production, raising doubts about its effectiveness for
oosporein production in these media, as observed in this investigation.
onsidering these observations, CY 7­day static media emerges as the preferred choice for
initial scale­up production. Its capacity to produce key orsellinic acid­derived compounds,
including oosporein and phenicin, underscores its potential for large­scale oosporein pro­
duction while providing valuable insights into the complex nature of biosynthetic interac­
tions. Therefore, CY 7­day static media is recommended for further scaling up oosporein
production, especially given the relatively lower sucrose content compared to CY90.

3.5
Optimal media selection for large­scale fungal cultivation
The decision regarding the preferred media for large­scale cultivation is based on a com­
prehensive evaluation of fungal morphology, data analysis, and dereplication of known
and unknown compounds. Solid media were dismissed from consideration primarily due
to the absence of the majority of targeted compounds. Additionally, practical considera­
tions, such as the ease of purification and extraction, further underscored the preference
for liquid media over solid alternatives. Among the liquid media options (CY, CY90, YES,
and PDB), shaking conditions were rejected due to the preference for orsellinic acid pro­
duction over oosporein. Considering that CY90 contains 90 g/l of sucrose, while YES has
a higher concentration of 150 g/l, it’s plausible that the varying levels of sucrose could
influence metabolism, potentially leading to higher production of orsellinic acid in shaking
media. In static conditions, PDB was not selected due to limitations in mycelium forma­
tion. Static YES media, known for producing many different new pigments, suggested
high competition between oosporein and new pigment production, making it unsuitable
for large­scale production. In the comparative analysis between CY, which contains 30
g/l of sucrose, and CY90, which has a higher concentration of 90 g/l, it was challenging
to produce phenicin in CY90. This suggests that the higher sucrose concentration may
not have specific efficacy in enhancing oosporein production in this investigation. Conse­
quently, CY90 was not chosen as the preferred media for large­scale production.
Additionally, the successful first scale­up of oosporein production resulted in the acqui­
sition of 140 mg of crystallized oosporein. This, coupled with the discovery of a new
Compound 10, further emphasizes the dominant oosporein production in CY static 7­day
media. Therefore, the decision was made to proceed with CY static media for large­scale
production.
In conclusion, this media demonstrates remarkable capabilities in producing oosporein,
characterized by consistent dark red coloration and dense biomass, making it an excel­
lent choice for large­scale pigment production. Furthermore, optimization efforts resulted
in achieving 98% crystallized oosporein from CY static media.

4. Conclusions and future perspective
Dereplication analysis revealed that B.bassiana produces oosporein, which is derived
from orsellinic acid in both solid and liquid media under static and shaking conditions,
showcasing its adaptability to different environments. Additionally, B.bassiana synthe­
sizes other targeted compounds such as orsellinic acid and phenicin. Further analysis
of MS/MS data showed that B.bassiana can produce a variety of oosporein analogues.
Two potential analogues were identified: compound 9, with [M­H]‾ 291.0504, is proposed
to be formed through the radical coupling of compound 6, representing half of oosporein,
and the reduced form of compound 4, an intermediate of phenicin. Compound 10, with
[M­H]‾ 289.289.0362, a hybrid of half of oosporein and half of phenicin formed by radical
coupling as well. However, the biosynthesis pathway of the isomeric form of compound
10, labeled as 10*, remains unidentified in this investigation. These two analogues have
not been reported from B.bassiana. Additionally, compound 11 with [M­H]‾ 563.0835 and
its isomeric form 11* were proposed to be formed by the tetramerization of compound 10
through a condensation reaction.
Among the various media tested, liquid media under static conditions were chosen for
several reasons. Firstly, in all solid media, crucial targeted compounds such as orsellinic
acid and phenicin were absent. However, despite the preference for oosporein produc­
tion in OAT with agar among the solid media, the liquid media were ultimately selected
for their ease of extraction compared to solid media. When comparing static and shaking
conditions in liquid media, static conditions were found to be better for oosporein pro­
duction. This preference stems from the observation that higher sucrose concentrations
favored orsellinic acid over oosporein in shaking media. These findings underscore the
multifaceted role of sucrose in fungal metabolism and emphasize the importance of media
selection in optimizing oosporein production. Among the static and shaking liquid media,
CY static 7­day was chosen for its ability to optimize oosporein production. The acquisi­
tion of 140 mg of crystallized oosporein from this media highlights its efficacy in facilitating
robust oosporein production, positioning it as a prime candidate for large­scale pigment
manufacturing.
Other static liquid media such as CY90, PDB, and YES were not chosen for large­scale
production. Despite CY90 containing a higher concentration of sucrose as a nutrient
source, it did not demonstrate specific advantages compared to CY. YES displayed the
ability to produce a large variety of new pigments, leading to high competition between
oosporein and new pigment production. Although PDB showed a preference for oosporein
production, the biomass in PDB was significantly lower compared to other media.
These findings set the foundation for future research projects to delve deeper into oosporein
biosynthesis and related compound production in B.bassiana. The insights gained pave
the way for precise manipulation of metabolic pathways and enzymatic processes, op­
timizing growth conditions for maximum oosporein yield. This knowledge holds poten­
tial applications in various industrial sectors, particularly in redox flow batteries, where
oosporein’s contributions can be significant.
