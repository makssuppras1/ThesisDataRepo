Abstract
This thesis explores the development and optimization of a DIVERSIFY cassette for tar-
geted gene integration in Komagataella phaffii. Initially, a construct expressing gRNA
and MAD7 was created to facilitate site-specific integration at the XII-1 locus. Using
USER cloning, a plasmid was engineered to express both gRNA and MAD7 endonuclease,
which was validated through successful fragment amplification and sequencing. Despite
initial challenges in in vivo assembly due to suboptimal DNA fragment concentrations,
the project progressed to use fusion PCR to improve fragment concentrations. Success-
ful integration of the DIVERSIFY cassette was demonstrated through the detection of
β-glucuronidase (uidA) activity. Further validation confirmed successful integration at
the target locus using colony PCR and genomic DNA analysis. Functionality tests using
I-SceI enzyme indicated effective gene replacement, with a 70% efficiency in swapping
uidA for mCherry. However, attempts to use MAD7 endonuclease for RFP gene integra-
tion were unsuccessful, likely due to insufficient double-strand breaks or issues with the
transformation. This thesis highlights the effectiveness of the DIVERSIFY cassette for
site-specific recombination and suggests improvements for future applications.

Aim
Numerous non-conventional fungal species have a range of relevant physiologic and metabolic
properties necessary for development of efficient cell factories. However, utilization of
these species is impeded by the lack of adequate genetic engineering tools. To facilitate
the application of non-standard species, the DIVERSIFY platform was constructed. This
tool enables heterologous gene expression in multi-species strains. We will work on de-
veloping the DIVERSIFY toolbox, by expanding the repertoire of non-conventional yeast
platform strains and creating a DIVERSIFY cassette for application in multi-species fun-
gal platform strains. In addition, we will apply recently developed in vivo DNA assembly
methods for construction of new DIVERSIFY platform strains, and for heterologous gene
expression cassettes in these strains.

Introduction
Yeast, particularly Saccharomyces cerevisiae, has been widely utilized as a cell factory
in biotechnology due to its robust fermentation capabilities, ease of genetic manipula-
tion, and well-understood biology. As a versatile eukaryotic microorganism, yeast can be
engineered to produce a variety of valuable compounds, including biofuels, pharmaceu-
ticals, and industrial enzymes [52]. For instance, yeast has been optimized to produce
ethanol [49], an important biofuel, through fermentation processes . Additionally, it is
used for the biosynthesis of complex molecules like insulin and other therapeutic proteins.
The ability to tailor yeast metabolism and pathways through genetic engineering makes
it an indispensable tool in synthetic biology and industrial biotechnology. [52] [35] [36]
Traditionally, the selection of host strains when using yeast as a cell factory involves
choosing strains that exhibit desirable traits such as high growth rates, robustness under
industrial conditions, and the ability to produce high yields of the target product. Com-
mon selection criteria include tolerance to stress factors like high ethanol concentrations,
acidity, and osmotic pressure, which are often encountered during industrial fermentation
processes. [5] Additionally, the genetic background of the yeast strain is a crucial factor.
Strains with well-characterized genetics and available genetic tools are preferred, as they
facilitate easier manipulation and optimization of metabolic pathways. Saccharomyces
cerevisiae is frequently chosen due to its long history of use in brewing, baking, and
bioethanol production, as well as its well-documented genetic toolbox. [52] [31] [50] Al-
though S. cerevisiae is widely used in biotechnology, relying solely on one strain or species
may not yield optimal results. This method can be restrictive, as various yeast strains or
even species can display a significant diversity in their metabolic and physiological traits.
For instance, previous studies have provided evidence that the synthesis of specific com-
pounds, such as vanillin-β-glucoside, can exhibit significant variations across different
strains of S. cerevisiae, even when identical genetic modifications are used. [26] [10] This
observation implies that the metabolic structure of the strain can have a significant im-
pact on the efficiency of production. Given this, manufacturing processes with a variety
of yeast strains or species may be more efficient. This approach allows leveraging the
metabolic characteristics of different strains or species to select the most suitable host for
chemical production. [52] Despite the potential benefits, this multispecies approach is not
widely adopted, likely due to the increased complexity it introduces, including the need
for species-specific genetic tools and the development of expertise in handling multiple
types of yeast. [26] [52]

2.1
DIVERSIFY cassette
To overcome the limitations of using a single yeast strain or species for biotechnology
purposes, a more versatile approach is necessary. Many yeast strains and species have
metabolic variability, thus no one organism is suited for all production processes. [46]
The DIVERSIFY platform allows various species to be used as cellular manufacturing
platforms. This strategy lets researchers leverage species-specific metabolic capacity to
improve industrial process efficiency and customisation. DIVERSIFY standardizes gene
integration across various organisms, and expands the toolkit available for synthetic bi-
ology but also opens up new possibilities for optimizing the production of valuable com-
pounds. The DIVERSIFY principle is based on a Common Synthetic Gene Integration
site (COSI) that allows for the integration of a single Gene-Expression Cassette (GEC)
into multiple species. This approach minimizes the need for species-specific gene-targeting
substrates, enabling efficient identification of optimal cell factory hosts for heterologous
production. [26]
The DIVERSIFY cassette (Shown in figure 2.1), is constructed with a reporter gene
(uidA) flanked by two targeting sequences (A1 and B1) that facilitate homologous re-
combination. This cassette allows for the insertion of a Gene-Expression Cassette (GEC)
containing the gene of interest (GOI) into defined genomic loci across different DIVER-
SIFY strains. [26] The UP (upstream) and DW (downstream) regions in the DIVERSIFY
cassette refer to the locus-specific sequences flanking the COSI site. The UP region is
located before the uidA gene, while the DW region is positioned after it, both serving
as homologous sequences that facilitate the targeted integration of the Gene-Expression
Cassette (GEC) into the genome of the host organism. These regions are crucial for en-
suring precise recombination and successful gene insertion. [26] The uidA gene serves as a
reporter encoding β-glucuronidase, which allows for easy quantification and identification
of transformants. Transformants with correctly integrated GECs into the COSI-1 site are
identified through a blue/white screening method on solid medium containing X-Gluc,
where white colonies indicate successful integration (No β-glucuronidase production) and
blue colonies indicate unsuccessful targeting (β-glucuronidase production). This selec-
tion process simplifies the identification of desirable transformants in the screening of
gene expression outcomes. [26]

sequences, A1 and B1, facilitates homologous recombination in the DIVERSIFY cassette.
The locus-specific sequence upstream (UP) and downstream (DW) regions allow accurate
integration of the DIVERSIFY cassette in target strains. Using a blue/white screening
approach on X-Gluc media, white colonies indicate successful targeting while blue colonies
suggest poor integration. Figure inspired from [26]

2.2
Chromosomal integration
By inducing double-strand breaks (DSBs) in the DIVERSIFY cassette integrated in a
cell of interest, the DIVERSIFY platform takes advantage of the cell’s natural repair
pathways to achieve accurate and stable gene integration. Eukaryotic cells possess two
distinct mechanisms for repairing double-strand breaks (DSBs): Non-homologous end
joining (NHEJ) and homologous recombination (HR). These mechanisms are crucial for
the cell’s survival because a double-stranded break (DSB) would otherwise be lethal. [42]
In the non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) mechanism, damaged DNA ends are ligated
together directly by enzymes without the need for a complementary DNA strand to act
as a template. This can result in the occurrence of smaller DNA insertions and deletions,
which in turn cause mutations in the genome. [33] [14] [43] Homologous recombination
(HR) is a high-fidelity DNA repair mechanism that fixes double-strand breaks by using a
homologous sequence as a repair template, ensuring accurate restoration of the original
genetic information without introducing mutations. HR can be utilized to genetically
modify yeast by providing a repair template that shares homology with a particular
integration site, leading to accurate and error-free integrations. [33] [43] The DIVERSIFY
platform relies on the precision of HR to ensure that the provided repair template (Gene-
Expression Cassette) is accurately integrated into the genome at the location of the
DSB. [26] To achieve this, it is essential to inhibit the non-homologous end joining (NHEJ)
pathway, which, if active, would compete with HR and lead to imprecise DNA repair.
[51] [56] NHEJ could result in random insertions or deletions, preventing the correct
integration of the provided template. By disabling the NHEJ pathway, it increases the
likelihood that cells will utilize HR exclusively, thereby ensuring the precise and stable
incorporation of the template, which is crucial for successful gene integration in the
DIVERSIFY cassette. [26]

the need for a homologous template, often leading to insertions or deletions (indels). In

2.2.1
Homologous recombination (HR) in yeast
Homologous Recombination (HR) in yeast is initiated by identifying and removing the
double-strand break (DSB) ends, resulting in the formation of single-stranded DNA over-
hangs. Subsequently, the protruding ends are covered with the Rad51 protein, which
aids in locating a matching sequence and encourages the invasion of the strand into the
corresponding DNA double helix. [44] The invading strand binds to the complementary
sequence, resulting in the formation of a displacement loop (D-loop). The process of
DNA synthesis elongates the D-loop, and subsequently resolves the resulting structure,
thereby guaranteeing precise repair of the break while minimizing any loss of genetic
information. [48] [2]
2.2.2
MAD7 mediated HR
In order to introduce a DSB MAD7 could be utilized. MAD7 is an endonuclease that
recognizes and cuts DNA at precise locations as determined by a guide RNA (gRNA).
The gRNA is specifically designed to match a specific sequence in the yeast genome.
MAD7 recognizes the process of RNA-DNA hybridization and triggers a double-strand
break (DSB) at the specific location. [12] [41] The gRNA design involves the selection
of a sequence that is complementary to a specific target region in the yeast genome.
MAD7 identifies the RNA-DNA hybridization upon binding and induces a double-strand
break (DSB) at the exact site. [34] In order to achieve efficient gene editing, a donor
DNA template is introduced. This template contains the desired sequence modifications,
which are surrounded by regions that are similar to those surrounding the double-strand
break (DSB). This similarity helps in ensuring accurate repair through homologous re-
combination. The activity of MAD7 depends on the existence of a suitable Protospacer
Adjacent Motif (PAM) sequence located next to the target site. The PAM sequence for
MAD7 typically consists of ”YTTV” (where ”Y” indicates a C or T and ”V” represents
either A, C, or G). This particular sequence is essential because it is necessary for the
enzyme to attach to and cut the DNA at the specified site. The spacer sequence, which
directs the enzyme to the desired location, typically consists of 20-24 nucleotides and is
specifically designed to match the DNA sequence directly preceding the PAM site (see
Figure 2.3). [34]

designed guide RNA (gRNA), introduces a double-strand break at a precise location.
The gRNA is tailored to match a specific DNA sequence, allowing MAD7 to recognize
and bind the RNA-DNA hybridization site. Upon binding, MAD7 induces a DSB at

2.2.3
CRISPR/Cas9 mediated HR
Similarly, the CRISPR/Cas9 system operates on the same principle of targeted genome
editing. The Cas9 nuclease and a single guideRNA (sgRNA) make up the CRISPR/Cas9
system. Using a 20 nucleotide sequence known as the protospacer, the sgRNA guides
Cas9 to a particular location within the genome. The protospacer must exist immediately
upstream of the integration site to identify the integration site via base-pairing is that
a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM). [58] [57]. 5’-NGG-3 is the consensus sequence for
the Cas9 PAM, and N may represent any one of the four DNA nucleotides (see Figure
2.4) [39]. Thus, DSB induction and repair template integration by HR are made possible
by co-transforming a a CRISPR/Cas9-expressing plasmid that targets the integration
locus, along with a repair template flanked by homologous sequences. [32]
Figure 2.4: This figure depicts the CRISPR/Cas9 system for targeted genome editing.
The system consists of the Cas9 nuclease and a single guide RNA (sgRNA). The sgRNA,
which includes a 20-nucleotide protospacer sequence, directs Cas9 to a specific genomic
location by base-pairing with the target DNA sequence. This target must be adjacent to
a Protospacer Adjacent Motif (PAM) sequence, with the consensus sequence 5’-NGG-3’.
Cas9 induces a double-strand break (DSB) at the site, allowing for gene editing. Figure
inspired from [47]
2.2.4
DSBs using meganucleases
Unlike MAD7 and CRISPR/Cas9, using meganucleases is an option that can identify and
cut very specific, lengthy DNA sequences without guideRNA. Meganucleases are well-
known for its capacity to identify and cleave lengthy, very specific DNA sequences, which
are usually between 12 and 40 base pairs. [16] Compared to other genome editing tools,
meganucleases have a substantially lower chance of off-target effects, which makes them
especially effective for focused genetic alterations due to their high specificity. I-SceI, a
meganuclease first isolated from the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae, is one of the most
researched and extensively used proteins. [17] It is an excellent technique for causing DSBs
at specific sites since it can identify an 18-base pair sequence that is uncommon in most
genomes. [15] Meganucleases like I-SceI are limited because of their reliance on particular
recognition sequences, in contrast to the more flexible MAD7 and CRISPR/Cas9 systems,
which can be readily programmed to target different sequences by modifying the guide
RNA. Nonetheless, this inflexibility offers the benefit of unmatched precision, reducing
inadvertent genetic alterations. [16] [11]

2.3
In vivo assembly
To construct and integrate a new DIVERSIFY cassette into target cells, it is beneficial
to ensure that the assembly process is both quick and efficient in order to integrate the
cassette into multiple species. Traditional methods of assembling such cassettes can be
time-consuming, often requiring multiple cloning steps in E. coli. To streamline this pro-
cess, in vivo DNA assembly can be employed, allowing the DIVERSIFY cassette to be
directly assembled within the target organism. This approach not only accelerates the
assembly process but also enhances genetic engineering efficiency, reducing intermediate
steps and the risk of unwanted bacterial sequences. In vivo DNA assembly can be ef-
ficiently achieved by utilizing the fact that PCR fragments, which contain homologous
overlaps of approximately 30-50 base pairs, can be precisely fused together through ho-
mologous recombination (HR) (see Figure 2.5). This methodology was developed from
the discovery that a single DNA fragment can be effectively inserted into a linearized
plasmid through homologous recombination.
Subsequent research demonstrated that
multiple DNA fragments could be fused in vivo, using the same HR approach, leading
to the creation of more complex plasmids.
The method demonstrated the ability to
assemble up to six different DNA fragments in parallel, showcasing its versatility and
efficiency across multiple species of Aspergilli. [27] It allows for faster and more efficient
construction of plasmids and gene-targeting substrates in the target organism, facilitat-
ing high-throughput projects. Additionally, this approach reduces the risk of introducing
unwanted sequences and enhances the stability of the resulting vectors. [27]

2.4
The molecular biology of K. phaffii
In order to expand the versatility and efficiency of the DIVERSIFY platform, it is im-
portant to explore its application in different host organisms beyond the commonly used
Saccharomyces cerevisiae. Pichia pastoris (P. pastoris), recently reclassified as Koma-
gataella phaffii (K. phaffii), is a type of yeast that is commonly used in biotechnology
and molecular biology for the synthesis of recombinant proteins. K. phaffii is a host
organism that is highly desirable for gene expression and protein production due to its
genetic and biochemical characteristics. [53] [8]. K. phaffii has a relatively small genome,
consisting of around 9000 genes that are spread across four chromosomes. [21] The yeast’s
genome encompasses all the necessary apparatus for methanol metabolism. Methanol is
broken down through the alcohol oxidase (AOX) pathway, and the AOX1 gene, which
codes for alcohol oxidase 1, is highly responsive to methanol and can be strongly ac-
tivated. This promoter system is highly adjustable and can be suppressed by glycerol
or glucose, making it a potent tool for precise regulation of expression of recombinant
proteins. [40] [25] The yeast’s capacity to grow to high cell densities and its effective secre-
tory pathway facilitate the synthesis of substantial amounts of recombinant proteins. [9]
Frequently employed vectors in K. phaffii expression systems typically incorporate the
AOX1 promoter or a methanol-inducible promoter, with selection markers such as antibi-

The focus will be on designing a DIVERSIFY cassette and developing an endonuclease

3.1
Construction of DIVERSIFY cassette
The DIVERSIFY cassette in this project will consist of four fragments made up of the
following components:
• The uidA gene, encoding the enzyme β-glucuronidase, will be utilized as a reporter
gene within the DIVERSIFY cassette. The activity of β-glucuronidase provides
a straightforward and visual assay for successful integration and expression of the
cassette. This will be placed on fragment 2 as shown in Figure 3.1.
• The thymidine kinase (TK) gene will serve as a negative selectable marker in the
DIVERSIFY cassette. The TK gene allows for the selective elimination of cells
in which the gene is still present, making it an effective tool for confirming the
insertion of new genes into the cassette.
Upon exposure to nucleoside analogs
such as ganciclovir or acyclovir, cells expressing the TK gene will metabolize these
compounds into toxic forms that terminate DNA synthesis, leading to cell death.
Cells in which the TK gene has been replaced by the new gene of interest will
survive, enabling the selection of successfully modified cells. This will be positioned
as shown in Figure 3.1 on fragment 3.
• To facilitate precise homologous recombination, the reporter and TK genes will be
flanked by targeting sequences, referred to as DIVERSIFY UP and DW. These
sequences are designed to promote homologous recombination, ensuring accurate
integration of the cassette into the desired locus within the K. phaffii genome. As
shown in Figure 3.1, the DIVERSIFY UP will be added to fragment 1 and the
DIVERSIFY DW region to fragment 4.
• Surrounding the DIVERSIFY UP and DW sequences (seen in Figure 3.1) will be
regions homologous to the integration site XII-1 in the K. phaffii genome. These
XII-1 UP and DW regions will guide the DIVERSIFY cassette to the specific ge-
nomic location, promoting site-specific integration through homologous recombina-
tion. This approach ensures that the cassette is inserted precisely within the target

integrated genes.
• The expression of the uidA and TK genes will be driven by strong and well-

Appropriate terminators will also be included to ensure proper termination of tran-
scription and stability of the mRNA transcripts.

3.2
Selection
Resistance markers serve as a tool to ensure that only those cells that have the desired
genetic elements will survive under selective conditions. This approach is particularly ben-
eficial for preventing contamination by unwanted strains during the experimental process.
A yeast resistance marker is a useful tool to guarantee successful plasmid transformations.
Nourseothricin (NTC) is an antibiotic composed of multiple streptothricins. It specifi-
cally inhibits protein synthesis and induces errors in the process of translation. NTC
can affect a diverse range of organisms, such as bacteria, yeast, filamentous fungi, and
plant cells. The presence of the resistance marker Nourseothricin N-acetyl transferase
(NAT) will deactivate NTC, enabling cells to thrive in its presence. [24] Hygromycin
(HYG) is an antibiotic that targets protein synthesis by inhibiting ribosomal function.
It is particularly effective against a range of microorganisms, including bacteria, yeast,
and filamentous fungi. HYG disrupts translation by binding to the ribosomal RNA and
preventing proper protein synthesis, which ultimately leads to cell death. In genetic en-
gineering, the use of HYG resistance markers provides a powerful selection tool. [7] [13]
Both of these resistance markers will be used in this project.

Uracil-Specific Excision Reagent (USER) cloning is a highly adaptable and effective tech-

inclusion of extra sequences or marks, which may arise from restriction enzyme-based
cloning techniques. This method can assemble multiple fragments simultaneously , which
makes it very adaptable for building complex genetic constructs. [22] [19].

3.4
DNA Delivery and Integration Strategies
A reliable and effective technique for delivering DNA across the cell membrane is essential
for the integration of the DIVERSIFY cassette and other DNA components (such as
repair templates or endonucleases) into novel species. In this situation, electroporation
seems to be a very successful method that can introduce exogenous DNA into yeast
cells by transformation. This method involves applying a brief, high-intensity electric
pulse to yeast cells, which temporarily disrupts the cell membrane and allows DNA from
the surrounding environment to enter the cells. [3] The first step in the procedure is to
prepare electrocompetent yeast cells by growing them to the ideal transformation stage
in the mid-logarithmic phase.
The cells are then cleaned and submerged in a buffer
solution that contains lithium acetate. This solution increases the permeability of the
cell membrane, and makes transformation possible. The cell suspension and the DNA
to be introduced are mixed during the electroporation process. The cell-DNA mixture is
placed into a cuvette designed for electroporation. Then, a brief, intense electric pulse
Genetic tools for construction of multi-species fungal platform strains
14
==PAGE:20==
goes across the electrodes and induces temporary disruptions in the cellular membrane,
facilitating the introduction of DNA into the cells. After electroporation, the yeast cells
are put in a recovery solution, which helps the cells recover from the stress of the electric
pulse and allows the cell membrane to close. During this healing phase, the cells express
antibiotic resistance genes or selectable markers present in the inserted DNA. [28] The
efficiency of electroporation can be affected by a number of variables, including DNA
content and purity, cell density, and the electroporation buffer. [18]
3.5
Validation strategies
Using strong validation techniques is essential for verifying the incorporation of the DI-
VERSIFY cassette and evaluating the gene switching functionality inside it. By using
these techniques, it ensure that the cassette is properly incorporated and functions cor-
rectly. As mentioned in earlier sections, the first step in the validation process will be
to insert double-strand breaks (DSBs) and then include new repair templates into the
DIVERSIFY cassette. Colony PCR will be used to verify the incorporation of the DI-
VERSIFY cassette and the presence of repair templates. Colony PCR is a technique used
to amplify specific DNA sequences directly from bacterial or fungal colonies. This tech-
nique eliminates the need for DNA extraction by lysing the cells and releasing their DNA
using the heat from the PCR reaction’s first denaturation stage. [1] For this procedure,
new colonies must be used since aged colonies may show signs of plasmid loss or genetic
degradation that might affect the precision of the PCR results. Fresh colonies improve
DNA accessibility and increase the likelihood of producing clear PCR results. [54] [59].
Sanger sequencing may be used to validate the colony PCR findings even further. Sanger
sequencing, also known as chain-termination sequencing, is a DNA sequencing method
where a complementary strand is synthesized by using a single-stranded template from
the sample. Chain-terminating dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs) are integrated at certain
stages of this process, which terminates the DNA synthesis and results in fragments of
varying lengths.
Afterwards, capillary electrophoresis is used to determine the exact
nucleotide sequence of these fragments.
This method provides precise verification of
the PCR-amplified sequences, confirming the accuracy of the genetic modifications or
constructs. [6]

small glass beads. The tube contents were homogenized in a FastPrep machine at speed
4 for three cycles of 20 seconds each. The tubes were centrifuged briefly using a table-top
mini centrifuge. Transfer 150 µl of supernatant from each tube to a 1.5 Eppendorf tube,
add 15 µl of 5M NaCl, 400 µl ice-cold 96% ethanol, and spin at 10.000 xg for 3 min at

the pellets were dried by heating the tubes in a 50°C heating block. The dried pellets
were resuspended in sterile MilliQ water, vortexed, and then centrifuged at 13,000 xg for
30 seconds. For each tube, 150 µl of supernatant was transferred to a new Eppendorf
tube and saved as gDNA.

Test DIVERSIFY cassette functionality using MAD7
induced breaks
In this section, the functionality of the DIVERSIFY cassette was tested using the MAD7
endonuclease (pDIV849 and pDIV855) to induce targeted breaks in the K. phaffii genome.
The goal was to assess whether the MAD7 enzyme could effectively facilitate the replace-
ment of the uidA gene, responsible for blue coloration, with a gene encoding RFP (both
linearized and circular pDIV666). By introducing site-specific breaks at the DIVERSIFY
cassette integration site, we aimed to enable precise recombination events that would
result in the loss of the blue color and the expression of RFP.

To achieve this, K. phaffii cells containing the DIVERSIFY cassette were co-transformed

gene. Successful recombination and gene replacement would be indicated by the disap-

The transformation result revealed blue colonies with no expression of RFP. This indicates

at the intended site. The results of the gel electrophoresis showed no bands, confirming
that the RFP gene had not been integrated into the genome as expected. There are

had sufficient double-strand breaks generated by the MAD7 endonuclease.
Problems

uptake or expression in the K. phaffii cells. Homologous recombination may have failed,
preventing effective integration, if the homologous arms around the RFP gene were not
properly matched to the target location. Given that the plasmid used in Section 5.4
demonstrated RFP integration into the DIVERSIFY cassette, this does not seem likely.
Another possible explanation is a failed transformation due to ineffective electroporation
and inefficient DNA absorption. The competent K. phaffii cells had been stored in the

possibly contributing to a poor transformation.

Building general DIVERSIFY cassette using USER
cloning
In this section, the focus is on constructing a new version of the DIVERSIFY cassette,
without strain-specific regions, which includes a TK marker. This updated cassette is
intended to function as a versatile tool for development of strain-specific cassettes focused
on K. phaffii, as well as potentially other organisms. To achieve this, the plasmid encoding
the DIVERSIFY cassette will be assembled using the USER cloning procedure.
The choice of USER cloning over in vivo assembly stems from the goal of increasing the
efficiency of plasmid construction. USER cloning, known for its precision and ability to
seamlessly join DNA fragments, offers a higher likelihood of successful assembly, reducing
the risk of errors and improving the overall yield of the desired construct. This section
presents the results of the USER cloning process and the successful creation of the general
DIVERSIFY cassette, which will lay the groundwork for future applications in strain-
specific genetic modifications.

Conclusion
In this project, we developed a functioning DIVERSIFY cassette for targeted gene inte-
gration in Komagataella phaffii. The construction of a gRNA-MAD7 expressing construct
facilitated precise integration at the XII-1 locus, validated through colony PCR and PCR
preformed with gDNA. Although initial in vivo assembly faced challenges, including low
DNA fragment concentrations and inefficient transformation, subsequent fusion PCR im-
proved fragment quality and concentration, leading to successful assembly and integration
of the DIVERSIFY cassette. The functionality of the DIVERSIFY cassette in K. phaffii
was validated by the effective replacement of the uidA gene with mCherry, demonstrating
the system’s capability for gene replacement. The success rate for replacing uidA with
mCherry was 70%, as confirmed by colony PCR. Despite these successes, attempts to use
MAD7 endonuclease for integrating RFP were unsuccessful, suggesting the need for im-
provements in either the endonuclease’s efficiency or the transformation process. The aim
of this project was to expand the repertoire of non-conventional yeast platform strains and
creating a DIVERSIFY cassette for application in multi-species fungal platform strains.
The repertoire was expanded by the Komagataella phaffii with the DIVERSIFY cassette
expressing the uidA gene. A general DIVERSIFY cassette for multi-species application
were constructed by USER cloning without locus-specific sequences but was never veri-
fied.

7
Future perspectives
Building on the advancements achieved in this thesis, there are several promising di-
rections for future research that could further enhance the capabilities and applica-
tions of the DIVERSIFY cassette in Komagataella phaffii.
One of the most promis-
ing strategies involves replacing the MAD7 endonuclease system with the more widely
used CRISPR/Cas9 system. CRISPR/Cas9 is a well-established tool in genome editing,
known for its robustness, precision, and high efficiency in creating double-strand breaks.
By transitioning to Cas9, we could potentially overcome the challenges observed with
MAD7, such as issues with effective double-strand breaks and integration efficiency. The
use of Cas9 might streamline the editing process, reduce off-target effects, and improve
the overall success rate of targeted integrations in K. phaffii.
Beyond optimizing the endonuclease system, expanding the use of the DIVERSIFY cas-
sette to other yeast species presents an exciting opportunity for broader application in
synthetic biology and industrial biotechnology. Hanseniaspora uvarum and Ogataea poly-
morpha are two yeast species with significant biotechnological potential. Hanseniaspora
uvarum is known for its role in natural fermentation processes, particularly in the wine
industry, and could benefit from targeted genetic modifications to enhance flavor profiles
or fermentation efficiency. [30] [4] Ogataea polymorpha is already used in industrial pro-
cesses due to its ability to grow at high temperatures and utilize methanol as a carbon
source, making it an excellent candidate for metabolic engineering. [29] By incorporating
the DIVERSIFY cassette into these species, we could unlock new possibilities for strain
improvement and pathway engineering.
Future work could focus on refining the transformation protocols to enhance the effi-
ciency of DIVERSIFY cassette integration in various yeast strains. This could involve
optimizing electroporation conditions, testing different integration sites, and exploring
alternative transformation methods tailored to each species’ unique physiology.
It would be worthwhile to revisit the in vivo assembly method, which encountered ini-
tial difficulties due to suboptimal DNA fragment concentrations. Given the advance-
ments in molecular biology techniques and a better understanding of the DIVERSIFY
cassette’s requirements, a renewed attempt at in vivo assembly could lead to more suc-
cessful outcomes. A possibility could be development of a DIVERSIFY cassette without
the thymidine kinase (TK) marker. The TK marker, while useful for selection, may have
unintended toxic effects on the cell, potentially limiting the overall efficiency and stability
of the genetic modifications.
