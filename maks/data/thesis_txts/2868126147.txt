Abstract 
This study assessed the energy use and climate change resilience of office buildings at 
the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). Energy simulations in IDA ICE were 
conducted for present and projected future weather scenarios, including heatwaves, 
using climate files based on EURO-CORDEX data and the IPCC’s RCP8.5 high-emission 
scenario. To assess simulation accuracy, energy use and indoor temperature results 
were compared with measurements conducted by DTU. The study also compared 
results using national average and localized climate files and evaluated the impact of 
window opening during the heating and cooling seasons. 
A significant gap was observed between measured and simulated energy use and 
indoor temperatures in all buildings, mainly due to modeling assumptions and the 
exclusion of laboratory energy consumption for research purposes from the 
simulations. Window opening during the heating season increased heating 
consumption by 6.5% to 24%, whereas during the cooling season it had a minimal 
impact on cooling consumption. Window opening during the cooling season led to a 
significant reduction in average indoor temperatures, with a decrease of 7.6oC 
compared to fully closed windows. Simulations using the DRY climate file with national 
average data exhibited increased heating and cooling energy consumption and 
improved thermal environment compared to simulations using the localized climate 
file, highlighting the need to produce more local climate files. 
The simulations of future scenarios revealed decreased heating energy consumption, 
but increased cooling energy consumption of the buildings due to rising outdoor 
temperatures. The buildings had a poor thermal environment that was worsened in the 
future scenarios and during heatwaves, with high indoor temperatures and significant 
overheating. Building 115, an old poorly insulated building, had the highest heating 
consumption per heated floor area and a poor thermal environment, requiring envelope 
improvements and the installation of mechanical ventilation to enhance its 
performance and resilience against climate change. Building 220, despite its high 
insulation and mechanical cooling, exhibited significant overheating and poor thermal 
environment in combination with increased cooling consumption in the future, 
==PAGE:6==
6 
 
highlighting the need of reducing occupancy of the zones to the designed levels or 
upgrading the ventilation system. Building 409, featuring glass façades showed the 
lowest heating energy consumption, but also significant overheating and poor thermal 
environment in the cooling season. To improve its present and future thermal 
environment, installing additional shading systems and implementing active cooling 
are recommended.

1 Introduction 
The building sector is one of the main contributors to global energy use and greenhouse 
gas emissions. In the European Union, buildings are responsible for approximately 40% 
of energy consumption and around one-third of energy-related greenhouse gas 
emissions. For this reason, the EU is aiming for an energy efficient and fully 
decarbonized building stock by 2050 [1].  
Non-residential buildings account for a significant portion of total energy use in Europe, 
with office buildings being among the highest energy consumers [2]. Energy 
consumption in non-residential buildings mainly arises from space heating and cooling, 
electrical equipment and lighting [2]. Space heating and cooling are significant 
contributors to building energy consumption, with approximately two-thirds of heating 
energy produced by fossil fuels, further driving greenhouse gas emissions. In addition, 
rising temperatures due to climate change are increasing electricity demand for cooling 
[3]. The significant increase in greenhouse gas emissions caused by the building sector 
does not align with the Paris agreement commitments and thus further actions must be 
taken to tackle climate change [4]. 
Climate change has various consequences on the ecosystem and humans. One 
consequence is the impact on the indoor environment of buildings, which can affect 
thermal comfort and occupants’ well-being. Extreme weather events like heatwaves 
are expected to become more frequent and severe, affecting more people. The high 
outdoor temperatures caused by climate change also increase indoor temperatures in 
buildings, leading to reliance on mechanical cooling. This results in increased energy 
consumption and further greenhouse gas emissions from energy production. 
Evaluating the impact of climate change on buildings is therefore essential to identify 
strategies that mitigate its effects [5], [6].  
This report specifically focuses on university office buildings, analyzing the impact of 
climate change on energy consumption and thermal environment to assess their 
climate change resilience.

1.1 Objective 
This study evaluates the energy use and climate change resilience of office buildings 
located at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). It examines the impact of climate 
change on the buildings’ energy consumption and thermal environment through 
simulations using present and projected future weather data spanning 2001 to 2100. 
Simulations are conducted for entire years and during heatwave periods to assess the 
effects of extreme events. In addition, potential vulnerabilities of the buildings to 
climate change are identified. The findings will contribute to DTU’s ongoing efforts to 
reduce energy-related greenhouse gas emissions and to improve indoor environmental 
quality. 
 
1.2 Climate change 
Climate change is the long-term alteration of weather patterns that is caused by human 
activities. These activities include burning fossil fuels, industrial processes, agriculture 
and deforestation, which lead to significantly increased greenhouse gas emissions. The 
greenhouse gases trap heat from the sun in earth’s atmosphere, causing global 
temperatures to rise, which leads to environmental and societal impacts [7].  
In 1988, the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) and the United Nations 
Environment Programme (UNEP) established the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate 
Change (IPCC) to give policymakers frequent assessments of climate change, its 
effects and risks and potential mitigation strategies. These assessments aim to make 
governments adopt policies that will address climate change. 
 So far six IPCC Assessment Reports have been published, some of which laid the 
groundwork for the Kyoto Protocol (1997) and the Paris Agreement (2015). The Kyoto 
Protocol is an international treaty that industrialized countries committed to, for the 
reduction of greenhouse gas emissions. It was the first global agreement that 
industrialized countries, and the European Union committed to collectively reduce 
emissions by 5% below 1990 levels during the period of 2008-2012. The Paris 
Agreement is another international treaty, but unlike the Kyoto Protocol it involves all 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
countries. Each country must set a national plan that will reduce emissions, with a 
future goal to reach net-zero emissions in the second half of the century. In addition, 
the objectives included limiting the rise in global temperatures to well below 2oC, with 
efforts to restrict it to 1.5oC above pre-industrial levels [8]. 
The IPCC has introduced greenhouse gas emission projections based on assumptions 
about economic and technological development, energy use, population growth and 
policies taken. The first scenarios were included in the IPCC First Assessment Report 
(Far), including economic, population and technological assumptions. In 2000 the 
Special Report on Emissions Scenarios (SRES) was published which was used in the 
Third Assessment Report (TAR) and Fourth Assessment Report (AR4) of IPCC. The SRES 
introduced long-term greenhouse gas emission scenarios based on variations of socio-
economic development, which were used for the creation of future climate models 
under different pathways [8].  
However, the SRES did not include climate change mitigation policies and led to the 
creation of Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs) which were introduced in 
the IPCC Fifth Assessment Report (AR5). Unlike previous scenarios, RCPs include 
climate policies and agreements. Furthermore, RCPs are defined by radiative forcing 
levels and not by socio-economic drivers, which allows for extensive research and 
modelling of climate impacts globally and locally. Radiative forcing measures the 
balance between the energy the planet receives from the sun and the energy it radiates 
back, and it is expressed in W/m2. Each RCP scenario covers the energy imbalance that 
might be caused by future greenhouse gas emissions, measured by the additional 
energy on earth in the year 2100. The four RCP scenarios are: 
• RCP2.6: a low emission scenario with radiative forcing of 2.6 W/m2 by 2100. 
• RCP4.5: an intermediate emission scenario with radiative forcing of 4.5 W/m2 by 
2100. 
• RCO6.0: a less ambitious scenario with radiative forcing of 6 W/m2 by 2100. 
• RCP8.5: a high emission scenario with radiative forcing of 8 W/m2 by 2100. 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
RCP2.6 is the most ambitious scenario which assumes rapid decarbonization with 
significant reduction of greenhouse gas emissions and global temperatures 1.5oC to 
2oC above pre-industrial levels.  
RCP4.5 assumes moderate climate mitigation policies with use of renewable energy 
and fossil fuels, resulting in moderate emission reduction and global temperatures 2oC 
to 3oC above pre-industrial levels. 
RCP6.0 assumes continued use of fossil fuels and small climate change mitigation 
policies, resulting in global temperatures 3oC above pre-industrial levels. 
RCP8.5 also known as “business-as-usual” scenario assumes no measures against 
climate change, resulting in global temperatures increase of 4oC to 5oC above pre-
industrial levels [9]. 
 
Figure 1: IPCC Representative Concentration Pathways [9] 
In IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (AR6) the Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) 
were introduced to complement RCPs in a socio-economic dimension. SSPs consist of 
variation of population and economic growth, technological development and 
international cooperation scenarios, which combined with RCPs promote research and 
decision-making for climate change mitigation [8].  
In Denmark, the average annual temperature has increased approximately by 1.5oC 
since the 1870s and it is expected to rise further [3]. Nevertheless, Denmark has 
achieved significant greenhouse gas emissions reduction. From 1990 to 2020, Denmark 
achieved a reduction of 42% (not including emissions from land use, land-use change 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
and forestry or LULUCF), contributing to the 34% reduction achieved collectively by the 
EU and UK. This reduction was driven by the Danish energy sector, which achieved 48% 
of the total emission reduction. In addition, Denmark established the Danish Climate 
Act which in 2020 set targets of reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 50% to 54% 
(including LULUCF) in 2025 and by 70% (including LULUCF) in 2030 compared to the 
levels of 1990, aspiring to become carbon neutral by 2050. Projections from April 2024 
indicate that Denmark will reach a 55.5% emission reduction for its 2025 goal and 68% 
reduction for its 2030 goal [10]. 
The production of primary energy in Denmark decreased by 2.8%, while the production 
and consumption of renewable energy increased by 391% in 2023 compared to 1990. In 
2023, more than half of primary energy production (54.1%) came from renewable 
energy sources, most of which was used for heating households. In 2021, the energy 
consumption of commerce and public services accounted for 14%, while households 
accounted for 31%, meaning almost half of the energy consumption was covered by 
these two sectors [11]. 
 
1.3 Sustainability at Technical University of Denmark 
The Technical University of Denmark (DTU) is an internationally known university 
leading in technical and natural science fields with a strong focus on sustainability. Its 
main campus is located near Lyngby, north of Copenhagen, with more than 100 
buildings covering 451,911 gross m2. DTU also has research facilities around Denmark 
and two smaller campuses located at Ballerup and Risø, the latter used for research 
purposes.  
DTU supports global efforts of reducing CO2e emissions and continually takes 
measures to improve its climate footprint. From 2013 to 2020 it has decreased its 
emissions coming from heating and electricity by half by transitioning to district 
heating. 85% of DTU’s emissions originate from purchased goods and services, 25% of 
which relate to building and construction projects and their operation and maintenance 
[12].

2017-2020. However, a reduction of CO2e emissions has been achieved by using lower

consumption. Most of CO2e emissions come from electricity used for ventilation

In the period of 2017-2019 to 2021, a decrease of 22% in energy-related CO2e 
emissions was achieved and in 2022 DTU had set goals for further reduction. The 
objectives were the optimization of the foundation for CO2e reduction of energy 
consumption that can be directly influenced by 2023 and the reduction of CO2e 
emissions related to energy consumption by 2025. 
In addition, DTU has implemented initiatives to improve its energy-related climate 
footprint, such as purchasing renewable energy and planning a heat pump in 
collaboration with the district heating company that heats the campus. Furthermore, it 
is working on making its energy consumption information more widely available to 
aspire other universities to do the same, but also to raise awareness in their own staff 
and students. Furthermore, it is researching new technologies and improvements that 
will further reduce its buildings’ CO2e emissions. 
Except from the reduction of energy-related emissions, DTU focuses on circular 
economy principles in its operations and sustainable campus development. Several 
goals have been set across areas like waste, water, transportation and buildings. For 
new constructions, DTU aims to increase sustainability certifications and improve 
existing buildings. It also prioritizes high indoor environmental quality both in newly 
constructed and existing buildings. This will be done by using digital tools, 
questionnaires and monitoring systems. The gathered data will guide future 
improvement actions [12].

2 Methodology 
This chapter outlines the methodology and assumptions made during the study. It 
provides a detailed description of the processes undertaken, from building selection to 
the analysis of the results. 
The first step involved investigating the building stock of DTU Lyngby campus and 
selecting the buildings to be studied. The rationale was to choose a mixture of old and 
new buildings, since the campus consists of various types of structures. 
 After selecting the buildings, all necessary data were gathered. This included 
architectural and HVAC drawings and information on building materials, which were 
downloaded from the DALUX platform. This platform is used by DTU’s Campus Service 
(CAS) and hosts all relevant drawings and documentation related to each building. Data 
regarding the operation of the HVAC systems, such as schedules, heating and cooling 
setpoints and other parameters were collected through interviews with Campus 
Service’s staff. In addition, energy use data for the buildings was extracted from 
BMSnet, which is the building operational platform of the campus and contains 
historical and present data. Building inspections were also conducted to gather 
supplementary information that could not be retrieved through other methods. 
 The next step involved researching which climate files would be used for present and 
future scenarios, as described in detail in the next sections.  
Subsequently, the selected buildings were modeled and simulated in IDA ICE. The 
simulations were conducted for both present and future scenarios. Microsoft Excel, in 
combination with Python, was used to extract the results and generate graphs. The 
energy results were compared with real-time data from BMSnet. For the comparison of 
the thermal environment, measurements from the study “Bedømmelse af indeklima i 
bygninger på DTU Campus Lyngby med TAIL og APV” by Ida Birk Feldskou and Lina Fiil 
Præstegaard were used [13]. This study examined the indoor environment of DTU 
buildings 115, 220 and 409 by conducting measurements in various rooms from March 
11, 2024, to March 25, 2024. The simulation results for the same period were compared 
with these measurements. The final steps of the study involved discussing the results 
and drawing conclusions.

Since the building was constructed in the 1970s, the insulation thickness of the 
external walls is limited to 6-7 cm, while the basement walls and floor lack insulation 
entirely. The roof is the only well-insulated element, having been renovated. 
The building’s windows are fixed, except for a 1.00 m x 0.80 m portion of each glazing,

The building has windows on all facades, with the west and part of the north ground

by common open areas in the center. From the second to the fifth floor, the west

Heating system 
The building is heated by a combination of district heating and air heating. Water 
radiators are installed in the offices and laboratories located along the facades, while 
the remaining occupied areas, mainly meeting rooms and smaller laboratories, are 
heated by the ventilation system. Bathrooms and some of the technical rooms are also 
heated by water radiators. In addition, a floor heating system is installed in parts of the 
open areas on the ground and first floors on the west side of the building [15], [16]. 
Cooling system 
Cooling is provided by the ventilation system which includes active cooling. In addition, 
several cooling units are installed in laboratories and some technical rooms that 
require lower temperatures than other areas [15], [16]. 
Ventilation system 
The building uses mechanical ventilation to ensure sufficient air quality and provide 
cooling during the warm months. Most rooms are equipped with a combination of 
supply and exhaust diffusers, except for areas like elevators, staircases and some 
technical rooms. The ventilation system is controlled by a VAV system based on indoor 
temperatures [15], [16].

The building is well insulated, with high insulation thicknesses in all elements except for

The facades consist of glass curtain walls with triple glazing and aluminum mullions.

Heating system 
The building is heated by a combination of district heating with water radiators and air 
heating through the ventilation system. Water radiators are installed along the interior

Ventilation system 
The building has mechanical ventilation to maintain indoor air quality and required 
temperatures. Air supply diffusers are installed in all open offices, office rooms, 
meeting rooms, some common areas and the basement showers. Exhaust diffusers are 
located in all bathrooms, kitchens and two meeting rooms in the center of the ground

on the same spots of the first floor above the bathrooms. 
The air flow in the rooms is controlled by a VAV system that is temperature and CO2 
controlled. This means that airflow increases when CO2 levels and room temperatures

airflow is provided.

2.2 Dynamic simulations 
For the energy and indoor environment simulations, IDA ICE software was used. This 
dynamic simulation tool provides hourly data on the indoor climate in individual rooms 
as well as the total energy consumption of a building. The simulations were conducted 
by modeling the building geometry and its properties, materials, location and 
orientation, weather data and HVAC systems. Moreover, data about each zones’ 
occupants, equipment and lighting were used as inputs. After creating the building 
model and uploading the relevant climate files containing local weather data, yearly 
simulations were run. The results include data about a building level like total energy 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
use and on a zone level where hourly data of different parameters like indoor 
temperatures were extracted. 
 
2.2.1 Climate files 
 To study the effect of climate change on the buildings’ energy and thermal environment 
performance, simulations were run for different scenarios, including present and future 
years. The buildings’ location was set to Copenhagen from IDA ICE’s database. The first 
scenario was run for the year 2023 with the climate file “DRY_2001-2010_epw-fil”. This 
file is currently used in Denmark for energy simulations to document a building’s energy 
and thermal environment performance according to BR18. It contains hourly 
meteorological data of various parameters for a reference year, which combines 
representative months with data for the period 2001-2010. The data were collected 
from three meteorological stations across Denmark to ensure representation of the 
whole country [19], [20], [21]. 
For future scenarios, climate files produced by Aalborg University’s study “Generation 
and analysis of typical meteorological years for future weather and detection of 
heatwaves” were used and the methodology of the study is described in the next 
paragraphs. 
 This study generated climate files for Copenhagen covering a period from 2001 to 2100 
by using a methodology proposed by the Weather Data Task Force, IEA EBC Annex 80. 
These files include typical meteorological year (TMY) data, which contain hourly 
weather data for a representative year that reflects average weather conditions over a 
multi-year period. In addition, climate files containing periods of heatwaves were 
generated. 
The future climate files were generated based on the RCP8.5 “business-as-usual” 
scenario, which assumes no significant actions are taken to mitigate climate change. 
Data from the World Climate Research Program’s Coordinated Regional Downscaling 
Experiment for Europe (EURO-CORDEX) which produced regional-scale climate 
forecasts from 1950 to 2100 were also utilized. These forecasts were adjusted by using 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
measurements of weather data from a meteorological station in Copenhagen for the 
period 2000 to 2019 [22]. 
Typical Meteorological Year (TMY) files 
The TMY files that were generated by Aalborg University’s study followed NF EN ISO 
15927-4 standards by selecting 12 representative months from multiple years, 
depending on the studied period. For the selection of the representative months, the 
parameters considered were dry-bulb temperature, global horizontal radiation of 
equivalent first order, relative humidity and wind speed of second order. Cumulative 
Distribution Function and Finkelstein-Schafer statistics were used to statistically 
analyze and compare each month’s conditions with long-term averages of 20-year 
periods and then select the representative months. Three TMY files in epw form were 
generated, which can be used in energy simulation software like IDA ICE, representing 
the periods 2001-2020 (present), 2041-2060 (mid-term future) and 2081-2100 (long-
term future) [22]. 
Heatwave Year (HWY) files 
For the generation of the heatwave files a method suggested by G. Ouzeau et al. was 
used by Aalborg University’s study to identify heatwaves, which adapted to fit the 
EURO-CORDEX dataset. In Denmark, a heatwave is defined as at least three 
consecutive days that the maximum temperature exceeds 28oC in more than 50% of 
the country. Instead, this study used thresholds of percentiles of daily mean 
temperatures from 2001-2020. Thus, three thresholds were defined: 
• Spic is the 99.5th percentile of the temperature distribution of 2001-2020 and it is 
the threshold to detect a heatwave. 
• Sdeb is the 97.5th percentile of the temperature distribution and it is the threshold 
to identify the heatwave duration. 
• Sint is the 95th percentile of the temperature distribution and it is the threshold to 
identify the end of the heatwave. 
The files were generated for the most intense, the most severe and the longest 
heatwaves during the periods of 2001-2020, 2041-2060 and 2081-2100. The intensity in 
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
oC was calculated as the sum for every heatwave day of the positive difference of the 
daily mean temperature and Sdeb divided by the difference of Spic and Sdeb. It is noted in 
the report of the study that although there might be thermal adaptation this was not 
considered. This means that future thresholds might be higher because people might 
be adapted to the higher temperatures [22].

were identified and can be seen in the table below. The heatwave file for the year 2054 
was not included in the simulations due to numerical issues that caused the

2.3 Simulation models 
The studied buildings were modelled in IDA ICE based on Revit models and HVAC 
drawings downloaded from the DALUX platform. The materials of the building elements 
were modelled according to Tables Table 1 - Table 3. The thermal bridges of the building 
envelopes were set to “Preserve wall volume” to align with the position of the insulation 
and the values were selected from IDA ICE’s options depending on each building’s 
construction period. The pressure coefficients of the building envelopes were set as 
semi-exposed since DTU campus is not densely built. To simplify the models and 
reduce simulation time, the surroundings of the buildings and the corridors connecting 
them to other buildings or to the underground tunnels of DTU campus were excluded. 
The simulations for Building 115 took around 12 hours for each whole-year scenario, 
whereas simulations for Buildings 220 and 409 lasted approximately 6 days and 1 day, 
respectively, for each scenario.

2.4 Extraction of results and figures 
The simulation results were extracted, and figures were generated using Microsoft 
Excel and Python scripts. Results such as energy use and overheating hours for each 
zone were directly exported as excel files and then graphs were plotted. However, for 
hourly temperature data, due to the 8,760 values per zone for each simulated year and 
the large number of zones in the buildings, Python scripts were employed. These scripts 
filtered the temperature values only for occupied hours and combined the values of all 
occupied zones to produce boxplots showing the temperature ranges of each building 
for each simulated period. 
The energy consumption results of the simulated present scenarios (DRY 2023, TMY 
2001-2010) were compared with the energy consumption of the buildings measured by 
Campus Service for the year of 2023. For Building 220, a direct comparison of 
measured cooling energy consumption with simulation results was not feasible due to 
the presence of special laboratories, freezing rooms and other spaces with cooling 
units that impact energy use. It was observed that in the winter months the measured 
cooling energy consumption of Building 220 was on average 54.6 MWh and in the period 
from May to October, the consumption rose above this value. Since it is unlikely that 
cooling is required during winter and based on the simulated cooling energy usage 
diagram, a calculation was made. The differences between monthly cooling energy 
consumption from May to October and the value of 54.6 MWh were summed and the 
result was compared with the cooling energy consumption of the simulations. 
To evaluate the thermal environment of the buildings, it was examined if the zones were 
complying with BR18’s overheating limits for offices, which are: 
==PAGE:52==
52 
 
• Operative temperatures must not exceed 26oC for more than 100 hours 
annually.  
• Operative temperatures must not exceed 27oC for more than 25 hours annually. 
It was also assessed if the operative temperatures of the zones were within the ranges 
of Table B.5 of EN:16798-1:2019 to determine their thermal environment categories. 
 
3 Results 
This chapter presents the energy and thermal environment performance of each 
building across the simulated scenarios. 
Present scenarios 
• DRY 2023: Simulations based on weather data representative of the whole of 
Denmark. 
• TMY 2001-2020: Simulations based on localized weather data specific to 
Copenhagen. 
Future scenarios 
• TMY 2041-2060: The mid-term future scenario simulated with a climate file 
based on weather data for Copenhagen. 
• TMY 2081-2100: The long-term future scenario simulated with a climate file 
based on weather data for Copenhagen. 
Firstly, the two present scenarios are compared to analyze the impact of using the 
same climate file for the entire country versus a climate file for the local area. Another 
comparison is made between the present (TMY 2001-2020), mid-term future (TMY 2041-
2060) and long-term future (TMY 2081-2100) simulated scenarios, which were run with 
weather data only for Copenhagen to study the effects of climate change on building 
energy and thermal environment performance. Finally, heatwave scenarios with various 
durations are compared to evaluate the performance of the buildings against extreme 
events.

4 Discussion 
4.1 Energy performance of buildings 
The energy performance of buildings was evaluated by firstly assessing the accuracy of 
simulation models and comparing simulated energy consumption with measurements 
from Campus Service. The evaluation also included a comparison between the two 
simulated present scenarios, since different climate files were used. The DRY 2023 
scenario was simulated with weather data gathered from multiple weather stations 
around Denmark representing the entire country, whereas the TMY 2001-2020 scenario 
was simulated with local weather data. In addition, the impact on energy consumption 
from window opening was assessed in Building 115 and Building 220 as described 
previously. Lastly, scenarios representing the present, mid-term future and long-term 
future with local weather data were analyzed to assess the impact of climate change on 
the buildings. 
Measured and simulated energy performance 
To evaluate model accuracy, the actual heating and cooling energy consumption of the 
buildings was compared with the simulated consumptions of the present scenarios. In 
all the buildings, simulated consumptions were lower than the actual consumptions 
==PAGE:80==
80 
 
measured by Campus Service. The largest difference between actual and simulated 
consumption was observed in Building 220 (85% for heating energy and 81% for cooling 
energy) and the smallest one in Building 409 (7.2% to 22.4% for heating energy). Some 
of the factors for this difference were likely the size and complexity of the model of 
Building 220, a 6-floor building with more than 300 zones, compared to the smaller, 2-
floor Building 409 with fewer zones. Modeling a complex building requires numerous 
assumptions, which can significantly influence building energy performance and 
sometimes lead to unrealistic results. 
Furthermore, Buildings 115 and 220, which had the largest gaps in measured and 
simulated energy consumption, have laboratories which use additional heating and 
cooling energy for research purposes. This energy consumption was included in the 
measurements by Campus Service, but it was not considered in the simulations. 
Another factor is occupancy assumptions. In Building 409, occupancy could be visually 
observed, but this was not feasible in the larger Building 220. In addition, it was 
assumed that occupancy of the zones was always at 100% of the capacity to study the 
worst-case scenario, but offices are rarely fully occupied. 
The difference between the measured and simulated heating energy consumption was 
also evident when comparing the buildings. According to measurements by Campus 
Service, Building 115 had the highest consumption per heated floor area in 2023, 
followed by Building 220 and Building 409. However, simulations showed much lower 
consumption for Building 220 compared to the other buildings, likely because they did 
not account for the energy demand of laboratories. 
In reality, the older and less insulated Building 115 had the highest heating energy 
consumption, reflecting its inefficient building envelope. Improvements such as adding 
extra insulation to the building envelope and replacing the double-glazed windows 
would likely reduce its heating energy consumption. The larger and highly insulated 
Building 220 followed, with its higher heating energy use likely attributed to the 
additional demand of laboratories. The smaller office Building 409 had the lowest 
heating energy consumption, probably due to its compact size, the lack of laboratories 
and the higher solar gains through the glass facades. 
==PAGE:81==
81 
 
Window-opening simulations 
Window opening during the heating season also influenced energy consumption. 
Simulations showed that opening windows daily from 13:00 to 17:00 increased heating 
energy consumption by around 24%. Although it is unlikely that all windows of the 
building will remain open for half of the daily occupied hours during colder months, 
window opening can still impact energy consumption. For instance, in a more moderate 
window opening scenario from 12:00 to 13:00 daily, heating energy consumption 
increased by 6.5%. Therefore, window opening during the heating season can 
sometimes significantly increase heating energy consumption, with the level of impact 
depending on occupant behavior. 
For Building 220, two window opening scenarios were analyzed, since it was not 
possible to add window opening during the cooling season due to simulation errors. 
Instead, a simpler model of the 5th floor of the building was simulated with a scenario 
without window opening and a scenario with window opening during the cooling 
season. The windows were modelled to open when operative temperatures in the zones 
exceeded 23oC and occupants were present. The impact on cooling energy 
consumption was not significant, since it decreased only by 1% in the window opening 
scenario. 
Comparison of present simulated scenarios 
The simulations revealed notable differences in energy consumption between the 
present scenarios. DRY 2023 had higher heating energy consumption than TMY 2001-
2020 in all buildings, with differences ranging from around 13% to 16%. A similar trend 
was observed in the cooling energy consumption of Building 220, with DRY 2023 having 
approximately 8% higher consumption. These differences align with the climate file 
data, where DRY 2023 had lower outdoor temperatures during the heating season, 
requiring more heating and higher outdoor temperatures along with higher maximum 
solar gains during a big portion of the cooling season, requiring more cooling. 
 
 
==PAGE:82==
82 
 
Climate change impact 
The evaluation of the future scenarios showed reduced heating energy consumption of 
the buildings in relation to the present scenario (TMY 2001-2020). In the mid-term future 
scenario (TMY 2041-2060), the buildings exhibited around 3% to 7% lower heating 
consumption, while in the long-term future scenario (TMY 2081-2100) they had 
approximately 15% to 26% less consumption compared to the present scenario. The 
heating energy consumption followed the trends of the outdoor temperature 
distributions of the climate files that were used in each scenario, where temperatures 
rose slightly in the mid-term future and considerably in the long-term future. Therefore, 
the higher outdoor temperatures that were noted during the heating season led to lower 
heating energy consumption. Overall, the reduction was on similar levels in all buildings 
except for the long-term future scenario in Building 409 where a reduction of 26% in 
heating energy consumption was observed. This was probably due to the structure of 
the building, which consists of glass facades, and thus it’s affected more by the high 
outdoor temperatures in combination with the higher maximum solar gains that were 
observed compared to the other buildings. 
The cooling energy consumption of Building 220 was higher in the future scenarios to 
counter rising outdoor temperatures. The long-term future scenario (TMY 2081-2100) 
had the highest cooling energy consumption, which was expected since it also had the 
highest outdoor temperatures. Thus, Building 220 exhibited reduced heating energy 
consumption but increased cooling energy consumption. Overall, the total energy 
consumption for heating and cooling increased in the future scenarios and reached 
around 116,400 kWh (3.1% increase from TMY 2001-2020) in the mid-term future 
scenario and 118,400 kWh (4.9% increase from TMY 2001-2020) in the long-term future 
scenario. 
Heatwave simulations 
The heatwave scenarios further highlighted the impact of higher outdoor temperatures 
on cooling energy consumption. In Building 220, consumptions were on similar levels 
for the heatwaves of 2045 and 2055, whereas the heatwaves of 2097 and 2090 had 
consumptions approximately 1.5 and 2 times higher, respectively, than those of 2045 
==PAGE:83==
83 
 
and 2055. This aligns with the outdoor temperature trends of the heatwaves, where the 
heatwave of 2090 had the highest outdoor temperatures, followed by the heatwave of 
2097 and then 2055 and 2045 which had outdoor temperatures on similar levels. 
Summary 
In summary, the analysis showed a performance gap between simulated and measured 
energy consumption of the buildings (with the measured energy being higher), likely due 
to modelling assumptions such as zone occupancies, window opening during the 
heating season, but also due to the exclusion of laboratories’ energy use from the 
simulations. The larger buildings with laboratories had greater differences between 
measured and simulated energy consumption, compared to the smaller Building 409 
consisting of offices, which had the lowest measured heating energy consumption per 
heated floor area. This suggests that laboratories have the biggest impact on the energy 
consumption gap between measurements and simulations. 
Rising outdoor temperatures in the future scenarios led to reduced heating energy 
consumptions across all buildings. However, the increased outdoor temperatures of 
the future scenarios had a negative impact on Building 220, which has mechanical 
cooling. Higher cooling energy consumptions were observed in the future scenarios of 
the whole-year simulations, as well as the heatwave scenarios. The consumptions 
followed the outdoor temperature trends of the climate files and the scenarios with the 
highest outdoor temperatures had the highest cooling energy consumptions. 
 
4.2 Thermal environment of buildings 
The comparison of measured and simulated air temperatures in various offices was 
conducted in Buildings 115 and 220, since there weren’t any measurements available 
for operative temperatures. The measured temperatures were compared with the 
results of the present simulated scenarios to evaluate the accuracy of the building 
models. This was followed by the assessment of thermal environment in each building 
by analyzing the distribution of hourly operative temperatures in the occupied zones 
across different scenarios. The analysis included determining the thermal environment 
==PAGE:84==
84 
 
categories of the operative temperature distributions of the zones according to EN 
16798-1:2019 and evaluating compliance with BR18 overheating limits. In addition, for 
Building 220, which was simulated without window opening, the thermal environment 
evaluation included the two previously described window opening scenarios to assess 
the impact of window opening during the cooling season. Similarly with energy 
performance, there was a comparison of the present scenarios to assess the impact of 
using national and local weather data and a comparison between the present and 
future scenarios to evaluate the impact of climate change on the thermal environment 
of the buildings. 
Comparison of measured and simulated air temperatures 
The comparison between measured and simulated air temperatures revealed 
contrasting results. In Building 115, the measured temperatures were significantly 
higher than the simulated ones. This mismatch was likely not due to increased internal 
gains by occupancy and equipment, since the zones were visually observed before 
modelling. Instead, it was probably due to higher actual solar gains that the climate 
files might have failed to capture for the specific period. The multi-year averaged 
weather data used in the simulations might not have reflected the actual weather 
conditions during the period that measurements took place. 
In contrast, Building 220 had measured air temperatures within categories II and III of 
Table B.5 of EN 16798-1:2019, whereas the simulated temperatures of the zones were 
considerably higher, exceeding category IV’s range. This mismatch was probably due to 
the higher internal gains in the simulations, as the number of occupants was based on 
assumptions and the worst-case scenario of 100% occupancy was modeled. In the 
case of Building 115, where most offices have 2 to 3 occupants, this did not have a big 
impact, but in Building 220, where offices have significantly more occupants, this led to 
higher simulated temperatures. In addition, the assumed closed windows during the 
heating season and the model complexity and assumptions that were described in 
Section 4.1 probably also contributed to the differences. 
For both buildings, the simulated air temperatures of the DRY 2023 scenario were, on 
average, 1oC to 2oC higher than those of the TMY 2001-2020 scenario, with the latter 
==PAGE:85==
85 
 
showing a broader range of temperature distributions. This aligns with the slightly 
higher direct normal solar radiation that is observed in the climate file of DRY 2023 
during March and the higher maximum solar gains that were observed in the Appendix 
for DRY 2023. 
Thermal environment during the heating season 
For Building 115, most of the operative temperatures during the heating season were 
lower in TMY 2001-2020, compared to DRY 2023. The TMY 2001-2020 scenario had a 
much wider distribution and most temperatures below the heating setpoint of 21oC. The 
difference was likely due to the lower direct normal solar radiation that was observed 
during most of the heating season, compared to the climate file of DRY 2023 scenario. 
The future scenarios followed outdoor temperature trends, with the long-term future 
scenario (TMY 2081-2100) having the highest indoor temperatures. Interestingly, the 
future scenarios showed improved thermal environment compared to TMY 2001-2020, 
with temperature distributions being within categories I and II of EN 16798-1:2019. This 
likely happened because outdoor temperatures in the heating season were lower in 
TMY 2001-2020 compared to future scenarios and the heating system of the building 
couldn’t maintain the heating setpoint in the zones. In the future scenarios, the higher 
outdoor temperatures aided in the maintenance of the heating setpoint and improved 
the indoor thermal environment. 
In Building 220, the present scenarios had similar operative temperature distributions 
during the heating season, with TMY 2001-2020 being slightly lower, probably due to 
lower direct normal solar radiation observed in its climate file. However, the differences 
were smaller than those in Building 115, indicating that Building 220 is less affected by 
solar radiation. This is further supported by comparing the distributions of maximum 
solar gains, where Building 115 had overall higher distributions. In the future 
simulations, operative temperatures increased, with the long-term future scenario 
having the highest distribution, since it had the highest outdoor temperatures. The 
indoor temperatures in the zones of Building 220 were significantly higher than the 
heating setpoint, causing thermal discomfort. The high internal gains from occupancy, 
combined with the lack of window opening, contributed to the poor thermal 
==PAGE:86==
86 
 
environment of present scenarios, which worsened in future scenarios due to the 
higher outdoor temperatures.  
In Building 409, the temperature distributions had minimal differences in the two 
present scenarios and the mid-term future scenario during the heating season. The DRY 
2023 scenario had much lower outdoor temperatures, but this was offset by higher 
heating consumption compared to the other scenarios. The mid-term future scenario 
had a slightly higher operative temperature distribution than TMY 2001-2020, following 
the slightly higher outdoor temperatures observed in the climate files. In the long-term 
future scenario, where outdoor temperatures were notably higher, indoor temperatures 
were the highest among the scenarios. The thermal environment for most zones was 
acceptable across all scenarios, with at least half of operative temperature 
distributions being within the first three categories of EN 16798-1:2019. However, the 
higher indoor temperatures in the long-term future led to a worsened thermal 
environment in some zones. In contrast, the present and mid-term future scenarios had 
more extreme indoor temperatures likely due to the higher direct normal solar 
radiation. 
Thermal environment during the cooling season 
In Building 115, the present scenarios showed similar operative temperature 
distributions, with TMY 2001-2020 being slightly higher. This was probably due to the 
higher outdoor temperatures from May to July and the higher direct normal solar 
radiation for most of the cooling season that were observed in TMY 2001-2020. For the 
future scenarios, operative temperatures increased slightly, with the long-term future 
scenario (TMY 2081-2100) having the highest temperature distribution and the worst 
thermal environment, following outdoor temperature trends. The mid-term future 
scenario (TMY 2041-2060) displayed the widest range and highest maximum outlier 
temperatures, with the maximum temperature being significantly higher than the other 
scenarios. A similar trend was observed in the climate files used during the simulations 
of the scenarios, where the distribution of the mid-term future scenario had the highest 
maximum temperature. This in combination with the highest direct normal solar 
radiation from June to mid-July could lead to the extreme indoor temperatures that 
were observed in the mid-term future scenario. 
==PAGE:87==

Overheating hours increased significantly in the long-term future scenario, which also 
had the highest outdoor temperatures, while the mid-term future scenario exhibited a 
notable decrease in overheating hours compared to the present scenarios. This could 
be attributed to the lower outdoor temperatures of the mid-term future scenario from 
mid-July to September compared to present scenarios. None of the scenarios complied 
with BR18 overheating limits, as overheating hours were approximately 6 to 7.5 times 
higher than the limit of 100 hours for the 26oC threshold and 20 to 25 times higher than 
the limit of 25 hours for the 27oC threshold. 
For Building 220, the present simulated scenarios showed a notable difference in 
average operative temperatures, with TMY 2001-2020 being 1.4oC higher than DRY 2023 
and having higher temperature outliers. The difference was probably due to the slightly 
higher outdoor temperatures and higher direct normal solar radiation that were 
observed in the climate file of TMY 2001-2020 for most of the cooling season. A similar 
difference was observed in Building 115, which indicates that simulations using the 
DRY climate file exhibit lower indoor temperatures than those using the local climate 
file, potentially leading to inaccurate assessments of thermal environment. Future 
scenarios showed decreased operative temperatures compared to TMY 2001-2020, 
with the long-term future scenario having the lowest operative temperatures, despite 
its higher outdoor temperatures. This was likely achieved by mechanical cooling, since 
as it was already observed, the cooling energy consumption was increased in the future 
scenarios. This suggests that Building 220 is resilient against the rising outdoor 
temperatures of the future, but at the cost of higher energy consumption. Like in 
Building 115, the mid-term future scenario showed the highest maximum outlier 
operative temperatures for the reasons previously discussed. Finally, the high internal 
gains from occupants and their equipment, in combination with the lack of window 
opening have also caused thermal discomfort during the cooling season with most of 
the indoor temperature distributions being out of the range of category IV of EN 16798-
1:2019 for all scenarios. 
Overheating hours slightly increased in the future scenarios compared to TMY 2001-
2020, despite them having decreased indoor temperatures. Also, a difference is noted 
with Building 115, which had decreased overheating hours in the mid-term future 
==PAGE:88==

scenario. This probably happened because overheating in Building 220 occurred during

Thermal environment during heatwaves 
In the heatwave simulations, Building 115’s indoor temperatures followed the trend of 
the outdoor temperatures of the climate files of the respective scenarios. Thus, the

heatwave of 2097 and then 2055 and 2045. The operative temperature distributions of 
the heatwave scenarios were considerably higher than the limit of category IV of EN 
16798-1:2019, except for the bottom 25% of temperatures of the heatwave of 2045 
which fell within categories II and III. Therefore, the longer and warmer heatwaves of 
2090 and 2097 have the biggest impact on the building. 
Building 220 showed a different trend than Building 115. In this case, it was the 
heatwave of 2055 that had the highest operative temperature distribution followed by 
the heatwaves of 2090, 2097 and 2045. Thus, the heatwaves of 2090 and 2097 had 
lower indoor temperature distributions than 2055, despite having higher outdoor 
temperatures. This was due to the use of mechanical cooling, since the heatwave 
scenarios of 2090 and 2097 exhibited nearly double cooling energy consumption than 
the other heatwaves. Despite the lower indoor temperatures, the heatwave of 2097 had 
the highest maximum temperature outliers, with a similar trend observed in the outdoor 
temperatures of the heatwave scenarios. The thermal environment of the heatwave 
scenarios was poor since most of the operative temperature distributions were out of 
category IV range of EN 16798-1:2019.

Summary 
The comparison of measured and simulated indoor air temperatures revealed 
differences in both Buildings 115 and 220, probably due to occupancy assumptions and 
the use of climate files which may not predict accurately weather conditions for 
specific periods. In the simulations of present scenarios, notable differences were 
observed and TMY 2001-2020 had an overall worse thermal environment. This suggests 
that using the same climate file with national average weather data, regardless of the 
building’s location in energy simulations might inaccurately indicate an improved 
thermal environment. Interestingly, the mid-term future scenario showed the most 
extreme indoor temperatures in all buildings, despite not having the highest outdoor 
temperatures. This was attributed to the highest extreme outdoor temperatures 
observed in its climate file. 
During the heating season, future scenarios showed increased indoor temperatures for 
all buildings but had different effects. In Building 115, the rising outdoor temperatures 
of the future scenarios improved the thermal environment by helping maintain the 
heating setpoint, which could not be maintained in the present scenario. In contrast, 
Building 220 exhibited a poor thermal environment in the present scenario by having 
indoor temperatures significantly higher than the heating setpoint, likely due to high 
internal gains from the occupants. The rising outdoor temperatures of the future 
scenarios further worsened the thermal environment. In Building 409, indoor 
temperatures remained stable in the mid-term future, maintaining an acceptable 
thermal environment. However, temperatures rose in the long-term future, leading to a 
slightly worse thermal environment. 
In the cooling season, the buildings exhibited contrasting behaviors. In Building 115, the 
indoor temperatures increased in the future scenarios, with the long-term future 
scenario having the highest temperature distribution and the worst thermal 
environment, following outdoor temperature trends. The building was significantly 
affected by the warmer and longer heatwaves of 2090 and 2097, resulting in 
significantly higher indoor temperatures compared to the cooling season of the future 
scenarios. The indoor temperatures exceeded the ranges of category IV of EN 16798-
1:2019 in all heatwave scenarios. On the other hand, Building 220 had decreased 
==PAGE:91==
91 
 
indoor temperatures in the future scenarios of the whole-year simulations as well as in 
the warmer heatwaves. Nevertheless, the indoor temperatures exceeded the range of 
category IV of EN 16798-1:2019 in all the scenarios. Therefore, the complaints from 
occupants to Campus Service about high indoor temperatures in Building 220 were 
confirmed. The zones are currently occupied by more people than originally designed, 
which significantly impacts their thermal environment and highlights the need to 
reduce occupant numbers. In Building 409, indoor temperatures also decreased in the 
future scenarios, but the thermal environment remained poor with high indoor 
temperatures. This likely happened due to the glass facades of the building, which are 
more sensitive to solar gains, causing temperatures to increase. However, the actual 
indoor temperatures in Buildings 220 and 409 are likely lower than the simulation 
results, since the occupancy assumptions and lack of window opening affected the 
results notably. 
Overheating hours exceeded BR18 limits in all scenarios for all buildings. Future 
scenarios showed further increases in overheating hours, except for the mid-term 
future scenario in Buildings 115 and 409, which exhibited a notable decrease. This 
reduction was probably due to lower outdoor temperatures during part of the cooling 
season. Building 220 experienced overheating during both the heating and cooling 
season due to its high indoor temperatures throughout the year and had significantly 
more overheating hours than Buildings 115 and 409. 
Overall, Building 220 had the highest indoor temperatures, probably due to its higher 
internal gains from the greater number of occupants. This trend persisted during the 
cooling season, where Building 220 had significantly higher indoor temperatures and 
overheating hours than the other buildings in all simulated scenarios, despite having 
mechanical cooling. Building 409 also had significantly high temperatures during the 
cooling season, but they were lower than Building 220. The high indoor temperatures of 
Building 409 are likely attributed to the high solar gains through the glass facades. In 
contrast, Building 115 had the lowest temperatures, benefiting from natural ventilation 
through window opening in the cooling season, whereas in the simulations of the other 
buildings windows were assumed always closed. This had a notable impact on the 
indoor temperatures as it was observed in Building 220, where the indoor temperatures 
==PAGE:92==
92 
 
of the fifth floor were on similar levels to Building 115 when window-opening was 
enabled.  
Building 220 proved resilient to the rising outdoor temperatures of the future, since it 
was able to maintain and even lower indoor temperatures, even during extreme 
heatwaves. However, this came at the cost of significantly increased cooling energy 
consumption. Thus, while Building 220 can maintain a stable thermal environment 
under climate change, this would require a significant amount of energy. A similar 
behavior was observed in Building 409 where night cooling of the ventilation system 
lowered indoor temperatures in the future scenarios despite higher outdoor 
temperatures. In contrast, Building 115 relies solely on natural ventilation, making its 
thermal environment more vulnerable to rising outdoor temperatures, which is likely to 
worsen, especially during extreme events like heatwaves. 
Finally, it should be noted that the climate files used in the simulations were based on 
RCP8.5, which represents the worst-case climate change scenario and assumes no 
actions are taken to mitigate climate change. However, this scenario is probably 
unrealistic, since measures have already been implemented to address climate 
change. In addition, the worst-case scenario where all occupants were present daily 
and used their equipment was assumed in the simulations, resulting in higher internal 
gains than would occur in reality. All of these factors combined with the lack of window 
opening in the cooling season probably led to higher indoor temperatures in Buildings 
220 and 409 than would be observed in real conditions. Nevertheless, the high 
occupancy levels of Building 220 and the solar gains through the glass facades of 
Building 409 probably still affect indoor temperatures, but to a lesser extent than the 
simulations suggest. 
 
4.3 Limitations 
Several limitations were encountered during the data collection, modeling and 
simulation of the buildings. While Buildings 115 and 409 were easier to visually inspect, 
this was not the case for Building 220 due to its size and restricted access to 
specialized facilities like laboratories. Observations in selected zones of each floor 
==PAGE:93==
93 
 
were therefore used to estimate average occupancy per m2 and then calculate 
occupancies for the entire building. Occupants along with their associated equipment, 
influence internal gains and indoor temperatures, making accurate estimation 
important. In addition, occupancy schedules had to be assumed, since it is difficult to 
predict how long occupants stay in the zones and how often they leave. Another 
assumption was 100% occupant presence in the zones, as a worst-case scenario, due 
to the difficulty of predicting daily attendance. 
The absence of data regarding equipment in zones required the assumption of typical 
office building equipment. Furthermore, internal blinds, which impact solar gains and 
indoor temperatures, were ignored in the simulations, since their use depends on 
occupant behavior. 
Some thermal properties of the building materials were unavailable, so assumptions 
were made. In addition, no data about thermal bridges and infiltration of building 
envelopes were available, so typical values based on the construction period were 
used. These factors influence heat losses and energy performance, so extensive 
research was conducted to find accurate values. 
The surroundings of the buildings were not modelled to avoid extended simulation 
times and model complexity. However, since DTU campus is not densely structured, 
the impact of surroundings on solar gains is likely minimal. 
Another limitation involved estimating window opening frequency during the heating 
season, as this depends on occupant preferences. The windows were assumed as 
always closed in the simulations, but since some occupants will likely open them 
during the heating season, this will affect heating energy consumption. 
Further challenges were encountered during the modelling of Building 220. The open 
common areas required numerous vertical and horizontal openings, causing numerical 
issues during the simulations. To address this, the openings were removed, and 
common areas like corridors and staircases were modelled as closed zones. Since only 
occupied zones were studied, this probably did not have a notable impact on the 
results. Temperature-controlled window opening was causing simulation errors in 
Buildings 220 and 409 and had to be excluded. To study the effect of closed windows 
==PAGE:94==
94 
 
during the cooling season, a simplified model of the fifth floor of Building 220 was 
simulated. 
Limitations were also encountered during the HVAC system modeling. In Building 220, 
data about the power of radiators of the heating system was unavailable, so a winter 
day with an outdoor temperature of -12oC was simulated to estimate the required 
heating power. In addition, there was no information about the airflow rates of the 
ventilation system in the zones of Building 220, so they were designed according to EN 
16798-1:2019 based on occupant numbers mentioned in the building drawings. Since 
the simulations revealed poor thermal environment for all scenarios, the accuracy of 
ventilation airflows has a significant impact on thermal comfort. 
Finally, laboratories in Buildings 115 and 220 consume heating and cooling energy for 
research purposes. Since there was not enough information available about heating 
and cooling units, room setpoints, and similar details, they were ignored in the 
simulations. This likely contributed to the significant difference between actual and 
simulated energy use that was observed in the buildings. 
While challenges during this study required certain assumptions and simplifications, 
extensive research was conducted to ensure their accuracy and minimize their impact 
on the results. Nevertheless, some assumptions might have had significant influence, 
highlighting the need for further studies, which will be discussed in the next section. 
 
4.4 Future studies 
Some of the assumptions made in this study could significantly influence energy 
performance and thermal environment of the buildings, highlighting the need for further 
investigation into their impact. For example, full occupancy was assumed in all zones, 
even though not all employees are in the workplace daily, due to factors like leave or 
remote work. Running simulations with varying occupancy percentages could provide 
insight into the effects on energy consumption and indoor temperatures. 
The poor thermal environment of Building 220 that was observed in this study highlights 
another area for further research. One likely reason for this issue is the higher number 
==PAGE:95==
95 
 
of occupants in the zones than originally planned, which limits the effectiveness of 
mechanical cooling. It would be interesting to study the impact of increased occupancy 
on cooling energy consumption and indoor temperatures of buildings. 
Since internal blinds were ignored in this study, future research could examine their 
effect on cooling energy consumption and indoor temperatures. This could include 
both user-controlled blinds, which depend on occupant behavior and automatic blinds, 
which respond to weather conditions. 
Another factor that affects energy consumption is window opening during the heating 
season, as observed in this study. While only two window opening scenarios were 
considered, where windows were opened in all zones of Building 115, additional 
studies could explore variations in the number of zones with opened windows and the 
duration of window opening. This could provide insight into heating energy 
consumption and thermal environment. In addition, since Buildings 220 and 409 were 
simulated with closed windows throughout the year, further simulations should be 
conducted where windows can be opened during the cooling season, which will provide 
more accurate results on the thermal environment of the buildings. 
Furthermore, an interesting research topic would be the energy consumption of 
specialized laboratories, since no data were available during this study, and it played a 
role in the difference between actual and simulated energy use of the buildings. 
The energy and thermal environment performance gap between measurements and 
simulations could be resolved by implementing monitoring of the building for an 
extended period. This approach would allow assumptions made due to unavailable 
data and building complexity to be addressed by adjusting model parameters based on 
feedback from building monitoring, leading to more realistic simulation results. 
Finally, this study evaluated the impacts of climate change on buildings of DTU Lyngby 
campus by analyzing a selection of old and modern buildings to represent DTU’s 
building stock. However, the limited number of buildings included suggests that further 
analysis of additional buildings is needed to draw more comprehensive conclusions. In 
addition, a more detailed study of Building 409 would be valuable to assess the impacts 
==PAGE:96==
96 
 
of heatwaves on a glass façade building, since this study did not conduct a full analysis 
due to time constraints. 
 
4.5 Takeaways 
The main takeaways of this study are: 
• There was a performance gap between measurements and simulations both in 
energy consumption and thermal environment of the buildings due to modelling 
assumptions. 
• Window opening during the heating season can notably increase heating energy 
consumption, depending on occupant behavior. 
• Simulations using a national average climate file (DRY) exhibited increased 
energy consumption and an improved thermal environment compared to 
simulations using Copenhagen’s local climate file. 
• The future scenarios simulations showed reduced heating energy consumption, 
increased cooling energy consumption and poor thermal environment of the 
buildings, especially during heatwaves. 
• The mid-term future scenario exhibited the most extreme indoor temperatures, 
whereas the long-term future scenario resulted in the worst thermal 
environment in most cases. 
• Building 115, being older, had the highest heating consumption per heated floor 
area and a poor thermal environment during the cooling season, especially 
during heatwaves. It will require improvements to the building envelope and 
mechanical ventilation to enhance performance and mitigate climate change 
impacts. 
• Building 220 is modern and well-insulated, could maintain indoor temperatures 
under future rising outdoor temperatures (in expense of higher cooling 
consumption), but exhibited poor thermal environment in all scenarios. 
Reducing occupancy to designed levels or replacing the ventilation system will 
be necessary to improve its performance and tackle climate change. 
==PAGE:97==
97 
 
• Building 409, which is modern and consists of glass facades, had the lowest 
heating consumption per heated floor area, could maintain indoor temperatures 
under future scenarios (without active cooling), but had a poor thermal 
environment during the cooling season in all scenarios. It will need additional 
shading systems and active cooling to improve its performance and tackle 
climate change. 
• The assumptions of closed windows during the cooling season and 100% 
occupancy of the zones, along with smaller modeling assumptions led in 
unrealistically high indoor temperatures in Buildings 220 and 409, however the 
real temperatures are probably still high. 
 
5 Conclusions 
This study investigated the energy use and climate change resilience of university office 
buildings located at the Lyngby campus of Technical University of Denmark (DTU). This 
was done by conducting energy and thermal environment performance simulations in 
IDA ICE and comparing each building’s performance under present and projected 
future weather conditions. The study also assessed simulation accuracy, the 
differences between using national average and local climate files and the effects of 
window opening during the heating and cooling seasons. 
The study revealed that simulation results did not align with real-world data, since 
significant differences were observed between measurements and simulations for both 
energy consumption and indoor temperatures. These differences are likely attributed to 
building complexity, modeling assumptions and the lack of data on laboratory energy 
consumption for research purposes. This highlights the need for future studies in 
occupancy schedules and attendance, occupant behavior and laboratory energy 
consumption in energy simulations. The findings suggest that a building’s actual energy 
class and thermal environment might differ from the simulations results, emphasizing 
the need for further research into the gap between measurements and simulations and 
how this can be accounted beforehand, during the design process. 
==PAGE:98==
98 
 
The window opening simulations demonstrated that heating energy consumption can 
increase notably when windows are opened during the heating season, depending on 
occupant behavior. During the cooling season, window opening had minimal impact on 
cooling energy consumption. However, it had a substantial effect on the thermal 
environment, since the indoor temperatures were much higher when windows were 
closed. 
Simulations using national average and local climate files revealed notable differences, 
both in energy consumption and in indoor temperatures. The simulations using the DRY 
climate file (currently used for energy simulations in Denmark) showed higher heating 
and cooling energy consumption and improved thermal environment than those using 
local weather data for the region of Copenhagen. This suggests that using a single 
climate file for all of Denmark might provide inaccurate energy and thermal 
environment performance results, highlighting the need for localized climate files. 
Future scenario simulations showed reduced heating energy consumption in all 
buildings due to rising outdoor temperatures, with the greatest reductions observed in 
the long-term future (2081-2100). However, cooling energy consumption increased in 
Building 220, both in the future yearly simulations and during the heatwaves to 
counteract the higher outdoor temperatures. The thermal environment was poor in all 
buildings during the cooling season, even with the reduction in indoor temperatures 
observed in the future scenarios for Buildings 220 and 409. Interestingly, the highest 
extreme indoor temperatures across all buildings occurred in the mid-term future 
scenarios (2041-2060), following the outdoor temperature patterns. 
Building 115 is not resilient against climate change due to its poorly insulated building 
envelope and lack of sufficient ventilation. The building had the highest heating energy 
consumption per heated floor area among the examined buildings (according to 
Campus Service), a poor thermal environment throughout the year and significant 
overheating during the cooling season, which was especially severe during heatwaves. 
Future improvements to its building envelope are essential to reduce heating 
consumption and enhance thermal environment. In addition, a mechanical ventilation 
system with natural night cooling or mechanical active cooling will be necessary, since 
==PAGE:99==
99 
 
the future rising temperatures and extreme heatwaves will have a significant impact on 
the thermal environment of the building. 
Building 220, a modern and well-insulated building, demonstrated lower measured 
heating consumption per heated floor area than Building 115. However, despite its 
highly insulated building envelope and mechanical cooling system, it had significantly 
higher indoor temperatures and a worse thermal environment than all of the studied 
buildings, with overheating throughout the year. This was mainly attributed to the 
occupancy levels that exceed the designed capacity in combination with modeling 
assumptions. While the building is resilient to future rising outdoor temperatures and 
extreme heatwaves, this comes at the cost of higher energy consumption, which could 
increase the building’s emissions unless the energy mix relies mainly on renewable 
sources. It is worth noting that the reported indoor temperatures and energy 
consumption are probably overestimated, because the simulations did not include 
window opening and assumed the worst-case scenario of full occupancy. Thus, 
reducing occupancy to the designed levels could improve the present and future 
thermal environment. Another option would be to redesign the airflows and upgrade the 
ventilation system or install additional cooling units to cover the cooling demand, 
however this would still result in higher energy consumption. 
Building 409 had the lowest measured heating energy consumption per heated floor 
area, probably due to its small size, lack of laboratories and higher solar gains. While 
the building can maintain and lower indoor temperatures under rising outdoor 
temperatures in the future, the solar gains through the glass facades probably 
contribute to a poor thermal environment. Installing additional shading systems might 
help lower indoor temperatures, but at the cost of daylight. Since the building relies 
only on natural night cooling, implementing mechanical active cooling might be 
necessary to maintain an acceptable thermal environment. A more detailed analysis 
should be conducted to evaluate the impact of heatwaves on the building’s thermal 
environment. 
Several limitations, like assumptions about occupancy and occupant behavior, 
equipment and laboratory energy consumption combined with modeling 
==PAGE:100==
100 
 
simplifications influenced the results. While efforts were made to minimize their 
impact, further studies are required to assess their effect on energy simulations. 
In conclusion, Building 115 is not resilient to climate change and presently has a poor 
thermal environment. Upgrading its envelope and installing a mechanical ventilation 
system are recommended to address these issues and improve its present and future 
energy and thermal environment performance. Given the presence of similar buildings 
on campus, DTU should consider developing a renovation plan for these buildings. 
Building 220 exhibited a poor thermal environment due to its high occupancy levels. 
While it can maintain indoor temperatures under future rising outdoor temperatures 
and heatwaves, this comes at the expense of increased energy consumption. Reducing 
occupancy to the designed levels or upgrading the ventilation system is suggested to 
improve its thermal environment and reduce cooling energy consumption. Finally, 
Building 409 can also maintain indoor temperatures under rising outdoor temperatures, 
but has a poor thermal environment. Installing additional shading systems with caution 
to daylight levels and implementing active cooling are recommended to improve 
thermal environment. Further studies should be conducted to evaluate the impact of 
heatwaves on the building.
