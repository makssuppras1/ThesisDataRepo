Abstract
This thesis investigates the detection of false base stations (FBS) in modern mobile net­
works by analyzing user equipment (UE) behavior and network signaling patterns. Moti­
vated by the increasing prevalence of FBS attacks, which exploit vulnerabilities in mobile
network protocols and UE network selection mechanisms, compromising confidentiality,
integrity, and availability, this project combines experimental evaluation with the develop­
ment of a network­side detection algorithm.
Controlled experiments were conducted in 2G GSM and 4G LTE environments to eval­
uate UE responses when false base stations broadcast matching Mobile Country Code
(MCC) and Mobile Network Code (MNC) identifiers, using transmission power to influence
UE network reselection. Key signal metrics such as Reference Signal Received Power
(RSRP) and signal quality were analyzed, revealing that, under network disruptions, such
as complete signal loss or forced downgrades, UEs often attach to any available network
matching their home Public Land Mobile Network (PLMN) identifiers, including less secure
technologies such as 2G.
Building on these insights, a lightweight and scalable detection mechanism was designed
to perform anomaly detection by cross­referencing reported network identifiers, such as
MCC, MNC, Location Area Code, MME Group ID, and MME Code against baselines of
legitimate operators. The algorithm is meant to operate in near real time, using existing
signaling data without requiring infrastructure modifications or hardware upgrades. The
challenges discussed include distinguishing between legitimate roaming events and FBS
activity, as well as the influence of device and operator variability on detection perfor­
mance. The results demonstrate that network­side validation of the UE­reported network
history is a possible countermeasure against FBS attacks. This approach offers a low­
overhead solution that enhances mobile network security and adapts to evolving threats,
significantly contributing to the protection of mobile communications.
Keywords: False Base Stations (FBS), Mobile Network Security, UE Behavior, Detection
Algorithm, 2G (GSM), 4G (LTE), 5G, Network­Side Detection.

Introduction
In recent years, smishing attacks facilitated by SMS blasters have become increasingly
common in countries such as Hong Kong[1], the Philippines[2], New Zealand[3], and the
United Kingdom[4]. These portable devices, often operating as false base stations (FBS),
bypass traditional SMS infrastructure and Sender ID protections by broadcasting directly
to nearby phones. The result is a growing wave of fraudulent messages that can lead to
serious privacy violations and financial losses estimated to amount to billions of dollars
annually [5].
Because FBSs operate outside the boundaries of the mobile network infrastructure, they
are therefore difficult to detect. Law enforcement agencies often face delays in identifying
and locating these devices, which gives attackers enough time to exploit victims. While
some countries have begun to crack down on both users and suppliers of SMS blasters,
the persistent nature of the threat highlights the urgent need for network­level detection
and prevention mechanisms.
This thesis investigates vulnerabilities in mobile networks with a specific focus on FBSs
and their use in SMS­based attacks. Through a series of controlled real­world experi­
ments, the behavior of both user equipment (UE) and mobile networks is examined when
exposed to FBSs. These experiments provide insight into how devices respond during
network reselection, fallback, and connection attempts, providing insights that can be
leveraged to identify malicious activity.
Based on these observations, the thesis proposes a lightweight network­side detection
mechanism that uses existing signaling data, such as the Mobile Country Code (MCC),
the Mobile Network Code (MNC), the Location Area Code (LAC), and MME­specific pa­
rameters, to detect anomalies without requiring hardware upgrades or changes to the
communication protocol.
Finally, the report explores additional defense strategies at both network and UE levels,
while also discussing the broader implications of false base station attacks. These include
threats such as SMS injection, IMSI catching, location tracking, and service downgrades,
all of which underline the need for adaptive and scalable security solutions in today’s
mobile networks.

2.1
Mobile Networks Architecture
Mobile networks have evolved significantly over the years, transitioning from early circuit
switched networks designed primarily for voice communication to the advanced packet
switched networks that we use today. This section gives a short introduction to the differ­
ent generations of mobile networks, based primarily on the descriptions and terminology
of the book ”From GSM to LTE­Advanced Pro and 5G” by Martin Sauter [6].
While 2G, 4G, and 5G are discussed in detail, 3G has been deliberately excluded from
the scope of this project. The primary reason for this omission is the ongoing phase out of
3G networks, both globally and within Denmark. By the end of 2025, no mobile network
operators in Denmark are expected to maintain active 3G infrastructure. As a result, the
project’s focus is on the generations that are still in use today.
2.1.1
2G (GSM)
Global System for Mobile Communications (GSM) was originally designed as a circuit
switched network, although it has evolved over the years, and the physical circuit switching
has been virtualized and the network nodes are now connected over Internet Protocol (IP)­
based broadband connections. Even though GSM dates back to the early 1990s, it is still
in use today. GSM differs from its successors in several ways, which means that it comes
with different unique security vulnerabilities that can be exploited. Knowledge of GSM is
therefore still highly relevant today.
A GSM network is split into three subsystems.
• Base Station Subsystem (BSS) which is also called the radio network. The BSS
contains all the nodes and functionalities needed to establish a wireless connection
between mobile subscribers and the network via the radio interface.
• Network Subsystem (NSS) which is also known as the core network. Responsible
for switching of calls, subscriber management, and mobility management.
• Intelligent Network Subsystem (IN) which is an optional part of GSM. It comprises
of Service Control Point (SCP) databases and one of the main functionalities of IN
is the prepaid billing service.

Base Station Subsystem (BSS)
BSS is responsible for handling radio communication between mobile devices and the net­
work. The BSS consists of a Base Transceiver Station (BTS), a Base Station Controller
(BSC) and a Mobile Station (MS). The BTS manages radio transmission and reception
with mobile phones, the BSC, Controls multiple BTS units, manages handovers, and allo­
cates radio resources. Finally, the BSC connects to the Mobile Switching Center (MSC),
enabling communication within the GSM network, which is described further in the next
section.
The Network Subsystem (NSS)
The most important responsibilities of the NSS are call establishment, call control, and
routing of calls. The NSS is also responsible for subscriber management.
The classical NSS architecture can be seen in Figure 2.1. The classical NSS architecture
consists of several different components, including BSS, MSC, Gateway Mobile Switching
Center (G­MSC) and Visitor Location Register (VLR).
The MSC is the central component in mobile communication networks. It is responsible
for all connections between subscribers, establishing and maintaining a connection as
part of the Call Control (CC) and finally Mobility Management (MM) of subscribers.
The G­MSC is another key component of the GSM core network, responsible for con­
necting mobile networks to external networks, such as the Public Switched Telephone
Network (PSTN) or other mobile networks.
The Home Location Register (HLR) is a database that holds information of all subscribers
in the network such as the International Mobile Subscriber Identity (IMSI) number. The
IMSI is stored in the subscriber’s Subscriber Identity Module (SIM) and in the HLR, and is
thus the key to all information about the subscriber. Another important part of the HLR is
the Authentication Center (AuC). The AuC contains an individual key per subscriber. For
many operations in the network, the subscriber is identified by use of this key, for instance,
during the establishment of a call.
Each MSC has an associated VLR, which holds the records (information) of each sub­
scriber that is currently served by the MSC. These records are only copies of the original
records, which are stored in the HLR. The VLR is mainly used to reduce signaling between

2.2
Location Tracking Across Mobile Network Generations
In GSM networks, location tracking is based on the Location Area Identifier (LAI), which
combines Mobile Country Code (MCC), Mobile Network Code (MNC), and Location Area
Code (LAC). The LAI uniquely identifies a location area, which is a group of neighboring
cells. When a mobile device moves between location areas, it performs a location update
to notify the network of its new position. This mechanism significantly reduces signaling
overhead by avoiding updates at every individual cell transition and instead updating only
when the device crosses location area boundaries.
As mobile networks evolved to LTE and 5G NR, the underlying mobility management
concept remained, but the architecture was adapted to better support packet­switched
communication. In these newer generations, the LAC is replaced by the Tracking Area
Code (TAC), and the corresponding mobility domain is known as a Tracking Area (TA).
A TA functions similarly to a location area by grouping multiple cells together. Devices
perform a Tracking Area Update (TAU) when they move from one tracking area to another,
ensuring the network maintains accurate and up­to­date location information.
Although the specific terminology changes from LAI and LAC in GSM to TA and TAC in
LTE and 5G NR, the core operational principle remains consistent across generations.
In all cases, the network uses geographic grouping of cells to minimize signaling traffic,
optimize paging, and efficiently manage subscriber mobility. This scalability is essential for
maintaining service continuity, especially in dense urban deployments and high mobility
scenarios [6].
2.3
Identity Management in Mobile Networks
In mobile telecommunication networks, each subscriber is initially identified by a unique
and permanent identifier known as the IMSI. The IMSI is stored on the SIM card and
is used during the first connection and registration phases to authenticate the subscriber
with the network. However, since the IMSI does not change, transmitting it in plaintext can
pose serious privacy concerns. Adversaries can exploit it to track or identify users without
Exploring and Analyzing Security Vulnerabilities in Modern Mobile Networks
11
==PAGE:25==
authorization. To address these risks, mobile networks employ temporary identifiers in
subsequent interactions.
One such temporary identifier is the Temporary Mobile Subscriber Identity (TMSI). In ear­
lier generations of mobile networks, once a subscriber has successfully attached to the
network, the IMSI is replaced by a TMSI. This temporary identity is used for tasks such
as paging and signaling throughout the session. The TMSI functions as a short­lived
pseudonym, assigned for a limited time, and frequently updated. This helps hide the
user’s actual identity, significantly reducing the risk of exposure by avoiding frequent over­
the­air transmission of the permanent IMSI.
In LTE and 5G network architectures, the Globally Unique Temporary Identity (GUTI)
serves a similar role. Like the TMSI, the GUTI acts as a dynamic alias for the IMSI and
is assigned during the initial connection setup. It is also designed to be updated regularly
and is used during events such as network registration or attach. Because the GUTI is not
static and changes over time, it is difficult for adversaries to link it back to the subscriber’s
real identity even if it is intercepted.
TMSI and GUTI are essential for the privacy and security architecture of the mobile net­
work. They limit the broadcast of sensitive subscriber information and prevent direct ex­
posure of the IMSI in routine communications. This system minimizes the risk of tracking
and unauthorized identification, improving the privacy of the subscriber.
Beyond these core subscriber identities, telecommunication networks rely on other iden­
tifiers. The Mobile Station Integrated Services Digital Network Number (MSISDN), com­
monly known as the phone number, is a fundamental identifier used for routing calls,
charging fees, and linking subscribers to their service profiles in the HLR. In conjunction
with the MCC and MNC, the MSISDN uniquely identifies a subscriber’s home network,
playing an important role in billing, call routing, and overall user management.
In contrast to user­based identifiers, the International Mobile Equipment Identity (IMEI)
uniquely distinguishes the physical mobile device itself, independent of the subscriber.
Stored securely on the mobile hardware and managed by the SIM card, the IMEI allows
the network to identify the device, monitor its capabilities, and enforce network policies,
such as blacklisting stolen devices. Unlike phone numbers that are assigned to the sub­
scriber and can change, the IMEI is permanently tied to the equipment.
In packet­switched networks such as LTE and 5G, IP addresses serve as the primary
identifier for data sessions. When a mobile device establishes a data session, a Packet
Data Protocol (PDP) context is activated in the 2G and 3G networks, during which a dy­
namic or static IP address is assigned to the user by the GGSN. In newer architectures
such as LTE and 5G, similar functions are performed by the P­GW and the UPF. This IP
address facilitates end­to­end connectivity and routing of IP packets between the mobile
subscriber and external networks. The use of IP addresses in packet networks also sup­

ports session continuity, mobility management, and QoS enforcement, ensuring robust
data transfer even as the device roams [6].
With the arrival of 5G, mobile networks introduce a more robust approach to protecting
permanent subscriber identities. Instead of transmitting the IMSI in cleartext as in previ­
ous generations, 5G uses the Subscription Permanent Identifier (SUPI) as the long­term
identity. To protect it from interception, the SUPI is never sent directly over the air. It
is concealed using a cryptographic function, resulting in a concealed identifier called the
Subscription Concealed Identifier (SUCI). The SUCI is transmitted during the initial reg­
istration procedure, ensuring that the permanent identity remains protected from passive
eavesdroppers or IMSI catchers during the vulnerable early stages of network access.
In 5G SA networks, the SUCI is encrypted directly on the SIM card using the Elliptic
Curve Integrated Encryption Scheme (ECIES). This means that the SIM must support the
necessary cryptographic operations, otherwise it cannot generate a valid SUCI, and must
therefore be replaced [8]. This requirement presents a practical limitation on how quickly
5G SA can be adopted, as operators need to ensure that subscribers are equipped with
compatible SIMs. The introduction of Embedded Subscriber Identity Modules (eSIMs) can
help ease this transition, as it allows remote provisioning of updated profiles that support
SUCI encryption without requiring a physical card replacement.

2.4
Network Identification and Home Network Prioritization
The MCC and MNC are essential numerical identifiers used in cellular networks to uniquely
identify mobile network operators around the world. The MCC is a three­digit number that
represents a specific country, while the MNC is typically a two­ or three­digit number that
specifies the particular operator within that country. The MCC and MNC form a unique
identifier for a mobile network, referred to as PLMN.
Each mobile network broadcasts its MCC and MNC as part of the system information
transmitted by its base stations. When an UE searches for available networks, it reads
these broadcast codes and compares them with the information stored on its SIM card.
The SIM contains the subscriber’s home network identifiers, which are the MCC and MNC
of the operator that issued the SIM and manages the subscription.
If the UE detects a network whose MCC and MNC match the ones on the SIM, it identifies
that network as the home network. If there is no match, the UE considers the detected
network a roaming network, which can lead to different service conditions and additional
charges for the user.
Prioritizing the home network during the network selection process is important for both
technical and economic reasons. From a performance point of view, connecting to the
home network typically ensures better service quality and lower latency. Economically, it
helps users avoid roaming fees and allows operators to manage their subscribers more

14
==PAGE:28==
efficiently. For this reason, most mobile devices are configured to prefer the home network
when available, automatically selecting it over others with a lower priority [6].
2.5
Subscriber Identity Modules: SIM and eSIM
2.5.1
Traditional Physical SIM Cards
The SIM card or Universal Integrated Circuit Card (UICC), is a portable chip that securely
stores subscriber data used for identification and authentication on mobile networks. It
contains the IMSI and secret cryptographic keys that allow the network to verify the user
and establish secure communication.
When a device powers on, the SIM provides the IMSI and helps initiate authentication us­
ing its Secret Key used in SIM authentication (Ki), generating responses and encryption
keys. Without the SIM, the network cannot distinguish between legitimate and unautho­
rized users, making it essential for network security.
The SIM card also stores subscription information and supports device interoperability,
allowing users to move their SIM to another phone while retaining access to their services
[6].
2.5.2
Embedded SIM (eSIM) Technology
An eSIM is a programmable SIM card that is built directly into a device’s hardware, rather
than being a removable physical card like traditional SIMs. The eSIM performs the same
task as a traditional SIM, which is the identification and authentication of users seeking ac­
cess to a mobile radio network. However, with the eSIM, the user’s identification module
is not personalized for a single application during manufacturing. Instead, personalization
occurs later through a remote over­the­air process. A user can download and activate mo­
bile operator profiles, making it easy to switch between carriers or plans without changing
hardware [10].
The eSIM works the following way:
1. The device contains an eSIM chip, which acts like a traditional SIM but is rewritable.
2. When a user wants to add a new mobile plan, the carrier sends a profile over the
air, which is installed on the eSIM.
3. Its possible to switch between profiles via device settings without needing a new
physical SIM.
Remote SIM Provisioning (RSP) is the protocol specified by the GSM Association (GSMA)
for the purpose of downloading and installing SIM profiles into the secure element. RSP
reduces the logistical and production costs, and gives subscribers the flexibility of chang­
ing operators online. As earlier mentioned, the SIM profile contains security­critical in­
formation such as the IMSI and subscriber key. The UE uses these credentials from

the SIM profile in the Authentication and Key Agreement (AKA) procedure to enable the
subscriber’s access to the mobile network. The secure delivery of the SIM profile to the

2.6.2

A PLMN is essentially a network operated by a mobile service provider. It is uniquely
identified by a combination of a MCC and a MNC. When a UE powers on or is instructed
to perform a PLMN search, it follows a systematic procedure to identify available PLMNs.
PLMN selection occurs before network attach and determines which operator or network
the UE will attempt to connect to. Only after selecting a PLMN does the UE perform cell
selection and attempt to attach to the network, as we will see in the following section.
This section describes the PLMN selection procedure in 4G, as it is the most relevant
for our later experiments. The core principles of PLMN selection remain largely identical
across other network generations, including 2G, 3G, and 5G, with only minor differences
in how they are implemented. Therefore, the overall PLMN selection procedure is nearly
identical across generations.

and maintain connectivity in a mobile network. These processes ensure the UE camps

adapting dynamically as the UE moves or network conditions change.

3. Cell Selection Criteria
A cell is seen as suitable when certain radio conditions are met. The UE evaluates:
• Signal level (Srxlev): The received signal strength must exceed the config­
ured thresholds adjusted by offsets and compensations.
• Signal quality (Squal): The signal quality, typically measured by RSRQ, must

The combined criterion S = Srxlev > 0 and (optionally Squal > 0) must be fulfilled
for normal coverage conditions. If not met, enhanced coverage parameters are

scenarios. ”Srxlev” represents the cell selection Receive (RX) level value in dB,
while ”Squal” indicates the cell selection quality value, also measured in dB. ”Srxlev”
and ”Squal” is calculated according to TS 36.304.

Once a cell meeting the criteria is found, the UE camps on that cell, tuning to its con­
trol channels to receive system information, registration area updates (e.g., tracking
area codes), and paging notifications, enabling transition to connected mode when
required.
5. Periodic Cell Reselection
While camped, the UE regularly searches for a better cell according to the reselec­
tion criteria, which might trigger a cell change. This change can also involve switch­
ing Radio Access Technology (RAT) or core network types if the newly selected cell
belongs to a different network configuration.
2.6.4
Handover Decision in 2G and 4G
Handover is the process that ensures continuous communication during an active session,
such as a call or data transmission, when a mobile device moves from one cell to another.
It is typically triggered when the signal quality between the mobile device and its current
Exploring and Analyzing Security Vulnerabilities in Modern Mobile Networks
22
==PAGE:36==
base station decreases due to factors such as movement toward the cell edge, increased
interference, or network congestion.
In GSM networks, the handover decisions are mainly based on signal strength and quality
measurements, such as the RSSI, which the UE’s continuously monitor and report to the
BSC. Based on these reports, the BSC, sometimes in collaboration with the MSC, handles
the handover process between cells or even across MSCs to maintain call continuity. The
decision logic in 2G relies on fixed thresholds, hysteresis margins, and timers to prevent
unnecessary handovers while ensuring a stable voice connection in relatively predictable
radio environments.
Since the parameters are preconfigured and do not respond to rapid changes in the envi­
ronment, 2G handovers can be less efficient under fast­moving or fluctuating conditions.
The strategy is quite deterministic, focusing on voice service continuity rather than flexi­
bility or optimization.
4G LTE introduces a much more dynamic handover process. In LTE, the UE reports a
broader range of radio quality indicators, such as RSRP, RSRQ, and Channel Quality In­
dicator (CQI), to the serving eNodeB. These measurements are evaluated using adaptive
algorithms that take into account both current conditions and historical trends, enabling
smarter and more timely handover decisions.
One of the key innovations in LTE is the X2 interface, which allows direct communica­
tion between neighboring eNodeBs. This significantly reduces latency during handover
by letting the target eNodeB prepare relevant parameters, such as timing advance and
frequency configuration, in advance. As a result, the UE can quickly re­synchronize with
the new cell without needing to read all system information, making the process much
faster and smoother.
LTE also supports S1­based handovers, which are coordinated through the core network
between eNodeB and the MME. This method is slightly slower due to added signaling but
is used for inter­region handovers and still ensures that the UE’s session and quality of
service are preserved during the transition.
The combination of the X2 and S1 handover paths gives LTE a highly flexible and effi­
cient handover framework. This enables more effective load balancing, better interference
management, and a generally better user experience [6].
2.6.5
Roaming
Roaming, in a national context, allows subscribers of one network operator to utilize the
infrastructure of another operator within the same country, often facilitated by agreements
between them. This typically requires over­the­air updates to the SIM card to allow access
to the partner network. Even when a national roaming agreement is in place, UEs prioritize
their home network. This means that a device will generally remain connected to its home
Exploring and Analyzing Security Vulnerabilities in Modern Mobile Networks
23
==PAGE:37==
network’s 2G, 3G, or LTE, even if a partner network’s LTE, for example, offers a stronger
signal. To encourage devices to utilize the shared network in specific scenarios, the home
network can broadcast a list of equivalent PLMNs. This list identifies partner networks that
the UE should treat as equal to the home network, allowing it to connect to them. Without
this mechanism or specific signaling instructing the UE to look for other networks, the UE
will stick to its home network. This preference for the home network makes it unlikely
that a UE spontaneously switches to an unknown network or a FBS [6]. A UE would
therefore typically only connect to a partner network, if coverage from the home network
is completely unavailable.
International roaming enables a mobile user to access services comparable to those pro­
vided by their home network while traveling abroad, by connecting to a foreign operator
known as the Visited Public Land Mobile Network (VPLMN). This is made possible through
roaming agreements between the user’s home network operator and foreign operators, a
concept similar to national roaming agreements. When a mobile phone is powered on in
a foreign country, it automatically scans for available networks and attempts to connect
to a compatible one by initiating signaling with the nearest suitable base station. To grant
the UE access to voice, SMS and data services, the visited network checks the SIM card
details and contacts the HPLMN to verify the subscriber’s credentials. The home network
then authenticates the user and authorizes the use of services on the visited network [15]
[6].

the network. For data services, the mobile initiates a packet­switched attach procedure

A key step in this process is the ”Request, Ciphering On/Off” message sent from the SGSN
to the MS. This message indicates whether encryption (ciphering) of the communication

3
Mobile Network Security and Threat
Landscape
The security of mobile telecommunication networks is based on three main security tools:
integrity, encryption, and authentication. These are essential for keeping all communica­
tion private, reliable and trustworthy.
Integrity checks make sure that important messages, like those for setting up calls, have
not been changed while they’re traveling across the network. This is usually done with
cryptographic checksums or Message Authentication Codes (MACs) that go with each
message. These checks help prevent attacks where someone might try to change mes­
sages or trick the network.
Encryption scrambles the data, turning it into a secret code so that only the right people
can read it. This protects the information from being eavesdropped on. While integrity
checks for signaling messages are often required, encryption for these same messages
can sometimes be optional in certain network designs, such as GSM.
Authentication is how the network confirms that you are who you say you are before letting
you connect. This process typically involves the network challenging the subscriber’s
SIM card with a random number and the SIM card using a secret key Ki to generate
a cryptographic response. Mutual authentication means that not only does the network
check the user’s identity, but the UE also checks the network’s identity. This ensures that
both sides are legitimate, creating a secure connection where only authorized devices
can join and benefit from all other security features [6].
3.1
Security Features in GSM, LTE & 5G
3.1.1
Security Features in GSM
In 2G GSM networks, the fundamental point of communication occurs over the Um in­
terface, which constitutes the radio link between the UE and the BTS. This over­the­air
transmission serves as the primary link for both voice and data exchange. To provide con­
fidentiality for this critical link, GSM incorporates the A5 family of encryption algorithms.
The predominant variants are A5/1 and A5/2, with an additional mode, A5/0, explicitly
indicating an ”unencrypted” state. Despite the role of encryption within GSM’s security
architecture, its operational deployment is effectively optional, as network operators have
the option to select A5/0 for various operational or regulatory considerations. This option­
ality represents a significant vulnerability, as adversarial entities, such as those operat­
ing a FBS, can potentially make UEs establish connections over unencrypted channels,
Exploring and Analyzing Security Vulnerabilities in Modern Mobile Networks
30
==PAGE:44==
thereby facilitating passive eavesdropping or active manipulation of communications. Be­
yond the air interface, other critical interfaces within the GSM core network are typically
either unencrypted by default or rely on separate, often implicit, security measures.
The primary authentication mechanism in GSM is based on a challenge­response pro­
tocol. This procedure utilizes the subscriber’s secret key Ki, securely stored on the SIM
and within the network’s AuC. During this process, the network sends a Random Number
(RAND) challenge to the mobile device, which later computes a unique Signed Response
(SRES) using a one­way cryptographic function embedded within the SIM card. This au­
thentication procedure is unilateral, which exclusively verifies the subscriber’s identity to
the network. There is no mechanism that allows the mobile device to verify the legitimacy
of the serving network. This fundamental absence of mutual authentication implies that,
when a mobile device encounters a FBS, it possesses no inherent means to determine
whether the network entity is genuine. A FBS can thereby successfully impersonate a
legitimate network, potentially capturing sensitive information or misdirecting traffic [15].
3.1.2
Security Features in LTE
The security architecture of LTE provides a defense against FBS attacks, primarily through
its air interface (LTE­Uu) and advanced authentication mechanisms. The air interface is
secured by mandatory encryption and integrity protection, which collectively serve as the
initial line of defense. Encryption is applied to all radio transmissions between the UE
and the eNodeB through ciphering mechanisms at the Medium Access Control (MAC)
or Radio Link Control (RLC) layers. To counter replay attacks and enhance ciphertext
diversity, a continuously updating counter (COUNT­C) is incorporated.
LTE implements robust mutual authentication through the EPS Authentication and Key
Agreement protocol (E­AKA). This ensures that both the UE and the network mutually
authenticate each other, ensuring that the UE can verify the legitimacy of the serving net­
work. This requirement directly mitigates FBS threats by enabling a UE to detect inconsis­
tencies in network identity, as FBSs typically lack the necessary shared secrets and proper
sequence number management to generate correct cryptographic responses. However,
despite this enhanced mutual authentication, challenges persist where a FBS could spoof
the network identity or mislead the UE during initial connection setup, particularly through
manipulated signaling occurring before the establishment of full authentication.
For core and roaming interfaces, LTE leverages Internet Protocol Security (IPSec) mecha­
nisms on critical links such as the S1 (eNodeB to MME/S­GW), S5/S8 (S­GW to PDN­GW,
especially for roaming), and X2 (eNodeB to eNodeB) interfaces. IPSec can be deployed to
ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and mutual authentication of both signaling and user
plane traffic across network boundaries. The application of IPSec on these interfaces is
vital for safeguarding against FBS­type attacks that might otherwise target inter­network
links during roaming handovers or inter­base station communication.

Despite these security measures, certain vulnerabilities persist in LTE. These include FBS
attacks involving downgrade attacks and the exploitation of broadcast system informa­
tion. For example, FBSs can broadcast misleading system parameters to induce insecure
or downgraded attachment scenarios, or manipulate broadcasted network information to
confuse the UE’s network selection process. These risks highlight the ongoing challenge
of detecting and mitigating FBS attacks [15].
3.1.3
Security Features in 5G
5G introduces significant safeguards against FBS attacks, while simultaneously acknowl­
edging certain legacy vulnerabilities. On the air interface, designated as NR­Uu, 5G en­
sures both encryption and integrity protection. This framework dictates that user­plane
data and signaling are secured through robust cryptographic algorithms, stronger than
those in previous generations, often employing keys of 128 bits or more. These design
principles ensure that integrity and confidentiality are enforced on the NR­Uu air interface,
actively countering eavesdropping and illegitimate manipulation by FBS devices.
In the domain of authentication, the 5G Authentication and Key Agreement protocol (5G­
AKA) provides mutually authenticated procedures between UEs and the network. This
mutual authentication and the use of strong cryptographic challenges and responses
counteracts FBS attempts by effectively preventing unauthorized entities from masquerad­
ing as legitimate network nodes.
Within the core network, 5G’s Service­Based Architecture (SBA) and the role of the Secu­
rity Edge Protection Proxy (SEPP) further contribute to the network security. The security
of communication between Network Functions (NFs) within the SBA is fundamentally sus­
tained by Transport Layer Security (TLS), ensuring that inter­NF communications are both
encrypted and integrity­protected. The SEPP plays a critical role in securing roaming in­
terconnections by employing IPSec to protect signaling exchanges with foreign networks
[16].
3.2
Overview of Security Threats in Mobile Networks
3.2.1
Security Threats In 2G
GSM has a fundamentally weak security architecture. One of the main reasons is that
encryption is optional and authentication occurs only in one direction. This means that
the network verifies the identity of the subscriber, but the subscriber does not verify the
identity of the network. The result of this practice is that there is no guarantee that the user
is connected to a legitimate network. Mobile operators can choose a configuration called
A5/0, in which no encryption is applied. This means that voice and data are transmitted
in cleartext and can easily be intercepted by anyone within range.
The design decision was originally made to simplify international compatibility and allow
Exploring and Analyzing Security Vulnerabilities in Modern Mobile Networks
32
==PAGE:46==
compliance with local regulations. It severely compromises both the confidentiality and
the integrity of user communication. The lack of mutual authentication makes it possible
for attackers to set up FBSs using relatively inexpensive hardware. FBSs can pretend to
be legitimate parts of the mobile network. Because encryption is not required, attackers
can intercept signaling messages and user data without special access privileges. This
allows them to continuously track users and reveal their identities by collecting IMSIs
during the network registration process.
At the time GSM was designed, the possibility of active attacks involving FBSs was not
seriously considered. It was assumed that adversaries would not have the technical and
financial capability to operate their own base stations. This assumption was rooted in the
belief that such attacks would be too expensive compared to other methods of eavesdrop­
ping, such as tapping wired connections or physically bugging devices. As a result, GSM
security focused primarily on protecting against passive eavesdropping and large­scale
fraud, leaving it particularly vulnerable to more sophisticated active threats that became
feasible with technological advancements and the availability of low­cost radio equipment.
GSM also relies on outdated cryptographic mechanisms. It uses a 64­bit session key
called Kc and SIM­based algorithms such as A3, A8, and A5. These algorithms have
known weaknesses and are vulnerable to cryptographic attacks. In some cases, they
can even allow attackers to clone a SIM card, giving them direct access to the victim’s
mobile identity and services. These issues give adversaries multiple entry points to carry
out attacks on GSM networks.
Some of the threats in GSM are highlighted below [17]:
1. Base Station Spoofing and IMSI Catching
GSM networks are particularly vulnerable to base station spoofing and IMSI catch­
ing due to their lack of mutual authentication. Attackers can set up FBSs that mimic
legitimate ones, forcing nearby devices to connect by emitting stronger signals.
Once connected, the attacker can intercept communications and disable encryp­
tion, gaining access to sensitive data. Additionally, they can capture the device’s
IMSI, a unique identifier used to track users and link them to their activity. These
attacks compromise confidentiality, integrity, and user privacy, enabling eavesdrop­
ping, data manipulation, and user surveillance without detection.
2. SMS Exploitation
Building on base station spoofing, SMS exploitation involves sending malicious con­
figuration messages to connected devices. Attackers can modify routing settings,
disable security features, or redirect traffic. This compromises confidentiality by al­
lowing message interception, and integrity by altering device settings. It also poses
a risk to availability, as misconfigured devices may be unable to access services.
3. Encryption Suppression

==PAGE:47==
In GSM, encryption is not mandatory and can be suppressed. Attackers can manip­
ulate terminal capability messages to make it appear that encryption is unsupported,
resulting in all communication being transmitted in plaintext. This attack undermines
both confidentiality and integrity, as sensitive data can be intercepted and modified.
While availability remains unaffected, users often receive no warning due to disabled
encryption alerts on many devices.
3.2.2
Security Threats In LTE
LTE improved security with mutual authentication, better encryption, and key manage­
ment via E­AKA. Despite user data and signaling being encrypted, LTE still faces key
security challenges, categorized into four main groups [18].
1. Location Tracking
In LTE networks, location tracking can be exploited through the cleartext transmis­
sion of the Cell Radio Network Temporary Identifier (C­RNTI). Attackers can pas­
sively monitor a UE’s presence within a cell and across multiple cells, especially
during handovers if the new C­RNTI is not encrypted. Continuous packet sequence
numbers also allow attackers to correlate old and new identifiers, enabling device
tracking throughout the network.
2. Bandwidth Stealing
Bandwidth theft can occur during the UE’s Discontinuous Reception (DRX) period,
when its radio is off but still allowed to transmit. Attackers may inject data using the
UE’s C­RNTI to misuse network resources. Another method involves sending fake
buffer status reports to the eNodeB, misleading scheduling, and resource allocation,
which can reduce bandwidth availability for legitimate users.
3. Denial­of­Service (DoS) Attacks
DoS attacks in LTE can target individual UEs or the entire cell. Attackers can use
C­RNTIs to interfere with scheduled transmissions or flood the network during DRX
periods. Fake buffer reports can mislead the eNodeB into overestimating traffic and
blocking new users. Even secure protocols such as 3GPP E­AKA are vulnerable to
exploits like synchronization attacks and FBSs.
4. IMSI Catching
IMSI catching remains a significant threat in LTE networks because the IMSI is trans­
mitted in plain text during the attach procedure. It is only with 5G that the IMSI,
referred to as SUCI, is properly concealed and encrypted, greatly improving sub­
scriber privacy.
Finally, LTE networks are also susceptible to downgrade attacks and FBSs. An attacker
can send a forged RRC Connection Release message to a UE, forcing it to disconnect
from the LTE network and reselect a less secure 2G network.

3.2.3
Security Threats In 5G
Despite the enhanced security features of 5G compared to earlier generations, active
threats to the network still persist. Below are some of the most common and impactful
attacks seen in 5G environments [19]:
1. Eavesdropping and Traffic Analysis
Eavesdropping is a passive attack where an attacker intercepts communication be­
tween two legitimate users without interrupting the network. It violates data con­
fidentiality and user privacy. Even if messages are encrypted, traffic analysis can
reveal patterns such as location and identity. These attacks are hard to detect since
they do not disrupt communication and stronger encryption is essential to defend
against them.
2. Jamming
Jamming is an active attack where a malicious node intentionally disrupts commu­
nication by interfering with radio signals, preventing legitimate users from accessing
the network. Unlike passive attacks, jamming fully stops communication, affecting
network availability. Detection and resource allocation strategies are used to miti­
gate jamming and improve communication reliability in 5G networks.
3. Denial­of­Service (DoS) Attacks
Denial­of­Service (DoS) and Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks flood the
network with malicious requests, exhausting its resources and causing service out­
ages. These active attacks target network availability and can affect multiple layers
due to the high device density in 5G systems. They may target batteries, memory,
sensors, radio, or CPU resources, posing a serious threat to network stability.
4. Man­in­the­Middle (MITM)
In a Man­in­the­Middle (MITM) attack, the attacker takes control of the communica­
tion channel between two users, intercepting and potentially modifying or replacing
messages. This compromises data confidentiality, integrity, and availability. MITM
attacks actively disrupt secure communication, making them a critical threat to 5G
networks.
Since 5G no longer transmits the IMSI in plain text, passive IMSI catching is effectively
mitigated. However, FBSs still remains a significant threat in 5G networks, as attackers
can still lure users to connect to false stations, by jamming the 5G frequency.
3.3
False Base Stations (FBS)
3.3.1
What is a False Base Station?
A FBS can be defined as a cellular base station that is used for malicious and usually illegal
purposes. These base stations are hardware and software tools capable of executing
Exploring and Analyzing Security Vulnerabilities in Modern Mobile Networks
35
==PAGE:49==
passive or active attacks on mobile devices [20].
FBSs present considerable security risks to mobile networks. They exploit vulnerabilities
in the UE’s cell selection and reselection procedures, tricking the device into connecting
to the FBS instead of a legitimate one.
During normal operation, the UE monitors signals from surrounding base stations and
selects the cell with the strongest and most suitable signal based on parameters broad­
cast by the network. FBSs exploit this by broadcasting signals that appear stronger or
more attractive to the UE, causing it to camp on or connect to the FBS. Unlike legitimate
handovers, no cooperation or authorization occurs between the FBS and the legitimate
network infrastructure [21].
This deceptive behavior allows FBSs to intercept, manipulate, or disrupt communications,
posing significant security threats to users and operators.
3.3.2
How False Base Stations Work
FBSs rely on two primary strategies to lure an UE away from a legitimate base station.
First, the FBS must broadcast the PLMNs identifiers, that correspond to a legitimate net­
work. Second, it must transmit this information at a signal strength significantly higher
than that of the genuine base station [5]. These two strategies are fundamental to the op­
eration of a FBS and closely reflect how FBSs function in real­world scenarios, as will be
demonstrated in Section 6, ”FBS Simulation and UE Behavioral Analysis Experiments.”
In Figure 3.1, the presence of a FBS is illustrated. The FBS has copied values from a
legitimate base station and poses a threat for UEs with corresponding PLMN values. In
case of jamming the FBS might also pose a threat for other UEs.

Once a UE is captured by a FBS, the attacker can initiate a variety of attacks, including
impersonation, interception, and eavesdropping, compromising both the security of the
UE and the integrity of the mobile network. A common and important tactic that FBSs often
attempt, is to downgrade the UE onto less secure network technologies. Downgrading a
UE from a modern mobile network such as 5G or 4G to a 2G network allows the attacker
to exploit additional vulnerabilities, primarily due to the absence of robust encryption and
security mechanisms in 2G networks.
The downgrade from modern to legacy networks is achieved by making the UE believe
that its current secure connection is no longer available, forcing it to connect to a less se­
cure generation. Attackers can facilitate this either by sending deceptive signaling mes­
sages, such as Registration Reject or Connection Release messages, or by actively jam­
ming the frequency bands used by legitimate base stations. As a result, the UE perceives
the attacker’s 2G FBS as the only viable option and connects to it, thereby becoming more
susceptible to further attacks.
The transition process where the UE goes from a legitimate base station to FBS is espe­
cially alarming, as it can happen without the user’s knowledge, making it difficult to detect
and mitigate [21].

Related Work on False Base Station
Detection
4.1
Active Detection Method (Network­Side Analysis)
The solution in the paper ”Defeating IMSI Catchers” [22] proposes replacing the IMSI with
changing pseudonyms (PMSIs) that only the SIM’s home network can link to the SIM’s
identification. This approach is designed to be implemented by the mobile provider, as
it requires modifications to the SIM cards and the authentication server within the home
network.
The paper emphasizes that the change is transparent to the serving network and does
not require alterations to the existing infrastructure or network equipment.
A solution called the Precheck Sequence­Based Detection (PSD) scheme has been in­
troduced to address the security challenges posed by FBS during the handover process
in cellular networks [21]. This scheme is designed to ensure that UE connects to a legiti­
mate base station rather than an FBS. By using a precheck sequence, the PSD scheme
mitigates the risk of FBS impersonation, which happen when a FBS transmits signals with
characteristics and power levels similar to those of a legitimate base station to deceive
the UE.
The PSD scheme works by analyzing the structure of received signals, which are orga­
nized into blocks and symbols. To verify the authenticity of these signals, the scheme
introduces additional symbols into the existing signal sequence. These symbols are se­
lected from a pre­designed long table of symbol sequences. Each UE undergoing han­
dover is assigned a specific sequence from this table, serving as a verification mechanism
to confirm the legitimacy of the base station to which the UE is being handed over.
The PSD scheme is designed to enhance the security of the handover process without
significantly impacting network efficiency. However, it requires certain resources, such
as memory space to store the table of sequences and additional synchronization efforts.
These requirements are managed within the network, utilizing the capabilities of the base
stations rather than placing the burden on the UE itself.
4.2
Machine Learning Based Detection
A machine learning­based approach has been proposed to detect FBSs with high ac­
curacy, even when these stations employ detection evasion techniques. This method
integrates temporal graph analysis with anomaly detection algorithms to improve detec­
Exploring and Analyzing Security Vulnerabilities in Modern Mobile Networks
39
==PAGE:53==
tion capabilities while minimizing false positives, a common issue in traditional manual
inspection methods [21].
The approach uses temporal graph analysis to monitor connectivity and hand­off events
between UE and base stations. By analyzing these temporal dynamics, the method can
detect abnormal patterns that may indicate a FBS. The PageRank algorithm is applied
to evaluate transitions between base stations during a sampling period, assessing the
legitimacy of connections within a wireless coverage area.
To improve detection accuracy, the method uses different anomaly detection algorithms,
such as the Temporal Graph Isolation Forest (TGIF) and the Local Outlier Factor (LOF).
These algorithms analyze radio measurement reports, such as the RSRP and SINR, pro­
vided by UEs to the core networks. By identifying anomalies in these reports, the system
can detect FBS activity, even when adversaries attempt to evade detection by changing
locations or modifying transmission strength.
The proposed algorithm demonstrates strong performance in detecting FBSs. It was eval­
uated on 83,136 data entries from 10 datasets, where FBS configurations varied, including
changes in location and antenna settings. Despite these variations, the ensemble algo­
rithm achieved an average precision score of 90%, highlighting its reliability in correctly
classifying true positives.

• Active Detection (Network­Side Analysis): Network­side solutions include IMSI

• Machine Learning­Based Detection: Combines temporal graph analysis and anomaly
detection on UE radio data to spot abnormal patterns, achieving around 90% accu­

Together, these methods advance the detection and mitigation of FBS threats, improving
mobile network security.

The purpose of this experimental setup is to create a controlled and configurable test

with base stations and how signaling procedures function across different generations.
A central objective of the project is to explore how FBSs can exploit weaknesses in mo­

5.1
Hardware and Software required
This section presents the hardware and software components used to simulate mobile
network environments. To effectively investigate various aspects and vulnerabilities, we
specifically chose to simulate the 2G GSM and 4G LTE network environments. The se­
lection of these generations is driven by their distinct security profiles and widespread use
in the real world.
2G networks were included due to their historically weaker security mechanisms, such
as optional encryption and lack of mutual authentication. These vulnerabilities make 2G
highly susceptible to passive eavesdropping and active attacks such as downgrade at­
tacks, where modern devices are forced to revert to a less secure 2G connection, expos­
ing communications to compromise. These weaknesses allow FBS attacks to occur.
4G networks were chosen because they remain widely deployed globally, forming the
backbone of most mobile data traffic. While 4G introduced significant security improve­
ments over 2G, including mutual authentication and mandatory user plane encryption, it
still has certain vulnerabilities to FBS attacks compared to the robust security architecture
of 5G. 4G lacks mandatory integrity protection for all control plane messages, meaning
that an attacker can send malicious control signals whose integrity the device cannot ver­
ify. Furthermore, 4G does not have SUPI concealment as found in 5G, leaving the IMSI
potentially vulnerable during specific network interactions. These weaknesses in 4G’s
security design, particularly when contrasted with 5G’s comprehensive integrity protec­
tion and identity concealment, highlight its continued relevance as a target for network
impersonation and downgrade exploits, making it interesting for experimental research.

5.6
Limitations of the Experimental Setup
Our experimental setup provides a valuable environment for studying cellular network
behavior and the operation of false base stations, but there are several limitations that
affect its ability to fully replicate real­world scenarios.
The use of the SDR USRP B210 constrains transmission power and range. Commer­
cial base stations operate at significantly higher power levels and cover large geographic
areas, our setup is limited to short­range communication within a lab environment. This
restricts the ability to simulate scenarios that involve interference, mobility, and handovers
between multiple cells.
The scale of the test setup is also limited. In real­world deployments, base stations man­
age a large number of simultaneous connections, which can influence signaling behavior
and network load. Our experiments typically involve only one user device.
Although our use of open­source tools, such as OpenBTS and srsRAN, offers flexibility
and insight into protocol­level behavior, they may not fully implement all features, opti­
mizations, or security mechanisms present in commercial network equipment. Therefore,
some signaling procedures and network responses observed in the lab may differ from
those encountered in live operator networks.
However, for simulating FBSs, our experimental setup was reasonably well equipped. Ad­
versaries are unlikely to have access to much more advanced hardware than a standard
computer and a USRP B210 that we also used and found sufficient for capturing mobile
phone connections.
Finally, only a small number of mobile phones and SIM cards were used in our exper­
iments, which introduce limitations in terms of device diversity and behavior. Different
smartphone models and operating systems may handle network interactions and signal­
ing procedures in slightly different ways. SIM cards from various operators may imple­
ment different security features or configurations. The findings based on our limited set of
devices and SIM cards may not fully capture the variability in the real­world environment.

6.1.1
Scenario 1: Network Selection During Cold Start and Flight Mode
Objective of the Experiment
The purpose of this experiment is to investigate how mobile devices perform network
selection when reconnecting to a mobile network after being fully disconnected. This
includes two common scenarios. A cold start, which is when the UE is powered off and
then turned back on, and reactivation from flight mode, where the device’s radio interfaces
are temporarily disabled and then re­enabled.
The experiment specifically examines whether a FBS can attract a UE solely by broad­
casting the correct MCC and MNC values that match the device’s home network. This
scenario exploits a fundamental weakness in current mobile network protocols, where ini­
tial cell selection and reselection often prioritize broadcast network identifiers and signal
strength over cryptographic authentication, allowing a malicious entity to impersonate a
legitimate network during the pre­authentication phase.
Furthermore, it explores the influence of transmission power on the UE’s selection be­
havior, assessing whether the device connects to the false network or instead prioritizes
a legitimate network operator to which the UE is actually subscribed. Figure 6.1 illus­
trates the experimental scenario described above, including the presence of both false
and legitimate base stations.

enough to attract UEs, even when legitimate base stations are available.

concern, as it enables attackers to intercept devices at their most vulnerable moment ­

Conclusion
This scenario demonstrates how fallback behavior can be exploited to retain control of a
UE. Devices prioritize maintaining connectivity to the connected network, and will often
downgrade to available 3G and 2G networks without user intervention. This however,
also underlines the fact that it is very difficult to capture a UE onto a FBS, once it is
already connected to a legitimate network. The network is unlikely to initiate a handover
to another unknown base station, if it is not listed in the configured neighbor cell list. In
such cases, jamming the current connection becomes necessary to force a reselection,
as demonstrated in scenario 2.
This experiment demonstrates that once a UE loses connection to a 4G FBS, its fallback
behavior is not always uniform and can vary depending on the device type and SIM op­
erator. While some UEs, such as the iPhone with a HI3G SIM, consistently fall back to
lower­generation networks like 3G or 2G, others like the Android device with a TDC SIM
may attempt to reconnect to a legitimate 4G base station or revert to 2G, depending on
the circumstances. These observations suggest that fallback behavior is influenced by a
combination of signal conditions, device manufacturer, and operator configurations. Nev­
ertheless, the fact that UEs can be forced onto older, less secure technologies remains a
critical vulnerability, as attackers may exploit this behavior to maintain persistent access
and surveillance.
It is worth noting that from an attacker’s perspective, there are more direct methods for
forcing a UE to downgrade from an LTE to a 2G connection. One such technique involves
sending a RRC Connection Release message to the UE, prompting it to disconnect and
subsequently connect to a 2G FBS [29]. However, this type of forced downgrade was not
explored in our experiments. Instead, our focus was on observing the UE’s natural fall­
back and network selection behavior, in order to better understand how devices prioritize
connectivity in real­world scenarios without explicit manipulation.

False Base Station Detection Algorithm
7.1
Detection Approach and Concept
During our experiments with FBSs, we observed that when an UE connects to a mobile
network, it includes its previous LAI as part of the signaling procedure. The LAI consists
of the MCC, MNC and LAC. In addition, the UE may also transmit identifiers such as the
MMEGI and MMEC from its last connected MME. According to the 3GPP standard, the
network operators can access and see the old LAI of a UE, meaning it should not be a
problem to implement, if not already enabled [30].
Figure 7.1 shows an example where the UE was previously attached to a commercial
HI3G 4G network with LAI 238/06/40103, and then reattaches to our srsRAN 4G network.
As seen in the figure, the receiving 4G network is able to extract the previous LAI, revealing
which network the UE was connected to before.
Figure 7.1: Old Location Area Identification values
Figure 7.2 further illustrates that it is also possible for the network to extract the previous
visited MME group and MMEC, which can be used to further strengthen the detection
algorithm. In this scenario the previous visited MMEGI is 32768, and the MMEC is 192.
Figure 7.2: Previous MME group and MME code
Utilizing previous LAI values presents a useful opportunity for detecting FBSs and anoma­
lous network behavior. We are therefore suggesting a detection rule that looks like the
following.
Exploring and Analyzing Security Vulnerabilities in Modern Mobile Networks
81
==PAGE:95==
If a UE connects to a mobile network and its previous LAI has a different MCC/MNC
than the legitimate network, flag it as suspicious.
This rule would make sense, because in real operator environments a phone should not
suddenly return from a completely different PLMN without an international or national
roaming context. If the old LAI reports, say, MCC=001 MNC=01, but the expected PLMN
is 238/01 (Denmark, TDC), that’s highly anomalous.
To further improve the reliability of the detection mechanism, we propose incorporating
additional parameters such as the LAC, MMEGI, and MMEC into the algorithm. While the
LAC alone may not serve as a definitive indicator, since it is not a unique identifier across
all operators, data from OpenCellID [31] suggests that most operators tend to operate
within specific and separated LAC ranges from each another.
For example, TDC typically uses LACs around 2900, HI3G around 40103 (also seen in
Figure 7.1), and Telia around 24033. These ranges are relatively distinct, meaning that
observing an unexpected LAC value such as 1000, especially when combined with an
abnormal MCC/MNC, can strengthen the suspicion of malicious activity.
Finally, the MMEGI and MMEC are used to uniquely identify a MME within a network.
If a network suddenly observes a MMEGI or MMEC value that does not correspond to
any known or expected MME within its infrastructure, it may indicate that the UE was
previously connected to a FBS.
Incorporating all relevant parameters into a comprehensive detection rule, we propose
the following:
If a UE connects to a mobile network and its previous Location Area Identification
contains a MCC/MNC combination that does not match the current network, and
especially if the LAC, MMEGI or MMEC also fall outside expected ranges, flag the
event as suspicious.
Naturally, this method may generate some false positives. However, if all the parameters
correspond either to the home network or to known roaming partners, the event can be
safely whitelisted. It is highly unlikely that an attacker could replicate all these values
correctly to successfully evade detection with a FBS.

that may indicate the presence of a FBS.

Discussion
8.1
Detection Strategies and Algorithmic Improvements
8.1.1
Measurement Report Monitoring
One of the challenges in detecting FBSs lies in the behavior of the UE’s. UE’s only mea­
sure and report signals from cells that are explicitly listed in the neighboring cell list pro­
vided by the serving base station. A FBS that is not part of this list will often go unnoticed,
making it difficult to collect any signal measurements related to it under normal circum­
stances.
UE’s could be configured to periodically scan and report information about all detectable
surrounding cells, not just those on the neighbor list. These extended measurement re­
ports can include parameters such as signal strength (RSRP/RSRQ), cell ID’s, frequen­
cies, and timing information.
By collecting and analyzing these measurement reports, it becomes possible to identify
anomalies or unexpected cell broadcasts. For example, a FBS might operate at a fre­
quency typically not used in a given location, have an unusual signal strength, or broad­
cast a cell ID that does not match the expected network configuration. These deviations
can serve as indicators of potential attacks or unauthorized networks.
8.1.2
Unexpected Network Detection
Another detection strategy, building upon measurement report monitoring, involves con­
figuring the UE to identify anomalies in the broadcasted PLMN information. Specifically,
the PLMN would monitor for the sudden appearance of networks with unusual or suspi­
cious names, such as “Range” or “TEST­PLMN”, in areas where only known operators,
such as Telia or TDC, are typically present.
To enable this functionality, the UE must be configured to periodically scan for surrounding
networks and report its observations to the serving network. For this approach to be
effective, the UE must also have access to a reference list of expected PLMNs for its
current geographic location. This highlights the importance of location­awareness, as the
set of legitimate operators naturally varies by country and region.
8.1.3
Clustered Disconnection Detection
An effective strategy for detecting FBSs involves monitoring for time­correlated discon­
nection and reattachment events across multiple UEs. If a large number of devices in the
same geographic area suddenly disconnected from the network and subsequently reat­
tach with unexpected MCC/MNC values, this pattern should be treated as suspicious. For
Exploring and Analyzing Security Vulnerabilities in Modern Mobile Networks
86
==PAGE:100==
example, in a high­density environment such as a shopping mall, if a significant number
of users simultaneously lose network access, this deviation from the base station’s typical
attach/detach ratio could signal the presence of a FBS attempting to lure UEs into con­
necting to a fake network. Detecting such anomalies requires establishing a baseline for
normal connection behavior and continuously monitoring for abrupt deviations.
When disconnections occur in clusters, particularly within a short time window, this strongly
suggests external interference. Detecting these anomalies furthermore requires the op­
erators to correlate connection logs across multiple UEs and to maintain a baseline of
expected network behavior for specific regions.
8.1.4
Roaming Partner Configuration Awareness
Our proposed detection algorithm relies on validating network identifiers such as MMEGIs,
MMEC and LAC. For the detection mechanism to function reliably, these identifiers must
be known by the network.
Naturally, operators has full visibility into their own infrastructure, including the specific
MME identifiers associated with their PLMN. This means that if a UE attempts to attach
using the operator’s MCC/MNC but presents an unknown MMEGI or MMEC, the operator
can immediately flag this as suspicious.
However, the situation becomes more complex in roaming scenarios. When a UE at­
taches to a network with a different MCC/MNC, belonging to a legitimate roaming part­
ner, the operator must also be aware of that partner’s corresponding MMEGIs, LACs, and
MMECs. Without this information, the operator has no reliable means of verifying whether
the presented identifiers are legitimate or maliciously fabricated by an attacker. There­
fore, knowledge of key roaming partner configurations is essential for effective anomaly
detection and secure network operation.
Most operators in Denmark however, maintain close to 100% coverage, meaning domes­
tic roaming is not very common. As a result, observing a UE attach using an MCC/MNC
that does not match its HPLMN is a strong indicator of potentially malicious activity. That
said, such an event could still represent a legitimate roaming scenario. Therefore, to en­
sure the robustness of the detection algorithm, operators must maintain an up­to­date
record of network identifiers associated with their authorized roaming partners.
8.2
Network Behavior Exploitation by Adversaries
8.2.1
RRC Connection Release Downgrades
Attackers can leverage the RRC Connection Release procedure to strategically force the
UE onto older and less secure cellular technologies. When an UE concludes an active
session on a modern network, the serving base station sends an RRC Connection Re­
lease message. An adversary can either spoof this message or inject a crafted one, con­
Exploring and Analyzing Security Vulnerabilities in Modern Mobile Networks
87
==PAGE:101==
taining instructions that direct the UE to reselect a cell operating on 2G that lacks mutual
authentication.
8.2.2
Legacy Network Exploitation
As mobile operators decommission their 3G networks, attackers are presented with a
unique opportunity. In the absence of legitimate 3G infrastructure, an adversary operating
a 3G FBS faces virtually no competition. If a device is forced to downgrade from 4G or
5G, whether due to signal jamming, loss of connection, or manipulation, it will attempt to
connect to the next available network technology, which is often 3G.
Because operators have shut down their 3G services, the attacker’s false network may
be the only 3G signal available. This gives the attacker a significant advantage, greatly
increasing the likelihood that the UE will connect to their malicious network. This strategy
exploits both the fallback behavior of mobile devices and the gap in network technologies
left by the operators.
8.2.3
Network Selection After Flight Mode or Restart
When an UE exits flight mode or restarts, it initiates a new network selection process.
During these transition states, the UE actively scans for available cellular networks to
register and establish connectivity. This increases the likelihood that a FBS captures
the UE as it attempts to connect. However, our observations indicate that activating and
deactivating flight mode did not consistently force the UE to re­evaluate and connect to the
FBS, which might suggest variations in the device specific network reselection or caching
behaviors.
One potential mitigation against FBS attachment during these transitions is the use of
cached PLMN and cell parameters. If the UE always try to reuse previously stored PLMN
and cell information upon powering on, rather than initiating a full network scan, the
chances of connecting to a rogue base station are reduced. This behavior minimizes
exposure to unauthorized signals and increases the likelihood of rejoining the last known
legitimate network. Naturally, this is not an option if the UE has relocated since the last
time it was powered on, which means that a full network scan is needed.
8.3
User Mitigation Strategies
8.3.1
Disabling 2G on User Devices
A straightforward mitigation strategy against FBS attacks involves preventing UEs from
connecting to 2G networks. Many Android smartphones offer the option of disabling 2G
connectivity. By forcing devices to operate exclusively on 3G, 4G, or 5G, the attack surface
for adversaries running a FBS is significantly reduced, as the device will no longer fall back
to this less secure standard.

88
==PAGE:102==
8.3.2
Monitoring for Suspicious Behavior
Users can play a crucial role in mitigating FBS attacks by being aware of unusual phone
behavior. Recognizing certain events could alert them to a possible compromise. Some
indicators of FBS attacks can be:
• Sudden Loss and Suspicious Reconnection: Your phone might suddenly lose its
mobile signal entirely, only to then reconnect to a 2G network or offers no data
service. This can be a sign of a forced downgrade or connection to a false base
station. Especially if you are in a location that typically has good coverage.
• Frequent and Unexplained Disconnects/Reconnects: Your device repeatedly dis­
connects from and reconnects to the cellular network, especially without a clear
reason like entering an area with known poor coverage, could be a red flag.
• Receiving Strange Messages: This includes receiving fake SMS messages from
unknown or spoofed numbers or unexpected prompts to install some suspicious
applications.
Raising user awareness about these specific signs could lead the public to take action,
such as toggling airplane mode to break free from an adversary’s grasps and contacting
their mobile provider to report the suspicious activity.
8.4
Limitations and Real­World Variability
8.4.1
Diverse Device and Network Behavior
It is important to note that the behavior of the device and the network shows significant
variability. Our experimental findings only reflect a sample of the huge mobile ecosystem,
as phone behavior varies significantly between device models, SIM types, and global net­
work configurations. The diversity underscores the fundamental challenge of generalizing
experimental results across the mobile landscape.
8.4.2
No Handover to False Networks
FBSs cannot perform handovers from legitimate cellular networks. Unlike real base sta­
tions, they operate independently, lacking coordination for mobility management.
When an UE is actively communicating, the legitimate network controls handovers to
maintain connection continuity. FBSs lack the necessary authentication keys and core
network integration to participate in or initiate these handovers.
FBSs primarily target and capture idle UEs, which are not actively engaged in communi­
cation.

8.4.3
Contextual Threat Assessment
The risk of FBSs varies with the environment. Poor cellular coverage in areas such as
rural locations, underground spaces, or buildings with thick walls makes devices more
likely to connect to a FBS.
High user density areas such as airports, train stations, and urban centers, are attractive
targets. Attackers might use jamming to force devices onto their FBSs.
8.5
Physical Security Considerations
Although much of the focus on cellular network security is on digital threats, a physical se­
curity threat is also present. Considering the scale of networks like TDC’s with over 3,000
base stations spread across Denmark [32]. This extensive infrastructure, particularly in
remote locations, offers potential points of compromise. If an attacker gains physical ac­
cess to a legitimate base station, they could disrupt its operation or facilitate the use of a
FBS. This means that a compromised base station could allow a FBS to take over mo­
bile connections in an area. This situation could persist until personnel physically reach
the site to resolve the issue. Operators using their own fiber optic infrastructure, such as
TDC, do enhance security compared to shared lines. However, the physical security of
the base station sites itself remains critical in preventing such exploits. Protecting these
physical sites is essential to mitigate this attack vector.

Conclusion
This project has explored security vulnerabilities in modern mobile networks, with a spe­
cific focus on SMS blasters and FBSs. A comprehensive analysis has been performed
on how FBSs operate and the potential impact they pose, highlighting the serious threats
users may face when exposed to such attacks. Given that FBS spam is estimated to
cause billions of dollars in financial losses annually, the project further explores the be­
havior of both user equipment and FBS setups in real­world environments.
Several practical experiments have been carried out to better understand the interactions
between FBSs and UEs, and also to be able to support the development of effective
detection strategies. Through these experiments, the behavior of both the UE and the
network has been systematically analyzed. Based on these observations, a practical
detection mechanism has been proposed to identify and mitigate the presence of FBSs.
The proposed detection algorithm uses information about the UE’s previously visited LAI,
which is transmitted by the UE to the network during the attach procedure. The LAI in­
cludes the PLMN identifier (MCC/MNC) and the LAC, allowing the network to cross­check
this data against expected values based on the UE’s HPLMN. To further enhance the ro­
bustness of the algorithm, additional network identifiers, such as the MMEGI and MMEC,
are also incorporated into the validation process.
The proposed detection mechanism is a lightweight, scalable approach that operates us­
ing network­side signaling data. By cross­validating UE­reported identifiers against pre­
defined legitimate baselines, the algorithm can flag anomalies indicative of FBS attacks in
near real time. The solution is designed to be minimally invasive and highly deployable.
It does not require new hardware or changes to existing signaling protocols. Implemen­
tation involves only a lightweight script running on the network side, making integration
straightforward and low cost.
Future work could investigate the use of machine learning to improve detection accu­
racy by modeling normal network behavior and adapting thresholds dynamically. Broader
testing across different devices and conditions would also help validate and refine the ap­
proach. Overall, this project offers initial insights into mobile network vulnerabilities and
proposes a practical detection method that may support efforts to counter FBS threats.

Future Perspectives
This thesis explored UE behavior in the presence of FBSs and proposed a detection
method using previously connected network data. Experiments revealed how parameters
like LAC and PLMN mismatches indicate abnormal activity, offering a basis for future
detection system development.
One area worth exploring further is the integration of anomaly detection into real world
operators infrastructure.
Much of our detection logic relies on analyzing unexpected
MMEGIs, LACs, or PLMN values. These mechanisms could be expanded into real­time
analytics for operators, potentially using machine learning to model normal network be­
havior in specific regions and automatically flag suspicious deviations. However, this
would require access to large­scale datasets, something beyond the scope of this project
but highly valuable for future research.
Another direction is the use of centralized logging and extended reporting of UE measure­
ments. UEs continuously collect signal data, such as RSRP, RSRQ, and neighboring cell
information, which are often reported back to the network. If these data were aggregated
and analyzed on a scale, it could provide strong indicators of FBS activity. For exam­
ple, if a cluster of devices suddenly reports an unknown or unusually strong cell ID, it
might suggest an FBS operating in the area. The concept of extended measurement re­
ports, where UEs measure on all nearby cells, could allow operators to remotely validate
suspected FBSs. This would shift part of the detection burden onto the UE while still
keeping operators in the loop. Future work could explore how such capabilities could be
made more standardized and accessible. Collaborating with standardized bodies such
as 3GPP could help formalize these ideas into updated specifications, especially for use
in urban or high­risk areas where FBSs are more likely to appear.
We also observed significant variability between devices and configurations, underlining
the importance of more extensive cross­device testing. Our experiments focused on a
few smartphone models and SIM types, but a broader dataset could help generalize our
findings and improve the robustness of detection algorithms.
While our project focused on understanding and detecting FBSs within the constraints
of available hardware and software tools, there is a clear path towards more automated,
real­time and large­scale solutions, both from the network side and user side.
