Abstract
Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is the most malignant and common primary brain tumor. [1] Standard
treatment is resection and chemoradiotherapy, however, incomplete resectioning of GBM can result in tumor
regrowth. [2] Several methods are used to ensure full removal of tumor tissue. Five-aminolevulinic acid
(5-ALA) induced protoporphyrin IX (PpIX) is the most common optical method to detect GBM tumor
margins. [3] However, this method is not without complications. Because of this, the Biophotinic group at
Tyndall National Institute has developed a tissue recognition (TR) system using endogenous fluorophores to
detect GBM during surgery. In this thesis, I will characterize the TR system’s signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and
dynamic range (DR), comparing it to a commercial spectrometer. Moreover, 1024 phantoms will be produced,
consisting of an absorber, a scatterer, and 3 fluorophores: nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NADH), flavin
adenine dinucleotide (FAD), and PpIX. All of those will be varied in 4 different concentrations. Cross-talk
between the fluorophores will be analyzed, and the fluorescent signal will be compared to the commercial
spectrometer. The results indicate that the TR system has a better DR than the spectrometer, however,
the spectrometer’s SNR is superior. Analyzing the cross-talk between the fluorophores, it is found that stray
light appears in the last detector. Comparing the fluorescence signal to the spectrometer, the results are
promising, showing that the detection of fluorescence is comparable to the spectrometer. Except for the
detection of PpIX, where the TR system is not on par with the spectrometer. This work is one of many steps
toward a fully functioning TR system, that can change the way we detect GBM during surgery.

Resume
Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) er den mest ondartede og hypige primære hjernetumor. Standardbehan-
dlingen er resektion og kemoradioterapi, men ufuldstændig resektion af GBM kan resultere i tumorregrowth.
Flere metoder anvendes for at sikre fuldstændig fjernelse af tumorvæv. Fem-aminolevulinsyre (5-ALA) induc-
eret protoporfyrin IX (PpIX) er den mest almindelige optiske metode til at detektere GBM-tumorgrænser.
Denne metode er imidlertid ikke uden komplikationer. På grund af dette har Biophotinic-gruppen på Tyndall
National Institute udviklet et vævsregistrerings (TR) system, der bruger endogene fluoroforer til at detektere
GBM under operationen. I denne afhandling vil jeg karakterisere TR-systemets signal-til-støj-forhold (SNR)
og dynamiske område (DR), og sammenligne det med et kommerciel spektrometer. Derudover vil der blive
produceret 1024 fantomer, bestående af en absorber, en scatterer og 3 fluoroforer: nicotinamid adenin dinuk-
leotid (NADH), flavin adenin dinukleotid (FAD) og PpIX. Alle disse vil variere i 4 forskellige koncentrationer.
Krydstale mellem fluoroforerne vil blive analyseret, og den fluorescerende signal vil blive sammenlignet med
det kommerciel spektrometer. Resultaterne indikerer, at TR-systemet har en bedre DR end spektrometeret,
men spektrometerets SNR er overlegent. Ved analyse af krydstale mellem fluoroforerne konstateres det, at
vildfarent lys vises i den sidste detektor. Ved sammenligning af fluorescenssignalet med spektrometeret er
resultaterne lovende og viser, at detektionen af fluorescens er sammenlignelig med spektrometeret. Undta-
gen for detektionen af PpIX, hvor TR-systemet ikke er på niveau med spektrometeret. Dette arbejde er
et af mange skridt mod et fuldt fungerende TR-system, der kan ændre måden, vi detekterer GBM under
operationen.

Introduction
Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is considered a grade IV brain tumor by the World Health Organization. It
is the most malignant and common primary brain tumor, representing 16% of all cases. The median survival
time of patients undergoing treatment for GBM is 10 to 14 months with optimal treatment. However even
when patients undergo treatment, the survival rate after three years is only 3 to 5% (only sporadic reports of
survival exceeds five years). [1] The standard treatment for GBM is a combination of surgical resection and
chemoradiotherapy. Regardless of maximum surgical removal of the tumor and aggressive adjuvant therapy,
there is a very high risk of tumors locally recurring. One of the challenges of GBM treatment is incomplete
resectioning of the tumor. This is due to the highly infiltrative nature of GBM, making it almost impossible
on a cellular level to resection the tumor completely. [2]
Several techniques have been developed to maximize the surgical resectioning of GBM, all with its own ad-
vantages and disadvantages. These techniques consist of neuronavigation, intraoperative MRI, intraoperative
ultrasound, cortical functional mapping, and many different optical techniques. However one of the most
popular ones is the use of exogenous fluorophore Protoporphyrin IX (PpIX) induced by the precursor five-
aminolevulinic acid (5-ALA). The use of 5-ALA has been clinically approved for fluorescence-guided surgery
of malignant glioma in many countries. This technique has gained popularity due to the fluorescence of PpIX
which makes it possible to image intra-operatively and guide the surgeons in real-time when removing the
tumor. Moreover, with this method, the concern of brain shift is no longer a worry. [3] However, the use of
5-ALA, is not without complications. Firstly, intraoperative use of 5-ALA requires total darkness in the op-
erating room. This can make it difficult for the surgeons to visualize blood vessels and other neuroanatomical
structures during the surgery. [4] Secondly, 5-ALA has phototoxic side effects that last up to 48 hours after
administration. Therefore the patient must avoid exposure to sunlight or intense white light for 48 hours.
[5] In addition, 5-ALA is an oral drug, that must be administered 3-4 hours before surgery. This can be a
logistical issue for the patient and the medical staff and requires greater presurgical planning. The drug is
also expensive. The price of 5-ALA is around $2650 per patient. [4]
To overcome these limitations of 5-ALA, a new optical method has been developed at Tyndall National
Institute, by the Biophotonics group. The system is referred to as tissue recognition (TR) system. This
diagnostic method uses endogenous fluorophores to categorize healthy tissue from GBM. Using endogenous
fluorophores overcomes the limitations of 5-ALA. Since endogenous fluorophores are present in the body
without the use of drugs, it does not cost anything, and there is no need for administration before surgery.
Moreover, it is not phototoxic, removing the obstacle for the patient to avoid exposure to the sun and strong
white light 48 hours after surgery. This new system is also intraoperative, and non-sensity to light. Moreover,
the system is based on a handheld probe, making the system easy for surgeons to use. The system will be
described in Section 3.2.

1.1
The Scope of this Project
The overall aim of this thesis is to examine the TR system developed at Tyndall National Institute. This is
accomplished based on several objectives:
• An overview of existing literature. This focuses on the use of endogenous fluorophores for GBM surgery.
The literature will be compared to the TR system, highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of
the TR system.
• System characterization, based on the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and dynamic range (DR) of the TR
system. This will be compared to a commercial spectrometer, used as a benchmark for the system.
• Fluorescence phantoms. Three fluorophores will be examined. Cross-talk between them will be inves-
tigated, normalization of the fluorophores will be done, and at last, comparing the fluorescence signal
acquired from the TR system with the data obtained by the spectrometer.
This project was conducted in a collaboration between the Bioimaging group at the Department of Health
Technology at DTU and the Biophotonics group at Tyndall National Institute in Ireland. All the experiments
took place at Tyndall National Institute.

Contrast Mechanism in Light and Tis-
sue Interactions
This section will give a brief introduction to the theory of contrast mechanisms in light and tissue interactions.
I will start with a description of the relevant interactions between light and matter. Then I will present the
theory of contrast mechanisms relevant to my thesis.
2.1
Interactions of Light and Matter
This section will introduce the concepts of the interactions of light and matter, specifically absorption,
scattering, reflection, and refraction will be described.
2.1.1
Absorption and Scattering
Absorption of light takes place when electromagnetic energy is transferred from light to matter. Absorption
is a process where an atom or molecule is excited from a lower energy initial state (Si) to a higher energy final
state (Sf), due to an incoming photon, which is removed from a radiation field. This process can generally
be described by:
Si + photon →Sf
(2.1)

Radiative Relaxation
When a system absorbs an electromagnetic wave, the system is brought into an excited state. Afterward,
the system will go through a relaxation process. Relaxation can be divided into two categories: radiative
and non-radiative. The process of radiative relaxation emits one or more photons, whereas, non-radiative
relaxation will leave the system in its ground state, without emitting photons. [6] The former of interest in
this thesis.
Radiative relaxation can be separated into two categories: elastic and inelastic transitions. Elastic transitions
are when the emitted photon’s energy is identical to the photon energy initially absorbed by the system. This
type of transition can be spontaneous or stimulated. Spontaneous emission is when radiative de-excitation
of the system happens without any pre-existing radiation. The photon emitted spontaneously will have a
random polarization state and direction. Stimulated emission, in contrast to spontaneous emission, is when
a radiative de-excitation occurs due to an incident photon. In this case, the emitted photon will have the
same frequency, phase, polarization, and direction as the incident photon. Inelastic transitions occur when
the emitted photon’s energy is different from the photon energy of the absorbed photon, and are broadly
categorized as luminescence. Luminescence is when a system emits one or more photons due to the system
going from an excited state to a lower energy state. Luminescence can be divided into several subgroups.
The one of interest for this thesis is photoluminescence, specifically fluorescence. [6]
Fluorescence is a phenomenon that emits a photon after the system has undergone a non-radiative internal
conversion, forcing the photon into the lowest possible vibrational state. This transition is inelastic, resulting
in a longer wavelength. This process is visualized in Figure 2.3. The lifetime of this process can vary a lot
and is in the order of nano- to milliseconds. [6]

Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide and Flavin Adenine Dinucleotide
NADH and FAD are endogenous fluorophores. [13] They are present in the cell in an oxidized form (NAD+ and
FAD) or a reduced form (NADN and FADN2), which is used as electron donors and acceptors in the generation
of ATP in the cell and cellular respiration. Glycolysis and oxidative phosphorylation are important ways of
cellular energy metabolism which are related to ATP synthesis. NADH molecule is produced by glycolysis,
where a glucose molecule decomposes into pyruvate molecules, and two NAD+ molecules is reduced to two
NADH molecules as well as two ATP molecules. Moreover, the most effective way of producing ATP molecules
is through oxidative phosphorylation. In this process NADH is the primary donor, while FAD is the primary
electron and proton acceptor when it comes to the mitochondrial respiratory chain. This is important since it
is known that the majority of cancer cells will have an elevated glycolysis. [12] In addition oxygen deficiency
is common for malignant cancer tissue. [15] This is interesting, since in low oxygen conditions, cancer cells
will pass to glycolysis, skipping the reactions of oxidative phosphorylation where FAD is formed. Moreover,
since there is a need for ATP, the cell will switch to anaerobic glycolysis. This means that NAD+ will be
reduced to NADH-producing pyruvate and ATP. This results in a greater concentration of NADH, which
then leads to the fluorescence intensity increasing in cells with hypoxia. Moreover, FAD will have a smaller
concentration, due to the omitted reactions of oxidative phosphorylation. This makes it possible to analyze
the ratio of NADH and FAD, called the redox ratio. There are several methods to find this ratio, depending
on the subject which is being studied and the method of measurement. [12] Zhang et al. found that after rat
glioma cells were exposed to photodynamic therapy in vivo, the redox ratio, FAD/(FAD+NADH), increased.

5-Aminolevulinic Acid and Protoporphyrin IX
PpIX is an exogenous fluorophore. 5-aminolevulinic acid (5-ALA) is non-fluorescent, however, 5-ALA induces
the production of PpIX, which when illuminated with blue light will fluoresce red. The metabolic pathway
of 5-ALA to PpIX is illustrated in Figure 2.5. 5-ALA is administered exogenously, where action transporters
such as peptide transporters (PEPTs) 1 and 2 will introduce the 5-ALA into the cell. 5-ALA is also pro-
duced by the cells itself, this is called endogenous 5-ALA. More specifically it is produced by an enzymatic
reaction of 5-ALA synthase (ALAS) in the mitochondria from glycine and succinyl-CoA. In the cytoplasm,
porphobilinogen (PBG) is produced by having two 5-ALA molecules condensed by ALA dehydratase (ALAD).
Then via the porphobilinogen deaminase (PBGD) reaction, hydroxymethylbilane (HMB) is produced by the
condensation of four PBG molecules. Living cancer cells will then convert the HMB to uroporphyrinogen
III (UPG III). This is done by the uroporphyrinogen III synthase (UROS). The next step is for the UPG
III to be converted to coproporphyrinogen III (CPG III) by the decarboxylation of all acetate groups. By
ATP-binding cassette transporter B6 (ABCB6), CPG III is transported to the mitochondria. CPG III is
converted to protoporphyrinogen (Ppgen) in the mitochondria, and then to PpIX, which is fluorescent. Via
ferrochelatase, the fluorescent PpIX is converted to heme, which is non-fluorescent. [17] The reason why
PpIX, is more dominant in cancer cell, is due to a change in the metabolic activities of the enzymes in the
porphyrin-heme pathway. This means that the PBGD activity is increased, while the ferrochelatase activity
is decreased. [18] In addition, it has been found that the 5-ALA influx transporter PEPT1/2, is expressed in
a higher degree in cancer cells than healthy cells, resulting in the accumulation of PpIX in cancer cells. [17]

A study by Lin et al. [19] describes how an intraoperative and portable optical spectroscopy system can detect

they were targeting with this system, but specify that it has a wavelength of 460 was analyzed. With their

and 76% respectively. In this study, they also describe a new algorithm. This new algorithm achieves an
overall sensitivity of 89% and specificity of 76% for the system. An important thing to mention about this
system is the need to turn off overhead lights in the operating room, for the system to work. The surgical
lights was not turned off, however, they were pointing away from the patient.

Dynamic Range Protocol - Spectrometer
For the DR, a measurement at the maximum spectrometer intensity have to be recorded. Therefore the LED
is tuned up to a value, right below the saturation point. For this measurement, the integration time is set to
100 ms. This is so the spectrometer saturates at a lower LED voltage. When this measurement is captured,
the LED is turned off. 50 measurements are captured, without any light, at integration times of 10 ms, 50
ms, and 100 ms. To find the DR, the maximum intensity of the maximum recording is found. Moreover, the
σ of the dark measurements at the wavelength of the maximum peak of the LED is found for each integration
time. The maximum is then divvied with the σ of the dark measurements for each integration time. This
gives us the linear DR for each integration time. To find the DR in dB the equation 10 · log10(linear DR) is
used.
Dynamic Range Protocol - TR System
The maximum measurement of the TR system is needed to find the DR. For this measurement, the ND
filter is removed. This is so the TR system will saturate at a lower LED voltage. When this measurement
is captured, the LED is turned off. 50 measurements are captured, without any light, at an average of 1
measurement, 10 measurements, and 100 measurements. Only the channel of 470nm is used for the maximum
measurement and the dark measurements. Since only one measurement is captured, giving us the maximum
value, no processing is necessary. For the dark measurements, the σ is found at the wavelength of 470nm
measured at different averages. The maximum is then divided with the σ of the dark measurements for each
average. This gives us the linear DR for each average. The DR in dB is found in the same way as for the
spectrometer.

6.1
Comparison of Literature and the TR system
In this section, I will discuss the literature I have included in this thesis. I will compare the literature to the
TR system, and elaborate on the advantages and disadvantages of the different systems.
6.1.1
The Literature
The system which is closest to the TR system is the system by Lin et al. [19]. They use a high-pressure
nitrogen laser at 337nm for the fluorescence, which is close to the TR system’s LED at 340nm. Moreover,
they also use DRS, however, they use a halogen lamp, whereas the TR system uses the LDs and LEDs
themself. Lin et al. do not disclose which biomarkers they are targeting, but based on the wavelength they
are using and the fluorescence they are detecting, it is assumed that it is NADH. If this is true, I would have
expected the use of another light source, to target FAD, as it is the redox ratio of the two, that indicates if it
is cancerous tissue one is dealing with. [12] Like the TR system, it is intraoperative and can be used without
turning off the operation room lights. Lin et al. disclose the sensitivity and specificity of their system when
they apply their algorithm to the data. This is not comparable to my work, as no tests were conducted with
in vivo or ex vivo tissue samples.
The work from Marcus et al. [20] also uses a nitrogen laser at the wavelength of 337 nm. However, in this
case, it is a time-resolved, investigating the lifetime of 390 and 460 nm light. Like Lin et al. they do not
disclose which biomarkers they are interested in, but these wavelengths again resemble the excitation and
emission of NADH. [12] The lifetime of 390 nm is rather confounding since NADH has an emission maximum
of 470 nm which is not close to 390 nm. A theory could be, that they are trying to excite collagen, yet as
they do not disclose this, this is purely speculative. In addition to exciting 337 nm, I would have expected
the system to have an excitation light source around 370 or 450 nm as this is the excitation of FAD. [12]
NADH and FAD also have significantly different lifetimes, hence they should be easily separated. [12] Their
system cannot be used intraoperatively, which is a drawback since the surgeons will have to operate again
at a later point if they discover, unremoved cancer in the brain. In their first work, they do not mention the

6.1.2
Tissue Recognition System
The TR system used in this thesis has eight light sources ranging from 300 to 680 nm. These wavelengths
are optimal, based on the excitation and emission of the endogenous fluorophores investigated. Much of
the literature included uses NIR light. Based on my research, this will not excite endogenous fluorophores
like NADH, FAD, tryptophan, and collagens. Since these studies, do not include the biomarkers they are
targeting, it is very difficult to compare them to the TR system. An advantage of using NIR light is that the
light is attenuated less in this region (see Figure 2.1). However, we are interested in studying the superficial
tissue, hence penetration depth is not as relevant. If we were to detect deeper in biological tissue, using
visible light would be sub-optimal.
Comparing the TR system to Lin et al. [19] the TR system is more optimal as it can detect both NADH
and FAD. It is important to detect both NADH and FAD, as it is the redox ratio of the two, that indicates
if tissue is cancerous. [12] An addition to the TR system could be to include fluorescence lifetime as they

source lower than 300 nm.
The choice was in the end NADH and FAD for endogenous fluorophores, due to the excitation wavelengths
and the ease of use, as the liquid phantoms are simple to prepare. I also chose to use PpIX, which is an
exogenous fluorophore. This is because it is a common fluorophore used in GBM surgery, as described in
Section 1.
6.3.2
Cross-talk
The results from the analysis of the cross-talk between the different fluorophores turned out as I expected.
Especially for the illumination channel of NADH (340 nm) together with the detection channels of NADH
(470 nm), FAD (515 nm), and PpIX (680 nm). It is shown that no cross-talk occurs between these channels,
meaning that no stray light from the LED of 340 nm travels through the dichroic prism to the detectors for
the 515 and 680 nm. As for the illumination channel of PpIX (440 nm) together with the detection channels
of NADH, FAD, and PpIX, the results also appear promising. Since the slope of the curves for PpIX/NADH
and PpIX/FAD does not change. However, for the illumination channel of FAD (390 nm) together with the
detection channels of NADH, FAD, and PpIX, the results are less satisfactory. Especially with the FAD/PpIX
combination, where the light is too intense. A reason for this could be that light leaks from the illumination
source of FAD, through the dichroic prism to the detector of 680 nm.
As I point out in Section 6.3.2, the signal should be stronger with an increasing concentration of scatterer. This
is however not the case for the data presented in Figure 5.4. A reason for this error could be, the fluorophore
stock solutions which was mixed daily. This could have resulted in a variance between stock solutions that
otherwise should be the same. In hindsight, action should have been taken to ensure consistency.

and 5.6, curves have the same shape. This makes me believe, that the normalization of the fluorescent signal
should be tweaked. For Figure 5.7, no distinct trend can be observed, making me believe there is something
other than the normalization wrong. The error could lie in the quality of the dichroic prism. The detection of

in the spectrometer, does not interact with this dichroic prism.
6.4
Comments on the Tissue Recognition System
Two of the light sources and detector parings are not used in this thesis. Those are 300 and 590 nm. The
channel of 300 nm is not used intentionally, since previous studies have shown cross-talk in this detection
channel. [25], furthermore this LED is very weak, especially after coupling it to the fiber probe. However, if
cross-talk were not a problem, and the signal was stronger, this channel would be optimal for the excitation
of collagens. Moreover, if it was possible to find a light source with a shorter wavelength than 300 nm, this
would be a great channel to use for the detection of tryptophan. Based on my study of the fluorophores I
believe the light source of 590 is not required and does not serve any practical purpose for the TR system.
6.5
Future Works
If the system should be improved, I would recommend looking at the system’s chosen wavelengths and com-
pared them to the fluorophores which are being targeted. From a theoretical point of view, the wavelengths
in the TR system, make perfect sense (except for 590 nm), nevertheless, when the system is tested on human
tumors, these excitation and emission peaks might shift. Therefore, it is crucial to have the spectrometer,
which possesses a higher spectral resolution, as a reference. If the emission peaks are different in practice,
the spectrometer should detect this.
An improvement that could be made to the system in the future, would be to use fluorescent lifetime as a
contrast mechanism, as the lifetime of NADH and FAD, are significantly different. [12] This would, however,
require an optimization of the electronics used in the TR system.
Today, the detectors can sample 658
SPS, yielding a resolution of milliseconds. To resolve the lifetime differences of NADH and FAD nanosecond
resolution is required. However, if that is necessary, is also a question.
There is a problem with the dichroic prism and cross-talk between the channels. This problem needs to be
addressed in a future version of the TR system.

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to examine the TR system developed at Tyndall National Institute and compare
it to exciting literature.
When comparing the TR system to literature, focusing on endogenous fluorophores, I believe that the TR
system is superior, based on the choice of excitation wavelengths and detectors. It was difficult to compare
the TR system to the literature, since most of them, did not disclose what fluorophores they were targeting.
The examination of the system was done in two parts: 1) a system characterization, determining SNR and DR
of the system, and comparing it to a spectrometer, which serves as the benchmark for the TR system. 2) an
experiment of 1024 fluorescent phantoms, varying 5 parameters, the absorber, the scatter, and 3 fluorophores.
All of these 5 parameters were varied in 4 different concentrations. All of this work was done to determined,
the TR systems ability to detect fluorescence, and investigate if cross-talk between the fluorephores was a
problem. Detection of fluorescence by the TR system, was compared to a spectrometers capability to detect
the fluorescence.
The results from the work I carried out, ponits towards the TR system having better DR than the spectrom-
eter, while the spectrometes SNR is superior to the TR system.
For fluorescence detection, I show that the TR system do detect changes in fluorescence, when the fluorophore
concentration changes, specifically for NADH and FAD. These results was compared to the spectrometers
detection of fluorescence. The trends for the different curves looks very similar. This is a very satisfying
result, due to the spectrometer being the benchmark for the TR systems. The trends for PpIX does not look
as I would have expected. When comparing the PpIX data to the spectrometer, they look very diferent. Due
to this, I believe it is the dichroic prism, that introduces this error.
It is believed that the dichroic prism also introduced an error in the cross-talk between the fluorophores.
Especially for the illumination source of 390 nm, being detected at the detector of 680 nm.
The other
illumination/detection channel pairings, the cross-talk looks acceptable.
Based on my findings, I believe that the Biophotonics group at Tyndall National Institute, have created a
system that can detect endogenous fluorophores, like NADH and FAD, however, more resceach need to be
done, to test if this system also works outside the test tubes. Moreover, such errors as the cross-talk, and
the the detection of PpIX needs to be addressed.
