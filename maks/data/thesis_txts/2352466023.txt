Summary
There are many ways to prove that a cryptographic cipher behaves non ideally.
One method is integral cryptanalysis where we construct a set of plaintexts and
track how the property of the set propagates through diﬀerent rounds of the
cipher. Recently Todo [Tod15b] deﬁned new integral properties that can target
low degree S-boxes. Boura and Canteaut [BC16] deﬁnes these properties with
parity sets and show how they can target maximal degree S-boxes with speciﬁc
characteristics.
The goal of this thesis is to apply the theory of division properties and parity sets
on selected ciphers. We compute distinguishers on one of them: RECTANGLE
a lightweight cipher proposed by Zhang et al.
[ZBD+15].
We present two
low data distinguishers on 4 rounds that are more eﬃcient than the existing
4 round distinguisher. Furthermore we present a conditional distinguisher on
8 rounds with parity sets. We explain the condition and argue that the same
distinguisher is found by computing the complete parity set. It is shown how
the high complexity of computing the parity sets may be lowered. However,
we conclude that it is ineﬃcient to compute these large sets compared to for
example bit-based division properties. Furthermore, we ﬁnd several classes of
optimal 4 bit S-boxes that are vulnerable to an analysis with parity sets.
We analyze the lightweight cipher SKINNY by Beierle et al. [BJK+16] and ﬁnd
new integral distinguishers on 7, 8 and 9 rounds with division properties. We
give a preliminary analysis on the cipher SAFER by Massey [Mas93] and show
that modular addition may be target by parity sets.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The purpose of this thesis is to study new insights into integral cryptanalysis.
In integral cryptanalysis we construct a set of plaintexts with a given integral
property. The new insights give us more detailed properties and enable us to ﬁnd
better integral distinguishers for many ciphers. The concept of distinguishers on
cryptographic ciphers is a way to prove that the cipher does not behave ideally.
In linear and diﬀerential cryptanalysis we construct distinguishers that hold with
a probability p < 1. With integral cryptanalysis we construct distinguishers that
hold with probability p = 1.
1.1
Previous work
Integral cryptanalysis was formalised by Knudsen and Wagner [KW02] and
played a crucial role in the preliminary analysis of the cipher SQUARE [DKR97]
a predecessor of the cipher RIJNDEAL [DR13] which became the Advanced En-
cryption Standard. Todo [Tod15b] presents a new distinguishing property called
the division property. The division property formulates properties that are more
detailed than traditional integral properties. Very recently Boura and Canteaut
[BC16] formulated division properties by the notion of parity sets. They show
how to construct an 8 round distinguisher on the cipher PRESENT [BKL+07]
==PAGE:12==
2
Introduction
with parity sets. The division property and parity sets require a lot of com-
bined properties to hold all at once. This leads to high data and computational
complexity. The complexity is often a constraint as we show later.
1.2
Our contributions
The motivation behind this thesis is to give the reader an introduction in to the
new aspects of integral cryptanalysis by outlining where the properties by Todo,
Boura and Canteaut diﬀer from traditional integral cryptanalysis. We believe
that there has been a lack of transparency on the challenges in the practical
implementation of the analysis with parity sets. We seek to provide a better
understanding of these challenges and oﬀer solutions to them. Furthermore we
construct distinguishers on the cipher RECTANGLE with parity sets. One of
them is a conditional 8 round distinguisher that was found by computing only
part of the parity set in order to lower the computational complexity. We also
ﬁnd a class of strong S-boxes that are weak if we analyze the inverse S-box and
show the implications of this relationship. Furthermore we, study division prop-
erties on the lightweight cipher SKINNY [BJK+16]. They allow us to construct
some distinguishers with less data on 7, 8 and 9 rounds. However, we do not ﬁnd
a distinguisher for more rounds than the existing 10 round distinguisher. A pre-
liminary analysis of SAFER [Mas93] provides an analysis of modular addition
with parity sets. Finally, we give an outline of directions for further studies.
1.3
Structure
The structure of the thesis is as follows. In Chapter 2 we give a brief introduction
into the world of cryptography. We explain the aspects that the reader should
be aware of when reading the document. In Chapter 3 we provide the theory
behind traditional integral properties, the division property and parity sets. In
Chapter 4 we introduce the design of RECTANGLE. In Chapter 5 we give a
thorough analysis of RECTANGLE and present our results. In Chapter 6 we
analyze optimal 4 bit S-boxes, SAFER and SKINNY and give our results. In
Chapter 7 we reﬂect on further problems to be studied. Chapter 8 concludes
our studies.

Chapter 2
Cryptology
Cryptology is an area of mathematics where we develop and study systems for
secure communication in the presence of an adversary. The expanding need for
secure communication in the era of telecommunication has lead to increasing
interest in cryptology.
Generally there are two disciplines in cryptology: cryptography and cryptanaly-
sis. The former is an abbreviation for designing secure algorithms (also known as
cryptosystems) and the latter is the ﬁeld where we try to break these algorithms.
Of course it is clear that the evolution of cryptography aﬀects cryptanalysis and
vice versa. Cryptosystems take various forms: message authentication functions
and encryption ciphers. In this thesis we will only go into detail with encryption
ciphers, more speciﬁc block ciphers.
2.1
Block ciphers
In a cipher we encrypt messages known as plaintexts using a key to get a ci-
phertext. In order to get the plaintext from a ciphertext we must decrypt the
ciphertext with the same key. Encryption and decryption can mathematically
be considered mappings. These mappings should be unique for every key and
==PAGE:14==

such ciphers are called ideal ciphers. They are, however, infeasible to imple-
ment in reality. In general the mapping should implement Shannons principles
of confusion and diﬀusion. These concepts are described by Massey [Mas88]:
– Diﬀusion is spreading out the inﬂuence of one plaintext digit on other
plaintext digits.
– Confusion is how well statistical properties of the plaintext is transformed
such that they cannot be measured in the ciphertext.
We can deﬁne a cipher by the notion of the set of plaintexts: P, set of cipher-
texts: C and the set of keys: K. Then the cipher implements two functions the
encryption function
EK(P) := E(P, K) : P × K →C,
and the decryption function
DK(C) := E−1(C, K) : C × K →P,
which means P = DK(EK(P)).
Block ciphers are commonly constructed iteratively by means of a round trans-
formation over a given number of rounds.
A round transformation is a key
dependent transformation of the plaintext. Cryptographers often see the total
number of rounds as a security/eﬃciency trade oﬀ. The master key is usually
alternated iteratively from round to round using a key schedule. Let us denote
the user supplied key: the master key, k0, then the expanded key is denoted by

2.3
Cryptanalysis
In cryptanalysis, the cryptanalyst has the role of the adversary and it is assumed
that he knows everything except the secret key. We mainly talk about two types
of attack.
==PAGE:17==
2.3 Cryptanalysis
7
– Distinguishing attack, is when the adversary can distinguish between the
cipher and an ideal cipher.
– Key recovery attack, is when the adversary can learn a part of the key.
Of course distinguishing attacks are not in itself able to break a cipher, however
they can usually be used to launch a key recovery attack. Distinguishing attacks
are primarily found on hash functions to prove that they do not behave as
random oracles, which is a measure of strength in hash functions. All ciphers
are trivially broken if we try out every key in the keyspace. Such an attack
exists for all ciphers and is called a brute force attack or exhaustive search. For
a cipher to be considered secure it must be impossible within a feasible time to
retrieve the key.
In some applications the adversary is limited to a set of actions which limit the
strength of the attacks that may be performed. The attacks may be grouped
into the following categories:
– Ciphertext-only attack: The adversary is only able to observe ciphertexts
transmitted on the channel. (Weakest)
– Known-plaintext attack:
The adversary is able to observe ciphertexts
transmitted on the channel and the corresponding plaintext. (Weak)
– Chosen-plaintext attack: The adversary can choose a plaintext and get
the corresponding ciphertext. (Strong)
– Chosen-ciphertext attack: The adversary can choose a ciphertext and get
the corresponding plaintext. (Strongest)
Also the eﬀectiveness is a good measure of feasibility of an attack. Eﬀectiveness
can be measured according to the following units.
– Computational complexity: The amount of computational operations the
adversary needs to do. Is often measured by the number of encryptions
and is therefore comparable to brute force attacks.
– Data complexity: The number of plaintext/ciphertext pairs needed to ex-
ecute the attack.
– Memory complexity: The storage space required while executing the at-
tack.
==PAGE:18==
8
Cryptology
– Statistical complexity: The probability of a succesfull attack.
A cipher is considered broken if the key can be retrieved with less complexity
than a brute force attack. It is important to notice that attacks are often con-
sidered theoretical as they often require an infeasible amount of computational
power and/or data storage. The attacks we will present in this thesis will have
probability 1 and therefore we can be certain of their success.

Chapter 3
Integral cryptanalysis
The following chapter is used to introduce theory on integral cryptanalysis,
division property and parity sets. We give examples of the advantages of division
property and parity sets. During this and the following chapters we will use
hexadecimal and binary representations of set elements. We expect the reader
to be familiar with such representations.
Integral cryptanalysis was originally introduced by Daemen et al.
[DKR97],
as an attack on the block cipher SQUARE. Therefore it was known as the
SQUARE attack. SQUARE was designed to have good characteristics against
the most prominent attacks of that time: linear and diﬀerential cryptanalysis. In
diﬀerential cryptanalysis we observe the propagation of diﬀerences between pairs
of plaintexts. Integral cryptanalysis can be seen as an extension of diﬀerential
cryptanalysis. We construct a set of plaintexts and observe how the property
of the set changes through rounds of the cipher.
Integral cryptanalysis was
formalized by Knudsen and Wagner [KW02] and has been studied by Biryukov
and Shamir [BS01] on black box models of substitution permutation networks.
Let us start out by deﬁning the integral properties we can observe for a given
multiset. A multiset is a set that may contain copies of some of its elements.
Remember once again that these properties are the only properties we are in-
terested in - i.e. we do not worry about the individual elements of the multiset.
The size of the multiset is deﬁned as the sum of multiplicities for all elements.
==PAGE:20==

Definition 3.1 ([BS01]) A multiset M consisting of m bit words
1. Has property C (constant) if it contains an arbitrary number of repetitions
of a single value.
2. Has property P (permutation) if it contains exactly one of each one of the
2m possible values.
3. Has property E (even) if each value occurs an even number of times (in-
cluding no occurrences at all).
4. Has property B (balanced) if the XOR of all elements in the set is the zero
vector 0m.
5. Has property M (multiple) if it has either property P or property E.
6. Has property U (unknown) if we cannot determine if the set has any of
the properties above.
We will compare the integral properties to division properties and parity sets
in Section 3.1 and Section 3.2. The propagation of the deﬁned properties were
studied in [BS01] and we summarize the results in Corollary 3.2.
Corollary 3.2 ([BS01]) Let us divide the plaintexts of a set into k words.
1. Property C1...Ci−1PCi+1...Ck is preserved by a layer of arbitrary S-boxes
provided that the i-th S-box is bijective.
2. Property M1...Mk is transformed into property M1...Mk by a layer of bi-
jective S-boxes.
3. Property M1...Mk is transformed into B1...Bk by an arbitrary linear map-
ping on n bits, and by an arbitrary aﬃne mapping when the size of the
multiset is even.
4. Property C1...Ci−1PCi+1...Ck is transformed into property M1...Mk by an
arbitrary aﬃne mapping when the size of the multiset is even.
5. Property B1...Bk is transformed into property U1...Uk by a layer of bijective
S-boxes.
These properties allow us to evaluate the propagation through a substitution
permutation network such as AES which was done in [KW02]. Example 3.1 will
be used later to show how we can ﬁnd better distinguishers using the division
property and even better ones with parity sets.

3.1
Division property
A generalization of the integral properties in Deﬁnition 3.1 was published by
Todo [Tod15b]. The generalized properties are called division properties. With
division properties we are able to gain an advantage against low degree S-boxes.
Notice that the propagations given in Corollary 3.2 is assumed to be equal
for all S-boxes. The ﬁrst cryptanalytic attack on the full MISTY1 cipher is
presented by Todo in [Tod15a] using the division property. Todo is able to use
the low degree of S-boxes in MISTY1 and ﬁnds a distinguisher on 6 rounds.
We will introduce some of the ideas that are relevant in order to understand the
connection to parity sets. We will not give the necessary information to conduct
an analysis with division properties but only refer to [Tod15b].

determine the division property of a given multiset we need the bit product

Lin(S) is a measure for the resistance against linear cryptanalysis and Diﬀ(S)
is a measure for the resistance against diﬀerential cryptanalysis.

against algebraic attacks. Furthermore, they argue that the results ease the

ciphers often require an S-box with a compact algebraic normal form.

class and the results are given in Proposition 6.2.

Chapter 7
Discussion
In this chapter we comment on the open questions of this thesis and give direc-
tions for future work. The pros and cons of parity sets are discussed together
with the future of integral cryptanalysis.
Xiang et al. [XZBL16] compute integral distinguishers with bit-based division
properties that were introduced by Todo and Morii in [TM16]. The work of
Todo and Morii was limited by the complexity of computing the propagation
of bit-based division properties.
This problem is solved by formulating the
propagation with mixed integer linear programming as is done by Xiang et al.
We did not dive deep in to the content of [XZBL16]. However, it seems like
bit-based division properties are able to capture the same properties of the S-
box as parity sets. Xiang et al. analyze 6 ciphers including PRESENT and
RECTANGLE. Their results on the two ciphers are given in Table 7.1. The
results shows that our 8 round distinguisher probably holds if we computed
the complete parity set of RECTANGLE for 8 rounds. Furthermore, they are
able to extend both our distinguisher on RECTANGLE and the distinguisher
on PRESENT by Boura and Canteaut [BC16].
Since we do not compute the complete parity set we cannot check if there are
weight 1 elements that are missing from the parity set after 8 rounds. This
problem is among the many drawbacks of computing parity sets. Furthermore

computation time of parity sets. Another advantage of bit-based division prop-
erties is that they can be propagated easily through common operations such
as Copy, And and Xor which are needed to analyze Feistel ciphers. To the
knowledge of the author the only way to analyze a Feistel cipher with parity
sets is to consider the whole round transformation as an S-box. Depending on
the blocksize of the Feistel cipher it would require large computational eﬀort to
compute the corresponding VS(u).
It seems like there may be a connection between bit-based division properties
and parity sets. So despite the computational ineﬃciency of parity sets they
could become a valuable tool for evaluating the strength of an S-box against
bit-based division properties.
Integral cryptanalysis is a powerful tool but it seems like we need to ﬁnd better
properties in order to fully utilize the power of integral properties. The work
done by Todo and others seems to point towards a future with more detailed
properties. It seems like diﬀerential and linear cryptanalysis usually construct
distinguishers on more rounds than integral cryptanalysis.
One way to ﬁnd
integral distinguishers on more rounds would be to introduce probabilities on
integral properties as suggested in Section 6.3. However, it is not clear if such
probabilities can be found from the analysis in the appendix of [BC16].
7.1
Future work
We analyzed SKINNY in Chapter 6 and observed that the cipher performed
well against division properties but did not perform well against parity sets.
Therefore we believe that an analysis on SKINNY with bit-based division prop-
erties may give useful results. Furthermore, in light of our analysis of SKINNY
we also hope that there may be ways to introduce probabilities on the division
properties if we use the weight distribution of VS(u).
Propagation of parity sets still needs to be formulated for operations such as
Copy, And and Xor which would allow us to analyze ciphers in a diﬀerent

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This thesis shows how to ﬁnd integral distinguishers with parity sets. It was
shown how parity sets are based on division properties and how these two new
properties gives a better characterization of a set of plaintexts. The analysis
by Boura and Canteaut [BC16] of PRESENT was applied to RECTANGLE
[ZBD+15] though we were unable to compute the complete parity set due to
computational limits.
A distinguisher on 8 rounds RECTANGLE was found by computing a simpliﬁed
parity set.
However, it was argued that the distinguisher would hold if we
were able to compute the complete parity set.
Some new eﬃcient low data
distinguishers on 4 rounds RECTANGLE were also found. Results by Xiang
et al.
[TM16] shows that bit-based division properties are more eﬃcient to
compute and it seems like these division properties has the same advantages as
parity sets. No such link was shown and it is still an open question if this in
fact is true.
We also include studies of other ciphers. Those results of those studies are listed
here.
– Some classes of optimal 4 bit S-boxes were shown to be weak against parity
sets.
==PAGE:62==
52
Conclusion
– A class of S-boxes that are strong against parity sets but for which the
inverse S-boxes is weak was found.
– A preliminary study of SAFER [Mas93] showed that the modular addition
may be weak against parity sets. However, it seemed that an analysis
would require to many resources compute in the scope of this thesis.
– We were not able to reproduce the higher order integral on 10 rounds of
SKINNY [BJK+16] with division properties. However, we ﬁnd distinguish-
ers on 7, 8 and 9 rounds with less data than the existing distinguishers.
– An observation of the weight distribution in the VS(u) leads to an open
question on a way to introduce probabilities in integral cryptanalysis.
We conclude that parity sets are a powerful tool to asses the strength of an
S-box. They are, however, computationally ineﬃcient to compute and it seems
like the advantages of parity sets are shared by bit-based division properties.
On the other hand if is possible to introduce probabilities on division properties
as discussed in Section 6.3 then parity sets may become useful.
If a direct link between bit-based division properties and parity sets are found it
is currently much more eﬃcient to compute bit-based division properties on the
principles proposed by Xiang et al. [TM16]. Therefore it seems like bit-based
division properties are better than parity sets.

AES
The Advanced Encryption Standard [DR13] has a round function that consists
of the operations SubBytes, ShiftRows, MixColumns and AddRoundKey in the
given order. We assume that the reader is familiar with the standard and have
therefore chosen to not include details on the cipher.
We carefully construct a set of plaintexts in the following way: Let the ﬁrst byte
have the P (permutation) property, which means that each of the plaintexts’ ﬁrst
byte has one of the values from 0 to 28 −1 = 255. Let all other bytes hold the C
(constant) property. Then we obtain the 3 round distinguisher shown in Figure
A.1. The results from Corollary 3.2 can be used to check each operation.
– SubBytes transforms the individual byte properties.
– ShiftRows does not tranform any properties but only moves them around
– MixColumns works like a linear mapping on each column of the state
which is why the properties are aﬀected according to other properties in
the column.
– AddRoundKey does not tranform any properties and is therefore omitted
in the evaluation.
