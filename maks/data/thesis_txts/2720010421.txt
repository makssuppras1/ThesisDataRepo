Abstract
The phenomena causing an amplitude growth for an H-shaped bridge deck section with
chord-to-height ratio of 5 is known as torsional flutter. Its existence has from several studies
been found to depend on the reduced velocity, cf. (Malthe & Rønne (2017), Matsumoto
et al. (1992), Kubo et al. (1992) and Larsen (2002)). The mechanisms governing the
oscillation amplitude growth and limitation remains undetermined. It is the aim of the
present project to describe the mechanisms to gain a deeper understanding of the flow
phenomena.
The study uses a commercial, CFD software to simulate an H-shaped bridge deck section
undergoing a forced, torsional oscillation with a fixed amplitude.
Sub-, at critical and post-critical conditions for flutter based on previous studies are
simulated for a zero incidence, β = 0◦and yawed flow, β = 45◦. Despite a yawed flow,
the primary vortex is seen to propagate perpendicular to the bridge deck geometry. The
yawed and zero incidence flow therefore share the same mechanisms causing the flutter
phenomenon.
Two mechanisms are found to govern the transition from a stable condition to an unstable
condition. The mechanisms depend on the velocity of the primary vortex which is found
to scale linearly with the reduced velocity. At an unstable condition, the amplitude will
grow until a stabilizing effect overcomes the destabilizing effect. Using two-parameter
surface fit models, a new approximation for the critical point of flutter is estimated. The
models can be used to predict the limited cycle oscillation amplitude at a post-critical
reduced velocity.
The instability would still occur for small amplitudes but the starting conditions are
governed by different physics. For small amplitudes, the mechanics and onset of flutter
are Strouhal number dependent.
The primary vortex is found to show strong two-dimensional characteristics. Beneath the
primary and secondary vortex, three-dimensional effect appear as streaks of positive and
negative spanwise velocities.

Introduction
On the morning of November 7th 1940, the most infamous suspension bridge having
an H-shaped bridge deck section collapsed. The Tacoma Narrows suspension bridge
(TNB) collapsed during a storm with recorded wind speed reaching 19 m/s, cf. Ammann
et al. (1941). The average direction of wind was directly from south, approximately 45◦
with respect to the bridge, cf. Malthe & Rønne (2017). The bridge was known prone
to self-induced oscillations, thus having adopted the name, Galloping Gertie. Usually,
TNB experienced vertical oscillations with a frequency of 0.6 Hz caused by its long slender
body, cf. Modarres-Sadeghi (2021). However, on the day of the collapse, the vertical
oscillations changed to a twisting motion of 0.2 Hz. The motion had been described as a
single degree of freedom, torsional oscillation with amplitude of ±35◦and a center node at
quarter points, cf. Farquharson (1949). The incident had been filmed. The film showed
concrete dust at the leading edge of the bridge revealing an expanding vortex propagating
across the bridge deck. This leading edge vortex was believed to cause the instability
phenomenon called torsional flutter. The existence of the vortex and the onset of instability
on the bridge deck has been examined cf. (Scanlan & Tomko (1971), Larsen (2002)), but
mechanisms governing the oscillation amplitude growth and limitation remains unknown.
1.1
Previous Work
The investigations of an H-shaped bridge deck section is a thoroughly investigated problem
with wind tunnel experiments going back to Ammann et al. (1941) and Farquharson
(1949). It was assumed that inertia forces are dominating and the gravitational term was
insignificant due to torsional motion. Hence the phenomena should be Froude and Reynolds
number independent. The claim of Reynolds number independence was strengthened by
the presence of the sharp corners on the upstream and downstream vertical girders, cf.
Simiu & Scanlan (1996). Since the flow always separated, the existence of the primary
vortex was independent of inertial effects and thereby the Reynolds number.

1.1
Previous Work
29 January 2023
The experimental studies in a wind tunnel was carried out by, cf. Ammann et al. (1941)
with a wind perpendicular to the bridge deck section. The studies found the aerodynamic
damping would switch from positive to negative once the reduced velocity exceeded a
critical value. Above this critical value, a negative aerodynamic damping was found to
cause a diverging torsional motion. Extrapolating from the collected data, the studies
predicted a reduced velocity, which was approximately half of that recorded on the day.
The observed phenomena was initially concluded as cause of a periodic vortex shedding
from the wake exciting a torsional, natural frequency. Therefore it was thought to be a
resonance problem. However, as was already evident by early observations, cf. Ammann
et al. (1941), the aerodynamic damping was negative hinting at an aerodynamic instability.
In the case of forced resonance by a periodic excitation from the wake, the aerodynamic
damping would be positive and a lock-in phenomena would occur. For a self-induced
phenomena, the oscillator would be two-way coupling with the forcing flow resulting in a
flow-induced instability, cf. Jenkins (2013).
It was later shown by Billah & Scanlan (1991) that the torsional oscillation of the bridge
was out of sync with the von Kármán vortex street and Larsen (2000) found it would only
cause limited torsional oscillations of ±0.4◦.
What did however influence the oscillations of the bridge section was vortices created by
its own geometry from separations points as seen by flow visualizations by Kubo et al.
(1992) and Nakamura & Nakashima (1986). They found the motion of the model sections,
both rectangular and H-shaped, induced vortices causing aerodynamic excitation. The
oscillations were seen to be self-induced by the torsional motion. The mode of excitation
depended on the wind velocity.
Motion induced vortices have been found for H-shaped bridge sections, cf. (Malthe &
Rønne (2017), Chrisensen (2017), Komatsu & Kobayashi (1980)), rectangular and fence-like
sections cf. (Nakamura & Nakashima (1986), Nguyen et al. (2018)), π-shaped sections, cf.
Gao et al. (2020) and semi closed-shapes sections, cf. Zhu et al. (2020).
Nguyen et al. (2018) found for a rectangular cylinder with a chord-to-height ratio of five
( B
H = 5), the leading edge vortex was responsible for triggering the torsional motion. The
impingement of leading edge vortex on the surface close to the trailing edge caused a
moment acting in direction of motion, implying a negative damping.
The generation of the vortices from a separation layer on an H-shaped bridge deck section
was similar to a surface-mounted fence, cf. Orellano & Wengle (2001). From the separation
point, a 2D roll-up of the shear layer occured due to the presence of a recirculation zone
behind the fence. From the recirculation bubble, vortices were shed due to a shedding
type instability. The shear layer type instability, called Kelvin-Helmholtz instability, too
was present, but through Proper Orthogonal Decomposition with Galerkin Projection was
shown not to participate to the vortex shedding, cf. Orellano & Wengle (2001).

Lin et al. (2005) and Nguyen et al. (2018) found that turbulence intensity had an insignif-
icant influence on the bridge flutter phenomenon but could suppress the vortex street.
The insignificance of turbulent fluctuations could explain the lagging influence of choice of
turbulence model found by Chrisensen (2017).
Malthe & Rønne (2017) found for an H-shaped bridge section, the critical reduced velocity
was increased with a factor
1
cos(β) depending on the yaw angle, β. This was in accordance
with the independence principle, cf. (Bourguet et al. (2014) and Lou et al. (2016)).
Matsumoto et al. (1992) found that the chord-to-height ratio of H-shaped bridge sections
was important. If B
H > 3.4, torsional oscillations began at a certain critical wind velocity.
The motion diverged, since the separation bubble reattached to the surface and convected
downstream as a motion-induced vortex. The excitation energy is supplied near the leading
edge and the downstream, with respect to rotational center, surface. They found B
H ≈5.5
to be the most unstable case. For B
H > 10, the torsional mode became stable and 2-DOF,
coupled oscillations occurred known as classical flutter.
Kubo et al. (1992) found in case of different approaching wind velocities, the separated
flow took equal time to reach the position on the surface corresponding to equal phase lag.
Thus the vortices travelled across the sections with a constant velocity proportional to the
free stream velocity, U ∗
V ∝aVr, a = 0.21. This observation was named the traveling time
constant theory of separated flow entertainment.
Komatsu & Kobayashi (1980) found the leading edge vortex to build up behind the leading
edge and synchronized with the motion of the geometry. The study was limited to vertical
motion but the described phenomena shared similarity to torsional motion. The vortices
produced a reduction of pressure on the surface, generating an aerodynamic exciting
force from the pressure difference to the vortex core. During one cycle the vortex will
propagate down the deck and grow in size until it is shed from the trailing edge into the
wake. The ranges of velocity for self-sustaining oscillations occurred only when the section
had initially been set in motion. Therefore the study found that Kármán Vortex street
could act as a trigger for the oscillations. The self-induced oscillations were found to be
10 times greater than the resonant induced oscillations. Komatsu & Kobayashi (1980)
removed the trailing edge vertical girder from an H-shaped bridge deck section and found
the oscillation growth to still occur at non-resonant wind velocities.
Larsen (2016) expanded on the observations of torsional motion which lead to a simple
theory about the expanding vortex. Malthe & Rønne (2017) found in accordance with
the theory, that the recorded damping of the bridge motion depended on the how far
the vortex had travelled across the deck. The damping was found to be a function of
non-dimensional reduced velocity above 10◦amplitude. Chrisensen (2017) simulated an
elastic response of the bridge and found similar critical reduced velocities for flutter. Both
studies found the oscillations to have a limited oscillation amplitude.

1.2
Project Specification
The aim of this project is to delve deeper into the understanding of the mechanisms
governing the self-induced flutter by H-shaped bridge sections. The investigation is carried
out by simulating the forced oscillation of an H-shaped bridge deck with a fixed amplitude
for a zero incidence and yawed free stream velocity. Simulation of a forced response
is advantageous as the oscillations are known to reach a limited cycle oscillation and a
simulation of an elastic response is very computationally demanding. The simulations will
be carried out using a commercial software, STAR-CCM+.
The findings of the project seek to relate the aerodynamic response on the bridge deck
section to the primary vortices propagating across the geometry. The relation lead to a
description of the mechanisms governing torsional flutter. In literature, torsional flutter
is also known as torsional galloping, cf. Modarres-Sadeghi (2021). The study seeks to
investigate the applicability of the simple analytical model of Larsen (2016) since previous
studies have found the underlying theory to sufficiently describe the phenomenon.
The project will be organized as the main objectives are introduced.
• A convergence study of mesh and time discretization based on the findings of
Chrisensen (2017). Both periodic convergence and convergence criterion of the
moment coefficient based on the findings of Larsen (2002) is required for sufficient
convergence.
• Description of the common properties observed across all reduced velocities. The
observations should comply with previous findings.
• Description of the mechanisms governing both the destabilization and stabilization
of oscillation amplitude
• Estimation of the critical point of flutter from a necessary condition for oscillation
growth.
• Investigation of the starting conditions of flutter for small amplitudes of motion.
• Investigation of the influence of yaw on the onset of flutter mechanisms and the
spanwise characteristics of an H-shaped bridge deck section.
The objectives will be accomplished by simulating three reduced velocities at three fixed
oscillation amplitudes for a zero incidence flow. Further three reduced velocities are
simulated at two fixed oscillation amplitudes for a 45◦-yawed flow. Additional simulations
are carried out at a critical reduced velocity with small amplitudes of motion.

2.2
Turbulence Model
29 January 2023
The aim of computational fluid mechanics is to properly resolve the Navier-Stokes equa-
tion. There are six equations to solve for a compressbile flow and four equations for an
incompressible flow. One from conservation of mass and three from Navier-Stokes equation.
Direct solution of the Navier-Stokes equations are primarily applicable for laminar flows,
where many analytical solutions are known.
The Navier-Stokes equations are numerically computed by discretizing the equations in
space and time. Depending on the accuracy and stability, different schemes can be chosen.
Fluid flow is computed by discretizing the domain into a mesh and for each time step
solving the Navier-Stokes equations on each element in the mesh. When the flow becomes
turbulent, the required resolution of mesh scales as N 3 = O(Re9/4). Direct solution of
Navier-Stokes equations are therefore only feasible for limited flow problems.
2.2
Turbulence Model
Turbulence models are used to reduce the computational effort needed to properly resolve
turbulence characteristics in fluid flow. A turbulent fluid flow is characterised by high
unsteadiness, three-dimensionality and containing many scales of coherent vortices called
eddies. Eddies are continuously broken down into smaller eddies described as the Energy
Cascade, cf. Kundu et al. (2012). How much of the Energy Cascade that is modelled
depends on the turbulence model.

2.4
Bridge Flutter
Several types of flutter has been observed in many physical systems. The most well-known
type of flutter is the classical, 2-DOF flutter existing due to the coupling of rotational
and vertical eigenmodes, cf. Simiu & Scanlan (1996). However, the flutter observed for
a B
H = 5, H-shaped bridge deck section is described as a 1-DOF, torsional flutter. In
literature, torsional flutter is equivalent to the phenomena known as torsional galloping,
cf. Modarres-Sadeghi (2021).

1-DOF torsional flutter is experienced under two different conditions. For nonlinear
characteristics of lift in the vicinity of stall and for structures undergoing strongly separated
flows, cf. Simiu & Scanlan (1996). The latter is connected to suspension bridges. In the
literature, this nonlinear aerodynamic phenomenon is often treated as linear because:
1. The supporting structure is treated as linearly elastic and its action dominate the
response.
2. The starting condition for flutter has a small amplitude which separates stable and
unstable regimes.
Based on the equations of motion for a bridge deck, see section 2.3, a linear expression
derived by Scanlan & Tomko (1971) has been extensively used to predict the onset of flutter.
It is a coefficient based expression, where the A∗
2 coefficient is particularly important.
When this coefficient changes sign from negative to positive, the torsional motion becomes
unstable as it acts on a damping term. Thus this coefficient predicts negative damping.
Since the condition for 1-DOF flutter is the strongly separated flow, it appears for many
different geometries as mentioned in section 1.1. For the geometries having a sharp corner
leading edge, the point of separation does not depend on the Reynolds number, Simiu
& Scanlan (1996) as it always exist. vortices are created by a roll-up of the separated
shear layer due to the recirculation zone present by the vertical girder, cf. Komatsu &
Kobayashi (1980). The separation bubble for a non-oscillating deck will shed vortices due
to a shedding instability, cf. Orellano & Wengle (2001). Through experiments, cf. (Malthe
& Rønne (2017), Komatsu & Kobayashi (1980)) and numerical simulations, cf. (Chrisensen
(2017), Larsen (2000)), it has been found, that the 1-DOF flutter needs both an initial
perturbation and a critical reduced wind velocity in order to exist. At the critical reduced
wind velocity, the creation of vortices is synchronized with the movement of the bridge, cf.
Kubo et al. (1992) and will reach a limited oscillation cycle with an amplitude depending
on the reduced velocity. Larsen (2016) has formulated a simple theory explaining the
vortex interactions with the bridge oscillations called The Expanding Vortex Theory.
2.5
The Expanding Vortex Theory
Two vortex models are presented in Larsen (2016). A Travelling Point Vortex Model and
The Expanding Vortex Model. The concept of the first model does not fit observations and
is therefore omitted. The Expanding vortex model introduces the following assumptions:
• The upwind corner of the vertical girder is the primary source of circulation, eq. (2.31)
and a continuous supply of circulation in time is needed.
• Vortex has a diameter roughly equal to the vertical height once fully developed.
• A vortex induced lift force is acting on the deck section at the centre of the vortex.

2.6
Von Kármán Vortex Street
From experiments, cf. (Ammann et al. (1941), Farquharson (1949), Nakamura & Nakashima
(1986), Matsumoto et al. (1992)) and simulations, cf. (Chrisensen (2017), Larsen (2000))
it has been seen, that a vortex street will develop from the trailing edge of the H-shaped
deck section. The influence of the vortex street on amplitude growth has been deemed
insignificant, cf. (Scanlan & Tomko (1971), Larsen (2000)).
The vortex street has been argued to be important for the existence of the bridge flutter,
cf. Komatsu & Kobayashi (1980). The perturbation of the trailing vortices was found to
cause small, limited oscillation amplitudes, cf. Larsen (2000), and could potentially offer
enough perturbation to initialize bridge flutter. The initial perturbation could equally stem
from turbulence causing random fluctuations. The vortices at the trailing edge are created
similarly to those at the leading edge. Due to the sharp edge corner on the vertical girder,
the flow separates creating a shear layer. For a starting flow, two voriticies with equal size
and strength will be created by roll up of the shear layer from each side of the vertical
girder. Which of the vortices shedding first depends on perturbation by an instability,
cf. Williamson (1996). From the wake of the trailing edge, shed vortex, a recirculation
zone is created by the swirling motion of the vortex. This results in a suction, therefore
a roll up of the shear layer of the opposite side of the vertical girder. The vortex gains
strength and will similarly detach into the wake and the event will repeat for the opposite
side of the vertical girder. The frequency of perturbation causes a lock-in phenomena.
When the shedding of the vortices is such, that it coincides with the natural frequencies of
the structure the amplitude of oscillations will increase. The magnitude of amplitude is
dependent on the damping and stiffness of the system.

The following mesh settings have been used:
• Surface remesher: Improves overall quality of an existing surface and optimizes it
for the volume mesh .
• Trimmed Cell Mesher: Uses a predominantly hexahedral template mesh with
minimal cell skewness. The cells are aligned with the flow, increasing accuracy
and convergence rate. For a periodic boundary condition, it creates an interface
with matching cells for accurate data transfer. It is recommended for external
aerodynamics, cf. CD-adapco (2022).
• Prism Layer Mesher: Generates orthogonal prismatic cells next to wall surfaces
or boundaries. Prism layers are necessary to resolve the near wall flow due to steep
gradients in the viscous sublayer, properly determine wall forces, stagnation and
separation of fluid flow. Reduces numerical diffusion near the wall and greatly
enhances accuracy as a result.
For the prism layer mesher, 10 prism layers with thickness of 0.125 relative to base size
has been chosen. The resulting amount of modelling depends on the non-dimensional wall
number, y+ and will be investigated during convergence analysis in section 4.

Spatial Model
All three spatial components will be simulated. It is assumed, that the flow is incompressible
as the flow is Ma < 0.3, no shocks occur and no heat transfer is simulated. From the
incompressible flow assumption, constant density often follows. Fluid density is invariant
throughout the continuum when compressibility effects are low and temperature effects
are negligible.

For an incompressible or mildly compressible flows flow, the segregated flow solver is
recommended. A pressure-velocity coupling algorithm is employed. The mass conservation
constraint on velocity field is fulfilled by solving a pressure-correction equation from
continuity equation and moment equation. A prediced velocity field is sought that fulfills
the continuity equation, achieved by correcting the pressure CD-adapco (2022). Therefore
the governing equations are solved in an uncoupled manner. For the segregated flow solver,
the Semi-Implicit Method for Pressure-Linked Equations (SIMPLE) algorithm is used. In
the algorithm, the continuity and Navier-Stokes equation are solved in a semi-implicit
manner. Convergence is achieved by iteration using corrections to velocity and pressure
variables.
3.3.2
Temporal Model
The flow is unsteady since the bridge section is undergoing a forced movement and is
shedding vortices. A temporal solver is needed. The Courant or the Courant-Friedrichs-
Lewy (CFL) number is a non-dimensional number representing how many grid points
a fluid element moves in one time step and is therefore very important to keep low for
temporal accuracy:

∆x
(3.5)
The requirements to the Courant number depends on the temporal method. If the method
is explicit, then the Courant number has to be less than 1 and is recommended for laminar
or inviscid regime models. A less strict criteria is necessary for an implicit method, thus
it is the one utilized. The implicit method utilizes inner iterations to reach equilibrium
at each time step. The best practices for implicit method is a Courant Number of about
5-6 as a limit. A 2nd-order temporal discretization scheme is used, as it provides more
accurate results for hexagonal grids although it is not as robust as a 1st-order scheme.
3.3.3

The simulated fluid flow is in a gaseous state and the bridge section is solid. The solid

Even though separation points and vortices can exist in laminar flow, the flow around a
real bridge deck section will be turbulent. Therefore a turbulence model is needed. The
influence of choice of turbulence model will be investigated during convergence analysis,

The first model will be the RANS, k −ω SST, model section 2.2.1. The Shear Stress

The RANS models will simulate the mean flow and fully model the turbulence.
The second model is the LES with WALE Subgrid Scale model, section 2.2.2. The fidelity

modelled scales will be. This turbulence model will result in approximately 80 % resolved

4.1
Numerical Schemes and Error
Errors occur due to discretization. The rate at which the truncation error goes towards
zero as grid spacing goes towards zero depends on the order of truncation error O(∆xn).
If it does so, the scheme is consistent, cf. Bingham et al. (2020). At each finite volume
(cell), the momentum equation is solved using numerical schemes. Special treatment is
needed for boundaries and pressure gradient discretization. The convective flux term is
discretized using a 2nd order upwind scheme, also called Linear Upwind Difference Scheme.
The scheme is 2nd order accurate, thus formally having a convergence rate of 2nd order,
O(∆x2). It introduces linear interpolation of cell values on either side of upstream or
downstream face. According to CD-adapco (2022), the order of accuracy of the diffusion
flux is also of 2nd order. A consequence of using an upwind scheme is the potential,
unnaturally fast decay of turbulent kinetic energy. The convective scheme is not used for
LES simulations, where in stead a Bounded Central-Differencing scheme is used.

4.1
Numerical Schemes and Error

Depending on local conditions, either central differencing is mixed with a second order
upwind scheme or a first upwind difference scheme is used. It provides a compromise
between accuracy and robustness. The accuracy depends on local conditions and the favor-
able scheme is automatically chosen. An Upwind Blending factor of 0.15 is recommended,
CD-adapco (2022).
The choice of scheme also influences the amount of numerical diffusion introduced. Its effect
is a smearing of discontinuities and steep gradients in finite volume convection schemes.
An upwind scheme will always have more diffusion than the true solution, especially if the
flow is not aligned with mesh lines. A central difference scheme is prone to dispersive errors
and stability problems, therefore the blended scheme is useful to combat both weaknesses.
At each time-step, the solution requires information from a previous time. Dependent on
the order, n, of scheme, n amount of previous (or future) time-steps are needed. The chosen
scheme is a 2nd order scheme, called a Backward Differentiation Formula, CD-adapco
(2022). It is less stable than a 1st order scheme due to increased dissipative errors but is
more accurate. The error of temporal discretization stems from the size of time step and
is investigated using the Courant Number.
Error and discrepancies from reality is expected when using turbulence models due to the
chaotic nature of turbulence. The less modelled flow, if the discretizations are of good
quality, the less error. Ideally a DNS simulation is used, but is far too computationally
expensive. A RANS simulation is the least computationally expensive due to the less strict
requirement on mesh refinement, but it is expected to yield greater discrepancies than a
good quality LES simulation.
Additional errors or deviations could come from a simplified geometry but would not
influence the general flow characteristics. The simplified geometry is necessary for a reduced
computational effort. Round-off errors are unavoidable in numeric computations but will
not be the greatest error contribution. The residuals in each cell represents the degree to
which the discretized equation is satisfied, cf. CD-adapco (2022). A normalized residual
monitor is generally used to monitor the behavior of the solver. Ideally, convergence should
occur at each time-step however the convergence criterion is arbitrary and chosen by the
user. As a rule-of-thumb, a three-order-of-magnitude drop is possibly acceptable for each
time-step, cf. CD-adapco (2022).

5.3.1
Sub-Conclusion
Two mechanisms govern the transition from a stable condition to an unstable condition.
The first mechanism is the reduction of moment arm during the returning movement.
An increasing unbalance occurs on the upstream surface between the addition of energy
during the first quarter of motion and the removal of energy energy during the second
quarter motion. The mechanism is most important as the bridge deck is approaching peak
pitch and just after it has reached peak pitch. The first mechanism on the downstream
surface becomes increasingly important for an increasing reduced velocity. When the
displacement of the primary vortex is greater than half-chord during a half-period, the
primary vortex again adds energy to the movement. The mechanism is most important
when the bridge deck has crossed zero pitch. The second mechanism is the lift difference
on upstream top and bottom surface due to a newly created vortex on one side and the
previous vortical structures on the opposite side. The mechanism is only important at
zero crossing. The lift difference is low, when the displacement of the primary vortex is
low. When the displacement increases as a function of the reduced velocity, a recirculation
zone is growing upstream from the secondary vortex. Their combined interaction results
in an adverse pressure gradient on the surface. A high pressure difference occurs on the
upstream surface mitigating vortex lift, increasing lift unbalance. When the displacement
of primary vortex is greater than half-chord during a half-period, the adverse pressure
gradient is shifted towards rotational center and its effect becomes increasingly small.
These observations can be seen in the pressure field at T ∗= 1, fig. 5.15.
5.4
The Stabilization Effect of 1 DOF, Torsional Flutter
The driving mechanism for destabilizing the oscillation amplitude is position of the primary
vortices during a period of motion. This theory is supported by the previous knowledge
about the relation between reduced velocity and vortex core velocity (Kubo et al. (1992),
Larsen (2002), Malthe & Rønne (2017)). The previous studies also found for a critical
reduced velocity, the amplitude would reach a limited cycle oscillation (LCO). This
indicates, that the mechanism for instability must have an amplitude dependency. The
phenomena has been found to be caused by an induced lift on the downstream surface. The
lift is generated by a pressure difference between the top and bottom downstream surface
side of the deck section, fig. 5.13. See also appendix C.1 for additional maximum pitch
simulations. Depending on the reduced velocity, the limiting lift will behave differently.
A new primary vortex is always created on the upstream deck surface that is returning
towards zero crossing after having reached peak pitch. On this side, the previously fully
developed vortices have been washed off, leaving a low pressure zone due to free-stream
exposure on the downstream surface. This is a common property for all reduced velocities.
The pressure zones are illustrated in fig. 5.15.

The transition between timing of limiting lift is hypothesised to either be smooth or
discrete. The theory behind a smooth transition stems from the position dependency.
The primary vortex travels increasingly farther than half-chord during a half-period, cf.
fig. 5.15 . The negative pressure will increasingly influence the limiting lift as it influences
the pressure difference between top and bottom. This effect can be seen in the interval

The theory behind a discrete transition comes from the complex nature of a vortex system.
Once the primary vortex has travelled a sufficiently longer distance than the half-chord
corresponding to a specific reduced velocity, the covering mechanism will no longer induce
a limiting lift. In stead the wake entering mechanism governs the stabilizing effect.
No matter the choice of theory, the stabilizing effects become increasingly strong as the
amplitude of oscillations increase until an equilibrium is reached between the destabilizing
and stabilizing contributions. This is investigated by varying the maximum pitch for a

5.4.1
Sub-Conclusion
The stabilizing effects are governed by a single mechanism depending on the magnitude of
reduced velocity. In the sub- and just above critical regime, the stabilizing effect stems
from the high pressure zone by the covering effect from the fully developed primary vortex
on one side. On the other, a low pressure zone due to free-stream exposure, cf. (Vr = 2.12
and Vr = 3.54, fig. 5.15). The resulting pressure difference induces a lift in the direction
opposite to the torsional movement thus removing energy, cf. fig. 5.13. This effect is
strongest at zero crossing, cf. fig. 5.9.

It was the primary interest to investigate the compliance for the primary vortex, as it was
found to drive the instability, hence the choice of investigating the upstream surface. The

yawed flow, can be predicted with the cosine law. This implies, that for an H-shaped
bridge deck section, the influence of spanwise velocity is negligible. The governing velocity
component is the velocity in the streamwise direction, parallel to a zero incident flow.
The same mechanisms of flutter will govern a yawed flow. For a similar yawed to zero
incidence reduced velocity, the limited cycle oscillation amplitude will be equivalent as the
sign change in the work coefficient is expected to occur at an equivalent condition.

structures. The disruption is increasingly equal with an increasing reduced velocity. For
the yawed flow, the primary vortex at T ∗= [0.64 : 0.85] is seen to curl up and propagate
downstream following the geometry in an equal manner to the zero incidence flow. This
observation is the primary cause of an H-shaped bridge deck section following the cosine
law. The primary vortex is created in an equal manner, independent of the spanwise
velocity and dependent on the velocity component perpendicular to the bridge deck section.
Torsional flutter will occur at an increased reduced velocity predicted by the cosine law.

5.7.1
Sub-Conclusion
Visualizing the vortical structures with an isosurface and plotting vorticity components on
the isosurface shows the two-dimensionality of the primary vortex. The secondary vortex
shows more three-dimensional properties.
Comparing the isosurfaces of a yawed to zero incidence flow, the primary vortex is created
in an equal manner, independent of the spanwise velocity and dependent on the velocity
component perpendicular to the bridge deck section. The primary vortex propagates
perpendicular to the bridge deck geometry without influence from the spanwise velocity.
If the relationship between the perpendicular velocity component and the frequency of
oscillation is kept, the yawed flow can be predicted according to the cosine law.
Beneath the primary and secondary vortex, streaks of positive and negative spanwise
velocities are present seen from a contour plot on a plane close to the deck surface. The
streaks are dragged across the deck surface and their strength grow with an increasing
reduced velocity. The streaks are suppressed by an increasing free-stream spanwise velocity
as seen for the yawed case.

Future work
For small amplitudes, the physics of the flow are not governed by flutter mechanisms.
The primary vortex is not created on the upstream surface caused by torsional motion.
In stead, the shear layer from the upstream separation point attaches and curls up on
the downstream surface. The vortex rapidly reaches the downstream vertical girder,
suppressing downstream shear layer creation. At a critical reduced velocity, the initial
amplitude growth is attributed to a coinciding lift history frequency at a factor higher
than the torsional frequency. When the amplitude of motion is sufficiently high, the flutter
mechanisms begins to govern the torsional motion growth. For future work, additional
simulations with amplitudes of motion lesser than θmax = 10◦can determine when the
governing mechanisms change.
The primary vortex is shedding due to an underlying shedding instability. The instability
is dependent of B
H, cf. Matsumoto et al. (1992). The study found, that a reduction in
ratio lead to an increase in shedding frequency. When B
H < 3.4, the torsional oscillations
would not occur. The shedding instability might be dependent on a Reynolds Number
based on the height of the upstream vertical girder, ReH = HU
ν . However, (Nakamura &
Nakashima (1986), fig. 8 and fig. 10) found the Strouhal number for a varying vertical
girder height to be constant. This was obtained by non-dimensionalizing with the chord
for a varying vertical girder height. They additionally found the characteristic frequency
to show no different result, if the downstream vertical girder is removed. They concluded,
that both cases, with and without downstream vertical girder, was susceptible to vortex
excitation. In future work, the Reynolds number dependence on shedding instability
should be investigated. The initial conditions for amplitude growth might be dependent
on the Reynolds number.
It is unknown, whether the torsional flutter mechanisms will occur, when the downstream
vertical girder is removed. Komatsu & Kobayashi (1980) found heaving motion would
still occur at non-resonant wind velocities when the downstream vertical girder was
removed. The experimental study was limited to heaving motions, thus its influence on
torsional motion needs further investigations. As previously mentioned, Nakamura &
Nakashima (1986) found an H-shaped bridge deck section with chord-to-height ratio of 5
to be susceptible to vortex excitation. The excitation occurred both with and without the
downstream vertical girder. Simulations at a critical reduced velocity with the downstream
vertical girder removed should be conducted.

7
Conclusion
Two mechanisms have been found to govern the transition from a stable condition to an
unstable condition. The mechanisms depend on the displacement of the primary vortex
during a half-period of motion in accordance with the predicted theory of Larsen (2016).
The first mechanism is the reduction and sign change of moment arm during the returning
motion. The second mechanism is the pressure difference and vortex lift unbalance on
upstream top and bottom surface. It is the combined effect of the secondary vortex and the
recirculating, upstream, cut-off shear layer which creates a high-pressure zone mitigating
vortex lift. This effect is strongest when the displacement of the vortex is approaching
half-chord during a half-period.
At an unstable condition, the amplitude will grow until a stabilizing effect overcomes the
destabilizing effect. For sub- and just above critical regime, a pressure difference on the
downstream surface stems from the sheltering effect from an upstream primary vortex on
one side surface and free-stream exposure on the other. The pressure difference results in a
lift always acting opposite direction to the torsional movement. In the post-critical regime,
the limiting lift occurs after zero pitch crossing when the primary vortex enters the wake.
Two condition criteria combined with two-parameter surface fit models are used to predict
the critical point of flutter. The models can predict the limited oscillation cycle amplitude
at a resolved reduced velocity.
The starting conditions at a critical reduced velocity is governed by different physics. The
shedding frequency based on the lift history shows a Strouhal number significantly different
than the motion based one. The instability occurs since the direction of peak lift for a
critical reduced velocity act in the direction of torsional motion.
Comparing the iso-surfaces of a yawed to zero incidence flow, the primary vortex is created
in an equal manner, independent of the spanwise velocity. Thus the physical properties
and onset of flutter of a yawed flow can be predicted with the cosine law. It requires
the condition, that the corrected reduced velocity from a yawed flow results in the same
reduced velocity as a zero incidence flow.
Three-dimensional effects appear beneath the primary and secondary vortex as streaks
of positive and negative spanwise velocities. The streaks are suppressed by an increasing
free-stream spanwise velocity as seen for the yawed flow.
