Abstract 
Fresh waters cover a very small part of the Earth – less than 1 %. Nevertheless, they are life sustaining 
habitats to 6 % of all described species. Unfortunately, the pressure on those important habitats and their 
biodiversity is increasing due to stressors like habitat degradation, exploitation, pollution, and climate 
changes. As the pressure on biodiversity increases the need for quick and efficient monitoring tools for 
freshwater organisms becomes urgent. Traditional monitoring methods are time-consuming, inefficient and 
sometimes invasive to the target organisms. Detection of DNA excreted from organisms into the 
environment (environmental DNA) has shown great potential as a method for biomonitoring during the last 
decade. 
In this study we used an eDNA metabarcoding approach on water samples from streams located in Nature 
Park Åmosen (Western Zealand, Denmark) in order to investigate the fish diversity. The objective was to 
determine the presence of fish species and compare the results to a list of species obtained by traditional 
catch and survey methods over many years. Furthermore, we investigated whether there was a significant 
difference between sampling sites located upstream and downstream of lake Tissø based on species 
composition in each sample.  
The main part of the water samples were taken at seven different sites at three different dates in a four 
month period (February-May, 2018). The water was filtered and eDNA was extracted from the retained 
organic material. Subsequently, PCR and library building was executed in order to prepare the samples for 
paired-end sequencing in an Illumina MiSeq sequencing platform. Finally, the sequences were assigned 
back to species.  
In total 21 species were detected by the eDNA approach, while 27 species had been detected in the same 
area by traditional survey methods. However, two of the species detected by traditional methods were 
only detected in private ponds and not in the stream system and three species have not been observed 
since the beginning of the 1900s. 
The analysis comparing samples from sites located upstream of Tissø and downstream of Tissø based on 
species composition, revealed a tendency towards a separation between these areas, however, this was 
not significant. Three-spined stickleback (Gasterosteus aculeatus), bleak (Alburnus alburnus), and ruffe 
(Gymnocephalus cernua) were only detected in the Tissø inlet and the downstream sites which could 
indicate a greater similarity between Tissø inlet and the downstream sites than between Tissø inlet and the 
other upstream sites. 
The present study demonstrates the potential of eDNA sampling and metabarcoding as an efficient 
monitoring tool for freshwater habitats. We managed to detect almost all the expected fish species known 
to occur in the survey area. Based on this study, we consider that an eDNA approach is a valid alternative or 
supplement to traditional survey methods.

Introduction 
Freshwater habitats cover less than 1 % of the Earth’s surface and yet they are inhabited by and supportive 
to 6 % of all described species (Dudgeon, Arthington et al. 2006, Strayer and Dudgeon 2010). Fresh waters 
are complex environments influenced by upstream and downstream networks, catchment areas and the 
surrounding land. They are often delimited environments, which make them especially vulnerable to 
external threats, because many organisms have a restricted, local range.   
Freshwater environments have great value as a natural resource, which has made them highly impacted 
through time and today human activity poses a sever threat to freshwater ecosystems. Pollution, habitat 
degradation, alteration of the environment, introduction of non-native species, exploitation and climate 
change are some of the large stressors to freshwater ecosystems (Dudgeon, Arthington et al. 2006). Due to 
the interface between the habitat itself and its surroundings, the complex impact from human activities, 
and the large number of stakeholders having some sort of interest in the activities, it is a challenging task to 
conduct proper management of freshwater ecosystems and their biodiversity.  
As the pressure on biodiversity increases the need for quick and efficient monitoring tools for freshwater 
organisms becomes urgent. A method that in the last decade has shown increasing potential for that 
purpose is detection of environmental DNA. 
   
The present study 
In 2015, a camera trap project was established in Nature Park Åmosen as collaboration between the Nature 
Agency in Western Zealand and the Natural History Museum of Denmark. The project was an attempt to 
register the elusive Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra). The species was believed to exist in a very small population 
in Western Zealand, but the proofs have been few (Søgaard, Wind et al. 2015). The year after the setup of 
camera traps, an otter was detected and since then it has been filmed frequently in the same area along 
with a long row of other mammals and birds (Statens Naturhistoriske Museum 2018). The success of 
detecting one of Denmark’s most elusive animals and the wildlife in general woke the interest of doing a 
more thorough survey of the wildlife in Nature Park Åmosen. The aim of this thesis was to do eDNA 
metabarcoding in order to investigate the fish diversity in the streams of Nature Park Åmosen. Field work 
for sample collection and subsequently laboratory work and data analysis has been conducted. We did an 
analysis in order to see if upstream and downstream sampling sites of lake Tissø could be distinguished 
based on species composition. Furthermore, the results from the eDNA analysis were compared to 
historical fish data provided by the National Fish Atlas, in order to assess if eDNA detection can be a reliable 
alternative to conventional catch and survey methods. In addition, the potential of the eDNA method as a 
more integrated management tool is discussed.     
 
Nature Park Åmosen and the fish diversity  
Nature Park Åmosen (Western Zealand, Denmark) was officially established in 2014. The area is 
approximately 8000 hectares and extends from the Great Belt in west to the village Undløse in east 
(Schmidt 2017) (Figure 1). The central parts of the nature park are the two large lakes Skarresø and Tissø 
and the two bog areas Lille Åmose located between Skarresø and Tissø and Store Åmose located southwest

of Skarresø. The landscape of the nature park shows large variation and varies between forest, bog, 
agriculture and meadows – all connected by the stream systems Åmose stream and Halleby stream. 
The bog areas are of great interest to archaeologists because they contain some of the most well preserved 
Stone Age findings in Europe (Matthiesen and Jensen 2005). These findings have in many years been 
threatened due to a comprehensive drainage conducted in the 1960’s. This was done in order optimize the 
area for agriculture. Drainage of the area meant that degradation of the vulnerable Stone Age items 
increased rapidly. Besides that, the landscape and the diversity of habitat areas changed, which had 
negative consequences for the flora and fauna in the area. In the recent years restoration projects have 
been conducted. The aim is to reestablish the original wet environment and raise the water level in order 
to protect the cultural heritages and improve the habitats for a large range of organisms.

Approximately, 80 % of Nature Park Åmosen overlaps with Natura-2000 areas, which means that these 
areas contain certain habitat types or species that Denmark is particularly obligated to preserve and protect 
according to the Habitat Directive of EU. These areas are selected with particular reference to the presence 
of the spined loach (Cobitis taenia), the Desmoulin's whorl snail (Vertigo moulinsiana), the great crested 
newt (Triturus cristatus), the Eurasian otter (Lutra lutra) and the significant large number of resting and 
breeding birds (Schmidt 2017). 
Throughout time, a significant freshwater fishery has been conducted in the two large lakes Skarresø and 
Tissø (Schmidt 2017). Species such as pike (Esox Lucius), pike perch (Sander lucioperca), bream (Abramis 
brama), and eel (Anguilla anguilla) have been caught.   
An updated list of registered fish species in the sampling area has been provided by the Danish national 
mapping project – the National Fish Atlas. 27 different species has been observed in the area over time 
(Appendix 1). However, the latest observation of the European river lamprey (Lampetra fluviatilis), the 
European brook lamprey (Lampetra planeri), and the burbot (Lota lota) goes back to the beginning of the 
1900’s and have not been observed since.  
The spined loach is a rare species in Denmark and Åmosen is one of the few places where it can be found. 
Today it is found in stream systems on Zealand, Funen, and Lolland-Falster. In Åmosen the density of the 
species has been estimated to be relatively low compared to other areas (Møller 2012a). The spined loach 
requires moderate current conditions, sand bottom for hiding and weed for breeding, where fry can hide 
and grow up. Approaches such as drainage, straightening of streams and extreme weed cutting are big 
threats to the species and have been conducted in many areas during the last century including Åmosen. 
Earlier the species was classified as vulnerable, but today the level is raised to the “least concern” category 
on the red list of Denmark. Nevertheless, it is not threatened it is not common though and Denmark is 
obligated to do an effort to secure its presence in the future.     
In December 2017, a large release of rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) from a marine fish farm in the 
Great Belt occurred near the inlet to the river system of Åmosen (Palm and Madsen 2017). Rainbow trout is 
not a native species in Danish waters. It was imported to Denmark 100 years ago from North America in 
order to be used in aquaculture and have since been farmed on land and offshore. Due to release from fish 
farms, the species can be found in almost any stream that connects to a fish farm. Rainbow trout that 
escape from offshore aquaculture also migrate into streams (Rasmussen 2012). Even though, the species is 
widespread it has not succeeded in establishing a sustainable spawning population in the wild in Denmark. 
Spawning occurs but fry is rarely found and because of that rainbow trout is not considered as an invasive 
species. In other places around Europe, more permanent self-sustaining populations of rainbow trout have 
been recorded (Rasmussen 2012, Stanković, Crivelli et al. 2015).  
In Denmark, the large escapes of rainbow trout that occur regularly cause great concern among anglers and 
other stakeholders. The large number of rainbow trout that suddenly invades the stream systems is 
thought to damage the spawning areas of native trout and threat eggs and fry  of the native trout by 
predation (Palm and Madsen 2017). The presence of rainbow trout in the wild in Europe has shown to 
induce several negative impacts on native populations of fish. Hybridization with other congeneric trout, 
competition for space and food, predation, introduction of diseases and habitat alteration are mentioned 
as negative impacts caused by rainbow trout (Stanković, Crivelli et al. 2015). Despite the concerns and the

Background on environmental DNA 
Environmental DNA  
Environmental DNA (hereafter eDNA) refers to DNA material that can be obtained directly from an 
environmental sample such as ice, sediment, air or water without getting into direct contact with the 
source organism (Rees, Maddison et al. 2014, Thomsen and Willerslev 2015). All organisms leave tracks of 
DNA to their surroundings in the form of excreted cells from for instance skin, urine, hair or fecal matter 
and this is what is utilized for eDNA detection. By analyzing an environmental sample it is possible to detect 
the presence of DNA from organisms of interest and thereby get a presumption of their presence.  
eDNA detection was first used as a tool in microbiology. Some bacteria cannot be cultured in laboratories 
so in order to get a more real picture of the community of bacteria as it occurs in nature, soil was 
investigated with an eDNA approach (Ogram, Sayler et al. 1987). It succeeded and in the last decade, eDNA 
detection has been a field of growing interest in ecology, too, in order to detect macro-organisms. The 
method has successfully been applied in a variety of environmental systems and used for detection of a 
variety of organisms (Thomsen and Willerslev 2015, Cristescu and Hebert 2018).  
The scientific literature points out that the eDNA method is cost and labour cheap compared to traditional 
survey methods. Less man-hours are needed in the field for sampling compared to traditional surveys, the 
equipment is relatively easy to handle and require a minimum of training and the amount of data achieved 
by this method is large. The method has also shown to be non-invasive to the organisms because no direct 
contact is necessary, which will often be the case when using traditional survey approaches (Davy, Kidd et 
al. 2015, Sigsgaard, Carl et al. 2015, Wilcox, McKelvey et al. 2016). These features has made eDNA 
detection a welcome alternative in the work of biodiversity research and monitoring. 
Another feature of interest regarding eDNA is that several studies have found evidence for a positive 
correlation between eDNA quantity and species biomass (Pilliod, Goldberg et al. 2013, Eichmiller, Bajer et 
al. 2014, Yamamoto, Minami et al. 2016). This means that the abundance of a target species potentially can 
be estimated based on the amount of eDNA detected in a sample. This connection between amount of 
eDNA and biomass has great potential in management and conservation matters e.g. calculation of invasion 
of certain species, rates of extinction or other matters of interests. Yet caution has to be taken because 
studies also have shown that many factors such as life stage of the organisms, feeding conditions, 
environmental conditions and season, all impact the DNA excretion rate of the organisms. If these 
parameters are not taken into account it can skew the biomass estimate (Maruyama, Nakamura et al. 2014, 
Klymus, Richter et al. 2015, Buxton, Groombridge et al. 2017).  
The collection of eDNA samples in aquatic environments is normally done by filtering a suitable amount of 
water through a filter with an appropriate pore size (Rees, Maddison et al. 2014). The organic matter is

then captured by the filter and stored for further analysis. The amount of water that passes through the 
filter depends a lot on the condition of the water like the content and size of organic matter and other stuff 
making the water turbid. Normally, one should strive for a sampling volume of water at 1-2 L. Another used 
method uses ethanol to precipitate the nucleic acid in the water during centrifugation (Hinlo, Gleeson et al. 
2017). Using this method only requires a small volume of water – typically 15 ml.  
The most common ways to store water samples and at the same time minimize degradation of DNA 
material in the samples are to freeze them or keep them in ethanol until further treatment can be 
conducted. The next step in the process is to extract the DNA from other cell material in the samples. For 
this purpose commercial extraction kits can be used. A number of manufactures produce different kind of 
kits ready for use (Rees, Maddison et al. 2014). Next, the DNA material is amplified by polymerase chain 
reaction (PCR) and later sequencing can reveal the DNA content of the samples. The DNA sequences can 
then be compared to a reference sequence database e.g. the one provided by NCBI in order to connect the 
extracted DNA sequences to species, genus or family level.  
eDNA can be analyzed in different ways. Species specific detection is an approach where samples are being 
analyzed for one or few specific organisms (Thomsen and Willerslev 2015). This is done by amplifying 
extracted eDNA from environmental samples by using species specific primers that only attach to unique 
DNA sequences for that specific organism of interest. This has been done several times especially in the 
search for rare and invasive organisms (Ficetola, Miaud et al. 2008, Thomsen, Kielgast et al. 2012a, 
Takahara, Minamoto et al. 2013). Another option is to do multispecies detection also called metabarcoding. 
This approach will be described in more details later in this thesis, but overall metabarcoding makes it 
possible to detect a large range of organisms from one environmental sample without prior knowledge of 
which organisms that actually are present (Thomsen and Willerslev 2015). The primers that are being used 
for this purpose are universal primers and target a variable region in the mitochondria. This approach 
makes it possible to target DNA from different species. When the sequenced DNA is matched with a 
reference database it is possible to identify a large range of organisms.  
 
eDNA in freshwater systems 
Some of the first eDNA studies done in freshwater had the aim to detect the presence of invasive or rare 
species. Ficetola et al (Ficetola, Miaud et al. 2008) successfully managed to detect the presence of the 
invasive species American bullfrog (Rana catesbeiana) from water samples collected from ponds in France. 
Thomsen et al (Thomsen, Kielgast et al. 2012a) showed that a diversity of rare and threatened freshwater 
species including fish, amphibians, crustaceans, insects and mammals was able to be detected based on 
eDNA obtained from water samples. The ability to detect the presence of a species even at low densities 
increased the expectations that eDNA detection could become a significant tool in freshwater conservation 
and management work.  
Studies have been conducted in lakes, rivers, streams, ponds, wetlands, and artificial containers and a large 
range of organisms have been identified – in addition to those already mentioned above, reptiles, mussels, 
and plants have also been successfully identified in freshwater environments (Scriver, Marinich et al. 2015, 
Thomsen and Willerslev 2015). The eDNA tool has therefore been given credit for success in a large 
diversity of both habitats and organisms.

Besides detection of freshwater species, studies have also investigated if freshwater samples can be used 
for detection of terrestrial animals since many animals are in contact with the water or live in the 
catchment area. Deiner et al (Deiner, Fronhofer et al. 2016) found that eDNA samples analyzed by a 
metabarcoding approach revealed a large range of eukaryote families representing both aquatic and 
terrestrial species known to live in the catchment area of the investigated river. Ushio et al (Ushio, Fukuda 
et al. 2017) also demonstrates that universal mammal primers successfully can be conducted for detection 
of mammals from forest pond water. A similar study has been done where universal primers for birds 
successfully have been developed and tested to see if birds can be detected in environmental samples from 
ponds (Ushio, Murata et al. 2018). These studies emphasize that the eDNA metabarcoding approach not 
only can be used in biomonitoring work for a single habitat but also can reflect the diversity of organisms 
across the land-water interface.           
For eDNA detection to become a significant tool for monitoring and research of freshwater macro 
vertebrates, the method has to be competitive to traditional sampling and survey methods. Traditional 
survey strategies such as direct observation, use of traps and different fishing technics are challenged by 
time consuming processes, selectivity of different equipment, and the need of specialists to identify caught 
species and different life stages of those. Mesocosms studies have shown that the eDNA detection method 
are capable of detecting all taxa in experimental tanks (Kelly, Port et al. 2014, Evans, Olds et al. 2016). 
Several studies have tested these results in natural freshwater environments by comparing results from 
eDNA detection and traditional survey methods such as gillnet fishing and electrofishing with historical lists 
of species known to occur in the research area (Civade, Dejean et al. 2016, Lim, Tay et al. 2016, Shaw, 
Clarke et al. 2016, Valentini, Taberlet et al. 2016, Nakagawa, Yamamoto et al. 2018). The general 
conclusions are that eDNA detection equal or outperform the conventional survey methods and is more 
efficient in detecting the vertebrate diversity including rare species.      
 
Detectability of eDNA in freshwater  
The detectability of eDNA in freshwater environments depends on four factors – production, dispersal and 
transport and degradation of the DNA material (Strickler, Fremier et al. 2015). 
The production of eDNA, meaning the DNA excretion rate from organisms, seems to vary due to several 
factors. It has been shown that juvenile sunfish excrete more eDNA material per bodyweight than adults, 
but the release rate of DNA was 3-4 times higher in the adults than in the juveniles due to their larger body 
size (Maruyama, Nakamura et al. 2014). Klymus et al (Klymus, Richter et al. 2015) found a corresponding 
result with a positive relationship between amount of eDNA and biomass. They also found that feeding 
could increase the excretion rate tenfold. Season and activity level of the organisms do also impact the DNA 
release rate (de Souza, Godwin et al. 2016, Buxton, Groombridge et al. 2017).  
Transport of eDNA material in freshwater is an area of investigation only few studies have been dealing 
with and they have primarily been conducted in lotic ecosystems. It has been demonstrated that the 
concentration and the flux of eDNA originated from carp decreased exponentially with distance 
downstream from the point source (Nukazawa, Hamasuna et al. 2018). Other studies have just investigated 
the distance of detectability of eDNA for different species. Pilliod et al (Pilliod, Goldberg et al. 2014) found 
that eDNA from the stream-dwelling Idaho giant salamander (Dicamptodon aterrimus) only was detectable

within the first 5 meters downstream from where the organism was located. In another study, Deiner et al 
(Deiner and Altermatt 2014) were able to detect the two research organisms Daphnia lingispina and Unio 
tumidus at a distance of approximately 10 kilometers downstream from the lake outflow where the 
organisms lived. Furthermore, Civade et al (Civade, Dejean et al. 2016) found a detection distance for fish at 
two to three kilometers, which corresponds very well to results found by Nukazawa et al (Nukazawa, 
Hamasuna et al. 2018), who found that eDNA entering the river via  effluent from a carp farm could be 
registered 3 km from the point source. These different results are obviously influenced by the different 
experimental setups, different target organisms and different physical conditions in the environment, but it 
gives a clue of the complexity that prevails when investigating highly dynamic environments such as 
streams and rivers. 
Degradation determines the persistence of eDNA in the environment and have been a subject of great 
interest. Studies show that eDNA degrades exponentially with time (Strickler, Fremier et al. 2015, 
Yamanaka, Motozawa et al. 2016) and that eDNA in freshwater environments persists from less than one 
day to approximately three to four weeks, which gives a reasonable estimate of current existing organisms 
in the area (Dejean, Valentini et al. 2011, Barnes, Turner et al. 2014, Pilliod, Goldberg et al. 2014). Many 
factors such as water temperature, pH, radiation, microbial activity and the interaction between those 
factors have been investigated in connection to eDNA degradation. Strickler et al (Strickler, Fremier et al. 
2015) found that degradation was lowest at cold temperature, low radiation and slightly alkaline 
conditions. Higher degradation was observed at high temperatures, moderate radiation and neutral pH. For 
the scientists, the higher degradation at neutral pH was a surprise. The explanation is that besides the 
physical factors affecting the degradation of eDNA directly, they also impose an indirect threat in terms of 
microbial activity. The neutral pH most likely favored microbial growth that led to higher degradation. In 
general, the presence of microbes is a significant contributor in the degradation processes and their 
enzymatic efficiency depends on the physical conditions such as temperature and pH.   
A significant challenge regarding detectability of eDNA is that unwanted and inhibitory compounds often 
will be an unavoidable catch when collecting water samples for eDNA detection. The most common 
inhibitors in freshwater samples are humic substances and phenolic compounds (Wilson 1997). Co-
extraction of inhibiting compounds when filtering water samples is a well know challenge when doing 
eDNA detection (McKee, Spear et al. 2015, Sigsgaard, Carl et al. 2015). These compounds, often referred to 
as inhibitors, can prevent the extraction and amplification processes from working properly and 
consequently lead to false negative results (Stoeckle, Beggel et al. 2017). Inhibition has been shown to be 
especially pronounced during autumn where large amounts of dissolved organic matter enters the lakes 
and streams due to leaf litter (Jane, Wilcox et al. 2015, Eichmiller, Miller et al. 2016).     
 
False negative results and false positive results  
Like for any survey method, sources of errors need to be considered carefully during the survey process. 
For the eDNA approach one have to be aware of particularly two types of possible bias: false negative 
results and false positive results. 
False negative results occur when a present organism is not detected in the procedure (Rees, Maddison et 
al. 2014, Lahoz‐Monfort, Guillera‐Arroita et al. 2016). These kinds of errors might occur when the amount 
==PAGE:12==
Master’s thesis 
Malene N. Månsson 
01.10.2018 
11 
 
of eDNA from a particular target organism is very sparse and miss detection. One way to overcome this 
problem is to make sure to amplify the present DNA sufficiently. This can be done by increasing the number 
of sampling replicates and the number of runs of PCRs. Another source to this kind of problem is as 
previous mentioned co-extracted inhibiting compounds that inhibit the amplification process and thereby 
mask the presence of target DNA (Thomsen and Willerslev 2015). Also use of primers with an insufficient 
affinity to some kinds of DNA sequences might course false negative results.    
The detection of an organism at a site where it is not present is called a false positive result. Typically, these 
kinds of errors arise during the laboratory process where contamination, PCR errors or sequencing errors 
occur (Thomsen and Willerslev 2015). Another source to false detections can be found in the environment. 
Humans, predators, or other animals moving from site to site are likely to transport eDNA from one place 
where the potential target organism is present to the survey area where the viable organism itself might 
not be present (Merkes, McCalla et al. 2014, Goldberg, Turner et al. 2016, Sigsgaard, Nielsen et al. 2017). 
Sewage water from urban areas can also contribute to detection of false positive results. Nakagawa et al 
(Nakagawa, Yamamoto et al. 2018) found 11 marine fish species in the upper-middle part of a large river 
system many kilometers away from the sea and the native habitats of the detected marine species. The 
authors assumed, since all the species were commonly used in Japanese food, that the eDNA originated 
from sewage water from nearby urban areas. These kinds of errors skew the picture of species distribution. 
Resuspension of sediment from the riverbed or the lakebed can also course false positive errors. eDNA is 
better preserved in sediment than in water, which means that organisms that were once present at a 
particular site potentially still can be detected as being present if eDNA from the sediment is released to 
the water column (Turner, Uy et al. 2015). Therefore, it is important to be aware of resuspension of 
sediment during sampling events for instance coursed by heavy rain, strong currents or human disturbance 
of the riverbed. 
Preventing false positive results require great awareness during the laboratory processes in order to 
prevent cross-contamination. Typically, control samples/blank samples are added at each step during the 
laboratory process in order to be able to identify possible contamination.  
To prevent both types of errors, choosing primers with a sufficient specificity, able to target the specific 
organism or group of organisms of interest is very important. Using primers with a low specificity in species 
specific detection can result in amplification of non-target DNA and when doing multispecies detection 
some primer sets might favor some kinds of DNA fragment leading to ‘masking’ of low-abundance species 
(Rees, Maddison et al. 2014, Evans, Olds et al. 2016).    
In order to control for false negative and false positive errors more studies advocate for running occupancy 
models as a more integrated approach when working with eDNA (Ficetola, Taberlet et al. 2016, Lahoz‐
Monfort, Guillera‐Arroita et al. 2016). Eliminating these kinds of errors will make sure that managers do not 
take action or fail to act based on untrue detections and thus waste valuable resources.       
 
Metabarcoding 
High throughput sequencing (HTS) also known as next-generation sequencing is a sequencing technology 
that makes it possible to produce a vast amount of DNA data simultaneously. This is a great improvement 
==PAGE:13==
Master’s thesis 
Malene N. Månsson 
01.10.2018 
12 
 
from the formerly used Sanger sequencing, where only one DNA fragment could be sequenced at a time. 
Consequently, sequencing was a very expensive and time consuming process. Now, that mass DNA 
sequencing is a possibility due to HTS, more data can be produced in a relatively short amount of time and 
at low cost.    
eDNA metabarcoding is the combination of HTS and the ability to extract DNA fragments from 
environmental samples and connect those to taxonomic levels (Thomsen and Willerslev 2015, Deiner, Bik 
et al. 2017). The method is considered as a fast and cost-efficient way to survey species richness in natural 
communities (Thomsen, Kielgast et al. 2012b, Valentini, Taberlet et al. 2016).  
Fish specific primers for metabarcoding 
Five different universal primer sets have been used for eDNA metabarcoding of fish in previous studies. All 
of them target regions in the mitochondria. Evans et al (Evans, Olds et al. 2016) developed two primer sets 
for Actinopterygii (Ac12S and Ac16S). Riaz et al (Riaz, Shehzad et al. 2011) designed vertebrate-specific 
primers that later were used by Kelly et al (Kelly, Port et al. 2014) in order to investigate marine bony fishes 
in a mesocosm aquarium tank by a metabarcoding approach. They were able to identify seven out of eight 
species of bony fish to taxonomic family or genus level, but the primers failed to detect elasmobranches. 
MiFish primers, designed by Miya et al (Miya, Sato et al. 2015), are assessed to show excellent coverage for 
the target group (95.8 %) and excellent taxonomic resolution on species (86.8 %), genus (93.8 %), family 
(98.8 %) and order (100 %) level (Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018). The primers were not able to detect 
elasmobranches; however they were able to detected non-target organisms such as spotted dolphins 
(Stenella attenuata). Valentini et al (Valentini, Taberlet et al. 2016) developed the “Teleo primers”, which 
also show excellent coverage (98.05 %) and taxonomic resolution (species (81.5 %), genus (90.6 %), family 
(98.9 %) and order (100 %)) (Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018). However, the resolution for some families with 
many closely related species (e.g. Cyprinidae and Gadidae) was not ideal, because identification at species 
level could not be reached. The fragments amplified by the Teleo primers are significantly shorter than 
those amplified by MiFish primers. Based on that fact, the authors behind the Teleo primers highlight the 
trade-off between optimization of the robustness of the amplification and the loss of taxonomic resolution. 
Since the eDNA method deals with fragmented DNA more of the extracted DNA can be amplified if the 
primers are able to attach to smaller fragments. On the other hand the risk of not being able to distinguish 
two species based on a small fragment is higher than if a larger fragment is amplified (Deiner, Bik et al. 
2017).         
The process of sequencing  
A number of different HTS platforms for sequencing is available on the market (Reuter, Spacek et al. 2015). 
Illumina machines are one of the dominating produces and have also been used in this thesis study. Thus, 
the following description of a sequencing procedure is based on the use of Illunima’s HTS platforms. 
After extraction of DNA from environmental samples and amplification by PCR the first step of Illumina HTS 
workflow is to prepare a sequencing library. The aim is to prepare the DNA fragments so that they can be 
sequenced on the Illumina platform, and for this purpose the fragments need a certain structure. There are 
a number of different ways to prepare samples for sequencing but common for all of them is that they add 
adapters to the DNA fragments (Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018). The fragment will be flanked by two different 
adaptors called P5 and P7 (Figure 2). These adaptors consist of different parts. One part is a primer binding 
==PAGE:14==
Master’s thesis 
Malene N. Månsson 
01.10.2018 
13 
 
site and one part is complementary to the flow cell oligonucleotides of the sequencing machine. The P7 
adaptor also contains an index/barcode for recognition, normally consisting of 8-12 bases (Illumina 2017). 
 
 
Figure 2: Structure of a DNA fragment that can be sequenced on Illumina platforms (Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018). 
 
The flow cell is a glass slide within the Illumina machine with a varying number of separated lanes 
depending on the sequencing platform. These lanes are covered with two types of oligonucleotides that are 
complementary to the respective P5 and P7 binding sites on the adaptors attached to the DNA fragments. 
When the library is complete, it is loaded into a flow cell. Next, amplification of the fragments are 
conducted via a process called bridge amplification. Each fragment hybridizes to a complementary oligo on 
the flow cell surface and bends over, creating a bridge between the two types of oligos. Polymerase 
generates the complementary strand resulting in a double stranded bridge that subsequently is denatured 
creating a forward and a reverse strand. This process is repeated a number of times. Each original fragment 
will be copied approximately thousand times forming one cluster of identical forward and reverse DNA 
fragments (called cluster generation). After the amplification the reverse strands are cleaved and washed 
off leaving only the forward strands in the cluster (Illumina 2017, Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018). Millions of 
distinct clusters have now been created simultaneously all over the flow cell and are ready for sequencing. 
Sequencing by synthesis (SBS) is a technology used by Illumina platforms. A primer kick starts the 
sequencing process by attaching to the adaptor primer binding site on the P5 end of the DNA fragment. 
This is called read 1 (R1). Subsequently, polymerase incorporates reversible terminator-bound 
deoxynucleotides (dNTPs) into a DNA strand. The dNTPs are fluorescently labeled. For each sequencing 
cycle only one base is added to each strand because the dNTPs are terminated, which prevents further base 
incorporation. When the terminated bases have been incorporated to all strands, they emit a specific color 
corresponding to a specific base either A (adenine), T (thymine), C (cytosine) or G (guanine ) and a picture 
of the flow cell with the light emissions is taken. The cluster formation is a way to enhance the fluorescent 
signal – one cluster will emit one color. After the picture has been taken one can, based on the fluorescent 
signal, determine which type of nucleotide that has been added to which fragment/cluster. Next, the 
==PAGE:15==
Master’s thesis 
Malene N. Månsson 
01.10.2018 
14 
 
terminators are cleaved off so that a new sequencing cycle can begin, meaning that a new reversible 
terminator-bound dNTP can attach and a new light signal being emitted. The number of cycles determines 
the length of the read. In many cases researchers decide to use paired-end sequencing (this has also been 
used in this study). This strategy enables both ends of the DNA fragment to be sequenced. After R1, the 
index at P7 is read. Subsequently, a new bridge amplification process is conducted. Polymerase generates a 
doubled stranded bridge, which denatures leaving a forward and a reverse strand. This time the forward 
strand is washed away leaving the reverse strand for sequencing. This is called read 2 (R2). Paired-end 
sequencing has the advantages that the R1 and R2 can be aligned as read pairs and give a more precise 
result of the sequenced fragment (Illumina 2017).          
eDNA metabarcoding data analysis 
The output from Illumina platforms are FASTQ files. When only a single strand is sequenced one file is 
generated per library and when doing paired-end sequencing two files for each library are generated. 
FASTQ files contain an identification code, the DNA sequence itself, a quality score and a quality score 
(termed the Phred quality score, Q) for each nucleotide in the sequence, providing the probability that a 
base call is incorrect (Figure 3). If all nucleotides in a sequence reach a quality score of 30 (Q30) or more it 
is very unlikely that the sequence contain errors (Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018).  
Figure 3: Example of a FASTQ record for one sequence. The upper line is an identification code for the sequence. Line 
number two is the DNA sequence itself. The third line is a quality score and the fourth line contains the quality score 
for each nucleotide. Each sign or letter represents a score (Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018).   
 
The raw read data is ran through a “pipeline” – programs used for sequence handling and curation. A 
variety of programs can be used for this purpose. In this study DADA2 has been used (Callahan, McMurdie 
et al. 2016). Other possibilities are OBITools, QIIME, MOTHUR and others (Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018).  
Preliminary steps in the bioinformatics process are sequence quality assessment, demultiplexing of the 
samples and trimming of the sequences, meaning that the previously added adaptors are removed (Deiner, 
Bik et al. 2017, Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018). Demultiplexing is the process where each sequence is assigned 
back to its initial PCR. This is necessary since all the libraries have been pooled together before sequencing. 
This can be done due to the unique tags added during the library building.    
Another necessary trimming process is based on the quality assessment of the reads. Either whole reads 
can be rejected if the number of bases with a low quality score is too high or trimming from the 3’-end of 
the sequence, where the quality score is often decreasing, can be carried out. The score of the first few 
bases of a sequence are often relatively low, as well, meaning that a removal of bases from the 5’-end also 
occur occasionally. Paired-end sequencing is a way to improve the accuracy of the ends of the sequences. 
This is done by merging the forward and reverse reads in order to reconstruct a full-length sequence. An 
==PAGE:16==
Master’s thesis 
Malene N. Månsson 
01.10.2018 
15 
 
overlap of approximately 20 bp between the forward and reverse strand is normally used for the merging 
process (Deiner, Bik et al. 2017).      
Chimeras and singletons, which can be categorized as spurious sequences, are two types of errors one 
should be aware of during bioinformatics. Chimeras are by-products of PCR amplification and are a 
combination of two or more sequences. Singletons are MOTUs (Molecular Operational Taxonomic Unit) 
that appear only once in the data set. They are likely to be false positives, very rare taxa, contaminants or 
unremoved chimeras. These types of errors are important to exclude from the data, since they can skew 
the estimation of the diversity severely (Deiner, Bik et al. 2017). 
The above-mentioned steps are common procedures for metabarcoding data handling in order to filter out 
errors and experimental noise from the data set. Further procedures are often conducted in the statistical 
software program R and choice of further analysis depends on the aim of the study.

Survey area and sampling 
Collection of water samples was performed at 7 different sites in the river system located in Nature Park 
Åmosen in Western Zealand, Denmark (Figure 4).  
 
Figure 4: Map of a part of Nature Park Åmosen. Sampling sites are named and marked.  
 
The sampling sites for eDNA samples were chosen in an attempt to make a transect covering most of the 
different streams in the nature park. This could not be achieved sufficiently since we had to opt out the

the different sampling sites varied from agriculture, settlements and roads, meadows and forest (Appendix

during transport and at the end of the day stored at -20 degrees until extraction could be executed.

Extraction of eDNA from water samples 
The extraction procedure took place in a laboratory meant for DNA extraction and sampling preparation, 
separated from post-PCR and PCR procedures. To prevent contamination, the extraction process was 
carried out in a flow hood and all surfaces were cleaned with a 5 % bleach solution followed by a 70 % 
ethanol solution. Before adding any reagents to the sampling filters, they were cleaned outside with bleach. 
Subsequently, DNA material was extracted from the filters using the commercial extraction kit Qiagen 
DNeasy Blood and Tissue. The manufacturer’s protocol was followed with modifications by Eva Egelyng 
Sigsgaard (Appendix 3). 
For each extraction round, an extraction control was made containing no DNA. Only reagents were added.

PCR amplification 
For the PCR amplification, tagged versions of the MiFish primer set were used. MiFish primers are fish 
specific primers (excl. cartilaginous fish) that target a small region of the mitochondrial DNA 12S rRNA gene 
(Miya, Sato et al. 2015). The primers (MiFish-U-F: 5’-GTCGGTAAAACTCGTGCCAGC-3’ and MiFish-U-R: 5’-
CATAGTGGGGTATCTAATCCCAGTTTG-3’) amplify a product of 163-185 bp. 
For many of the samples the final extraction material looked brownish and dirty. A clear end product is 
preferable since the discoloration could indicate the presence of humic substances in the extraction 
material, which can act as inhibitors in the following PCR procedure (Wilson 1997). One way to overcome 
inhibition is to dilute the extracted DNA material (Jane, Wilcox et al. 2015, McKee, Spear et al. 2015). Thus, 
in order to investigate our suspicion about the presence of inhibitors in the extraction material we made a 
dilution series (1:1, 1:8 and 1:32) of three different samples with different degrees of discoloration and ran 
a qPCR analysis on an Agilent Stratagene Mx3005P machine. qPCR (quantitative polymerase chain reaction) 
is a modified PCR method, that measures the amplification of target DNA during the PCR process and not in 
the end of the process as in conventional PCR. Then it is possible to follow the amount of amplified DNA of 
each sample for each PCR cycle.   
The samples were diluted in ddH2O. Each reaction contained 1 µl MiFish primer (forward and reverse), 0.2 
µl TaqGold polymerase, 2 µl dNTPs, 2 µl MgCl2, 2.5 µl AmpliTaq buffer, 0.25 µl BSA, 13.05 µl H2O, 1 µl SYBR 
green, and 3 µl DNA-template. The thermocycling parameters were set at 95 °C for 10 min, 45 cycles of 94 
°C in 30 sec, 50 °C for 30 sec and 72 °C for 1 min. Storage temperature was 4 °C. The result showed that the 
highest dilution at 1:32 did best (see Results). Based on that, we decided to dilute all the extracted samples 
into 1:32 for the following PCR process in order to prevent or minimize inhibition.       
The PCR mastermix contained for each PCR reaction 1 µl MiFish primer (forward and reverse. For each 
sample the primer set had a unique identification tag), 0.2 µl TaqGold polymerase, 2 µl dNTPs, 2 µl MgCl2, 
2.5 µl AmpliTaq buffer, 0.25 µl BSA, and 14.05 µl H2O. It was made in a DNA free laboratory. Subsequently, 
the mastermix was brought to another laboratory meant for sample preparation where 3 µl diluted DNA-
template was added. The total volume for each PCR mixture was 25 µl. PCR thermocycling parameters were 
set at 95 °C for 10 min, 45 cycles of 94 °C for 30 sec, 50 °C for 30 sec and 72 °C for 1 min and a final 
extension of 72 °C for 5 min. Storage temperature was 10 °C.  
Three PCR setups were made and each of them was run twice (in total six PCR runs) – giving four replicates 
of each sample. Six negative control samples (not containing DNA) were included for each PCR 
amplification round in order to check for potential contamination. Instead of adding DNA template to the 
mastermix, pure water was added. The control samples were all tagged with the same tag number. Also, a 
positive control sample (a mock sample) was prepared and included in each PCR reaction. It is important 
that such a sample is composed of DNA from species that do not appear in or nearby the study area. A 
mock sample with known concentrations of the species can be very helpful in the data analysis process and 
useful in detecting possible contamination (De Barba, Miquel et al. 2014, Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018) 
DNA derived from tissue from ten exotic species from the Persian Gulf was used for the mock sample. 
Based on concentration measurements obtained from a Qubit fluorometer, the mock sample was prepared 
so that the final concentration of DNA was as follows: Rhabdosargus haffara 0.9 ng/µl, Scarus persicus 0.45 
ng/µl, Diplodus sargus kotschyi 0.225 ng/µl, Acanthopagrus bifasciatus 0.113 ng/µl, Pomacanthus

maculosus 0.056 ng/µl, Apogonichthyoides nigripinnis 0.028 ng/µl, Sphyraena obtusata 0.014 ng/µl, 
Hemiramphus far 0.007 ng/µl, Upeneus tragula 0.004 ng/µl, and Allenbatrachus grunniens 0.002 ng/µl. The 
mock was tagged with a unique tag and amplified together with the other eDNA samples.   
The lengths of the PCR products were verified with gel electrophoresis on a 2% agarose gel. 
 
Library building  
The PCR products were assembled into six pools – one pool for each PCR setup. The extraction controls 
were distributed between the pools so that four pools contained four replicates of one extraction control 
and two pools contained four replicates of two different extraction controls. Each pool contained 4 µl PCR 
product of each water sample, 4 µl of each negative control sample, 4 µl of each extraction control sample 
and 2 µl of the mock sample. The pools were then purified with the MinElute® PCR purification kit and two 
of the products were tested on an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer to make sure the purification process had 
preserved the DNA in the samples. Subsequently, libraries were built from each pool using approximately 
400 ng purified PCR product. Illumina TruSeq® DNA PCR-free Low Throughput Library Prep Kit for 24 
samples was used and the manufacture’s protocol with few modifications was followed. A blank control 
sample was included. Each library was provided with a unique index. Finally, the concentration and 
fragment sizes were checked on an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer. 
The libraries were then pooled together containing a concentration of 4.2 nM of each library and send to 
sequencing at the National DNA Sequencing Center, Copenhagen. Paired-end sequencing was conducted 
on Illumina MiSeq platform.     
 
Bioinformatics                
The software package DADA2 (Callahan, McMurdie et al. 2016) was used for correcting amplicon errors in 
the raw sequencing output. The sequences were then assigned back to species using BLASTn function in 
NCBI’s reference sequence database. BLAST hits with an incomplete coverage between the eDNA sequence 
and the NCBI reference sequence were removed and so was hits with a match <80 %.         
Further analysis of the BLAST results were made with the software program R version 3.4.2 and conducted 
in RStudio version 1.1.442. The analysis was only based on the fish species and only those that were 
considered as reliable findings – exotic species and contaminants were sorted out. 
A distance (degree of difference) analysis between the PCR replicates from each sample was conducted. 
The assumption is that replicates from the same site contain close to the same species distribution or at 
least have some significant overlaps. Replicates that were too different were removed. 
Furthermore, PCR replicates that contained less than 200 reads per PCR and less than two MOTUs 
(Molecular Operational Taxonomic Unit) were also removed. The threshold of 200 reads was verified by the 
sequencing depth graphed in rarefaction curves. The curves were generated by resampling of the reads 
within one replicate in order to check if too many samples were sorted out. It is a way to maximize the 
diversity, while minimizing the number of lost replicates. Species accumulation curves, generated by 
==PAGE:21==
Master’s thesis 
Malene N. Månsson 
01.10.2018 
20 
 
resampling of the replicates for one sampling event by each site, were conducted in order to check if the 
number of replicates was sufficient in detecting the present diversity.       
In general, resampling provides a measure for the accuracy of the statistical tests. It is a way to get to know 
that uncertainty that will occur when only small fractions of an ecosystem community are sampled.         
A clustering analysis and a redundancy analysis were conducted based on resampled data.    
The species composition of the sampling sites was classified by hierarchical clustering. The Raup-Crick 
dissimilarity index was calculated and used as a measure of the probability that compared sampling sites 
have a non-identical species composition. The index also presumes that two sites sharing rare species are 
more similar than sites sharing more common species (Raup and Crick 1979). The analysis was then based 
on the presence/absence of taxa for each site and pictured in a cluster plot.  
An ordination analysis/redundancy analysis (RDA) was conducted in order to investigate whether upstream 
sites and downstream sites of Tissø can be distinguished based on the eDNA composition. Each sampling 
site was characterized as an upstream or a downstream location. This was set to be the explaining or 
constrained variable and R was asked to find the best arrangement of replicates along gradients.    
Species accumulation curves for downstream sites and upstream sites was conducted and so was a species 
accumulation curve for the whole survey area. In order to estimate the total diversity of fish in the entire 
area four regression models (Arrhenius, Gleason, Gitay and Lomolino) were fitted to the accumulation 
curve for the whole area. The model with the lowest AIC (Akaike information criterion) was used to 
estimate the total diversity.  
Finally, the final species list based on eDNA results was compared to the data obtained by traditional survey 
and catching methods delivered by the National Fish Atlas and pictured in a Venn diagram.

Test results for inhibition 
During the process, several control tests have been conducted.  
The first partial result was gained when checking for inhibition by running a qPCR. Three dilutions (1:1, 1:8 
and 1:32) were made for each of three samples with different degree of dirtiness (dirty, medium and 
clean). The samples with the highest dilution did best together with the clean sample diluted to 1:8 (Figure 
5). After approximately 25 cycles the amount of DNA increased and reached a plateau after 45 cycles. The 
other samples were slower in amplifying the DNA and did not reach a reasonable saturation. Based on this 
observation all extracted eDNA samples were diluted 1:32 before conventional PCR was conducted in order 
to prevent inhibition.

DNA and the length of the PCR products. The lengths of the PCR products were expected to be ~240-250 bp 
including primer and tags, which was also the general result according to the gel pictures.

Environmental DNA results 
The species list obtained from the analysis of the sequencing output showed that the MiFish primer set was 
able to target a large diversity of species – not only fish. Several reliable and less reliable findings of 
mammals, birds and amphibians were also detected. In addition, the list also contained a variety of bacteria 
and fungus and a single species of algae (127 different species in total, Appendix 4).    
Exotic bird and fish species (except for those used in the mock sample) and species such as wolf (Canis 
lupus) and bowhead whale (Balaena mysticetus) were considered as lab contaminants or as a result of 
insufficient matches in the NCBI database.  
The control samples were only contaminated by human (Homo sapiens) and bowhead whale. 
Five out of ten mock species were detected. These were R. haffara, D. sargus, P. maculosus, A. nigripinnis 
and U. tragula.     
Furthermore, DNA sequences from 21 fish species considered as reliable findings were obtained from the 
final and clean data set (Table 2). For two of the eDNA sequences the final species identification was 
changed. Based on manual BLASTing and knowledge of species distribution the assignment of Iksookimia 
koreensis, an endemic species to Korea, was changed to spined loach (Cobitis taenia). Spined loach was 
probably sorted out from the BLAST hits because the coverage of the eDNA sequence was less than 100 %. 
When the sequence was tested manually the coverage for C. taenia was 98 % and the match was 100 %. 
The match for I. koreensis, for which the coverage was 100 %, was only 95 %.  
The assignment of dace (Leuciscus leuciscus) was changed to ide (Leuciscus idus). In Denmark dace is 
exclusively found in Jutland and only in west-facing streams. Despite many reports of catches from other 
places in Denmark it has always been proven to be identification errors. The species is closely related and 
easily confused with ide or roach (Carl 2012a). Since sequences of roach had been sequenced and 
successfully identified and sequences of ide had not, despite it is known to appear in the survey area, the 
MiFish primer set was tested manually with reference sequences from the NCBI database for both ide and 
dace in the software program Geneious 10.0.9. It was found that the primers could not attach properly to 
the ide sequence. Based on this knowledge and the knowledge of species distribution, it was assumed that 
the sequence found in the eDNA material was more likely to be ide than dace, and therefore it was 
changed.

too different and consequently they were removed from the dataset. Samples collected in October at 
Stridsmølle and Halleby stream and samples collected at Vasebro, Skellingsted Bro and Stridsmølle in 
February were all sorted out and were not included in the further cluster analysis and redundancy analysis. 
A closer look at the removed samples showed that many of the replicates already had been removed 
earlier in the processing (when samples with <200 reads and samples with <2 MOTUs had been sorted out) 
and that some of the replicates had no reads at all.  
Sequencing depth was investigated by rarefaction curves and found to be sufficient for the majority of the 
samples (Appendix 9). 
After the filtration, the number of replicates for each filter sample varied between 1 and 4. In most of the 
cases this number of replicates turned out to be insufficient in order to achieve a saturation of species 
diversity for the specific filter sample (Appendix 10). The samples that reached saturation or came near had 
mainly 4 replicates. This indicates that a higher number of replicates per filter sample would have been 
preferable (Bonin, Taberlet et al. 2018).     
 
Upstream and downstream habitats 
One of the goals of the data analysis was to investigate a possible difference between sites located 
upstream and downstream of Tissø. This was done based on re-sampled data.  
Based on the Raup-Crick dissimilarity index a clustering of the sampling sites gave an indication of which 
sites that had a similar species composition. By assigning each site a color depending on it as being an 
upstream or a downstream location it was possible to see if there was some sort of pattern (Figure 8). Four 
of the five downstream sites are gathered near each other in the dendogram and thus indicate a similar 
species composition between the sites. However, a PERMANOVA test conducted on the Raup-Crick index 
showed that the upstream/downstream location of the sites had no significant importance to the cluster 
formation (P 0.239).

Discussion 
Ecological aspects 
The present study demonstrates that freshwater eDNA can be used in the work of monitoring research of 
freshwater fish. Environmental samples were taken in the stream system of Nature Park Åmosen in 
February, Marts and May 2018 and a metabarcoding approach was used in order to investigate the 
presence of fish species. 21 species were detected by the method. Historical data received from the 
National Fish Atlas revealed a total of 27 species that have been detected by traditional survey and catching 
methods over time in the same area.         
European river lamprey, European brook lamprey and burbot have, according to the National Fish Atlas, not 
been reported in the survey area since the beginning of the 1900s. The lack of detection of these species by 
the eDNA method is therefore a reasonable result. Grass carp (Ctenopharyngodon idella) and wels catfish 
(Silurus glanis) have only been detected in a private pond in the survey area (information from National 
Fish Atlas), and since all the eDNA samples were taken in the river system the lack of detection of these 
species was expected. The sixth species that was not detected by eDNA but previously observed in the area 
was flounder. Even though flounder (Platichthys flesus) is considered as a marine-living species, it has been 
caught in Tissø and Halleby stream several times (information from National Fish Atlas). Both locations 
were covered by the eDNA sampling. The species spawn from February to May and only in water with a 
salinity that excesses 10 ‰ (Muus, Nielsen et al. 2006, Nielsen 2012), which does not apply to the 
freshwater survey area. Since the sampling period took place in the same months as the spawning period, it 
is reasonable assume that the majority of potential flounder in the area would seek out more salty water 
and pull out from the stream system and that is why the species was not detected. Another option could be 
that they simply had not moved so far into the river system in the sampling period.   
The redundancy analysis displayed an overlap between samples from Vasebro, Tissø inlet and Tissø outlet 
and in the dendogram the samples from the inlet of Tissø were also clustered together with samples from 
Vasebro and the outlet of Tissø. This indicates a similarity between these sites and during a manual 
counting of species and a look into the species composition for each site, three-spined stickleback, bleak 
and ruffe appeared to be detected only at these three sites (except for a single registration of three-spined 
stickleback in Halleby stream). The results from the samples collected from Tissø inlet seems to be more 
alike the results from Vasebro and the outlet of Tissø than the results from the other upstream samples. 
Ruffe and bleak prefers slow-moving water or larger lakes (Hölker and Thiel 1998, Ogle 1998, Carl 2012b), 
which corresponds very well to the results generated from this study where they were found at sites near 
the lake and downstream of the lake. It is also possible that the detections at Vasebro derive from 
transported eDNA from the lake (Civade, Dejean et al. 2016).    
The three-spined stickleback is considered as a very common species and has been registered in all kinds of 
waters and almost everywhere in Denmark. However, it is remarkable that no certain registrations of the 
species has been made in large parts of the Åmosen stream system (Møller 2012b). Whether it is due to 
unprecise or inadequate species determination or because there really is no population of the species in 
this area is unclear. However, the results from this study substantiates that the species does not appear in 
the upper parts of the stream system.

Other species of particular interest was the spined loach and the rainbow trout.    
The spined loach was detected at Undløse Bridge, Skellingsted Bridge, Tissø outlet and at Vasebro. As 
expected the rare species was registered, but at a limited number of sites. In addition, the number of reads 
was low compared to the other species, which could indicate that it is not a common and widespread 
inhabitant of the stream system (if we assume that number of reads counts as a proxy for the abundance).    
Rainbow trout was detected at Skellingsted Bridge, Halleby stream, Stridsmølle and Tissø outlet – mainly 
upstream locations. The release of rainbow trout from an offshore fish farm in Great Belt a few months 
before the first eDNA sampling event gave an expectation of detecting rainbow trout in the stream system. 
According to the number of reads the amount of the species was not that significant as expected and most 
of the findings were done in the upper parts of the stream system, far from where they enter the stream 
system. It is possible to assume that the invasion of rainbow trout into the stream system was not that 
significant as one could expect. 
Besides expected and exotic fish species a large range of other animals were detected (Appendix 4). A large 
range of birds known to live in or visit the area were detected. Some of them such as the anseriformes 
spend a lot time in the water and therefore they are not a surprise to be found in the samples. Shew 
(Neomys fodiens and Sorex araneus), roe deer (Capreolus capreolus), reed deer (Cervus elaphus), rodents 
like squirrel (Sciurus vulgaris), rat (Rattus norvegicus) and different species of mice (Myodes glareolus and 
Apodemus flavicollis), common newt (Lissotriton vulgaris) and common toad (Bufo bufo) were also 
identified and are known to be present in the area. Fox (Vulpes vulpes), pine marten (Martes martes), otter 
(Lutra lutra) and badger (Meles meles) are also known to be present in the survey area but have not been 
detected. We cannot expect to get an adequate picture of the terrestrial biodiversity when using fish 
specific primers, but the detection of some of the known semiaquatic and terrestrial animals from the area 
confirm that freshwater samples also can be used in order to extent the information about biodiversity to 
the catchment area (Deiner, Fronhofer et al. 2016). Almost any animal needs to seek freshwater at some 
point for drinking, foraging or to passage. Obviously, these activities leave tracts of eDNA that in the same 
way as when searching for aquatic species can be detected in the water. Many things are unknown about 
this use of water samples but it is worth working on since it contains large potential. Generating large 
amounts of information about freshwater habitats and their catchment areas can become a great resource 
to future biomonitoring.          
 
Preventing inhibition  
This study revealed that potential eDNA inhibition must be considered in order to avoid detection errors. As 
previously described many of the samples looked brownish and dirty especially those collected in February. 
For samples taken upstream of Tissø in February it was only possible to filter 270 ml or less before 
complete clogging of the filter (Table 1). The majority of the samples sorted out in the data analysis were 
just taken in February. Thus, this could indicate that these samples, despite dilution, potentially contained a 
high degree of inhibiting compounds and probably more than samples taken in Marts and May since the 
sequencing of February samples was less successful and many replicates were sorted out. We have a 
presumption that some kind of excavation work or similar further up the stream could course the very dirty 
water in the upper part of the stream. 
==PAGE:33==

Studies reveal that methods used in order to reduce inhibition have been to add bovine serum alburnin 
(BSA) to the master mix, which binds the inhibitor molecules or otherwise modify or regulate components 
of the PCR master mix (Hedman and Rådström 2013, McKee, Spear et al. 2015). However, McKee et al 
(McKee, Spear et al. 2015) states that pre-PCR treatments in order to remove inhibitors is more ideal.  
It is known, that the yield of eDNA can be increased by a larger amount of filtered water (Turner, Miller et 
al. 2014, Eichmiller, Miller et al. 2016), but the presence of inhibitors will often result in a reduced amount 
of filtered water because the filters easily will clog. The presence of inhibiting compounds thus inhibits the 
detection of eDNA in two ways. It prevents a suitable amount of water to be filtered and it inhibits the 
further laboratory procedure. One way to overcome the clogging problem is to use filters with a larger pore 
size. Unfortunately, this can lead to loss of eDNA material because more material can slip though the filter. 
Turner et al (Turner, Miller et al. 2014) suggest pre-filtration of the water before it is filtered through the 
final filter used for eDNA extraction.  
Commercial inhibitor removing kits are also a possible way to get rid of problematic inhibitors. These kinds 
of purification kits have shown to be very effective, but are more expensive and costly methods to use 
compared to for instance dilution. Additionally, it might also reduce the eDNA yield (McKee, Spear et al. 
2015, Williams, Huyvaert et al. 2017). 
Dilution as a method to prevent or minimize inhibition is described in a number of studies (Jane, Wilcox et 
al. 2015, McKee, Spear et al. 2015, Eichmiller, Miller et al. 2016). This is an easy and cheap method. 
However, when diluting the samples it is not only inhibiting compounds that will be diluted – also the eDNA 
material will be diluted and therefore there is a risk of reducing the sensitivity of the assay and get false 
negative results if only low concentrations of eDNA is present.  
In this study dilution of the samples and the addition of BSA to the master mix were assessed to be 
adequate action in order to minimize inhibition because all expected species were found.    
 
Method considerations 
A large range of possible biases must be taken into consideration when interpreting eDNA results. Besides 
filtration of inhibitors, catch of eDNA from organisms not present in the habitat disturbs the true picture of 
the biodiversity. Resuspension of sediment can course those kinds of bias. Even though the method of 
eDNA has been emphasized to be a method that can be used independent of weather (Thomsen and 
Willerslev 2015), heavy rain or strong currents can resuspend sediment material from the bottom and 
release preserved eDNA.  
Biases are also likely to occur during the laboratory procedure. Handling of samples, extraction of eDNA, 
PCR amplification, library building, sequencing and data analysis can all cause errors on their own.  
Especially when doing metabarcoding the choice of primers have great importance to the outcome. Some 
universal primers might have a greater affinity for some sequences, meaning that some species are more 
likely to be detected than others (Thomsen and Willerslev 2015). For instance did the fish specific primers 
developed by Valentini et al (Valentini, Taberlet et al. 2016) not reach the species level for the families of 
Cyprinidae and Gadidae. Those kinds of limitations are important to be aware of. Since many Danish 
==PAGE:34==
Master’s thesis

freshwater species belong to the Cyprinidae family (Table 2) and MiFish primers are able to distinguish 
between species in the Cyprinidae family, the use of this type of primer set was the most suitable.  
One way to overcome or minimize bias problems is to add extraction control samples and PCR control 
samples in order to check for contamination in each step of the laboratory procedure. If contamination is 
detected in a control sample, the results for the rest of the replicates might not be reliable.  
Mock samples can be used to assess the degree of false negative results (De Barba, Miquel et al. 2014, 
Cristescu and Hebert 2018). In this study, only five out of ten mock species were identified, meaning that 
five species that were known to be in the sample have not been detected. This could indicate a general 
underestimation of species detected, but on the other hand, all the expected species were found.   
The cluster analysis and the redundancy analysis showed an insignificant result when distinguishing 
upstream and downstream sites. A way to strengthen the study would be to increase the number of 
samples and expand the area of sampling especially with more samples taken downstream of Tissø. It is 
likely that some species e.g. three-spined stickleback would be more abundant when approaching the sea 
and a higher salinity. If the sampling period was expanded to cover more of the year, it might be that 
flounder would have been detected, as well. 
 
Future perspectives of eDNA in a management context 
Many studies have investigated the ideal combination of procedures in order to gain the highest and most 
precise yield of eDNA from environmental samples (Deiner, Walser et al. 2015, Eichmiller, Miller et al. 2016, 
Hinlo, Gleeson et al. 2017, Geerts, Boets et al. 2018). Among other things, this is done in an attempt to 
standardize the approach so it prospectively will be easier to compare studies and results. Standardized 
protocols will make the implementation of an eDNA approach in management and conservation matters 
much easier and more favorable. Despite, this effort standardization of the eDNA approach is not a straight 
forward task.  
The marked is full of products (samplings systems, commercial laboratory kits, sequencing platforms etc.) 
that all have some advantages and disadvantages compared to others. The best approach also depends on 
the specific task, the environment, and the target organisms. Deiner et al (Deiner and Altermatt 2014) 
found that a combination of filtration of the water and extraction with the Qiagen DNeasy extraction kit 
exhibited the highest detection rate of eukaryotes in a lotic environment, whereas a combination of 
precipitation and the MoBio PowerWater extraction kit did best in the detection of eubacteria. Hinlo et al 
(Hinlo, Gleeson et al. 2017) found a similar result. Other studies have investigated other protocols and 
extraction kits and thus found other ideal combinations (Eichmiller, Miller et al. 2016, Geerts, Boets et al. 
2018).  
Other limitations regarding eDNA as a monitoring tool arise because we do not achieve any demographic 
knowledge about the target organisms based on this method. We gain exclusively presence/absence 
information or to some degree and with certain reservations it is also possible to gain information about 
biomass based on the number of reads (Klymus, Richter et al. 2015, de Souza, Godwin et al. 2016). When 
doing traditional surveys thorough examination of the target organisms also reveal information about sex, 
==PAGE:35==
Master’s thesis 
Malene N. Månsson 
01.10.2018 
34 
 
age, life state, health condition, etc., which are very important information in order to assess the condition 
of a species and make plans for future management.    
Even though there still is a large degree of uncertainty regarding the use of an eDNA approach there is 
consensus within the scientific literature that eDNA-based methods in a very useful method for 
conservation and management monitoring (Thomsen and Willerslev 2015, Cristescu and Hebert 2018). 
Today biomonitoring programs are expensive to carry out, require a large degree of taxonomic expertise 
and can be invasive to the habitat or target organisms (Thomsen and Willerslev 2015, Cristescu and Hebert 
2018). The use of an eDNA-based approach would in many cases be a good alternative or supplement to 
traditional survey methods.

APPENDIX 3 
Extraction of eDNA from water samples 
 
Use of Qiagen DNeasy Blood and Tissue kit. Protocol modifications by Eva Egelyng Sigsgaard 
 
 
Turn on the oven at 56 °C 
 
Make sure the lids are tightened and clean the outside of filters with bleach   
 
Add 720 ul ATL buffer + 80 ul proteinase K to the filters through the inlet (the end with the white lid). 
 
Make an extraction control (CNE) using 180 ul ATL + 20 ul proteinase K. This control is included through the entire process. 
 
Make sure the lids are tightened, wrap the ends with parafilm, and shake the filters for 10 s. 
 
Put samples in an agitator (using parafilm or tape to secure them) and place it in the oven at 56 °C for 3 hours. 
 
Take out the samples and shake again for 5 s. Remove parafilm. 
 
Label 2 ml DNA LoBind eppendorfs (two per sample). 
 
Attach a 3 ml luer lock syringe to the inlet end of the filter. Holding the filter above the syringe, pull the liquid out of the filter. 
 
Transfer half the liquid into each of the two labelled 2 mL eppendorfs. 
 
Spin samples if necessary 
 
Add 400 ul AL-buffer + 400 ul ethanol (96-100 %) to each of the two tubes. Use 200 ul AL-buffer + 200 ul ethanol for the CNE. 
 
Vortex + spin 
 
Tranfer 600 ul of each sample to a spin column and centrifuge at 6000 G for 1 min. 
 
Discard the flow-through (the liquid in the collection tube) into the Qiagen waste bottle. Dry the opening of the collection tube on 
two layers of paper towel and reassemble the spin column and the collection tube. 
 
Add 600 ul more of the sample to the spin columns, repeat centrifugation, and discard flow-through again.  
 
Repeat a third, and fourth time. 
 
Replace collection tubes with new ones 
 
Add 500 ul AW1-buffer to each sample and spin at 6000 G for 1 min. Discard the flow-through and replace collection tubes  
 
Add 500 ul AW2-buffer to each sample. Spin at 13.000 G for 2 min. (all the ethanol needs to be removed).  
 
Discard the flow-through and replace collection tubes with 1.5 ml DNA LoBind eppendorfs with the lids removed. 
 
Add 60 ul AE buffer to each column. 
 
Samples are put in the oven (or heat block) at 37 °C for 10 min. 
 
New DNA LoBind eppendorfs (1.5 ml) are labeled, with a short name on the lid and an extraction number and the date on the side. 
 
Spin at 13.000 G for 1 min. 
 
Add 60 ul AE buffer to each column. 
 
Incubate a second time at 37 °C for 10 min. 
 
Spin at 13.000 G for 1 min. 
 
Transfer flow-through to the labeled eppendorfs and place the tubes in the freezer. 
 
And you’re done! 
