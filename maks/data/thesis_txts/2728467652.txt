Abstract
Concerns about sustainability have given Timber-Concrete Composite (TCC) beams
new life, inspiring renewed research into their structural behaviour. This project will
specifically address the interaction of time-dependent effects and the potential imper-
fections of a prefabricated notched TCC beam. The study demonstrates that imper-
fections have a significant effect on composite effects, deflection, and time-dependent
behaviour. An ideal beam was shown to have no imperfection which produced higher
composite effects and exhibit less deflection in both elastic and time-dependent be-
haviour. Notably, imperfections at specific notch 1 and 2 were shown to be beneficial
in the time-dependent deflection caused by moisture-induced effects. A comparative
analysis of the three models, (1) the analytic γ-method, (2) the numerical framework
model, and (3) the 2D model, is additionally conducted and examined, demonstrat-
ing that the models differ in predictions according to the chosen modelling approach,
suggesting opportunities for refining these models.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
After the I and II World Wars, a shortage of steel for reinforcement in concrete initi-
ated the early development of Timber-Concrete Composite (TCC) beams as a means
to refurbish old historical buildings.
However, these preliminary designs failed to
comply with modern building regulations, leading to a halted further development.
Nowadays, the growing inclination towards sustainability has resurrected the TCC
beam, prompting considerable research and interest [Yeoh et al., 2011]. This con-
temporary focus on reducing carbon footprints and enhancing sustainability has had
a profound impact on the construction industry, driven by a surge in consumer de-
mand for more environmentally friendly building solutions. This trend is pushing the
boundaries of structural design as innovative solutions arise, such as FUTURECEM®,
which significantly reduce carbon emissions from concrete production [Aalborg Port-
land, 2023].
Incorporating wood into building construction is another method to
reduce emissions that has been gaining traction over the past decade. The use of
wood not only provides an environmental alternative but is increasingly being ad-
mired for its aesthetic appeal, as exemplified by Mjøstårnet and Brock Commons
Tallwood House [Block, 2019, Talmazan, 2016].
Wood offers several structural benefits such as high tensile strength and vibration
absorption, although it does have a lower modulus of elasticity compared to con-
crete, raising potential serviceability limit state issues due to increased deflection.
The concept of entirely wooden structures, while offering certain advantages, also
brings challenges such as vulnerability to horizontal movement. A viable answer to
these considerations might be found in the TCC beam, which optimally leverages
concrete’s compressive strength at the top and wood’s tensile strength at the bottom.
In addition, this composite beam benefits from an increased mass thanks to concrete,
addressing issues of horizontal movement, while the wooden component promotes the
usage of environmentally friendly construction materials and provides the aesthetic
option of a free wooden ceiling.
This project strives to promote this solution by deepening the understanding of a
prefabricated notched TCC beam, with an emphasis on time-dependent phenomena,
which include; creep, hardening and shrinkage of concrete & creep, and swelling of
timber. The benefit of this approach is the potential for disassembly, allowing for
re-usability of the TCC beam in various locations and mitigating the initial impact

2.1
A TCC beam
A TCC beam is subjected to a positive bending moment, timber is under combined
compression and tension, while concrete is under combined compression and ten-
sion. However, under ideal circumstances, the concrete would take the compressive
forces, while the wood counterpart would take the tension stresses, resulting in a fully
composite effect and a structurally optimal beam. Conversely, in the absence of a
composite effect, the concrete would also be subject to tension forces, leading to a
significantly weaker beam given that the tensile strength of concrete is substantially
inferior to its compressive strength. Achieving a fully composite effect of a TCC beam
is impractical; the reality of the TCC beam is a compromise, where some degree of
composite effect is accomplished. Figure 2.1 represents the stress distribution for the
three potential scenarios pertinent to this study: ”No composite”, ”Full composite”,
and ”Partial composite” effect.

Thereby, the connection system in a TCC beam must provide a high composite action
between timber and concrete to improve the effectiveness of the structural system.
Some of the commonly used connections are steel fasteners such as self-tapping screws
[Quang Mai et al., 2018], coach screws [Yeoh et al., 2008], notch connections, and
hybrids connections [Zhang et al., 2021].
Almost all shear connections are semi-
rigid thus only providing partial composite action [Zhang et al., 2022]. Jiang and
Crocetti have recently tested a full-scale TCC floor with ductile notch connectors
with a span of 6.5 m [Jiang and Crocetti, 2019].
Their results showed that the
addition of shear reinforcement in the concrete notch can significantly increase the
load-carrying capacity, but it does not affect the structure’s elastic response at the
serviceability load [Lamothe et al., 2021]. The use of notches as a shear connection
has two basic advantages. On one hand, the high stiffness of the notch in elastic
conditions minimizes the deformations at a service level. On the other hand, the
notch is able to fail in a ductile way enabling ductility of the system at the ultimate
load limit [Boccadoro et al., 2017].

1. The location of the neutral axis in the composite cross-section should be in

2.2.1
Notch influence
The composite effect can be achieved by using notched connections. Figure 2.2 illus-
trates the positioning of the notches in relation to the supports. In a simply supported
beam, shear forces attain their maximum magnitude at the supports and are at their
minimum at the midpoint of the beam span. The notches function by developing a
discrete interaction between concrete and timber where shear forces are transferred
by the friction interaction between the compressive contact of timber and concrete.
This effect situates itself between the lower-bound solution, defined by the absence
of a notch, and the upper-bound solution, characterized by full composite interaction
where the plane section remains plane [Zhang et al., 2021].
Zhang et al.
showed that the concrete layer can, as a result of the discrete con-
nections, be divided into two types of regions; B-region and D-region as shown in
Figure 2.3. B-region represents the Bernoulli region and is only valid far away from
the notches according to Saint-Venant’s principle. The D-region represents a complex
region, where the assumption about plane section remains plane does not predict the
stress distribution correctly therefore further analysis is needed through either strut
and tie or various FEM models [Zhang et al., 2022].
CL

Beam regions consisting of Bernoulli regions and discrete disturbed

floor span in the tangential direction, but the resistance in the vertical direction is
low and tend to result in gap openings or uplifting of the concrete while undergoing
bending, which affects the composite action of the floor. This is the first problem with
a D-region, depicted in Figure 2.4. In order to mitigate these issues steel fasteners

When it comes to load transfer, the notch has an active and a passive side. The
quantity and size of steel fasteners used in a hybrid notched connection are impor-
tant parameter as it has and direct influence on the shear rigidity and ductility of the
connection. If an insuﬀicient quantity or size is used the connection’s shear capacity
may be minimally affected, resulting in only a physical connection between concrete
and wood in the vertical direction. Zhang et al. [2022] showed that self-tapping screws
are effective in preventing gaps from forming between wood and concrete. thereby
increasing the composite action, but they have no impact on whether cracks form in
concrete corners at the notch. To date, there is no standard technique to reinforce
and prevent uplifting at the same time [Zhang et al., 2022].
In ideal design, a ductile behaviour is preferred and a ductile notch connection system
was achieved in Boccadoro et al. [2017] and tested at full-scale with a span of 5.66
m. The TCC floor is made of Laminated Veneer Lumber (LVL) connected to normal
concrete. The predictability of timber failures parallel to the grain is improved when
LVL materials are used instead of solid wood or glued laminated timber, making duc-
tile designs achievable. In the testing in Boccadoro et al. [2017], the maximum load
reached was about the same in all specimens after high plastic deformations. The
structural aspects of the composite member most critical to the model are the shear
carrying in unreinforced concrete and the secondary effects which may occur during
notch yielding (e.g. gap opening and enlargement of concrete cracks). These issues,
which can affect the composite member’s ductile structural behaviour and are influ-
enced by a number of challenging-to-quantify elements, can be managed by adding
vertical reinforcement as depicted in Figure 2.6 sub-figure (V). In the experiment,
they installed two Ø6 mm rods in long holes in the notch area in order to carry only
vertical forces and not to prevent plastic LVL deformations. Each composite slab
consisted of a beech 40 mm thick LVL plate and a 160 mm thick concrete layer. The
notches were 250 mm long and 15 mm deep. Tests on specimens with and without
vertical fortification of various kinds were conducted in this series. Premature fracture
and gap opening was observed in the specimens without vertical reinforcement and
they develop a very restricted degree of ductility where the failure of the composite
member is governed by the compressive failure of the LVL material followed by a
compressive failure of concrete [Boccadoro et al., 2017].

Reinforcement and screws
Zhang et al. [2022] investigated different reinforcement levels of a notch in order to
develop a high-performance notch connection. In their experiment, they test three
beams with varying degrees of reinforcement in accordance with Figures 2.6 and 2.7
sub-picture (VI). The first beam has six rows of reinforcements in the notch, the
second beam has 9 rows of reinforcements, and the last beam has 12 rows of rein-
forcements. The length of the notches was 180 mm and the depth 25 mm with a
timber shear length, lv in front of the notches of 500 mm. The notches are reinforced
with vertical Ø 7 mm self-tapping screws and Ø 6 mm horizontal hooked steel rods.
Their lengths are 160 mm and 300 mm respectively. Their findings show that by
reinforcing the notch a significantly lower end-slip was achieved indicating a higher
composite eﬀiciency. From the four-point bending test, it was also observed that a
higher ultimate bending stiffness and higher, more predictable load-caring capacity
were achieved compared with a non-reinforced notch. The floor bending qualities
were improved by the reinforced notches in terms of consistency and reliability. A
lesser degree of reinforcement might be adequate to prevent the notch failure and
enlargement of concrete cracks, as the different levels of reinforcement had similar
bending stiffness and peak loads. However, the final failure for both reinforced and

2.3.2
Shear performance of TCC
Jiang et al. [2021] investigated the long-term shear performance of CLT-concrete
composite floors with screw connections. The paper aims to evaluate the influence
of using lightweight aggregate concrete instead of normal concrete and the composite
effect for both indoor and outdoor climates.
The results showed that the use of
lightweight aggregate concrete increased the creep coeﬀicient by about 1.2 and 2.5
times compared to normal concrete for indoor and outdoor climates respectively, the
lightweight aggregate concrete did not significantly affect the long-term deflection, a
lower composite action increased the long-term deflection, and the outdoor climate
increased the deflection more rapidly than the indoor climate [Jiang et al., 2021].
2.3.3
Finite element models
Fragiacomo et al. [2007] investigated the long-term effect of a notched TCC beam
with bolts in notches and in-situ cast concrete [Fragiacomo et al., 2007]. In doing so,
the authors use an experimental test of 133 days to withhold it against an FE model
translated into 50 years based on the initial conditions of the experiment. Lastly, the
paper uses closed-form solutions to test the accuracy of the FE model. The test speci-
mens were subjected to a 4-point bending. This led to several observations where the
moisture content in the wood deck increased from 6.5% to 14.0% due to bleeding of
the fresh concrete in the first 5 days, and after 15 days it decreased and stabilized at
6.5%. The effect on the mechanical properties of a shored or unshored construction
type is negligible. After installing the unshored concrete beams and applying the
dead load, the elastic deflection that was measured was significant. Furthermore, a
large shrinkage of concrete was expected due to the dimension of the concrete beam

and the relative humidity. The use of the FE model underestimated the deflection of

Further research from Fragiacomo et al. [2014] presents a 3D model exposed to vary-

2014]. The model was tested against a simpler 1D model whereas the advantages
and disadvantages of the models are investigated and discussed. The results show
that the 3D model closely captured the instantaneous and long-term behaviour of the
TCC beam. The 3D model is slightly more accurate than the 1D model, however,
the error compared to the measurement is below 3% (1.4% and 2.9% respectively).
They conclude that this difference can be seen as insignificant. The computational
time is far larger for the 3D model than the 1D, however, the 3D model does not need
experimental data on the shear stiffness and creep behaviour of shear connectors.
Khorsandnia et al. [2015] develops an FE model to predict the long-term behaviour
of TCC specimens – here the focus will be on the TCC beam.
The model uses

A viscoelastic material acts like a viscous and elastic material and will exhibit time-

3.1.2.1
Generalized creep theory
The creep coeﬀicient, which depicts the relationship between the initial slip and the
slip creep throughout long-term testing, can be used to describe long-term behaviour.
The long-term behaviour of timber, concrete, and shear connection simultaneously
determines the time-dependent behaviour of TCC systems. Consequently, compared
to a single component, the TCC structure’s creep behaviour is substantially more
complicated. Environmental factors, such as relative humidity and changing temper-
atures, also have an impact on the long-term functioning of TCC structures [Shi et al.,
2020]. The deformation rate is a function of the material’s properties, exposure time,
temperature, and the applied structural load. Creep is a time-dependent phenomenon
that goes through three stages as seen in Figure 3.1.
• Primary creep: In the initial stage, the strain rate is relatively high, but
decreases with increasing time and strain due the fact that the material is
experiencing an increase in strain hardening.
• Secondary creep: The strain rate diminishes to a minimum and becomes near
constant as the secondary stage begins. This is due to the balance between work
hardening and thermal softening. No material strength is lost during these first
two stages of creep.
• Tertiary creep: In tertiary creep, there is an acceleration of the rate and
possibly ultimate failure. The strain rate exponentially increases with stress
because of necking phenomena or internal cracks, cavities or voids decrease the
effective area of the specimen. These all lead to a decrease in the effective cross-
sectional area and an increase in strain rate. Strength is quickly lost in this
stage while the material’s shape is permanently changed.

3.1.3
Rheological models
Rheological models offer a mathematical framework for characterising the behaviour
of materials. Famously among these are the Spring, Dash-pot, and Maxwell Model.
In addition, to stress-based mechanistic symbolism, Hygrosorption and Shrinkage
are shown to capture further behaviour of wood and concrete, respectively.
The
mechanistic symbolism and the underlying principles of each element are illustrated
in Figure 3.2.
Figure 3.2: The mechanical symbolism of Spring, Dash-pot, Maxwell Model, Hy-
grosorption, and Shrinkage and their given strain behaviour
The spring instantaneously responds to changes, deforming instantly under a load and
then resetting back to its original state once the load is removed. The dash-pot, on
the other hand, introduces a time-dependent viscous behaviour. Its strain increases
with time and remains constant even after the removal of the load, signifying an irre-
versible change. The Maxwell model combines these behaviours: it deforms instantly
like a spring but with a viscous component that, once deformed, does not revert back.
To develop the material behaviour further additional spring and dash-pot elements
can be employed providing a Kelvin-Voigt or Burgers model. These elements can
capture the recovery of materials after the load is removed [Yildirim et al., 2020].
In contrast to the previously mentioned mechanical symbols that deform with stress,
hygroexpansion allows the system to alter its strain based on changes in moisture,
represented as u. An increase in moisture leads the system to expand, while a de-
crease causes it to contract [Mirianon et al., 2008]. In parallel with the hygrosorption
mechanism, there is shrinkage, which increases the strain independently of moisture
or external stress changes [Boumakis et al., 2020].

3.3
Material behaviour of wood
The following section will focus specifically on the material behaviour of wood in the
sense of short-term and time-dependent effects.
3.3.1
Mechano-sorption
Exposure to mechanical stress and alterations in moisture content significantly influ-
ence wooden structures. Due to its porous, hygroscopic, non-homogenous nature and
its cellular structural composition, wood is classified as a viscoelastic material. The
mechanical characteristics of wood are notably dependent on both temperature and
moisture content. Creep effects, or time-dependent deformation, play a crucial role
in structural design and longevity in the context of the wood’s lifecycle. Changes in
creep are a consequence of interplays between mechanical conditions and moisture
content, resulting in an acceleration of creep during increased moisture levels and a
reversible blocking of strain during a reduction in moisture. This intricate process,
known as mechano-sorption, occurs when the wood is simultaneously subjected to me-
chanical stress and sorption due to shifting hygrothermal conditions. Therefore, to
fully comprehend the effects of wood’s hygroscopic nature, all mechanical properties
across the anatomical directions of wood should be depicted as functions of moisture
content [Hoffmeyer and Davidson, 1989].
3.3.2
Sorption hysteresis
Wood exhibits sorption hysteresis. This implies that, under identical surrounding
climatic conditions, the moisture content is elevated when equilibrium moisture con-
tent (EMC) is achieved through desorption compared to absorption, as depicted in
Figure 3.4. Thus, for a specified relative humidity, the EMC during desorption sur-
passes that during absorption. Desorption is established by bringing an initial piece
of wood into equilibrium with progressively decreasing values of relative humidity.
Conversely, absorption is measured in the opposite direction, i.e., from a dry state to
sequentially higher relative humidity values. When subjected to fluctuating environ-
mental conditions over time, the wood sample undergoes a hysteresis loop [Fredrikson,
2023].

3.6
Numerical applied theory
It becomes increasingly harder to solve structural systems analytically with the in-
creasing complexity and scale of the problem looked upon. By discretizing the prob-
lem into smaller components - referred to as elements - it is possible to numerically
solve differential equations arising in engineering modelling. This method is known
as the Finite Element Method (FEM) and is widely used in multiple fields including
structural engineering.
The FEM approach is characterized by the static equation introduced in (3.39).
K · D = R
(3.39)
In (3.39) K is the stiffness matrix, which describes the system’s overall stiffness, D
is the system’s displacement vector, and R is the load vector.
FE Analysis requires that the following steps be taken:
1. To prepare the FE model, the analyst must
a) Discretize the structure or continuum by dividing it into finite elements
b) Prescribe how the structure is loaded
c) Prescribe how the structure is supported
2. To perform the calculations, the software must
a) Generate the stiffness matrix k of each element
b) Connect elements together, that is, assemble the element k matrices to
obtain the structure or ”global” matrix K
c) Assemble loads into a global load vector R
d) Impose support conditions
e) Solve the global equations stated in (3.39) for the vector D of unknowns.
In structural problems D contains displacement components of the nodes
3. Post-process the information contained in D. In stress analysis, this means
computing strains and stresses.
3.6.1
Types of errors
The use of a FE analysis introduces three types of errors:
1. Modelling Error: Accurately modelling a real structure always involves cer-
tain simplifications or assumptions, which consequently introduce discrepancies
in the computational model.
==PAGE:42==
28
3 Theory
2. Discretization Error: Both the physical structure and the mathematical
model possess infinite degrees of freedom (d.o.f), while the FE model has a
finite number of d.o.f. As the number of utilized elements increases, the dis-
cretization error decreases, resulting in a more accurate representation of the
actual structure.
3. Numerical Error: Inherent limitations in computer processing introduce nu-
merical errors through the rounding of numbers during matrix construction and
equation solving.

3.6.3
Element types
Finite elements resemble fragments of the structure. Nodes appear on element bound-
aries and serve as calculation points that fasten the elements together. The mesh is
one of the fundamental parts of finite element analyses. The mesh is a representation
of a larger geometric domain by smaller discrete cells. There are generally two types
of mesh shapes a triangular or a quadrilateral. All elements that share a node have
the same displacement components at that node. Nonetheless, each shape can rep-
resent either a plane-stress or plane-strain element, a distinction further elaborated
subsequently.
3.6.3.1
Plane-stress
The plane stress element (Corotational Plane Stress 4-node, CPS4) denotes that no
out-of-plane stresses will be generated (σ3 = 0); stresses can only exist in-plane as
defined in (3.44).
σz = σzy = σzx = 0
(3.44)
The plane-stress analysis utilises the analogy of a thin shell. This element can undergo
compression or extension within the plane of analysis, and employ Poisson’s ratio
during this process as strains will be induced in the out-of-plane direction.
3.6.3.2
Plane-strain
The plane strain element (Corotational Plasticity Enhanced 4-node, CPE4) denotes
that there can be no out-of-plane strain (ε3 = 0); it can only exist in-plane as defined
in (3.45).
εz = εzy = εzx = 0
(3.45)
This results from the constraining effects of boundary conditions (i.e. immobility) in
the off-plane direction. Instead, the fixation of movement will induce the generation
of stress.

5.3.3
Contact formulation
Applying general contact implies the program is creating contact wherever it finds
it relevant. Specifically, in the commercial software program ABAQUS the user can
specify contact pairs areas where contact should be applied. A master-slave contact
method is used to calculate the contact pair, where the master surface, is permitted
to penetrate the slave surface. An example of this is seen in Figure 5.6.
Thus, this relationship is crucial for establishing proper contact. If the slave surface
is not properly meshed, the master surface will penetrate the slave surface in an
error-prone manner. With complex geometries, where there may be sharp corners or
various surface curvatures, this is more obvious. The following guidelines should be
adhered to in order to maintain proper surface interaction:
1. The mesh on the slave surface needs to be much finer.
2. The slave surface ought to have a milder or equivalent hardness.

Normal behaviour
The response of the contact interface in a direction perpendicular to the contact sur-
faces is referred to as normal behaviour. To ensure accurate and realistic results, it
is crucial to determine the proper contact formulation and behaviour based on the
physical issue that is being simulated. The decision is frequently influenced by the
materials, boundary conditions, and desired level of simulation accuracy.
For modelling typical behaviour, ABAQUS offers a number of interaction algorithms,
including:
1. Hard Contact: In this formulation, once the contact surfaces come into touch,
they do not penetrate one another, and the normal forces arise immediately. It
presumes that the touching bodies will behave in a perfectly rigid manner.
2. Penalty Method: In order to enforce the non-penetration requirement be-
tween contact surfaces, this technique provides a penalty stiffness. The stiffness
value impacts the distribution of contact pressure and dictates the resistance to
penetration.
3. Tie Constraint: This formulation assumes that the surfaces are tied together,
and no relative motion is allowed in the normal direction. It is often used for
simulating bonded or glued interfaces.
4. Surface-to-Surface (S2S): By taking into account the geometrical interac-
tions between the surfaces, the S2S contact formulation enables a more realistic
==PAGE:61==
5.3 Utilization of ABAQUS as software
47
depiction of the contact behaviour. It computes the contact pressure distribu-
tion taking into account the actual contact area.
5.3.3.2
Tangentiel behaviour
The response of the contact interface in directions parallel to the contact surfaces
is referred to as tangential behaviour. ABAQUS provides a number of choices for
tangential behaviour definition, including:
1. Frictionless Contact: The tangential forces between the contact surfaces
cannot be exchanged, according to this formulation. This suggests perfect slip
with no resistance to tangential relative motion.
2. Coulomb Friction: Frictional behaviour between the contact surfaces is in-
troduced by this formulation. When determining the resistance to sliding or
relative motion in the tangential direction, it takes into account both static and
kinetic friction coeﬀicients.
3. Contact Pairs: Using user-defined subroutines to describe specialised contact
behaviour, ABAQUS also offers the option to build contact pairings with spec-
ified behaviour, such as setting a friction law or defining friction coeﬀicients.

5.5.1.4
Choice of modelling element
There are generally two modelling elements either a plane stress element or a plane
strain element. The plane stress element assumes that the three stress tensors out
of the plane are equal to zero as seen in (3.44). The plane strain element assumes
that all strains out of the plane are equal to zero as seen in (3.45). The plane stress
solution is suitable for modelling thin plates as the tensors out of the plane tend to go
towards zero. The plane strain solution that no elongation out of plane exhibits is a
rough assumption for this model due to the fact that the radial expansion of wood is
high, which ultimately increases the stress making a predictive creep function based
on the Time-Power Law very complicated thus the plane stress element is chosen
to accommodate for this. Thus, a coupled temperature-displacement 4-node plane
stress element (CPS4T) is chosen to accommodate for moisture changes as well as
creep behaviour.

Influence of Imperfection
The focus of the following discussion will move from nonlinearity material behaviour as

Configuration 1(1-0-0)400 (Figure 6.15b)
For the experiment, the introduction of an imperfection to the first notch noticeably
reduces the composite effect according to the experimental data, compared to the
configuration with no imperfections. This reduction in the composite effect is quanti-
fied as 6%, culminating in a greater deflection under the same load. Hence, predicting
the precise bending stiffness through purely analytical solutions may pose a challenge
for practical application.
Noteworthy, the composite effect in Table 6.7 predicted by the γ-method closely aligns
with the composite effect observed for the experimental data when one imperfection
is introduced. Despite this the γ-method does not incorporate any adjustments to the
calculations to account for the presence of one imperfection. However, the γ-method
begins to underestimate the deflection once a load of 20 kN is applied. Nonetheless,
the γ-method can reasonably accurately capture the deflection of the TCC beam when
one imperfection is introduced in a low-loading region.
On the contrary, the framework model underestimates the composite effect by around
6% when an imperfection is introduced to the first notch for the first 15 kN. This un-
derestimation is also reflected in the force-displacement curve, which lies beneath the
experimental data for the first 15 kN after which it gradually starts to under-predict
the deflection. This suggests that the γ-method provides a more accurate model once
an imperfection is introduced to the first notch, despite the fact that the γ-method
does not account for the imperfection, unlike the framework model.
The force-displacement curve of the 2D model aligns closely with the experimen-
tal data with a slight overestimation until an actuator load of 30 kN is reached after
which the deviation starts to become less noticeable. This is also demonstrated by the
slightly smaller composite effect of 74%. This suggests that the 2D model provides
a good fit for the experimental data when an imperfection is introduced at the first
notch. The γ-method and the 2D model remain superior in describing the beam’s
deflection with an imperfection at the first notch at lower loading levels.

6.4.1.3
Benefits and drawbacks of modelling approaches
The γ-method
accurately approximates the deflection of the (0-0-0)400 configu-
ration, while still providing a conservative solution.
This approach, proposed in
the new EuroCode 5, is well-suited to notched TCC beams. However, its eﬀicacy
diminishes with the introduction of imperfections, a factor unaccounted for in the
method’s calculation procedure. Given that most notched TCC beams currently are
constructed with in-situ concrete cast on top, leaving no room for imperfections, the
method can be considered reliable in these instances. Nevertheless, additional mea-
sures must be adopted for prefabricated solutions to account for the introduction
of imperfections. The advantage of this solution lies in its simplicity, relying solely
==PAGE:103==
6.4 Validation of short-term experiments
89
on analytical solutions, thus avoiding the need for numerical modelling. However,
the inconsistency of the composite effect, depending on the number and location of
imperfections, complicates its practical application. Therefore, a conservative solu-
tion is typically preferred, possibly necessitating further reduction of the connection
stiffness, for instance, utilizing a connection stiffness possible of replicating the worst
case i.e. 1(1-1-0)400 for the γ-method. EuroCode 5 suggest reducing the slip modulus
Ks by 2/3 whereby predicting the moment resistance at the Ultimate Limit States
(ULS) [EuroCode 5]. This way of predicting the ULS values is merely empirical and
should therefore be verified as it may not consider the effective stress distribution at
the structural collapse [Lamothe et al., 2021].
The framework model
incorporates imperfections and consistently provides a con-
servative prediction for the composite effect. The model notably aligns well with the
experimental data for the (0-0-0)400 and (0-0-1)400 configurations, where no, and one
imperfection to the last notch are respectively applied. On the contrary, the model
shows a lack of ability to precisely replicate the test data for (1-1-0)400. Neverthe-
less, given its reasonable approximation for prefabricated notched TCC beams with
varying imperfections and its comparatively low computational cost (attributable to
its 1D nature). The framework model presents a viable option describing a notched
TCC beam with and without imperfections.
The 2D model
outperforms the framework model in all but the (0-0-1)400 con-
figuration and demonstrates superior compatibility to the γ-method in all but the
(1-0-0)400 case where the composite effect aligned. The 2D model accounts for imper-
fections and thus seemingly being the optimal choice, however, at a cost of increased
complexity. This complexity not only dramatically increases the computational cost
relative to the other two methods but also may lead to convergence issues due to the
interactions between supports, loads, concrete, and wood. This makes the 2D model
more challenging to use and, with finer mesh applications, the simulation run-time
could become a controlling factor.
In conclusion, both the framework and 2D models demonstrate a satisfactory abil-
ity to describe the displacement behaviour in the linear-elastic region up to a load of
40 kN, both with and without the introduction of imperfections. On the other hand,
the γ-method exhibits more significant deviations once imperfections are taken into
account. These findings suggest that the framework and 2D models are better suited
for capturing the effects of imperfections and predicting the deflection behaviour of
the notched TCC beam in practical applications, providing more reliable results in
such scenarios.

6.4.3
Preliminary discussion
From the prior analysis, it is evident that imperfections significantly influence the
deflection of the notched TCC beam, the displacement of the notches and the com-
posite effect of the beam.
It has been observed that the closure of imperfections
incrementally improves the framework model’s composite effect, ultimately aligning
it with the 80% composite effect characteristic of the configuration without introduc-
ing imperfections. This outcome is anticipated, as the full effect of the notches comes
into play at that stage, allowing a similar transferability of forces. Contrarily, for the
2D model, the load required for the closure of notches in configurations 1(1-0-0)400
and 1(1-1-0)400 exceeded that of the framework model, whereas, for configuration 1(0-
0-1)400, it was lower. Furthermore, the improvement of the 2D model’s composite
effect as imperfection closes was generally lesser than that observed for the framework
model.
Noteworthy, in configurations 1(1-1-0)400 the transition from one open imperfection
to none did not lead to an increase in the composite effect for the 2D model as ex-
pected. This is explained by notch 2 not getting activated within the 40% load case
thereby not contributing to the composite effect. Another contributor to this dis-
crepancy can be the increased complexity of the 2D model as a result of the chosen
elements, contact interaction, and geometry. In simpler terms, while the framework
model captures the impact of open or closed imperfections by adjusting the composite
effect based on the displacement of the modelled springs, the 2D model’s behaviour
is more complex, with multiple modelling considerations influencing the composite
effect, not just the displacement of the notches.
A final remark deserving attention: the numerical models are modelled with linear-
elastic material properties. Predominantly in the case of the 2D model, high-stress
concentrations can occur at the notches due to the contact interaction, thus com-
plicating the stress-strain curve for the notches. This complication may potentially
cause a deviation in the model’s prediction of notch displacement from the actual
behaviour, underscoring a degree of limitation within the model’s approximation.

7.1.1.3
Transition from elastic to time-dependent behaviour
Initially, the time-dependent deflection across various configurations is presented in
Figure 7.1. It is crucial to emphasize that in both modelling procedures, the shrink-
age strains were applied during the transition from a loading phase to the time-
dependency phase, ”time zero”. The models propose that the TCC beam undergoes
loading 28 days subsequent to its fabrication, culminating in the incorporation of the
shrinkage strains spanning from day 0 to day 28, being imposed instantaneously at
this point. This introduction of shrinkage strains is applied to the entire concrete
element, resulting in the contraction of the concrete beam, thereby widening the gap
between the concrete and the wood interaction surface. This increases the deforma-
tion of the beam ultimately resulting in the gap being closed, thus, the introduction
of shrinkage leads to an instantaneous increase in deflection. Nevertheless, within
the framework model, the combined moisture content change and shrinkage strain
lead to a decrease in deflection during the transition, originating from the swelling
strains surpassing the initial shrinkage strain. An essential component of the frame-
work model is the application of a boundary condition inducing a moisture content

alteration across the entire beam, resulting in a reduction in mid-deflection at time
zero. The primary factor behind this phenomenon is that the initial moisture con-
tent of the beam is set to 10.5%. As the simulation transitions from a static general
state (after load application) to a visco step, the boundary condition regarding the
moisture content evolves. Initially, on January 1st, the moisture content is set to 8%,
which corresponds to a decrease in the swelling strains at the initial time, thereby
reducing displacement. In contrast, the 2D model, which only considers a moisture
content change at the free surface perimeter, does not result in a reduction in mid-
displacement at time zero. The initial changes in moisture content to the free surface
perimeter increase elastic strains, resulting in a slightly elevated total strain in the
bottom of the wood, resulting in a minor increase in deflection. Thus, as swelling
strains decrease, elastic strains increase, and this trend can be explained by the con-
traction of the wooden section of the beam as swelling decreases. The notches in the
2D model resist contraction causing an increase in stress concentration around the
notches. This principle also applies to an increase in swelling strains caused by a
decrease in elastic strain.
7.1.1.4
Annual fluctuations in deflection
Upon closer examination of Figure 7.1, it becomes evident that the framework model
exhibits substantial annual fluctuations in deflection, primarily driven by the changing
moisture content within the beam. The highest deflection each year aligns perfectly
with the annual local extrema of the moisture content. This observation supports
the anticipation that the contraction of the wooden beam, induced by diminishing
moisture content, increases the deformation of the springs, subsequently amplifying
spring force and the resulting composite effect. In addition, the elastic strains propa-
gate further due to the decrease in the modulus of elasticity. These variables amplify
the total strain to its maximum level on that specific day, resulting in the most sub-
stantial deflection. Additionally, it is observed that the local fluctuation in amplitude
is elevated with an increased notch effect of notch 1 and 2. This highlights that the
configurations with no imperfections on these notches yield the most extreme annual
deflection amplitude. Indicating that notch 1 and 2 are more sensitive to moisture
content fluctuations.
The matching mechanisms are present in the 2D model, where the maximum mois-
ture content applied to the beam’s exposed surface perimeter generates the highest
deflection. However, the yearly variance in deflection is noticeably less pronounced
compared to the framework model. This reduction in annual fluctuations can be as-
cribed to two key factors: firstly, the moisture content only interacts with the beam’s
external surface perimeter in the 2D model, and secondly, the inclusion of moisture
diffusion. The combination of these elements results in less uniform strain modifi-
cations across the beam with fluctuation in moisture content. It deserves emphasis
to mention that an increase in swelling strain correlates to a drop in elastic strain,
thereby resulting in a minor increase in the total strain within the lower fibres of the

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
A discussion of the three proposed models to clarify the time-dependent behaviour
of a notched TCC beam will be done in the following chapter. The emphasis will be
placed on the suggested improvements, advantages, and limitations of these models.
8.1 γ-method
Within the modelling methodology of the γ-method, there was evident negligence in
accommodating the imperfections inherent to the prefabricated concrete and timber
components. The integration of these imperfections is essential for accurately predict-
ing the behaviour of prefabricated notched TCC beams, as shown in the validation
of short-term behaviour. Herein, the γ-method displayed an insuﬀicient capability to
predict alterations in composite effect. This insuﬀiciency was emphasised during the
subsequent analyses wherein discrepancies between the γ-method and the numerical
models across various configurations underestimated the average deflection. In light
of design considerations and practical application, a conservative approach dictates
that the most damaging scenario is to be selected. For short-term test experiments,
the deflection of configuration 1(1-1-0)400 is considered predominant. However, under
time-dependent loading, the focus might go towards 1(0-0-1)400, particularly since
the 2D model projects increased deformations for this specific configuration. Yet, it
should be remarked that the framework model predominantly exhibited maximum
deflection in 1(1-1-0)400. Hence, a thorough experimental examination of real-time
data is crucial to comprehensively address this concern.
In the validation of the time-dependent analysis as specified in Section 6.5, it was
observed that the γ-method exhibited significant discrepancies when compared with
the experimental data. The analysis demonstrated that the γ-method persistently
underestimated deflection, particularly under diminishing loads. Such observations
suggest that the chosen subset of time-dependent phenomena inadequately repre-
sents the time-dependent deflection of a notched TCC beam. One hypothesis is that
the incorporation of shrinkage might yield a considerable influence on this.
As a
consequence, an improved modelling methodology accounting for shrinkage may be
considered.
A potential approach to address the shrinkage effect might involve introducing supple-
==PAGE:142==
128
8 Discussion
mentary stress, regardless of the fact that shrinkage primarily contributes to a strain
increment over time without inherent stresses. This concept is supported by a number
of researchers, including Elaghoury and Bartlett [2019], Gilbert Professor [2001], who
propose enlarged internal forces, which subsequently induce an increased deflection
in the context of purely reinforced concrete beams. Dias et al. [2018b] advise that
although the incorporation of shrinkage in concrete in the context of a notched TCC
beam results in stress-free strains, the potential of slip can be hindered by connectors,
thereby inducing eigenstresses. This introduces a level of complexity that restricts the
possibility of a purely analytic solution. Moreover, the combined effects of creep and
shrinkage further complicate the computation process by necessitating the inclusion
of additional creep coeﬀicients. These complications suggest that deriving a purely
analytic solution for a notched TCC beam might present significant challenges. Yet,
if a simplified expression for a shrinkage-induced force increment could be developed,
it might address these issues and yield an acceptably accurate outcome. Nonetheless,
it is important to note that such an investigation extends beyond the current project
but could be interesting in regard to future work.
8.2
Numerical models
Within the scope of this study, an essential element is based on the numerical mod-
els and their subsequent predictions. During the preliminary validation of short-term
behaviour, it became evident that both the framework and 2D model demonstrated ad-
mirable accuracy in predicting the behaviour of the prefabricated notched TCC beam
and the accompanying imperfections arising from the prefabrication. Furthermore,
in examining the time-dependent behaviour the precision with which these numerical
models could predict the experimental deformations, with the proposed modelling
methodologies employed, is admirable. These two findings emphasise the potential
of using these numerical models in simulations related to prefabricated notched TCC
beams, especially in the context of short-term and time-dependent test simulations
that introduce imperfections.
In a case study analysis with an initial moisture content set at 10.5% and a load
parameter of 30 kN, it was observed that both the framework and 2D model mir-
rored the average deflection predictions. However, when the initial moisture content
was altered; discrepancies amplified between the two models, primarily due to the
distinct methodologies deployed for capturing moisture content variances. Notably,
the framework model displayed significant variations with changes in moisture, thus
signifying the crucial importance of precise moisture content measurement for the
effectual utilization of this model. Further, the absence of moisture diffusion in the
framework model implies an obvious area for improvement; to increase its accuracy,
incorporating this phenomenon is essential, necessitating the implementation of ap-
propriate sub-routines. This feature is inherently present in the 2D model through
the material properties. Additionally, the introduction of temperature-dependent ma-

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
This study demonstrated the essential role of imperfections in shaping the behaviour
of prefabricated notched TCC beams. Through a detailed investigation of various im-
perfection configurations and their interplay with time-dependent deflection, several
noteworthy observations have come to light.
Firstly, this study demonstrated the influence of improved composite effects on de-
flection behaviour. This highlighted the complicated interplay between imperfections
and time-dependent phenomena introducing a layer of complexity when imperfections
are present. It was shown that less imperfection positively influences the average de-
flection, due to the improved composite effect, making the ideal configuration one
without imperfections. Yet, a beneficial impact on the time-dependent deflection was
seen when imperfections were introduced to notch 1, as noted in configuration 1(1-0-
0)400, and to both notches 1 and 2, as seen in configuration 1(1-1-0)400. This effect
was largely due to the unrestrained movement around notch 1. This emphasized the
essential role imperfections played in notch displacement, closely intertwined with
shrinkage and moisture content variations. Although individually of modest magni-
tude, the cumulative effects of strains indicate tendencies, where the interrelationship
between strain and deflection behaviours across different configurations provided in-
sights into the amplification or mitigation of deflection. Here, imperfections, espe-
cially at notch 3, play a prominent role in intensifying strains and magnifying deflec-
tion. The significance of creep strains, as highlighted by the elevated overall deflection
response in configuration 1(0-0-1)400. Further, the study reveals that the presence
of imperfections, particularly within the first two notches, appears to diminish the
time-dependent deflection.
Secondly, the comparative analysis between the γ-method, framework and 2D models
uncovers a complex relationship between the numerical models; while the frame-
work model adeptly adjusts composite effects based on displacement, the 2D model’s
behaviour is notably more complex, influenced by various modelling considerations
and increased dimensions. The 2D model exhibits pronounced stress concentrations
around the notches, necessitating further analysis, and resulting in discrepancies in
deflection predictions when compared to the framework model. The elevated complex-
ity increases the computation cost and introduces additional convergence issues. The

γ-method tends to underestimate the time-dependent deflection, while both numerical
models adeptly represent the time-dependent phenomena as evident from the valida-
tion of time-dependent deflection. Consequently, there are substantial time benefits
to be gained by employing a framework model.
Future research could potentially
mitigate some drawbacks of the framework model by incorporating sub-routines.
Collectively, these observations emphasize the many different aspects of utilising pre-
fabricated notched TCC beams due to potential inherent imperfections and their in-
tricate interplay at the contact interaction surfaces. Such insights provide invaluable
guidance for structural design and engineering practices, necessitating a comprehen-
sive understanding of the complex behaviours that govern prefabricated notched TCC
beams under long-term loading.
