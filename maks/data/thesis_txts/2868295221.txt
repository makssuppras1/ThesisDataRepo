Abstract
A study has been conducted on the performance of three implementations of LiDAR based
navigation approaches for application in GNSS denied environments. The initial two ICP
models are introduced using point to plane correspondence matching both with and with­
out Robust outlier rejection. Additionally, a feature based approach is implemented using
ground segmentation and edge detection algorithms. The methods’ performance is evalu­
ated on their ability to accurately determine the position of the UGV using data GNSS/INS
solutions as well as the KISS­ICP algorithm. Three test scenarios were used for the com­
parisons including UGV in a simple test environment and on campus with both a UGV and
a Van. Results indicate that ICP algorithms have the potential to assist in navigation in
GNSS inhibited environments, however, only for a limited distance. The study also shows
the difficulties associated with producing a viable feature based solution.

Introduction
Autonomous vehicles have long been a subject of science fiction which has long been an
inspiration for both authors and engineers alike. The applications of autonomous vehicles
are enormous and it is therefore a topic which has attracted much interest. A critical part of
any autonomous vehicle is the ability to know where the vehicle is located. This seemingly
easy problem has been cause of many headaches as no singular technique is known to be
optimal in all situations. For Instance, GNSS would seem to be a generally good position
estimator, but what if the signal is blocked or somehow interfered with? A single meter
of error can be the difference between driving on the road or halfway onto a sidewalk.
GNSS alone is therefore not enough, as these issues are not uncommon and are difficult
to completely avoid.
UGVs based services such as Starship Technologies[1] are beginning to make headway
in cities across the globe, and all require adequate positioning techniques in urban areas.
Urban areas can be particularly troublesome in this regard as GNSS signals may be ob­
structed and multipath contribute to worsened accuracy. Figure 1.1 shows an example of
an unmanned ground vehicle (UGV) with a GNSS receiver trying to generate positioning
solutions outside of DTU Space. This urban environment, in which the UGV is located,
has a clear impact on the performance of GNSS based solutions.

Positioning Estimation
2.1
GNSS
Global Navigation Satellite System is the general term used to describe the method of
estimating your position using signals from satellites orbiting Earth. Within GNSS, there
are several separate systems such as the American GPS and the European Galileo satel­
lites. These systems vary in orbit constellation and in the signal structure itself, however,
all have the same exact goal: To allow computation of a position a certain point in time[3].
The satellites do this by sending out a very precise timestamp which is synchronised with
all the other satellites in their constellation. This can be used to determine the distance
between the satellite and the signal receiver in it’s simplest form by using Equation 2.1[3]:
d = c · (tr −ts)
(2.1)
Where c is the speed of light and tr is the time at which the signal arrives at the receiver
sent from a satellite at the time ts. As the speed of light is high, it can be seen from Equa­
tion 2.1 that a small error in the timestamps can result in distance errors. For reference,
an error of 1/1000 s results in an error of 300 km, and so it becomes evident that extreme
clock precision is needed to achieve positions of any value.
And herein lies some of the faults of the GNSS positioning method. As the position esti­
mation is solely reliant determining accurate time measurements, any interference of the
signal between the satellite and the receiver can lead to inaccurate results. One of such
interferences can occur due to multi­path. Multi­path is the term used for what occurs
when the receiver manages to acquire a signal which has been reflected off of surfaces
before hitting the receiver[3].
Figure 2.1: Illustration of multi­path[4]
This is particularly common in urban areas where tall buildings can obscure the direct
GNSS signal, but also function as reflectors for signals which are propagating in other
directions, which the receiver may detect. As the least squares solution assumes a direct
path, a multi­path observation will lead to errors in the final solution as the signal can be
off by several meters[3].
In addition to the multi­path problem, it is possible for a vehicle or person to be at a location
where the signal from a large range of satellites are simply blocked. This means that a
smaller number of satellites will be visible to the receiver, and the results will be worsened.

The accuracy of a positioning solution is also dependent on the geometry of the visible
satellites, and small changes in the atmosphere, ionosphere and simply some clock drift
will accumulate and lead to worsened results[3].
The final case in which GNSS methods will result in no positioning solutions occurs when
there are no longer 4 satellites detected by the receiver. This can for example occur while
driving into a parking garage, or standing next to large tall structures where the signal will
be blocked by the concrete between the receiver and the satellite above. In cases like
these, the limits of the GNSS positioning solution become the most clear, and so other
methods that can either enhance the GNSS solution or even completely replace it are
needed.
2.2
LiDAR
The acronym LiDAR stands for ”light detection and ranging”[5] and is simply put a laser
transceiver which can determine distances within it’s field of view at different angles with
respect to itself. LiDAR is implemented using pulsed or modulated laser beams to detect
the travel time from the LiDAR to a specific object by using Equation 2.2[6]:
dobject = c · ttravel
(2.2)
Where ttravel is the travel time from the LiDAR to an object at a distance dobject for the laser
beam which moves at the speed of light c. As the LiDAR is likely to see returned signal
from particles in the air, buildings, cars and the ground, the resulting combination of angles
measurements and range estimates will generate a 3D image of the surroundings[5].
Figure 2.2: Illustration of the LiDAR concept to detect and image objects[6]
This ranging ability is where the true strength of the LiDAR comes from, as the observer
will be able to produce an accurate 3D map of the surroundings without the need for ad­
vanced processing of stereo 2D images which can be taken by a cameras. The LiDAR
is also not sensitive to the lighting situation of the scene, and so no additional effort has
to be made in night lighting or due to mirrored reflections[5]. Unlike the GNSS approach
to distance measurements, LiDARs do not require any communications in order to func­
tion properly[5]. This gives LiDARs the unique advantage of being able to be used for
navigation in environments where camera­based or GNSS navigation techniques would
fail.
The LiDAR is, however, reliant on a reflected signal. The signal is sensitive to both reflec­
tivity of different materials as well as atmospheric affects which may introduce some error
or simply less data concerning a particular object. This means that in environments where

there is very low reflectivity, the LiDAR will have a difficult time producing a dense point
cloud as there won’t be any return signals with which to measure distances. In addition,
if a LiDAR is placed in a scene with very few objects, for instance a flat desert, a LiDAR
based positioning approach will also encounter difficulties in determining directions as the
whole area will seem symmetric.
In general, the LiDAR despite potential atmospheric interference is quite accurate in de­
termining distances with errors of the Livox MID­360 being 2­3 cm[7].
2.2.1
LiDAR Categories
The imaging mechanism of LiDARs are classified in three categories[5]:
• Mechanical LiDARs
• scanning solid­state LiDARs
• flash LiDARs with non scanning architecture
Additionally LiDARs can also be categorized by their signal types which are comprised
of[5]:
• Time of Flight (ToF)
• Amplitude Modulated Continuous Wave (AMCW)
• Frequency Modulated Continuous Wave (FMCW)
The primarily used LiDARs in odometry currently are the scanning solid state LiDARs
which use either ToF or FMCW. This is because the scanning solid state LiDAR eliminates
the need for mechanical rotations by for example applying mirror Microelectromechanical
technology[5]. This both increases the life span of a traditional mechanical LiDAR and
maintains the ability to scan across a certain FOV[5].
ToF LiDARs operate as shown in Figure 2.2 by emitting a pulse of light, and calculating the
travel time. This is a straight forward method which does provide high resolution distance
measurements[5]. One limitation of this method is, however, it’s susceptibility to noise
in an environment with many light sources. FMCW functions by changing the frequency
of the projected light and analyzing the frequency shift of reflected light. The shift is pro­
portional to an objects distance and can therefore be used to measure distances[5]. This
method reduces the effects of interference and can be used to determine the velocities
of the objects within a scene, making this method particularly powerful. This method is,
however, pricier in general which should be taken into account when deciding on a LiDAR
for a particular system[5].
LiDARS can furthermore be categorized based on the dimension the LiDAR is capable
of measuring. These are simply referred to as 1D, 2D and 3D LiDARs[5]. A 1D LiDAR is
the simplest implementation. A 1D LiDAR is capable of determining the range to objects
that are in the same axis as the laser beam as shown in Figure 2.3[8]. This means that
there are no rotating elements, and only the objects located in this axis will be detected.

2.2.2
The Point Cloud
As the LIDAR registers both an angle and a distance, these can easily be converted into
a Cartesian coordinate system w.r.t. the LIDAR. These points that are either spherical or
Cartesian coordinates are known as the point cloud, and will at a given time represent
information about the surfaces in the surrounding environment.
Figure 2.6: Illustration of two point cloud images taken of a rabbit at different angles[9]
Let us assume that we have 2 LiDAR point clouds taken at different positions which are
relatively close to each other. We know from our own vision that it is possible to somewhat
accurately know how much one must have moved from 2 such positions. The same
principle is in effect what point clouds are used for. They are images of almost all the
same objects taken at different angles and distances. This creates the basis from which
position estimations can be generated, as the initial point cloud for the most part is the
same point cloud but with some translation and rotation.
Sampling Strategies
As point clouds may have a large number of points, computation times can often become
large and cumbersome. Finding methods which can reduce computation times therefore
becomes an important aspect of the ICP algorithm, especially if real time positioning is
needed[10]. Finding ways to reduce the amount of points while maintaining good accuracy
is key in producing a high quality ICP algorithm.
The first step one can take to reduce data points is through voxelization. Voxelization
simply partitions the data into a 3D mesh, which is of a lower quality than that of the point
cloud itself. This can help when there are multiple points measured at almost the exact
same point, and also simplifies structures which may help combat over fitting models[10].
An additional step taken is statistical outlier removal, where a statistical model is employed
on regions in the point cloud from which outliers are detected and removed. Given a point
pi, we create a model based on the nearest Np points. This can be done by assuming that
the points within a region follow a Gaussian distribution. From this distribution a certain
mean and standard deviation can be determined within this local region[10]. For example,
the x­coordinates the distribution can be determined by Equation 2.3:

2.2.6
Initial Transformation Matrix
All though some algorithms may be more susceptible than others at finding local minima,
all ICP algorithms have this issue. Another technique which can be used to overcome
some of the local minima, and which can improve computation times, is by initializing the
ICP algorithm with a good initial guess of transformation matrix[10]. The initialization of
the transformation matrix can help improve performance and computation time as a good
initial transformation matrix can help avoid certain minima, and may also be closer to the
real truth.
To find a good initial transformation matrix estimate, the physics of the LiDAR movement
may be taken into account. This could be constraining accelerations to physically plausi­
ble values, or applying knowledge from additional sensors to the calculations[5].
In the KISS­ICP algorithm they have taken a very simple approach to this transformation
initialization by simply assuming that the movement is constant between point cloud im­
ages[16]. Then the small accelerations or decelerations can be accounted for by applying
the ICP algorithm[16].
2.2.7
Featurebased Sampling
Feature­based sampling is another sampling strategy which aims to maintain high accu­
racy and reduce computation times. The algorithm is able to achieve this by applying an
algorithm which is capable of detecting specific points which contain the most necessary
information on the structures in a point cloud.
There are multiple methods that can be applied in this regard, but for this Thesis, much
of the feature extraction logic will be based on the LOAM[17] and LeGO­LOAM[18] algo­
rithms which achieved good performance.
LOAM is a feature based algorithm which aims to match sharp edges and planar patches
with one another in order to determine the pose of a LiDAR[17]. This means that LOAM
had to determine a method within a point cloud environment to detect edges and planes
reliably and accurately. For this, LOAM set some rules for selected feature points[17]:
• The number of selected points or planar points cannot exceed the maximum of the
subregion (2)
• None of its surrounding points are already selected
• It cannot be on a surface patch that is roughly parallel to the laser beam, or on the
boundary to an occluded region.
The result of applying this algorithm can be seen in Figure 2.10, where the indoor edges
and planar patches are detected.

edges and the ground plane instead of the more general approach used in LOAM[18].
This is achieved by initially converting the Cartesian point cloud into a range image with
an angular resolution close to that of the LiDAR[18]. This requires transforming the point
cloud into spherical coordinates, with the radius values acting as the pixel value which
is positioned according to it’s angular coordinates. Then The point cloud is segmented
into Ground and non­ground points using a ground detector[18]. Vertical edges are then
found afterwards on the non­ground points in the range image[18].
Ground Detector
To create a good ground detector, the pseudocode made by D. Zerma et. al[19] seen in
Figure 2.11 is used. The main parameters to fine tune in this algorithm are NLPR, Thseeds,
and Thdist. LPR is the Lowest Point Representative, which is set to the average of the
NLPR lowest points in the point cloud[19].

Figure 2.11: Pseudo code of ground detector [19]
It can be seen that the ground is found by assuming that it can be defined by a plane
made up by the lowest points within the point cloud. This is true in many cases, however,
the ground is not always flat, meaning that the plane model would not be able to detect
an environment where the ground slopes differently around the observer.
To accommodate this case, the range image is applied to the algorithm instead of using the
Cartesian point cloud directly. By using the range image, it is possible to derive multiple
planes for each column in the image, which should improve the accuracy of the ground
detector[18]. This would essentially result in using Figure 2.11 as a function which can be
used as input to a for loop for each individual column.
After the points which belong to the ground have been selected, it is possible to separate
the point cloud into 2 separate labels which will be defined as PG for ground points and
PNG of not ground points.
Edge Detector
After the ground has been defined, the vertical edges can be found by applying an algo­
rithm to PNG. Initially, the points in PNG are segmented into clusters[18]. This is done so
that smaller, perhaps moving objects can be filtered out of the edge detection algorithm.
For example, the LiDAR is unlikely to measure the same point on a leaf with two separate
measurements, so points like these are to be removed.
Afterwards the edges themselves can be determined efficiently using fewer overall points.

The ground and edge points each have their strength when it comes to the ICP algorithm.

3.5.3
Initial Transformation Matrix
For this report, a similar logic is followed to that of KISS­ICP[16]. After each individual
registration, the output transformation matrix will become the initial transformation matrix
for the next point cloud. This should in effect result in a similar assumption to that used in
KISS­ICP. Assuming that the point cloud collection is conducted at a constant frequency,
this should result in an assumption of constant translational velocity as well as rotational.
This approach does have the drawback that inherent bias in the algorithm or the data ac­
quisition may lead to small transformations which will be amplified between registrations.
This can lead to the point cloud drifting or rotating while the observer is still.
3.5.4
Range Image
The range image is made using spherical coordinates, and inserting range values into the
respective bins. The important part of the image is mainly specifying the resolution. Due
to the complexity of the feature based algorithm, several resolutions are experimented
with based on a minimum resolution set by the MID360 LiDAR. The angle resolution val­
ues tested were in the range σr = 1.3◦−3.39◦where a final resolution of σr = 1.95◦.
Worse resolution should provide simpler structures and improve the accuracy of the edge
detector itself, however will limit the lowest resolution of the final ICP algorithm.
As multiple points may exist within a single pixel of the range image, a method must be
used to accommodate this. For this algorithm feature based algorithm, the mean of the
range values that are found within a single pixel are used as the pixel range. This has the
effect of potentially maintaining some information about all the points within a pixel, but
may contribute to a smoothing out of certain edges.

as some of the optimization will occur using the other parameters. This could be solved

a large amount of time to implement, it was decided not to implement as the feature
extraction itself is of higher priority and effect.
5.2
Learnings
The major difficulty for the feature based approach stems from the edge detector algo­
rithm. The idea behind the Feature based approach is to reduce the number of points
in the point cloud to the key information holding edge points. If the edge detector is not
able to accurately detect not just the edges, but the same edges between range images
consistently, the method will not work. Figure 5.9 shows the c­image of a point cloud
looking at what is essentially a straight wall. All though it can be seen that the roughness
values of c are large at the ends, certain outliers and data gaps also result in high rough­
ness estimates. This seemingly inconsistent calculation of roughness values leads to an
algorithm where it is difficult to trust in consistent detection of edges.
A potential idea which could possibly simplify the calculations for edge detection would
be by using a CNN based edge detector. It is known to be possible to implement CNN
networks to be trained to detect edges within an image[26]. Training a network on a
number of labeled range images may yield an easily programmable alternative to the
classical feature detection algorithm used in this project[27]. It is, however, evident from
algorithms such as LeGO­LOAM that the classical approach is achievable and can yield
good results[18] despite the poor results found in this project.
All though the Robust ICP algorithm seems to be functioning reasonably well, it still lacks
those final meters worth of accuracy in order to be successfully implemented. One aspect
of the current algorithm which may improve performance is the use of an adaptive robust
kernel. Currently, the robust kernel used in the algorithm is static, meaning the same
statistical model is applied in each image. This works well enough in environments where
the point cloud is dense and with many viable features, but in poorer environments moving
objects may cause issues for the static robust kernel[10]. An adaptive robust kernel would
be able to adapt it’s outlier rejection techniques based on a given situation. KISS­ICP has
also implemented this type of outlier rejection and may be a contributing factor to the
superior accuracy of KISS­ICP[16]. However, based on the limited effect of using the
robust loss function compared to the simple point to plane method, it would seem that a
highly urban environment still produces enough valuable features so that these functions
do not ezplain the deviation between models.

Another, perhaps more important change which should be made to the Robust ICP al­
gorithm is to sequentially add the point cloud images together into a global point cloud
while conducting ICP. Currently the algorithm only compares two point clouds with one
another, which is the most basic approach. By using a global point cloud to transform
the next point cloud onto, the resulting target point cloud should be much denser, and
would therefore improve likelihood that the next point cloud will have the best data point
correspondences possible.
Even with these improvements, it would be questionable to believe that these alone would
put the Robust algorithm on par with KISS ICP, but even if we were to assume that was
the case, the ICP algorithms can not replace the positioning solutions of GNSS/INS in the
general case. It is, however, evident that when the signals from GNSS are obstructed or
in another way interfered with, a LiDAR based ICP approach to position estimation would
be a viable alternative for a limited amount distance as demonstrated in the experiments
above. The horizontal component in particular seems accurate, which is another argu­
ment for the use of such algorithms on UGVs, as the height component is not necessarily
critical for ground navigation purposes.

Conclusion
In conclusion, two ICP algorithms have been successfully implemented and tested in three
different testing environments. No major impact has been observed by implementing the
robust kernel compared to the results achieved using the simpler point to plane model as
seen during the car experiment in Figure 5.1 and in the UGV driving around campus in
Figure 4.12. The algorithms were bench­marked against KISS­ICP[16] which has been
found to be significantly more accurate than the models produced in this report, however
the pure LiDAR based approach still shows significant drift after a certain amount of time
as seen in Figure 5.1. Deviations in the z­axis are particularly large for all ICP algorithms,
and Figure 5.6 showed significant drift in the roll and pitch orientations which are a con­
tributing factor to the large z­axis deviations that have been observed.
Applying KISS­ICP on the Figure 1.1 in the introduction, Figure 6.1 shows that at least
semi accurate and stable position estimates are achievable with the LiDAR data alone. In
addition Figure 6.1 shows that if a suitable combination of GNSS/INS and LiDAR based
positioning estimates are used, it is possible for them to generate better positioning solu­
tions in difficult urban environments such as that experienced near DTU Space.
