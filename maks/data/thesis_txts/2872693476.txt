Abstract  
Transient gene expression (TGE) in HEK293 cells is a major platform for the production 
of viral vectors and other advanced biologics. However, its scalability is severely limited 
by the cell density effect (CDE), a phenomenon characterized by a sharp decline in spe-
cific productivity at high cell densities (HCD). Understanding and mitigating this phenom-
enon could have a substantial impact on the manufacturing costs and ultimately the ac-
cessibility of advanced therapeutics to patients. The main objective of this thesis is to 
investigate the extracellular matrix (ECM) as potential target for cell line engineering to 
mitigate the CDE and increase the productivity of HEK293 cells. For this, HEK293 knock-
out cell lines were generated through CRISPR-Cas9 targeting of specific genes involved 
in the biosynthesis and homeostasis of the ECM, and tested for their HIV-1 Gag virus-like 
particle (VLP) and recombinant adeno-associated virus (rAAV) productivities. The double 
xylosyltransferase1 and 2 knockout cell line (XYLT1-/-;XYLT2-/-) demonstrated a significant 
mitigation of the CDE, largely restoring VLP productivity at HCD even in fed-batch condi-
tions, without the removal of the exhausted medium. While more targeted deletions of the 
CS biosynthesis through the double CSGALNACT1-/-;CSGALNACT2-/- or the single 
VCAN-/-, and HSPG2-/- deletions yielded some benefits for VLP production, intervention 
upstream of the GAG biosynthesis pathway was a more effective strategy. These results 
did not directly translate to the rAAV production, where HSPG2-/- and CSGALNACT1-/-
;CSGALNACT2-/- cells exhibited the highest productivity at high cell density (HCD), albeit 
at significantly lower levels compared to low cell density (LCD). The present work provides 
strong evidence that engineering of the ECM is a viable strategy to mitigate the cell density 
effect, providing a clear path towards the development of a CDE-tolerant HEK293 cell line.

1. Introduction  
1.1 Biopharmaceutical production landscape  
Mammalian expression systems have become indispensable in modern biotechnology 
due to their unparalleled ability to produce complex therapeutic molecules. These mole-
cules, known as biopharmaceuticals or biologics, encompass a range of products includ-
ing recombinant proteins, nucleic acids and cell or tissue-based therapies. The global 
market for biopharmaceuticals reached an estimated $570 billion in 2023, marking a sub-
stantial increase from $343 billion in 2021, driven by several factors, including continued 
innovation, increased R&D investments, and rising demands from the healthcare system. 
One of the main drivers for this market growth is recent advancements in therapeutic 
technologies, mainly represented by the emergence of monoclonal antibody (mAb) ther-
apies, which accounted for 80% of total protein-based biopharmaceutical sales in 2021 1. 
The contribution of COVID-19 is also evident by the surge in regulatory approvals that 
persisted even after the pandemic was over, as it showcased the capacity of the biophar-
maceutical industry for rapid innovation and increased the flexibility of regulatory bodies 
for all types of pharmaceuticals 1. Beyond such public health crises, long-term demo-
graphic and health trends also fuel the demand for biopharmaceuticals. The rising global 
population and increasing prevalence of chronic diseases, to which the World Health Or-
ganization (WHO) attributes 74% of all annual deaths worldwide, are major contributors 
to the growing demand for novel therapeutics. In response, companies are heavily invest-
ing in research and development of products that target these conditions. The cell and 
gene therapy (CGT) market is one prominent example in the biopharmaceutical space 
that has gained a lot of momentum in recent years. Cell and gene therapies offer unique 
solutions to diseases previously considered untreatable, by targeting the underlying ge-
netic and molecular drivers. These two biopharmaceuticals are interconnected as they 
both utilize gene delivery methods like viral vectors; these vectors can serve as the prod-
uct itself in gene therapy or as an essential tool to genetically modify the cells used in cell 
therapies. The year 2024 was significant for the CGT space, with the approval of 9 new 
products, bringing the total number of cell and gene therapies listed on the U.S. Food and 
Drug Administration (FDA) website to 44 (35 excluding the HPC cord blood products) as 
of May 2025 2. Specifically, 10 gene therapy products are currently on the market, includ-
ing 2 approved in 2024, all of which are adeno-associated virus (AAV)-based therapies. 
Despite the challenges the gene therapy market is facing in 2025, investment opportuni-
ties still exist, fueling the drive for continued innovation to make these transformative treat-
ments more broadly accessible and effective. 
 
1.2 AAV-based gene therapies  
Gene therapy is broadly defined as interventions that modify gene expression by admin-
istering a healthy gene (gene replacement), suppressing the expression of a pathogenic 
gene (gene silencing), or correcting a specific mutation inside the cells (gene editing) 3. 
Gene replacement therapy, in particular, can be performed ex vivo, where cells are genet-
ically modified outside the body and then administered to the patient, or in vivo, where 
genetic material is delivered directly into the patient to target cells or tissues 4. Effective in 
vivo gene delivery requires vectors to protect the nucleic acid from degradation, while 
==PAGE:5==
 
5 
facilitating effective transportation through the cell membrane and ultimately into the nu-
cleus. While non-viral methods exist, viral vectors are often favored for their high efficiency 
in gene transfer. Among these, adeno-associated viruses (AAVs), small, non-pathogenic 
human parvoviruses, first discovered as contaminants in adenovirus preparations in the 
late 1950s, have emerged as leading candidates for in vivo gene therapy 5–7.  
AAVs have a 4.7 Kb genome containing the Rep and Cap genes, flanked by 145bp T-
shaped inverted terminal repeats (ITRs) 8,9. Four promoters, in combination with alterna-
tive splicing events give rise to ten proteins, four Rep (Rep78/68 and Rep52/40) and six 
Cap (VP1/VP2/VP3, AAP, MAAP, and X) 10. The AAV genome is encapsidated into an 
icosahedral, non-enveloped capsid formed through the self-assembly of the VP proteins, 
often with the help of the assembly-activating protein (AAP) 11. The main function of the 
Rep proteins is the initialization of replication of the rAAV genome, a process closely as-
sociated with the integration into the preintegration site AAVS1 of chromosome 19 9. A 
successful AAV infection typically requires the co-infection of a helper virus, which can be 
either an adenovirus (Ad) or any type of herpesvirus 12. At least 12 naturally occurring 
serotypes have been discovered, with varying degree of tropism for human tissues 13. AAV 
infections are generally considered asymptomatic and are found worldwide, with sero-
positivity reaching over 90% for AAV2 14. Its strong safety profile, along with the diverse 
tissue tropism and capacity for long-term gene expression, have placed AAV as one of 
the most promising gene therapy vectors 15. 
In order to be turned into effective gene therapy vectors, AAVs need to be engineered into 
recombinant AAVs (rAAVs). rAAV vectors are produced by replacing the native viral coding 
sequences with a therapeutic gene expression cassette, which is flanked by the AAV's 
inverted terminal repeats (ITRs). The complete removal of viral coding sequences in-
creases the packaging capacity of rAAVs, while reducing the immunogenicity and cyto-
toxicity risk involved in the in vivo delivery 7. The original helper virus functions are either 
provided by co-transfected plasmids, containing Ad genes like, E2A and E4orf6, or sub-
stituted by other helper viruses, depending on the production platform used. Due to their 
significant manufacturing complexity, recombinant adeno-associated viruses (rAAVs) are 
produced exclusively in eukaryotic cell-based platforms, where they remain one of the 
most challenging biopharmaceutical products to manufacture. 
 
1.3 VLP production and vaccine development 
The significant manufacturing complexity of rAAVs is not unique; it reflects the inherent 
challenges of producing any viral-based biologic. From its early days, the effort to harness 
viruses for medical purposes has faced numerous obstacles, yet monumental achieve-
ments in immunology and vaccine development have steadily propelled this field forward. 
Edward Jenner (1749-1823) is celebrated as the scientist who, after careful observation, 
testing and publication of his results, introduced vaccination to the world and eradicated 
smallpox 16. Although he was neither the first to observe that infection with cowpox con-
ferred immunity to smallpox, nor the first to practice inoculation with cowpox for this rea-
son, his work laid the foundations of immunology and vaccination 17. Following Jenner’s 
work and the establishment of vaccination as one of the most effective methods for pre-
venting infectious diseases in humans and livestock, numerous human and veterinary 
vaccines have been developed, saving an estimated 150 million lives in the past 50 years 
18. Recently, the COVID-19 pandemic recapitulated the critical importance of vaccines in 
==PAGE:6==
 
6 
combating global health crises. Notably, the development and rapid approval of genetic 
(DNA and RNA) vaccines were pivotal in combatting the pandemic. These vaccines, un-
like other types, rely on delivering genetic material encoding antigens, so that an immune 
response is generated against the antigenic proteins synthesized by the human cells. 
However, due to challenges such as the inefficient cellular uptake of naked nucleic acids 
and the inherent instability of RNA molecules, lipid-based delivery systems are often em-
ployed to transport the genetic material into cells. These delivery systems can be either 
synthetic lipid nanoparticles (LNPs), widely used in mRNA COVID-19 vaccines, or extra-
cellular vesicles (EVs), which naturally mediate intercellular communication and can be 
repurposed for vaccine delivery 19–21. The therapeutic potential of EVs is not limited to 
vaccines, as they can also be engineered to deliver various RNAs, including siRNA, 
miRNA, lncRNA, and circRNA 22.  
Virus-like particles (VLPs) have also shown great promise for vaccine development. VLPs 
are self-assembling, multiprotein structures that mimic the conformation of native viruses, 
but lack the viral genome, rendering them unable to replicate. They can be composed of 
proteins derived from viral capsids, cores, or envelopes; monolayer assemblies of parti-
cles from multilayered viruses; or even nonviral or synthetic proteins that self-assemble 
into capsid-like structures 23,24. By presenting desired antigens in a repetitive pattern, 
VLPs efficiently promote B cell receptor cross-linking and recognition by natural IgM anti-
bodies. This effectively activates the complement pathway and promotes germinal center 
(GC) formation, ultimately leading to the production of long-lived plasma cells. Loading 
VLPs with RNA or CpG motifs can further enhance their immunogenicity by stimulating 
toll-like receptors (TLR7/8 or TLR9), boosting IgG2a and IgA responses, plasma cell for-
mation, and affinity maturation 25.  
HIV-1 Gag VLPs are composed primarily of the Gag polyprotein, which self-assembles 
into a structure resembling the immature HIV-1 virion. The HIV-1 genome comprises nine 
genes that encode fifteen viral proteins. Three of these genes encode the Gag, Pol, and 
Env polyproteins, subsequently proteolyzed into individual structural and enzymatic pro-
teins common to all retroviruses. The gag gene encodes for four proteins: MA (matrix), CA 
(capsid), NC (nucleocapsid), and p6. These Gag proteins, along with the two envelope 
(Env) proteins (surface glycoprotein, SU, and transmembrane glycoprotein, TM), consti-
tute the core of the virion and its outer membrane envelope26. The N-terminus of the Gag 
MA protein undergoes myristoylation, a post-translational modification that targets this do-
main to the plasma membrane, facilitating its colocalization at lipid rafts 27. During viral 
budding, the immature HIV-1 virion is formed as the host cell membrane constricts around 
the assembling Gag proteins and is eventually pinched off, resulting in an enveloped par-
ticle whose lipid bilayer is derived from the host cell 28. The expression of the Gag poly-
protein alone is sufficient to trigger its assembly, oligomerization, and budding from the 
cell membrane, leading to the formation of VLPs independently of other viral components. 
Consequently, Gag VLPs have significant potential as a platform for vaccine development, 
not only against HIV-1 but also for other pathogens, as their envelopes can be functional-
ized to display heterologous antigens through pseudotyping 29. Beyond their direct use as 
vaccines, the inherent ability of proteins like Gag to self-assemble into VLPs and bud from 
cells makes them valuable tools for studying and optimizing viral particle production and 
secretion. The relative ease of their formation and quantification can serve as an indicator 
of a cell line's capacity for producing complex biologics, including viral vectors like rAAVs.

1.4 Mammalian expression systems 
The selection of an appropriate expression host for recombinant protein production de-
pends on the specific requirements of the target protein, including proper expression, ac-
curate folding, and essential post-translational modifications (PTMs). The latter include 
diverse types such as phosphorylation, glycosylation, ubiquitination, acetylation, methyl-
ation, lipidation, and proteolysis; each altering protein function, localization, or stability to 
regulate cellular processes 30,31.  
In the case of VLPs, several of them have been produced in yeast 32, plant 33, insect 34, 
and mammalian cells 35, but not all hosts are capable of generating functional HIV-1 Gag 
VLPs. This limitation is primarily due to the inability of some hosts to properly glycosylate 
envelope proteins, which is critical for Gag VLP functionality. While plants can achieve 
human-like glycosylation patterns 36, difficulties in the downstream process and standard-
ization of dosages 37, make mammalian expression systems the preferred choice. Among 
mammalian expression systems, Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells are the most exten-
sively used hosts for recombinant protein production, accounting for 70% of approved 
biopharmaceutical products globally 1. Despite that, CHO cells are not the most common 
platform to produce Gag VLPs, as they cannot correctly myristoylate the MA protein, lead-
ing to inefficient targeting and assembly in the plasma membrane 38. This makes Human 
embryonic kidney 293 (HEK293) cells the most used platform for HIV-1 Gag VLP produc-
tion. 
Recombinant Adeno-Associated Virus (rAAV) production primarily utilizes two main types 
of host systems: mammalian and insect cell lines. Among insect cell lines, Spodoptera 
frugiperda (Sf9) cells are the most common choice, typically employing the baculovirus 
expression vector system (BEVS) which provides the necessary helper functions without 
requiring genes from mammalian viruses like adenovirus or herpesvirus 39. Mammalian 
cell lines are also widely used, with HEK293 cells being the leading platform. The reason 
lies in the original creation of the cell line in 1977, when human embryonic kidney cells 
were transformed through exposure to sheared adenovirus 5 (Ad5) DNA 40. During this 
process a 4-Kb adenoviral genome fragment containing the E1a and E1b genes was in-
tegrated into chromosome 19 of host cells 41. Functionally, the E1a protein induces cell 
cycle arrest at the S phase, while E1b inhibits apoptosis by directly interacting with the 
Bak/Bax system 42,43. Consequently, the native expression of the two adenoviral genes 
has helped in establishing this cell line as the standard platform for rAAV production. Var-
ious cell lines have since been based on HEK293, like the HEK293SF-3F6 which is 
adapted for suspension cultures in serum-free media, and exhibits increased adenoviral 
vector production used for gene therapy 44. The complex nature of rAAV manufacturing 
has given rise to various production strategies, typically involving multiple-component sys-
tems. 
 
1.4 Production strategies for HEK293 cultures  
Generation of a stable cell line that constitutively expresses the product of interest is a 
critical step in recombinant protein production. This process involves integrating the target 
gene into a stable genetic locus within the host genome, ensuring stable long-term ex-
pression. Techniques such as CRISPR-Cas9 and recombinase-mediated cassette ex-
change (RMCE) are commonly employed for precise and efficient gene integration. 
==PAGE:8==
 
8 
Following transfection, extensive screening is required to identify high-performing clones 
with stable expression levels. Combined with the long incubation periods needed for cell 
adaptation and growth, the development of a cell line capable of stable gene expression 
(SGE) typically takes 3 to 4 months. 
In contrast, transient gene expression (TGE) provides a faster and more cost-effective 
alternative, particularly for applications that require rapid protein production. TGE circum-
vents the need for genome integration and thus eliminates the time-intensive clonal se-
lection and characterization, by relying on episomal expression of plasmid DNA in the host 
cells. This approach is especially relevant for HEK293 cells, which exhibit high transfec-
tion efficiencies and compatibility with cost-effective, DNA complexation-based transient 
transfection reagents, like polyethylenimine (PEI). Additionally, HEK293 cells can deliver 
high product yields in TGE systems, making them a preferred choice for producing recom-
binant proteins during preclinical studies, vaccine development, or early-phase biophar-
maceutical production. The most common TGE approach for HEK293 cells is the trans-
fection with 3 plasmids, referred to as triple transfection 45. It involves the co-transfection 
of three plasmids: one helper plasmid delivering the necessary adenoviral helper genes 
(pHelper), one plasmid expressing the rep and cap genes (pRepCap), and one plasmid 
carrying the gene of interest (GOI) cassette flanked by the AAV ITRs (pGOI).  
While TGE is not preferred for large-scale commercial manufacturing of recombinant pro-
teins due to the temporary nature of expression, it is widely used to produce viral vectors 
for gene therapy applications 46. For rAAVs, transient transfection in HEK293 cells offers 
specific advantages tied to the nature of the product. Since the Rep protein induces a 
cytotoxic effect to the cells, stable cell lines are especially difficult to obtain and require a 
tightly controlled Rep expression 47. Transient transfection circumvents the problem by 
limiting the expression of Rep to the production window, and thus, increasing the flexibility 
for growth prior to the transfection. Additionally, rapid production of different rAAV sero-
types is possible by interchanging the plasmid containing the cap gene 48.  
Despite its superiority for viral vector production, TGE comes with specific drawbacks that 
make its large-scale application particularly expensive. A single therapeutic dose from a 
gene therapy product can reach upwards of 7,5x1015 to 1,5x1017 viral genomes (vg), de-
pending on the delivery method 49, while post-purification yields can only reach around 
1x1013 vg/L 50. In addition, the high costs of producing plasmid DNA (pDNA) under the 
current good manufacturing practice (cGMP) standards necessary for clinical applications, 
together with the high cost of the culture media and downstream purification and quality 
control procedures, result in an extremely expensive scale-up process 51. Although pro-
cess optimization efforts have come a long way, from transfection optimization through 
DOE 52, to downstream processing advancements 53, rAAV yields still remain low.  
 
1.5 Cell density effect  
One of the biggest bottlenecks of TGE for large-scale production of recombinant proteins 
is the cell density effect (CDE). In general, this effect is characterized by the decreased 
cell-specific productivity of mammalian cells observed when transient transfection is car-
ried out beyond the mid-exponential growth phase 54. Factors like the cell line, culture 
mode (batch, fed-batch, perfusion) and culture media, heavily influence the densities at 
which CDE is observed.  
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
The CDE has been reported since the early 1990s, when researchers noted a decreased 
productivity of antibodies in high cell densities of hybridoma perfusion cultures, attributing 
it to nutrient depletion 55. Subsequent metabolic studies in the mid 1990s by Zeng et al. 
concluded that CDE was the sole factor contributing to a decreased nutrient consumption 
in high cell densities 56. It was hypothesized that the effect could be attributed to an uni-
dentified component present in the culture medium. The same effect has also been ex-
tensively reported in transient transfection systems, as it is the main factor prohibiting the 
successful scale-up of these bioprocesses. Different strategies, including continuous per-
fusion processes and media replacement prior to transfection, only achieved increased 
productivity by delaying the onset of CDE 57.  
Current understanding of the CDE, attributes the effect on two independent factors: the 
presence of an extracellular inhibitory compound, and cellular physiological changes in 
HCD. Successful tackling of the first factor has been widely demonstrated by including a 
media replacement (MR) step prior to transfection, in order to remove possible inhibitory 
compounds and by-products from the exhausted medium 58,59. Recent advances towards 
the elucidation of the specific physiological changes at HCD, have pointed towards struc-
tural changes in the extracellular matrix that affect the uptake and delivery of the DNA into 
the nucleus 60.  
 
1.6 The potential of the ECM in mitigating the cell density effect 
The extracellular matrix (ECM) is a complex network that provides structural support and 
regulates various cellular processes. It comprises a variety of collagen superfamily and 
non-colagenous molecules that are secreted locally and assemble into an organized 
meshwork. Proteoglycans (PGs), key molecules of the ECM, are composed of protein 
cores covalently linked to long glycosaminoglycan (GAG) chains through a mostly con-
served tetrasaccharide linker structure: GlcA–Gal–Gal–Xyl–Ser–protein core. The initial 
step of GAG synthesis involves the addition of a xylulose residue to a serine residue of 
theprotein core by β-xylosyltransferases (XylT), encoded by the XYLT1 and XYLT2 
genes 61. Both enzymes are essential for GAG biosynthesis, and mutations in these genes 
in humans are associated with ECM-related diseases 62. In HEK293 cells, only the XYLT2 
gene shows quantifiable expression levels 63. Beyond the ECM assembly, XYLT genes 
have also been shown to influence other cellular functions, as XYLT2 deficient HEK293 
cells exhibit reduced proliferation and increased senescence, highlighting the importance 
of these enzymes in maintaining cellular viability 64. 
After the synthesis of the initial tetrasaccharide linker, GAG chains are further elongated 
by the addition of a fifth monosaccharide that determines the fate of the molecule. In the 
case of heparan sulfate (HS) and heparin, this sugar is N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc), 
while for chondroitin sulfate (CS) and dermatan sulfate (DS), it is N-acetylgalactosamine 
(GalNAc). These reactions are catalyzed by GlcNAc transferase I (GlcNAcTI) and GalNAc 
transferase I (GalNAcTI), respectively. In the case of CS/DS synthesis, the addition of 
subsequent GalNAc residues is mediated by another enzyme, GalNAc transferase II (Gal-
NAcTII), which shares high homology with GalNAcTI 65. These two enzymes are encoded 
by the CSGALNACT1 and CSGALNACT2 genes, respectively. 
Protein cores directly dictate the type and function of the proteoglycan by having binding 
sites specific to one or multiple GAG chains. One example is versican, a large protein 
structure that is encoded by the VCAN gene and has binding sites for CS GAG chains 
==PAGE:10==
 
10 
that form a multi-domain CSPG 66. In contrast, perlecan is another multi-domain core pro-
tein, encoded by the HSPG2 gene, that can bind either HS, CS, or keratan sulfate (KS) 
GAG chains 67.  
PEI is rich in positively charged amino groups that interact with the negative phosphate 
groups of DNA to form DNA/PEI polyplexes. The interaction of these polyplexes with the 
cell membrane facilitates their endocytosis and ultimately the delivery of the DNA in the 
nucleus 68,69. Additionally, the uptake of DNA:PEI polyplexes is triggered by a coalescence 
phenomenon of HS and CSPGs in the cell membrane 60. In HCD conditions these inter-
actions have been shown to be affected by the presence of HS and CS in the exhausted 
medium, either present in extracellular vesicles (EVs), or as free GAGs that have been 
released from the base membrane 60.

genes in mitigating the cell density effect for the production of VLPs and rAAVs. The tar-

2. Materials and methods  
2.1 Cell lines and culture conditions  
HEK293 (HEK293SF-3F6) cells were thawed from cryovials stored at -180°C in nitrogen 
tanks and maintained in 125mL vented baffled shake flasks (Corning, NY, USA). Unless 
stated otherwise, cell cultures were incubated at 37°C, 5% CO, and 85% relative humidity, 
with shaking at 130 rpm. Hycell TransFx-H (Cytiva, MA, USA) media were supplemented 
with Glutamax (ThermoFisher Scientific, MA, USA) at a final concentration 4mM, and Plu-
ronic F-68 (ThermoFisher Scientific, MA, USA) at a final concentration of 0.1%. Cells were 
passaged every 2-3 days at concentrations of 0,3-0,5 2x106 cells/mL, achieving viabilities 
over 98%. Cell counting and viability assays were performed in the NucleCounter NC-250 
automatic cell counter (Chemo-Metec, Allerød, Denmark) after staining with AO•DAPI (So-
lution18) (Chemo-Metec, Allerød, Denmark), following the manufacturer’s instructions. 
 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
2.2 Knockout strategy and transfection of HEK293 
To achieve knockout of the target genes, previously designed sgRNAs (design process 
not shown) targeting sequences, two at the start and two at the end of each gene, were 
utilized to completely delete the gene sequence. The relative positions of the sgRNAs and 
primers used for screening potential knockouts, as well as the expected sizes of the am-
plified DNA when the respective primers were used, are detailed in the Appendix. HEK293 
wild-type (WT) cells were transfected with a 1:1 molar ratio of an equimolar sgRNA mix 
and a GFP-Cas9-expressing plasmid, with a total concentration of 1.25 μg/μL. Transfec-
tions were conducted in 6-well plates at a cell density of 2x106 cells/mL, using a 1:1 volu-
metric ratio of the DNA mix to the transfection reagent. PEIpro (Polyplus, France) was 
used as the transfection reagent, following the manufacturer’s instructions. Following 
transfection, the cultures were incubated for 72 hours. 
 
2.3 Single cell sorting and screening potential knockouts 
Fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) was performed in the SH800S Cell Sorter 
(Sony Biotechnology, CA, USA) where GFP-positive single cells were selected for each 
transfection reaction and sorted to 384-well plates. The clones were incubated without 
shaking for 14 days and cells in each well were counted using Celigo image cytometer 
(Revvity, MA, USA). Wells containing more than 1000 cells were selected and expanded 
to 96-well plates using the liquid handler epMotion® 5070 (Eppendorf, Germany). 70 μL 
samples were taken from wells with more than 50% confluency, and DNA was extracted 
using QuickExtract DNA extraction solution (LGC Biosearch Technologies, UK), following 
the manufacturer’s instructions. Touchdown PCRs for screening of the potential knockouts 
were done using Phusion High-Fidelity polymerase (Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA) 
and according to the manufacturer’s specifications. The appropriate screening primer 
pairs (Supplementary table S1) for each gene were selected based on the expected band 
sizes for potential deletions (Supplementary Figures S1-S2), so that all the possible dele-
tions were screened for.  
 
2.4 Transient transfection of HEK293 for VLP and rAAV production  
Transient transfections for VLP production were carried out in 125 mL vented baffled 
shake flasks, whereas for rAAV production were carried out in 3 mL 6-well plates (Corning, 
NY, USA). LCD and HCD cell cultures for transfections were obtained by inoculating at 
0,3x106 cells/mL, 3 and 5-7 days before transfection, aiming for cell densities of 2x106 
and 5-8x106 cells/mL, respectively, at the day of transfection. The specific cell density for 
each cell line used in HCD cultures was determined from its growth curve as the highest 
viable cell density (VCD) reached over a 14-day batch culture. All conditions were tested 
with and without the addition of a media replacement (MR) step prior to transfection. MR

2.6 rAAV yield quantification  
Samples were lysed using lysis buffer (20 mM MgCl2, 1% Triton X-100 in 500 mM Tris 
buffered solution, pH 7.5) for 1 hour at 37°C with 130rpm shaking. The lysate was centri-
fuged for 20 minutes at 4000xg and the supernatant was stored at -70°C. Then, 5 μL of 
sample were treated with DNAse I (Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA) at 37°C for 16 
hours at a final concentration of 0.133U/μL in a 10X DNAse I reaction buffer with MgCl2 
(Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA). DNAse I activity was inhibited by the addition of 4μL 
of EDTA (Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA) and a 30-minute incubation at 70°C. Pro-
teinase K (5μL) was added to the mix and incubated for 2 hours at 55°C, before being 
inactivated at 95°C for 15 minutes. 2μL of this mix was used as a template to set up a 
qPCR reaction mix with primers PR3510 and PR3511, targeting the GFP gene of the pGOI 
plasmid, using the Brilliant III Ultra-Fast SYBR Green QPCR Master Mix (Agilent Technol-
ogies, CA, USA) and according to the manufacturer’s specifications. The qPCR was per-
formed in a QuantStudio 5 real-time PCR system (Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA) 
and results were analyzed in ThermoCloud (Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA).  
Transfected lysate control (TLC) and saturate lysate control (SLC) were used to ensure 
the quality of the generated data. SLC was prepared when lysate form a non-transfected 
culture was mixed with 40ng of the pGOI plasmid prior to the DNAse I incubation. Prepa-
ration of the TLC included a single transfection with the pGOI plasmid, lysis and quantifi-
cation. Together these two controls indicate the effectiveness of the DNAse I treatment in 
digesting the pGOI plasmid, either when it was spiked into the lysate, or when it was 
transfected without the other two plasmids for rAAV production.  
 
2.7 Immunocytochemistry and confocal microscopy  
Samples were taken from cultures at 2x106 cells/mL and digested with either heparinase 
I or chondroitinase ABC. Chondroitinase ABC: reactions were set up with the appropriate 
buffer (60mM sodium acetate, 0.02%BSA in PBS, pH 7,4) at a final concentration of 
100mU/mL. Heparinase I (New England Biolabs, MA, USA): reactions were set up with 
the provided buffer at a final concentration of 25 mU/mL. Chondroitinase and heparinase 
reactions were incubated for 3 hours at 37°C. Cells were then fixed with 4% formaldehyde 
for 15 minutes. Prior to the primary antibody incubations, samples were incubated with a 
blocking buffer (3% BSA in PBS) for 1 hour at room temperature. CS-56 chondroitin sulfate 
primary antibody (Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA), and F58-10E4 heparan sulfate 
primary antibody (1:200 in PBS+3%BSA) (AMSBIO, UK) were added to the appropriate 
samples and incubated at 4°C for 16 hours. After washing, goat anti-mouse IgG secondary 
antibody, Alexa Fluor 647 (1:1000 in PBS+3%BSA) (Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA) 
was added and incubated for 1 hour at room temperature. Both antibodies were washed 
with PBS+0.1%Tween-20. Lastly, the nucleus was stained with Hoechst 33342 (1:2000 in 
PBS) (Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA) for 10 minutes at room temperature, and cells 
were imaged in the Leica SP8 confocal microscope (Leica Microsystems, Germany).  
No-antibody control (NAC) and no-primary-antibody control (NPAC) were prepared in par-
allel and following the same treatments as the samples, but without the addition of either 
both or the primary antibodies, respectively. The NAC was used to assess the level of 
autofluorescence in the samples, while the NPAC was used as a measure of the non-
specific binding of the secondary antibody and a baseline for signal detection.

3. Results 
3.1 Cell line engineering  
In order to test the hypothesis that the structural organization of the ECM has a major role 
in the cell density effect, we aimed to create mutant cell lines of specific genes that are 
part of the ECM structure and homeostasis. In total, 4 rounds of transfections that included 
12 target genes were selected for creating 8 single mutant (VCAN-/-, ADAMTS1-/-, HPSE1-
/-, HPSE2-/-, AGRN-/-, SULF2-/-, MMP2-/-, and MMP9-/-), 4 double mutant (CSGALNACT1-/-
;CSGALNACT2-/-, HPSE1-/-;HPSE-/-, NID1-/-;NID2-/-, and SULF1-/-;SULF2-/-), and 1 triple 
mutant (ADAMTS1-/-;XYLT1-/-;XYLT2-/-) HEK293 cell lines. A CRISPR-Cas9 system was 
employed to create the knockouts. As part of the strategy, two single-guide RNAs (sgR-
NAs) targeted the initial sequences of each gene, while another two targeted the terminal 
sequences. Following transfection, single cells were sorted based on GFP fluorescence 
using FACS. Each transfection reaction was sorted into a 384-well plate and after a 14-
day incubation clones that demonstrated viable growth were expanded into 96-well plates, 
enabling screening to confirm the potential gene deletions. Primers with binding sites 
flanking each Cas9 cut site (Supplementary Figure S1-S2), allowed the accurate screen-
ing of each possible combination of sgRNA cuts, while also testing for the absence of a 
sequence, termed “intra band” and defined by the two screening primers flanking the sec-
ond cut site, which reveals the deletion of a major part of the gene. 
In total, 5 homozygous deletions were achieved from which 5 cell lines were created (Fig-
ure 1). Due to the limited timeframe, sequencing confirmation of the deletions was possi-
ble for two of them, namely VCAN-/- and CSGALNACT1-/-;CSGALNACT2-/-. For VCAN-/-, 
a heterozygous cell line with a deletion between the cut sites 2 and 5 (Supplementary 
Figure S2) was transformed with the respective gRNAs (Figure 2A). PCR amplification of 
the intra band with primers PR4871/PR4872 (Fwd2-Rev2) (Supplementary Table S1) re-
vealed the absence of an intra band, while the junction PCR with primers PR4871/PR4874 
(Fwd2-Rev5) showed a 200bp band that indicated a cut between cut sites 2 and 5 (Figure 
2B). Sequencing of the 200bp junction band revealed a cut between exons 2 and 15, thus 
confirming the knockout (Figure 2C). CSGALNACT1-/-;CSGALNACT2-/- was created by 
transfecting a CSGALNACT2-/- mutant cell line with the appropriate gRNAs targeting the 
CSGALNACT1 gene (Figure 3A). PCR amplification with primers PR4564/PR4565 (Fwd1-
Rev1) showed the presence of a smaller intra band compared to the WT (Figure 3B). 
Sequencing of the intra band revealed a small deletion of 26bp between cut sites 1 and 
2, which in turn created a premature stop codon in the position of the 30th amino acid 
(Figure 3C).  
Although sequencing confirmation of AGRN-/-, SULF2-/-, and ADAMTS1-/-;XYLT1-/-;XYLT-
/- was not possible, all three displayed a clear junction band while having no intra band

frozen for storage at -180°C. The rest of the clones had a heterozygous deletion for at 
least one of the target genes, so new transfection rounds are required to achieve the

3.2 Gag VLP production in mutant HEK293 cell lines  
The newly created VCAN-/- and CSGALNACT1-/-;CSGALNACT2-/-, along with the already 
established HSPG2-/- and XYLT1-/-;XYLT2-/- HEK293 mutant cell lines were selected to 
test the HIV-1 Gag VLP productivity following transfection of LCD and HCD cultures. 
Gag::eGFP was transiently transfected and samples were taken at 0, 24, 48, and 72 hours 
post transfection (hpt). VCD remained relatively stable following transfection, as the mild 
toxicity of PEI and the expression of VLPs hinder the growth of the cells 69, with LCD 
cultures remaining at 2-4x106 cells/mL in both batch and MR cultures (Figure 4). Viabilities 
in LCD batch cultures dropped to about 65-70% at 72hpt for all cell lines, whereas media 
replacement prior to transfection was able to keep viabilities around 80% at 72hpt. In HCD 
cultures these trends persisted, albeit being more pronounced. In batch mode, VCD re-
mained at 4-7x106 cells/mL and viability dropped significantly reaching levels of approxi-
mately 40% in 72hpt. MR greatly rescued viabilities, with most cell lines remaining above 
60% at 72hpt and VCDs ranging from 5-11x106 cells/mL. Interestingly, both HSPG2-/- and 
XYLT1-/-;XYLT2-/- exhibited significantly higher viabilities compared to the rest in HCD con-
ditions, at 55% and 80% in batch and MR cultures, respectively.

3.4 Imaging of heparan and chondroitin sulfates in the ECM of XYLT mutant 
cell lines 
In order to visualize the quantity and distribution of HS and CS in the ECM of the cell line, 
each GAG was separately stained in cells coming from the single mutant XYLT1-/- or 
XYLT2-/- or the double mutant XYLT1-/-;XYLT2-/- cell lines. Since XYLT1 and XYLT2 cata-
lyze the initial step in GAG synthesis, the mutant cell lines lacking either one or both of 
those enzymes are expected to have partial or complete reduction of heparan sulfates 
and chondroitin sulfates in the EXM. In the case of chondroitin sulfates, XYLT1-/- and 
XYLT2-/- single mutants had a small but noticeable reduction in the CS present compared 
to WT (Figure 9). On the other hand, the amount of CS in the ECM of XYLT1-/-;XYLT2-/- 
did not seem affected by the knockouts, as their levels were comparable to those of WT. 
Labeling of heparan sulfates presented some challenges, as WT cells did not match the 
expected levels of HS in the ECM. Despite that, XYLT1-/- and XYLT2-/- seemingly had low 
levels of HSPGs, while in the XYLT1-/-;XYLT2-/- samples no detection was possible. Alt-
hough immunostaining of the HSPGs was not a complete success, the absence of detect-
able signal coming from the double knockout could potentially indicate the complete ab-
sence of HSPGs from the ECM.

4. Discussion 
The present thesis builds upon previous research that identified the accumulation of CS 
and HS GAGs in the ECM environment at high cell density conditions and hypothesized 
that they sequester DNA/PEI polyplexes by inhibiting their interaction with cell surface 
==PAGE:23==
 
23 
proteoglycans, and subsequently their entrance inside the cell 60. The primary goal of this 
thesis was to investigate the role of the ECM in the cell density effect by generating KO 
cell lines of key ECM-related genes. The complete deletion of the GAG biosynthetic path-
way through the knockout of XYLT1 and XYLT2 genes (XYLT1-/-;XYLT2-/-) greatly miti-
gated the CDE for HIV-1 Gag VLP production, outperforming the other mutants tested and 
the WT at HCD. This effect persisted even for fed-batch cultures, where inhibitory factors 
were not removed through media replacement. Eliminating the chondroitin sulfate biosyn-
thesis (CSGALNACT1-/-;CSGALNACT2-/-) also showed some degree of CDE mitigation at 
HCD, particularly after MR. In contrast, these interventions did not have the same clear 
benefits for rAAV production. 
While cell-surface HSPGs are key mediators of the DNA:PEI polyplex uptake during tran-
sient transfection at LCD 60, free or extracellular-vesicle-bound HSPGs that accumulate 
in the exhausted medium at HCD can competitively bind polyplexes, hindering their up-
take through the membrane. Similarly, CSPGs can bind polyplexes but do not mediate 
polyplex internalization 60, contributing to this competitive inhibition at HCD conditions. 
The high VLP productivity of the double XYLT mutant provides strong evidence that the 
presence of both HS and CS GAG chains in the ECM at HCD is a major driving factor of 
the CDE. Cells lacking surface HS and CS GAGs are more likely to uptake polyplexes, 
possibly through an alternative uptake pathway. Keratan sulfate (KS) could mediate the 
internalization of the DNA:PEI polyplexes in the absence of other HSPGs, as its synthesis 
is not dependent on XYLT enzymes 71. 
While VLP production provided clear results on the effectiveness of the XYLT mutant cell 
line, visualization of the ECM GAGs via immunostaining yielded complex results. A major 
limitation of the ECM staining was revealed by the failure of adequate HS staining in the 
WT cells. Despite this shortcoming, comparison between the XYLT mutant cell lines could 
still reveal relative differences. While the absence of HS staining in the XYLT1-/-;XYLT2-/- 
mutant supports the knockout’s efficiency in eliminating HS biosynthesis, widespread CS 
GAGs were detected, possibly indicating an alternative, XYLT-independent synthesis 
pathway. Further investigation is needed to determine whether synthesis of CSPGs could 
be XYLT-independent.  
The clear effect of the ECM mutant cell lines in mitigating CDE for VLP production did not 
directly translate to rAAV production, as the knockout cell lines did not significantly in-
crease the productivity compared to the LCD levels. It can be hypothesized that the added 
complexity of the multi-plasmid polyplexes affects the interactions with cell surface GAGs 
of a modified ECM structure, potentially making the transfection process less efficient. 
Previous results showed that the ratio between the three plasmids in the triple transfection 
greatly influences the rAAV productivity 52, but the specific interactions of these plasmids 
with PEI, and the ones of the subsequent polyplexes with the cell surface remain un-
known. Alterations in the structure of the ECM could influence these interactions, ulti-
mately changing the plasmid ratio inside the cell. Moreover, the reduced productivity after 
media replacement at LCD highlights the effects that the increased stress of the MR pro-
cess has to the cells. Unfortunately, the failure of the DNAse I treatment during the sample 
preparation rendered the absolute quantification of the rAAV productivity unreliable. While 
these results hint at specific nuances of the mutant cell lines during rAAV production, they 
should be interpreted with caution until they are validated with new experiments.  
Future work will build upon these findings to gain a more comprehensive understanding 
of how the ECM affects the cell productivity at high cell densities. This includes western 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
blot analysis of the double mutant XYLT1-/-;XYLT2-/- to test for XYLT1 and XYLT2 expres-
sion. Furthermore, the staining protocol for HS and CS requires optimization to enable 
more accurate detection and quantification of these GAGs within the ECM. Imaging the 
ECM of the CSGALNACT1-/-;CSGALNACT2-/- cell line would also shed light on the CSPG 
synthesis pathway, possibly helping interpret the productivity phenotypes observed. Val-
uable insight into the signaling role of each proteoglycan in mediating the CDE could also 
be obtained by transcriptomics data of WT cells that have been exposed to exhausted 
medium coming from the various knockout cell lines. To assess specific productivities in 
a highly controlled environment, batch and fed-batch cultures will be performed using an 
Ambr15 system, which utilizes 15-mL mini-bioreactors with stable O₂ and pH control. Fi-
nally, further rounds of cell line engineering are needed to obtain all the desired knockout 
cell lines and eventually characterize them. Collectively, these results will advance our 
understanding of the cell density effect and inform the rational design of a HEK293 strain 
engineered to mitigate this production bottleneck. This will drastically improve the yields 
of HEK293 cultures, reduce manufacturing costs, and ultimately make advanced biophar-
maceuticals more accessible to patients.
