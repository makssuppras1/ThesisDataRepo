Preface
This report is part of the requirements to achieve the Master Degree at the Technical University
of Denmark. The work has been completed in the Electrical Engineering department during the
spring semester of 2016, under the supervision of Associate Professor Nenad Mijatovic.
In my master project I derived the model of a synchronous condenser, based on the studies
reported by students and engineers in recent years.
The achievement of the results was not straightforward: I have analyzed different models and tested
various resolution strategies, before ﬁnding the ones that in my opinion most faithfully represent
the operating conditions of a synchronous condenser.
One of the most challenging part was the design of the magnetic equivalent circuit. I have dedicated
two months to analyze the magnetic ﬂux propagation in a synchronous condenser, in the attempt
to implement an algorithm that derives the internal parameters of an electrical machines, based
solely on its dimensions. At the beginning I tried to optimize the circuits already provided in the
literature, with low achievements. As a consequence, at the suggestion of my supervisor, I have
designed a completely new magnetic circuit, with more successful results.
Some difﬁculties arose also during the inclusion of the cross saturation effects in the model. The
strategies proposed by some scientiﬁc papers involved methods that would have negatively affected
the performance of the model, by increasing the computational burden and thus the time required
to perform the simulations. Eventually, the method proposed by a researcher has been tailored for
the study of the synchronous condenser, and the ﬁnal model was derived accordingly.

Abstract
This thesis presents the modelling of a synchronous condenser through the use of the differential
equations-based software Simulink. Special attention is paid to the study of the saturation effects
of the machine, from which three different synchronous machine models are derived.
In the ﬁrst model the core of the machine is assumed linear: the saturation of the magnetic circuit
is neglected and the internal parameters are held constant, regardless of the operating conditions of
the machine.
Conversely, lookup table inductances are used in the development of the other two models: the
saturation of the machine is taken into account, and the cross saturation effects is solely included in
the third model.
The values of the mutual inductances of the last two models are expressed as a function of the
mutual ﬂuxes in the d- and q-axis, and derived through the design of the magnetic equivalent circuit
of the machine. Despite the topologies proposed in the literature, a new and more accurate magnetic
circuit is presented in the attempt to study the magnetic ﬂux propagation without the use of a ﬁnite
element software.
Various tests are carried out to derive the steady state characteristics and compare the performances
of the models. The results displayed that the inclusion of the saturation of the core may affect the
operating conditions of the machine, since different values of current and voltage are computed
by the models with constant and lookup table parameters. Furthermore, due to the relatively low
current ﬂowing in the q-axis, the study of the cross saturation effects turned out to be unnecessary
when performing simulations in steady state. In the more particular case in which the machine
operates with stable voltage at the terminals, a new model with constant parameters may be used
in the simulations, provided that the values of the inductances are properly chosen based on the
degree of saturation of the core.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The modelling of a system consists in translating into equations one or more physical phenom-
ena that regulate the functioning of the system itself. Usually, the development of a model is based
on ﬁnding a balance between the accuracy of the results, and the speed through which such results
are achieved. With particular reference to this thesis, different methods and strategies have been
investigated in the aim of developing the model for a synchronous condenser. Based on this, the
following questions will be answered during the project:
• What assumptions can we make in order to simplify the modelling of a synchronous con-
denser, without affecting the performance of the model?
• How can we include the saturation effects in the model? Is there any difference in the
machine’s performance when the saturation is taken into account?
• Is it necessary to include the cross saturation effects in the model of a synchronous condenser?
1.1
State of the art
The synchronous machine models proposed in the literature present similar base structures:
starting from a three-phase global reference frame, the magnetic ﬂuxes in the d- and q-axis are
derived through Park’s transformation. Finally, currents, electromagnetic torque and rotational
speed are computed from these variables.
What differentiate one model from the other, is the inclusion of the saturation of the core and its
effects on the internal parameters of the machine. As explained in the following chapters, some
methods require a change in the internal structure of the model which may drastically affect the
computational speed of the algorithms involved.
Unfortunately, despite the several approaches to the saturation effects proposed in literature, none
of them is tailored to the study of a synchronous condenser model.

Thesis structure
Chapter 2 opens with a description of the operating points of a synchronous condenser, and
with a comparison between this machine and other means of supplying reactive power. The main

different scenarios. At ﬁrst, the synchronous condenser is connected to an ideal voltage source and
its steady state performance characteristics derived. Then, the grid is provided with an impedance
and the effects of the excitation ﬁeld on the bus bar voltage tested with and without and inductive
load connected in parallel to the machine.

CHAPTER 2
Synchronous condenser
Reactive power compensation is the regulation of the reactive power delivered or absorbed
by devices connected to a grid, aimed at improving the performances of AC power systems. Par-
ticularly, the objective of reactive power compensation is twofold: load regulation and voltage
stabilisation [1].
In load regulation, the purpose is to compensate the loads connected to the grid with inductive or
capacitive reactive power, so that their power factor is increased around the unit value. Thanks to
the optimized power factor, a relatively small amount of reactive power is provided by the grid,
lowering the current ﬂowing through it and thus minimizing the line losses. The system connected
to the grid may entail dead loads and live loads. A dead load system is mainly made of lamps,
resistors or reactors and is deﬁned by a constant ratio between the terminal voltage and the current.
On the contrary, live loads consist of synchronous and induction motors and are characterized by a
constant active power demand, regardless of the terminal voltage [2].
Voltage support is aimed at reducing the voltage ﬂuctuation at the terminals of a transmission
line and to improve the stability of AC systems by delivering reactive power. When faults occur
near to systems load, is highly likelihood that an unstable situation takes place. In particular,
due to the nature of live loads, a voltage drop at the terminals of these devices may result in an
increasing demand of current to the grid. This leads to a further decrease in the line voltage and to
the consequent collapse of the grid.
For these reasons, means of supplying reactive power, such as Static VAR Compensators (SVCs) or
Synchronous Condensers (SCs), are required to improve the stability and the efﬁciency of the grid.
2.1
Synchronous Condenser Operation
Synchronous Condensers differ from other typologies of synchronous machines for the lack
of prime mover at the shaft. Because ideally there is no any exchange of active power between
the machine and the grid, stator voltage and current line are always 90◦out of phase. Since no
electromagnetic torque is generated by the machine, the magnetic poles of the rotor and of the
stator are aligned, meaning that rotor and stator ﬂuxes are in phase [3].

reactive power could be supplied to the grid by inducing a negative current in the rotor. However,
the change of rotor ﬁeld ﬂux direction would cause instability and loss of synchronism. Operating
in underexcited mode is very important when the machine is installed in proximity to cables or
capacitive loads.

Comparison of SVC and Synchronous Condenser
SVCs consist of capacitors connected to the grid through thyristors that regulate the reactive
power delivered to the system.
The main advantage of SVCs lays on their relatively low cost in term of installation and maintenance.
The rotating parts of the synchronous condenser, indeed, make the machine more expensive due
to the fast deterioration of the mechanical components and the active power required to hold the
synchronism speed.

Final considerations
Compared to SVCs, synchronous condensers present a more ﬂexible
control: their supplied reactive power is variable continuously by simply controlling the ﬁeld
voltage, which is an outstanding advantage over other means of supplying corrective k-Var. At
fault occurrence they are reliable devices because their performance do not depend on the terminal
voltage: despite their low dynamic, they successfully clear the faults and stabilize the grid.
On the contrary, due to their high costs, static var compensators are more suitable when the objective
is to solely compensate the reactive power of the loads.

Magnetic Equivalent Circuit Design
The study of the magnetic ﬂux distribution in an electrical machine is usually carried out
for determining the internal parameters of the machine, such as the inductances of the windings.
Several methods are illustrated in literature for the derivation of these parameters, most of which
are based on a Finite Element Analysis (FEA) or on the design of a Magnetic Equivalent Circuit
(MEC).
This chapter starts with listing the main characteristics that differentiate the FEA from MEC study,
and then it focuses exclusively on the linear magnetic circuit and on the different methods employed
for solving it. Subsequently a detailed study of the non-linear MEC and the implementation of
an algorithm that ensures its resolution is presented. Finally, the derivation of the most accurate
topology of a magnetic circuit is discussed.
3.1
Mutual inductance derivation with FEA and MEC
The Finite Element Analysis is based on the discretization of the object of study in small
triangular shapes. On the sides of each triangle a linear distribution of the magnetic (or electric)
ﬁeld is assumed and the intensity of such ﬁeld on each vertex is calculated so as to minimize the
energy enclosed in each triangle. This method turns out to be highly accurate and enables a detailed
study of the ﬁeld distribution in the object [7].
When dealing with synchronous machines, the ﬁnite element analysis of the ﬂuxes can be employed
for the derivation of the mutual inductances in the d- and q-axis. In particular, Lmd is derived
by aligning the d-axis of the rotor with the axis of one phase (for example phase a). Then the
armature’s windings of each phase are loaded with different currents [8]. In particular, deﬁned with

3.7
Final considerations
In this chapter we went through the study of the magnetic equivalent circuit of a synchronous
machine.
It has been shown that two of the most common strategies implemented in the resolution of a
MEC are the node potential and mesh based method. Because in a magnetic circuit the number
of meshes equals the amount of nodes, the computational burden required by the two methods is
approximately the same. However, the former results to be more intuitive since we can directly
apply this method on the magnetic circuit, without any transformation of the components.
When the non-linearity of the core is considered, the MEC must be solved by implementing one of
the above methods in an iterative algorithm. Two strategies have been discussed, and particular
emphasis has been placed on the Newton-Raphson algorithm because ensures a faster convergence
to the solution.
Finally, we speciﬁcally went through the design of the magnetic circuit, trying to ﬁnd the best
topology that most faithfully represent the distribution of the magnetic ﬁeld intensity in the air-gap
of the machine. The topologies presented in the literature provide very accurate results when the
only the d-ﬂux magnetizes the machine. However, the more the mmf is displaced from the d-axis,
the worst are the results provided by the MEC. As a consequence, a squared grid topology has been
investigated: unlike the previous MEC, the new derived magnetic circuit provided very accurate
results, with negligible errors.

In this chapter the derivation and comparison of different time varying models of synchronous
machine is presented. These models are implemented in the differential equations-based software
Simulink, on the basis of the formulae presented in the second chapter.
Determining the steady state operating point of a machine is relatively simple as the equations
involved in the calculations are not time dependent but composed by constant parameters. Provided
the terminals and ﬁeld voltage, the line current, the electromagnetic torque and the power delivered
to the grid are easily calculated by solving a linear system.
Conversely, when the machine is subjected to small transients, such as a variation in the ﬁeld
voltage, time dependent terms like derivatives must be included in the system. The new equations
involved in the analysis are no longer linear but differential, and a different approach must be
followed for deriving the operating points of the machine.
Differential equations can be solved by implementing a state space model of the form

In this chapter we are going through the study of three different types of synchronous machine

4.1.5
C model: ﬁnal considerations
Figure 4.5 reports the complete block diagram of the constant parameter model, from now on
also called C model. The operation of the machine is ruled by two linear state-space models.
The electrical subsystem is a time variant state space model, as the elements of matrix ω change
with time. Its input is represented by the the vector V and provides the values of ﬂux and currents
for determining the electromagnetic torque. On the contrary, the the mechanical subsystem is a time
invariant state space model since the inertia J and the friction coefﬁcient kf are constant parameters.
This subsystem is fed by the output of the electrical subsystem and provides the mechanical speed
of the machine. From the mechanical speed, the position of the rotor θ is determined. Since the
input of the electrical subsystem is determined by θ and the nonlinear Equations (2.3) and (2.4),
we can state that overall, the synchronous machine model is nonlinear. Despite the possibility
to simulate the transients, this model does not faithfully resemble the operation of the machine
because the saturation effects are neglected.

4.2.3
LUT model: ﬁnal considerations
Thanks to this lookup table strategy, the saturation effects are included in our model. The
values of the inductances are given through a ﬁnite element analysis of the machine, where the
machine is separately loaded with the d and q current and the air gap ﬂuxes derived. In this analysis
we are assuming that the magnetic circuit of the d and q-axis are independent and that the ﬂux of
one does not affect the saturation of the other. Besides, the leakage inductances are constant and
not affected by the saturation of the machine. This assumption simpliﬁes the study of the saturation,
but does not resemble completely the real conditions of a machine.
In literature we can ﬁnd more examples of synchronous machine models that further simplify the
simulation of the saturation of the core. In the models proposed by Matlab, for example, the mutual
inductance Lmq is assumed constant, whereas only Lmd is ﬂux dependent. This assumption is
reasonable provided that Φmd remains relatively small. In this operating condition, indeed, the
q-axis magnetic circuit would not reach the saturation and Lmq would not change.
4.3
Study of cross saturation effects
In synchronous machines, with the term cross saturation we indicate the study of the saturation
of the motor when both currents in the d and q-axis are exciting the machine. By referring to the d
axis, we have previously seen that the synchronous inductance Lmd depends on the intensity of the
ﬂux Φmd. In particular, the greater is Φmd, the lower is the mutual inductance because larger is the
reluctance of the magnetic circuit.

4.3.6
FPM model: ﬁnal considerations concerning
Thanks to the FPM model the derived operating points of the synchronous condenser take into
account the effects of the cross saturation. The FPM machine is similar to the LUT model, since
both of them make use of lookup table parameters. Speciﬁcally, the lookup table block concerning
Lmd is the same, whereas the Lmq is derived differently. In LUT, the q-axis mutual inductance
depends on Φmq: because in a synchronous condenser operating in steady state Φmq does not reach
the saturation threshold, Lmq is always constant. On the contrary, since in FPM Lmq is computed
from Φmd, it’s more likely that its value varies over the simulation time.
We have to point out that these improved lookup table parameters are derived based on the
assumption that Φmq << Φmd. This means we can faithfully simulate the operating conditions
of a synchronous condenser when it operates in steady state or when small transients occur in the
machine. The above assumptions no longer hold true when the SC is subjected to grid faults.

5.5
Final considerations
In this chapter the effects of the saturation on the machine’s performance have been analyzed.
It has been demonstrated that neglecting the core saturation causes a wrong estimation of the
capability of the SC. In particular, the ﬁeld current required by the machine to deliver a speciﬁc
amount of reactive power is underestimated in the C model. This leads to the risk of overheating
the ﬁeld winding and thus of damaging the synchronous condenser.
When the terminal voltage is held stable, the ﬂux along the d-axis is constant, meaning that constant
are the values of the mutual inductances in the FPM model as well. For this reason, the FPM
machine can be replaced with an updated C model, where Lmd and Lmq are properly chosen to
emulate the degree of saturation of the FPM machine. In this way, there is no need to include
lookup table blocks in the model and the simulations can be performed in less time.
Finally, it has been shown that the inclusion of the cross saturation does not affect the derivation of
the operating points between LUT and FPM models: the magnetic ﬂux in the q-axis, indeed, is so
small that even if it is better estimated in FPM, it does not inﬂuence the performances of the model.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The development of different time varying model if synchronous condenser have been described
in this thesis. While the ﬁrst model presents constant parameters, LUT and FPM models take into
account the saturation effects in the derivation of the operating point of the SC.
In LUT the mutual inductances have been made varying as a function of the mutual ﬂuxes: in
particular, Lmd is derived based on Φmd, whereas Lmq is computed according to the intensity of
Φmq. Their values are updated in each time step through the use of lookup tables blocks. It has
been found that the saturation of the core may drastically affect the operating point of the machine:
different values of voltages and currents have been displayed by the models, and neglecting the
saturation may lead to a wrong estimation of the capability limits of the synchronous condenser.
Furthermore, in this case the d- and q-axis of the machine are assumed to belong to two indepen-
dent magnetic circuit, since the ﬂux of one axis do not affect the saturation of the other. As a
consequence, because in a synchronous condenser Iq, and thus Φq, is relatively small compared to
Id, the q-axis will never reach the saturation threshold and Lmq results constant, regardless of the
loading of the machine.
For this reason, the development of a SC model has been deepen with the analysis of the cross
saturation. This study has been carried out through the design of a magnetic equivalent circuit of
the machine. Despite the several topologies proposed in the literature, a new MEC has been derived
that faithfully resembles the air-gap ﬂux density distribution of a synchronous machine.
It turned out in the case the d-ﬂux is relatively large compared to Φmq, then the q-ﬂux does not
affect the saturation of the core. As a consequence, it is possible to apply the frozen permeability
method and derive both Lmd and Lmq as a function of Φmd.
Despite the improvements introduced in the last model, no difference was visible between the
results of LUT and FPM. When a synchronous condenser operates in steady state, indeed, the
ﬂux in the q-axis is so small that it does not affect the performances of the machine, even if the
cross saturation effects are included in the model. Besides, in the particular case the voltage at
the terminal of the machine is held stable, the degree of saturation of the core is approximately
constant, meaning that the values of Lmd and Lmq do not change over time, regardless of the
amount of reactive power delivered to the system. For this reason, it is possible to replace FPM
with a constant parameter model with updated inductances and still record accurate results.

A.5
Park transformation
Equations (A.34) - (A.45) present the electrical performance of a synchronous machine in
steady state, as a function of the line currents and voltages. We have observed that such equations
contain inductive terms that vary with rotor position, and thus with time. The inclusion of time
variant variables in the performance analysis of a synchronous machine may considerably increase
the calculation burden and thus the time required for deriving the operating points.
In order to get a time independent equation system, the stator quantities are transformed using the
Park transformation. According to this procedure, stator voltages, currents and ﬂux linkages are
decoupled into two new components each, referred to the rotating d- and q-axis.
In the following sections, the derivation of the Park transformation is presented in three steps.

A.9
Final considerations
The Park’s transformation is a powerful tool in the study of a synchronous machine, since it
turns a three-phase AC system into two equivalent DC circuits.
The further simpliﬁcations introduced with the normalization of the inductances allows us to have
a more intuitive approach to the analysis of the magnetic ﬂuxes of the machine. Besides, when the
rotor circuits are referred to the stator, the derivation of the per-unit quantities is straightforward
since it requires the calculation of the solely stator base quantities.
