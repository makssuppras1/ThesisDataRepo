Abstract
Neural networks are used for many diﬀerent applications ranging from image
and face recognition to sound processing. Their complexity makes them com-
putationally expensive. Because of this hardware acceleration is often used to
speed-up the process of both training and inference. Field programmable gate
arrays (FPGAs) are often used as a hardware accelerator for neural networks. In
recent years, many new frameworks, researches and implementations emerged.
The main obstacle during the design process of neural networks on FPGA is
the complexity of various operations (e.g. convolutions). In our work, we are
examining FPGA optimizations of convolutional neural networks. The main
aim was to examine, evaluate and design activation functions, which according
to some researches account for almost 20% of all computations. We summarized
and tested many diﬀerent methods to approximate diﬀerent activation functions
that aim for a design that is available for FPGA. Results we achieved show that
we can implement very complex operations that these functions consist of, in a
very eﬃcient form with only a small error. We were able to substitute operations
such as divisions and multiplications with shift-add algorithms that consume less
resources and are faster. These implementations of activation functions can be
used with neural networks implemented on FPGA.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Machine learning refers to computers being able to learn to do certain tasks,
whether it is recognizing faces, reading some text, or ﬁnding patterns in some
data. Similar to living beings, computers can learn based on experience. In case
of machines the experience is usually fed in form of data. Tom Mitchell deﬁnes
machine learning as follows "A computer program is said to learn from
experience E with respect to some class of tasks T and performance
measure P, if its performance at tasks in T, as measured by P, im-
proves with experience E" [Mit97, p. 2]. Another deﬁnition might be that
machine learning algorithms allow computers to perform certain tasks without
being given any instructions and they learn to perform these tasks based on
learning on some mathematical models.
The earliest ideas of machine learning date all the way back to 1940s. However,
the ﬁrst implementations of machine learning that actually showed some success
date to 1980s with advent of neural networks. Even though the ﬁrst models are
very old, the machine learning gained popularity only in recent years. This is
mainly because of the fact that machine learning is a computationally complex
and expensive process.
However, with development of neural networks and
improvements in parallel programming models on hardware accelerators such
as GPUs, machine learning gained popularity.
It is currently used in many
ﬁelds, such as anomaly detection, image processing, face recognition and other
applications.

1.1
Neural Networks on FPGAs
Even though neural networks are computationally expensive, they can beneﬁt
from progress on the ﬁeld of parallel programming and hardware acceleration.
Platforms such as CUDA [Coo13] oﬀer programmers with powerful frameworks
==PAGE:15==
1.2 Motivation and Scope
5
for parallel programming on GPUs. This was used in many diﬀerent implemen-
tations of neural networks and is used by popular frameworks for deep learning
such as PyTorch [PGC+17] or Tensorﬂow [AAB+15].
In recent years FPGAs became popular platform for implementing neural net-
works as well. However, development of neural networks on the FPGA is not
a new idea and the ﬁrst implementations were built already in 1990s such as
[CB92]. FPGAs oﬀer variety in terms of reconﬁgurability while relatively low-
power consumption is their another beneﬁt. It is perfectly shown in a paper
by Microsoft Catapult. In [ORK+15] authors show that even though GPU has
much better throughput (roughly 2-3 times), the FPGA has around 10% of
power consumption of the GPU. More recently Microsoft Catapult and many
other platforms are using FPGAs to allow for real-time A.I. such as in [FOP+18].
These two researches show both the potential of reconﬁgurability of the FPGAs
as well as their low-power consumption.
When implementing neural networks on FPGAs we have to face various diﬀer-
ent problems. One of the key problems is the problem of under-utilization of
the FPGA. FPGAs oﬀer a lot of processing elements in form of LUTs and DSP
blocks. However, poor memory management might result in under utilization.
The reasons for this might be ranging from a poor bandwidth of the bus to inef-
ﬁcient representation of the neural network. In [ZSF+18] Zhang et. al propose
a uniﬁed representation of neural networks on FPGAs. It is based on a Caﬀe
library used for HPC.
The other problem in the neural networks are activation functions. They are
often more complicated functions. Especially non-linear function such as sig-
moid, tanh, or softmax are complex and computationally expensive operations.
According to [LWL+15] activation functions can take up to 20% of the compu-
tational time of the neural network. On FPGAs we have to additionally aim
to make the operations much smaller in terms of resources which often requires
replacing multiplications and divisions with other operations (e.g. shifts and
additions). It is important to make activation functions for the FPGA imple-
mentations of neural networks as eﬃcient and fast as possible.
1.2
Motivation and Scope
As we mentioned in the previous section, activation functions are very complex
operations and take a large amount of computational time.
Our work was in cooperation with Grazper Aps who develop inference engines for

embedded solutions. The goal is to ﬁnd eﬃcient implementations of activation
functions that are suitable for inference on FPGAs. This means we do not have
to compute derivatives of the functions that are required for back-propagation.
FPGAs have several constraints when it comes to more complex operations
such as divisions, multiplications and elementary functions (e.g. exponential
or logarithm).
It is therefore important to ﬁnd a way to develop solutions
that are capable to perform these operations much more eﬃciently in terms of
resources (e.g. memories, logical elements, DSP blocks, etc.) as well as latency,
or throughput. There are already many existing solutions and in our work we
examine these existing solutions. They vary from approximating the activation
function to eﬃcient computations of diﬃcult parts of the function (e.g. obtaining
exponential function). In the end, we evaluate their hardware requirements in
terms of logical elements used and timing constraints of the designs.

2.2
Function Approximation Methods
There are many ways to approximate functions. A very good background can
be found in book Digital Arithmetic [EL04]. In this book many methods and
hardware implementations are shown. The most precise approximation method
is to use high-order polynomials. However, using such method in hardware is due
to its complexity absolutely unacceptable most of the times. Another solution
is to use tables. This method is simple. We can use any kind of software to
pre-compute the values and store them in tables. This could theoretically be a
solution for a small range of inputs with a lower precision requirements. But
with growing precision and especially range, tables would be too large and such
solution slow and ineﬃcient.

2.2.2
Polynomial Interpolation
To obtain a high-order polynomial we can use an interpolation as well. We can
apply Horner’s rule, and perform it sequentionally or in a pipelined manner.
This method requires solving N+1 linear equations for N breakpoints and ob-
taining coeﬃcients. The coeﬃcients can be stored in a table. Similar methods
are often used in hardware where the combination of tables and interpolation is
commonly used. The basic idea is to approximate the function using a polyno-
mial as shown in (2.11)
f(x) = c1 + xc2 + x2c3 + ...xncn
(2.11)
The coeﬃcients are pre-computed from systems of N linear equations for each
breakpoint. This method would require a huge memory to store all the coeﬃ-
cients. However, for some functions we can reduce the range of inputs and this
allows us to reduce the size of tables.
2.2.3
Piece-wise Linear Approximation
Piece-wise linear approximation (PWL) was proposed in multiple papers and is
used in diﬀerent implementations. Beneﬁts of this method are high precision,
small hardware utilization and high speed. The key idea is to split a function into
multiple ranges in such manner that we have to use only shifts and additions.
It was used for approximating functions such as tanh [WMJ17] or for sigmoid
[ACHG97].

Sigmoid Approximation Methods
Implementing a sigmoid function in hardware, especially on the FPGA is much
more complicated than in software. Direct implementation of the sigmoid func-

here are mostly based on simple operations such as shifts and additions.

point arithmetics is its relative imprecision. In FPGAs, ASICs and embedded
systems ﬁxed-point arithmetic is often used instead of ﬂoating-point arithmetic.

Evaluations showed us the best methods for approximating activation functions.
For sigmoid and tanh we decided to omit A-Law and recursive iterative method

4.1
Sigmoid Designs
Evaluations of diﬀerent methods show us that iterative method presented in
[BT01] and A-Law method [MH89] had the worst results. Even though iterative
method might provide us with a certain degree of design freedom and we could
apply pipelining the error was much worse than in other implementations that
should be simpler and smaller as well.
Because of that, we decided not to
consider them furthermore.
Figure 4.1 shows architecture of linear interpolation using a multiplier and ta-
bles. From evaluations we concluded that the best step was 0.125 (2−3). A
beneﬁt of using this step is the fact that it is a power of two and because of this,
==PAGE:62==
52
Design and Veriﬁcation
we can use part of a number as an address. We saturate the values towards 1
at f(4). We are also using the fact that yx≤0 = 1 −yx>0. Because of this we
only have to compute coeﬃcients for values from 0 to 4. The resulting design is
using two tables with 32 rows each, one multiplier and an adder.
Quadratic interpolation is using two multipliers and we are using formula from
(2.19). This formula requires two multiplier, since instead of evaluating xc2 +
x2c3 (if we would have x2 we would need 3) we evaluate x(c2 + xc3). We were
using step of 0.25 (twice the amount for the linear approximation). Resulting
design is in Figure 4.2. It uses two multipliers and two adders and three tables
containing 16 rows each.
Architectures for Alippi and PLAN approximations are much simpler and are
based only on shifters and adders. Figure 4.3 shows an architecture of an Alippi
algorithm implemented in hardware.
For Alippi we ﬁrst have to obtain the
fractional part of the number. Since the Alippi’s method has to contain the sign
and it is approximating values for negative parts, positive inputs are converted
into negative. Afterwards an absolute value of the integer part is obtained and
added to the number, thus obtaining fractional part of the value. This is then
shifted by 2 and 0.5 is added. The last part shifts the addition by the amount
speciﬁed by integer part (division by 2|INT (x)|).
Figure 4.4 shows an architecture for the PLAN approximation. The hardware
is much simpler than for the Alippi architecture. In the ﬁrst step, comparator
is used to assign the input into one of four intervals. For negative inputs the
output is subtracted from 1 (f(−x) = 1 −f(x)).

4.3
Exponential Linear Unit Designs
To approximate Exponential Linear Unit (ELU) we can use the interpolation
in a similar way as we use it for other functions. The design is very similar to
the one in Figure 2.2. Negative part of ELU saturates closely to -4. The design
consists of two tables with 32 rows.
We also tested diﬀerent shift-add algorithms. The architecture of CORDIC was
shown in Chapter 2 in Figure 2.1. CORDIC is often available in libraries of
many synthesis tools such as Xilinx Vivado [Xil], or Intel Quartus [Int]. We
are using a range-extended CORDIC with iterations starting at −2 which oﬀers
us with a suﬃcient range for ELU (range of convergence spans up to -5). The
design contains two additional subtractors which are responsible for computing
yin −yin × (iteration −2) and xin −xin × (iteration −2).
The other shift-add algorithm we evaluated is BKM. BKM was used to generate
elementary functions in Spinnaker [MLS+18]. BKM is an iterative algorithm and
the architecture of one stage is shown in Figure 4.6. The inputs to the stage are
called E and L. The other input is logarithm (stored in the table that contains
pre-computed results for ln(1+2−i)). The next value is unchanged if the L input
is smaller than ln(1 + 2−i) and is decided by di bit. The similar way is applied
to the next E output. It is used to replace multiplications with di, which would
produce 0 results. Totally we require two multiplexers, two adders, shifter and
a table containing logarithms. Table can also be replaced with constant value
if we pipeline the design.
Additionally we need to add two constant multiplications to the input, so we can
increase range of convergence as is shown in [MLS+18]. However, we changed
this by using comparators and range reduction. This allows us for a smaller
size of the design that does not require any multipliers. The design is in Figure
4.7. BKM is used for a certain range of values - in our case it is between 0
and −4.8696. Above this range the output is the same as the input and below
−4.8696, the output is −1. If we would like to use a larger range we would have
to use larger multiplexers.

also have to change the number of iterations. Our implementation is using 10
clock cycles in total (from -2 to 8). If we would increase α the relative error
would stay the same, but absolute error would increase approximately by α. To
tackle it we would have to change the number of iterations, or we could slightly
change the algorithm as in [HHB91] to have even greater range of convergence.
However, the other shift-add algorithm, BKM might be even better solution. We
were using 8 iterations and BKM could produce αex. The main problem is pre-
processing of the input which requires two additional constant multiplications.
In order to achieve higher speed we have to split the whole pre-processing in
two stages, so we would produce the result in almost 10 clock cycles. This is
very slow. To improve this, we can pipeline the desing, which would increase
the size more than 8 times in terms of registers and logical elements. Overally,
we consider BKM as the best method, because it allows for more ﬂexibility
and has smaller and simpler design than other methods. On the other hand, it
has the highest error of all implemented methods and is slower than the linear
interpolation. The choice is highly dependant on the amount of resources in
terms of DSP blocks available in the design as well as desired ﬂexibility.
To sum up, the best methods for sigmoid and tanh are PLAN and PLATanH
respectively. With ELU the situation is slightly more complicated, but we con-
sider BKM as the best method.
5.2
Other Optimizations of Neural Networks
Apart from approximating activation functions we have to deal with more prob-
lems such as representation of neural networks on the FPGA.
We have to make many trade-oﬀs, such as better performance or better ﬂexibil-

Results and Conclusions
ity. This issue was addressed in [ZSF+18]. The idea was to create a uniﬁed repre-
sentation of neural networks on the FPGA. Authors created a hardware/software
library called Caﬀeine. Caﬀeine library consists of multiple layers, with the high-
est level containing Caﬀe deﬁnitions for neural networks that can be translated
into hardware. Caﬀeine adds to this so the implementation can be directed at
FPGAs. As the work shows, computations within network are centered around
convolutional layers. On the other hand fully-connected layers which are usu-
ally used to make predictions take only small amount of computations, but take
a huge proportion of memory. This shows another trade-oﬀbetween memory
overhead and computational overhead. It is addressed by caching weights in
DRAMs on FPGAs. An eﬃcient way to process all of this data on the FPGA
can be through loop tiling. This means changing the loop in a manner that
increases utilization of processing elements on the FPGA. The framework also
adresses the parallelism and bandwidth optimizations.
Caﬀe, one of the most popular frameworks for deep learning was used in many
other implementations. A survey of these methods can be found in [VKB18]
which shows multiple diﬀerent tools used for mapping convolutional neural net-
works on FPGAs.
Snowﬂake, was presented in [GZCC17]. The processing is based around traces
which are contigous regions of memory. These traces are used to hide the la-
tency that is associated with various operations such as loads or stores. Larger
parts of traces are split into vectors with length of 16-bits. Computations are
based on multiply-accumulate units (MACs). The architecture consists of con-
trol cores, whose function is straightforward - controlling the computations,
compute cores, data distribution networks and memory interfaces. Snowﬂake
oﬀers 13 instructions that operate over data manipulation, memory access, or
control ﬂow. In [CZGC17] authors show a way to eﬃciently translate higher
level machine learning models into Snowﬂake hardware.
FP-DNN [GLX+17] is based on the TensorFlow framework. Contrary to previ-
ous libraries the authors divided operations into two categories - computation-
ally intensive and memory intensive and concentrate on diﬀerent optimizations
in each category. Framework’s architecture consists of multiple layers. The ﬁrst
layer is a model mapper that is responsible for mapping model onto hardware
and software model. Software model is in form of a C++ program running on
a host which is connected via PCI-e Bus with the FPGA. The FPGA is addi-
tionally connected with an oﬀ-chip DRAM to satisfy memory requirements of
neural networks. The library is combining HLS templates and RTL templates
to achieve a better eﬃciency. When it comes to support of diﬀerent models,
FP-DNN has templates for LSTM layers, CNN layers, and FC layers. Tests
show that performance of networks implemented in FP-DNN can be faster than
in CPUs, but GPU implementations still have the best performance.

5.3 Concluding Remarks
69
5.3
Concluding Remarks
In our work we mostly concentrated on implementation of activation functions
on the FPGA. Results show us that implementing various activation functions
on the FPGA is possible. In our work, we concentrated on inference part, so
the designs did not have to necessarily be available for back-propagation. This
is especially useful for implementations that are deployed on the ﬁeld and are
using pre-trained models. Methods were not only precise but often fast and
small. It is very important to have fast and eﬃcient activation functions since
computing activation functions takes a huge part of computation time of the
neural network. Simulations in neural networks show that we can use approxi-
mations with almost no impact on precision. Since both tanh and sigmoid have
range [-1, 1] and [0, 1] respectively, we could use much more fractional bits for
the output than for the input. This would deﬁnitely improve precision of the
approximations. Exponential Linear Unit (ELU) was bit more diﬃcult, because
there were no direct approximations, but iterative methods such as CORDIC
and BKM could be a good solution. However, with these methods we have to
overcome problem with range convergence.
5.4
Future Work
Even though we examined approximations for multiple activation functions,
there are still some activation functions that are very diﬃcult to implement.
For instance a softmax function is still problematic to implement, but could we
could use already available shift-add algorithms.
Current trend in research and development of neural networks on FPGAs is
however, in creating uniﬁed models and frameworks for neural networks that
would allow faster prototyping, easier and more eﬃcient development as well
as better portability. Apart from having eﬃcient activation functions we have
to consider a proper bandwidth utilization and suitable memory and processing
element utilization.
Eﬃcient activation functions, better uniﬁcation of development in terms of avail-
able frameworks, as well as improved utilization of FPGAs are all areas where
further research can improve performance of neural networks on FPGAs. This
will help to utilize FPGAs as hardware accelerators for neural networks that can
ﬁnd their use in various applications ranging from low-power embedded systems
to a huge data center-oriented applications.

VHDL Testing
We built our design using VHDL hardware deﬁnition language. To test our
design we built an environment that feeds inputs from range −2i to 2i −1 where
i is a bit width. Since we use bit widths of up to 16 bits, we can easily test the
whole range. The environment sends data to the function and receives the input.
With algorithms that are iterative environment stores the data upon receiving
a valid signal. We tested 8, 12 and 16 bits for sigmoid and tanh designs, with
4, 8 and 12 fractional bits. Tanh was using 5, 9 and 13 bits for interpolation.
Sigmoid and tanh are both using approximations that can be performed within
one clock cycle and because of that we do not have to use any valid signal to
check for result however, we still wait for half a cycle to obtain them. With ELU
we are using CORDIC and BKM which are both iterative shift-add methods and
require some valid_out signal. The output data is stored in text ﬁles and we
processed them in Jupyter notebook. We obtained values similar to evaluations
used for C++ and Python implementations.
