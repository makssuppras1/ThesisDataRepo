Abstract
In this thesis, the ﬂow-induced vibrations of a pivoted rigid cylinder subjected to strong shear ﬂow have
been investigated experimentally and numerically. The motivation of the project was the mechanical
failure of steel cages, supporting the ﬁltration media in industrial bag ﬁlters. The experiments were
conducted using the ﬂow from an axisymmetric jet, from where the ﬂow-induced vibrational response of
a long slender cylinder was investigated. The cylinder was pivoted at the top with a spring, allowing
control of its natural frequency. The cylinder investigated had an aspect ratio of 18.75, mass ratio of
330 and mass-damping 2.11. The vibrational response was investigated within an interval of reduced
velocities from 25 to 85, and shear parameter 0.20 to 0.45. The turbulence intensity was 13 to 18%. The
experimental results concluded that two primary mechanisms were causing vibrational responses of the
cylinder. At low shear parameters and reduced velocities, the vibrations were proven to be random, and
the cause assumed to be turbulent ﬂuctuating pressures. At higher reduced velocities, the vibrational
response had clear elliptical trajectories. The cylinder was found to be ﬂuid elastically unstable within
the shear ﬂow, and no signs of elliptical motions was found to be present below the critical velocity.
The critical velocity and amplitude response of the cylinder were found to be dependent on the shear
parameter. At the shear parameter 0.20, the critical reduced velocity was found to be 42. However
at higher shear parameters, the critical velocity was lower, and at lower shear parameters higher. The
amplitude response of the turbulence-induced vibrations were found to be low, typically less than 0.5
diameters. Beyond the critical velocity, the vibrational amplitude were found to increase linearly with
the reduced velocity. The amplitude response was found increase for higher shear parameters, at constant
reduced velocity. The numerical investigations were carried out using a computational ﬂuid dynamics
model using the software STAR-CCM+. The numerical model was capable of modelling ﬂuid-structure
interaction and rigid-body acceleration. The model was built with a two-dimensional approach, using
the Unsteady Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes momentum conservation.
The turbulence model was
modelled through Menters SST k-ω model. The numerical model failed to predict any vibrational response
within the experimental window, however the tendency of ﬂuid elastic instability was found. The critical
velocity of the numerical model was found to be higher, and mesh convergence tests concluded that the
solutions were not mesh-independent, nor did the mesh investigations show signs of convergence towards
asymptotic solutions.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The origin of this thesis lies in mechanical fatigue and failure of long steel cages, supporting the ﬁltration
media in industrial bag ﬁlters. The steel cages, with ﬁltration bags, are often located in close proximity
of high ﬂuid velocities, and the cause of failure is observed to be high amplitude vibrations. The cause of
vibrations are currently unknown. This thesis deals in the nature of ﬂow-induced vibrations, and seeks to
shed light on the possible ﬂuid mechanics, capable of causing vibrations and mechanical failure in regard
to the ﬂow within the ﬁlter component. The emphasis of the thesis lies in experimental measurements,
and numerical modelling of ﬂow-induced vibrations, as well as physical approaches to determining ﬂow-
induced instabilities of structures.

==PAGE:14==
1.1 Background
1.1
Background
This chapter contains an overview and introduction of the ﬁltering process, followed by the technical
operating conditions of a spray-drying system. It contains the underlying mechanics and reasons for
investigation of ﬂow-induced vibrations, in regard to this project.
Particle ﬁltration
The need for particle ﬁltration spans over a wide range in industrial production. In modern society, rules
for emissions and industrial eﬃciency are continuously increased, and as consequence, the requirement
for better technology and performance rises. Flue gases from combustion processes must be puriﬁed, and
with particle scales ranging from micrometres to nanometres, the need for higher ﬁltering performance
increases. Other industrial equipment does not deal with harmful gases, but needs ﬁltering as the particles
are part of the product, and not an unwanted by-product. This project revolves around these types of
ﬁltrations, namely bag ﬁltering associated with spray-drying of food and dairy products. For classiﬁcation,
the term bag ﬁlter is used for the entire structure containing multiple steel cages and bags, while the
term steel cage or bag is the term for a single bag used to ﬁltrate air and particles, within the bag ﬁlter
component. Emissions is not the only requirements in ﬁltering, sanitary rules also applies to the process
of spray-drying. A better ﬁlter performance does not only increase the output, but allows for a smaller,
simpler and cheaper ﬁlter. As each spray-drying facility manufactured does not have similar geometrical
ratios, ﬂuid ﬂow characteristics entering the ﬁlters does not have standard recognizable patterns. This
emphasizes the need of a ﬂuid mechanical approach of investigation, if the cause of vibrations are to be
uncovered. It is the intention that this study will reveal and clarify the background of the ﬂow-induced
vibrations, and propose methods to reveal and counteract high-amplitude vibrations.
Operating principle and ﬂow conditions
The operating principle of spray-drying, and an illustration of the bag ﬁlter component is shown in
overview at Figure 1.2. At Figure 1.2, air mixed with remaining particles is drawn out of both the drying
chamber and ﬂuidized bed. The bag ﬁlter serves as the last component before the air leaves the process.
It is therefore imperative that the ﬁltering happens as smooth as possible, in order to not become a
bottle-neck. The ﬁlter consists of roughly 250 steel cages and bags, each hanging from the top, which
can be seen in Figure 1.1 and 1.3.
Figure 1.1: Partially opened bag ﬁlter, allowing overview of steel cages. Figure commercially available
from GEA.

To avoid heavy build-up of powder on the bags, a jet of compressed air is blown through the bags at
an adjustable frequency. This prevents build-up of particles on the bag surface, thus preventing large
pressure drops over the bags. This is illustrated at Figure 1.3.
The area of interest in this project is the ﬂow inlet conditions inside a bag ﬁlter. The only method
for air to leave the ﬁlter is through the bags, meaning that essentially all of the entering air is distributed
and forced through the bags inside the ﬁlter. This process is happening at relative low velocities through
the bag fabric, compared to inlet and surrounding ﬂow velocities. Inlet air coming from cyclones and
ﬂuidized bed has high ﬂuid velocities, and due to geometrical constraints, a diﬀuser before passing the
air to the bag ﬁlter is not a viable solution. In order not to impose the bags close to the inlet with high
drag and shear, the inlet air is dispensed into the ﬁlter at the outer rims of the ﬁlter. This avoids the air
from being in direct contact with the initial bags. This is illustrated in Figure 1.4 (a). It is important
to note that Figure 1.4 is only illustrating the cages within the ﬁlter, as the actual number of cages are
much higher and their relative size smaller. The inlet conditions helps reducing the ﬂuid velocity before
contact with the bags. This reduces both drag and lift imposed on the bags, and avoids excessive tear of
the bag ﬁltration media, due to particles shearing the surface. The inlet conditions has the implication
that some of the outer cages will experiences high velocities in close proximity. However, they are only
partially subjected to the high velocities, due to the inlet being atop of the ﬁlter. This can be seen in
Figure 1.4 (b). The cages are only partially subjected to high velocities, and this matter has to be taken
into account when dealing with the applied force from the ﬂuid.

1.2 Thesis Statement
1.2
Thesis Statement
1.2.1
Purpose
The purpose of this project is to gain knowledge and insight into the ﬂuid mechanical phenomena, capable
of causing mechanical vibrations in relevance of the ﬁltering process. Due to the vibration and mechanical
parameters being yet unknown, part of this project also requires qualiﬁed assumptions, based on literature
study, to narrow the project to a single experimental and numerical approach of an appropriate ﬂow-
induced vibration phenomena.
1.2.2
Motivation
Both low and high amplitude vibrations of the steel cages have been ﬁlmed during operation of the ﬁlter.
The mechanical fatigue of the steel cages are assumed to be due to a determinable (and not random)
motion, since the high amplitude vibrations are primarily observed to be repeating harmonic oscillations.
Mechanical fatigue and failure of the steel cages within the bag ﬁlters eventually leads to temporary
shut down of the spray drying process, while manually changing the cages. Spray-drying plants often
run at almost full capacity for long periods at a time, and it is therefore undesirable having to shut
down and wait for replacement cages. Increasing the structural stiﬀness of the cages in vicinity of high
velocity ﬂow, should theoretically decrease the maximum amplitude of vibrations. However, increasing
the stiﬀness requires either more expensive materials or more mass. Increasing the weight of each cage
is an unwanted solution, since replacement of bags and cages happens manually, and staﬀcannot be
required to do heavy lifting due to rules of physical health implications. Applying mechanical constraint
to the bags will require the constraints to somehow link the steel cages to the structure of the ﬁlter. Not
only will this disable easy maintenance and replacement from the top of the ﬁlter, it will also require
the constraint to pass through the ﬁlter bag, and thereby be positioned in the sanitary part of the ﬁlter.
This will require extra care since the particles are not allowed to enter the bag without being ﬁltered,
and the constraints will have to receive special care. Due to geometrical constraints, the inlet air cannot
simply be diﬀused without reworking the design of the plant, which would essentially require more space
and materials.
For these reasons, the project deals with around investigating the ﬂuid mechanical cause of vibra-
tions. The ability to understand and physically explain the cause of vibrations, may help to ﬁnd a solution
from a ﬂuid mechanical point of view, without violating the above mentioned problems.
1.2.3
Objectives
The objective of the project lies in
Litterature Study: Give a brief overview of literature regarding ﬂow-induced vibration relevant
to the scope of the project.
Narrow the scope of the project: Reduce the project scope to a single ﬂow-induced vibration
phenomena, named the ﬁeld of study, which is considered likely to be the cause of mechanical
failure.
Dynamical Study: Investigate the dynamics of the slender cylinder motion, and its experimen-
tal/numerical approach in ﬂow-induced vibrations.
Experiment: Design and conduct an experiment that captures and evaluates the ﬂow-induced
vibrations. The experiment also serves as experimental validation of the numerical model.
CFD: Design and conduct a computational ﬂuid dynamic model, studying the numerical ability to
predict vibrations. This includes discussion of ﬂuid-structure interaction, and various parameters
required to model vibrations.

1.3 Literature
1.3
Literature
This chapter contains a presentation and discussion of the relevant literature, regarding ﬂow-induced
vibrations. As the literature on ﬂow-induced vibration mechanisms extents quite heavily, only literature

Many of these phenomena are not considered relevant, such as vibrations caused by internal ﬂow

1.3.1
Vortex-induced Vibration
The basic mechanics of vortex-induced vibrations is the occurrence of vortices shed from a bluﬀbody, as
the shear boundary-layer separates unsteadily. The motion is generated by alternating boundary-layer
separation, where the inner-most part moves at much lower velocities than the outer layer due to viscous
friction caused by the bluﬀbody surface. This causes the two shear layers to roll into the wake and
coalesce into discrete swirling vortices [1]. As a result, the roll-up and shedding of these vortices induces
the bluﬀbody with an alternating lift and drag force, which in turn may cause vibrational response if
the bluﬀbody is capable of displacing.
In the last decades, multiple studies of both free oscillations and structurally dependant oscillations
due to vortex-shedding has been conducted. Compilations and discussions worth mentioning are referred
in the bibliography of [2–15]. Although the classiﬁcations and parameter-dependence of vortex-induced
vibrations contains almost limitless combinations, an introduction to only two primary types will be given
for convenience. These are free oscillations and structurally dependant interaction.
In freely-oscillating experiments, objects such as cylinders are subjected to cross-ﬂow and can dis-
place in either one or two degrees-of-freedom. The structural parameters, such as mass are generally
low, and there is no natural frequency response of the structure. This experimental and/or numerical
approach is typically used to study the formation and classiﬁcation of the vortices. Response of freely

oscillating bluﬀbodies have been found to have multiple shedding modes, from which types of vortices
can change abruptly [5,14]. The response of freely oscillating bluﬀbodies are typically characterized by
3 regimes named ’branches’. These are; the initial branch, upper branch and lower branch. The branches

Vibrational characteristics of vortex-induced vibrations of structures primarily concerns the lock-in
phenomena.
During lock-in, the vortex shedding frequency locks on to the natural frequency of the
structure, creating positive feedback and amplifying vibrational response. During this entrainment, the
vortex shedding occurs in phase with the oscillations of the structure. The vortex-shedding frequency,
and consequently the Strouhal number, is as such capable of deviating from the steady-state vortex-
shedding frequency. The structure geometry, ﬂow characteristics and ﬂuid-structure interaction are all
primary factors aﬀecting the response. For the simple case of a pivoted cylinder, many studies concerning
dimensionless parameters of one and two degrees of freedom have been conducted in order to investigate
responses and uncover the eﬀect of scaling (e.g. [17]).
Arrays of structures and wake-interaction may further complicate vortex-induced vibrations. The
simplest conﬁguration of two cylinders in tandem will have signiﬁcantly diﬀerent responses. The arrange-
ment and wake characteristics are dependant on diameters and spacing between the cylinders, known as
spacing ratio [18]. It has been observed that the Strouhal number of cylinders are strongly dependant on
the distance of the spacing ratio [19]. Wake-galloping can occur for an downstream structure catching the
wake of an upstream bluﬀbody. Amplitudes as high as 2-3 times that of normal vortex-induced vibrations
can occur by wake-induced excitation [20]. Increasing the amount of cylinders or their packing typically
results in more complex phenomena, which are often qualitatively similar to another phenomena, known
as ﬂuid elastic instability.
1.3.2
Galloping & Flutter
Galloping and Flutter are gathered in the same discussion, as they occur due to the same response of
ﬂuid-structure interaction. Flutter is a term used in aerospace engineering for torsion-plunge instabilities
of air-foils. Galloping is the engineering term for one-degree-of-freedom bluﬀbody vibrations, due to
either plunge- or torsion-instability [1]. The mechanics responsible for ﬂutter and galloping is attributed
to instantaneous changes of lift and drag, as a structure changes its relative angle of attack towards
the ﬂuid, as it displaces. Coincidentally, analysis of galloping implies no instabilities of structures with
circular cross-section within a uniform ﬂow. This is due to circular cross-sections not changing lift and
drag relative to the ﬂow, as the structure is rotated or translated within the ﬂow.
Galloping and Flutter traces all the way back to 1907 where galloping of a D-section cylinder was
described [21]. In 1930, the instability were assigned to the variation in lift and drag relative to the
ﬂuid [22]. Galloping and ﬂutter typically utilizes quasi-steady theory (explained in section 1.5), which
allows an objects speciﬁc lift and drag coeﬃcients by conducting experiments in steady ﬂow, at various
angles of attack. Although this theory has proven very eﬃcient, it is limited to regions where transient
forces does not occur, regardless of steady-state boundary conditions. Galloping and ﬂutter typically
occurs above ﬂuid velocities that involves vortex-induced vibrations.
However structural parameters
may create susceptibility to both galloping and vortex-induced vibrations within certain ranges of ﬂuid
velocities, which questions the reliability of quasi-steady theory in these cases [22,23]. It is a requirement
that the ﬂuid velocity has to be well above the range of vortex-induced vibrations, before utilizing quasi-
steady theory.
Recent discussion regarding applicability and mathematics of quasi-steady theory on
galloping can be found in [24]. As a physical example of galloping, power conductor lines are known to
experience galloping, which results in economic problems as power lines fail, e.g. [25].
The response characteristics of galloping and ﬂutter are large oscillation amplitudes as the instan-
taneous structural dampening changes from positive to negative. This overthrows structural vibrations
into being self-excited. Galloping of structures often implies a sudden exponential growth of oscillations
at a critical ﬂuid velocity. While ﬂutter of air-foils are generally classiﬁed into several types depending
on degrees-of-freedom and structural ﬁxing, the vibrational response is often similar.

1.3.3
Fluid Elastic Instability
Fluid Elastic Instability is a term used to describe unstable motions of elastic structures due to alternating
ﬂuid forces caused by the motion itself [1]. Closely packed cylinders subjected to cross-ﬂow, as in shell-and-
tube heat exchangers, have been observed to perform synchronous ’ovalling’ motions above certain ﬂow
velocities. The same phenomena may be seen in power transmission lines, however this is termed ’wake-
galloping’. The mechanic is explained by wake-coalescence of packed cylinders, where the coalescence
alternates once the cylinders displace. The change of wake coalescence implies alternating lift and drag
unto the cylinders, causing vibrations. This phenomena is so common in tubular heat exchangers, that
standards have been developed to ascertain that manufactured heat exchangers will not be subject to
ﬂuid elastic instability (see TEMA [26]).
Studies of Blevins (See [1]) explained the primary driving force of ﬂuid elastic instability as the time
lag between alternatively moving cylinders, and thereby the alternating ﬂuid force unto the cylinders.
H.J. Connors [27] observed a row of tubes suspended on piano wires in a wind tunnel to be unstable,
and attributed this to the displacement-induced ﬂuid forces, as the cylinders displaced harmonically in
elliptical ﬁgures. It has been discussed where either this phenomena will be visible for a single elastic
cylinder within a rigid setup, or requires the interaction of multiple elastic cylinders.
In 1983, [28]
signs that single elastic tubes could be unstable due was observed, and both time-lag and displacement
mechanisms was used to explain the instability. Since then, mechanisms and studies in revealing the
complexity and simpliﬁcations of models have been investigated, see for instance [29–32] for newer studies
of ﬂuid elastic instability in tube arrays. Fluid elastic instability has been observed for single cylinders,
without relation to wake-coalescence of arrays. Cylinders within ﬂuids with high shear gradients have
been observed to be unstable, purely due to structures relative velocity and displacement within the shear
ﬂow [33].
The characteristics of ﬂuid elastic instability is characterized in synchronous ovalling motions of
multiple cylinders or tubes. Oscillations are not occurring until a critical velocity, dependant on the
structure and ﬂow, is reached. Once the critical velocity is reached, amplitudes have been observed to
raise rapidly, up to a fourth potency of ﬂuid velocity [1]. This is another reason as to why ﬂuid elastic
instability is a dangerous phenomena, and may cause destruction in heat exchangers. For single cylinder
tests, somewhat similar responses have been found [33].
As a speciﬁc critical velocity was reached,
vibrations could occur of structures within a shear ﬂow. However, the amplitude did not increase as
rapidly, but resembled a a linear relationship with the ﬂuid velocity.
1.3.4
Turbulence-induced Vibrations
Turbulence and random excitation forces may cause unstructured vibrations, classiﬁed as random or
chaotic. Chaos refers to a state where there is little or no apparent physics that deﬁnes the sequence of
force and resultant vibrations. To determine if vibrations are chaotic or deterministic in nature, statistical
tools such as frequence analysis, probability density function, poincar´e maps and Lyapunov exponents are
often necessary [1,34]. Turbulent coherent structures, intensities, and ﬂuid-structure boundary interaction
of turbulence are rarely generic. Each case of turbulent vibrations is speciﬁc, and generic results cannot
be measured and extrapolated for systematic use. Some structures inﬂuenced by similar forces, such as
wind excitation may experience similar vibration, and the results worth sharing, e.g [35,36]. Common for
vibrations induced by turbulence is a random pattern of accelerations, which are attributed to turbulent
ﬂuctuating pressures on the structural surfaces [1].
Characteristics of turbulence induced vibrations is unstructured movement patterns.
Vibrations
may come in small patterns, or lumps, with the structure undergoing sudden accelerations. Turbulence
characteristics are classiﬁed as being either narrow-band or broad-band [1]. This refers to the frequency
spectrum of the excitation force. In narrow-band turbulence, excitation occurs within a small band of
frequencies, and vibrational excitation happens within this band of frequencies. In broad-band turbulence,
there is no speciﬁc frequency of excitation, and the response pattern becomes highly unstructured in
both amplitude and time. Statistical analysis is used to determine where either the excitation force and
structural response, is caused by something within a speciﬁc band of frequencies.

1.4 Field of Study
1.4
Field of Study
The phenomena involving ﬂow-induced vibrations extends heavily, and it is rarely certain what may be
causing vibrations until research and investigations are conducted. This chapter serves as a discussion
and argumentation for the direction of the experimental and numerical approach. The primary three
factors expected to be possible explanations of the ﬂow-induced vibrations experienced in the bag ﬁlters
are: vortex-induced vibrations, ﬂuid elastic instability, and vibrations due to turbulence.
Galloping and ﬂutter are not considered, since these are occurring for non-cylindrical cross-sections, and
limited to one degree of freedom or torsional-plunge instabilities.
Motions due to turbulence are expected to be part of the vibrations.
This is since the motion
often stops and accelerates, at random and indeterministic time intervals. Unless the occurrence of these
vibrations can be measured to happen at certain intervals, the vibrations are assumed to be caused by
turbulence. It is however unlikely, that vibrations due to turbulence is the only mechanic present. The
mechanisms capable of causing deterministic vibrations in relation to cages within a bag ﬁlter component
will be discussed and evaluated, in order to narrow the ﬁeld of the project.
When dealing with ﬂow-induced vibrations, there are one important factor that has to be considered.
As ﬂuid forces on structural surfaces are very dependent on the boundary layer, changes of the boundary
layer will cause a change of vibrations accordingly. Studies by Frannson et. al. [37], concluded that
by applying suction to a cylinder, the boundary layer would alter, and the Strouhal number of vortex
shedding change. It is not unlikely, that since the ﬁlters are continuously sucking in air, the boundary
layer is aﬀected, and so will the vibrational response be. It is a factor that is yet not investigated, and the
mechanical implications in regard to vibrations are unknown. Before being able to investigate the eﬀect
of suction, it is considered a good approach to investigate the mechanism(s) that may be responsible for
the observed vibrations. Without basic knowledge of the mechanisms, eﬀects of further complications
cannot be known to be relevant or not.
Vortex-induced vibrations is a subject heavily investigated in ﬂuid mechanics. It is often not the
lack of knowledge in the mechanics that lies in the problem of counteracting vortex-induced vibrations.
It is typically the lack of data from ﬂow ﬁelds in the machinery, or structural vibration measurements,
which allows for qualitative analysis. In regard to a bag ﬁlter, a few observations are very noteworthy.
Vortex-induced vibrations are known to generate ﬁgure 8 trajectories. The observed vibrations in the
ﬁlter are elliptical. However, this cannot be used as veriﬁcation that it is not vortex shedding that are
responsible for vibrations within the ﬁlter. Khierkhah et. al [38] concluded that structures that are
able to transfer energy in diﬀerent degrees of freedom, may perform elliptical motions induced by vortex
shedding. As such, it is diﬃcult to ascertain if the vibrations are caused by vortex-shedding, without
investigations of the structural response to perturbations, or excitations.
Another form of vortex-induced vibrations are relevant in arrays of cylinders. Wake-galloping in-
duced by vortex shedding.
Wake galloping is a likely cause, since vibrational amplitudes are known
to be much higher than those of structural response to its own vortex shedding. Finding evidence of
wake-galloping requires knowledge of the ﬂow, such as the reduced velocity, and spacing ratio between
the cylinders. The best way to analyse if wake-galloping is occurring is through acquisition of data.
The last phenomena considered capable of causing observed vibrations, is ﬂuid elastic instability.
The ﬁrst consideration, is instability of tube arrays, where ﬂuid are forced through cylindrical tube packs,
e.g. heat exchangers. Fluid elastic instability of this type is known to be dependent on the packing of
tubes, and the single tubes position in the array [39]. Fluid elastic instability within tube arrays are not
comparable to the ﬂow in a bag-ﬁlter. The diﬀerence lies in the inlet conditions within a bag ﬁlter, where
the air is sent around the cylinder array, and not in between. The steel cages in the center of the ﬁlter
are observed to not undergo vibrations, and it is not all of the cylinder along the walls that has high
amplitude vibrations.
Fluid elastic instability of a single structure are considered relevant to this project. During ﬂuid
elastic instability of a single structure, the structure is located within a non-uniform ﬂow, or has a non-
symmetrical cross-section. As lift and drag changes resulting from either movement or position in the
ﬂow, the vibrations becomes self-excited once the amplitude or ﬂuid velocity reaches a critical point. An
example relevant to this project is investigations by Yu et. al [33]. Yu et al. conducted experiments
of a small wire within a 2 dimensional shear ﬂow, having a constant shear gradient. The wire had a
high mass ratio of 5112 (Non-dimensional quantities are explained in section 1.5) and a shear parameter
between 0 and 0.14. The tests were conducted at reduced velocities between 100 and 1400, which is
way beyond velocities found in a typical bag ﬁlter. The wire did experience ﬂuid elastic instability, once
critical velocity was reached. The critical velocity proved dependent on the shear parameter. While

In this chapter, non-dimensional properties that are important to the project is presented. It is assumed
that the reader is familiar with the ﬂuid mechanical numbers, the Reynolds number and the Strouhal

is important to understand the approach of using non-dimensional numbers of ﬂow-induced vibrations,
as both ﬂow and structural parameters are coupled. When ﬂuid parameters are coupled to structural
parameters, the free stream ﬂuid mechanical parameters are used. These are not coupled or linked to the
structural ﬂuid boundary interaction that occurs, once the structure is positioned within the ﬂuid. In
practical applications, the quantities are attained through knowledge of the ﬂow, prior to the insertion of
the structure within the ﬂow. This enables full information of the free stream values passing the volume
where the structure is later positioned. This is the general method adapted in this project. As a side note,
in some practical applications, the structure has been built before ﬂow-induced vibrations was known to
occur. The exact ﬂow passing the structure may therefore be diﬃcult to asses, and the approach is made
through estimations of the free stream values in front of the structure.

Chapter 2
Method
The method chapter is an introduction to the methodological approach of investigating the vibrational
response of long slender cylinders, located in strong shear ﬂows. Prior to the experimental and numerical
investigations, the project speciﬁcation is presented. This chapter contains the known parameters and
conditions that are governing the scope of investigation. The project speciﬁcation is important, since it
determines and presents both the physical knowledge, and non-dimensional quantities of the industrial
equipment assumed to be important in the vibration analysis. Once the knowledge of ﬂow, dynamics and
geometrical conditions of the vibrations within a bag ﬁlter has been determined, an experimental model
was constructed and used to investigate the ﬂow-induced vibrations.
The method chapter primarily revolves around two sections.
They are the introduction to the
experimental design, and the presentation of the computational ﬂuid dynamics model. It is important to
note that a section is presented in between these, which investigates the dynamics required to couple the
experimental and numerical model.
The experimental approach requires a ﬂow with a shear gradient. This is further split into two
requirements. The ﬁrst is the generation of a shear ﬂow, and the second being the measurement method
from which ﬂow data can be extracted. The generation of a ﬂow proﬁle are attained using an axisymmetric
jet. The axisymmetric jet is known to form a shear mixing layer between the quiescent ambient ﬂuid and
high velocity ﬂuid from the nozzle. The axisymmetric jet, its ﬂow proﬁle and how to control the velocity
magnitude from the nozzle is presented in chapter 2.2.1. The acquisition of data from the ﬂow is attained
using a hot-wire constant temperature anemometer (abb. hot-wire CTA). The method of using hot-wire
CTA is presented in chapter 2.2.3. A scaled down cylinder model, capable of investigating vibrational
response to the ﬂow has been constructed. The vibrational approach is using a long slender rigid plastic
tube, that is capable of rotating from a pivot point at its top. The pivot is controlled by a spring, which
also serves to control the natural frequency of the cylinder. The cylinder model is presented in chapter
2.2.2. The technique used to measure the vibrations of the cylinder is through the use of strain gauges.
Strain gauges are capable of measuring strain of a structural surface, and as the cylinder vibrates there
are more than plenty of inﬁnitesimal strains occurring, that are easily measured by use of strain gauges.
The measurement technique involved in using strain gauges are embedded in chapter 2.2.3.
Once the experimental technique has been presented, the method presents the numerical approach.
The numerical approach involves a computational ﬂuid dynamics model created with the software STAR-
CCM+. Since the computational model is built from non-dimensional quantities in a two-dimensional
domain, the dynamics involved to rewrite the cylinder motion are investigated. The non-dimensional
approach to ﬂuid-structure interaction are presented are presented in chapter 2.3. The numerical model
itself is presented in chapter 2.4.

Experimental Design
An experimental approach is conducted in order to search for vibrational response within a shear ﬂow.
In order to do so, an experimental ﬂow proﬁle has to be generated, and a cylindrical model capable
of vibrating constructed. The ﬂow proﬁle, with variable gradient, is produced using an axisymmetric
jet. The axisymmetric jet forms a shear mixing layer from its nozzle, which grows proportional to its
distance from the jet. This allows for both variable ﬂow velocity, by increasing the jet nozzle velocity, and
a variable gradient, by positioning the cylinder diﬀerently within the ﬂow. To capture the vibrational
response of the structure within the shear ﬂow, a cylindrical model is constructed. The experimental
model is a slender plastic tube (made of PVC), capable of rigid body rotation from a spring at its top.
The cylinder resembles the classical mechanics of a ’compound pendulum’.

The dynamics of a compound pendulum are presented. This chapter serves to illustrate the equation of
motion, and its relation to ﬂow-induced motions. The end of the chapter contains the non-dimensional
solution to ﬂuid-structure interaction of a structure, translating with only a single degree of freedom.
The computational ﬂuid dynamics model is built in a two-dimensional plane, since motion of struc-
tures within high velocity ﬂuids are computationally expensive. Due to the experimental cylinder being
capable of rotational motion in three dimensions, this chapter contains theory required to rewrite the
motion to a two-dimensional translating motion of a small cylinder cross-section. It is important to note
that the experimental model has three degrees of freedom. It may translate in the x- and/or y-direction,
and it may vibrate with small amplitudes in its normal direction, due to the choice of the spring. It is
assumed that the motion in the normal direction is small and negligible. By doing so, the experimental
model is considered to have only two degrees of freedom. The rotations about the pivot point, θx and
θy. This allows the experimental model to be treated similarly to a compound pendulum. In order to
establish the equation of motion, the motion is analysed in a single degree of freedom. This assumes
that the degrees of freedom are decoupled, and no energy are transferred between them. The assumption
of no motion in the normal direction, and decoupled degrees of freedom are investigated in the results
chapter.

2.4.4
Wall laws and Convective Courant number
Wall laws
In turbulent ﬂows, ﬂuid parameters within boundary layers are often modelled instead of calculated
directly. This can be done since all turbulent boundary layers behave approximately equal when non-
dimensionalized, in regions very close to surfaces/walls. Therefore, when applying a volume discretization,
unless cells are suﬃciently small to resolve the boundary layer, the standard non-dimensional turbulent
proﬁles are used to determine the ﬂow. These turbulent proﬁles and their algorithm of application are
known as ’wall laws’.
The use of wall-laws are monitored by the y+ value, as it describes the non-
dimensional distance from the wall, for which wall laws are used instead of directly resolving the ﬂow.
For ﬂuid-structure interaction, the y+ value wish to be kept low (< 1), in order to make sure the boundary
layer is well resolved, and the resulting force acting on the rigid body wall is not purely dominated by
use of turbulent boundary layer approximations.

The Convective Courant number (or simpliﬁed Courant number) can best be described by considering

2.4.6
Considerations on 2D simulations
Although the simulation would preferably be carried out in two dimensions, the DFBI algorithm does not
have a two-dimensional formulation. This is due to the integrated pressure and shear terms, governing
the movement, which requires cell surface area to integrate forces over. Instead, the simulation is carried
out by creating a pseudo-2D domain. This domain is technically three dimensional, but thin enough
to only cover 1 cell in the third dimension. The domain walls in the third dimension is imposed with
symmetry, such that momentum or mass are not allowed to exit in this direction.
This creates the
pseudo-2D domain, where translation or rotation of the cylinder in 2-dimensions are possible.

Accuracy
The accuracy of a single measurement may be estimated through two contributions. The ﬁrst being
the standard error introduced by calibrating the hot-wire. The second is the error introduced by using
measurement equipment to compute the air density, and measuring the pressure inside the jet. This
error propagates through the use of Bernoulli’s equation. The hot-wire calibration, using the 4th order
polynomial ﬁt, was computed to have a deviation to the measurement points with an average error of
0.0015 percent. The post-calibration however contained considerable higher error. The average error
of the post-calibration compared to the original calibration was 3.15 %. The considerably higher post-
calibration error is assumed to be due to changes of air temperature and pressure during the experiment,
since the measurements were taken over several hours in a room where doors were often opened and closed.
Supposedly, this could lead to much diﬀerent temperature and pressure conditions before and after the
experiment. The average error introduced by the measurement equipment, and the use of Bernoulli’s
equation is computed in Appendix B. Although data sheets have not been found for all the measurement
equipment, assumptions of uncertainties still proved to introduce only a small error of ≪0.1 m/s.
The total error introduced are presented as the measurement ±2 standard deviations. The error
introduced by calibrating the hot-wire are considered to be lower than the highest error found, being

Analysis of motion signals
To illustrate vibrational signals, the vibrations induced by the ﬂow is shown in Figure 3.21 and 3.22. As it
can be seen, some signals indicated diﬀerent behaviour than others. Prior to the investigated instability,
many diﬀerent velocities and shear gradients were measured. This was done to investigate if any vibrations
indicated diﬀerent responses. From spectral analysis of the turbulent ﬂow, the ﬂuid is known to contain
not one but a multitude of frequencies. The force frequency from the turbulence is therefore broad-band.
Due to this wide range of frequencies, ﬂuid forces stemming from turbulence does not excite a system
at a speciﬁc frequency, and as a result, the vibrations does not attain a speciﬁc pattern. Vibrations
caused by broad-band frequencies, such as turbulence, are often chaotic in nature. With ﬂow-induced
vibrations caused by broad-band turbulence, it is often suﬃcient to use theory of random motion, and
analysis through statistical tools [1]. In the measurement region, the ﬂow was measured to have 13-18%
turbulence intensity, as was seen in Figure 3.10. The ﬂuid force exerted unto the cylinder, along with
a high turbulence intensity is a typical cause of random motion vibrations. Figure 3.21 is a classical
example of ﬂow-induced vibrations caused by turbulent broad-band frequencies.

3.2 Experimental vibration results
3.2.7
Discussion of critical velocity
It has been found that the vibrational response to the ﬂow undergoes a very qualitative change, once
a critical velocity is reached. The probability density function indicated that the induced motion went
from being random, to deterministic.
Since the motion is driven at much higher reduced velocities
than vibrations induced by vortex shedding, the probability of vortices being locked on to the natural
frequency are small and improbable. The measurement area is in the quasi-steady region, and the eﬀects
of transient ﬂow behaviour should be small or negligent.
With the qualitative change of vibrational
behaviour at a critical velocity, and ovalling motions, the investigated instability falls under the category
of ﬂuid elastic instability. It is common for ﬂuid elastic instabilities to not be present until a critical
velocity, however the implications of this are signiﬁcant.
In terms of dynamics of machinery theory,
the qualitative change of vibrational behaviour happening when system parameters are changed slightly,
is known as a bifurcation [34].
Bifurcation processes are in themselves complicated, and sensitive to
the structural and excitation parameters involved. As an example, it was proven in the experiment that
changes of shear parameter K, resulted in changes of not only the amplitude, but also the critical velocity.
Similar changes of system parameters, such as damping or mass ratio, or the ﬂuid Reynolds number, are
as such also expected to have impact of not only the amplitude, but also the critical velocity.

which was proven due to the bifurcation process. It is said from Vibrations and Stability, by Jon Juel
Thomsen [34] that ”Bifurcations can only occur when a system is structurally unstable. A structurally
stable system retains its qualitative properties even if its parameters are slightly perturbed. A structurally
unstable system does not.” The structural stability refers to structural properties and parameters, such
as mass ratio, natural frequency and force applied. It is therefore known, and should be considered,
that the physics involved has the capability of changing qualitatively to small perturbations of system
parameters.
It is proposed to consider eﬀects of numerical damping. Numerical damping are capable of counter-
acting motion, and will accumulate with reﬁnement of meshing, and decreases in timestep.
A few remarks on simulated time
By investigating the signals from the medium grid, it can be observed that at >1700 non-dimensional
time, the amplitude is consistent. The time simulated to reach this point has to be considered. The
coarse and ﬁne can be seen to require up to 4- or 5000 non-dimensional time before the amplitudes are
consistent. The amount of cycles required to be simulated before a stable point is reached, are attributed
to translation in multiple degrees of freedom. Using initial conditions of the cylinder motion proved to
have little or worse eﬀect on the time requirement. Increasing the number of cells resulted in smaller
time steps required, due to the CCN, and the computational time increased from a few days to a week.

3.3 Computational Fluid Dynamics
3.3.10
Discussion of the CFD assumptions
The CFD model was unable to predict the onset of instability in range of the experiment. The primary
assumptions, and changes going from the experiment to CFD modelling are discussed. The diﬀerence of
the experiment and CFD model are split into two contributions. There are direct changes of parameters,
that can be measured explicitly, and there are the indirect physics of ﬂuids and cylinder motion, that are
lost by going from the experiment to the CFD model (implicit properties).
Explicit properties
The direct measurable changes consists of the averaged viscous damping factor, the inlet proﬁle, and
the cylinder natural frequency. The viscous damping factor computed from experimental tests proved
a diﬀerence of about 14% of the two impulse tests. The natural frequency had a diﬀerence of 3% in
each direction. These diﬀerences are neglected in the CFD model. The cylinder resided in a ﬂow ﬁeld
that is known to have changed 1.36% from the experiment. While these are all known discrepancies,
their percentages are small, and the model results are not expected to change drastically through these
simpliﬁcations.
Implicit properties
As the model is simulated in a pseudo-2D domain, the physics of ﬂuids involving three dimensions are
eliminated. This includes several phenomena, from the possibility of oblique vortex shedding, to turbulent
structures and ﬂuctuations in the third dimension. Turbulence is known to be three dimensional, and
while the model is not expected to be very sensitive to turbulence, it is a factor that is eliminated by
only having a single cell in the third direction. Fluid velocities in the third direction may also have had
a great inﬂuence on the experimental investigation, which has been neglected in the CFD model.
Fluid boundary layers around moving structures are generally complicated. It is unknown if the
ﬂow around the experimental cylinder may have caused forces that are not captured in the CFD model.
While the rewriting of motion from 3 to 2 dimensions assumed only very small motions, the actual
deﬂection of the cylinder in the experiment was about 1-2 diameters. Fluid force may cause a lift eﬀect
at high amplitudes on the cylinder, as the ﬂuid may be pushed under the cylinder. This causes an eﬀect
resembling the lift on a wing proﬁle. Eﬀects such as these will cause higher amplitudes, as the cylinder
is accelerated upwards. Eﬀects such as these are also neglected in the 2 dimensional model.
The physical cylinder in the experiment was coupling energy between the multiple degrees of freedom.
From the CFD simulations, it was obvious that without motion in the y-direction, there would be no
alternating lift and drag apart from the vortex shedding mechanism. Slight motions in the y-direction
caused responses of motion along the x-direction, as the streamwise velocity unto the cylinder changed
due to the shear gradient. Without translation of the cylinder in the ﬂow ﬁeld, the prediction of critical
velocity or amplitude is lost. It is expected, that the coupling of energy may act as a strong accelerator
of vibrations, when the shear parameter is high.

Chapter 4
Conclusion
Flow-induced vibrations in regard to steel cages located in bag ﬁlter components have been investigated
throughout this project. The perspective was to investigate ﬂuid elastic instability, since many of the
steel cages were located in vicinity of high ﬂuid velocities and shear ﬂow.
A jet ﬂow was used to investigate ﬂuid elastic instability of a pivoted cylinder undergoing rigid
deﬂections.
The ﬂow was produced using an axisymmetric jet, and the ﬂow proﬁle measured using
hot-wire CTA. The ﬂow proﬁle was observed to contain a narrow band of high-energy frequencies, from
which the source was unknown. The narrow-band was expected to be due to Kelvin-Helmholtz vortices.
From knowledge of the ﬂow characteristics, the average ﬂuid momentum and shear gradient exerted unto
the experimental cylinder was computed. A series of vibrational measurements were then conducted,
from were diﬀerent vibrational characteristics was observed. At low reduced velocities, the vibrations
took on the characteristics of random motions, indicating that the vibrations were being driven by
ﬂuctuating turbulent pressures, on the cylinder surface. The random motion vibrations was found to
involve rapid accelerations of the cylinder, with no particular preference of directions or trajectories.
At higher reduced velocities, the vibrations was observed to become deterministic. Through statistical
analysis, the vibrations clearly indicated the onset of an instability. Investigating the trajectories and
amplitudes indicated a clear qualitative change of vibrational behaviour. The ﬂow-induced vibrations
involved the onset of an instability, which was contributed to the cylinder being ﬂuid elastically unstable.
The instability occurred at a critical velocity, from where the cylinder was driven in elliptical trajectories.
The elliptical trajectories was observed to stretch along the streamwise velocity, when the reduced velocity
was increased, and observed to rotate if the shear parameter was changed. The critical velocity was
found to be dependent on the shear parameter, decreasing for higher shear parameters and increasing for
lower shear parameters. The magnitude of vibrations was similarly found to be dependent on the shear
parameter. Higher shear parameters resulted in increases of amplitudes, at constant reduced velocity.
The turbulent ﬂuctuating pressures proved to have the capability of destabilizing motions caused by
the ﬂuid elastic instability. This created transitional regions of random and structured behaviour. In
the transitional regions, the trajectories could be elliptical for several hundred cycles, before stopping
altogether, and then accelerated into an elliptical trajectory again.
A computational ﬂuid dynamics model was created, simulating the instability by use of a 2 di-
mensional cylindrical cross-section. The motion was modelled using the STAR-CCM+ dynamic ﬂuid
body interaction algorithm. The numeric model was found to have similar behaviour as the experiment,
however with a higher critical velocity and lower amplitudes.
Sensitivity tests of key computational
parameters concluded that the cylinder amplitudes were very sensitive to key parameters, such as the
overset mesh iterator and convective courant number. Mesh convergence tests proved that the motion
simulated was subjected to not only quantitative changes, but the solution was capable of altering dras-
tically or disappearing altogether. The simulated amplitudes were found to decrease by using a coarser
mesh, and disappear altogether on a ﬁner mesh. This concluded that no solutions independent of the
mesh were to be found, in the investigated region. Simulating translation of a structure in two degrees
of freedom at high reduced velocities was found to require a high amount of simulated time.

Chapter 5
Future Works
In this chapter, the results are summarized and put in perspective to analysis of cylindrical structures
within large industrial components, such as bag ﬁlters. The current knowledge attained is put in per-
spective to approaching ﬂuid elastic instabilities, and future investigations are suggested. The chapter
is divided into three parts. The ﬁrst part treats the ﬂuid elastic instability and the knowledge attained
throughout this project. The second part proposes a general approach to ﬂow-induced vibrations, and
suggest methods to counteract vibrations or where to ﬁnd guidance. The last part presents suggestions
on how to improve future CFD computations, in relation to predicting ﬂuid elastic instabilities.
5.1
Understanding ﬂuid elastic instability
Important information that has been discovered in this project, is the existence of an instability and
bifurcation. The problem of bifurcation analysis lies in the systems ability to qualitatively change the
equilibrium states, as system parameters are varied. By changing system parameters such as the mass
ratio, damping or ﬂuid force, it is unknown where either the critical velocity and amplitude may drastically
alter, or disappear completely. As example, the change of going from a rigid deﬂection, as conducted
in this project, to elastic deﬂection, as occurring in the ﬁlter, cannot be known without experimental
investigations. The eﬀect of introducing a higher Reynolds number, which may be the case in the ﬁlter,
can equally not be known. Fully investigating instabilities will require either an experimental model
with exact same conditions as the physical problem, or a full sensitivity study of the system parameters,
aﬀecting the bifurcation. It is important to note, that while this may sounds impossible, the experimental
theory and methods utilized in this project to investigate ﬂow-induced vibrations are general. With the
acquisition of ﬂow or vibrational data, it will likely prove unnecessary to perform full scale testing of
either the ﬂuid elastic instability, or perform bifurcation analysis.
It was found that a reduction of the shear parameter may completely remove the onset of the ﬂuid
elastic instability, or drastically reduce the amplitudes. By reducing the ﬂuid velocity unto the structures,
the reduced velocity can be made lower than the critical velocity, and the induced vibrations will disappear
completely. In short, making sure that the ﬂow does not cross the structures critical velocity counteracts
this ﬂuid elastic instability. Introducing a much higher turbulence intensity may also disrupt the high
amplitude vibrations, however the turbulence have been observed to only stop the motion eﬃciently
within certain transitional regions, as observed in this project.

The suggestion of using CFD to simulate the ﬂow ﬁeld ’without’ the structure, are to be under-

geometrically subtracted. As recalled from the non-dimensional quantities section, this is one method of

only be suggested as guide-lines, as there are almost an inﬁnite amount of structural parameters aﬀecting
the qualitative response of ﬂow-induced vibrations. It is therefore advices to exercise caution and reﬂect
over the result while investigating. Brief description and methods to counteract the primary ﬂow-induced
vibrations are explained below. Motion induced by ﬂuid elastic instabilities of cylinders in shear ﬂow will
not be discussed, since it has been explained.
Counteracting broad- and narrow-band turbulence
The counteraction of turbulence induced vibrations are suggested to follow same methodology, regardless
of the turbulence being broad- or narrow-banded. Although the visual eﬀect and amplitudes may be
diﬀerent between, the method used to decrease excitation are general. Turbulence induced vibrations are
caused by a product of ﬂuid velocity magnitude (addressing the momentum) and the turbulent intensity.
Reducing either will result in lower amplitudes. If the turbulence is narrow-banded, changing structural
geometries may also reduce the amplitudes, since coherent turbulent structures are dependent on the
interaction with structural geometries and boundary/mixing layers.
Counteracting ﬂuid elastic instability of cylinders in arrays
The general approach of reducing vibrations caused by ﬂuid elastic instability in cylinder and/or tube
arrays, are by making sure the ﬂow velocity is below the critical velocity of the instability. This may
be done by etc. increasing the natural frequency of the structures, thus decreasing the reduced velocity.
However, if the reduced velocity becomes low (≈2-8) this implies the structure may be susceptible
to vortex-induced vibrations instead. Therefore this approach has to be treated carefully. Generally,
the design phase of constructing structures becomes important, where physical dimensions and non-
dimensional ﬂow quantities may be changed directly. The design state are generally used to ensure the
structures avoids ﬂuid elastic instabilities, once the ﬂow is aﬀecting the cylinder array. Standards of
constructions and methods are given explicitly by the Tubular Exchanger Manufactureres Association
(TEMA) [26]. A few suggestions are also to be found in Flow-induced Vibration by Robert D. Blevins [1].
Counteracting wake-galloping and vortex-induced vibrations
Wake-galloping and vortex-induced vibrations both requires lock-in of a structures natural frequency, to
either a wake or a vortex shedding mechanism. They are therefore discussed under the same topic. The
lock-in mechanism may be counteracted by either reducing the velocity, or increasing it, until the vortex
shedding is no longer locked on to the natural frequency of the motion. This may eﬀectively also be done
by changing the natural frequency of the structure, thereby changing the reduced velocity. However, it
should be noted that by increasing or decreasing the ﬂuid velocity in a large array of structures, wake-
galloping or vortex-induced vibrations may simply occur for diﬀerent structures in the array instead.
Another proposition would be to change the ﬂuids angle of attack toward the structure, thereby trying
to avoid uniform or oblique vortex shedding. A third, and often used mechanism, is the application of
helical strakes or the modiﬁcation of the structures surfaces. By doing so, the vortex shedding cannot
happen simultaneously along the structural length, by breaking the vortex formation apart. This method
is applied in many mechanical problems, and it is explained in etc. Flow-induced Vibration [1].

3.3 Modelling ﬂuid elastic instability using computational ﬂuid dynamics
5.3
Modelling ﬂuid elastic instability using computational ﬂuid
dynamics
If the computational ﬂuid dynamics model are to be improved, there are primarily four concerns that
are expected to alter the results and increase the amplitude response. These are forced motions, energy
transfer in the equation of motion, modelling the problem in three dimensions and investigating numerical
damping. These are brieﬂy explained, along with a discussion on why it is expected to aﬀect the results.
The cylinders motion may be forced, instead of computed. This is an approach often utilized when
dealing with simulations that would otherwise require a lot of simulated time. Once the motion is imposed,
investigations of of lift and drag coeﬃcients, along with damping in the system may be used to determine
where either the energy input and output is in equilibrium. The problem of running forced motions, are
that correct trajectories and velocities has to be imposed. In an experiment It could be observed that the
trajectory is complex and changes according to many parameters in this experiment, as the orbit motion
stretched and rotated within the ﬂow. Therefore it may be diﬃcult to asses whether a forced motion are
governing a stable equilibrium or not. It is however a possible approach, and it cannot be known where
either this method could uncover low reduced velocity equilibriums, unless it is investigated.
A diﬀerent approach would be to use an algorithm which enables transfer of energy between the
motions multiple degrees of freedom. As an example, insertion of a stiﬀness matrix containing not only
stiﬀness in the x- and y-direction, but also allowing cross-stiﬀness, could eﬀectively allow the cylinder to
be pushed in other directions than just towards the center. It was proven in the experimental approach,
that the experimental cylinder was capable of transferring energy between multiple degrees of freedom.
The trajectory analysis of the ﬂuid elastic instability was always observed to require co-motion of both
degrees of freedom, and it is therefore assumed that a better and more complex description of the cylinders
equation of motion would yield better results.
Modelling the cylinder with the actual axisymmetric inlet ﬂow, and the cylinder motion to rotate
about a pivot point in a three dimensional domain is expected to give a much better representation of
the forces exerted unto the cylinder. As it is in itself quite simple, this project does however prove how
computationally expensive this can be expected to be.
Investigations of numerical damping in rigid-body ﬂuid-structure interaction are proposed.
The
number of time steps required to simulate oscillations of high reduced velocities are high. Numerical
damping are assumed to be present. Numerical damping may present itself as a forces acting opposite
to the cylinder motion, due to the ﬂuid spatial or temporal discretization. With a high number of time
steps per cycle, any numerical damping will accumulate and cause loss of the cylinders inertia for each
oscillation. The eﬀects of numerical damping will increase with a decrease of time step, and may also
increase with the spatial resolution of the cylinder boundary layer. As such, numerical damping will
have the capability of damping and reducing amplitudes with a higher spatial or temporal resolution. As
this was observed within the mesh convergence tests, investigations of numerical damping are considered
relevant in future investigations.
