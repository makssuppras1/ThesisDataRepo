Abstract
Brain stroke is a major cause of serious physical disabilities. An important part
of the treatment is rehabilitating, which in most cases requires supervision by a
physiotherapist. This is becoming increasingly diﬃcult, as with more frequent
stroke incidents the therapists are time-constrained and can not be physically
present at all of the required rehabilitation sessions.
In this thesis a networking solution has been developed to connect two exoskele-
ton arms, enabling remote instruction of stroke patients during rehabilitation.
The system was built using two LEGO exoskeletons, each with a Raspberry Pi
attached, and a Unity application with a networking solution built into that.
Two experiments were conducted to test the eﬀectiveness of the patient-therapist
collaboration. One experiment, Mirror, (N = 40 participants) tested if the sys-
tem increased the accuracy in performing exercises, if performing the exercises
remote had any negative impact on the accuracy, and if participants could more
easily remember the exercises after sensing how another person did the exercise,
a so-called body swap. Another experiment, Border, (N = 12 participants) tested
if the exoskeleton could be used as an aid for individual exercise sessions without
real-time supervision, but based on borders for motions that the therapist had
deﬁned. Also, the Border experiment tested if the participants could remember
the borders better after using the exoskeleton to guide them, compared to only
being shown the borders by the therapist.
The Mirror experiment showed a very high signiﬁcant improvement of 73%-80%
when doing exercises with the assistance of the exoskeletons, compared to doing
it without any assistance. This was true even when the participant was not
allowed to watch the instructor of the exercise, but only to feel the instructor’s
movement guidance from the exoskeleton. The Border experiment also found a
signiﬁcant improvement of 7.5%-12.2% doing exercises when using the assistance
of the exoskeleton compared to no assistance. It was also found that there was
no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between providing visual feedback and physical feed-
back for the participants.
Doing the exercises remotely had no negative impact on the participants’ ability
to perform exercises, which supports that health professionals may consider a
remote coupling of two exoskeletons systems like the one we have demonstrated
in this thesis. In the discussion section of the thesis we point out some im-
portant limitations of the current experiment, namely that participants had no
prior experience with the exoskeleton, they only tried it for a very short time,
the conditions for the experiments were not ideal as they were mostly performed
at the exhibitions with limited space, and ﬁnally, the exoskeleton was made of

LEGO, which had severe drawbacks in motor precision as well as in the con-
struction, as the exoskeleton was not adjustable and did not perfectly ﬁt all the

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Brain stroke is a disease of frightening frequency: it is estimated, that one in six
living people will experience brain stroke at least once in their lives. Every year
approximately 12.000 cases of brain stroke are registered in Denmark, where
2/3 of the aforementioned cases are ﬁrst-time strokes. There are approximately
30.000 to 40.000 living people in Denmark, who suﬀer from the consequences of
a stroke [1]. More global data is concerning as well: there were recorded more
than 100 million cases of stroke in 2019 worldwide. Also in 2019, stroke was the
main cause of 6.6 million deaths. Stroke is a leading cause of serious long-term
disability, and with such a high number of worldwide stroke cases, the amount
of people experiencing mobility reduction is also concerning. More than half of
stroke survivors over 65 years old suﬀer from mobility reduction [2].
While the impact of stroke on mobility is massive, it can be signiﬁcantly re-
duced using therapy. The average patient receiving a program of focused stroke
rehabilitation performs better than approximately 65.5% of patients not under-
going the same treatment [3]. However, a number of studies indicate that in
30% to 66% of stroke patients, the paretic arm remains without function when
measured 6 months after stroke [4].
==PAGE:9==
1.1 Motivation
2
Recovery can continue for months or years after the stroke — well beyond the
formal rehabilitation period. Good rehabilitation outcome seems to be strongly
associated with high patient (and family) motivation and engagement. Also,
training should be given preferably in the patient’s own environment [5]. How-
ever, the exercises are currently done on very simple systems, which are not
networked. It is therefore impossible for the therapist to remotely monitor the
patient’s progress, as well as verify that all the necessary exercises were per-
formed. Some patients can also feel less motivated when performing exercise at
home without direct medical supervision. [6]
The best way to satisfy all of the aforementioned requirements, as well as com-
bat corresponding issues, is by using technology.
While research shows that
improvements in strength and range of motion are feasible when the physio-
therapy intervention is just largely delivered via videoconferencing [7], patients
undergoing long term rehabilitation will vastly beneﬁt from a technical solution,
which can provide additional features. This solution could, as an example, pro-
vide a reliable way of performing remote rehabilitation sessions with a physical
therapist; a possibility to safely and eﬃciently exercise on a daily basis without
a monitoring physician; a possibility to thoroughly monitor the overall progress
of the patient etc.
Diﬀerent robotic technologies exist intended for upper limb rehabilitation, such
as endpoint manipulators, cable suspensions and exoskeletons [8]. While the
former two are in most cases non-portable and require a complicated setup, cur-
rent technology can allow for an exoskeleton to be constructed in a portable,
lightweight and user-friendly manner [9], making it an excellent research candi-
date for this master thesis. Using the exoskeleton as the main technology also
provides an opportunity to construct a body projection a.k.a. an avatar of the
person undergoing rehabilitation. These avatars can be then viewed through
a virtual reality environment, which is a technology currently used in the ﬁeld
[10], [11].
It has been proven that a virtual reality environment can create strong reactions
in the human mind, give a sense of body ownership and help with rehabilitation
[12]. However, there is still no easy way for doctors and therapists to use this
technology to eﬀectively treat and rehabilitate patients. One of the ways body
ownership can be used for rehabilitation is through the eﬀect of body swap [13].
This project is built on the idea that an immersive virtual experience combined
with the exoskeleton as a way to control the patient’s upper limb can provide
a strong enough illusion of body swap to impact the rehabilitation of stroke
patients in a positive manner.

1.2
The project context
This thesis was carried out in close connection with the ReHyb project. It is a
European, international research project, bringing together 11 partners from 6
countries in order to ensure the multifarious cooperation needed to accomplish
the goals set by the project. The goal of the project is to develop a hybrid upper-
limb exoskeleton that uses its own sensing and novel actuation capabilities for
generating the digital twin of the user [14]. It is funded by and a part of the
Horizon 2020 Framework Programme of the European Union 1. ReHyb provided
motivation, context and valuable guidance for this project. It was also suggested
that this project concentrates on the upper-limb exoskeleton and researches the
concept of body swap between the therapist and the patient under rehabilitation.
This thesis is written at a research group at the Department of Technology, Man-
agement and Economics at the Technical University of Denmark, which is also a
part of the ReHyb consortium. The goal of this project is to use the exoskeleton
as a novel interface for the control of avatars in a body-swapping experiment.
The participants will begin with avatars that resemble themselves to establish
a baseline and familiarization of the environment but are then transferred into
the body of the other subject.
1.3
The thesis project
The upper limb exoskeleton, which is used in the project, was previously de-
signed as a part of a special course at the Technical University of Denmark.
The frame is made out of Lego and the motors are from the Lego Mindstorms
series, which are controlled by a Raspberry PI 2 via a BrickPI header 3. The
exoskeleton currently has two degrees of freedom, making it possible to track
and control the angle of the elbow as well as the rotation of the wrist. While
the currently used exoskeleton is a proof of concept and will not be used as
a medical solution, the motors are strong enough to make it a good tool for
studying the body swap eﬀect. Using Lego as the building material also allows
keeping the prototype easily customizable and low-cost.
During this project, the eﬀect of the body swap between the physiotherapist and
the patient will be explored. This is achieved by synchronising the exoskeletons
which are worn by the therapist and the patient. One of the exoskeletons will

Added value
Exploring how two synchronised exoskeletons can be used to both guide and
control the rehabilitation process will help the other researchers and rehabili-
tation specialists to gain insights into the eﬀectiveness of using the concept of
body swap for rehabilitation. This will encourage as well as direct further re-
search in this area. It can also provide valuable info for the designers of the real
exoskeleton and help improve some parts of the construction which do not seem
obvious.
Making the connection between the therapist’s and patient’s exoskeletons fully
remote can help the therapists administer their time better, and more eﬀectively
treat patients. Remote rehabilitation enables therapists and doctors as well as
patients to save time and hassle transporting to and from oﬃces or homes.
This also contributes to creating a more comfortable environment for patients,
allowing them to be rehabilitated from their homes.
This system can also give an opportunity for the therapists to have multi-
patient rehabilitation sessions, where every participant is independently per-
forming their own exercise. The therapist is then simultaneously tracking the
progress of all of the patients and has the possibility of correcting them as
needed.

3.1
Stroke Rehabilitation
The current way to rehabilitate a stroke patient includes a variety of diﬀerent
professionals to determine and provide the treatment necessary. Depending on
the problem, the team handling the rehabilitation can consist of a Speech Thera-
pist, nurse, physiotherapist, ergo therapist, doctor and sometimes a psychologist
[1].
This team decides if a patient needs rehabilitation and what kind. If possible
doctors and physiotherapists recommend that treatment begins from day one
after the incident.
Otherwise as soon as possible, and often long treatment
course.
==PAGE:16==
3.1 Stroke Rehabilitation
9
The process of a stroke patient in Denmark is divided into several phases. Firstly
is the acute phase, which entails the ﬁrst weeks of the treatment. This is where
the diagnostics happen, as well as the most acute rehabilitation and prevention
of complications. The second phase is the transitional phase, where rehabilita-
tion planning begins, and where patients, who are eligible for rehabilitation, are
being oﬀered what’s available. The rehabilitation will be from a patient home if
it’s possible but otherwise will take place in an institution. This phase is from 3
weeks until 3 months. The ﬁnal phase is the adaptation phase, where a patient
will be adapted to the new life with whatever disabilities remain for them, as
well as some rehabilitation for special needs. This ﬁnal phase happens 3 months
after the incident [15].
The aim of this project is mostly to help in the second, transitional phase where
most of the rehabilitation takes place, however, there might be a possibility for
using the ﬁndings of this thesis in the other phases as well. Hopefully, with
improved rehabilitation technology the third phase will be less relevant in the
future, as patients will have fewer lasting disabilities to live with.
3.1.1
Functional Electric Stimulation (FES)
A big research topic when talking about rehabilitation and especially stroke re-
habilitation is the use of FES. Often used in conjunction with other technologies
like Biofeedback, this technology has been proven to provide improved rehabil-
itation results, when used to enhance the training of the patients. There were
also not found any evidence suggesting a diﬀerence in eﬀectiveness depending
on the time after the stroke happened [16].
This evidence has been backed up by other papers, showing that patients can
still receive positive results using FES from 6 or more months after rehabilitation
[17].
Something related to FES called EMS has also been explored. While FES is a
treatment used by physiotherapists, EMS presents a more raw form of electrical
muscle stimulation.

normal strength levels. (...) However, studies of contraction torque have not

tion, to be signiﬁcantly greater than that of MVIC"[18]. MVIC being short for

3.2
Exoskeleton for rehabilitation
This project primarily builds on the work done by Barnabas Homola, regarding
his paper "The impact of torque and sound on a VR interface for a rehabilitation
exoskeleton" [19]. His thesis explored whether the use of added torque of an
exoskeleton in VR could achieve the eﬀect of Virtual Hand Illusion (VHI). He
concluded that it did in fact have a real impact and also resulted in better
performance in motor perception tasks.
Barnabas also experimented with sound eﬀects, but the results showed that they
did not have the same impact on immersion, and as such, they will not be used
in this project.
In his experiment, he used a virtual arm, that would be hit by a falling ball.
When the ball hit, the program would apply force to the exoskeleton, which
corresponded to how the ball hit the arm.
The Body Swap project draws inspiration from this experiment, but takes it a
step further, by involving more of the arm. Also instead of just small nudges
to the arm, this project aims to use the full range of motion of the exoskeleton.
However, the Virtual Reality part is not carried forward, as it was decided to
not be a benefactor for what this thesis tried to explore and achieve especially
because there were already results showing the impact of VR.

3.3
Body Ownership Illusions (BOIs)
When talking about BOI mostly VR comes to mind. This thesis does not explore
Body Swap with virtual reality but sees it as a natural development in future
work. However, the topic of BOI is still important for this project, as body
swapping does pose the question of "which body is mine". This project also has
a virtual arm that could impose BOI on the user.
When looking at previous work that has done research in BOI there are sev-
eral diﬀerent papers to refer to. A particularly interesting one looks into the
building blocks of what establishes BOI in the human mind and concludes that
having a virtual setting with a fake body in a ﬁrst-person perspective is vital
for eliciting BOI. With this in mind, they also discuss how a fake body that
accurately resembles the user’s actual body enhances this feeling. However, the
most interesting part in relation to this project is that they conclude that when
the before mentioned building blocks are not implemented to a satisfactory de-
gree than the contribution of congruent multisensory and/or sensorimotor cues,
can also impose BOI on a user [20].
Another concept that could be mentioned when talking about BOI is Rubber
Hand Illusion (RHI), which is a concept of a person seeing a rubber hand moving
in the same manner as their own and getting the feeling that the rubber hand
is part of their own body, thus imposing BOI.
A paper looked into the sensitivity of RHI, by performing various tests where

3.4
Body Swapping
In 2016 a group in Brazil made some experiments with the body swapping
theory to test the sense of body ownership and its realism. The experiment
used a Head-Mounted Display to give the illusion that a subject had another
person’s body. They observed that a system with body agency gave a higher
sense of realism and body ownership [13].
While the eﬀect of body swap was previously researched just as a concept [13],
[22] as well as within such areas like gender studies [23], [24] or medical appli-
cations [25], it was not used as with regards to rehabilitation. In this project,
the body swap is achieved by letting the patients upper limb be controlled by
the therapist, while the patient can also see the avatar representation of the
controlled limb in the virtual environment.
3.4.1
Neural Science
A way that body swapping has been achieved in the past is through brain science.
Neural scientists made it possible for a person to control another individuals
wrist through neural signals. The neural signals detected at one person’s brain
would be used to activate the FES on another persons wrist. The showcase also
demonstrates how this technology can not be aﬀected by outside forces, but only
functions when brain signals come through to attempt to move the arm [26].
Products are being developed that uses brain science to help with stroke re-
habilitation. NBETTER is a rehabilitation system, that uses neural feedback
to provide visual and mechanical feedback for exercising the brain and thus
improving recovery. It is a Brain Computer Interface (BCI) [27].
This project will not be using Neural Science directly to assist in rehabilitation,
but the technology seen using neural signals showcases how the movements of
the arms are linked to the brain. This enhances the idea of how the experience
of body swapping can have an eﬀect on the brain that might further accelerate
a patients rehabilitation.

4.2.1
Unity Networking APIs
Working with networking in Unity is tricky, as the platform currently does not
have an oﬃcially supported API for adding networking to a project.
Their current implementation has been deprecated for over 2 years [33], and
the oﬃcial replacement Unity MLAPI, while available to use, is still in early
development, with version 0.1.0 being out as of writing [34].
To solve this problem, a lot of third-party solutions has appeared over the years,
and research has gone into this project to determine which solution is the best
for this use case.
4.2.1.1
Mirror
Mirror is a popular Open Source High-Level networking API for Unity. It uses
the Client-Server structure, to deﬁne the communication between the diﬀerent
networking objects in a project. It has been tried and tested for MMOs (Mas-
sively Multiplayer Online), which are games with a high concurrent number of
networking objects [35].
One of Mirrors primary positives is the popularity. It has an active community
including a Discord server where the developers actively help people having
trouble with the API. This makes prototyping a lot more manageable, as one
would spend less time troubleshooting.
Mirror is Open Source, meaning it is possible to contribute to the project, in
case there are some features missing, that are essential to this project. Due to
the time frame of this project, however, this will not be relevant now but might
be for future development.
The Client-Server structure of Mirror can be both a pro and a con.
==PAGE:25==
4.2 Research
18
The structure allows for stable connections, that can handle large networks.
However this project will not require support for this, but only need to connect
a few devices at a time. The increased stability also comes at a cost, as Client-
Server solutions are less economic than others, as they require dedicated servers
to keep and maintain.
In conclusion, Mirror would be a good choice for this project primarily for its
ease of access and rapid prototyping potential, however, when the project is
no longer in prototyping, Mirror might need to be replaced with something of
another networking architecture.
4.2.1.2
Photon
Photon is another popular networking API for Unity. Same as Mirror, Pho-
ton uses the Client-Server architecture, but unlike Mirror, Photon is not Open
Source and does have paid services. [36] It does however have a free plan, which
includes all features and support for up to 20 concurrent users, which is plenty
for the scope of this project.
Photon provides several diﬀerent solutions to choose from, based on your needs.
PUN, their general-purpose solution.
Bolt, optimized for FPS and other fast-paced games.
Quantum, a deterministic networking solution.
Fusion, a new solution, currently in beta, combining the best parts of PUN and
Bolt eﬀectively putting those two in the legacy category.
It is tested on VR games, being used by VR Chat [37], a game with several
thousand players each day [38].
According to Photon, their Fusion solution performs signiﬁcantly better than
other solutions, with their Bandwidth and CPU usage being at least ﬁve times
lower than both Mirror’s and Unity’s new MLAPI. [39]
These benchmarks should be taken with a grain of salt, as they’re not made
from an impartial source, however, they are still promising.

natives. It claims to be extremely optimized, with low transaction overhead

synchronized between the connected Unity systems, whether they are hosts or
clients. To better explain how it works, an example is used:

change on all the other Unity clients. Those clients then detect the change and
send the data to the exoskeleton they are connected to.

5.3
Communication protocol
In order to establish unambiguous communication between the unity program
and the exoskeleton program, a certain set of messages was designed. All of
these messages are represented by a string, where the ﬁrst word always speciﬁes
the name of the message, and the rest of the message depends on this name.
There are two types of messages, which were already mentioned: control mes-
sages and function messages. The following sections will describe both of these
types in detail.
5.3.1
Control messages
This type of message is used in order to control the motors, thus providing the
main feature of the system, which is the body swap eﬀect. The format of the
message is:
1
control ,elbow_value ,wrist_value ,timestamp
==PAGE:40==
5.3 Communication protocol
33
An actual message can look like this:
1
control ,52.0 ,166.0 ,12:02:46.207082
These messages are sent by the host exoskeletons at most every 10 milliseconds.
The message is only sent if there was a change in motor values during the last 10
milliseconds, otherwise, no message will be sent until a change is noticed. This is
done in order to decrease the network load, since sending several messages with
the same values in a row does not bring any update and is therefore redundant.
When a control message is sent by the host exoskeleton, it is ﬁrst received by the
corresponding unity program. It uses the values to update the hand model and
forwards the message to the other unity program. The remote unity program
also updates the hand model and forwards the message to the client exoskeleton.
When the control message is received by the client exoskeleton’s program, a new
thread is being started, which is responsible for adjusting the motor values to
the received data. The thread is then immediately terminated. This is done
to be able to constantly update the motor values while not missing any of the
other incoming messages.

6.1.2
Mirror Experiment
The idea of this experiment was twofold. The ﬁrst goal was to test how well
people responded to the movements the exoskeleton told them to do, this was
tested by comparing how they move their arm without help from the exoskele-
ton, and when removing the help of their eyes when being instructed by the
exoskeleton. The second goal was to test the ability of this technology to work
over the network and examine if the delay and data packet loss would be too
much of a distraction.
For this experiment, two participants were needed to resemble a typical rehabil-
itation session: one to play the part of the therapist and one to play the patient.
The therapist would be instructed by the instructor, which would be one of the
authors of the thesis, to do a small series of movements with the exoskeleton
arm.
The patient would then be instructed to follow the movements of the
therapist, and also repeat them again afterwards, to test their memory. This
experiment was also in four parts, each part changing the environment to test
the impact of the exoskeleton in diﬀerent scenarios. The parts were deﬁned as
follows:
• Baseline 1: Arm engines oﬀ. The patient would have full control of their
arm and would have to follow the therapist’s movements based on visuals
only. Must be run ﬁrst.
• Mirror: Arm engines on.
The therapist would have control over the
patient’s arm, during the creation of the series. Then the engines turn oﬀ
when the patient tries to replicate the series from memory afterwards.
• Remote: Same as Mirror, but here the patient closes their eyes when
the therapist creates the series. This forces the patient to rely only on
what the exoskeleton arm physically instructs them to do. This simulates
a remote environment where a patient would be sitting at home with their
==PAGE:47==

exoskeleton, and thus would not be able to see the therapist while they
were instructing them.
• Baseline 2: Same as Baseline 1, but must be run last.
As stated above, Baseline 1 must be run ﬁrst, and Baseline 2 must be run last.
This is to test, whether the patients’ performance has improved, once they had
been through the other parts experiments. The Mirror and Remote could and
should be run in a random order each time the experiment was run.
6.1.3
Border Experiment
The idea of the Border experiment was to test out the eﬀectiveness of the ex-
oskeleton as a tool for unsupervised exercising. This was done by setting an
allowed range of movement, and testing how close the participants can be to
the borders without crossing them. Only the elbow joint was used in this ex-
periment.
For this experiment, a monitor was also used.
This monitor would show a
virtual representation of the patient’s arm. Only one participant was needed as
the therapist would be played by one of the project members. The therapist,
while in control of the patient’s arm, would move their arms in a concentric
movement, until they found a place to set the upper border. When stated to
the participant that this is the upper border and set by the system, they would
move their arms in an eccentric movement, until they found a place to set the
lower border. When done, the control would be switched over to the patient.
They then had to do four small exercises, in order to determine how well they
could respect the set borders.
On each of the exercises, they were asked to move their arm up and down three
times, trying to get as close to the borders as possible without crossing them.
The exercises were deﬁned as follows:
• Baseline 1: No feedback at all. The patient has to respect the borders
from memory alone. Must be the ﬁrst exercise.
• Visual: Visual feedback. The screen in front of the patient would turn
red when they crossed the borders.
• Physical: Physical feedback. The arm would lock the motors when they
reached the borders.

Recommendations on system usage for fur-

Here we list a couple of recommendations we have for using the system developed
during this project for conducting more experiments. These recommendations
are based on the observations made during conducting our own experiments.
==PAGE:54==
6.5 Recommendations on system usage for further experiments
47
• Give them time
For most people, this will be the ﬁrst experience with both the exoskeletons
and the body swap eﬀect. It is therefore essential to let the participants
familiarize themselves with the feeling. Give them a minute or two of just
playing around with controlling each other.
• Go into the detail
The more motivated and explained the parts of the experiment are, the
easier it is for the participants to follow them correctly.
For example,
instead of saying "do this" you could say "here is how it is currently done
in the rehabilitation sessions, and we will try to simulate it like this". Also
explain how many exercises you want them to do and how long it will take,
before starting the experiment.
• Keep it simple
The participants will still be concentrated on the technology and their
feeling of it, to it will be unlikely for them to perform a complex exercise.
Try to therefore keep the instructions simple and divide them into several
parts if necessary.

7.5
Counterbalancing
As mentioned earlier in the report, the order of some parts of the experiments
was randomized in an eﬀort to counterbalance the tests and ensure the data was
not aﬀected by the learning or tiredness of participants.
In order to determine whether the counterbalancing eﬀorts worked or not, a few
tests were made on the data, both on the Border experiment and the Mirror
experiment.
The method used was a one-way ANOVA test, that ﬁrst required the data to
be normally distributed. To check that the data was put into a Shapiro Wilks
test, found in the python library scipy.stats. For the Border experiment, a set of
percentage data were generated based on the improvement of Visual, Physical
and Baseline 2 compared to Baseline 1. There were a set for each of the two
groups, the one that tried Visual ﬁrst, and the one that tried Physical ﬁrst.
For Mirror two sets of data were also created, however they just included the
average correlation score for each group, ones that tried the Mirror part ﬁrst,
and ones that tried Remote ﬁrst.
This data was put into the Shapiro test to check for normal distribution with
the following result:
Mirror: Statistic 0.8974885940551758, PValue 0.07327007502317429.
Border: Statistic 0.9177548885345459, PValue 0.4893399775028229.
With a PValue of more than 0.05 for both tests, it can be concluded that the
data is normally distributed.
Then for the ANOVA test. For the Border experiment, two groups were created,
one which tried the Visual part ﬁrst (group A), and one which tried Physical

8.1

The point of the Mirror experiment was to test, whether the exoskeleton can be
used as a tool for conducting remote rehabilitation sessions between the patient
and the physiotherapist. This involved testing how accurately people respond to
the exoskeleton and the instructions given by a remote therapist (Repeat part)
as well as testing if using the exoskeleton helps to remember the exercise better
(Remember part).

training sessions spend less brainpower in the wonder of the equipment, and

later. Therefore, it is considered to be a signiﬁcant improvement.
Again ANOVA tests were performed in order to determine whether the diﬀer-
ences between the experiment parts were signiﬁcant. Again a signiﬁcance level
of 5% is used. It was stated in the Results section, that the diﬀerence in the
parts of the experiment was insigniﬁcant when looking at the upper borders
only, which was using the data that was plotted to ﬁgure 7.11. There could be
diﬀerent reasons why but the most probable is the physical properties or rather
limitations of the exoskeleton. The reason could be because of the exoskeletons
inability to change size depending on the wearer. People have very diﬀerent
sizes of arms. Some are thin, some are thick, some are long and some are short.
During experimentation, it was found that people with longer arms, generally
expressed more problems with bending their arms. This was because the elbow
joint of the exoskeleton did not match completely with the joint of the par-
ticipant’s arm. This could explain why there was no signiﬁcant improvement
when looking at upper borders. However, when looking at lower borders the
ANOVA test did determine a signiﬁcant diﬀerence. When thinking about the
argument, of the limited adaptability of the exoskeleton, and why it doesn’t
apply to the lower borders, the reason could be that when a participant equips
the exoskeleton they do so with a stretched arm, which is the position they will
most likely encounter the lower border. Therefore the exoskeleton has been at-
tached in an advantageous manner compared to when testing for upper borders.
Another reason could simply be that participants had an easier time controlling
the extension of their arms compared to the contraction.
Now since the ANOVA test determined that there was a signiﬁcant improvement
in the lower borders, it had to be discovered where that improvement was. A

Mirror Questionniare In chapter 3 the concept of BOI is mentioned. The

strong BOI eﬀect can trick the patient into thinking they are doing the move-

do help quite a lot even though they are not actually powerful enough to move

8.5
Technical discussion
As it is mentioned in section 8.3, the delay in communication turned out not
to be a problem. However, the issue encountered while conducting experiments
was packet loss.
Sometimes during the experiments, the exoskeletons would
not react to the sent command, as if the message was lost. This message was
also not found in the log ﬁles later when trying to analyze this behaviour. As
UDP was used for message exchange, the assumption that the message is lost
seems very reasonable. This is not a big problem for control messages, as the
message ﬂow is constant and the next received message will compensate for
a lost one due to the way the data is transmitted. However, message loss is
critical for function messages, as they are only sent once and the experience
of using the exoskeleton strongly depends on those messages. The suggestion
would therefore be to transfer the function messages to Unity program using
TCP instead of UDP, while still using UDP for the control messages.
Another slight issue noticed is the message format inconsistency. As it is now,
the control messages are represented by comma-separated strings, while the
function messages are represented by space-separated strings. There is no actual
reason why this happened. During the design of the communication protocol
comma felt like a good separator for the control messages as they contain a lot
of numbers, while space felt like a good separator for the function messages, as
master true is more intuitive than master,true. However, it led to the protocol
being inconsistent. This is a minor thing but might still need to be ﬁxed in the
later versions.

The scope of the project has changed signiﬁcantly in the ﬁrst months, mainly
due to a slight misunderstanding about the previous work done about the body

initial thought was then, how to improve it for rehabilitation usage. The ideas
were to make the system work in a VR environment as well as add spatial sensors
to the exoskeleton, which would help accurately represent the limb in the VR.
However, there was no existing body swap solution, so it had to be developed
from scratch. This was the main reason for the scope change, as developing the
base system turned out to be a big enough technical challenge. The VR part of
the project as well as the spatial tracking had to therefore be dropped in favor

Chapter 9
Future Work
During the experiments and conversations with professionals of the health and
rehabilitation ﬁeld, some of the additional features were found, that the profes-
sionals who conduct the rehabilitation could ﬁnd useful if implemented correctly.
The main one, brieﬂy mentioned in section 8.4 was a possibility of recording a
sequence of movements with the exoskeleton and saving the sequence, for later
repetition. This was not implemented in this project due to a lack of time and
the fact that the feature was outside of the scope.
Another feature, that was thought to be useful was the implementation of two-
way control of the exoskeletons. This means the exoskeletons are not assigned
master and follower roles, but instead the movements of both of them are de-
tected and transferred to the other exoskeleton, which adjusts accordingly. This
would allow the physiotherapist to help the patient apply some extra force dur-
ing the exercise if needed. This complex task was deemed due to being time-
consuming and also not required in order to get the data desired for writing the
thesis.
The user interface provided to control the exoskeletons and the experiments
are simple and only constructed to do the experiments designed in this thesis.
Further work could improve and simplify the interface to enable a therapist or
doctor to easily utilize the features of the implementation.

79
The original plan was to integrate body swap with VR to immerse participants
in the feeling of having their body under the control of someone else. However,
it was deemed outside of the scope of the project, as it was not needed for
completing the experiments designed.
In the future, the body swap project
should be extended to work with VR technology. This will not be a big challenge,
as the implementation is done through Unity which has great VR support.
Another feature that could improve the experience of telerehabilitation is spatial
tracking.
Adding some spatial sensors to the exoskeletons can improve the
representation of the VR model of the hand, thus giving a more immersive
experience for the patient. It will also help to ensure that the physiotherapists’
and the patients’ arms are lined up completely before performing the remote
exercise session.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
This paper explored whether or not the exoskeletons can be used in a system for
stroke rehabilitation using the eﬀect of body swapping. The system has been
developed using Unity and Raspberry Pis for handling the diﬀerent parts needed.
There were conducted several experiments on several participants, the data of
which has been analysed in order to determine the eﬀectiveness of the system.
The data showed that the system does help people do exercises set by a doctor
or physiotherapist, granting improved accuracy of up to 80% improvement for
patients trying to repeat instructions for motion sequences and up to 12% for
keeping motions within borders. This along with the fact that the system can
provide functionality that can be used by professionals working with stroke
rehabilitation to conduct remote rehabilitation sessions makes for the conclusion
that this Body Swap system will be useful for rehabilitation exercises. It was
also found that using the system in a remote environment did not reduce the
eﬀectiveness of the system but might even increase it.
The paper also explored how to design a telecommunication system for body
swapping, and found a robust solution using frameworks for Unity. Even mobile
networks’ relatively big delay turned out to be small enough to not render the
system unusable.
During research and collection of feedback from participants of the experiment,
diﬀerent technologies were discovered that might enhance the experience of the
==PAGE:88==

system and provide a discussion of how these technologies could be used in the
future along with body swap.
10.1
Recommendations on system usage
In order to successfully use this system for rehabilitation, two use cases are
recommended. The system can be used to conduct remote rehabilitation sessions
between a physician and the patient, who are also on the call together. The
experiments show that a video call is optional, as just being able to hear each
other when using the exoskeleton suﬃces.
The system can also be used as
incorporation to the daily exercises that patients do alone in order to increase
their accuracy and performance.
It is important that the exoskeleton should not be used as an assisting system
to help patients remember their exercises, as there was no improvement found
for this use case. At least not until further experimentation on the subject has
taken place.
