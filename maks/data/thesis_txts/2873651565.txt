Abstract
Cyber ranges are widely used for cybersecurity training, testing, and research. However,
the security of the cyber range platforms themselves has received little attention. This
thesis focuses on open-source cyber ranges and proposes a new framework for evaluating
their cybersecurity. The framework is based on findings from a systematic literature review
(SLR) that identifies specific threats, weaknesses, and possible countermeasures in cyber
range environments.
To understand how well current frameworks cover these threats, seven well-known cyber-
security standards were analysed, including NIST CSF, ISO/IEC 27002, and CSA CCM.
The analysis reveals that while these frameworks provide valuable general guidance, they
do not adequately address distinct risks associated with open-source cyber ranges. Issues
such as a lack of automated resets, insufficient security transparency, and weak protection
of scenario data are often not covered.
Based on these gaps, the Open-Source Cyber Range Security Framework (OSCR SF)
is introduced. It defines ten clear and practical security requirements that reflect the
specific risks of open-source platforms. The framework is applied to two real cyber ranges,
CyberRangeCZ and Docker Security Playground (DSP), revealing concrete strengths and
weaknesses in each and demonstrating the framework’s ability to differentiate security
postures.
The results show that OSCR SF provides a structured, repeatable, and transparent way to
evaluate the security of open-source cyber ranges, complementing existing standards and
supporting the development of safer platforms for education, testing, and research.
Keywords: Cyber ranges, cybersecurity, open-source software, security framework, risk
assessment, systematic literature review

Introduction
Cyber ranges are controlled environments used to simulate real-world networks, systems,
and cyber threats. They play an important role in cybersecurity education, training, and
system testing by allowing users to practice detecting, responding to, and recovering from
cyber attacks. These environments are now widely used in government, industry, and
academia to improve security skills and prepare for real incidents.
While many studies focus on how effective cyber ranges are for training, much less attention
has been given to the security of the platforms themselves. This is a concern, especially
because cyber ranges often manage sensitive data, simulate critical systems, and run
custom infrastructure that could be a target for attackers. If a cyber range platform is poorly
secured, it may expose vulnerabilities or become a risk to the organisations using it.
Open-source cyber ranges are particularly attractive because they are low-cost, flexible,
and often easy to modify. However, they also introduce unique challenges. Their source
code is public, they may rely on volunteer maintenance, and they often include many
third-party tools. Despite these risks, there is currently no standard way to evaluate the
security of open-source cyber ranges.
This thesis proposes a dedicated evaluation framework, which defines ten clear security
requirements based on a systematic literature review of known threats. The goal is to give
developers, users, and researchers a way to assess whether a platform has the necessary
safeguards in place. The framework is then applied to two open-source platforms to test
its practicality and highlight common strengths and weaknesses.
A systematic literature review (SLR) using the PRISMA methodology [1] identifies current
threats, vulnerabilities, and mitigation strategies specific to cyber ranges. The thesis
objectives are:
• Identify cybersecurity threats specific to cyber ranges.
• Evaluate existing security frameworks for their suitability to cyber range threats.
• Develop a structured security evaluation framework tailored to open-source cyber
ranges.
• Apply and refine the framework through practical assessments of real open-source
cyber ranges.
The resulting framework provides structured guidance, helping users determine if a cyber
range adequately meets their security needs.

1.1
Motivation and problem statement
Cyber ranges enable organisations, governments, and researchers to simulate attacks and
train cybersecurity professionals realistically. Yet, despite their crucial role, the security of
cyber ranges has received limited attention. A compromised cyber range poses a severe
risk of sensitive information leaks, operational disruption, or use in attacks on actual targets.
Open-source cyber ranges amplify these risks due to publicly accessible code, varied
security practices, and reliance on volunteer maintenance.
Current security standards like ISO/IEC 27002 [2], NIST SP 800-53 [3], and CSA CCM [4]
offer broad guidelines, but often lack actionable specifics relevant to cyber ranges. They
typically target general IT environments or specific domains like cloud and IoT, leaving
cyber range-specific risks inadequately addressed.
This thesis fills this gap by developing a practical framework explicitly for assessing open-
source cyber range security. It builds upon identified threats from the literature and
translates them into concrete, operational criteria.
1.2
Research questions and objectives
This thesis aims to develop and evaluate a security assessment framework for open-source
cyber ranges through the following research questions:
1. What threats do cyber ranges face?
2. How effectively do existing frameworks address cyber range-specific threats?
3. What security requirements are essential for evaluating open-source cyber
ranges?
4. How can these requirements form a practical evaluation framework?
5. How effective is the proposed framework when applied to open-source cyber
ranges?
The objectives are to:
• Review literature on cyber range security.
• Identify critical security requirements for open-source cyber ranges.
• Analyse existing security frameworks for suitability.
• Develop and present a tailored assessment framework.
• Apply and validate the framework on selected open-source cyber ranges.
• Provide practical guidance and recommendations for future research and practice.

2.1
Introduction
A cyber range is an interactive place for training, testing, and cybersecurity research.
Organisations use cyber ranges to practice how to respond to cyber threats and to check
their security measures without risking their production systems [5].
In the past ten years, cyber ranges have grown a lot. They now range from small private
labs to large cloud services and networks that connect different cyber ranges. Since cyber
ranges often replicate real systems, it’s crucial to keep the range itself secure. If a breach
occurs, it could lead to sensitive data being leaked or provide attackers with a plan to
target the actual systems they imitate [6].
A recent review showed that there is a significant lack of research on security threats to
cyber range environments, even though this issue is very important [7].
This systematic literature review (SLR) looks at the threats and vulnerabilities affecting
different types of cyber ranges, including open-source platforms, cloud-based ranges,
private or on-premises ranges, and federated ranges. It also surveys strategies to address
these security risks. The goal is to summarise findings from the past 15 years (2010–2025)
to create a framework for analysing the cybersecurity of open-source cyber ranges.
2.2
Methodology
This section outlines the systematic process followed in formulating and answering the
research question:
• How secure are cyber ranges, and what threats and vulnerabilities should be consid-
ered when assessing their cybersecurity?
The question was created based on an observed gap in the existing literature, which
predominantly emphasises the use of cyber ranges as educational or training tools, rather
than evaluating their security posture.
Google Scholar and DTU Findit were selected as the primary search platforms. The aim
was to identify academic and technical literature that specifically addresses vulnerabilities,
risks, threat models, and mitigations relevant to cyber range environments. An iterative
search strategy was employed, refining Boolean queries to locate sources with a substantial
focus on the cybersecurity of the range infrastructure itself. Table 2.1 summarises the
search and selection process. And figure 2.1 shows the flow diagram of the literature
search.

for digital twins or testbed design) that offered relevant insights were also included.

2.3
Cyber range types and security context
Cyber ranges can be classified by their ownership, deployment, and integration model.
Understanding these types is important because the threat landscape can differ across
them. Four common categories are highlighted here: open-source, cloud-based, private
(on-premises), and federated cyber ranges. These four categories were selected because
they represent the most commonly discussed deployment and integration models in the
literature. A review by Ukwandu et al. (2020) shows that open/public, private, and federated
ranges are comparably widespread, supporting the relevance of this categorisation for
cybersecurity analysis [8]. Each type introduces distinct security challenges based on
its architecture, level of exposure, and control model, making them a useful basis for
structuring the analysis of threats and mitigations. While other range classifications exist
(e.g., by purpose or user role), deployment-based categorisation allows for a clearer
assessment of external exposure, trust boundaries, and operational risk, which are central
to this thesis’s focus on cybersecurity of the cyber range itself. Below, each type and its
typical security context are outlined:
• Open-source cyber ranges: These are cyber range platforms whose software is
openly available for use and modification by the cybersecurity community (often
developed by academia or open consortia). For example, the KYPO Cyber Range
Platform (KYPO CRP) was released as an open-source range for hands-on edu-
cation [9]. It has, however, been discontinued, and a new and still active project,
CyberRangeCZ [10], a continuation of KYPO CRP.
Open-source ranges enable organisations to deploy their range environments without
licensing costs and benefit from community contributions. However, from a security
standpoint, using open-source range software means that the source code (and
any vulnerabilities within it) is publicly known. This can potentially make it easier
for attackers to discover and exploit flaws if the range is deployed with default
configurations or outdated versions. On the other hand, the transparency of open-
source code can lead to faster identification and patching of security issues by the
community.
Organisations adopting open-source ranges must pay close attention to applying
updates and security patches, and hardening the default setups (e.g., changing
default passwords, disabling unnecessary services). Supply-chain risks should also
be considered, for instance, by verifying the integrity of open-source components
and monitoring for any malicious code contributions. Open-source cyber ranges
offer flexibility and transparency, but require diligent maintenance and security best
practices by their operators, similar to any open-source software deployment.
• Cloud-based cyber ranges: Cloud-based cyber ranges are provided as services
(Cyber Range as a Service) or deployed on cloud infrastructure. These ranges
offer on-demand scalability and remote accessibility, often supporting multiple si-
multaneous user groups. Threat exposure is high in cloud environments because
they are accessible over the Internet by design. Attackers may attempt to breach a
cloud-hosted range just as they would target any cloud application [7].
Common cloud-specific threats include exploitation of misconfigured or default cloud
accounts, as well as web vulnerabilities in range portals. Indeed, adversaries might
seek initial access by abusing default credentials or unpatched software in the range
environment [7]. Once inside, they could attempt to move laterally or escalate
privileges in the cloud infrastructure.

Multi-tenancy adds another layer of risk: if the range service isolates different cus-
tomers or exercise teams imperfectly, one tenant’s environment might be accessible
by another, or an attacker could exploit a vulnerability to escape their isolated
segment. Cloud ranges thus inherit the full spectrum of cloud security concerns,
from network eavesdropping to cloud orchestration exploits. As one study notes,
cloud-based training environments face similar risks as other cloud applications, and
known lists of software flaws like the OWASP Top 10 and CWE Top 25 are directly
applicable [7].
• Private (on-premises) cyber ranges: A private cyber range is deployed within an
organisation’s facilities (on-premises data centres or closed networks) and typically
used only by that organisation. Because they often mirror internal systems and may
incorporate sensitive data for realism, private ranges carry significant insider risk
and data confidentiality concerns.
An internal or contractor attacker could physically or logically access the range and
extract valuable information. Physical security is a notable aspect: servers and
networking gear hosting the range must be protected against tampering or theft [7].
Unlike cloud services, a private range might not be accessible to external users,
which reduces external threat exposure; however, it also means the organisation
itself is fully responsible for security.
Human factors can pose threats in this environment: for instance, exercise par-
ticipants might inadvertently connect unauthorised personal devices to the range
network or introduce malware via USB, as cautioned by Noponen et al (2022) [7].
Moreover, if the range network is not properly isolated from the production network,
there is a risk that attacks simulated in the range (or malware used in exercises)
could spill over to operational systems.
Private ranges thus demand strict network segmentation (e.g., completely air-gapping
the range or using firewalled VLANs), access control, and monitoring to prevent
any crossover or leakage. Another challenge is content sensitivity: when company-
specific configurations or even real credentials are used to populate scenarios, the
range effectively contains the crown jewels of the enterprise. There have been
instances where real system credentials were reused in a range environment, a
dangerous practice since anyone breaching the range would obtain valid keys to the
actual system [7]. Therefore, organisations are advised never to use real passwords
or secrets in training environments, and to sanitise any production data if it must be
used.
• Federated cyber ranges: Federated cyber ranges involve connecting multiple
cyber range platforms or sites to operate jointly (for example, linking ranges from
different institutions for a large-scale exercise). In a federation, each range is typically
managed independently, but they agree on protocols to interconnect, often via VPN
tunnels or dedicated networks [11].
The federation allows resource sharing and broader scenarios (participants in dif-
ferent geographic locations can be in the same exercise, or one range can inject
traffic into another’s scenario, etc.). Security-wise, federation introduces trust and
interconnection risks. Each member of the federation must trust that the other con-
nected ranges maintain a certain security baseline; if one node in the federation is
compromised, an attacker could potentially pivot into other connected ranges.
A paper on federated range interconnection highlights the importance of using strong

VPN protocols (IPsec or SSL/TLS) rather than weaker ones like PPTP, which has
known security issues [11]. Thus, encrypted and authenticated communication
channels are a must to prevent eavesdropping or man-in-the-middle attacks between
ranges.
Additionally, clear federation policies are needed: e.g., agreements on how to handle
incident response if a breach occurs in the shared environment, and ensuring that
only necessary data is shared across ranges. Another aspect is the coordination of
user access; federated ranges might implement a unified identity management for
participants across sites, which itself must be secured to avoid unauthorised access.
Overall, while federation expands training capabilities, it also expands the attack
surface beyond a single administrative domain, requiring collaborative security man-
agement among all participants [12].
Cyber ranges can also be categorised by their sector or purpose (military, commercial,
academic, etc.) [8], but the above types (open-source, cloud, private, federated) cut
across those lines and capture the deployment differences that impact security. Table 1
in Ukwandu et al.’s review [8] indicates that public (shared) and federated ranges were
about as common as private ones in documented cases, highlighting that all these models
are prevalent. Each type comes with unique security challenges as described. In the next
section, we analyse the specific threats and vulnerabilities that can affect cyber ranges,
regarding these categories where applicable.
Katsantonis et al. propose a structured Cyber Range Design Framework (CRDF), which
identifies core architectural elements, operational phases, and layered learning strategies
tailored to various range types, including public, private, and federated deployments. Their
work highlights how the absence of design standards can lead to security inconsistencies
and inefficiencies across implementations [13].
2.4
Threats and vulnerabilities in cyber ranges
Cyber ranges face a variety of threats throughout their lifecycle. We organise the discussion
of threats into several categories: (1) design and deployment-phase issues, (2) threats
related to virtualisation infrastructure, (3) data and confidentiality risks, (4) network and
communication threats (including remote access and federation links), (5) human/insider
threats during exercises, and (6) cloud-specific attack vectors. Many of these threats are
interrelated and may compound each other. Below, we detail each category, citing findings
from the literature:
2.4.1
Design and deployment phase vulnerabilities:
Building a realistic cyber range is a complex task, often requiring reproducing elements of
actual systems. Mistakes made at this stage can introduce vulnerabilities that carry into
the operational phase [7]. One common pitfall is the reuse of real system configurations or
credentials in the range. Winter (2012) warns that when using copies of real system VMs
in cyber ranges, care must be taken to remove confidential or personal data before use,
to avoid exposing sensitive information [14]. Noponen et al. emphasise that using actual
usernames/passwords from a production network in a cyber range poses a major threat;
if outsiders gain access to the range, they would also have the keys to the real network.
This risk is easily overlooked when converting a real system into a virtual environment
under time pressure [7].
Another challenge in design is incomplete or inaccurate system replication. Suppose
certain components of the target system cannot be virtualised and are omitted or simplified.

In that case, it may not directly introduce a ”vulnerability” in the traditional sense. Still, it
can create a false sense of security or gaps that attackers might exploit if the range is
later extended or connected improperly. Frank et al. (2017) discuss a design life cycle for
cybersecurity testbeds and note that maintaining the platform over time (updating software
and scenarios) becomes difficult as complexity grows [15]. Unmaintained services in
the range can accumulate unpatched vulnerabilities. Similarly, Noponen et al. (2022)
identify interoperability issues during range creation as a risk factor; mismatches between
components could lead to insecure configurations or components left in default insecure
states [7].
To summarise, during deployment, threats include the unintentional inclusion of sensitive
information, the use of default or weak configurations, and the challenge of keeping the
range’s software components updated (since a range might use many VMs, custom scripts,
and networking rules that need regular patching). If these are not managed, the range can
harbour numerous known vulnerabilities (e.g., outdated operating systems in VMs) that
attackers could exploit.
2.4.2
Virtualisation and container threats:
Modern cyber ranges almost always rely on virtualisation (virtual machines, containers, or
both) to simulate networks and systems [7]. This reliance introduces a set of well-known
infrastructure threats.
Chief among them is virtual machine escape, an attack whereby malicious code running
inside a VM breaks out to the host or hypervisor layer [7]. If an attacker (or even a trainee
playing the role of ”red team” in an exercise) can trigger a hypervisor vulnerability, they
could gain control over the host server and thereby over all other VMs in the range [7].
For instance, vulnerabilities in popular hypervisors (VMware, Xen, etc.) have allowed
guest-to-host escapes in the past, emphasising that this is not just theoretical, as explored
in Zhao et al. [16]. In a cyber range context, a VM escape would be catastrophic: the
attacker could potentially observe or interfere with all simulated systems and even pivot
into the enterprise network if the range is connected.
Similarly, if containers are used (e.g., Docker containers to quickly spawn instances for
exercises), container breakout attacks are a concern. Noponen et al. (2022) pointed out
that container orchestration adds complexity and new attack surfaces [7]. Misconfigured
container privileges or vulnerabilities in container engines can allow an attacker to escalate
from one container to the host. MITRE’s ATT&CK framework now includes an ATT&CK
Matrix for containers, cataloguing techniques like ”Escape to Host” [7]. Within ranges,
containers might be used for specific services (like simulating IoT devices or hosting
vulnerable web apps for training); if those are compromised, an adversary could leap into
the underlying system, similar to a VM escape.
Another issue is the added complexity of virtualisation management: large ranges often
use scripts or automation to create and snapshot VMs. If those automation tools have
vulnerabilities (for example, an open-source range instantiation tool running with high
privileges [7]), an attacker could exploit them to gain broad access.
The isolation mechanisms that cyber ranges depend on are not foolproof. The risk of
hypervisor or container compromise is particularly relevant for cloud-based and multi-tenant
ranges, but also for any range where untrusted code might be executed (e.g., when trainees
have full OS access and might inadvertently run malware that tries to propagate). Keeping
hypervisors and container platforms patched is critical, as is using security features like
VM sandboxing, restrictive container profiles, and monitoring for escape attempts.

2.4.3
Data confidentiality and "blueprint" leakage:
Because cyber ranges can closely mimic real systems (sometimes described as digital
twins of operational technology), they carry the risk of revealing the inner workings of
those systems if compromised [7]. Hearn and Rix (2019) coined this the ”digital twin
blueprint” problem: if a hacker obtains the range environment, they essentially gain an
up-to-date blueprint of the real network, including layouts, software versions, and possibly
vulnerabilities [6]. This intelligence dramatically lowers the effort to attack the actual
systems, as the adversary can practice on the twin (the range) and identify weak points [6].
In critical infrastructure scenarios, this threat is acute: a range built to simulate a power
grid control network, if leaked, could guide attackers on how to disrupt the real grid.
Additionally, ranges often log participant actions and may collect credentials or config files
during exercises. If these logs are not protected or wiped, an attacker who later breaches
the range could glean sensitive information that participants entered (for example, VPN
passwords used by defenders during the drill).
Another aspect of confidentiality is that during exercises, sensitive communications occur
among participants (discussing incident response plans, etc.). If the range is not isolated
or if outsiders can eavesdrop on range traffic, these discussions could be intercepted [7].
Even observers in the same physical room (if the range exercise is conducted in a lab)
could overhear or see information if precautions aren’t taken [7]. Noponen et al. mention
that outsiders might misinterpret exercise content as a real attack if it leaked, causing
confusion [7].
If content escapes, it can damage both reputation and operations. In the case of open-
source ranges or those with public elements, we must assume that adversaries can
examine the cyber range setup. This points to the importance of not using any real
confidential data in the cyber range.
Any data a cyber range holds or produces, such as network diagrams, configurations,
exercise actions, and user credentials, can be targeted. The risk to confidentiality is highest
for private ranges that use company data and for federated ranges where multiple parties’
information may be accessible if security is weak.
2.4.4
Network and communication threats:
Cyber ranges often support remote participants (especially cloud-based and federated
ranges), meaning that users connect over networks to access the range. This opens
up communication-based threats such as man-in-the-middle attacks, packet sniffing, or
session hijacking.
If an attacker can intercept the traffic between a remote user and the range, they might
steal credentials or inject malicious commands. Fortunately, most ranges use encrypted
protocols for remote access (e.g., VPN, SSH, HTTPS) [7]. However, misconfigurations
(like using unencrypted channels or weak VPN pre-shared keys) could occur, especially in
ad-hoc range setups.
A 2021 case study on federated ranges compared VPN technologies and warned that
insecure choices (like PPTP) should be avoided in favour of IPSec or SSL VPNs with
strong encryption [11]. Even in local range setups, if participants connect their laptops
to a range Wi-Fi or switch, an insider might attempt to sniff traffic. For instance, if one
trainee in a competition tries to spy on another team’s unencrypted communications, this
constitutes a threat within the range environment.
Denial of Service (DoS) is another concern: an attacker could flood the range’s network
CHAPTER 2
10
==PAGE:20==
or services with traffic, disrupting the exercise. This could happen either from inside (a
participant triggering a DoS attack tool against the range infrastructure) or from outside
(if the range has an Internet-facing component, an external adversary might launch a
DDoS to sabotage the training event). Federated ranges are especially concerned with
availability; if one node is overwhelmed or its link is cut, it affects the whole exercise.
Additionally, if a range’s control interface (for instructors) is network-accessible, an attacker
might attempt to break in and take control of the exercise orchestration (e.g., altering
scenario parameters or scoring systems). Network-level attacks against ranges mirror
those against any networked system: interception, intrusion, and disruption. The stakes
can be high if, for example, a national-scale exercise is disrupted by an attack on the range
platform itself. Robust network security (encrypted channels, segmentation of participant
traffic, intrusion detection) is therefore an essential part of range design.
2.4.5
Human and insider threats during exercises:
The participants and administrators of a cyber range can themselves inadvertently become
a source of vulnerability. As noted by Deckard (2018), users do not always behave in
exercises exactly as they would in real incidents [17], which can lead to confusing outcomes,
but more pertinently, users might take unintended actions that jeopardise range security.
For example, if not explicitly forbidden, a curious trainee might try to ”hack back” against
the range infrastructure or use tools beyond the exercise scope. It’s been observed that
clear rules of engagement are needed; participants should be informed what actions are
off-limits (e.g., attempting to attack the hypervisor or other teams) [7]. Another insider threat
is the leakage of exercise information. Participants often sign Non-Disclosure Agreements,
especially if the exercise involves proprietary data or tactics [7]. Without an NDA or proper
briefing, a participant might share screenshots or solutions publicly, which could expose
vulnerabilities in the range or reduce the effectiveness of the training for others.
Additionally, if personal devices are allowed in the range, they could accidentally bridge
networks (for instance, a laptop connected to both the range and an external network could
route traffic out, defeating isolation). Noponen et al. cite that even well-meaning actions,
like an employee plugging in a company laptop to the range, could introduce malware or
leak info if that laptop has other network access [7].
Finally, the range operators themselves must be vigilant: an administrator with high
privileges could be an attractive target for social engineering. An attacker might phish a
range admin’s credentials (this is analogous to targeting a cloud admin) to gain entry [7].
This threat crosses over with cloud threats, where MITRE lists phishing and credential
stuffing as common initial access techniques against cloud environments [7]. In the context
of a cyber range, an operator’s compromised account could allow an outsider to shut down
VMs or access all exercise data.
To summarise, human factors require that range exercises implement policies (no unau-
thorised devices, NDA for sensitive scenarios, defined scope of allowed hacker tools) and
that admins follow strong security practices (unique passwords, 2FA, etc.) just as they
would on a production network.
2.4.6
Cloud-specific threats:
Many threats mentioned above manifest strongly in cloud-based environments. The cloud
management plane itself can be a target; an adversary might attempt to exploit the APIs
or web interfaces that control the range environment.
Techniques like acquiring stolen cloud credentials or exploiting public-facing applications
are documented by MITRE for enterprise cloud attacks [7]. In one case, an SLR source
CHAPTER 2
11
==PAGE:21==
noted that cloud range platforms should consider the OWASP Top 10 web vulnerabilities [7],
for example, if the range has a web portal for booking exercises or a scoreboard, SQL
injection or XSS flaws in that portal could lead to compromise.
Another cloud issue is scale abuse: an attacker who gains a foothold in a cloud range
might spawn numerous instances to either increase damage or cause financial/resource
exhaustion for the provider (analogous to cloud cryptojacking incidents, where attackers
use victims’ cloud accounts to run cryptocurrency miners).
Federated and cloud ranges also face potential supply chain threats if they rely on con-
tainers from public repositories or pre-built VM images; a tainted image could introduce a
backdoor. For instance, a popular vulnerable VM image used for exercises could itself
have an extra undocumented ”vulnerability” that allows the image creator remote access.
Although not reported explicitly in the literature, this is a conceivable risk given the reliance
on community VM images in some training events.
Lastly, cloud-based ranges must consider tenant separation. If a vulnerability in the range
orchestration software allowed one tenant’s scenario to connect to another’s, that would
be a serious breach of confidentiality. Ensuring proper identity and access management
within the cloud environment (each exercise’s resources isolated in separate VPCs or
resource groups, etc.) is essential to mitigate this.
In essence, a cloud cyber range should be secured following best practices for any
cloud deployment [7], including hardening accounts (no default passwords [7]), network
segmentation, continuous monitoring, and incident response planning specific to the range
infrastructure.
The literature though limited highlights multiple threats to cyber ranges: improper replication
of real systems leading to leaked credentials, physical and insider risks in on-premises
setups, network-level attacks on range connectivity, hypervisor and container escapes in
virtualised environments, and the danger of an attacker using the range as reconnaissance
for real targets [7][11][6]. Notably, Noponen et al. conclude that online virtual training
environments (especially cloud-hosted ranges) are exposed to the most serious threats,
requiring multiple defensive measures. They also observed that very few prior studies had
focused on these issues, indicating that our understanding of cyber range security is still in
early stages [7]. The next section discusses mitigation strategies drawn from the literature
to address the threats outlined here.
2.5
Mitigation strategies for cyber range security
To counter the above threats, researchers and practitioners recommend a variety of
mitigation strategies and best practices for securing cyber ranges. These strategies can
be mapped to the different threat categories:
• Thorough planning and ”cyber hygiene” in range design: The first step is
preventive; carefully plan what the range needs and include only necessary elements.
Before an exercise, define what level of detail is truly required. For instance, do not
import real user databases or credentials into the range if dummy data can serve the
training purpose.
One concept introduced is ”cyber exercise hygiene”, which refers to practices ensur-
ing that exercise content does not leak and cannot be mistaken for a real incident.
This includes measures like using code names for critical systems rather than real
names. The EU’s TIBER framework [18] for threat intelligence exercises recom-
mends assigning pseudonyms to real assets in all exercise materials. This way, even
CHAPTER 2
12
==PAGE:22==
if some information from the range is exposed, it won’t directly reveal the identity of
the actual systems.
Range designers should also enforce a strong separation between production and
range environments: for example, never allow direct copy-paste of configurations
without sanitisation, and ensure any sensitive information embedded in scenarios is
obfuscated [7].
• Automation and configuration management: Automated evaluation methods also
contribute to security by reducing the reliance on error-prone human assessments.
Bianchi et al. propose an automated framework that uses Red and Blue team logs
to build a tree-based model of attack-defence sequences, enabling objective post-
exercise analysis and enhancing configuration monitoring for defensive metrics [19].
Human error in setting up a range can lead to security gaps (e.g., an open port left
unprotected). Using automation tools to instantiate and tear down ranges can help
reduce misconfigurations. As noted in one review, many modern ranges employ
Infrastructure-as-Code and automated scripts, which, if properly designed, ensure
consistent security settings (firewalls on, default passwords changed, etc.).
Automation also allows quick reverting of environments to a known secure state
between sessions. Noponen et al. found that automation mitigates common human
errors during network and system configuration. However, the automation tools
themselves must be secure: scripts should be reviewed for potential backdoors and
run with the least privileges necessary [7].
• Hardening digital twins and simulated components: For ranges that function
as digital twins, applying software protection techniques can significantly raise the
bar for attackers. Hearn and Rix suggest techniques like software hardening, anti-
tamper mechanisms, and white-box cryptography to protect the range’s digital twin
assets [6].
These methods can lock software binaries and data to specific hardware or make
them extremely difficult to reverse-engineer. In practice, this might mean encrypting
configuration files of the range or requiring specialised dongles/keys to run certain
critical simulation components. If an attacker were to copy the range’s VM images,
these protections would render them unusable or at least not easily analysable on a
different machine.
Such measures prevent the propagation of the range’s internals beyond the intended
environment. This approach is especially relevant for high-value ranges modelling
critical infrastructure, where one might assume nation-state adversaries [7].
• Regular patching and update management: It may sound basic, but keeping all
range software up-to-date is repeatedly emphasised. The MITRE ATT&CK frame-
work [20] mitigations for enterprise include ”Update Software” and ”Network Seg-
mentation” as top recommendations.
Every component in a cyber range, from hypervisors to VM OS images to web
front-ends, should be maintained just like a production system with a rigorous patch
schedule. Range operators should subscribe to vulnerability alerts for all software
they use. For example, if a new VMware hypervisor patch addresses an escape
vulnerability, it should be applied to the range host before the next exercise.
Additionally, network segmentation within the range (logical segmentation) is advised.

==PAGE:23==
This could involve separating different teams’ network segments and the range control
segment, so that even if one part is compromised, it cannot freely access others.
Firewalls and VLANs inside the range can limit the blast radius of an attack [7].
• Secure account and credential management: Since credential compromise is
a common attack vector, ranges should enforce strict account policies. Default
credentials must be changed on all systems before an exercise begins. Using
centralised identity management (e.g., an AD or LDAP specifically for the range) can
help enforce strong password policies across all VMs.
Noponen et al. mention Privileged Account Management as a mitigation, which entails
practices like tiered administrative accounts and limiting their use. For instance, the
hypervisor admin account should not be used to log into any guest VM, and vice
versa, to prevent credential reuse.
Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) should be enabled for accessing critical range
components (such as the cloud console or VPN access). Account lockout policies
can thwart brute-force attempts (e.g., if someone tries guessing passwords on a
range VPN, the account locks after a few failures). User accounts known to be part
of previous data breaches (common password lists) should be reset or disallowed.
All the security measures for managing identities that you would use in a corporate
network should also be applied to the accounts in the cyber range [7].
• Monitoring and intrusion detection: Applying structured evaluation frameworks
helps range designers verify that a cyber range meets security and pedagogical goals.
Glas et al. demonstrate this by using the TARGET framework to evaluate the Iceberg
CRX. Their approach breaks down evaluation into dimensions like automation of
attacks, fidelity of tools, participant feedback, and monitoring coverage. For example,
their evaluation shows that automated monitoring can detect training gaps and
potential misuse in real-time [21].
Deploying monitoring tools within the range can help detect if something goes wrong.
For example, host-based intrusion detection on the range’s servers can alert if a
participant’s actions begin to target the hypervisor. Network intrusion detection
sensors can be placed at the boundary between the range and any external network
(or between teams in a competition setting) to flag unusual scanning or traffic that
deviates from exercise plans.
Given that certain attacks (like VM escapes or container escapes) might not be
obvious to participants or observers, having logs and alerts from the virtualisation
layer is important. Cloud-based ranges can use cloud provider security services
(such as AWS GuardDuty, Azure Security Center, etc.) to get alerts on suspicious
activities like new user creation, API misuse, or cryptocurrency mining in the range
project. Monitoring also includes keeping an eye on resource usage spikes, which
could indicate abuse.
• Securing communications: All remote access to the range should use encrypted
channels. VPNs used for federating ranges must use strong encryption (IPSec/IKEv2,
SSL/TLS with modern cyphers) [11]. Shared VPN credentials should be avoided;
instead, each participant could have individual certs or credentials to avoid a single
point of failure.
Within exercises, if participants need to simulate internet access, using a controlled
proxy or a pre-recorded traffic feed is safer than giving direct internet connectivity. In
CHAPTER 2
14
==PAGE:24==
cases where internet access is required (e.g., part of the scenario is to download
threat intelligence or updates), the range can be configured with an outgoing-only
connection through a filtering proxy to prevent any inbound connections.
For federated ranges, one suggestion is constant monitoring of interconnection links
and fail-safe measures: if one range detects a breach on its end, it should be able to
immediately sever the connection to others to contain the incident [11].
• Environmental reset and data wiping: After each exercise session, the range
should be cleaned of any user-entered data or changes. This is typically done by
reverting to VM snapshots. As highlighted by Winter (2012) [14] and reinforced by
Noponen et al., cleaning the range between sessions prevents one group’s data or
any compromise they introduced from affecting the next.
Logs from exercises can be archived securely for analysis, then wiped from the
live systems. This practice also addresses privacy concerns if, say, trainees typed
personal passwords during the exercise or if the scenario involved handling dummy
personal data. Nothing should persist that could be exploited later.
In cloud ranges, temporary instances should be destroyed and new ones created
for the next run, rather than reusing long-lived instances with potential lingering
malware [7].
• Participant training and access control: Mitigating training-phase risks requires
setting expectations with participants. Explicit briefings or rules of engagement are
recommended: participants should be told what is allowed and what is not. For
example, they should know not to connect any external network to the range (no
bridging Wi-Fi/cellular), not to attack the infrastructure, and to report any suspected
range security issue immediately.
In some cases, having an ”observer” or referee during competitive exercises helps
them notice if someone is deviating from the rules. Also, participants can be made
part of the defence: since they are usually security professionals, they can be asked
to practice good hygiene even during the exercise (e.g., use strong passwords for
any accounts they create in the range, don’t reuse credentials, etc.). This not only
prevents easy vectors for a colleague with bad intent, but also reinforces real-world
habits.
If the scenario permits, enabling user-level isolation (each participant gets non-
admin access unless needed) can reduce the impact if one participant’s account is
compromised. Lastly, requiring NDAs for exercises with sensitive content ensures
legal protection and deters information leakage after the fact [7].
Using different strategies together provides strong protection for cyber ranges. For example,
up-to-date hypervisor patches and monitoring can thwart a VM escape exploit, while 2FA
can prevent unauthorised logins even if an admin’s password is compromised. Noponen et
al. mapped threats to mitigations, emphasising strict network segregation for cloud ranges
and physical security for on-prem ranges [7]. They recommend treating the cyber range
like an extension of the corporate network regarding security policies, including considering
whether sensitive training should be conducted on the range or kept as tabletop simulations
to minimise leakage risks.

2.6
Conclusion
Securing cyber ranges requires a comprehensive approach. Although they are built for
experimentation, their security is critical. If vulnerabilities are left unaddressed, the very
environments intended for training can be exploited. This review of literature from 2010
to 2025 shows that cyber ranges face risks across design and deployment, virtualisation
stacks, data confidentiality, and insider behaviour, with the research base still increasing [7].
We outlined technical and procedural mitigations that reduce risk in practice. Key measures
include timely patching, secure configuration and segmentation, encryption, logging and
monitoring, clear rules of engagement, and responsible handling of exercise artefacts.
Where possible, these controls should be supported by automation to limit human error
and to keep configurations consistent over repeated builds.
These findings motivate a dedicated evaluation framework for open-source cyber ranges.
Such a framework benefits from a checklist that asks concrete, evidence-based questions,
for example: Are default credentials easy to change? How are VMs and containers
isolated? Is the secure configuration documented and applied?.
The review maps the threat landscape and practical defences for cyber ranges. There is
a need for clearer, standardised guidance and for documenting real incidents to inform
practice, similar to how OWASP supports web security. The next chapter presents well-
known cybersecurity control frameworks to identify how well existing solutions address the
threats and mitigations specifically for cyber ranges.

Comparative analysis of existing cyberse-
curity control frameworks
This chapter presents a comparative analysis of various security control frameworks to
evaluate their effectiveness in addressing the suggested mitigations outlined in the previous
chapter. While this is not a comprehensive list of all available frameworks, it includes a
selection of some of the most recognised ones.
3.1
NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 (NIST CSF 2.0)
NIST CSF 2.0 [22] is a publicly available framework managed by the U.S. National Insti-
tute of Standards and Technology. It is intended for voluntary use across sectors and
organisation sizes. The framework provides an outcome-based structure for organising
cybersecurity activities through six core functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Re-
spond, and Recover. CSF 2.0 is designed to align with existing standards and control
frameworks, and to support risk-based prioritisation rather than prescribe specific technical
controls.
3.1.1
Key features
• Outcome focus: Activities are expressed as desired outcomes, not implementation
steps.
• Structured taxonomy: Functions, Categories, and Subcategories organise work,
with Implementation Examples that illustrate possible practices.
• Alignment oriented: Maps conceptually to other standards and frameworks, en-
abling use with control sets such as NIST SP 800-53 and ISO/IEC 27002.
• Flexible and sector neutral: Tailorable to different contexts, maturity levels, and
risk appetites.
• Program support: Suited for building and assessing organisational cybersecurity
programs and communicating risk.
3.1.2
Relevance to cyber range security
Strengths: Several CSF outcomes relate to needs in cyber ranges. PR.AC supports
access control and strong authentication. PR.MA promotes timely maintenance and
patching. PR.DS and PR.PT support data protection, secure configuration, segmentation,
and logging. DE.CM covers security monitoring. The Governance function helps position
risks in an organisational context.
Limitations: CSF 2.0 is not a set of strict rules. It does not specify clear requirements
for using open-source cyber ranges. It also does not mention how to automatically reset
environments between exercises, how to keep exercise data clean, or how to ensure public
security practices for open projects. Instead, it focuses on achieving program-level goals
rather than the specific operations of container-based and temporary training environments.
Implication: CSF 2.0 offers a basic guideline and a shared language for evaluating open-
source cyber ranges. It should be used alongside detailed control frameworks or a cyber
range-specific framework. This approach helps ensure that we meet recognised goals
CHAPTER 3
18
==PAGE:28==
while addressing the unique risks linked to cyber range operations. CSF 2.0 is outcome-
focused and program-level. It does not define platform-specific controls or objective
evidence for repositories, build pipelines, or virtualisation stacks. It relies on mapping
to other frameworks, which leaves high assessor discretion and weak reproducibility for
open-source cyber ranges.
3.2
ISO/IEC 27002:2022
ISO/IEC 27002:2022 [2] This framework provides information security controls that help
implement an ISO/IEC 27001 based Information Security Management System (ISMS). It
organises 93 controls into four themes and offers guidance on how to choose and apply
them based on risk. The standard is neutral regarding technology and is designed to
fit various organisational contexts. We included ISO/IEC 27002 and not ISO/IEC 27001
because 27002 provides the detailed security controls needed for direct comparison with the
framework requirements, whereas 27001 focuses on management system processes and
certification, which are outside the scope of this framework’s technical control assessment.
3.2.1
Key features
• Comprehensive control set: 93 controls grouped under organisational, people,
physical, and technological themes.
• Implementation guidance: Descriptive guidance to support control selection and
operation following risk assessment.
• Alignment with ISO/IEC 27001: Designed to complement the ISMS requirements
and support auditability.
• Tailorable: Controls can be scoped and prioritised to fit context and risk appetite.
• Broad applicability: Sector-neutral and applicable to varied system types and
deployment models.
3.2.2
Relevance to cyber range security
Strengths: Several controls are useful for cyber ranges. Control 8.8 helps with managing
patches and vulnerabilities. Control 8.9 supports secure configuration standards. Controls
8.15 and 8.16 assist with logging and monitoring. Controls 5.15 to 5.18 focus on access
control, identity management, and authentication. Controls 8.20 to 8.22 enhance network
security and segmentation. Controls 8.13 and 8.14 provide backup and redundancy.
Control 8.31 ensures the separation of development, testing, and production environments.
Limitations: The standard does not require resetting the environment automatically
between exercises. It also does not cover how to manage data hygiene for different groups
during exercises. There are no clear methods for ensuring public security transparency or
for handling vulnerabilities in a community-driven way, which are important for open-source
projects. The guidance for temporary and container-based systems is vague and needs
further explanation.
Implication: ISO/IEC 27002 provides a solid set of controls that can be linked to cyber
range risks. When assessing open-source cyber ranges, they should be used alongside a
cyber range-specific framework. This will ensure that operational needs such as automated
reset or configuration, scenario data management, and security transparency, which are
not covered in the standard, are also addressed. ISO/IEC 27002 is an ISMS control
framework that assumes organisation-wide processes, roles, and audits. Applied to a
small open-source range, it becomes process-heavy while giving little direction for container

19

orchestration, temporary hosts, or public transparency practices. Important checks end up
inferred rather than tested.
3.3
NIST 800-53
NIST 800-53 [3] The text is a complete list of security and privacy controls created by
the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology. It helps support the NIST Risk
Management Framework and is widely used outside of U.S. federal systems. The controls
are organised into groups and can be customised with enhancements to suit different
levels of impact and mission needs.
3.3.1
Key features
• Broad coverage: 20 control families, including AC, CM, SI, SC, AU, IR, and SR.
• Tailoring and enhancements: Base controls with optional enhancements for higher
assurance.
• RMF alignment: Integrates with system categorisation, selection, implementation,
and assessment activities.
• Security and privacy: Includes both security and privacy requirements and supply
chain risk controls.
• Mappability: Can be mapped to other standards and used as a detailed underpinning
for higher-level frameworks.
3.3.2
Relevance to cyber range security
Strengths: Many controls relate to common needs in cyber ranges. The controls SI-2
and SI-5 help with fixing flaws and sending security alerts. CM-2 and CM-3 assist with
establishing baselines and managing changes for patching. AC-2 and AC-6 focus on
account management and the principle of least privilege. IA-2 and IA-5 work on strong
identification and managing authenticators. AU-2, AU-6, and SI-4 are for auditing and
monitoring activities. SC-39 ensures process isolation, while SC-34 emphasises non-
persistence, which is important for temporary environments. MP-6 deals with media and
data cleaning. The SR family focuses on managing supply chain risks.
Limitations: The framework is made for large enterprise systems and often needs signifi-
cant adjustments for smaller, container-based, or short-lived environments. It does not
require an automated reset of the environment between exercises, different credentials for
each exercise, or cleaning scenario data across groups. The framework includes a lot of
documentation and is not well-suited for quick, automated assessments commonly used
in open-source projects.
Implication: NIST 800-53 provides specific controls to help ensure the secure operation
of cyber ranges. When assessing open-source cyber ranges, it should be used alongside
a framework specifically for cyber ranges, which focuses on key operational needs such
as automation, configuration management, managing exercise data, and maintaining
transparency areas not covered in the main guidelines. NIST 800-53 is comprehensive
but costly to apply. It was written for federal systems and expects extensive tailoring
and documentation that open-source ranges rarely provide. It offers limited guidance for
automation-first workflows such as infrastructure as code, continuous compliance, and
reproducible lab builds.

3.4
ISA/IEC 62443
ISA/IEC 62443 [23] represents a set of standards designed to ensure the security of
Industrial Automation and Control Systems. This family of standards is modular, risk-
based, and oriented towards lifecycle management. It outlines specific roles, security
levels, and technical requirements for both systems and components, promoting a defence-
in-depth approach through the use of zoning and conduits.
3.4.1
Key features
• Layered architecture: Zoning and conduits for segmentation and trust boundaries
(62443-3-2).
• Structured requirements: System requirements and requirement enhancements
in 62443-3-3, and component requirements and secure development lifecycle in
62443-4-1 and 4-2.
• Role clarity: Guidance for asset owners, integrators, and product suppliers across
the lifecycle.
• Security levels: Target security levels aligned to risk and consequence.
• Lifecycle management: Policies and procedures for operation and maintenance in
62443-2-x.
3.4.2
Relevance to cyber range security
Strengths: Several requirements match the needs of cyber ranges. SR 1.1 to SR 1.8
focus on identifying users, verifying their identities, and controlling their access based on
roles. SR 6.1 to SR 6.4 deal with logging, auditing, and monitoring activities. SR 7.x covers
managing vulnerabilities and configurations, including keeping inventories and establishing
secure baselines. 62443-3-2 helps create segments using zones and conduits, which is
effective for isolating scenarios and management areas. Backup and recovery practices
in 62443-2-x support restoring systems to known states. These controls help prevent
configuration drift and enhance traceability in shared or multi-user environments.
Limitations: This series focuses on industrial settings that use long-lasting assets, rely
on vendor-led purchasing, and have strict goals for availability. The guidance mainly
addresses products and plants, offering little direction for automation-focused workflows
found in open-source platforms. These include practices like infrastructure as code, contin-
uous compliance checks, automated configuration validation, and detection of changes in
temporary hosts and containers. The standards do not cover community transparency for
open repositories or collecting evidence easily from public issue trackers. Implementing
the entire program can be resource-intensive compared to standard cyber range setups.
Implication: ISA/IEC 62443 provides a solid foundation for areas like segmentation,
role separation, configuration control, and monitoring. For open-source cyber ranges, it
works best as a starting point, but in the context of cyber ranges, it would benefit from
a tailored framework. This framework focuses on automation to reduce human error,
create consistent deployments, and ensure clear handling of security issues. ISA/IEC
62443 targets industrial control with long-lived assets, vendor supply chains, and strict
availability goals. These assumptions do not match community-maintained, short-lived
training environments. Zoning patterns help, but the domain and lifecycle mismatch leave
key evaluation needs uncovered.

3.5
CIS Controls v8
The Centre for Internet Security (CIS) Controls version 8 [24] is a set of clear and practical
guidelines designed to help reduce common security threats. Many organisations use it
because it is easy to understand and implement. This latest version updates the guidelines
for modern businesses and includes cloud and hybrid environments. It organises the
guidelines into three groups (IG1–IG3) based on the organisation’s ability and level of risk.
3.5.1
Key features
• Prioritised safeguards: 18 controls with more than 150 actionable tasks.
• Implementation Groups: IG1, IG2, and IG3 scale effort by maturity and risk.
• Prescriptive guidance: Task-level actions that are easy to operationalise.
• Framework mappings: Aligns with NIST CSF, ISO/IEC 27001, and others.
• Modern scope: Enterprise assets, cloud services, remote and mobile endpoints.
3.5.2
Relevance to cyber range security
Strengths: Several key controls support basic needs in cyber training environments.
These include secure configuration baselines (Control 4), managing access (Control
6), ongoing vulnerability management (Control 7), handling audit logs (Control 8), data
recovery (Control 11), and network monitoring and protection (Control 13). These controls
help maintain security, track activities, and contain issues in shared training systems. The
IG model makes it easy to plan improvements and show progress.
Limitations: The controls focus on business operations instead of temporary simulation
environments. The guidance for keeping containers separate, managing hypervisor risks,
and isolating multi-tenant labs is not very clear. The guidelines provide limited direction for
automation workflows that help reduce operator errors, such as using infrastructure as
code, conducting continuous compliance checks, validating configurations automatically,
detecting drift, and rebuilding scenarios consistently. The framework does not address
transparency practices for open-source projects or offer lightweight evidence from public
repositories and issue trackers.
Implication: CIS Controls v8 is a solid starting point for maintaining security and monitoring.
For open-source cyber ranges, it should be used alongside a framework tailored for cyber
ranges. This framework focuses on automation to reduce human error, ensures that
deployments can be repeated, promotes isolation for labs, and encourages clear handling
of security issues. CIS Controls v8 is a hygiene baseline rather than a system-specific
evaluation method. It lacks depth on hypervisors, containers, and multi-tenant isolation,
and it does not specify how to collect lightweight, objective evidence from public repositories.
On its own, it cannot discriminate well between stronger and weaker cyber range designs.

matter in practice for cyber range platforms.
The shortcomings of the frameworks come down to two things.
Wrong level of abstraction: Most frameworks are program or enterprise oriented. They
describe outcomes, policies, and governance rather than platform-level behaviours that
can be verified quickly on an open-source project. Controls for secure communication
and RBAC exist almost everywhere, but evidence expectations are policy documents and
audits, not observable artefacts such as code, configurations, build pipelines, and release
cadences. This invites assessor discretion and weakens reproducibility.

supply chains, and static environments. Cyber ranges are modular, short-lived, and
frequently rebuilt. They need automation first operation to limit human error, isolation
patterns for multi-cohort labs, drift control across templates and images, and simple ways
to collect public evidence from repositories and issue trackers. General frameworks either
treat these concerns indirectly or not at all, and domain-specific frameworks (for cloud,
ICS, or IoT) optimise for contexts that differ from training platforms.

4
Requirements and framework comparison
Following the reviews of the current literature on known threats to the security of cyber
ranges, as well as a comparative analysis of existing cybersecurity control frameworks,
this thesis will now present and argue for the identified requirements for the evaluation
framework. The 10 requirements for this framework are based on the findings from the
SLR on cybersecurity of cyber ranges, which is mostly based on the threats identified by
Noponen et al. [7].
1. Update and patch management
2. Access control and identity management
3. Configuration management
4. Network segmentation and isolation
5. Logging and monitoring
6. Secure communication
7. Security transparency and known vulnerabilities
8. Scenario data handling and hygiene
9. Exercise integrity and cheating prevention
10. Automated environment reset
These ten requirements form a solid basis for evaluating the cybersecurity of an open-
source cyber range. Users can adapt this list as needed, but if several of these requirements
are missing or weak, caution should be taken when using the range.
4.1
Detailed description of requirements
4.1.1
Update and patch management
Keeping hypervisors, VM images, container engines, orchestration tools, web portals, and
third-party libraries up-t-date reduces exposure to known flaws. In a cyber range, many
components execute untrusted user actions and sample malware, so outdated software
can become a direct path to compromise. A good update process keeps an accurate list of
assets, fixes the most serious issues first, tests changes in a test environment and regularly
rebuilds standard images. Setting clear patch deadlines, tracking known vulnerabilities
(CVEs), and removing unsupported components help close security gaps. Verifying the
source of images and scanning them for issues reduces the risk of introducing known
problems into training scenarios.
Mitigates: design and deployment-phase issues from unmaintained services and insecure
defaults; virtualisation and container threats such as virtual machine escape and container
breakout by closing known hypervisor and runtime flaws; cloud-specific threats in public-
facing services aligned with common web weaknesses; reduces the window for exploitation
in multi-tenant and federated setups.

==PAGE:36==
4.1.2
Access control and identity management
Role-based access control, unique credentials, removal of defaults, least privilege, and
multi-factor authentication limit what any account can do and make stolen credentials less
useful. In practice, this means mapping roles to tasks (participant, instructor, platform
admin), prohibiting shared accounts, and using just-in-time elevation with audit. Per-
session or per-exercise accounts and enforced credential randomisation reduce reuse
across scenarios. Strong secrets management for API tokens and keys, combined with
short-lived certificates for VPN or SSH, further restricts exposure. Clear joiner-mover-leaver
processes and account disablement after exercises close common gaps.
Mitigates: human and insider threats during exercises; credential abuse in cloud-specific
threats (phishing and credential stuffing); limits spillover risk in private ranges by containing
privileges; supports tenant separation and containment in cloud and federated ranges.
4.1.3
Configuration management
Using templates and version control, along with automated setup, makes systems stable
and secure. IaC, fixed based images, and peer-reviewed requests for changes help avoid
inconsistencies during frequent updates. To keep our systems secure, we strengthen their
defences by following CIS standards. We use straightforward, repeatable procedures and
regularly check for any configuration changes. We keep sensitive information separate
from templates and create builds that can be repeated, maintaining a history of changes
for audits and rollbacks. Continuous integration pipelines can check configurations, run
security tests, and prevent unsafe settings before going live.
Mitigates: design and deployment-phase issues such as insecure defaults and mis-
matched components; misconfigurations that enable lateral movement; cloud-specific risks
from misconfigured services or APIs; reduces attack surface in privileged automation.
4.1.4
Network segmentation and isolation
Keeping clear separation between teams, the instructor systems, shared services, and any
outside network helps limit damage and prevent problems from spreading to production
systems. In practice, this can mean using separate management networks, jump hosts,
strict filtering between internal systems, and separate VLANs or virtual networks for each
team. Outgoing internet access can be controlled through egress rules and proxies, which
also block incoming connections. For connected setups across sites, using a secure
connection point with strong authentication reduces the need to trust other sites. Health
checks can confirm that these parts are kept separate before and exercise starts.
Mitigates: network and communication threats (interception and intrusion); limits lateral
movement after a VM or container compromise; prevents accidental bridging to operational
systems in private ranges; supports safe federation through zoning and controlled conduits.
4.1.5
Logging and monitoring
Collecting audit trails and monitoring host, network, and control-plane activity helps distin-
guish training actions from real abuse and supports incident response. Useful telemetry
includes hypervisor and container runtime logs, authentication events, admin actions, and
north-south and east-west network flows. Time synchronisation and sufficient retention
enable forensics. Alerting should combine threshold rules for clear abuses with anomaly
detection tuned to expected exercise noise. Runbooks guide responses, and synthetic
canaries validate that detection still works during high activity.
Mitigates: early signs of virtual machine escape or container breakout attempts; man-in-
the-middle, session hijacking, and denial-of-service activity; policy violations and cheating;
enables post-exercise forensics and recovery.

4.1.6
Secure communication
Using strong VPNs, TLS, SSH, and authenticated APIs protects traffic among remote
participants, federated sites, and internal services. Mutual TLS for control-plane services,
per-user VPN certificates, key-based SSH, and rotation of tokens reduce interception
risk. Modern cypher suites and the avoidance of weak protocols improve resilience on
federation links. Certificate lifecycle management and automated renewal prevent silent
downgrade to insecure states.
Mitigates: network and communication threats, including man-in-the-middle, packet
sniffing, and session hijacking; reduces risk on federation links where weak VPN choices
create exposure.
4.1.7
Security transparency and known vulnerabilities
or open-source ranges, having public issue trackers, changelogs, and clear notes about
known vulnerabilities helps users understand risks and apply patches quickly. Publishing
a security contact, a disclosure policy, release notes that highlight security fixes, and a
software bill of materials for base images builds trust. Showing that security problems are
fixed quickly and dependencies are kept up to date signals active maintenance. Using
reproducible builds and basic tests for security-related components helps prevent old
problems from coming back.
Mitigates: design and deployment-phase blind spots by shortening time to remediation;
supply-chain concerns in community images and dependencies; reduces deployment of
components with known flaws.
4.1.8
Scenario data handling and hygiene
Do not use real passwords or sensitive production data. Anonymise data when needed,
and delete all files between sessions to protect confidentiality and keep scenarios accurate.
Useful measures include using unique secrets for each exercise, short-lived access tokens,
cleaned base images, and removing or securely storing logs. Any storage for flags or
submissions should be encrypted, and keys should be deleted when the exercise ends.
The documentation should clearly state that live data must not be imported into the range.
Mitigates: data confidentiality and “blueprint” leakage of layouts, versions, and secrets;
cross-exercise contamination where logs, flags, or temporary files leak; reputational and
operational risk if exercise content is mistaken for a real incident.
4.1.9
Exercise integrity and cheating prevention
Changing variables, separating user environments, hiding solution paths, and watching for
answer sharing help keep assessments fair and realistic. Giving each user their own flags
and login details, randomising ports and hostnames, and using different challenge versions
make copied answers less useful. Using scoring that shows if it has been tampered with,
signing challenge files, and checking for plagiarism increase trust in the results. Keeping
teams isolated prevents unwanted collaboration, while still allowing planned adversarial
play when needed.
Mitigates: human and insider threats during exercises, including manipulation of outcomes
and unauthorised collaboration; reduces lateral interaction between teams that bypasses
intended challenge paths.
4.1.10
Automated environment reset
Automatically rebuilding systems or reverting to snapshots returns them to a clean state
between sessions and reduces mistakes. Teardown and build processes that always
produce the same result, plus steps before and after that remove passwords and logs, help

credentials and artefacts that undermine data hygiene; errors introduced during manual
resets; speeds recovery after incidents detected by monitoring.
These requirements cover standard controls that all IT systems should have, plus extra
measures needed for running untrusted code, simulating real systems, and supporting
remote users. They address the threats found in the SLR, such as problems in design
and deployment, risks from virtual machines and containers, data privacy issues, leaks
of system blueprints, network and communication threats, insider risks during exercises,
and cloud-related threats. Where existing frameworks already work well, the requirements
match accepted best practices. Where there are unique risks in a cyber range, the
requirements focus on hygiene, isolation, integrity, and automation to close those gaps.

5
Application of OSCR SF to open-source cy-
ber ranges
This chapter serves as a case study for the OSCR SF, where two actively maintained and
publicly available open-source cyber ranges are evaluated: CyberRangeCZ and Docker
Security Playground (DSP). The goal is to demonstrate how the framework can be used
to evaluate real-world systems and identify concrete security strengths and weaknesses
across a variety of deployment models and complexity levels.
5.1
Selection criteria
To evaluate the proposed framework, we selected two open-source cyber ranges: Cy-
berRangeCZ [10] and Docker Security Playground (DSP) [26]. These were chosen for
their architectural diversity and operational characteristics, reflecting different deployment
models and security exposure patterns, and based on prior work by Emil Lundqvist at DTU,
who highlighted CyberRangeCZ as a solid choice and DSP as a lightweight option [27].
A key criteria for selection was deployment architecture. Cyber ranges can experience
different threats depending on their deployment; for example, cloud-hosted platforms face
risks like insecure APIs and multi-tenancy, while containerised or locally hosted platforms
encounter issues with privilege escalation and resource sharing. We chose CyberRangeCZ
for cloud or hybrid deployments and DSP for self-hosted, container-based setups.
Another important factor was system complexity. CyberRangeCZ is a modular platform
with features like role-based access control and distributed orchestration, representing
advanced cyber range capabilities. In contrast, DSP is a minimal setup focusing on
containerised security scenarios, allowing easier inspection of basic security practices.
We did consider other platforms; however, most of those screened hadn’t been updated
for at least 7 months, and thus, we considered them out of active development, at least for
now.
By comparing these two platforms, we ensure the evaluation encompasses both complex,
enterprise-grade deployments and simpler, developer-centric systems, allowing us to
analyse the framework’s adaptability across various cyber range types.
5.2
Scoring of requirements
We score each requirement as Fully satisfied, Partially satisfied, or Not satisfied. The
rules below are simple and evidence-based. We look for things that are visible in public
docs, code, or configuration.
1. Update and patch management
• Fully satisfied: There is a clear update process for the application, images
or other artefacts, and dependencies. Versions are pinned. Release notes
mention security fixes.
• Partially satisfied: Some parts are covered but not all. Pinning is inconsistent.
• Not satisfied: No update process. Versions are not tracked.

2. Access control and identity management
• Fully satisfied: Authentication is required. Role-based access is described
and used. Multi-factor authentication is available.
• Partially satisfied: Authentication exists, but roles are unclear or multi-factor
is missing.
• Not satisfied: No authentication or a single user default.
3. Configuration management
• Fully satisfied: Configuration is version controlled. Deployment is reproducible
using Infrastructure as Code. Baseline settings and basic checks are described.
• Partially satisfied: Some configuration is documented or there are examples,
but manual steps remain.
• Not satisfied: Setup is manual and configuration is not version-controlled.
4. Network segmentation and isolation
• Fully satisfied: Scenarios or tenants are isolated by default. The control plane
is separate. Lateral movement is limited, and defaults are documented.
• Partially satisfied: There is some segmentation, but it is not the default or
cross-tenant paths exist.
• Not satisfied: The network is flat or shared without isolation guarantees.
5. Logging and monitoring
• Fully satisfied: Security events can be collected in one place or queried
centrally. Retention is described. Administrative actions are auditable.
• Partially satisfied: Logs exist, but retention or audit trail is unclear.
• Not satisfied: No logging guidance.
6. Secure communication
• Fully satisfied: TLS is used for all user-facing services and administration.
The key and certificate lifecycle is described.
• Partially satisfied: Some channels use encryption but not all. UI or API TLS is
optional or not documented.
• Not satisfied: No clear evidence of encryption.
7. Security transparency and known vulnerabilities
• Fully satisfied: There is a public disclosure policy such as SECURITY.md. Se-
curity issues are tracked in public. Releases mention fixes or CVE IDs, and
response expectations are stated.
• Partially satisfied: A policy exists, but issues or fixes are not traceable in public
artefacts.
• Not satisfied: No disclosure policy and no obvious route to report issues.
8. Scenario data handling and hygiene

The framework OSCR SF contains 10 requirements for a secure open-source cyber range.
Here is how well CyberRangeCZ cover these requirements.
The first requirement revolves around update and patch management, and the docu-
mentation has a page concerning when the images were last updated [30]. However, there
is no documented procedure for routinely applying security patches to running sandboxes.
The original GitLab for the KYPO CRP [9] still contains the issues page, but one is only
able to see open issues, and not earlier and fixed issues. And since KYPO CRP has been
discontinued, this isn’t exactly useful. So, to conclude, CyberRangeCZ provides an update
history for the sandbox images, but they never mention anything about regular patches (at
least not from our research). This requirement can then be considered partially satisfied.
The second requirement concerns access control and identity management, and
CyberRangeCZ provides strong support in this area. The official documentation includes a
dedicated OpenID Connect (OIDC) setup guide, which explains how the platform integrates
with Keycloak for authentication and identity federation. The guide shows that it is possible
to use local Keycloak accounts, external identity providers (such as OAuth-based services),
as well as institutional directories like Kerberos and LDAP [31]. The documentation’s
“Roles” page outlines how abstract roles such as trainees, instructors, and administrators
are defined using collections of microservice-specific roles. This provides fine-grained
access control and reflects a well-structured role-based access model [32]. Based on this,
we consider this requirement to be fully satisfied.

==PAGE:43==
The third requirement addresses configuration management, and CyberRangeCZ
demonstrates a high level of maturity in this requirement. According to the official docu-
mentation, virtual machines, networks, and training scenarios are defined using human-
readable configuration formats.
These components are built using well-established
infrastructure-as-code tools such as Packer and Ansible, which ensure consistency and
repeatability in environment setup [28]. Sandbox definitions are managed through Git,
and instructors are required to supply a Git repository URL and a specific revision when
creating a new sandbox. This approach allows for full version control of configurations and
supports traceability and rollback if needed [33]. Based on this evidence, this requirement
is considered fully satisfied.
The fourth requirement covers network segmentation and isolation, and CyberRangeCZ
provides strong guarantees in this regard. The original KYPO CRP design paper explains
that the platform’s cloud networking is based on IEEE 802.1Q VLAN tagging and Q-in-Q
tunnelling. This allows each sandbox to have its own isolated virtual LAN, independent
of the underlying physical network addressing [29]. Each LAN is managed by a dedi-
cated LAN Management Node, which uses Open vSwitch to handle intra-LAN traffic and
inter-LAN routing. This setup ensures that sandboxes remain logically separated, even
when deployed on shared infrastructure. Based on this architecture, this requirement is
considered fully satisfied.
The fifth requirement is logging and monitoring, and CyberRangeCZ provides several
relevant features, although some limitations remain. The documentation describes a
central logging system that collects logs using Syslog-ng, Rsyslog, Logstash, and Elas-
ticsearch [34]. Logs from sandbox hosts are forwarded to a central server and enriched
with structured metadata. The logs page specifies that all trainee actions and training
events are recorded for both real-time visualisation and post-exercise analysis in a com-
prehensive dashboard. The KYPO CRP design paper also notes that the monitoring
component gathers network traffic metrics and supports full packet capture [29]. Therefore,
this requirement is considered fully satisfied.
The sixth requirement is secure communication. Users access sandbox environments
via SSH using a ProxyJump configuration, with SSH keys and configuration files provided
to facilitate secure remote access [35]. External users connect through VPN tunnels,
ensuring encrypted communication with the internal infrastructure. The platform also
employs OpenID Connect (OIDC) via a locally deployed Keycloak identity provider. OIDC,
which extends OAuth 2.0, enables secure authentication through token-based mechanisms
over HTTPS and supports integration with external identity sources. CyberRangeCZ
explicitly requires the use of JSON Web Tokens and a user info endpoint for validation,
and it uses OIDC to protect both user authentication and access to internal microservices
and APIs [31]. These design choices indicate that secure communication is consistently
enforced across the platform; this requirement is fully satisfied.
The seventh requirement is security transparency and known vulnerabilities, and it
appears to be unsupported in the current platform. While the documentation and repository
pages include standard licensing and contribution information [28], there are no references
to public vulnerability disclosures, CVE identifiers, or a responsible disclosure policy.
A quick search in the GitHub repository also didn’t reveal any SECURITY.md, which is a
general recommendation for all larger systems/platforms. Without any form of documented
vulnerability management or advisories, this requirement is not satisfied.
The eighth requirement focuses on scenario data handling and hygiene, and Cyber-
RangeCZ implements some supportive features in this area. Instructors must manually
CHAPTER 5
34
==PAGE:44==
deallocate and delete training pools after use, which ensures that the underlying sandbox
resources are freed [36]. Sandbox definitions cannot be removed while still linked to train-
ing pools, helping prevent accidental data loss. The Automatic Problem Generation (APG)
mechanism allows instructors to randomise per-session variables such as usernames,
passwords, ports, and other answer-related fields [37]. This enhances variability between
runs and reduces the risk of static answer reuse. However, APG focuses on training-level
answers rather than sanitisation of logs or anonymisation of real-world data, and it does
not enforce synthetic-only input or restrict the use of real credentials. Since these features
depend on instructor choices and are not embedded by default, this requirement is partially
satisfied.
The ninth requirement concerns exercise integrity and cheating prevention. Cyber-
RangeCZ demonstrates strong support for this requirement through multiple integrated
mechanisms. Its Automatic Problem Generation (APG) system randomises critical vari-
ables such as usernames, passwords, and ports for each training run, ensuring that
answers and access credentials differ across sessions [37]. The platform includes a
dedicated Cheating Detection module, which allows instructors to define and execute
multiple detection methods either during or after training runs. These include checks for
answer similarity, location similarity, time proximity of submissions, minimal solve times,
absence of commands, and use of forbidden commands. Detection events are logged and
can be reviewed in detail, providing traceable evidence of suspicious behaviour. Together,
these features offer robust protection against answer sharing, manipulation of outcomes,
and unauthorised behaviour[38]. As such, this requirement is assessed as fully satisfied.
The tenth and final requirement is automated environment reset, and CyberRangeCZ
does not appear to support this. The training workflow requires that instructors manually
deallocate sandboxes and delete them after each session [36]. There is no indication that
the platform includes a feature to automatically reset sandboxes to a clean state between
exercises. The documentation does mention resetting sandboxes after exercises [39], so
at least they make sure users are aware of it, but without automation, it can not be ensured
that past training data will not end up in future exercises. Therefore, this requirement is
considered not satisfied.
To summarise, CyberRangeCZ presents a well-structured and security-aware design, with
strong support for access control, configuration management, and network isolation. Some
areas, such as logging, secure communication, and data handling, are implemented to a
reasonable extent, but could benefit from additional features like automated alerting, TLS
enforcement, and anonymisation. Other important security elements, such as automated
environment reset and vulnerability disclosure, are not addressed in the available docu-
mentation. This, however, does not confirm that they aren’t available features, just that
they are not mentioned in the available documentation. Table 5.1 provides an overview of
how CyberRangeCZ performs across all ten requirements in the OSCR SF framework.

mentions updating npm packages using the command npm run update and performing

these configurations consistent and correctly applied is left entirely to the user. There is
no built-in mechanism to enforce standardisation or to check whether configurations have
been altered. Therefore, this requirement is considered partially satisfied.
The fourth requirement addresses network segmentation and isolation. DSP allows
users to define multiple networks and containers within labs using Docker networks,
providing a basic level of isolation [43]. However, there is no explicit enforced segmentation
between different labs or from the host system, and the default exposure of the interface
on http://localhost:18181 can pose risks if misconfigured. Thus, this requirement is
partially satisfied.
The fifth requirement, Logging and Monitoring, also has partial support. DSP uses the
Bunyan logger to record basic application events, and logs are written to a rotating file
(dsp.log) when login is enabled [44]. There is no comprehensive logging or real-time
monitoring of user activity or integration with external monitoring systems. Therefore, this
requirement is partially satisfied.
The sixth requirement, Secure Communication, is not covered by DSP. The platform’s
default communication occurs via plain HTTP on port 18181, without TLS or SSH support.

Recommendations for Improvements
DSP would need a substantial security overhaul to satisfy the OSCR SF requirements, and

as these are standard requirements in most, if not all, security control frameworks.
For CyberRangeCZ, it already satisfies most of the requirements, but lacks a little on the
more cyber range-specific requirements. Recommendations for CyberRangeCZ would
be a clear update and patch management process, improved data hygiene practices
such as automatic log sanitisation and guidance on the use of synthetic credentials, and
the implementation of an automated environment reset mechanism to ensure training
environments return to a clean state after each session.

6
Evaluation
After applying OSCR SF to two open-source cyber ranges, CyberRangeCZ and Docker
Security Playground (DSP), this chapter evaluates how well the framework serves its
purpose: to assess platform security in a structured, repeatable, and transparent way.
6.1
Findings
6.1.1
Purpose and scope
The goal is not to “rank” platforms, but to see whether OSCR SF produces clear, defen-
sible and reproducible outcomes across different types of ranges. The two case studies
were deliberately different. CyberRangeCZ targets multi-user training with production-like
deployments. DSP is a lightweight, developer-focused tool. Figure 5.1 and Figure 5.2
show the outcomes for each requirement, and Figure 5.3 summarises the comparison.
6.1.2
Comparison of results
The framework showed clear differences between the two platforms that matched their
intended use. CyberRangeCZ met most of the basic requirements that general security
frameworks also cover, such as access control, configuration management, and network
isolation. DSP missed several of these because it is made for local, single-user setups.
The biggest differences came from the cyber range–specific requirements in OSCR SF.
Features like exercise integrity and cheating prevention, scenario data hygiene, security
transparency for known vulnerabilities, and automated environment reset were either
missing or only partly in place. This pattern appeared even when the basic controls were
met. Recording “partially satisfied” results was useful, as it showed progress without
overstating the coverage of a requirement.
6.1.3
Usability and effort
Using OSCR SF was quick once the sources were found. Evidence could be collected from
public documentation, repositories, and configuration files without needing deep access
to internal systems. This is one of the benefits of open-source platforms. Organising the
assessment by each requirement helped guide the search and reduced the chance of
missing important areas.
The main challenge was dealing with incomplete or scattered documentation. In these
cases, the framework made it clear when there was no evidence. For selecting a platform
or planning its design, this is useful because it gives a starting point and a clear list of
actions to take next.
6.2
Observations from the evaluation
6.2.1
Scoring consistency
The scoring rules outlined in chapter 5 helped keep decisions consistent. Requirements
with clear, visible evidence were the easiest to assess. For example, access control could
be checked through role definitions and account setup, and configuration management
could be checked through versioned repositories and IaC files. Some requirements like
logging required a deeper dive into the repository code to find evidence of logging, at least
for DSP, CyberRangeCZ clearly disclosed their logging capabilities. Scoring the vulnera-
bility transparency was also a little difficult, none of them had a SECURITY.md file in their
CHAPTER 6
39
==PAGE:49==
repository, however that doesn’t mean that they are not transparent about vulnerabilities,
however in these cases there was no evidence of any vulnerability transparency. When
there was no evidence it was simply scored as ”not satisfied” for the given requirement.
This avoids making assumptions, but it may also miss features that are in place but not
well-documented.
6.2.2
Impact of evidence requirements
The results depend on how strict the evidence criteria are set. Using documentation, code,
and configuration files as acceptable evidence produced the outcomes in Chapter 5. If the
bar for “fully satisfied” were raised to require code or configuration proof, some current “fully
satisfied” results would drop to “partially satisfied” especially for secure communication
and logging, where statements exist but enforcement details are limited.
Requiring runtime checks for “fully satisfied” in cases where this is meaningful, such as
verifying TLS enforcement on user interfaces, would create an even larger shift. In contrast,
access control, configuration management, and network isolation were stable because
the necessary artefacts are present and specific. This variation is not a flaw; it simply
shows where adding small runtime checks in future assessments could strengthen the
conclusions.
6.2.3
Contribution and benefits
Chapter 4 showed that established frameworks cover general controls well but do not
address several needs that are common in cyber ranges. OSCR SF adds value by making
those needs explicit and easy to check. In practice, the framework exposed gaps that
general controls would not surface on their own, such as the absence of automated resets
that also sanitise logs and state, or a lack of public vulnerability handling. At the same
time, it confirmed where platforms already meet good practice. CyberRangeCZ appears
suitable for larger training scenarios with multiple users. DSP is convenient for single-user
labs but would need stronger defaults for access control and encrypted communication
before use in shared or exposed environments.

7
Discussion
7.1
Key findings
Applying OSCR SF to CyberRangeCZ and DSP showed that general-purpose security
frameworks remain valuable for core controls such as access control, configuration man-
agement, and secure communication, but they do not address cyber range–specific threats.
Requirements such as automated environment reset, scenario data hygiene, vulnerabil-
ity transparency, and exercise integrity were the main differentiators between the two
platforms.
CyberRangeCZ demonstrated strong implementation of fundamental controls but lacked
automated resets, a transparent vulnerability disclosure process, and had gaps in update
and patch management. DSP, while convenient for single-user setups, had weak defaults
for authentication, encryption, and logging, making it unsuitable for shared or internet-facing
environments without modification.
These differences were driven primarily by the cyber range–specific requirements rather
than the generic controls. While role-based access control and secure communication
are essential in any distributed system, safeguards like data hygiene and automated
resets become especially important for internet-exposed or multi-user ranges. The results
demonstrate the value of domain-specific evaluation criteria in identifying risks that general
frameworks are not designed to detect.
7.2
Limitations
This evaluation covered only two cyber ranges, selected for their clear architectural and
operational differences. While this contrast was useful for testing OSCR SF, the small
sample limits the generalisability of the findings.
The assessment relied entirely on publicly available documentation and repositories. If a
feature is implemented but undocumented, it cannot be credited, which may understate
actual security coverage. This was most apparent for requirements such as automated
resets and data hygiene, which are sometimes implemented informally or assumed to be
self evident.
A uniform baseline was applied to all platforms to maintain simplicity and comparability.
However, this approach means lightweight, single-user tools may appear weaker than
multi-user or enterprise-grade systems, which typically have more mature security practices
and larger development resources.
The framework is also limited by only being open-source specific, it relies too heavily
on publicly available documentation about the inner workings of a system to give proper
evaluation that it wouldn’t work with a closed-source cyber range, with being granted
access to the source files.
7.3
Threats against validity
Selection bias may be present due to the choice of platforms. The evaluation focused on
cyber ranges updated within the past seven months, excluding older or inactive projects
such as StratoCyberLab. While this ensured that the analysis reflected actively maintained
CHAPTER 7
41
==PAGE:51==
systems, it may have omitted platforms with different design approaches or security
practices.
Interpretation bias is another risk. Even with defined scoring rules, some judgement was
required to decide what counted as documentation versus configuration evidence, or
when to classify a requirement as partially rather than fully satisfied. Providing a scoring
guide and citing exact evidence sources reduces, but does not eliminate, this subjectivity.
Incorporating small runtime checks could further improve objectivity.
Construct validity depends on the requirements themselves. The ten OSCR SF require-
ments were derived from literature and mapped to observed threats, prioritising evidence
that can be quickly verified in open-source projects. Alternative mappings could place
greater emphasis on formal assurance or incident response processes. Continued appli-
cation across more platforms, combined with feedback from maintainers and instructors,
will help refine the requirements without reducing clarity.
Despite these risks, OSCR SF produced reproducible results, identified security gaps with
practical relevance, and offered specific improvement steps. As a lightweight complement
to broader standards, it provides a practical method for evaluating and improving the
security of open-source cyber ranges before they are deployed to real users.
7.4
Future work
Several changes or additions could strengthen OSCR SF.
Firstly, broaden the evaluation sample. Applying the framework to a wider range of open-
source and potentially closed-source cyber ranges would provide a stronger basis for
comparison. A larger dataset would make it possible to analyse which requirements most
often fail, why they fail, and whether certain architectural choices (for example, VM-based
versus container-based designs) correlate with specific security gaps.
Secondly, incorporate light automation to reduce effort and improve scoring consistency
without requiring privileged access. Possible tools include a repository scanner for security
indicators for example a SECURITY.md, a documentation linter like ESLint to flag missing
TLS guidance or role definitions, and quick tests to confirm claims (such as verifying
whether a UI is served over HTTPS). These could be bundled into an “OSCR SF verifier”
that generates an evidence report alongside the scores.
Thirdly, introduce a risk-aware profile system. The same ten requirements would apply, but
the profile would set expectations for typical deployment scenarios—such as single-user
local labs, classroom multi-user ranges, or internet-exposed/federated environments. This
would help prioritise improvements while keeping results comparable.
Finally, engage with practitioners through interviews or surveys. Feedback from cyber
range operators and instructors could reveal practical constraints—such as the cost of TLS
termination or the difficulty of implementing MFA in classrooms—and guide refinements to
ensure the requirements remain both clear and realistic.

8
Conclusion
This thesis has introduced the Open-Source Cyber Range Security Framework (OSCR
SF) to address common security gaps through ten practical, evidence-based requirements.
It was tested on two open-source cyber ranges with markedly different use cases and
architectures: CyberRangeCZ and Docker Security Playground (DSP).
The evaluation revealed clear security gaps, many of which align with threats previously
identified in the literature. CyberRangeCZ demonstrated strong coverage of fundamental
controls and partial alignment with several cyber range specific requirements, while DSP
fell short in most areas, particularly authentication, encryption, and logging. These results
confirm that OSCR SF can distinguish mature platforms from less mature ones and highlight
security concerns that general-purpose frameworks are not designed to detect.
An analysis of existing cybersecurity control frameworks showed that, while they remain
valuable for core requirements, they do not address the unique operational risks of cyber
ranges. This reinforces the need for specialised frameworks like OSCR SF to ensure
comprehensive evaluations.
The contribution of this work is both practical and conceptual. It highlighted a clear need
for a domain specific control framework. Practically, OSCR SF provides a lightweight,
repeatable method for assessing and improving the security of open-source cyber ranges.
Conceptually, it establishes a foundation for treating cyber range security as a distinct
discipline, encouraging further research and development towards safer and more resilient
platforms.

Scoring for OSCR SF.
1. Update and patch Management
• Fully satisfied: There is a clear update process for the application, images
or other artefacts, and dependencies. Versions are pinned. Release notes
mention security fixes.
• Partially satisfied: Some parts are covered but not all. Pinning is inconsistent.
• Not satisfied: No update process. Versions are not tracked.
2. Access control and identity management
• Fully satisfied: Authentication is required. Role-based access is described
and used. Multi-factor authentication is available.
• Partially satisfied: Authentication exists, but roles are unclear or multi-factor
is missing.
• Not satisfied: No authentication or a single user default.
3. Configuration management
• Fully satisfied: Configuration is version controlled. Deployment is reproducible
using Infrastructure as Code. Baseline settings and basic checks are described.
• Partially satisfied: Some configuration is documented or there are examples,
but manual steps remain.
• Not satisfied: Setup is manual and configuration is not version-controlled.
4. Network segmentation and isolation
• Fully satisfied: Scenarios or tenants are isolated by default. The control plane
is separate. Lateral movement is limited, and defaults are documented.
• Partially satisfied: There is some segmentation, but it is not the default or
cross-tenant paths exist.
• Not satisfied: The network is flat or shared without isolation guarantees.
5. Logging and monitoring
• Fully satisfied: Security events can be collected in one place or queried
centrally. Retention is described. Administrative actions are auditable.
• Partially satisfied: Logs exist, but retention or audit trail is unclear.
• Not satisfied: No logging guidance.
6. Secure communication
• Fully satisfied: TLS is used for all user-facing services and administration.
The key and certificate lifecycle is described.

• Partially satisfied: Some channels use encryption but not all. UI or API TLS is
optional or not documented.
• Not satisfied: No clear evidence of encryption.
7. Security transparency and known vulnerabilities
• Fully satisfied: There is a public disclosure policy such as SECURITY.md. Se-

• Partially satisfied: A policy exists, but issues or fixes are not traceable in public

• Not satisfied: No disclosure policy and no obvious route to report issues.
8. Scenario data handling and hygiene
• Fully satisfied: Guidance and checks prevent secrets or personal data in
scenarios. Example data is sanitised. Contributions are reviewed for sensitive
content.
• Partially satisfied: Guidance exists, but templates or examples still include
sensitive artefacts.
• Not satisfied: No guidance and real secrets or personal data are present.
9. Exercise integrity and cheating prevention
• Fully satisfied: There are clear integrity measures. For example, randomised
variants, time or attempt limits, submission checks, or scoring that shows
tampering.
• Partially satisfied: There are some deterrents or a stated policy, but the
measures are limited or optional.
• Not satisfied: No explicit integrity measures.
10. Automated environment reset
• Fully satisfied: A reliable automation returns the platform to a clean state and
removes user artefacts by default. Success is described or verified.
• Partially satisfied: Reset scripts exist but are optional or incomplete.
• Not satisfied: Only manual teardown and persistence remains.
