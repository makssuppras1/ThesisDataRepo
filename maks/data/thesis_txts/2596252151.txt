Abstract 
 
In this project, the relation of the fifth generation of wireless technology (5G) in the present rivalry 
between the United States (U.S.) and the People’s Republic of China (PRC) is analyzed. To 
understand how the 5G network has led to a rivalry between the U.S. and the PRC, the project is 
initiated with an analysis of the historical relation between the two nations. The analysis reveals that 
the relation between the U.S. and the PRC has become divergent, particularly due to the trade war 
and the emerging superpower status of the PRC as well as the highly competitive innovation strategies 
that the PRC has developed. Furthermore, it is inferred that the 5G network is at the center of the 
rivalry between the U.S. and the PRC. 
The technology behind the 5G network is then analyzed, and the technological possibilities and 
economic potential that 5G enables is presented.  The analysis demonstrates that the 5G network will 
be crucial for the development of innovative technologies such as the Internet of Things (IoT) and 
Artificial Intelligence (AI), which will enable the fourth industrial revolution (Industry 4.0). 
Furthermore, the different approaches of the U.S. and the PRC to develop the 5G network is 
presented, and it is revealed that the PRC have a competitive advantage over the U.S. in the 
deployment of the 5G network, particularly due to Huawei’s superior and cheap 5G network. 
The rivalry is then discussed in relation to the historical ties between the U.S. and the PRC, and it is 
revealed how the science and technology agreement including the comparative advantages between 
the two nations have changed considerably over time. This is explained through concepts from game 
theory, which reveals that the U.S. and the PRC’s relation has changed from being cooperative to 
being competitive due to the power the PRC has obtained during the past 40 years. Additionally, the 
PRC’s alleged security threat is analyzed, and a discussion of who will win the 5G race is presented. 
Finally, it is concluded that the results presented in the project demonstrates that the 5G network will 
be critical for the development of new technologies, and that 5G has caused a rivalry between the 
U.S. and the PRC.

Sammenfatning 
 
I dette projekt analyseres relationen af den femte generation af trådløse netværk (5G) til den 
igangværende rivalisering mellem USA og Kina. For at forstå hvordan 5G netværket har ledt til en 
rivalisering mellem USA og Kina, er projektet indledt med en analyse af den historiske relation 
mellem de to nationer. Analysen beskriver at relationen mellem USA og Kina er blevet divergerende, 
især grundet handelskrigen og Kinas gradvise status som supermagt samt de meget kompetente 
innovationsstrategier som Kina har udviklet. Ydermere udledes det at 5G netværket er kernen i 
rivaliseringen mellem USA og Kina. 
5G netværkets bagvedliggende teknologi bliver således analyseret, og de teknologiske muligheder og 
det økonomiske potentiale som 5G muliggør bliver præsenteret. Analysen demonstrerer at 5G 
netværket vil være kritisk for udviklingen af innovative teknologier såsom tingenes internet (IoT) og 
kunstig intelligens (AI), hvilket vil muliggøre den fjerde industrielle revolution (Industri 4.0). 
Ydermere bliver de forskellige initiativer fra USA og Kina til udviklingen af 5G netværket 
præsenteret, og det beskrives at Kina har en konkurrencefordel over USA i implementeringen af 5G 
netværket, hvilket især skyldes Huaweis overlegne samt billigere 5G netværk.  
Rivaliseringen bliver derefter diskuteret i relation til den historiske forbindelse mellem USA og Kina, 
og det beskrives hvordan videnskab og teknologiaftalen samt de komparative fordele landende 
imellem har ændret sig betydeligt over tid. Dette bliver forklaret gennem koncepter fra spilteori, som 
beskriver hvordan USA og Kinas forhold har ændret sig fra at være samarbejdende til at være 
rivaliserende grundet den magt som Kina har opnået over de seneste 40 år. Derudover analyseres 
Kinas påståede sikkerhedstrussel, og en diskussion af hvem der vil vinde 5G kapløbet præsenteres. 
Til sidst konkluderes det at resultaterne præsenteret i projektet demonstrerer at 5G netværket vil være 
kritisk for udviklingen af nye teknologier, og at 5G har forårsaget en rivalisering mellem USA og 
Kina.

1. Introduction 
  
1.1. Project background 
In 2018, the United States (U.S.) began imposing tariffs on imported goods from the People’s 
Republic of China (PRC), with the intention of changing the trade practices between the two nations, 
as the current practices were considered unequitable. The imposed tariffs have led to an ongoing trade 
war between the U.S. and the PRC with both nations continually imposing retaliatory tariffs on 
imported goods (Brown, 2020; Timmons & Adler, 2020). 
In the wake of the trade war between the U.S. and the PRC, evidence suggests that the key purpose 
of the imposed tariffs from the U.S. have been to target the PRC’s technological development (Liu & 
Woo, 2018). Tariffs have been imposed on industrially significant technology, and institutive actions 
have been executed by U.S. president Donald Trump to secure the information and communications 
technology and services supply chain of the U.S. (Trump, 2019a). This was intensified when the U.S. 
restricted the Chinese company Huawei, the world’s biggest and most advanced telecoms equipment 
maker (Kynge & Fildes, 2020), from trading with U.S. suppliers in May 2019 (Shepardson et al., 
2019a). The U.S. claimed that the equipment and software of Huawei posed a national security risk, 
with accusations of the Chinese government taking advantage of its equipment to spy the U.S. and its 
allies (Peck et al., 2019). Eventually, the U.S. banned several more Chinese technology companies 
(Yang, 2020). The PRC has reciprocated the bans by replacing U.S. technology and software, to 
decouple with the U.S. technology supply chain (Yang & Liu, 2019). This is in line with the Chinese 
president Xi Jinping’s “Made in China 2025” strategy which aims to make the PRC an independent, 
and world leading, technological powerhouse by 2025 (Lee, 2020).  
The trade war is thus the surface of a more complex conflict, namely a rivalry for technological world 
leadership. To lead technological innovations for years to come, a critical wireless network 
infrastructure is required: 5G. 5G, the fifth generation of wireless technology, will increase the speed 
and capacity as well as reduce the latency of the wireless network tremendously compared to today’s 
technology (Hill & Beaton, 2020). Through the superior technology metrics, 5G is expected to 
fundamentally change both industrial and public sectors, functioning as the infrastructure that enables 
digital opportunities such as autonomous robotics, artificial intelligence (AI), and augmented reality 
==PAGE:9==
2 
 
(AR) (Ericsson & Little, 2019). Due to the application possibilities of 5G in virtually everything 
digital, the network will according to the former European digital commissioner Günther Oettinger 
“(…) be the backbone of our digital economies and societies worldwide” (Markman, 2018).  
The radical technological innovations that 5G enables such as AI, AR, and Internet of Things (IoT) 
is expected to enable 13.2 trillion USD of global economic output annually by 2035, generating 22.3 
million jobs in 5G’s global value chain alone (Campbell et al., 2019). 5G’s potential to enable new 
technological innovation will unlock the fourth industrial revolution, often referred to as “Industry 
4.0”, and thus be the infrastructure behind the largest industrial transformation since electronic 
automation began in the 1970’s (MD, 2018; Ross, 2019). 
Due to the global technological and economic impact that the 5G network will have, it will be critical 
for the U.S. and the PRC to successfully lead in the expansion and development of the 5G network. 
The conflict between the U.S. and the PRC is thus not merely a trade war based on economic 
incentives, but a technological rivalry for global leadership within the fields of science and technology 
(Lau, 2019). 
 
1.2. Problem statement and scope 
The U.S. and the PRC’s mutually retaliatory actions illustrates a new division in the prosperous 
technological cooperation the nations have had for several decades. If the U.S. and the PRC continues 
to engage in a conflict and deliberately counteract the development of the 5G network, the capabilities 
of the 5G network may be used for acts of evil instead of good, for example through cyberattacks and 
security threats (Halpern, 2019). Eventually, this may lead to a destabilization in the relation between 
the U.S. and the PRC, with a separation of technological, economic, social and political cooperation. 
The unstable relation between the world’s arguably most powerful nations may have detrimental 
consequences for the technological development and innovation not only for the U.S. and the PRC, 
but for the whole world (Osmos, 2020). 
The scope of the project is to specifically analyze the U.S. and the PRC’s conflict for the development 
of the 5G network. This is due to the nations conspicuous rivalry while being the world’s two largest 
national economies, and further their political differences and historic significance. Substantial 
literature has been published on the trade war and its economic consequences for the U.S. and the

2. The Historical Relation between the U.S. and 
China 
  
In this chapter, the historical relation between the U.S. and China, and later the PRC, is analyzed. The 
analysis investigates how the technological, economic, and strategic relation between the U.S. and 
the PRC has changed over time until today, in order to understand some of the underlying factors that 
have led to the present 5G rivalry between the U.S. and the PRC. 
 
2.1. The beginning of the relation – the Old China Trade 
The relation between the U.S. and China dates to shortly after the United States Declaration of 
Independence was adopted on July 4, 1776. On February 22, 1784, the U.S. vessel The Empress of 
China began its voyage to China, arriving in Guangzhou (then Canton) half a year later to trade with 
Chinese Cohong merchants under the rules of the Canton system. The system was formed by the Qing 
dynasty, which was then a global superpower with many technological inventions, as later described 
by Joseph Needham (Chang, 2018). The Canton system limited the trade from the U.S. (and other 
foreign nations) through the port of Guangzhou, creating an import-export monopoly with strict 
control over the flow of goods (Dyke, 2007). Less than a year later, Chinese trading ships began 
arriving in the U.S., marking the beginning of prominent trade affairs between the two nations 
(Monroe, 2014).  
For almost 60 years, the U.S. traded with the Chinese entirely through Guangzhou, with Chinese 
merchants trading through Baltimore. After the three-year long First Opium War between the British 
and the Chinese ended in 1842, the Canton system collapsed, opening trade routes to four additional 
Chinese ports, amongst them Shanghai, as a result of the Treaty of Wanghsia. The treaty gave the 
U.S. the best trading conditions, various privileges and a favorable status amongst the Chinese in 
comparison to other foreign nations (Chang, 2018). 
Until the ratification of the Treaty of Wanghsia in 1844, the only interaction between the U.S. and 
China had been through their trade relations. No other formal diplomatic relations between the U.S. 
==PAGE:13==
6 
 
government and the Qing dynasty had been established beforehand (Darwin, 2008). This would 
change in the following century. 
 
2.2. The century of humiliation  
Beginning in 1839 with the First Opium War and until 1949 with the establishment of the People’s 
Republic of China (PRC) by Mao Zedong, a key figure in the Communist Party of China (CPC) and 
paramount leader of the nation until his death in 1976, China entered a period of more than a century 
that has since been referred to as the “century of humiliation” by the Chinese, due to a series of events 
where several nations, especially the British, French, and Japanese Empires, intervened China and 
signed treaties, which the U.S. were part of, that, according to the Chinese, were predominately 
unequitable (Chang, 2018). 
Numerous wars were fought, amongst them the Sino-French War and the Sino-Japanese War. While 
the U.S. were not directly involved in any war with the Chinese, they were part of the Eight-Nation 
Alliance under the Boxer Rebellion in 1899-1901, who fought against the Boxers, an anti-imperialist, 
nationalistic, Chinese movement, which was partly supported by the Qing Empire (Monroe, 2014). 
In addition to the battles that were fought, numerous treaties were signed during the period. Every 
ratified treaty between the U.S. and China were a result of other nations’ military defeats of China, 
and therefore the Chinese has since regarded them collectively as the “unequal treaties”, further 
contributing to the humiliation experienced during the Qing dynasty. The treaties gave several nations 
including the U.S. the rights to concession territories in major trade centers, allowing the trade centers 
to become de facto controlled by foreign nations. This resulted in China losing tariff autonomy, which 
they had been in control of during the Old China Trade. At its peak, more than 100 treaty ports were 
in control of by foreign nations, with U.S. concession in Shanghai and Tianjin; the latter as a result 
of the 1858 Treaty of Tianjin, which in practice had the same purpose as the Treaty of Wanghsia 
(Chang, 2018).  
Throughout the period, the U.S. and the Chinese experienced hatred against each other within local 
communities, which resulted in the implementation of restricting laws and an uprising between the 
two nations. Since the mid-19th century, 100,000 Chinese people had arrived in the U.S. in a 20-year 
period during the California Gold Rush. As a result of an unfavorable view of the Chinese, the U.S. 
==PAGE:14==
7 
 
prohibited further entrance of Chinese laborer’s through several acts. Few years later, the Chinese 
began specifically boycotting U.S. businesses and products (Monroe, 2014), which harmed U.S. 
industrialists who tried to accelerate industrialization in China, in order to make money (Varg, 1968).  
While hatred was experienced within local communities, the diplomatic ties between the U.S. and 
China grew larger. In 1899, the U.S. President John Hay negotiated an “Open Door”-policy with 
pertinent nations that, like the U.S., had significant diplomatic and economic interests in China. These 
nations were almost entirely nations that had enjoyed the benefits of the numerous treaties, which 
included the British, French, and Japanese Empires. The Open Door-policy encouraged equal trade 
opportunities in China for every nation, and avoiding any nation having complete supremacy over 
China. While no major power committed to the policy, with no formal recognition through the 
ratification of treaties, the policy was still respected, until Japan invaded China on multiple occasions 
during the 1930’s (Chang, 2018). In 1937, the U.S. came to militarily support China for the first time 
in their more than 150 year relation, in the Second Sino-Japanese war, as a result of Japans continued 
aggression towards China, which was considered a breach to the Open Door-policy (Sun, 1993). 
With the fall of the Qing dynasty in 1912 and the establishment of the Republic of China (ROC) by 
the National People’s Party (NPP), the U.S. was the first nation in the world to recognize NPP as the 
legitimate government of China in 1927, when the Chinese Civil War began. The Civil War began as 
a result of the NPP and the CPC’s clash in their claim for power in China. The CPC had been 
established nine years after the foundation of the ROC and was based on the principles of Marxism-
Leninism, and thereby distinct to the democratic principles of the NPP (Chang, 2018). 
Under World War II, the U.S. government aided the NPP regime with monetary aid, improving the 
relations between the U.S. and the ROC. Additionally, the U.S. abolished 60 years of immigration 
exclusion of the Chinese. The U.S. continued to aid the NPP, and despite U.S. efforts to negotiate a 
deal between the NPP and the CPC through the U.S. Army Observation Group, the NPP fled to 
Taiwan as a result of the CPC’s and Mao Zedong’s victory in the civil war in 1949, which led to the 
formation of the PRC on the Chinese mainland the same year. The U.S. continued to recognize NPP 
and the ROC as the legitimate government of China, which would affect the relation between the U.S. 
and China for years to come (Service, 1971). 
While the century of humiliation was not the sole reason for communism to succeed in China, it 
helped the CPC gain a large following, due to the negative outlook on the influence foreign nations 
had had since the First Opium War (Chang, 2018).

2.3. Relation after the formation of the People’s Republic of China 
The relation between the U.S. and China, under the leadership of the NPP, had been one of 
cooperation until 1949, with the U.S. supporting the NPP economically and militarily. However, in 
less than a few years after the establishment of the PRC and with the CPC’s control of mainland 
China, the U.S. embassy and officials had to flee from mainland China to the ROC in Taiwan, where 
the NPP had gained control. Shortly after, both the U.S. and the PRC seized all assets from each other, 
with the U.S. initiating the conflict as a result of the PRC’s support of North Korea in the Korean 
War (Redick, 1973). The U.S. did not initially declare their support of the ROC in the event of a war 
between the ROC and the PRC (Truman, 1950). This changed after the PRC’s involvement in the 
Korean War, and their closer ties to the Soviet Union, which resulted in the U.S. now officially 
declaring that they would protect the ROC and Taiwan in the case of an invasion. This was made 
possible through the Formosa Resolution of 1955 and the Sino-American Mutual Defense Treaty, 
which was effective until 1979 (Barnouin & Yu, 2007). In the immediate aftermath of the PRC’s 
control of mainland China, fear of communism influence later known as McCarthyism became 
widespread in the U.S., with anti-communist propaganda warning how communist sympathizers and 
spies were infiltrating the U.S. government. Several government officials were accused of “losing 
China to the communists” (Fried, 1990). Until 1971, the relation between the U.S. and the PRC 
remained hostile, with mutual embargo on trade, no diplomatic ties, and the U.S. maintaining their 
denunciation of the PRC as the legitimate government of mainland China (Monroe, 2014).  
The 22 years of uniform hostility changed during a series of events in 1971 where the relation between 
the U.S. and the PRC began to improve, mostly as a result of PRC’s frictions with the Soviet Union, 
which opened for opportunities to improve the diplomatic ties between the U.S. and the PRC. This 
eventually culminated with the unprecedented visit of Richard Nixon to the PRC in 1972, which was 
the first visit of a U.S. president to the PRC since the formation in 1949. The visit immediately 
improved the diplomatic relation between the two nations, which they pledged to strengthen through 
the issue of the Shanghai Communiqué. The Communiqué sought a normalization in the relation by 
ending the mutual embargo on trade, however, no concrete actions were planned to improve the 
economic relation. Most importantly, the statement led to the U.S. recognizing the “One-China 
policy”, and thereby acknowledging that there could not be two governments, the CPC and the NPP, 
leading mainland China. However, the U.S. did not specifically recognize the CPC and thus the PRC 
as the leading power in China. Foreign relations were also discussed, and it was agreed that the U.S. 
==PAGE:16==
9 
 
would reduce their support to the NPP; a step the U.S. already had begun during the 1960’s due to 
the ROC’s high economic growth. In the following year, Liaison Offices were established in Beijing 
and Washington, D.C. (Macmillan 2008). 
In 1976, Premier Zhou Enlai and Chairman Mao Zedong, the PRC’s two most powerful leaders, 
which had been in control since the formation in 1949, died. Shortly after, Deng Xiaoping became 
the paramount leader of the PRC (Monroe, 2014). With Jimmy Carter entering presidency in 1977, 
the U.S. and the PRC finally established normalization with the U.S. formally recognizing the PRC, 
and thereby ending the recognition of the ROC, in accordance with the One-China policy. The 
recognition was put into force through the Joint Communiqué on the Establishment of Diplomatic 
Relations on January 1, 1979, which was a restatement of the Communiqué issued seven years prior. 
While the U.S. shifted its diplomatic relations from the ROC to the PRC, the Taiwan Relations Act 
was signed into law few months after, allowing the U.S. to maintain commercial, cultural, and other 
relations with the ROC without diplomatic relations. Although the U.S. withdrew all military 
personnel from the ROC in Taiwan, and thereby ending the Sino-American Mutual Defense Treaty, 
the U.S. maintained close ties to Taiwan as the act stated that the U.S. “(…) shall provide Taiwan 
with arms of a defensive character and shall maintain the capacity of the United States to resist any 
resort to force or other forms of coercion that would jeopardize the security, or social or economic 
system, of the people of Taiwan.” (96th Congress, 1979). 
The technological and economic development that the PRC achieved in the years following the 
signing of the Communiqué would mark a stark contrast to the PRC under the leadership of Mao 
Zedong.  
 
2.4. Emerging technological partnership 
After several years with a stagnated economy in the 1970’s, the PRC, under the leadership of Deng 
Xiaoping, made an economic turnaround with the Chinese Economic Reform. The reform had the 
goal of “Four Modernizations”, including strengthening science and technology; agriculture; 
industry; and defense. The reform came within months of the renewed diplomatic relations with the 
U.S., with progressive changes of the Chinese economy from being central planned to being market 
oriented. The changes included less state ownership and the allowance of private businesses as well 
as opening the Chinese market to foreign investment. Due to the stark contrast the market policy had 
==PAGE:17==
10 
 
compared to the central planned policy under Mao, the policies became known as “socialism with 
Chinese characteristics”. The PRC adopted their Open Door-policy again, which had not been in force 
since the transition of power from the NPP to the CPC in 1949. This attracted international companies, 
who saw new market opportunities in the PRC, and especially U.S. companies, who in addition to 
being limited from investing in the PRC had been restricted by the trade embargo before the 
Communiqué was signed (Brandt & Rawski, 2008). The reforms had an immediate effect on the 
Chinese economy, with continued high annual GDP growth rates in the following years, as can be 
seen on figure 1.

and toys, which later led to the PRC being known as “the world’s factory”. On the contrary, the U.S.

(Brandt & Rawski, 2008). In 1985, the U.S. and the PRC had an equal trade balance, with U.S. exports

With the new economic reform into place and with official diplomatic relations between the U.S. and 
the PRC, Deng Xiaoping made a state visit to the U.S. in 1979, being the first paramount leader to do 
so. The first bilateral accord to be signed by the U.S. and the PRC was the U.S.-China Science and 
Technology (S&T) Cooperation Agreement in 1979, which “(…) agreed that the realms of science 
and engineering provided a natural common ground upon which the two nations could build mutual 
trust and broader bilateral relations” (Weiss, 2011). The agreement was the first time in history that 
the U.S. and the PRC came together to collaborate within the fields of science and technology. The 
S&T agreement was driven by concerns about the Soviet Union’s power and its use, dating back to 
the Sputnik crisis, which had also predominately been a key incentive for the U.S. and the PRC to 
establish diplomatic ties initially. With the S&T agreement the U.S. wanted to create a network of 
relations with the PRC, and thereby several bilateral government S&T programs were established. 
Since its formation, more than 30 bilateral programs have existed between the U.S. and the PRC, 
covering a wide array of fields including energy; nuclear safety; basic research; earth, atmospheric, 
and marine sciences; standards and metrology; agriculture; public health; and environmental 
protection. The most notable U.S. engagement with the PRC was the U.S. Department of Energy 
(DOE) cooperation with the Chinese Academy of Sciences (CAS) on the agreement for cooperation 
in high-energy physics and nuclear fusion, the former signed in 1979 and the latter in 1983. Other 
engagements included the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) protocol for cooperation 
in public health, biomedical research, health care, and health policy with the PRC’s Ministry of 
Health, as wells as the U.S. Geological Service (USGS) cooperation in water resources, earth 
sciences, mapping, and metals with CAS – both protocols dating to the establishment of the S&T 
agreement in 1979 (Suttmeier, 2014). 
The S&T agreement did not only establish a technology-driven government-to-government 
collaboration between the U.S. and the PRC, but also laid the foundation for technology exchange 
through commercial interactions and academic relations, creating three pillars that both the U.S. and 
the PRC would mutually benefit from, as seen on figure 2.

2.5. Worsening relation between the U.S. and the PRC 
In the period following the collaboration on the S&T agreement, the U.S. and the PRC continued 
their good relations, with frequent state visits, prosperous trade and investment, and further bilateral 
collaboration; some of them as part of the S&T agreement. The U.S. and the PRC held a third 
Communiqué in 1982, where the U.S. reaffirmed that they would cut back on sales of military 
equipment to the ROC in Taiwan, however, the U.S. went on, as they had done three years prior, to 
form a new foreign policy together with the ROC. With the Six Assurances policy, the U.S. reassured 
that they would still support the ROC, although former diplomatic ties had been cut (Suettinger, 
2003). While the U.S. and the PRC continued to have disputes over Taiwan, the relation between the 
U.S. and the PRC through the 1980’s was robust, with the U.S. and the PRC engaging in strategic 
and military alliances, eventually resulting in U.S. arms sale to the PRC in 1985 (Dumbaugh & 
Grimmnett, 1986).  
While the relation between the U.S. and the PRC was the best it had ever been by 1989 (Suttinger, 
2003), the Tiananmen Square protests in 1989 revealed how vulnerable and divergent the relation 
still was. While the goal of the protest was intricate, the protests were against the corruption present 
in the CCP and the lack of freedom and democracy within the Chinese mainland. The protests were 
deemed a threat to the communist government, who killed hundreds to thousands of protestors, which 
led to international condemnation of the CCP’s response to the protests. The U.S. responded to the 
events by, amongst others, suspending development activities in the PRC, imposing economic 
sanctions, and suspending arms sales to the PRC, which is still in effect to this day (Peck et al., 2019).  
The years prior to the protests had seen movements of liberalization in the PRC, and the likelihood 
of a democratic Chinese mainland was considered possible following the ten years of reforms and 
==PAGE:21==
14 
 
international collaboration since 1979, which had effectively opened the PRC to the world. However, 
in the years following the protests, the PRC further restricted freedom of speech, took greater control 
over media channels, and cancelled or delayed several reforms, amongst them market reforms. The 
PRC also invested heavily into their military and police and haltered their progression towards a more 
democratic nation in the following years by taking more control over its people (Suettinger, 2003). 
As a result of the international sanctions, the PRC’s economic growth rate was reduced to 4% in 1989 
and 1990 compared to an average annual economic growth of 12% for the past five years (World 
Bank, 2019). However, after initially imposing tighter control over the economy and more 
government centralization, the government of the PRC continued their market reforms which gave 
impetus to the Chinese economy, resulting in continued growth throughout the 1990’s. The relation 
between the U.S. and the PRC continued to be hostile, which culminated in 1995 with continued 
tensions in the Taiwan strait, as the U.S. and the ROC perceived the PRC’s military activities in the 
strait as a threat towards the Taiwanese people (Suettinger, 2003).  
Despite a temporary suspension of the S&T agreement immediately following the Tiananmen Square 
protests, the collaboration between the U.S. and the PRC continued to develop further throughout the 
1990’s in contempt of the tensions in government-to-government relations, with a rapid increase in 
foreign direct investment technology transfers between the U.S. and the PRC (Suttmeier, 2014). 
Despite the continued collaboration within the S&T agreement, the U.S. created the Cox committee 
in 1998 due to concerns of the PRC abusing strategic technologies to strengthen the PRC’s nuclear 
arsenal and missile capabilities that could be a threat to the U.S. The U.S. alleged that they had proof 
that serious violations had taken place, while the PRC denied the allegations. The continued 
increasing military spending from the PRC since the Tiananmen Square protests also worried the 
U.S., and in a few years, the belief that the cooperative technological relation between the U.S. and 
the PRC would result in mutual benefits had been challenged (Suettinger, 2003). 
 
2.6. The PRC’s first innovation strategy and economic growth 
In 2000, the U.S.-China Relations Act was signed, granting the PRC permanent normal trade relations 
with the U.S., as a result of the PRC’s membership in the World Trade Organization (WTO). Due to 
the PRC’s continued economic growth and the realization that the purpose of the initiatives formed 
under the government-to-government S&T agreements had become increasingly political and 
==PAGE:22==
15 
 
diplomatic, the Strategic Economic Dialogue (SED) and its successor the Strategic and Economic 
Dialogue (S&ED) was formed in 2006 (Suttmeier, 2014). Under both programs, 91 cooperative 
agreements were established between the U.S. and the PRC, mostly of economic and geopolitical 
character. The agreements also covered several dialogues that sought technological cooperation, 
amongst them the Cooperation on Energy and the Cooperation on Environmental Protection (U.S. 
Department of State, 2013). In the same year, the PRC introduced the “Medium-to-Long-Term Plan 
(MLP) for the Development of Science and Technology”, which presented innovation as a national 
priority for the PRC, which should be achieved by investing heavily in R&D in order to promote 
national innovation capabilities. The MLP addressed four challenges that were present in the PRC’s 
science and technology development: 
1) Innovation and commercial technologies are weak despite rapid economic expansion 
2) The national technological capabilities cannot meet the national needs 
3) Non-satisfactory technological innovation to strengthen national defense 
4) Lack of scientific knowledge being retained within the nation’s borders 
Common for all four challenges was the need for national innovation capabilities to transform the 
PRC from being the manufacturing center of the world, with dependence on foreign innovative 
technologies in exchange for market opportunities, to become the world’s bastion for innovation (Cao 
et al., 2006). The MLP gave concerns regarding investments and trade from the U.S., who also viewed 
the plan as a threat to the U.S. leadership in research and innovation (Suttmeier, 2014). 
In 2006, the PRC became the second largest trade partner of the U.S., and two years later became the 
largest holder of U.S. debt (Monroe, 2014). The concern of the PRC manipulating its currency by 
devaluating the Chinese Yuan against the U.S. Dollar, in order to boost the export of goods, and 
thereby economic growth, had been prevalent in the U.S. since the 1990’s (Brandt & Rawski, 2008). 
The former U.S. president Barack Obama claimed that the PRC’s trade surplus with the U.S. was 
directly related to the PRC’s currency manipulation (Palmer, 2008). By 2007, the U.S. trade deficit 
with the PRC had become 258 billion USD, with U.S. exports to the PRC more than five times smaller 
than U.S. imports from the PRC (Census, 2020). To keep export prices low, the PRC has continued 
to buy U.S. treasury’s, contributing to the U.S. debt to the PRC, which as of 2019 was 1.09 trillion 
USD (Amadeo, 2020). Although the U.S. Dollar – Chinese Yuan exchange rate has increased 
significantly since 1990, the fluctuations for the past 15 years have been minimal, as seen on figure

2.7. Present relation between the U.S. and the PRC 
In 2007, the global financial crisis emerged, which had an immense impact on the world’s economy. 
Despite negative growth rates in 2008 and 2009 in the U.S., as well as in most nations across the 
globe, the PRC experienced an almost unaffected growth rate, maintaining an economic growth of 
more than 9% in both 2008 and 2009 (World Bank, 2019). A year later in 2010, the PRC became the 
world’s second largest economy after the U.S. (Monroe, 2014).  
A formal agreement to extend the decades of collaboration between the U.S. and the PRC in the S&T 
agreement was signed during the Obama administration in 2011, to further build upon the

well as both nations by 2010 becoming each other’s main partner in scientific collaboration as 
measured by co-authored scientific research papers (Suttmeier, 2014). Furthermore, the PRC 
remained unsatisfied with the continued U.S. arms sale to Taiwan (McGegor, 2013).  
In 2015, the PRC announced the “Made in China 2025” strategy, which was a further development 
of the MLT innovation plan from 2006. The strategy seeks to resolve the problems identified under 
the MLP strategy, by making the PRC the world’s leading innovative nation, especially within high-
tech fields such as information and communications technology (ICT), AI, and IoT (Peck et al., 2018). 
Furthermore, the plan seeks to make the PRC entirely self-sufficient, which is a further departure 
from the collaborative partnership which was the intention of the S&T agreement (Milligan, 2019). 
The guideline of the Made in China 2025 strategy pledges that the PRC will be an innovative nation 
by 2020, an international leader in innovation by 2030, and a world powerhouse of scientific and 
technological innovation by 2050 (Xinhua, 2016). While the MLP addressed the unsatisfactory 
innovation capabilities of the PRC in 2006, the Made in China 2025 strategy’s pledge to make the

3. The Technology, Possibilities and Potential of 5G 
 
In this chapter, the technology behind 5G is analyzed. The increased connectivity and the underlying 
risks of the 5G network is revealed. Additionally, the technological possibilities and the economic 
potential of the 5G network is evaluated, and the U.S. and the PRC’s current 5G development is

3.1. 5G – the catalyst for the changing relations 
In recent years information and communications technology (ICT) have become highly involved in 
the rivalry between the U.S. and the PRC. In 2018, the U.S. accused the PRC’s 5G development of 
creating implications for the U.S. economy and security, further declaring the successful deployment 
of 5G a matter of national competitiveness (Peck et al., 2018). The PRC has also identified a strong 
ICT network as a national priority in the Made in China 2025 innovation strategy, with the national 
objective of integrating ICT and industry in order to accelerate the development of the PRC’s global 
innovation capabilities (Ma et al., 2018). 
After more than a year of contemplating blocking U.S. companies from buying equipment from 
foreign ICT companies that pose significant national security risks (Shepardson et al., 2019b), the 
U.S. under the leadership of president Donald Trump first signed the “Executive Order on Securing 
the Information and Communications Technology and Services Supply Chain” on May 15, 2019, 
which listed the concerns of exploitation of the vulnerabilities in the ICT network by foreign 
adversaries and declared the threat of exploiting the vulnerabilities a national emergency. The 
pervasive concerns related to ICT included its ability to store and communicate vast amounts of 
sensitive information, its role in the digital economy, and being the network behind critical 
infrastructure such as healthcare services. The ability to exploit these ICT attributes to commit 
malicious cyber-enabled actions were deemed a threat to the U.S people and the U.S. economy, and 
thereby the actions of “(…) any acquisition, importation, transfer, installation, dealing in, or use of 
any information and communications technology or service” were prohibited from companies or 
==PAGE:29==
22 
 
states defined as foreign adversaries by the U.S. (Trump, 2019a). The complete Executive Order is 
listed in Appendix B. 
While the PRC and Chinese companies were not directly referred to as a foreign adversaries in the 
Executive Order nor in the Message to Congress (Trump, 2019b), within two days, the Trump 
administration forbade Huawei from trading with U.S. suppliers and restricted domestic networks of 
using Huawei equipment (Shepardson et al., 2019a), listing concerns about how Huawei could build 
backdoors in its 5G equipment to provide the PRC government access into U.S. networks (Peck et 
al., 2019). Additionally, the U.S. banned hardware manufacturers and leading AI companies from the 
PRC (Yang, 2020). In April 2020, the U.S. tightened restrictions on the export of technology to the 
PRC, requiring U.S. companies to get licenses in order to sell to Chinese companies. The additional 
restriction came as a result of the concern that the supply of U.S. technology such as semiconductors, 
telecommunications equipment, sensors and electronic devices in general could be used to strengthen 
the Chinese military. Moreover, a ban was imposed on any exports to Chinese companies with 
connection to the Chinese military, irrespective of them being in the technology sector or not (Klein, 
2020). The ban of Huawei has had an impact on Huawei, with Huawei blaming the U.S. ban for 12 
billion USD of lost revenue in 2019 alone (Barrett, 2020). 
As explained in Chapter 2.7, the U.S. technology, especially the hardware such as semiconductors 
and chips, is critical for the PRC in order to build the 5G network, and therefore the PRC has been 
forced to be self-sufficient in making the necessary components for their 5G network (Yang, 2020). 
Being self-sufficient has been one of the primary technological objectives of the PRC since the MLP 
in 2006 and in the current Made in China 2025 strategy; to improve the national technological

companies like Apple and the ICT companies Qualcomm and Cisco on a list of “unreliable entities” 
(Vallejo, 2020).  
From cooperation in 1979 with the signing of the S&T agreement and the trade of sensitive 
technology such as military equipment in the 1980’s, the retaliatory tariffs on technological 
equipment and the eventual ban on equipment specifically required for the 5G network is irrefutable 
evidence on how important the 5G network is for the U.S. and the PRC. According to the World 
Economic Forum, the fourth industrial revolution will only be fully realized by using 5G networks, 
and therefore it is critical for the U.S. and the PRC to capture the potential of the 5G technology, 
which will be the required network infrastructure behind technological innovations such as Artificial

3.2. The technology behind 5G 
5G is the fifth generation of wireless technology that functions on digital mobile networks; 
communication networks where the last link is wireless. The first wireless type of mobile technology 
began with pre-cellular mobile radio telephones, often used mounted on briefcases, cars and trucks. 
This technology has since been referred to as the “zeroth generation” system, or 0G. Since then, in 
the early 1980’s, 1G introduced mobile voice calls through analog telecommunications using radio 
signals. In the early 1990’s, 2G introduced Short Message Service (SMS) through digital 
telecommunications, as opposed to analog, which allowed the transfer of data. With 3G arriving in 
the early 2000’s, wireless web browsing became possible, however, it was first used at a wider scale 
with the launch of 4G in 2009, which introduced higher data speed (Bartlett, 2019).  
As the world is consuming more data every year, global data traffic is doubling every 18 months, as 
can be seen on figure 6. If the world does not transition from 4G to 5G powered wireless networks, 
the increased data traffic would lead to a congestion of the spectrum bands, leading to breakdowns in 
the wireless network. 4G networks will simply not be able to keep up with the demand for data 
(Hovgaard, 2020). 5G uses new radio technology, utilizing super high frequency spectrum bands with 
much bigger bandwidth in the 2 GHz to 6 GHz spectrum, which can satisfy the connectivity needs of 
a world with increased data traffic (Wheeler, 2019). In order to use super high frequency spectrum 
bands, new infrastructure is required. This is opposed to the existing frequencies used on the radio 
spectrum for the 4G network, which was also used for the 3G network (Arrow, 2019). 
 
==PAGE:31==

Figure 6: Global wireless data traffic and year-on-year growth (EB – Exabyte, 1018 byte). Figure

The super high frequency spectrum bands will give 5G the capacity for the connectivity needs, which 
is also referred to as the bandwidth of the network. The bandwidth is the maximum amount of data 
that the wireless digital network, provided by an ICT telecommunications service provider, can 
transfer from one point to another. The bandwidth can best be described as a pipe – the wider it is, 
the more data can be transferred at one time. In this case, 5G’s “pipe”, or capacity, is expected to 
grow to up to 10 Gigabits per second (Gbps), more than 100 times faster than the average 4G speed. 
Through a greater use of the radio spectrum, 5G will thus allow far more devices to access the wireless 
network at the same time at a higher speed, with congestion no longer being an issue, with up to a 
million devices per square kilometer being able to access the network (Vella, 2019). In addition to 
the high capacity, 5G will reduce the time it takes for data to travel from one point to another, also 
called the latency or the response time, to an absolute minimum. Using the pipe analogy again, latency 
can be paraphrased to how quickly data is moving through the pipe. In conclusion, 5G will be able to 
transfer a lot more data faster (by means of the higher bandwidth capacity) with less time delay (by 
==PAGE:32==
25 
 
means of the lower latency) (Hill & Beaton, 2020). A comparison of the capacity, speed and latency 
(hereinafter referred to as the “connectivity”) metrics for 3G, 4G and 5G can be seen on figure 7.

5G will introduce wireless technology on a primarily Software Defined Network (Ahmad et al., 
2020), gradually replacing the hardware components of the 4G network and thereby virtualizing the 
network. The 5G network will require hardware commodity components in the form of small-cell 
cellular towers, and while initially some 5G networks will be using cables, the core network functions 
will operate through the cloud for data storage of the transmitted data traffic, eventually replacing the 
need for cables. Thus, the network is largely software based, which will require more software 
updates and less hardware replacements compared to the 4G network (Wheeler, 2019). While the 5G 
network will require less hardware components, five times as many cellular towers are necessary, due 
to the higher bandwidth (Harris, 2018). The network-related capital expenditures for the new 
infrastructure for ICT companies is thus expected to increase by 60% from 2020 to 2025, due to 
installation costs for new cellular towers (Grijpink et al., 2018). Due to the 5G networks connectivity, 
it may nevertheless eventually replace the need for cabled broadband (Hasan, 2020), and thereby the 
additional cost of cellular towers may be outweighed by the reduced cost for cabled broadband. 
The next generation of wireless technology, 6G, is expected to be released in 2030, following the 
trend of around ten years between the release of a new generation of wireless technology. Until then,

software defined network provides both strengths and weaknesses to the 5G network. Despite the 
cybersecurity opportunities and risks, the potential of the 5G network is unprecedented.

3.4. The potential of the 5G network 
While 5G may be associated mostly with smartphone usage at a consumer level, 5G’s pivotal role 
lies in its potential to digitize and thus transform the industrial sector through the greater connectivity 
capabilities (Arias et al., 2020). As 5G will give the industrial sector the required connectivity, the 
5G network can succinctly be described as the required infrastructure to transform industries into the 
fourth industrial revolution (Prasad, 2016; MD, 2019), also called Industry 4.0, as can be seen on 
figure 8.

The capacity of the 5G network will allow industries, that were previously limited by 4G’s bandwidth, 
the faster transfer of data with no risk of congestion due to the higher capacity. Most importantly 
however, the low latency will allow real-time response of data between digital devices without human 
interaction, commonly known as the Internet of Things (IoT). In industries, this will enable intelligent 
==PAGE:36==

automation, as the 5G network will connect devices through the IoT, which is referred to as industry 
convergence. Thereby it will be possible to merge information technology (IT) such as computers 
and operational technology (OT) such as machines (Prasad, 2016). The higher connectivity will allow 
intelligent machines (a combination of IT and OT) to receive data input from for example customer 
orders or production data, and thereby improve manufacturing, production efficiency and visualize 
operational performance through dashboards. Furthermore, the low latency will make 5G capable of 
transferring large amounts of data quickly, which will enable real-time AI decision-making (MD, 
2018). This will make it possible to link and control robots and medical devices with industrial 
equipment (Prasad, 2016). Additionally, the minimal response time will provide autonomous vehicles 
with a continuous stream of data delivering information instantly, making it possible to roll out safe 
fleets of autonomous vehicles (Ericsson & Little, 2019). Thereby, 5G’s greatest possibilities lies in 
unlocking IoT and intelligent automation, which will be revolutionary for data-driven industries, 
smart cities and infrastructure management since it will be possible to have several devices working 
reliably, securely and uninterrupted in the same area (Vella, 2019). Other examples of application 
possibilities are autonomous robotics, which has a significant potential within manufacturing, or 
enhanced video services, which provides diverse opportunities for the healthcare sector (Eldred et al., 
2019). The possibilities are endless, with potential to transform the way societies work, as the low 
latency will allow remote control of robots, which could be applied for controlling factory robots 
while being in a different country, and even allowing remote surgery (Vella, 2019). The 5G networks 
possibilities within Energy and utilities; Healthcare; and Public Safety demonstrates 5G’s potential

3.5. The U.S. and the PRC’s 5G development 
While the 5G network is still in its early stages, the U.S. and the PRC has already chosen different 
approaches of how to deploy it. In the PRC, leading internet platforms have taken equity stakes in 
state-controlled operators, while state-owned telecommunications companies have been cooperating 
with Huawei, the country’s largest distributor of the 5G networks equipment, to test network slicing 
in industries such as AR, autonomous vehicles, and smart manufacturing (Eldred et al., 2019). The 
indirectly nationalized 5G network distributers in the PRC, with Huawei and ZTE in the forefront 
being the PRC’s largest ICT companies (Aw, 2019), are cooperating with the PRC’s state-run 
approach on how to achieve nationwide 5G connectivity.  
Despite an increasing interest from some U.S. politicians to nationalize the U.S. 5G network in order 
for the U.S. to win the 5G race against the PRC (Lee, 2020), the Trump administration has opposed 
the idea of nationalizing the U.S. 5G network, with Trump claiming that “leading through the 
government, it won’t be nearly as good, nearly as fast” (Shepardson et al., 2019b). Despite these 
statements, Donald Trump signed into law the “National Strategy to Secure 5G of the United States 
of America” in March 2020, which stated that 5G will be a primary driver for the U.S. prosperity in 
the 21st century. The strategy is focused on facilitating domestic 5G rollout; assessing risks to & 
identify core security principles of 5G infrastructure; address risks to U.S. economic and national 
security during development and deployment of 5G infrastructure worldwide; and promote 
responsible global development and deployment of 5G cybersecurity risk (Trump, 2020).  In addition 
to the strategy, the U.S. has also pushed the U.S. ICT industry to build an alternative to Huawei’s 
equipment, with Senate lawmakers in January 2020 pushing the federal government towards creating 
a 1 billion USD fund to help develop the technology to outcompete Huawei domestically, but also to 
strengthen the U.S. development of the 5G network internationally. If the strategy does not work, the 
U.S. hopes for Nordic ICT companies Ericsson and Nokia to help the U.S. succeed in their strategy 
to limit the PRC’s deployment of the 5G network internationally, due to the alleged security risk of 
Huawei’s 5G equipment (Statt, 2020).

4. Methodology 
 
In this chapter, the methodology that lays the foundation for the analysis in the project will be 
explained. The methodological approach, data collection methods, and evaluation of the 
methodological choices are presented, in order to understand what was done in the writing process, 
and how it was done. The strengths and weaknesses of the methodological choices will be evaluated, 
and additional approaches will be discussed. The methodology outlines the approaches taken for the 
project in general and for the literature review in Chapter 3: The Historical Relation between the U.S. 
and China and Chapter 4: The Technology, Possibilities and Potential of 5G.  
 
4.1. Methodological approach 
The main objective of the analysis performed in the project has been to answer the research question. 
To do so, the methodological approach for the project has been a mixed-methods research, in which 
both qualitative and quantitative data has been collected. As no primary data in the form of interviews, 
observations, experiments or surveys has been performed as part of the methodological approach, all 
data has been collected from existing work, and is thereby secondary data.  
The qualitative data used in the project has been collected from reports, research papers, academic 
journals, online articles, books, and governmental legislation. The sources of the data have been both 
academic institutions, research groups, think-tanks, businesses, NGO’s, and governmental. Other 
qualitative data include videos from various platforms, although they have been used entirely as a 
background source to expand foundational knowledge and have thus not been cited directly. 
The quantitative data sources have mostly been statistics, for example from the World Bank Group, 
but also economic forecasts and performance metrics on wireless technology. No experimental data 
has been gathered, and thereby all data collected has been descriptive. 
As the project investigates a geopolitical rivalry, qualitative data has been essential to get an 
understanding of the conflict. Quantitative data has not been strictly necessary to answer the research 
question but provides insight to the scale of the conflict, for example through economic forecasts, 
==PAGE:45==
38 
 
statistics comparing the U.S. and PRC, and 5G’s potential performance metrics. It has therefore been 
included in the analysis.  
As the project revolves around a technology that is new, and a conflict that is ongoing, the validity 
and the reliability of the data sources have been evaluated. The qualitative data is mostly based on 
the respective author’s observation of the topic, while some quantitative data is based on forecasts. 
Examples include 5G’s potential in the healthcare sector, which is a qualitative prediction, and 5G’s 
global economic output in 2030, which is a quantitative forecast. While the observations and forecasts 
are based on research, data sources predicting the future has either been compared to one or more 
sources in order to confirm the validity or sourced from reports citing multiple results indicating the 
claim. The rationale behind this research methodology has been to avoid precarious predictions.  
 
4.2. Data collection methods 
The data have mostly been sourced through various search engines, depending on the necessary 
overview. Initially, the search for data has been performed through search engines such as DTU 
Findit, Google, Infomedia and encyclopedias to get an overview of the topic, while Google Scholar 
and ResearchGate, amongst others, has been utilized when greater detail has been required for the 
specific search. Researching directly on different platforms such as Springer (academic journals), 
Brookings (research papers), Financial Times (business articles), or The White House (governmental 
legislation) has also been performed, depending on the required source. Additionally, much relevant 
material has been sent or handed out to me by my supervisor.  
To determine if data have the quality to be selected as valid data for the project, sensational and brief 
data have been avoided. Furthermore, the platforms have been discussed together with my supervisor, 
and common sense through several years of research studies and knowledge about how to evaluate 
information sources from science studies have been applied. When evaluating academic journals, the 
recognized Journal Quality List by Prof. Harzing (2020) has been used to determine the quality of the 
journal, and thereby if it would be appropriate to use as data. 
Much quantitative data used in the project has been presented through figures. Some figures have 
been reconstructed to illustrate only the most important and/or relevant results for the project, or to 
==PAGE:46==

make the figures more presentable and concise. When figures have been reconstructed, it has been 
written in the figure text that the figure has been adapted and its original source. 
It has been decided to not collect data through surveys. No survey was deemed relevant for better

been performed on how industrial businesses evaluate the potential of 5G. The potential of 5G for

An interview was conducted with a telecommunications expert in order to expand the understanding

4.3. Evaluation of methodological choices 
Choosing not to conduct a survey for the thesis have limited the methodological approaches taken in 
the writing of the project, however, a survey was deemed too frivolous. As other secondary sources 
have performed extensive research which lies beyond the scope and resources available for the 
project, a survey would not add any value to the main objective of answering the research question. 
Despite omitting this prevalent research method, the strengths of applying a mixed-methods research 
and analyzing both qualitative and quantitative data from secondary sources outweighs the use of 
adding a method which contributes no value besides being an additional methodological approach 
taken. 
As most of the content in the project is in the form of a literature review, the balance between 
gathering data and writing about the research has been divided into different phases throughout the 
project’s duration. Initially, absorbing the knowledge about the historical relation between the U.S. 
and the PRC has been necessary, and understanding the means for the rivalry. Understanding the data 
and how it relates to the research question before performing the analysis has been prevalent for most 
of the data in the project, and thereby most of the writing has been completed when the essential data 
had been read and analyzed. Additionally, as the project encompasses a single technology but within

5.1. Underlying factors leading to the rivalry 
According to Pillsbury (2016), it has always been part of the PRC’s strategy to replace the U.S. as 
the global superpower. Pillsbury (2016) argues that the PRC is amid a 100-year marathon, which 
started at the foundation of the PRC in 1949, where the PRC plans to become the global superpower 
by 2049. While this may be a precarious claim, the PRC directly states in their Made in China 2025 
strategy that they will be a powerhouse of scientific and technological innovation by 2050, after 
becoming an international leader in innovation by 2030 (Xinhua, 2016).  
The rivalry between the U.S. and the PRC may go as far back as the century of humiliation. Pillsbury 
(2016) and Chang (2018) describes how the PRC still has a negative view of western powers based 
on many of the unequal treaties that were signed during the period from the mid-19th century to the 
mid-20th century, and the unequitable trade policies that were used against China, which became 
detrimental for the Chinese. Thereby, it is part of the PRC’s plan to become a superpower as they 
were under the Qing Dynasty, to restore past greatness. The unfavorable view of the U.S. and other 
Western nations following the century of humiliation is still present among communist nationalists 
in the PRC today, with policymakers in Beijing believing that international law is merely an 
instrument to keep the PRC in check and vulnerable (Chang, 2018). 
In the U.S., McCarthyism may still be present, with the U.S. still displaying a negative view of the 
PRC’s communist government, and especially towards the increased power the PRC is acquiring 
(MSC, 2020).  
Additionally, the rivalry between the U.S. and the PRC is also to a great extent related to the U.S. 
support of the NPP and the ROC in Taiwan. The U.S. was the first nation globally to recognize the 
==PAGE:50==
43 
 
ROC. This has impaired the relation between the U.S. and the PRC, as the U.S. has continued to 
diplomatically, economically and militarily support the ROC, which the PRC claim should be under 
their control. The relation between the U.S. and the ROC has only become stronger after Trump has 
entered presidency (Peck et al., 2019), which has further fueled the rivalry between the U.S. and the 
PRC.  
Furthermore, in terms of the economic conflict, the years following the Tiananmen Square protests 
saw a steep increase in the USDCNY exchange rate, as seen on figure 3. The U.S. still claims that the 
PRC are manipulating the exchange rate, further contributing to the unequal trade practices, which 
according to the U.S. has created the large trade deficit (WHOOTAMP, 2018).  The U.S. may realize 
that if they give impetus to the PRC’s growth of the 5G network, the large trade deficit may increase 
even more. 
Generally, the turning point for the relation between the U.S. and the PRC is suggested by Suttmeier 
(2014) to have been after the Tiananmen Square protests. The protests led to a temporary suspension 
in the relation between the U.S. and PRC, and the years that followed led to growing mistrust and 
tensions between the two nations. The Cox Committee from 1998 is evidence of the mistrust and 
tensions. The mistrust and tension have changed the cooperation between the U.S. and the PRC, 
which can be seen through the S&T agreement, as will be discussed in the next chapter. 
 
5.2. Changes in the science and technology agreement 
The gradual changes in the collaboration under the S&T agreement can help to understand how the 
U.S. and the PRC’s relation has become a rivalry manifested in leading the deployment of the 5G 
network. By 1979, the mutual interests of the S&T agreement were a further incentive for economic 
growth, especially for the U.S., due to the manifest asymmetry in capabilities between the U.S. and 
the PRC. When the agreement was signed, there was no commercial competition between the U.S. 
and the PRC that could affect geopolitical interests, due to the U.S. being in every aspect more 
powerful than the PRC, and thereby the U.S. would benefit economically by seeking to collaborate 
in technology and trade with a populous nation such as the PRC. Furthermore, the U.S. used the 
academic pillar of the S&T agreement for science diplomacy and as a projection of U.S. soft power 
to the PRC. This contrasts with the PRC, which has used the S&T agreement to build on scientific 
and technological capabilities. According to Pillsbury (2016), the U.S. has been central in building 
==PAGE:51==
44 
 
the economy of the PRC, and developing the countries scientific and military capabilities, which is 
also evident from the U.S. trade of military equipment to the PRC in the 1980’s (Dumbaugh & 
Grimmnett, 1986). However, Pillsbury (2016) argues that the U.S. has been naïve to believe that the 
PRC’s growth will bring further cooperation, diplomacy and economic prosperity, as the PRC 
allegedly is mostly interested in building national capabilities to become a global superpower, as it 
has previously been historically. 
Since the S&T agreement was established in 1979, the PRC has seen an increased share of Ph.D., 
postdocs, scientists and engineers, compared to the U.S. (Suttmeier, 2014), which inevitably has 
helped the PRC muster the necessary research capabilities needed to develop and, as of now, lead the 
deployment of the 5G network, which is also reflected by the technical contributions to the 5G 
network. With the U.S. federal investment in R&D falling to the lowest point since 1955, the U.S. 
innovation capabilities to win the 5G technology race is difficult, and the strategy of the U.S. may 
not be sufficient to win the 5G race. According to Senator Mark Warner of Virginia, the U.S. has 
“(…) always steered away from industrial policy” claiming that the U.S. “need to make public-private 
investments, or government investments, in ‘democracy 5G’.” to win the 5G race (Osnos, 2020). 
The S&T agreement was, at the time, established based on mutual win-win, positive sum assumptions 
on the outcomes that would come as a result of the collaboration between the U.S. and the PRC for 
both nations, and even globally. Whether this is true for both nations today, and especially for the 
U.S., which capabilities are no longer disproportionately larger the PRC’s in terms of technological, 
academic, economic, and militarily power, is questionable. According to Suttmeier (2014), the PRC’s 
collaboration with the U.S. has “undoubtedly played a major role in the development of Chinese 
wealth and power”, listing the transfer of U.S. technologies through companies and government-to-
government programs as one of the key reasons. The threat to the U.S. security has become a major 
concern, mostly due to the Made in China 2025 strategy, but also due to the unobtrusive technological 
and economic power that has steadily risen from the PRC during the past four decades, with the PRC 
now matching the U.S. as a commercial and military competitor (Peck et al., 2019). Today, the S&T 
agreement is a collaboration between the world’s, in many aspects, most powerful nations. The 
evolution in the S&T agreement now reflects the complexities wrought by the competition and 
mistrust found in the broader relation between the U.S. and the PRC. While the U.S. and the PRC 
openly traded sensitive technology in the 1980’s, a great concern surrounding technology leakage 
and national security from both the U.S. and the PRC government has emerged in the past years. A

5.3. The rivalry understood through game theory concepts 
The initial S&T agreement’s purpose was that through cooperation in science and technology, the 
U.S. and the PRC could achieve technological advancement, scientific results, and expand their 
innovative capabilities, with mutual benefits for each nation. This was and still is the objective of the 
S&T agreement signed in 1979, which “(…) agreed that the realms of science and engineering 
provided a natural common ground upon which the two nations could build mutual trust and broader 
bilateral relations”. At the point of signing the S&T agreement, the U.S. was however, as previously 
mentioned, disproportionately more powerful both technologically and by any macroeconomic 
indicator in comparison to the PRC. Thereby, the S&T agreement was signed with common interests, 
where the bilateral S&T programs and trade between the world’s largest national economy and the 
world’s largest national population would benefit each nation technologically and economically 
without distorting the role of the U.S. as the world’s most powerful nation.  
The rapid growth of the Chinese economy over the past 40 years has nevertheless created a situation 
where the technological and economic gap between the U.S. and the PRC has been narrowing in. 
Therefore the U.S., which for now is still the dominant power, will no longer, from the standpoint of 
the position of power, benefit from cooperating economically and through bilateral S&T programs, 
as this will only help the PRC gain power. Eventually, as the PRC is projected to continue expanding 
its power within technological and economic parameters, the U.S. can anticipate losing its role as the 
world’s technological and economic superpower to the PRC.  
The considerable difference of the U.S.’ previous relation with the PRC where the PRC was 
considered an ally to the gradual process of strategic competition can be understood through the 
concepts of game theory. Through a basic model, the cooperation and competition within S&T and

other nation before making its own strategy, that is, strategies are taken simultaneously) non-constant 
sum (the sum of the payoffs is not zero) game. There are two available pure (no randomization occurs) 
strategies – to either cooperate or compete in S&T.

5.4. 5G’s radical innovation and the PRC’s security threat 
Winning the 5G race is strategically important for the U.S. and the PRC as it will give access to the 
global supply chain of 5G equipment and software. With 5G equipment and software being a 
prerequisite for Industry 4.0 and the radical industrial advancement it brings, 5G is pioneering in 
terms of the innovation it enables compared to the previous generations of wireless technology. 5G’s 
innovation is radical, as the market it addresses is new (being the industrial and public sector as 
opposed to the end-use consumer) while network slicing and the additional technological possibilities 
such as AI and IoT within manufacturing and healthcare are new technological innovations. This is 
opposed to 4G, which targeted an existing market (consumer electronics) with no new technological 
innovation, only being a performance upgrade to 3G (Arrow, 2019), as can be seen on figure 17.

5.5. The changes in comparative advantage 
The PRC has moved from being a large exporter, to allowing investments in the country, to currently 
investing massively internationally with global investments increasing from 18 billion CNY in 1997 
to 1367 billion CNY in 2019 (Trading Economics, 2020). The PRC’s economic growth and ambitions 
to become an innovative nation has allowed a shift in the comparative advantage between the U.S. 
and the PRC. The comparative advantage theory suggests that countries will engage in trade with one 
another, exporting the goods that they have a relative advantage in productivity. Historically, the 
PRC’s comparative advantage with the U.S. has been its cheap labor, as Chinese labor could produce 
simple consumer goods cheaply. The U.S. comparative advantage has been in specialized, capital-
intensive labor, who produced sophisticated commercial goods (Hayes & Barnier, 2020). However, 
due to the PRC’s greater economic capabilities stemming from 40 years of high economic growth 
and increasingly lower average cost of producing goods, the comparative advantage has shifted. 
Today, the U.S. and the PRC both export sophisticated commercial goods, as seen on figure 4, and 
thereby, the effectiveness of the comparative advantage in 1979 is not the same as of 2019. Evidently, 
the balance of power has shifted, making the relative gap in comparative advantages between the U.S. 
and the PRC smaller. Furthermore, the comparative advantage (in theory) assumes no trade barriers, 
which has increased under the trade war, with U.S. tariffs almost six times higher at present compared 
to the beginning of 2018, as seen on figure 5. Finally, the protectionist goal of self-sufficiency in the 
Made in China 2025 strategy has also skewed the comparative advantage even further. 
Despite the shifted comparative advantage, the U.S. is still superior to the PRC in terms of 
digitalization. In the IMD World Digital Competitiveness Ranking 2019 which measures countries 
based on capacity and readiness to adopt and explore digital technologies as a key driver for economic 
transformation in business, government and wider society, the U.S. are ranked number 1, while the 
PRC are ranked 22nd (IMD, 2020). This demonstrates that overall, the U.S. still have the best 
capabilities within digital technologies (the full details on the Digital Competitiveness ranking for the 
U.S. and the PRC can be seen in Appendix C). However, the PRC is the country which experienced 
the greatest leap from 2018 to 2019 in the ranking, rising from the 30th spot to the 22nd spot in one 
year. Furthermore, the PRC is ranked number 1 in terms of business agility, which determines the 
level of preparedness of the nation to assume its digital transformation – a position it raised from 
number 19 in 2018 (IMD, 2019). This suggests that although the PRC is not world leading in digital 
technologies, they have the business agility to prepare themselves to become world leading, with the 
==PAGE:63==
56 
 
large leap from 2018 to 2019 confirming the trend. If the PRC continues to lead in patents and 
technological contributions to the 5G technology, U.S. ICT companies will lose licensing and royalty 
payments, which will impact the ability of U.S. companies to reinvest in R&D, maintain brand 
recognition, and achieve economies of scale (Peck et al., 2018). This will negatively impact the 
competitiveness of the U.S., creating a downward spiral. Furthermore, leading on standards will 
enable the PRC to lead in the development in the future generations and evolutions of wireless 
technology. The PRC may thus have the capabilities in the future to gain a comparative advantage 
over the U.S. in the development of innovative technologies and sophisticated commercial goods. In 
the next chapter, it will be discussed which nation will win the 5G rivalry. 
 
5.6. Who will win the 5G race? 
In can be argued that the U.S. was too late to develop a national strategy to secure the 5G network. 
The PRC launched the Made in China 2025 strategy in 2015, which already then had plans for how 
to develop the ICT network, which would later manifest in the 5G network. In contrast, the U.S. first 
developed a national strategy in March 2020 with the “National Strategy to Secure 5G of the United 
States of America”. Until then, the U.S. had relied on the initiative of the U.S. market to develop the 
5G network, despite acknowledging that the capabilities of U.S. companies to develop network 
equipment were uncompetitive (Peck et al., 2018).  
It cannot be deduced that had the U.S. followed the approach of the PRC, then the U.S would have 
had better capabilities to develop the 5G network. However, it can be argued that the U.S. have failed 
to find a solution on how to lead in technological development, and that they since the S&T agreement 
in 1979 have failed to foresee the radical change of the PRC’s capabilities to build the 5G network. 
The rivalry for the 5G network illustrates the divergent difference between the two U.S. and the PRC, 
and how ideological the rivalry is; the conservative, capitalistic market approach of the U.S., where 
the deployment of 5G, and the strategy to win the 5G race, is mostly dependent on the markets 
initiative, versus the state-driven and state-dependent strategy of the PRC. The PRC have been 
excelling in foreseeing the deployment of the 5G network as a public good, from which the Chinese 
population would benefit from, for example through smart cities, remote surgery, and other 
advancements within the public sector. Certainly, 5G will be the infrastructure needed for the PRC to 
successfully accomplish the goals set on their MLP 2006 and their Made in China 2025 innovation 
==PAGE:64==
57 
 
strategies, and thereby, 5G has been an essential public good for the PRC to capture. The U.S. on the 
other hand has not prioritized this before March 2020, but rather focused on holding its position as 
the world’s technological and economic superpower by counteracting the strategy of the PRC. The 
U.S. has thus been caught in a position where it has failed to develop a national strategy but has also 
acknowledged a lack of competitiveness amongst its own ICT companies. In addition to this, the 
PRC’s deployment of the 5G network has been extremely competitive, with low prices and better 
quality. However, the U.S. has been effective in trying to exclude Huawei globally, as seen on figure 
18, with many nations worldwide either fully or partially excluding Huawei. The rivalry shows how 
the U.S. and the PRC through the trade war and the restrictions and bans are creating a mutually 
worse situation for both parts which is the same as the inferior Nash equilibrium as presented in the 
payoff matrix on figure 16. 
If the U.S. does not find a solution to successfully deploy the 5G network, they may choose to limit 
Huawei’s 5G equipment to less sensitive parts of the 5G network, which has been decided by the 
United Kingdom (Kelion, 2020). Limiting Huawei to less sensitive parts of the 5G network may 
however be complicated, as a May 2020 study reported that the U.S. are dependent on the PRC for 
424 separate categories of imports out of 5910 of which 114 are critical for national infrastructure, 
which are more than the strategic dependence of the PRC in Canada and the United Kingdom (Rogers 
et al., 2020).   
According to Lee (2020), in order for the U.S. to become a global leader in the deployment of the 5G 
network over the next ten years, the U.S. must adopt flexible policies for launching the 5G network 
on different parts of the spectrum, find alternatives for the 5G equipment, and create a long-term plan 
which includes more R&D spending. Whether or not this will be possible may well depend on the 
strategy of the U.S. and thereby the approach for developing the 5G network. This may become 
critical if the U.S. wants to succeed in winning in the deployment of further evolutions of wireless 
technology, including new generations such as 6G.  
The only obstacle for the PRC currently is the increasing trend of nations worldwide limiting or 
excluding them from deploying the 5G network. It is thus complicated to settle who will win the 5G 
race, as it will be very dependent on how this trend will continue in the next years. However, in terms 
of domestic deployment and domestic development and capabilities of the 5G network, the PRC are 
superior to the U.S.

Chapter 5.6. The discussion as well as the literature review previously conducted will now be used 
to answer the research question in the conclusion in the next chapter.

6. Conclusion 
 
In this project, the 5G networks relation to the present rivalry between the U.S. and the PRC has been 
analyzed. The project has achieved four different objectives, which has helped answer the research 
question, which can be seen below: 
 
What impact will the 5G network have on the development of new technologies, and how does this 
affect the relation between the U.S. and the PRC? 
 
The analysis conducted in the project suggests that the 5G network will be critical for the development 
of new technologies, and that 5G has caused a rivalry between the U.S. and the PRC. 
Based on the extensive analysis of the possibilities of the 5G network within industrial and public 
sectors, it can be concluded that the 5G network will have a profound impact on the development of 
new technologies. The 5G network will most importantly impact the development of IoT and AI, 
among other innovative technologies, which will enable Industry 4.0. Due to the 5G networks higher 
connectivity, industry convergence will be possible, allowing new possibilities such as remote 
surgery and automated vehicles. In addition to the technological possibilities, the 5G network has 
great economic potential, with projections of 13.2 trillion USD in global economic output annually 
by 2035.  
Due to the impact of the 5G network in terms of technological possibilities and economic potential, 
the analysis suggests that 5G is the single most important factor affecting the relation between the 
U.S. and the PRC. This is evident through the mutually retaliatory tariffs between the U.S. and the 
PRC, which specifically aims to target industrially significant technology. Furthermore, the changing 
relations in the S&T agreement has been affected by the 5G networks potential, eventually leading to 
a lose-lose situation between the U.S. and the PRC, where both parts try to counteract the 
development of the 5G network. The most notable case of this has been the U.S. ban of Huawei in 
2019. While it is unsure whether the U.S. or the PRC will lead the 5G race, it is certain that 5G and 
further evolutions or generations of wireless technology will be at the center of the rivalry between 
the U.S. and the PRC for years to come.

7. Reflection and Proposal for Future Research 
 
In this project, it has been chosen to specifically analyze how the 5G network will affect the relation 
between the U.S. and the PRC. In extension of the project, it would be applicable to analyze how 
other radical innovative technologies such as IoT or AI will affect the relation between the U.S. and 
the PRC. Here it would be relevant to investigate in greater detail the specific technological 
possibilities (micro perspective), as opposed to the infrastructural importance the 5G network has 
(macro perspective).  
In addition to the direct impact of technologies in the rivalry between the U.S. and the PRC, it could 
be interesting to further investigate how the historical ties relates to the rivalry between the U.S. and 
the PRC, similar to the research by Pillsbury (2016) and Chang (2018). 
While the project has specifically analyzed the rivalry between the U.S. and the PRC, it would be 
relevant to extend the analysis to include other countries which are also highly involved in the conflict 
for the deployment of the 5G network, for example the United Kingdom, South Korea or Sweden.  
Finally, it would be interesting and applicable to make a prediction for how the rivalry between the 
U.S. and the PRC will change during the next evolutions of the 5G network and the future generations 
of wireless technologies such as 6G.

for the 5G network. It would also be interesting to research how the U.S. involving the Chinese 
military as being part of the conflict will impact the political landscape between the U.S. and the PRC 
in the future.

Appendix B: Executive Order on Securing the 
Information and Communications Technology and 
Services Supply Chain 
 
In the following, the Executive Order on Securing the Information and Communications Technology 
and Services Supply Chain from U.S. president Donald Trump is presented (Trump, 2019a). 
 
Section 1.  Implementation.  (a)  The following actions are prohibited:  any acquisition, importation, 
transfer, installation, dealing in, or use of any information and communications technology or service 
(transaction) by any person, or with respect to any property, subject to the jurisdiction of the United 
States, where the transaction involves any property in which any foreign country or a national thereof 
has any interest (including through an interest in a contract for the provision of the technology or 
service), where the transaction was initiated, is pending, or will be completed after the date of this 
order, and where the Secretary of Commerce (Secretary), in consultation with the Secretary of the 
Treasury, the Secretary of State, the Secretary of Defense, the Attorney General, the Secretary of 
Homeland Security, the United States Trade Representative, the Director of National Intelligence, the 
Administrator of General Services, the Chairman of the Federal Communications Commission, and, 
as appropriate, the heads of other executive departments and agencies (agencies), has determined that: 
(i)   the transaction involves information and communications technology or services designed, 
developed, manufactured, or supplied, by persons owned by, controlled by, or subject to the 
jurisdiction or direction of a foreign adversary; and 
(ii)  the transaction: 
(A)  poses an undue risk of sabotage to or subversion of the design, integrity, manufacturing, 
production, distribution, installation, operation, or maintenance of information and communications 
technology or services in the United States; 
==PAGE:86==
79 
 
(B)  poses an undue risk of catastrophic effects on the security or resiliency of United States critical 
infrastructure or the digital economy of the United States; or 
(C)  otherwise poses an unacceptable risk to the national security of the United States or the security 
and safety of United States persons. 
(b)  The Secretary, in consultation with the heads of other agencies as appropriate, may at the 
Secretary’s discretion design or negotiate measures to mitigate concerns identified under section 1(a) 
of this order.  Such measures may serve as a precondition to the approval of a transaction or of a class 
of transactions that would otherwise be prohibited pursuant to this order. 
(c)  The prohibitions in subsection (a) of this section apply except to the extent provided by statutes, 
or in regulations, orders, directives, or licenses that may be issued pursuant to this order, and 
notwithstanding any contract entered into or any license or permit granted prior to the effective date 
of this order. 
Sec. 2.  Authorities.  (a)  The Secretary, in consultation with, or upon referral of a particular 
transaction from, the heads of other agencies as appropriate, is hereby authorized to take such actions, 
including directing the timing and manner of the cessation of transactions prohibited pursuant to 
section 1 of this order, adopting appropriate rules and regulations, and employing all other powers 
granted to the President by IEEPA, as may be necessary to implement this order.  All agencies of the 
United States Government are directed to take all appropriate measures within their authority to carry 
out the provisions of this order. 
(b)  Rules and regulations issued pursuant to this order may, among other things, determine that 
particular countries or persons are foreign adversaries for the purposes of this order; identify persons 
owned by, controlled by, or subject to the jurisdiction or direction of foreign adversaries for the 
purposes of this order; identify particular technologies or countries with respect to which transactions 
involving information and communications technology or services warrant particular scrutiny under 
the provisions of this order; establish procedures to license transactions otherwise prohibited pursuant 
to this order; establish criteria, consistent with section 1 of this order, by which particular technologies 
or particular participants in the market for information and communications technology or services 
==PAGE:87==
80 
 
may be recognized as categorically included in or as categorically excluded from the prohibitions 
established by this order; and identify a mechanism and relevant factors for the negotiation of 
agreements to mitigate concerns raised in connection with subsection 1(a) of this order.  Within 150 
days of the date of this order, the Secretary, in consultation with the Secretary of the Treasury, 
Secretary of State, the Secretary of Defense, the Attorney General, the Secretary of Homeland 
Security, the United States Trade Representative, the Director of National Intelligence, the 
Administrator of General Services, the Chairman of the Federal Communications Commission and, 
as appropriate, the heads of other agencies, shall publish rules or regulations implementing the 
authorities delegated to the Secretary by this order. 
(c)  The Secretary may, consistent with applicable law, redelegate any of the authorities conferred on 
the Secretary pursuant to this section within the Department of Commerce. 
Sec. 3.  Definitions.  For purposes of this order: 
(a)the term “entity” means a partnership, association, trust, joint venture, corporation, group, 
subgroup, or other organization; 
(b)the term “foreign adversary” means any foreign government or foreign non-government person 
engaged in a long-term pattern or serious instances of conduct significantly adverse to the national 
security of the United States or security and safety of United States persons; 
(c)the term “information and communications technology or services” means any hardware, software, 
or other product or service primarily intended to fulfill or enable the function of information or data 
processing, storage, retrieval, or communication by electronic means, including transmission, storage, 
and display; 
(d)the term “person” means an individual or entity; and 
(e)the term “United States person” means any United States citizen, permanent resident alien, entity 
organized under the laws of the United States or any jurisdiction within the United States (including 
foreign branches), or any person in the United States. 
==PAGE:88==
81 
 
Sec. 4.  Recurring and Final Reports to the Congress.  The Secretary, in consultation with the 
Secretary of State, is hereby authorized to submit recurring and final reports to the Congress on the 
national emergency declared in this order, consistent with section 401(c) of the NEA (50 U.S.C. 
1641(c)) and section 204(c) of IEEPA (50 U.S.C. 1703(c)). 
Sec. 5.  Assessments and Reports.  (a)  The Director of National Intelligence shall continue to assess 
threats to the United States and its people from information and communications technology or 
services designed, developed, manufactured, or supplied by persons owned by, controlled by, or 
subject to the jurisdiction or direction of a foreign adversary.  The Director of National Intelligence 
shall produce periodic written assessments of these threats in consultation with the heads of relevant 
agencies, and shall provide these assessments to the President, the Secretary for the Secretary’s use 
in connection with his responsibilities pursuant to this order, and the heads of other agencies as 
appropriate.  An initial assessment shall be completed within 40 days of the date of this order, and 
further assessments shall be completed at least annually, and shall include analysis of: 
(i)  threats enabled by information and communications technologies or services designed, developed, 
manufactured, or supplied by persons owned by, controlled by, or subject to the jurisdiction 
or direction of a foreign adversary; and 
(ii)  threats to the United States Government, United States critical infrastructure, and United States 
entities from information and communications technologies or services designed, developed, 
manufactured, or supplied by persons owned by, controlled by, or subject to the influence of a foreign 
adversary. 
(b)The Secretary of Homeland Security shall continue to assess and identify entities, hardware, 
software, and services that present vulnerabilities in the United States and that pose the greatest 
potential consequences to the national security of the United States.The Secretary of Homeland 
Security, in coordination with sector-specific agencies and coordinating councils as appropriate, shall 
produce a written assessment within 80 days of the date of this order, and annually thereafter.This 
assessment shall include an evaluation of hardware, software, or services that are relied upon by 
multiple information and communications technology or service providers, including the 
==PAGE:89==
82 
 
communication services relied upon by critical infrastructure entities identified pursuant to section 9 
of Executive Order 13636 of February 12, 2013 (Improving Critical Infrastructure Cybersecurity). 
(c)  Within 1 year of the date of this order, and annually thereafter, the Secretary, in consultation as 
appropriate with the Secretary of the Treasury, the Secretary of Homeland Security, Secretary of 
State, the Secretary of Defense, the Attorney General, the United States Trade Representative, the 
Director of National Intelligence, and the Chairman of the Federal Communications Commission, 
shall assess and report to the President whether the actions taken by the Secretary pursuant to this 
order are sufficient and continue to be necessary to mitigate the risks identified in, and pursuant 
to, this order. 
Sec. 6.  General Provisions.  (a)  Nothing in this order shall be construed to impair or otherwise affect: 
(i)   the authority granted by law to an executive department or agency, or the head thereof; or 
(ii)  the functions of the Director of the Office of Management and Budget relating to budgetary, 
administrative, or legislative proposals. 
(b)  This order shall be implemented consistent with applicable law and subject to the availability of 
appropriations. 
(c)  This order is not intended to, and does not, create any right or benefit, substantive or procedural, 
enforceable at law or in equity by any party against the United States, its departments, agencies, or 
entities, its officers, employees, or agents, or any other person.
