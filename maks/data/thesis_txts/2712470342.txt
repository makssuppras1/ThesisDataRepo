Abstract
Indoor climate in schools have been on the agenda for years. Multiple studies have shown, that far
too many schools indoor climate doesn’t align with recommended criteria. This impacts the students
comfort and performance. The purpose of this report was to analyze the environment in a classroom
and bedroom, both to understand the current situation - but also to get an understanding of occu-
pancy preferences and their control needs.
This was done at a boarding school. Three scenarios were measured in both environments; no occu-
pants present, occupants present - no window opening allowed and occupants present - window opening
allowed. These scenarios were measured over an hour, both in a winter and summer situation. During
the measurements temperature, relative humidity, CO2, air velocity and surface temperatures were
measured. In relation to these measurements, the students filled out surveys to express their experi-
ence of the indoor climate.
The measurements showed that the largest problem in both environments were with the fluctuating
temperatures. The bedrooms also had problems with far too high CO2 concentrations. The general
consensus throughout the surveys, were that the ability to control the indoor climate was better.
The students in general preferred the bedrooms over the classroom, even though the CO2 levels and
draught rates in the classroom, where within the recommended intervals.
The current system has potential for being optimized in multiple aspects. Such as visualising the
values so the students know when to adjust and open the windows - or automate the control aspect
further.

1
Introduction
Since most people spend the majority of their day inside, whether that be at work or at home, indoor
climate is a large part of the climate most people are exposed to on a daily basis. Many different
aspects of the indoor climate can have a negative impact on both the occupants motivation and con-
centration, but also their general health.
This is also true for school environments, which in general has been shown to be fairly poor[22]. A
majority of classrooms have far too high CO2 concentrations, due to the lack of ventilation and the
constant supply from students being inside the room. The teaching environment makes it harder for
students to learn. Most classrooms rely on natural ventilation and therefore the students or teachers
to manually handle the indoor climate.
While multiple studies have been done on regular schools to understand the classrooms indoor climate
problems, there haven’t been many studies which considers boarding schools and the two different
environments they entail. Is there actually a difference in what occupants prefer depending on the
environment they’re in?
When it comes to indoor climate and comfort there is also the question of personal preferences. De-
pending on how sensitive one is to temperature changes, draught or smells - a comfortable indoor
climate can be very varied from person to person. Ensuring the environment is optimal for all occu-
pants are often not possible.
Ventilation systems often feature in newer buildings, while older buildings rely on window opening.
While ventilation systems are designed for the space, they leave little control to the occupants. Control
can be many different things, it can be more or less automatic or fully manual. Does perceived control
actually have an impact on the perceived thermal comfort and the intervals the occupant can handle?
1.1
Purpose
The purpose is to establish if there is a difference in how occupants perceive their comfort, both based
on their level of control, but also based on their environment - here whether it’s in the classroom or
sleeping quarters.

Background
2.1
Indoor climate
In general the indoor climate, as defined by WHO (The world health organisation), consist of 5 dif-
ferent environments: thermal-, atmospheric-, acoustic-, actinic- and mechanical environment. These
environments each provide their own aspect of indoor climate. In this study the thermal and atmo-
spheric environment will be the focus points to determine how an occupant experience the indoor
climate. Thermal environment consist of factors such as temperature, air movement and humidity,
while atmospheric tackles pollution, air quality and volume of fresh air[30].
These factors can be affected by both internal and external sources. The temperature can be influ-
enced by the sun or by the heat from occupants present in the room. Actions such as window opening
will also have an effect on the environment factors by affecting the draught, humidity, air quality and
volume of fresh air in the space.
The indoor environment quality (IEQ) builds on the quality of the environment in relation to the
occupants health. Indoor environments are complex and might expose the occupants to a variety of
contaminants, which pose a health risk. Understanding the sources of contaminants and being able
to control them can help preventing building related health problems[16].
2.1.1
Atmospheric comfort
The atmospheric comfort builds largely on the indoor air quality. The quality of the air can be con-
taminated by pollutants, which can induce health effects. The primary pollution sources spread gases
or particles - the sources can be people, candles etc. The concentration of these elements can be in-
creased by having inadequate ventilation. The concentration of some pollutants can also be increased
due to high temperatures and humidity levels.
The pollutants can origin from many different sources, such as building materials, furniture, to-
bacco products, cleaning products and heating and cooling systems. Furthermore there’s also outdoor
sources, such as radon, pesticides and general air pollution as seen on Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Sources of indoor air pollution[15].

These sources can produce different levels of pollutants - things like building materials release pollu-
tants continuously, while sources related to activities produce them intermittently. Most sources can
be handled through ventilation - if the ventilation is inadequate the pollution levels can accumulate
to such levels that they pose a health and comfort problem[17].
Air quality has a large impact on human health. Normally the CO2 concentration in the air is not large
enough to be dangerous. It is utilized as a measurement to determine the air quality in rooms. Large
CO2 concentrations can act as both a stimulant and depressant if it is present in an adequate amount
of time. The CO2 level can lead to symptoms such as headache, increased heart rate, dizziness and
depression among others. It can also affect both concentration and performance negatively - which
are the most common symptoms in building environments[1].
2.1.2
Thermal comfort
Thermal comfort can be defined as ’That condition of mind that expresses satisfaction with the ther-
mal environment’. It is part of a vital survival behavior. The right thermal conditions are needed
to survive. The human body has subconscious procedures, which ensures optimal conditions. More
conscious actions, such as adapting clothing or activity level can also help adjusting the thermal com-
fort level. The most important parameter in relation to thermal comfort is peoples expectations of
thermal comfort.
Discomfort can be caused by draught - this sensation depends on the air temperature, air movement
and air turbulence. The body senses the increased cooling of the skin - which can cause discomfort.
Temperature is in general a large factor in discomfort - most of the actions consciously taken to im-
prove thermal comfort is based on the experienced temperature[29]. High humidity in hot regions is a
major discomfort problem. It prevents sweating, which in return doesn’t allow the body to cool itself
down. Radiant temperatures can also lead to discomfort, if one surface is marginally warmer than
another, the temperature difference can lead to local discomfort. This is also possible with window
opening - which can lead to draught. Higher temperatures in classrooms can have an effect on the
students ability. People can accept large temperature ranges, when they are able to adapt. This
adaption could be both in terms of activity, clothing level or control. Figure 2.2 show different types
of local discomfort and activity levels[14].
Figure 2.2: Local thermal discomfort; 1. Radiation asymmetry problem: Cooling or heating of
parts of the body by radiation. 2. Large vertical air temperature differences, causing cold feet and a
warm head. 3. Uncomfortable floor temperatures, causing hot or cold feet 4. Metabolic estimation

Through the last 20 years there has been a drastic growth in interest for thermal comfort. The building
sector is one of the largest sources for CO2 emission, with thermal comfort in general being a primary
contributor. There has been a change in thermal comfort over the last 20 years, from being driven
by physical based models such as fangers comfort model – to being based on the adaptive comfort
model[25].
PMV and PPD reduces thermal comfort to a steady-state heat balance equation that could be used
anywhere in the world. PMV stands for predicted mean vote and PDD for predicted percentage of
dissatisfied[5]. The models were created by Povl Ole Fanger and based human thermal comfort on
skin temperature and sweat, trying to define what could be comfortable by balancing the two factors.
Multiple factors affect the humans comfort level: clothing, activity, air temperature, mean radiant
temperature, air velocity and humidity.
Figure 2.3: The PMV scale
PMV is a 7 point thermal sensation scale, see Figure 2.3, equilibrium is obtained when the inter-
nal heat production is the same as its heat loss.
The thermal sensation can be perceived better
when occupants have control of the indoor temperature. There’s different methods in the standards,
which are utilized for certain types of environments. These are used to gather information for the
factors considered in PMV. Based on the PMV the PPD can be established. The purpose is to try
and please 80% of the occupants[31], though this can be different from case to case due to negotiations.
The adaptive model views occupants as a component of the comfort system – this indicates that the
natural ventilation achieves thermal comfort over a broader temperature range through occupancy
control, than HVAC systems. The adaptive comfort theory considers the optimal indoor operative
temperature. The relationship between occupants and the building and its devices is commonly ex-
pressed by: Tc = a·T0+b, where Tc is expected comfort operative temperature, T0 is outdoor reference
temperature, a is the slope and b is the y-intercept. a and b are based on statically fitted data col-
lected in field studies[26].

2.2
Ventilation principles
Ventilation is introduced to handle pollution of the air, but also to maintain a comfortable indoor
climate. This is mainly achieved by removing pollution. There are several types of ventilation systems,
which all strive to achieve the same goal - but through different strategies.
2.2.1
Mechanical ventilation
Mechanical ventilation builds on a system which transports, conditions and supply air. This air can
solely consist of conditioned outdoor air, but can also be mixed with recirculated air from the room.
The system consist of an inlet and outlet fan, which supply and exhaust the air. An air handling unit
is usually present to handle the air quality. It contains components which filters, heat or cool the air.
Mechanical ventilation can be either balanced or non balanced. Mixed ventilation is balanced and
builds on achieving a uniform distribution by diluting the polluted indoor air with fresh air. Conven-
tionally the supply is place outside the occupied zone - the fresh air is mixed locally, while the warmer
air rises upwards. By introducing engines the fresh air can be forced to mix in the occupancy zone
leading to complete mixing. Displacement ventilation builds on the air being supplied near the floor.
The supply air temperature will be marginally lower than the desired room temperature, this allows
the air to circulate in the occupied zone and be heated up by internal heat sources. When the air is
heated sufficiently it ill rise up above the occupied zone. Both ventilation principles can be seen in
Figure 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Mechanical ventilation principles
A non-balanced type is exhaust ventilation - here mechanic outlets exhaust the warm air, to create
an under pressure and let fresh air in through openings and cracks.
2.2.2
Natural ventilation
Natural ventilation uses the natural air flow, which is mainly influenced by temperature differences,
wind and pressure. It can be both manually or automatically controlled. Manual control is fairly
common in relation to window opening and this strategy is often utilized in schools.
There are different types of natural ventilation, which depends on how the airflow enters the room.
One sided ventilation has the inlet and outlet on the same side of the room. This concept relies on the
room depth maximum being 2.5 of the height. Cross flow ventilation has openings on different sides
of the room, not necessarily in the same height. The inlet is usually in the facade with the main wind
5
==PAGE:12==
pressure. The draught through the room will ensure an air change. With this concept the depth of
the room should maximum be 5 times the height of the room. Both concepts can be seen in Figure 2.5.
Figure 2.5: Natural ventilation principles
The advantages of natural ventilation is that it contributes to a sustainable building. It requires no
electrical energy. Natural ventilation also allow the occupant to feel more in control and introduce the
external environments inside, which there is evidence for that occupants prefer. Natural ventilation is
limited in terms of how much cooling it can provide - especially if the environment is also humid[21].
2.2.3
Hybrid ventilation
Mechanical and natural ventilation can be combined as hybrid ventilation.
Most commonly the
mechanical ventilation is utilized during heating season - in this period natural ventilation might
cause draught and discomfort due to the cold temperatures outside. Outside heating season, natural
ventilation can be utilized optimally, the warmer temperatures outside lowering the risk of draught. By
using natural ventilation in warmer periods, the energy cost is lowered, which is one of the advantages
of hybrid ventilation.
2.2.4
Control strategies
Control can be many things and it is important to understand what different control strategies gives
to the occupant in terms of what level of control they feel they have over their own indoor climate.
The three most common ventilation strategies are CAV, VAV and DCV. CAV (constant air volume)
builds on having a schedule for a standard situation with uniform loads. The supply and exhaust
are constant. This strategy is best suited for rooms which state are always the same. VAV (variable
air volume) utilize a schedule with built in breaks, for situations with lower loads. This strategy
is optimal for rooms which operate with the same schedule of activity at all times. DCV (demand
controlled ventilation) depends on set points, which fluctuate the air volume. The set point can be
based on temperature, CO2 concentration or humidity. This strategy is customized to the current
situation in the room.
These strategies can use different controllers - on/off is the most common type. It has no middle
stages and switches only when the set point is crossed. If the temperature keeps fluctuating around
the set point, it might be beneficial to make sure a specific amount of time passes, before the stage
can be switched. Another form of controller is the PI, which as it approach the set point decreases
6
==PAGE:13==
the power supplied. This creates a band where the controller varies the time between on/off. Outside
the band its a normal on/off controller. A PID controller builds onto the PI, by adding compensation
for changes in the system. It provides the most stable form of control[28].
None of these strategies depends a lot on the occupants actions due to them being automatic. The
occupant has the most control in relation to manual control - the occupant has to adjust the indoor
environment based on their preferences. Manual control is often associated with window opening,
which is a very common strategy used in school environments to control natural ventilation. The
manual control can be more subtle in terms of the occupant being able to adjust set points with a
manual thermostat.
Window opening can be automated with a sensor and a motor - the sensor can measure different
factors, but the most common automatic window opening devices utilizes temperature sensors. This
type of installation is usually not utilized in a school environment, unless the windows are at a height
where occupants cannot reach them - such as ceiling windows.
In most cases automatic systems needs to be checked to ensure they are running optimally. The
systems are often maintained by one person, who in some cases manually adjust parts of the system,
depending on the season or changes in activity level in the room.
2.3
Occupancy behavior and satisfaction
In general a very important factor in terms of indoor environment and thermal comfort lies with the
occupants and therefore their behavior - both in terms of how they adjust their environment, but also
in relation to activities and general preferences.
Figure 2.6: The DNAS framework
The DNAS framework, looks into occupants behavior[8]. Drivers are the events which cause a change
in the occupant behavior – this event is affected by stimulating factors such as outdoor and indoor
conditions. Needs are the occupants’ requirements for satisfaction. Actions are interactions the oc-
cupant has with their environment, controls and activities – all done to satisfy their needs. Actions
can be things like changing clothes or adjusting the controllable systems. Lastly the systems are
controllable building elements, which can be interacted with, to satisfy or maintain comfort needs[8].
7
==PAGE:14==
All these factors affect the way occupancies interact with their thermal environment.
A study by Kontes et al.[8] focuses on the control aspect to achieve thermal comfort - by looking into
how different control strategies with thermostats affects the comfort. They introduce the DNAS frame-
work as a tool, which captures the key aspects in human-building environment interaction. The study
indicates that users interact with controllable elements in the building to restore comfort. Therefore
establishing a set of ordinal preferences can be key to user modelling.
In general user satisfaction depends on a number of predetermined factors, such as gender, age, body
mass, metabolic rate etc[25]. All of which are individual factors for each occupant. This means that
it can be quite hard to optimize the thermal comfort for every individual.
User behavior also depends on the environment. In a school environment a student might behave
differently than they would in a home environment. This can both be due to their personality traits,
but also the amount of individuals present in each environment.

3
The boarding school
To investigate the relation between control strategy and user satisfaction in a working environment
and a home environment a collaboration with a boarding school was established. A lot of studies
has been made for regular school environments, but seldom has this been combined with a home
environment. Therefore a boarding school is interesting to look into. This specific boarding school
was chosen due to the presence of both a mechanical ventilation system, but also rooms depending
on purely natural ventilation - which meant, this concept could be explored.
The school currently consist of 80 students and 11 teachers. The students range from 8th grade to
10th grade and all have dyslexia. Dyslexia has big consequences in terms of how one handles written
language. They have a hard time connecting letter sounds into words. Intensive teaching and IT tools
are a must for people with dyslexia[13].
Figure 3.1: Overview of the school 2022
The school is located in Zealand in a small town, Hjembæk. A majority of the school was built in 1907
by H. N. Hansen. The buildings were originally used as a regular school. It went through multiple
expansions in 1914, the 1940ies and the 1950ies. The school was close to being shut down in 1959,
1969, 1972 and 1974 - but due to a strong local community the school staid open. In 1974 the school
was reduced to only teaching students up to 3rd grade, becoming a introduction school for Svinninge

4.3
Equipment
The temperature, relative humidity and CO2 concentration was measured by a Vaisala GMW90R
sensor[9] connected to a HOBO UX120 logger[12] - It logged a value every minute and was set to
14
==PAGE:21==
measure from when it was started till when it was stopped. This ensured at least an hour of data for
each scenario. The HOBO logger can be seen in Figure 4.2. It measured voltages and transformed
them into temperature, relative humidity and CO2 values with a constant specified value. The logger
needed to be powered at all times to be able to measure.
Figure 4.2: The HOBO logger.
The air speed was measured with a Brüel & Kjær—Indoor Climate Analyzer n.1213, using a sensor
for air velocity. The instrument consist of a portable, battery or mains powered instrument which
simultaneously senses environmental conditions via five separate transducers. It doesn’t need to re-
calibrate when the transducer is changed. The measurements consider the mean and the Sd over 3
minutes. The mean produces two values; One calculated using a five-second time constant, which
damps the fluctuations sufficiently to make it readable. The other considering a 3-minute linear mean
air velocity value, which was updated every 10 seconds. The Sd produces the same two values; a 5
second and a 10 second one[18].
The height of the sensor were approximately 1.7 m at all times. The height was set to be at the edge
of the occupancy zone, if the edge had too much draught, it could be assumed the rest of the zone
would be worse, considering warm air moves upwards and therefore should lower the draught higher
up in the room.
Figure 4.3: Brüel & Kjær—Indoor Climate Analyzer n.1213.

and once at the end, to create a broader understanding of the temperatures. To measure the window

4.4
How
The measurements was done three times in each location and at two different times. This meant that
each location had three scenarios and two situations: winter and summer.
The first scenario was without occupants with the windows kept closed. This scenario was done to
establish a baseline for each room.
The second scenario the occupants were present, but the windows were still kept closed. Even if the
occupants would normally open the windows to adjust the indoor climate, they were not allowed to.
This was done to get an understanding of how limiting the manual control affected the occupants
experience of their thermal comfort. This scenario would also hopefully indicate a maximum in terms
of discomfort.
The third scenario the occupants were present and they were allowed to open the windows to adjust
the indoor climate.
This should allow them maximum flexibility in adjustments to their thermal
comfort. It should also reflect what type of indoor climate the occupants generally preferred.
Each measurement lasted 1 hour and the general method was inspired by the second method used in
a study in Britain made by Oseland[28]. They measured 1 hour and filled out questionnaires for each
location. This study also investigated different environments - here home, office and climate chamber.
It also had two different phases, the time between them being only 3 weeks - where the time between
measurements for the winter and summer situation in this study was 2 months.

After the data acquired from the measurements was handled, it could be compared across different
scenarios and zones. Due to this comparable nature, Table 1 was used for both the classroom and the
student quarters. This was also done due to the method utilized not accumulating enough hours to
determine if the dwellings actually lived up to the standards.
4.4.1
Questionnaires
To understand the occupants experience of the indoor climate, questionnaires were provided after
each measuring period, where they were present. These questionnaires focused on understanding the
students relation to temperature, draught, control and comfort. Furthermore a general questionnaire
was handed out at the beginning of each measurement period, to define how the students generally
experienced their day to day indoor climate.
The questionnaires included a 5 point scale, which was based loosely on PMV - this scale was utilized
for whether it was cold, warm or if there was draught and can be seen below. This scale was adapted
to make it simpler to understand for the students. By splitting the temperatures up into two scales
it meant they had to make a direct decision about both aspects.
Figure 4.5: 5 point scale in the questionnaire.
The questionnaires consist of questions about how the current scenario made them feel, both in terms
of their environmental experiences, but also in regards to the control. The questionnaires can be found
in Appendix D in danish.
4.4.2
Procedure
Procedure for the classroom
In the classroom measurements were initially executed over an hour, where window opening wasn’t
allowed. Afterwards the students had to answer a survey to determine if the measured environment
lived up to their satisfaction. Another session of measurements was done, this time students were
allowed to open windows if needed. The same survey was presented to them, to determine if the
added element of being able to adjust the environment themselves had any influence on their comfort.
Lastly the room was measured without occupants, to determine how the ventilation system handled
occupants - by comparing this set of measurements to the initial one.
Procedure for the student quarters
The measurements done in the students quarters were similar to those done in the classroom. Due
to the lack of a ventilation system, the initial measurements were regarding how the environment
changed with occupants, but without any window openings. The second measurements were again
concerning adding control, through window opening. After each set of measurements the same type
of survey was presented. The rooms were also measured without occupants, to get an understanding
of how they affect the living quarters indoor climate.

Procedure with the questionnaires

measurement period. The first time it took a lot longer to fill out, due to having to explain what

situation, specific words still had to be explained, but mostly the students could work on them

Air velocity data

5
Results
This section introduce the results from the measurements and questionnaires and relate the different
zones, situations and times to each other. This was done with the intention to further try to understand
how control affects the occupants thermal comfort and whether or not the environment had an impact.
The general tendencies seen in the results will be summarized, before the potential reasons behind
the results will be explored.
5.1
Overview of the indoor climate
Air velocity
The air velocity generally varied very little from room to room when the window was closed. Consid-
ering there is no ventilation system in the rooms, it makes sense that the air would be standing mostly
still, which the results confirms. The variation in air velocity with open windows could be explained
by multiple factors, such as the wind picking up its pace outside or the instrument being closer to the
window in some rooms than others. The direction the window is facing and its angle could also be a
factor in how the air enters the room.
Draught rate
The draught rate should not be higher than 20% - this was the case in the classroom at all measuring
points and both with the windows closed and open. As seen on Figure 5.1, the values both for summer
and winter didn’t exceed 12%. In general both the draught rate and the turbulence intensity were
fairly similar in all areas of the room independently of the scenario or situation.
Figure 5.1: The turbulence intensity and draught rate for the classroom.
It was a different case for the bedrooms, where most of the calculated draught rates were above 20%
during the summer situation, when the windows were open. BR18 states the DR can be higher than
the 20% if windows were open, but in some cases it reached above 100%. While most rooms had
21
==PAGE:28==
no draught, when the window was closed, 501 stood out - the fact there was draught in both closed
window scenarios could indicate a leakage in the window.
Figure 5.2: The turbulence intensity and draught rate for the bedrooms
Surface temperature
The surface temperatures were only taken for the summer situation. The temperatures were generally
higher when the window was kept closed. In the classroom the side closest to the window was slightly
cooler, than the other side. Furthermore the back of the room seemed to be warmer than the front in
relation to the students work spaces, but not at the actual walls.
Figure 5.3: The temperature on different surfaces in the classroom, during a period where the
windows were open vs. where they weren’t.

Thermal and atmospheric comfort
The measurements done over the 1 hour period in the three scenarios for the 7 locations during the
different seasons can be seen as graphs in Appendix E. These results showed a general tendency in
how little change there was over time.
The relative humidity was generally fairly constant - with a slight decline in most of the rooms during
the hour, all scenarios, both in winter and summer situations, were fairly similar. Hot air generally
has a lower relative humidity than cold air, which could explain the slight decrease over time. The
exception to this trend was room 202 in the winter situation (see Figure 5.5), this room had a large in-
crease when the window was opened. In other rooms the occupants decided not to open the windows,
preferring the current thermal comfort state, which meant the relative humidity should be expected
to be fairly stable.

The temperatures were fairly constant throughout the measuring period in all scenarios and both in
the summer and winter situation. The difference between the temperatures most likely depended on
the time of day they were measured. The rooms were generally colder during the morning measure-
ments. The winter situation for room 503 showed a curious tendency considering all scenarios end
at the same temperature level around 20.3 °C at the end, this trend was not present in the summer
situation.
The CO2 levels were fairly constant in most of the rooms as well, there was generally an increase
when the windows were kept closed. The only real difference being in those rooms where the window
was opened during the measurement period. Here room 201, 202 and 503 stood out for the winter

constant for a few minutes, before drastically increasing again. This drastic increase was probably due
to the loggers position. In room 503 the window was opened all of the period, which showed in the
CO2 level being almost the same as when no occupants were present. This was quite a large decrease
compared to when the windows weren’t allowed to be opened.

summer situation. This said, there was still students who never opened their windows - which impacted
their home environments. The reasons for opening the windows seemed mostly to be the expected
ones - smell, temperature and fresh air. There was no clear trend for which of them were the most
frequent reason, which could be due to some of the students expressing all of the factors being the
reason at the same time. The other factors presented was things such as hanging drinks outside the
window to cool down.

==PAGE:43==
5.2
Comparison between zones
The bedrooms in general showed very similar tendencies. The temperature levels depend on the time
of day the measurements was made, indicating that the outdoor temperature had a large say in how
the temperature was inside the room. The students who opened their windows in general had lower
temperatures, sometimes too low in relation to the recommended values. The variation of tempera-
tures over time didn’t seem to depend on the occupants in the room.
If the windows weren’t opened the CO2 concentration could be a large problem, due to it sometimes
reaching a level more than 1000 ppm above the recommended 1000 ppm. Most bedrooms had too
high CO2 levels at one point.
While the window opening should theoretically worsen the draught in the rooms, this didn’t seem to
affect the experienced indoor climate for the students much.
Generally the results showed that preference in temperature and therefore the window opening had
the largest say in how the indoor climate was shaped. The students who generally preferred warmer
temperatures both lived in 501 - which generally had the highest CO2 concentration. Temperature
wise this was not the case, which indicate that the location of the bedrooms had an effect on how
warm the rooms became.
5.3
DNAS analysis
In this study the DNA framework can be utilized on multiple scenarios to explain the occupant behav-
ior. In most cases the driver was a change in comfort level - this could be an increase in temperature,
creating discomfort or a change in the way the air felt. When the occupant experienced a discomfort,
they developed a need. This need would be to lower the temperatures or remove the heaviness of the
air or smells. To do this the occupant had to interact with their environment - in both bedrooms and
the classroom, the only possible system they could control was the windows. Opening the windows
was the action most frequently chosen to adjust the environment to their needs. In some cases the
occupants also adjusted their level of clothing - removing a shirt or putting another shirt on.
Looking at a specific situation; there was a student who had problems with how warm their bedroom
was and were unable to open the window, due to their roommates being sensitive to the cold and
therefore not having a need to lower the temperatures. This students driver was the warm temperature
in the room - which created a need for them to somehow lower the temperatures, if not in the room,
just for where they were. Due to the only adjustable building system not being an option, the student
decided to lie on the floor. This action was taken in a form of desperation to satisfy their need for a
colder environment, without disturbing the other students in the rooms satisfaction.

Discussion
6.1
Summary of the overall findings
While the ventilation system in the classroom could mostly handle the CO2 levels, it seemed to be un-
able to fully handle the temperatures. The window opening helped managing the high temperatures,
but in turn lowered the temperatures too much at times. Considering the students seemed to prefer
the windows being open, this might indicate that the ventilation systems lack of ability to handle
the temperatures were an issue. Even though the CO2 concentration was manageable, the students
still mentioned being unmotivated and tired when the room got too hot - furthermore the students
felt the air was heavy and unpleasant. Therefore it can be questioned if the ventilation system was
optimal for the room, considering the students generally felt the need to adjust the indoor climate
through manual control, no matter whether or not it was the winter or summer situation. That said,
the students need to open the windows, seemed to increase during the summer time.
In terms of the bedrooms, most students seemed to be content with the current system. The freedom
to open the window as the main ventilation was generally preferred. The majority of the problems
in the bedroom was in regards to individual preferences - the students who preferred warmer tem-
peratures, had a tendency to not open the window and therefore allow the CO2 levels to build up.
The students still seemed to prefer their bedrooms, over the classroom, due to the lower number of
preferences, which needed to be taken into account. This might be due to other factors outside of
indoor climate though, such as comfort and it being their own space. As with the classroom the
manual window opening brought the temperatures far too low.
The difference between the work environment and home environment was mostly about personal pref-
erences - the ventilation system handled the CO2 concentration better, but otherwise the temperature
levels and what the individual student preferred were the most saying factors. The students generally
preferred when they had the possibility to adjust the indoor climate themselves - but considering
none of the students expressed a want for actually adjusting anything when asked, this might be more
about the indoor climate being more comfortable like that, than the control aspect itself.
6.1.1
Understanding the results
In some rooms the window opening lead to a large increase in relative humidity - this might be due
to the air having hit the logger directly, because it was placed in the window sill. The high humidity
of the entering cold air could explain the increase on the graph. While all loggers were placed in
the window sill, some rooms had multiple windows - so another one could have been opened instead,
which was why this tendency wasn’t present in all rooms.
The temperatures varied quite a lot, depending on the time of day the measurements were made.
Higher temperatures were generally present around the 12-15 time. Here the position of the logger
could also explain the temperatures - being placed in the window sill, where the sun could hit it
straight on. Another explanation for the temperature variations, could be the location of the window
- which had an impact of the amount of heat entering the room from the outside.
The temperatures could also be affected by internal heat sources. Some rooms had more internal
heat sources - like room 503, where they had 3 computers running at all times. Here the number of
occupants could also play a role in the difference between similar rooms, if there were only 1 occupant
compared to two, it impacted the internal heat gain, but also the added CO2 concentration.
In some rooms the temperatures were constantly fairly low - especially room 501.
This could be
38
==PAGE:45==
related to a leakage from the window letting the colder outside air hit the logger. It seems unlikely
though, considering the radiator was right below the window sill and should therefore equalize the
difference in temperature. The students talked about the table being above the radiator and therefore
limiting the heat output - which again could explain the low temperatures. In some scenarios the
students had already ventilated the room before the measurements were done, which in turn could
explain very low temperatures present in scenarios with closed windows, where they were expected to
be much higher.
In relation to the CO2 concentration the time of day might have had an effect on the levels - some
rooms generally ventilated a lot during the day and if the scenarios were measured later in the day, the
levels might already be fairly low. The same could be said in relation to high values - some students
never opened their windows, so the time also had a say in the height of the concentration levels.
6.2
Improvements
To improve the CO2 levels in the bedrooms, a ventilation system could be installed in all the rooms.
Other options to handle the CO2 could be adding mandatory window opening every day to comply
with the recommended 3 times a day from the health authority - it would be the schools responsibility
to enforce it and might therefore not be optimal, due to it being hard to keep an eye on whether or
not the students actually did it. In relation to this, one could also automate the window opening
with a sensor and a motor. This would more be based on the temperatures than the CO2 levels and
potentially couldn’t deal with them. Another option could be the start up project Canairi[7] - a bird
which measure the CO2 concentration and falls over when it is above the recommended 1000 ppm.
This indicates the windows needs to be opened. When the levels are below 1000 ppm again, the bird
gets back up, to indicate the windows can be closed. This way it would be easy for the students to
know when their indoor climate wasn’t optimal and no one would have to supervise them. The bird
also makes it more of a game, than a task.
Figure 6.1: Canairi bird
Both the classroom and bedrooms had problems with the temperature intervals - due to tempera-
ture being more of a individual preference it might not be as big of a problem as the CO2 levels.
Some of the ways to improve the CO2 concentration, would automatically affect the temperatures as
well. Potentially setting up temperature indicators in the rooms, might help the students get a better
understanding of what intervals they prefer - even if said intervals weren’t within the recommended
ones. Putting a number on the discomfort, might encourage them to adjust the indoor climate through
window opening. Another way to tackle the temperatures would be by installing automatic blinds in
the windows - using a temperature sensor inside the room to determine when the blinds were closed

or open.
In general the relative humidity created very little issues - it was between 20%-60%. This indicate
there was no need for a dehumidifier or humidifier. The same can be said for air velocity - while
there with open windows was clear periods of draught, considering the windows were generally open
in short periods, this should not be a big problem and therefore there was no reason to adjust the
current system to tackle draught.
6.3
Findings in relations to other studies
In the study made by Oseland[28] the initial results were, that the homes were rated warmer than the
office and climate chamber and the office was rated the coldest. The comparison between the reported
thermal sensation(TS) and the calculated PMV, shows an almost equal thermal sensation around
the neutral point (tn). PMV is highly correlated with the air temperature, while TS is more varied
showing a difference between their tns. The difference of tn between the three measured environments
shows that there is a context between environment and thermal comfort.
This context between environment and thermal comfort was present in the surveys - the students
generally preferred the bedrooms over the classroom. In relation to the actual measurements, they
didn’t seem to impact this preference. The CO2 concentration was generally lower in the classroom,
the RH being within the 20-60% interval and the temperatures in both zones in an interval between
12-35 °C, where the classroom had neither outlier. The draught rates were also much better in the
classroom than in the bedrooms and the surface temperatures were in similar intervals for the two
zones. All measurements indicated that the classroom was slightly better than the bedrooms and
still the occupants preferred the bedroom. Therefore the context might be in terms of individual
preferences to the indoor climate and not the actual standards and recommended values.
In the second phase of the Oseland study the homes were in general rated colder than the office, but
this could be based on the time of day and not a preference. Both PMV and TS varied based on
the environments. The freedom in phase two allowed for a broader spectrum of reported activities.
Overall the subjects seems to be able to maintain comfort over a wider temperature interval. Allowing
the subjects to adapt to their environment and allow them control of the temperature appears to be
the optimum strategy for energy efficiency and comfort.
The students expressed they generally preferred when they had more control over their environment,
which support the conclusion in the Oseland study.
Allowing the students to open the windows,
improved their experience of the indoor climate, even when some of the scenarios in terms of values
where not actually improved. This indicate that the feeling of being in control had an impact on the
experience of the environment.
Another study by Kontes et al.[8] focused more on the control aspect. The results for one of the
buildings were evaluated. In the summer situation the mode utilizing the operative temperature for
control, allowed for a more constant PPD%. The discomfort with operative temperature control was
higher due to over cooling. By performing an experiment with the operative set point set at 25.5 °C
it was clear that humidity levels were important to consider when designing and building operative
temperature comport bounds. In the winter situation the operative temperature mode allowed for
lower dissatisfaction.
In general the manual control done on the boarding school had a tendency to lead to over cooling
independently of the situation. That said the window opening during the winter scenario was far less,
which indicate the overall dissatisfaction was lower - due to the lack of adaption. The importance
of the humidity levels, where not made clear in the measurement, but if they had been outside the
40
==PAGE:47==
recommended values one might have seen an impact.
The Kontes et al. study indicates that designing purely based on operative temperatures limits from
ISO 7730 can lead to invalid sensation estimates. Control in relation to the humidity is a crucial
factor to estimate an accurate thermal sensation and therefore establish efficient control. The studies
of the user behavior indicate that users interact with controllable elements in the building to restore
comfort. Establishing a set of ordinal preferences can be key to user modelling.
The students expressed that both the student quarters and classroom were fine, as long as they were
allowed to open the windows - which indicate that the standard temperature limits might not be an
optimal design criteria. It also imply that the students behavior has a big impact on their comfort
level - which support the Georgios D. Kontes et al. statement about establishing preferences being a
key factor.
A study investigated how varying degrees of control in a naturally ventilated environment affected
the occupant’s thermal comfort. It found that the occupant’s level of control affected diverse thermal
responses even though they experienced the same environment. Another study found a significant
correlation between perceived control and thermal comfort in HVAC buildings[25].
Perceived control had a large impact on the perceived thermal comfort in both the classroom and
student quarters. This indicate a correlation between perceived control and thermal comfort - just as
the above study implies. Even though they were exposed to the same environment in the classroom,
they had diverse thermal responses, due to personal preferences.
A mass-experiment was made in 2021 in danish schools for region Zealand and region the capital - this
experiment focused on how opening the windows during a break could improve the CO2 concentration
levels. The study showed that around 20% of the classrooms with this simple change could enter the
recommended level below 1000 ppm[22].
The measurements done on the boarding school also showed an improvement in CO2 concentration
levels with window opening, while it didn’t always bring the levels below 1000 ppm, it made a clear
difference. If the window opening was more controlled, in both the classroom and the student quar-
ters, the same tendency seen in the mass-experiment, might be seen on the boarding school.
Overall the results from this study show similar tendencies to other studies - the perceived control
had an impact on their comfort. There was a difference between environments, if not in the actual
measurements, then in how each environment was perceived. In general being allowed to open the
windows and therefore being in control had a positive effect on the students comfort.
6.4
Students
All the students are dyslexic, which added a challenge to the experiment, considering they had a hard
time just reading the questionnaires and understanding what was asked of them. It also limited the
detail degree of their responses. Considering some of the students really didn’t like writing, it might
also have affected their response in a negative way.
Their dyslexia also meant they needed more
guidance through the questions - leading to them potentially getting words put in their mouth by
questions which where meant to help them understand.
Outside of the dyslexia aspect, some of the students also didn’t want to cooperate and directly went
against instructions, both when during the measurements and when filling out the questionnaires.
Potentially this could have been avoided if another method had been used. If the questionnaires had
been more interactive or made into a game, they might have been more willing to participate. In

==PAGE:48==
terms of the measurement periods, if the importance of their participation had been highlighted more
they might have taken it more serious and followed the instructions.
Furthermore if they had gotten a proper look into what exactly the project was about and what
their participation could mean it might have motivated them to actively take part. While there were
problems with the cooperation with the students, in the end it all worked out okay - they improved
the responses from the winter to summer situation and seemed to have a better understanding of
what to do. This show that having had a test run through, might have been able to improve their
understanding.
Another factor which might have helped their participation, could be to actually give them something
for it, whether that be of a monetary value or in shape of edibles.
6.5
Method reflection
Both sessions of measurements took place over 2 days, this meant that every moment of the day was
used to its max, without much time to spare. This might have lead to mistakes or inaccuracy, due to
being in a rush. Potentially this could have been improved by improving the method or expanding
the time slot for the measurements. The lack of breaks between each scenario might also have been
clouding the students judgement of the difference between them - though it at the same time could
have highlighted them.
The biggest problem with the time available was that it meant there was quite a lot to do for one
person. While the loggers measured automatically the other instruments had to be manually moved
around and used. This took a lot of time, meaning there was no time to really double check measure-
ments.
In general measuring for 1 hour might not have been optimal, considering the recommendations and
standards for dwellings had temperature standards, which relied on number of hours. Having only
one hour for each scenario, meant it was impossible to put the measurements up against the standards.
6.5.1
Errors
During the measurements multiple aspects went wrong, potentially compromising the results. The
first measurements of the classroom were done without actually powering the HOBO logger, due to
the contact being switched off - this meant the students spend 2 hours in a closed environment in-
stead of the expected 1 hour. Therefore the thermal comfort might have been worse, than if the first
measurements had succeeded. That said it might not be an actual problem, considering it still reflects
a fairly normal scenario.
The air velocity measurements in the bedrooms for the winter situation was made the day prior at
around 7 pm, where the original HOBO logger room data was compromised and therefore not usable.
This meant the velocity was measured with an extra occupant present. The air velocity doesn’t fit with
the HOBO logger results, but should still reflect the same situation. In general the air velocity was
measured either in the scenarios with no occupants present or outside HOBO logger measurements,
so the scenarios wouldn’t be disturbed - this meant the air velocity doesn’t reflect all the scenarios or
the same time periods.
During the initial tests of the process in the bedrooms, some students left their rooms and interfered
with another zone, they also opened the window in a no opening scenario. These things were avoided
42
==PAGE:49==
during actual measurements.
During the winter situation, without occupants all the doors were closed in all rooms, except 502 -
where it was open for the first 20 minutes after a student had entered before the measurement period
started and didn’t close the door. The door being opened, didn’t show any clear differences in the
measurements.
In relation to the surveys, even if their responses were checked before submission, some responses
went missing, due to the students not actually pressing submit. The response rate was better in the
summer situation though - which might be due to the students having a better understanding of the
questionnaire or being supported better through the process.
6.6
Perspective
If this study was to be expanded it could be interesting to measure a longer period of time. This
could be done by changing the method and not basing it on different scenarios, but on a constant
measurement, in which window opening were logged. In this scenario the impact of control would be
removed, but it would be easier to compare to the standards for dwellings and also show a broader
image of the current situation on the school.
To dive deeper into the control aspect, it could be interesting to make control schedules, which would
determine when the students should open their windows. This could present a better understanding
of how window opening affect the CO2 concentration levels and how much venting is needed to keep
it below 1000 ppm. Furthermore when the needed venting was established, different control strategies
could be tested. This could be just utilizing different schedules, for different rooms, but it could also
be establishing other control elements than window opening.
In this study only one grade was involved in the experiment. Therefore involving the rest of the school
- or another boarding school could show different tendencies. Potentially comparing the experience
of a boarding school environment to that of a regular school and their homes, could highlight the
connection the students have to their private environments.
Considering the ventilation system in the classroom mostly dealt with the CO2 concentrations, but
not the temperature, one could explore the set points in the system and the air velocity in a program
like CFD, to get an understanding of what the optimal values would be.

7
Conclusion
The indoor climate on the boarding school was not optimal. While the CO2 levels were fairly well
handled in the classroom, the bedrooms had very varied concentrations, most of which were above
1000 ppm. The largest problem in both environments were the temperature. The temperature fluctu-
ated from being very low at 12 °C to being fairly high at 35 °C. The survey responses indicate that the
students didn’t have any problems handling the colder temperatures, but the warmer temperatures
had an overall negative effect on their well being.
In terms of control, there were a clear preference for having control - which allowed the students to
adjust their environment and be comfortable. The largest factor in terms of how the students expe-
rienced the environment, was their personal preferences.
To optimize the current system, multiple potential initiatives could be made. The system itself could
be optimized by adding ventilation systems in the student quarters. A simpler initiative might be to
introduce window opening schedules, either manually - still allowing the students control, or automat-
ically with a temperature sensor. One could also install instruments, such as a temperature indicator
or the canairi[7], to give the students indications that the indoor climate was not optimal.
This study shows the same conclusions as other studies. There was a context between environment
and thermal comfort. Control can affect how one experience the climate positively. The standards
recommended for temperatures might not be the optimal intervals for designing indoor climate. The
students felt comfortable in far lower temperatures, than the recommended values included. The be-
havior of the occupants also had a large say in what the optimal indoor climate could be - establishing
preferences might be key.
The method utilized in this study could be optimized - the time limit might have affected the accuracy
of the measurements. Only measuring one hour for each scenario, meant it wasn’t possible to utilize
standards for dwellings. The students dyslexia added a challenge in terms of them needing more help
with understanding the surveys. In general the students were not fully engaged in the study, which
might have had a negative effect on the results the survey yielded.
The study could be expanded in multiple ways - either by including more schools in the experiment,
both boarding schools, but also regular schools. The control aspect, could also have been explored
deeper, by testing control schedules or establishing other control aspects. The ventilation system
could also have been investigated, to understand why it didn’t handle the temperatures.
