Abstract 
Nowadays the mobile communication systems energy consumption comprises a significant 
portion of the energy consumption as a whole. The mobile systems energy consumption is even 
constantly growing. Lately the aforementioned facts made the designers, engineers, equipment 
manufacturers, political organizations and mobile operators focus their attention to the energy 
efficiency. Moreover, the world society is largely affected by the mobile communications boom 
and the increase of the consumed energy. Materials related to mobile communication systems and 
energy efficiency are presented in this master’s thesis. More precisely, materials related to Long 
Term Evolution technology are presented in energy efficiency perspective. Materials related to 
Long Term Evolution design and planning, as well as materials related to Long Term Evolution 
Energy Efficiency mechanisms were considered as a basis and starting points. Moreover, an 
Energy Efficiency analysis, conclusions and recommendations are presented in relation to the 
LTE Radio Access Network planning and upgrading. The main problem addressed in this paper 
is the Energy Efficiency improvement of mobile communication systems and particularly the 
improvement of Long Term evolution Energy Efficiency. Throughout this paper the following 
methods of investigation of the main problem are used: gathering and analysis of materials 
strongly related to the design and planning aspects of the Radio Access Network and Energy 
Efficiency; gathering and analysis of materials specific for Long Term Evolution Energy 
Efficiency; linking planning, design and Energy Efficiency mechanisms. The general conclusions 
of this paper regarding Long Term Evolution Energy Efficiency are as follows: Energy 
Efficiency maximization should be considered at every stage of the system lifecycle; Energy 
Efficiency is a matter of a subtle balance; Energy Efficiency improvements can be done at 
different system levels; more radical approaches which take into account more factors are more 
efficient. One important finding in this research work is that Energy Efficiency maximization is 
not always the optimal solution. Other factors such as equipment utilization should be considered 
as well. Another important finding is that both system fine adjustments and equipment 
optimization influence energy efficiency maximization at network level.

Lay Summary 
The key goal of this master’s thesis is to propose solutions for improvement of the energy 
efficiency of mobile communication systems. Such solutions are ideas, dependencies, parameters 
relations, mathematical models etc. In the presented paper Long Term Evolution is considered as 
the basis for energy efficiency improvement in the context of the modern (2017) mobile 
communication systems. A mobile communication system consists of a number of subsystems, 
blocks or components and the energy efficiency improvements can be made in all these system’s 
parts. Energy efficiency improvements in the Long Term Evolution radio access network were 
considered as most important in the presented material. The materials presented in the thesis are 
related to Long Term Evolution radio access network planning and design, Long Term Evolution 
energy efficiency and Long Term Evolution energy efficiency analysis and maximization. The 
key contributions presented are the energy efficiency analysis and maximization of Long Term 
Evolution base stations and radio access network planning. Generally, the Long Term Evolution 
radio access network planning and design and energy efficiency are used as a fundaments for 
Long Term Evolution energy efficiency analysis and maximization.

1. Introduction  
In the recent years energy efficiency of the mobile communication systems became and important 
parameter. The importance of energy utilization and energy loss limitation became more evident 
with the increase of the mobile communications usage. Mobile communication systems and more 
precisely the radio access network and base stations are related to significant power loss and 
inefficiencies. The increasing demand for mobile communication services leads to the additional 
deployment of energy inefficient mobile communication systems. As a result the energy 
inefficiency and power losses scale linearly or even exponentially. The main goal of this paper 
work is to propose means for energy efficiency improvements in Long Term Evolution Radio 
Access Network. The improvements can be done in an already deployed network or 
improvements can be done during the planning and design stages. Recently efforts were 
concentrated on improving the energy efficiency either by increasing the bitrate or by reducing 
the power consumption. One important point considered in this paper work is to look on the 
theoretical limitations of the energy conversion efficiency of a radio access network. Moreover, 
as a fundamental principle it is considered the importance of the energy conservation law and the 
energy conversion efficiency. By analyzing the base station power consumption components 
important dependencies can be observed and applied to energy efficiency principles. 
Furthermore, introducing energy efficiency parameters and considerations in the network 
planning and design can improve energy efficiency by nature. The basic results from the studies 
in the area of radio access network efficiency are related to: base station efficiency optimization; 
limitation of power losses in the radio signal cables; improvement of the signal to noise radio in 
the radio channel; direction of the radio beam; turning off power wasting blocks; reducing data 
transfer by data caching; optimized network structure; exchange of data between the mobile 
stations. The findings of this paper work are related to:  energy efficiency optimization of the 
base station blocks; the importance of energy conversion efficiency; network structure and energy 
efficiency dependences and network planning and energy efficiency considerations. An 
interesting note that can be made regarding the findings in this work is that for energy efficiency 
maximization the power consumption does not necessarily depend on network traffic. On the 
other hand, for energy efficiency maximization, the energy conversion efficiency should not 
depend on the network traffic. There are many energy efficiency aspects which should be studied 
in depth. For instance, the relation between energy efficiency, energy conversion efficiency and 
their theoretical limitations could be developed further. Another point is the balance between 
wasted power cost and network deployment cost.

A number of similar systemizations for describing the network lifecycle phases exist. The

Figure 1 represents the lifecycle phases of a network (Reference [1]). The lifecycle principle is

iteration principle is a key feature in the network lifecycle. This principle implies that the 
evolution of the networks is related to all phases of the lifecycle and all phases are interconnected 
and interdependent. Part of the planning phase is the dimensioning of the fronthaul, backhaul and 
core network interfaces(Reference [2]). The mobile BSs consume large portion of energy and 
there is a room for improvement of the EE. BSs are placed in the RAN for this reason the RAN 
and fronthaul dimensioning is related to the EE. A closer look on LTE RAN planning, design and 
dimensioning is needed in order to see how those aspects influence power consumption and EE. 
As part of the network planning process, the RAN dimensioning gives an overview of a possible 
implementation scenario (Reference [3]). The RAN dimensioning process also gives an initial 
view of the system parameters. The initial dimensioning of RAN is a part of the iterative planning 
process and the initial dimensioning is used for future improvements, upgrades and calculations 
in the planning. As a result the mobile communication requirements caused by the customer’s 
needs should be satisfied and an optimal configuration of the RAN architecture should be found.

At the same time RAN parameters should be efficient in terms of both network performance and 
energy consumption. As a starting point of the dimensioning a set of input parameters is used 
(Reference [3]).  The dimensioning process is used as a planning tool and this tool gives 
dimensioning results in function of the input dimensioning parameters, i.e. the dimensioning 
results are relevant to the given set of input parameters only. This implies that RAN dimensioning 
must be done with great care in order to avoid bottleneck effects. Basic input dimensioning 
parameters are: coverage, capacity and QoS. The main function of dimensioning is to translate 
input system parameters to the needed network infrastructure for both access and core network. 
Compared to the whole network lifecycle deployment mechanism, the dimensioning is relatively 
simpler. The dimensioning takes advantage of simplified mathematical functions, models and 
methods. However, the dimensioning introduces accuracy and comfort in the planning process. It 
has to be kept in mind that dimensioning directly influences network planning and a successful 
deployment depends largely on the subtle balancing of the input dimensioning parameters. The 
dimensioning tool produces results which are dependent on the input parameters and for this 
reason input parameters balancing can be more important for the final deployment than the 
dimensioning itself.

related to traffic types and also have to be considered.

Coverage dimensioning is used to determine the area covered by a single base station and the 
number of BSs needed to cover a given area. Due to the attenuation of the radio waves, a radio 
link budget (RLB) is made in order to estimate the needed transmit antenna power. The RLB 
takes into account all of the gains and the losses in the chain between the BS and the UE or 
between the UE and the BS. As a result a stable radio communication link can be established, no 
matter where the UE is placed around the BS.  
The purpose of capacity dimensioning is to estimate the needed RAN capacity. The RAN 
capacity is dictated mainly from the UE needs. The BSs capacity depends on the profile of users 
and the number of users. Furthermore, the supported capacity ensures a certain level of QoS to 
the customers. In LTE the network capacity and coverage are interdependent. There are many 
parameters which influence both capacity and coverage. Capacity and coverage balancing is a 
complicated matter and taking into account overall system EE makes the balancing even more 
complicated.   
The purpose of the transport dimensioning is to give an initial view of the network interfaces 
parameters. The transport dimensioning has to be done in such a way that minimizes the chance 
of having bottlenecks in the RAN. Moreover, support of QoS, future possible network upgrades,  
CAPEX and OPEX considerations have to be presumed.  
2.2 Dimensioning of LTE RAN. 
 The purpose of the LTE RAN dimensioning is to produce the required number of eNBs per area 
and the configuration of the eNBs. The initial LTE RAN planning itself comprises of data traffic 
analysis, coverage dimensioning, capacity dimensioning and transport dimensioning. It can be 
pointed out that as a result RLB, site density, site configuration, access gateways, hardware 
configuration and interfacing have to be described. 
2.2.1 Input dimensioning parameters. 
According to [3] it is of utmost importance to make a difference between LTE input and output 
dimensioning parameters. LTE dimensioning inputs are classified in the following three 
categories (Reference [3]): 
 Quality related inputs. 
 Coverage related inputs. 
 Capacity related inputs.  
Quality related inputs are dictated from the QoS ensuring a certain level of quality for the end 
user. Teletraffic engineering deals with issues related to the quality inputs and teletraffic 
engineering eases cell throughput and blocking probability estimation. Another important quality 
related consideration is cell edge performance criterion. This criterion is used for estimation of 
the cell radius and the site count. 
==PAGE:20==
6 
 
 Cell edge has to be taken into account in order to ensure the QoS level (Reference [3]). Three 
considerations could be taken into account for cell edge estimation: 
 User defined throughput at the cell edges. 
 Coverage maximization, modulation and coding scheme (MCS) minimization and site 
count minimization.  
 Cell radius. 
If the cell radius has a certain required value, then the transmit power and interference levels have 
to be balanced. The cell edge must support the required throughput. The second bullet of the 
abovementioned three point implies that the coverage has to be maximized and at the same time a 
minimal and less complicated MCS has to be used. Moreover, at the same time the number of 
sites has to be kept at minimum. All these considerations impact the overall EE of LTE.  
Coverage planning in LTE is mainly related to the RLB (Reference [3]). RLB input dimensioning 
considerations are: 
 Transmitter power. 
 Transmitter and receiver antenna systems. 
 Number of antennas. 
 Radio communication chain gains and losses. 
 Cell loading. 
 Propagation models. 
So far LTE bands include 900, 1800 and 2600MHz. The electromagnetic wave behavior of the 
three frequency bands is different. Generally, at higher frequencies more capacity can be 
leveraged. Another point is that frequencies above 1GHz approach the microwave range and such 
electromagnetic waves attenuate more in comparison with lower frequencies. The last two 
sentences combined together imply that more bandwidth comes at the price of more attenuation. 
There are even more other complicated dependencies that require the incorporation of models for 
the three different LTE bands. Not to forget to mention that channel types can be pedestrian and 
Vehicular (Reference [3]) are other dimensioning inputs. Geographical characteristics are one 
more type of input. It is characterized by the: area type - urban or rural; size and shape of the 
area. Required coverage probability is a factor that influences the cell radius. The coverage 
probability has to be carefully handled because the cell radius is non-linearly depending on it. 
The previous statement implies that a small deviation of the coverage probability can cause a 
large deviation of the cell radius and as a result the cell radius can be severely affected.  
LTE capacity planning inputs are: 
 Number of subscribers. 
 Service profile and service requirements. 
 Usage level.   
==PAGE:21==
7 
 
 Available spectrum and channel bandwidth. 
It could be pointed out that the capacity planning inputs produce LTE network dimensioning 
results (Reference [3]). Cell radius is dependent on capacity evaluation. The number of 
subscribers serviced by one cell depends on the traffic analysis and user’s data rates requirements. 
The complexity of LTE capacity planning could be facilitated by the use of system and link level 
simulations. However, the underlying principles remain the same. 
2.2.2 Output dimensioning parameters. 
In order to build the whole RAN the size of the most fundamental unit is needed. Namely, this is 
the cell size. Cell radius depends on coverage and capacity and the actual value of cell radius can 
be based on either coverage, capacity or both capacity and coverage. Once the cell radius is 
determined, the number of eNBs can be calculated. By leveraging the geometrical properties of 
the hexagonal shapes, the number of eNBs for a unit area can be calculated. However, in reality 
the cell shape is not a hexagon and this shape is used just for the sake of designing. Another 
important output dimensioning parameter is the capacity of a single eNB. It is based on the 
number of users per cell. Last but not least output dimensioning parameter is the interface 
dimensioning (S1 and X2 interfaces dimensioning parameters) and the backhaul dimensioning.  
The outputs of the RAN dimensioning can be used for calculations of the anticipated 
infrastructure expenses. Moreover the outputs of the dimensioning can be used for EE 
optimization of the LTE RAN and LTE system as a whole.  
2.3 LTE RAN Coverage planning.  
Coverage planning is one of the most important aspects of LTE RAN dimensioning. This part of 
the dimensioning places coverage at highest priority and no compromises with the coverage are 
made. The LTE RAN coverage planning takes into account the service requirements and 
produces as a result the assessment of the needed resources. The coverage planning takes into 
account the RLB, path loss, transmitted power, received power, antenna gain etc. Both the UL 
and DL RLBs are considered. The required signal-to-interference-plus-noise ratio (SINR) at the 
receiver is taken as a basis of the maximum path loss calculation (Reference [3]). As SINR 
implies, signal level, interference and noise ratio together influence the calculation functions. A 
radio propagation models are used to evaluate the UL/DL path losses and cell radius. There are 
different radio wave propagation models which are suitable for different deployment areas.

Figure 3 depicts a typical RLB components and it is used to clarify the power losses and gains in 
the signal chain between the transmitter and the receiver. As it can be seen from the picture the 
signal flows as follows (left to right; transmitter to receiver): 
 The useful modulated and amplified radio signal is radiated from the transmitter antenna. 
 The radio signal attenuation and respectively loss depends on the transmitter antenna 
efficiency and on the antenna radiation pattern (directivity). 
 In the free space the radio waves suffer atmosphere losses, path losses. The atmosphere 
losses are caused by partial transformation of the electromagnetic waves energy into 
thermal energy. With other words, the radio waves heat the atmosphere and this causes 
path loss. The path losses depend on the radio frequency, atmosphere humidity, meters 
above the sea level, particulates in the atmosphere (dust) etc. 
 The radio waves can suffer power level loss due to free space propagation power loss or

There are a number of aspects related to Required SINR that have to be considered. As previously 
mentioned, the frequency reuse factor in LTE is one and all eNBs operate at the same frequency 
range. Therefore interference limitation is a main issue in LTE and the interference levels at the 
edges dictate performance characteristics. Cell edges and cell size depend largely on the relation 
between Required SINR and throughput. For this reason the previously mentioned dependencies 
related to Required SINR have to be taken into account. Characteristics which influence the 
Required SINR are (Reference [3]): 
 Modulation and Coding Schemes (MCS). 
 Radio waves propagation model. 
LTE is based on advanced modulation schemes – QPSK and QAM. QAM scheme has a number 
of stable states (aka constellation points or symbols) and these states can be 16 for 16 QAM and 
64 for 64QAM. 16QAM is capable of delivering 4 bits per symbol and 64 QAM is capable of 
delivering 6 bits per symbol. Consequently 64QAM supports higher bit rates than 16QAM. The 
higher bit rates come with the price of higher SINR requirements. The reason for this is the 
higher SINR required by the hardware equipment needed in order to detect the constellation 
point’s parameters. As a general conclusion it can be pointed out that lower complexity MSC are 
more robust and can operate at lower SINR and lower bit rates. Higher complexity MCS can 
operate at higher SINR and higher bit rates. Required SINR can be obtained by the use of the 
following approaches (Reference [3]): 
 Link level simulation. For the simulation different set of simulation parameters and 
characteristics can be used. Such parameters are radio wave propagation model and 
antenna configuration.  For the simulation purposes noise is generated by the use of 
adaptive white Gausian noise (AWGN noise). This eases noise characteristics setup 
and SNR is taken instead of SINR. As a result from the link level simulations 
Throughput vs. average SNR tables are obtained. From these tables results the 
Required SINR is calculated.

 Alpha-Shannon formula. This formula is used for approximate estimation of the link 
level results. However, different results can be obtained by the variation of the values

propagation properties of LTE 2100. For this reason the coverage area of a single site of LTE 900

capacity it would make it necessary to place the eNBs close to each other and their coverage areas 
would overlap. Moreover, LTE frequency reuse factor is just one and the abovementioned 
scenario would introduce intersystem interference, limited BW and data rates. Another extreme 
scenario is to base the whole network in a given are on a 2100MHz frequency band only. In that 
case the network coverage is impaired. Considering the abovementioned two extreme scenarios a 
sort of conclusion can be made. Basically, in urban conditions it is a good solution to have a both 
the LTE 2100 and LTE 900. In this way the coverage and capacity balancing would be eased 
(Reference [4]). On the other hand EE would inevitably be increased as well.

3.1.1 Overview of teletraffic theory. 
The relation between the network traffic and energy consumption implies that it is a good idea to 
look a bit more into details of the network traffic nature. With other words, network traffic theory 
should ease the energy efficiency optimization. The object of the teletraffic theory is all types of 
traffic including data, telecommunication and network traffic (Reference [5]). The teletraffic 
theory addresses problems related to system design taking into account cost, GoS, traffic demand, 
capacity. Moreover, the teletraffic problems solve emergency problems which are caused by 
unexpected system overloading or faults. Emergency issues can be caused by technical faults.  
Mathematical mechanisms and methods are used in the teletraffic theory for building models of 
traffic demands, capacity and GoS requirements.  In the digital communication systems network 
traffic, data traffic and network load can be considered as one and the same thing. Network traffic 
can be generally defined as the volume of data passing thru 2 points in the network for a given 
period of time:

Network traffic systems can be optimized by the use of: 
 Network traffic control. The function of it is control, management and reducing of the 
network traffic. Those functions could be put into practice by the integration of scheduler. 
As a result of network traffic control network performance and parameters are improved. 
Congestion, latency and packet loss are minimized. Network traffic control is closely 
related to network parameters measurement and monitoring  and the knowledge of areas 
in the network which cause poor performance. One mechanism for Network traffic control 
is traffic shaping and policing. Basically, traffic shaping’s purpose is traffic bandwidth 
and burstiness limitation. Queuing and delaying of data transfer units is a common way 
for achieving bandwidth and burst limitation. Traffic policing implies prioritization of the 
data transfer units. This means that data can be dropped or low prioritized in case there is 
a difference between the actual data transfer parameters and some threshold parameters 
values that should not be reached.  
 Network traffic measurement and monitoring. It is used for quantitative evaluation of the 
traffic volume, parameters and type. 
 Network traffic simulation. It is a tool for measuring of the network efficiency. The 
complexity of the modern networks make the network analysis labored. There are many 
interconnected variables which are related with sophisticated laws. However, stochastic

 Network traffic generation model. Mathematical traffic generation model can be useful by 
itself for the network optimization.  As previously mentioned, a traffic generation model 
is a mathematical stochastic model of the network traffic.  There are different 
mathematical models used for traffic generation: greedy source model, Poisson traffic 
model, long tail traffic models, payload data model, standardized internet traffic models.

3.2.2 LTE BS energy efficiency. 
Here it is presented an overview of the typical BS building blocks and their relation to energy 
consumption. The base station EE is of a particular interest due to the large portion of energy 
consumed by it. Around 50% of the energy consumption comes from the BS (Reference [9]). 
Figure 9 represents the energy wasted by the main energy consuming blocks of an LTE BS.  
Namely these blocks are: 
 Pinput – the total electrical power that the BS consumes. It can be provided from the 
power grid or from renewable energy sources.  
 Cooling system – it is necessary for the air conditioning and heat regime stabilization of 
the BS sensitive electronics. 
 Main supply (AC-DC) – it rectifies the power grid sinusoidal voltage. After the 
rectification the shape of the voltage represents a half sinusoid which has to be filtered 
with the use of a filtering group. After filtering the voltage gets a DC shape and can be 
used for the powering purposes of the other power consuming blocks. 
 DC-DC – it is a DC to DC converting block. The baseband, RF, PA need DC voltages and 
currents of different value. Here comes the function of the DC-DC converters which 
basically take one DC voltage and convert it to another value DC voltage. The converted 
voltage can be higher or lower compared to the input voltage.  
 Baseband – it processes all the baseband related digital signals. 
 RF – it can be pointed out that the RF block deals with the transceiver functions of the 
BS. 
 PA – amplifies the RF signal to the power level needed to cover the antenna interface 
losses, the antenna losses and the transmission/reception path losses. 
 Antenna interface – represents the power losses caused by the attenuation of the coaxial 
cables which are implemented in one or other form for interfacing the PA and the antenna. 
In a RRH configuration the antenna interface losses are much smaller compared to the 
configuration without RRH. RRH configuration is also known as distributed configuration 
and the classical BS configuration is also known as integrated configuration. 
 Antenna – it is associated with a certain amount of power losses as well.  
 Pout – this is the useful output power generated from the BS. The complex 
electromagnetic waves generated from the BS are used for communication purposes. 
Unfortunately, the electromagnetic waves properties imply that the waves carry energy.

3.2.2.1 Baseband. 
Basically, the baseband unit functions are related to: modulation, demodulation, digital signal 
processing, RF signal processing. 
3.2.2.2 Radio frequency RF and transceiver. 
The etymology of the word transceiver comes from the combination of the two words transmit 
and receive. The function of the transceiver (TRx) is to transmit and receive RF signals in both 
DL and UL direction. From UE point of view the BS transmitter (Tx) is for DL and the BS 
receiver (Rx) is for UL. When receiving a signal from a given UE, the signal is being received by 
the antenna, the specific bandwidth is being filtered out and amplified to the needed level. The Rx 
amplifier has to have a certain signal-to-noise-and-interference-ratio (SINR) in order to make the 
amplified RF signal suitable for further demodulation and processing.  On the other hand, when 
transmitting to a given UE, the RF signal bandwidth is being filtered, the digital signal is being 
modulated and then amplified by the Tx. 
The transceiver is designed to meet the peak hour traffic load. This condition implies that the 
transceiver is not 100% efficient for traffic load outside of the peak hour. In order to increase the 
transceiver EE adaptive operation modes could be applied.  
 
3.2.2.3 Power amplifier PA. 
The function of the power amplifier is to amplify the power of the transmit RF signal. The signal 
coming from the transmitter has a low amplitude and correspondingly low electromagnetic 
power. The open air propagating RF signal suffers attenuation and for this reason it has to be 
amplified to a certain level. A large portion of the energy losses and energy inefficiency comes 
==PAGE:50==
36 
 
from the power amplifier. The complex RF signals, particularities of the semiconductor elements 
and impedance matching requirements make it very hard to optimize energy losses in the PA. The 
electric current flowing thru power transistors causes heating. This is the so called thermal effect 
of the electric current flowing thru semiconductors. That phenomenon is inevitable and it is 
extremely hard to be energy efficiency optimized. Typically, in a macro base station 
configuration the PA is the most energy consuming block. Another point is that the PA 
particularities show that this block has the highest potential for EE optimization. 
Key mechanisms for fighting PA inefficiency are: 
 Energy adaptive modes of operation. The principle of these modes is to make the 
consumed energy dependent on the BS traffic needs.  
 Sleep and shut-down modes of operation. Once again the idea is to make the consumed 
energy dependent on the BS traffic needs with the only difference that the PA can be 
totally shut-down or put into sleep mode. Sleep mode implies that in case of need the PA 
can be powered and ready for operation within a certain time period.  
 A theoretical solution to the PA overheating and cooling issues is the implementation of 
the unwanted heat energy into something useful. The idea is to take the thermal energy of 
the PA and transfer it where it is needed. This can be done in two ways. The first way is 
to convey the heat energy with a micro heat conductor. The second way is to transform 
the heat energy into electrical energy and then use it or transfer it where it is needed.

3.3.1 EE mechanisms related to antenna’s functions. 
 
3.3.3.1 RRH and energy efficiency. 
The RRHs allow energy efficiency improvement by limiting the energy loss in the BSs hardware. 
This is done in two ways: 
 RRHs does not waste additional energy for cooling. The RRH are designed in such a way 
that enables cooling by the surrounding air. 
 RRHs deployment avoids the use of long coaxial RF cables. The RF coaxial cables are 
associated with considerable energy loss due to attenuation. 
 
3.3.3.2 Energy efficient MIMO techniques. 
In the context of the global world society the demands for higher throughput and capacity are 
constantly growing. Consequently there is a constant need for mobile communication 
development. The increase in terms of capacity and throughput in the mobile communication 
systems can be achieved by the leveraging of the next few characteristics (Reference: [13]): 
 Increase of the number of BS per unit area. 
 Increase of the subcarrier radio channel BW. 
 Increase of the number of subcarriers radio channels. 
 Increase of the spectral efficiency (SE). 
Taking into account CAPEX, OPEX considerations and future possible changes in the mobile 
communication system it turns out that one of the most optimal solutions for satisfaction of the 
mobile communication needs is the improvement in efficiency of usage of the radio resources. 
With other words, the means for increase of the throughput per Herz in a single site is a key 
solution. In LTE such means for improvement of SE is the MIMO implementation. MIMO 
techniques are based on space time coding which uses multiple Tx and Rx antennas and it enables 
increase of the throughput and increase of SE. The Tx and Rx antennas configuration is organized 
in a way that minimizes the neighboring antenna correlation (Reference: [13]). The MIMO 
==PAGE:55==
41 
 
antenna configuration is described by ant [N, M] matrix. N represents the number of Tx antennas 
and M represents the number of Rx antennas (figure 10 )(Reference: [14]). LTE MIMO schemes 
depend on the antenna configuration: 
 SISO – single input single output. Actually, this scheme does not implement multiple Rx 
and Tx antennas but it is used in LTE. 
 SIMO – single input multiple outputs. It utilizes one Tx and multiple Rx antennas. 
 MISO – multiple input single output. It utilizes a few Tx antennas and one Rx antenna. 
 MIMO – multiple input multiple outputs. It can be pointed out that MIMO is a 
combination of SIMO and MISO. MIMO utilizes a number of Tx and Rx antennas. 
Moreover, LTE specifications define two MIMO modes of reception and transmition 
(Reference: [13]): 
o SU-MIMO – single user MIMO, where one user and a BS utilize a few Tx and 
Rx antennas.  
o MU-MIMO – multi user MIMO. In that mode a BS with a number of Tx and Rx 
antenna communicates with a few UEs and each UE can have one or more Tx/Rx 
antennas. 
In the transmitter and receiver sides in LTE MIMO multi element antennas or antenna cells can 
be used (Reference: [13]). These antennas can be used to concentrate the radio wave beam 
energy. Another point is that the same antennas can be used to change the 3D shape of the radio 
wave radiation pattern, for simplicity this is called beamforming. Moreover, such antenna 
configuration enables spatial multiplexing, where a number of parallel bit streams are formed and 
multiplexed.

3.3.3.3 Energy efficient beam forming and reconfigurable antennas in LTE. 
In general the antennas are characterized by their radiation pattern and the radiation pattern shows 
how the electromagnetic field of the antennas is situated in the surrounding space. The 3D shape 
of the radiation pattern mainly depends on: 
 The type of the antenna – dipole, half dipole, Yagi-Uda, sectorized, omnidirectional, 
reconfigurable, ferrite antenna etc.. 
 The placement of the antenna in the surrounding space – horizontally or vertically. 
The radiation pattern of the antennas can be changed if reconfigurable antennas are used. The 
LTE antennas are sectorized half wave dipole with reflectors. The angle between the reflectors 
and the dipole determine the shape of the radiation pattern and this is the underlying principle of 
the reconfigurable antennas. This can be beneficial in terms of EE (Reference: [16]).The antenna 
radiation pattern is related to the electromagnetic power distribution in the space. The EE is 
increased by distributing the needed amount of electromagnetic energy where it is needed. 
3.3.3.4 Energy efficient antenna muting techniques. 
LTE BSs capacity is increased by the addition of multiple transmitting (TX) antennas (ports). In 
this way the total number of users (UE) is divided between numbers of antennas. So each antenna 
is associated to a relatively limited number of users and this is advantageous in terms of BS 
capacity (Reference: [13]). For instance, LTE release 8 supports this multi-antenna transmission 
technique. However, multi-antenna transmission has energy efficiency issues. As previously 
mentioned, BSs are designed to support busy hours traffic needs. During the busy hours multi-
antenna transmission technique supports the capacity needs and the energy efficiency is high. 
However, out of the busy hours the capacity needs are lower. Out of the busy hours the multi-
antenna transmission equipment is still powered and it unnecessarily wastes energy. Finally, here 
comes the role of the antenna muting techniques (figure 11). Basically, the antenna muting 
techniques turn off the unnecessary antennas and the related to them BS blocks.

3.3.4 EE mechanisms related to BS’s functions.  
3.3.4.1 EE modes of operation and states of LTE BSs. 
The BS’s EE could be improved by applying different energy consumption modes to the BS. In 
each case the energy consumption modes are dependent on the data traffic served by the BS. 
There are many authors, companies, institutions that work in the area of LTE BS’s EE modes of 
operation and the different authors define different modes. For instance, different definitions of 
power modes are: sleep, power-off, stand-by, hibernate, dormant state of BS. In [17] the authors 
even define different sleep modes with different characteristics. The underlying principle in the 
EE modes of operation is the relation of the BS traffic and energy consumption. There are blocks 
inside the BS which can be powered off in case the traffic is low. For instance during off-peak 
hours the BS’s PA could be partially or completely turned off. Generally, what can be turned off 
to a certain extend is the BS as a whole or blocks inside the BS. 
3.3.4.2 EE scheduling algorithm optimization in LTE. 
Traffic scheduling is an important feature of LTE operation. Traffic scheduling is used for 
assignment of the limited shared resources to users and the scheduling algorithms are working in 
the DL direction. (Reference: [18]). Scheduling algorithm optimization is a matter of balance and 
there are many algorithms developed. Each algorithm is optimized for given conditions and each 
algorithm is best for its own design purpose. Generally, packet scheduling is based on throughput 
and fairness criteria. However, EE criteria can also be implemented in the scheduling algorithms. 
The algorithms have to be well balanced and none of the considered criteria should be impaired. 
Factors which influence the EE of the scheduling algorithms are: 
 Bandwidth 
==PAGE:58==
44 
 
 MCS 
 Channel conditions 
 Interference 
 Cell load 
 Throughput 
 Fairness 
One EE scheduling algorithm is the packet scheduling algorithm which allows for energy savings 
coming from reduced BS Tx power (Reference: [19]). Largely deployed scheduling algorithms 
are: Maximum Carrier to Interference Ratio (Max C/I), Round Robin (RR) and Proportionally 
Fair PF (Reference: [19]). A comparison of these scheduling algorithms based on EE criterion 
shows that Max C/I is the most beneficial (Reference: [19]). Besides, Max C/I is optimized for 
maximum carrier to interference ratio and Max C/I aims at limitation of the transmit power by 
nature. Limitation of the Tx power inevitably reduces energy consumption. However, Max C/I 
provides relatively lower levels of fairness. It appears that DL EE scheduling is an open field for 
future improvements since there are not many researches in this area (Reference: [19]). 
3.3.4.3 EE information caching near the BS in LTE. 
Another prospective mechanism for energy saving is the caching of information near the base 
stations. Transporting data from the origin source to the LTE BSs is energy hungry process 
(Reference: [20]). Reducing the volume of data transported to the BSs can lead to considerable 
energy savings. No doubt that information caching is a matter of precise balance. Complex 
algorithms are used for determination of the information to be cached. Moreover, another energy 
saving mechanisms such as sleep mode of BSs have impact of the information caching near the 
BSs. 
 
3.3.5 EE mechanisms related to the network’s functions. 
3.3.5.1 EE of LTE heterogeneous networks. 
A possible RAN design for expansion of the coverage and capacity is the Homogeneous Network 
design (HoNet), where the network consists of BSs of the same type. The HoNets can consist of 
Macro Cells although HoNets can be Micro Cell based. In recent years it became clear that the 
deployment of macro BS and HoNets for capacity and coverage provisioning is associated with 
significant energy consumption (Reference: [21]). An EE proved solution for the reduction of the 
energy consumption is the utilization of heterogeneous networks (HetNet)(Figure 12). LTE 
Advanced design includes HetNet which take the advantage of the co-operation of macro eNBs, 
micro, pico and femto BSs, and relay nodes (RNs) (Reference: [21]). In that HetNet the high 
energy consuming macro eNBs are used for outdoor coverage. Micro, pico, femto cells consume 
less energy compared to the macro cell and they are used mainly for capacity and throughput 
enforcement. Micro, pico, femto cells are suitable for areas with high concentration of users and 
==PAGE:59==

cell-edge areas with issues related to the overlapping of the cells. HetNets and small cells 
deployment have numerous advantages related to EE; there are possibilities of implementation of 
renewable energy sources; TCO, CAPEX and OPEX can be improved; Capacity and Coverage 
can be enhanced; the air pollution can be reduced (Reference: [21]).

3.3.5.2 EE self-organized networks and load balancing. 
Load balancing and self-organized networks (SON) are introduced in the mobile network’s 
design in order to improve utilization of radio resources, cell load characteristics, throughput, 
efficiency and user’s GoS (Reference: [22]). However, load balancing has also impact on the 
energy consumption and EE. There is a subtle balance between EE from one side and cell load, 
throughput, efficiency, GoS etc. from other side. One example of load balancing is a case where: 
 There is a network with unevenly distributed traffic among the cells. Respectively part of 
the cells are heavily loaded while the rest of the cells are relatively lightly loaded.  
 The edge users from the heavily loaded cells can be assigned to neighboring lightly 
loaded cells. In this way the load burden is shifted to the lightly loaded cells.  
There are EEnt SONs and load balancing algorithms developed which aim not only load 
balancing but also take into account energy consumption. One possible algorithm takes into 
account the possible near future performance of a target cell for load balancing (Reference: [22]). 
The proposed algorithm uses an EE scaling factor for evaluation of the energy savings and 
performance characteristics of the target cell. Two underlying principles in SON are EE and load 
balancing. As previously mentioned, EE can be improved by taking into account the 24 hour 
==PAGE:60==
46 
 
traffic pattern of the BSs and putting the BSs into EE mode of operation. In this way lightly 
loaded BSs can be dynamically switched to an EE mode of operation, BS’s blocks can be 
powered down and energy consumption can be reduced (Reference: [22]). Load balancing 
algorithms can be based on (Reference: [22]): 
 Handover approach. The main principle is to handover users from heavily loaded cells to 
lightly loaded cells.  
 Cell breathing approach. The principle of coverage area shaping and resizing by Tx power 
control is used. The Tx power of the cell’s sectors define the size and shape of the cells 
and the users under coverage as well. 
 Proportional fairness approach. The underlying principle is based on mathematical 
formulation of proportional fairness in a wide network context. However, this approach 
does not take into account EE optimization. 
 Game theory approach. Game theory principles are used to formulate the network 
balancing. EE is not considered as a factor. 
 Custom parameters approach. The principle here is to take into consideration a few 
prioritized parameters for the load balancing algorithm. For instance frequency reuse and 
load balancing perfectly fit together in a load balancing algorithm.  
Although different, all of these approaches aim at load balancing, where users are being 
handovered from heavily loaded cells to lightly loaded cells. However, this particularity 
contradicts to the EE principle. Load balancing aims at having users equally distributed among 
network’s BSs and EE aims at having more BSs put into energy saving mode of operation. As a 
consequence load balancing requires more BSs turned on and EE requires more BSs turned into 
energy saving mode. Apparently there is a need for trade-off between EE and load balancing. 
Consequently either EE or load balancing can be prioritized (Reference: [22]). 
3.3.5.3 EE physical random access channel (PRACH) optimization and radio 
resource allocation optimization. 
The user needs regarding the mobile communication systems are mainly related to DL data 
communication. Large portion of the mobile communication systems resources are focused on 
supporting the DL communication (Reference: [23]). Consequently, there is a large field for EE 
improvements in the DL communication and the main efforts for EE optimization are 
concentrated in the DL. In order to optimize the overall mobile communication system EE 
nothing should be underestimated and each possible aspect should be considered. Such possible 
aspects are the optimization of the communication when connection is being established; 
synchronized; interrupted or handovered. Moreover, the EE optimization of the signaling 
communication can have an impact on the overall EE. The UL communication mechanisms could 
be EE optimized as well and in recent years efforts were put in this direction (Reference: [23]). 
More specifically, the random access procedure energy consumption optimization is considered 
as an effective solution. Random access procedure is actually active even when no user data is 
==PAGE:61==
47 
 
communicated. Recent studies show that in some cases with specific setup conditions energy 
savings coming from random access channel optimization can reach up to 82% (Reference: [23]). 
The random access procedure in LTE is performed in the physical random access channel 
(PRACH). Basically, the PRACH slots are strongly related to cell’s load and therefore there is 
interrelation between PRACH, cell load and EE. Once again the balancing of these factors can 
lead to a significant energy savings and at the same time performance is not impaired.   
3.3.5.4 EE cloud RAN (CRAN) implementation. 
One of the optimizations which CRAN can employ is the relative limitation of the number of BS 
(Reference: [24]). In CRAN the number of BBUs in the BBU pool is smaller than the number of 
BSs in the classical architecture. Centralized processing in BBU pool influences directly system 
efficiency and energy efficiency. Temperature conditioning equipment and some BS energy 
consuming equipment can be reduced which also improves energy efficiency. BBU pooling 
makes it possible to put BBUs in sleep, stand-by or power-off mode during off-the-peak hours. 
At the same time coverage is not negatively affected.

energy sources. For instance, their direct implementation in the BSs decreases the energy 
consumed from the power grid and increases the overall energy efficiency.

3.3.7 EE mechanisms related to the UE.  
3.3.7.1 UE EE. 
UE energy consumption influences the overall EE of the mobile communication system. The 
energy consumption of the UE can be divided in two sections: 
==PAGE:62==
48 
 
 UE energy consumption related to mobile communication system. 
 UE energy consumption not related to mobile communication system. 
The energy consumption related to mobile communication system is the energy wasted for 
powering the UE mobile communication blocks. Such blocks are: Baseband, RF, Power 
Management, Audio Amplifier, Processor etc. (Reference: [25]) The UE energy consumption not 
related to mobile communication system can be caused by a number of applications and the 
associated blocks. For instance playing an off-line game, playing internally saved multimedia 
files, using graphical applications are all activities which waste energy. The EE of the UE can be 
expressed by the battery lifetime (Reference: [26]). The battery lifetime depends on the energy 
consuming processes: data communication, interfacing blocks usage (including display usage), 
applications etc. Improvement of the UE EE must not affect UE or mobile communication system 
performance. One way of improving UE EE is the optimization of the UE’s cellular subsystem 
and the cellular network itself (Reference: [26]).  The analysis of the LTE UE’s blocks 
consumption shows that the power consumption and EE are mostly associated with the levels of 
Tx and Rx powers and data rates (Reference: [26]). In general higher data rates appear to be more 
energy efficient. Another way for EE optimization is the application of Discontinuous Reception 
(DRX) mode of operation of the LTE UE. The DRX method is based on the dynamic powering of 
the UE’s communication blocks and this method enables low power consumption in sleep mode. 
An open field for EE improvement of UE is the limitation of the wasted energy while the UE is 
not in sleep mode.

A few conclusions could be made:  
 For this conclusion there are three conditions which have to be accounted : 
- One of the macro BS’s blocks is strongly linearly related to the traffic. 
- The power consumption of the rest of the BS’s blocks is constant and it is not strongly 
related to the traffic. 
- The aim is overall ECE and EE maximization. 
It could be concluded that in that case ECE and EE maximization inevitably leads to 
underutilization of the capacity potential of the macro BS. 
 For this conclusion there are again three conditions which have to be accounted : 
- 
Nearly linear relation between traffic and ECE. 
- 
Small deviation of the ECE. 
- 
Large deviation of the traffic. 
In that case it can be concluded that ECE maximization is not generally the optimal 
solution. The traffic and ECE have to be balanced for optimal results.  
 Another conclusion is that for ECE and EE maximization radical approach should be 
implemented. It appears that the power consumption improvement of a single block 
could have significant drawbacks.  
 ECE has to be as close to 1 as possible and it has to be independent from the traffic for 
EE maximization.  
 For EE maximization EE can be both dependent and independent from the traffic. There 
is a number of theoretical dependencies which could exist. One case is when the EE is 
high, the traffic values are high and the EE is not dependent on the traffic. Second case is 
when EE is high, traffic is low and EE is not dependent on the traffic. Third case is when 
EE is low traffic is high and EE is not dependent on the traffic. Fourth case is when EE is 
low, traffic is low and EE is not dependent on the traffic. Basically, the possible 
combinations of dependencies are: EE low/high; traffic low/high; EE dependent or 
independent from the traffic. Thus the theoretically possible combination of 
dependencies is C=2^3=8.

Tables 7 and 8 summarize the design parameters and results of Okumura-Hata model. The model 
can be applied to LTE 900 MHz and LTE 2100 MHz frequency bands. Four different area types 
are defined – rural, sub urban, urban and dense urban area types. These area types have different 
characteristics which result in difference in the maximum affordable path loss. As a result for 
these area types the maximum affordable path loss has different values. Moreover, the area types 
comprise different portions of the total area. LTE 900 MHz and LTE 2100 MHz can be used 
simultaneously in a Hetnet. As a result coverage and capacity are boosted without compromising 
the SINR and respectively interference generation is reduced. EE and Hetnet scenarios 
dependencies are later analyzed. As already mentioned, the presented design tables take into 
account different area types and specific parameters related to the area type. It can be pointed out 
that Okumura-Hata model for 900MHz gives the smallest number of eNBs. This result is related 
to the properties of the radio waves. Generally, 900MHz radio waves attenuate less than 2100 
MHz radio waves. This effect is caused by a number of factors one of which is the proportion of 
the wavelength and the size of the air molecules. At the same time Okumura-Hata 900MHz band 
provides fewer capacity resources because the cell density is not as high as the Okumura-Hata 
2100. As a conclusion it can be pointed out that LTE 900 is more appropriate for coverage 
provisioning.  As already mentioned, the coverage models are based on the RLB parameters from

three cases provide sufficient coverage. However, Walfisch-Ikegami 2100 provides the most 
capacity. Walfisch-Ikegami model is more precise in the coverage estimation for urban areas. It 
takes into account specific urban parameters and as a result it gives more reliable output data.

areas.  
 LMAPL is then applied to Okumura-Hata model for urban and dense urban areas. 
 It is considered that 30% of the total area (187     ) is covered by micro cells. The same 
amount of area (30% of the total area (187     )) is considered for the pico cells as well. 
As a result, the coverage model gives a RAN parameters more closely related to EE. Moreover, 
the coverage model provides means of relation of the different cell types and the area types. 
Macro cells are supposed to provide mainly outdoor coverage. For this reason it is possible to 
take advantage of the coverage model for rural areas. Microcells are supposed to provide 
coverage and enhanced capacity. For this reason it makes sense to apply the urban area coverage 
model to the micro cell BSs. Picocells are usually deployed in areas with significant capacity 
needs, indoor or outdoor. The closest match to the picocells characteristics in the coverage model 
is the dense urban area design characteristics. 
Figure 26 displays the EE vs BS density for the three types of BSs. As previously mentioned, it 
can be noted that the EE metric prefix is (hecto bps)/W; hbps/W. This prefix is chosen for the 
ease of comparison and it represents hundreds of bps per Watt. Moreover, for the three types of 
BSs there are three different values of the EE. The 3 different values of the EE contribute to the 
overall RAN EE. It can be concluded that the mix of different types of BSs which serve different 
design purposes increase their functional efficiency in general. More particularly the mix of 
different types of BSs increase the overall RAN efficiency. As a result of the Okumura-Hata 
designation in a Hetnet there are different BS densities for the three types of BS. Surprisingly the 
smallestt BS density is obtained for the Micro BSs. Generally, this result is related to the 
frequency band used, the BS’s antenna height, the MS transmit power, the maximal affordable 
path loss and the coverage model design considerations. Not to forget to mention that the Micro 
BSs are related to the urban coverage model designation. Micro BSs have intermediate EE value 
which is related to the proportion of the consumed power and the maximal bitrate supported at 
the cell edges (Table 10). Macro BSs have intermediate BSs density and the lowest EE. Pico BSs 
provide the highest EE and the highest BS density.  However, it would be inappropriate to base 
outdoor coverage on picocells only. Figure 27 displays the total bitrates provided by the three 
types of BSs at cell edges (blue line) and the total power consumption of the three types of BSs 
(brown line) vs the BSs densities for the three types of BSs. It can be pointed out that the Micro 
BS provides coverage at 30% of the total area with total bitrate of all the Micro BSs equal to 
0.63Gbps. All the Micro BSs power consumption is 30.4kW.  Furthermore, the Micro BSs 
number is the smallest – 152 versus 525 for the Macro BSs and 753 for the Pico BSs. The Macro 
BSs power consumption is 1.1MW which is relatively high value. At the same time the Macro 
BSs bitrates are 2.2Gbps which is comparable with the bitrate provided by the Pico BSs. Not to 
forget to mention that the Macro BSs are covering 100% the whole area - 625 square kilometers

Another point is that SINR and EE should be more precisely related for EE maximization. Signal 
to noise plus interference ratio is vital for EE due a number of reasons. Generally, lower transmit 
power of a group of cells would increase EE and decrease neighboring cells interference. The 
noise level which is not generated by the mobile communication equipment should not be 
underestimated as a factor as well. However, it is questionable if noise level reduce is generally a 
good idea. There are many other systems operating in the 900/2100 MHz bands and not all of 
these systems can be liquidated. Consequently, the noise generated from these systems cannot be 
totally liquidated.

4.3.2 Capacity planning and EE.

these models is to provide radio coverage data. The capacity planning aims at providing a view of 
the capacity requirements in a network. In reality both coverage and capacity requirements have 
to be fulfilled and accounted. Moreover, EE maximization is of a particular interest. One network

4.4 Summary. 
This chapter is related to the EE analysis and maximization of the LTE RAN and BSs. Metrics of 
EE and ECE are presented at first place. The EE and ECE metrics and the interdependent 
parameters are crucial for the EE/ECE maximization. Moreover, it is essential to try to find new 
dependencies and interrelations which contribute to the overall RAN and BS EE. Furthermore, 
maximization of EE might not be the optimal solution. In that case a more detailed view on the 
balancing parameters and dependencies is of interest. Another point mentioned in the metrics 
subsection is the importance of the level at which EE is improved. For instance, the levels of EE 
improvement may be semiconductor elements level, BS’s building blocks level, network level, 
system level etc. At second place in this chapter it is represented LTE macro BS EE analysis. A 
real macro BS data is considered for the analysis. However, due to lack of sufficient statistical 
==PAGE:96==
82 
 
data from MNO the analysis is based on a number of assumptions and approximations.  
Nevertheless, the principles and the logics applied are correct and some of the numbers are a 
closest match to the reality. In the LTE macro BS EE analysis sub point there are presented  and 
analyzed dependencies between the BS traffic, BS consumed power, BS’s blocks consumed 
power, EE and ECE. One important conclusion based on this sub point is that in general the EE 
maximization in some cases is not the optimal solution and balance of other parameters is more 
important. At third place in this chapter are presented and analyzed dependencies between radio 
coverage models, network structure and EE. There are number of input dimensioning parameters 
which define the RLB and the coverage models output dimensioning parameters. Initially BS 
power consumption and EE are not considered as input dimensioning parameters. However, their 
relation with the coverage models is of interest and a sort of relation was established. 
Furthermore, an EE analysis of the results of the coverage models is presented. Another point is 
that Okumura-Hata coverage model is modified in order to relate EE, network structure, bitrates 
and power consumption. Analysis of this relation is presented as well. A graphical comparison of 
Okumura-Hata and modified Okumura-Hata is presented. Another point presented in this chapter 
is the dependency between capacity considerations, RAN structure and EE. A Hetnet is 
considered as a base for the analysis and comparison. There are two different cases considered. In 
the first case the relative number of Macro BSs in a Hetnet is constant and in the second case this 
number changes in function of the Micro BSs number. As a result there are observed different 
mathematical dependencies and relations which could be used in the RAN lifecycle phases.

5. Conclusion. 
The materials presented in this paper are directed at the energy efficiency of mobile networks and 
more precisely the focus of this paper is the EE of LTE RAN. The master’s thesis structure is 
built as follows: LTE RAN planning and design; LTE energy efficiency and EE analysis. In order 
to ease the EE analysis the related information is structured in the aforementioned fashion. In the 
LTE RAN planning and design chapter the key points are: RAN lifecycle; RAN design, planning 
and dimensioning; RLB and fundamental parameters; radio coverage models. In the LTE energy 
efficiency chapter the key points are: teletraffic theory and fundamental principles; network 
traffic and energy consumption; EE of LTE BS and EE of LTE RAN; LTE energy efficient 
mechanisms. In the EE analysis chapter the key points are: EE metrics; LTE Macro BS EE 
analysis and EE of LTE RAN. The general conclusion is that there is a lot of room for 
improvement of the LTE EE. There are many mechanisms and details that should be accounted. 
No doubt that in the future the LTE EE will be increased by combining, synchronizing and 
coordinating the current knowledge about LTE EE. One of the main goals in the presented paper 
is to propose means for EE improvement. As a conclusion to this statement it can be pointed out 
that for EE improvement the EE itself has to be accounted in each phase of the RAN lifecycle. 
Moreover, one conclusion related to the analysis chapter is that for a LTE BS EE improvement 
the EE can be improved at the cost of impaired bitrate. During the finalization stage of this paper 
it became more clear that for EE maximization it is essential to look into the most fundamental 
principles and dependencies of the wireless access network. The wireless part of the system 
serves the main purpose of the mobile communication system and for this reason it is important 
to clarify the limiting factors and the theoretical boundaries. Unfortunately, the EE analysis 
presented is based on a number of assumptions which makes it incorrect to a certain extend. 
However, a strong side of the presented analysis is that fundamental principles and dependencies 
are followed as much as possible. Hopefully this paper can find an application as a starting point 
for the definition of more precise models for EEnt coverage and capacity estimation. Another 
possible application is the more precise definition of EE in terms of ECE. The material can be 
used for EE Hetnet design as well. One topic for possible future research is the estimation of the 
usefulness of EE improvements in each phase of the network lifecycle. Another interesting area 
for future research is the estimation of the usefulness of systems with high EE and low 
supportable bitrates. Last but not least, an interesting question is if the maximum supportable 
LTE bitrates are going to be restricted by the maximum allowable radiated power. If the EE and 
bitrates reach tremendous levels the radiated by the antennas power will be significantly 
increased as well. Then a limitation will be the maximum allowable power radiated from the 
antennas which is safe for the human population.
