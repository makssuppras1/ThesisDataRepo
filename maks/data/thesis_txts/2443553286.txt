Abstract
This project deals with the problems of sub-optimal blood glucose regulation in peo-
ple with type 1 diabetes and with outdated standards when it comes to logging food
intake leading up to an appointment at the diabetes ambulatory.
The project employs a lean UX design approach with multi-step validation through
predominantly qualitative user feedback.
The design process finalized with an application prototype, which allows quick food
tracking coupled with CGM data to show information about key settings in BGL
regulation.

1.1
Background
The newest reports show that 29.457 people in Denmark at the start of 2017 were
diagnosed with type 1 diabetes (T1D). T1D accounts for roughly 10% of all cases of
diabetes [1], but while largely outnumbered by type 2 diabetics, type 1 is in all cases a
chronic disease. It requires a lot of time and understanding to manage properly. T1D
not managed properly increase the risk of long term complications. The consequences
are severe and can in worst cases include cardiovascular diseases, kidney problems,
amputations and blindness [2].
T1D is managed by keeping track of and managing blood glucose levels (BGLs) by
injecting insulin with either an insulin-pen or insulin-pump. Doing that, allows glu-
cose to move from the blood stream to the cells [3].
Eating carbohydrates will cause the BGL to rise, which makes it important to cor-
rectly estimate the amount. Most diabetics count carbohydrates [4] (estimates using
different methods) which then decides how much insulin is injected for the meal.
Making estimations is much faster than the alternative tools such as weighing and
calculating carbohydrate content or scanning barcodes of food items and entering
macros for every single item eaten into an application. Estimations while easier and
faster have shown to be lacking in accuracy [5] [6] [7].
T1D healthcare professionals in Denmark will sometimes ask the diabetic to fill out a
diabetes diary with information like food intake, exercise and mood in the days/weeks
leading up to an appointment. There is in the healthcare system no official digital
tool or application to do this. The common procedure has been to use ones own tool
of preference or use forms handed out by the ambulatory or printed out forms from
diabetes focused websites [8]. Writing everything down with pen and paper is incon-
venient as many do not carry that around in their daily lives. The numerical data is

1.2
Motivation
A potential way of resolving some of the mentioned problems could be to use a mix of
modern technologies to design a product that can help T1 diabetics help themselves.
The amount of people with CGMs is on the rise, which means that not only is the
potential for helping people take better care of themselves higher, but the motivation
is intrinsic from the individuals themselves. If it is possible to motivate people by
success-stories within this realm of diabetes-care, it could affect the willingness of
others to be more responsible for their own health.
1.3
Scope
Many factors play a role in BGL regulation. Some matter more in terms of BGL
variation and some are hard to track due to a lack of measurability. This field will
be explored to define which factors makes most sense be of focus in the project.
For the application to provide valuable information to the user, it requires the user
to actually use the application to track their intake. For them to do that requires the
process from tracking intake to being able to view information to be worth it. There
is therefore a natural order in which that final design can best be achieved. The
food logging part of the application needs to be at a level where people would use
it. That process in itself can be time consuming and but will at the point of design
and prototyping be the start focus in this project. The connection with a CGM and
information presentation will although equally important to the final goal, be the
secondary endeavour.
Investigations of pain points and validation of the problems described [1.1] will be
part of defining which areas to focus on and which can be delimited.
The goals of the project can therefore be summed up to be, first of all creating a
tool for people with T1D to painlessly log their daily food intake and present in an
informative and valuable way.
The target group for such a tool is immediately people with T1D and healthcare
professionals based on the standards discovered. The target area might change based
on problem and market validation results and investigative findings.

The outline of the project will be as follows:
The Investigation chapter is of descriptive nature. It presents an overview of the
topics deemed most relevant to research when dealing with the problems described in

[1.2]. It also presents an overview of the current and alternative methods used to
solving the problem.
The Analysis chapter is where methodology and target group is defined and used
to analyze the findings from the Investigation. The analysis will form requirements
for the design of a solution. The requirements will be validated before being used as
building blocks for the first prototype.
In Design and Prototyping, scoping of the requirements will allow for the build
of a prototype. Users will test the prototype and measurements will be recorded.
Learning from the analysis of measurements gives way for valid adjustments. This is
an iterative process and will conclude when the prototype is ready for a test.
The Testing chapter is where the final test procedures and execution is described. It
will be a separate test from the iterative design process, to delimit bias.
The Results and Discussion chapter will be where the output of the test is pre-
sented and discussed in order to determine what the final outcome of the project and
research question is. The discussion will also touch on other parts of the project that
in retrospect should have been different or if other unsuspected factors might have
had an impact on the results.
The Conclusion and Future Work chapters is where the project will be summed
up and conclusive findings will be presented. There will then be some thoughts and
ideas on how the topic could be taken further in the future, and if anything would be
particularly interesting to look into.

problem and a great way to figure out how to proceed.

and talk to them for 1-1½ hours at either their homes or at a restaurant/café, in both
cases over a meal.

Like mentioned, the interview was pilot tested beforehand to ensure that questions
were understandable and to get an idea of how long the interview would last. It was
tested on my wife, who also has T1D, making the lessons learned more viable.
Out of the 6 interviewees every person have had experience with using a diary. It
seems clear that a diary is used in situations where a person is new to T1D or is hav-
ing trouble regulating their BGL. This information coupled with frustrations about
paper diaries and time consuming food tracking applications indicates that there is
both a problem and a market for a solution. The sheer number of diabetics and the
fact that more and more get diagnosed every year [11] is a quantitative indicator that
the market will remain.
From the interviews, it is seen that many different factors can influence a persons
BGL. It is also very different how often people go to control visits depending on the
success of their regulation. Furthermore peoples understanding of and reasoning for
changing settings related to BGL regulation varies a lot. From all of this information
it seems relevant to investigate broadly what T1D is and how it works, BGL influence
factors and the system surrounding it all.
2.2
What is diabetes
When most people hear the word diabetes they associate it with obesity and unhealthy
eating. While that is understandable, because it is usually the most discussed in the
media, it is not as clear cut. Diabetes is usually divided into type 1 and type 2. Type
2 diabetes is most often what is discussed in the media, because it is by far the most
frequent (80% of diagnosed diabetics), where type 1 accounts for 10%. It is also type
2, where healthy eating and living can make the biggest difference [3].
This project is focused on type 1 diabetics, who all have to measure blood glucose
levels and take insulin.
Type 1 diabetes (T1D), is an autoimmune disorder, which in layman terms means
that something in the body is seen by the body as not belonging to it. It is the
same function the human body uses to combat virus and bacterial infections.
In
the case of T1D, the cells of the pancreas that produces the hormones insulin and
glucagon is attacked. Insulin and glucagon are the two hormones that respectively
are responsible for lowering and heightening blood glucose levels in the bloodstream.
Insulin lowers the blood glucose level by allowing it to pass from the bloodstream to
the cells. Insulin is therefore crucial to staying energized and functioning. Glucagon
then helps when BGLs get too low, in transforming glycogen stores in the body to
become glucose and restoring BGLs to a healthy state.
A little bit of insulin is required for the body to stay functioning and larger amounts
are needed when carbohydrates are consumed. Carbohydrates are part of many food
items and will be broken down by the body to glucose.
Glucose will then make

the BGL rise. Carbohydrates vary in complexity, which has an effect on the speed of
change in BGLs. Insulin is injected into the body either by pen or pump to counteract

glucose level gets too low, food with carbohydrates is used to make it rise again.
An insulin chock is the result of too much insulin. The individual will passes out and
likely go into seizure. A glucagon pen can be used, which forces the body to use the
release glycogen stores in the body and make the BGL rise again.
T1D can develop at any point during a persons life, but is in 80% of cases diagnosed
between ages 1 and 15 [12]. It is a lifelong disease that requires a level of acceptance
and attention to manage properly.

2.2.3
Good and bad regulation
Regulating blood glucose levels is not always the same. It would be incorrect to say
definitively that a well regulated diabetic is good at regulation, or on the contrary,
that a poorly regulated one is bad at regulating. There are many factors that play
a role, but understanding diabetes and learning to regulate properly is of course in
any case important in the long run. New diabetics might still have their pancreas
working to a decent extend, which is called the honeymoon phase. During this it can
be quite easy to regulate blood glucose, because the body helps a lot. It can in some
cases cause people to not take it very seriously. On the other side, people who are
good at regulating, might be limited to do so with a good result, because of some of
the other diseases often associated with T1D. These include thyroid disorders, celiac
disease and addison’s syndrome [17]. Having other diseases like these that for exam-
ple delimit what you can eat, or how your body responds to treatment based on other
factors like stress, fatigue or the like can make it very hard to regulate correctly.
One of the reasons why diabetics often go to control visits at the ambulatory is to
check for bad regulation, which can show in several ways. Changes in blood pres-
sure, cholesterol or feet problems are taken very seriously because these are some
areas where late complications can become severe. Diabetics can have bad blood
circulation in the feet due to blood vessels narrowing and hardening. This can make
it hard to feel touch on the feet, which in turn can make them not feel pain, an-
noyance, even open wounds. Combine that with a reduced ability to heal and high
risk of infections, can become very serious and in worst cases end in amputations [18].
Before it comes to such complications though, signs of bad regulation shows in the

In Denmark the goals are not set by age groups, but rather the individual. Like
described, many things can influence the difficulty of being well regulated. Having
rather frequent control visits makes it possible for the doctor to know the intricacies
of the patients regulation and set a goal that is both realistic and can help as a mo-
tivational factor to improve. Setting a goal too low or not taking into consideration
other factors can result in diabetics losing hope and will to try and improve. This is
unfortunately a quite common topic in T1D forums and communities.
An infographic about HbA1c and rough target values in Denmark can be seen in
appendix [C], which interestingly show that it is more acceptable for elderly people
to have higher values [20], whereas in England it is the exact opposite. The reason
might be that it in Denmark is realized that elderly people who have had diabetes
for a long time did not have the tools available today. They are more likely to have
complications because of that today, which makes it harder for them to regulate
their blood glucose. In England they might have the same realization, but instead
of setting a realistic goal, try to force it as low as possible. The reason could be
in the hope of slowing down the long term complications of the elderly. In the end,
intentions are undoubtedly to do the best for the patients, but with different methods.

read the signs that the person might have high or low blood glucose levels. Pictures

If suspecting high or low levels it is a common rule to always check it to be on the
safe side. Most diabetics can also feel when levels are rising or falling fast, and can
thereby react and get it back on track within a decent time frame. Modern technology
in the CGM devices can have a big part to play in this area as alarms can be set to
go off when BGLs go beyond a target range set by the individual. A continues graph
of BGLs can also be produced, which can help in identifying when settings might
need to be changed. There are three major settings, which is what the doctor will
also be looking at and change at control visits if needed. Many diabetics struggle
with these and let the doctor do it, while others take control and change themselves
when needed. The difference between the groups seem to be largely the ability to
understand what the settings mean, how they affect their regulation and when to
change them. The three setting are:
Basal rates and time blocks:
Basal rates are small amounts of insulin that is given throughout the day. The body
of a person without T1D also releases small amounts throughout the day with poten-
tial valleys and peaks mostly based on time of day and other hormone levels [21]. The
body needs this insulin to constantly feed the cells with energy to make us function
normally. Basal rates is an attempt to mimic the function of a normal person. Time
blocks are used to be able to set different rates at different times. The reason why it
==PAGE:38==
24
2 Investigation
can be difficult to adjust basal rates correctly, is that there are so many other factors
like the ones described in this very section that can influence why BGLs might rise or
fall. There are some common rules and guidelines, as to how to go about adjusting
basal rates. The first one is to try and keep it as simple as possible with as few time
blocks as possible. The reason behind this is that complex basal rates are hard to
change and see through for both the diabetic and healthcare professionals. Complex
basal rates are also not how rates in normal people function, and most often a result
of other BG influential factors mistaken as a need for changing basal rates. Many
people who do adjust their basal rates, and especially newer diabetics often make the
mistake of changing the rate at the specific time of day where they have identified
a need for change. This is duo to them thinking of basal rate working like a bolus
injection (taking insulin for a meal). The difference is that bolus injections are large
amounts of insulin in almost no time, where basal rates are small doses injected over
the length of the entire day. This means that when treating a peak, the basal rate
should be increased in the hours leading up to the peak. One way to go about setting
a basal rate and checking it for changes once in a while is doing some fasting periods.
This requires the diabetic to not eat, drink, exercise or do other things that might
influence BGLs for some hours leading up to the timeframe marked for testing. It
can only be a couple of hours that can be tested at a time, as longer fasting would
influence the results in a non-measurable way making the results invalid. The fasting
BGL should be checked often or registered with a CGM and basal rates can be set
according to how it looks.
Insulin sensitivity:
Like the words suggest, insulin sensitivity is how effectively your body can use insulin.
In Danish measurements it is a ratio which shows how many mmol/L your BGL falls
when taking 1U of insulin. Insulin sensitivity, like basal rates can also change a bit
during the span of a day, and can therefore also be set differently over time blocks.
This ratio is used in connection when correcting BGLs and is therefore adjusted if
trends around those situation are spotted. An example could be when BGL correc-
tions (not in relation to a meals) have a tendency to stay at a high level or fall to a
low level after having leveled out.
Carbohydrate to insulin ratio:
This ratio shows how many carbohydrates it take to counteract 1U of insulin. This
ration is used when calculating how much insulin is needed when consuming carbo-
hydrates. Similar to the other two, this ratio can also change throughout a day. It
should be adjusted with tendencies of high or low BGLs show after the bolus injec-
tions graph has leveled out. This means that only the steady BGL, which shows
after most of the insulin’s effect have worn off, can be used to estimate if changes
are needed. It is difficult, especially for people without a CGM, because they cannot
effectively see where on the graph they are (if the BGL is steady or if its still trending
down after a meal).
==PAGE:39==

Assuming that all the settings talked about above are correctly set, the biggest factor
for keeping a steady BGL is correctly estimating how many carbohydrates are in the
food and drink that is consumed. Most diabetics learn to count carbohydrates by feel.
They quickly learn that in certain items there is a certain amount and use estimations
based on size of items or portion size. For things that seem similar to other things,
they often assume that they have the same or at least close to the same carbohydrate
content. It is different from person to person and also between countries what method
of carbohydrate counting is typically used, and how much work the individual put into
doing it accurately. Studies have shown that counting carbohydrates, while achieved
to a reasonable degree, is not a fail proof system, and that continues education on
the subject appears necessary [7]. Some diabetics do weigh all the food that they
eat and calculate very accurate amounts of carbohydrates, even considering things
like fat and fiber content, which also affects how carbohydrates are digested. Doing
that though does take an enormous amount of time and effort, which most diabetics
don’t seem to think is worth the effort compared to the margin of error there might be.
Exercise can make BGLs fall, because the body more effectively uses insulin. The
time frame where this is the case can differentiate quite a bit from person to person.
There are other factors that can have an impact on BGL management, but to a lesser
degree than the ones described. They will not be covered as they are not influential
enough and will therefore not be used in the project. Some of the factors are: Stress
levels, adrenalin, medication and mood.

2.2.5
Subconclusion
In this section the basic workings of T1D was explained, in particular in terms of
what the disease does and how BGL regulation is a central component in managing
it and reducing long term complications.
The most common and central tools for managing T1D on a day to day basis was
presented explaining their function and how they might be of use in the project. It
was deduced that the combination of insulin-pump, CGM and smartphone could po-
tentially produce valuable information.
A dive into the practices of how the public healthcare system works revealed tools
are administered based on the doctors evaluation of what is best for the patient. It
also presented the short review of the application ’Kulhydrat’ and a typical example
of a diabetes diary that would be used up to an appointment. Both could be argued
to be ineffective.
The basics of BGL regulation was investigated and showed that a common indicator
for overall regulation is HbA1c, but also that many factors can influence how difficult
it is to manage. It was discovered that the most influential factor in regulation is
estimation of carbohydrates eaten during meals. Furthermore the understanding of
and ability to change settings like basal rates, insulin sensitivity and carbohydrate
ratio can make a big difference.
Finally it was investigated what the data looked at by the doctor at control visits
show. It was found that there is quite a big difference between the value of the data
from a patient with and without a CGM.
From the investigative findings about different aspects of T1D, it can be concluded
that the focus will be on diabetics with a CGM, due to the following reasons. The
first reason is that to get a CGM, people have to evaluated by the doctor to be
motivated to improve their regulation. They are also evaluated to have the ability to
do so and have a regulation that can benefit from it. This means that the people are
not perfectly regulated and potential betterment would be able to be registered.
Analysis is required to determine exactly what aspects of BGL regulation is most
important and best to use in a solution. It needs to be considered in connection
with target group, technological possibility, usability and validated by professional
healthcare individuals. This will be included in the Analysis chapter [3].

Keto refers the metabolic state ketosis, which is a state where
the body starts burning fat as a way of fueling the body. This
state is achieved by reducing or cutting carbohydrates from your diet or simply run-
ning out of glycogen stores to be used as fuel. A keto and low carbohydrate diet is
therefore a diet that focuses on losing weight by burning fat as fuel, instead of sugar.
This is simply achieved by cutting way down on carbohydrates and focusing on the
other two macros of fat and protein.
The reason why this diet can be problematic, especially for type 1 diabetics is that
when the body enters ketosis, it starts creating ketones, which as explained in [2.2]
is very nauseating and uncomfortable. It can escalate pretty quickly and become
quite dangerous to a point where it starts damaging the brain. To lower the level
of ketones, the body requires insulin. When a type 1 diabetic therefore lowers their
ketone-level by taking insulin, they also need carbohydrates to not go into insulin
shock. Consuming carbohydrates is of course not very purposeful when doing a low
carbohydrate diet. Type 1 diabetics are therefore threading a fine line when doing
such a diet and need to know these things, what to be on the lookout for and how to
react if things go south.
When starting the application you are taken through a goal setting step, where it is
decided what macros you want to follow. Obviously there is focus on lower carbohy-

3.1
Target Group
It was concluded that the application is targeted at people with T1D that uses in-
sulin pump with a CGM. This ensures that both the motivation and ability to improve
BGL regulation is present in the individual. It also insures that the individual can
improve based on a sub optimal BGL regulation. The combination of insulin pump
and CGM also makes it possible to track the effects of meals, boluses and changes in
regulation, which is needed to effectively make changes. Some people that are part
of the target group might have been asked by healthcare professionals to track their
intake in a period leading up to a consultation. People in this category are likely to
be less motivated to use the application, which means that key experiences for them
need to be of simplicity and minimal time spent, while still collecting the required
information.
Another group of people might for their own reasons want to track intake in combina-
tion with their diabetes data. The majority will in this case likely to be individuals

who are having trouble with controlling their BGLs, and people who are looking to
get a better overview and thereby control of their diabetes.
For the design iterations and testing in this project, only people of at least 18 years
of age will be included. Reasoning is that children and young teens are very unlikely
to be invested in understanding and improving their diabetes administration outside
of ambulatory control visits. There could also be potential legal problems due to the

3.2
Quantified Self
The QS movement is based on an interest in learning and finding out things about
ourselves through tracking personal data. There are no rules about data having to be
of a specific nature. It can be anything from objective to subjective, from often used
heart rate data to the uncommon tracking of sneezes. The idea is that regular people
take it into their own hands and try to find out truths about themselves through
data. Often projects are based on a specific problem or annoyances, like finding out
what could be influencing factors in sleeping badly, or what food groups/items are the
cause of indigestion problems. It is also possible to go differently about it, by track-
ing many different things with for example continues trackers like watches, phones
even CGMs. From the data, it might then be possible to find trends and patterns
explaining things that were not even considered.
A part of the target group are as described in section [3.1] a group of people that
by intrinsic motivational factors are interested in learning more about their diabetes.
Some track simply to get the feeling of being in control, rather then being controlled.
Others track to improve on their well-being and increased chances for staying healthy
longer. Whatever their goals are, creating an application where it is fast and easy to
register intake and display data in a way that is easy to understand, should give both
the self-motivated and the doctor-prompted individual a good value for their effort.
With the project problem in mind, both parts of the target group and combined with
the QS mentality, it should be possible to show data within the application targeted
at the most relevant and workable factor of BGL regulation. An interesting bonus
feature could be to make it possible to export data from the application to be used
in personal projects.

3.4
BGL Regulation
It was from the investigation discovered that some aspects of BGL regulation are
influential than others. Some are also more easily measured, making them more use-
ful when used in statistics. The following influential factors of BGLs and settings
hereof will be discussed to decide which can be included in a solution: carbohydrate
counting, exercise tracking, stress tracking, insulin sensitivity, carbohydrate ratio and
basal rates.
Carbohydrate Counting:
It was already evident at the very start of the project as also explained in the back-
ground explanation [1.1] that the vast majority of people with T1D do estimate the
amount of carbohydrates in their food, and that these estimations in many cases are
not up to par. The reasoning for still using this method is because it saves a lot of
time. In order to figure out if bad carbohydrate estimations are a part of why an
individuals overall regulation is lacking, that data needs to be included. Estimations
of carbohydrates will therefore be a part of the application.
Another approach of making sure carbohydrate counting would not be the issue could
be to make the user weigh and calculate everything. The downsides to this would be
the immense amount of work needed and the heightened risk, that people would not
use the application. It will therefore not be used.
Exercise Tracking:
Physical exercise is known to have an effect on insulin sensitivity [28]. It is a factor
that needs to be in the back of a diabetics mind (insulin is more effectively used), or it
can easily lead to insulin-shock. Tracking exercise though, is a difficult thing. There
are so many exercise forms, intensities, durations, frequency in which to exercise that
including it in the application would make it too labour intensive to register for it to
be efficient. Even if registered the effect would be difficult to work with due to people
reacting differently to exercise, patterns in which they do so and for example aerobic
==PAGE:58==
44
3 Analysis
build up patterns. Exercise tracking will therefore not be part of the application.
Stress Tracking:
Stress can have effects on glucose metabolism [29], and is a factor often associated
with T1D. It is natural that a disease, which is life long and take significant time and
attention to manage can affect stress levels. Hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia, like
illustrated in section [2.2.3] can also influence the body and mind in ways that causes
stress. Despite stress obviously being closely tied to T1D, it is like exercise something
that is hard to quantify. It is something that can be a long term factor or brief and
affect people very differently. It would also be hard to differentiate stress from other
immeasurable factors and fix it due to its nature. Stress will therefore not be part of
the application.
Insulin Sensitivity:
Insulin sensitivity is as described a measure of how much the BGL is lowered for
every unit of insulin. It is also something that can change throughout the day. It
can as described above be influenced by exercising and is one of the settings in focus
at control visits at the diabetes ambulatory.
Insulin sensitivity can be argued to be least influenced in situations where the BGL
is stable, but needs to be corrected. These situations should be able to be identified
from CGM data and recorded. It is possible, especially in the connection with data
from the intended application, because there are clear indicators for when insulin is
taken in connection with a meal and when it is not.
Insulin sensitivity is a setting that seems very relevant for the solution and goes well
in connection with the data available and will therefore be part of the application.
Carbohydrate Ratio:
The carbohydrate ratio is the indicator for how many grams of carbohydrate counter-
acts one unit of insulin. This ratio is used in connection with meals, to calculate how
much insulin is taken. The ratio can like insulin sensitivity be different at depending
of time of day and also isolated in terms of when it is used. To clarify it is relevant in
connection with meals and would in the context of the intended application be very
relevant due to the intended form of logging meals described in the carbohydrate
counting paragraph. It is crucial to consider in the instance of a sub-optimal BG
measurements that both the carbohydrate estimation and ratio could be to blame (or
both). It will only be in the control of one factor, that it will be more clear what is
to blame. Also consider that just because a persons average carbohydrate estimation
error per meal is 25% that every meal is be estimated wrong. What can be done
when trends suggest that the BGL after meals end outside target range is one of two
things. If the trend is consistently either lower or higher than the target range, it is
very likely that it is the carbohydrate ratio which is to blame, and it can be changed.
If trends just show inconsistency it is more likely to be a problem with carbohydrate
counting, which can be tested by weighing and calculating carbohydrate intake for
a period. In either case there is a possibility of wrongly identifying the cause, but

because the data is recorded in the application (meals and estimations) along with
CGM data, it will be easier for the doctor or dietitian at control to help figure it out.
The carbohydrate ratio is a big part of good BGL regulation and an important part
of correcting errors and will therefore be included in the application.
Basal Rates:
Basal rates are the small doses of insulin that is required for the body to function,
without taking food into account. It is a replacement for how a healthy pancreas
would produce insulin and and is highly influenced by the hormone levels of the body.
As known, these levels can change drastically throughout a persons life. Examples of
drastic changes can be for example puberty, pregnancy and menopause. That is not
to say that outside of these changes that the hormone levels are unchanged. They
change regularly and can also be influenced over time by what a person does. As
explained in section [2.2.3], basal rates can be very difficult to adjust due to the need
for elimination of all other possible influential factors. In the intended application it
would be hard to isolate changes in BGL that is caused by basal rate error because it
would need to be separated from other factors that are not tracked. Basal rates will
therefore not be included in the application.

Food Tracking Features
The reason for using the application can vary between people. Reasons could for
example be to live more healthily, to keep track of diabetes related data and fell
in control or simply be better regulated. All of the reasons are possible, but only
through the linear use of the application resulting in better BGL regulation. Because
the application does not change depending on reason for using it, there is no need for
defining the users goal, like many of the investigated applications do.
It was in the decided that only the absolutely necessary features should be included in
the application. The main reason was to keep it as simple and low effort as possible
while still being able to register the data needed. A necessary feature is the food
logging, where it was decided to use carbohydrate estimation. Adding a picture to
the estimation is a small task and would give a lot of context. Not only can a picture
bring context to estimations for one self and healthcare professionals, but also work
as a visual recollection cue and self-realization tool. Pictures will therefore be part of
the applications main feature.
Other features that seemed to work well in other applications, such as social moti-
vators of community and gamification features are excluded. The reason again is to
streamline the application and because the target group of people with CGMs should
already cover the aspect of motivation for using the application.

The last step before going into the first design build of the prototype is to validate the
requirements and initial scoping. To do this a qualitative interview was conducted
with Line, a dietitian who is specialized in diabetes at the hospital of Kolding. In this
section the questions asked, the points made by Line and what was validate, change
or removed because of them is presented.
3.7.1
Questions and answers
The interview lasted for 45 minutes and sometimes went a little off topic. Only the
relevant points have been included in order to keep it at a reasonable length. Here
are the questions and answers:
1. In which situations do you talk to T1 diabetics?
We are a team that cover several ambulatories including the one for diabetes
patients. The biggest part of people with T1D that I talk to are people that
have just been diagnosed. When just diagnosed with diabetes there is always
scheduled a meeting with a dietitian. The main point of the meeting is to in-
troduce the patient/family to carbohydrate counting. Apart from that group
there can be a number of reasons for me talking to a T1 diabetic. They can
be divided in two groups. The first group are people that have requested a
meeting with me, usually with the goal of dietary advice to live healthier or
loose weight while still managing their diabetes. Another common reason are
people seeking advice on diet and exercise. When they start exercising they
notice significant changes in regulation and seek advice because of that. The
second group are people that have been strongly suggested by the doctor to
meet with me. This group mostly consist of people that have problems with
living healthily, problems estimating carbohydrates or are stuck in bad habits
that need to be changed. A bad habit that is seen a lot is people counting
insulin instead of carbohydrates. It is often seen in pen users, that calculate
the amount of insulin they need. When they over time learn that an apple is
x units of insulin instead of y grams of carbohydrates they run into problems
when their carbohydrate ratio changes more than just a little.
2. What parameters need to be satisfied for a patient to be asked to fill out a diary
leading up to an appointment?
Some people have diaries themselves, sometimes with clear goals and sometimes
I think just because they are used to it. But for us to ask them to fill out a
diary is dependent on the situation. New diabetes are encouraged to keep a
==PAGE:63==
3.7 Validation
49
diary because it is a good tool to get more familiar with this new reality they
find themselves in. They are therefore also expected to bring the diary for many
of the first control visits. It is to make sure that they have become comfortable
and adopted good practices. Outside of the new diabetics, diaries are suggested
when there is a suspicion that the individual is not estimating well. It can often
be seen in very varying results in BGLs are meals. Diaries can also be especially
helpful in the group mentioned before that are looking to become more healthy
or start to exercise. It works similarly to new diabetics in that they stand before
a big change and lack overview. The diary helps make some structure and gives
the ability to go back and check things if necessary. It can of course also help
me in helping them, because I can actually see what has happened.
3. How do people fill out diaries? Do you give them one or are they free to choose
for themselves?
Because new diabetics are encouraged to keep one for a longer period of time
they usually get a book, or in some cases choose to use an application. In the
case that it is short term up to an meeting it is usually just in paper form or
with some sort of application. Either just a text program or an actual food
logging application. I do not give out, because I found that people often choose
to do it using something else, and it is just a waste of material. They are free
to choose whichever form works best for them.
4. What are the applications you most often see used and do you see any benefits
to an application?
The most common ones I think is Lifesum and different note/text applications.
For the patient there might be many different benefits depending on what their
reason for using one is. For me it can sometimes be informative to see some of
the information recorded in food tracking applications that might not otherwise
have been recorded in a paper diary. But again, it is dependent on the goal of
using it and how good the individual is at communicating their experience with
me during the meeting.

with the requirements and scoping layout.
5. What is your opinion on the overarching goal of the application?
It think it is a really good idea. I think that people who do not have a CGM
could also benefit from it, but I see the point in needing the continuous mea-

Intended Test Design
As described in section [4.5] the project came to an extended halt. Therefore, it will
here be described the test I intend to do before the defence of the project.
With a 5th iteration build that includes sorting and descriptors for how to adjust
insulin sensitivity and carbohydrate ratio, it is intended to present test participant
with the same data presented in two ways. The first way will be the Diasend data
(which is what the doctor uses). The other will be the same data but plotted into
the prototype. The test participants will be asked to adjust insulin sensitivity and
carbohydrate ratio based on each set. It will with the output, because it is in form
of continuous data be possible to run a one-tailed paired sample t-test to determine
if the magnitude in change is significant. Ultimately that will hint at a purpose of
the study, which was to create a tool to help T1 diabetics track their food intake and
administer their diabetes.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The focus of the project have been to explore the possibility of combining modern
technologies in order to extract enriched data and use it to help T1 diabetics in man-
aging their BGLs.
The process started with research on a broad spectrum of topics related to diabetes
and food tracking. Using a lean UX design approach the project and trajectory was
step validated throughout. It was found that by combining the use of smartphones
to track food intake via an application and CGM data it is possible to give it value.
The value lies in the ability to extract trends relating to the important administrative
settings of insulin sensitivity and carbohydrate ratio.
A prototype application was created.
Several iteration were build, measured and
evaluated. Work on the prototype ended abruptly in December due to family health
problems. The prototype was not finished and could therefore not be tested. Then
intention is to do so in the time before the defence of the thesis.
At this point in time, it is not possible to conclude that the overall purpose of creating
a tool to help T1 diabetics track food intake and improve their diabetes administra-
tion have been met.

HVORDAN?
Diabetes vælger i 
hvert nummer et emne, 
som illustreres 
grafisk.
Sådan forstår  du dine blodsukkertal
Hvordan svarer dine egne blodsukkermålinger til   langtidsblodsukkeret (HbA1c), som lægen måler? Læs med her. 
Tekst: Helen Heidemann / Grafik: Rie Lynggaard Topp
Hvad er 
HbA1c?
HbA1c er en betegnelse for en blodprøve, der 
henviser til glykeret hæmoglobin. Blodprøven 
fortæller, hvad det gennemsnitlige glukoseni-
veau (sukkerniveau) i blodet har været over de 
seneste cirka to måneder.
Hb står for hæmoglobin og findes i de røde 
blodlegemer. Det er et proteinstof, hvis 
funktion er at transportere ilt rundt i blodet. 
Hæmoglobin giver blodet sin røde farve.
A står for den mest almindelige type 
hæmoglobin.
Tallet 1 og bogstavet c henviser til underty-
per af hæmoglobin A, som glukose binder sig 
til.
HbA1c fortæller altså, hvor meget glukose 
(sukker) der har været bundet til hæmoglobin 
(proteinstof) gennem den seneste periode.
Hvad er eAG 
eller M-BG? 
eAG står for det engelske “estimated 
average glucose”. På dansk bruges M-BG, 
der står for estimeret middelblodglukose. 
Tallet er et beregnet gennemsnit for dit 
blodsukkerniveau igennem de seneste 
cirka to måneder. Det opgives i millimol 
pr. liter og kan sammenlignes med de tal, 
du selv måler på dit blodsukkerapparat. 
Måler du blodsukker cirka fem gange om 
dagen eller mere og ser på apparatets 
gennemsnit af dine blodsukkermålinger 
for de seneste fire uger eller mere, får du 
dermed et pejlemærke på, hvad dit esti-
merede middelblodglukose er og dermed 
dit HbA1c (langtidsblodsukker).
Kilder: Vejledninger fra Dansk Selskab for Almen Medicin og Dansk 
Endokrinologisk Selskab
Langtidsblodsukker, der måles i procent. Enheden bruges 
ikke længere.
Den nuværende enhed: HbA1c mmol/mol
Langtidsblodsukker, der måles i millimol pr. mol.
Middelblodglukose mmol/l (eAG eller M-BG)
Et gennemsnit af dine seneste blodsukkermålinger i 
millimol pr. liter.

Hvad skal HbA1c ligge på?
Diabeteslægernes faglige selskaber anbefaler:
	Værdier under 48 mmol/mol Kan tilstræbes
	 de første år efter diagnosen, hvis det er muligt
	 uden større risiko for hypoglykæmi 
	 (lavt blodsukker). Ser ud til at være vigtigt for
	 forebyggelsen af følgesygdomme på lang sigt.
	 Værdier under 53 mmol/mol En stram kontrol
	 kan blive tiltagende vanskelig jo længere tid, 
	 man har haft diabetes. Individuelle mål er
	 afgørende. Her opvejes risikoen for lavt blod-
	 sukker over for risikoen for mikrovaskulære 
	 følgesygdomme – altså skader på de små 
	 blodkar, dvs. skader på øjne, nyrer og nervebaner.
	 Værdier under 58 mmol/mol Hvis man har 
	 tendens til svingende blodsukre, lave blodsuk-
	 re, har en høj alder, har haft diabetes i mange 
	 år og har makrovaskulære følgesygdomme – 
	 altså skader på de store blodkar, dvs. årefor-
	 kalkning, der særligt rammer hjertet 
	 (kransårerne), hjernen og benene (pulsårerne).
	 Værdier mellem 58 og 75 mmol/mol Kan 	
	 være acceptabelt i nogle tilfælde.
