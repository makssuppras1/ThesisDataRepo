1
INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Timber buildings are generally known to be structurally eﬃcient and having a lower carbon footprint com-
pared to other traditional building materials. Therefore, tall timber buildings can be an attractive option for
the increasing high-rise construction. However, the relatively low weight and stiﬀness properties of timber
can be a problem regarding the dynamic performance. Specially when exposed to high winds, tall buildings
may experience signiﬁcant drifts which causes concerns for serviceability and human comfort.
Nowadays tall buildings are absolutely crucial in order to cope with the increasing scarcity of land and the
rapidly growing migration of population from rural areas to urban cities. As a result cities become more
dense thus tall buildings are more eﬃcient in terms of land consumption but also saving costs on transferring
electrical energy as the power grid becomes smaller.
The increasing building height increases the buildings’ ﬂexibility thus more insuﬃcient inherent damping.
This ultimately results in excessive levels of vibration under wind actions. Although innovations in structural
systems has permitted eﬃcient handling of increased lateral loads, the dynamic nature of wind causes
discomfort to building occupants posing serious serviceability issues.
In this regards external damping
systems are implemented in the construction of the building to reduce excessive vibrations.
1.2
Problem description
Lateral forces are the major factor which distinguishes tall buildings from other low rise buildings and wind
force is one of the most signiﬁcant one. The design of tall buildings essentially involves a conceptual design,
approximate analysis, preliminary design and optimization, to safely carry gravity and lateral load. The
design criteria are, strength, serviceability, stability and human comfort. In this report the serviceability will
be of more importance hence the focus will be on the dynamic performance in terms of the human comfort
due to high wind loads, storey drifts, and accelerations.
1.3
Aim and objectives
This thesis aims to analyze the dynamic behavior of tall timber buildings under wind loads. This includes
among others the storey accelerations.
Furthermore, the inﬂuence of a mechanical damping system is
investigated to control accelerations and building motions.
1.4
Limitations of scope
This project covers a case study which evaluates the dynamic performance of the tall timber building in
Norway, Mjøstårnet.

2.1
Single-degree-of-freedom Systems
When a structure is subjected to dynamic load, that is time-varying load it will have time-varying responses.
The response amplitude depends on both the external excitation and the system itself. This system is
composed of three types of internal forces which are the inertial force, damping force, and the restoring
force. Conceptually the model can be described as a masss, spring, and a damper system shown in Fig. 1. In
general such system containing only one mass thus one displacement is known as Single-degree-of-Freedom
systems (SDOF).
Figure 1 Single-degree-of-freedom (sdof) model

(MDOF). There are diﬀerent methods to determine the response of a MDOF system but in this project the

the mass the space/elements within the nodes are without mass but the replaced with by equivalent masses
in the nodes. The lumped mass at a node of the structure is the sum of the mass contributions of all the
structural elements connected to the node.

Mode shapes
Mode shapes describe the shape for which a structure will naturally displace. In high rise structures the
lateral displacement is typically of primary interest.
An undamped structure would undergo simple harmonic motion without change of deﬂected shape, however,
if free vibration is initiated by appropriate distributions of displacements in the various DOFs. If we consider
the simple cantilever beam with two lumped masses as ﬂoors, both ﬂoors vibrate in the same phase, i.e., they
pass through their zero, maximum, or minimum displacement positions at the same instant of time. Each
characteristic deﬂected shape is called a natural mode of vibration of an MDOF system. (Chopra, 2012).
Figure 5 shows an example of the ﬁrst 5 mode shapes of a 100m tall building simpliﬁed as a cantilevered
beam.
Mode shapes of low order tend to provide the greatest contribution of structural response.

3
Damping in Structures
The damping which occurs in structures can be considered to be either inherent damping, that is, damping
which occurs naturally within the structure or its environment, or added damping which is that resulting
from specially constructed dampers added to the structure. The damping process is about energy dissipation
of the vibrating system. The damping aﬀects the amplitude leading to a decay of motion over time. It can
be impossible to analytically determine the damping ratio of a high rise building thus it should be determined
experimentally. By means of vibration records the general dynamic properties of lightly damped structures
can be determined. From displacement measurements the natural periods and frequencies can be evaluated
thus ﬁnding more precise damping ratios. Multiple studies have been carried out for high rise structures and
there are among other factors the follow important ones to mention
• Inherent structural damping
• Damping due to friction between structural connections
• Aerodynamic damping
• External damping (Passive- and active vibration control)
In the design process it is apparent that vibration measurement data cannot be used to assess the buildings
damping performance. Therefore, the damping assessments should be based on experience and empirical
data. From previous and similar existing structures the damping values can be used. In Eurocode DS/EN
1991-1-4 it is provided some values for the logarithmic decrement of typical structures. The logarithmic
decremement of damping is given as
δ = δs + δa + δd

where
• δs is the logarithmic decrement of structural damping
• δa is the logarithmic decrement of aerodynamic damping for the fundamental mode

External damping devices and vibration control
When the inherent damping in a structure is insuﬃcient, it can be increased either by adding vibration
dampers to the structure or by manufacturing the structure, or a part of it, out of a layered material with
very high damping properties.
There are two types of structural vibration control provided by the addition of mechanical devices, active
and passive control. Active control requires a power supply to activate the damper. Active vibration control
is a technique for reducing unwanted vibration by using a sensor to measure the motion or force from
the vibrating source then use a powered actuator to generate a force to resist the unwanted motion. On
the other hand, passive energy dissipation systems have emerged as special mechanical devices that are
incorporated within the structure to absorb unwanted vibrations. Energy dissipation may be achieved either
by the transformation of kinetic energy to heat or by the transferring of energy among vibrating modes. The
ﬁrst mentioned mechanism includes hysteric devices. Hysteric devices operate on principles such as yielding
of metals and frictional sliding. Another one is viscous devices which involves viscous solids or ﬂuids.

Tuned mass dampers - TMD
A tuned mass damper (TMD) is a device consisting of a mass, a spring, and a damper that is attached to a
structure in order to reduce the dynamic response of the structure. The frequency of the damper is tuned
to a particular structural frequency so that when that frequency is excited, the damper will resonate out of
phase with the structural motion. Energy is dissipated by the damper inertia force acting on the structure.
Considering a simple system the model in Figure 6 shows a primary system m0 representing the primary
structure and the vibration absorber (TMD) with m. In the present example it is convenient to describe the
motion of the combined system by the motion of the structure x0 and the relative motion of the damper xd

The tuned mass damper also consists of spring with stiﬀness k and a viscous damper with parameter c.
The parameters must be chosen so the damper mass is suﬃcient to generate the desired damping but also
to produce a suitable ampliﬁcation by resonance. The system should also be able to extract the maximal
energy by suitable tuning of damping. The mass often consist of steel blocks.

Linear friction dampers
Linear friction dampers - Damptech paper The following section will cover a description of the friction
damper. Information are provided from Damptech A/S.
The linear friction dampers oﬀer certain advantages such as high energy-dissipation capacity and stable cyclic
behavior, which are considered the core advantages of all friction damper types, including the rotational
friction dampers. Although good damper properties there are some disadvantages such as:
• If the engineer cannot predict the friction coeﬃcient, the device cannot provide reliable friction.
• Dimensional limitations. Most national codes limit the length of slotted holes, since the steel plate
can be weakened.
• Abrasion of friction surfaces after a limited number of loading cycles with subsequent capacity degra-
dation. The damper is not reliable over a project’s lifetime.
• Stick-slip phenomena. Sticking occurs when the device does not slip at the predicted design force
and a higher force is needed to activate the friction. A linear friction assembly is more susceptible
to sticking than rotational friction, because rotation friction has the advantage of variable angular
displacement and velocity
Working principle of Damptech’s friction damper device (FDD): The friction damper device consists of
several steel plates rotating against each other in opposite directions. The steel plates are separated by
shims of high tech friction pad material producing friction with the steel plates. When the external forces
become large enough the steel plates of the dampers start to rotate and mechanical energy is converted into
heat in the friction layer between friction pads and the steel plates. When the applied forces are reversed,
the plates will rotate in opposite way and the damper continues to convert mechanical energy into heat and
18
==PAGE:22==
thus reducing the vibration of the structure. FDDs can be combined in many diﬀerent ways and exist with
many diﬀerent geometries and shapes. FDDs can work in two directions which means that they are much
more ﬂexible than FVDs.
Friction
Friction is one of the most important parameters to analyze. Whole mechanism of the rotational friction
damping devices is based solely on friction. The classic rules of sliding friction were discovered by Leonardo
da Vinci in 1493. The same rules were later rediscovered by Guillaume Amontons in 1699. Amontons
presented the nature of friction in terms of surface irregularities and the force required to raise the weight
pressing the surfaces together.
Charles-Augustin de Coulomb (1785) developed the understanding of friction even further. Coulomb inves-
tigated the inﬂuence of four main factors on friction the
• Nature of the materials in contact and their surface coatings;
• The extent of the surface area.
• Pressure on surface
• The length of time that the surfaces remained in contact (time of repose).
Coulomb friction, named after Charles-Augustin de Coulomb, is an approximate model used to calculate the
force of dry friction. It is governed by the model
Ff ≤µFn
(3.32)
where
• Ff is the force of friction exerted by each surface on the other. It is parallel to the surface, in a
direction opposite to the net applied force.
• µ is the coeﬃcient of friction, which is an empirical property of the contacting materials.
• Fn is the normal force exerted by each surface on the other, directed perpendicular (normal) to the
surface
In the static case, the frictional force is equal to the force, which is needed in order to prevent motion
between the surfaces. In this case, rather than providing an estimate of the actual frictional force, the
Coulomb approximation provides a threshold value for this force, above which motion would commence.
This maximum force is known as traction.
Physical mechanisms behind friction are under research even today. It has been shown that, at a microscopic
level, the actual area of contact between surfaces is a very small fraction7 of the full area. This actual area
of contact, caused by "asperities" (roughness) increases with pressure. The development of the atomic force
microscope (ca. 1986) enabled scientists to study friction at the atomic scale,[30] showing that, on that
scale, dry friction is the product of the inter-surface shear stress and the contact area. It helps to explain
the proportionality between normal force and static frictional force between dry surfaces
Coeﬃcient of friction
The coeﬃcient of friction, often symbolized by the Greek letter µ, is a dimensionless value which describes
the ratio of the force of friction between two bodies and the force pressing them together.

Arthur Morin introduced the term and demonstrated the utility of the coeﬃcient of friction [29].
The
coeﬃcient of friction is an empirical measurement – it has to be measured experimentally, and cannot be
found through calculations.
While it is often stated that the coeﬃcient of friction is a "material property," it is better categorized as a
"system property". That is because coeﬃcient of friction for any two materials depends on system variables
like temperature, velocity, atmosphere and also aging and deaging times; as well as on geometric properties
of the interface between the materials.
V-shaped FDD mechanism
A V-shaped FDD is assembled by several parts which are all centered on a bolt. Small pressure plates
distribute pressure from the bolt head or the nuts to disc springs. Larger pressure plates distribute the
pressure of disc springs to the damper device consisting of metal plates and friction pads.
Figure 12 V-shaped friction damper
Figure 12 shows an assembled 3-unit friction damper devise with notation of the individual parts. Steel
plates are specially made with grooves and polished areas suitable for the friction plates. The grooves are
2mm deep and its radius ﬁts the size of a FP. The groove is shaped in a way which does not let the friction
pad to come in contact with the bolt. Friction between the friction pads and steel plates is created by
pre-stressing the bolt. Resulting frictional moment will resist external forces as they would try to rotate the
plates around the bolt.

Case study - Mjøstårnet
7.1
Mjøstårnet as built
The following section is analyzing the tall wooden building Mjøstårnet in terms of the dynamic performance.
Initially an introduction about the building is presented. Then the aim is to ﬁnd the horizontal acceleration
based an Eurocode approach presented in section 5. The method in Eurocode demands calculation of the
building’s natural frequencies which will be done by modal analysis. For this reason a FEM-model is created
in FEM design which will be the basis for the dynamic analysis. Furthermore, the mean wind speed is
calculated based on wind data set. The mean wind speed is given for three diﬀerent return periods as seen
in subsubsection 7.1.2.
7.1.1
Introduction to building
Mjøstårnet is a tall timber building with 18-stories and a total height of 81 m. Mjøstårnet is located in
Brumunddal a little town north of Oslo. The building was completed in 2019 and has a net area of 11300
m2. The tower consist of oﬃces, hotel, apartments, and a restaurant. The idea to build Mjøstårnet was
a vision based on the green shift, and that tall buildings can be built using local resources and sustainable
wooden materials.
The structural system consists of large scale glulam trusses along the façades as well as internal columns
and beams.
The trusses handle the global forces in horizontal and vertical direction and give the building its necessary
stiﬀness. CLT walls are used for secondary load bearing of three elevators and two staircases. The CLT walls
do not contribute to the building’s horizontal stability [1]. The facade consists of elements attached to the
outside of the timber structure and make up the envelope of the building. The elements include insulation
materials and external panels and do not have any contribution to the stiﬀness.
The footprint of the building is about 17 x 37 m2. The foundation is a solid thick concrete plate in the base
which is supported by piles that handle the compression and tension forces. Piles are driven to the bedrock.
The four corner columns are subjected to the largest compression force of 11500 kN and maximum tension
force of 5500 kN [1]. These columns’ cross section is 1485 x 625 mm2. The internal column cross section
are 725 x 810 mm2 and 625x630 mm2 respectively. Dimension are presented in Table 9.
The ﬂoors are built with concrete and wooden decks. From level 2 to 11 are prefabricated wooden decks
based on Moelven’s Trä8 building system. As the modelling for this ﬂoor system is complicated the decks
are modelled as CLT decks. Floors 12 to 18 are 300 mm concrete ﬂoors meaning extra mass is added to
comply with the light weight of the wooden building. Each ﬂoor acts as a diaphragm. The glulam beams
supporting timber ﬂoors are 395x585 mm2 and the beams supporting concrete ﬂoors are 625x585 mm2.
The largest diagonal cross section is 625x990 mm2. All glulam elements are connected by use of slotted-in
steel plates and dowels.

7.1.5
Comfort criteria
The comfort of the occupants is important both for tenants and stakeholders.
The parameter that is
often used to measure the structure’s performance under wind-induced vibration is acceleration and not
displacement. The reason is that people better perceive the rate of velocity change than the displacement.
Wind-induced accelerations are typically described as fractions of the acceleration of gravity (milli-g). [3].
American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE, 2015) published monograph on wind-induced vibrations where
some limit values of milli-g’s are described:
• 1 milli-g: Most people cannot perceive motion
• 3 milli-g: Over 50% can sense motion
• 10 milli-g: Vast majority of occupants can perceive motion
• 35-40 milli-g: fear and safety threshold. Some occupants can lose their balance

7.3
Application of a tuned mass damper (TMD)
An alternative solution to dissipate the vibration and dampen the excessive accelerations is to install a tuned
mass damper at the top of the building. By installing a frame like structure connected to a mass by a spring,
the entire model will be changed as another signiﬁcant vibrating mass is added at the top of the structure.
The TMD is tuned such that it goes out of phase when the motion reaches the natural frequency of the
building. When TMD is starting to vibrate the springs will absorb the energy due to the relative movements
between main structure and TMD-mass. The principle is shown Figure 35.
Figure 35 TMD principle on top of Mjøstårnet.
The TMD is a heavy block of material that rests on a low friction bearing hence the rollers drawn underneath
the mass. It was not possible to model the TMD in FEM-design so an ETAB model was created. The
ETABS model was modelled such that it almost replicated the Mjøstårnet in terms of natural frequency and
period. However, the TMD parameters were chosen from a simpliﬁed method through an iterative process.
This was done since the acceleration was not found from a frequency domain analysis, hence the dynamic
ampliﬁcation factors could not be determined.

Conclusion
The results from the case study of the Mjøstårnet indicate that the mass plays a decisive factor in order to
satisfy human comfort criteria. The peak acceleration at the top of the building was calculated and found to
be satisfying for the case where the timber tower had concrete slabs included in the upper 6 ﬂoors. A second
analysis covered the acceleration calculations in case of when the entire timber building was built with CLT
decks. It was clear that discomforting acceleration occurred due the mass reduction. For this reason a
TMD solution was proposed, however, the design was highly simpliﬁed since it was not possible to model a
TMD in FEM-design. Therefore, the TMD should be investigated in depth. Nevertheless, the eﬀect of the
TMD presence showed decent reduction in the displacement of the main building. The modal analysis in
FEM-design gave similar natural frequency results to the results from the consultant engineering company.
This was an important veriﬁcation as there were multiple uncertainties when modelling the building from
PDF-drawings.
The parametric study where the frame structure was compared to the steel- and concrete frame showed
that the timber system performed very well regarding the displacement when induced to instant wind load.
However, the downside was overly sized member dimensions to cope for deﬂection criteria.
A diﬃcult parameter which can be a topic worth investigating further is the assignment of the correct
damping ratio in the model. The approach in this project was deﬁning the Rayleigh coeﬃcients based on
the natural frequencies. However, multiple factors plays a role in deﬁning a more correct global damping
matrix which among others can be the stiﬀness of the connections.
