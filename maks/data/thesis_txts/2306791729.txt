SUMMARY 
The introduction of Building Information Modeling (BIM) in the construction industry is ascending 
in importance for the AEC (Architecture, Engineering and Construction), but also the FM (Facilities 
Management) professional due to the anticipated value it intends to give with respect to 
productivity and quality raise, as well as project cost and time reduction. The development of BIM 
is rapid mainly due to the widely accepted deficiencies of the traditional 2D or 3D construction 
practices, where drawings and fragmented collaboration processes prevailed. Nevertheless, BIM 
processes are not yet streamlined and companies implementing BIM cannot fully acknowledge the 
potentials of the fully integrated aspects of BIM. The term “Com-BIM” is used in this specific 
research to refer to the comprehensive BIM concept in the context of Integrated Project Delivery 
(IPD), which represents according to industry specialists the appropriate basis for the 
accomplishment of fully integrated BIM processes under the scope of early collaboration between 
stakeholders. 
Taking the above mentioned facts into consideration, this research focusses on presenting an 
alternative way of realizing the benefits and potentials of BIM-based processes by comparing them 
against non-BIM processes. Moreover, the comparison is supposed to highlight drawbacks of actual 
BIM practices with reference to the fully integrated concepts. For the accomplishment of this main 
objective, an extended literature review was conducted regarding concepts of BIM evaluation 
methods, so that a tool could be developed that would be used as the basis for the realization of 
the objectives. Three categories are pointed out through the literature research with respect to 
performance assessment of construction projects, namely traditional construction practice, BIM 
Capability and business value of BIM evaluation methods. Each approach added input to the 
development of the assessment tool, which is presented in a conceptual form, since further 
research on each sub-part is required to flesh it out with more parameters. The main innovation of 
the tool is the alignment with the project timeline. Special focus was given on the detailed study of 
the planning, design and construction phase, as fully integrated BIM concepts rely mainly on early 
collaboration of stakeholders. The assessment tool includes mostly qualitative, but also 
quantitative evaluation methods and is characterized by two different approaches with respect to 
project process and project performance.  
The assessment tool is used as a reference for the establishment of a questionnaire and the 
conductance of Interviews, which represent the case study providing the main results, which are 
accompanied by literature sources wherever the answers of the Interviewees were considered 
incomplete. The Interviewees are either experienced professionals of traditional non-BIM and 
modern BIM-based construction practices or employees representing an innovative company 
regarding implementation of BIM concepts.  
The analysis of the results is fulfilled through the classification of each assessment tool category 
into traditional non-BIM, fully integrated BIM-based and modern BIM-based. The difference 
between the last two is critical, since the modern-BIM based category represents the actual 
construction practice realized in Denmark, whereas the fully integrated is associated with the 
potential establishment of the fully developed “Com-BIM” concept. The traditional non-BIM 
construction practice uses the construction industry of Greece over the past 50 years as a reference 
due to the expertise of the respected Interviewee into this specific area.    
BIM-processes dominate in most of the assessment tool categories through streamlining processes, 
but also with the variety of tools they offer. Provision of incentives for collaboration, partially 
shared risk allocation and streamline of traditional contractual methods are among the main 
advantages with respect to planning methods. Partially integrated team collaboration during the 
==PAGE:5==

project process, cyclic information flow, 3D-Model based information exchange and 4D cost 
estimation are clearly prevailing over traditional linear, fragmented and document-based 
processes, when it comes to the actual project processes form the planning phase until the delivery 
of the construction project. These aforementioned facts lead to time and cost reduction, better 
quality and safety through a variety of implemented BIM parameters. Among them, visual 
representation, “What-if” search and parametric function, simulations and establishment of digital 
building-ID are highlighted mostly.  
On the other hand, critical drawbacks regarding further evolution of the Danish processes are 
pointed out. Among them, the inability of all the main stakeholders to get involved early in the 
project phase due to distinctive knowledge and specialization areas and the great information loss 
of the BIM Model between the project phase changes are considered to account for the creation 
of the barriers. The mapping of the main stakeholders’ requirements in the BIM Model during the 
early project phases and the set of clear milestones to check compliance throughout the project 
are recommended under the scope of aligning project process with the existence of the BIM Model. 
Moreover, the development of BIM competency benchmarks to enable shared risk allocation 
structures through ensuring collaboration liability is proposed to surround the solution framework.  
Last but not least, further research is proposed with mainly two directions. On the one hand, a 
detailed analysis of the pre-planning and operation and maintenance phases is instructed and on 
the other hand the conductance of interviews with representatives from every main stakeholder 
field is recommended.

1. INTRODUCTION  
The necessity of the introduction of a new conceptual philosophy and replace traditional 
fragmented construction practices that rely upon fully specialized individuals and document-based 
exchange of data is compelling. Many reasons may be stated in favor of this need. Initially, buildings 
consume large proportions of our scarce resources (Bim & Jones 2009), which is opposed to the 
general trend towards sustainability, reduced energy consumption and recycled materials. 
Moreover, based on (Eastman et al. 2011), 57% of the money spent on the construction sector are 
non-value-added and lead to waste. In parallel to that, according to (Teicholz 2004), a steady 
productivity decline is observed over the last 40 years in the US construction industry. Last but not 
least, the complexity of buildings is increased due to emerging supplementary parameters requiring 
careful consideration (sustainability, hybrid building uses and many others).  
These above mentioned facts contribute to the revolution that takes place during the last 10-15 
years, which “will fundamentally change the very fabric of the audiovisual design and integration 
business” (Bim & Jones 2009). This evolution is expressed by the introduction and development of 
Building Information Modeling.  
 
1.1 Problem Statement  
Building Information Modeling (BIM) represents the current expression of the construction 
industry. When using the word BIM, people do not refer to the software that it accompanies. On 
the contrary, it outlines a complete philosophy that provides a paradigm shift in the way buildings 
are dealt with during their entire lifecycle (Elmualim & Gilder 2013). This creates the necessity for 
exhaustive changes in the way people perceive construction processes, especially related to the 
construction coalitions and the structure of the project process interactions. 
The advancement of collaboration within the AEC industry (Elmualim & Gilder 2013) is more than 
up to the minute and accompanied by involvement of the AEC fraternity during the early design 
phases of the conceptual design. “Comprehensive BIM” represents techniques that are 
distinguished by expanding the use of BIM to the entire AEC team. These techniques define a 
complete new philosophy (Owen et al. 2010). However, even if the need for adoption of the fully 
integrated BIM philosophy seems a one-way street, the process is not yet widely implemented. The 
main reasons that lie behind this are (Elmualim & Gilder 2013): 
 Lack of capital to invest in getting started with software and hardware 
 
 According to some industry specialists, benefits of implementing the “comprehensive BIM” 
philosophy do not outweigh the cost to implement it or are not tangible enough to prove that 
they do” 
 
 There is no specified process of fully integrated BIM implementation that enables construction 
industry specialists to establish a thorough BIM execution planning process. 
Moreover, according to (Young Jr. et al. 2007), the absence of official evaluation methods that 
assess the positive impact of BIM on the process and outcome of a construction project prevents 
the opportunity of companies to measure their qualifications and compare them against industry 
benchmarks, but mainly destructs them from exploiting the real opportunities of integrated BIM-
based methods through the detailed study of implementation strategies.

1.2 Aim and Objectives 
The main objective of the resent thesis research is the comparison between non-BIM traditional 
construction practices that prevailed in the construction industry prior to the emergence of the 
Building Information Modeling concept with modern BIM-based construction methods. This 
comparison takes place under the scope of highlighting the benefits of implementing BIM, but also 
the drawbacks obstructing the further development towards the realization of the actual potentials 
of integrated BIM-based processes. In order to fulfill this objective, the development of a 
conceptual form of a project evaluation tool has to be accomplished based on a through and critical 
literature review of available evaluation methods. 
The aim of this process is the triggering of interest regarding a new approach that evaluates project 
outcomes and highlights BIM benefits, but also delinquencies, based on the qualitative and 
quantitative study of BIM parameters with respect to project processes. Especially in the case of 
Integrated Project delivery (IPD) concepts, which represent the state-of-the-art externalization of 
BIM philosophies, special focus has to be given on the early project phases.  
 
1.3 Methodology 
The methodology part includes the documentation of all processes followed in order to fulfill the 
main objective of this thesis, namely the comparison of traditional non-BIM with modern BIM-
based construction concepts using an assessment tool that includes performance metrics. 
Following that, the scope of highlighting advantages of using BIM-based collaborative methods will 
be succeeded and at the same time drawbacks of the existing BIM-based methods with respect to 
fully integrated state-of-the-art construction practices will be pointed out. 
The main principle characterizing the methodology part is the development of a linear framework 
incorporating the sequence of steps to be accomplished, in order to come up with valid results. 
This specific framework draws its inspiration from the research methodology framework 
established in (Barlish & Sullivan 2012). The main difference lies in the linearity of the present 
framework, as opposed to the iterative concept of the former. The linearity accounts for the 
possibility of creating loops that add input to the process or get output out of it. Moreover, the 
present methodology framework is established based on the focus on early project processes that 
requires a qualitative analysis. The complete framework is illustrated in Figure 1. Below, an analysis 
of each step is provided: 
 Literature Review: 
An extended literature research (more than 60 articles) was conducted. The research aimed at 
investigating different ways of quantitatively and qualitatively evaluating construction projects, 
both the ones characterized by the implementation of BIM-based processes, but also the 
traditional non-BIM projects. The research focused on both examining efficient project 
assessment methods, but also pointing out areas where the available research is insufficient. 
 
 Theoretical background: 
Through the extended literature review, a theoretical background was created, focusing 
towards two different aspects. On the one hand, the theoretical background aims at defining 
the term BIM and developing the main perception of it, which is followed in this specific report. 
The reason for that lies on the existing confusion around BIM concerning its conceptualization 
from each stakeholder and the need to place it within a detailed and specified context. On the

stated that the research focusses only on building construction projects, since distinctive

2. THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
The theoretical background includes all the essential information regarding basic terms to be used 
in the main report. It is divided into two subchapters, one of them referring to the definition and 
perception of Building Information Modelling (BIM) and the other one incorporating an extended 
literature review and selection of the most important concepts of construction projects’ 
performance assessment methods. The analysis of each sub-chapter is composed of two parts.  The 
“literature review” part includes the fundamental aspects investigated, whereas the “thesis 
perception” part incorporates the prevailing aspect accrued from the critics of the literature 
review. 
 
2.1 Building Information Modelling (BIM) 
In the reviewed literature, all sources include a definition of Building Information Modeling (BIM), 
when their main area of interest is closely related to the term. Following that and considering the 
diverse apprehension of BIM across various stakeholders (Barlish & Sullivan 2012), there is not 
among the main scopes of this thesis to delve into the different BIM perceptions. However, the 
need to define the perceived philosophy of BIM that is used in this thesis created the necessity to 
briefly highlight some relevant definitions. 
Based on the literature research, the term BIM is used either to describe the digital representation 
of the complete construction process (technology), or to refer to the introduction of a completely 
new construction philosophy (process) through the digitalization of the mechanism.  
 
2.1.1 BIM Definition (Literature Review) 
According to (Eastman et al. 2011), through the BIM technology, a virtual aggregate model, 
composed of various discipline models, of a building is created that assists AEC specialists to the 
complete project phase, from pre-design until lifecycle management.  
 
 Design: Better analysis, control and simulation of the building 
 
 Construction: With the help of precise building geometry and the extended information 
included in the computer generated models, construction, prefabrication and procurement 
activities become more efficient and easily managed. 
 
 Lifecycle: Incorporates all the data needed for the maintenance of the building throughout its 
lifecycle. 
 
Moreover, (McGraw Hill Construction 2014) defines BIM as “The process of creating and using 
digital models for design, construction and/or operations of projects”, whereas (Succar et al. 2012) 
includes a more holistic aspect of BIM by claiming that BIM is a set of interacting policies, processes 
and technologies that generate a “methodology to manage the essential building design and 
project data in digital format throughout the building’s lifecycle.” (Penttilå 2006) 
 
 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
Mostly cited in the literature is the definition credited to the National BIM Standard (NBIMS) Project 
Committee of the Building Smart Alliance (NIMBS Committe 2007):  
“A Building Information Model is a digital representation of physical and functional characteristics 
of a facility. As such, it serves as a shared knowledge resource for information about a facility 
forming a reliable basis for decisions during its lifecycle from inception onward. The BIM is a shared 
digital representation focused on open standards for interoperability.” 
This definition is considered as the most appropriate because it incorporates the aspect of 
interoperability and lifecycle being introduced by BIM. However, even in this case, BIM is mostly 
considered as the modernization of traditional construction practice through its digitalization and 
not the introduction of a complete new philosophy inaugurated through and enabled by the 
establishment of a new software technology. As (Owen et al. 2010) claims, in many cases, the 
implementation of BIM has been analogous to the introduction of CAD-Design, as it replicates the 
current process virtually. 
A short but quantitative definition that approaches the BIM collaborative concept is expressed by 
(Pishdad et al. 2011), based on which, BIM may be expressed as the virtual design and construction 
with a prerequisite for collaboration. Based on this definition, “comprehensive BIM” (Cavallero 
2006) consolidates techniques that expand the applicability of BIM to the entire AEC industry by 
establishing new collaborative relationships between specialists. The use of BIM as a collaborative 
process could be facilitated through the wider implementation of integrated project delivery 
methods, according to (Becerik-gerber & Kent 2010). Based on (Owen et al. 2010), when enabling 
the coupling of BIM with IPD, efficiency is improved, errors are almost avoided and alternative 
approaches are explored holistically from the early design phase.  
 
2.1.2 Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) (Literature Review) 
Integrated Project Delivery is according to the American Institute of Architects (AIA) “a project 
delivery approach that integrates people, systems, business structures and practices into a process 
that collaboratively harness the talents and insights of all project participants to optimize project 
results, increase value to the owner, reduce waste and maximize efficiency through all phases of 
design, fabrication and construction.” (Council 2007) 
It was introduced to the construction industry to improve cost, schedule and quality of modern 
high complexity projects over traditional construction methods (Becerik-gerber & Kent 2010) and 
its creation was triggered by the wide acceptance and implementation of the simple form of BIM 
(technology). According to (Becerik-Gerber & Rice 2010), in a survey of 424 respondents from 
architecture and engineering firms, IPD was cited as the most effective project delivery method in 
facilitating the use of “Comprehensive BIM”. The main idea behind the formulation of this type of 
contract and construction philosophy was the need to take full advantage of the “comprehensive 
BIM” principles, which required early involvement of most of the project stakeholders in the 
planning and design phase. However, most of them used to get involved in the phase where they 
were contractually required to perform the major work (Kam et al. 2013), so that they covered 
themselves legally against lawsuits, when the traditional design-bid-build or design-build contracts 
were applied. Additional problems related to the cross-purposed interests of involved stakeholders 
due to focus on individual costs/benefits resulting from the structure of the traditional construction 
practice are shortly pointed out below (Bendewald et al. 2010): 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
 Paper-based documentation results in errors and omissions which lead to unanticipated field 
delays and increased costs.  It is well known that these problems increase the rate of lawsuits 
and question liability between parties. 
 
 The use of rules of thumb is many times related to oversizing of construction elements. 
 
 The absence of interoperability led to increased Requests for Information (RFI’s) and 
corresponding construction delays. 
Moreover, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) NIST conducted a study of the 
additional costs incurred as a result of inadequate interoperability between stakeholders, which 
represents one of the main shortcomings of traditional construction practices (Gallaher et al. 2004). 
This study was accomplished by comparing business activities and costs of traditional construction 
methods with hypothetical scenarios defined by efficient information flow and absence of 
redundant data entry. Even if the estimates of the survey are considered speculative due to the 
absence of accurate data, they show a clear trend and highlight deficiencies of the traditional 
stakeholder engagement and collaboration methods that should be taken into consideration. 
Following that, the main concern of this new type of contract was to incentivize stakeholders 
through legally protected ways to develop mutual trust with each other and collaborate during the 
initiation of the conceptual phase of the project. Based on (Bendewald et al. 2010; Ghassemi & 
Becerik-Gerber 2011; AIA California Council 2010), the main principles that describe the perception 
of IPD are the following: 
 Early involvement of key participants 
 Shared risk and reward 
 Multi-Party Contract 
 Collaborative decision – making and control 
 Liability waivers among key participants 
 Jointly developed and validated project goals 
 
2.1.3 BIM (Thesis Perception) 
Last but not least, taking the literature analysis of BIM and IPD into consideration, the perception 
to be used in this thesis is based on the integration of these two terms. IPD creates the synergetic 
atmosphere required for the collaborative aspect of BIM as it aligns the expectations of all 
stakeholders and motivates them to work closely together from early design to occupancy of the 
project (Becerik-gerber & Kent 2010). According to that, BIM is not perceived as a technological 
advancement, but as a unified process change (Eastman et al. 2011) that enables integrated design 
process and facilitates communication (Cavallero 2006). The term “Com-BIM” will be used to refer 
to the “comprehensive BIM” concept in the context of IPD.

2.2 Performance Assessment of Construction Projects 
As it can be concluded from the above mentioned facts, there is an existing confusion around BIM 
which is highlighted by the frequency and variety of definitions, as well as the different 
conceptualization of the term by distinctive stakeholders. This confusion leads to deficiencies that 
prohibit the wide implementation of “Com-BIM” in the construction industry. There is a large 
number of construction projects conducted under the “Com-BIM” concept (Cheng et al. 2012). 
Notwithstanding the proven positive outcome of these projects, there is a respected number of 
stakeholders that cannot still perceive BIM benefits and claim that it is not beneficial to invest on 
the switch from traditional construction practice to integrated construction process. Furthermore, 
many firms decide to establish their BIM philosophy only partially, meaning that they use BIM to 
yield benefits regarding coordination and quantification of building elements, but their BIM 
implementation process ends with the creation of conventional construction documentation 
[design-focused BIM according to (Cavallero 2006)]. This results in limitations regarding the 
voluminous possibilities of BIM, as they do not take advantage of the collaborative aspects of “Com-
BIM” in the context of IPD. 
The main driver of this resistance against the implementation of “Com-BIM” is the absence of 
official development of BIM performance assessment methods to qualitatively and quantitatively 
measure the positive impact of BIM on project outcomes. According to (Elmualim & Gilder 2013), 
30% of the respondents out of 143 responses of a survey conducted in the British construction 
industry claim that the assessment methods cannot persuade them about benefits overweighing 
costs or are not tangible enough to warrant the use of BIM. Without a valid set of metrics accepted 
by the construction industry as a whole, no meaningful performance may be achieved and financial 
investments may be misplaced (Succar et al. 2014). Furthermore, various firms may take advantage 
of the confusion around the term and the absence of official metrics and commit “BIM Wash”, 
which according to (Succar 2011) is the attempt to hide imperfections while at the same time 
promoting a detective view of one’s BIM capability. In a simple Figure “BIM Wash” is illustrated 
through: 
BIM CLAIM > BIM COMPETENCY  
BIM Wash is a trend that is expected to propagate in the market, since the demand for BIM-based 
solutions is continuously increasing, whereas the BIM Competency of companies cannot be 
objectively assessed. (Succar 2011) 
For all these reasons, the need for the establishment of tangible and meaningful performance 
assessment methods acknowledged by the entire construction industry, that provide analysis of 
the costs and benefits of implementing “Com – BIM” is more than compelling. This is the only way 
for stakeholders to be persuaded to invest in the adoption of the new philosophy if not done yet, 
or to advance their implementation and investment strategies (Dodge Data & Analytical 2015) by 
being in the position of assessing their individual performance and consequently comparing it 
against industry benchmarks (Succar et al. 2014). In the next subchapter, a literature review of the 
most important performance measurement cases is presented. Based on the conducted literature 
review, three categories of project performance assessment methods were pointed out, namely: 
 
 Traditional construction practice  
 BIM capability  
 Business value of BIM

2.2.1 Traditional Construction Practice (Literature Review) 
Even if BIM delineates the new and innovative philosophy defining the modern construction 
practice, it was not created from scratch. On the contrary, it constitutes an evolvement of the 
traditional construction principles that incorporates advancements in technology, human needs 
and other parameters. Based on the above mentioned fact, it is critical to include a short literature 
review of the main performance indicators of traditional construction, as BIM metrics and value 
assessments were created based on the absorption of these indicators.  
Until 1950, performance measurements were directly connected to financial parameters (Bassioni 
et al. 2004). However, soon it proved that managerial decisions could not adapt to up-to-date 
information, as the results from financial performance measurements were released after the 
outcome of their initiatives (Bassioni et al. 2004). Such shortcomings were emphasized and 
criticized by many authors [(Kaplan 1984); (Bourne et al. 2000); (Norreklit 2000)]. This lead to the 
necessity of developing additionally non-financial information metrics. The main performance 
measurement frameworks used from managers and construction specialists throughout the last 20 
years are the “Performance Pyramid” (Dixon et al. 1990), the “Balanced Scorecard” (Kaplan & 
Norton 1992) and the European Foundation for Quality Management (EFQM) Excellence Model. 
An important approach to the traditional performance measurement in construction is analyzed in 
(Cox et al. 2003). In this article, the authors try to identify a set of common indicators to be used 
by construction stakeholders, based on a thorough literature review of construction industry 
publications. Following that, a research is conducted built on qualitative and quantitative Key 
Performance Indicators (KPI’s) mentioned in the studied literature. These indicators are then 
evaluated according to well-structured research hypotheses, from where the most widely used 
KPI’s are extracted. The KPI’s with the greatest significance and the highest percentage of 
respondent confidence (Cox et al. 2003) are analyzed in the following points: 
 
 Units/MHR [quantitative]: This method measures the number of completed units put in place 
per individual man-hour of work (Halligan et al. 1994). 
 
 $/Unit [quantitative]: The dollar value associated with putting one complete unit in place, 
including material costs, labor costs, waste and equipment costs (Cox et al. 2003). 
 
 Safety [qualitative]: The parameter of safety can be measured both as qualitative, but also as 
quantitative. It is assessed as a quantitative index through incidence rates and Experience 
Modification Ratings (EMRs) (Cox et al. 2003), whilst its objective of eliminating losses due to 
poor working practices (Hislop 1993) is considered as qualitative. 
 
 Total cost [quantitative]: This index involves comparison of total construction cost after the 
completion of the project with the budgeted cost allocated for this specific project (Cox et al. 
2003). 
 On-time completion [quantitative]: the parameter of time is involved in the metrics through this 
index, which serves as a holistic measurement of performance to schedule duration (Cox et al. 
2003). 
 
 Quality control/reworks: Even if rework is traditionally responsible for 6-12% of the total 
construction cost (Cox et al. 2003), it is difficult to quantitatively assess this index. A possible 
way proposed by (Cox et al. 2003) is by “calculating the change in the number of man-hours and 
material costs for repairing work in place or rehandling materials”.

2.2.2 BIM Capability (Literature Review) 
A great extent of articles related to performance assessment of BIM-based methods are directed 
towards the capabilities of companies, individuals, or even industries to implement BIM. Thus, their 
research is focused on the development or evaluation of assessment tools that measure the ability 
of concerned parties to materialize construction project process through the use of BIM methods. 
This is accomplished by the provision of milestones, criteria, checkpoints that place companies or 
individuals in a certain capability level with respect to others. Such a benchmarking tool enables 
according to (Sebastian & van Berlo 2010) clients to select firms that possess the required know-
how. Moreover, designers, engineers and contractors will be able to reflect their capabilities, 
improve their deficiencies and withstand the competition through a state-of-the-art proficiency.   
This part of research is considered as very important for the development of the construction 
project assessment tool, since it is impossible to evaluate the outcome or the process of a BIM-
based construction project without examining the BIM capability level of the involved parties. 
Furthermore, as mentioned earlier, through the establishment of an evaluation methodology that 
incorporates the assessment of capability levels of concerned parties, it will be easier to avoid the 
proliferation of BIM wash incidents and compare under a common basis. Last but not least, the 
existence of a valid performance assessment tool that incorporates BIM Capability evaluation will, 
according to (Succar et al. 2014) close the gap between “expected BIM deliverables” and 
“unforeseen requirements”.  The mismatch between the aforementioned terms increases risks, 
costs and BIM implementation difficulties (Succar et al. 2014). 
Taking this into consideration, a detailed analysis of such literature sources was conducted, which 
highlighted the most widely accepted BIM Capability assessment concepts. Most of them represent 
milestones that define the specific ability of a company or individual to implement BIM methods, 
whereas one of them [Point of Adoption by (Succar & Kassem 2015)] embodies a framework that 
combines various steps and milestones. The short description of the concepts is also followed by 
critics regarding their expected efficiency. 
 
2.2.2.1 Concept 1 – iBIM (Integrated Building Information Modeling) 
The UK maturity model - also known as the iBIM model (the name of its highest level) or the BIM 
Wedge (due to its famous shape) - was developed by Mark Bew and Mervyn Richards in 2008 
(Succar et al. 2012). This specific model incorporates the BIM strategy of the UK, along with industry 
initiatives required to fulfil it. It consists of four levels defining the BIM maturity. The UK perception 
of maturity may be described as “the construction supply chain’s ability to operate and exchange 
information” (Khosrowshahi & Arayici 2012). 
More specifically, Level 0 represents the computer aided design or CAD tools that create drawings 
and draw elements using IT software. This is actually the initial step evolving the construction 
industry from generating drawings by hand. Levels 1-3 illustrate the varying degrees of modelling 
collaboration and eventually the exchange of fully integrated interoperable data, leading to the 
deployment of BIM philosophies for the Lifecycle Management of construction outcomes. Level 1 
points out the step from traditional CAD drawings to the creation of 2D information through the 
3D model. In Level 2, 3D models are shared along the project team through a single online area, 
known as a common data environment. Last but not least, Level 3 visualizes the fully integrated 
information model, which is entirely developed in a common data environment and accessible by 
all stakeholders, setting the basis for the implementation of BIM in the lifecycle management 
project phase.  
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
 
Figure 2 The UK BIM Maturity Model (Khosrowshahi & Arayici 2012) 
 
Through the analysis of the illustration, it can be pointed out that the UK BIM Maturity Model 
represents more an industry strategy rather than a maturity assessment tool. What is meant by 
that is does not include clearly defined assessment metrics that may evaluate abilities of 
individuals, organizations or teams to implement BIM. On the contrary, this specific model mostly 
defines a strategic plan, which includes codes, data, and some steps required to jump from the one 
level to the other. Many countries (Finland, Denmark, United States of America and others) have 
also developed such a strategic BIM plan, either by adapting the UK model into their needs, or by 
introducing a completely new one. Last but not least, in the description, the writers clearly state 
that Level 3 and part of Level 2 are not yet clearly delineated. 
 
2.2.2.2 Concept 2 – IU BIM Proficiency Matrix 
The BIM Proficiency Matrix, developed by the Indiana University Architect’s Office, is a simplified 
excel worksheet that “is used to assess the proficiency of a respondent’s skill at working in a BIM 
environment” (IU 2009). This matrix includes 8 categories (Figure 3) which are considered to 
incorporate all the essential parameters influenced by the implementation of BIM based working 
methods. Moreover, each category is divided into four subparts, which intend to provide a holistic 
view of each category. This excel worksheet is filled up by inserting one point to each part that 
corresponds to the individual member’s capability. Then, points are tallied and the total BIM 
Maturity score is calculated. Based on the total score, assessed parties are placed in one out of five 
levels, as shown in Figure 3. 
 
==PAGE:22==
14 
 
 
 
 
Figure 3 IU BIM Proficiency Matrix (IU 2009) 
 
Taking this measurement tool into consideration, both advantages and shortcomings were 
highlighted. As far as the benefits of the tool are concerned, the IU BIM Proficiency Matrix enables 
the assessment of BIM capabilities of individuals. Following that, organizations may use it to 
evaluate their employees, but also clients, contractors or other stakeholders may take advantage 
of it and choose experienced BIM specialists. Secondly, the measurement tool is simple and the 
existence of clear explanations in each part permits the fast completion without concern over 
misunderstandings. Furthermore, the provision of space for the placement of photos that prove 
the corresponding capabilities makes the tool much more authoritative. Last but not least, when 
compared to concept 1, this tool is clearly targeted towards the assessment of the ability of 
individuals to perform within projects using BIM based working methods, whilst the word maturity 
is specified more appropriately.  
On the other hand, the IU BIM Proficiency Matrix is characterized by critical shortcomings that 
decrease its trustfulness and validity. Initially, the matrix is oriented towards assessing the BIM 
capabilities of individuals, but not the project outcome. Thus, this specific measurement tool 
cannot evaluate the BIM performance of a company, as a set of individuals, regarding the successful 
or not conductance of a project. Moreover, even if there is a reference to the capability levels, this 
specific matrix does not provide a clear BIM framework that incorporates both maturity and 
capability parameters. Another important shortcoming is the absence of weighted responses based 
on the significance of each part, which also affects negatively the validity of the measurement. 
Lastly, the absence of graphical representation of results in the Excel Worksheet hinders the easy 
and substantial perception of the results.

2.2.2.4 Concept 5 – BIM Adoption 
The BIM Adoption concept (Succar & Kassem 2015), introduced by the professor Bilal Succar, 
represents a framework that is focusing towards including all aspects of BIM implementation 
concepts into one graph. Two main parameters are incorporated into this graph, which is illustrated 
in Figure 6, namely the Capability Stages and the Maturity Levels: 
 
 Capability Stages (minimum ability) 
Refer to major performance milestones to be crossed towards higher and higher BIM Capability 
(Succar et al. 2013). These milestones include the pre-BIM, object-based modeling, model-based 
collaboration and network-based integration, until the post-BIM Stage (not defined yet) is 
reached (Succar et al. 2014). Specific variables define each milestone.  
 
 Maturity Levels (performance milestones) 
Refer to the gradual improvement in quality, repeatability and predictability within each 
Capability Stage (Succar et al. 2013). Respectively to the Capability Stages, which refer to the 
minimum ability, Maturity Levels refer to the incremental performance milestones within each 
stage (Succar 2009). Specified milestones apply to this category as well.  
 
The BIM Adoption aims at demonstrating how measuring organizational BIM abilities requires a 
multi-faceted approach and combining different BIM performance metrics (Succar et al. 2012). 
More specifically, taking Figure 6 into consideration, the y-axis (Performance) incorporates the 
Capability Stages. On the other hand, the y-axis (Time) includes the Maturity levels, namely the 
gradual increase in performance with respect to time. Capability Stages are classified with numbers 
(1, 2 and 3), whereas Maturity Levels with letters (a, b, c, d and e). Important to mention are the 
points of Adoption (1a, 2a and 3a), classified with PoA, PoA2 and PoA3 that define the jump from 
the one Capability Stage to the other, which is directly related to performance improvements over 
time.  
The BIM Adoption concept is a holistic approach for evaluating the competency of BIM 
implementing organizations and being able to develop industry benchmarks, based on which 
companies can evaluate their BIM performance. However, the process is BIM-oriented and cannot 
assess a building project process.

2.2.3 Business value of BIM (Literature Review) 
A great number of the reviewed articles does not encounter BIM as just an IT investment focused 
towards evolving technological processes. On the contrary, BIM-based methods are considered 
from this part of literature as a business investment affecting the whole company. Furthermore, it 
is highlighted that the BIM implementation does not only bring changes to the software and 
hardware development, but mainly to the complete structure of the company. Following that, 
company processes, managerial relationships and models, relationships between different 
companies endeavor to adapt to the modern circumstances. From this standpoint, researchers 
strive to create “BIM business cases” that, according to (Barlish & Sullivan 2012), provide a proper 
benefit analysis measuring not only returns, but also calculating investments required for the 
implementation of comprehensive BIM-based working methods. Last but not least, based on 
(Becerik-Gerber & Rice 2010), benefits are classified into the following three categories: 
 
 Tangible benefits: quantifiable in monetary terms 
 
 Semi-tangible benefits: quantifiable, but not in monetary terms 
 
 Intangible benefits: described qualitatively (Becerik 2006) 
 
According to the available literature that assesses the business benefits of BIM, a proper analysis 
has to incorporate both quantifiable, but also qualitative benefits and costs in order to ensure a 
holistic approach that considers both project outcome and project process. Under these 
circumstances, as properly described in the methodology part, X articles were chosen to be 
analyzed further, according to the maximum fulfillment of evaluation criteria developed under the 
scope of expressing the BIM perception of the current thesis. More specifically, selected articles 
had to incorporate most of the following variables: 
 
 Iron Triangle [Time, Cost, Quality] 
 Quantifiable Metrics / KPI’s (Key Performance Indicators) 
 Cost-Benefit Analysis 
 Performance Assessment of collaborative processes 
 BIM Maturity Index 
 Reference to Integrated Project Delivery (IPD)

3. PROJECT ASSESSMENT TOOL (Development of framework) 
The framework of the assessment tool includes all known and suspected parameters, as well as 
their relationship to each other, which influence or measure the project performance. Since the 
framework was created to be used both in traditional non-BIM and modern BIM construction 
projects, all parameters had to be checked for validity with both concepts. However, as the modern 
construction practice is defined by the wide implementation of BIM based and integrative methods, 
these terms had to be additionally incorporated, in order to create the basis for the comparison of 
the two approaches.  
It was among the main intentions of the tool development procedure to follow the project timeline. 
Since a construction project is composed of different processes and milestones, the project 
performance assessment tool should also correspond to these processes. As a result, the main tool 
consists of three processes (A, B and D) and one milestone (C), as shown in Figure 12. The green 
color indicates the main areas (B and C) of focus in the context of this research. This decision was 
taken on the one hand because these processes outline the main project processes and on the 
other hand due to the focus of “Com-BIM” concepts on the early project phases. 
 
Figure 12  Assessment tool alignment with Project Timeline 
 
 (A): The “pre-Project Process” comprises all essential steps to be taken prior to the initiation of 
the main Planning phase. As a result, it corresponds to the pre-Planning project phase, where 
an analysis of various parameters is conducted in order for the client to reach to a decision 
regarding the feasibility of the main idea. Since this phase is not directly related to the main 
project process and the information provided is limited, no extended analysis is accommodated. 
 
 (B): “Project Process” covers the whole project timeline form the initiation of the project until 
the end of the construction phase, where the building project is delivered to the client. Due to 
this fact, it incorporates the main Planning phase, the Design and the Construction phase. The 
“Project Process” (B) category includes all variables that may impact the project performance, 
from important decisions during the project planning phase (contracts, team selection etc.) to 
team collaboration principles and BIM implementation tactics and capabilities. Based on the 
comprehensive literature review, advantages of BIM-based working methods mostly depend on 
the collaborative structures introduced through the “Com-BIM” concept. Following that, it was 
among the main intentions of the development of the present assessment tool to consolidate 
parameters that assess these structures and compare them against traditional ones. That is why 
the “Project Process” area is mainly directed towards these conceptualizations, at the same time 
including also basic project processes necessary to be compared.  
==PAGE:33==

 (C): The “Project Performance” Category encompasses parameters that reflect the measured 
project performance. Based on this, category C represents the main project milestone (point 
arrow and rhombus) with the intention of evaluating the project outcome. Since the content 
has to enable the comparison of traditional with modern construction aspects, Key Performance 
Indicators (KPI’s) were selected that are widely used in the construction fraternity and 
incorporate BIM and non-BIM variables.  
 
 (D): The “Project Lifecycle” category considers the parameters that are directly connected to

4. INTERVIEW PRINCIPLES (Analysis of the interview structure concept) 
As it was clearly stated in the methodology chapter (1.3), the Interview sessions provided two 
crucial elements to the research: 
 Evaluation of the developed assessment tool framework 
 Input for the analysis of the results 
The interviews took place during the last two months of the research and were used as case studies. 
The main focus of the selection of the Interviewees was the need to cover as many categories as 
possible, based on the experience of the selected parties, or the fame of the company they are

new approach for the evaluation of projects and highlighting of benefits of BIM based methods, 
the main principle behind the interviews was the dedication of more time (one hour and a half to 
two hours) per Interviewee rather than the creation of a big sample of answers. Through this 
process, the development of the conceptual form of the assessment tool was strengthened, as 
semi-structured questions resulted in the concurrent evaluation of the tool. In total, four people of 
the construction industry were interviewed. Their names, accompanied with a few details of their 
background, as well as the symbolic name used to refer to their statements, are presented below: 
 Traditional Greek construction practice  
Dr. Christos Anagnostopoulos (Code Name: CHR) is a Professor of the Geotechnical Department 
and Head of the Civil Engineering Department in the Aristotle University of Thessaloniki, Greece. 
Apart from the academic background witnessed through his placement as Head of Department, 
he has a great carrier in the construction industry sector, where he worked as an engineer and

5.2.1.1 Contracts (B1.1) 
The contracts create a mutual or legal binding agreement between the client and one or more 
parties for the satisfactory completion of the construction project from conception to occupancy 
(Cmaa 2012). Since a building project is constituted of multidimensional activities, the contracts 
have to cover an extensive amount of options and guidelines that define processes to be followed, 
but also protect the client and other stakeholders against failures. 
The category incorporates contract types that establish the relationships between involved parties, 
procurement methods defining the process of awarding the contracts (Molenaar et al. 2014) and 
other agreements that are supplementary to the main contracts, but influence the project process 
and outcome to a great extent.

a) Contract type (B1.1.1) 
The contract types to be used as a reference for this specific category were chosen based on an 
extended literature review, combined with the input provided by the interviews. Following that, 
two contract types, Design – Bid – Build (DBB) and Design – Build (DB) were selected to represent 
the common construction practice, whereas Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) is used as the optimal 
case for the implementation of the “com-BIM” philosophy: 
 DBB: It is the contract type that dominated the construction industry prior to the development 
of BIM based processes (Bim & Jones 2009). Also known as the representative of the traditional 
construction method, DBB is defined by the clear distinction and linearity of the project 
processes (Molenaar et al. 2014). Two main contracts are signed up in this contract type, namely 
one between the owner and the design team and one between owner and primary contractor,

a-a) Procurement methods (B1.1.2) 
Closely related to the contract types are the procurement methods. What is meant by procurement 
methods is the process that the owner follows in order to award the contract to the design team 
and the contractor (Molenaar et al. 2014). Through the literature review, three different 
possibilities of procurement methods were highlighted: 
 Low bid: This specific method implies for the case that the client chooses the design team and 
primarily the contractor based on the lowest cost in the bidding phase. The cost proposal of the 
contractor results from the careful examination of all required sets of drawings. 
 Best value: This method of contract awards examines two parameters, namely cost and quality. 
In general, the owner that chooses this method awards the contract based on the amount of 
value added to the project. In this method, the owner completes an evaluation of financial and 
technical parameters and chooses the primary contractor based on a weighted average of the 
two aforementioned parameters.  
 Sole source: In this procurement process, the owner selects the primary contractor mostly 
based on non-financial parameters. This means that the choice depends above all on the 
competency of the contractor due to experience, technical capabilities or established 
relationship with the client. In this case, no bidding process is conducted.  
Last but not least, contract types and procurement methods have evolved over the years and many 
variations have been developed. However, the aforementioned types will be used as the reference 
for the analysis of the interviews.

b) Other Agreements (B1.1.3) 
In the category of other agreements, both Design (“Ydelsesbeskrivelser”, ABR 89/92) and ICT 
specifications (“IKT specification”) are included (Ydelsesbeskrivelser 2012). This category assesses 
every supplementary document assisting the main contracts and providing a strategy for 
implementation during the whole project process. As the main contracts, these types of 
agreements are also signed up in the early planning phase of the project. 
ICT Specifications or Agreements are an official contract equivalent to the common ones that 
defines the establishment of ICT activities. Based on (Ydelsesbeskrivelser 2012), an ICT specification 
develops the technical protocol for “collaboration, processes and digital deliveries.” The ICT 
Specification is concurrent to the Design Specification. The first one defines the requirements and 
strategy for implementation of digital tools, whereas the later includes all requirements for 
deliveries to be fulfilled during the project process. The existence of ICT specifications promotes 
the use of Building Information Modelling in the construction industry, in order to raise quality and 
efficiency throughout the project (Committee 2014). The ICT Specification can be either attached 
to the consultancy agreement together with the design specification, or created by the ICT manager 
as part of the design management. 
 
Traditional

 “For construction projects, additionally to the main contracts, an agreement is signed up that

alignment with requirements and obligation of all involved parties. The ICT and Design

contractor).

5.2.1.2 Compensation methods or Incentives for Cooperation (B1.2) 
Compensation methods include all the efforts, measures or initiatives taken in order to align the 
interests of stakeholders, planning for earlier collaboration between the main concerned parties. 
These initiatives are mostly combined with financial parameters, so that stakeholders are 
motivated to interact with each other more actively in order to meet or exceed goals. In parallel to 
financial incentives, building of trust between the main stakeholders in order to collaborate early 
was also mentioned as one way of providing compensation methods. Last but not least, the 
assurance of competency, especially in the form of BIM Capability, is considered as well. These 
three concepts will be analyzed and compared in the following subsections, based on the interviews 
that were conducted: 
 
a) Financial incentives (B1.2.1) 
Financial incentives is the main compensation method of providing motivation for stakeholders, in 
order to follow early collaboration strategies. Usually, financial incentives are translated into a fixed 
percentage added to the project cost, which is used in case of delays in time or additional project 
cost. However, if the main stakeholders make it to finish on time and within cost, fulfilling the 
requirements defined in collaboration with the client, then this percentage will be shared to them 
as bonus. Following that, financial incentives are implemented mainly for two reasons: 
 Motivation for early collaboration – alignment of goals 
 Reduced project time and cost 
The results of the interviews for both non-BIM (traditional) and BIM (modern) regarding the two 
main scopes of implementing financial incentives are: 
 
Traditional (Reduced project time and cost) 
In the traditional construction practice, according to CHR, the main philosophy is that every 
member involved in the construction process is paid after satisfying the requirements. This means 
that the architect will get paid after handing-in the detailed building drawings, the structural 
engineer when delivering the structural system design, the contractor when the project is 
constructed and so on.  
“The payment is conducted after finishing the job”, as CHR claimed.  
However, there is a possibility of providing financial incentives, only in the case of big complex 
private projects. These incentives are translated into bonus (percentage of total project cost added 
to salary) usually in case of finishing the project earlier, since this equals earlier initiation of the 
building operation phase, meaning earlier initiation of the business activity of the client. 
“Financial incentives may be given in cases of big private projects. This is translated as a 
percentage of bonus per day of finishing the project earlier”, according to CHR.  
Nevertheless, even in the case of providing bonus, this is specifically directed towards one of the 
main stakeholders, namely the primary contractor, leading to the conclusion that it is not used as 
a motivation for early collaboration. Following that, the provision of financial incentives in the 
traditional construction practice is not in favor of collaborative and interactive project processes.  
==PAGE:50==
42 
 
Modern (motivation for early collaboration and reduced project time and cost) 
Based on KIM, the provision of financial incentives in favor of early collaboration and alignment of 
goals is a very important feature of project planning. He also mentioned “THE ØRESUND LINK” as 
an example of successful implementation of this initiative. “THE ØRESUND LINK” is a bridge 
construction connecting Denmark and Sweden. Based on (Ramboll n.d.), this specific project was 
accomplished in order to strengthen the cultural and economic cooperation between the two 
countries, but also as a general European planning initiative to connect big northern European cities 
together (Tunnel across Femern). According to KIM, the owners of the bridge 
(“Øresundskonsortiet”, constituted 50% of the Danish government and 50% of the Swedish 
government) added 5% in the proposed project cost on the condition that the main stakeholders 
will collaborate early in the project phase and finish on time and within cost. The result according 
to KIM was that the project finished earlier and with a lower final project cost that the planned 
one.   
“It is the biggest project in Denmark and probably the only one that finished before time and 
with a lower final cost”, he said, adding that “the main reason was that everyone was 
collaborating from the early phases in order in order to gain this shared 5%”. 
Moreover, Ramboll, the main client consultant that decided in collaboration with the client to offer 
this motivation, is implementing this idea in other projects as well. Last but not least, Interviewer 
3 also agreed that providing financial incentives for early collaboration would indeed offer 
motivation.  
Taking everything into consideration, financial incentives are 
considered as important for the accomplishment of early 
collaboration processes and team spirit. Furthermore, when 
comparing BIM with non-BIM construction practice, it is 
concluded that the presence of BIM aligns as a philosophy with 
the existence of incentives for early collaboration between the 
main stakeholders. Moreover, there are examples that this 
concept has been applied successfully, meaning that it represents 
an actual practice and not a potential benefit.

5.2.1.3 Risk Allocation (B1.3) 
All construction projects are unique and carry their own risks. As the complexity of a project rises, 
risks increase and many times influence the positive or negative outcome of the project process. 
Based on (Lam et al. 2007), “risk can be defined as a hazard, a probability of it to occur and the 
potential of losses and resulted gains.” Risks are confronted by every party that is involved in the 
project process. Nevertheless, risk is proportional to the level of involvement. This means that the 
more important the role of a party is with regard to development and successful completion of the 
project the greater the risk they carry (Peckiene et al. 2013), which is translated in higher gains or 
losses based on the outcome.  
On that sense, it is being obvious that the allocation of risk is very critical for the project process 
and interactions between stakeholders. Moreover, risk allocation, which is defined through the 
main contract that the client signs up with the stakeholder(s), may vary from individual to shared, 
based on the distribution of responsibilities in the involved parties. The most common example of 
individual party risk allocation is the contract between client and primary contractor, when the 
client gives full responsibility of the conductance of the construction work to only one party 
(contractor). On the other hand, shared risk allocation is among the main principles of IPD, since as 
the financial award for achieving jointly developed goals, it aligns the interests of involved project 
stakeholders and forces them to interact from the early phases in favor of succeeding. In other 
words, shared risk enforces the team spirit aspect. In the following Figure (19), risk allocation is 
placed in relation to the tree main contract types mentioned earlier.

5.2.1.4 Owner’s – Commissioning Requirements (B1.4) 
Based on the National Institute of Building Sciences, the owner’s project requirements (OPR) are 
composed of project goals, measurable performance criteria, cost considerations, benchmarks, 
success criteria and supporting information for the project. The owner’s project requirements 
(OPR) are closely related to the commissioning process, which is an intensive quality assurance 
process that ensures alignment of requirements with the design and construction phase. 
Moreover, the commissioning process is applied at the end of the construction process to secure 
mapping of design with the final outcome. In other words, the commissioning process evaluates 
the project performance outcome with respect to the project scope set by the client during the 
planning phase, but also ensures operation and maintenance quality. Taking this into consideration, 
for the limits of this research, the owner requirements represent the scope of the project, whereas 
the commissioning process refers to the steps taken to ensure that the scope is met during the 
project lifecycle.  
On that sense, it is becoming obvious that the clear specification of the project scope through the 
client’s requirements is a vital project-planning step. Based on (Forgues 2006), ill-defined 
requirements and more specifically requirements that lack of planning and consulting processes or 
are not specified into detail may necessitate numerous adjustments during the project process.  
Since this specific field is under extended and ongoing investigation, it is not yet clear how it can 
be assessed. Following that, some important parameters taken from the literature review and 
interviews will be mentioned, which will then be assessed with reference to traditional and modern 
construction practices. These parameters are: 
 Client’s involvement in the specification of the requirements, but also during the project phase 
for quality assurance. 
 Existence of commissioning initiatives 
 Collaborative processes from the early phases, in order to assist the specification of the project 
scope with multidisciplinary knowledge. 
 
Traditional (Design Team responsible of mapping requirements in the design) 
In the traditional Greek construction practice, the requirements are set during the planning phase 
between the architect and the corresponding engineer, taking the respective regulations into 
consideration. Moreover, as CHR claims: 
“An external party is assigned with the supervision of the alignment with the requirements. 
Nevertheless, this party gets actively involved only in the case of an emergency. This means that it 
is up to the experience of the design team to map the requirements of the client in the building 
design.” 
The linear process defining the traditional construction practice influences the alignment of the 
client’s requirements with the project process. After the briefing process, the architect becomes 
responsible of managing the requirements and translating them into drawings by combining limited 
know-how (no constructability aspects) and assumption in the case that the requirements are not 
comprehensive. Following that, when the architectural idea is translated into engineering 
principles, another important feedback loop exists due to the absence of intensive collaboration 
between the client, the architectural team, the engineers and the contractor. Moreover, the 
passive role of the client during the design and construction phase strengthens the freedom of the 
architect and other stakeholders to apply their creativity over strictly following the requirement 
specification.  
==PAGE:57==
49 
 
Modern (Absence of active requirement supervision) 
In the modern construction practice, the degree of client involvement during the design and 
construction phase is almost the same as in the traditional practice, since integrated project 
delivery methods are not yet widely implemented and streamlined. As a result, the client specifies 
the requirements during the planning phase, but does not have active involvement in the rest of 
the project phase, leading to loss of a great extent of the initial project scope. As KIM underlines: 
“One of the biggest problems is that the client is considering and specifying the requirements 
in the early project phases, then abandons the process and comes back before the initiation 
of the operation phase, where the result does not incorporate his expectations.” 
On the other hand, the Danish construction industry has developed some commissioning initiatives 
(Værdibyg 2013) for the assurance of alignment of project processes with the client requirements 
specified prior to the initiation of the project. However, based on KIM’s opinion, “it is a standard 
that no one uses and is also paper-based.”  
Last but not least, the existence of BIM influences both positively and negatively this specific 
category. On the one hand, BIM-based processes already applied in the Danish construction 
industry promote the collaboration between architects and engineers during the design phase 
through discipline models, leading to multidisciplinary analysis of requirements and less 
assumptions or individual judgement. Moreover, other BIM processes like consistency control 
ensure the validity of discipline design with respect to the main design. On the other hand, the 
transfer of information through the BIM Models mostly form the design to the construction phase 
creates a loop that is usually combined with loss of information related to the initial requirements. 
As KIM stated: 
“In my opinion, one of the biggest problems nowadays is that what the client and the 
designers are thinking in the early phase and the requirements they set are being overlooked 
throughout the project process and finally not considered.” 
Taking everything into account, the modern construction 
industry has advantages over the old one with respect to 
OPR’s and commissioning processes, but the process is not 
yet streamlined. Moreover, since paper-based initiatives are 
considered as old-fashioned, it seems that the procedure of 
ensuring digital mapping of OPR’s with the project process 
will be pending for a lot of time.

Regarding contracts, even if it seems that traditional and modern construction practices follow the

process through the variety of tools it offers. That is the reason why BIM enables the move towards 
Integrated Project Delivery concepts, even if this is not yet achieved in the Danish construction 
industry. There is an extended argumentation on reasons that obstruct the wide implementation 
of integrated project delivery contracts. As far as the Danish construction is concerned, both legal 
and procedural parameters exist. Legal parameters are closely related to existing laws disabling the 
re-structuring of the industry and concurrency of stakeholder involvement, like for instance the law 
stating that all public construction projects have to undergo a tendering phase.  
Moreover, the inability of supplementary contract types to assist and strengthen the IPD concept 
is an important drawback. Especially ICT Specifications, which were developed as a contract type 
that promotes the use of BIM models within collaborative processes by defining guidelines for 
discipline communication through IT technologies, are deficient, based on the results of the 
interviews. The most significant reason was the fact that ICT contracts assess mostly the planning 
and design phase, leaving the construction phase uninvolved. This creates a huge problem to 
construction companies trying to develop BIM based methods focusing towards early project phase 
involvement, since ICT Specifications do not bind project processes directly with the existence of 
the BIM Model.  
As far as the procedural parameters are concerned, these are expressed through the rest of the 
categories incorporated in this field. Compensation methods or in other words provision of 
incentives for early collaboration and streamlined project process have already added value with 
respect to reduced project time and cost mostly through financial incentives and provision of trust. 
Financial incentives are given in the case of BIM based methods both for delivery on time and within 
cost, but also for early collaboration, compared to the traditional methods where early 
collaboration is not pursued through the provision of incentives. Furthermore, trust is achieved in 
BIM based methods through the early and visual transmittance of information between 
stakeholders due to the existence of the BIM model. This also sets the basis for the further 
development of trust provision by incorporating “open-book accounting” policies. Nevertheless, 
competency is a parameter that is not yet developed, even if it is considered as crucial for the 
existence of liability, which constitutes a vital variable towards implementation of IPD concepts.

transferring of risk among stakeholders. Even if traditional and modern methods are characterized 
by the same contract types, the existence of BIM in the modern methods results in sharing the risk 
during the design phase due to the obligation of architects and engineers to develop a discipline 
BIM Model and work concurrently. On the contrary, in the traditional construction industry, risk 
allocation is mostly individual. More specifically, in the Greek construction industry, risk is 
transferred from client to the primary contractor. 
Last but not least, the deficient or absent supervision of the initial requirements by the client during 
the design and construction phase results in non-alignment of the final result with the initial ideas. 
The implementation of BIM processes on the one hand facilitates the visualization of the as-built 
model form the client and promotes the collaborative and multidisciplinary concept analysis during

a) Collaboration Type (B2.1.1) 
This specific category describes the platform used for the collaboration of the various project team 
disciplines. In other words, the type of discipline collaboration refers to the concept defining the 
way various disciplines interact with each other during the project process. For the specification of 
the discipline collaboration type the approach of (Vestergaard et al. 2012) was considered and 
combined with the Capability Stages included in (Succar et al. 2012) and explained in the literature 
review part. Following that, the type of collaboration may vary based on the following Figure: 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 23 Discipline Collaboration Type, modified from (Vestergaard et al. 2012) 
Taking the Figure into consideration, the four categories are explained further: 
 Document based (A): This is the traditional collaboration type, which is based on the exchange 
of 2D drawings. The existence of a 3D model is possible, however it is used on an individual basis 
without any collaborative processes. Usually the profession using the model may take 
advantage of some very specific advantages of 3D modelling, like mostly visualization aids. Since 
no information is added to the model, it does not represent an object-based model with 
information data. 
 Hybrid (B): In this collaboration type, some project processes are conducted based on the 3D 
BIM Model and then shared to another project member either through the 3D Model, or 
through 2D documentation. The critical parameter defining this collaboration type is the “one-
way” information sharing. This means that there are no model interchanges between the 
project members.  
 Model-based (C): The model-based collaboration type is characterized by two-way collaboration 
between project members through interchange of models (interoperable exchange). This 
means that two project team members collaborate with each other through a BIM Model 
including information data. However, this collaboration type is limited by one-to-one 
collaboration principles and exchange of discipline models only. No general model including 
information of all project members is created and shared. 
 Network-based (D, E): The network-based type of collaboration is constituted out of local server 
integration (D) and fully integrated model server (E). In the first case, collaboration between 
project team members is conducted through sharing discipline models in a local server (of a 
company for example), which can be linked, checked for collision, cost estimation, simulations 
and others by every project team member with access to the server. However, the fully 
integrated model server (E) is the concept representing the integrated supply chain which is 
completely based on one aggregate model. This aggregate model is constituted out of various 
discipline models, which are uploaded into a network-based location accessible by different 
disciplines and organizations. Project members entering the network are able both to send are 
receive information.

and deciding to move on with the project realization, the architectural team undertakes the 
responsibility of translating the conceptual idea of the client into drawings. This process finishes 
when the architectural design “expresses the vision of the architect on how the building could be 
built”. Then, the structural engineer takes over and designs the structural system of the building. 
The MEP engineer is many times underestimated in this process, since indoor quality and energy 
consumption are not considered critical during the design phase. Moreover, MEP engineers usually 
depend on “rules of thumb” to propose the system to be implemented in the building and that is 
why they are being consulted at a later design stage. Moreover, the client is not part of this process 
after handing in the concept and the requirements to the respected stakeholder. He is being 
notified only in the case of a dead end between project members, where he has to take the crucial 
decisions. Last but not least, the contractor receives the drawings after the end of the design phase 
and transacts the construction of the project without influencing the planning and design phase, 
unless a DB contract is to be applied. 
This simplified description, which is based on the experience of CHR, proves the linearity and 
fragmentation of traditional construction practices. Individual members (architect, structural 
engineer, MEP engineer and others) have clearly defined goals and limitations and they are trying 
to fulfill their requirements without necessarily interacting with each other. As CHR states, 
interaction between stakeholders is possible mostly in the design phase, when the architect may 
consult the structural engineer in order to efficiently combine architectural creation with structural 
system. In order to explain this, he used the following example: 
“Let's say that the architect wants to design a dance hall with the dimensions 30*50, which 
will not have any structural part in between. He decides to design a concrete slab of 10cm (he 
cannot use steel). Of course this is not possible. The architect should ask the structural 
engineer: I want to design the dance hall of these specific dimensions without having 
intermediate structural parts that obstruct its function. What is the thickness of the structural 
elements needed? Are we going to use beams, or just a pre-stressed slab? Then, after taking 
back the feedback of the structural engineer, you would have a clear idea of the ceiling height. 
Following that, the architect should also consult the MEP engineer. Especially in a space with 
this functionality as the dance hall of the example, the MEP requirements are of great 
importance. In that sense, the architect should consult the MEP engineer as well and ask him 
about the space requirements”. 
However, this interaction is directly proportional to the project complexity, takes place on 
individual basis and is not systematic. This also means that it is strongly dependent on “the 
intentions of the architect”, who is not obliged to develop his design in collaboration with the 
engineers in favor of expressing his creativity. The proportionality of interaction to project

d) Space Management (B2.1.2) 
Space management or spatial coordination is an iterative process that to a great extend influences 
the interaction between various members of the project team. This term incorporates all sub-
processes which are essential for the efficient and effective combination of architectural space, 
structural system and MEPF facilities, under the scope of fulfilling the requirements set by the client 
during the initial project phases. Since space management is directly related to the Planning and 
Design phases, it is being clear that architects and engineering consultants are mostly affected by 
this coordination process. The space management concept may be either linear document-based 
or cyclic model-based. 
A linear document-based space management approach is defined by clearly specified limitations 
among architects, engineers and contractors. Each discipline ensures that specific information is 
included in the design drawings and then delivers them to the next one. The coordination in the 
case of interactive collaboration between two or more disciplines is conducted through an overlay 
process of drawings. 
A cyclic model-based space management approach is characterized by the facilitation of the spatial 
coordination process through the use of a BIM Model.  
 
Traditional (linear document-based) 
In the traditional construction practice, a linear document-based approach with respect to spatial 
coordination is followed. As described in Chapter 5.2.2.1(b), linear is translated into sequential 
delivery of design drawings from the architect and engineers. In the case of need for interaction 
between two or more disciplines, which takes place unofficially and based on the project 
complexity, as stated in a previous Chapter, two ways exist. Either the two members use 2D 
drawings and sketches in a physical meeting, or they take advantage of the Sequential Composite 
Overlay Process (SCOP) technique. As explained by CHR, the SCOP technique, which is described as 
the placement of 2D blueprints on top of each other in an illuminated surface, is used “mostly by 
structural and MEPF engineers, as well as MEPF subcontractors, in highly complex projects.” 
Taking this into consideration, the spatial coordination process is characterized by unorganized 
procedures, which become complicated as the project complexity increases. This results in change 
orders and requests for information (RFI’s), which increase time and project cost, based on 
Interviewer’s 1 statement.

5.2.2.2 Communication Management (B2.2) 
The complexity that characterizes a building project requires effective collaboration between 
project stakeholders, as described in Chapter 5.2.2.1(b). However, an effective collaboration 
cannot be achieved without an adequate communication management. The communication 
management incorporates all parameters that assess frequency, type and quality of 
communication. Since the building project constitutes a dynamic process with lots of interactions, 
lack of strong and efficient communication flow may lead to ambiguous and unclear perception of 
drawings, reports and others, which may affect the project outcome in a negative way (Oberlender 
2000). Taking this into consideration, two subcategories were chosen to evaluate traditional and 
modern construction processes with respect to communication management, namely 
communication type and information exchange. 
a) Communication Type (B2.2.1) 
This particular category assesses the way involved parties communicate with each other. It is 
divided into physical and digital communication type: 
 Physical is the type of communication characterized by personal and direct interaction between 
parties. The physical communication type may take place either in personal through physical 
appearance of people, or digitally through Skype for example, as long as there is verbal 
interchange. 
 
 On the contrary, digital communication is defined by absence of verbal interaction. People in 
this specific type of communication exchange information only through written means or with 
the help of models and reports in digital format.  
Traditional (Physical) 
The most common type of communication in the traditional construction practice is the physical, 
since the existence of digital information exchange tools is limited and people are forced to meet 
or talk through the phone in order to collaborate. According to CHR, “discussions between involved 
parties have to take place physically. It is easier to find a solution to a problem when you draw on 
a paper and talk in personal.” Taking this into consideration, CHR claims that direct interaction 
between project stakeholders may provide faster solutions and a wider range of perspectives. On 
that sense, it is desirable, even if it cannot solve the problem of distance. 
Modern (Physical - Digital) 
The combination of physical and digital communication constitutes the main aspect of the modern 
BIM based construction industry. Stakeholders try to combine the immediacy of physical 
communication with the convenience of digital interaction. On the one hand, physical 
communication, as stated by ADH “through workshops where we meet with other project 
stakeholders” gives the opportunity for faster problem-solving. On the other hand, the diversity of 
digital tools offered nowadays enables the fast information exchange, which is at the same time 
visual, solving the difficulty of arranging appointments and bridging the potential distance. 
Moreover, digital communication provides a proof of information exchange in case of serious 
disagreements.  
Taking everything into account, the combination of physical and digital communication type 
represents the optimal case, which is already implemented in the Danish construction fraternity. 
However, as CHR highlighted, the continuous provision of software and digital tools may result in 
increasing the underlying fear of losing physical contact, which is critical and determinant, 
especially during the initial project phases. On that sense, the existence and development of BIM 
may generate a disadvantage. 
==PAGE:79==
71 
 
b) Information Exchange (B2.2.2) 
The information exchange category consolidates the process of information flow between 
stakeholders throughout the project lifecycle. In the AEC industry, the exchange of information 
between involved disciplines is determinant for the success of a building project. Following that, 
the detailed research and comparison between information exchange processes in the traditional 
non-BIM and modern BIM construction practice was considered essential.  
As a result, two different types of information exchange are considered, namely 2D document-
based, characterized by exchange of documents only on demand (1-1 type) and in predetermined 
milestones, and Model- based, which uses a BIM Model as a central data storage platform that 
enables visualization of information and daily access. Based on these types of information 
exchange, critical results from the interviews with respect to traditional and modern construction 
practice are presented below. 
 
Traditional (2D, Document-based) 
The traditional non-BIM construction practice depends on 2D drawings when it comes to 
collaboration and information exchange between disciplines during the project process. Based on 
CHR, through the detailed imprinting of all building details in plan views, sections and details, it is 
possible to achieve a proper communication and collaboration between two or more disciplines. 
However, two difficulties were pointed out that have to be mentioned, since they influence 
information flow to a great extent. Initially, the integrity of exchanged data depends on the 
experience of each discipline, as they are the ones deciding where to place the sections of the 
building that clarify all the details. Moreover, “as the building complexity increases, there is a high 
possibility of missing something that is not shown in the drawings.”

In general, the absence of visualization of crucial aspects of the building blocks the efficient 
information flow and creates loops when data has to be transferred from one discipline to another. 
This results in a great number of Requests for Information (RFI’s) and communication latency 
problems. Moreover, even in the case that a 3D Model exists and adds value with respect to

visualization, it does not represent an entity that combines different disciplines and is not object-
based, meaning that it does not transfer information. Taking this into consideration, the case of

Modern (3D Model-based) 
The modern integrated construction practice is characterized by the visual information exchange. 
All processes, either physical or digital, are conducted based on an aggregate BIM Model that 
incorporates discipline models, which are constituted of geometry and information data directly 
related to the geometry. On that sense, as CHR states, there is a clear advantage of BIM based 
methods over traditional ones through the as-built model concept, where the building is designed 
and virtually built by incorporating data from all disciplines.  KIM also agrees on that, stating that 
“the construction industry is a visual industry.” This results also in setting the basis for the 
involvement of all stakeholders in the early project phase, since the BIM based communication flow

5.2.2.3 Cost Estimation (B2.3) 
The cost estimation is among the main procedures outlining a project. Its contribution is critical for 
all project phases, especially for the Planning and Design phase. The cost estimation is performed 
both by consultants and contractors and may be document-based or model-based, according to 
two main parameters, quantity takeoff and price estimation. 
 A document-based cost estimation uses Excel files as the main tool. Quantities are measured 
based on 2D drawings and hand-written schedules. Prices are defined either in existing 
documents (for example price of concrete with specific capacity) or through experience-based 
statistical data. 
 
 A 4D BIM Model cost estimation uses object-based modelling as the main platform. The quantity 
takeoff process is conducted by selecting objects from the model. Then, these quantities are 
assigned to prices in a software, which uses common libraries combined with sub-contractor 
catalogues.  
Traditional (Document-based) 
In the traditional construction practice, three different cost estimations are performed: 
 The initial cost estimation is carried out in the pre-Planning phase and partially formulates the 
feasibility study of the project. This process is based on the experience of the client’s consultants 
and is fulfilled through fixed prices/m2 according to the area of intervention and anticipated 
quality of design.  
 The second cost estimation takes place during the Design phase, where the estimator considers 
architectural, structural and MEP designs. This estimation is accomplished by multiplying the 
area of elements with the respected price/m2, while considering thickness and weight.  
 The third cost estimation requires the fulfillment of the detailed Design phase in order to be 
carried out. In this case, estimated elements are calculated based on their detailed drawings, so 
that all quantities are considered. In private projects, the second and third estimation form 
usually one process. 
==PAGE:82==
74 
 
According to CHR, the cost estimation procedure of the traditional construction practice is defined 
by critical drawbacks that result in increased construction cost or lower construction quality. 
Initially, it is more than usual to encounter problems with reference to fixed unit prices, since every 
construction project is characterized by new conditions that affect them.  
“I may decide to build on an island, where the unit prices are different than in the mainland.” 
Moreover, the experience of the estimator to consider parameters like site conditions, logistics and 
others is compelling. Otherwise, a potential cost estimation may differ considerably from the actual 
project cost.  
“If I decide to build a house in a place that there is no existing road, I have to consider the 
road design and associated costs as well. This means that I cannot just estimate based on 
volumes and weights, but also consider such parameters.”  
Last but not least, the possibility of replacing one building element with another one (other material 
for example) is time-consuming and requires many sub-processes. This is what CHR explains in the 
following statement: 
“ If I decide to change the type of a wall, I have to search the corresponding Excel file, change 
the thickness and the details of the new materials, go back to the drawings and check other 
parameters that might get influenced (architectural space, aesthetics etc.), so you easily 
understand how complicated this process is.” 
In general, document-based cost estimation is defined by high complexity and customization, 
leading to the conclusion that the possibility of making mistakes or omissions is high. 
 
Modern (4D BIM Model-based) 
The modern cost estimation processes are conducted based on a 3D BIM Model and a cost 
estimation software. Since this method is already applied in the Danish construction industry, it is 
supplementary to include an additional part as the fully integrated case. 
The 3D BIM Model is used for the quantity takeoff. Every object included in the model is extracted 
to a cost estimation software, which includes updated libraries with prices. The whole process 
results in precise quantity takeoff and cost estimation. In a more state-of-the-art mechanism, as 
the one MTH uses, quantities and prices are included into one software. Through this update, 
stakeholders are able to visualize and compare prices at the same time.  
The main advantage of the modern cost estimation method that was pointed out both by CHR and 
ADH is the opportunity to easily conduct a “What if” search. As CHR stated: 
“I believe that the opportunity to conduct a "What if" search in a matter of minutes is the 
most important feature when comparing the two methods.” 
What is meant by that is the ability to change parameters in the BIM Model and directly get the 
cost change. Moreover, this cause-and-effect process is extended through the ability to execute 
simulations of changes with respect to building performance, in order to observe consequences in 
relation to cost, time and quality. As ADH claims:  
“The thing we can do is pin point some problems and propose alternatives of how to solve 
them, without using extra time, only by taking advantage of the model's possibilities. Instead 
of sitting in front of an Excel file and preparing the cost estimation, we also highlight the 
problems and propose alternatives by changing some values or elements.”

5.2.2.4 Other Processes (B2.4) 
The category with the name “Other Processes” includes other initiatives found in the literature 
review that perform an outstanding role with respect to influencing the project outcome. The name 
was intentionally left general, as this category is considered to develop more as other studies with 
respect to non-BIM and BIM processes are conducted. Two parameters are highlighted in this 
research, namely co-location and prefabrication. Nevertheless, other parameters that correspond 
to existing or new BIM uses (simulations, virtual reality and others) may be added as part of further 
research. 
 
a) Prefabrication (B2.4.1) 
Prefabrication or offsite manufacturing is the construction process, based on which building 
elements are not constructed through formworks on site, but they are transferred as fixed parts 
and get assembled on site. The prefabrication concept is considered as part of the movement 
towards automated production and on that sense, the evaluation of traditional and modern 
construction practices with respect to offsite manufacturing is crucial. Among the most important 
advantages of prefabricated processes, apart from the reduced construction time and streamlined 
construction process, is the reduced amount of waste produced due to the reduced need to build 
things on site.  
However, the prefabrication concept is characterized by important requirements, in order to be 
followed successfully. Initially, an extended collaborative process is needed, since architects, 
engineers and contractors and subcontractors have to be coordinated in order for an element to 
be prefabricated. In parallel to that, professionals have to extend their area of interest and consider 
various parameters when designing, since their design is directly dependent on the other 
disciplines. 
Taking this into consideration, two aspects of the prefabrication concept were assessed in order to 
highlight advantages and disadvantages of the two construction methods (BIM and non-BIM): 
 Influence on Project time and mostly on Design and Construction time 
 Proportion of building elements that are usually prefabricated. For this reason, three main 
categories were created, based on the three main design disciplines: 
1. Architectural Elements [%] (windows, doors, furniture, interior partitions, non-structural 
walls) 
2. Structural Elements [%] (slabs, walls, columns, beams) 
3. MEP Elements [%] (ventilation ducts, heating and cooling systems, pipes, fire sprinklers, 
connections) 
 
 
 
Traditional (extended Project Time) 
In the traditional construction practice, the prefabrication concept is not that common, based on 
CHR. The reason is firstly that construction experts have years of experience based on a specific 
construction method and it is difficult to adopt a new one. Moreover, the traditional project 
structure does not account for interaction and integrated collaboration between stakeholders in 
favor of prefabrication. Last but not least, the amount of available design tools hampers processes 
required for planning for prefabrication.

b) Co-location (B2.4.2) 
The co-location concept is the respective design meetings concept in the construction phase. It is 
placed in the category other processes, because it does not influence the collaboration between 
disciplines during the early project phases (Planning and Design phase), which is the main area of 
interest for the fully integrated “com-BIM” processes. Co-location is translated as the creation of 
common spaces in the construction site, so that stakeholders can cooperate during the 
construction process. Since the co-location concept is not considered to have any difference 
between traditional and modern practices, no diversification will be made in this subchapter.  
Based on CHR, the importance of co-locating different disciplines on site is of great importance 
mainly with respect to solving sudden problems that may occur. The co-location advantages follow 
the conceptual idea of the design meetings, where the existence of many disciplines results in more 
efficient and with wider perspective problem-solving process.

Taking Table 22 into account, communicative and collaborative structures that form the 
interactions of project stakeholders, combined with other processes or technologies that outline 
these structures or supplement them, are the main area of intervention, when it comes to 
comparing traditional with modern-based construction processes. Modern BIM-based methods, as 
shown in Table 22, have either an actual or a potential benefit over traditional ones in every 
category, leading to the conclusion that BIM does not only offer a variety of tools, but it also 
changes the perception of project structures. Even in the case of potential benefits, which means 
that processes are not fully consolidated yet, BIM has already clearly outpaced the traditional 
methods. The difference with respect to “B1.Planning Methods” lies in the fact that none of the 
incorporated categories is considered to face critical barriers towards achieving fully integrated 
“com-BIM” concepts. 
More specifically, as far as collaboration management is concerned, the network-based discipline 
collaboration type defined by exchange of information through BIM models uploaded in a project 
web is not yet widely achieved in Denmark mainly due to coordination issues with the models, since 
discipline models are not developed concurrently. These coordination issues result in barriers with 
respect to further integration of the network-based process.  
The project team interaction, which is directly associated to the type of collaboration, is another 
important parameter where modern BIM based processes predominate, as they brake fragmented 
traditional structures through early interactions between project team members. Nevertheless, the 
client’s and primary contractor’s involvement is not achieved yet due to aforementioned reasons 
(B1. Planning Methods). That is why the cyclic integrated collaboration structures are visualized 
mainly in the design phase through the interaction of the architectural team with the engineers. 
These interactions are accomplished through design meetings or charrettes, if the meetings take 
place early in the project. The significance of these meetings is exceptional, since they accelerate 
the decision-making process. Moreover, when conducted using an updated BIM Model as the main 
platform, they are streamlined, as opposed to discussing based on 2D drawings. The attendance 
proportion is however not 100% yet, since clients and primary contractors are not officially 
involved.  
The spatial coordination (Space Management) is another basic project process instructing 
collaboration of project members. The provision of tools like linked files and clash detection 
through BIM based methods enable the facilitation of space management processes, since building 
space is not encountered as the addition of individual discipline space requirements but as one 
entity incorporating building space needs. When the construction requirements will be actively 
involved in this process and the discipline models concurrently developed, then it will reach the 
most efficient level.  
Collaboration structures are supplemented and influenced by the flow of information between 
project members. Especially as project complexity rises, effective communication management 
possesses a vital role. In this context, physical communication type is pointed out as advantageous, 
since it may provide faster solutions and wider range of perspectives. The existence of BIM however 
facilitates digital communication through a variety of tools that among other bridge the potential 
distance between stakeholders. The underlying fear of decreasing physical communication in favor 
of digitalized processes is a potential disadvantage of BIM concepts that has to be deeply 
acknowledged.  
Closely related to the communication type is the information exchange flow. The complexity 
characterizing the traditional construction methods, where information is shared only on demand 
and on individual basis (1-1), leads to many requests for information (RFI), reworks and delays that 
result in project cost and time raise. This complexity is resolved through the use of one aggregate 
==PAGE:88==
80 
 
BIM Model, composed of discipline models, that is accessible on project web by every involved 
party at any time. This means that through BIM, information is visualized through an as-built model, 
establishing the circumstances for easier and more precise information flow that decreases 
reworks and RFI’s, resulting in decreased project cost and time.  
Alongside to communication and collaboration management, cost estimation is also among the 
main project processes influencing the performance with respect time, cost and quality. The ability 
for precise quantity takeoff form the model (5D) and direct association to price libraries is not the 
only advantage of BIM over non-BIM Excel-based methods, as the concurrent visualization of 
building, comparison of prices and simulation of project performance based on differentiation of 
elements streamlines the cost estimation process and result in more specific bidding prices and less 
change orders.  
Lastly, as far as other processes are concerned, that specific category was developed in order to 
incorporate parameters that add value to the project if employed efficiently. Prefabrication 
initiatives align with the standardization and automation concepts introduced through BIM. 
Moreover, as the option for prefabricated elements does not affect the design phase, compared to 
the need for an extended design phase to account for all construction details in the traditional 
practice, modern BIM based methods are clearly advantageous. Last but not least, the co-location 
concept, the location of different design disciplines in the same place on site to encounter sudden 
construction problems within a multidisciplinary environment is mentioned as an actual practice 
and important initiative by both methods.

5.3 Project Performance (C) 
The Project Performance field, which includes the evaluation of the performance of the project is 
further classified into the Key Performance Indicators category. 
 
5.3.1 Key Performance Indicators (KPI’s) (C1) 
Key Performance Indicators are used to measure the project or organization performance 
throughout the construction industry (Chan & Chan 2004). Since the assessment tool is clearly 
directed to compare non-BIM with BIM practices, the KPI’s were selected based on a composition 
of traditional and modern ones. Moreover, it was among the main intentions of this report to 
include common and widely used KPI’s so that they are easily accepted and understood, but at the 
same time cover different project aspects. Following that, the most common traditional KPI 
category, namely the Iron Triangle, was selected, assisted by the critical parameter of safety. The 
Iron Triangle is constituted out of time, cost and quality, which represent the three main variables 
evaluating project performances over the past and recent years. Additionally, the sustainability 
parameter is added, since it is among the leading factors in the recent years that define the 
successful or unsuccessful outcome of a construction project. Last but not least, the return on 
investment (ROI) parameter is considered due to existing opinions stating that the ROI in the case 
of BIM projects is usually negative due to the high cost of implementing BIM processes.

why dashed lines are used for this part of the framework. 
In parallel to proposing a way to measure project performance based on KPI’s, the main focus of

5.3.1.2 Cost (C1.2) 
Cost is another important variable outlining the success or failure of the construction project. Based 
on (Bubshait & Almohawis 1994), “cost is defined as the degree to which the general conditions 
promote the completion of a project within the estimated budget.” Two different categories are 
selected to describe the cost of a project, which are directly related to each other. The project cost, 
as the actual cost of the whole project process and the investment cost, which refers to amount of 
money spent as an investment for the implementation of software and hardware tools in a 
company. In the following parts, both categories are analyzed and the benefits of BIM are 
highlighted.  
 
a) Project Cost (C1.2.1) 
The project cost is the category directly related to the amount of money spent for the realization 
of the project. In other words, the project cost includes “the overall cost that a project incurs from 
inception to completion.” This overall cost includes any cost rises during the project process from 
variations, modifications during the construction period, as well as from legal claims.   
 
Measured by 
The metric that measures project performance with reference to cost is called project cost change. 
Project cost change is the increase or decrease of project cost with respect to the original budget 
set by the client prior to the initiation of the project. Following that, it is defined by subtracting the 
final project cost from the initial budget, as shown in Equation 5. As in the case of the project time, 
a positive number equals cost decrease, whereas a negative number the opposite.

5.3.1.5 Return on Investment (ROI) (C1.5) 
The Return on Investment metric is a common way of evaluating the outcome of an investment, 
since it compares the gains anticipated from an investment against the cost of the investment. 
However, as the investment gets more and more complicated, it is difficult to find a proper way of 
measuring the return on investment, since experienced economists are required. As the intention 
of this research is not related to deepening on the financial aspects of ROI, this specific metric was 
placed in order to highlight the importance of considering it when trying to overcome the drawback 
of high BIM investment. Last but not least, KIM introduced a new aspect with respect to ROI, 
namely the increase of competency, which has to be acknowledged when evaluating the positive 
or negative outcome of a BIM investment.

5.3.1.6 Discussion (C1) 
The successful or unsuccessful outcome of the project processes that were analyzed in the previous 
chapters is evaluated through metrics. The most common metrics used in the construction industry 
are the Key Performance indicators (KPI’s). For this specific research, the most widely used KPI’s 
were chosen, enriched with modern metrics that are associated with BIM based methods and 
analyzed in two ways. Initially, a formula is proposed for each KPI, in order to quantify the 
evaluation of the project outcome. However, as no case was used in the research, these formulas 
are not implemented. In parallel to that, a Table with respect to each KPI was developed, showing 
the actual benefits of BIM-based over traditional methods. These resulted from a combination of 
content analysis of the literature and through the answers of the Interviewees. Since some of the 
beneficial parameters introduced through BIM  were already analyzed in the “Process” parts (A1, 
B1, B2), in this discussion part (C1), a thorough research was conducted to come up with the BIM 
parameters that influence more than one KPI’s, that way increasing their significance. These are 
presented below: 
 
 Visualization or visual representation is mentioned as a benefit for decrease project time, 
increased project quality and higher labor safety in the construction site. As far as project time 
is concerned, visualization of the building, combined with the required information included in 
the objects results in efficient information exchange, but also in better understanding of all 
building aspects regardless specialization of stakeholders. Following that, it is easier for 
subcontractors and client to get involved in the project process. Moreover, active involvement 
of all stakeholders generates better quality results due to the easier and more adequate 
alignment of initial expectations with the design and construction. Last but not least, safety is 
also positively influenced through the visualization of the as-built model, seeing that labor 
training on site may take place based on the as-built model. 
 
 “What if search” is a term translating the main advantage of BIM over traditional methods based 
on CHR. This specific parameter affects three KPI’s in total. The “What if search” is described as 
the ability to examine more than one options concurrently and hence evaluate their impact. In 
other words, this term may be described as the parametric design in object-based modelling. 
The difference is that it implies on more project aspects than just design issues. The “What if 
search” through BIM-based methods is conducted easier, faster and has a wider range of 
solutions, as various libraries and objects can be applied  to one model. That way, project time 
is decreased. Moreover, project schedule is positively affected, because in the case of a change, 
an easy overview of the consequences with respect to time may be ensured. Lastly, all changes 
can be easier and more effectively implemented to all designs without the need to redesign, 
leading to critical time and cost savings. An example with reference to BIM based “What if 
search” was mentioned by CHR: 
 
“Let's say that a redesign of the building's plan view is needed, in that a new additional edge 
for the construction of an elevator is proposed. In order to achieve this, additional 
reinforcement is needed. If the architect proposes this, the structural engineer will get noticed 
about the change and add the required reinforcement, the MEP engineer will perhaps redirect 
the pipes, but also the subcontractor will get noticed about the necessity of providing the site 
with the additional reinforcement on time. In the traditional construction practice case, the 
possibility of ignoring the order of the additional reinforcement is high. Following that, if the 
reinforcement is not on site on the time, the slab is not able to be placed on top of it, leading 
to remarkable delays.” 
==PAGE:101==
93 
 
 Simulations represent the third BIM benefit to be highlighted. The ability to simulate the model 
easy and fast, considering various parameters that influence the design process, is mentioned 
because it directly affects energy and sustainability issues, which are not only vital for the 
development of the construction industry, but they are also included in the Building Regulations 
and Requirements. Through BIM-based simulations, which take advantage of the information 
that is already included in the model elements, an easier control and test of energy consumption 
may be applied during the design phase, which in turn results in buildings with better indoor air 
quality. Considering that this process takes place early in the design phase, an important amount 
of money is saved, since omissions or mistakes may be predicted on time. 
 
 Digital Building ID is the process of mapping all building elements in the model, so that 
everything that exists in the inner or outer space of the building is classified. The digital building 
ID is fulfilled through the classification process. Apart from providing operational safety to 
residents of the building, since they are familiar with the cable and other MEP devices 
placement through the BIM Model, digital ID plays an outstanding role with reference to 
operation and maintenance of the building, which will be analyzed in the next Chapter. Since 
operation and maintenance costs account for the larger amount of building costs, as compared 
to design and construction costs, this benefit is considered as vital. 
In parallel to these main benefits, lots of other BIM parameters were mentioned. Most of them are 
known and already explained in this research, like class detection and linked files, 4D and 5D 
building design and others.   
Opposed to the benefits of BIM methods with respect to the project outcome, costs of 
implementing these benefits are usually considered as the main barriers against the introduction 
or evolution of the methods. KIM is familiar with this potential drawback and gave greater value to 
the parameter of development of collaborative processes with external parties. As he stated: 
“The cost of this parameter is hidden into the projects, so if you have a building project, this 
cost is included into the project cost, for which the customer is paying. It is much higher than 
the BIM software cost and the internal collaborative procedures, because companies now 
how much they spent for that based on a project, so they can develop a standard way and 
plan exactly the cost for the next projects. However, collaborative processes with external 
parties is a huge cost per project. Maybe 5-10% of the cost of the project.”

5.4 Project Lifecycle (D) 
The Project Lifecycle field, which evaluates the project after the initiation of its operation phase is 
further classified into the Facilities Management main category. 
 
5.4.1 Facilities Management (D1) 
Facilities management is an extended and crucial field that covers the whole project lifecycle from 
the delivery until the end of the lifetime of the building or the need for renovation. As such, it also 
accounts for the greatest financial burden compared to the rest of the project phases. Taking these 
two parameters into consideration, the facilities management field requires extensive analysis, 
because any streamlining of the process will result in higher percentage of savings. Nevertheless, 
since the field demands a thorough research by itself and the Interviewees were not professionally 
directed towards this area, no extended analysis is provided. As the assessment tool focusses on 
evoking further research, the facilities management category is proposed to be among the main 
fields of further investigation. 
The advantage of actual BIM-based practices over traditional non-BIM is the classification of 
elements and the corresponding development of the digital building ID that can be used throughout 
the operation and maintenance phase. Classification is the mapping of the model elements to 
specific categories and the further differentiation of elements of the same category based on floor, 
number, material and others. Based on this, classified elements are easily accessible be facilities 
management organizations and can be efficiently maintained and inspected. The importance of the 
classification parameter was emphasized by KIM as well: 
“The classification parameter is the most important. You cannot use any operation and 
maintenance facility without classifying.” 
However, various barriers exist in the effort towards taking advantage of BIM-based methods to 
facilitate the operation and maintenance procedure. Initially, the VDC Model that is handed-in by 
the delivery of the building project is not re-used by the facilities management organizations. On 
the contrary, it is used for data extraction of the essential information, which is later transferred 
into the FM Model. Of course, this requires a great extent of additional time and cost. On top of 
that, based on KIM’s experience, “all operation and maintenance organizations are working ad-hoc 
with a poor planning.” Following this statement, facilities management companies are not yet able 
to support the process of re-using the design and construction BIM Model.  
Last but not least, the importance of stating clear operation and maintenance requirements during 
the planning phase of the project is pinpointed by KIM as well: 
“In the planning phase, when I make an agreement, I set up a lot of requirements about 
various data that have to be collected and inserted into the FM system.” 
However, the change of the BIM Model throughout the different phases of the project results in a 
great information loss until the initiation of the building facilities management, leading to the 
inability of BIM Model re-use.

6. RECOMMENDATIONS (Proposal of framework for action) 
Various benefits of BIM-based methods were pointed out and proven through the comparison 
against traditional non-BIM processes in the analysis of the results. This comparison was based on 
a conceptual form of tool that evaluates project processes and performance in alignment with the 
project timeline. Moreover, potential benefits of state-of-the-art “com-BIM” methods were 
highlighted through the comparison against actual BIM-based methods applied in the Danish 
construction industry. However, these potential benefits are not yet fully acknowledged due to 
existing barriers. The main barriers against the progress towards fully integrated methods were 
analyzed and examined in the Discussion parts of each main field of the assessment tool. In the 
recommendation part, an effort is taken to propose a framework for action, constituted of 
guidelines that combine various aspects of the main problems into one initiative concurrent to the 
project timeline. 
Two main prerequisites of “com-BIM” integrated processes are considered as difficult to be 
accomplished by the existing Danish construction structures: 
 Early involvement of all main stakeholders 
Since the main involved parties (client, architectural team, MEPF team, primary contractor and 
facilities management consultant) are characterized by distinctive knowledge and specialization 
fields, active involvement of each party early in the project process is not possible. Following 
that, a way has to be proposed so that all stakeholders are able to collaborate early in the project 
and on specific time spots. 
 
 Re-use of the BIM Model throughout the project: 
As it was highlighted during the interviews with the professionals of the BIM-based construction 
industry, BIM Models change between phases, since every stakeholder is focusing towards 
readjusting and adapting the BIM Model to his needs. However, this results in a great 
information loss (KIM) with respect to initial requirements and ideas. This information loss takes 
place mostly between a phase change, where the Danish construction industry is still to an 
extent fragmented and linear. The main phase changes refer to the transition from planning to 
design phase, where the ideas of the client are translated into a BIM Model, design to 
construction phase, where the BIM Model is transformed into a VDC Model and construction to 
operation-maintenance, where the VDC Model is re-adjusted to a FM (Facilities Management) 
Model. Following that, a way to re-use the BIM Model without changing it has to be proposed. 
 
Taking this into consideration, Figure 47 illustrates an abstract form of the activities of the main 
stakeholders with reference to the project timeline. In addition to that, two initiatives are 
proposed, which are also based on recommendation from the professionals of the Danish 
construction industry and mainly KIM. At first, the design team (architectural, structural and MEPF 
team) has to extend its activities backwards (red color) and at the same time narrow the differences 
between initiation and end of each sub-team phase. This step should be combined with a 
concurrent extension of the client’s activity, so that the aforementioned parties can conceptualize 
the design concurrently and early in the project. Secondly, it is essential for the client, the primary 
contractor and the FM consultant to somehow set their requirements and goals (green color) and 
collaborate with the other main stakeholders during the requirement and goal statement process.

Based on the aforementioned initiatives, Figure 48 was developed to illustrate the 
recommendation framework. Initially, it is observed that the activities of the design team are 
proposed to be almost concurrent. This can be achieved, since the knowledge field of these sub-
teams covers the various aspects of the design of a building project. A small time difference with 
respect to involvement is intentionally left so that each sub-team develops its conceptual design.  
The activities of the design team are directly related to the extension of the client’s activity, in order 
to create the background for active collaboration between these stakeholders during the planning 
phase, when the client’s requirements and ideas are translated into building design. Moreover, it 
can be witnessed that small activity fields of the primary contractor and FM consultant are 
incorporated in the planning phase, leading to the creation of an area of collaboration (red hatched 
area). Within this area, the client, primary contractor and FM consultant have to state their 
requirements and as a team define the clear and detailed project goals. The process of stating the 
requirements during the planning phase is already partially fulfilled in the existing construction 
practice. However, the non-alignment of requirements with the BIM Model results in great 
information loss, as explained earlier in this Chapter. That is why the recommendation framework 
proposes the mapping of the requirements and goals of the stakeholders in the BIM Model. More 
specifically, requirements may be included in the consistency control software (Solibri, Navisworks 
and others) the same way as Building Regulations are already included. This step may on the one 
hand limit the freedom of each involved party to re-adjust the BIM Model and on the other hand 
assist stakeholders of different knowledge fields in keeping track of the project development.  
In parallel to that, since all stakeholders cannot be involved and follow all the project processes, 
clear milestones (green dashed lines) have to be set and meetings held, so that all involved parties 
monitor the project development, the alignment with their requirements and at the same time 
update the existing requirements based on the available project information. Additionally to that, 
smaller milestones are required, in order to coordinate and check the consistency of the design. 
These milestones may be held internally in the design team.

The recommendation framework affects directly other important problems highlighted during the 
analysis of the results that set barriers to the further implementation and development of “com-
BIM” processes: 
 Deficiency of ICT Specifications: 
ICT Specifications are not feasible in the Danish construction industry, based on the 
Interviewees, since they do not bind the project process with the development of the BIM 
Model and at the same time do not consider the construction phase. Nevertheless, through the 
conceptual framework that is proposed, a basic project process (set of requirements) is bound 
with and premises the development of the BIM Model and the successful fulfillment of the 
milestones. Moreover, the construction phase is incorporated in the ICT Specifications through 
the mapping of requirements and the update conducted during the milestones.  
 
 Partially shared risk allocation: 
The risk allocation is expressed mainly through two parameters (contracts not considered), 
namely early involvement of stakeholders and existence of compensation methods for the 
provision of incentives for early collaboration. The first parameter is already covered through 
the proposed framework. Compensation methods consist of financial incentives, which are 
already applied in the Danish construction industry, and liability. Liability issues are further 
divided into trust and competency. Trust is also provided in the Danish construction practice 
through informative BIM Models accessible by all involved parties.  
 
Competency is a variable that is not yet acknowledged in the modern BIM based construction 
industries. Usually, a company is considered as competent based on the experience and 
accomplished projects. However, as the existence of BIM in the modern integrated practices 
becomes more and more compelling, new competency benchmarks with respect to BIM 
capabilities have to be introduced. Based on ADH, MTH uses a BIM Score metric that evaluates the 
integrity of the BIM Model they receive from the client built upon some requirements they have 
==PAGE:106==
98 
 
set. Nevertheless, the evaluation of the BIM Model is only one step. Through this recommendation 
part, the development of BIM Capability and Maturity indexes is proposed, which will enable 
construction industry stakeholders to evaluate their BIM Capabilities against benchmarks, but also 
raise the liability of the involved parties, which will be ensured regarding BIM-competent 
stakeholders.  
The proposal with respect to BIM Capability and Maturity indexes refers to the implementation of 
the “Point of Adoption” model, developed by (Succar & Kassem 2015). The “Point of Adoption” 
conceptual model, which is extensively analyzed in Chapter 2.2.2.4, assembles Capability (minimum 
ability) with Maturity (performance milestones within each capability stage) into one framework 
that can be easily used by all stakeholders. An essential prerequisite is the development of clear 
and commonly used criteria, which are easily comprehended by the construction industry, so that 
benchmarks can be created.

7. CONCLUSION 
The final Chapter of the thesis summarizes the principal conclusions and offers suggestions for 
further research.  
(NIMBS Committe 2007) states: “While only a very small portion of facility life-cycle costs occur 
during design and construction, these are the phase where our decisions have the greatest impact.” 
This statement represents the optimal way of expressing the reflection that lies behind the main 
focus of the present thesis. Various surveys have been already conducted that come up with 
numbers proving the efficiency of BIM processes. Nevertheless, the development of fully 
integrated “Com-BIM” concepts is still pending, leading to the conclusion that the deliberate study 
of processes, rather than outcomes, is compelling. 
The process of the development of the conceptual assessment tool to assist the aforementioned 
aim through the comparison between the triptych traditional non-BIM, fully integrated and modern 
BIM-based (actual practice) is ongoing. The word “conceptual” is intentionally adopted to pinpoint 
the need for further development of the tool, mainly with respect to Pre-Planning and Facilities 
Management processes. In any case, the alignment of the tool with the project timeline reveals the 
main scope of the thesis.  
Through the Interviews with four industry professionals, one of them representing the Greek 
traditional document-based construction practice and the other three the modern Danish BIM 
based practice, various categories were assessed and important results highlighted. The already 
widely known BIM benefits were certified through the comparison between BIM and non-BIM 
based processes in alignment with the categories of the assessment tool. Benefits were pinpointed 
both through the comparison of processes based on a specified context, but also through the 
parameters affecting Key Performance Indicators, as captured either by the Interviewees, or the 
literature research.  
Most importantly, the second comparison between actual Danish and fully integrated construction 
practices, as perceived throughout the literature, revealed important deficiencies. Among them are 
defective ICT Agreements, absence of BIM Competency benchmarks to evaluate the maturity and 
capability of companies and develop liability among project stakeholders, partially shared risk 
allocation and absence of alignment between project processes and BIM Model with respect to 
requirements and information loss. These parameters hinder the streamline of the project 
processes and generate additional problems regarding early collaboration of stakeholders, which 
represents the main prerequisite for “Com-BIM” methods. 
Taking the main drawbacks into consideration, a framework for solution is proposed based on the 
analysis of the results and the recommendations of the Interviewees. The advantage of this 
framework is that it is composed of a general approach that incorporates all drawbacks mentioned 
and points out the holistic approach of the tool. More specifically, the mapping of requirements of 
the main stakeholders in the BIM Model through the use of a consistency check software is 
proposed. Along with that, the establishment of clear milestones to check for compliance and 
update the requirements is considered as mandatory. Through these initiatives, stakeholders will 
be able to partially get involved in the early project phases, interact, collaborate, set their 
requirements and at the same time supervise them through the milestone phases.  
Lastly, the main aim of the research, namely the triggering of interest regarding evaluation of BIM 
based methods through the careful study of the intangible benefits and drawbacks that lie within 
the analysis of project process is considered successful. Further research is needed to prove the 
==PAGE:108==
100 
 
advantages of this approach, since stakeholders of different areas of expertise (architect, client, 
and MEPF engineer) have to be interviewed. Moreover, methods to quantify the processes have to 
be proposed, in order to add more limitations to the extent of the replies.

APPENDIX B (Transcription of recorded Interviews) 
Traditional Greek construction practice (Christos Anagnostopoulos, CHR) 
C: Could you please evaluate the framework that I just explained to you? 
CHR: I think that it clearly surrounds the project phases. In my opinion, the delivery methods have 
something happening before, since it is not possible to talk about contracts and risk, if you have 
not gone through the necessary predesign phase. In the traditional construction practice, the client 
that decides to build something goes through a concept and feasibility study, prior to assessing the 
contracts and risk. This initial predesign phase defines the building use and the budget. 
C: Do you think consultants are needed in this phase? 
CHR: Yes of course, it would be very important to have experienced people making the initial 
feasibility study based on the area's conditions, since much money (10%) can be saved through a 
well-planned project.  
C: How many times during the project process do stakeholders make cost estimations? 
CHR: Usually three. There is an initial cost estimation (Pre-Planning phase) based on the experience 
of professionals. This means that according to the area of intervention, there are some fixed 
prices/m2 that help you estimate based on the total m2 of the building. Moreover, these prices 
vary according to the prospected quality of construction. These are used as guidelines. The same 
occurs with time schedule, based also on the financial capability of the primary contractor. These 
are numbers that may exist in the statistics, but are usually fixed through the experience of 
construction specialists. 
The second cost estimation is during the design phase, when the estimator takes into consideration 
the architectural, structural and MEP designs. This cost estimation is important since it is based on 
final drawings of the building. Moreover, in this phase, many details have been given regarding 
floor height, openings, structural system etc.  
The third cost estimation occurs after the construction details of the (Detailed Design Phase) 
project are finished. This cost estimation is the most detailed one, since every single element of the 
building is considered. Usually, in private projects, the second and third cost estimations are 
connected into one.  
C: Based on your experience, which process do you follow for the second cost estimation? 
(24:09) CHR: As an example, for the structural engineering part, based on 2D drawings, you 
calculate the volume of the structural parts and additional reinforcement. You know the weight of 
the material and the price per m2 and then you conduct the respective calculations.  
The same way, you calculate the architectural elements. However, there is a difference between 
private and public projects. In public projects, since the architectural elements are targeting 
towards showcasing the aesthetics of the building, a much more detailed estimation is needed. In 
private projects, the cost estimation has also a level of detail, but is fluctuative because of the 
possibility of the client changing opinion regarding the architectural proposal.  
As a simple example, he may change the floor material from wood to marble.  
==PAGE:121==
113 
 
 
(25:41) C: How precise is this estimation compared to the final cost of the project based on your 
experience? 
(25:44) CHR: Assuming that the planning and design decisions will not change dramatically, it is 
critical to have an experienced engineer, who knows also the local characteristics of the area where 
the construction project will be placed.  
As an example: If I decide to build a house in a place that there is no existing road, I have to consider 
the road design and associated costs as well. This means that I cannot just estimate based on 
volumes and weights, but also consider such parameters. Moreover, this may affect the Project 
Time. 
As an example: I may decide to build something in a place without electricity installations, which 
means that I have to add that in my construction planning phase. In parallel to that, I may decide 
to build in an island, where the unit prices are different that in the mainland.  
Following that, we usually do not encounter problems with regards to quantities. On the other 
hand, it usually the case to make mistakes regarding unit prices and this is mostly affected by the 
experience concerning local conditions.  
(27:39) C: I would like to explain you in short the cost estimation process followed in the case of 
BIM-based projects. Taking MTH company as the case, they use a software called Itwo that makes 
the quantity takeoff from an updated BIM Model. Following that, elements taken from the BIM 
Model are assigned to prices from a digital library without the need to intervene in the quantities, 
dimensions, materials, etc. How do you think this process creates better conditions with regard to 
cost estimation? 
(29:20) CHR: When something is completed automatically, usually the possibility of a mistake is 
small. When something is conducted by an individual (custom), then the possibility of a mistake is 
increased dramatically. My personal opinion is that the cost estimation process through the use of 
BIM-based methods is by far more advantageous, because of the opportunity to change 
an  element (for example type of wall) much easier. In our case, if I decide to change the type of a 
wall, I have to search the corresponding Excel file, change the thickness and the details of the new 
materials, go back to the drawings and check other parameters that might get influenced 
(architectural space, aesthetics etc.), so you easily understand how complicated this process is.  
I believe that the opportunity to conduct a "What if" search in a matter of minutes is the most 
important feature when comparing the two methods.  
Moreover, I have to state that according to my opinion, it would be important in the case of BIM 
based methods to be able to provide ways of transferring human experience into the software as 
a general supervising method.  
As an example, Beagle 2 was a British landing spacecraft sent to Mars, which lost its route and was 
destroyed. It turned out that the reason was that engineers used a software that did not change 
the units from Imperial to SI units in all subparts, leading to a wrong orbit.  
(32:42) C: The project process you described seems to be a Design-Bid-Build or Design-Build 
process. Is it true that the project is either assigned or won based on cost? 
==PAGE:122==
114 
 
(33:08) CHR: In public projects, in the case of Design-Bid-Build, the main contractor (construction 
company) is chosen based on cost. In the case of Design-Build contracts, where one contractor is 
responsible both for the design and construction of the building, then the contract is signed up 
based on financial and technical parameters. The reason for this is that the quality of the design 
has to be assessed as well. Both financial and technical parameters are considered and a weighted 
average is calculated.  
In private projects, two factors affect the choice of the contractor. The cost (usually the most 
important one) and the reputation of the contractor. There are construction companies which are 
famous for their construction quality, which is usually combined with increased construction cost. 
This means that it is up to the client to decide based on the dipole cost - quality.  
(35:52) C: However increased construction cost may result in decreased maintenance and 
inspection costs, right? 
(36:01) CHR: Yes indeed. Additionally, it is a matter of functionality as well. 
As an example, when digging a hole to let a pipe go through, in the case of expensive construction, 
after digging the hole, gravel will be placed and then the pipe will be encapsulated. This means that 
the pipe will 99% not fail. In the case of the cheap construction though, after digging the hole, the 
pipe will just be placed on top, there is 20% possibility of a failure.  
(36:36) C: I would like to talk about financial incentives. Does this constitute a process used in 
Greece? 
(36:43) CHR: Usually, there is no specific process related of providing financial incentives in case of 
meeting cost, time and/or quality requirements. The main logic is that someone is hired in order to 
fulfill his scope. Moreover, the payment is conducted after finishing the job.  
Financial incentives may be given in cases of big private projects. This is translated as a percentage 
of bonus per day of finishing the project earlier.  
(37:50) C: What about the risk allocation? 
(37:53) AC: In public projects, the existence of a mistake or delay or lower quality is among the 
main responsibilities of the construction company-contractor. The primary contractor is fully 
responsible for everything that goes wrong. There is no possibility of sharing the risk between 
stakeholders.  
In case of a mistake, everyone is trying to prove that the law and the contract were followed 
precisely and in case of disagreements, things are getting solved through the court. 
(42:07) C: I guess that if this takes place during the construction phase, the consequences are even 
more important with regard to time and cost of the building? 
(42:15) CHR: of course, yes. There are many times that this has happened.  
(42:24) C: How do you think the modern BIM based method has an advantage over the traditional 
in relation to what we just talked about? 
(42:26) CHR: I have the impression that the advantage of this method is the possibility to plan 
through the various simulations that can be conducted the possible mistakes to occur. This means 
==PAGE:123==

that almost every mistake or omission or clash between different disciplines may be easily spotted 
prior to the initiation of the project, that way saving money and time.  
Example: Let's say that the client decides after the design phase of the project to add an elevator 
in the building, in the place where a staircase was designed before. This requires the cutting of the 
slab so that the elevator can be placed. However, such a move may lower the slab capacity and 
create failures on the edges that attach to the elevator shaft. Of course in such a case, the BIM
