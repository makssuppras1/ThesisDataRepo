Abstract
In this work, a polarimetric SAR interferometry (PolInSAR) processing facility is implemented. Further-
more, an existing tomographic SAR (TomoSAR) facility is improved through the implementation of a 3D
TDBP SAR focuser on GPU, leading to improved quality of subsequent tomographic cubes. Two sites
at the Antarctic continent are investigated. PolInSAR data was acquired at a site at Dome C in the dry
snow zone in the central Antarctic, while both tomographic and PolInSAR data was captured at a site at
Shackleton ice shelf at the eastern side of the continent situated in the percolation zone. Data was acquired
in January and February 2024 with the POLarimetric Airborne Radar Ice Sounder (POLARIS) instrument
operated by DTU. At the site at Shackleton ice shelf, coherence magnitudes was found to be best described
by a Two-layer coherence model including an infinite uniform volume, a surface scattering contribution,
and scattering from a subsurface layer. PolInSAR model inversion suggests that the subsurface layer is
located between -8 m and -11 m depth. Through TomoSAR analysis, a distinct four-layer subsurface
scattering structure is identified, which is shown to persist as a continuous feature throughout the scene
with a very dominant subsurface scattering layer located at approximately -9 m. The same layer structure
is observable in ice sounder images of the centre line. This directly couples geophysical parameters ex-
tracted through PolInSAR model inversion to features observed in TomoSAR and ice sounder. For the
scene at Dome C, PolInSAR analysis is carried out employing the simpler Uniform Volume under Surface
(UVuS) model, assuming an infinite uniform volume below a surface scattering layer. Model inversion
based solely on coherence magnitudes as well as model inversion based on complex coherences is carried
out. It is shown that the coherence magnitudes for all scattering mechanisms are reasonably well described
by the UVuS model, when employing the pure coherence magnitude inversion. Employing the complex
inversion scheme, it is found that some scattering mechanisms are well-described in the complex plane,
with retrieved model parameters comparable to those found through pure magnitude inversion, while oth-
ers are not. Regardless of the method used, an unexpected small one-way penetration depth was retrieved.
Furthermore, insignificant surface scattering was indicated by the PolInSAR analysis. This is in direct
contradiction to polarimectric analysis, which indicates very dominant surface scattering. Furthermore,
polarimetric analysis suggest very complex scattering mechanics at this site, possibly introduced by snow
dunes on the surface. However, further analysis will be needed, if full understanding of the observations
at the Dome C site is to be established.

Introduction
In recent decades, in step with the growing climate crisis, a growing interest has been shown in monitoring
the Earth’s climate systems. Particularly the Earth’s polar regions have received much attention due to
their status as key-markers of climate change. In a climate scenario with a rising global mean tempera-
ture, the polar regions may suffer larger relative temperature increases than other parts of the planet due
to complex climatological feedback mechanisms in these regions. Combined with the fact that immense
quantities of freshwater are bound in the polar ice sheets, the increase in melt caused by these warming
events will lead to global mean sea level rise the order of several meters [2]. Thus, a clear understanding of
large-scale ice sheet evolution under climate change is crucial in order to predict and potentially mitigate
the consequences of the climate crisis in the polar regions.
Currently, ice sheet mass balance on a continental scale is monitored from space though several meth-
ods including gravimetry, altimetry, and SAR interferometry [3]. However, these techniques are all asso-
ciated with changes in the ice sheet surface. Traditional methods for mapping subsurface structures are
primarily restricted to radar sounding or ice core extraction. Common for these methods are that they both
provide point-like measurements, lacking the spacial coverage needed for large-scale monitoring of ice
sheet subsurface dynamics.
In resent years, advanced SAR techniques, most notably polarimetric SAR interferometry (PolInSAR)
and SAR tomography (TomoSAR), haves received increasing attention due to their potential for provid-
ing exactly large-scale information about subsurface structures of glaciers and ice sheets. In April 2025,
ESA’s most recent Earth Explorer mission, BIOMASS, was launched. BIOMASS is a space-bourne P-
band SAR, the main objective of which is to map the earth’s forested regions through exactly PolInSAR
and TomoSAR. However, the BIOMASS mission holds the secondary objective of mapping the Earth’s
ice bodies, thus facilitating the application of PolInSAR and TomoSAR to ice bodies on a larger scale in
the near future, but also illustrating the need to understand the mapping of glaciological conditions into
SAR observables.
In this report, PolInSAR and TomoSAR techniques will be applied to SAR data acquired by air-bourne
P-band SAR over two glaciologically different sites at the Antarctic continent in an attempt to extract
information about subsurface structures. The data was gathered in January and February 2024 during the
Antarctic summer, and the main goal of the campaign was to assess the feasibility of P-band PolInSAR and
TomoSAR techniques in the context of the BIOMASS mission, but also test the feasibility of the Antarctic
continent as an area of calibration for the BIOMASS satellite.
The goal of the work is first of all to implement the required PolInSAR and TomoSAR processing facil-
ities necessary for processing the acquired SAR images. In terms of PolInSAR processing, the facility
will be implemented from scratch. In terms of TomoSAR, an already existing processor will be improved.
Furthermore, polarimetric analysis will be carried out in order to access the expected dominant scattering
mechanisms of the two sites. Results will then be presented and and compared in order access the cohe-
1
==PAGE:8==
siveness of the two methods, and a glaciological interpretation will be attempted.
This report is structured into the following chapters:
1. Chapter 2, Theory contains a theoretical introduction to the expected glaciological conditions of
the sites investigated. Second the chapter provides a theoretical foundation for all SAR techniques
applied in this work; SAR polarimetry (PolSAR), polarimetric SAR interferometry (PolInSAR), and
SAR tomography (TomoSAR).
2. Chapter 3, Data contains an introduction to the POLARIS P-band SAR and the data processing
levels.
3. Chapter 4, Algorithm details the implemented PolInSAR and TomoSAR processing chains through
in-depth descriptions and flow charts.
4. Chapter 5, Results, discussion, and conclusion contains a presentation of the generated results from
polarimetry, PolInSAR, and TomoSAR. The chapter is structured, so a polarimetric analysis of both
sites are first provided. After this, results from the two sites will be presented separately, and a sub-
conclusion will be drawn to each site. Finally, a conclusion will be given, summarising everything
presented earlier in the chapter.

2.1
Glaciological conditions of the scenes
In this section, a more detailed introduction to the two scenes investigated in work is provided. Further-
more, an assessment of the glaciological conditions expected at the two sites will be given. On fig. 2.1
maps of the two scenes and their location on the Antarctic continent are shown together with outlines of
the nominal SAR footprints. The first scene is located at the Shackleton ice shelft, which is a floating mass
of ice, still connected to the continent in one end. Shackleton ice shelf is a part of the Eastern Antarctic
ice sheet and extents over the ocean for tens of thousand of square kilometres with a thickness of approxi-
mately 200 m. It’s surface is located at approximately 20 m to 40 m above sea-level. [4].
The second scene is located at Dome C also called dôme Concordia. Dome C is located at the Antarctic
Plateau at an altitude of 3000 m. Dome C is also home to the Concordia research station.

What makes the two scenes interesting is their location in different ice zones. A glacier or an ice sheet
can be split into four different zones or facies with different glaciological characteristics depending on
the elevation [5]. The four ice zones are illustrated on fig. 2.2. The first three facies are located in the
accumulation zone, where the net mass balance is positive, while the last zone is located in the ablation
zone, where the glacier or ice sheet is subject to a net mass loss. The site at Shackleton ice shelf is located
in the so-called percolation zone, while the site at Dome C is located in the dry snow zone. The four
canonical ice facies from highest to lowest elevation are as follows:
1. Dry snow zone.
In the dry snow zone, the temperature is below the freezing point throughout the year, and thus no
surface melt occurs. The dry snow zone is characterised a gradually densifying firnpack due to com-
paction of accumulated snow. The firn volume is expected to be locally uniform and characterised
by low radar backscatter. The firnpack in the dry snow zone is usually less dense than the firnpack
in the other ice zone.
2. Percolation zone.
The percolation zone is characterised regular summer melt. Melt water then percolates down into
4
==PAGE:11==
the firnpack, where it refreezes, forming several different structures known as ice inclusions. These
structure include layers, ice lenses, and ice pipes. These structures can be comparable to radar
wavelength, strongly increasing the radar backscatter.
3. Wet snow zone.
The wet snow zone is generally subject to the same processes as the percolation zone, however, in
the wet snow zone, more extensive melt occurs. This results in the entire winter snow-pack being
wet by the end of the summer.
4. Ablation zone.
In the ablation zone, the ice sheet is subject to a net mass loss. During the summer, the entire winter
snow-pack is melted away, and the underlaying glacial ice is laid bare.
Figure 2.2: The different ice zones and their characteristics [5].
At radar wave lengths, penetration into the ice is expected, allowing for the mapping of subsurface features.
The penetration depth into the ice or firn-pack is mainly dependent on two parameters; the frequency of
the radar and permittivity of the ice or firn, which is generally associated with the liquid water content of
the ice or firn. A larger penetration depth will be expected for lower frequencies and dryer ice/firn. All
data, analysed in this work, has been acquired at the low frequency P-band. For the site at Dome C, the
firn-pack will be dry even during the Antarctic summer. Therefore, a large penetration depth would be
expected at this site. At Shackleton, some surface melt might be present, however, this is not expected to
hinder the analysis significantly.
The surface of Antarctica is dominated by longitudinal snow dunes with lengths ranging from 100 m
to 1 km [6]. The dunes are primarily cause by elongation, meaning that the dunes grow by depositing
particles at their tips, which are transported along their flanks. Generally, the longitudinal snow dunes
run in parallel with the dominant wind direction as shown on fig. 2.3. It is seen that the predominant
5
==PAGE:12==
orientation of these dunes runs approximately in parallel with the along-track direction for the site at Dome
C. These highly oriented surface features will undoubtedly have an impact on the scattering dynamics of
the scene. Polarimetric analysis of SAR images under the presence of snow dunes, albeit Sastrugi dunes
and not longitudinal waves, was carried out in [7] for a scene in Svalbard. Here, a model of snow dune
scattering mechanics was necessary in order to adequately describe the total scattering dynamics of the
scene. Therefore, the presence of longitudinal dunes is expected to have a noticeable influence on the
scattering dynamics of the scene.

2.2.2
SCH-coordinate system
While the slant-range azimuth coordinate system has it’s merits, namely because it is native coordinate
system of the radar, a different coordinate system has been chosen to represent the SAR images in this
7
==PAGE:14==
work, namely the Spherical Cross-track Height or SCH coordinate system. The SCH coordinate system
is a sensor-aligned, curve-linear spherical coordinate system, which is closer related to the physical di-
mensions of the scene than the slant-range/azimuth coordinate system. The SCH coordinate system was
first introduced in [9]. The 𝑆-coordinate represents sensor along-track, which is the position along the
sphere that best approximates the ellipsoid given a reference position, (𝜆0, 𝜙0), and a reference heading,
𝜂0, where 𝜆denotes latitude, 𝜙denotes longitude, and 𝜂0 denotes heading with respect to north on the
WGS-84 reference ellipsoid. The along-track axis is thus related to the azimuth axis in the azimuth/slant-
range coordinate system. The last 𝐻-coordinate then represents height above the reference sphere. Finally,
the 𝐶or cross-track coordinate represents the distance on the reference sphere in the direction perpendic-
ular to both 𝑆and 𝐻, and the cross-track coordinate is thus related to the ground-range. Throughout this
work, the terms cross-track and range and azimuth and along-track will be used somewhat interchangeably,
as they are directly related. However, it will be apparent in which coordinate system, results are formally

2.3
Tomographic SAR imaging
The content of this section is based on [11], [12], and [13] unless explicitly stated.
Tomographic SAR imaging or more conveniently TomoSAR is a multi-baseline SAR imaging tech-
nique, where information from multiple radar acquisitions over an area is combined in order to not only
resolve the imaged scatterers in the two-dimensional along-track/cross-track plane, but also in the eleva-
tion or cross-range direction. The ability to resolve targets in the cross-range is achieved by effectively
forming a second synthetic aperture in the cross-range direction similar to the azimuth or along-tack syn-
thetic aperture formed in traditional SAR focussing. This allows for full three-dimensional imaging of the
scene, and in the case of media, which are penetrable by microwaves e.g. ice bodies and forests, the inter-
nal scatterers can be resolved. On fig. 2.6 the conventional 2D SAR imaging scenario and 3D TomoSAR
scenario are sketched. In a 2D imaging scenario, all scatterers in the range resolution cell are integrated
over the cross-range direction and focused onto a single pixel. In the TomoSAR imaging scenario, the
range resolution cell can be resolved into smaller parts called voxels with a cross-range resolution of Δ𝑣.

Three-dimensional TomoSAR image, or tomographic cubes as they will be referred to from here on,
can be formed by employing two different methods. One method involves focusing and co-registering

tomographic cubes by subsequent spectral estimation. Another method consists of directly focussing each
range compressed image onto a 3D reconstruction grid. This method is more computationally heavy, how-
ever, it enables correcting for wave-propagation effects, such as refraction effects discussed in section 2.4,
directly in the three-dimensional focusing. This can in theory remove mislocation and defocussing effects,
improving the quality of the tomographic cubes. Furthermore, a target located at height 𝑧0 will be better

2.3.2
Geometric differences between the 2D and 3D method
Employing the two different methods, the mapping of imaged targets into the cross-track/height plane is
different depending on the method. This is illustrated on fig. 2.8. First, it is important to note that for the
2D method, the height axis is always defined with respect to the reference surface. Second thing to note
is that the resolved height axis is the projection of the slanted cross-range axis onto the vertical axis. This
means that a target physically located at (𝑌𝑡, 𝑍𝑡) will be located at (𝑌𝑟𝑒𝑓, (𝑍𝑡−𝑍𝑟𝑒𝑓)) in the subsequently
formed tomographic cube. The mislocation in height can easily be corrected, as the height of the reference
surface is know, however errors in the reference surface height, most commonly a DEM, will lead to errors
in the absolute height of the imaged target. If the 3D method is employed, targets will be correctly located
in the height plane, as the radar images will be focused directly in the 3D space. Also, the physical slanted
cross-range geometry will be preserved in the tomographic cubes, ensuring targets will correctly located
in the cross-track dimension, albeit the point-target response slanted in the cross-track/height plane.

2.3.4
Phase calibration
Apart from what has already been introduced, another processing step is needed, when dealing with SAR
tomography, namely that of phase calibration. Phase calibration is a crucial step in the TomoSAR pro-
cessing chain, especially when larger errors are expected in the navigation data. When the SAR images
16
==PAGE:23==
are terrain flattened, or in case of the 3D approach focussed onto the reference grid, small errors in the
navigation data will lead to phase errors called phase screens. The presence of phase screens can lead to
effects ranging from reduced vertical resolution to complete vertical defocussing. Even small errors in
sensor position in the order of fractions of a wavelength can significantly degrade the vertical imaging
quality. Formally, the phase screens will be given as
𝛼𝑛(𝑋) = 4𝜋
𝜆(−sin 𝜃𝑛Δ𝑌𝑛(𝑋) + cos 𝜃𝑛Δ𝑍𝑛(𝑋)),

where 𝜃𝑛is the incidence angle to a given surface pixel or voxel for the 𝑛’th track. Δ𝑌𝑛(𝑋) are Δ𝑍𝑛(𝑋)
are the horizontal and vertical position errors as function of along-track position. Phase calibration thus
consist of estimating the sensor position errors and counter-rotating with the calculated phase screens.
A more precise, but computationally complex, method for phase calibration consists of defocussing the
surface-focussed images, using the corrected sensor positions, and re-focussing these images onto the
reconstruction grid [16][17]. However, in this work, the simpler approach is employed to reduce processing
complexity. In this work, the Phase Centre Double Localisation (PCDL) algorithm, first introduced in
[13], is used to estimate the sensor position errors. The algorithm consist of estimating phase screens by
iteratively optimising several figures of merit in the complex domain based on the data itself. The algorithm
is valid as long as three or more unique tracks are available. The implementation of this algorithm was one
of the main task in the work carried out in [10], and the PCDL software developed then is simply reused
in this work. Therefore, the technicalities of the PCDL algorithm is beyond the scope of this report, as
no actual work has been carried out on the subject. One important thing to note, however, is the PCDL
algorithm introduces a roto-translation in the coordinate system describing the scene. This means that
subsequent tomographic cubes will be subject to geometric distortions. In this work, this roto-translation
is neglected, as relative parameters, e.g. scattering depth and possibly the location of subsurface layers, are
of more interest than absolute vertical accuracy. Furthermore, the impact of this roto-translation is usually
small.
2.4
De-focusing and mislocation due to wave propagation in ice
Another challenge when dealing with SAR mapping of ice bodies in general is the fact that radio waves
are subject to refraction effects at the air-ice interface caused by the different dielectric properties of the
two media [16][18][19]. This alters the propagation path from the radar to subsurface targets as compared
to targets stationed in air, which will degrade quality of both 2D and 3D imaging of these targets. First, the
change in propagation path will result mislocation of subsurface targets leading to geometric distortions in
the subsequent images. Second, the refraction of radar waves will lead to defocusing of subsurface target
leading to losses of both intensity and resolution of subsequent images.

2.4.2
Defocusing
Apart from the mislocation of subsurface targets, an arguably more critical consequence of refraction is
the defocusing of SAR images. In the TDBP SAR focusing algorithm employed in this work, the phase
history over the entire synthetic aperture is calculated based on round-trip delays from the known sensor
positions to a given target in order to focus this particular target in the along-track direction. However,
the round-trip delay from sensor to target as a function of along-track position over the synthetic aperture
in a refraction scenario will depart from the round-trip delay assuming a target in air. This means that
the calculated phase history will no longer be correct, leading to defocusing in the azimuth or along-track
direction. This mismatch grows as a function of distance from the broadside point, i.e. the point of closest
approach. This means that for a given target depth, the defocusing effect increases as a function of aperture
size, or more formally as a function of integration angle, 𝜓, being the angle between the extreme points
of the synthetic aperture and the surface scattering point. Since the defocusing effect is more pronounced
for larger synthetic apertures, it means that images focused to smaller azimuth resolutions will be subject
to larger defocusing, thus resulting in a trade off between azimuth resolution and image focusing quality.
This might be especially relevant for P-band systems, as the larger wavelength requires large synthetic
apertures, in the order of kilometres, to attain the desired azimuth resolution. The defocusing effect also
applies to the range direction, however this can be considered negligible. On fig. 2.13 the integration angle
is illustrated as well as the impact of refraction on the round-trip delay as a function of along-track position.

2.5
PolInSAR
Polarimetric SAR Interferometry or PolInSAR is an advanced single-baseline model-based SAR technique,
which combines two different SAR techniques, namely SAR polarimetry (PolSAR) and SAR interferom-
etry (InSAR). PolInSAR is a useful technique when studying volumes. Most notably, it has shown great
potential in extracting forest height and surface topography in forested areas, but can also be useful for
characterising subsurface scattering mechanics of ice bodies, which is the application in this work. The
method consist of estimating geophysical parameters, charactering the imaged volume, through model in-
version of measured complex coherences.
In the following section, PolInSAR will be introduced by first introducing InSAR as a stand-alone tech-
nique in section 2.5.1. This will be done with a focus on complex coherence. Then, PolSAR will be
introduced in section 2.5.2. As a pure PolSAR analysis will also be carried out, relevant theory is intro-
duced in this section as well. Afterwards, these principles will be combined to form the theoretical basis for
PolInSAR analysis in section 2.5.3. Here, complex coherence models and inversion schemes will likewise
be introduced.
2.5.1
InSAR
All content in this subsection is based on [20].

The temporal coherence is caused by changes in the scene over the temporal baseline. This is highly
dependent of the rate of physical change in a given scene. For instance, water bodies will typically exhibit
very low coherence values, as the water surface changes very quickly compared to typical temporal base-
lines, while mountainous areas typically exhibit large temporal coherences, as the scatterers remain stable
even for long temporal baselines. In the context of ice mapping, the typical cause of temporal coherence
loss is due to ice movements. In the ice mapping scenario, images acquired with a temporal baseline of
minutes or hours typically exhibit a large temporal coherence, reasonably assumed as unity, while images
with temporal baselines of days and weeks typically exhibits larger or even total decorrelation.

2.5.3.1
PolInSAR models
In this work, the two different coherence models are employed. The two models and their general charac-
teristics are listed below:
1. Uniform Volume under Surface
The Uniform Volume under Surface or UVuS model is a simple coherence model, which assumes
a single surface scattering layer with an infinite uniform volume beneath. The model has very few
model parameter, and is expected to be useful in areas, where no ice inclusions are expected, such
as the dry snow zone.
2. Multilayer model
The Multilayer model also assumes a surface above an infinite uniform volume. However, scatter-
ing from one or several subsurface scattering layers are included in the model. The model can be
expanded to an arbitrary number of subsurface layer, however, in this work, two layers are assumed,
namely a surface layer and a subsurface layer. The model is expected to be useful in the percolation
zone, where the subsurface scattering mechanics becomes more complex due to ice inclusions.

potential atmospheric decorrelation. The coherence bias will be a single parameter estimated for each

magnitude, to what is observed in the data. The coherence bias will be multiplied onto the modelled
coherence such that

• Level 1c

In this work, mainly level0c data is used, which is then azimuth focussed manually. The choice of focussing

Beyond the processing listed above, navigation data accuracy has been improved through the co-registration
algorithm introduced in [33]. This co-registration step is very importantly NOT a part of this work, but it is
a crucial step as huge errors was present in the navigation data, leading to very poor coherence magnitudes
using un-co-registered navigation data. Apart from better co-registration, the algorithm will drive the phase
towards zero for a single polarisation, thus changing the location of the reference phase. This introduces
a new phase contribution to subsequently formed interferometers, meaning that the interferometric phase
will appear flat, i.e. close to zero, for at least some polarisations.

4.2
TDBP processor
In this section the main SAR processor used in this work will be described. The algorithm will be intro-
duced in general terms, and the differences between the 2D and 2D implementations will be highlighted
along the way.
The processor focusses and co-registers range-compressed SAR images by employing the Time Domain
Back-projection (TDBP) algorithm described in [36]. The advantage of this algorithm is that it auto-
matically co-registers SAR images from different acquisitions to a common reference grid provided that
navigation data of sufficient accuracy is available. Another advantage is that the algorithm allows for com-
pensation for non-linear flight tracks, which would pose a problem for other focussing algorithms, e.g. the
Range-Doppler algorithm.
The algorithm itself is very simple and consists of estimating the phase history over the synthetic aper-
ture by calculating the round-trip delay from a given sensor position in the aperture to the target of interest.
Once the phase history is known, radar echoes over the entire synthetic aperture can be combined in phase,
thus focusing the target of interest in the azimuth direction.

By inspecting eq. (4.1), the downside of the algorithm can easily be recognised. Assuming a large
reference grid and a fine azimuth resolution, hundreds of millions of interpolations and range calculations
may be necessary in order to focus a single SAR image onto the surface. To focus an image onto a 3D
reconstruction grid, billions of calculation may be needed. This means that the algorithm can be very com-
putationally heavy, at least if each pixel is focussed sequentially. However, each pixel in the reference
grid can be focussed independently, meaning that the algorithm is highly parallelisable. Thus, a parallel
implementation on GPU can greatly increase the computational feasibility of the algorithm. As already
stated, an earlier version of the processor was made during the work on [10]. This processor was written
entirely in Python and focussed each pixel sequentially. During the course of this work, the processor has
been rewritten and improved in several ways. Most notably, the processor has been implemented on GPU
using the CUDA programming framework [37], which allows for code written in the C-like CUDA pro-
gramming language to be run on NVIDIA GPUs. The CUDA source code is then compiled and exposed to
Python through the Nanobind library [38], which compiles a stand-alone shared library with the .cpython
extension, which is callable from Python. This allows for data import, export, and preliminary processing
to be handled in Python, while the SAR focussing itself is carried out in on GPU, where each pixel of the
reference grid is processed in parallel. On fig. 4.2 a flow chart of the TDBP processer is shown. Arrows
crossing the Python/CUDA boundary signifies transfer of data between CPU RAM and GPU VRAM. It
is worth noting that when the shared library is called from Python, an intermediate C++ layer handles the
extraction of C data-pointers from numpy data structure, facilitates VRAM memory allocation, memory
copy, and initialisation of the CUDA GPU kernel. In the same manner, the C++ layer handles the copy of
the focussed SAR image from VRAM back to RAM and exposes this as a Python type.
The difference between the 2D and 3D implementation, apart from the dimension of the reference grid,
is the introduction of the refraction correction detailed in section 4.2.3. Separate binary files are compiled
for the 2D TDBP processor and the 3D TDBP processor.
Navigation data is converted to both SCH and UTC on the Python side and copied to VRAM, while
the reference grid is converted from SCH to UTC on the GPU. The choice of converting navigation data
on the Python side is made to save processing time, as this conversion is handled quite efficiently in a
vectorised manner on the Python side. On the other hand, if the reference grid was to be ported directly
to the GPU in the UTC coordinate system, three matrices of size 𝑁𝐶× 𝑁𝑆each containing the 𝑈, 𝑇, and
𝐶𝑐coordinates would have to be copied to VRAM to focus images in 2D, while three 𝑁𝐻× 𝑁𝐶× 𝑁𝑆
arrays would be needed in the 3D scenario. Instead, a regular SCH-grid is used, which ensures that only
an 𝑁𝐶-vector containing cross-track coordinates, an 𝑁𝑆-vector containing along-track coordinates, and an
𝑁𝐶× 𝑁𝑆-matrix containing the DEM height will have to be copied to VRAM in the 2D scenario. In the
3D scenario, 𝑁𝐻, 𝑁𝐶, 𝑁𝑆vectors and the DEM heights will have to be copied.

Thus, the non-trivial part of common band filtering is the choice of low-pass filtering scheme. The
most simple approach would be to choose a single bandwidth for each range- or cross-track-line, e.g.

adaptive frequency-domain approach has been adopted in order to retain as much bandwidth as possible.

4.5.1
Adaptive multilooking scheme
As already stated, when using an SCH grid, the spacial resolution is dependent on incidence angle. This
means that the equivalent number of looks will also be dependent on incidence angle. Apart from this,
the common-band filtering scheme will reduce bandwidth further in areas with a small common-band.
Therefore, an adaptive multi-looking approach has been adopted, where the number of cross-track looks
are inversely proportional to the sum of the filter weights of the Hamming window used in the common-
band filtering. This ensures that more cross-track looks are used in the near-range and areas with a smaller
common-band, ensuring an approximately constant equivalent number of looks across the scene.

5.2.2.1
Vertical resolution
On fig. 5.11 the vertical tomographic resolution is shown for both the NE and SW direction. It is immedi-
ately apparent how the vertical resolution varies significantly throughout the scene for both directions. This
spacial variation of the vertical resolution is a direct consequence of the large variability of the baselines,
already discussed. This can have both positive and negative effect. First of all, very large baselines will
lead to improved vertical resolution, but they also reduce the height of ambiguity in a imaging scenario,
where the baselines are close to equidistant. However, in this imaging scenario, the baselines are very
non-equidistant, and thus a definite height of ambiguity cannot be defined. Instead different artifacts may
be introduced. These can be mapped through simulations, and results of such simulations are presented
and discussed in section 5.2.2.2. Given that 10 baselines are available, the unplanned large baselines may
generally be considered to yield a positive effect, as few artifacts are expected due to the large number
of baselines. It is observed that the vertical resolution is very fine for the vast majority of the scene in
both directions, especially when compared to the scene investigated in [10], where the vertical resolution
exceeded several hundred meters in large parts of the scene. For this scene, the vertical resolution is below
20 m for the vast majority of the scene in both directions, and even exceeds 10 m in large regions. This
means that very fine subsurface features might be resolved, even with techniques retaining the Fourier
resolution.

Depth of maximum coherence and scattering depth
In this subsection, two different measures will be introduced. First of all, the depth of maximum coherence.
The depth of maximum coherence is simply defined as the depth beneath the DEM height, at which the
tomographic coherence cube has it’s maximum for a given along-track/cross-track position. Therefore, the
depth of maximum coherence should be seen as a way of localising the dominant scatterer throughout the
scene. Even though the coherence cubes retain the Fourier resolution, and thus have resolution for resolv-
ing the layer-structure in the majority of the scene, the maximum coherence has been shown to originate
from the dominant second layer i.e. the first subsurface layer. Thus, the depth of maximum coherence
is considered a desirable method for extracting the height of this layer on a basis of the entire scene and
not just a smaller patch, as was done in section 5.2.2.5. On fig. 5.17 the depth of maximum coherence is
shown for both directions. It is observed that no clear common spacial pattern is visible between the two
direction. Instead, the depth of maximum coherence seems loosely related to the vertical resolution on

The second measure to be presented is the scattering depth. The scattering depth is in this work defined
as the difference between the top and bottom signal in the MUSIC tomographic cubes. For each along-
track/cross-track position, the top and bottom signal is found as the first and last entry in the height signal
vector having a power in dB larger than some threshold. The threshold is found for each along-track/cross-
track position through the Otsu adaptive thresholding algorithm [43]. Furthermore, a hard constrain is set,
such that the deepest signal can’t be more than 50 m below the DEM surface. This is set, as no signals
are observed at this depth in the tomographic cross-sections presented above. The scattering depth is
then a measure of the extent of the subsurface scattering volume, however, it is not a perfect measure for
several reasons. First of all, it is based on the MUSIC spectral estimator, which is parametric, and thus the
scattering depth can easily become dependent on the number of eigenvalues chosen. Second, the scattering
depth will be influenced to some degree by the vertical resolution, even though a super-resolution technique
is employed. However, the implemented segmentation can be shown to roughly capture the envelope of the
Capon tomograms (see appendix C.1), and thus the method is considered a somewhat accurate measure of
subsurface signal extent. On fig. 5.18 the extracted scattering depth for the two flight directions are shown.
The situation is mostly the same as with the depths of maximum coherence, namely that no clear common
spacial structure is observed, and fluctuations are seen to somewhat correlate with the vertical resolution.
The scattering depth is not narrowly distributed, however mean values of 27.5 m and 27.3 m for the SW
and NE direction, respectively, can be extracted. Histograms are available in appendix C.1. This extracted
scattering depth is relatively small, however, not unreasonable assuming a lot of power is scattered away
at the subsurface layers.

beam-formers. It must also be noted that in this specific scenario, where targets are relatively shallow, the
impact of defocussing effects are expected to be relatively small, and thus the impact of refraction effect
are primarily related to mislocation of target. If deeper targets were present, the need for correcting de-
focussing effects might be more pronounced. However, a general GPU-accelerated 3D TDBP framework
has developed during the course of this work, which can form the basis for future work, where e.g. a better
refraction correction algorithm can be implemented allowing for improved imaging in deeper scattering

5.2.3
Discussion and conclusion on Shackleton ice shelf
From the tomographic analysis it can be concluded that a clear layer structure is present, consisting of a
surface layer plus three distinct subsurface layers. Through analysis of a cross-track tomogram generated
as the average over a small along-track interval, an interlayer spacing of approximately 9 metres is found.
Furthermore, the layer structure has been shown to be continuous throughout the scene. Also, this exact
layer structure is observed in ice-sounder imagery of the centre-line of the swath. In both directions, the
first subsurface layer appears as by far the most dominant scatterer. Based on the coherence tomograms,
the mean depth of this layer across the entire scene is found to be -8.2 m and -9.5 m for the SW and NE
direction, respectively. The exact nature of these layers, however, is not apparent. A nearby assumption
would be that they are the summer surfaces of previous years. However in [44] the annual solid precipi-
tation is modelled as ≈1000 kg/m2. This would translate into significantly less than 9 metres assuming
standard firn densities, disproving this theory. More likely, the layers are simply ice inclusions and thus
directly related to past melt events. Similar layers was found in the percolation zone in Greenland [45],
which were found to be related to exactly melt events through comparison with ice cores. This strongly
suggest that the same is true for the layers, identified in this work, and the layer structure may thus hold
information about these exact melt events. However, in order to fully grasp the nature of the layers ob-
served in this scene, additional information from external sources would be needed.
In regard to the PolInSAR analysis, it can be concluded that the two-layer model describes the mea-
98
==PAGE:105==
sured coherence magnitudes reasonably well. Generally, the data is well-described during the initial taper,
where the volume-only contribution is dominant and up until the first coherence maximum, corresponding
to the large fringe-pattern in the coherence magnitude images. For larger absolute 𝑘𝑧𝑉𝑜𝑙-values, however,
it seems that the models describe the data poorly. This is especially true for the 𝐻𝑉+ 𝑉𝐻, the minimised
coherence scattering mechanism, and to some degree the 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉scattering mechanism. This might be
attributed to the small number data points in this region. However, it must be concluded that more complex
models might be needed in order to fully describe the measured coherences.
In regard to model parameters, the extracted parameters are generally in line with the expectations. Namely,
that traditionally surface-associated scattering mechanisms; 𝐻𝐻, 𝑉𝑉, 𝐻𝐻+ 𝑉𝑉, and the maximised co-
herence scattering mechanism exhibit smaller one-way penetration depths and larger surface-to-volume
ratios than the volume-associated 𝐻𝑉+ 𝑉𝐻and minimised coherence scattering mechanism. Interest-
ingly, the 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉scattering mechanism behaves more like the volume-associated scattering mech-
anisms. This indicates that this scattering mechanism, associated with double-bounce scattering, might
more likely be related to some subsurface scattering process than surface scattering events. This is not
unthinkable, considering the presence of subsurface layers. However, the small surface-to-volume ratios
for this scattering mechanism don’t suggest a surface-related scattering process.
Moreover, a subsurface layer between -8 m and -12 m is suggested, with the extracted depth varying
between scattering mechanisms. This is significant, as this is very close to the position of the dominant
subsurface layer observed in the tomographic images and, for that matter, in the ice sounder profile as well.
This suggests, that scattering from the dominant subsurface layer is what causes the periodicity in the mea-
sured coherence magnitudes. In supplement to this, it can be concluded from the tomographic analysis that
the largest surface-to-volume ratio is most likely associated with the subsurface layer, while the weakest
is associated with the surface. Finally, the estimated one-way penetration depth for the 𝑉𝑉polarisation
is seen to be reasonably close to the scattering depth, extracted from the tomographic images. While the
two measures cannot be compared on a one-to-one basis, as the one-way penetration depth is related to the
volume alone, while the scattering depth is a measure of the entire subsurface signal extent. However, the
one-way penetration depth is a least not disproven by the tomographic analysis. On the contrary, physical
parameters extracted through PolInSAR model inversion are seen to agree well with what was observed
through tomography.
One important thing to note is that only a two-layer model was inverted, while several subsurface
layers was observed in tomographic images. This choice was made, as no higher order fluctuations were
observed in the measured coherence magnitudes. The exact reason behind this is not apparat. However, it
might simply be that return from the dominant layer is so powerful that other contributions are ”drowned”.
But, as already stated, more complex models will most likely be needed, if a better representations of the
measured coherences is to be achieved. That being said, the relatively simple Two-layer model has been
shown to provide cohesive information, when compared to TomoSAR and ice sounder, suggesting that
the model might be sufficient for extracting information about the dominant subsurface layer in a SAR
mapping scenario in the percolation zone.
In regard to polarimetry, the scene has been shown to be relatively uniform in terms of scattering
mechanics, and the assumption of combined surface and volume scattering would agree well with the
extracted PolSAR observables.

One very important thing to note in regard to the complex model inversion, however, is the fact that,
the phase field is basically estimated as the residual phase between the model and the data after model
parameter estimation. Thus, the complex model fit can to some degree be said to be self-fulfilling. How-
ever, during the inversion, some constrains are imposed on the phase field. First, a correlation structure is
assumed, forcing the field to be spacially smooth and not simply a hard difference between the modelled
and measured coherence phase. Furthermore, a single field per baseline is estimated across multiple scat-
tering mechanisms simultaneously. Following the theory of the UVuS-model (see paragraph 2.5.3.1.1)
the ground phase contribution, and in this case also the processing phase contribution, should be indepen-
dent of scattering mechanism. Thus, the estimation of the field, as the phase residual after the models are
fitted, should be considered reasonable, as long as it is estimated across multiple scattering mechanisms
simultaneously. However, it is clearly seen that not all scattering mechanisms are fitted equally well by
the complex UVuS model. This is especially true for the maximised coherence scattering mechanism and,
to a lesser extent, also true for the 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉and minimised coherence scattering mechanism. However,
for the 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉and minimised coherence scattering mechanism, the coherence magnitude is not de-
scribed particularly well by the UVuS-model, even when employing the pure magnitude inversion. This
may indicate that coherences for these scattering mechanisms are simply poorly described by the UVuS-
model, and different models, e.g. a NUVuS-model, might be needed to describe the data adequately. The
maximised coherence scattering mechanism, however, is very well-described by the UVuS-model, given
the pure magnitude fit, while it is very poorly described by the UVuS-model, using the complex inversion
scheme. This makes it difficult to draw a final conclusion to the validity of the complex UVuS inversion.

which is invariant of scattering mechanism, which describes the complex coherences well for at least some
of the investigated scattering mechanism.
5.3.3
Discussion and conclusion on Dome C
In this section, a PoInSAR analysis has been carried out based on data from the Dome C site. It has been
shown that, based on pure coherence magnitude, the UVuS model described the data very well for most
scattering mechanisms, however, the model failed to capture the steep initial taper of the 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉and
minimised coherence scattering mechanism. Following the complex inversion, the 𝐻𝐻, 𝑉𝑉, 𝐻𝑉+ 𝑉𝐻,
and 𝐻𝐻+ 𝑉𝑉scattering mechanisms was very well-described in the complex plane. The 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉
and minimised coherence scattering mechanism was less accurately captured, while maximised coherence
scattering mechanism was extremely poorly described by the model. However, the phase of the maximised
coherence scattering mechanism was seen to be subject to some very strange patterns, which cannot be
readily explained.
Generally, comparable model parameters were found between the two inversion schemes, at least for
the well-described scattering mechanisms. In terms of inverted parameters, very small one-way penetra-
tion depths with a strong dependency on polarisation or scattering mechanism were found. Generally, a
significantly larger penetration depth was expected for the scene, than what was found through model in-
version. In [47] radar extinction coefficients are modelled. Around the area of the Dome C site, a one-way
attenuation due to absorption of approximately -12 dB/km was estimated, yielding a one-way penetration
depth in the order of hundreds of metres at present incidence angles. It might be noted that the attenuation
of radar waves in a medium is related to both absorption and dispersion, and as such, the physical one-way
penetration depth can be expected be smaller. However, the discrepancy is still very significant.
Also, very small surface-to-volume ratios were found through both pure magnitude and complex in-
version of the UVuS model. Albeit, the surface-to-volume ratios found through complex inversion were
slightly larger. This is directly contradicted by polarimetry, where dominant volume scattering was sug-
gested. This discrepancy is not understood.
Furthermore, it was observed that 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉closely resembles the minimised coherence scattering
mechanism, being the optimised volume-dominated scattering mechanism. Something similar was seen
at Shackleton, however in contrast to Shackleton, 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉displayed even lower coherence magnitudes
than 𝐻𝑉+ 𝑉𝐻at Dome C. It might be that absorption is simply strong for this scattering mechanism,
however, this is contradicted by the strong relative power of this scattering mechanism (see section 5.1). It
might also be that the scattering mechanism is characterised by some subsurface scattering process, how-
ever, no ice inclusions are expected in the dry snow zone, so this is unlikely. Another theory could be that
the scattering mechanism is associated with the Snow dunes on the surface, however, no clear conclusion
can be drawn to the subject.
Finally, It must be noted that, very strong and scattering mechanism dependent coherence biases were
found to be needed in order to adequately describe the data. This suggest that some strong decorrelation
effect is still present in the data. The presence of a strong uncorrected decorrelation source might also
explain some of the observed discrepancies. Furthermore, very complex scattering mechanics were sug-
gested through polarimetry. These can possibly be attributed to the presence of snow-dunes perpendicular
to the radar line-of-sight. However, the exact reason behind these scattering mechanics cannot readily be
assessed. Generally, further work will be need in order to fully understand both the PolSAR and PolInSAR
results for the site at Dome C.

5.4
Conclusion
During the course of this work, a PolInSAR processing facility has been implement from scratch. The
processing facility includes adaptive range common-band filtering to reduce baseline decorrelation effects
and, in theory, leave only the desired volumetric decorrelation. Model inversion schemes have been im-
plemented employing two different coherence models; the simpler UVuS model and the more complex
two-layer model. Maximum likelihood parameter inversion has been implemented for both models on
the basis of measured coherence magnitudes. Furthermore, a more complex state-space model inversion
scheme has been developed for the UVuS model, which jointly estimates a baseline dependent, but polari-
sation invariant, random phase field. The phase field contains ground and processing phase contributions.
This enables UVuS model inversion based on measured complex coherences as opposed to model inversion
based solely on coherence magnitudes.
Furthermore, an already existing TomoSAR processing chain has been improved. The main improve-
ment consists of the implementation of a TDBP SAR focussing algorithm on GPU in order to significantly
improve performance compared to an already existing sequential TDBP processor. This enables the focus-
ing of range compressed SAR images directly onto a three-dimensional reconstruction grid. This improves
the quality of subsequent tomographic cubes in terms of azimuth focussing, but also allows for the inclusion
of refraction correction directly into the SAR focussing. In this work, a very simple refraction correction
step was implemented, however, it has been proven sufficient for at least shallower targets through simu-
lations. In the future, the framework might be improved even more by including more advanced refraction
correction algorithms.
The PolInSAR and TomoSAR processing facilities have been used to analyse data acquired in Antarc-
tica in January and February 2024. Two sites located at two glaciologically different sites were investi-
gated, namely a site at Dome C in the dry snow zone and a site at Shackleton ice shelf in the percolation
zone.
For the site at Shackleton, both PolInSAR and 𝑉𝑉TomoSAR data was available. Through PolInSAR
inversion, it was found that the Two-layer coherence model, assuming a subsurface scattering layer at a
depth between approximately -8 m and -11 m, describes the measured coherence magnitudes reasonably
well across multiple scattering mechanisms. Especially the 𝐻𝐻, 𝑉𝑉, 𝐻𝐻+ 𝑉𝑉, and optimised surface-
dominated scattering mechanisms were found to be well-described by the model. Most notably, the large
fringe-pattern, caused be interference between the surface-reflected and subsurface-reflected radar signal,
was recreated by the models. Some discrepancy was generally found between the modelled and measured
coherences in the near-range. This indicates that a more complex coherence model might be needed in
order to fully describe the measured coherences.
Through TomoSAR analysis of the scene, a clear four-layer subsurface scattering structure was iden-
tified. The four layers were found to appear in a semi-periodic fashion with a period of approximately
nine metres starting at the surface. These layers are most likely related to previous melt events, and thus
large scale monitoring of these structures can be of glaciological interest. The interlayer power ratios were
found to be such that the first and second subsurface layers were the most powerful scatterers, while the
power from the surface, and especially the third subsurface layer, was significantly weaker. Generally, the
first subsurface layer was found to be the dominant of the four layers.
It was shown that according to all indications, the four layer structure is a continuous feature, which per-
meates the firn-pack over the entire extent of the scene. This was first of all indicated, as the same structure
was observed in vertical cross-sections imaged from both sides of the swath. Furthermore, a smaller area,
where the vertical resolution was particularly fine, was investigated. Here, the layer structure was indeed
112
==PAGE:119==
observed as being continuous. Also, the exact layer structure was observed in an ice sounder profile for
the centre-line of the swath.
Through analysis of the generated coherence tomographic cubes, the mean depth of the dominant subsur-
face layer was found to be -8.2 m and -9.5 m depending on the viewing direction. This resonates extremely
well with the extracted subsurface layer depth found through PolInSAR analysis. The estimated TomoSAR
scattering depth, i.e. the estimated width of the subsurface signal, was also seen to be comparable with the
estimated PolInSAR one-way penetration depth. Thus, it is strongly suggested that while the Two-layer
model doesn’t describe the data perfectly, information about physical subsurface structures can indeed be
extracted through PolInSAR model inversion, at least for this particular scene. However, in the future,
the application of more complex models might lead to even better estimation of physical parameters. Fi-
nally, results from pure PolSAR analysis seem to agree well with the assumption of a volume permeated
by strong surface-scattering layers.
For the site at Dome C, where only PolInSAR data were available, it was shown that based on pure
coherence magnitude inversion, the UVuS model describes the measured coherence magnitudes very well
for all scattering mechanisms, except for the 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉and optimised volume-dominated scattering mech-
anisms. For these two scattering mechanisms, the very steep taper of coherence magnitude as function of
|𝑘𝑧𝑉𝑜𝑙| wasn’t captured by the models.
Surprisingly, very small, but polarisation-dependent, one-way penetration depths was found across all scat-
tering mechanisms. Large one-way penetration depths were expected in dry snow zone, where the firn is
dry and homogeneous. Also, the surface-to-volume ratios were found to be very small or numerically zero
for all scattering mechanisms. This is in direct contradiction with polarimetric analysis, where very strong
surface scattering was indicated.
In regards to complex UVuS inversion, it was found that especially the 𝐻𝐻, 𝑉𝑉, 𝐻𝑉+ 𝑉𝐻, and
𝐻𝐻+ 𝑉𝑉scattering mechanisms were very well described in the complex plane with retrieved model
parameters comparable to those found in the pure magnitude fit. However, the 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉and optimum
volume-dominated were not as well-described, and the optimum surface-dominated scattering mechanism
was extremely poorly fitted by the complex inversion scheme. Furthermore, after counter-rotation with
the estimated random phase field, the complex 𝐻𝐻−𝑉𝑉coherence somewhat resembled, what would be
expected of a NUVuS-model, however NUVuS inversion was not carried out.
Taking all this into account, a clear conclusion to the feasibility of the complex UVuS inversion of mea-
sured complex coherences cannot be drawn. However, it is strongly indicated that the inversion is feasible
for at least some of the scattering mechanisms. Though pure PolSAR analysis, very complex scattering
mechanics are indicated for the site at Dome C, which might be related to the presence of longitudinal
snow dunes on the surface. However in general, further work will be needed in order to fully understand
both the PolSAR and PolInSAR observations at Dome C.
In this work, it has been shown that for at least the site at Shackleton ice shelf, cohesive information
about subsurface structures can be extracted though PolInSAR and TomoSAR techniques. In the light of
the newly launched BIOMASS mission, providing large scale data sets, the application of these techniques
at a larger scale forms a solid basis for future work.
Also, in this work, polarimetric TomoSAR was not carried out due to the lack of polarimetric data.
However, polarimetric TomoSAR techniques can be used to segment the tomographic signal into e.g. a
surface and a volume contribution, significantly increasing the quality of the subsurface mapping, poten-
tially mapping even deeper subsurface stuctures. The implementation of polarimetric processing in the
already existing tomographic processor, could also be highly desirable in the future.
