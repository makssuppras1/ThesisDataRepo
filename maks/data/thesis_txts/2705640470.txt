Abstract
Introduction
individuals of distinct genetic ancestries have shown significant differences in disease
prevalence, treatment, and drug response. Accounting for genetic ancestry could have a
major impact on studies within precision medicine. Genetic ancestry is not registered in a
standardized way in the Danish health­care system but can be derived from genotyping
data. However, the analysis and visualization of population structure remains a bottle­
neck due to the complexity of genetic data. Dimensionality reduction is often used when
visualizing population structure derived from genotype data.
Materials and methods
Here we propose Variational Autoencoders (VAE) as a dimensionality reduction technique
that captures non­linear relationships while further preserving the data geometry. VAE are
deep generative models that employ a coder­decoder structure to learn latent represen­
tations of the data, which can later be used for either visualization or further analysis with
less covariates.
Results
We benchmarked VAE against classical embedding methods like PCA and UMAP using
Illumina Global Screening Array genotype data from the Danish Blood Donor Study and
the Copenhaben Biobank to elucidate genetic ancestries. In addition, we generated a
reference continuous color scheme for ancestry by projecting a low representation of the
human origins dataset into the CIELAB color space.
Discussion
Our VAE framework can be applied to assign genetic ancestry from genotyping data in a
systematic way, so that the information can be utilized in precision medicine. The con­
tinuous color scheme generated can be used to visualize genetic distances perceptually
with colors.

1
Introduction
In this project, we use variational autoencoders (VAEs) to cluster and visualize population
structure based on genotyping data from a large cohort of 370,319 individuals included in
two danish bio banks. Further, we benchmark their performance against other methods.
For visualization of genetic ancestry, we create a reference color palette using the CIELAB
color space that allows for a continuous color scheme of genetic distances.
1.1
Population structure genetics and precision medicine
Precision medicine is an emerging initiative striving to treat and prevent diseases at a
higher resolution by accounting for individual traits. [1]. This novel strategy aims at en­
hancing prognosis and treatment in many areas relative to health care by creating strati­
fied patient subgroups. Therefore, achieving targeted treatments by accounting for effects
such as inter­individual variation, ethnicity, and more. [2, 3]. Highly dimensional datasets
linking omic analysis to phenotypes are necessary for precision medicine strategies, al­
lowing to associate, e.g., genetic traits with phenotypes or diseases. The expansion of
data gathering techniques such as genotype arrays and next­generation sequencing has
created a new paradigm where precision medicine is possible. Nonetheless, using data
for medical decisions implies using advanced algorithms capable of yielding data­driven
decisions [4]. For instance, the employment of High­performance computing (HPC) and
Machine learning algorithms is necessary for data­driven decision­making in medicine.
[5].
Diverse effects such as genetic inter­individual variation, lifestyle, and socio­economic
factors should be included in medical decision­making. The focus of this manuscript in
regards to precision medicine will focus solely on genetic components. Although, many
more traits such as the microbiome, the epigenome, and the metabolome are also of
interest for precision medicine [6]. Genetic data has shown great potential as a catalyst
for precision medicine due to the quantity of information comprised in the genome and its
stability throughout patient life. For instance, Genome­wide Association Studies (GWAS)
are commonly used to establish genotype­phenotype relationships. Genetic ancestry is a
major confounder in GWAS and accounting for it may improve the stratification of patients
into more representative sub­groups [7, 8]. Given the complexity and high dimensionality
of ancestry data, dimensionality reduction methods are required.
By identifying specific patient groups, the diagnosis and treatment of diseases can be­
come tailored to the patient. A given trait can be used to stratify a group, if its presence or
absence significantly impacts the patient’s prevention, diagnosis or treatment compared
to a control. [2, 3]. These traits can be found through genotyping and conducting GWAS,
which estimates statistical associations between gene variants and phenotypes. [9].

Genetic ancestry has been reported as a confounding factor in GWAS, showing associ­
ations between ancestry and specific phenotypes. For instance, reference intervals from
clinical laboratory tests have shown differences in genetic ancestry groupping. [10, 3, 7].
Further, genetic ancestry can also explain drug response diversity in patients. This genetic
variability has reported common differences between ancestries in drug­metabolizing en­
zymes, protein transporters, or receptors, together with other factors. Suggesting that
stratifying patient groups according to genetic ancestry could improve health care, e.g, by
adapting drug dosage, laboratory biochemical reference intervals according to ancestry.
[11, 12, 13].
Population structure defines the genetic variation between populations driven by diverse
evolutionary effects. Divergent allele frequencies between populations are used to ac­
count for population structure. When these allele frequencies are involved in disease
predisposition or drug response they can be used to stratify patient groups. Nevertheless,
the classification of patients into ancestry groups is complicated by its ethical implications.
Many studies use self­identified race/ethnicity (SIRE) to classify individuals. However, it
has been shown that this practice can lead to biased results since SIREs have a low
resolution for ancestry mixtures. Defining ancestry groups through genetic analyses pro­
vides more accuracy in patient sub­grouping by accounting for mixed backgrounds and
quantitatively measuring genetic ancestry instead of self­identification. [14, 15].
1.2
SNPs and Genotype array data
Single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) have been widely employed for genetic stud­
ies. SNPs correspond to substitutions of one base at a specific location on the genome.
Since SNPs are inherited from progenitors, two allele copies are found in diploid organ­
isms. Considering two alleles of a SNP as A and B, distinct genotypes can be found such
as homozygous as AA or BB, or heterozygous as AB. Regarding their location, these poly­
morphisms can be found both in coding and non­coding regions of the genome, although,
they are more frequent in non­coding regions, due to natural selection [16]. SNPs can be
used to establish genotype­phenotype relationships, haplotype mapping or linkage dis­
equilibrium [17]. Additionally, genetic variation between human populations can also be
estimated by using the frequency of specific SNPs. [18].
1.3
Dimensionality reduction techniques
With the expansion of data gathering techniques such as genotyping arrays and next­
generation sequencing, the size of biological datasets has been significantly increased.
Reducing the complexity of the data is desired for analyses. In fact, as the dimensionality
increases, the data becomes more and more sparse. The required data grows expo­
nentially with the dimension to support reliable and significant results. This is known as
the curse of dimensionality, which was first described by Richard E. Bellman [19]. Di­
mensionality reduction transforms high­dimensional data into a meaningful representa­
tion with fewer dimensions. Intrinsic dimensionality is defined as the minimum number

of parameters necessary to account for the observed properties of the data. The goal of
dimensionality reduction is to approach the data to its intrinsic dimensionality preserving
data geometry. [20].
Dimensionality can be reduced by either excluding some features from the data namely,
feature selection. Feature selection is empirical and used in cases of multicollinearity be­
tween features to filter them out if they present high correlations. It is also used when
explanatory variables are constant across all observations or for irrelevant variables for
the analysis. Feature extraction is an alternative to feature selection. It corresponds to
applying a function that maps the data to a lower dimension. The concept is to transform
the data into new features combining the initial ones. To achieve intrinsic dimensional­
ity, either (1) linear or (2) non­linear feature extraction is recommended. In short, linear
techniques reduce the data complexity by projecting it into a linear subspace with a lower
dimension. For non­linear techniques, there are several approaches. Some methods
preserve the global geometry of the data while others preserve local properties [21]. To
end with, there is also a group of methods that use mixtures of linear models [22]. In the
coming sections, some of the most employed dimensionality reduction techniques will be
discussed towards their use for SNP­array data.
1.3.1
Principal component analysis (PCA)
PCA is an algorithm for linear dimensionality reduction proposed by Pearson (1901) and
Hotelling (1933) [23, 24]. PCA is still widely employed for data compression and rep­
resentation. In short, the algorithm reduces dimensionality by linear feature extraction.
Uncorrelated variables capturing the most variance are generated as linear functions of
the original variables that maximize the data variance. The mathematical foundations of
this method will be further explained in section 2.4. Since its publication, this algorithm has
been adapted to different data types or goals, some examples are sparse [25], nonlinear
[26] or robust PCA [27].
In population genetic studies, PCA is extensively employed and is considered the gold
standard for visualizing population structure data. For instance it is integrated in many
popular packages intented for genetics analysis, such as EIGENSTRAT [28] or PLINK
[29]. In addition, corporations such as 23andme (Sunnyvale, California) or MyHeritage (Or
Yehuda, Israel) use principal component analysis to determine genetic distances between
populations. Despite being a standard tool for genetic ancestry, PCA might not be the best
option to visualize and compress data [30]. The first two principal components frequently
account for a small amount of variance. This results in relying on ten principal components
on most cases. Moreover, while using linear transformations to reduce dimensions, PCA
fails to capture non­linear relationships [31]. Hence, contributing to a loss of information
at the cost of reducing dimensionality. It has been suggested that other methods could
be beneficial for their use in population structure studies [32]. In this manuscript, VAEs
will be tested as a competing alternative to PCA.

1.3.2
Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection for Dimension
Reduction (UMAP)
UMAP is a novel dimensionality reduction technique reported as an alternative to t­distributed
stochastic neighbor embedding (t­SNE). The algorithm first creates a weighted graph for
all data points with their k­nearest neighbors. Subsequently, the low dimensional space
is created with a random initialized weighted graph. The embedding is then optimized by
minimizing the difference between the two weighted graphs. The mathematical aspects
of this algorithm are showcased in section 2.5.
UMAP has reported outstanding performances in terms of data scaling, showing signif­
icantly low computing times. Regarding the preservation of the structure, UMAP achieves
a better balance between local and global structure than other methods such as t­distributed
stochastic neighbor embedding. Overall, the data scaling of UMAP is far superior to t­
SNE, both in terms of computing time and preservation of the global structure. Further,
while being a stochastic algorithm, the similarity from run to run that UMAP shows is not
present in t­SNE. Suggesting that UMAP can yield more reproducible results than t­SNE.
This is why t­SNE was discarded in the benchmarking of this project. However, in con­
trast to PCA, a direct interpretation of distances or cluster sizes that these two non­linear
techniques offer is not possible. Moreover, hyper­parameter tuning is slightly more intu­
itive for UMAP but remains a challenge. To end with, UMAP has the added versatility of
generating results with a custom number of dimensions. Even if UMAP presents some
advantages over t­SNE, the preservation of data structure binds this method to visualiza­
tions purposes solely. [33].
1.4
Deep generative modeling
Research in biology and medicine is increasingly becoming more and more data­dependent.
The increased complexity and quantity of data have opened new opportunities for the ap­
plication of deep learning in this area. For instance, some of these models have been
extensively applied to disease prediction, image recognition and many others areas re­
lated to computational biology [34, 35].
Deep learning is divided into two categories, generative and discriminative modeling. The
first learns about the distribution of the data and is thus more appropriate for pattern recog­
nition and clustering. The latter focuses on learning boundaries between data classes,
making them more suited for classification problems. Fundamentally, discriminative mod­
eling learns the decision boundaries in the data space whereas generative models learn
the most probable distribution of the whole data. Learning data distribution comes at a
cost for generative models, because they make them computationally expensive. Gener­
ative models can be used for dimensionality reduction and, since they model distributions,
they can generate new data points by sampling. These models are not mutually exclusive;
for instance, a generative model can reduce the complexity of the data, and its output can
be coupled with a discriminative model. Generally, discriminative models fall under the
class of supervised learning, meaning that the data points need to be coupled to a label

to infer the class of new data points. This is not the case of unsupervised learning where
the learning process is not based on label prediction [36, 37, 38].
1.4.1
Variational autoencoders
Variational autoencoders are generative, they learn the true joint distribution of the data
using an encoder­decoder structure. They differ from classical autoencoders by their
mathematical formulation and regularization strategy. Briefly, VAEs encode the input data
x into a latent space z, with lower dimensions. The latent dimension is used to reconstruct
the input as x∗, and the error between the reconstructed data and the original input is
calculated. This enforces the VAE to learn only the most important features that reduce the
reconstruction error. Additionally, the latent space is regularized to improve its properties.
The mathematical foundations of VAEs will be introduced in section 2.6.
PCA can be understood as AEs. Considering a N­dimensional dataset, the encoder rep­
resents the data with M < N dimensions that are the orthogonal directions that capture
most variance. The decoder reconstructs the data using the mean value on the N −M di­
rections that are not represented. This can be represented as a two­layer network where
the hidden and output layers are linear, and that minimize the squared reconstruction er­
ror. When PCA is computed using a deep learning approach, the weight vectors might not
be orthogonal but they will tend to have equal variances. VAEs have a similar encoder­
decoder architecture. Nonetheless, VAEs are based on bayesian inference, and they
contain non­linear layers and regularization strategies to prevent overfitting. Given those
similarities and differences, comparing those two techniques is of interest.

ficulty appears in assigning labels to every observation for coloring. Indeed, the simplest
strategy consists of assigning labels that correspond to either self identified race/ethnicity
(SIREs) or country of birth. However, this approach does not account for mixtures and/or
has a very low resolution for them. As it can be seen in figure 1.2, the portion of Africans
or South Americans having no mixture with other populations is very low [39]. This indi­
cates that continuous measures for labeling are preferred over discrete labels. Creating
a continuous color scheme that corresponds to genetic distances in color space would
allow displaying this mixtures with a quantitative resolution.

2.1
Cohorts and experimental setups
Given that this manuscript entails two different projects, a different dataset was chosen to
match each project’s motivations. To investigate the applicability of VAEs to reduce geno­
type array dimensions and capture population structure, we used a large public danish
cohort. A reference and publicly available dataset was used to establish a reproducible
continuous color scheme that describes ancestry. This renders the color scheme a refer­
ence that can be applied to any study, benefiting the scientific community and easing the
reproducibility of results due to the open­access of the data.

Danish cohort: DBDS and CHB
The datasets used for the VAE project were the Danish Blood Donor Study (DBDS) and
the Copenhagen Hospital Biobank (CHB). [40, 41]. The joint cohorts provide 627,806
SNPs from 370,319 donors in total. The genotyping was carried out using the Global
Screening Array by Illumina. The majority (89%) of patients in this cohort were born in
Denmark, and the distribution of ancestries is shown in figure 2.1.

Genotyping arrays
Genotyping arrays are used to quantify SNP variants. Affymetrix (Santa Clara, CA) and
Illumina (San Diego, CA) are the most common providers. The methodology used for
quantifying SNPs is very similar for the two platforms. An overview of the experimental

2.6
Variational autoencoders (VAE)
Classical Autoencoders (AE) correspond to a less complex version of VAE, they will be
introduced to ease the complexity of this section. AEs are unsupervised learning algo­
rithms that learn compact representations of the input data by using an encoding­decoding
structure. There are three building blocks for an AE, namely an encoder, a decoder and
a lower­dimensional representation of the data or latent space. The algorithm encodes
the data into a latent representation with lower dimensionality. The dimension constrain
constitutes a bottleneck that forces the algorithm to summarize the signal more efficiently
without overfitting. Without this constrain, the algorithm would learn the identity matrix.
The decoder then reconstructs the latent representation. Gradient descent is used for
minimizing the reconstruction error between the input and the decoded latent represen­
tation.
AEs have presented several downsides for generative purposes. The latent represen­
tation generated does not have any regularization to maintain the data structure. Fur­
ther regularization approaches are fundamental to avoid overfitting and ensure adequate
properties of the latent representation. AEs can reconstruct inputs but are not generative
since the learned compression corresponds to an arbitrary function. While having the
same encoder­decoder structure, VAEs instead compress the data into a latent space in
the form of a probability distribution. This results in a model that is generative since it is
possible to sample from the latent space.
VAEs can be defined as probabilistic autoencoders that limit overfitting and provide mean­

Learning criteria and learning rate scheduler
The learning criterion corresponds to the sum of the reconstruction error as the binary
cross entropy, and the regularization term which is the KL divergence. To avoid over­
regularization, the KLD term was slowly introduced by adding a scaling factor that controls
its contribution in the loss function. This was done by setting the scaling factor to zero until
convergence of the reconstruction error term. In the following epochs the scaling factor
is linearly increased until reaching a value of 1, which is later maintained. The missing
values in the input data were ignored in the back­propagation of the model by setting their
corresponding reconstruction error indices to zero.
The learning rate was scheduled by monitoring the divergence of the latent space of the
current iteration with the previous one. Therefore, when the VAE becomes stable and
reaches convergence, the latent spaces tend to reassemble across epochs and thus re­
duce the learning rate.

CIELAB color space for reference ancestry palette
The CIELAB color space expresses colors with three values: L* for lightness, and a*
and b* that form a plane spanning the combination of four colors: red, green, blue, and
yellow. CIELAB is a 3D color space intended as a perceptually uniform space that covers
the entire range of human color perception. The relative difference between two colors is
approximated by defining each color as a point in a three­dimensional space. Thus, color
differences can be accounted for by taking the Euclidean distance between two given
points in the data space.

4.1
Low dimensional embeddings
The decisions over which embedding better preserves the structure of the data are pri­
marily based on the evaluation of the latent spaces with different classification models.
Although, it must be noted that the three methods yielded very different results from a
visual point of view. PCA efficiently captured ancestry signals for both datasets since dif­
ferent populations were projected and clustered differently. Although the clusters contain
high variance, this mainly reflected the admixture of the populations for both the 2D and
3D representations. PCA performed well in both datasets showing a good scaling capac­
ity.
The embeddings generated with UMAP on the danish cohorts yielded poor representa­
tions of the data where few clusters could be distinguished in the low dimensional spaces.
A further hyper­parameter search might improve the embedding, nonetheless, due to high
computation times and memory requirements we could not surpass 75 neighbours with
the uwot software. This is due to the size of the dataset computed, where the training
consists of 40,000 individuals with 100,000 SNPs each. UMAP performance was heavily
affected by the size of the data, the embeddings on the joint cohorts and the human ori­
gins dataset showed very different results. The human origins dataset, while consisting of
6000 observations, yielded better results clustering ancestries. This might be due to the
amount of neighbors selected as a hyperparameter. For a smaller dataset like the human
origins, the amount of neighbours needed to create a representative embedding might be
less than that required for a larger dataset, thus affecting the scalability of the method.
The grid search computed on VAE showed that the structure of the neural network does
not influence the reconstruction error with the testing settings established in this project.
To compute the grid search, each set of hyperparameters was only computed once with
a given seed. To obtain more robust results and ensure that the architecture of the VAE
does not influence the reconstruction error, it would be necessary to compute each hy­
perparameter set with different seeds and compute the mean reconstruction error. The
latent spaces obtained for each set of hyperparameters show that even if the reconstruc­
tion error is not affected by the architecture, the latent space generated has a different
structure. Mainly, the clusters are arranged in a similar fashion, but there are major dif­
ferences when these latent spaces are compared. Nonetheless, running the VAE with
a same set of hyperparameters twice yielded almost identical latent spaces. The grid
search could be further studied by also assessing a measure of how structured the latent
space is in addition to the reconstruction error.

4.2
Evaluation of low dimensional embeddings
In the danish cohorts subset, the embeddings were compared using KNN and elastic net
to predict the ancestry labels given the embedding coordinates. Overall, VAE show better
results in both the 2D and 3D embeddings in both KNN and elastic net predictions. This
is summarized by the precision, recall and F­1 score that are higher for VAEs in all cases
(Fig.3.19 and 3.20). Some ancestries like Oceania, south and north America are not well
predicted. In the case of Oceania this might be due to the lack of samples given that the
representation of the Oceanic ancestry in this dataset is poor. For the American samples,
since those samples come from a mixture of Europeans with other populations, predict­
ing the ancestry label is not an easy task since the genetic ancestry of those samples is
very sparse. North Asia is also poorly predicted, this might be also explained by the het­
erogeneity of individuals in this category. Most representatives found in this group come
from Russia, where there is a vast genetic mixture with East Europe and Asia. UMAP
generated the least structured latent spaces and this can be also noted from the perfor­
mance of both KNN and elastic net on the UMAP embeddings. The clustering algorithms
(Apendix A.4) did not show any differences in the comparison of embeddings and both
k­means clustering and DBSCAN did not capture the clusters generated with the dimen­
sionality reduction methods. Nonetheless, this clustering results are preliminary, and a
more thorough hyper­parameter search could improve the performance of the clustering
methods. Given that the ancestry groups contain high intra­cluster variance, splitting the
ancestry groups into more specific regions could also help on this issue. Nevertheless,
since the clustering did not allow any assessment of the embeddings structure, the cluster­
ing analysis was not taken into account to evaluate the dimensionality reduction methods.
In the human origins dataset, PCA and UMAP yielded better predictive results than VAE
by evaluating the KNN performance (Apendix A.13). The variational autoencoder was
not able to reconstruct the samples as well as it did for the Danish cohorts. A further as­
sessment of the embeddings could be performed to conclude which is the best method to
represent the human origins dataset in a low dimension. Additionally, the VAE should be
more tailored to the downscaling of the dataset. Indeed, the VAE was implemented and
designed by testing on the Danish cohorts. The KL divergence might be over regularizing
the reconstruction error, preventing its optimization. This is due to the annealing proce­
dure that is introduced in the loss computation. As explained in the methods section. The
KL divergence term is slowly introduced in the loss when convergence is reached. Per­
haps, having a patience parameter that delays the KLD inclusion in the loss might help
the VAE perform better on smaller datasets where convergence takes more epochs.

5
Conclusion
Overall, VAE have been reported as a useful tool for dimensionality reduction of genetic
ancestry for large datasets.
The latent spaces generated with a large danish cohort
showed more structure by yielding better predictive results compared to PCA and UMAP.
The 2D latent space generated with VAE represents ancestry in two dimensions, where
each can be interpreted as an effect. The first dimension accounts for the north­to­south
variation and the second dimension represents the divergence from the European ances­
try. Additionally the latent space is regularized with the KLD divergence and provides a
latent representation that follows a normal distribution with zero mean and unit variance.
Nevertheless, the VAE did not perform well on the human origins dataset, mostly due
to the sample size. UMAP and PCA generated embedding with more structure since
KNN reported better results when using those embeddings to predict ancestry labels. In
this project, UMAP coordinates where used to establish a continuous color scheme that
represents ancestry with color differences. This color scheme is capable of representing
distinct genetic ancestries, but has low resolution for ancestries that are closer.
5.1
Future perspectives
Given the results obtained, the future steps consist on improving the VAE to be applicable
to smaller datasets. The grid search scheme that was employed in this project should be
re­designed by computing more examples of each set of hyper­parameters with different
seeds. The stochasticity of the model should be also evaluated and accounted for. In
addition, a group of samples were not well reconstructed by the VAE consistently accross
all ancestries. Investigating the cause of the low reconstruction of these samples could
give some insights on how to improve the performance of the VAE.
The preliminary results obtained on the color scheme project showed that continuous
color schemes can help better understand and visualize genetic ancestry data. The hu­
man origins dataset was projected into the CIELAB color space, establishing a reference
continuous color scheme for ancestry. Although these analysis and results need to be
further exploited. The embeddings of the human origins dataset can be further improved.
This will enhance the properties of the low dimensional space, presenting better clusters
explained by ancestry. The reproducibility and use of this color scheme should be tested
with other datasets and applied on different studies to explore the potential use­cases.
VAE are a good candidate as a embedding to project into the CIELAB space since the
latent spaces obtained spread the variance of the points in the unit interval. If a struc­
tured latent space can be obtained with this dataset, the low color resolution for similar
ancestries would be improved due to regularization strategies offered by VAE.

A.1
t­distributed stochastic neighbor embedding
The approach of dimmensionality reduction for t­SNE relies on SNE with some improve­
ments in terms of efficiency and computation times. The main idea is that a low dimen­
sional space is created and then the data structure is preserved by an iterative process.
Indeed, the learning is optimized by keeping the distance between points as similar be­
tween the low­dimensional representation and the real data. The inputs for t­SNE are the
data and a hyperparameter called perplexity.
Considering a dataset X = {x1, x2, xm}, where M = {1, ..., M} with dimmensionality
N x M. The starting point is to compute the conditional probability that a point xi would
pick xj as its neighbour such as in equation 2.7. As it can be seen in the equation,
the probability is computed by using the euclidean distance, it must be noted that the
distance can be switched for any other metric such as the manhattan [48], Aitchison [49],
etc. This probability is high for close points and infinitesimal for far appart datapoints. The
parameter σ2

the value for the σ2 parameter depends on the perplexity hyperparameter, further details

probability pi|i is set to equal zero. It must be noted that the probability of pi|j is not equal
to pj|i since the distribution is different for every point. For instance pij is averaged such

A.4
Clustering of the embeddings
To assess the quality of the clusters two clustering algorithms were employed, namely k­
means clustering and hierarchichal clustering. We assessed the quality of the latent space
by evaluating the clustering results, by visual inspection of the clusters and the proportion
of classes per cluster. If the algorithm is able to cluster the data each cluster should
be associated to a single ancestry group. To compute k­means clustering the amount
of clusteres was estimated by using the number of classes present in the dataset. For
hierarchichal clustering the hyperparameter that one can use is the minimum amount of
observations per cluster. This hyperparameter was tested with different values spanning
from 25 to 400. The results of k­means clustering can be seen in figure A.3 and A.4. for
hierarchichal clustering HDBSCAN was used, the results are shown in figure A.5 and A.6

A.5
Evaluation of the embeddings on the Human origins
dataset
We performed the same analysis as for the joint danish cohorts for PCA, VAE and UMAP
but only on 3D embeddings that yielded. This is mainly explained by the nature of the
CIELAB color space in which colors are explained in three dimensions. The idea is to
project our 3D coordinates into the CIELAB space, creating a reference color palette in
three dimensions. Then when a new point is projected into this 3D space a color will be
attributed to the observation depending on its relative location in the CIELAB space.

A.8 and the VAE in figure A.11. For this section, splitting the data into train and test can

UMAP and VAEs embeddings contain the whole dataset.

Introduction
Significant differences in disease prevalence, treatment, and drug response have been reported in
individuals of distinct genetic ancestries. Accounting for genetic ancestry could have a major impact
on studies within precision medicine. Genetic ancestry is not registered in a standardized way in
the Danish health­care system but can be derived from genotyping data. However, the analysis
and visualization of population structure remains a bottleneck due to the complexity of genetic data.
Dimensionality reduction is often used when visualizing population structure derived from genotype
data.
Materials and methods
Here we propose Variational Autoencoders (VAE) as a dimensionality reduction technique that cap­
tures non­linear relationships while further preserving the data geometry. VAE are deep generative
models that employ a coder­decoder structure to learn latent representations of the data, which can
later be used for either visualization or further analysis with less covariates.
Results
We benchmark VAEs against classical embedding methods using Illumina Global Screening Array
genotype data from the Danish Blood Donor Study and the Copenhaben Biobank to elucidate ge­
netic ancestries. In addition, we generated a reference continuous color scheme for ancestry by
projecting a low representation of the human origins dataset into the CIELAB color space.
Discussion
Our VAE framework can be applied to assign genetic ancestry from genotyping data in a systematic
way, so that the information can be utilized in precision medicine. The continuous color scheme
generated can be used to visualize genetic distances perceptually with colors.
