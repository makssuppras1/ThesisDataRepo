Development of 3D simulation model of the heart for validation of vector
ow imaging
by Rasmus Hvid
A three dimensional model of the blood ow inside the human left ventricle holds
the potential to be a useful tool for quantifying the accuracy of ultrasound (US)
techniques. In this study a framework for 3D models of the human left ventricle (LV)
is developed for nite element (FE) simulation. The model includes segmentation of
the left ventricle from cardiac computed tomography (CT), prescribed movement by
mathematical modeling and inlet/outlet conditions derived from US. The LV model
solves the Navier-Stokes equation for non compressible uids in the domain dened
by the moving boundary using the arbitrary Lagrangian Eulerian (ALE) formulation
in COMSOL version 5.2a.
The model framework was successfully developed and
implemented in COMSOL which proved to be convergent in a mesh convergence study.
Examples of input variables to the model were compared with physiologically expected
values which shows an equivalence. The model framework in this thesis is exible and
has the potential to be further expanded in future work.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Simulating blood ow by computational uid dynamics (CFD) modeling is a new tool
in cardiovascular medicine which provides physicians patient-specic data which is
otherwise unobtainable. This data can be used for making treatment decisions based
on the specic case instead of relying on large population studies. In general there
are many potential uses for robust cardiovascular CFD models [12]. This project aims
primarily to support research regarding ultrasound ow techniques.
1.1
Motivation
Currently there is no gold standard for the measuring performance of US 3D vector
ow in the heart.
At this time there is no image modality that captures the 3D
velocity eld with sucient precision to use as baseline in a direct comparison. In
order to test the performance of existing and new ultrasound velocity ow estimators
a known input is desired for in silico testing of the velocity estimators. Knowing the
true 3D velocity eld as input gives the opportunity to quantify estimation errors in a
controlled environment. A simulation tool already exists which provides a foundation
for testing transducer geometry, acquisition techniques and their representation of a
given known velocity eld. A framework for modeling velocity elds inside LV using
CFD will therefore provide a useful tool for in silico testing of the performance of
various 3D vector ow techniques of the human heart.
Previous work in the group has focused on simulating the blood ow inside the
human left ventricle (LV) in 2D. The 2D ow simulations have been implemented
with rigid walls, moving walls and uid structure interaction (FSI)[2]. The purpose
of this thesis is to expand the moving wall methods to 3D. The work formulation is
as follows:
Create a framework for 3D nite element (FE) CFD model of LV with
the purpose of aiding in designing 3D US ow imaging techniques.
1.2
Scope
The scope of this thesis is limited to developing a framework and proof of concept for
creating CFD models of LV. Background knowledge of technical and clinical elements
are consequently in the scope of this thesis as well. A brief state of the art is also in
the scope to place the results of this thesis in the current literature. The results will
therefore include a case simulation rather than an extensive parameter study as that
is considered beyond the scope.

Chapter 2
Theory
Simulations are often expressed by using ideal, or simplied, versions of the true
system which makes the usability of the simulation results dependent on the extend
of the simplications made. Therefore specic knowledge of the true system combined
with the aim of a given simulation are essential elements in creating useful results. This
section presents essential physiology and anatomy of the human heart for simulating
blood ow in LV.
2.1
Clinical background
The heart is a muscular organ which, much like a pump, propels blood throughout the
body as it contracts. This is a vital function that is necessary for transportation of
oxygen and nutrients, expulsion of carbondioxide among other transportation needs.
The blood circulation system of the human body has two circuits which are con-
nected by the heart (gure 2.1); The systemic circuit which delivers oxygenated blood
to the entire body and returns the deoxygenated blood and the pulmonary circuit
which delivers the deoxygenated blood to the lungs where it is oxygenated and re-
turned.

2.1.1
The anatomy of the heart
The heart consists of four main chambers; the left side atrium and ventricle and the
right side atrium and a ventricle (gure 2.1). The ventricles are the primary pump-
ing chambers, propelling blood through the pulmonary and systemic circuits during
contraction. The atria are connected to veins and pump blood into the ventricles
during atrial contraction which facilitates lling of the ventricles. The right side of
the heart (right atrium and ventricle) receives deoxygenated blood from the systemic
circuit and pumps it to the lungs through the pulmonary circuit. The left side of
the heart (left atrium and left ventricle) then receives the oxygenated blood from the
pulmonary circuit and delivers it to the whole body through the systemic circuit. To
prevent backow of blood during contraction the heart has four valves located at the
inlet and outlet of each ventricle. The valves are opened and closed by pressure dif-
ference which ensures unidirectional ow. In LV the valve connected to LA is called
the Mitral valve and the valve connected to Aorta is called the Aortic valve. The vol-
ume of LV contains small muscles called papillary muscles which are connected to the
cord-like tendons, called chordae tendineae, which are connected to the Mitral valve.
This serves as reinforcement for withholding the great pressure dierences occurring
in the heart.

2.1.2
The Cardiac Cycle
The human resting heart rate (HR) depends on sex, age, tness and many other fac-
tors. The average resting HR of a healthy individual weighing 70 kg is 75 beats per
minute (bpm) and is considered normal if it is between 60 and 100 bpm. Each heart
beat is a cycle consisting of a ventricular systole and diastole. The (ventricular) sys-
tole is where the ventricles contract and the diastole is where the ventricles are lled.
The muscle contractions in the heart is controlled by electrical signals, the propa-
gation of action potentials, following very specic conductive pathways in the heart.
The electrical impulse originates in the sinoatrial (SA) node, creating atrial contrac-
tion, travels through the atrioventricular (AV) node which delays the impulse from
propagating further for about 100 ms, before propagating to the ventricles, creating
ventricular contraction. The delay caused by the AV node allows time for lling of the
ventricles. The electrical potentials in the heart are great enough to measure using
external electrodes attached to the skin. The output is called an electrocardiogram
(ECG) which is depicted in its general form in gure 2.2. It is convenient to describe
the heart cycle using ECG as the contractions are driven by electrical currents. How-
ever for the purpose of this thesis it is more convenient to describe the cardiac cycle,
lasting 0.8 seconds for at HR of 75 bpm, according to phases dened by the systolic
and diastolic movement of LV.

Isovolumetric contraction is the phase immediately before contraction where

2.1.3
Heart movement
The movement of the heart, and LV, is a result of several complicated interactions
between the passive properties of the myocardium, the uid properties, the contractile
forces (and even the direction) of the myocardium bers. All of which are aected by
the electrical impulses which leads to many more interactions. Despite this the LV
movement of a healthy individual at rest can be generalized as a contraction in the
radial and axial direction with a twist around the long axis. The twist is due to the
direction of the myocardium bers as shown in gure 2.4.
Cardiac Output and other cardiovascular metrics
A common metric used to estimate the function of the heart is the cardiac output
(CO). CO is a measurement of the volume ow of each ventricle. It is calculated by
CO = HR ∗SV
(2.1)
where HR is heart rate and SV is stroke volume. The normal range of resting CO
is between 4.0 and 8.0 L/min. This metric is only valid for a healthy heart because
it does not take regurgitation into account. Regurgitation is when a valve does not
close properly causing backow. This aects the CO because it uses SV which in the
regurgitation case is only partially delivered to the systemic circuit.
The stroke volume is a measure of how much blood is pumped out of the ventricle
in each heart cycle. It can be calculated from the end diastolic volume (EDV) and
the end systoliv volume (ESV). The normal range for SV is between 55-70 mL.
SV = EDV −ESV
(2.2)
Another useful measure is the ejection fraction (EF) as mentioned above. EF is a
measure of how big a fraction of EDV is ejected and is often expressed as a percentage:
EF[%] = EDV −SV
EDV
∗100%
(2.3)
The human ejection fraction ranges from 55-70% with a mean of 58%.
2.2
State of the art
Use of cardiovascular CFD has proven useful in many aspects of research and medicine.
A review by Morris et. al. [12] lists clinical uses such as valve prostheses, stent design,
heart failure and aortic aneurysms. It also states some of the advantages such as
less needs of invasive procedures and the possibility of estimating physics which are
otherwise impossible to measure, such as wall shear stress and ow patterns*. Because
of its many applications there are several dierent approaches for building the models.
The main approaches are FSI, multiscale and lastly pure CFD such as the model in
this thesis.
There are several ways of obtaining the boundary geometry and movement. There
is cardiac CT which has the advantage of fast acquisition and great temporal and spa-
tial resolution. However CT is invasive as it requires ionizing radiation. Furthermore
CT cannot be used to estimate uid ow. Cardiac MR is another way of segmenting
the boundary geometry and movement[13]. The acquisition time is however longer
which results in fewer time instances of less accurate geometries which might hide
some ow features in the resulting model[15]. MR does have the advantage of being

2.5
The ALE formulation
In general the PDEs of model physics are dened in either a spatial or a material
coordinate system.
The spatial formulation, also called the Eulerian formulation,
uses axes which are xed in space where the axes of the material formulation, also
called the Lagrangian formulation, are xed to a material in a reference conguration
and follows the deformations of the material.
The Lagrangian formulation is useful when simulating solids where deformation
may occur. This is especially true for solids with anisotropic material properties as
they become independent of the material orientation when dened in the Lagrangian
formulation.
The Eulerian formulation is useful for describing physics in a xed point in space.
This is often the method of choice in uid mechanics as it is impractical, and has little
value, to follow singular particles in uid ow.
The Eulerian formulation is however inadequate when there are moving boundaries
involved. The boundaries are best described by the Lagrangian formulation, and the
uid domain best by the Eulerian. The solution is combining both formulations in a
special way referred to as the Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian formulation (ALE). This
formulation uses Lagrangian coordinates for the boundaries and Eulerian coordinates
for the uid domain. This works by simply dening the Eulerian nodes inside the
reference conguration of the boundaries which are dened in the Lagrangian formu-
lation resulting in two coordinate systems. When the boundaries deform the positions
of the Eulerian nodes are updated using a Laplace smoothing operation. As example
let x and y be spatial coordinates of the spatial frame and X and Y be the reference
coordinates of the material frame. For the transient case the Laplace equation is then
dened as:

Deformed Geometry is a COMSOL interface for handling mesh movement. In other
words it solves for the material coordinates. COMSOL has another interface called
Moving Mesh Interface which is less useful for this type of simulation because Mov-
ing Mesh Interface views the domain as a solid. The Deformed Geometry Interface
views deformation as addition or removal of material, which is the case for this type
of simulation. Another choice is the FSI Interface which is able to calculate interac-
tions between solids and uids. As the dependent variables for the ALE coordinates
and the uid only appear inside their own interface (thereby their own PDEs) it is
not necessary to use the FSI Interface. The Deformed Geometry Interface uses the
ALE formulation for handling deformation of the boundary and material frame. The
following denitions are made in the Deformed Geometry Interface:

4.0.5
Study
All results are obtained from the same study setup which is briey described for
reproducibility.
The study type is a time dependent study which solves the time
instances from t = 0 to t = Tperiod∗nCycles with the step size tStep. The parameters
are all dened in the model parameters for convenience (see appendix B). The study
solves for all physics dened, meaning both the mesh coordinates from the prescribed
mesh deformation in the Deformed Geometry Interface and the pressure function and
velocity eld in the Laminar Flow Interface. Neither mesh renement or remeshing is
used. The solver is a time dependent solver with time stepping done by the backwards
dierentiation formula (BDF) method with minimum order 1 and maximum order 2.
The time dependent solver calls a segregated solver which splits the solution, during
calculation, into two groups; one for ALE and one for laminar ow. Both groups
calls a direct solver; ALE calls MUMPS with default settings and laminar ow calls
PARDISO with default settings.[6]
4.0.6
Plotting results
There are many tools for visualizing the study solutions. COMSOL has several mod-
ules that lets the user customize plots from a GUI interface. The plots can be exported
as images, sequences of images or videos in several formats such as .gif. The video
format is useful for identifying transient events but is unfortunately not useful at all
for printing on paper. Most of the plot types in the results (chapter 5) are standard
plots with few modications. One of the plot types does however require a brief ex-
planation as a custom plot metric is created for visualizing vortex rings at the inlet.
The idea of the metric is to use the knowledge that the inlet is in the center of the
cylindrical coordinate system. Vortex rings from the inlet can therefore be quantied
by direction and magnitude of vorticity and shear rate. What the plot metric should
quantify is in other words where the magnitude of vorticity in the (ˆφ) direction is high
and the shear rate low. The low shear rate helps identifying the center of the vorticity.
The simplest way of dening the plot metric, a fraction, shows to be sucient. The
method is as follows:
Create a volume plot from the unitless metric:

(4.1)
where ω is vorticity and ˙γ is shear rate. The reason to add one to the denominator
is to avoid dividing with zero.
The vortex rings are now visualized by applying a manual data range to the volume
plot.

Chapter 6
Discussion
6.1
Results interpretation
The EF from the case simulation (40%) is lower than expected in vivo (55-70%).
However the EV is within normal range which consequently places CO in normal
range as well. The low EF could be due to the inclusion of the inlet cylinder as part
of the LV domain volume. It can be seen from data in table 5.1 that EF is aected by
the input parameters which indicates that it is indeed possible to simulate movement
that are within the ranges of human physiology. For the scope of this thesis there is
no need for comparing the ow and pressure patterns quantitatively with literature
and known physiology. They are however still plotted in gure 5.4 and 5.5 where
features such as vortex ring formation and inlet jet look similar to the literature. [2],
[1], [13]. In gure 5.4(D-F) it is clear that the parameters chosen in this model results
in regurgitation which is not wanted.
6.2
Limitations and considerations
As mentioned there are several limitations to the created model. In this section the
consequences and possible solutions to model assumptions in this thesis are discussed.
The inlet is dened as a cylinder which obviously a simplication. A consequence
of this is a rigidity that is not dened in vivo. The inlet velocity prole is assumed
at which is a fair assumption but leads to diculties with the no slip boundary
condition at the inlet cylinder walls (visible in gure 5.4(E)). This may be corrected
by either applying a velocity inlet prole that has zero velocity at the boundary or
simply dening a slip boundary for the inlet cylinder. This is an acceptable solution
because the inlet cylinder is not part of the domain of interest. The inlet radius is not
dened from any measure from the anatomy of which the LV is segmented which is an
unknown in the model. The time dependency in the model is very crudely simplied
by sine square functions which is not sucient for representing true physiology. The
obvious solution is to use other time dependent functions. These could be derived from
physiological data. The time dependency of the movement creates another problem
which is the inlet and outlet conditions. In the best case scenario the inlet and outlet
conditions are dened from an interpolation of US data. This is however not ideal
for the inlet velocity as the inlet volume ow has to be matched with the volume
change of LV. A possible solution is, instead of dening inlet from US data, to simply
dene the inlet velocity to match the volume change of LV as done in [14].
The
problem is for now solved by allowing ow over the outlet during diastole which is
denitely a limitation (visible in gure 5.4(E-F)). Describing the inlet velocity as a
sine function also completely removes the eect of atrial contraction that creates a
peak in velocity during end diastole. This potentially causes the vortex rings from
the inlet to be under represented. The computation time could possibly be improved
==PAGE:58==
40

by meshing more manually which was not done in this project. Another potential
problem with the mesh is that it is not truly periodic meaning that all nodes are not
returned exactly to the reference conguration after one cycle. This leads to poorer
cycle convergence than otherwise obtainable and longer computation as a periodic
mesh could be reused for all cycles. For unknown reasons only COMSOL version 5.2a
is able to run the simulations which makes the denition of the no slip wall condition
more complicated than necessary as COMSOL version 5.3 has built in tools for this.
Furthermore laminar ow is assumed which does not show turbulent ow features.
6.3
Future work
Being able to show that this 3D is more true to reality than a similar 2D model would
be of value. As mentioned above there are many parameters that can be tweaked in
a general parameter study for optimizing the robustness of the model. The result of
dening inlet as volume ow calculated from LV volume change would be interesting.
In general the model is easily modied to run with other styles boundary conditions
such as in [14] and [1]. Visualization can be improved by implementing new methods
for visualizing vortex rings such as [9] or iso surfaces of pressure and vorticity as applied
in [14]. Lastly the ambition with this particular model is to prescribe movement from
cardiac CT scans which requires 3D volume registration and specialized morphing
techniques. Implementing this is the obvious next step as it also solves the inlet/outlet
issues discussed above.

7.1
Conclusion
In this project a framework for modeling LV has been successfully developed and
demonstrated.
The model is able to solve FE CFD from several combinations of
movement parameters showing that the model is adaptable to better simulate specic
cases when needed.
The model can produce temporal 3D velocity elds that, in
some features, show equivalence with in vivo measurements. Therefore this model is
considered to serve as rst proof of concept. Based on the results further work on
connecting the inlet ow with the LV volume changes may further improve the model.
