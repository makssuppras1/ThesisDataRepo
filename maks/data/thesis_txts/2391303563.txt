Abstract
In order to provide the best QoS to network applications, it is crucial to know the class of application that
generated the traﬃc. Traditionally, this has been done using port-based or DPI-based solutions by classifying
individual packets. However, these techniques suﬀer from being too simple, too rigid or too computationally
expensive. To improve the performance of classiﬁcation, current research rely on ﬂows rather than individ-
ual packets. Rather than using lookup tables or registers to ﬁnd an associated class, most current research
utilises machine learning techniques to group ﬂows into relevant classes. In this thesis, machine learning
algorithms are used on a ﬂow-based dataset to classify networking applications. Three algorithms of increas-
ing complexity are used; k-Nearest Neighbours, Random Forest and Neural Network. The models’ ability
to generalise is improved by tuning parameters to minimise the prediction error. The dataset is based on a
series of TCP/IP dumps containing traﬃc from 9 diﬀerent networking applications. The potential applica-
tions of the models in discussed is terms of traﬃc controlling and managing. In particular in combination
with Diﬀerentiated Services and SNMP & sampling based ﬂows.
Results show that the Random Forest classiﬁer performs the best, while the Neural Network performs
the worst. Sample sizes and model complexity are probable causal metrics for the variation in performance.
Modest results indicate that there are grounds for machine learning algorithms in traﬃc classiﬁcation; con-
trolling and managing.

Introduction
Consumers today use a variety of applications that are connected to the Internet, YouTube, Facebook,
BitTorrent, various video games, etc.. The increased usage of such applications, in particular video streaming
services, has increased the consumption of data globally. The total Internet traﬃc for 2017 is expected to be
1,3 zetta bytes - 1,3 trillion gigabytes - and is expected to grow up 2 zetta bytes by 2019 [35]. The enormous
increase in data consumption results in equally high demand for bandwidth. Not all applications have the
same requirements for service Quality of Service (QoS), that is, gaming may be sensitive to latency, but not
require a lot of bandwidth, while video streaming services, such as YouTube or Netﬂix, can deal with latency,
but not with low bandwidth. Knowing which traﬃc was created from which types of applications/services
could help provide the best QoS for that traﬃc. And knowing the type of traﬃc could help better utilising
network resources.
Classiﬁcation of traﬃc relies on identiﬁcation of patterns in data packets. Speciﬁcally, the process relies
on the extraction of certain ﬁelds in various protocol layer headers. Traditionally this meant that traﬃc
classiﬁcation was done on an packet individual level, implemented in schemes such as port-based classiﬁcation.
Figure 1: Header ﬁelds used in traditional port based classiﬁcation.
10
==PAGE:12==
The prominent techniques in current research relies on the collection of traﬃc ﬂows. Most techniques
vary in their deﬁnition, but all of them rely on matching the source and destination address and ports across
packets. The collection of ﬂows allows the derivation of more traﬃc speciﬁc behaviour. For the purpose
of accuracy in traﬃc classiﬁcation, utilising ﬂows is a good thing, however, it also introduces timing issues
for traﬃc controlling. Classiﬁcation should happen from the beginning of the ﬂow and not the end. As
such, much of the process relies on the right way to collect data in a timely manner. Timing is particularly
important for traﬃc shaping and policing, but less so for analysis.
Many analysis tools in large scale
networks rely on packet sampling techniques, rather than the collection of full ﬂows, and tries to infer ﬂow
characteristics. This requires less storage and memory, but does not necessarily yield the best data foundation
for ﬁne grained classiﬁcation.
Relative location of classiﬁers and data collection modules consequently
becomes another important aspect, particularly in when performing traﬃc conditioning.
Some of these
issues are addressed in well known architectures such as IntServ (IS) and DiﬀServ (DS), but requires more
exploration with the application of machine learning.
1.1
State of the art
There are three overarching approaches to Internet traﬃc classiﬁcation.
A brief description is given on
1) port-based classiﬁcation and 2) Deep Packet Inspection (DPI)-based classiﬁcation. The primary section
of the state of the art will be on 3) ﬂow-based classiﬁcation - the approach used in machine learning initiatives.
The initial approach to traﬃc classiﬁcation utilises TCP/UDP port numbers for application inference.
Classifying strictly based on port numbers is inﬂexible making it fragile to 0-day-applications, tunnelled
applications and applications using randomised port numbers. Both the DPI and ﬂow-based approaches are
more dynamic in their classiﬁcation and incorporate more information into their decision making.
DPI gives access to packet details for improved application identiﬁcation. This is often done with reg-
ular expressions to identify application ﬁngerprints[36]. Consequently there needs to a large database of
up-to-date application ﬁngerprints. Due to to the increasing amount of 0-day-applications it is increasingly
diﬃcult to maintain such a registry[36]. As well as suﬀering from the same issues as the port-based approach
(perhaps to a lesser degree), DPI faces a range of other problems. It must know application signatures
beforehand (some may need to be reverse engineered[8]), encryption and privacy. And, as a last mention,
inspection of each package is computationally costly.
Flow-based is the Machine Learning approach. These methods rely on patterns in message exchanges and
packet metrics (e.g. packet size, total length of bytes, etc.) to separate traﬃc ﬂows into distinct application
groups. Contrary to DPI, ﬂow-based approaches aim to utilise behaviour to infer the application, which
provides a more robust classiﬁcation. That is, classiﬁcation will be resilient to encrypted and tunnelled
11
==PAGE:13==
traﬃc. Another signiﬁcant advantage of ﬂow-based classiﬁcation is the reduced computational complexity
over DPI.
Satoh, Akihiro et al[24] proposed a solution based on subﬂows for a more dynamic classiﬁcation. Contrary
to prior classiﬁcation solutions commonly based on initial the packets of a ﬂow, their solution is an attempt
to classify on various segments of the full ﬂow. This is particularly relevant in environments where the initial
subﬂow is not captured, namely mobile IP networks. Moving between access points breaks up the ﬂows of
traﬃc into smaller segments, making classiﬁcation based on the initial ﬂow less robust. Instead subﬂows are
constructed from transition points in application behaviour, e.g. IMAP mail fetching. The rationale for this
being that there is a predeﬁned sequence of messages exchanged at a behaviour transition for each application
making class separation easier. It is also expected that such behaviour transitions will occur multiple times
over the lifetime of ﬂows. The results of highlights an interesting problem associated with mobile IP networks.
Traﬃc ﬂows will be broken up and that requires a robust classiﬁcation scheme able to predict on various ﬂow
segments. Another suggestion for Internet traﬃc classiﬁcation through subﬂows(albeit with a with a diﬀerent
deﬁnition of subﬂow from the above) was given by Xie, Guowu et al[37]. The incentive is to overcome issues
experienced in traditional supervised and semi-supervised methods, i.e, inability to adapt to classes outside
the trained data (zero-day-applications), low accuracy and suﬀering from the curse of dimensionality. A
subset of features per application helps reduce the computational complexity incurred by high-dimensional
datasets. Thus, the features used to detect one application ﬁngerprint may not be the same as another. The
intuition behind, is that some features are more descriptive of some applications over others and therefore,
when a feature describes little/no variance of the application class, that feature can be omitted. Tongaonkar,
Alok et al[36] developed the SANTaClass classiﬁer which composes major contributions over prior schemes
and industry DPI classiﬁcation standards. The system works in real time and is able to automatically create
new application signatures that can be used for classifying future traﬃc. The whole process is based on
thresholds. Each ﬂow is evaluated against each known class of traﬃc, and the most likely class is selected.
If none of the calculated probabilities cross a certain threshold, the ﬂow is put into temporary classes by a
clustering algorithm. The unknown classes then have to be manually labelled later. Bujlow, Tomasz et al[4]
presents a classiﬁcation method using the C5.0 decision tree algorithm intended to determine satisfactory
values for QoS parameters for 7 application classes. Data collection for the test setup was done via a volunteer
system. Users volunteered to have client software installed on their device to collect traﬃc. This way data
ﬂows could be associated with applications to establish ground truth labels. The system was designed to
classify ﬂows at arbitrary times in their lifetime. Furthermore, ﬂows are classiﬁable from small representative
segments - some ﬁxed byte length, amount of seconds or number of packets. The best results are acquired
from C5.0 boosting although other conﬁgurations of the C5.0 algorithm produce very similar results.

1.2
Scope
The goal of this thesis is to explore the application of Machine Learning algorithms in traﬃc classiﬁcation
for traﬃc management. That is, how does traﬃc classiﬁcation using machine learning techniques ﬁt in, in
terms of (i) traﬃc analysis and (ii) traﬃc conditioning. The implemented algorithms aim to predict classes of
network applications based on TCP dumps, giving an indication of feasibility for application distinction based
on both the algorithms and the available data. The datasets are based on simple metrics in packet protocol
headers in relation to their associated traﬃc ﬂows, hence, it is not necessary to query external lookup sources
for model training. Following these results, the integration of a classiﬁer with existing network management
tools is discussed - speciﬁcally Simple Network Management Protocol (SNMP) and DiﬀServ (DS).

Machine learning techniques
Machine Learning encompasses a wide range of methods that allow machines to learn from a dataset. With
the ever increasing availability of data the need to make sense and learn from it becomes equally important.
Machine learning aims to learn patterns in data and to use the learnt patterns to predict new samples of
data. This chapter introduces the branches of machine learning and basic theory on general concepts and
3 widely used algorithms. The main focus of machine learning in this thesis will be based on supervised
learning and classiﬁcation. The three included algorithms are thus described with that intention in mind.
2.1
Types of machine learning
Machine learning is often ordered in a taxonomy of three.
These are supervised learning, unsupervised
learning and reinforcement learning. Each of the categories have scenarios in which they are more suitable
and as such none is universally better than the other.
• supervised learning: Inducing a function which maps input to output, which can in turn be used as a
general function to map new input to output.
• unsupervised learning: The algorithm does not have a notion of the correct categorisation beyond what
it infers from its input data. It tries to divide inputs into groups, segmenting the data.
• reinforcement learning: Performing several tasks without any feedback until all tasks have been per-
formed, also known as delayed feedback. Based on these trial and errors the algorithm tries to optimise
for the highest possible reward.

Dataset
There are many potential pitfalls when selecting and processing a dataset for machine learning. Datasets
can be skewed disproportionally to represent more of one class or some classes may just not be represented
at all. For instance, imagine we wanted to train a model to recognise apartment buildings. If the apartment

the algorithm is trained on a dataset which does not contain suﬃcient examples of non-apartment buildings

TCP/IP traﬃc conditioning
Today’s Internet is perhaps one of the biggest engineered systems ever made. The Internet is made up of
millions of connected devices and communication links. This chapter explores the diﬀerent layers of the
TCP/IP protocol stack, traﬃc queueing, shaping & policing and the network management protocol SNMP.
3.1
TCP/IP protocol stack
The TCP/IP protocol stack is made up of 5 layers as can be seen from ﬁgure 13. Here is a brief outline of
responsibility of each layer in the stack.
• In the application layer resides communication protocols for network applications.
An application
layer protocol enables user applications to communicate over a network. All applications on separate
end systems that wish to exchange information must utilise the same protocol to establish a common
ground for communicating. There are numerous application layer protocols, and constantly more being
added. Well known protocols are Hyper Text Transfer Protocol (HTTP), Simple Mail Transfer Protocol
(SMTP), File Transfer Protocol (FTP) and Domain Name Service (DNS). Note that an application
layer protocol is not linked with a speciﬁc end user application. Many end user applications may be
using the same type of application layer protocol [3].
• The transport layer is a host-to-host-oriented layer. When an application layer message (data and
protocol header from the application layer) is received by the transport layer protocol, it encapsulates
the message and builds a new transport layer protocol header on top - the whole thing is called a
segment. This layer is home of the Transfer Control Protocol (TCP) and User Datagram Protocol
(UDP) protocols. The TCP protocol provides a connection-oriented service with reliable data transfer,
ﬂow control and congestion control. Contrary to TCP, UDP is a bare bones connection-less protocol
that provides none of those features[10]. As TCP is the most prominent of the two protocols, we will
explore the TCP segment brieﬂy. Figure 14a shows the various header ﬁelds in a TCP segment. The
port number is used in multiplexing and demultiplexing data to direct it to the correct application.
Many application layer protocols use a standard port number, e.g. 80 for HTTP, 25 for SMTP and
28
==PAGE:30==
53 for DNS. The sequence and acknowledgement numbers are closely related; the sequence number
indicates the order of packets while the acknowledgement number indicates the most recently acknowl-
edged sequence number. The receive window is used to for ﬂow control purposes, i.e., to avoid buﬀer
overﬂow at the receiver The internet checksum is used to verify the integrity of the segment at the
receiver. The SYN, RST and FIN ﬂags are used in establishing and terminating connections. The SYN
bit is used in TCP’s 3 way handshake for connection establishment wherein initial values for client and
server sequence numbers are also exchanged. The RST is used to indicate that a requested port is not
open for service. And lastly, the FIN bit is used for tearing down a connection.
• The network layer is the layer responsible for navigating packets across the Internet. The Internet’s
protocol is the Internet Protocol (IP). A common analogue for the network layer is the postal service,
as both are in charge of package delivery from source to destination, i.e., which route to take to the
destination. The IP header contains addressing information, as a postal package would, such as source
and destination address in the form of an 32bit IPv4-address. The network layer is therefore also the
highest layer of operation for networking devices, i.e., switches and routers. As was the case when
moving a message to the transport layer, the IP encapsulates a transport layer segment in a datagram.
However, there are cases where a segment may be fragmented into several IP datagrams. This happens
when diﬀerent link layer mediums supports diﬀerent sized frames (link layer packets)[10]. Figure 14b
shows the format of an IPv4 datagram.
• The data link layer is responsible of packet delivery on a per link basis, i.e., from node to the next
hop node. The most common link layer protocols are Ethernet and Wiﬁ. During a packets traversal
through a network it may come across several diﬀerent link layer protocols. Each of the protocols
provide diﬀerent services, e.g., some may implement reliable data transfer while others may not, and
there may support diﬀerent frame sizes[10].
• Physical layer The physical layer handles the transmitting packets on bit level. That is, how bits are
transferred from one network node to the next. Depending on the physical material being used in the
link, the protocols for encoding, decoding and transmitting the bits will change[10].

3.2.2
Multiple classes of service
Diﬀerent classes of traﬃc beneﬁt from diﬀerent speciﬁc QoS. This problem has been attempted addressed in
many ways, in particular, services such as DiﬀServ (DS) and IntServ (IS) try diﬀerent QoS parameters for
diﬀerent types of ﬂows. DS has an 8 bit dedicated IP header ﬁeld (ﬁgure 15), which supersedes the ToS IP
header ﬁeld, to indicate which class of traﬃc is being sent, allowing DiﬀServ (DS)-capable routers to treat
the traﬃc according to its class label. IS is a more detailed version of DiﬀServ. IS, contrary to DS classes,
treat traﬃc on a per ﬂow basis, i.e., each ﬂow of traﬃc can reserve resources at routers in the network. IS
management is thus signiﬁcantly more demanding than DS and does not rely on traﬃc classiﬁcation directly.
3.2.3
IntServ
As mentioned, IntServ is a the more complex version of IntServ and DiﬀServ. IntServ aims to manage
traﬃc on a ﬂow level basis, by utilising resource reservation and admission control. This is achievable by
implementing ﬂow statefulness in routers, but in turn compromises the idea that only end-systems should
31
==PAGE:33==
contain ﬂow state information [27]. IntServ capable routers must implement a service called traﬃc control,
which is comprised of three components.
• Packet scheduler.
The packet scheduler handles the departure of packets, similar to a queueing
mechanism[27].
• Classiﬁer. Each packet is mapped to a class and all packet of the same class are provided the same
QoS. A packet is not necessarily mapped to the same class in all routers; as such, nearby routers may
map one ﬂow to one class, while backbone routers may map multiple ﬂows to one class. The eﬃciency
of the classiﬁcation is of vital importance to the performance to provide the correct QoS in a timely
fashion.
• Admission control. As each class requires some resource reservation, the router must be able to ad-
ministrate its resources when new ﬂows request resource reservation.
In order to setup reservations the IntServ scheme utilises the ReSerVation Protocol (RSVP), which is a
signalling protocol capable of coordination per-hop resource allocation[10]. To request resource reservation
setup an application must transmit a list of QoS parameters to the routers’ admission control for clearance,
i.e., is the request servicable. The list of parameters is called a ﬂowspec.
The IntServ thus provide ﬁne grained control over ﬂows, but at the cost of high complexity. Every router
must be capable of classifying data and maintaining ﬂow state information to provide the required QoS[2].
It is also necessary for traﬃc ﬂow to be routed on strictly IntServ capable nodes, else the QoS cannot be
upheld[27].

• Default PHB. The class employs best-eﬀort service, i.e. lowest level of priority. Any packet that arrives
at a DiﬀServ node without a DSCP mapping automatically gets mapped to this PHB.
• Class selector PHB. The pattern ”xxx000” where x ∈1, 2 is reserved for backwards compatibility with
the original ToS IP header ﬁeld, which gave an order of priority when packets were subject to shaping
& policing [2].
• Expedited Forwarding (EF) PHB. Traﬃc that maps to this class must be served at a conﬁgurable
rate. The departure rate from BA nodes must be equal to or greater than the deﬁned rate. The
traﬃc should be treated without regard of all other classes, i.e., other traﬃc should not inﬂuence the
departure rate of EF traﬃc[31]. Another important aspect of the Expedited Forwarding (EF) PHB
class is to provide a solution with low loss, latency and jitter. These issues commonly arise with queue
forming, which occurs when the arrival rate of traﬃc exceeds the departure rate. Implementation of a
queueing scheme, such as WFQ, at the BA can execute this behaviour.
• Assured Forwarding (AF) PHB. The standard AF PHB is implemented as 4 classes with 3 tiers of
packet drop precedence (see table 1). Each class is allocated a certain amount of buﬀer space and
bandwidth at DiﬀServ nodes. If congestion occurs for some class and packets needs to dropped, they
will do so in order of precedence, i.e., dropping from the High tier ﬁrst[30][2]. It is suggested to use an
active queue management system such as Random Early Detection (RED) in combination with AF by
[30]. RED utilises a probabilistic dropping mechanism based on how full the buﬀer is [18].

3.2.5
Network management
A crucial role in optimising network performance is network management. Network management includes,
among others, monitoring and conﬁguration of hardware and software in a network. The following sections
will introduce network management in terms of the Simple Network Management Protocol (SNMP) standard.
Network management architecture
A network management architecture is comprised of four key components; a managing entity, managed
devices, a management protocol and management information[10]. The managing entity is software running
on centralised network monitoring hardware. It manages the collection and conﬁguration of management
information.
Managed devices are routers and switches including their software.
There may be several
managed objects within a managed device, e.g. network interface cards. For each managed object there
exists associated information contained in a Management Information Base (MIB). A managed device also
includes a network management agent which handles communication with the management device. Lastly, a
management protocol enables the querying and conﬁguration of managed devices from the managing device.
Managed devices may also utilise the protocol to to alert the management device of unexpected incidents
such as hardware or software failure.
The Internet standard management framework SNMPv3
The following section is comprised of four categories encompassing the SNMPv3 architecture[32]. The four
categories are:
• Data deﬁnition language, also known as the Structured Management Information (SMI), is the struc-
tured language used for deﬁning types and formats of management objects residing in the MIB. It also
details the rules for writing and altering of objects[32][10].
• MIB modules holds the managed objects describing the current state of the network. The managing
entity can query and/or edit object values remotely by via the network management agents associated
with managed devices. Objects can contain information on hardware identity and status, physical
location, protocols, etc..
• Protocol operations.
The operations of the application layer SNMP is conducted using 4 types of
messages[26].

– SetRequest is used to modify one or more object values. A response message containing the status

Discussion
This section will discuss the application of the implemented model; where in a network it can be used and to
what purpose. To form a better understanding of packet capturing and ﬂow deﬁnition, we investigate and
compare popular network monitoring and analytics tools. Lastly, dimensionality reduction will be a subject

6.1
Packet sampling and ﬂow deﬁnition
There are several network monitoring tools that can be deployed in various types of networks. With increasing
network complexity and volumes of traﬃc, the need for eﬃcient sampling techniques and ﬂow deﬁnitions
becomes crucial for network monitoring and analysis.
In high volume networks it becomes infeasible to analyse all traversing network packets, it is therefore
more reasonable to take samples of data and try to infer the true characteristics from them[9]. Sampling
can be deﬁned in terms of sampling time or sampling volume. That is, one second of sampling for every T
seconds (1/T) or one packet sampled for every N packets (1/N). Additionally, it must deﬁned how large
each sample should be. Assume that traﬃc is divided into buckets of size k × T or k × N. Within that
bucket interval, a sample of k seconds/packets must be taken. By setting the bucket size, we also determine
the time between samples[5]. Figure 28 illustrates an example of this setup.

dicting average packets size and IAT. The dataset used is from a They experiment with sampling rates of

The consequences of choosing a large bin size is that short term ﬂows could be missed entirely by the
sampling algorithm. That is, the time between samples may be big enough that a ﬂow may be initiated and
completed between two samples. Depending on the amount of traﬃc through a router there may also be a
need for randomising the sampling process. In high volume routers it is more likely that traﬃc ﬂows will
have their packets interleaved with each other and thus ensures low bias to any particular ﬂows. However,
this may not be the case for lower volume routers where fewer ﬂows dominate the volume and the ﬂow
interleavedness may be signiﬁcantly less random. A possibility in this case can be to randomise the sampling
in each bin, i.e. not start sampling at the same packet count in each bin. Another option is to increase the
sampling volume and frequency.
Sampling the packets also means that ﬂow initialisation and termination are less likely to be captures, e.g.,
TCP’s 3-way handshake (using SYN ﬂag) and connection termination using FIN ﬂag process. Capturing
ﬂow setup and termination will also produce more reliable characteristics such as mean packet size and
IAT[9]. As such, we might force sampling of packets when the SYN or FIN is set. Lower sampling rates
in high volume routers does drop ﬂow granularity and it may be harder to determine when a ﬂow has
terminated. Some value for ﬂow timeout, with respect to the bin size, should be implemented to separate
an old ﬂow from a new ﬂow. If the timeout value is less than the bin size all ﬂows will timeout between
samples, which might not be ideal for long running connections, frequent in streaming, VOIP and Peer-2-Peer
applications[9]. Ultimately, we will have an overestimation of ﬂow volume. It is possible that diﬀerentiating
the timeout interval based on the application layer protocol could improve ﬂow volume accuracy.
Accurately measuring ﬂow characteristics such as the number of packets, mean packet size and IAT can
be diﬃcult when using subsets of ﬂows. According to [22], the minimum number of samples for a class to
estimate the true amount of class occurrences should be ≈1000. If there is an insuﬃcient amount of data,
increasing the sample rate or looking at samples over a longer time period can improve accuracy.
There are several monitoring tools that can help analyse traﬃc through packet sampling and ﬂow con-
struction. As an example, Sample Flow (sFlow)[25] is widely adopted packet sampling network monitoring
tool. SNMP also has an MIB object for management of sFlow nodes from an SNMP managing entity.

In order to determine the optimal point of implementation for a classiﬁcation model we need to deﬁne
its purpose. There two primary applications; traﬃc analysis and traﬃc controlling. The two applications
may be implemented in diﬀerent settings; a home network, a corporate network or a ISP core network. Each
combination of application and point of implementation induces diﬀerent requirements. It is clear that the
collection of traﬃc ﬂows is more complete in networks of smaller scale, but that the training of a model
is more attractive with a larger dataset. Traﬃc shaping and policing (traﬃc control) performs better the
earlier the classiﬁcation is implemented. That is, if classiﬁcation does not happen before, e.g., a DS domain,
then that traﬃc will be classiﬁed into a default best-eﬀort class. For analysis, the classiﬁcation can happen
before or in the same network domain as the analyser. Another interesting diﬀerence is that analysis is not
as sensitive to the delay induced by collecting complete ﬂows of traﬃc before classiﬁcation. Traﬃc control
needs the classiﬁcation of packets in order to apply the correct shaping and policing, but the classiﬁcation
needs ﬂows before labelling packets. A possible scheme to circumvent this paradox is illustrated in ﬁgure 29.
Traﬃc ﬂows from some domain (all traﬃc is processed, i.e., no sampling) to its egress router. The egress
router is responsible for keeping track of ﬂows and for classifying them. Once a suﬃcient amount of traﬃc
arrives at the DS ingress node, it can train/update a classiﬁer based on a set of predeﬁned DSCP classes.
Once the model has been trained it is fed back to the connected domain egress routers to classify ﬂows. As
the ﬂows are built up over time, domain egress routers should update their classiﬁcation of ongoing ﬂows
routinely. The longer the ﬂow the more likely that the classiﬁcation is correct. Traﬃc arriving at the ingress
DS node is then subject to class appropriate traﬃc conditioning. Training and updating the model still
requires manual labelling of data to, however, automation steps could be employed by implementing some
classiﬁcation probability thresholds to ﬁnd new types of traﬃc as was done in [36]. Regardless, building the
initial model will be cumbersome.

of granularity compared to the very speciﬁc classes used in the implementation chapter. E.g., applications
such as YouTube and Ted provide a very similar service and likely need the same QoS. It would therefore
be advantageous to group such applications/service under the same higher level class of traﬃc, e.g. ”video

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to predict classes of Internet traﬃc using machine learning based on simple met-
rics attainable from packet headers. And subsequently, to discuss feasibility of machine learning classiﬁers
in traﬃc management.
The performance of the kNN, NNET and RF algorithms was tested on the same 486 ﬂows extracted
from the Crawdad datasets.
The dataset had 9 application classes and consisted of 16 derived features
primarily based on packet sizes and arrival times. Of the three algorithms, RF showed the most promising
results with ≈64% accuracy and ≈68% precision. kNN performed second best with ≈51% accuracy and
≈49% precision and NNET performed the worst ≈10% accuracy and ≈6% precision. The extremely poor
performance of the NNET algorithm is likely due to high complexity (many tuneable parameters) and due
to the relatively small sample size of 54 per class making it unable to learn the underlying patterns.
The application of a machine learning classiﬁer in terms of traﬃc monitoring and controlling was then
discussed. The process of collecting ﬂows introduces timing constraints to the classiﬁcation. Due to delay
sensitivity in traﬃc controlling, a scheme that could mitigate timing issues was proposed. The proposed
scheme combined the DS architecture with the steps required to build a classiﬁer and classify traﬃc. For the
purpose of monitoring, the architecture of SNMP and packet-based sampling was discussed. Packet based
ﬂow collection works well in high volume networks, where it is infeasible to collect all ﬂow data. However,
this setup comes at the cost of traﬃc class transparency - less detailed datasets will likely produce less
accurate classiﬁcation.
7.1
Future work
Future work includes the development of a speciﬁc set of application classes for improved development of a
representative dataset. This is both to improve the possible separation of traﬃc classes and to enable the
inclusion of more complex machine learning algorithms such as neural networks. Additionally, experiments
in this thesis have all been conducted locally on a single computer, a logical follow up would be to implement
a classiﬁer into an experimental network setup.
