ABSTRACT 
In the last two decades, there has been a rapid growth in the amount of distributed gen-
eration largely based on renewable sources connected to the power system. Wind power, 
as one of the main sources of renewable energy, has gained popularity worldwide. Re-
cent development in wind energy sector suggests that wind generation system with full 
power converter as one of the dominated type not only because of its high efficiency, 
but also because the complete control of the power transfer process improve the grid 
capability.  
The full converter permanent magnet synchronous generator (PMSG) based wind tur-
bine is modelled in detail. Special focus were given to the low voltage ride through ca-
pability of full converter wind turbine. The grid fault ride through strategies are pro-
posed and verified with European Network of Transmission System Operators for Elec-
tricity (ENTSO-E) grid code. 
Overcurrent protection schemes for radial network assume single direction current flow, 
however this is no longer available when the distributed generation is introduced. This 
thesis focuses on evaluating the inverter-based distributed generation (low fault current 
contribution), particularly, full converter wind turbine on radial overcurrent protection. 
A realistic small radial system and its overcurrent protection scheme are developed in 
Real Time Digital Simulator (RTDS), real time digital simulator. The main study is car-
ried out with the aggregated model of wind farm that a single equivalent unit represents 
the entire wind farm. The performance of the overcurrent protection after the presence 
of full converter wind turbines is finally analysed.

INTRODUCTION 
 
1.1 
Wind energy 
Recently, under the pressure of fossil energy crisis and the environmental protection 
concerns, there is an increasing demand of renewable energy in the worldwide. One of 
the global challenges is the urgent need for more reliable, clean and secure sources of 
electricity. Wind power is now the fastest growing source of electricity generation 
around the world due to its benefits of pollution-free and cost-effective nature. The 
global cumulative wind power capacity from 1997 to 2013 is shown in Figure 1.1 
 
Figure 1.1 Total global installed wind power capacity [2] 
As can be seen in this figure, the wind power capacity is rapidly growing from 7.48GW 
in 1997 to 318.529GW in 2013, in countries like Denmark and Spain the wind power 
provide the total consumption with 35% and 20% share respectively, referred to year 
2014. This trend is expected to continue with falling costs of technology, energy securi-
ty concerns and the need to address environmental issues [1][2]. The wind turbine can 
be generally classified as fixed-speed wind turbines and variable-speed wind turbines 
based on whether the operation speed is controllable. The main drawback of fixed-speed 
wind turbine is that the operation speed cannot be controlled with the fluctuations of 
wind speed, which results in lower energy conversion efficiency. With rapid develop-
ment of power converters, the variable-speed wind turbines are the most used type in 
new wind turbine installations around the world, double-fed induction generator (DFIG) 
with reduced-capacity power converters and full converter permanent synchronous gen-
erator (PMSG) wind turbines are the most popular ones that almost dominate the market

nowadays. For DFIG type, the stator circuit is connected directly to the grid and the 
rotor circuit is connected to the grid using a pair of back-to-back pulse width modulated 
voltage source converters, however only 30% of the rated power is transferred through

capability to control the speed of the generator [3]. As large-scale wind turbines have 
draw great attention nowadays, the direct-drive PMSG based wind energy conversion

1.2 
Full converter wind turbine concept 
Full converter wind turbine, commonly referred as type D wind turbine is one of the 
most popular wind turbine concept and increasingly penetrating the market. The main 
structure of variable speed wind turbine with multipole permanent magnet synchronous 
generator (PMSG) and full-scale power converter is shown in figure 1.2 
 
Figure 1.2 Variable speed multipole PMSG wind turbine configuration [4] 
It can be seen from figure 1.2 that type D wind turbine can be either geared or gearless, 
the benefit of connecting to the turbine without gearbox is significantly reducing the 
construction, operation, and maintenance costs [5]. The gearless solution is very benefi-
cial for offshore and rural area applications. Moreover, because of the permanent mag-
net excitation of the generator the excitation system can be removed which again reduc-
ing the weight, costs and maintenance requirements [6]. The generator is connected to 
the power grid through full-scale back-to-back converter as shown in figure 1.2, the 
output power of the generator is fed into the converter and the converter converts the 
varying generator frequency to the constant grid frequency [7]. Hence this converter 
must be rated at the rated power of the wind turbine generator, making it the most ex-
pensive wind turbine concept to manufacture. The primary function of the generator 
side converter is to control the speed of the generator to achieve the maximum power 
extraction from the wind, thus the operating speed range of type D wind turbine is larger 
than the DFIG configuration. The main objective of the grid side control is to control 
the DC-link voltage by feeding the active power into the grid, moreover it can inde-

pendently control the active and reactive power injection by using voltage orientation 
control method. The advantage and advanced features of type D wind turbine can be 
realized by a more complicated control system and the main tasks of the control systems 
are to 
• Control the electrical power extracted from the wind power captured by the blades 
using maximum power tracking scheme.  
• Limit the power capture in high wind speeds by reducing the power coefficient of the

1.3 
LVRT requirement 
Before 2003, there were no specific requirements from utility grids for a low voltage 
ride through (LVRT) performance of wind turbines. Thus, wind turbines were allowed 
to be disconnected in case of a fault in the network. However, the immediate disconnec-
tion of large wind power plants would put additional stress on the already troubled sys-
tem. In order to guarantee the power system stability as long as the increasing penetra-
tion of wind power, transmission system operators set grid connection requirement often 
know as “ grid codes “ for wind generators. The fault ride through capability of modern 
wind turbines addresses primarily the ability of remaining grid connected and providing 
active and reactive power support during grid faults. Typically, this capability specifies 
a voltage against time curve that the wind turbine is not allowed to be disconnected if 
the voltage during and following a fault is settled above a specified value[9]. The FRT 
requirement considered in this thesis is based on ENTSO-E grid code. ENTSO-E re-
quires from each TSO a specification of a voltage-against-time-profile which express 
the lower limit of the voltage at the common point of coupling during a symmetrical 
fault, this profile comprises of a set of parameters for times and voltages within ranges 
defined by the grid code[10]. The FRT requirement defined by ENTSO-E is illustrate in 
figure 1.3 below, one thing need to be emphasized here is that the FRT requirement 
doesn’t require the actual voltage recovery curve to be exactly the shape as in figure 1.3, 
but more aiming to demonstrate a lower limit. Whether the FRT requirement is fulfilled 
can be demonstrated by the test response to exam if the actual voltage profile remains 
above this limit.

1.4 
Protection issues in radial system 
To reduce the greenhouse gas emission, the European policy is to generate 20% of the 
total consumed energy with renewable energy sources by 2020[13]. This leads to an 
increasing demand of distribution generation connected to the power system. Distribu-
tion generation can be generally classified into two categories: inverter based DG and 
rotating machine DG. One of the most important types of inverter based DG, which 
already largely installed in Denmark is wind power and commonly connect to radial 
system. These generations affect the operation and coordination of the distribution grid 
protection, the impact of the converter based DG is different from that of synchronous 
and induction generators. The short-circuit current of a synchronous machine reaches at 
high values at the fault beginning that mainly depends on the sub-transient reactance. 
However, the short circuit current contribution from type D wind turbine is limited by 
its control strategy below 1.2 pu to avoid the damage of the IGBTs, which are sensitive 
to high current[14]. Most of the traditional distribution system operates radially which 
means the power flows from the upper generations to the downstream customers along 
the feeders in a single direction[15]. Protections in such a system are normally based on 
overcurrent relays, which provide a reliable and low cost solution. The assumption of 
single direction power flow is no longer suitable when wind turbines are introduced into 
the radial system. This time, power can flow in both directions, affecting the operation 
and coordination of overcurrent relays. As the size of wind turbine increase, wind power 
generations have the potential to create problems for system protections and the major 
protection issues are: 
• Blinding of protection commonly referred to the failure or delayed operation of the 
relays  
• Sympathetic tripping commonly referred to mal-tripping of a healthy feeder. 
Blinding of protection occurs when the grid contribution to the short circuit current is 
very low or below the pickup settings of the feeder relay. This is because in radial sys-

1.5 
RTDS-real time digital simulator 
The RTDS simulator was originally developed from a research project done at the Man-
itoba HVDC Research Center in the 1980s. Then in 1994, RTDS Technologies Inc. was 
founded[19]. The RTDS is a special purpose multi-processor computer system that is 
optimized for power system simulations[20]. It is capable of solves electromagnetic 
transients in real time. By using RTDS simulator, a system response over 1 second is 
computed in exactly 1 second and for a given time step of 50 microseconds, all calcula-
tions required to determine the power system state are solved within this time step. 
Wind power generation unit considered in this thesis is type D full power converter 
wind turbine, to properly represent high frequency switching and circuit dynamics, 
RTDS small time step technique is selected, each voltage source converter circuit is 
solved as a sub-network than can be interfaced to the main network solution. The main 
network is solved with a time-step size of 50 microseconds, whereas the VSC circuit is

1.6 
Motivation and goals of the study 
In the last decades, there has been a continuous increase in the amount of DG (primarily 
powered from renewable resources) connected to power system. Modern wind turbines 
require an inverter interface to connect to the system. Grid codes require wind turbines 
remain connected through the whole duration of fault under specific circumstances. This 
requirements force wind turbine feed short circuit current into the grid, which might 
influence the correct operation of the protective relays. Problems introduced in section 
1.4 are commonly associated with the connection of traditional DG unit such as syn-
chronous and induction generators. However, a relatively small amount of study focus 
on if these problems still arise when the inverter based DGs are connected into the sys-
tem. This project aims to evaluate the effect of type D wind turbine during fault on radi-
al system protection. The project goals are summarized as: 
• Analysis and development of the PWM control schemes to accurately control three 
level neutral point clamped converters and general implementation of DC-link voltage 
balancing. 
• Deep analysis and development of an accurate overall dynamic model for type D wind 
turbine and detailed implementation of this model in RTDS. 
==PAGE:17==

9 
• Deep analysis and development of an appropriate fault ride through control schemes 
for both generator side converter and grid side converter, detailed implementation of 
this FRT schemes. 
• Validation of the response of type D wind turbine during fault, this should comply 
with the grid code of ENTSO-E introduced in section 1.3 
• Analysis and detailed modelling of the inverse-time over current relays and the small 
radial system, to see the impact of type D wind turbine during fault on overcurrent relay. 
 
1.7 
Project limitation 
The limitations within this project are state here, the investigation is carried out with 
balanced three-phase fault, it is true that the three-phase fault is not the most frequent 
grid faults, it is the only one considered in this work because: (1) it is often the most 
severe fault condition, (2) it is often assumed that a not cleared fault may develop into a 
three-phase short circuit[22] and (3) quite limit standard requirements for wind turbine 
during unbalanced fault conditions and also a more complex control schemes is need-
ed[23]. The exact parameters of the wind turbine model using in this work is largely 
based on [24]. Parameters for components, such as filter, line impendence and size of 
the load were estimated. All the PI controller parameters were tuned beaded on the re-
sponse of the simulation, no attempt given to detailed calculation of these parameters. 
The whole wind farm is represented by aggregated model of one wind turbine, in chap-
ter 4, the situation that the retained wind turbine voltage fluctuating against 0.9pu is not 
given, because strong oscillation were observed and this case was given in Appendix E 
for interested reading, this phenomena require an more advanced solution to buffer the 
switching between different control modes or require a supplementary reactive power 
compensation. The test system used in this project doesn’t represent any radial system 
in real world, it is chosen based on the purpose of demonstration that if the problem of 
sympathetic tripping and blinding of protection would occur.

2.1 
Three-level-neutral-point-clamped (NPC) voltage-
source-converter (VSC) and sinusoidal pulse width modulation 
technique (SPWM) 
The ongoing development of wind turbine technology indicates a trend towards high 
power rating wind power units. This also indicates that the corresponding electrical ma-
chine and power converters should operate at higher voltage levels to achieve maximum 
efficiency[25]. Multilevel converters are particularly attractive for power rating above 
2-3MVA and the three level neutral point clamped (NPC) converter is the most widely 
used[26][27]. In most cases, the NPC converter would operate in medium voltage lev-
el[28]. One advantage of NPC converter is that the switching device is only need to 
withstand half of the DC-link voltage, another thing need to mention is that it provide 
better output voltage spectrum[27]. The common topology of NPC converter is shown 
in figure 2.1. 
 
Figure 2.1 Three level neutral point clamped converter 
The main characteristic of the three level topologies is that the output voltage from one 
leg of the converter could have three values. Each leg has four IGBTs in series with 
anti-parallel free wheeling diode and also two clamped diodes are connected to the 
physical DC midpoint. There are 6 possible current paths because that there are three 
==PAGE:20==
Modeling of Full Converter PMSG Wind Turbine

voltage levels and two current directions. These possible paths are illustrated in figure 
2.2 below. 
 
Figure 2.2 Current paths for three level neutral point clamped converter 
It can be seen that if the two upper IGBTs are turned on the output voltage is positive 
(+UDC/2). If the two middle IGBTs are switched on the output voltage is 0. If the two 
lower IGBTs are turned on the output voltage is negative (-UDC/2). It needs to be em-
phasized that all these states are independent of the current direction. 
To control the NPC converters, sinusoidal pulse width modulation technique is selected 
in this project. This technique requires a reference signal, which varies sinusoidally, is 
compared with a fixed frequency triangular carrier wave. The easiest way to explain this 
would be start with the classic two level topology, consider figure 2.3 below. 
 
Figure 2.3 Sinusoidal pulse width modulation[29] 
It could be seen that the value of the reference signal is exceeds that of the triangle wave 
then S1 will be turned on, conversely, if the amplitude of the reference signal is less than 
that of the triangle wave then S1 will be turned off. The IGBT S2 will be controlled in a 
complimentary fashion. The signal Vout will be similar to that shown in figure 2.3c.

These converter legs require certain firing pulse input to obtain the desired output wave-
forms. The 4 IGBTs of one converter leg are controlled by 4 consecutive bits in a firing 
pulse word. These 4 bits must aligned in such a way that the least significant of these 4 
bits in the firing pulse word coincide with the least significant bit (LSB) in the final ap-
plied firing pulse word. The LSB controls IGBT1 and the second LSB bit controls 
IGBT2 and so on[29]. Before getting the final firing pulse word for the converter legs, 
the triangle carrier wave and a comparator for the modulating wave and carrier wave 
need to be defined. High resolution firing pulse words need to be produced within the 
small time step through the use of firing pulse generation component[21]. This compo-
nent requires an instantaneous stating point and a rate of change as input. These infor-
mation are obtained from the modulating waves, which the triangle wave is assigned to 
synchronize with. The control schematic that used for gird side converter triangle wave 
generation is shown in figure 2.5 
 
Figure 2.5 19 times PWM phase-locked triangle wave generation

The phase and frequency are obtained from the phase lock loop using the phase voltage 
at the point of common coupling as input. Multiplying the phase signal by 19 and fixing 
it between 0 −2𝜋 yields the associated phase signal for the triangle wave. The non-
linear gain function shown as Y=F(X) in figure 2.5 is defined in such a way that within 
one period of the triangle wave, this wave only reach one time the upper limit which is 1 
pu and the rising up and dropping down rate are the same. The phase signal of the tri-
angle wave is multiplied with 1 2𝜋 to scale the phase to 0-1 which match the X axis of 
the non-linear gain function and the output is used to assign the starting point infor-
mation to the triangle wave generator. The frequency is multiplied with ±38 to give the 
slope information for the triangle wave, ±38 comes from ±19×2 and this is because it 
has 2 segments within one period of the triangle wave due to the selection of single po-
lar PWM, the plus and minus sign indicate the direction of the slope. Then a signal se-
lector is used to select the slope based on whether the starting point is beyond half peri-
od. After the starting point and the slope was determined. The triangle wave generator 
generate the triangle wave through extrapolation and boundary wrapping, if the first hit 
is the upper limit, then the wave goes downward with the slope of -38, and if the first hit 
is the lower limit, the wave will rise up with slope of +38. After the triangle carrier 
wave is carefully designed, the comparator parameters need to be defined to have the

Direct-drive permanent magnet synchronous genera-

Permanent magnet synchronous generator (PMSG) is the key component in type D wind 
turbine because it transforms the mechanical power obtained by the blade into electrical 
power. In this section, the mathematical model of the PMSG is given as well as the 
power and torque analysis. The working principle of permanent magnet synchronous 
generator is very similar to the traditional synchronous machine and this time the field-
ing winding is replaced by permanent magnet. Before having the mathematical model of 
the PMSM, some assumptions need to be made: 
(1) The damping effect in the rotor is negligible, damper resistance is given large values, 
so it is disable[31]. 
(2) The magnetic saturation effects are neglected 
(3) The eddy current and hysteresis losses are neglected[32] 
Figure 2.9 shows the schematic diagram of a three-phase, two pole PMSG. 
 
Figure 2.9 Schematic diagram of a three-phase PMSG[33] 
When the rotor is driven by the prime mover, the permanent magnet rotes in the space at 
speed 𝜔! and this magnetic field cuts the stator windings, thus three voltages which are 
displaced by 120 degree are induced[33]. The fixed abc axis represent the direction of 
the MMFs of phase a, b and c which are induced by the alternating stator current. The 
resultant magnet field is the combination of the magnet field produced by the stator 
windings and the field produced by the permanent excitation. 
Two axis are defined here, one called d-axis which centred at north pole and the other 
one named q-axis, 90 degrees ahead the d-axis. 𝜃! denotes the relative position of the 
rotor with respect to the axis of phase a. the positive direction of the current in stator

The primary task of the generator side converter is to maintain the maximum turbine

2.6 
Fault ride through control schemes 
When a fault occurs in the external grid, the voltage drops at the terminal of the wind 
turbine. The active power transferred to the grid is reduced proportionally to the grid 
voltage dips. However the input power of the wind turbine extracted from the wind can 
not be reduced in the same step, thus a power imbalance immediately occur and the 
voltage of the DC-link will rise up rapidly. Recall equation 2.43 and 2.51 here, from 
equation 2.43. it can be seen that the power delivered to the grid is proportional to the 
grid voltage, from equation 2.51 it can be seen that if a severe grid fault occur which the 
terminal voltage of the wind turbine drops to nearly zero, there is almost no power can 
be transferred to the grid and all these exceed power has to flow into the DC-link, this is 
mainly because the type D doesn’t “see” a fault happened, the DC-link decouple the 
generator dynamics. 
The primary goal of the generator side FRT is to avoid the rapid increase in the DC-link 
voltage, this could be done by reducing the generator power during fault, which means 
moving the power imbalance point from the DC-link to the generator, thus the DC-link 
==PAGE:39==
Modeling of Full Converter PMSG Wind Turbine 
31 
is protected during grid faults. Two ways could achieve this, (1) pitching the turbine 
blade and (2) de-loading the wind turbine by reducing the generator electromagnet 
torque. As mentioned in [40], the typical pitch rate is normally 5~10°/𝑠 , however this 
is too slow to prevent the DC-link voltage rise up, therefore, de-loading the turbine by 
pitching the blade is not investigated in this project. By reduce the generator torque, the 
generator will accelerate, thus, the power imbalance would be finally stored in the rotor 
inertia. De-loading the turbine can be achieved by the generator side converter.

3.2 
Over current relay in radial system 
The protections are primarily designed to isolate the faults from the system within a 
time that avoids further damage to the system components. In order to maintain the sup-
ply to the customers, minimum necessary faulted section should be isolated. The current 
flow in a radial system is in only one direction, the relays are coordinated in the up-
stream direction, which the units farthest from the grid connection should operate 
first[43]. Referred to figure 3.7, the relay breaker unit CB1, CB2 and CB3 are respon-
sible to clear faults located at the corresponding zone, Zone1, Zone2 and Zone3. The 
relays are all time-coordinated, thus if a fault occur, the downstream relay are defined to 
operate faster than the upstream units[43]. The coordination time interval (CTI) give a 
safety margin for the fault to be cleared before the back up units tripped. The protection 
in figure 3.7 may featured with definite-time relay or inverse-time relay, inverse-time 
relay offers shorter clearing time, the main drawback of the definite-time relay is that 
the highest fault current occurs when a fault happened in Zone 1 close to CB1, however 
the clearing time of relay at CB1 is the longest. 
 
Figure 3.7 (a) Zones of protection,(b) Definite-time protection,(c) inverse-time protec-
tion[43]

be pressed to produce a 25ms pulse which is slightly longer than one cycle at 50HZ. 
Then a pulse is produced by the AND gate, which combines the information from the 
zero-crossing detector and the fault button, the “ point on wave delay” function can be 
achieved by a silder, a gain value and a pulse duration timer set to detect a rising edge, 
when the pulse rise to one from the pervious AND gate, the output of the duration timer

duration logic, which comprise a silder to control the fault duration and another pulse 
duration timer to detect a falling edge to initiate the fault when the pulse from the pervi-

provide the bit information, this value is multiplied by the pulse from the fault duration 
timer logic to create a pulse than can control the fault branch, in the end, a dial compo-
nent is used to control the signal selector that determine where the fault will happen.

CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 
 
5.1 
Conclusion 
This project contains two essential goals. One is to develop a dynamic model in RTDS 
that could represent the accurate behavior of full converter wind turbine during fault and 
its fault ride through capability that must fulfill the ENTSO-E grid requirement. The 
second goal is to find the impact of full converter wind turbine during fault on the radial 
system over current protection. According to the results presented in chapter 4, some 
general conclusions were extracted: 
• The utility grid in feed level has a significant impact on the probability of sympathetic 
tripping, a relatively weak grid, increase the possibility of false tripping due to the de-
layed operation time of relay 3 on adjacent feeder, the fault current sensed by relay 1 on 
the healthy feeder need to be seen in a composite way, 
• The capacity of the wind farm has a strong influence on sympathetic tripping. If the 
retained voltage of the PCC point is lower than 0.9pu during fault, the reactive power 
injection of wind farm affects the possibility of false tripping most .The increasing of 
the size of the wind farm will improve the performance of relay 3 on the faulted feeder 
by reducing the operation time due to the relatively sufficient reactive support into the 
system, however, this will do a negative contribution to the performance of relay 1 on 
the healthy feeder by increasing the fault current sensed by it, results in a higher proba-
bility of sympathetic tripping. If the retained voltage of PCC point is higher than 0.9pu, 
a larger size of wind farm will increase the risk of sympathetic tripping not only due to 
the pure backward active current but also due to the significant growth of reactive cur-
rent during fault. However, the fault current contribution from the full converter wind 
turbine is still quite limited. 
• Generally speaking, the fault location at the end of feeder 2 increase the probability of 
sympathetic tripping due to the delayed operation of relay 3, however, the fault current 
sensed by relay 1 reduced slightly, which actually lower the risk of false tripping. 
• The fault current contribution from the wind farm has a more significant effect on 
blinding of protection when the external grid is relatively weak, a ‘weak” grid has high-
er possibility of blinding of protection. 
==PAGE:86==
Conclusion and Future Work 
78 
• The size of the wind farm can affect the operating time of the upstream overcurrent 
relay protecting the same feeder as the wind farm connected. With a larger capacity of 
wind farm, the possibility of blinding of protection increased. 
• The problem of sympathetic tripping and blinding of protection were not observed in 
any simulations in this project, it seems very much unlikely to happen unless some ex-
treme conditions occur. In general, this might because the fault current contribution 
from the full converter wind turbine is very limited and well regulated. However, it 
doesn’t mean it completely won’t happen in a specific distribution system equipped 
with different protections, the severity of the problems of sympathetic tripping and 
blinding of protection need to be assessed with the specific case that of interest based on 
the general consideration given above.

It might be summarized that when the voltage drops below 0.9pu, the FRT scheme re-
sponse this abnormal condition immediately and boost up the PCC voltage, however, as 
soon as the voltage rise up above 0.9pu, the grid side controller prioritize the active 
power injection, thus, the reactive power injection reduced due to the limited capacity of 
the grid side converter, this results a second voltage dip, then this phenomena continues 
until the fault was cleared, this condition might require a supplementary reactive power 
compensation or a more advanced solution to mitigate the switching among different 
control modes than the author proposed in this project.
