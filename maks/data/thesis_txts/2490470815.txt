Abstract
Background Inﬂuenza virus cause respiratory infections worldwide causing estimated 290,000 to
650,000 deaths each year. In developed countries most deaths associated with inﬂuenza occur
among people 65 years of age or above. Thus, vaccination against inﬂuenza is recommended for
elderly by World Health Organization. However, the beneﬁts of vaccination are limited as the
immune system in elderly generally has a reduced ability to produce an eﬃcient immune response
towards new antigens. Elderly has been described with increased concentrations of inﬂammatory
markers in circulation, which creates a chronic low grade inﬂammation. Thus, inﬂammation could
possibly be correlated with ineﬃcient vaccine response in elderly. However, the underlying molec-
ular mechanism of poor vaccination eﬃcacy in elderly needs to be investigated further.
Aim and methods The aim of the thesis was to investigate the impact of inﬂammation on the
response to inﬂuenza vaccination in Göttingen minipigs. Two groups had ALZET® mini-osmotic
pumps implanted subcutaneously in the neck. In one group lipopolysaccharide (LPS) was continu-
ously administered to induce inﬂammation while saline was continuously administered in the other
group, which served as a control group. The pigs were vaccinated twice with a vaccine containing
three inactivated inﬂuenza A strains. Diﬀerences in gene expression between the groups before
and after vaccination was examined by high throughput quantitative real-time polymerase chain
reaction. The study also examined the production of both non-neutralizing and neutralizing an-
tibodies by enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay and hemagglutination inhibition test, respectively.
Results The results demonstrated a clear inﬂammatory response towards LPS in the LPS treated
group with increased rectal temperature, CRP levels and neutrophil numbers. However, the re-
sponse declined and normalized prior to the second vaccination. Five genes found to be signiﬁcantly
upregulated (p-values < 0.05 and FC >1.5) solely after vaccination in the pigs receiving LPS com-
pared to the pigs receiving saline. Four out of ﬁve upregulated genes are interferon stimulated
genes; ISG15, ISG20, IFI44 and HERC5. The last gene, IRF7, is an interferon stimulating gene.
All genes are known to be involved in antiviral immune responses to inﬂuenza A virus infection.
The data also showed a tendency of a less eﬃcient NP antibody response in the LPS treated group
compared to the control group, while no diﬀerences were observed in HA speciﬁc antibodies in
both groups before or after vaccination.
Conclusion The present study indicated that a shorter period of inﬂammation during or before
vaccination has a tendency to alter the transcription of some circulating antiviral genes and to result
in a less eﬃcient non-neutralizing antibody response. Thus, inﬂammation might be correlated with
the poor vaccination eﬃcacy observed in elderly. Especially, interferon stimulated genes seems to
be involved in the molecular mechanism behind low grade inﬂammation and poor vaccine eﬃcacy.

Introduction
Inﬂuenza is a worldwide disease and causes in consequence of complications 290,000 to 650,000
estimated deaths annually [1]. In developed countries most deaths associated with inﬂuenza occur
among people 65 years of age or above. Thus, vaccination against inﬂuenza is recommended for
elderly by the World Health Organization (WHO) [1]. However, the beneﬁts of vaccination are
limited as the immune system in elderly generally has a reduced ability to produce an eﬃcient pro-
tective immune response towards new antigens [2]. As the global population is ageing this creates a
public health concern. Increased concentrations of inﬂammatory markers in circulation creating a
chronic low grade inﬂammation has been described in older adults above 65 years [2]. This creates
a question of a possible correlation between low grade inﬂammation and the ineﬃcient vaccine
response in elderly. Several studies has demonstrated lower antibody responses after vaccination
in elderly compared to younger adults [3, 4, 5, 6]. However, the molecular mechanism behind low
grade inﬂammation and poor vaccination eﬃcacy seen in elderly needs to be investigated further.
This master thesis investigates inﬂuenza vaccine response in Göttingen minipigs comparing a group
with lipopolysaccharide (LPS) induced inﬂammation to a control group. The overall aim is to
investigate the impact of inﬂammation on the response to inﬂuenza vaccination. To do so, the
following hypotheses were tested:
1. The pigs introduced to LPS have increased expression of inﬂammation-related genes com-
pared to the control group.
2. Inﬂammation will lead to an altered transcription of circulating antiviral genes after vacci-
nation.
3. Inﬂammation during vaccination with inﬂuenza A virus vaccine results in a lower antibody
response.
To test the ﬁrst two hypotheses, the response of a set of proinﬂammatory and inﬂuenza related
genes was studied at diﬀerent time points by using high-throughput quantitative real-time poly-
merase chain reaction (qPCR). The qPCR was conducted to determine any relationship between
inﬂammation and an altered immune response upon vaccination with an inactivated inﬂuenza A
virus (IAV) vaccine consisting of subtype H3N2, H1N1 and H1N2. A set of 24 diﬀerent genes was
evaluated by qPCR. These genes were selected based on a pilot project and literature studies. The
24 genes represent several groups of the innate immune response; transcription factors, cytokines
and receptors, interferon stimulated genes (ISGs), and genes associated with the adaptive immune
response to vaccination. To test the last hypothesis, the production of antibodies against inﬂuenza
A was investigated by enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) and hemagglutinin inhibition
(HI) test and compared between the group with LPS induced inﬂammation and the control group.

Background
2.1
Inﬂuenza virus
Inﬂuenza virus causes respiratory infections worldwide, with regular seasonal epidemics and occa-
sional global pandemics where up to 40% of the population can be infected within a year [7, 8].
Inﬂuenza virus belongs to the Orthomyxoviridae family and is a single-stranded negative sense
RNA ((-)ssRNA) virus. Inﬂuenza virus are classiﬁed into four types; type A, B, C and D [1, 8, 9].
Type A can infect both humans and a wide spectrum of animals, such as birds, pigs and horses
while type B and C infect only humans [10]. Type D mainly infects cattle [1, 8, 9]. Upon infection,
type A and B cause similar symptoms, however, antigenic drift of the two main viral antigens,
neuraminidase (NA) and in particular hemagglutinin (HA), occur more slowly in type B compared
with type A. By accumulation of mutations antigenic drift results frequently in structural changes
of the viral glycoproteins which often results in a weakened binding of the antibodies generated
during the previous inﬂuenza infections. Thus, the main reason for recurrence of virus is anti-
genic drift. In contrast, type C has milder symptoms and most humans have developed antibodies
against this type of inﬂuenza early in life as it is much more antigenically stable than type A [9].
Inﬂuenza A virus (IAV) is the predominant type of inﬂuenza causing the majority of severe cases
in humans. It demonstrates the greatest genetic diversity and infects the widest range of species
[8]. The surface of the IAV consists of three viral proteins inserted into the lipid envelope; the
glycoproteins, HA and NA, and the transmembrane ion channel, matrix protein 2 (M2) (Figure
2.1). Underlying the lipid envelope, matrix protein 1 (M1) is present as the main component of
the viral capside. Eight viral (-)ssRNA segments, encoding one or two proteins, represent the viral
genome inside the envelope. Each of the segments are associated with nucleoprotein (NP) and
viral RNA polymerase resulting in the ribonucleoprotein (RNP) complex. Three segments encode
an RNA polymerase; basic polymerase 2 (PB2) in segment 1, basic polymerase 1 (PB1) in segment
2 which also encodes the proapoptotic peptide, PB1-F2, and polymerase acidic (PA) in segment 3.
The ﬁve next segments encode HA, NP, NA, the matrix proteins (M1 and M2) and nonstructural
protein (NS), respectively (Figure 2.1) [8, 11].
IAV can be divided into subtypes based on HA and NA. Currently, 18 HA subtypes and 11 NA
subtypes have been identiﬁed, but mainly, H1N1 and H3N2 are currently circulating in humans
[10]. IAV of signiﬁcance in pigs are swine adapted H1N1, H3N2 and H1N2 subtypes [12]. Novel
inﬂuenza virus subtypes are formed due to antigenic shift. Antigenic shift occurs when a single cell
is co-infected with more than one inﬂuenza virus type and RNA segments are exchanged to create
a novel combination of the surface proteins HA and NA. As HA is the most abundant surface
glycoprotein, and has the highest concentration of epitopes, HA is the dominant glycoprotein

2.1.1
Virus life cycle
Inﬂuenza virus enters the host through the oral or nasal cavities and invade the respiratory epithe-
lial cells to establish an infection [14]. The host cell is entered via receptor mediated endocytosis
as HA binds to the sialic acid (SA) receptors on the cell surface (Figure 2.2, A). HA of human
and swine inﬂuenza virus have the highest aﬃnity towards α2,6-linked SA receptors while HA
of avian inﬂuenza virus prefers α2,3-linked SA receptors. Swine respiratory epithelia has both
α2,6- and α2,3-linked SA receptors and can be infected with both human and avian inﬂuenza
virus. After endocytosis of inﬂuenza virus, the acidic environment of the endosome leads to a
conformational change of HA which exposes a fusion peptide. The fusion peptide leads fusion of
the viral-containing vesicle and the endosomal membranes releasing the RNP complex into the
cytoplasm (Figure 2.2, B). New viral proteins are synthesized in the cytoplasm and new (-)ssRNA
is synthesized in the nucleus to form a new RNP complex. New virus particles are packed and
budded oﬀat the cell surface (Figure 2.2, C). HA will bind to SA receptors on the cell surface
again, but the enzymatic activity of NA ensures cleavage from SA residues leading to release of
the new virus particles [7, 11, 15].

2.2
The innate immune system
The immune system consists of two defensive systems; the innate immune system and the adaptive
immune system. The innate system is in born and without memory, while the adaptive immune
system is speciﬁc and develops over time when the host is exposed to diﬀerent antigens. The in-
nate immune system has a rapid response towards invading pathogens, while the adaptive immune
response evolves over days or weeks depending on previous exposure [16, 17].
An invading pathogen will face several phases of the innate immune system, which aim to pre-
vent infection as illustrated in Figure 2.3. The ﬁrst line of defense is the physical and chemical
barriers formed by mucus and epithelia [16, 18]. The epithelial cells are bound together by tight
junctions to form the physical seal [16, 19]. Depending on the location, the epithelia have several
eﬀective defense mechanisms to avoid infection. In the respiratory system, one defense mechanism
is movement of mucus, a viscous gel-like ﬂuid, by ciliated respiratory epithelial cells [16, 18]. The
mucus contains important components to avoid infection, such as lysozyme and lactoferrin. It also
contains SA which can bind HA surface proteins of the infectious virus hindering viral interaction
with the underlying epithelial cells [18]. If a pathogen evades the ﬁrst line of defense, it will face
preformed soluble molecules, such as proteins from the complement system, which targets the
pathogen and induce lysis and phagocytosis by resident cells of the innate immune system, such
as macrophages. If the pathogen overcomes the eﬀector molecules, eﬀector cells will be recruited
to the site of infection and activated [16]. Macrophages are the major phagocyte population res-

ident at homeostasis and are the ﬁrst cells to encounter the pathogens [16, 19]. During infection,
monocytes from the blood stream are recruited to the site of infection where they diﬀerentiate
into macrophages. The second type of phagocytes recruited to the site of infection is granulocytes
including neutrophils, eosinophils, and basophils in which neutrophils have the highest phagocytic
activity. Macrophages and granulocytes recognize and kill many pathogens without involvement of
the adaptive immune response. However, if the innate immune system is not successful in clearing
the pathogen, the adaptive immune system becomes activated. It is activated by transportation of
antigens to the lymphoid organs, predominantly by the last class of phagocytic cells, the dendritic
cells (Figure 2.3) [16].

Innate immune cells recognize microbes by pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) char-

pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) on immune cells and activate downstream signaling cascades

receptors leads to production and release of cytokines, which aﬀect the activity of the cell it-
self (autocrine) or another cell (paracrine) [16]. The eﬀect depends on the cytokine and the cell
involved, but typically they aﬀect cell activation, division, apoptosis or movement [17].

2.3
Antiviral innate immune response
The innate immune system is crucial in recognition and rapid clearance upon viral infection. Dif-
ferent PRRs can recognize viral PAMPs, including TLRs and RLRs. RLRs comprise melanoma
diﬀerentiation-associated gene 5 (MDA5), laboratory of genetics and physiology 2 (LGP2) and
retinoic acid inducible gene I (RIG-I) [20]. Additionally, the nucleotide binding oligomerization
domain-like receptor family pyrin domain containing 3 (NLRP3) inﬂammasome complex is poten-
tially also activated during inﬂuenza virus infection [21]. NLRP3 is the best characterized protein
in the NLRP family, which is a sub family of the NLRs [16]. As illustrated in ﬁgure 2.4, several
TLRs are activated by viral components. TLR4 is mainly known to detect bacterial components,
however, TLR4 has been found to detect viral envelope proteins or viral proteins released into
the extracellular space from several viruses as well [22]. Examples are Ebola virus glycoproteins
[23], vesicular stomatitis virus glycoprotein G [24], respiratory syncytial virus fusion protein [25]
and IAV by host danger associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), such as high mobility group
box 1 (HMGB1) [26]. TLR3, TLR7, TLR8 and TLR9 are expressed in endosomes. TLR3 detects
double-stranded RNA (dsRNA). TLR7 and TLR8 detect single-stranded RNA (ssRNA) while
TLR9 detects DNA with unmethylated CpG sites (Figure 2.4). Both RIG-I, MDA5, LGP2 and
NLRP3 are activated in the cytoplasm. RIG-I detects ssRNA while MDA5 recognize dsRNA.
LGP2 modulates RIG-I and MDA5 signaling upon viral stimuli. The mechanism of action is not
known, but it is thought to enhance MDA5 signaling while inhibiting RIG-I signaling [27, 28].
NLRP3 forms a multiprotein inﬂammasome complex upon cellular damage [7, 14, 16].

2.4
Mechanisms of vaccination
The most widely used inﬂuenza vaccines are inactivated inﬂuenza vaccines (IIVs) and live attenu-
ated inﬂuenza vaccines (LAIVs). The IIVs is recommended annually in Denmark for people in a
high risk group of getting complications from inﬂuenza infection, such as elderly people, pregnant
women and people with chronic diseases [31]. The recommendation of LAIVs is limited in Europe
to children from age 2 to 18 and is not recommended for people in a high risk group [32, 33].
LAIVs comprise a weakened version of the viral pathogen and are administered intranasal [34].
The vaccine resemble more closely a natural infection with stimulation of both the humoral re-
sponse, inducing IgA and IgG, and the cell-mediated immune response [32]. Contrary to LAIVs,
IIVs poorly stimulate the cell-mediated immune response [35]. The IIVs contain HA and NA pro-
teins and some contain NP proteins as well [33]. Annual vaccination with inactivated vaccines
comprising IAV subtypes H3N2 and H1N1 and one or two inﬂuenza B virus determined by WHO
is the primary strategy for prevention of inﬂuenza A and B [1]. Protection after natural inﬂuenza
infection is mainly mediated by HA-speciﬁc antibodies in serum and mucosa whereas NA-speciﬁc
antibodies, antibodies against internal inﬂuenza proteins and T cell responses reduce the severity
of the infection by enhancing viral clearance [36]. Thus, serum HA-speciﬁc antibodies are the most
commonly measured correlate of protection against inﬂuenza [33, 37].
In ﬁgure 2.7 the mechanism of vaccination with IIVs is illustrated. IIVs are administered intra-
muscular to activate a humoral immune response (Figure 2.7, A). To create a response against
the vaccine, it is important to ensure a suﬃcient signal through vaccine antigens and/or adjuvants
to induce an inﬂammatory reaction mediated by cells of the innate immune system [38, 39]. The

2.5
Low grade inﬂammation and vaccination in elderly
Chronic low grade inﬂammation (LGI) is characteristic of the aging process and is a strong risk fac-
tor for development of chronic diseases, LGI due to aging is also known as inﬂamm-ageing [19, 40].
Inﬂamm-ageing is characterized by an elevated basal level of pro-inﬂammatory mediators, such as
TNF-α, IL-6 and C-reactive protein (CRP) [19, 40, 41]. Several mechanisms of inﬂamm-ageing in
elderly have been proposed including accumulation of senescence immune and non-immune cells,
increase in production of reactive oxygen species, and changes in permeability of the intestinal
mucosa leading to increased transfer of bacteria and bacterial products, such as endotoxin (LPS),
Page 11 of 98
==PAGE:22==
Chapter 2 | Background
into the bloodstream [19, 40].
Inﬂuenza vaccination is recommended for adults above 65 years of age. However, the beneﬁts of
vaccination are limited in elderly. A review of 31 vaccine antibody response studies conducted
from 1986 to 2002 showed seroconversion (subjects with a 4-fold increase in antibody titers) and
seroprotection (subjects with HI antibody titer ≥1:40 post-vaccination) rates signiﬁcantly higher
in the young age group (comprised of individuals aged 17-59) compared to the elderly age group
(comprised of individuals aged 58-104) [6]. All individuals included in the study were vaccinated
with an inactivated inﬂuenza vaccine with components of H3N2, H1N1 and inﬂuenza B virus. In the
group of elderly, 35-51% seroconverted compared to 58-62% in the young group. Of the elderly, 67-
74% were seroprotected compared to 78-84% of the young [6]. In agreement, the antibody response
after vaccination has also been demonstrated to be lower in the elderly than in younger adults in
several other studies [3, 4, 5, 6]. It is therefore unfortunately evident that current inﬂuenza vaccines
do not match the changing risk proﬁle of the aging population. However, antibody titers after
inﬂuenza vaccination of elderly are not always reliable for predicting protection after vaccination
[35]. As antibody titers only reﬂect the humoral immune response, the cell-mediated immune
response should ideally be evaluated as well to determine the eﬃcacy of the vaccine.

Experimental background
2.6.1
RNA quality, quantity and purity
The quantity and quality of RNA is important as these factors aﬀect the accuracy of gene expres-
sion results when performing qPCR. The purity and integrity of RNA are crucial factors which
aﬀect qPCR performance [42, 43]. The quantity and purity can be assessed by NanoDropTM Spec-
trophotometer as RNA and DNA have an absorption maximum at 260 nm [43]. Beer-Lambert’s
law can be used to determine the quantity as the absorbance at 260 nm is proportional to the
concentration of nucleic acid in solution [44]. To assess the purity, the absorbance ratio at 260 nm
vs 280 nm (A260/A280) is used for detection of protein contamination, while the absorbance ratio
of 260 nm vs 230 nm (A260/A230) detects a lot of other contaminants with absorbance at 230 nm,
e.g. carbohydrates, salts and phenol. Both ratios should be approximately 2 for pure RNA [44, 45].
The Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer evaluates the quality of RNA samples. RNA samples are separated
by electrophoresis in channels of a microﬂuidic chip and detected by laser-induced ﬂuorescence
[43, 46]. The results are visualized as electropherograms where the RNA integrity can be evaluated
based on the 18S and 28S ribosomal RNA bands as well as smaller degradation products. Degraded
RNA entails a decrease in signal intensities of the two ribosomal bands simultaneous with an
increase in shorter fragments of degradation products.
RNA quality is assessed by the RNA

RNA integrity is classiﬁed based on a numbering system from 1 to 10 with 10 being the most

2.6.2
Quantitative polymerase chain reaction
Quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) is a method for quantiﬁcation of nucleic
acids. With this method a wide range of target genes can be studied in diﬀerent samples using
gene-speciﬁc primers [48]. Reverse transcription-qPCR (RT-qPCR) technique is a highly sensitive
method for quantiﬁcation of messenger RNA (mRNA) as very low concentrations of RNA can be
measured and after data-preprocessing valid data can be generated even for low integrity RNA
[47, 49, 50]. The method consists of three steps; i) RNA is reverse transcribed to complementary
DNA (cDNA), ii) the cDNA is pre-ampliﬁed to ensure a quantiﬁable amount of the qPCR product
and iii) a simultaneous ampliﬁcation and detection of the PCR products for quantiﬁcation in real
time [49]. Design of primers is important to ensure speciﬁc and eﬃcient ampliﬁcation. To increase
the speciﬁcity of the primer, the target sequence should be unique for the gene of interest. The
primer sequence can be blasted (detection of local similarity between sequences) in a database,
such as the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) database Basic Local Align-
ment Search Tool (BLAST) [51], to ensure uniqueness. The size of the primer should be 75 to
150 base pair long with a nucleotide content of guanine and cytosine between 50% and 60%. Long
stretches of guanine or cytosine should be avoided, but at the end of the primers a guanine or a
cytosine is recommended. Further, a melting temperature of 55 to 65°C is recommended for an
optimal primer design [50]. The conversion of RNA to cDNA is conduced as DNA polymerase is
used in the qPCR and to increase stability as RNases are present in the environment which creates
a risk of RNA degradation whereas DNA is generally more stable [48, 50]. The quantiﬁcation is
performed in real time by measuring increase in ﬂuorescence signal as it is proportional to the
amount of RNA originally present in each sample [49]. The ﬂuorescence is detected by either a
non-speciﬁc DNA binding dye or speciﬁc primer-probes. The non-speciﬁc DNA binding dye binds
to the groove of the newly synthesized double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) in each ampliﬁcation cycle
creating an increase in ﬂuorescence during each PCR cycle (Figure 2.8, A) [52]. The output is
given as a quantiﬁcation cycle (Cq) value which describes the cycle at which the ﬂuorescence rises
above a deﬁned threshold. A high concentration of mRNA is equal to a low Cq value as less cycles
are needed to reach the threshold [49].
Non-speciﬁc products and primer-dimers can be formed during the PCR which makes a melting
curve analysis highly important to ensure speciﬁcity. Melting curves are performed at the end of
the PCR cycles by increasing the temperature [50, 52, 53]. The amount of ﬂuorescence will change

2.6.3
Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay
The enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) is a rapid test for detection and quantiﬁcation
of biological agents. There are three main methods; direct, indirect and sandwich ELISA. All
three systems can be used in a fourth type called competition/inhibition ELISA [54, 55]. Only the
competition/inhibition ELISA has been used in the present project and will described further.
The competition/inhibition ELISA is mainly used to measure the concentration of antigen or
antibody in a sample. Sample antigen or antibody competes with a reference for binding to the
immobilized antibodies or antigens on the solid phase. Thus, signal output is inversely proportional
to the amount of antigen or antibody in the sample [54, 55]. If the test sample is added before
the enzyme-labeled antibodies, it is a direct inhibition ELISA. If the test sample and the enzyme-
labeled antibodies are added simultaneous, the assay is a direct competition ELISA [54]. In this
project, a direct inhibition ELISA is used to measure the concentration of antibodies against the
NP gene which is highly conserved between IAV subtypes. The method is illustrated in Figure
2.9. Inﬂuenza A viral antigens are immobilized to a solid phase (Figure 2.9, A). The serum sample
is added before the enzyme-labeled antibodies. Thus, antibodies speciﬁc for IAV in the serum
sample will bind the antigens blocking the binding of the enzyme-labeled antibodies (Figure 2.9,
B and C). Substrate is added to the assay resulting in color development (Figure 2.9, D). Thus,
high amounts of antibodies in the sample results in low color production where low amounts of
antibodies in the sample results in high color production [54, 56].

2.6.4
Hemagglutination inhibition test
The hemagglutinin inhibition (HI) test detects antibodies against the HA head.
HA speciﬁc
antibodies are the majority of neutralizing antibodies generated upon infection or vaccination.
Antibody-mediated neutralization is the most important mechanism in prevention inﬂuenza infec-
tion of host cells. Neutralizing antibodies can prevent binding of virus to the SA receptors, hinder
the fusion process or interfere with the release of new virus particles [57]. In this project, the HI
test is used to identify HA speciﬁc antibodies towards inﬂuenza A. Inﬂuenza virus agglutinate red
blood cells through interaction of HA with surface receptors on the red blood cells. In suﬃcient
quantity of virus particles, the interaction of HA with the red blood cells form a network of linked
blood cells. This formation prevent the red blood cells from precipitating as a small pellet. In
the presence of HA speciﬁc antibodies, the antibodies will inhibit the agglutination. Thus, the red
blood cells will precipitate as a small pellet. The method is illustrated in Figure 2.10. Virus and
serum samples are incubated together (Figure 2.10, A). If the serum samples contains HA speciﬁc
antibodies towards the virus, they will bind the virus (Figure 2.10, B). Red blood cells are added
and in presence of antibodies interaction between virus and red blood cells are prevented (Figure
2.10, C). Thus, the hemagglutination is inhibited and red blood cells will precipitate. The HI titer
of the sample is deﬁned as the highest dilution of serum that inhibits red blood cell hemagglutina-

3.1
Experimental setup
Göttingen minipigs (seven males and seven females) aged 14 to 17 months undergone surgery for
implantation of ALZET® mini-osmotic pumps subcutaneously in the neck caudal to the right
ear. The 14 pigs were randomly divided into two groups of seven. One group was given 10µg
LPS/kg/day (LPS treated group) and the other group was given 0.9% NaCl in the same volume
(control group). The ﬂow rate of the ALZET® 2ML4 pump was 2.5 µl/hr. Whole blood samples for
gene expression analysis and serology were collected in PAXgeneTM (Qiagen®) and serum tubes
(BD), respectively, at the 13 time points shown in Figure 3.1. The pigs were vaccinated twice
with 2 mL RESPIPORC FLU3 (IDT Biologika GmbH) containing three strains of inactivated
Inﬂuenza A virus/swine/Bakum/IDT1769/2003 (H3N2), Haselünne/IDT2617/2003 (H1N1) and
Bakum/1832/2000 (H1N2). The ﬁrst vaccination was given 3 days after implantation of the pumps
and the second vaccination was given 18 days after the ﬁrst vaccination (Figure 3.1). As seen in
Figure 3.1, the pigs were challenged with inﬂuenza A/Swine/Denmark/12687/2003(H1N2) at day
29 of the study. Samples after day 29 was not included in the gene expression analysis as it would
not be possible to distinguish between vaccine response and challenge response. Samples from
day 1 and day 22 to day 32 were used to measure the antibody response by ELISA and samples
from day 1, day 29 and 30 were included in the HI test. Additional data on temperature, CRP

3.2.1
Primer design
Primers were designed using Primer3 (v. 0.4.0) [60]. The genes of interest were found in Ensembl
[61] and the coding sequences were used to design two sets of primer pairs of each gene. The product
size range was chosen to be between 70 and 100 nucleotides and the primer melting temperature
(TM) to minimum 59◦C, optimal 60◦C and maximum 61◦C. The thermodynamic parameters were
based on Breslauer et al., 1986 [62]. Primer pairs were designed in a way were at least one of the
primers overlapped an exon junction to prevent possible ampliﬁcation of any remaining gDNA. If
the above criteria for primer design were impossible to achieve, primer pairs on each side of an
exon junction were made. If the gene only contained one exon, the primers were simply made
on the exon. Some genes were not present in Ensembl and were found in NCBI [63] where the
information on exons was lacking. In this case, the genes were made without counting for exon
junctions. To make sure that the primer pairs were speciﬁc to the gene of interest, the sequences
were blasted with the NCBI database BLAST. The primers were provided by Sigma-Aldrich in a
100µM primer solution and later diluted in low Tris-ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (TE) buﬀer
(VWR®) to 20µM containing both the forward and reverse primer.
3.2.2
RNA extraction and puriﬁcation
RNA was extracted and puriﬁed prior to this project. Whole blood samples were collected and
instantly stabilized in PAXgene Blood RNA TubesTM (Qiagen®). The samples were puriﬁed with
the PAXgene Blood miRNA Kit (Qiagen®) according to the protocol of the supplier (see appendix
C). Following the puriﬁcation of RNA, the quantity and purity was assessed on the NanoDrop ND-
1000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc). As described in section 2.6.1, the quantity
and purity was assessed based on the absorption at 230 nm, 260 nm, and 280 nm. The quality
of the RNA was determined by using the Agilent RNA 6000 Nano Kit (Agilent Technologies) and
the Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies) according to the protocol of the supplier (see
appendix D).
3.2.3
cDNA synthesis and pre-ampliﬁcation
The extracted RNA was converted into cDNA using the QuantiTect® Reverse Transcription Kit
(Qiagen®).
To ensure technical replication duplicates of each RNA sample were made.
The
cDNA reactions were conducted by adding a total of 500 ng RNA, 1.5 µl gDNA Wipeout Buﬀer
and RNase-free water ending at a total volume of 14 µl. The samples were incubated on a ther-
mal cycler (Biometra TRIO 48) for 2 min at 42◦C and subsequently cooled to 4◦C. This step
results in removal of the genomic DNA. Afterwards 6 µl reverse transcriptase (RT) master mix
(Quantiscript Reverse Transcriptase, RT Primer Mix, Quantiscript RT Buﬀer, H2O in a 1:1:4:2
ratio) was added to the samples except the -RT samples which included all the above mentioned
Page 21 of 98
==PAGE:32==
Chapter 3 | Material and methods
components but the enzyme reverse transcriptase. The samples were incubated on the thermal
cycler for 15 min at 42◦C, then for 3 min at 95◦C to inactivate the enzyme, following cooling to 4◦C.
Prior to pre-ampliﬁcation the samples were diluted 1:10 in low TE-buﬀer. First, a primer mix
was made containing all selected primers by adding 5µl of each 20µM primer solution and adding
low TE-buﬀer to a ﬁnal volume of 500µl. 2.5µl primer mix (20µM) was then added to 3µl Taq-
Man PreAmp Master Mix (Applied Biosystems®), 2µl low TE-buﬀer and 2.5µl cDNA. The pre-
ampliﬁcation samples were incubated on the thermal cycler at 90◦C for 10 min followed by 19
cycles of 95◦C for 15 seconds and 60◦C for 4 min. The number of pre-ampliﬁcation cycles was
selected based on prior projects and results from the pilot project.
Final, the samples were exonuclease treated to remove excess of primers. The exonuclease was
diluted to 4U by adding 3.5µl MiliQ, 0.5µl reaction buﬀer and 1µl exonuclease I 20U per cDNA
sample (New England Biolabs®). 4µl exonuclease I 4U was added to each of the pre-ampliﬁed
samples and incubated on the thermal cycler at 37◦C for 30 min followed by 80◦C for 15 min. The
samples were cooled to 4◦C. The pre-ampliﬁed and exonuclease treated samples were pooled for
a dilution series before samples were diluted 1:10 in low TE-buﬀer and stored at -20◦C prior to
qPCR.

3.2.5
Data pre-processing
The data set was visually inspected by using Fluidigm Real-Time PCR Analysis software (v. 4.5.2)
to remove technical induced variation. The samples were sorted by evaluating and removing sam-
ples based on the melting and ampliﬁcation curves. To ensure valid data, the -RT negative control
was compared with the samples associated to the speciﬁc primer assay. The data was considered
valid if the samples had 5% or less presence of gDNA compared to the -RT control. Additional,
the other negative control, NTC, was inspected to ensure valid data without contamination.
The sorted data set was analyzed by GenExTM (MultiD). First, the data set was corrected in the
relation to qPCR eﬃciency. Data with an qPCR eﬃciency between 80% to 120% and R2 > 0.95 was
Page 23 of 98
==PAGE:34==
Chapter 3 | Material and methods
accepted. To remove technical variation the data set was normalized with 3 reference genes (B2M,
GAPDH and PPIA) validated on similar material in a prior project (data not shown) using geNorm
[64] and NormFinder [65] based on a panel of 7 reference genes. Samples with a Cq diﬀerence of
more than 1.5 in more than 15% of the technical replicates were removed. cDNA replicates were
averaged and samples with 20% or more missing values were removed, any remaining missing data
was assigned a Cq value of +1 from the highest Cq value in the data set of the speciﬁc gene. The
data set was converted into relative quantities to compare fold change (FC) of the genes between
the two investigated groups.
3.2.6
Data analysis
For statistical analysis the data set was converted into log2 scale in order to approach a normal data
distribution. Statistical signiﬁcant diﬀerence in expression of each speciﬁc gene between the control
and the LPS treated group were determined by a two-tailed t-test at each time point. Further, the
standard deviation (SD) was calculated as the standard error of the mean (SEM). The changes
in gene expression were considered statistically signiﬁcant if p-values < 0.05, and biologically
signiﬁcant if FC were above ±1.5. A principal component analysis (PCA) was performed on auto
scaled log2 values to visualize clusters of individual samples. In the present study, two PCAs
were performed to compare mRNA expression proﬁles of all samples to investigate clustering due
to LPS treatment. This multivariate analysis clusters samples individually based on similar gene
expression proﬁles, where distance indicates gene expression variance within the diﬀerent groups or
samples of the data set. The PCAs were performed in RStudio (v. 2.1). The ﬁrst PCA compared
expression data of all samples before implantation of the pumps with all samples one day after
implantation of the pumps divided into LPS treated group and control group. The second PCA
compared all samples from day 2 and day 3 after implantation of the pumps divided into LPS
treated group and control group.

3.3.2
Hemagglutination inhibition test
The HI test was performed with the IAV strain the pigs were challenged with (inﬂuenza A/swine/
Denmark/12687/2003(H1H2)) and developed with a 0.65% solution of guinea pig red blood cells.
Serum samples from day 1, day 29 and day 30 were diluted from 1:10 to 1:1280. The assay was
performed at Statens Serum Institut. For further details see protocol in appendix E. The HI titers
were expressed as the reciprocal of the highest dilution of serum which causes complete inhibition
of the 8 hemagglutination antigen units/50 µl of virus. If the starting dilution of 1:10 did not
inhibit hemagglutination an HI titer value of 5 was assigned. For average antibody titers of each
group the geometric mean titre (GMT) was calculated by multiplying all values in the group and
taking the nth root of this number (n is the number of pigs in each group). In agreement with
FDA guidelines for pandemic inﬂuenza vaccines [59], a HI titer of ≥40 was considered protective,
equaling to a GMT of 40.

Discussion
5.1
Validation of the animal model
In the present study, the transcriptional antiviral immune response after inﬂuenza vaccination
was investigated in a porcine model. Pigs have a high similarity to humans in its physiology and
immune system which makes a porcine model one of the most appropriate and reliable models for
human vaccines [68]. In this study, the low grade inﬂammation induced by LPS in pigs has been
sought to reﬂect the age-related chronic low grade inﬂammation observed in elderly.
Inﬂammatory responses to LPS treatment and surgery
After implantation of the osmotic pumps, a clear inﬂammatory response towards LPS was observed
with increased rectal temperature, CRP levels and neutrophil numbers as described in section 4.
In agreement, LPS deposited in human airways demonstrated dose dependent systemic increase
of body temperature, elevated blood CRP concentration and blood neutrophils [69].
Further,
regulation of gene expression was also observed 2-3 days after implantation of the pumps. The
transcriptional response to LPS stimulation in this study are in accordance with a wide range of
results previously described in the literature (see below and Table 5.1), with an increase in MyD88,
TNF, CXCL10 and IL10 gene expression after LPS stimulation. Variation in time point for gene

Studies have demonstrated increase in gene expression of both MyD88, IL10 and TNF after LPS
stimulation (see Table 5.1) supporting the observed increase in gene expression of these genes in
the LPS treated group compared to the control group. In agreement with Hobbs et al. (2018)
expression of IL10 was observed subsequent to TNF expression in this study with a one day de-
layed response. Gene expression of CXCL10 was also increased after LPS stimulation. Induction of
CXCL10 gene expression has been described via activation of NF-κB [76]. TNF has been described
to have the ability to activate NF-κB in neutrophils [76] and MyD88 is an important adapter pro-
tein in PRR activation of NF-κB. Thus, as both MyD88 and TNF gene expression is increased in
the LPS treated group, an activation of NF-κB is expected which form the basis of the observed
up-regulation of CXCL10 in the present study.
Besides the increase in inﬂammatory related genes, a signiﬁcant down-regulation of three genes
was observed three days after implantation of the pumps; DRB1, CAMK4 and NR4A2. DRB1
belongs to major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II beta chain paralog, which is involved
in antigen presentation to CD4+ T cells. A down-regulation could indicate a weakened process-
ing and presentation of antigens. In agreement with the ﬁndings in this study, down-regulation
of MHC class II genes have also been demonstrated in swine peripheral blood mononuclear cells
(PBMCs) [77] and human PBMCs [78] after stimulation with 1µg/mL LPS. CAMK4 has demon-
strated to be important in the signal cascade involved in dendritic cell survival. CAMK4 proteins
have demonstrated to accumulate during diﬀerentiation of human monocyte-derived dendritic cells
after LPS stimulation [79]. Thus, the observed decrease in CAMK4 gene expression in the present
study could potentially indicate a minor importance of dendritic cell survival short time after LPS
stimulation.
The down-regulation of NR4A2 observed in this study conﬂicts with results described in the lit-
erature where NR4A2 gene expression has been observed to increase rapid (1-2 hours) after LPS
stimulation in human cells [80, 81]. However, NR4A2 gene expression might be regulated diﬀer-
ently in an in vivo model with a continuous stimulation with low LPS concentration compared to
an single bolus in vitro LPS stimulation. Otherwise, the observed down-regulation of NR4A2 gene
expression in this study could be based on a preference for a more pronounced TLR4 response as
it has been demonstrated that a more pronounced TLR4 response, with increased TNF protein
secretion, is induced after LPS stimulation in NR4A2 depleted cells [80, 81]. Thus, the decreased
gene expression of NR4A2 observed in this study might support the observed increase in TNF gene
expression.

Pigs are an outbred species, like humans, which generally entails larger inter-individual biological

variation. Thus, a high number of animals are required to achieve adequate statistical power [18].
The results from this study might be aﬀected by the low number of pigs in each group (seven in
the control group and ﬁve in the LPS treated group). Additionally, as described in section 4, some
pigs had problems with the pumps. Even though the pigs with clear problems with the pumps
were removed from the data set, it is not certain that all pigs were exposed to the same amount
of LPS at the same time. The osmotic pumps used in the present study are driven by water as
when water is absorbed through the outer membrane, the water expands a compartment within
the pump, the osmotic layer, compressing the ﬂexible impermeable reservoir [82]. This enable the
LPS to be released in a controlled manner. If the pumps are not implanted exactly at the same
site in each pigs tissue environment a lesser aqueous environment can result in a delayed onset of
the pump. The PCA performed in this study supports a slow onset of the pumps as a distinct
clustering of samples based on treatment ﬁrst was observed three days after the implantation of
the pumps (Figure 4.5, B). The slow response to LPS observed in this study is very diﬀerent
compared to when LPS is given as a single bolus with a rapid but short-lived response to LPS
stimulation within hours (see Table 5.1). The continuous stimulation with low LPS concentration
(10µg LPS/kg/day) used in this study could be another or a supplemental explanation of the ob-
served later and prolonged response in gene expression compared to the rapid and short-lived gene
expression response to a single bolus stimulation. Additionally, a great distance between samples
in the LPS treated group was observed in the PCA (Figure 4.5, B) which indicates a great variance
in gene expression within the LPS treated group. This supports diﬀerence in onset of the pumps
and/or large inter-individual biological variation.
Gender-related diﬀerences in gene expression after seasonal inﬂuenza vaccination have been ob-
served in healthy humans (aged 19-41) [83]. Several genes were only activated in females, while
others were only activated in males. It was demonstrated that females generate a stronger immune
response to vaccination within 24 hours compared to males while it was suggested that males re-
tained the immune response longer than females [83]. Thus, gender-related diﬀerences might also
aﬀect the results creating another source of technical variation between the pigs.
Before implantation of the pumps a signiﬁcant but slight increase in TNF (FC=1.59) was unfor-
tunately found in the LPS treated group. This could be a result of acute psychological stress as
TNF has been described with a signiﬁcant increase from baseline to 31–50 minutes post stress [84].
In agreement, an increase in neutrophil counts (p value = 0.2) before implantation of the pumps
was also observed in the LPS treated group (Figure 4.3). However, the stress is not reﬂected in
the temperature and CRP levels. Thus, the slight increase in TNF mRNA might be too low to be
measured on protein levels.

In summary, the pigs stimulated continuously with low levels of LPS compared to saline revealed
a signiﬁcant increase in expression of inﬂammation-related genes. Thus, the ﬁrst hypothesis pre-
sented in section 1; the pigs introduced to LPS have increased expression of inﬂammation-related
genes compared to the control group, can be accepted. Other genes were also regulated which all
supported a clear response to LPS in the treated group. However, the initial response towards LPS
declined and normalized prior to the second vaccination (Figure 4.1, 4.2, 4.3). Thus, a chronic
inﬂammation in the pigs exposed to LPS was not established during this study. Therefore, the
model reﬂects a shorter period of inﬂammation at the stage of ﬁrst vaccination and before the
second vaccination.
5.2
Altered transcription of circulating antiviral genes after vaccination
As the animal model does not reﬂect a chronic low grade inﬂammation, the observed altered tran-
scription of antiviral genes after vaccination describes alteration when having a shorter period of
inﬂammation during or before vaccination.
Six genes were signiﬁcantly up-regulated 1 day after vaccination/4 days after surgery in the LPS
treated group; ﬁve ISGs; CXCL10, ISG15, ISG20, HERC5 and IFI44, and one interferon stim-
ulating gene, IRF7. Two genes, HERC5 (p-value = 0.052) and MyD88 were up-regulated 1 day
following the second vaccination. Genes found not to be regulated prior to vaccination (at day
1, 2 and 3 after surgery) but only after vaccination included four ISGs; ISG15, ISG20, IFI44
and HERC5 and one interferon stimulating gene, IRF7. The following discussion will be focusing
on these ﬁve genes as they are assumed to be primary regulated in response to the vaccine and
not or minor in response to LPS. All ISGs encode for proteins, which based on literature could
be regulated in consequence of a vaccine with inactivated inﬂuenza and not due to LPS from
a Gram-negative bacteria. IRF7 expression could be regulated by LPS and it should be noted
that a tendency of regulation of IRF7 (p=0.06) was observed in response to LPS at day 2 after
implantation of the pumps. A discussion of MyD88 regulation regarding vaccine response will
also be included as up-regulation of MyD88 was observed after the second vaccination where no
inﬂammatory response (temperature, CRP, neutrophils or expression of pro-inﬂammatory genes)
to LPS was seen.
All genes regulated only after vaccination has all previously been reported to be involved in the re-
sponse to IAV infection. Thus, an increase in gene expression of these genes is most likely a response
towards vaccine antigens. HERC5 is an enzyme which catalyzes ISG15 conjugation to a lot of pro-
teins in IFN-stimulated cells. ISG15 conjugation inhibits IAV gene expression and replication in
human cells [85]. ISG20 impairs viral polymerase activity resulting in inhibition of both replication
and transcription levels of matrix protein 1 (M1) and nucleoprotein (NP) genes. Further, ISG20
Page 38 of 98
==PAGE:49==
Chapter 5 | Discussion
inhibits viral protein expression [86]. Based on results described by Dediego et al. (2019) in human
cells, it has been demonstrated that IFI44 regulates IFN responses negatively upon IAV infection
resulting in repressed ISG expression levels when IFI44 is expressed [87]. Even though the innate
immune responses are critical for controlling virus infection, the negative-feedback mechanism of
IFI44 is important to maintain the cellular homeostasis [87]. Thus, the up-regulation of IFI44
might have caused the a more limited up-regulation of the ISGs in this study. Gene expression of
transcriptional factors such as IRF7 have been demonstrated to be induced signiﬁcantly after in-
ﬂuenza vaccination in a study conducted in elderly humans [88]. Early innate immune response in
mice has demonstrated to be dependent on the adaptor protein, MyD88, as without the molecule
the innate immune response failed to launch [34]. MyD88 is up-regulated both in response to
LPS stimulation and vaccination, demonstrating its importance in both bacterial clearance and in
reaction to viral antigens during vaccination. As described in section 2.3, activated TLRs upon
viral infection uses MyD88 as adaptor proteins. Therefore, the observed up-regulation of MyD88
after the second vaccination is highly probable due to inﬂuenza vaccine antigens.
A study have compared the gene expression response towards inﬂuenza vaccination in young peo-
ple (age 21-30) with elderly (age ≥65). The study demonstrated diﬀerent time points of gene
expression after vaccination depending on age [3]. Thus, the altered translational response of ISGs
and IRF7 upon vaccination observed in this study in the LPS treated group could potentially be
expressed at another time point in the control group. If more time points had been included in
the study, diﬀerences in time of gene regulation between the LPS treated group and the control
groups as between elderly and young individuals could potentially have been demonstrated.
The altered transcription of circulating antiviral genes after the ﬁrst vaccination can not be con-
cluded to be entirely a result from vaccination as responses towards LPS were still observed at day
4 (increased serum CRP and neutrophil counts). The lack of a non-vaccinated, LPS treated con-
trol group makes it diﬃcult to distinguish between response to LPS and response to vaccination.
As the response towards LPS declines before the second vaccination, the altered transcription of
MyD88 and HERC5 is more reliable as a response to vaccination. Thus, there is a tendency of an
altered transcription of circulating antiviral genes due to a shorter period of inﬂammation during
or before vaccination. However, hypothesis two; inﬂammation will lead to an altered transcription
of circulating antiviral genes after vaccination, can not be accepted for certain.
5.3
IAV antibodies
The direct inhibition ELISA described in section 2.6.3 detects serum antibodies against IAV nu-
cleoprotein (NP) which are highly conserved between IAV subtypes [89]. Several studies have
demonstrated lower antibody responses after vaccination in elderly compared to younger adults

[3, 4, 5, 6].
In agreement, a tendency of lower non-neutralizing NP antibody responses upon
vaccination was observed in the LPS treated group in this study representing the elderly with
LGI. However, no diﬀerences in neutralizing antibodies were found by the HI test before and after
vaccination in both groups. The HI test is described by FDA to be appropriate for evaluation of
immune responses elicited by the vaccine [59]. However, FDA also underline that vaccines may
induce protection against disease through immunological mechanisms other than, or in addition
to, HI antibodies [59]. One mechanism could be NP antibodies as it has been demonstrated that
NP antibodies can trigger the activation of natural killer cells in inﬂuenza-infected humans [90].
Based on the direct inhibition ELISA, it is not possible to determine if the higher antibody re-
sponse in the control group is due to higher titers of NP antibodies or NP antibodies with higher
aﬃnity or both. Nevertheless, even though NP antibodies are not neutralizing, non-neutralizing
antibodies to conserved proteins like NP appears to contribute to protection against inﬂuenza
virus [91]. This supports the observed tendency of lower non-neutralizing NP antibodies and a
tendency of higher amounts of IAV observed in the nasal cavities of pigs in the LPS treated group
after challenge as described in section 4. The HI test in the present study was not conducted with
strains from the vaccine but the strain the pigs were challenged with. Thus, the vaccine might not
induce HI antibodies against the strain used for experimental infection. Potentially, a diﬀerence
between the control group and LPS treated group could have been observed if the HI test was
conducted with the vaccine strains.
Antibody titers reﬂect the humoral immune response and not the cell-mediated immune response
which ideally should also be evaluated to determine the eﬃcacy of the vaccine. CD8+ CD28- T
cells have demonstrated to accumulate in persons who fail to produce protective antibodies after
inﬂuenza vaccination [92, 93]. Thus, measuring the frequency of CD8+ CD28- T cells could po-
tentially be a reliable marker for cell-mediated immune responses upon vaccination [92, 93]. In
elderly, it has been demonstrated that inﬂuenza-speciﬁc T cell responses have demonstrated bet-
ter correlate of protection than antibody responses [35]. Thus, the measured antibody responses
in this study might not reﬂect the complete vaccine response. Potentially a diﬀerence in T cell
responses could be observed between the control group and the LPS treated group. This could
have supported the observed tendency of lower vaccine eﬃcacy in the LPS treated group with
both lower NP antibody responses and higher amounts of IAV present in the nasal cavities after
challenge.
In summary, a tendency of lower NP antibody responses in the LPS treated group compared to
the control group was observed. However, it is not possible to determine if the lower antibody
response is due to higher NP antibody titers or NP antibodies with higher aﬃnity or both. No

the third hypothesis; inﬂammation during vaccination with inﬂuenza A virus vaccine results in a

6
Conclusion
The investigation of inﬂuenza vaccine response in Göttingen minipigs was conducted in a model
reﬂecting a shorter period of inﬂammation during or before vaccination with an inactivated vac-
cine containing tree inﬂuenza A strains; H3N2 (2003), H1N1 (2003) and H1N2 (2000). A clear
response to LPS treatment was observed both transcriptional, cellular and in CRP responses.
The transcriptional results were established by comparing the gene expression between the LPS
treated group with the saline treated control group. A lot of the genes signiﬁcantly up-regulated
in response to LPS treatment were in agreement with previously described studies regarding LPS
stimulation both in vivo and in vitro in human and animal cell lines. The genes found to be reg-
ulated solely after vaccination in the LPS treated group included four ISGs; ISG15, ISG20, IFI44
and HERC5, and the interferon stimulating gene, IRF7. These genes are all involved in antiviral
responses to IAV infection. Additional, a tendency of lower NP antibody responses were observed
as a result of inﬂammation, but no diﬀerences were observed in HA speciﬁc antibody responses
in both the control group and the LPS treated group. Based on the results from the present
study, it can be concluded that a shorter period of inﬂammation during or before vaccination
have a tendency to alter the transcription of circulating antiviral genes and a tendency to result
in a lower non-neutralizing antibody response. Thus, inﬂammation might be correlated with the
poor vaccination eﬃcacy observed in elderly. Especially, ISGs seems to be involved. The results
obtained in this study can be relevant in order to get a better understanding of the mechanisms
behind the observed tendency of limited beneﬁts of vaccination in elderly above 65 years of age.
However, a high degree of animal to animal variation was observed in the study so the results need
to be validated and reproduced.

Future perspectives
In future studies, the model in the present study should be improved in several parameters. A
higher number of experimental animals should be included to increase the chance of achieving
adequate statistical power. Additional, only one gender should be included to avoid gender-related
diﬀerences. Another approach to achieve reliable data would be a sensitive assay to measure the
amount of LPS in the blood to ensure that all experimental animals included in the data set received
approximately the same amount of treatment. A control group which is not vaccinated but treated
with LPS should also be included to make sure that the vaccine response can be distinguished from
the response to the treatment. Otherwise, the time for vaccination should be after the response to
treatment has declined. Future work including the present experimental model could be focusing
on reproducing the age-dependent change in gene expression described by Thakar et al. (2015) by
including more time points after vaccination. To reproduce the results from Thakar et al. (2015),
the model should ideally reﬂect a state of chronic inﬂammation as observed in elderly with elevated
basal level of pro-inﬂammatory mediators during the entire study. Regarding the evaluation of
the vaccine response, a HI test could be conducted with the strains from the vaccine to achieve
more accurate HI responses. Additionally, as it has been demonstrated that inﬂuenza-speciﬁc T
cell responses have demonstrated better correlate of protection than antibody responses in elderly,
cell-mediated immune response should also be evaluated in determination of the vaccine eﬃcacy.

Protocol: Manual Purification of Total RNA, Including 
miRNA, from Human Whole Blood Collected into 
PAXgene Blood RNA Tubes 
Important points before starting 
 
Make sure that the kit box is intact and undamaged, and that buffers have 
not leaked. Do not use a damaged kit. 
 
When using a pipet, ensure that it is set to the correct volume, and that 
liquid is carefully and completely aspirated and dispensed. 
 
To avoid transferring samples to the wrong tubes and plastic consumables, 
ensure that all processing tubes, microcentrifuge tubes, and rotor adapters 
are properly labeled using a permanent pen. Label the lid and the body of 
each microcentrifuge tube, the body of each processing tube, and the outer 
wall of each rotor adapter. 
 
Spillages of samples and buffers during the procedure may reduce the 
yield and purity of RNA. 
 
Unless otherwise indicated, all steps of this protocol, including 
centrifugation steps, should be carried out at room temperature (15–25°C). 
Because of the sensitivity of nucleic acid amplification technologies, the 
following precautions are necessary when handling samples to avoid cross-
contamination: 
 
Carefully pipet the sample into the bottom of the tube without moistening 
the rim of the tube. 
 
Always change pipet tips between liquid transfers. Use aerosol-barrier pipet 
tips. 
 
Avoid touching the spin column membrane with the pipet tip. 
 
After vortexing or heating a microcentrifuge tube, briefly centrifuge it to 
remove drops from the inside of the lid. 
 
Wear gloves throughout the entire procedure. In case of contact between 
gloves and sample, change gloves immediately. 
Things to do before starting 
 
Blood must be collected in PAXgene Blood RNA Tubes according to the 
instructions in the PAXgene Blood RNA Tube Product Circular.

Procedure 
1. Centrifuge the PAXgene Blood RNA Tube for 10 min at  
3000–5000 x g using a swing-out rotor. 
Note: Ensure that the blood sample has been incubated in the PAXgene 
Blood RNA Tube for a minimum of 2 h at room temperature (15–25°C), in 
order to achieve complete lysis of blood cells. 
Note: The rotor must contain tube adapters for round-bottom tubes. If 
other types of tube adapter are used, the tubes may beak during 
centrifugation. 
2. Remove the supernatant by decanting or pipetting. Add 4 ml RNase-
free water to the pellet, and close the tube using a fresh secondary 
Hemogard closure. 
If decanting the supernatant, take care not to disturb the pellet, and dry the 
rim of the tube with a clean paper towel. 
3. Vortex until the pellet is visibly dissolved, and centrifuge for 10 min 
at 3000–5000 x g using a swing-out rotor. Remove the entire 
supernatant by decanting or pipetting, and discard. 
Small debris remaining in the supernatant after vortexing but before 
centrifugation will not affect the procedure. 
Note: Incomplete removal of the supernatant will inhibit lysis and dilute the 
lysate, and therefore affect the conditions for binding RNA to the PAXgene 
membrane. 
4. Add 350 µl Buffer BM1, and vortex until the pellet is visibly dissolved. 
5. Pipet the sample into a 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tube. Add 300 µl 
Buffer BM2 and 40 µl proteinase K. Mix by vortexing for 5 s, and 
incubate for 10 min at 55°C in a shaker–incubator at 400–1400 rpm. 
After incubation, set the temperature of the shaker–incubator to 65°C 
for use in step 20. 
Note: Do not mix Buffer BM2 and proteinase K together before adding 
them to the sample. 
6. Pipet the sample into a PAXgene Shredder spin column (lilac) placed 
in a 2 ml processing tube, and centrifuge for 3 min at full speed (do 
not exceed 20,000 x g). 
7. Carefully transfer the entire supernatant of the flow-through from 
the PAXgene Shredder spin column to a new 1.5 ml microcentrifuge 
tube without disturbing the pellet in the processing tube. 
8. Add 700 µl of isopropanol (100%, purity grade p.a.), and mix by 
vortexing.
