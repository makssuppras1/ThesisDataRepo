Abstract
This project investigates cooperative localization in drone swarms using Joint Commu­
nication And Sensing (JCAS) technologies. Radio signals from the inter­swarm commu­
nication network are exploited to determine the positions of swarm members. The first
phase addresses a static scenario, applying Multi­Dimensional Scaling (MDS) and Ma­
chine Learning (ML) methods, where ML is particularly useful in handling disturbances,
randomness, and equipment impairments that degrade positioning accuracy. The second
phase extends the problem to moving swarms, where an iterative localization approach
based on the Kalman Filter (KF) is developed to continuously refine position estimates
from radio measurements. The proposed methods were tested under various noise con­
ditions and demonstrated acceptable performance.

Introduction
In recent years, civilian Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) have become indispensable
tools across numerous sectors, thanks to their advancing capabilities and affordability.
Originally limited to hobbyist aerial photography, UAVs now play a critical role in infrastruc­
ture inspection, agricultural monitoring, logistics, security surveillance, and mining opera­
tions. Their applications continue to expand, enabling tasks such as search­and­rescue
missions, real­time reconnaissance, and even cargo transport. [1] In most advanced mo­
bile networks, like 5G and 6G, Non­Terrestrial Networks (NTNs) are integrated into the
network architecture and standards, including support for drone operations. This integra­
tion is likely to drive a significant increase in drone adoption in the future. [2] Moreover,
besides civilian applications, UAVs have many military applications as well, such as Mili­
tary Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance (ISR), border and maritime patrol. [3]
Recent progress in swarm robotics, especially in aerial robotics, has enabled ground­
breaking innovations exemplified by UAV swarms. These coordinated groups of drones
harness the advantages of airborne agility, rapid movement, and wide­area operational
reach. Drone swarms are transforming industries through diverse applications, enable au­
tonomous monitoring, delivery services, search and rescue operations, and entertainment
applications in civilian domains. Military uses include surveillance and reconnaissance,
combat support, and logistics. These systems leverage collective coordination to execute
tasks more effectively than individual drones. In drone swarms, formation control, which is
one of the possible research areas within drone swarms, ensures coordinated movement
among individual robots to maintain predefined spatial arrangements, a critical capabil­
ity for applications demanding synchronized operations and emergent collective behav­
ior. [4] Localization of the UAVs is essential for precise formation control in drone swarms.
As UAV applications spread across diverse domains, security emerges as a critical con­
cern. These systems’ heavy dependence on civilian Global Positioning System (GPS)
signals for positioning and navigation makes them vulnerable to malicious interference,
particularly GPS spoofing attacks that can compromise signal integrity. [5] Furthermore,
in GPS­denied environments such as urban canyons or indoor spaces, UAVs require al­
ternative positioning systems to maintain operational capability. This necessitates robust
navigation solutions independent of satellite signals. Some of the existing works related
to localization in literature are summarized in [6].
1.1
Project Scope and Limitations
This work addresses the problem of cooperative localization in a swarm of n UAVs. Sev­
eral methods were implemented using different techniques, as this is a well­researched
field. A common assumption in this work is that at least four UAVs within the swarm
have access to absolute coordinates linked to a global reference frame (e.g., via GPS).
The UAVs considered in this task leverage three primary sensing modalities: Radio Fre­
quency (RF)­based ranging using Received Signal Strength (RSS) measurements over
a wireless network, RF­based Angle of Arrival (AoA) measurements, and Inertial Mea­
surement Unit (IMU) measurements. Some methods assume that all of these sensing
techniques are available simultaneously, while others were evaluated using only a subset
of the modalities.

In addition to sensing, the UAVs are able to communicate with each other, thereby sharing
measurements across the network. The core challenge lies in fusing these distributed and
noisy measurements in order to reconstruct the relative 3­Dimensional (3D) positions of
the UAVs in real time, despite uncertainties arising from both sensing and communication.
As part of this project, the current state of the art was also reviewed in order to position the
proposed work within the broader research context and to identify existing approaches,
their strengths, and their limitations.
In this project, all of the measurements, techniques, and methods were modeled in simu­
lated environments. In real­world scenarios, however, additional phenomena may occur,
such as multipath propagation and more complex noise characteristics, which were not
explicitly captured in the simulations. Furthermore, while communication between UAVs
is discussed in the research section, it was modeled in a simplified manner, similar to
several related studies. Specifically, the communication process was not implemented
using a full communication stack, but instead approximated through estimated delays.
The main limitations of this project are summarized below:
• Simulated Environment: All measurements, techniques, and methods were mod­
eled in simulated environments, without explicitly accounting for real­world phenom­
ena such as multipath propagation and more complex noise characteristics. In ad­
dition, the Gazebo simulator imposed a limit of 10 UAVs, which constrained the
maximum swarm size that could be tested.
• Simplified Communication Model: Communication between UAVs was modeled
in a simplified way, similar to several related studies. As a result, real­world com­
plexities in data sharing, such as bandwidth limitations, packet loss, and network
congestion, were not considered, or were considered trough missing measurements.
• Simplified Model Tuning and Architectures: In many cases, the models were
tuned empirically through manual adjustments of hyperparameters based on ed­
ucated guesses. More systematic optimization techniques could have been em­
ployed. Moreover, in real­world applications more complex models would likely be
required (for example, an extended state vector in the case of Kalman filtering).
• Computational Limitations: While some simulations were executed on a dedi­
cated server, others had to be run on a personal laptop with limited computational
resources, which restricted the scale and number of experiments that could be per­
formed.

Methodology
The main scope of this thesis is to investigate cooperative localization in swarms of Swarm
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (SUAVs). This includes both a literature review and the imple­
mentation of selected methods. The developed approaches aim to enable cooperative

localization among swarm members under different operating conditions. Both static sce­
narios (with stationary UAVs) and dynamic scenarios (with moving UAVs) are considered,
presented in this order, since dynamic cases introduce additional sources of uncertainty.
The objective is to design and evaluate methods that could realistically be deployed in
UAVs swarms. In the comparative analysis, this practical perspective is taken into ac­
count; for example, the simulated volume in which localization is performed is significantly
larger than in several related studies, where methods are often evaluated in much smaller
environments. The final goal is to identify and recommend a method that can provide it­
erative, real­time position estimates with small errors, while maximizing the update rate,
using RF­based signals as the main sensing modality. During the preliminary research,
it was recognized that cooperative localization is a broad problem. Within this domain,
many subtopics remain active research areas, including scheduling, delay and latency in
swarm communication, antenna design, and distance measurement technologies. These
aspects are discussed in this thesis and were part of the literature review chapter 2. How­
ever, as also noted in the limitations section, certain simplifications were necessary to
maintain focus on the central problem. It is important to emphasize that cooperative lo­
calization is inherently complex, involving numerous design choices and methodological
alternatives. For the purpose of this thesis, the scope was deliberately narrowed in order
to provide a focused contribution while still highlighting the broader challenges of the field.
As for the first thing the current literature is reviewed in chapter 2, where the related
emerging technologies and use cases, the basics of localization and swarm localization ­
including some of the related research papers ­, the radio based localization techniques
and related related terms and moreover the cooperative localization in UAV swarms are
reviewed. The research is carried out in simulated environments, which environment’s
architecture and components are discussed in chapter 3, including the used software
components, wave propagation models and antenna characteristics. Two simulated en­
vironments were used during the research. The reason of this, that in one environment is
was simpler and quicker to test methods, which were not require IMU data. In the more
complex environment the UAVs were simulated in a physical simulator software, which
gives the opportunity to extract IMU data from the simulations and also uses a more pre­
cise method to simulate ElectroMagnetic (EM) wave propagation. However this other
simulator also have drawbacks, these are also discussed in the mentioned chapter. The
data collection is discussed in this chapter. The details of the implemented localization
methods are discussed in chapter 4, including the environment, the used hardware, the
programming language and the algorithms. The results are presented and compared
across multiple aspects in chapter 5, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of the
different methods. Building on these findings, additional questions and observations are
discussed in chapter 6. Finally, a conclusion is provided in chapter 7, summarizing
the main achievements of the project, outlining its limitations, and highlighting possible
directions for future work.
1.3
Research Questions and Contributions
During the literature review, two main research questions were identified and subse­
quently addressed in this thesis.
The first observation was that no publications were found in which Multi­Dimensional Scal­
ing (MDS)­based localization techniques were directly compared with Machine Learning
(ML)­based methods. Both of these areas are well researched individually—for example,
several of the ML approaches implemented in this work are also discussed in [9]—yet
no study was identified that considered them side by side in the context of cooperative

localization. In addition, Graph Neural Network (GNN)­based localization was explored,
since such models can natively handle missing measurements (i.e., missing edges in the
graph), which makes them an appealing candidate for this problem.
In [10], the authors present a distributed collaborative localization method for UAV swarms
that integrates Super Multi­Dimensional Scaling (SMDS) with patch dividing/merging and
GPS data. In their approach, SMDS is first applied to determine relative coordinates within
each patch, after which the patches are merged into a global map to convert relative po­
sitions into absolute coordinates. To address scalability, a low­complexity algorithm is
introduced to significantly reduce the computational burden when dealing with large num­
bers of UAVs. The authors conclude that localization accuracy and robustness could be
further improved by incorporating IMU data and fusing it with other measurements through
filtering techniques. However, this proposal was not implemented or experimentally vali­
dated.
In this thesis, simulating very large swarms was not feasible, and for small swarms a
distributed approach would not be meaningful. Instead, the integration of IMU data with
MDS­based methods was identified as a research question. This line of inquiry is related
to the work in [11], where SMDS was integrated with a Kalman Filter (KF).
In summary, the following research questions were investigated:
• Can ML­based methods be used as an alternative to MDS­based methods for co­
operative localization of SUAVs?
• Can an MDS­based method be effectively integrated with a KF for dynamic cooper­
ative localization of SUAVs in 3D space?
The main engineering tasks carried out in this project were as follows:
• Starting from an initially broad task, the scope was narrowed after the literature
review, the research direction was defined, and the state of the art was analyzed.
• Custom simulation environments were developed for the task, and data collection
was performed.
• Advanced localization algorithms were implemented based on findings from the sci­
entific literature and compared across multiple aspects, with a focus on the SUAV
use case.
• For the static scenario, the implemented algorithms were compared both with each
other and directly with ML­based methods, which constitutes a novel contribution to
the field.
• For the dynamic scenario, in addition to integrating MDS with the KF, a joint KF was
also implemented and evaluated.

2.1
Emerging Technologies and Use Cases
Numerous technologies are fundamentally interconnected with UAVs. This section pro­
vides a review of the key enabling technologies that facilitate and expand the operational
capabilities of Unmanned Aircraft Systems (UASs)/UAV systems.
Artificial Intelligence (AI) serves as a critical enabler for UAV swarm systems, substan­
tially improving their operational performance and functional capabilities. By implementing
AI­driven methodologies, these systems achieve full autonomy, allowing them to oper­
ate effectively in dynamic environments and execute time­sensitive decision­making pro­
cesses. [14] [5] Applications of AI in UAV involves many application scenarios, like path
planning, UAV control, object detection and tracking, predictive maintenance, defense
applications and swarm intelligence. Recent years have seen growing research focus on
incorporating cutting­edge AI technologies into UAV swarm systems. ML, Deep Learn­
ing (DL), and Reinforcement Learning (RL) techniques are being increasingly applied to
enhance swarm capabilities. These AI approaches allow drone swarms to learn from
operational experience, process live sensor data for decision­making, and dynamically
adjust to environmental changes. The implementation of such intelligent systems has
demonstrated measurable enhancements in swarm performance metrics including oper­
ational effectiveness, system resilience, and mission robustness. [15] [14] The creation
of AI algorithms capable of supporting autonomous navigation and path planning remains
one of the most significant challenges facing the industry. For effective autonomous oper­
ation, UAVs must demonstrate the ability to navigate complex and dynamic environments
while avoiding obstacles and making instantaneous decisions based on sensor inputs.

2.2
Introduction to Localization and Swarm Localization
Methods
This section begins by formulating the initial problem of swarm localization and presenting
a high­level overview of the key challenges and objectives. Subsequently, it reviews the
existing methods proposed in the literature. For many applications, the precise location of
the target is critical to mission success. For GPS­equipped SUAVs (especially with Real
Time Kinematic (RTK), which provides cm level accuracy), this task is typically straightfor­
ward. However, GPS signals are susceptible to obstruction, environmental interference,
or deliberate jamming, potentially degrading localization accuracy. To mitigate these limi­
tations, supplementary localization techniques must be integrated to enhance positioning
reliability. Within localization, Cooperative Localization (CL) has been extensively re­
searched in the fields of mobile robotics and UAVs. The core principle of this approach
involves a team of UAVs working collaboratively to estimate their own positions and/or the
location of a target of interest. By sharing sensor data and leveraging relative measure­
ments, the swarm enhances overall localization accuracy, particularly in GPS­denied or
challenging environments. [23] [24] This work is focusing on CL of the SUAV members.
A swarm of UAVs comprises aerial robots (drones) collaborating to achieve a specific
goal/objective. As noted in Chapter 1, UAV swarms have diverse applications. Abood,
Hussein, and Gaata [25] compare single and multi­UAV systems, highlighting the advan­
tages of multiple UAVs, categorizing their applications, and reviewing recent research
developments. According to that study, the key advantages of employing multiple UAVs
in a swarm configuration can be summarized as follows:

• Reduced operating cost – shared resources lower costs.
• Concurrent actions – simultaneous coverage of multiple areas.

Increasing the number of UAVs introduces greater complexity, necessitating strategies for

Some of the key application areas of UAV swarms can be summarized as follows (with
further details provided in [25]):
• Video surveillance: Video surveillance involves using cameras to monitor and
record activities in specific areas for security, safety, or operational purposes.
• Photogrammetry: Photogrammetry involves determining the precise positions of
surface points using quantitative image data.
• Networks: Enhancing communication in some areas, e.g. during military opera­

• Monitoring: Using UAVs to monitor road traffic or construction sites is highly ben­
eficial but underutilized due to limited flight times and higher costs compared to
alternatives like turrets or fixed balloons.
• Carry loads: Numerous studies explore cooperative load­carrying by multiple UAVs
for payloads exceeding the capacity of a single UAV.
• Search and rescue: With an UAV swarm search and rescue missions can be done
in a more efficient/quicker way.
• Additional applications: Examples include forest firefighting, meteorological mon­

Localization refers to the system used to determine an object’s position. Every position
is defined relative to a reference system [26]. The process of determining an object’s posi­
tion or location is termed positioning or geo­referencing [27]. It is important to note that the
terms positioning and localization are sometimes used ambiguously in the literature. In
wireless networking, UAV positioning may refer to placing or adjusting UAVs in 3D space
to optimize coverage or performance. In contrast, in localization research, positioning
often denotes the process of determining the absolute position of an object, for example
using GPS. In this thesis, the distinction follows the latter convention: localization refers to
estimating a UAV’s relative position based on inter­UAV measurements, while positioning
can be understood as assigning coordinates in a global reference frame, typically through
the use of anchors. However, for consistency, in the following sections the term localiza­
tion will be used throughout, even in cases where the position is ultimately expressed in
a global reference frame. In the context of SUAVs, position estimation is used to localize
a target or another UAV [28],[29]. In this thesis, the goal is to localize the UAVs within the
swarm based on relative measurements between members, and in dynamic scenarios

to also incorporate IMU data. Subsequently, with the aid of Anchor Nodes (ANs), these
relative positions are transformed into a global reference frame. There are different tech­
niques for localization, and in the literature different models are available, but the goal
is always to create a method which provides fast, reliable and highly accurate position
estimates [9]. Khelifi and Butun [30] provides a comprehensive overview of critical re­
quirements for localization approaches and examines key prospects and challenges in
obtaining localization data for SUAVs:
• Localization Accuracy: Localization accuracy measures how precisely a location
estimate matches the actual position, influenced by range measurement errors, UAV
noise calibration, network positioning, random actions, inaccurate models, and en­

2.3
Radio­Based Localization Techniques
In this section the focus is on Radio­Based localization techniques related things which
should be considered. Wireless, radio signals are EM waves used to transmit data with­
out physical connections. They operate across various frequencies, typically in the RF
range, from a few kilohertz to hundreds of gigahertz. The propagation of electromag­
netic waves occurs differently at different frequencies, resulting different communication
characteristics.
EM waves enable localization methods beyond data transmission by leveraging signal
propagation time (assuming known propagation speed), received signal strength (with
known transmitted power and a path loss model to estimate distance), and antenna pat­
terns to measure the AoA of radio signals, accurate UAV localization can be achieved. Dif­
ferent frequencies require distinct propagation models, each offering specific advantages
and disadvantages for localization due to varying propagation characteristics. Generally,
higher frequencies experience greater attenuation, while lower frequencies propagate fur­
ther. However, higher frequencies enable higher data rates for communication. One of
the most commonly used systems employing EM waves is radar, where the operating
frequency significantly influences performance, with higher frequencies (e.g., SHF, EHF)
offering greater resolution and accuracy for short­range UAV localization, while lower fre­
quencies (e.g., HF, VHF) enable longer­range detection but with reduced precision due
to larger wavelengths.
Yadav and Sharma [47] highlights the importance of accurate sensor node localization
in wireless sensor networks and location­based services, emphasizing hybrid machine
learning (e.g., neural networks, support vector machines) and optimization strategies to
model complex sensor­location correlations for precise node placement, while surveying
recent advancements, classifying localization algorithms, and outlining challenges and
future directions for integrating these techniques. This paper not specifically discuss UAV
localization, but some of the mentioned techniques are relevant to this topic, like it dis­
cuss localization schemes, and mention the different range­based localization methods,
which methods are important to know and understand in this topic. Range­based local­
ization techniques estimate sensor node positions in wireless sensor networks by mea­
suring node­to­node distances using methods like RSS, Time of Arrival (ToA), Time­of­
Flight (ToF), Time Difference of Arrival (TDoA), AoA, Round­Trip Time (RTT), and Doppler
Effect, employing trilateration, multi­lateration, triangulation, or multi­angulation, offering
high accuracy under line­of­sight conditions but facing challenges from signal fluctuations,
interference, and non­line­of­sight environments.
• Received Signal Strength (RSS):Estimates distance based on the strength of the
received signal, using a log­normal shadowing model where signal attenuation in­
creases with distance, but is affected by environmental factors like interference.
• Time of Arrival (ToA): Measures the time a signal takes to travel from a transmitter
to a receiver, requiring precise time synchronization to calculate distance based on
signal propagation speed.
• Time­of­Flight (ToF): Determines distance by measuring signal travel time, using
radio frequency (RF­ToF) or infrared (IR­ToF) signals, commonly for indoor position­
ing.
• Time Difference of Arrival (TDoA): Calculates distance by analyzing time differ­
ences in signal arrivals at multiple nodes, requiring synchronization and converting
12
Cooperative Localization in UAV Swarms Using Radio Signals
==PAGE:21==
time disparities into distances using signal speed.
• Angle of Arrival (AoA): Uses antenna arrays to measure signal arrival angles from
anchor nodes, estimating node position via trigonometric calculations, effective in
clear line­of­sight conditions.
• Round­Trip Time (RTT): Measures the time for a signal to travel from a sender to
a receiver and back, used in acoustic or radio­based systems for precise ranging in
applications like indoor navigation.
• Doppler Effect: Estimates distance or velocity by analyzing frequency shifts in sig­
nals due to relative motion between nodes, useful for tracking but sensitive to mul­
tipath interference.
• Trilateration: Uses distances from three anchor nodes to locate a node at the inter­
section of three circles, effective with RSS but less accurate in noisy environments.
• Multi­lateration: Extends trilateration by using multiple anchor nodes and minimiz­
ing distance errors, improving accuracy in complex settings.
• Triangulation: Determines node position using angle measurements from two an­
chor nodes, relying on basic trigonometry for AoA­based localization.
• Multi­angulation: Employs angle measurements from multiple anchor nodes to
compute node position, enhancing accuracy when reliable angle data are available.

2.4.1
Communication Technologies for UAVs
Since some of the relative localization methods ­ like MDS suppose the there is some
kind of communication between UAVs within the swarm, it is important to examine this
aspect as well, since it can influence the update rate and the scalability of the method.
Effective communication mechanisms are critical for the successful deployment of drone
swarms. Large­scale swarms require a fully distributed communication system that scales
efficiently and supports robust many­to­many interactions. Fundamentally, a drone swarm
operates as a dynamic wireless mesh network [58]. A common challenge in wireless net­
works, particularly in drone swarms, is the unknown and dynamically changing network
topology due to the relentless mobility of nodes [59]. The communication medium for
drone swarms must be adaptive and robust to handle dynamic changes in network topol­
ogy. At the startup of a multi­hop radio network, the absence of prior communication
infrastructure creates a bootstrap challenge, requiring the exchange of information to de­
termine the network topology, identify participating nodes, and configure medium access
control parameters. This process is characteristic of a wireless mesh network, which
autonomously configures and maintains itself as nodes are added, moved, or removed,
without prior knowledge of the topology. It is important to review wireless mesh networks
and evaluate the two most common communication techniques suitable for drone swarm
applications.
Wireless Mesh Networks (WMNs) provide a low­cost, scalable, cooperative solution for
wireless connectivity in applications like homes, businesses, and disaster scenarios, us­
ing an ad­hoc topology where nodes relay data, maximize bandwidth, and ensure spectral
efficiency through frequency hopping, though challenges include encouraging coopera­
tive node behavior and securing communication in hostile environments due to vulner­
abilities in the shared wireless medium. WMN can be designed using a routing tech­
nique or a flooding technique [60][58]. Routing technique involves message propagation
through node­to­node hopping until reaching the destination, using self­healing algorithms
to maintain network reliability by reconfiguring around broken paths and optimizing energy
use with multiple available paths [61]. Flooding technique ensures guaranteed mes­
sage delivery by broadcasting to all nodes in a network, utilizing a Synchronised­Flooding
approach to mitigate broadcast storms—where excessive retransmissions cause packet
collisions—and prevent energy­wasting avalanches, while employing time division access
and high­accuracy synchronization to maintain reliability across diverse network topolo­
gies, though it increases network traffic and may benefit from comparisons with routing­
based systems that optimize node usage and energy efficiency [62]. Routing consumes
less energy due to fewer operating nodes, while flooding­based messages are more effi­
cient by avoiding routing table overhead, with modern systems using adjacent node sig­
nals to reduce power needs; routing technologies are expected to have lower energy
consumption in smaller networks with few hops, whereas flooding­based technologies
excel in large networks or with small message sizes. Range and coverage in Drone
Swarm Communication (DSC) are enhanced in flooding­based mesh networks due to
energy summation from received nodes, outperforming sophisticated routing networks
with multiple propagation paths reducing dead spots, though latency in DSC varies by
application, with flooding­based mesh topologies offering lower overhead and consistent
performance across diverse conditions compared to routing’s potential hop­related de­
lays [58]. [9] presents an high­level communication architecture for SUAVs on Figure

Rigidity
The rigidity theory, which is an important part in case of cooperative localization is de­
scribed in this subsection based on Hamaoui [89], III. Background, A. Global Rigidity.
Most localization schemes operate under the fundamental assumption that a given set
of distance measurements corresponds to a unique coordinate configuration, up to rigid
transformations (translation, rotation, and reflection). In rigidity­theoretic terms, this re­
quires the underlying constraint graph G to be globally rigid—a property ensuring that
the graph admits exactly one geometric realization in the embedding space. Although
complete connectivity provides a sufficient condition for global rigidity, it represents a
conservative approach that is far from necessary, often requiring significantly more mea­
surements than the theoretical minimum. According to [90], given only partial range mea­
surements in p­dimensional space, achieving global rigidity requires that the constraint
graph G on N > p + 1 vertices satisfy two essential conditions: it must be vertex (p + 1)­
connected and redundantly rigid. A graph G is vertex­k connected if removing any k −1
vertices leaves the graph connected, while disconnecting the graph requires the removal
of at least k vertices. The condition of redundant rigidity further demands that G must
remain rigid, flex free, with the removal of any one edge. These necessary conditions
are also sufficient for global rigidity when p = 2 [91], but this equivalence breaks down
for p ≥3 [92]. While no combinatorial characterization of global rigidity has been estab­
lished for dimensions p ≥3 [93], an algebraic characterization based on the associated
stress matrix provides both necessary [94] and sufficient [95] conditions. Utilizing the
rigidity and stress matrices, constraint graphs can be systematically classified into four
distinct categories: flexible, locally rigid, globally rigid, or complete. This classifica­
tion directly reflects the geometric degeneracy of the feasible solution space. Flexible
configurations admit infinitely many realizations, while locally rigid configurations permit
only finitely many distinct solutions. Globally rigid graphs yield a unique realization (up
to Euclidean transformations), and complete graphs represent the most constrained case
with maximum redundancy. Vertex connectivity κ serves as a practical predictor of rigidity
class, with graphs exhibiting κ ≥5 showing strong correlation with global rigidity. This re­
lationship suggests that localization algorithm performance should improve monotonically
with both rigidity class and vertex connectivity, as higher connectivity provides greater
constraint redundancy and numerical stability [89].
The mentioned characterizations apply specifically to range­only measurement scenar­
ios. However, incorporating additional geometric constraints (AoA measurements) can
significantly relax the connectivity requirements for global rigidity. [96] demonstrates that
when both range and bearing measurements are available concurrently along each edge,
an p­dimensional realization of G achieves global rigidity if and only if the graph is 2­
connected—a dramatic reduction from the (p+1)­connectivity requirement for range­only
systems. A connected graph G is called 2­connected, if for every vertex x ∈V (G), G­x is
connected. This result assumes that bearing measurements are referenced to a global co­
ordinate frame. When bearing measurements are instead taken relative to each agent’s
local body frame, the theoretical framework becomes more complex. In this case, the
connectivity threshold for global rigidity should intuitively fall between the 2­connectivity
sufficient for global bearings and the more stringent algebraic conditions required for pure
range­based localization. [89]
A principled testing approach should progress from simpler, well­conditioned scenarios to
increasingly challenging cases, following the rigidity hierarchy. Specifically, testing should
begin with globally rigid configurations (high vertex connectivity) before advancing to lo­

failure cases is equally critical, as it reveals the practical limitations and breakdown points

The MDS­MAP algorithm for Wireless Sensor Network (WSN) localization is limited by

Traditional KF refinements

Poilinca et al. [11] the authors propose a computationally efficient two­phase localization
and tracking framework that combines SMDS with a Kalman filter. In the first phase, noisy
distance measurements are processed using SMDS to provide an initial estimate of the
target’s position. In the second phase, these position estimates are refined with an ex­
tended Kalman filter that incorporates acceleration measurements from low­power device
sensors, modeled with Gaussian noise on both axes. Although information theory proves
that such a sequential approach is sub­optimal, the study demonstrates through simula­
tion that the resulting accuracy is nevertheless sufficient for practical applications. The
experiments were carried out in a 2 × 2 m area with four anchors placed at the corners.
The target followed circular and polar trajectories, and both distance measurement noise
(σd = 0.1−0.5) and acceleration noise (σa = 0−3) were considered. Results show that
the SMDS+KF method consistently achieves lower root­mean­square errors than SMDS
alone. Even with highly noisy acceleration inputs, the Kalman filter remained robust, and
when acceleration data were too unreliable, an empirical version of the filter (ignoring
acceleration) still yielded better accuracy than pure SMDS. The authors conclude that,
despite its theoretical sub­optimality, the SMDS+KF pipeline offers a simple yet effective
low­cost solution for localization in wireless sensor networks. It provides a good trade­off
between computational complexity and positioning accuracy, with robustness preserved
across a wide range of noise conditions. In this thesis, the method was extended with
additional complexity to operate in 3D space for UAV swarm localization, with communi­
cation and computational latencies also taken into account.
This sequential two­phase approach is considered sub­optimal because the KF does not
directly use the raw distance and angle measurements. Instead, SMDS first computes
position estimates from these measurements while ignoring the dynamics, and only af­
terwards the KF refines the trajectory using acceleration. As a result, some information
is lost in the SMDS compression step compared to a joint estimation scheme that would
fuse all measurements simultaneously.

Machine Learning Approach
This part of the thesis provides a brief introduction to Machine Learning (ML), presents
selected localization­related research within the field, and outlines the fundamental prin­

Introduction to Machine Learning
ML is not a new technology, but in recent years it has gained significant popularity. This
rise is largely due to advances in computational power, which have made it feasible to train
models that appear to demonstrate intelligent behavior. Machine learning models are built
using a combination of algorithms and data. Beyond the choice of model architecture and
training method, both the quality and the quantity of data play a crucial role; in general,
larger and better­structured datasets enable improved performance. The data collection
process and the structure of the dataset used in this project are described in chapter 3.
An ML model can be understood as a mathematical construct with predictive power: given
the independent variables of a data record, it produces an estimate of the dependent
variable. Various approaches exist for training such models, and the most appropriate
method depends on the task and the available data. The main training paradigms are
supervised, unsupervised, semi­supervised, and reinforcement learning. [108]
In this thesis, the focus is on supervised learning, where predictions are made using a
labeled training dataset. Supervised learning can be further divided into classification and
regression. Regression, which aims to predict continuous values such as coordinates, is
particularly relevant in this context. A wide range of algorithms can be used for regression,
but in this work two methods are examined in more detail—those that delivered the best
performance for the studied localization task.
Neural Network
Neural networks can be understood as an extension of classical linear
models. In a linear model, the output is expressed as a weighted sum of a fixed set of basis
functions, which are predetermined transformations of the input variables. The limitation
of this approach is that the choice of basis functions strongly affects performance. Neural
networks address this by letting the basis functions themselves be parameterized and
learned from data, alongside the model weights.
A basic feed­forward neural network, also called a Multi­Layer Perceptron (MLP), is built
from layers of computational units (or neurons). Each unit first computes a weighted sum
of its inputs, often written as

By stacking multiple layers of such units, the network can model highly nonlinear functions.
A key theoretical result is that a two­layer feed­forward network with suitable activation
functions can approximate any continuous function on a compact domain to arbitrary ac­

In practice, however, deeper architectures are used, since they can represent hierarchical
structures in the data and achieve the same accuracy with fewer units.
Training a neural network involves adjusting all weights and biases to minimize an error
function that measures the difference between predictions and targets. This is usually
done using gradient­based optimization methods, such as stochastic gradient descent.
Because all operations in the network are differentiable, the gradient of the error can be
computed efficiently using the backpropagation algorithm, which propagates error signals
layer by layer through the network.
Once trained, a neural network can generalize from the examples it has seen and predict
outputs for unseen input data. This combination of flexible representation and scalable
training methods has made neural networks a foundation of modern machine learning.

Random Forest Regressor
Random Forest is an ensemble learning method that com­
bines the predictions of multiple decision trees to improve accuracy and stability. It is a
supervised algorithm that can be applied to both classification and regression tasks. In the
regression setting, the output is a continuous value obtained by averaging the predictions
of many trees. More information about decision trees can be found at [110].
Each tree is trained on a random subset of the data (bootstrap sampling) and built using
a random subset of features (feature sampling), which ensures diversity among the trees.
The final prediction is obtained by aggregating the outputs of all trees. This reduces the
high variance of individual decision trees and improves the model’s generalization.
Random Forest Regression is widely used for tasks such as predicting house prices,
analyzing high­dimensional data, and modeling complex, non­linear relationships.
Its
advantages include robustness to overfitting, ability to handle outliers.
However, the
method also has drawbacks: it can be computationally expensive and memory inten­
sive, less interpretable than simpler models, and may struggle with highly imbalanced or
noisy datasets. Despite these limitations, Random Forest is a powerful tool for continuous
prediction tasks and remains a standard baseline in many applications [111].
Localization with Machine Learning
Artificial intelligence (AI) and ML techniques have become key enablers for SUAV local­
ization, as they can learn environmental properties and adapt the behavior of the swarm to
improve position estimation. Owing to their mobility, SUAVs can autonomously enhance
their performance using AI/ML methods, achieving more accurate localization. However,
these approaches also face challenges: adapting to dynamic conditions requires handling
an ever­growing volume of training data, which limits scalability. In addition, they increase
computational complexity and are generally unsuitable for on­board execution due to the
restricted processing capacity available for model training and decision­making [9].

Scenario/world.
For the experiments, the KSQL Airport world was used [113], which
replicates San Carlos Airport (ICAO: KSQL) in California. The airport scenery introduces
a structured and realistic environment with open spaces, buildings, and potential obstruc­
tions. This provided a richer testbed than an abstract simulation space, allowing for more
representative evaluation of localization methods. Within this setting, swarms of up to 10
UAVs could be deployed, and IMU data was generated by the simulator to emulate real
flight dynamics.
RF propagation modeling.
To enhance realism, a GPU­accelerated ray­tracing tool
was integrated with Gazebo. This tool exploits Nvidia RTX hardware for real­time ray
tracing [114], enabling accurate modeling of RF propagation. It accounts for multipath
effects, reflections from surfaces, and material properties (e.g., metals or composites),
as well as mobility and dynamic obstacles. Although the tool does not directly provide
RSS, it outputs path­loss values that are strongly correlated. Custom integration code
was developed to collect these RF measurements during simulations, ensuring consistent
data availability for training and evaluation. A more detailed description of the ray­tracing
approach can be found in [115].
Flight­control stack.
The UAVs in Gazebo were controlled via PX4­Autopilot, an open­
source flight controller widely used in both academic and industrial applications [116]. PX4
was integrated into the simulation through ROS and the MAVROS MAVLink node, which
served as a bridge between the autopilot software, Gazebo’s physics engine, and higher­
level control modules [35]. This setup allowed bi­directional communication of telemetry,
sensor data, and control commands, providing a realistic representation of a real­world
UAV communication and control architecture.
Limitations.
Despite its advantages, the environment has several limitations. The most
notable is scalability: Gazebo could reliably simulate a maximum of 10 UAVs simulta­
neously, which constrained the maximum swarm sizes that could be studied. Another
limitation is its restricted support for antenna modeling. Only isotropic and Gaussian an­
tenna models are available, and isotropic antennas were used in this thesis due to their
simplicity and compatibility with the framework. This prevented the direct simulation of
AoA measurements, since the limited antenna models cannot fully capture the directional
properties required for accurate angle estimation. As a result, AoA measurements were
derived indirectly through additional processing.

Furthermore, the simulator provides path­loss values rather than raw RSS measurements.
Although RSS can still be calculated by fixing transmitter power and antenna gains, this

abstracts certain physical effects, such as hardware impairments and detailed antenna
radiation patterns.
Advantages.
Despite these constraints, the environment provides important advan­
tages. Its main strength lies in the ability to simulate dynamic swarm behavior while
incorporating accurate wave­propagation modeling using GPU­accelerated ray tracing.
This enables the realistic replication of reflections, obstructions, and other environmental
effects, significantly improving the fidelity of performance evaluations compared to the
simplified static simulator. In this way, the environment represents a crucial intermediate
step between purely idealized models and real­world experiments.
Use within this thesis.
In this thesis, the Gazebo­based environment was used to col­
lect realistic datasets for both static and dynamic scenarios. In particular, it provided IMU
data and ray­traced RF measurements, which were essential for testing ML­based meth­
ods and Kalman filter integration. While dynamic behavior could not be simulated in the
basic Python framework, the Gazebo setup enabled more representative evaluation of
algorithms under motion, realistic propagation conditions, and controlled noise assump­
tions. Thus, it served as the primary environment for data generation and advanced test­
ing, complementing the lightweight simulator described earlier.
3.3
Data Collection in the Realistic Environment
Purpose and scope.
The realistic simulation environment was not only used to evalu­
ate localization methods, but also to generate datasets required for training and testing
the implemented algorithms. The goal of the data collection process was to obtain real­
istic measurements that approximate what would be available in a real swarm of UAVs,
including RF propagation characteristics, IMU outputs, and positional ground truth. These
datasets serve as the foundation for comparing MDS­based and ML­based localization
methods under controlled but representative conditions.
Collected data.
For each UAV, the following information was extracted during simula­
tion:
• Path loss values between pairs of UAVs, computed using the GPU­accelerated ray­
tracing model. Only the lowest­loss single path was stored for each link, approxi­
mating the dominant propagation component.
• Raw IMU sensor data (accelerometer and gyroscope readings), representing noisy
motion dynamics.
• Ground­truth positions of each UAV, recorded in both local and global coordinate
frames.
The collected features for each node are summarized in table 3.1. In total, every dataset
contains more than 64,000 rows per UAV, which was sufficient for both training and vali­

Implemented localization methods
In this thesis, three main approaches were implemented for cooperative localization. The
first approach involved implementing several MDS­based methods, following descriptions
and formulations found in the scientific literature. The second approach focused on train­
ing machine learning models using data collected from the Gazebo simulation environ­
ment, applied to the same localization task. These two categories of methods were pri­
marily evaluated in static scenarios. The third approach employed KF­based techniques,
both in combination with MDS­based methods and as a standalone joint KF.
4.1
MDS­based Methods
This thesis implemented multiple MDS­based methods, built sequentially, since some of
them are closely related to each other. These algorithms were integrated with KF using
IMU data to enable real­time localization for SUAVs in section 4.3. The implementation of
all of this methods is implemented using Python 3.11.11 in PyCharm environment. The
exact implementation can be seen in the attached code, in this part the most important
features are mentioned and the implemented algorithms. The problem formulation is
the following. MDS­based methods address localization geometrically, excluding commu­
nication within the algorithm. However, communication between UAVs/nodes or a central
entity is required for data collection and computation. Consider a p = 3 dimensional Eu­
clidean space consisting of N nodes, among which NA nodes are designated as ANs
with known, error­free locations. The remaining NT = N−NA nodes, referred to as Tar­
get Nodess (TNs), have unknown locations to be estimated in a p dimensional space,
where p = 3 in this case. This is the general problem formulation of the MDS based local­
ization task that is implemented in this section, however it is important, that the problem
formulation and/or the assumptions are slightly different in case of each method. These
differences and assumptions are mentioned here in this section.
The first method implemented was CMDS, as outlined in algorithm 1. This algorithm as­
sumes a complete Euclidean Distance Matrix (EDM) (D) is available. In cases where
the complete EDM is not available, the alternating rank­based EDM completion method
(Algorithm 2 in [74]) was employed. While not originally part of the main algorithm, this
step was implemented in this thesis to enable the simulation of scenarios with missing
measurements. Without the optional Procrustes analysis, the resulting point configura­
tion preserves distances but remains ambiguous up to translation, rotation, and reflec­
tion. In most localization applications, this ambiguity is impractical, making alignment to
a global reference frame essential. This alignment relies on ANs, requiring at least three
non­collinear anchors in 2­Dimensional (2D) or four non­coplanar anchors in 3D. During
testing, the error of different methods was evaluated by comparing true positions with
those obtained from the method and transformed back to the global reference frame.

The implemented MDS­MAP(P) method estimates node positions in partially connected
networks. Each node computes a local map using distances to nearby nodes within a
specified hop limit (e.g., 2 hops), identified via Breadth­First Search (BFS) on the con­
nection matrix. Local distance matrices are built from the input squared distance matrix,
with unconnected pairs assigned a default distance of 1. Floyd­Warshall computes short­

local maps. These maps are merged into a core map by selecting maps with the most
common nodes and aligning them using Procrustes analysis. Finally, the core map is
transformed to global coordinates using anchor node positions. This method excels in
distributed settings, as each node can compute its local map independently, reducing
centralized computation. It handles sparse networks effectively, estimating unknown dis­
tances via shortest paths. The use of local maps makes it scalable for large networks,
and Procrustes alignment ensures robust merging and global coordinate accuracy. The
implemented algorithm can be seen on algorithm 3.

4.2
Machine Learning­based Methods
For MDS­based methods, the global position of target UAVs can be estimated using dis­
tance measurements between UAVs, and with additional AoA measurements in case of
SMDS, and four ANs with known positions. Various methods exist to address missing
measurements (like the implemented EDM completition algorithm), while noisy and miss­
ing measurements pose an additional challenge. Machine learning­based methods, offer
advantages over MDS by learning from noisy or incomplete distance data to predict po­
sitions. If these models are computationally lightweight, they can be deployed on UAVs,
potentially outperforming MDS­based methods.
4.2.1
Datasets
Multiple datasets were prepared to evaluate the models, differing in the type of mea­
surement data while keeping the number of ANs fixed at four. This design ensures a
consistent reference framework while allowing the impact of different sensing modalities
to be analyzed. The datasets can be grouped into the following categories:
1. ANs positions combined with ground­truth distance measurements.
2. ANs positions, ground­truth distance measurements, and AoA measurements.
3. ANs positions together with path loss values (RSS).
4. ANs positions, path loss values, and AoA measurements.
To simulate realistic conditions, noise was added to certain datasets, and measurements
were intentionally removed to mimic missing data. This preprocessing step took place
before model training. The training data (X) was flattened, with missing pathloss or dis­
tance measurements filled with a value of 200, and missing AoA measurements filled with
999. These numbers are outside of the range that can be seen in the data, and intend to
clearly distinct the missing data.
4.2.2
Baseline Models
Baseline models were first implemented using scikit-learn [118] to verify whether the
created datasets contained sufficient information for learning. These models served as a
reference point for evaluating the performance of the other implemented methods. These
models were chosen for their simplicity. The initial task focused on a swarm of five UAVs,
consisting of four ANs and a single TN. The objective was to learn the position of the
TN based on the measurements and the known positions of the ANs. The implemented
baseline models include a MLP, a Support Vector Regressor (SVR) with a Radial Basis
Function (RBF) kernel, and a Random Forest Regressor (RFR), all trained to predict 3D
positions (x, y, z) of target UAV(s). During the training of these baseline models no pre
processing on the data was performed.
The MLP model, built with MLPRegressor, consists of three hidden layers with 128, 64,
and 32 neurons, respectively, using ReLU activation, and an output layer for 3D coor­
dinates. It was trained using the Adam optimizer and Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss.
The SVR model, implemented as a MultiOutputRegressor with an RBF kernel, handles
multi­dimensional output for 3D positions. The RFR model was configured with 100 esti­
mators. These baseline models were useful to test that the data is enough good to learn
patterns from it, and it was a good way to test the quality of the created data. During these

Graph Neural Network
In this thesis, it was also investigated whether GNNs could be applied to cooperative lo­
calization in SUAVs. Since the data can naturally be represented as a graph, and because
GNNs inherently support the handling of missing edges and features, they appear to be
a promising direction for this problem. Several different types of GNN architectures were
tested, including models based on EGNN, GINEConv, NNConv, and GATv2Conv layers, taken
into consideration that both edge and node features should be handled by the layer. All
implementations were created using the torch_geometric.nn framework.
Despite the theoretical suitability of graph­based learning for this task, the results obtained
were not encouraging. Even in the baseline case, where the true distance measurements
were provided without any noise, the tested GNN models consistently underperformed
compared to the previously discussed models, such as the Deep Neural Network (DNN)
and the RFR. For example, using the SimpleEGNNLocalization architecture (attache in
implementation), the mean error of the predictions was approximately 28.98 m, with an er­
ror variance of about 266.92 m2. The other GNN based methods provided similar results.
These results are significantly worse than those achieved with the other approaches pre­
sented in this thesis.
Several possible explanations can be identified for this poor performance. First, while
GNNs are effective at modeling relational structures, they typically require very large and
diverse training datasets to generalize well. In this work, the dataset was limited, and
this may have restricted the ability of the models to capture the underlying spatial rela­
tionships. Second, the architectures themselves may not have been optimally designed
for this specific problem, as the tested models were relatively standard implementations
adapted from existing libraries. More tailored designs, with careful tuning of hyperparam­
eters and graph­specific features, could potentially yield better performance. In this case,

GNNs have strong theoretical appeal for cooperative localization, their practical appli­
cation is non­trivial and requires further research. In particular, future work could focus
on specialized architectures designed for geometric reasoning, and hybrid models that
combine classical localization techniques with GNNs to leverage the strengths of both. Al­
though the results presented here were unsatisfactory, they highlight important challenges
and possible directions for improvement in applying graph­based learning to SUAV local­
ization. Since all of the obtained results were unsatisfactory, even in the baseline case,
and because the training time of GNNs was significantly higher than that of the other
tested methods, it was decided not to pursue this approach further within the scope of
this work.
4.3
Localization with Kalman Filter
MDS­based localization encounters delays from measurement, communication, and com­
putation. In contrast, Dead Reckoning (DR) using IMU data (e.g. [119]) offers faster po­
sition estimation. However, IMU sensor errors can accumulate, leading to inaccurate
estimates over time (see fig. A.9). To address this, combining distance and/or AoA mea­
surements (MDS methods) with IMU data (KF­based methods) improves accuracy. In
this thesis the implemented MDS based methods were integrated with an EKF, similar
to [11]. In this context the IMU data means the linear acceleration, the angular velocity
and also the orientation. Orientation is actually not available as a raw IMU data, it is cal­
culated by the flight controller (PX4 in this case) from sensor measurements, but here in
the implemented filter they were used to calculated the kinematics.
More techniques were considered during the integration phase. Since SMDS can predict
positions using only a single AN, it initially appeared to be a promising solution, as in that
case no global position information would be required. One could assume that a chosen
UAV is always placed at the origin, and all others are localized relative to it. While this is
indeed possible with SMDS, it introduces a moving reference frame. The problem is that,
between two SMDS updates, the estimated states expressed in this coordinate system
become inconsistent.
To illustrate, consider a swarm of five UAVs, none of which has access to a global refer­
ence. If one UAV is arbitrarily chosen as an AN, SMDS can still reconstruct the swarm’s
relative geometry (assuming global AoA measurements are available). However, as the
swarm moves, the coordinate system itself shifts with the chosen anchor. This means
that even a UAV which has not moved (according to its IMU data) will still receive a new
position at the next SMDS update—an inconsistency caused purely by the moving refer­
ence. To avoid this issue, it was assumed that at least four true ANs exist, providing a
stable global reference frame.
Another approach, which served as a prototype for the final implementation, was to initial­
ize the swarm positions using an MDS estimate. After initialization, each UAV propagated
its state using its own IMU data and a local estimator, while periodically receiving updated
positions from MDS. This approach already acknowledged that the MDS­based update
rate is slower than dead reckoning, but it still implicitly assumed that the updates reflect
the current measurements, which is not realistic.

4.3.1
MDS and KF Integration
This section presents a fusion framework for SUAV localization, integrating IMU data
with delayed MDS position estimates. An EKF tracks a 6­dimensional state vector of
3D position and velocity. MDS derives global position estimates from inter­UAV distance
measurements, incorporating a delay to model real­world computational and communi­
cation latencies. Delayed measurements are managed through state buffering and re­
propagation, ensuring temporal consistency. The approach balances high­frequency IMU
updates with lower­frequency MDS corrections for robust localization, which could be de­
ployed on target UAVs. The computational complexity also highly dependent on the used
MDS­based method. A more complex MDS­based method could be more precise, but
can introduce more latency.
In the implemented method in the first step an MDS based position estimation is per­
formed. It is assumed that the UAVs have zero speed/acceleration at this time. In this
model it is assumed, that the IMU data is arriving with the same speed like the data col­
lection rate, so T = TIMU = 0.1s. This time is the timestep of the KF. As for the state
estimator an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) was used, since it can have low computa­
tional complexity (the Jacobians are calculated analitically), but at the same time it can
work with nonlinear systems. The noise distribution coming from the MDS­based meth­
ods were tested, plotted in chapter 5, and in most of the cases, they can be considered as
Gaussian noise. The frequency of the MDS based updates are smaller than the frequency
of the IMU updates, which means that the delay between two MDS based updates are
higher (TMDS > TIMU). More TMDS was tested during the simulations, it is a parameter
which can be changed. After the initial MDS based position estimation, the UAVs (TNs)
start to estimate their position based on the IMU data, using EKF (predict), and in every
TMDS time receives an update from the MDS­based method. The MDS updates are de­
layed (because of the communication), and this is taken into consideration by saving the
state of the filter and buffering IMU data, and when an update arrives, it is propagated
through the saved filter state with the buffered IMU data. The high level working of the
filter can be seen on fig. 4.1.

surements, all of the examined MDS­based methods achieve nearly perfect results, i.e.,
they yield zero localization error. This serves as an important baseline and confirms that,
under ideal conditions, the algorithms behave as expected. In contrast, when the largest
examined distance measurement error of 10 m is considered, the performance differences
between the methods become clear. Specifically, the SMDS method (without AoA error)
provides the most accurate estimates, achieving an MAE of approximately 6 m and an
error variance of about 5.7 m2. For the other methods, namely MDS, MDS­MAP, and
MDS­MAP(P), the errors are considerably larger, with MAEs of roughly 13 m and error
variances of approximately 25 m2.
Another clear observation is that there exists an approximately linear relationship between
the standard deviation of the distance measurement noise and the resulting localization
MEDE for all tested methods. The same holds true for the error variances, which also
grow roughly linearly with increasing measurement noise. However, the sensitivity of the
methods to noise is not identical. For MDS, MDS­MAP, and MDS­MAP(P), the slope of the
linear relationship is noticeably steeper, indicating a stronger sensitivity to measurement
errors. In contrast, SMDS exhibits a more moderate increase in errors as noise grows,
which highlights its relative robustness. The reason behind this improved robustness is
that, in the case of SMDS, more measurements are available to the algorithm, and these
additional measurements can be fused effectively, provided that no AoA error is present.
In the absence of AoA errors, the SMDS method provides the most accurate results
among all the tested approaches, both in terms of MEDE and error variance. This clearly
demonstrates the strength of SMDS under ideal conditions, where the additional mea­
surements can be effectively utilized by the algorithm. However, the results also confirm
a critical limitation of this method: when Gaussian noise with a standard deviation greater
than 10◦is added to the angle measurements, the localization accuracy becomes worse
drastically. Under such conditions, the method produces very large errors, and both the
MAE and the error variance fluctuate significantly, reaching extremely high values. This
behavior indicates that SMDS is highly sensitive to large angular errors, which can domi­
nate the performance of the method and render it unreliable in such scenarios.

Joint Kalman Filter
For the joint EKF, simulations similar to the previous ones were performed, but under
slightly different assumptions. Here, it is assumed that each UAV can transmit its IMU
data at every timestep to a central entity. Whereas the earlier approach assumed that
the filters run individually on each target UAV, in this case a single joint filter is used to
localize all targets within the swarm. If the estimated positions from this joint filter had to
be communicated back to the individual UAVs, additional delays would arise; however,
such delays are not considered in these simulations. Instead, it is assumed that if all UAVs
can provide their IMU data at a given timestep, they can also share their measurements
accordingly. This represents a stricter requirement than in the previous case.
The joint EKF produced better results in all cases compared to the previously presented
MDS methods integrated with the EKF. This outcome was expected, since in these ex­
periments it was assumed that, for the joint EKF, no communication delay is present and
that all IMU and distance measurement data can be transmitted to a central entity (for
example, a ground station) at every timestep, where the computations are performed.
On fig. 5.16b and fig. 5.17b it can be observed that both the mean distance measurement
errors and the error variances are lower compared to the previously discussed cases.
Although the plots still exhibit error spikes, these are noticeably smaller than those seen
in the SMDS integration. The error curves are not completely flat and show some fluctu­
ations, which are reflected in the reported variances. It is reasonable to assume that the
residual errors in these cases, as well as the overall performance of the filter, could be
further improved by incorporating a richer state representation within the KF. Moreover, in
scenarios with missing measurements, a richer state representation is expected to yield
better performance compared to filters with fewer states.
Moreover, in this centralized setup the central entity must also communicate the results
back to the individual UAVs whenever they require location information for decision­making.
This additional step introduces communication overhead, which is not present in the dis­
tributed approaches.

6
Discussion
It is important to emphasize, in the case of SMDS, that the observation regarding the oc­
currence of large errors under substantial angle measurement noise must be interpreted in
light of how the noise was modeled in the simulations. Much of the related literature relies
on somewhat different experimental conditions. In particular, the test areas considered in
most prior works are typically much smaller (in case of [11] as well), and alternative noise
models are often applied (with different distribution). Moreover, for SMDS, a common
practice in the literature is to introduce noise directly to the angle between vectors. By
contrast, in this thesis the noise was applied to the underlying measurements themselves.
This distinction is significant, as it directly affects how errors propagate through the algo­
rithm and, ultimately, how the performance is evaluated. As a result, it is expected that
the outcomes obtained here differ from those reported in [73], where angular noise had a
much smaller influence on the performance. In the present simulations, however, intro­
ducing angular noise beyond approximately 10◦leads to a much stronger degradation of
localization accuracy, with errors dominating the results once this threshold is exceeded.
Another important observation concerns the ML­based methods. In this thesis, the train­
ing and test datasets were both collected within the same physical area, meaning that the
models primarily performed interpolation rather than extrapolation. While this setup is ac­
ceptable for distance measurements, it also implies that the anchor node positions used
as inputs to the models were limited to values drawn from that specific region. Conse­
quently, if the models were tested with anchor positions that differ substantially from those
seen during training, their performance would likely degrade significantly. In contrast, this
limitation does not apply to MDS­based methods, which do not rely on prior training data
in the same way. This restriction should therefore be regarded as a limitation of the ML­
based approach, although further experiments would be necessary to fully validate this
assumption.

7
Conclusion
Problem and goal.
This thesis addresses cooperative localization in swarms of SUAVs
using inter­swarm RF measurements (RSS­based ranging, AoA) and IMU data. The core
problem is to fuse distributed, noisy measurements to recover agents’ relative/absolute 3D
positions in both static and dynamic scenarios, possibly under partial connectivity. The
goal is to design, implement, and compare practical algorithms—including MDS­based
approaches, ML­based methods, and iterative KF/EKF estimators—in order to identify a
method that combines low localization error with high update rates, making it suitable for
real­time deployment. All evaluations were carried out in simulation under varying noise
conditions.
Main contributions/results
The main research questions were the following:
• Can ML­based methods be used as an alternative to MDS­based methods for co­
operative localization of SUAVs?
• Can an MDS­based method be effectively integrated with a KF for dynamic cooper­
ative localization of SUAVs in 3D space?
To answer the research questions, custom simulation environments were developed and
used to collect data for the task. Based on insights from the scientific literature, advanced
localization algorithms were then implemented and compared with a focus on the SUAV
use case. In the static scenario, the implemented algorithms were evaluated both against
each other and directly against ML­based methods, representing a novel contribution to
the field. In the dynamic scenario, MDS was integrated with the KF, and in addition, a joint
KF was implemented and assessed. Based on these experiments, the research questions
can be briefly answered as follows:
• RQ1: Yes. In several cases, ML­based models provided better results than MDS­
based methods in terms of accuracy, prediction time, and handling of missing con­
nections. However, their applicability depends on multiple factors, which are dis­
cussed in detail in section 5.2.2 and chapter 6.
• RQ2: Yes. This was demonstrated in tests where simulated IMU data was fused
with MDS­based methods under varying measurement errors. Nevertheless, under
different constraints, other methods may achieve superior results.
Limitations
The main limitation of this thesis is that all evaluations were carried out in
simulation. Real­world effects such as multipath propagation, packet loss, and complex
noise characteristics were not explicitly modeled. Another limitation is that certain sim­
ulation cases were not explored. For example, the effect of AoA errors on ML­based
methods was not investigated, even though such errors are relevant in practice. Includ­
ing these cases would likely provide a more complete picture of the methods’ robustness.
In addition, the reported computational times are specific to the chosen implementations
and hardware. In this work, connections between UAVs were modeled as random rather
than adaptive. In reality, however, connectivity should depend on factors such as inter­
UAV distance or link quality, meaning that UAVs separated by large distances would nat­
urally experience missing measurements. Finally, the study considered only a subset
Cooperative Localization in UAV Swarms Using Radio Signals
75
==PAGE:84==
of MDS­based methods (MDS, MDS­MAP, MDS­MAP(P), SMDS). While this provided a
solid baseline for comparison, many other variants exist in the literature table 2.2, that
were not implemented here. Including such methods would allow a broader assessment
of relative performance.
Future work
Several extensions of this work could be pursued. First, incorporating non­
Gaussian noise models or conducting real­world experiments with UAVs would enable
more realistic validation. Second, richer state representations, such as those described
in [122], could improve estimation accuracy in dynamic scenarios. Third, dynamically as­
signing anchor nodes depending on GPS availability would make the system more robust.
Fourth, additional simulation studies could investigate cases not covered here, such as
the impact of AoA errors on ML­based methods. Finally, an interesting direction would
be to integrate ML­based models with filtering techniques such as the KF, combining the
flexibility of data­driven methods with the robustness of recursive estimation.
