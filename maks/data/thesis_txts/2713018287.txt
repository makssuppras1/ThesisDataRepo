Abstract
Ports and harbours are often affected by accumulation of sediment in the basin and at the navigation
channel and main factors which influence the deposition of sediment material are the hydrodynamic
parameters and the orientation and width of the entrance.
To avoid navigability problems connected to drastic changes in bathymetry into the harbour and at
its entrance and prevent the economic loss related to the reduction of space, thus the reduction of
the number of ships that can be accommodated, periodic maintenance such as dredging is required.
When the deposition of a consistent amount of sediment occurs during a relatively short time-
span, the maintenance can become expensive and the construction of additional structures or the
extension of existing ones could represent a more efficient solution, as well as a long-term one.
In order to plan and assess the effectiveness of changes in the layout of the harbour, it is necessary a
thorough understanding of the conditions that generate the problem. This represented the primary
goal of the present thesis with regards to the harbour of Korsør and allowed to investigate, in a
later step of the study, three new design proposals for the marina. The whole process was carried
out using some of the modules contained in the software package MIKE 21 powered by DHI Group.
The same software was used to examine the effect of incident waves into the harbour. Over the
years, Korsør Marina has experienced significant wave disturbance during violent storms and it
often represents a risk for moored ships. Indeed, calm conditions inside the harbour are essential
to ensure the safety of anchored yachts, hence, the functionality of the structure.
As for the used models, MIKE 21 Spectral Wave FM is often employed in connection to other MIKE
21 models as it can provide the required input data. Indeed, the first step of the study consisted
of several simulations in SW which allowed to obtain, from recordings of wind speed and direction,
inputs such as wave parameters (i.e. significant wave height Hs, peak wave period Tp, directional
standard deviation DSD and mean wave direction MWD) and radiation stresses. Those outputs
were in turn required to run other models, i.e. Littoral Drift, Coupled Model FM and Boussinesq
Waves model.

ii
With regards to the organization of the thesis, in Chapter 1 and 2 the Introduction to the problem
and the process of data collection are respectively described. In chapter 2 to 5 the used models
are presented in terms of governing equations, available options and their applicability. The setup
used for each of them is reported. In particular, Chapter 2 is dedicated to the Spectral Waves
FM (SW) model, which is employed both separately and in the integrated model Coupled Model
FM. A number of simulations were run in SW: 1 simulation using real, but modified, recordings of
wind speed and direction and 1 for each 1-week storm that was designed in order to identify the
origin of the sand accumulating into the marina. From the results obtained using the 3.6-years of
recordings, data were extracted at specified points along the coastline to simulate the longshore
transport in Littoral Processes Fm - Littoral Drift, which is described in Chapter 3. The whole
chapter deals with the sand deposition in Korsør harbour and the two models utilized to investigate
it, Littoral Processes FM (LT) and the Sand Transport module (ST), are introduced. As for the
wave disturbance problem, it is analysed with the help of the Boussinesq Waves (BW) model. The
results of each of the analysis are summarized and presented in Chapter 6.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Korsør Marina is located on the West coast of Zealand, Denmark, South of the Great Belt Bridge.
Its entrance faces South and it currently consists of 6 piers, half of them fixed (piers 1, 2 and 5)
and the remaining half floating (piers 3, 4 and 6). In Figure 1.1, it is reported the original layout
of the marina (included numbering of the piers) with the recent extension of pier 4 added in the
attempt to provide suitable mooring conditions to the ships. Indeed, over the years, the harbour
has been affected by wave disturbance problems and, although the changes had the sole purpose
of mitigating the wave conditions at the piers, it was observed that during violent storms yachts
anchored at the outer arm of pier 4, which points North and shelters pier 5, and between piers 3
and 4 are exposed to particularly large waves and often need to be moved to a more protected area.

2
Introduction
The problem of disturbance into the harbour has been previously studied for the original design
of the harbour (before the extension of pier 4) by the DHI Group, [1], and Orbicon A/S, [2], and
their observations and results, together with the details to the problem description provided by two
representatives of the Board of Directors of Korsør Marina, have been considered as starting point
and inspiration for the present analysis. In both mentioned documents, the wave disturbance is
described in relation to the width of the entrance, ≈80m, and reflection of waves at the northern
breakwater further into the marina. Waves approaching a boundary which prevents them to prop-
agate undisturbed can be dissipated, transmitted or reflected. Generally, a combination of all these
transformations occurs. Reflection is a physical process occurring when waves meet a barrier that
prevents them to continue and propagate in the same direction and are reflected back, changing the
direction of propagation. Wave reflection is an important aspect to address when determining the
optimal layout of a harbour as a) reflected waves can propagate towards more sheltered portions of
the harbour and be a risk for moored boats, b) the interaction between approaching and reflected
waves can give rise to steep and unstable waves, especially hazardous for small size boats[3].
In the report by DHI, waves incoming from two directions, South and South-southwest, were iden-
tified as main responsible for the generation of disturbance inside the harbour. The current layout
of the harbour and two new proposals were investigated for varying wave parameters and varying
reflection coefficients and it was assessed that increasing the absorption, thus preventing reflection,
at the northern breakwater of waves incoming from SSW and SW, the ratio between the wave
height inside and the wave height outside of the harbour would be reduced by 20-30%.
The harbour as it is today is shown in Figure 1.3 and Figure 1.3. In these aerial pictures, it is possible
to observe the second problem this thesis is going to analyse and discuss: a great accumulation of
sand at the entrance, especially along the southern breakwater, and inside the harbour, mostly at
the northern part and at pier 6. Dredging processes in the marina have been carried out in 2017
and 2020. Until now the wave conditions which cause the deposition of sand right outside and into
the marina are undetermined, therefore, the directions of motion of the sediment accumulating in
the marina is unknown.
Based on the available information, the purpose of the study was to acquire a deeper understanding
of the causes of the two mentioned problems, investigating the sea state conditions and the occurring
phenomena, and to assess which changes could be beneficial to improve the viability of the harbour
and possibly increase its revenue through additional floating bridges. To achieve the goal, different
models of the software package MIKE 21 powered by the DHI Group were used.

Chapter 2
Collection of Data
No knowledge regarding bathymetric data of the area or sea-state conditions was available prior
to the beginning of the study, hence, the first step of the analysis consisted of collecting useful
information to generate input for the models.
Many sources were explored and, in some cases, used in early stages of the project, such as wind
speed w10 (wind speed at 10m above the mean water level) and wind direction recordings available
on the open data portal of the Danish Meteorologial Institut (DMI) [4], but the data kept for the
final version of the models are:
• 40 years of wind speed, w10, and wind direction recordings downloaded from MetOcean Data
Portal (DHI Group) [5]
• Bathymetric data obtained from EMODnet Bathymetry (European Directory of Marine Or-
ganizations) [6]
The bathymetric dataset requested on the EMODnet-Bathymetry portal was sent as ASCII file
and employed in the geographic information system free-source application QGIS to extract water
depths at random points inside the area of interest. The list of points with geographical coordinates
and related water depths was saved as .cvs file and uploaded as input for the generation of the
unstructured meshes then used as input for the models. The bathymetric data was accurate enough

local scale. In opposition, the parametric formulation is selected for the small scale simulation as
the boundary conditions were applied as wave parameters and generally for nearshore propagation
the two spectral formulations lead to slightly differing results but with very different computation
times [8].
SW also allows to choose the time formulation between Quasi-stationary formulation and Insta-
tionary formulation. The Quasi-stationary formulation consists of an iterative method determining
a steady state solution for each time step of the simulation, thus, for each time that the forcing
conditions change. This solver ignores the time required to reach the steady state. In general, the
steady state is not immediately reached since short period waves travel slower than long periods
waves and take more time to propagate across the domain. In the quasi-stationary formulation, all
the waves propagate across the domain at the same instant the boundary condition is applied, this
causes a negative time shift of the steady state, which is already achieved at the beginning of each

Therefore, this option was chosen for the small scale model simulating the propagation of waves

Sand Transport in Korsor Marina
The main goal of the analysis is to identify the conditions which lead to the significant deposition
of sand observed into and right outside the harbour.
The problem is investigated with two different models of MIKE 21.
The first one is Littoral
Drift , which is part of the Littoral Processes FM package and . LP FM is a quasi-3D sediment
transport module based on a deterministic description of coarse sediment transport. The Littoral
Drift module applies the deterministic formulation to all the points in a defined cross-section using
as hydrodynamic input parameters the results obtained from the wave simulation and calculating
littoral drift and annual drift [9]. In this case, it is mainly used to acquire a general idea of the
sediment transport processes along the stretch of coast around Korsør and as support for the results
obtained from the ST model. Therefore, the relevant information obtained from the simulation are
the dominant direction and distribution along the profiles of the longshore current and sediment
transport.
The second mean of investigation, the Sand Transport model, calculates the sediment transport
capacity and bed level change for non-cohesive sediment considering both the results of simulations
in SW and calculations of mean horizontal velocity from the hydrodynamic module [10]. Again, the
focus is not posed on the magnitude of sediment material transported and accumulated as the initial
objective of these simulations is to replicate the pattern (or patterns) of deposition which gives the
accumulation shown in Figure 1.3 and Figure 1.3 so to identify the conditions which generate them.

The second part of the littoral drift model directly regards the sediment transport calculation.
A longshore current generated by waves incoming at an angle α ̸= 0 can transport a significant
amount of sediment in the direction parallel to the shoreline.

force acting on the sand particles and the sediment motion begins.

MIKE 21 - Coupled Model FM
Coupled Model FM is an integrated model as it allows to combine different modules. In the present
case the hydrodynamic, the SW and the sand transport module were selected.
While Littoral Drift FM represents a preliminary estimation of magnitude and direction of the
sediment flow in specified cross-section along the coastline, the module Sand Transport of MIKE 21
is considered to reach a more thorough understanding of the sand transport processes nearby and
inside the harbor. The unstructured mesh is common to all the modules of the integrated model
Figure 3.5.
Afterwards, three more domains, each of them corresponding to a different design, were created in
order to investigate possible changes in the pattern of deposition of sediment due to changes in the
layout of the harbor. The new design proposals are discussed in Chapter 6.
As for the SW module, its setup is described in Chapter 3.
4.2.1
Hydrodynamic Module
The Hydrodynamic module solves the two-dimensional incompressible Reynolds averaged Navier-
Stokes equations.

In the present case, the radiation stresses are obtained as output of the calculations by the SW

With regards to the boundary conditions, the water level was specified as constant and equal to 0,

Wave disturbance - Boussinesq
Wave Model
The assessment of operational and design conditions of ports and harbours represents the major
application of Boussinesq Waves 2DH module, which was used in the present study, as it is suitable
for the simulation of the combined effects of the most important wave phenomena allowing to
analyse the wave disturbance in port or harbors [11].
The BW model consists of two types of module: 1DH Boussinesq Wave Module and 2DH (two
horizontal space coordinates) Boussinesq Wave Module. The model solves the enhanced Boussinesq
equations in terms of the surface elevation, η, and depth averaged velocity components [12].
For each time step, the equations are applied to each of the element of a rectangular grid which
represents the discretization of the domain. The model takes into account most of the common
phenomena occurring in coastal engineering structures, such as:
• Shoaling
• Refraction
• Reflection
• Diffraction
• Wave breaking
==PAGE:43==
5.1 Model Input
31
• Partial reflection and transmission
• Non-linear wave-wave interaction
• Bottom friction
• Moving shoreline
• Frequency and directional spreading
For the sake of simplicity and since the wave disturbance problem is assumed to be caused by
reflection, as it is stated in [2], the study was conducted applying the linear wave theory.
5.1
Model Input
Needed input to run the simulations in BW model are:
• A bathymetry file
• Sponge and Porosity layers
• Wave generation file
5.1.1
Bathymetry
The regular waves generation tool only allows to generate waves whose wave front is parallel to the
generation line which has to correspond to one of the boundaries of the domain. Therefore, in order
to define the wave disturbance due to waves incoming from different directions, two bathymetries
with same overall characteristics but different orientation with respect to North have been created,
5.1a and 5.1b. These 2 bathymetries allowed to study the effects of waves from 180°, 210°, 270°
and 300°. As the entrance to the harbor faces South, the directions of greater relevance for the
analysis were considered to be 180° and 210°.
As the bathymetry of the area is unknown (only for few points coarsely spaced the water depth is
known) and the only information regarding the water depth inside the harbor is the mean water
depth, Dm = −2.2m, a constant depth equal to Dm for the entire domain is assumed. To achieve
a high enough resolution of the final results, the grid spacing is chosen so that a number of grid
points of at least 10 is contained in a wave length. The dispersion relation, Equation 4.29, was used
to calculate the wave number k, which led to the wave length L (Equation 5.1) at depth Dm and
for a wave period T = 2.8s.

The boundary conditions are specified in terms of Sponge and Porosity layer maps which respectively

Chapter 8
Conclusions
Sand accumulation and wave disturbance are two common problems for ports and harbours and
can potentially obstacle the normal activities of the engineering structure causing economic losses.
Different types of measures can be taken in order to stem the consequences, e.g. deepening of the
harbour basin, or to prevent a critical event from happening again, e.g. built hard structures to
shelter the port from the action of waves. An efficient solution to either of the two problems has
to be based on a proper analysis of the hydrodynamic conditions, which play the major role in the
matter, with respect to the layout of the structures.
The present study was an attempt to identify the wave conditions which cause both mentioned
problems applying numerical modelling to the specific case of Korsør Marina and asses which one
among the proposed layout alternatives shows the best performance under those conditions.
With regards to the deposition of large amounts of sediment into the harbour and at its entry, the
results obtained studying the effects on the sediment motion of 9 design storms having different
directions show:
• All the storms caused accumulation of sediment material in front of the entrance to the
harbour, which is the more affected zone as shown in Figure 1.3, but consistently higher
amount of sand in that location are observed for storms having 220 ≤MWD ≤240°
• Waves incoming from southerly directions, between 160° and ≈240°, are responsible for the
deposition inside the harbour. In particular, they cause deposition at the northern part of the
==PAGE:89==
77
harbour basin. In general, not significant amount of sand was ever observed at the southern
part of the basin.
The results obtained for the proposed new layouts did not show great advantages with respect to
the current design of the harbour, although under certain conditions the effect of the storms was
mitigated as the entrance was significantly reduced. An exception is the design option in Figure 6.5.
This alternative corresponds to the greater reduction of the width of the entrance and to a drastic
change of its orientation, which in this case faces West rather than South as it does in the current
layout. This alternative, which would represent the last step of a two phases project (together with
the second design alternative, which represents the first phase), prevented the sand from entering
into the harbour in almost all the studied conditions, except for two cases: waves incoming from
220° and 240°. In these two conditions, the results showed that the entrance was completely filled
with sediment, going from a water depth of -2.2m to more than -1.5m, which is not observed in
any of the other designs for the same storm, being the entrance wider. In this particular condition,
the reduced width of the entrance seem to represent a disadvantage and this could generate an
unavoidable problem to take into account, especially considering the dominance of waves incoming
from SSW. Overall, this last design alternative might attenuate the necessity of maintenance inside
the harbour, as this is the most pressuring issue at the moment, but considering the importance
of a clear entrance and its limited width, it might require more frequent deepening of the channel
between the breakwaters.
However, considering the totality of results, it is the design which performed better during the
simulations for amount of sand accumulated inside the harbour and, in most of the cases, at the
entrance.
Regarding the wave disturbance analysis, as expected, a reduction of width of the entrance intro-
duces a mitigation of the effects of incident waves inside the harbour. Nonetheless, in some cases for
the first two proposed layouts (Figure 6.1 and Figure 6.3) the improvement is marginal and might
not solve the problem or the reduction is related to a single area and not generalized to the whole
harbour basin, as it is clear in Figure 7.23. As found for the deposition of sand into the marina, the
layout ND2S2 showed the best response to all the conditions to which it was exposed during the
simulations, therefore, with respect to all the considered directions. In fact, the amplification factor
in the most exposed zone of the harbour, zone C, in none of the simulations exceeds 0.3. Again,
as the orientation of the entrance is different from all the other layouts, in this case the worst case
scenario are not the southerly directions. Indeed, this layout presented the worst performance when
exposed to waves incoming from West and also in this case the calculated amplification factor was
below 0.3 in all the zones.
