Abstract
The Skjold ﬁeld is located in the Danish part of the North Sea and has been in production
since 1982. In 1986 water injection started to be applied on the ﬁeld with breakthrough
in 1990 and has today reached water cuts >97%. The ﬁeld consists of small-scale faults
and numerous micro-fractures which in conjunction with the larger-scale faulting provides
a good network of highly permeable ﬂuid conduits. The ﬁeld has now reached a mature
state where tertiary recovery methods might be applied to increase the recovery factor of
the ﬁeld from its current value of approx. 38%. Most of the remaining oil is located in
the less fractured Hatten area.
A study has been made where lean hydrocarbon gas injection has been applied on the
Skjold ﬁeld using an industrial reservoir simulator Eclipse with a dual permeability setting,
diﬀusion and in compositional mode. A PVT model using the industrial programme
PVTsim is used to tune a six component SRK equation of state (EOS) to match a PVT
study conducted on a Skjold live oil in 1985. Diﬀusion eﬀects is investigated showing
negligible inﬂuence on the production ﬁgures.
Four scenarios for further development of the ﬁeld has been simulated where scenario
two to four honours the current constraints of the ﬁeld; (1) Full ﬁeld gas injection, (2)
Gas injection in the Hatten area with maintained water injection, (3) Gas injection in the
Hatten area with blowdown, (4) Blowdown of the ﬁeld. The base case is a continuation
of current water injection method. Gas injection did yield an incremental recovery
of oil while also showing negligible inﬂuence of diﬀusion. However, after running an
economical analysis of each scenario, the blowdown of the ﬁeld seems to be the most
viable solution with the current assumptions. The stochastic economical analysis did
reveal great sensitivity towards oil prices which along with the technical uncertainties
related to a blowdown may make other options more favourable.

Foreword
This master thesis project is the result of 5 months of intense work and challenges. It is
the ﬁnal report of ﬁve years studies at the Technical University of Denmark. The past
two years has been primarily focused on oil and gas related topics covering many subjects.
Our motivation have been to conduct a project which is related to real life challenges
and the obvious solution was to contact Maersk Oil which we would like to thank for
allowing us working on the project with direct access to data, expertise and guidance.
It has been ﬁve very educational months working on the Skjold model in the reservoir
simulator to test both the theories from the literature and our own thoughts. With
no experience in reservoir simulators or tuning of EOS models it has truly been an
exhilarating process and interesting to work on a project of this size where it is easy to
see the applications and the relevance to the industry.
We would like to thank Nicolas von Solms, Vural Sander Suicmez, Niels Lindeloﬀand
Jack Orman for their guidance throughout the project. Being able to access guidance
and support on a daily basis has been amazing. It has been a pleasure working with such
competent and kind supervisors.
All good things come to an end...

1. Introduction
The Skjold ﬁeld was discovered by Maersk Oil in 1977 with the exploration well Ruth-1
and with ﬁrst oil in 1982. Located in the Central Graben area within the Danish sector
of the North Sea the Skjold ﬁeld is part of the Sole Concession 8th July 1962 covering
1636 km2 of the Danish continental shelf which was given to A.P. Mller-Mrsk. Danish
Underground Consortium (DUC) was at the same point of time established to assist
A.P. Mller-Mrsk with its obligations and consisted of A.P. Mller-Mrsk, Shell and Gulf
where the latter acted as the operator. Few years later Texaco and Chevron joined the
consortium. In 1974 Gulf left the consortium and Mrsk became the operator. Chevron
left the consortium in 1987. However, as a consequence of the merger with Texaco in
2001 Chevron was again a part of DUC. The latest change in the consortium happened
in 2012 where Nordsfonden entered the consortium and represents the Danish state’s
participation. Today, the partners in DUC have the following equity share distribution;
A.P. Mller-Mrsk holds 31.2%, Shell 36.8%, Nordsfonden 20% and Chevron 12%. Figure
2.1 illustrates the major ﬁelds on the Danish continental shelf and infrastructure.
The Skjold ﬁeld was initially produced through natural depletion, however, due to
insuﬃcient inﬂux from the aquifer water injection was initiated in 1986 to maintain
reservoir pressure and was quite successful due to a strong spontaneous imbibition eﬀects.
After years of water injection the spontaneous imbibition eﬀect has been exploited and
as of today the water cut is >97% which has led to conducting an analysis to asses the
feasibility of implementing tertiary recovery method by injecting lean hydrocarbon gas,
both from an economic and technical point of view. A full ﬁeld model of Skjold has been
developed by HOT Engineering containing both a static and a dynamic representation of
the ﬁeld as part of the Maersk Oil project ’Locate The Remaining Oil’. The static model
will be used as it is while the dynamic model will be modiﬁed to support gas injection.
Reservoir simulation concerning water injection has been done in a black oil model where
three phases are assumed to exist; oil, gas and water. Injection of hydrocarbon gas will
cause the oil and gas phase to interact and exchange components. As a consequence,
reservoir simulation will be conducted in a compositional reservoir model with dual
permeability setting. The dual permeability setting accounts for the fact that ﬂuid ﬂow
would be dominated by ﬂow in fractures and matrix and allows the speciﬁcation of
porosity and permeability in the fractures and matrix. An EOS model will be constructed
with PVTsim with data from a PVT study conducted on a live oil from the Skjold ﬁeld
1
==PAGE:17==
in 1985. The number of components in the EOS model will be reduced by a lumping
procedure resulting in an overall reduction of computation time required. Fractures in the
reservoir may cause diﬀusion to play a vital role in the production rates due to diﬀusion
from the fracture space to the reservoir matrix unlocking more oil volumes. Diﬀusion
eﬀects will therefore be investigated to determine the eﬀect on the overall recovery. A
deterministic and stochastic economic evaluation will be conducted upon ﬁnalization of
the optimization procedure. The stochastic evaluation is done to asses the certainty of
the cases and the impact of oil & gas prices and OPEX cost.
1.1
Literature Study
Mature ﬁeld development is a broad subject. It can be divided into two main parts:
(1) well development, and (2) reservoir development. Development plans can be made
depending on ﬁeld type, history and prospects. A key issue is to identify the remaining
oil both in terms of volume and location. Techniques to improve recovery factor such as
tertiary recovery methods, inﬁlls and optimal placement of new wells are the elements of
reservoir development.
Mature oil ﬁelds can be referring to ﬁelds reaching the peak of their production or
producing ﬁelds in declining mode. Another deﬁnition could be bundled to ﬁelds reaching
their economic limit after primary and secondary recovery eﬀorts [1].
Despite the technical feasibility in many circumstances, the economics of tertiary
recovery ﬁeld applications fails to become commercially viable. An advantage of devel-
oping mature ﬁelds through tertiary recovery is the data, information and experience
gathered throughout the production life that signiﬁcantly minimizes uncertainties relating
to geology and technology. One major uncertainty is the ﬂuctuations in oil prices. The
following points may be considered when planning to implement tertiary recovery on
mature ﬁelds:
• The eﬀectiveness of the project, i.e. the total incremental oil recovery by the
enhanced oil recovery (EOR) method applied.
• The eﬃciency of the process related to total cost/total recovery. Secondary recovery
techniques tends to have higher recoveries and lower total cost relative to those
of tertiary recovery methods. Precise knowledge of the location and distribution
of the remaining oil is essential in the selection, design and evaluation of tertiary
recovery techniques.
• The objective for implementing tertiary recovery methods depends on the man-
agement strategies which may be inﬂuenced by the size of the company. Field
development strategies through tertiary recovery may therefore either focus on long
term og short term incremental recoveries. Due to the high risk associated with
tertiary recovery methods small companies may avoid high risk investments and
focus on faster recovery rather than the ultimate recovery. Big size companies may
target higher ultimate recovery as they can aﬀord long-term investments.

2. The Skjold Field
The lithology of the reservoir is chalk which has been displaced by a slightly elongated
salt piercement dome and heavily fractured, most dominant in the crestal part of
the reservoir.
A series of ring faults can be observed on the sides of the reservoir.
The productive formation of the ﬁeld consists of Lower Tertiary (Danian) and Upper
Cretaceous (Maastrichtian/Campanian) chalk which varies in thickness from 250 to 700 ft
[76 to 210 m], with the thinnest chalk found in the crest. The initial total oil column hight
is estimated to approx. 1,300 ft [400 m]. The porosity ranges from 15-50%. Diﬀerent
permeabilites exist in the matrix and the fractures of the reservoir where the matrix
permeability in most of the ﬁeld is less than 1 md [23]. The ﬁeld consists of small-scale
faults and numerous micro-fractures which in conjunction with the larger-scale faulting
provides a good network of highly permeable ﬂuid conduits.
The cumulative oil production in January 2016 was 304 MMSTB which yields a current
recovery factor of 38.4% (based on original oil-in-place (OOIP) of 790 MMSTB) [23, 24].
Figure 2.1 is an overview of ﬁelds in the Danish North Sea where the location of the
Skjold ﬁeld can be seen. Skjold is an unmanned satellite to Gorm consisting of to
platforms; Skjold A and Skjold B. There are two pipelines from Gorm to Skjold which
supplies Skjold with lift gas and water for water injection with the dimensions of 12”
water injection pipeline and one 6” lift gas pipeline. The production stream from Skjold
is transported to Gorm for processing through a 12” multiphase export pipeline. Figure
2.2 is a map of the well paths and locations in the ﬁeld.

3. Reservoir Recovery
3.1
Primary recovery
Field development plans can have diﬀerent strategies depending on the characteristics of
the ﬁeld. The cheapest development method uses the inherent energy of the reservoir
which is commonly referred to as primary recovery. The driving mechanisms in primary
recovery can be one of the following:
Rock and liquid expansion drive
Upon the commencement of production the
pressure will start to decline. Pressures above the bubblepoint will lead to an expansion
of the ﬂuid and rock matrix as pressure decline. The expansion of the rock will cause
the porosity of the reservoir to decline in time. In reservoirs where expansion drive is
dominating the pressure decline will be rapid. This recovery mechanism is considered
the least eﬀective and yield low recoveries [25, 26].
Solution Gas Drive
If the reservoir pressure declines below the bubble point pressure,
gas will start to come out of solution in the pore space resulting in an pressure increase
pushing more oil out of the pore space. Depletion drive is characterized by an increase
in GOR towards a maximum where after it declines. Typically oil recoveries are in the
range of 5% to 30% [25, 26].
Gas-cap drive
can be applied if there is a gas zone above the oil layer. The theory
behind the mechanism is that as oil is produced and the pressure in the reservoir drops
resulting in an expansion of the gas-cap due to the additional gas released from the
oil hereby maintaining the pressure in the reservoir. The eﬀectiveness of gas-cap drive
depends on a number of parameters such as size of the gas-cap, vertical permeability, oil
viscosity and the production rate. Gas-cap drive requires a low oil production rate in
order to allow the gas and oil to separate and maintain a uniform ﬂat oil gas contact
plane. Gas-cap drive usually yields recoveries between 20% and 40% [25, 26].
Water drive
Naturally water is present in the formation below the oil bearing zone
and can help maintaining the pressure of the reservoir. Even though water is only slightly
compressible (in many cases assumed incompressible), the vast amount of water can
propagate up in to the reservoir from an underlying aquifer maintaining the pressure.
10
==PAGE:26==
The eﬀectiveness of water drive depends on the production rate, the homogeneity of
the reservoir and the activity of the aquifer. If the reservoir is heterogeneous the water
front will not propagate in an uniform fashion while too high production rates can have
the consequence of water channels breaking through to the well prohibiting water to
displace oil located in tighter sections. The water drive mechanism is regarded as the
most eﬃcient primary recovery method resulting in recoveries of 35% to 75% [25, 26].
The methods stated above can be exploited in combination with each other though
normally one of the mechanisms will be dominating. The common denominator for the
methods is that there is no pressure maintenance from outside of the reservoir.
3.2
Secondary Recovery
If primary mechanisms above is not suﬃcient to develop the ﬁeld it is necessary to
maintain the pressure in the reservoir by adding energy to the reservoir. This is typically
done by either water or gas injection. Generally there are two cases; peripheral/central
ﬂooding and pattern ﬂooding. In peripheral or central ﬂooding the injectors are grouped
together in either the gas-gap or the aquifer. This placement method is usually applied
when the reservoir has an anticline structure. For pattern ﬂooding the wells are drilled
based on a ﬁxed pattern. The most common pattern design is the ﬁve-spot pattern, i.e.
one producer surrounded by 4 injectors. The applied pattern will depend on the nature
of the reservoir. The ﬁxed pattern design will yield a faster development of the ﬁeld but
result in more produced injectant. The peripheral or central ﬂooding will in most cases,
if applied correctly, yield the highest recoveries [25].
A pressure gradient will be developed between the injector and producer with high
pressures near the injector and lower pressure near the producing well. The pressure
gradient will act as a driving force for ﬂow towards the producer. It is possible to reduce
the investment cost for injectors by transforming wells that was used as producers during
the primary recovery stage into injectors which can be used during the secondary recovery
stage, hence the investment cost for injectors can be kept at a minimum if the wells
have been planned correctly however the drilling of additional wells may be necessary [25].
In general water injection outperforms gas injection except for some cases where the
reservoir formation either has
• Very low formation permeability
• Reservoirs with swelling clay
• Steeply dipping reservoirs
• Thick oil reservoirs
• Thin oil reservoirs
11
==PAGE:27==
The feasibility of gas injection is very dependent on the availability of gas. For example
CO2 can be expensive in many cases except if there is a natural source available as seen
in certain areas in the US. Lean hydrocarbon gas has a value itself and can be sold on
gas market if commercially viable, i.e. price and able to transport onshore or transform
to LNG (liqueﬁed natural gas). Injecting N2 is less eﬀective compared to the former two.
The recovery factor globally is between 20-40 % after primary and secondary development.
This recovery range relates well to the recovery observed on Skjold of approx. 38% as
mentioned in section 2. To achieve higher recovery factors enhanced oil recovery will
have to be applied which would depend on the feasibility of the applied tertiary method
[25].
3.3
Tertiary Recovery (EOR)
The purpose of EOR is to manipulate the parameters aﬀecting the recovery of oil such as
the interfacial tension (IFT), oil volume expansion, rock wettability, capillary pressure,
oil/water viscosity, oil density and oil composition. Aﬀecting these parameters can be
done by injecting ﬂuids into the reservoir which could be chemicals, ﬂuids interacting
with the oil (e.g. gas) or or by in-situ combustion. Commonly, EOR is applied as a
tertiary recovery method but it can be implemented in the secondary or primary phase
in order to develop a reservoir quicker which can be advantageous if high cash ﬂows in
the early stage of the ﬁeld development is the objective. Generally, EOR can be divided
into three diﬀerent areas:
• Thermal ﬂooding
• Chemical ﬂooding
• Gas injection
3.3.1

Chemical ﬂooding is the addition of chemicals to the injected ﬂuid such as surfactants,
polymers or alkalis. The objective of chemical ﬂooding is to increase the capillary number
(Nc) deﬁned as the ratio between viscous forces and capillary forces. Nc is given in

Polymer solutions are Newtonian ﬂuids hence the solutions will have a low viscosity when

the shear rate close to the wellbore is usually high but decreases as the distance from the

shear rates due to the entanglement of the polymer chains. As the shear rate increases
the polymers will align and the solution becomes less viscous [25].
The retention of the polymers in the reservoir can reduce the permeability of the reservoir
and thereby reduce the recovery. The degree of retention will be dependent on the
permeability of the formation, the molecular weight of the polymer, the clay content of
the reservoir, the anionic charges in the polymer and the cationic charges in the reservoir.
Usually polymer retention is determined from core ﬂooding experiments in the laboratory
[25].
An important factor when implementing polymer ﬂooding is the stability of the polymer.
The stability can be split into four diﬀerent groups i.e. chemical, biological, mechanical
and thermal stability. Chemical stability is the resistance to high salt concentrations and
free radical groups in the reservoir. High salt concentration can reduce the viscosity of
the polymer solution due to screening of the backbone. This eﬀect will allow the polymer
to curl up and thereby occupy a smaller volume. Free radical groups can scissor the
polymer thereby reducing its size signiﬁcantly. Biological stability is important as micro
organisms present in the reservoir can digest the polymers. This is mainly a problem
for biopolymers therefore ﬂooding with such polymers is often done with pesticides to

polymer solution is subject to high shear rates in the near well bore region which can
break the polymer backbone. The higher molecular weight a polymer has the more prone
it will be to mechanical degradation. Thermal stability is important to consider since
polymers will only be stable up to a certain temperature [25].
Surfactant
Surfactants are amphiphilic organic molecules meaning they are soluble in both water and
organic solvents. Surfactants has a tail which is hydrophobic, typically a hydrocarbon
chain and a hydrophillic head. At low concentrations the surfactant molecules will be

Micelle Concentration’ (CMC) the molecules will cluster together in spheres/tubes. Once
the CMC is reached the properties of the solution will change signiﬁcantly. Depending
on which phase is continuous, the tail or the head will either be at the surface of the
sphere/tube. If water is the continuous phase the head will be pointing outward and
the tail inward. The opposite is applicable if oil is the continuous phase. The formation
of micelles will signiﬁcantly increase the solubility between oil and water. If surfactant
ﬂooding is properly managed a microemulsion is formed which are thermodynamically
stable systems however sensitive to a number of parameters such as temperature and
salinity. Thorough investigation of the reservoir conditions is necessary for a successful
surfactant ﬂooding [25].

Thermal ﬂooding was developed to target heavy oil, extra heavy oil and bitumen forma-

The idea behind thermal EOR is heating the reservoir and thereby reducing the
viscosity of the oil as a result of strong temperature dependency of viscosity. The two
main methods of thermal ﬂooding is steam injection either as steam drive or cyclic steam
stimulation and in-situ combustion.
Cyclic Steam Stimulation
Cyclic steam stimulation (CSS) is also known as the ”Huﬀand Puﬀ” method or steam
soaking consisting of three diﬀerent phases:
1. Steam injection phase
2. Steam soaking phase
3. Production phase
During phase 1 steam is injected into the reservoir at a high rate typically for a couple of
weeks. In phase 2 the well is closed to allow heat transfer to take place in order for the
oil to heat up. After a few weeks of soaking the process enters its ﬁnal phase. In phase 3
the well is bought back online and the well is produced between one to six months - until
the oil rate drops oﬀ. Once a cycle has been completed the entire process is repeated [25].
Steam Drive
Unlike CSS, steam drive is a multiple well development method resembling more that
of water ﬂooding with an injector and a producer. Steam is injected into the reservoir
15
==PAGE:31==
which will propagate towards a producer. Heat will be transferred from the steam to
the surroundings such as ﬂuid in the reservoir and formation rock but also to formation
above and below the reservoir. The transfer of heat will cause the front of the steam
front to cool down and condense into water resulting in two propagating fronts. Steam
drive reduces the viscosity of the oil and vaporizes the lighter components. The volume
expansion caused by the vaporization eﬀect will provide pressure maintenance and cause
the vaporized components to be produced more easily. It is normal to use CSS on the
wells before implementing steam drive [25].
In-Situ Combustion
In-situ combustion ignites the hydrocarbons present in the reservoir by injecting oxygen
resulting in a large temperature increase. In order to maintain the combustion and
drive the oil towards a producer air is injected into the reservoir on a continuous
basis. Approximately 10% of the OOIP is burned by this process, primarily the heavy
hydrocarbons while the lighter groups af pushed forward by the front. Due to the
burning of oil signiﬁcant amounts of ﬂue gas will be produced which poses a corrosion
and environmental challenge. Another challenge with in-situ combustion is the high
temperatures reached in the reservoir during the process of approx. 600◦C which can
lead to pipe/well failures [25].
3.3.3
Gas Injection
Gas injection can be an excellent recovery method due to the higher injectivity. the
interactions between the gas and oil phase and the lower IFT as compared to that of
water and oil. Certain challenges may arise from gas injection, e.g. as gas has a lower
viscosity the mobility factor will be higher yielding a lower sweep eﬃciency. Furthermore,
gas has a lighter density and tends to primarily displace the top of the reservoir due to
the gravity forces. In a chalk reservoir gas will be the non-wetting phase where water is
typically the wetting phase thus gas will displace the oil in the large pores while water
displaces oil in the tighter pores due to capillary eﬀects. If the injector is located at the
bottom of the reservoir the buoyancy eﬀect will cause gas to migrate upwards which may
lead to viscous ﬁngering and early breakthrough [25].
The gas used for injection can be CO2, lean hydrocarbon gas, ﬂue gas or nitrogen. The
choice of gas depends on many variables such as the availability, reservoir characteristics
and ﬂuid properties. It is important to determine the minimum miscibility pressure
(MMP) between the injected gas and ﬂuid in place to asses whether it is a miscible or
immiscible displacement process. The MMP will depend on the injected gas, the oil in
the reservoir and the temperature in the reservoir. Miscible displacement will yield a
better recovery since a single phase will be obtained, however immiscible gas injection is
still eﬀective since there will be a lower IFT between the oil and gas relative to oil and
water, interactions between the phases, reduction of the oil viscosity and the swelling of
oil due to the solubility of gas in the oil increasing its volume [25].
16
==PAGE:32==
Miscible Gas Injection
In miscible gas injection the IFT between gas and oil will disappear as only one phase
exists enabling the injected gas to dissolve in the oil phase. This process will reduces the
viscosity of the oil and causes it to expand [25]. Generally, there are two ways to achieve
miscibility; ﬁrst contact miscibility (FCM) and multiple contact miscibility (MCM). In
FCM the injected gas and oil will become one phase instantaneously. This will require
higher pressure as compared to the MMP. Alternatively, injecting an enriched gas slug
that is miscible with the oil where after a cheaper drive gas is used can be applied. A
swelling test is usually conducted in order to determine the FCM pressure [25]. The
theory behind a swelling test is explained in section 4.5.1.
MCM exploits the mass transfer between the gas and oil phase as the oil is being displaced.
This will cause the two phases to become more alike and eventually become miscible. The
mechanisms behind the interchange of components can be vaporizing drive, condensing
drive or a mix of the two. In vaporizing drive components from the oil phase will go into
the gas phase which usually happens at the interface between the front and the ﬂuid
being displaced. Condensing drive is the opposite where components from the gas goes
into the oil phase which happens at the back of the front. Vaporizing drive will generally
be dominant if a lean hydrocarbon gas is used and vice versa if a ’rich’ gas is used.
Condensing drive will typically require lower pressure than vaporizing drive, however
the process tends to be more expensive. The MCM pressure is typically determined by
an experiment such as slim-tube, rising bubble test, the vanishing of IFT of oil-solvent
mixtures, the mixing cell method or with a PVT cell. The diﬀerent PVT tests will be
extensively described in section 4 [25].

as presented on ﬁgure 3.1. The determining factor is where the injection gas and reservoir
oil is in the diagram compared to the limiting tie line (dashed line from m to n on ﬁgure

easier to achieve miscibility. In many cases miscibility will not be achieved, however good
recoveries are achievable due to the interchange of components even at pressures below
the MMP as described earlier.

3.3.4

When gas injection is implemented in highly fractured reservoir with low matrix perme-

higher permeability of the fractures. In such cases the diﬀusion can be the mechanism

provides a large contact area for diﬀusion to work upon, making the fracture spacing
an important parameter as a smaller fracture spacing will yield a greater surface area.

its viscosity and causing it to swell, resulting in a recovery increase [28, 29, 30, 32]. For
liquid-liquid diﬀusion the viscosity of the ﬂuids will be the governing parameter while for
gasses the molar density will be the most important parameter [31]. Articles state that

will be larger in the two-phase region than in the single-phase region. The Y-factor is
small for oils with large amount of released associated gas with decreasing pressure and
large when small amount of gas is released.

4.3
Constant Volume Depletion Experiment
The constant-volume depletion (CVD) experiment is generally performed on condensate
mixtures and in rare cases also on volatile oils. Figure 4.4 is a graphical illustration of
the constant-volume depletion experiment.
27
==PAGE:43==
Figure 4.4: Graphical illustration of the diﬀerent steps in a constant-volume experiment.
The constant volume depletion experiment is performed in a windowed cell allowing
retrograde liquid volumes to be visually measured and reported. The test consist of a
pressure expansion followed by a constant pressure removal of excess gas to return the
cell content to a constant volume. This is typically repeated in six pressure stages down
to the abandonment pressure of around 50 bar.
The cell resembles the CME experiment but is equipped with a valve on top allowing
the gas to be removed. The experiment is started at the saturation pressure where
the saturation point pressure and volume are recorded. The volume of the cell is then
increased which causes the pressure to decrease below the saturation pressure and two
separate phases appears. The volume of the cell is then decreased to the saturation
volume by the removal of excess gas to maintain a constant pressure. The percentage
of the molar amount of gas removed from the initial gas volume and the liquid volume
as percentage of the liquid volume initially in place at the saturation point is recorded.
This procedure is repeated until desired pressure is reached.
The main objective of the constant volume depletion experiment is to simulate the actual
production process for a reservoir. When production commences from an oil and gas
reservoir, temperature and volume are often constant. However, compaction or other
geomechanical phenomena may aﬀect the volume of the reservoir. Temperature may
also locally change from the overall reservoir temperature caused by the injection of
water or other substances at lower temperature than the reservoir temperature. During
production the pressure of the reservoir will decrease as ﬂuids are produced. When the
saturation pressure is reached the reservoir ﬂuid will split into two phases, a liquid and a
gas phase. Assuming that all the production comes from the gas zone, the mixture of
the produced gas will have the same composition as the gas removed from the cell. The
ﬂuid produced from a reservoir in the time between an arbitrary pressure P1 to a lower
pressure P2 is equal to the removed gas of the cell at pressure stage P2.

4.4
Separator Test
Separator experiments are carried out for both oil and gas mixtures. The main objective
of the separator test is to simulate the compositional and volumetric changes a ﬂuid
undergoes during production. The separator chain is constructed in such a way that it
resembles the ﬁeld separators. Figure 4.5 illustrates how a separator chain can be build.
Figure 4.5: Graphical illustration of three staged separator experiment.
The reservoir ﬂuid is placed in a closed cell with a ﬁxed temperature and pressure.
The ﬂuid may then split into a liquid and a gas phase. The released gas from the oil
is transferred to standard conditions where its volume, gas gravity and composition is
measured. The liquid is transferred to another cell at a lower pressure and temperature.
The evolved gas is then removed again and its properties measured. The oil from the
last separator test is referred to as the stock tank oil meaning that the oil can be stored
at standard conditions without liberating gas. The main objective of the separator test
is to yield a rough estimate on the relative volumetric amounts of gas and oil produced
from a given reservoir.
4.5
EOR PVT Experiments
4.5.1
Swelling Test
Swelling tests are performed on reservoir ﬂuids to study how they react to gas injection
(can also be performed for gas condensate mixtures). The initial saturation pressure is
determined by the conventional methods such as described CME experiments in section
4.1. A known molar amount of injection gas is transferred to the PVT cell and the
pressure is increased high enough to ensure that all the gas is dissolved in the oil. The
saturation pressure and saturation volume are recorded. More gas is injected into the oil
and a new saturation pressure and the swollen volume is recorded. This procedure is
repeated in a number of stages. The last stage(s) may have a saturation point changed
from a bubble point to a dew point which indicates that the critical point is reached. The
main objective of the swelling test is to yield data on the swelling behaviour caused by
the injection of gas with a certain composition into an oil sample. It does also yield the
extra pressure needed to dissolve the injected gas. The swelling gas/oil ratio is deﬁned as
the cumulative volume of the injected gas at standard conditions per initial oil volume.

4.5.2
Equilibrium Contact Experiment
Swelling experiments provides information about the reservoir ﬂuid when saturated with
injection gas in diﬀerent quantities, however it does not provide any information on the
phases below the saturation point. The equilibrium contact experiment can provide
information on the mixtures of the reservoir oil and injected gas. It is often observed
that one of the gas-oil mixing ratios is close to the point where the saturation point shifts
from a bubble to a dew point which will be close to the critical point.
Figure 4.7: A graphical illustration of a equilibrium contact experiment.
4.5.3
Multi-Contact Experiment
A reservoir containing an under-saturated oil will be able to dissolve some injection gas
until a point where the oil no longer can accommodate more gas and splits into two
phases, an oil and a gas phase. The gas phase will have a diﬀerent composition from that
of the injected gas which is caused by a process where some of the lighter components
from the reservoir oil are taken up by the gas and other lost to the oil phase. The new
gas phase developed from the split will be pushed away from the injection well and make
a new contact with fresh oil located further away from the injection well. A multi-contact
30
==PAGE:46==
experiment is thus designed to simulate those processes.
The process is distinguished by two diﬀerent types of contact, forward and backward.
In a forward contact process the gas phase is removed to a subsequent stage and mixed
with fresh oil. In a backward experiment the oil phase from the ﬁrst contact is removed
and mixed with fresh injection gas.
Figure 4.8: A graphical illustration of a forward and backward multi-contact experiment.
The main objective of a multi-contact experiment is to obtain experimental data for
the phase behaviour near miscible conditions, however miscibility must not be developed
because there would not be any gas or oil to move to the next stage. Forward multi-
contact experiment is designed to resemble a gas front moving through the reservoir
which changes composition through the multiple contacts with the oil while a backward
experiment resembles the ﬂuid near the injection well. The experiment is therefore
designed to either be 100% vaporizing gas drive or 100% condensing gas drive which were
explained in section 3.
4.5.4
Slim-tube Experiment
A slim-tube test is performed to estimate the MMP of a given solvent injected into
a reservoir oil. Figure 4.9 is a schematic illustration of a slim-tube apparatus. The
slim-tube consist of a narrow steel tube with an internal diameter of about 5/16 inches.
and a length between 5 and 40 meters which is ﬁlled with glas beads of a speciﬁc mesh
size.

5. Cubic Equations of State
A cubic equation of state is a thermodynamic equation relating state variables which
describes the state of matter under a given set of physical conditions. They provide a
mathematical relationship in which they correlate two or more state functions such as
temperature, volume, pressure or internal energy. Equations of state are used to describe
the properties of ﬂuids, ﬂuid mixtures and solids.
The cubic EOS models are most widely used in the petroleum industry to describe the
PVT properties of a given reservoir ﬂuid under certain conditions. They are called cubic
as they are of third degree when solved with respect to volume where the highest value
represents the gas phase and the lowest the liquid phase. The middle value has no physical
meaning. Petroleum ﬂuids are often located in very high pressure environments, however
upon production the hydrocarbon will experience a diﬀerent physical environment which
will cause changes in the initial ﬂuid property. Similarly, when commencing production
from a reservoir ﬁeld pressure will drop and the ﬂuid properties will change. These
changes are important to cover and properly describe which the cubic EOS models can
help to predict.
The advantage of the EOS models is its applicability over wide ranges of temperature and
pressure to mixtures of diverse components from light gases to heavy liquids. Reservoir
ﬂuids for example are complex mixtures of hydrocarbons and non-hydrocarbons with a
wide range of chemical compounds that varies in complexity from very simple molecules
as methane to much more complex compounds as asphaltenes.
EOS models can be used for the representation of vapour-liquid equilibria (VLE), liquid-
liquid equilibria (LLE) and supercritical ﬂuid phase equilibria. They are also able to
describe gas, liquid and supercritical ﬂuid phases without any conceptual diﬃculties [33].
Many equations of state have been proposed in the literature however only the most
widely used by the petroleum industry will be discussed in this chapter.

5.4
EOS Tuning
Equations of state calculations on petroleum ﬂuids are frequently burdened by the large
number of fractions necessary to describe the hydrocarbon mixture for accurate phase
behaviour. However, the cost on computer resources required for compositional reservoir
simulation increases signiﬁcantly with the number of components used in describing
the reservoir ﬂuid. The number of components is therefore reduced by lumping the
components in the system which can signiﬁcantly increase the speed of the simulation
process. In the literature many diﬀerent lumping strategies are used such as the Whitson
38
==PAGE:54==
lumping scheme [45], Lee’s lumping scheme [46] etc.. Many articles uses diﬀerent lumping
groups depending on how many groups is desired and what kind of simulation is done,
e.g. in CO2 injection must CO2 be kept as a standalone [47, 48, 49]. Alavian et al. [47]
gives some pointers to lumping they have used while obtaining good results,
• Non-hydrocarbons should be lumped the following way, N2 with C1, CO2 with C2
• Hydrocarbons are only allowed to be lumped with their neighbouring hydrocarbon
numbers e.g. C7C10 would be C7 + C8 + C9 + C10, C7C10 as in C7 + C10 is not
allowed
• Partial lumping of original components is not allowed, i.e. splitting C3 into two
groups.
As discussed in section 5.2 the EOS parameters (i.e. ai and bi) can be calculated using
the critical properties such as Tc, Pc and ω. These are often well deﬁned for the pure
components however, determining these properties for the heavy fractions and lumped
components rely on empirical correlations and the use of mixing rules. Caution must
be taken when interpreting the predictions from the EOS model as theses would have
inherent errors caused by the empirical correlations and applied mixing rule.
Tuning of the EOS is therefore an important step prior to conducting any simulations
for meaningful and reliable predictions. The tuning process is conducted by adjusting
the parameters in the EOS model with the objective of matching the laboratory ﬂuids
PVT data and predicted EOS results [50].

5.4.2
Regression Procedure
A procedure has been developed to improve the match between speciﬁc PVT data
properties without deteriorating the properties at T and P which is not covered by
the data set and only with small inﬂuence on properties which is outside the range of
the regression. The coeﬃcients in the Tc, Pc and m correlations is used as regression
parameters instead of regressing individual component properties as (Tc, Pc and ω of
the pseudo components). This is done to insure smooth development in Tc, Pc and
acentric factor with molecular weight. Generally there is no consensus of which tuning
procedure to apply however the book ’Phase Behaviour of Petroleum Reservoir Fluids’
[51] recommend the Christensen [52] method given below
1. Make regression to the experimental saturation points. Allow the plus molecular
weight to be adjusted by up to 10%, keeping the weight composition constant.
2. Evaluate whether the deviations between experimental and calculated data suggest
deﬁciencies in density predictions. If that is the case, allow the volume translation
parameter of the C7+ components to be adjusted by 100% (same percent for all
C7+ components).
3. Determine the two or three most sensitive coeﬃcients in Equations (5.25)-(5.27)
(those with the largest impacts on the calculation results for a given percentage
change).
4. Perform a parameter regression using the most sensitive coeﬃcients determined in
the preceding step (max. adjustment 20%).

7. Gas Injection Scenarios
In this work three diﬀerent scenarios will be investigated by conducting reservoir sim-
ulations followed by an economic evaluation to determine the feasibility and the most
commercially viable solution. There will be two reference cases:
• Continue current water injection on all injectors.
• Blowdown of the ﬁeld where the water injectors are shut in, i.e. pressure support is
terminated.
Blowdown of a reservoir refers to the process where the pressure support is stopped. A
reservoir with pressures above the oils saturation pressure may have dissolved gas in the
oil. When pressure starts to decline upon production with no pressure support rock and
liquid expansion drive will be the driving mechanism. Once the saturation pressure is
reached, gas depletion drive takes over and gas will come out of solution which will make
the GOR to increase rapidly.

7.1
Full Field Gas Injection
Full ﬁeld gas injection was performed in the simulation model to test the overall perfor-
mance of the gas injection. Two key considerations was taken into account:
1. Water injection has been conducted in Skjold since 1986 for pressure support and
areas where water has displaced oil are well swept. Gas injected would migrate
through diﬀerent pathways due to the density and mobility diﬀerence between
water and gas allowing gas to displace oil in areas which has not been fully swept
by water injection.
2. The interactions between oil and gas could either be miscible or immiscible which
depends on the MMP for the given composition of gas and oil. Immiscible gas
injection would displace oil much like water but in a diﬀerent way, since there will
be some interactions. Gas will sweep diﬀerent pores and parts of the reservoir, as
explained in section 3.3.3, yielding higher production. Miscible oil displacement
would be the favourable displacement type yielding the highest recoveries.
The water injectors can be converted to gas injectors which requires relatively small
changes on the facilities (connecting the water injection pipeline at Gorm to the compres-
sors). The water injectors on Skjold were drilled and perforated to utilize water injection
supporting a bottom up displacement. Gas injection is normally performed as a top
down displacement taking advantage of gravity due to the density diﬀerences between
the ﬂuids which will reduce the chance of viscous ﬁngering as explained in section 3.3.3.
The produced water during normal water injection in the ﬁeld are recirculated and no
disposal is needed at the current moment. However, changing to gas injection would
require water to be disposed or used at Gorm/Rolf as water injection.
The main objective of the full ﬁeld gas injection simulation case is to estimate the total
amount of gas needed and analysing well-by-well performance. The Skjold ﬁeld is highly
fractured with exception of the Hatten area as can be seen in section 8.3. According
to literature, performance of gas injection could be highly dependent on the magnitude
of fractures, the fracture spacing and on other eﬀects such as diﬀusion from fracture to
matrix.
Data from this scenario would enable the determination on whether full ﬁeld gas injection
is both technically and economically viable or focusing on speciﬁc parts of the ﬁeld is a
better option. The technical feasibility is regarding the constraints of the ﬁeld such as the
pipeline and compressor capacity. Investments in new facilities, which often requires high
50
==PAGE:66==
capital expenses, would only be justiﬁed in case very high incremental oil production is
observed compared with the base case of continuing water injection.
7.2
Localized Gas Injection
An alternative solution to the full ﬁeld gas injection is to conduct a localized gas injection
in certain parts of the ﬁelds. This can be done by targeting the areas where the largest
remaining oil volumes are located or using the data from the full ﬁeld gas injection and
locate the areas where gas injections seems to show the most promising results. Localized
gas injection can also be a solution if the amount of gas in the full ﬁeld gas injection
exceeds the constraints. There are three ways of conducting localized gas injection:
1. Utilizing gas injection in the areas at which the most promising results was obtained
by the full ﬁeld gas injection while maintaining water injection in the remaining
parts of the ﬁeld.
2. Utilizing gas injection in the areas at which the most promising results was obtained
by the full ﬁeld gas injection while shutting down the water injection in the remaining
parts of the ﬁeld.
3. Initiate blowdown of the ﬁeld until the point where oil production becomes less
than the oil production from the base case. Re-inject the gas that has come out of
solution during the ﬁrst part of the blowdown.
Case two and three initiates the blowdown of the ﬁeld. However, the theory is that gas
injection can be initiated to recover more oil. Moreover the water injection pipeline can
be converted to a gas pipeline minimizing the need for investments in infrastructure.
Case one would require both a water injection pipeline and a gas injection pipeline.
Depending on the amount of gas needed to perform gas injection and also maintaining
gas lift support investments in additional pipelines may be required. Previous studies
performed on blowdown of the Skjold ﬁeld by shutting in the water injection yields an
increase in oil and gas production. The increase in oil production is mainly caused by the
solution gas drive mechanism explained in section 3.1. Gas production is increased due
to the associated gas coming out of solution. The pressure drop in the reservoir would at
some point reach a critical point where the pressure in the wells can no longer lift the oil
to surface, furthermore reducing the pressure will make the oil/gas interactions negligible
and thus the beneﬁts from re-injecting associated gas would diminish.

8. Computational Method
The calculation of behaviour in a reservoir model can be made in two diﬀerent ways:
• Using the black-oil model which is based on simple interpolation of PVT properties
as a function of pressure
• Using a compositional model which is based on a thermodynamically consistent
model, e.g. cubic equations of state (EOS)
A reservoir simulator keeps track of the overall composition in each grid cell as function
of time. The composition in a cell will change caused by both to-cell ﬂuxes and phase
ﬂuxes and also by production or injection in wells.
The current model for the Skjold ﬁeld is build around the E100 mode using the simple
black oil model. Simulations has therefore all been conducted in the E100 mode. The
E300 compositional mode can be run with a proper EOS model which should be tuned to
the composition of injected gas and the composition of the oil. The oil composition will
change upon commencing gas injection due to the interactions between the oil and gas
described in section 3. Oil would gradually lose its lighter components to the gas which
would make the oil more rich in heavy oil components. The EOS model can capture the
eﬀect of the changing compositions due to interactions and thereby give a more accurate
representation

Static Model
The ﬁrst reservoir geological study became available in 1984. As a result from 3D-seismic
observations in certain wells where a decrease in reservoir quality as function of reservoir
thickness and strong fracturing of the crest was observed. Estimated OOIP was 600
MMstb. A subsequent study revealed unswept areas and recovery could be increased by
completing additional wells. The ﬁeld development plan from 1992 is another full ﬁeld
modelling and simulation study which proposed installation of two additional well-head
platforms and 11 new wells to develop the eastern and northern sectors of the ﬁeld. OOIP
was estimated to 767 MMstb however lacking in representing the full ﬁeld interpretation
of faults. Two main fracture orientations were assumed (NW-SE and NE-SW). The
model was based on an 11-layer dual porosity conﬁguration with 100 m cell size.
The current dual permeability model covers an area of 18.2 km2 by a rectangular outline
of 5200 m by 3500 m. The deeper sections of the wells SKJ-23G and SKJ-30A/B are not
included in the model.

History Match E300 with Compositional Model
The ﬁrst step when implementing a new model is to see how well it matches with
production data. The starting point was the black oil model. The model is built in the
E100 environment and modiﬁcations had to be made in order to run E300 compositional
simulations. An analysis of keywords in the E100 model showed that the keywords VISCD,
LX, LY and LZ all was not compatible in E300.
75
==PAGE:91==
The VISCD keyword accounts for viscous displacement in the matrix due to pressure
diﬀerence in the fractures and thereby the displacement of ﬂuids from the matrix. This
is reasonable to ignore when the fracture permeability is high since the pressure gradient
will be negligible. However, if the fracture permeability is more moderate the eﬀect of
viscous displacement can be signiﬁcant and should not be ignored. No alternatives exist
for the VISCD keyword in E300.
E100 uses LX, LY and LZ keywords to calculate the value of SIGMAV in equation (6.23)
as described in equation (6.24). The SIGMAV value had to be deﬁned directly in E300,
which was done using the values of the LX, LY and LZ keywords thus the SIGMAV used in
the two models should be identical.
The next step after making the original model compatible with E300 was to change
the ﬂuid model to an compositional model. The tuning of the EOS model was done in
PVTsim which has the option to make an output ﬁle specially designed for the use in
Eclipse. The output ﬁle with the tuned EOS model was implemented in Eclipse where
only the unit system had to be speciﬁed.
In total four simulations have been done in order to make the E300 history match.
Between each history match only one parameter was changed as described below:
1. A history match in E100 where the SIGMAV keyword had been modiﬁed and directly
deﬁned to check the sensitivity of the history match to the new deﬁnition.
2. A history match in E100 with the new deﬁnition of the keyword SIGMAV and the
VISCD removed. Ths was done to check the history match in E100 with model
modiﬁcation made.
3. A history match in E300 where the modiﬁed E100 model is run in E300 to check
that the same results are applied on the new platform.
4. A history match in E300 with the implementation of the EOS model, i.e. run in
compositional mode.

8.6
Diﬀusion Analysis
As described in section 3.3.4 the inclusion of diﬀusion in the simulations can have an
eﬀect on the overall production of ﬂuids especially in highly fractured reservoirs.
All the scenarios presented in section 7 will be conducted both with and without diﬀusion
to determine whether diﬀusion has an eﬀect on the simulations. The keyword DIFFUSE is
entered in the DATA ﬁle which turns on diﬀusion. The keywords DIFFAOIL and DIFFAGAS
are speciﬁed in PROPS ﬁle which speciﬁes the activity corrected diﬀusion coeﬃcients along
with the value of the diﬀusion coeﬃcients to be used in the simulation. Only a single
value of diﬀusion coeﬃcients can be used in Eclipse which is a simpliﬁcation since the
diﬀusion coeﬃcients in reality will vary over time due to the change in pressure.
A sensitivity analysis will be conducted in order to evaluate the eﬀects of including
diﬀusion in the simulations. The following simulation scheme will be used:
1. Normal diﬀusion coeﬃcients
2. No diﬀusion coeﬃcients
79
==PAGE:95==
3. High case - Normal diﬀusion coeﬃcients multiplied by a factor 5
4. Low case - Normal diﬀusion coeﬃcients divided by a factor 5
The diﬀusion coeﬃcients used in the simulations are from the work of M. Ghasemi
et al. [32]. An argument for using these diﬀusion coeﬃcients is that the live oil used in
their experiments has considerable amount of methane, even though the experiment is
conducted with CO2 injection in contrary to the lean hydrocarbon gas injection used in
this work. The experimental diﬀusion coeﬃcients estimated in the work of M. Ghasemi
takes into account the tortuosity of the reservoir and the presence of water as discussed in
section 3.3.4. The Extended Sigmund correlation could also have been used which would
have given other uncertainties such as estimating mixing ratio κ and how to account for
the presence of water and tortuosity.
The EOS model used by M. Ghasemi et al. have diﬀerent components than the model
used in this work hence the diﬀusion coeﬃcients had to be lumped appropriately. The
diﬀusion coeﬃcients from M. Ghasemi et al. and the lumped diﬀusion coeﬃcients used
in the simulations can be seen in table 8.10. Detailed calculations of the diﬀusions
coeﬃcients can be found in appendix.

10. Economic Evaluation
An economic evaluation of each scenario was conducted to evaluate the commerciality of
each scenario and how they compare to each other. A Net Present Value (NPV) analysis
will be performed to investigate whether gas injection can be a commercially viable
solution. The calculations will be made before taxes, deductions and on 100% equity
share.
The NPV is the diﬀerence between the present value of cash inﬂows and the present
value of cash outﬂows. A positive NPV indicates that the projected earnings exceeds
the anticipated costs. Generally, a positive NPV will be proﬁtable while a negative
NPV results in a net loss. The discount rate used in the calculations will vary between
industries as this is related to the time value of money. In this work a discount rate of
10% will be used in the economic evaluation.
Another parameter often used to evaluate projects is the Internal Rate of Return (IRR).
This parameter does however require a capital expenditure (CAPEX) since it is the
return on the investment made.
To do a proper economic evaluation it is necessary to understand the cost associated
with the diﬀerent processes ranging from the compression of the injection gas to the
separation process related to the production streams. The facility engineers at Maersk
Oil supplied ballpark ﬁgures for the diﬀerent operations. The operating expenses (OPEX)
are presented in table 10.1.
No cost estimates on gas injection was available, however gas-lift operations can be
assumed to resemble gas injection and the cost was therefore assumed to be of the same
order of magnitude. Note, that gas-lift only needs to enter the well bore thus argument
can be made that less pressure is required to inject the lift gas into the well bore in
comparison to gas injection.

Besides the NPV calculation a stochastic evaluation will also be done using the
Crystal Ball add-on for excel. 10,000 trials are completed for each case to ensure a
reliable sampling size. The stochastic analysis is done until the year where each case
have their peak NPV in the regular NPV calculation. The variables are the oil price, gas
price, exch. rate and the OPEX costs. The max oil and max gas price are set as
Max Oil Price = Oil Price + 20 $
(10.3)
Max Gas Price = Gas Price + 2.5 $
(10.4)
The oil price will follow a right shifted log-normal distribution with minimum at 25 $/bbl,
a mean at 50 $/bbl and a std. dev. on 10 $/bbl. The right shifted log-normal distribution
will yield a lower chance of a high price environment. The gas price will have BetaPERT
distribution which is a hybrid between a normal distribution and a triangle distribution.
It will have the form of a normal distribution but it will not extend to +/- inﬁnity but
instead end at a deﬁned minimum and maximum value. The parameters will be the
following: Minimum 5 $/Mscf, likeliest 7.5 $/Mscf, maximum 10 $/Mscf. The exch.
rate will also follow a betaPERT distribution but will have the following parameters:
Minimum 5 DKK/$, likeliest 6.9 DKK/$ and a maximum 9 DKK/$
The OPEX costs are all assumed to follow a betaPERT distribution the likeliest is set as
the ballpark ﬁgure given in table 10.1 while the minimum and maximum is set as 15%
below and above the value stated in table 10.1. For detailed overview of the calculations
see appendix.

11. Discussion
All of the cases that have been run shows an abrupt increase in gas production when
gas injection is initiated. This is evident on ﬁgure 9.4, 9.13 and 9.21. The very abrupt
increase is suspected to be caused by extensive fracture network in many areas of the
Skjold ﬁeld. Fractures can thus work as high conduit channels for the injected gas ﬂowing
directly towards producers without interacting much with oil in the matrix.
The wells and perforations in Skjold are conﬁgured to support water injection, i.e. the
injectors are quite deep whereas the producers are closer to the top. This bottom up
injection scheme will stabilize the displacement front in water injection due to gravity
forces. The opposite eﬀect is true for gas as the density of gas is much lower than the oil
in place leading to gravity override which may cause viscous ﬁngering as explained in
section 3.3.3.
Changing well patterns require signiﬁcant investments, however well perforations can be
changed to support gas injection in the crest allowing a top down displacement. A second
option is conformance treatment that would reduce the permeability in the fractures
thereby increasing the sweep eﬃciency. Both of the mentioned solutions has CAPEX cost
associated and therefore may be economically infeasible to conduct on a mature ﬁelds
such as Skjold, however depending on the completion technology zones can be closed
with insigniﬁcant amount of CAPEX.
11.1
Full Field Gas Injection
In the ﬁrst attempt all the water injectors were switched to gas injection solely by chang-
ing the type of injection ﬂuid. It was evident that transferring the same BHP values from
water to gas injection would result in a declining pressure proﬁle. This can be seen on
ﬁgure 9.1 where the water injection has a constant pressure of 2800 psi while the full ﬁeld
gas injections (PRED E300 COMP G ALL) declines. The pressure decline to around 1000 psi
is far below the saturation pressure and this case is therefore classiﬁed as a blowdown of
the ﬁeld making it incomparable with the water injection base case. Investigation of the
individual well’s bottom hole pressures revealed that several wells had lower bottom hole
pressure relative to the water injection case. Each well was then optimized with regard
to pressure attempting to yield similar pressure curves as for the water injection case.
This can be seen on ﬁgure 9.1 where the ﬁnal case (PRED G ALL DIFF FINAL) resembles
the pressure proﬁle of water injection reasonably well.
122
==PAGE:138==
Production ﬁgures for the oil and gas production in the ﬁnal full ﬁeld gas injection
with no diﬀusion case can be seen on ﬁgure 9.2 and 9.3. An abrupt increase in oil
production can be seen going from approx. 7,000 stb/d in the water injection proﬁle to
more than 11,000 stb/d. The production declines with another peak occurring in 2023
where the production increases to 11,000 stb/d from 10,000 stb/d. The sudden increase
observed between year 2022 and 2023 in both production rates and pressure is caused by
the wells SKJ-11A, SKJ-7B and SKJ-16. Figure 9.5 shows the injection rate of SKJ-11A
increases fourth fold while both SKJ-7B and SKJ-16 decreases.
Figure 9.3 shows that gas production increases abruptly upon the initiation of gas
injection and steadily starts to reach an asymptotic value. The gas production is several
orders of magnitude higher than the water injection case reaching values of more than
300 MMscf/d. The increase in gas production is expected due to the high volumes of gas
injected. However, it was expected that the increase would gradually increase.
It is evident from ﬁgure 9.8 and 9.9 that the majority of the oil and gas production is
coming from the Hatten area. Indeed, this was the area with most of remaining oil in
place. The gas injection is also highest in the Hatten area where also the majority of the
injectors is located.
11.2
Localized Gas Injection
Figure 9.12 and 9.13 shows the rates for the ﬁnal optimized case of localized gas injection.
Gas injection is started in 2019 which leads to an increased oil production from approx.
7200 STB/d to approx. 10200 STB/d where after it declines towards the production
levels of water injection. The increase is presumably due to gas sweeping diﬀerent pores
and parts of the reservoir as the theory states (section 3.3.3).
The bump observed for oil production mid-2023 is due to the change of injectors from
SKJ-3A and SKJ-7B to SKJ-11A and SKJ-16, new parts of the reservoir is being swept
as a consequence of changing injectors.
The ﬁnal bump in oil production in 2029 is due to the initiation of a blowdown. The
blowdown initially leads to reduced gas production since gas is no longer recycled through
the fractures however the net gas production increases signiﬁcantly and is approx. 40
MMscf/d. The solution gas drive mechanism starts being eﬀective during the blowdown
as gas comes out of solution as explained in the theory section 3.3.3. It is interesting to
note the increased oil and gas production are oﬀset relative to each other. The oﬀset
is suspected to be due to gas coming out of solution where it will be immobile until it
reaches the critical gas saturation and starts to ﬂow. It is evident from ﬁgure 9.13 that
the scenario is gas deﬁcient in the start-up phase and when switching injectors.
11.3
Blowdown with re-injection
A blowdown of the ﬁeld is initiated in 2018 with the objective of re-injecting the associated
gas produced once it has reached suﬃciently high production rates. Figure 9.18 shows that

several cases were conducted. It can be seen that oil production increases immediately
after the initiation of the blowdown caused by the solution gas drive mechanism. In
2019 associated gas from the oil production is re-injected into the Hatten area yielding a
limited increase in oil production. Only two injectors are used due to the compressor
limitations namely SKJ-3A and SKJ-7B from 2019-2021. In 2021 two diﬀerent injectors
are activated yielding an increase in oil production. The increase can be due to the sweep
of diﬀerent places in the ﬁeld. Switching between diﬀerent injectors seems to ad value
when the injection rates are limited by the compressor constraint.
Re-injecting the associated gas has the advantage that the supply of gas will be indepen-
dent of uptime in other parts of the ﬁeld. Figure 9.21 compares the gas production to gas
injection. It is evident that gas production is high enough during the entire re-injection
period yielding excess production which could have been utilized in case compressor
capacity was available. The maximum gas production is reached in 2021 with a gas
rate of approx. 85 MMscf/d and injectors are running at constant rate of approx. 55
MMscf/d. A dip in gas production is observed in 2021 where the injectors are switched.
Injection stops in 2029 and the ﬁeld goes through a regular blowdown. Injecting gas in
Hatten area is not suﬃcient in itself to act as pressure support for the ﬁeld and a steady
decline is observed also during the re-injection period.
11.4
Blowdown
The oil and gas production for pure blowdown starting in 2018 is presented on ﬁgure 9.26
and 9.27. It is noted the increased oil and gas production as a consequence of solution gas
drive is oﬀset relative to each other as it is in the blowdown at the end of the localized
gas injection case (section 11.2). The oil production peaks in mid-2018. The increase
is similar to the increase observed for the blowdown in the localized gas injection case.
The maximum gas production of approx. 45 MMscf/d is located 2021 and is almost 5
MMscf/d greater relatively to the localized gas injection case. The greater beneﬁt of
conducting a blowdown in 2018 compared to 2028 is suspected to be due to additional
barrels located in the reservoir.
A blowdown is most often done when a decision of abandonment of the ﬁeld has
been taken. Once a blowdown is initiated the ﬁeld will after a short period of time stop
producing due to depressurization. The blowdown can be initiated as a standalone case
or after having initiated gas injection to produce trapped and associated gas. There is
however some signiﬁcant uncertainties linked with a blowdown primarily linked to the
compaction of the reservoir formation due to lower pore pressure as the compaction table
(table 8.8) is estimated based on a correlation and does not reﬂect the actual nature of
the reservoir. The depressurerization of the ﬁeld will increase the stresses on the wells
due to the reduced pore pressure which may result in severe well integrity problems. The
compaction of the pores and fractures can also lead to errors in the simulated production
ﬁgures. A proper study of the rock physics and mechanics has to be conducted prior
to initiating a blowdown to fully understand the response from the formation once the
124
==PAGE:140==
reservoir pressure is lowered.
11.5
Diﬀusion
It was expected that the inclusion of diﬀusion in the simulations would result in a visible
increase in oil production due to the extensive amount of fractures in the Skjold ﬁeld.
In the full ﬁeld injection diﬀusion analysis on ﬁgure 9.6 is it evident that the change
in oil production seems to be relatively small even when the diﬀusions coeﬃcients are
multiplied and divided by a factor 5. No clear diﬀerence is observed between the cases
where diﬀusion is included and omitted in the simulations. The eﬀect of diﬀusion seems to
be more predominant on the oil production which is expected as the diﬀusion coeﬃcients
of the gas into oil is an order of magnitude greater than the diﬀusion coeﬃcients of
oil into gas (table 8.10). The diﬀusion of lighter components into the oil phase would
make it less viscous and hence increase its ﬂowing properties hereby increasing overall oil
production. It is observed that the diﬀerence between the cases without diﬀusion and
the high diﬀusion cases increases with time. The diﬀusion is as previously explained a
very slow process where molecules diﬀuses from the fracture network into the matrix.
Diﬀusion is thus a slow process relative to convective ﬂow and diﬀusion eﬀects may
become more predominant with time.
According to theory diﬀusion should have a more signiﬁcant eﬀect in the fractured areas
as explained in section 3.3.4. This is not entirely what is reﬂected in the results on ﬁgure
9.8. By comparing the diﬀerence on the high and low diﬀusion case it is evident that
the Hatten area is showing a higher sensitivity towards the magnitude of the diﬀusion
coeﬃcient, i.e. diﬀusion eﬀects are more pronounced in this area even though it is less
fractured. A possible explanation of the increased sensitivity in the Hatten area, is
that the area has the most of the remaining oil which also contributes to the higher
sensitivity to diﬀusion. Another explanation is that more gas is being injected in that
area relative to other parts of the ﬁeld, as seen on ﬁgure 9.9 and ﬁgure 9.10, yielding a
higher concentration which would aﬀect the diﬀusion process as it is also dependent on
concentration. This explanation is further backed by the two other cases i.e. localized
gas injection and blowdown with re-injection on ﬁgure 9.15 and 9.23 where a signiﬁcant
smaller volume of gas is injected. It is evident on these ﬁgures that the sensitivity towards
the diﬀusion coeﬃcients is insigniﬁcant since the oil production rate is more or less the
same in the diﬀerent diﬀusion cases.
11.6
Black Oil versus Compositional Simulations
Compositional simulation is conducted to reﬂect the nature of changing composition in
the oil and gas phases due to the mechanisms of vaporization and condensation. These
mechanism are most predominant when the pressure is close or above the MMP. The
slim-tube simulations showed that the Skjold oil had a MMP of 7300 psig which is more
than two times higher than the current reservoir pressure of approx. 2900 psig. The gas
injection would therefore most likely be immiscible with no or little interactions between
125
==PAGE:141==
the oil and gas phases making the compositional simulation unnecessary due to longer
computational time.
To investigate this hypothesis a series of simulations was done for all the extreme cases
i.e. water injection, full ﬁeld gas injection and blowdown which are presented on ﬁgure
9.32-9.37. It can be observed that the two diﬀerent models agrees on the general shape of
the production proﬁles. For the water injection and blowdown the black oil model does
yield a higher oil production and lower gas production compared to the compositional
model. For full ﬁeld gas injection the compositional model does however yield both a
higher oil and gas production. This is suspected to be due to interactions between the
oil and gas phase taking place even though the pressure is well below the MMP, which is
interactions not captured by the black oil model. One can argue that the compositional
model reﬂects real reservoir behaviour more accurately.
11.7
Economics
It is interesting that the blowdown has the highest NPV of all the cases investigated
even though it is the case with the lowest cumulative oil production. The lower OPEX
combined with the high net gas production compensates for the lower oil production. In
the gas injection cases the gross gas production is high but the produced gas will have to
be re-injected again leaving little to no gas left to be exported.
There is some uncertainty linked to the OPEX cost as they are estimated based on the
current production data. Cost associated with the diﬀerent operations will change as
gas injection is a completely diﬀerent production method. The lack of experience with
gas injection can also lead to higher costs than estimated. From the tornado charts i.e.
ﬁgures 10.2, 10.4, 10.6, 10.8 it is evident that the NPVs of the cases are not particularly
sensitive towards the OPEX costs as compared to the oil price, gas price and exchange
rates. It is therefore acceptable that the OPEX costs is not calibrated entirely accurate
to gas injection. Note, that the NPV is very sensitive to the exchange rate. This is due
to the fact that in the economic model the expenditure is in DKK but the income is in
USD.
In the stochastic evaluations on ﬁgures 10.1, 10.3, 10.5, 10.7 it can be observed that the
economics in the blowdown case is more robust since the spread is lower. This can be
observed on the general shape of the distribution and on the smaller distance between
P25 and P75. The spread is highest for water injection and localized gas injection which
indeed are the two cases with the latest abandonment date.
In the model used to calculate the OPEX the produced gas is not taken into account
as no cost is associated with gas production in the model supplied by Maersk Oil. Gas
produced with water injection is associated gas from the oil. Cost of processing and
exporting produced gas from a gas cap or gas injection must be taken into account.
No CAPEX cost is included in the economic evaluation due to the fact that Skjold is an
existing ﬁeld and the implementation of gas injection would require negligible investments.
The abandonment/decommissioning cost of the ﬁeld have not been included in the
economic model, as no reliable estimates on decommission cost of the top sites is available.
126
==PAGE:142==
In case abandonment/decommissioning cost is included in the economic evaluation the
NPV would decrease even further for all the cases. Assuming decommissioning at the
maximum NPV the inclusion of ABEX cost would have a greater impact on the blowdown
scenario due to timing eﬀects. However, if the full abandonment of the ﬁeld can be
postponed until the expire of the license in 2042, a higher NPV value can be achieved.
With the current setup one of the compressors can deliver the required gas-lift for Gorm,
Rolf and Skjold. This setup has the advantage that one compressor can be maintained
while the other is delivering gas-lift. If gas injection is implemented both compressors
will run at maximum capacity may lead to more downtime which is not reﬂected in the
economic evaluation.
11.8
Optimization
A thorough investigation of the production in each well was conducted to optimize the
over all ﬁeld production. It was evident from the well-by-well analysis that some wells
over-performed while others under-performed. An analysis of the GOR and oil production
was done for each well to determine wells with abnormally high GOR relative to other
wells. These were shut-in as the high GOR may have been caused by a connecting path
between the producer and injector leading to undesired recycling of the injected gas.
As a consequence of the gas injection the GOR has increased signiﬁcantly in the produc-
tion stream. This is suspected to make the separate gas-lift procedure unnecessary or
ineﬃcient. Gas-lift has the positive eﬀect of reducing the density of the ﬂuid column in
the wellbore and thereby making it easier to lift the ﬂuid to surface. Too much gas in the
wellbore will increase the friction counteracting the reduced density. The balance is to
ﬁnd the optimal amount of gas in the well bore. The diﬀerent cases have been run with
and without gas-lift in order to determine which wells gas-lift could be omitted. Several
wells showed an increase in production by omitting gas-lift. In the optimized cases on
ﬁgure 9.17 and 9.25 no signiﬁcant change in production rates is observed but the gas-lift
rates are lower in these cases meaning there is further room for optimization.
The key learning from the optimization procedure is the timing of closing wells, injection
rates and gas-lift can add value to the ﬁeld. The set-up used for the simulation cases can
not be implemented directly in the actual ﬁeld since the model used in the simulations is
only an analogue model and not a real representation of the actual ﬁeld.
The EOS model used in this work is based on a PVT study from 1985 and does
not have any H2S in its description but after several years of water injection Skjold has
been soured caused by microbial H2S production. H2S is a good miscibility promoting
component and the inclusion of H2S in the model may have lowered the MMP which not
necessarily makes the displacement miscible but increases the interactions and thereby
achieving better recoveries.
The parachors used to calculate the IFT between phases could have been optimized
if experimental data was available. The IFT will have an inﬂuence on the miscibility
between the two phases and therefore the recovery factor.

12. Conclusion
12.1
EOS model
A six component EOS model has been constructed with PVTsim with tuning of the
model based on the data from a PVT study on Skjold. The model shows good ﬁt with
the experimental work done on a Skjold live oil. The MMP of the Skjold Oil is according
to our EOS model 7267 psig which is well above the current reservoir pressure of approx.
2900 psig. It is noted that the PVT study is from an old PVT study (1985) and the
composition in the ﬁeld may have changed over the years. The EOS model has been
successfully implemented in Eclipse 300 compositional mode with dual permeability
setting. The model has been used to make a history match which showed good agreement
both with the black oil model and the historic production data. Subsequent to the history
match predictive gas injection simulations were successfully conducted using the same
EOS model.
12.2
Eclipse simulations
The diﬀerent gas injection scenarios was conducted and compared to a base case of
continued water injection. It was evident that gas injection is technically feasible.
Full ﬁeld gas injection
did result in an increase in oil and gas production. However,
the injection rate of approx. 400 MMscf/d on a ﬁeld level exceeds the amount of available
gas. Furthermore, the computational time increased by several orders of magnitude
compared to the base case.
Localized gas injection
gas injection was initiated only in the Hatten area where
most of the remaining oil is located. Overall ﬁeld cumulative oil production increased by
2 MMstb relative to the base case while gas production increased by 52 bcm. The highest
NPV (and time of abandonment) was reached in year 2035 reaching 6.142 billion DKK.
Blowdown with re-injection
was also initiated in the Hatten area showing a decrease
in oil production of 6 MMstb which was oﬀset by an increase in gas production of 76
bcm. The highest NPV is reached in year 2032 reaching 6.336 billion DKK. This case
128
==PAGE:144==
has the advantage of not being gas deﬁcient at any point compared to the localized gas
injection case.
Blowdown
of the ﬁeld was initiated in 2018. The cumulative oil production decreases
by 8 MMstb which is oﬀset by an increase in gas production of 87 bcm. The highest
NPV is reached in 2028 and reaches a value of 6.730 billion DKK.
Localized gas injection and blowdown with re-injection have been optimized through
a series of iterations, ﬁrst a full ﬁeld gas injection simulation was conducted to isolate
the most responsive areas. Thereafter the simulations focused on said area where several
simulations was done with a well-by-well analysis on each simulation conducted.
It was concluded that the inclusion of diﬀusion did not change the over all production
of the ﬁeld. However, there is some uncertainty related with the diﬀusion coeﬃcients as
they did not represent the diﬀusion coeﬃcients of the speciﬁc oil and gas in the Skjold
ﬁeld. It was evident from the sensitivity study that the inclusion of diﬀusion did not
have a signiﬁcant impact on the results.
Gas lift cannot be omitted for all wells, however, a well-by-well analysis revealed that
gas-lift on certain wells can advantageously be stopped to boost, there is room for further
improvement.
12.3
Feasibility
The most economically viable scenario is a blowdown of the ﬁeld. It has a maximum
NPV of 6.73 billion DKK which outperforms the gas injection scenarios. A full ﬁeld
gas injection is not economically viable, however an alternative solution would be to
either conduct gas injection in the Hatten area with and without water injection in the
rest of the ﬁeld. The most economically viable solution is to re-inject associated gas
during blowdown of the ﬁeld as seen by the NPV values above. It is emphasized that the
decommissioning cost and cost for producing gas is not included in the current model
while also assuming no investments are needed to switch from current water injection to
gas injection.
There is some uncertainty with a blowdown as this can cause well integrity problems, i.e.
well collapse due to depressurization of the ﬁeld, which could severely damage the overall
economics of the project. The same applies with the introduction of gas injection which
is a task never conducted before oﬀshore in the Danish part of the North Sea and the
lack of experience and competency may lead to higher costs than anticipated.
The stochastic evaluation concluded that the NPV is highly sensitive to oil and gas
prices and also the applied exchange rates while OPEX cost had little or no inﬂuence. The
blowdown scenario had the lowest spread and therefore the least amount of uncertainty
129
==PAGE:145==
making it an even more favourable choice. Eventually, the conclusion may switch to
favour other scenarios depending of the gain of the extra gas produced relatively to the
oil lost during blowdown and the commodity price.

13. Further Work
It is important to understand the critical gas saturation in the blowdown scenario as
this is the saturation where gas becomes mobile. Until this point gas will be trapped in
the pores hence the volume expansion of gas will displace oil and thereby boost the oil
recovery. Thus a study to understand the critical gas saturation would reduce the number
of uncertainties linked with gas injection and the blowdown cases. A quick understanding
can be obtained through a sensitivity study where the critical gas saturation is varied
much like what was done in the diﬀusion sensitivity study.
Diﬀusion did not have much eﬀect in the sensitivity simulations on the localized gas
injection case or on the re-injection case where only the diﬀusion coeﬃcients was varied.
It would be interesting to do a fracture sensitivity simulation for each case as well as
literature states that fracture spacing is an a important factor for the diﬀusion process.
It was noted in the literature [56] that the grid size in a static model used for a black
oil simulation in many cases is not ﬁne enough for a compositional simulation, hence a
grid sensitivity study should be conducted to understand the eﬀects a ﬁner grid would
have on the production rates. The grid sensitivity could be done in a box model in
Eclipse. This method would make it possible to quickly conduct multiple simulations
with diﬀerent grid sizes. The grid is not expected to have a signiﬁcant eﬀect since the
production rates for the compositional and black oil simulations on ﬁgure 9.32-9.37 are
very similar.
In this work only one simulation per case has been used in the economical model.
Good practice when forecasting is to make a low, mid and high case and the parameters
in Eclipse should be varied to reﬂect these cases subsequently conducting an economic
analysis to obtain a range of NPVs.
The economic model used in this work does not have a cost associated with the produced
gas and this cost should be added to get an accurate economical model. Furthermore the
decommission cost is suspected to have a signiﬁcant impact on the NPV values.
Stochastic evaluations are excellent to yield a range of possibilities however it can be
diﬃcult to ﬁt a distribution to the oil and gas prices as these cost does not follow any
common distribution. Furthermore, the current set-up has one oil price and one gas price
input and forecast to increase the subsequent years making the model unable to simulate
ﬂuctuations in oil and gas prices. It is therefore suggested to have the oil and gas prices
131
==PAGE:147==
for each year as a stochastic variable allowing the prices to ﬂuctuate in both negative
and positive directions relatively to the main input.
