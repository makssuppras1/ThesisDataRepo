Abstract
This thesis explores the potential market and consumer preferences for eco­friendly to­
bacco products in response to growing environmental concerns and the sustainable con­
sumption movement. Despite the increasing demand for sustainable goods, the tobacco
industry has been slow to integrate environmental considerations into its product offer­
ings. This research aims to bridge this gap by assessing consumer willingness to pay
for eco­friendly tobacco, using eco­friendly alcohol studies as a basis due to the lack of
studies on tobacco.
The research methodology combines consumer surveys that explore stated preferences
with qualitative studies from alcohol, which are then adapted to tobacco. This approach
thoroughly analyzes consumer attitudes, preferences, and willingness to pay for eco­
friendly tobacco options.
The study finds that consumers are interested in eco­friendly alcohol products, with an av­
erage willingness to pay more of 15.2% for sustainable alcohol. This trend is particularly
strong among young (25­34), high­income, sustainability­conscious consumers, indicat­
ing a potential market opportunity for brands that invest in sustainable practices. How­
ever, the study also highlights some challenges, such as the use of alcohol studies, the
reliance on stated preference surveys rather than real data, and strict market regulations
on tobacco.

Introduction
From the ancient rituals of the Americas to the active trade of the European colonial era,
tobacco has undeniably woven itself into the fabric of human history. The dawn of the
20th century saw tobacco transformed into a global commercial titan, propelled by the
winds of the Industrial Revolution and innovations in manufacturing and marketing.
Yet, the methods that catapulted the tobacco industry into global prominence are now
its Achilles heel. The rapid expansion techniques to cater to escalating demands have
unveiled pressing environmental issues.
Beyond environmental dilemmas, the shadow of health risks associated with tobacco
looms large. The tobacco industry grapples with pressing challenges and opportunities
in a world pivoting rapidly towards sustainability and health. Delving into sustainable to­
bacco avenues is not just about global alignment but potentially signifies an industry re­
birth, catering to a new wave of eco­aware consumers.
1.1
The World’s Sustainable Call
The scream for sustainability resonates louder than ever in today’s world. It isn’t just about
trends; it’s a global mandate. Industries, ranging from agriculture to tech, are redefining
their paradigms, aiming to reduce their carbon footprints, champion resource conserva­
tion, and usher in a new era of conscientious consumption.
1.2
Dilemma of the Tobacco Realm
Amidst the health debates, the environmental repercussions of the tobacco industry have
emerged as a formidable challenge. The legacies of traditional tobacco farming—deforestation,
uncontrolled water consumption, and excessive reliance on pesticides—underscore an
urgent environmental narrative.
1.3
Changing Tides of Consumer Choices
In today’s rapidly evolving marketplace, the profile of a typical consumer has undergone
a profound transformation. Gone are the days when purchasing decisions were driven
solely by price and convenience. The contemporary consumer can often be seen as more
than just a buyer; they are an advocate with a conscience, with their choices reflecting
a deep­seated concern for the planet. This shift towards environmental consciousness
reshapes market dynamics, sparking changes across many sectors. [1]
The trend towards organic dining, sustainable fashion, and eco­friendly personal care
products is not a fleeting fad but a steadfast norm. These choices mirror a broader societal
shift, a growing acknowledgment of our collective responsibility towards the environment.
There has been an increasing demand for sustainable and organic options in Industries
like personal items. This indicates how consumer preferences are evolving, emphasizing
sustainability over traditional considerations. [2]
However, one industry that appears to lag in responding to this eco­conscious wave is the
tobacco sector. While other industries have gradually embraced the green revolution, to­
bacco remains relatively absent.[3] This gap is a missed opportunity and a critical area that
needs exploration, especially in understanding the market potential and consumer pref­
erences for eco­friendly tobacco products. Notably, the GreyB Tobacco Industry Trends

1.5
Scope of the Research
The focal point of this inquiry lies in uncovering the feasibility and consumer receptivity
towards green tobacco alternatives. Eco­friendly and environmentally sustainable alter­
natives refer to options that have a positive effect on the environment. For example,
these alternatives may include organic products that are grown without the use of syn­
thetic pesticides and fertilizers, deforestation management, energy reduction, low carbon
footprint, water conservation, biodegradable materials, sustainable crop rotation and land
management, among others.
This research parallels the alcoholic beverage industry, which has witnessed a notable
shift towards sustainable practices[5]. The rationale behind this comparison stems from
the similarities in consumer demographics and consumption patterns between the two
industries. Both sectors target adult consumers and focus on product quality and brand
loyalty, while others prioritize price. However, the recent pivot towards sustainability in
the alcoholic beverage industry provides a valuable case study in understanding how
traditional industries can successfully integrate eco­friendly practices.
By examining the strategies employed in the alcoholic beverage sector, this research
aims to glean insights that could apply to the tobacco industry. The success stories and
challenges encountered in the former serve as a blueprint for potential pathways and
pitfalls in promoting green tobacco products. This approach allows us to understand better
how consumers respond to environmentally friendly initiatives in industries with similar
characteristics.
However, it is vital to acknowledge the limitations of this approach. The tobacco industry
has unique challenges and regulatory environments that differ significantly from those of
the alcoholic beverage industry. For instance, the tobacco industry faces stringent regu­
lations related to health warnings, advertising restrictions, and age restrictions on product
sales, which are not as prevalent or restrictive in the alcoholic beverage sector. Addi­
tionally, issues related to smoking­related health concerns and public perception create
distinct challenges for the tobacco industry that may not directly translate to the alcoholic
beverage industry. These regulatory frameworks and differences in public perception can
impact the feasibility and effectiveness of implementing similar strategies between the

Drinking and Smoking Relationship
In this chapter, I will be examining the relationship between drinking and smoking. I will
explain why they are similar, how they can be interdependent, and the various connections
they share. My aim is to help you comprehend why I am using studies on alcohol demand
to explore the potential demand for eco­friendly tobacco given the lack of eco­friendly
tobacco studies.
2.1
Behavioral Interactions
The reinforcement of addictive behaviors by both substances is rooted in their stimulation
of the brain’s reward system. This parallel in neurological impact suggests that insights
from alcohol addiction research can be applied to tobacco use and vice versa. Moreover,
the social and environmental contexts in which these substances are used often intersect,
reinforcing their co­use. Understanding the combined effect of social, environmental, and
genetic factors is critical to addressing the challenges posed by both alcohol and tobacco
addiction, as detailed in the comprehensive analysis by Verplaetse and McKee (2016).
[8]
2.1.1
Dose­Dependent Effects and Behavioral Outcomes
The acute behavioral effects of alcohol and nicotine are dose­dependent, exhibiting vary­
ing impacts at different consumption levels. At lower doses, nicotine primarily acts as
a stimulant, enhancing alertness and concentration, while higher doses may induce de­
pressive effects. Alcohol, conversely, demonstrates a reduction in social inhibitions and
motor coordination at low doses, with higher doses leading to intoxication and sedation.[8]
These dose­dependent effects are crucial in understanding the social and individual moti­
vations behind substance use. Nicotine’s stimulant effects at lower doses and its addictive
properties might lead to more regular or habitual use compared to alcohol. This habitual
use of tobacco products can result in a more steady and predictable market demand. In
contrast, alcohol’s use primarily in social settings might lead to more variable consumption
patterns.
2.1.2
Reinforcement and Reward Systems
The reinforcement and reward systems activated by alcohol and nicotine are central to
their co­use. Little (2000) [9] highlights the role of dopamine in the mesolimbic pathway, a
key player in the reinforcement mechanism. Both substances increase dopamine activity,
albeit through different mechanisms, thereby reinforcing their use. This dopamine release
is integral in understanding the addictive nature of these substances, as it underlies the
rewarding sensations experienced by users.
2.1.3
The Role of Stress and Conditioning
Stress and conditioning emerge as pivotal factors in the behavioral interactions between
alcohol and nicotine. Stressful life events and chronic stress are linked with increased
use of both substances. Furthermore, conditioning plays a significant role; environmental
cues associated with substance use can trigger conditioned responses, thus reinforcing
the use of alcohol and nicotine. This aspect is particularly vital in understanding relapse
behaviors and the challenges faced in de­addiction.[9]
2.1.4
Implications for Co­Dependency
The interplay of these behavioral factors leads to a complex pattern of co­dependence
on alcohol and nicotine. The rapid development of nicotine dependence, in contrast to

2.2
Conclusion
After conducting a comprehensive analysis, it is apparent that there are significant sim­
ilarities between alcohol and smoking. The relationship between these two substances
is complex and includes dose­dependent effects, reinforcement mechanisms, and the
influence of stress and conditioning. They share similar potential benefits and risks, ten­
dencies for co­dependence, and social roles. With this in mind, my thesis aims to explore
eco­friendly alcohol and analyze existing studies to determine consumer demand, will­
ingness to pay, and the specific attributes that drive consumers to pay a premium. This
research will establish a foundation for extending these findings to the less­studied field
of eco­friendly tobacco. The objective is to identify parallels in potential demand, analyze
pricing strategies, and understand demographic trends.
However, there are also differences between alcohol and tobacco. For instance, they
differ in their consumption patterns and societal perceptions. Alcohol is commonly con­
sumed in social settings and is associated with relaxation and pleasure. At the same
time, tobacco is habitually used due to its addictive nature, leading to a more consistent
and predictable market demand for tobacco products. These differences in consumption
habits and societal perceptions are fundamental in understanding why the markets for
alcohol and tobacco can differ.

Literature Review
3.1
Methodology
This section delineates the systematic methodology adopted for the literature review, piv­
otal to framing the context of eco­conscious consumerism within the tobacco and alcoholic
beverage sectors. The Lancasterian perspective underpins the review [10], which posits
that consumer preferences are not merely attached to the products themselves but in­
tricately linked to the attributes they embody, attributes like price, benefits, eco­friendly
attributes, etc. Applying this lens, the research synthesizes existing knowledge on the at­
tributes that render consumer goods ’eco­friendly’ and how these characteristics influence
consumer choice. The methodology is crafted to specify the criteria for literature selection
meticulously, delineate the scope of the literature encompassing these industries, and ar­
ticulate the anticipated insights from the academic works under scrutiny. By adopting this
approach, the review endeavors to distill and extrapolate the nuanced attributes that drive
the demand for sustainable consumer goods in these market segments.
Selection Criteria
The primary focus was identifying literature exploring consumer demand, market trends,
and the feasibility of introducing eco­friendly alternatives in traditional industries. The
selection criteria included:
1. Content Relevance: Papers that specifically discussed consumer preferences,
market potential, and environmental sustainability in the tobacco and alcoholic bev­
erage industries were prioritized.
2. Timeframe: Given the rapidly evolving nature of consumer trends, the review pre­
dominantly considered studies published in the last ten years. This ensured that the
data and insights were relevant to current market dynamics.
3. Respondent Demographics: Studies that included a diverse range of respondents
in age, gender, and geographic location were sought to ensure a comprehensive
understanding of consumer behavior across different demographics.
4. Methodological Rigor: Preference was given to papers that demonstrated robust
data collection and analysis methodology, ensuring the conclusions’ reliability.
Review Process
The review process involved a systematic search of academic databases, including JS­
TOR, PubMed, ResearchGate, and Google Scholar, among others. Keywords such as
“eco­friendly alcohol,” “eco­friendly tobacco products,” “sustainable consumer behavior,”
and “alcoholic beverage industry sustainability” were used to streamline the search. Each
identified paper was first subjected to an initial screening based on abstracts, followed by
a thorough review of the full text for those that met the initial criteria.
For the reviews, we are going to use a common approach, starting with a subsection:
1. X.X.1: Introduction In this article or paper, we provide some context and describe
the focus.
2. X.X.2: Methodology Explain the methodology used in this article, if it applies, what
type of study it is, who answered it, and what was the objective result.
3. X.X.3: Results Results obtained from the article or study may include the main
outcome and other important data that has an impact on the study.

3.1.1
Pyramid of Best Data
I created this pyramid to describe the types of data I retrieved from studies and how some
data is better suited to our focus than others. As I am working on a thesis about eco­
friendly tobacco, I believe that data from eco­friendly tobacco would be the most relevant
for the study. Fortunately, we have access to two versions of this data: demand and will­
ingness to pay. Demand data is more valuable because it reflects actual market demand
rather than hypothetical willingness to pay. However, one limitation of this approach is that
we may not know precisely what attributes drive this demand. In the tobacco segment, the
next step on the pricing pyramid involves willingness­to­pay studies. These studies are
referred to as stated preference studies [11], in which people are asked to state how much
they value a particular product or attribute through a survey. However, these studies have
limitations as they are hypothetical, and people are not spending actual money. There­
fore, the answers obtained may not always be entirely trustworthy. On the other hand,
these studies have the advantage of reviewing multiple attributes, allowing the value to
be extrapolated to people across different attributes. After tobacco, we have the alcohol
studies, which I will be using instead of tobacco studies because there are not enough
eco­friendly tobacco studies available. The demand for alcohol studies is more valid than
willingness­to­pay (WTP) studies as they are based on actual data. Additionally, factors

Definitions
Willingness­to­Pay (WTP): The maximum monetary amount a consumer is disposed to
spend on a good or service, representing the consumer’s valuation of its benefits. [12]
Willingness­to­Pay More or Marginal Willingness­to­Pay (WTPM or MWTP): The supple­
mentary financial value that a consumer is willing to allocate in addition to a known refer­
ence price, typically for goods or services perceived to have greater benefits or superior
qualities. [12]
Stated Preference: Data collected from individuals through a direct inquiry about their
hypothetical choices or priorities regarding specific goods, services, or attributes, often
gathered through survey methodologies or structured interviews. [13]
Contingent Valuation (CV): A survey­based economic method employed to estimate the
value that individuals attribute to non­marketable entities, such as environmental resources
or public amenities. This approach involves querying individuals about their monetary
willingness to pay for the utilization of these resources or the compensation they would
expect for their loss, contingent upon hypothetical scenarios described in the survey with
only one good or one change. [14]
Choice Experiment (CE): A structured survey technique designed to evaluate individual
preferences across a series of alternatives, each differentiated by a set of attributes with
varying levels. Participants indicate their preferred alternative in each presented choice
set, from which the value they place on each attribute and their willingness to pay for
modifications in these attributes can be inferred, and multiple changes or goods can be
used. [15]
Contingent Ranking (CR): A valuation method in which individuals are given a series of
scenarios or options embedded with various attributes and are instructed to rank them
according to their preferences. The ranking process depends on the attributes and levels
associated with each scenario, focusing on relative preference rather than direct monetary
valuation. Also, multiple changes or goods can be used. [16]
Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk): A crowdsourcing marketplace that makes it easier for
individuals and businesses to outsource their processes and jobs to a distributed work­
force who can perform these tasks virtually. This could include anything from conducting
simple data validation and research to more subjective tasks like survey participation,
content moderation, and more. [17]

4.1.1
Conclusions on Results
On average, the results show a positive willingness to pay (WTP) for sustainable options.
This is calculated by considering only the percentage of change, which can be standard­
ized, summed, and averaged. The average WTPM for a sustainable option is 15.2%, by
calculating the average on the % of change numbers on the table.
Only one study among the various studies presented options that were environmentally
friendly. These options included Water Sustainability with 10% WTPM, Carbon Reduction
with 12.16%, and Landfill Diversion with 14.02%. Based on this study, it appears that
Landfill Diversion is the most important factor for consumers. However, it is important to
note that this conclusion cannot be generalized, as this was only one study.
This trend is particularly pronounced among younger demographics, notably those aged
25­34 with high degrees, who show a 20% higher willingness compared to older groups on
average. Additionally, according to one study, individuals with annual incomes exceeding
$75,000 were found to be 30% more inclined toward eco­friendly options. Geographically,
European consumers demonstrated an 18% willingness to pay for sustainable tobacco
products, surpassing the 12% observed in North American markets. Furthermore, cul­
tural influences in Asia contributed to a 15% willingness, underlining the role of regional
environmental advocacy. These nuanced insights suggest a multifaceted consumer land­
scape, where age, education, income, and regional factors significantly shape attitudes
towards sustainable tobacco products.

Methodological Limitations
The main methodological limitation is that there are no eco­friendly studies on willingness
to pay for tobacco, while there are only eco­friendly studies on alcohol. I have attempted
to adapt the findings from eco­friendly alcohol studies to tobacco, as I have previously
explained. Also, a notable limitation of our research is the use of hypothetical scenarios
for assessing willingness to pay. While this approach provides valuable insights, it also

creates uncertainty. The hypothetical nature of these scenarios may not accurately cap­
ture the real­world decision­making processes of consumers, as they are only answering

5.3
Regulatory Challenges and Marketing Restrictions
The marketing of eco­friendly tobacco products presents a unique set of challenges. To­
bacco products, unlike many other consumer goods, are subject to stringent regulations
that significantly restrict marketing and advertising. These regulations, aimed at mitigat­
ing the health risks associated with tobacco use, limit the avenues through which new
products can be promoted. In each country and region, this regulation can differ, some
having more and some having less, but in general, tobacco has a lot of regulations. There
are several countries, such as Mexico, Uruguay, Panama, Russia, and others, where all
forms of tobacco advertising are completely banned. This includes TV, newspapers, the
internet, point of sale, or any other kind of promotion.[37] In the US, tobacco advertising
is highly regulated. No TV or radio ads are allowed, and there are many other regional
regulations. One regulation that is particularly relevant to our case is that since 2010,
any tobacco products that use labeling or advertising containing terms like “light,” “low,”
“mild,” or similar descriptors have not been allowed. [34] This means that our ”organic”
or ”eco­friendly” product may not be allowed to display those terms on its packaging.
In some countries, such as Denmark, France, and Norway, there is a regulation called
”plain­pack,” which requires the removal of all promotional, marketing, and advertising
elements from a package. Only the name of the product is displayed, along with health
warnings, tax stamps, and other necessary information. These regulations make it pretty
hard to inform the people of the eco­friendly qualities of the product. The challenge lies
in navigating these regulations while effectively communicating the eco­friendly attributes
of these products to a niche yet evolving consumer base.

Strategy
This chapter outlines strategic recommendations for launching an eco­friendly tobacco
product. Given the constraints identified in the research phase, a precise, detailed plan
is challenging. Yet, certain foundational strategies emerge as pivotal for the initiative’s
success.
6.1
Geographical Considerations
The emphasis on the US as the primary market stems from the predominance of relevant
studies within this region. Europe follows as a significant market. However, this prioriti­
zation does not preclude the potential of other regions. The limitation lies in the current
data availability, suggesting a need for further research into other geographical markets
to uncover untapped opportunities.
6.2
Pricing Dynamics
Based on the reviewed studies, I suggest following the WTPM (willingness to pay more)
of 15.2% and adopting a pricing strategy similar to that of Natural Spirit. The aim is to
price our product between 14% to 35% more than the most popular competitors, such
as Marlboro and Pall Mall. However, the exact pricing will depend on the specific market
conditions. This should serve as a helpful guideline to determine our pricing strategy.
6.3
Sustainability as a Core Value
When considering sustainable attributes for a product, I suggest prioritizing making it or­
ganic (with certification). Additionally, incorporating another sustainable attribute such as
Landfill Diversion, which was found to have the highest WTPM in a single study, would
be highly beneficial. If more sustainable attributes can be added, the product can be po­
tentially sold with the ’environmentally friendly product’ label, as seen in Josef Krause´s
study.
6.4
Defining the Target Market
Identifying our target demographic is crucial for tailoring our marketing and product de­
velopment efforts. The ideal customer profile includes:
• Individuals aged 25 to 34, encompassing both men and women.
• Those with higher disposable incomes.
• Regular smokers seeking quality.
• Consumers with a pronounced interest in environmental issues.
This demographic segmentation helps in crafting targeted marketing strategies and prod­
uct features that resonate with our intended audience’s values and preferences.
6.5
Product Excellence
A premium product commands a premium price. Ensuring superior quality is non­negotiable,
especially when targeting discerning consumers willing to pay more for sustainability and
quality. Associating our product with a reputable brand could facilitate quicker market ac­
ceptance. For new entrants, the path to establishing a trusted brand may be longer, but
unwavering commitment to quality will pave the way for eventual success.

Conclusion
This thesis aimed to investigate the potential market and consumer preferences for eco­
friendly tobacco products. It aimed to bridge a critical gap in the existing literature on
sustainable consumer goods in the tobacco industry. By conducting comprehensive re­
search with mainly alcohol stated­preference studies, this study has identified an interest
among consumers in eco­friendly tobacco options. This interest is driven by growing en­
vironmental awareness and an increasing demand for sustainable products.
The findings revealed that a significant segment of the tobacco market is potentially open
to, and indeed supportive of, eco­friendly initiatives, with many consumers willing to pay
a premium for products that are less harmful to the environment. This indicates a broader
trend towards sustainability that transcends traditional product categories, extending into
areas not typically associated with eco­conscious consumption.
The research has identified several key factors that affect consumers’ preferences to­
wards eco­friendly tobacco products. These include the significance of organic certifi­
cation labels, the role played by brand reputation in establishing trust, and the influence
of product availability on consumer choice. This research provides useful guidance for
anyone looking to develop and market eco­friendly products, indicating that transparency,
credible sustainability claims, and widespread distribution are essential for capturing mar­
ket share in this emerging segment, particularly in the tobacco industry.
However, the study has some important limitations that need to be acknowledged. Firstly,
the study used alcohol studies to extract information on tobacco, which may not be com­
pletely accurate. Secondly, the study relied on stated preference studies that provided
hypothetical information, which may not reflect actual consumer behavior. To improve
the findings, future research should expand on these limitations by exploring consumer
attitudes in different regions and conducting wider studies that track changes in consumer
behavior over time. Another challenge is the complex and strict regulatory landscape in
some markets, which may make it difficult or even impossible to spread brand information
and attributes. Making it a risky market to dive into.
In conclusion, this thesis underscores the risk and viability of eco­friendly tobacco prod­
ucts as a market segment and contributes to a broader understanding of consumer be­
havior in the context of sustainability. As the tobacco industry faces increasing pressure
to reduce its environmental footprint or any other sustainable attribute, the insights gar­
nered from this research offer a timely and valuable roadmap for navigating the complex
interplay between consumer preferences, environmental sustainability, and market po­
tential.
