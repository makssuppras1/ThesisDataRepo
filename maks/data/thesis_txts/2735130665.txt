Abstract
To achieve a climate neutral and resilient power system based on renewable energy,
investments in flexibility solutions are of fundamental importance. Thanks to a decrease
in Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) technology cost, a fast and significant increase
in fluctuating wind generation, and an increasingly weak transmission grids, co­located
projects where Wind Farm (WF) and BESS share the same Point of Connection (POC)
are gaining attention. On the one hand, BESS can contribute to compensating for wind
forecast errors and providing balancing support. On the other hand, with its fast response,
BESS can also provide frequency services, supporting grid robustness and boosting the
revenue stream.
The goal of this MSc thesis project is to optimally operate the wind­battery farm and de­
tect the BESS behavior when providing support to the wind farm. By focusing on the UK
electricity markets, the benefits and drawbacks of jointly optimizing WF and BESS against
optimizing the two assets individually are discussed. Furthermore, the consideration of
wind stochastic nature is analyzed in overplanted wind­battery farm optimization. The fo­
cus is from the wind­battery owner’s perspective; thus maximizing its profit, while including
feed­in constraint at POC. It is indeed worth mentioning that the project will consider the
WF production as input data, hence the scheduling optimization strictly refers to BESS.
However, WF data influence the BESS profile.
The mentioned topics of focus for this research are structured in two parallel analyses.
Firstly, a historical imbalance analysis is conducted to determine the true impact of imbal­
ance cost. To do so, the optimal wind­battery co­optimized configuration is investigated
when considering the potential of covering imbalance error employing BESS utilization.
This is compared to an individually optimized approach where both assets participate in­
dependently in the Day­Ahead (DA) market, including eventual WF costs of imbalance.
Secondly, the analysis focuses on investigating optimal operation for overplanted wind­
battery farms in DA market under constrained export capabilities, while including wind
stochastic nature via a two­stage stochastic algorithm.
Both analyses give rise to interesting outcomes when considering the objective of this
project. More in detail, the most economically efficient optimization configuration was
demonstrated to be the independently optimized assets operation method, allowing the
BESS to utilize its full capacity for remunerative purposes in the DA market and not to
simply cover the imbalance caused by the WF as analyzed in the co­optimized configura­
tion. Lastly, after analyzing the overplanted farm including the stochastic nature of wind,
resulted in an added degree of flexibility for the WF to deviate from the forecasted energy
production. This allowed to have a more flexible, resilient BESS scheduling profile with
lower congestions of the grid connection. However, this brings lower revenues compared
to the deterministic approach.

Introduction
The following chapter begins with an introduction to the motivations and key driving factors
behind the research conducted in this thesis along with a link between those and the
analysis conducted. Furthermore, the research questions that are addressed in the thesis,
are listed and explained in section 1.2. Lastly, section 1.3 includes a brief summary for
each chapter, aiming to give an overview of this report.
1.1
Motivation
To achieve a climate neutral and resilient power system based on renewable energy, in­
vestments in flexible solutions are of fundamental importance. Thanks to a decrease in
BESS technology cost, a fast and significant increase in fluctuating wind generation, and
an increasingly weak transmission grid, co­located projects where wind farm and BESS
share the same POC are gaining attention. On the one hand, BESS can contribute to com­
pensating for wind forecast errors and providing balancing support. On the other hand,
with its fast response, BESS can also provide frequency services, supporting grid robust­
ness and boosting the revenue stream. Furthermore, currently numerous WF projects rely
on Power Purchase Agreements (PPA) to reduce risk and volatility associated with fore­
cast and wholesale prices. However, this may reduce the profitability of energy production
as prices for PPA may be lower than those offered in the wholesale market. Secondly, the
wind forecast influences BESS operational strategies. On the one hand, the BESS could
minimize the wind forecast error, thus decreasing the imbalance cost. On the other hand,
assuming the rationale of avoiding curtailment in an overplanted farm, the wind forecast
would ensure to not exceed the feed­in grid connection during windy days by bidding the
BESS at a lower power than its installed power capacity. Lastly, due to the BESS being a
relatively newly introduced asset in the electric power system, it is still unclear what is the
best operational configuration leading to the optimal economic operational configuration
and optimization. All the aforementioned reasoning paved the way for the researched
topic analyzed in this thesis.
This MSc thesis project investigates short term trading optimization of wind­battery farm
accounting for uncertainties due to wind production forecasts. The goal is to optimally
operate the wind­battery farm and detect the BESS behavior when providing support to
the wind farm. By focusing on the UK electricity markets, the benefits and drawbacks
of jointly optimizing wind and BESS against optimizing the two assets individually are
discussed. The focus is from the wind­battery owner’s perspective; thus maximizing its
profit, while including feed­in constraints at POC. Additionally, the wind­battery operation
in overplanted farms is investigated. An overplanted farm has a feed­in grid connection
lower than the sum of the power capacity of each asset composing the farm. Therefore,
trading optimization is of major importance to achieve the highest revenue stream by
avoiding unnecessary curtailment and associated penalties. Furthermore, focusing on
the co­location of WF and BESS, the uncertainty in the unpredictability of wind power
plays a crucial role. Indeed an accurate wind forecast would maximize profits for the WF
developer, no matter the co­located BESS. Therefore, taking into consideration multiple
scenarios of wind production profiles when scheduling the BESS could result in more
accurate DA bids, potentially resulting in fewer hours where the grid capacity is exceeding
resulting in economic losses.

1.2
Contribution and research questions
This thesis centers around short­term trading applications for co­located offshore wind
parks together with a large­scale BESS located in the UK. Starting by investigating the
difference between Behind The Meter (BTM) and Front of The Meter (FOTM) co­location
configuration, the study focuses on assessing the most advantageous options for opti­
mization in terms of economical return. Furthermore, this research analyzes whether
stand­alone or co­optimized solutions yield the most economically favorable outcomes.
The analysis accounts for the unpredictability of wind forecasts, and grid limitations result­
ing in overplanted wind­battery farms. The research questions addressed in this thesis
are the following:
• What is the economic operational difference between co­optimized wind­battery
farms and the individual optimization of their assets? When assessing the ad­
vantages of incorporating a BESS alongside a wind farm, it becomes evident that
this integration can enhance the reliability and security of the overall power supply
from a grid perspective. However, the economic viability of co­optimizing a BESS
with the wind farm when co­located remains to be conclusively determined. The lit­
erature review shows that adding a BESS to a wind farm allows for higher revenue
than having a wind farm alone. The aim here is to analyze whether co­optimization
is the most remunerative solution or if independently optimized assets would bring a
higher revenue. This assessment will rely on the use of detailed optimization mod­
els to facilitate a thorough analysis and identify the most cost­effective solution. To
answer this question, the described models will be developed thoroughly.
• What are the most limiting constraints on making the wind­battery assets fea­
sible? It is commonly thought that simply adding a BESS to a wind farm will surely
add value to the system. However, other factors should be taken into account such
as grid constraints, to assess thoroughly the feasibility of the project. Furthermore,
a sensitivity analysis regarding the main assumptions taken will be investigated, and
their impact on the project’s operational feasibility.
• To what extent will the uncertainty in wind forecasts impact the feasibility of
wind­battery assets? While achieving a perfect weather forecast may remain a
challenge, can we adjust bid amounts to account for wind forecast uncertainty?
To address this, developing a stochastic optimization model that incorporates wind
forecast uncertainty is essential, this will be developed as part of the thesis. Such
a model can quantify the potential advantages that BESS can offer to wind farm
developers.

Wind­Battery farm
This chapter sets the foundation for the analysis carried out in this thesis, which examines
the integration of WF with BESS in co­located settings.
The analysis starts by looking into the current advancements in combining WF and BESS,
highlighting how such an integration can mitigate the variability of wind power and improve
the stability and efficiency of the power system along with higher economical returns for
operators, included in section 2.1. Furthermore, section 2.2 presents the differences be­
tween BTM and FOTM configurations for wind­battery farms, which are crucial for under­
standing how these systems interact with the power grid and markets. The concept of
overplanted power plants is then introduced, focusing on the reasons for overplanting,
its challenges, and how strategic management of these systems can maximize profits
while avoiding power curtailments. This is accompanied by the definition of overplanted
farms included in section 2.3. Data from wind farms and BESS provided by Ørsted’s
Short Term Trading Optimization department forms the basis for this thesis, enabling the
analysis of historical power production, energy prices, and the operational performance
of these systems. These data are described in section 2.4. They are crucial for enabling
the development of case studies and simulation of realistic scenarios aiming to identify
optimal strategies for wind­battery systems operation. In summary, this chapter outlines
the scope of the thesis, focusing on the best operational configuration when integrating
WF and BESS jointly, along with wind­battery overplanted farm optimization. It previews
the technical and economic analyses that follow, aiming to contribute to the efficient and
sustainable operation of renewable energy systems in the power grid.
2.1
State of the art
Today’s electric power system is witnessing rapid changes, with an increasing share of
power generation coming from renewable energy sources. This increase is occurring
at a very fast rate, pushing the electricity generation industry closer to carbon neutrality
goals. However, this transition also challenges grid stability and controllability due to the
stochastic nature of renewable energy sources. This highlights the importance of more
controllable electricity sources, such as BESS.
The advantages of a growing number of co­located wind­battery farms in the electric
power system are discussed in [1]. The combination of a BESS with a WF results in
a more versatile operation of the combined farm increasing the controllability of produc­
tion, compared to having a WF alone [2]. After all, this enhances system efficiency, and
the BESS sizing can be adjusted according to the WF size. Additionally, BESS are highly
valuable in small networks, such as island systems, when other energy storage solutions
may fail. This is due to their ability to store and dispatch energy rapidly, providing grid
stability and continuity of energy supply.
Moreover, as highlighted in [3] another advantage of co­located wind­battery farms is their
contribution to increasing the share of green energy in the electricity grid, thereby lowering
overall CO2 emissions and reducing WF curtailments. A study based on a WF operating
in Australia’s electricity market demonstrated that a co­located wind­battery system can
better regulate its dispatch [4]. Participating in both the wholesale and the ancillary ser­
vice market with co­located plants can result in additional returns compared to standalone
assets in the same markets. Similarly, [5] shows that from a WF owner’s perspective, a
co­optimized wind­battery system participating jointly in DA and Frequency Containment

Reserve Normal Operation (FCR­N) markets can significantly increase profits and assist
the grid operator in stabilizing the grid. A similar finding based on the Danish spot market
is available in [6], where BESS integrated with a WF can increase revenue by 15.8%
while reducing fluctuation penalty by 64.9%.
Contrary to the benefits highlighted in the aforementioned literature it was found in other
sources that utilizing BESS for imbalance covering purposes does not result in economic
benefits. The multistage stochastic model for a co­located wind­battery farm described
in [7] claims that the reduction of imbalances does not necessarily provide a higher eco­
nomic return and can even increase the imbalance when the optimization model aims
to increase the system’s revenue. Studies found in the literature have showcased that
numerous WF projects rely on PPA agreements to reduce the risks and volatility associ­
ated with wholesale prices [8]. On the other hand, this reduces the profitability of energy
production as prices of PPA agreements are generally lower than those offered in the
wholesale market nowadays [9]. Furthermore, it has been observed that the number of
PPA providers is declining due to the increasing risks linked with the volatility wholesale
price risk [10].
Therefore, by introducing battery storage, it is possible to lower risk and unpredictability by
allowing the WF operator to shift power injection to hours with higher prices. This creates a
potential for analyzing the best economic operation for a co­located wind­battery system.
However, sometimes technical limitations of the grid or economic constraints cause the
operator to design an overplanted co­located wind­battery farm. An overplanted wind­
battery farm is characterized by a feed­in grid connection (in terms of power capacity)
that is lower than the sum of the capacities of each asset composing the farm. Therefore,
the trading optimization of co­located assets is crucial for achieving the highest revenue
stream possible for the farm, and determining how to operate these assets to avoid un­
necessary curtailment and associated penalties. This need for research is supported
by [4], which found that improving forecast accuracy in wind­battery systems can lead to
higher system revenue due to better regulation and precision when dispatching, along
with a lower upfront investment in the BESS. Additionally, the authors of [11] show that
accounting for the stochastic nature of market prices due to wind fluctuations can improve
the operational scheduling of BESS, hence increasing their profit. Therefore, a key as­
pect of obtaining the best bidding strategy in the wholesale electricity market involves
considering the stochastic nature of wind in the optimization function. Similar stochas­
tic optimization technologies for co­located BESS are already showcased and utilized by
Arenko [12]. Lastly, overplanted co­located facilities are foreseen to play an important role
in an efficient green transition. For this reason, the Danish Transmission System Opera­
tor (TSO) Energinet has drafted a first set of technical requirements governing co­located
facilities and is calling for a conversation with the industry to qualify the draft before it is
implemented [13]. All the aforementioned reasons motivate the analysis conducted in this
thesis about co­located wind­battery farms.

Overplanted power plant
One of the analysis of this thesis focuses on how to better optimize the BESS schedule for
a co­located wind­battery farm being subjected to a feed­in power limitation also known
as overplnated farm. Therefore, it is crucial to give a more detailed description of such
terminology.
The term ”overplanted electrical power plant” in the context of hybrid power plants refers
to the practice of adding more generating capacity, such as wind turbines or solar panels,
to better utilize the existing electrical infrastructure. This can be done to maximize the
use of resources and area availability, as well as to increase the overall power output
of the hybrid power plant. However, overplanting may require curtailment, which can be
addressed by incorporating flexible storage capabilities to optimize the dispatch sched­
ule and maximize revenue from the market [17]. The concept of overplanting is often
discussed in the context of hybrid renewable power plants, where different renewable
energy sources are combined to enhance overall efficiency and output. It is a strategy
that aims to make the most of the available resources and infrastructure, ultimately con­
tributing to the economic viability and energy contribution of the hybrid power plant. At
the same time, this configuration requires a more peculiar and organized scheduling of
the different assets, to aim the most efficient operation to maximize profits. This will be
further analyzed in the project when WF and BESS are co­located and co­optimized in an
overplanted HPP configuration.

3.1
Power grid set up and role
Electricity markets typically consist of four key segments: generation, transmission, dis­
tribution, and retail. In Europe, distinct energy market areas are established, and divided
into separate regions. These regions are interconnected, enabling the flow of electricity
across different countries and areas. Each of these energy markets operates under a
specific set of rules within a common market framework. The transmission grid, char­
acterized by high voltage, is essential for transporting electricity over long distances. In
Europe, this grid’s operation and management are overseen by TSOs, often regarded
as non­commercial natural monopolies. TSOs are also responsible for the high­voltage
transmission of electricity over long distances. They manage the backbone of the elec­
tricity system, ensuring the stable and secure operation of the transmission grid. They
are crucial in maintaining the balance between electricity supply and demand across vast
geographical areas. TSOs also facilitate cross­border electricity trade as well as hav­
ing the responsibility for the interconnectivity of regional and national grids, enhancing
energy security and market efficiency [18]. On the other hand, Distribution System Oper­
ator (DSO)s are entities responsible for operating, maintaining, and developing the distri­
bution networks for electricity. These networks typically operate at lower voltages and are
crucial for delivering electricity from the high­voltage transmission grid to end consumers,
including residential, commercial, and industrial users. DSOs ensure a reliable and effi­
cient distribution of electricity and manage network connections and local grid constraints.
They also play a key role in integrating distributed energy resources, such as rooftop solar
panels and small­scale wind turbines, into the grid [19] [20] [21].

3.3.1
Electricity forward agreement
Electricity Forward Agreement (EFA) is a system used to specify load profiles when trading
electricity. Load profiles refer to the volume of electricity generated or the amount of
demand in a specific time frame. The EFA day begins at 23:00 of the previous night and
runs until 23:00 of the next day. There are in total 6 EFA blocks and each of them has a
period of 4 hours. In total, there are 48 settlement periods, each of them is 30 minutes
long, hence there are 48 of them in a 24­hour day temporal span. Furthermore, the EFA
blocks are divided into first and last settlement periods, namely EFAa and EFAb. In cases
of over­the­counter and when closer to real time the electricity is traded for a single EFA,
either EFAa or EFAb. On the other hand, when electricity is traded in future and forwards
markets more typical trading products are used. In this case, each product can denote
a group of EFA blocks that are bundled together. The Baseload products run across all
the six EFA blocks and are characterized by having the same volume of electricity traded
across the 24 hours. Another grouping of EFA blocks is present for weekdays only, this is
referred to as peak products and it is a bundle of EFA 3,4 and 5. The off­peak products are
in EFA 1,2 and 6. During the weekends due to the commonly lower electricity consumption
all the EFA blocks are referred to as Off­peak products. Lastly, there is also a distinction
between Day­time and Overnight products, Day­time covers the EFA blocks 3,4,5 and 6,
while Overnight products cover EFA bocks 1 and 2 [26].
3.3.2
Forwards and futures markets
It is shown that over 90% of electricity is traded in advance of delivery in forwards and
future markets. These types of electricity trading are beneficial for both generators and
suppliers. On one side the generators will have the security of a future income knowing
that someone will buy their electricity, and on the other side, suppliers have the guarantee
of having someone who will deliver them electricity allowing them to meet their customer’s
demand. The benefits are present in both parties, reducing the exposure to volatile spot
market prices [26]. Forwards markets are not strictly standardized and are contracts be­
tween two parties who agree to buy and sell electricity at an agreed price on a set date.
Commonly known and referred to as over the counter trading. Once the price is agreed
it is defined as a locked price and it will not vary, no matter the price fluctuation that will
take place from the day the agreement has been signed to the delivery date. On the
delivery date, the agreed electricity will be delivered at the agreed price. On the other
hand, future markets are more standardized contracts and have a lower flexibility, since
the trading mechanism is the same as an exchange market. The variety of products to
buy and sell is vast. For example, the Intercontinental Exchange provides two products.
The UK­based electricity futures which are traded seven days a week 24 hours a day,
and the Peak Electricity Futures, are traded from Monday to Friday, during peak hours
from 7:00 to 19:00. The peculiarity of both Forward and Future markets is the require­
ment to deliver electricity across a full month, the electricity traded can be for periods up

==PAGE:28==
to five years. It is common to trade electricity for a larger period the earlier in advance
the contract is stipulated compared to the delivery date. When trading electricity a few
days or hours ahead of delivery time, the amount traded is lower and with a lower time
granularity [26]. Central to this market is the electricity pool system, where electricity is
traded in two main markets: the day­ahead market, allowing for trading up to one day
before delivery, and the balancing market, which operates closer to real­time to manage
supply and demand [28].
3.3.3
Spot market: day­ahead and intraday trading
It is relatively complex to have a perfectly balanced electricity grid only using electricity
traded in the forward and future markets, therefore, more accurate trading is required to
meet the more precise and up to date forecast to meet the consumers’ demand. This
action is commonly referred to as ”adding shape” to the market. The corrective trading
actions take place in the Spot market. As the name suggests the transactions are referred
to electricity that will be delivered on the spot hence here and now delivery. The spot
market is composed of the Day­Ahead market: where the electricity is traded to meet the
next day’s demand, and the intra­day market: where the electricity traded will be delivered
on the same day.
In both markets, the trading takes place in a different way than the over the counter trad­
ing used in future and forwards markets. In this case, generators and suppliers make a
bid specifying the amount they are willing to buy or sell and the respective price. The
bids are delivered to trading platforms known as power exchanges. The market price
also known as market clearing price, is the price at which the electricity is bought/sold.
The most widely known and utilized power exchanges in UK are NordPool and EPEX
SPOT [23] [22]. The electricity supplier can offer to buy electricity to meet their customer’s
demand at a specific price that has been decided for each hour or half hour basis. On the
other hand, the generator produces electricity to meet the demand in that specific time
frame at the established price. Intraday trading can take place until gate closure, after this
additional electricity trading can take place to make sure demand is met. This operates on
a half­hour based auction. The DA market is operated daily by EPEX SPOT and it is part
of the European Market Coupling. There are 24 hourly contracts available on the suction
and they correspond to the 24 hours of the following day. Furthermore, they follow the
same time frame of the EFA model. The first hour traded on D­1 will be the hour of the
next day (D) between 00:00 and 01:00. Gate closure in the UK is at 09:20 GMT on the
day preceding delivery [29]. After the market clearing process, the results including the
prices for each hour on the next day are published. This usually occurs a few hours after
gate closure [30].
In the DA market, the single hours orders can contain up to 356 price/quantity combina­
tions. The bid made by the participants must contain the price and the volume quantity,
which can be positive, negative, or null. There is also the possibility of having block or­
ders, which have the same price for several hours. The maximum volume per classic
block order is 500 MW in the UK. The bis can either be executed or rejected entirely.

respond to the outcomes of the daily interconnectors capacity auctions. Additionally, the
Great Britain 30 minute Day­Ahead auction, which occurs at 15:30 GMT, provides an op­

3.3.4
Balancing Mechanism
The balancing mechanism or balancing market in the UK, is an integral part of the elec­
tricity market, designed to ensure the stability and reliability of the electricity grid. This
mechanism is overseen by National Grid Electricity System Operator (NGESO), respon­
sible for balancing the system [31]. It exists to address imbalances between electricity
supply and demand. These imbalances can occur due to the unpredictable nature of elec­
tricity generation and consumption. The power system is always out of balance, meaning
that supply and demand are never perfectly matching. The balancing actions provided by
NGESO are preventive attempts to avoid possible blackouts, therefore keeping the grid
at 50 Hz. Participants in this market have to register their assets as Balancing Mecha­
nism Units with Elexon, the organization responsible for managing the balancing market.
This market plays a pivotal role in maintaining the delicate balance between supply and
demand in the UK’s electricity grid, ensuring both the stability of the grid and the efficiency
of the electricity market.
3.3.5
Imbalance cost and one price scheme
A generator or supplier is out of position in a specific period when it did not respect its
delivery or consumption of electricity. This is a cause of imbalance in the system. To re­
store the stability of the system, containment actions are actuated and these have a price,
known as the system price. The price of imbalance is an economical amount charged by
Elexon for not being in the stipulated position. The amount is charged per MWh. When
a participant generates an imbalance, it must pay for that shortfall of electricity at a price
that reflects the cost seen by NGESO for balancing the system. Commonly used termi­
nologies for defining system imbalance are long and short. When production is greater
than demand, the system is defined as long. In this case, the system price is based on
how much it costs for the NGESO to reduce generation or increase demand. For the op­
posite case, when demand is greater than production, the system is defined as short, and
the system price is based on how much it costs for the NGESO to increase generation or
reduce demand [32].
Considering the UK electricity market, the imbalance is paid following the one price scheme.
In a one price scheme electricity market, the price of electricity is determined by the inter­
section of the demand and supply curves for each hour. This price is differentiated from
zone to zone when transmission capacity constraints exist. In the one­price system, the
purchase and sales prices of imbalance energy are identical, and the price of imbalance
energy is determined based on the regulations carried out during a specific hour. The
single price scheme aims to provide the least cost source of electricity generation to meet
customer demand at any given time. This mechanism of the market, in order to determine
the electricity price during the balancing market only looks at the system state, if the sys­
tem state is short (= lack of power in the system) then the balancing price will be higher
than the DA price. On the other hand, when the system is long (= excess power in the
system) the balancing price will be lower than the DA price. This encourages strategic
bidding, as the WF developers could profit by knowing the side of the imbalance. As can
be seen in the below table it does not matter if the WF helps or not the system state. How­

difficult to take place.

4.2.2
DA overplanted ­ stochastic model
Different sources of the literature showed the potential of incorporating the stochastic na­
ture of wind into the model, providing a higher precision on the foresight of events. For this
reason, to address the research question of how to adjust wind­battery farm optimization
including wind forecast uncertainty a stochastic model optimization approach has been
thought to be the most suitable approach. The stochastic model builds on the determinis­
tic model by adding uncertainties to its forecasts. Instead of predicting a single outcome,
it gives a range of possible scenarios for what might happen on a given day. This means
it might show a lower expected profit compared to the perfect predictions of the determin­
istic model. However, it can be seen as a safer choice due to the consideration in the
model that things might not go as the forecast predicted. References [33] and [34] are
the main sources of information for the development of the two­stage stochastic model.
The proposed model is a two­stage stochastic program, the first stage decision is made
upon the single forecast of WF power production, which is subject to the second stage
constraints. The second stage is built to take into consideration a set of possible discrete
scenarios, each with its probability of realization. The excess grid power limitation needs
to be satisfied in every scenario. Meaning, that the charging and discharging decisions
made in the first stage need to anticipate the possible overload that could happen taking
into account the second stage realizations.
4.2.3

operational data. Various scenario generation and reduction techniques are commonly

generate higher revenue. The increased capacity also allows the farm to have more time
steps where joint production is allowed, potentially increasing the revenue. Furthermore,
it was noticed how a potentially high decrease in revenue can come from a small decrease
in export cable capacity.

6
Conclusion and Future work
This thesis investigates the most economically effective operational mode for a wind­
battery farm. The project is based on a wind­battery farm composed of a 1.3 GW WF
and a 300 MW/600 MWh BESS.
Firstly, the potential of reducing the imbalance between (WF) forecast and power produc­
tion via BESS utilization is analyzed. The investigation on the WF cost of imbalance was
conducted, followed by two parallel deterministic models aiming to determine if it is more
economically effective for a wind­battery farm to control the two assets independently or
co­optimized.
Secondly, the research investigates the optimal scheduling of (BESS) in co­located wind­
battery farms, taking into account the most limiting constraints such as feed­in power
limitations. Therefore, a wind­battery overplanted deterministic optimization was devel­
oped to verify the feasibility of such a farm.
Finally, the work explores the potential impacts of incorporating the stochastic nature
of wind in co­located, grid constrained (overplanted) wind­battery farms. A two­stage
stochastic model was developed to address how the feasibility of the model will be im­
pacted when including the uncertainty of the wind forecast.
The analyses have been carried out focusing purely on the economic­operational aspects
related to the 2­h BESS scheduling optimization and following the research questions
outlined in section 1.2 and concluded in this chapter:
• What is the economic operational difference between co­optimized wind­battery
farms and the individual optimization of their assets?
In order to assess which of the operational modes has the best economical returns
between individually optimized or co­optimized assets in a wind­battery farm, both
configurations were investigated and evaluated.
For the individually optimized case, a DA BESS optimization was developed and
the overall PnL for the WF combining both DA market and imbalance cost were
calculated. To investigate the co­optimized operation of wind­battery farm, a deter­
ministic optimization model to minimize the imbalance cost of the WF with the BESS
was developed. The result, combined with the PnL from the WF of the DA market
and remaining imbalance, showed a lower overall PnL than what has been obtained
when operating the wind­battery farm with an individually optimized approach. The
BESS generates a higher revenue when fully deployed for energy arbitrage pur­
poses in the DA market. When the BESS is utilized for covering the WF imbalance,
it is not able to cover fully the imbalance generated by the WF. This is seen in
situations where the BESS is fully charged/discharged and cannot accommodate
further energy. Furthermore, although the energy imbalance is decreased, since
the imbalance price is determined a posteriori, deploying the BESS to minimize the
energy imbalance could not result in lowering the cost of imbalance for the farm.
In fact, it could result in a higher cost of imbalance, lowering the overall PnL of the
wind­battery farm. To conclude, authors in [5] highlighted that co­optimized wind­
battery farm can generate higher returns when participating in both the wholesale
and ancillary services markets. However, this thesis quantifies and shows that an
independently optimized wind­battery farm including DA market revenues and im­
balance costs can result in up to 8% higher profit than operating the same farm with
Short­term trading of co­located wind­battery farm under uncertainty
49
==PAGE:60==
a co­optimized approach.
• What are the most limiting constraints on making the wind­battery assets fea­
sible?
Based on this analysis, the grid connection capacity plays an important role in facil­
itating the feasibility of wind­battery farms since it could lower the capability of the
farm to deliver energy to the grid at the most remunerative hour of the day. The de­
veloped deterministic model for an overplanted wind­battery farm proved to deliver
only 0.5% less energy than the unconstrained case, when considering a grid con­
nection capacity of 1.3 GW. This was obtained by accurately scheduling the BESS
participation on the DA market based on the WF forecast, considering the WF pri­
ority of delivery. However, the modified schedule for energy arbitrage of the BESS
resulted in a PnL reduction of 6.5%, highlighting the impact the grid connection ca­
pacity has. of scheduling the BESS at times of high prices.
• To what extent will the uncertainty in wind forecasts impact the feasibility of
wind­battery assets?
A two­stage stochastic model has been developed to address the possible benefits
of utilizing a stochastic approach to determining the most optimal BESS schedule
for the DA market in an overplanted wind­battery configuration. This takes into con­
sideration various scenarios of possible wind production realization. The stochastic
model resulted in a more conservative approach when determining the BESS sched­
ule in respect to the deterministic optimization model.
On the one hand, the stochastic approach has shown to reduce the number of half
hours excess production from 12 to 10 when considering real time power produc­
tion of the WF. This is achievable thanks to the stochastic approach providing more
room for unforeseen increases in wind production while remaining below the power
export capacity. On the other hand, the stochastic approach for overplanted wind­
battery farm provided a PnL 6.55% lower than the deterministic approach. The de­
scribed results align with what has been stated in [11], which highlights the benefits
of stochastic optimization, providing better BESS operational scheduling. Further­
more, the high dependency between grid connection capacity size and profitability
of the overplanted wind­battery farm has been highlighted in the sensitivity analysis.
This showed that increasing the grid connection capacity of 5% and 10% results in
having a PnL increase of 10% and 14% respectively.
Furthermore, there is indeed potential for future work and improvements on different as­
pects of both analyses developed, which are listed here below:
• The models developed in this project are based on the available data being aver­
age power values. This paves the way for further analysis where data with higher
resolution could be included and analyzed. For instance, power production value
could be included, potentially changing the grid constrained model analysis.
• When determining the economic difference between co­optimized and individually
optimized wind­battery farm, only DA market and imbalance costs were considered.
It would indeed be valuable to include in the analysis other aspects such as partici­
pation in intra­day and balancing market with the WF, as well as balancing market
and frequency markets for the BESS.
• The developed imbalance model for a co­optimized wind­battery farm, has the BESS
to minimize the energy imbalance. This is not highly robust and is characterized
50

by a high running time, leaving room for improvement, and potentially aiming to
create an optimization that gives the same output, but in a much faster and robust
computational manner. Furthermore, the imbalance model developed only uses 30
minutes average power data, it would be interesting to investigate this application
with power data with higher resolution.
• Every model developed in the project does not take into account any warranty as­
pects for the BESS. This aspect could be indeed implemented, aiming to create the
most realistic optimization model.
• When including the wind uncertainty for the wind­battery overplanted farm, the de­
veloped two­stage stochastic algorithm utilizes as inputs randomly generated sce­
narios. This could definitely be improved by developing a scenario generation model
based on historical data, which will most likely be able to provide more precise re­
sults.
• Furthermore, the stochastic optimization should be evaluated with accurate math­
ematical evaluation tools. This is definitely something that could be further investi­
gated to provide a strong mathematical evaluation method like Expected Value of
Perfect Information (EVPI) and Value of Stochastic Solution (VSS). Thus, potentially
proving the benefits of such a stochastic model.
• The conducted analysis for the overplanted farm did not consider any grid connec­
tion cost. Indeed there are potential research topics to further investigate how the
same analysis can be influenced when including capital investment costs like grid
connection infrastructure.
