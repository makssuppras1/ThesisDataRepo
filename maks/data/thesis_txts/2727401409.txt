Abstract 
 
This thesis contributes to the study of contemporary Danish offshore wind policy, by 
employing an STS-based methodology for studying the inscriptions of the two state 
procurement instruments, the Thor auction and the ‘open door’ policy. Through a vast 
empirical analysis, anchored in the subfields of ANT and valuation studies, as well as 
drawing on economic studies and evaluation of auctions, it interprets the two schemes as 
two different instantiations of “the good offshore wind economy”, before applying the 
notions of de-scripting and overflowing to understand the latest developments and 
entanglements of the schemes and the implications of these on Danish offshore wind 
policy. 
The thesis finds that the schemes during their use phases have developed two distinctive 
inscriptions of the good offshore wind economy, that however during the latest 
developments of the schemes have started to become entangled, through contesting de-
scriptions by their market agencements, which leaves challenges for the Danish offshore 
wind policy in the future.

Chapter 1 – Introduction 
In 2021, the total installed capacity of offshore wind power connected to the Danish 
power grid reached 2.3 GW, when the 600 MW Kriegers Flak wind farm was 
commissioned (Energistyrelsen, n.d.-a). Kriegers Flak is the latest offshore wind farm to 
be procured through an auction, which is the government’s main support and procurement 
mechanism for offshore wind, where the general procedure is that developers place bids 
on a concession for a predefined project and win by having the lowest bid and thus lowest 
requirements for state subsidies. The auction scheme was introduced in 1999 and has 
since been responsible for about 1.8 out of the total 2.3 GW (Energistyrelsen, n.d.-a).  
In December 2021, another auction has awarded a concession for a new offshore wind 
project, namely the 1 GW ‘Thor’ in the North Sea (Energistyrelsen, 2021b). The 
conclusion of this auction attracted a lot of attention, as several participating bidders had 
submitted the minimum allowed bid price of 0,01 øre/kWh, and with that effectively 
expressing zero need for state support funds for the first time in Danish history 
(Energistyrelsen, 2021b). In fact, the bid price is expected to result in a net payment from 
the developer to the state during the operation phase of the project due to the new 
configuration of the Contract-for-Difference (CfD) remuneration scheme of the auction 
(Jansen et al., 2022). Furthermore, as multiple developers had bid the minimum price, the

winner of the auction, German energy major RWE, was awarded the concession as the 
result of a most unexpected and first of its kind lottery draw (Energistyrelsen, 2021b).  
Not long after, another eye-catching development in the realm of offshore wind politics 
occurred when the Danish Energy Agency (DEA) throughout May and June 2022 
received a total of 43 applications for offshore wind projects through the so-called ‘open 
door’ policy (Energistyrelsen, 2023a) – the governments secondary offshore wind 
procurement method, which allows project developers to apply for pre-investigation 
rights and potentially construction permits for wind farm projects in areas of the Danish 
seabed not designated for tenders. These applications represent projects worth as much 
as 16 GW of offshore wind capacity, corresponding to around eight times the current 
amount.  
Like the auction scheme, the ‘open door’ (OD) policy was conceived in 1999, but since 
then, it had only yielded 120 MW of installed offshore wind capacity (Energistyrelsen, 
2023a). Both the volume and timing of the many new applications rolling in during the 
Spring of 2022 were therefore striking, as the scheme was subject to a significant 
amendment to come into effect on July 1st, 2022, changing its “open” characteristics 
significantly (Aftale Om Justering Af Åben Dør-Ordningen for VE-Anlæg På Havet Af 
2. Juni 2021, 2021). 
These two major “events” represent a major disruption of the two policies and left much 
media attention, controversies, and debate in their wake (Frandsen, 2022; Hebsgaard, 
2022; Madsen, 2023). Therefore, this thesis seeks to investigate the parallel developments 
occurring within the realm of these two equally old but very different policies. Based on 
the major Science and Technology Studies (STS) subfields of Actor-Network Theory 
(ANT) and valuation studies, it aims to identify the historical and contemporary 
characteristics and dynamics of the schemes through the identification of their 
“inscriptions” by employing an extensive empirical study. An assessment will be 
undertaken of how they each represent a version of “the good offshore wind economy” 
as imagined by the state to validate their function for development of offshore wind. For 
the analysis of the Thor auction, a cross-disciplinary approach utilising an economic 
dissection of its specific design to enrich the STS-based framework for interpretation of 
its inscriptions and impacts on Danish offshore wind policy will be applied and presented.

This comparison of the intended designs of the policies will then act as the foundation on 
which to analyse the contemporary events as described above, and how they represent a

addition, it will investigate how the market agencements of the two developments with 
their almost simultaneous appearance became entangled, and what implications these 
entanglements have for the status of Danish offshore wind policy.  
Research questions           
In summary, two main research questions will be addressed, summarizing the research 
objectives described above:  
RQ 1: Using an STS-based framework of semiotic inquiry to identify inscriptions of 
policy schemes, how can the Thor auction and the ‘open door’ scheme be 
understood and interpreted as different instantiations of ‘the good offshore wind 
economy’?  
RQ2: By comparing the case studies and using Actor-Network-Theory, how can the 
unforeseen developments of the Thor auction and the ‘open door’ applications be 
understood as overflowing and entanglement of the two policy schemes, and where 
did these developments leave Danish offshore wind policy?

Chapter 2 – Empirical field: Danish offshore wind policy  
This section will present an overview of the relevant historical developments of Danish 
offshore wind policy laying the foundation for the establishment of the auctions and the 
‘open door’ scheme in 1999. Then, it will focus on the events after the implementation of 
the two policies, briefly summarizing their timelines. It will then zoom in on the 2018 
Energy agreement and how it in the following years set the stage for the main object of 
study in this thesis, namely the recent dynamics of the conclusion of Thor auction and the 
sudden boom of applications through the ‘open door’ scheme. 
Before 2018  
The (brief) history of the emergence of Danish offshore wind policy 
The origin of Danish wind policy in general is usually traced back to the 1970s where the 
first oil crisis created international pressure and awareness towards solving the problem 
of the high energy prices (Energistyrelsen, 2011). The first support policy scheme for 
==PAGE:11==
5 
 
wind power development appeared in 1976 and was a direct investment support from the 
state, covering part of the investment expenses for private people installing any renewable 
energy technology for their own consumption of heat or electricity (Energistyrelsen, 
2011). As highlighted by Ossandón et al. (2023) who traced the lineage of wind power, 
this scheme was mainly motivated by the wish to support any initiatives lowering the 
Danish oil imports and was thereby not specifically targeting wind power.  
The first policy specifically doing so was the grid tax exemption and refund policy, 
essentially the feed-in-tariff, which came in 1983 to accommodate the need to connect 
turbines to the grid, as they had grown in size and capacity throughout the 80s. At that 
time, the expected budgetary expenses to cover this policy for the state was just a few 
million DKK per year (Energistyrelsen, 2011). In 1992 the political agenda of mitigating 
climate change was incorporated in wind power policy, as the grid tax exemption was 
changed to be labelled a “direct renewable energy support”. With this, wind power started 
to be seen more as a legitimate power source for the grid with the added potential to 
decrease the CO2 emissions from the power sector. 
It was during this phase that the initiative for the first offshore wind in Denmark – and in 
the world – was taken. Vindeby Wind Park and Tunø Knob were erected in 1991 and 
1994, respectively, both yielding 5 GW of installed capacity (Energistyrelsen, 2011). Both 
farms were created by Danish turbine manufacturers, Bonus and Vestas, respectively, and 
represented the sandboxes where offshore wind was first designed, tested, and proven for 
feasibility and future prospect (Ørsted, 2017a).  
Following 
the success 
of these projects, the government 
developed the 
“Havmøllehandlingsplan for de danske farvande” in 1997 as a part of their overall 
strategic action plan “Energi 21”, the purpose of which was to identify and reserve 
potentially suitable areas for future offshore wind development (Energi 21, 1996). 
Thereby, the first dedicated government strategies for offshore wind power development 
were enacted. 
By 1999, the rise of wind power had caused the support expenses through the feed-in-
tariff policy to reach 1,4 billion DKK. In light of the “few million” DKK that was 
expected in 1983, pressure was put on the Ministry of Finance to lower the support 
==PAGE:12==
6 
 
payment expenses, also as the supports were to a large extend financed by the power 
consumers through the PSO tariff (Energistyrelsen, 2011). 
 
The 1999 electricity supply reform –the birth of the auctions and the open door 
In 1999, the Ministry for Climate, Environment, and Energy Supply proposed a major 
energy supply reform, bringing with it the liberalization of the energy sector and the 
vertical unbundling, as demanded by the European Commission in the Electricity 
Liberalization Directive from 1996 (96/92/EC, 1996). With this reform, the split in 
interests and responsibility between the state, grid operators, and the power producing 
actors became clearer and simpler. In addition, Denmark became a part of the common 
Nordic power market Nord Pool in 2000 (Ossandón et al., 2023).  
Furthermore, the law for the first time stated clear procedures for how offshore wind 
should be precured, the necessity of which were also prescribed by the EU directive 
(96/92/EC, 1996). The law stated that “the exclusive rights to exploit energy from water 
and wind resources from the sea territory and in the exclusive economic zone (EEZ) 
belonged to the state”. (Elforsyningsloven. LOV Nr 375 Af 02/06/1999., 1999). 
Furthermore, it specifies that permissions to investigate areas suitable for wind power 
extraction and any subsequent proceedings leading to a potential wind power plant were 
to be controlled and supervised by the state. It was underlined in the details of the proposal 
for the law, that this procedure could be initiated in two ways (Forslag Til Lov Om 
Elforsyning. 1998/1 LSF 234., 1999):  
First, the state would be able to establish a centralized auction procedure with the aim of 
issuing permissions for wind power in the areas such as the ones identified in the 
“Havmøllehandlingsplan for de danske farvande”. The auction call would have potential 
developers apply at the same time, which would Ensure “equal terms” for all of them 
(Forslag Til Lov Om Elforsyning. 1998/1 LSF 234., 1999). The law proposal assigns 
importance to the auctioning  procedure towards reaching the goal of covering 20% of 
the Danish electricity supply with renewable electricity by the end of 2003 – and 
achieving this “as cost-effectively as possible”, echoing the growing need to halt the 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
proportional increase in the support expenses for the wind power through the feed-in-
tariff. 
Second, the details in the law proposal mentioned an alternative to the centralized auction 
procedure. An “open door” should be arranged as a special procedure for projects not 
applicable to the centralized procedure.  
With this reform, the birth of the auction scheme and the OD policy for offshore wind 
development in Denmark was official, and it through these two policies that all offshore 
wind farms in Denmark have been erected since 1999.

In the first auction call for Horns Rev II, the premise was that four prequalified developers 
could offer to build the park, the dimensions of which was largely already configured by 
the government. The prequalification consisted of assessing and choosing a maximum of 
five developers based on their experience with building offshore wind farms as well as 
their recent financial performance (Energistyrelsen, 2004). The bid would consist of a 
price, a detailed site layout and design plan, and a time schedule for the construction. The

the content of the bids, namely 1) the bid price 2) the layout of the park, and 3) the time

The auction was conducted as a “EU auction” in two rounds, where the four developers 
that had applied for prequalification were invited to a briefing with the governing body, 
The Danish Energy Agency (DEA) when the auction conditions were published in 
October 2004 before submitting their initial bids in January 2005 (Deloitte, 2011; The 
Danish Energy Agency, 2004). Throughout the first half of 2005, a running dialogue on 
the auction and the design of the park took place between DEA and the participating 
bidders to test ideas and formats. The final bids were submitted in June 2005, before 
DONG was declared the winner. 
Horns Rev II set the standard and precedence for the design of the auction, and in the 
following years, the remaining four auctions that were announced as parts of the new 
energy agreements coming in 2008 and 2012 did not deviate much from this pattern 
(Energistyrelsen, n.d.-a; Lov Om Ændring Af Lov Om Elforsyning, Lov Om 
Naturgasforsyning, Lov Om Varmeforsyning Og Lov Om Energinet.Dk, 2008). As 
technology developed, the capacity of the projects of the auctions increased with each 
round. After seeing a spike in prices with Rødsand II and especially Anholt, which 
occurred for unique contextual reasons, the prices, and thereby the support expenses 
decreased – as intended – for Horns Rev III and Kriegers Flak, which thus became the 
cheapest and largest of the five.  
The use of the ‘open door’ policy  
The procedure for applying through the OD for a developer was first to make an official 
application requesting permission to start the pre-investigation of a wind farm of a certain 
character in a delimited part of the sea, containing basic information and motivation for 
the offshore wind project. Then, there would be a public announcement of the application 
and a call for other potentially interested developers to apply as well to “ensure equal 
terms”. If the ministry found the permission reasonable and not in conflict with other state 
interests, the permission could be granted. Only at that point would the real investigation 
and planning process start to find out whether a project is technically and environmentally 
==PAGE:15==
9 
 
feasible (Elforsyningsloven. LOV Nr 375 Af 02/06/1999., 1999). All these obligations 
rest on the developer, who thus carries all the risk.  
While the five auctions had contributed with 1,8 GW of offshore wind power after the 
commissioning of Kriegers Flak, the usage of the ‘open door’ policy is a different story. 
During the first 10 years of the existence of the scheme from 1999 to 2009, a series of six 
smaller nearshore projects came through the door and were established. In total, the 
projects summed to only 120 MW of capacity (Energistyrelsen, 2023a).  
Between 2010 and 2018, another six applications came through the OD. While the total 
planned capacity of these projects was 900-1300 MW, none of these projects have yet 
been realized. Even though first applications for these projects were sent between 2010-
2012, the case work for granting pre-investigation rights for the most part still ongoing 
today, where only two of the projects have received a construction permit 
(Energistyrelsen, n.d.-a).  
The 2018 Energy Agreement – and beyond 
By 2018, the installed offshore wind capacity in Denmark had reached 1.3 GW and was 
with the finished auctions and ongoing projects Horns Rev III and Kriegers Flak going to 
increase to 2.3 GW (Energistyrelsen, n.d.-a). In the summer of 2018, the government 
together with a broad coalition of parties agreed a new Energy bill to set forth the green 
initiatives for the coming decade (Energiaftale, 2018). The centerpiece of the bill was the 
announcement of three auctions in the coming years for the realization of minimum 2.4 
GW of new offshore wind capacity before 2030, the first of which would be the Thor 
auction, starting in 2019/2020. In addition, the initiation of a major analysis, 
“havvindsanalysen”, was announced, which would support the ambitions for making the 
North Sea a globally front-running region for offshore wind development. In addition, a 
large screening of the Danish seabed would commence for the identification of sites 
allowing up to 10 GW of offshore wind. 
Thor auction 
The first of the three auctions announced in the 2018 energy agreement was the 800-1000 
MW Thor offshore wind park located about 25 km off the small harbour Thorsminde at 
==PAGE:16==
10 
 
the western shore of Jylland. The auctioning procedure started in 2019 when the site was 
accepted for auctioning. Then followed a thoroughly planned and scheduled market 
dialogue covering both technical site-specifications, the specific auctioning procedures, 
and conditions as well as the demand.  
The Contract Notice with the initial auction material and conditions was published 
September 2020, and the deadline for the application for pre-qualification for interested 
developers was end of 2020. The Contract Notice decreed a maximum of 10 prequalified 
developers. Six developers and developing consortia were prequalified, and they 
submitted their initial bids by Q1 2021. 
The auction then followed the previously applied two round bidding process for 
prequalified developers with a market dialogue after the initial bidding round, allowing 
for potential adjustments to the auction conditions before final bidding round. With the 
final bids submitted by November 2021, DEA had received more than one bid with the 
lowest possible price at 0.01 øre/kWh and the largest possible capacity of 1000 MW, and 
DEA thus called for a lottery draw on December 1st 2021 to determine the concession 
winner, as per the auction conditions (Energistyrelsen, n.d.-b). The lottery draw awarded 
the concession to German developer RWE with the strike price of 0.01 øre/kWh. Both the 
de-facto strike price of zero and the lottery draw resulting from multiple participants 
bidding that price were first ever occurrences in Danish auction history.  
OD escalation 
As a last part of the ‘Havvindsanalyse’ iniated with the 2018 energy agreement, DEA 
published an analysis of the role of the OD policy scheme on March 18th, 2021 
(Energistyrelsen, 2021a).  
Consequently, on June 2nd, 2021, the political coalition voted for an amendment that 
would change the conditions of the OD (Aftale Om Justering Af Åben Dør-Ordningen for 
VE-Anlæg På Havet Af 2. Juni 2021, 2021). The change went through the procedures in 
the Danish parliament during spring 2022 and was finally approved and proclaimed to 
come into effect from July 1st, 2022. 
==PAGE:17==
11 
 
During that same period of democratic processing of the amendment, DEA experienced 
an explosion of applications for offshore wind farms through the OD. Between April 4th 
and June 30th DEA had received a total of 43 applications. Out of these, 16 projects were 
eligible to be processed for awarding of a pre-investigation permit1 – 16 projects which 
in total represented up to 12 GW of capacity (Energistyrelsen, 2022b)  corresponding to 
around six times the current installed offshore wind capacity in Denmark.  
Chapter 3 – Literature review 
Auction studies and focus on Danish offshore wind  
As a part of the EU funded AURES (Auctions for Renewable Energy Support) research 
programme, Kitzing & Wendring (2015) studied the design and outcome of the Danish 
auctions for offshore wind before the conclusion of the Kriegers Flak tender, picking apart 
the data of the undertaken auctions, comparing their processes and the effect of the 
specific design choices. Central to the auction format is the remuneration scheme. For the 
Danish tenders, the support was paid out as a “sliding premium”, where the bid price 
translated to a guaranteed price per kWh produced for the developer for the first 10 TWh 
as the state will pay the amount remaining if the developer would sell their power in the 
Nord Pool spot market for less than their bid price. On the other hand, if selling the selling 
price in the spot market is higher than the bid price, the difference would be discounted 
from the eligible support payments to come, thereby Ensuring that the overall price for 
the produced power equals the bid in the auction – thus the sliding feature. This scheme 
was later labelled as a one-sided Contract-for-Difference. Kitzing & Wendring 
highlighted that with this remuneration scheme, the operators have to participate actively 
in the spot market, and are subject to forecast risks and balancing costs, which makes 
them well integrated in the liberalized electricity market – in contrast to the feed-in tariff, 
which guarantees the same price for every kWh. Thus, the remuneration scheme of the

Danish auctions achieved a balance between support and competitive behavior with this

The effects and applications for auctions for offshore wind have been and are currently 
being studied widely. In a recent study, Jansen, Beiter, Riepin, Müsgens, Guajardo-
Fajardo, Staffell, Bulder & Kitzing (2022) provided a quantitative overview of concluded 
auctions for offshore wind using a comprehensive data set of various instantiations of 
auctions in many different countries. They conclude that the auction scheme is still on the 
rise, and that the share of offshore capacity being procured through auctions will rise from 
the current 24% to 97% by 2030 (Jansen et al., 2022). They also find that less developed 
markets have larger tendency of revenue stabilization, while more developed markets and 
regions, such as Northern Europe, the design approaches have started to vary as a result 
of the high competitiveness. The authors also conclude on the specific outcome of the 
Thor tender with zero-bids and zero subsidies. They find that the outcome indicates a 
willingness to pay for the access to the sea and likens the outcome to that of an actual 
seabed lease auction. In addition, they problematizeWhile the analysis in this thesis does 
not expect to decipher the outcome differently, the economic details and implications of 
the design and outcome will be expanded by digging into the economic documentation of 
the auction, and they will in addition be placed in the context of the STS-based part of the 
analysis, to enrich the understanding of both the political inscriptions and overflowings, 
as explained in the coming sections. 
STS 
Semiotic inquiry of policy instruments – the overall framework 
The centerpiece of the analysis in this thesis is the identification of the inscriptions of the 
Thor auction and the ‘open door’ policy to compare them and interpret their 
entanglements. This idea for the framework, which is detailed in Chapter 4, is inspired by 
Ossandón, Pallesen, Karnøe & Georg (2023), who in their working paper “Making good 
economies with bad economic instruments: A brief history of wind powers changing 
economies” assess the different “economies” of wind power historically in Denmark 
through three different governmental support schemes. They label their work a semiotic 
inquiry, with the aim of shedding light on a range of elements constituting and nuancing 
==PAGE:19==
13 
 
the inscriptions of the policies, by employing the concepts of Actor-Network Theory 
(ANT) and valuation studies such as problematization, resource-making and “the good 
economy”. Semiotics means interpretation of signs, and with this label they emphasize 
the importance of investigating not only the legal policy documents that explicitly 
formulate the schemes, but their surroundings through an array of various document types 
which they identify with an “archeological dig” following the “paper trails” of the laws. 
One of the schemes under investigation by Ossandón et al. is the tender scheme, which is 
the most recent of the three schemes of their study. They pay attention to its first 
inscriptions from 1999 and the announcement of the first tender in 2004, before 
concluding their provisionary findings with a comparison between the inscriptions and 
the “good economies” of three schemes, thus summarizing the developments of Danish 
wind power in the period between the 1970’s and around 2000. 
 
As a continuation of the spirit and approach of these studies, this thesis focuses on the 
inscriptions of the tender, or auction, policy instrument. The focus is shifted to the 
inscriptions of the recent Thor auction specifically, using the findings from Ossandón et 
al. on the original inscriptions of the scheme as the platform to embark from, while 
recognizing its entire “use phase” of offshore wind deployment in Denmark from 1999 to 
2021. Furthermore, the comparison will be with the ‘open door’ policy and take place on 
a more contemporary stage. In addition, instead of replacing one another as the dominant 
support scheme for wind power in the country like the three schemes in the study of 
Ossandón et al. largely did, the two schemes investigated in this paper were born with the 
same electricity market reform in 1999, and the co-existing of the schemes alters the 
framing of the study. While the provisionary findings of Ossandón et al. on the 
inscriptions of the tender scheme are echoed, the altered framing in this thesis results in 
an alteration in the labelling of the constituents of the inscriptions. 
 
Ossandón et al characterized the three policy instruments with their “script/program”, 
“problematization”, “user/beneficiary”, implicit valuation of “wind power”, and their 
overall inscription of the envisioned “good economy” through two different iterations of 
their working paper. In this thesis, the valuation of wind power will be narrowed in to 
focus strictly on “offshore wind power”, while also adding the “seabed” to the topic of 
==PAGE:20==
14 
 
resource-making. Consequently, the lens of “the good economy” will be narrowed in and 
labelled “the good offshore wind economy”, to better enable the comparison of the two 
contemporary instantiations of offshore wind policy schemes. The exact interpretation 
and origins of each of these categories will be summarized in the following sections. 
 
Lastly, Ossandón et al. focus their study entirely on the inscriptions of the policies, what 
Akrich (1992) called the “first task” in a semiotic inquiry of a technical instrument. This 
thesis will also incorporate the “second task” as proposed by Akrich, which entertains 
what she labels the de-scription of the instrument that materializes empirically through 
the interactions with the users of the scheme in its “specific milieu”. This entails that to 
get a full picture of the influence of a policy instrument, the study of the “response” of its 
intended – or unintended – users to its designed inscriptions must be included in the 
assessment. This approach will be utilized heavily to track the developments of the policy 
instruments throughout their use phase from 1999 to 2022, which in that sense will be 
treated as multiple iterations of inscriptions and de-scriptions of the policies.  
Constituents of the inscriptions of the policy schemes 
“The good economy”  
A central part of the inscriptions of the policy instruments and the conclusions to Research 
Question 1 is the interpretation and comparison of the “good offshore wind economy” 
inscribed into the policies. The notion of “the good economy” originates from a study by 
Asdal, Cointe, Hobæk, Reinertsen, Huse, Morsman & Måløy (2021), in which they coin 
the term for describing valuations of “the good” in economies embodied in political 
visions, specifically comparing the oil economy with the more recent bioeconomy in 
Norway. “The good” is extracted from the ways in which the policies are “inserted” into 
society and assessing the imagined and realized benefits of doing so. This entails an 
empirical analytical approach with focus on tools of valuations that define the good 
economy. Asdal et al. also use a broad document analysis to identify these empirically 
rooted signs of “the good”. In a similar fashion, this thesis tracks the “good offshore wind 
economy” through document analysis to assemble a semiotic interpretation of how the 
Danish policy makers have labelled each policy scheme “good” for the offshore wind 
economy.  
==PAGE:21==
15 
 
Problematization 
The ANT-rooted notion of problematization applied as a part of the inscription of the 
policy schemes stems from the work of Ossandón & Ureta (Ossandón & Ureta, 2019). 
They study problematization in relation to the deployment of market-based policy 
instruments in a neoliberal economy. They focus on how policy makers of a neoliberal 
state, in this case Chile, initiated a market-based policy scheme, staged to provide the 
solution to the problem of the given state, thus “marketizing” specific policy areas. They 
highlight the “marketization” of Chile through several reforms in the 1970s as well as 
more recent policy movements that “re-problematize” these reforms. Ossandón & Ureta 
claim that “the close study of problematization shows the development and consolidation 
of a particular mode of governing collective concerns”. As such, they interpret economic 
policy instruments as the modes of repairing market failures. Similarly, through the 
empirical study of the auctions and OD scheme, which are both market-based instruments 
for offshore wind development in Denmark, this thesis applies the term of 
“problematization” to track the manner in which policy makers have interpreted a certain 
“state” of the policy for offshore wind development and their imagination of how to 
“repair” it. 
Valuation of a resource – resource-making  
Valuation, the procedure of assigning value, is a central, implicit part of both the act of 
problematization and the interpretation of “the good economy”. The interpretation of 
value associated with political instruments is captured by Muniesa (2017) in his paper 
“On the Political Vernaculars of Value Creation”, who calls value “the crucial motto of 
science and technology politics” and the “incarnation of the very idea of the financial 
viability of things”. In addition, it is also inherently what defines the process of “resource-
making”, as coined by Ossandón et al. (2023). That is, how and with what devices, value 
is assigned to, e.g., wind to enable it as an energy resource. In their working paper, 
Ossandón et al. tracks how the wind resource evolves through the valuation it is assigned 
with the three different policies. From being “a source of domestic energy”, they conclude 
by rending “wind power” “a national energy resource that plays a central part in 
decarbonizing the energy sector” (Ossandón et al., 2023). This thesis will develop this 
valuation to assess the valuation of offshore wind specifically and how it evolved 
==PAGE:22==
16 
 
throughout the period under investigation and plays a role of the inscriptions and 
entanglements of the auction and OD schemes. In addition, attention will also be directed 
towards the physical foundation of the offshore wind resource that is the seabed. While 
value being politically assigned to the seabed as a part of an energy resource is far from 
new, with e.g., offshore oil and gas, assigning the seabed the value in relation to offshore 
wind effectively started with the commissioning of Vindeby in 1991 and was first 
embodied in the 1999 electricity reform, and it is therefore an equally important part of 
making offshore wind a resource, similar to the way land has become an asset and a 
resource for the development and marketization of onshore wind (Kirkegaard et al., 
2022). 
Overflowing and contestation of policy instruments over time   
In their paper “Introducing the lens of markets-in-the-making to transition studies: The 
case of the Danish wind power market agencement”, Karnøe, Kirkegaard & Caliskan 
(2022) also assess the processes that make wind power ‘valuable’. With the overall 
premise of viewing wind power as a ‘market-in-the-making’, Karnøe et al. place agency 
in ‘market agencements’, in which agency is viewed as distributed between actors using 
socio-political, economic, and technical ‘devices’ to assign valuation to wind power.  
In addition, the authors introduce the perspective of transition studies, here for the wind 
power market agencements, which emphasizes the need to frame specific “states” of the 
market agencement to assess its valuation of wind power. However, as quoted by Karnøe 
et al. “framings are fragile and might require re-framing due to overflowing”, and this 
entails that these framings are a part of a dynamic process which represents the 
“transition”. In this thesis, this approach is recognized through the work with the long use 
phase of the policies from 1999 to 2022, where the two-step analysis of “inscription” and 
“de-scription” as proposed by Akrich and interpreted by Ossandón et al. can be viewed 
as an iteration of framing and overflowing of the policy schemes, triggered by the 
continuous interactions between the legal infrastructure of the policy scheme and its 
surrounding market agencement.

Chapter 4 – Methodology 
The core of the analysis in this thesis is a case study of the Thor auction and the ‘open 
door’ policy and the dynamics leading up to their recent eventful conclusions. Like the 
outline in Ossandón et al. (2023), the aim is not to assess any of the policy instruments as 
either good or bad, but rather to identify the “scripts” and track the “empirical notions” 
of the “good offshore wind economy” of each policy, and to use that to assess their 
entanglements in the recent events and their implications for Danish offshore wind policy. 
The analysis section presents insights enriched by the cross-disciplinarity of Science and 
Technology Studies (STS), specifically the practices associated with Actor-Network-
Theory, and studies on offshore wind auctions rooted in economic theory. The precise 
methodology will be expanded in the following.  
Scope 
This analysis is motivated by the conclusion of the Thor auction in December 2021 and 
the wave of applications coming through the OD in the spring of 2022, two events whose 
impacts created a stir in the political environment, attracted lot of media attention and 
initiated debates on the future for offshore wind policy (Frandsen, 2022; Hebsgaard, 
2022; Madsen, 2023). To frame the purpose of the analysis that is illustrating the 
inscription of the policies that culminated in these events, the scope of the analysis 
therefore ends on July 1st 2022, when the final amendment of the OD scheme entered into 
force. Consequently, the analysis concerning the last part of RQ2 about the implication 
of the events for Danish offshore policy will not take into account any events happening 
after July 1st 2022, but rather only evaluate the status and outlook at that point in time. A 
view on contemporary developments will be offered in Chapter 6.  
Data 
The insights are to be constructed from the synergy between two main sources of data. 
Interviews 
The primary data to uncover the dynamics of the two policies consists of interviews. The 
aim was to collect data from both a policy maker and a private developer to get insights 
==PAGE:24==
18 
 
from both sides of the process of designing and using policy schemes. Thus, two 
interviews were conducted. The first was with The Danish Energy Agency (DEA), the 
legal entity responsible for both the entire work and conduction of all auctions for 
renewable energy as well as handling the OD applications. The other was with a Danish 
offshore wind developer, who both participated as a part of a bidding consortia in the 
Thor auction and has been active with applications through the ‘open door’ policy.  
The interviews were conducted as semi-structured interviews, meaning that they followed 
a prepared interview guide intended to extract the necessary knowledge, but also were 
loose enough to allow for pursuing topics of interest when they arose during conversation. 
The interviews were conducted in Danish, were recorded, and afterwards transcribed 
manually in Danish. Quotes featuring in the analysis are translated to English by the 
author. Interview guides and transcripts can be found in the Appendix. 
The Danish Energy Agency - DEA  
The first interview was conducted as a double interview with two special consultants 
working for DEA, assigned with working with the ‘open door’ policy and the auctions for 
offshore wind, respectively, thus both having expert knowledge on the policies. The 
purpose with the interview was to uncover the events of both case policies and gather 
background knowledge on the policies themselves and the political motivations behind 
the design and actions behind them. Both interviewees were asked for background 
information on their education, career, experience within the field of energy policy, and 
specific involvement and daily work with the ‘open door’ policy, and the Thor auction, 
respectively. Then, the interview proceeded with a line of questioning dedicated to the 
Thor auction, thus questioning one of the consultants directly. Here the focus was to 
uncover the outcome of the dialogue phase and the effect of the auction specific design 
choices. Afterwards, the other consultant was interviewed directly with a line of questions 
focusing on the ‘open door’ policy. Here the focus was on getting perspectives on the role 
of the ‘open door’ policy, its purposes, and how the history of the policy aligns with the 
time after 2018 and up to the 2022 developments. The last part of the interview was set 
up as a discussion between both, focusing on the entanglement of the two schemes, asking 
questions revolving around which effect, if any, they had on each other.

After the conclusion of the interview, a mutual agreement was made that the interview 
would only be used as background for confidentiality reasons. Therefore, only the 
interview guide, and not the transcript, is included in the Appendix. The learnings from 
the interview were used to structure and form the analysis, and specific findings were 
instead attempted to be shown through other data and analysis.   
Danish offshore wind developer 
The second interview was with a manager in a Danish offshore developer (called either 
the Danish offshore developer or simply the Developer throughout this thesis and the 
transcript), who had personally been involved in activities in the ‘open door’ scheme as 
well as the Developer’s participation in a bid for the Thor auction. The interview started 
with getting background information on education, career, and experience and the work 
carried out within the company in relation to both policies. Then, in similar fashion to the 
interview with DEA, the line of questioning was split into three, focusing on first the two 
policies individually before ending with a line of questioning on their entanglement. The 
focus in this interview was to gather as much insight as possible on how events played 
out seen from a developer and active participant, or “user”, of the policies. Therefore, 
simply getting his view of how things played out was part of the questioning. In addition, 
questions detailing the motivations of the Developer, perspectives, and specific actions of 
participation with regards to the ‘open door’ policy were paramount. 
It is important to mention, that the author of this thesis works in the same Danish offshore 
developing company as a student assistant in a different department. That being said, any

Documents 
The other part of the foundation on which the analysis of the policies is conducted is an 
extensive document research and analysis. Many types of documents were identified and 
included in the pool of data to create a sufficient “network of documents”, in similar 
fashion to Ossandón et al., on which to base the analysis. In the middle stands the political 
agreements, bills, and statutes that make up the proposals and jurisdictions of the energy 
policies and set the stage for the Thor auction and the ‘open door’, defining them as they 
were imagined and intended. Supporting the formation and amendment of the laws is an 
extensive number of analyses, investigations, and reports. These can stem from 
preparation work prior to the formulation of laws, can be integral part of processes 
determined by the laws, such as seabed screenings, or can be consultancy analyses made 
by DEA or ordered from third parties to assess a given policy situation.  
Surrounding these central legal documents are the reactions and debates on their content. 
These can have the form of political debates during the proposal phase of the policies, 
media articles and reactions after their publishing, or hearings or complaints being put 
forward during their use phases. These have an equally important role in piecing together 
the inscriptions and effects – or ‘de-scriptions’ – of the policies, reflecting interpretations 
in and by its context and ‘specific milieu’.  
The most important database for documents for the analysis is ens.dk. DEA is the 
responsible entity for handling applications through both policies, processing all cases, as 
well as making public information about it in the form of press releases. The page contains 
a database on all currently existing offshore wind farms in Denmark as well as the ones 
in the pipeline, including both farms procured through auctions and all those applied for 
under the ‘open door’ scheme. For each wind farm, documentation for auction conditions, 
all foregoing analysis, complaints and their case work, amendments, and concession 
contracts can be found. In addition, the newer auctions have their own subpages 
containing all published documents and press releases in chronological order. Since the 
latest developments around the ‘open door’ policy, a subpage has also been created for 
the OD containing documentation and clarifications. Another important data source in 
this regard is the material coming directly from the developers participating in the 
==PAGE:27==

policies. This can be in the form of hearing input for auction designs or the material of 
their applications for projects through the OD. 
Besides DEA’s database, legal documents have been procured either via the database of 
the Danish parliament at ft.dk or directly from retsinformation.dk, which contains all bills 
and statutes in the Danish constitution. Media articles, debates, and press releases have 
been collected using infomedia.dk, containing all articles from media outlets in Denmark, 
from ft.dk, where debates taking place inside the Parliament can be found, or from topic 
specific media such as EnergiWatch or offshorewind.biz. In several cases, Google has 
been used to broadly identify sources of information on e.g., specific wind farms, which 
includes statements or press releases from developers, manufacturers, or other actors in 
the energy system. 
The inscriptions of Thor and OD  
As mentioned in Chapter 3, the method for analyzing the inscription of the two case 
policies (answering RQ1) is inspired by the framework applied by Ossandón et al. in their 
working paper on Danish wind power policies. The individual analyses of the policies 
will be structured in roughly the same manner, with minor differences in the approach 
due to their different characteristics. 
The conclusion for each policy consists of a table summarizing its inscriptions. The table 
has five attributes: 
• The script: the explicit, official procedure programmed into the policy. What 
actually happens and what the expected outcome is. 
• Problematization: the underlying state or condition that the script attempts to 
“repair” to achieve an expressed political goal.  
• “Users”: The actors targeted to participate in the “program” of the policy scheme 
• Valuation of offshore wind and the seabed: How and what valuation is inscribed 
to offshore wind and to the seabed as resources of the policy schemes. This 
follows the approach of Ossandón et al. (2023) on tracking the making of wind 
into a resource, focusing on the role of specific devices in the market agencement. 
==PAGE:28==
22 
 
• The good ‘offshore wind’ economy: the desired economic reality which the policy 
scheme aims to achieve for offshore wind development and who this offshore 
wind economy benefits.  
The phrasing of these inscriptions will condense the findings of the document analysis 
and the interviews, which will be presented with individual structures for each policy, as 
detailed in the following. 
The Thor auction 
The analysis of the Thor auction will first present a summary of the inscriptions of the 
auction scheme, as it was before the 2018 Energy Agreement and the announcement of 
Thor. This will be based on the work by Ossandón et al. (2023) supplemented with the 
summary of all previously held Danish auctions presented in the Chapter 2 as well as the 
work by Kitzing & Wendring (2015) on Danish auctions for offshore wind. 
To set the scene for Thor, the political developments leading to the declaration of the Thor 
auction will be laid out. These are based on document analysis of the bills and legal 
documents published by the Danish state as well as studies of developments in the 
offshore wind sector and outcomes of the contemporary offshore wind auctions in fellow 
North Sea region countries Netherlands and Germany. 
Then, the auction material and proceedings leading to its final formulation will be 
thoroughly dissected. To understand the economic ramifications of the Thor auction, 
special attention is paid to the specific design of the new two-sided CfD-scheme, which 
is the centerpiece of the auction and acts as the economic device that both embodies much 
of the political intent behind the auction and is crucial to its conclusion and the aftermath 
following it. The CfD-scheme will be analyzed economically, assessing the expected 
effect according to studies of auctions and this type of valuation schemes, as well as in a 
more practical manner, picking apart the economic value of the project as defined by the 
scheme, using the underlying assumptions on costs and prices provided by DEA in Excel 
spreadsheets. The conclusions of this will be included in the STS-based work on the 
inscriptions, with the aim of this cross-disciplinarity enriching and nuancing the findings.  
==PAGE:29==
23 
 
Lastly, the findings on the inscriptions of Thor throughout these sections will be 
summarized in the inscriptions table as initially described, with the changes compared to 
before 2018 being highlighted. 
The ‘open door’ scheme 
To identify the inscription of the OD scheme as it was embodied in the last amendment 
of July 1st 2022, an in-depth analysis of the entire history of the scheme has been 
conducted, starting with identifying the first inscriptions from its birth in 1999. Its 
development will be illustrated by analyzing the two “waves” of project applications that 
came between 1999 and 2017, as detailed in Chapter 2. The characteristics of the 
applications will be presented in tables to identify patterns that relate to how the policy 
was “de-scripted” by the applicants, and how these de-scriptions ended up as inscriptions 
in the scheme again via problematizations embodied in the political work around the 
various amendments it has had. To detail these processes, insights from the interview with 
the Danish offshore developer will be presented. 
The combination of the iterative, back-and-forth analysis of the historical developments 
of the policy leads to the notion of “re-scripting”, adding another layer of iteration when 
tracking the dynamics of inscription and description through the history of the policy 
scheme. One can also say that the developments follow a re-problematization of the 
context surrounding the scheme, which triggered a re-inscription, or ‘re-scription’, one 
that however cannot ignore the past dynamics leading to the current situation, in which 
case the phrasing “re-scripting” seems fitting. 
Like Ossandón et al. (2023), this analysis will use these terms both as verbs, nouns, and 
adverbs, i.e., to ‘inscribe’, an ‘inscription’, and to be ‘scripted’, in the analysis of the 
policies, addressing both acts and attributes associated with them and the development 
around them, thus being true to the ANT-rooted main object of interest, namely the 
dynamics, the fluid change of state, and the both human and non-human agents creating 
these dynamics. 
Three sources are combined to piece together what this analysis declares the “inscription” 
of the policies. First, the phrasing of the bills and legal statutes themselves are used. Bills 
==PAGE:30==
24 
 
or proposals typically contain an additional description of the suggested new legal 
paragraphs or amendments, and the final statute contains the legally binding phrasing of 
the law. Second, any analyses or reports being procured by the government as groundwork 
for law or amendment proposal are used. Third, the content of the democratic processing 
of the bills and statutes are used, such as phrasing in debates or hearings taking place in 
the Danish Parliament, as these present further perspectives on the laws, whether be it in 
the form of concordance or opposition.  The entire political history of the policy has been 
tracked by going through all entries on ft.dk related to the Danish name “åben dør”. 
Overflowing and entanglement of the schemes 
The second part of the analysis attempts to answer RQ2, namely how the unforeseen 
developments of the Thor auction and the ‘open door’ applications can be understood as 
overflowing and entanglement of the two policy schemes, and where these developments 
left Danish offshore wind policy by July 1st 2022.  
First, the overflowing of the Thor auction will be argued for by showing contestations of 
the inscription and discrepancies in the design phase and design elements compared to 
the actual outcome of the auction. They will be backed up by empirical insights from the 
interview with the Danish offshore developer, providing an insider’s view on the 
outcome.  
Then, the events with the “third wave” of OD applications coming in during spring 2022 
will be analyzed in detail. Here, a similar approach as in the inscription section will be 
used, where the characteristics of the applications will be illustrated in a table before the 
contestations of the inscriptions of the scheme embodied in these applications will be 
highlighted by comparing them to the previous categories of inscriptions.  
Throughout this analysis, the same framework of notions from the inscriptions part will 
be used to detail the new dynamics in the schemes, and these dynamics will then be 
described with the notions of overflowing and contestation to describe how the two 
schemes changed. 
Lastly, empirical insights from the Danish offshore developer will be presented to 
interpret the correlation between the timing of Thor auction concluding and the “third 
==PAGE:31==
25 
 
wave” of OD applications following right after, and thus suggest how the dynamics of 
these two schemes became entangled.

With provisionary findings by Ossandón et al. (2023) of the original inscriptions of the 
auction scheme as it was established in 1999 as the starting point, and the summary of the 
characteristics of the different auction rounds provided in the empirical field as supporting 
material, the inscriptions of the auctions as they were before the 2018 Energy Agreement 
and the announcement of the Thor will be given in the following.  
The main script of the auctions had essentially remained the same from 1999 to 2017. 
The scheme was an answer to the problematization of realizing the potential of offshore 
wind according to national policy objectives despite the high cost of the technology. The 
centerpiece of the auction mechanism essentially solving the problem was the revenue 
stabilization through the sliding premium tariff, and auctions had unambiguously implied 
payments from state to developer from Horns Rev to Kriegers Flak. (Forslag Til Lov Om 
Elforsyning. 1998/1 LSF 234., 1999). While the talk of the town following the outcome 
of Kriegers Flak was the historically cheap winning price (Energistyrelsen, 2016), the 
rhetorics were still centered around “subsidies”, this time just that they were lower than 
expected, and the long-term objective of the auctions to decrease costs and improve 
competitiveness of offshore wind thus remained throughout the period.  
Offshore wind was in 2018 recognized and well-established as a ‘national energy source’, 
labelled by Ossandón et al. (2023) in their inscription of the auction format, through the 
numerous auctions concluded up to this point and the capacity they had added to the grid. 
In addition, as land had become a part of the valued resource for onshore wind 
development (Kirkegaard et al., 2022), the seabed had become a part of the valued 
resource in the offshore wind development. It can be argued that this valuation was 
already established with the “Havmøllehandlingsplan for danske farvande” in 1997 as 
mentioned in the Chapter 2. A main part of the screening work done by the state to identify 
potential sites was scouting characteristics of the seabed, such as water depths and sea 
floor quality, and as such the seabed was formally a main object of investigation in the 
work identifying a site feasible for an auction. In addition, the characteristics of the seabed 
is a main driver of the assessment of the costs of a wind farm (Energistyrelsen, 2022a), 
and as such, a monetary value becomes attached to a site through the evaluation of a 
specific piece of seabed. Together with an assessment of the wind resource to estimate 
==PAGE:33==
27 
 
the potential yield of the site, the technical and economic ‘devicing’ of the wind resource 
and the seabed characteristics embody the economic value of a potential wind farm. 
The user of the auction were the larger players within wind power development. As 
described in the Chapter 2, the tendency from Horns Rev to Kriegers Flak was to mainly 
see larger developers participating – and, most notably, winning, as DONG, E.ON and 
Vattenfall represent. As found by Ossandón et al., the auction conditions also catered for 
either a company with large equity or a state-owned company. Specifically, this relates to 
the projects for auctioning  continuously increasing in size with each auction round, with 
the demands on technical and financial capability and experience set forth in the pre-
qualification criteria following. Later rounds saw joint ventures between developers in 
attempts to pre-qualify for participation as well as strengthen the position to place the 
most competitive bid (Energistyrelsen, n.d.-a). 
To summarize, the support of the overall policy goal behind the auctions, namely 
increasing the share of green power in the Danish electricity grid, the auction scheme 
embodied an inscription of the “good offshore wind economy” where the pace and size 
of the offshore wind development was controlled by the state. In addition, this 
development should be as cheap as possible for the state and thereby for the Danish power 
consumers, who partly financed the support of the farms through the PSO tariff. In support 
of these objectives, the design of the scheme with continuously stricter pre-qualification 
criteria and harsher penalties for non-compliance with the concession contracts and 
fulfillment of the project timeline inscribed a clear preference for experienced developers 
with large equity.  
A new era of offshore wind auctions  
With the 2018 Energy agreement, where the coming of the Thor auction was declared, 
new perspectives on the future auctions were presented. The “havvindsanalyse” initiated 
with the agreement had among its objectives to  
“[…] contribute to the most optimal market conditions, so the potential 
for offshore wind can be commercially utilized as quickly as possible.”  
Furthermore, it  
==PAGE:34==
28 
 
“[…] should, when offshore wind becomes subsidy free, create models 
for how revenues can be created for the state as a result of the 
utilization of the wind resource.” (Energiaftale, 2018) 
These formulations of political ambitions of “commercial utilization” and “when offshore 
wind power becomes subsidy free” were signs of the breaking of new ground for the 
political agenda with the auctions for offshore wind, indicating a near future where the 
long-standing auction objectives and role of subsidies would be altered. As the first of the 
three auctions announced in the energy agreement, Thor was set to be the first concrete 
policy output of the bill starting the new era of Danish offshore wind development. 
Looking at the capacity alone, the 1 GW mark for a single park is almost double the size 
of Kriegers Flak’s 600 MW and constituting about a 25% increase of the total installed 
offshore wind capacity. Both Kriegers Flak and Thor consist of 72 turbines, and the 
markup in capacity is thus captured by the upgrade from 8 MW turbines to 14 MW 
turbines (Siemens-Gamesa, 2017) – each model being the newest and most efficient from 
supplier Siemens Gamesa at the point of procurement, with the 14 MW rated SG 14-236 
DD turbines ordered for Thor even being a prototype still at today’s date, showcasing the 
significant technological developments in the wind turbine industry taking place in that 
period. 
This development in turbine technology and the impact that has on how an offshore wind 
farm is designed and planned are main drivers in the decreasing trend of the very central 
measure that is the LCOE. As detailed by Danish offshore wind analysts and consultants, 
AEGIR, in an NREL workshop presentation (AEGIR, 2022), the efficiency gains 
stemming from the decreasing CAPEX and OPEX following the upscaling of both the 
individual turbine and the park capacity, and the increasing experience of the industry, 
developers, and the rest of the operations supply chain have together cut the Levelized 
Cost of Energy (LCOE) for offshore wind down from 100 EUR/MWh in 2015 to 50 
EUR/MWh in 20222.

There was also political awareness that the technology was maturing and that the state 
expenses to support the build-out of offshore wind could significantly decrease with this 
new wave of auctions and their characteristics. The agreement text of “Energiaftalen

“It is expected that offshore wind within a few years can produce green

2018) 
These ambitions were at that time not fetched out of nowhere; recently, in April 2017 and 
March 2018, auctions in fellow North Sea Region countries Netherlands and Germany 
had concluded with the prospect of the first subsidy-free offshore wind farms in the world. 
Vattenfall had won auctions for four areas 20 km off the Dutch coast worth 1.5 GW of 
capacity (Vattenfall, n.d.), and DONG (now Ørsted) had won three auctions in the German 
part of the North Sea worth 590, of two of the auctions, in total 480 MW, were won with 
zero-bids (Ørsted, 2017b).  
The auctions had characteristics in common that, described by both the developers and 
by the researchers in the AURES II research project, were crucial to the subsidy free

outcomes (Geipel et al., 2019; Jakob et al., 2019). Most importantly, the grid connection 
was ensured and paid for by the government through the regional TSOs. Vattenfall

securing grid connection and right to produce power free of charge while deselecting 
subsidies (Geipel et al., 2019; Jakob et al., 2019).

The changes of the Thor auction conditions 
With the first public information on the content and conditions of the Thor auction, the 
“Invitation to Dialogue” material, distributed in November 2019, there were, however, 
==PAGE:37==
31 
 
several indications that this specific auction was not expected to be subsidy free. First, in 
contrast to the recent German and Dutch auctions and for the first time in Danish auction 
history, the responsibility of constructing the offshore substation and connect the park to 
the grid on land fell on the concession winner. That includes conducting all necessary pre-
investigations, EIAs, and covering all costs (Energistyrelsen, n.d.-b).  
In addition, the remuneration scheme had undergone a complete redesign. Instead of the 
hourly calculation of the sliding premium used for all previous auctions, the premium 
would be a fixed amount for the entire year, defined as the difference between the 
concession winner’s bid price and the average hourly spot price of the previous year, 
dubbed the ‘reference price’, meaning that the yearly premium could be negative as well 
of positive. Furthermore, instead of just subtracting a potential negative premium from 
the upcoming subsidy payments, thus effectively just temporarily pausing the payments, 
the negative premiums would be paid directly to the state. The remuneration scheme was 
therefore now a so-called two-sided Contract-for-Difference. The setup is illustrated by 
DEA in Annex 3.9 of the final auction conditions and is depicted below.  
 
Figure 3: Illustration of the symmetrical payment method of the Thor CfD (Energistyrelsen, n.d.-b) 
==PAGE:38==

ENS was aware that this model could procure scenarios where the concession owner 
would lose the incentive to produce electricity, scenarios which would be “a socio-
economical liability”. To mitigate some of this risk, a “minimum price” of 3 øre/kWh was 
implemented, corresponding to the DEA’s assumption of the marginal cost of production 
(Energistyrelsen, n.d.-b). This effectively ensures the concession owner a minimum 
revenue of 3 øre/kWh of electricity produced, as they are only liable to pay the difference 
between the spot price and the minimum price in hours where the spot price is lower than 
the yearly premium plus the minimum price. The setup is illustrated by DEA in the Annex 
3.9 to the final auction condition and can be seen in Figure 5.  
In addition to securing a minimum price for the electricity, a “budget cap” was put on 
both ends, limiting the state’s total subsidy expenses to be maximum 6.5 bn DKK (over 
maximum 20 years of operation) and limiting the total payments from the concession 
owner to a maximum 2.8 bn DKK over the support period, mitigating additional risk for 
the concession owner with the revised CfD-scheme, but also mitigating risk for the state 
with regard to their subsidy budgets. This total budget cap differs from the state’s previous 
subsidy capping strategy with maximum TWh or full load hours eligible for subsidy and 
is technically more restrictive.

(Energistyrelsen, n.d.-b) 
==PAGE:39==

Furthermore, the DEA implemented a “budget evaluation cap” of 3.7 bn, referring to the 
net present value of the total subsidies over the 20-year subsidy period. If all bid prices 
resulted in a breach of the cap, further approval by the Danish Parliament would be 
needed to accept the lowest price. However, if the lowest price was below the cap, it 
would be accepted automatically, thus inscribing further incentive for bidders to keep 
their bids below the threshold ensuring this. As an assisting tool for bidders to evaluate 
whether they would comply with this, a spreadsheet was provided with fixed parameters 
of discounting rate (CPI forecast), electricity price forecast, and full load hours of 
production.  
During the market dialogue phase of the auction in late 2019, a collection of “several 
market players” submitted a concern over this updated CfD-model. They stated that the 
fixed premium based on a reference price for the previous year “poses risks” for the 
concession owner, “which will result in higher bid prices”. The risks posted in their 
concern included:  
• Cashflow risk, with increased fluctuations/year-by-year remuneration volatility 
• Price events in market last year affecting current year 
• Weak wind year following a strong wind year creating an unnecessary 
disadvantage in some scenarios 
• Exposure to risk of a larger difference (typically 10-20 %) between the hourly spot 
price (the wind capture price) realized by the wind farm owner and the spot market 
price average 
The concern was concluded with a suggestion to modify the model back to something 
alike the hourly-based definition of the premium used for determining remuneration in 
previous auctions. 
In their answer, DEA made it clear they were aware that this change put “the short-term 
risk on the concession owner while the state carried the long-term risk” (Energistyrelsen, 
2020). They further stated that: 
==PAGE:40==
34 
 
“It is therefore also acknowledged that the increased associated risk 
may lead to higher offered bid prices and higher overall subsidy costs.” 
(Energistyrelsen, 2020) 
Their arguments for the rationale for using this CfD-model for Thor is that 1) it gives the 
incentive for the concession owner to “maximise the value of the delivered electricity”, 
rather than maximising the quantity, as was the mechanism of the hourly-based CfD-
model, and in relation to that 2) to incentivize the concession owner to consider design 
solutions for the wind farm that can mitigate any potential long-term cannibalisation 
effect for offshore wind production in the North Sea Region. And 3) to ensure more 
predictability in the state budget with the fixed annual premium. They also state, that they 
do not expect the model to have any negative effect on the competition in the auction 
(Energistyrelsen, 2020). 
Economic assessment of zero-bid auction outcome 
Even if it was a surprise that the aforementioned auctions in Germany and the Netherlands 
yielded subsidy free wind farms (Geipel et al., 2019; Jakob et al., 2019), the conclusion 
of the Thor auction with five out of six participating consortia submitting zero-bids and 
the concession being awarded to RWE in a lottery draw was a ground-breaking outcome. 
With a zero-bid here in the specific setup of the two-sided CfD-model, the concession 
winner will effectively pay the reference price back to the state for each kWh of electricity 
produced until the cap is reached, assuming that the reference price will always be 
positive. Thus, not only will Thor be subsidy free, it will also net an income for the state 
of 2.8 bn DKK.

bids in relation to the budget evaluation threshold (Energistyrelsen, n.d.-d, Bilag 11), the 
cash flow reveals that the cap of 2.8 bn. DKK will be reached within the first three years 
of operation, under the assumption of the forecast DEA uses of the electricity price3.

The ‘open door’-policy 
This section will present the findings on how the final inscriptions of the OD came about 
in 2022, by analyzing all the applications and political developments related to it since it 
was introduced in 1999.  
First inscriptions of the OD  
As described in the Chapter 2, the ‘open door’ policy had existed for almost 20 years by 
2018, and the policy was – in contrast to the auctions – not as such defined with a 
structured set of rules and procedures when it was introduced. In the proposal of the 1999 
Energy Supply Law, the type of projects labelled as relevant for the OD were: 
“[…] small, nearshore projects, which are not part of the centralized 
auction procedure, or projects building on the discovery of new areas, 
which are not part of the centralized auction procedure.” (Forslag Til 
Lov Om Elforsyning. 1998/1 LSF 234., 1999) 
==PAGE:44==
38 
 
With this phrasing, a clear separation between the OD and the auctions was indicated, but 
there was a lack of clarity regarding which type of projects the policy specifically 
targeted. In essence, with this sparse formulation, the OD could be seen an open mailbox 
for project ideas and sketches, with which any interested developer could potentially get 
access to the state-owned seabed. In line with this lack of formal procedure and purpose, 
the six projects that came through between 2000-2010 were quite diverse in terms of their 
characteristics. Table 4 below summarizes the applications, the actors behind the projects, 
and what can be labelled as their “secondary” policy objectives. The label “secondary” 
should be interpreted with the assumption that the primary objective of building wind 
farms in general is tied to its physical attribute of adding green electricity to the public 
grid along the lines of the national climate and energy policy – which was the main 
inscription of the auctions as the “primary” policy instrument. 
Despite their many differences, there are notable patterns in the characteristics of these 
projects. First, all projects had small installed capacities compared to the wind farms 
being erected through the auctions. They were also all placed very near to the shore, with 
Frederikshavn, Middelgrunden, and Avedøre Holme being erected right outside harbor 
areas. Furthermore, they all had unique contextual motivations, or “secondary 
objectives”. These varying objectives fit well with the diversity of the agents constituting 
the various takers of initiative and developer groups. Notably, for all six projects, there 
was some form of local initiative or public ownership involved driving the development, 
whether it was a so-called ‘laug’, which is a group of citizens organized for the purpose 
of developing and owning wind turbines, an entity governed by a municipality, such as a 
utility company, or a state owned company such as DONG (at that time 100% state 
owned) or Sund & Bælt A/S – or a combination of these variations of agents. This entailed 
that the construction of these wind farms would come with some form of public benefit 
either in the form of revenues from the power sales or increased local economic activity 
and growth.

Political activity and amendments 
Only two amendments were made to the OD scheme from its birth in 1999 up to the era 
starting with the reform in 2018. The first was the “først-til-mølle” amendment in 2008, 
which was intended to solve the “lack of clarity around whether the minister would start 
an auction” following the reception of an application (Lov Om Ændring Af Lov Om 
Elforsyning, Lov Om Naturgasforsyning, Lov Om Varmeforsyning Og Lov Om 
Energinet.Dk, 2008). This amendment was seen purely as a minor clarification making 
the process more efficient. It was hardly debated in the democratic process prior to the 
amendment8, and does thus hardly classify as a re-problematization. It was specified that 
the first coming application for a specific area would secure the applicant the sole right 
to pre-investigate it for a limited period only. The description of the proposal as first set 
forth in the Danish Parliament states that on one hand, the clarification was ”[…] assumed 
to stimulate the investigation of the options for development and extraction of the energy 
at sea […]”. And further, ”on the other hand, the conditions and deadlines of the proposal

expectations into the policy, specifically by placing more emphasis on the agency of “the 
market”, underlining the market-based nature of the scheme. 
The second amendment came in 2017 in the aftermath of the case work on two OD 
applications in 2015, which received resistance from residents and owners of vacation 
homes on the coastlines in the vicinity of the projects (Lov Om Ændring Af Lov Om Om 
Fremme Af Vedvarende Energi  (Kommunal Indsigelsesret) [...], 2016). In these cases, 
the municipality boards had wished for a bigger say in the assessment, and the amendment 
ensured an affected municipality the right to object a project pending a pre-investigation

municipal shore. In addition to placing more influence with the municipalities with 
regards to the case processing, the projects falling within the 8 km perimeter were also 
included in the so-called “grøn ordning”, a scheme designed to redirecting a share of the 
income from the power sales from onshore wind farms to the municipality in which they 
are placed, as well as the “køberetsordning”, which mandated the developer to offer 20% 
of the ownership for sale for affected citizens and vacation home owners to buy (Lov Om 
Ændring Af Lov Om Om Fremme Af Vedvarende Energi  (Kommunal Indsigelsesret) 
[...], 2016). During the 1st processing of the amendment in the Danish Parliament, the 
following retrospective interpretation of the development of the scheme was given by 
climate spokesman Jens Joel (S):  
“I think that one of the problems that has arisen is that it luckily has 
become so cheap to establish this [nearshore wind farms], but that – at 
the time it was made [the OD scheme] – no structure for managing it 
[the scheme] was made, because ‘it would probably never become that 
cheap’ [to establish nearshore wind farms].” (Jens Joel (S), 
(Folketinget, 2016) ) 
In relation to this, these amendments to the scheme seem tied to the development of the 
six projects applications that came through the OD in the “second wave” between 2010 
==PAGE:49==

and 2017, whose characteristics can be interpreted as representing the development and 
significant cost decrease in turbine technology and wind farm construction. They 
answered to the increasing prospect of competitive project suggestions from private 
developers by adding more structure and management of the scheme. In short, this can be 
interpreted as a political realization of the need to create explicit structure for this policy, 
which had so far only carried the characteristics that the previous projects had given it. 
Specifically, these inscriptions represented problematization of the characteristics of the 
second wave in an effort to reestablish some of the lacking public connection and benefit 
that the “secondary objectives” of the first wave of projects represented and had indirectly 
inscribed into the policy, e.g., with the “køberetsordning” mimicking the public 
ownership of the ‘laug’.  
It is also found that the 8 km limit for the activation of these amendments were justified 
with the main concern not to bother citizens close to the shore, as ”[…] it has been 
assessed that turbines erected more than 8 km from the shore do not affect the coastline 
significantly.” (Lov Om Ændring Af Lov Om Om Fremme Af Vedvarende 
Energi (Kommunal Indsigelsesret) [...], 2016). The technical characteristics of the 
offshore wind turbine at that point in time thus acts as a technical device to influence the 
valuation of the seabed greatly. In addition to this, it seems that the amendments added 
some of the political inscriptions of onshore wind development onto the nearshore 
development, especially embodied by the expansion of “køberetsordningen” and “grøn 
ordning” to nearshore OD projects. Thus, semiotically, nearshore wind policy was tied 
closer to onshore wind policy with these amendments, and the 8 km perimeter limiting 
the effect of the legislation established a clear political valuation of the seabed, with the 
interpretation of what is nearshore, and what is offshore with the 8 km as the technical 
device inscribing this value.  
Seen from the developers’ side, it did not seem that the nearshore wind market and the 
access to it that the OD provided was particularly attractive in the 2010s, despite the 
encouraging debate on the topic in the Parliament. The verdict from the offshore wind 
manager in the Danish offshore developer was, that the scheme was seen as “niche”. At 
that time, the core markets were onshore wind and solar, where project development was 
==PAGE:50==

more mature and lead time of projects could be 2-3 years. In contrast, the nearshore 
projects were more expensive and estimated to have 5-10 years of lead time. 
“So, these factors made it this “niche-area”, where many thought, they 
didn’t want to participate, and then it was players like us, who are 
maybe more prone to go into immature markets […] who entered it [the 
OD].”(Offshore Manager) 
In addition, his view suggests that the OD also was a “niche product” with the DEA, as 
the case work with them and other authorities was not streamlined.  
“And nothing against the DEA for that, it was just their prioritizing, of 
course also because the portfolio [of OD projects] was so small. So, for 
all [involved actors], it [OD] was just something hanging out in the 
corner and taking a long time”. (Offshore Manager) 
Nonetheless, European Energy and HOFOR made the choice to step through the door 
with larger projects following the trend of time and technology. The ripples of the 
processing of these applications resulted in a re-problematization of the OD scheme with 
the significant amendments in 2017 to follow. These amendments semiotically tied the 
nearshore OD projects closer to onshore wind development, established that it was 
something that impacted the local area, and reaffirmed the necessity of some form of 
“public good” involved with the development of these projects.  
2018 energy agreement & enhanced political attention 
The attention that the OD scheme had received that culminated with the amendment in 
2017 carried into the work around the influential 2018 Energy Agreement. The analysis 
of the OD scheme released by DEA in March 2021 was “motivated by the realization that 
the conditions around the OD had changed significantly since it was introduced with the 
1999 electricity supply reform”:  
“A series of political decisions have been made on the establishment of 
large public auctions for offshore wind, and latest on the establishment 
of energy islands. […] Finally, the applications received under the 
scheme [OD] in the latest years differ significantly from previously, in 
==PAGE:51==
45 
 
the sense that the applications concern continuously bigger and not 
necessarily nearshore projects.” (Energistyrelsen, 2021a) 
Thus, what this analysis presented yet another re-problematization of the scheme, this 
time with an even clearer intent; the problematization of the trend of continuously bigger 
farms moving further away from the shore, and thus further away from its previous 
inscription and precedence. This concurs with summary of the characteristics of the 
“second wave” of projects from 2010-2017, and the trend was only continued with the 
two applications that came in 2019 – officially the only two between 2017-2021 – from 
private developer, Wind Estate, for projects rated at ~250 MW at 4 km from nearest shore 
and 500 MW at 13 km from nearest shore, respectively (Energistyrelsen, n.d.-a, 2023b).  
In addition, the analysis boasted several reasons why a revision of the scheme was due 
time (Energistyrelsen, 2021a). The first mention is that: 
“The price of offshore wind has decreased significantly, and as stated 
in Energiaftalen 2018 it must in the future be possible to erect offshore 
wind farms without state subsidies. Projects realized through the OD 
have the option to get subsidies, and the state has no option to generate 
revenue on these projects.” (Energistyrelsen, 2021a) 
With this direct mention of the phrasing in the 2018 Energiaftale of the changing future 
of offshore wind, the OD scheme became directly tied to this overall political offshore 
wind ambition that also applied to Thor. Furthermore, it mentions the fact that the OD 
projects leave the state with no option to generate revenue off the projects – an issue that 
had never been verbalized before in relation the OD scheme but had just been 
incorporated into the auction conditions for Thor. 
The conclusion of the analysis was, in short, that the OD either had to be shut or modified 
significantly as to “better suit the political goals and handle a series of identified 
challenges” (Energistyrelsen, 2021a), indicating the need for further inscriptions to and 
more strict framing of the OD.

This resulted in the amendment that was announced to come into effect on July 1st 2022, 
as mentioned in the Chapter 2. It was stated in the amendment agreement from summer 
2021 that it would:  
“[…] bring the scheme back to its original purposes, and keep the door 
open for smaller nearshore projects that can get local backing.” (Aftale 
Om Justering Af Åben Dør-Ordningen for VE-Anlæg På Havet Af 2.

With this short statement, it is implied that a) the recent applications through the OD 
represent a deviation of the original purpose, which b) was to procure small, nearshore 
wind farms that c) had local backing. These purposes are in line with the findings 
presented in the previous sections on the scripting, de-scripting and re-scripting that 
occurred with and in between the first and second waves of the OD projects, and they 
seem to have solidified as the overall political interpretation of the scheme at the point in 
time for this agreement.   
While the agreement states five key amendments to the OD scheme, three of them are 
particularly relevant to its ‘re-scription’. The first is the proclamation of “Clear framings 
for the scheme”. In the legal amendment, this goal was essentially translated into a valid 
reason for the Minister to reject incoming applications. It stated that: 
“Permissions for pre-investigation are given for areas where the 
minister finds that extraction of energy could be relevant, unless there 
are important considerations decisively against the authorization, 
including important environment, safety, defense & emergency, 
shipping, aviation, fishery, larger infrastructure projects and overall 
planning and socio-economic considerations.” ((Lov Om Ændring Af 
Lov Om Fremme Af Vedvarende Energi, 2022), §22 stk. 7) 
where the latter part of the sentence written in bold is the addition in the amendment. This 
amendment showcases a stark contrast between the lack of initiative for defining the 
nature of the projects before the amendment and the indication of a sovereign right to 
reject almost any project should it conflict with national political agendas or concerns 
(Aftale Om Justering Af Åben Dør-Ordningen for VE-Anlæg På Havet Af 2. Juni 2021,

2021; Lov Om Ændring Af Lov Om Fremme Af Vedvarende Energi, 2022). The second 
amendment introduced that for an application to be eligible for consideration, it had to be 
within 15 km of the shore. This was a tangible inscription underlining the definition of a 
nearshore project and making the scheme restrictive to adhere to this definition. In line 
with the second amendment, the third increased the perimeter for municipal right to object 
from 8 to 15 km but also changed that right to be a veto right. Again, this was a significant 
clarification and tightening of the inscription for the OD scheme, indicating how 
important the local cooperation and backing should be for the project development by 
ensuring it would apply for all eligible projects. All in all, this represents a tightening of 
the amendments from 2017 but with two different problematizations, as the 2017 
amendment focused on ensuring the local co-development and benefits, while the latter 
problematized the OD in the context of nation offshore wind policy objectives. 
To conclude on these findings, from having close to no inscription, the OD was re-
problematized iteratively by the Parliament, to a large degree triggered by the nature of 
its “own developments”, i.e. the de-scriptions embodied in the waves application activity, 
which finally resulted in a much more restrictive set of inscriptions. The below table 
summarizes these inscriptions of the OD scheme – both from when it was declared in

July 1st amendment.

embedded in the 2022 represents a clear distinction between “nearshore wind” and 
“offshore wind”, valued through the 15 km device, and representing the separation of the

2022, it was clear that the OD policy represented a different version of “the good offshore 
wind economy” than that of the Thor tender, by inscribing the values of the public and 
local benefit of offshore wind correlated with the local co-development of small, 
nearshore wind farms, thus detaching it from the national policy goals of deploying large 
capacities of offshore wind.

Overflowing and entanglement of the schemes 
RQ2: By comparing the case studies and using Actor-Network-Theory, how can the 
unforeseen developments of the two schemes (the Thor zero-bids and the “third 
wave” of OD applications) be understood as overflowing and entanglement of the 
schemes? 
This section focuses on the deviations from the scripts of the two policy schemes that 
occurred with the conclusion of Thor and with the “third wave” of applications that came 
through the OD in Spring 2022. It uses the empirical findings with the ANT terms de-
scripting and overflowing to illustrate how the dynamics contested the policies and 
underline the entanglement between different policy objectives and the devices of their 
market agencements.  
Overflowing in the outcome of Thor 
As was shown, the outcome of the auction stands in sharp contrast to the market dialogue 
phase in which both developers and DEA explicitly talked about high costs, high risks, 
and the expectation of higher bid prices. In the eyes of the Danish offshore developer, 
who was part of one of the consortia participating in the Thor auction, it was just “that 
good a case”. Asked whether the added responsibility of grid connection and substation 
works added much risk and uncertainty, the Offshore Manager responded: 
“No, I think it was just viewed as economy. I mean, it [the costs of 
grid connection] was just included in the case work, and the case was 
just that good, and it just swallowed it [the costs].” (Offshore 
Manager) 
In discussing whether adding other criteria than the price criteria would have changed the 
situation with regards to zero-bidding, he pointed out that 
“They [concession winners RWE] have also announced that a big part 
of the towers is being constructed with green steel. That is on a 
==PAGE:56==
50 
 
voluntary basis. You wouldn’t necessarily do that if the case was 
completely underwater.” (Offshore Manager) 
further underlining the high value assigned to the Thor site by the developers. The costs 
are notoriously hard to estimate as they er very site specific and ride on many 
uncertainties, but as stated in the 2021 IRENA report on renewable power generation 
costs, newly commissioned projects had a decrease of 29% in LCOE just from 2020 to 
2021 (IRENA, 2022) – that is, right in the period when Thor was underway in the auction 
process. Even so, the outcome can be seen as the developers in market agencement de-
scripting the auction design differently than was scripted, thus to a degree breaking the 
script by bidding zero, and that the market dialogue phase of the auction designed to align 
the views on the auction conditions from the policy maker and the bidders to a degree 
failed to do so.  
The outcome was branded as a large success for the state (Energistyrelsen, 2021b), as 
Thor officially would be the first offshore wind farm to be procured subsidy free through 
a Danish auction, and it would even realize the vision of the 2018 energy policy of 
generating revenues for the state. However, the zero-bids, labelled the “sharp price” by 
the director of DEA (Energistyrelsen, 2021b) can still be seen as an overflowing of the 
auction’s design and framing. First, the CfD model as the economic device created a 
valuation of the zero-bid for the state which was no better than any bid in the large 
“capped” price range from 0 to ~41.25 øre/kWh would be, and the state therefore 
potentially missed out on even larger revenues. That also meant that the real “willingness 
to pay” from the developers remains unknown, as per the function of the cap. Second, as 
formulated by Jansen et al., 2022, the Thor auction with its CfD model and bid price 
became a “defacto seabed lease”, with the price of 2.8 bn DKK, which is an entirely 
different approach and policy scheme for activating seabed for offshore wind in itself.

To summarize, two “paths” of political infrastructure for procuring offshore wind power 
were created in 1999. One, the auction path, that was thoroughly inscribed from the 
beginning, leading to a clear picture of its users, goals, and its view of “the good offshore 
wind economy”. However, with the latest iteration of the auction, namely Thor, the 
tweaked CfD-remuneration model resulted in an overflowing of the script of the auction 
in the sense of an unexpected outcome that leads to new perspectives of what the good 
offshore wind economy can be for the Danish state. 
The other, the OD path, whose initial inscription was merely to be an alternative, with the 
expectation that the projects would contrast those that were procured through the auction. 
After the first “wave” the OD had gained a form of “framing”, constituted not by law, but 
by its realized outcome, that supported this expectation. However, the OD “path” was in 
multiple phases contested through the dynamics of its market assemblage, and with the 
third wave, a new configuration of the market assemblage containing different types of 
users contested the scheme with applications resembling the characteristics of the 
offshore wind farms being procured through the auction. This development was partly 
facilitated by technical and economic devices attaching a new valuation to the scheme as 
seen from the developer’s perspective, embodied in the potential of digital screening and 
demarcation of the seabed and its valuation by the state as well the rise of the new business 
prospect and national policy agenda that is PtX.

Entanglement - how the Thor auction affected the third wave of OD applications  
By July 1st 2022 the DEA had 12 GW worth of valid applications for the construction of 
offshore wind farms on their desk, coming through the OD scheme right before it was 
amended. In contrast, “at least 4 GW” was the envisioned capacity for auctioning by 2030, 
as expressed in the 2022 climate agreement (Klimaaftale Om Grøn Strøm Og Varme 2022, 
2022). In this light, the overflowing of the auction scheme embodied in the outcome of 
the Thor auction has nowhere near as large implications as the overflowing of the OD 
scheme happening in Spring 2022. However, the events of the Thor auction might have 
had an effect on the third wave of OD applications, with the conclusion of Thor occurring 
in December 2021, just months before. This section explores the entanglement of the two 
policies and market assemblages. 
Political ambitions in Denmark 
For an auction to be carried out successfully with the desired outcomes, sufficient 
participation and thereby competition is required. The first aspect concerns the general 
increase in the interest for developing offshore wind in Denmark. Starting already with 
the 2018 Energy Agreement, the Danish state were promoting the future prospect of wind 
power in Nordsøen, which was to become “a globally leading region for offshore wind” 
(Energiaftale, 2018). This political discourse was only intensified with the 2020 and 2022 
energy agreements, continuously pushing the ambition level higher. The 2020 proclaimed 
the construction of two pioneering “energy islands” with the associated prospect of the 
construction of 5-12 GW of offshore wind, and the 2022 agreement eyes the potential of 
up to 35 GW in the Danish part of the North Sea with the aim of being exporters of green 
power to Europe (Klimaaftale for Energi Og Industri Mv. 2020, 2020; Klimaaftale Om 
Grøn Strøm Og Varme 2022, 2022).  
Valuation of Danish offshore wind following Thor  
The outcome of the Thor auction also increased the attention on Danish offshore wind. 
The zero-bidding and willingness to pay the state for the concession conveyed an image 
of a site with an enormous value – a value which was only enhanced by the cap of 
payments to the state inscribed into the economic valuation device that most likely 
represented a lower bound to the willingness to pay.

In a sense, these quotes indicate that what happened could be interpreted as a Klondyke-
effect. If someone finds gold, and attention is raised about it, others will follow and start 
digging. While the attention on Danish offshore wind can be seen as a good thing and a 
benefit to the government and their long-term strategies of turning the North Sea into a 
globally leading offshore region, the entanglements of the Thor auction and the following 
rush of OD applications has created a large disruption of these plans and a series of 
challenges to handle, as the 12 GW of OD applications do not comply with the visions 
and goals of the energy agreements.  
Entanglement between “offshore wind economies” 
The Offshore Manager of the Developer does not think that there will be that many cases 
as good as Thor, which he calls a “high quality” case with reference to both its site-
specific attributes but also the degree to which DEA had performed pre-investigations 
and prepared the site for the auction. And with all the entanglements and overflowing on 
the OD scheme happening in the wake of Thor, making the OD projects of the Danish 
offshore developer very uncertain, he simply states. 
“And that why I say, it’s a pity it all started with Thor.”  
With reference to the set of three auctions planned by the government with the 2018 
Energy Agreement. The development might have looked entirely different had another 
site kicked things off that didn’t produce such a pure case. 
Asked whether the fact that both the auctions and the OD schemes have been active 
offshore wind legislations had made the political agenda on offshore wind less clear, the 
Offshore Manager says,  
==PAGE:66==
60 
 
“Not really. Until recently, when things went sideways, they functioned 
in parallel and were as such complementing each other. […] we had 
many applications ourselves […] but their characteristics were so 
different that I don’t think they conflicted [with the national agenda]. 
But when the big projects came in, that is, those of CIP and CE 
[Copenhagen Energy], this is here, in my opinion, where it [the 
balance] gets tipped, where there is a spillover.” (Offshore Manager) 
Thus, from the perspective of a developer, whose intention it is to follow the inscriptions 
of the OD, the two schemes are viewed as having their separate justification and 
objectives, which underlines the findings of the previous sections. The overflowing only 
happened with the applications in Group 2 of the third wave.  
When engaging in questioning on whether the OD scheme can be adapted again, the 
Offshore Manager interrupts and proclaims: 
“The scheme is dead. The scheme is dead. […] I mean, it does not exist 
anymore, there are the old projects [from the second wave and the new 
ones from the third wave before July 1st] and it’s about how do they 
handle those. […] There isn’t space for more. So, there won’t be any 
more OD in Denmark.” (Offshore Manager) 
With this, the severe consequences of the overflowing of the OD scheme for the future 
Danish offshore development was underlined, and the state faces a grand challenge in 
handling these 12 GW of OD applications. 
Chapter 6 – Discussion  
The methodology and its strengths and weaknesses 
This study has illustrated how a specific offshore wind policy instrument can be 
interpreted by identifying its “inscription”. This was done by analyzing empirical data 
that describes the dynamics between the agents in the specific market assemblage 
revolving in the political environment of the instrument, and categorize these findings 
into five parts, namely the “script”, the “problematization”, the “users”, the “valuation of 
==PAGE:67==
61 
 
offshore wind and the seabed”, and “the good offshore wind economy” that together 
constituted the inscription. For the purpose of creating as much meaning as possible in 
each of the categories, the ANT-based document analysis was complemented by a more 
economical-based analysis of the tender, to pick a part the economic valuation device of 
the policy and its economic implications. In addition, the “insider’s view” coming from a 
private developer who is a “user” of both schemes was applied to represent the 
interpretations of the policy in its user “milieu”.  
A key enabler of the analysis of these inscriptions was to choose a point in time, at which 
the dynamics had resulted in a certain “state” – for the Thor auction its conclusion with 
the lottery draw in December 2021, and for the ‘open door’ policy the entering into force 
of the amendment of July 1st 2022. It is in this framing that the strength of this chosen 
methodology lies; that the in-depth retrospective empirical analysis of the entire history 
or “use phase” of the policies undertaken is able to enrich the understanding of exactly 
these “states” at those points in time of the two schemes while also understanding the 
previous dynamics in their ‘wake’ that led to define them. In that sense, the points in time 
could be moved backwards in the entire span between 1999 and 2021/2022 to achieve the 
same insights, as the inscription is always an iterative process of dynamics between the 
policy instrument and the agents in its milieu.  
However, the dynamics of the two schemes did not stop at the chosen points in time with 
these events. As per definition of a dynamic system, the agents in the specific milieu of 
the policies continuously “act”, and as shown throughout the analysis, the motivations for 
these actions can be many and varied and be a result of dynamics in other entangled 
systems or market assemblages. In this fact lies also the more embedded limitation of the 
approach; that it is “snapshot” of a certain state of the policies defined by its past, which 
gives it limited relevance for predicting the future developments or directly indicate 
guidelines of action moving forward. In that sense, the same attributes making the 
analysis suitable for understanding the past and present also makes it unsuitable for 
assessing the future or providing a solution to the problem that the entanglements 
represent.  
==PAGE:68==
62 
 
Contemporary developments 
The policies under investigation in this thesis happen to currently be two of the most 
debated schemes within Danish energy policy, which is not surprising considering the 
large degree of contestation and overflowing occurring during the events explored in this 
thesis. And just very recently before the completion of this thesis, the government 
announced agreements and initiatives disrupting the two schemes completely.  
First, it was announced that a total of 6 GW of new offshore wind farms would be offered 
for auctioning with a completely revised scheme, where the participating developers 
would bid on how much they would pay the state for the concession. In addition, the state 
would become a co-owner of all the parks with a share of 20% (KEFM, 2023).  
Second, it was announced that a new revision of the Danish “Havplan” would result in 
the rejection of all open door-applications pending pre-investigation permit except three 
(Kærgaard, 2023).  
Relishing the retrospective strength of the methodology and setting today as the current 
“state”, a tentative attempt to correlate the findings in this thesis with these newest 
developments can be given. For the auctions, the extinction of subsidies is definitive for 
the coming 6 GW, which implies that the state now sees offshore wind power as fully 
competitive, thus completing the long-term political objective of the auctions to 
eventually render subsidies unnecessary. Instead, the scheme seems to turn into actual 
auctions for the seabed, underlining and augmenting the valuation of the seabed as an 
asset and resource for the state. The degree of disruption of the OD with the new Havplan 
policy fits well with the degree of contestation and overflowing happening with its third 
wave of applications. With the now de-facto closing of the OD, it was true what the 
Offshore Manager of the Danish developer proclaimed, namely that the scheme was dead 
by July 1st, 2022. While many other developments, events, and dynamics during the last 
year would have to be analyzed in line with the methodology to provide a sound 
conclusion of the new inscriptions of the two policies and its relation to the findings in 
the scope of this thesis, the first indications seem to confirm how the policy schemes 
incrementally develop with a strong dependency of the iterative rounds of inscription and 
==PAGE:69==
63 
 
de-scription of its past. A study moving the point in time to this current new “state”, would 
be relevant future work. 
Chapter 7 – Conclusion 
This paper presents a study of the two policy schemes for offshore wind procurement that 
have been active in Denmark since 1999, namely the auction scheme and the ‘open door’ 
policy. Through the lens of ANT and valuation studies, it investigates them as two 
different instantiations of “the good offshore wind economy” by curating their 
inscriptions, as they were empirically found through a thorough research of their long use 
phase, shedding light on their characteristics under the additional terms, “script”, 
“problematization”, “user”, “valuation of offshore wind and the seabed”. It finds that the 
Thor auction, while inscribing the same overall policy goals of deploying large capacities 
of offshore wind in a timely manner, differed from the previous inscriptions of the auction 
scheme by embodying an altered instantiation of the “good offshore wind economy” and 
valuation of the seabed – one where revenues for the state enter the equation – primarily 
embodied in the economic device that is its revised CfD-remuneration model.  
Even with the altered inscriptions of the good offshore wind economy embodied in the 
design of Thor to accommodate the visions of the 2018 Energy Agreement, the outcome 
of the Thor tender with the many zero-bids can be seen as an overflowing of the scheme. 
The 2.8 bn DKK cap of the payments from developer to state built into the two-sided CfD 
model stalled the valuation of the Thor site in the bid price interval of 0-41.25 øre/kWh, 
enabling the bidders to “race to the bottom” without showing their true willingness to pay 
for the seabed.  
Then, the paper finds that the OD policy with its 2022 amendment, after multiple 
iterations of problematization, scripting, and de-scripting in the form of waves of 
applications and policy amendments, had developed another instantiation of the “good 
offshore wind economy”. One that roots the “good” of the scheme in nearshore parts of 
the sea through the device of a 15-km distance from shore limitation to applications while 
embodying the value of local benefits of nearshore through the attachments of onshore 
wind policy instruments to the OD projects, thus clearly separating it from the inscription 
of the auction scheme. 
==PAGE:70==
64 
 
While this inscription was becoming legally binding through democratic processing 
during Spring 2022, a third wave of OD applications overflowed the scheme in a race to 
secure first-mover access to investigate seabeds for offshore wind. The third was triggered 
by several factors, including by the maturing of the market assemblage for developing 
PtX and the application of GIS-software as a technical device allowing the distinction of 
the valuation of the seabed inscribed by the state to justify OD applications. In addition, 
it might have been helped on its way by the developments of the Thor tender, through 
which larger players have gained an impression of Danish offshore wind as being 
extremely valuable, underlined by the positive willingness to pay. The timing of the 
applications coming in right before the closing of the open door might indicate a 
Klondyke-effect, where developers finding gold – along with those who didn’t – will 
expand their search for more. In that light, rushing through an open door with access to 
free seabed, after just recently seeing someone pay 2.8 bn DKK for it seems worth it. 
However, this situation now presents a problem for the Danish state who now have to 
handle 12 GW of capacity applied for through the OD scheme while still finding room 
for its ambition to initiate 4 GW of tenders in the coming years. 
While the strength of the applied methodology lies in its flexible retrospective analysis, 
that enables moving the focal point in time backwards through the history of the schemes 
to create an exhaustive understanding of their inscriptions, the limitations lie in the fact 
that the schemes are dynamic and still developing, which limits its applicability for 
drawing conclusions and learnings on the future. However, come a certain point in time 
in the future, where new developments occur that disrupts Danish offshore wind policy 
again, this thesis can act as the background to enable the understanding of these new 
developments in another retrospective analysis.
