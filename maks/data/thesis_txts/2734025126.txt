Abstract
In today’s rapidly evolving business landscape, the adoption and implementation of Artifi-
cial Intelligence (AI) stands as a critical frontier. This transformative technology is reshap-
ing industries, driving innovation, and creating new operational paradigms. As businesses
navigate the complexities of adopting AI, they encounter a landscape rich in technologi-
cal and theoretical knowledge but often lacking in practical guidance. This gap between
theory and practice presents unique challenges and opportunities, underscoring the need
for actionable strategies that align with the rapid technological advancements in AI.
This thesis explores the enhancement of AI implementation with a focus on Small and
Medium-sized Enterprises (SMEs) within the European Union. The study contributes to
the field in two significant ways: firstly, through a comprehensive literature review of exist-
ing research on AI implementation, and secondly, by developing five practical frameworks
aimed at demystifying the process of AI implementation and advancing the overall under-
standing in this area.
The five proposed frameworks are grounded in insights from current literature and fur-
ther refined through interviews with five professionals experienced in AI implementation.
These frameworks are designed to facilitate discussions between AI consultants and busi-
ness decision-makers, providing a strategic, rather than technical, approach to AI adop-
tion. The first framework offers a top-level overview of the AI implementation process,
while the subsequent four target specific aspects of adoption: guiding initial AI projects,
determining automation levels, addressing risks and barriers, and executing pilot projects.
My research identifies six potential barriers to AI implementation: 1) Lack of qualifications
and training, 2) Limited financial resources, 3) Concerns about operational timeframes
and ROI, 4) Inadequate data quantity and quality, 5) Data governance and legal com-
pliance issues, and 6) A general lack of awareness or vision of AI’s potential. Addition-
ally, eight success factors were recognized, facilitating AI implementation: 1) Building a
strong business case, 2) Effective data management and analytics, 3) Top management
support and solid infrastructure, 4) Employee involvement, 5) Establishing trust in AI sys-
tems, 6) Adhering to AI ethics, 7) Implementing robust risk management strategies, and
8) Thoughtful design and deployment of AI solutions.
The academic literature predominantly focuses on theoretical aspects and ideas about AI,
with a noticeable gap in practical application. This thesis fills this gap, offering frameworks
that, while general and theoretical, are rooted in practical insights from industry interviews.
These frameworks are intended as a foundation for further development and can be tai-
lored to specific industries, given their broad and general nature. The rapid evolution of
AI technology, particularly generative AI, highlights the need for ongoing adaptation and
refinement of these frameworks to stay relevant and effective in the face of continuous
technological advancements.

Introduction
Artificial Intelligence is a transformative technology that holds the potential to revolutionize
the modern economic landscape. A report by PwC anticipates AI’s contribution to be as
much as $15.7 trillion to the global GDP by 2030, showcasing its unparalleled commercial
opportunity in the evolving economy (Dr. Anand S. Rao and Gerard Verweij, 2017).
Historically, the benefits of AI were perceived to be within the exclusive domain of tech
giants like Google, Amazon, and Tesla etc. who had the data to leverage AI. Yet, today,
AI is fortified by data and technologies through, Open sources AI, of-the-shelf solutions
AI for data gathering tools as well as by refined methodologies, and more accessible
high computing capabilities. This evolution has made AI a promising and accessible tool
for strategic initiatives of also smaller and resource-limited companies. The landscape
has changed dramatically with initiatives like OpenAI’s ChatGPT launch November 2022,
which have democratized AI advantages, allowing even smaller, resource-limited compa-
nies to explore AI adoption.
The magnitude of AI’s impact the recent years have been so disruptive that many com-
pare its advancements to historical disruptive innovations such as discovery of electricity
(Anthony Williams, 2018) and the Steam Engine (McKinsey & Company, 2022). This
sentiment is also reinforced by industry leaders like the CEO of Google, who envisions a
world transitioning to be ”AI-first” in the coming decade (Pichai Sundar, 2017).
The competitive edge provided by AI is multifold, notably by swifter decision-making,
heightened efficiency, cost reductions, enhanced productivity, and Superior quality (Polisetty
et al., 2023). AI also can fortify a company’s workforce as well as it can increase flexibil-
ity, task execution, individualized information, and alleviation from monotonous cognitive
tasks if introduced correctly (Stowasser, 2023).
1.0.1
AI Adoption in EU Enterprises
Small and Medium-sized Enterprises (SMEs) are predominant in Europe, employing 100
million individuals and contributing to more than half of the GDP across all sectors in EU
(Watney and Auer, 2021). However, the journey from aspiring to integrate AI to actual
implementation is fraught with challenges especially for this type of enterprises. Despite
the rise of open-source AI solutions easing access to sophisticated technologies, SMEs
in EU presently are still categorized as slow AI adopters (Wei and Pardo, 2022)
IBM’s survey indicates the top factor an increase in AI adoption are, that ”advancements
in AI making it more accessible” (IBM, 2022). However, accurate data on AI adoption
among SMEs is limited, and the term ’AI’ is interpreted variably across different sources.
For this thesis, AI refers to machines simulating human intelligence, capable of tasks
requiring human cognition, this will be further elaborated on in the next chapter.
Despite AI’s potential, many companies exhibit reluctance towards its adoption. In 2021,
only 28% of EU large enterprises and 8% of EU SMEs utilized AI technologies (Euro Stat,
2022). This is an interesting point, especially considering the immense potential benefits
of successfully implemented AI, as demonstrated by a study on AI implementation that re-
ported a 91% increase in success among small businesses (Huang and Rust, 2018). This
thesis therefore aims to explore the challenges hindering SMEs from AI adoption, thereby
developing a range of frameworks which could help facilitating a more rapid integration of
AI in companies.

1.1
Motivation and Problem Definition
This thesis is both driven by my own passion and interest for AI and my practical involve-
ment in an AI startup working with AI implementation. As well as the academic motivation
of addressing the gap in current academic literature regarding AI adoption. Despite the
evolution of AI technology, making it more accessible, SMEs still lag behind larger en-
terprises in adopting AI. This work aims to explore and mitigate these potential adoption
barriers, drawing on empirical data from my own experience as well as academic re-
search. It focuses on identifying and overcoming challenges to facilitate more effective
and widespread AI implementation.
1.1.1
Problem Questions
Given the above, this project will address the following research questions:
• RQ1: What are the common barriers and success factors in AI implementation for
SMEs as identified in current literature?
• RQ2: What relevant frameworks already exist to facilitate AI implementation in
SMEs
• RQ3: How can the insights acquired from the literature review be integrated into
new practical frameworks for AI implementation?
1.2
Purpose statement
This thesis purpose is to contribute in two significant ways: initially, it will explore the
various factors and preconditions influencing SMEs’ AI adoption from a theoretical stand-
point. Subsequently, it endeavors to develop practical frameworks that can demystify the
AI implementation progress and help making this process more accessible.
1.3
Structure of the Thesis
Theory This section commences with a concise preliminary study, introducing the concept
of AI as it pertains to this thesis. As well as a thorough literature review follows, to address
research questions 1 and 2.
Methodology The methodology, will be divided into two distinct parts. The first part will
be presented in the beginning of the literature review explaining methodology to address
research questions 1 and 2. Following the literature review, the second part of the method-

question 3. This part will describe in detail how insights derived from the literature review

Conclusion The concluding section synthesizes the main discoveries derived from the
literature review and the application of frameworks in relation to the research questions.
Finally, this thesis is concluded with a number of proposals for potential directions for
future research.

2.1
Artificial Intelligence
In scientific discussions, clear terminology is essential. Without a unified understanding
of ’Artificial Intelligence’, there’s a potential for misunderstandings. This section aims to
clarify the various definitions of AI found in literature and set a clear definition for the
purpose of this thesis.
This section begins with a brief overview of AI’s historical development, followed by an
exploration of the diverse definitions of AI in contemporary discourse. Subsequently, it
categorizes AI technologies and their real-world applications. Finally, a very short defini-
tion of SMEs is established.
2.1.1
Ai background
The concept of ”intelligence” is inherently broad and its exact definition remains a topic of
debate. However, the primary goal for AI has remained consistent: to create machines
that mimic human thinking and actions. The term ”Artificial Intelligence” was first intro-
duced in 1956 by John McCarthy at a conference at Dartmouth College (Drave et al.,
2021). Since then, both the understanding and practical applications of the term AI have
grown significantly. Early AI systems, which were largely logic-driven, might involve rules
like ”IF it rains THEN carry an umbrella.” These rudimentary systems were revolution-
ary for their time as before 1949 computers lacked the capabilities of being able to store
commands, they could only execute them (Rockwell Anyoha, 2017). But when compared
to the advanced AI models of 2023, these early AI systems seem elementary in their
approach. Nevertheless, even today, simple inductive logic programming (ILP) such as
the aforementioned example, is still considered a part of AI, though many outside the
data science community might not equate such basic logic rules with the sophisticated AI
available today (Zhang et al., 2023; Utgoff et al., 2011).
Recent breakthroughs and milestones in AI, such as the development of advanced lan-
guage models and their applications in natural language understanding, image recog-
nition, and autonomous systems, have propelled the field into new realms of possibility.
These developments have reshaped our perception of AI’s capabilities, making it essential
to appreciate the rapid advancements that continue to redefine the boundaries of artificial
intelligence (Lasse Skytt, 2023).
2.1.2
Various definitions of AI
In order to define AI for this thesis, a selection of definitions from recognized and author-
itative sources is presented. This approach ensures a well-rounded understanding and
provides a solid foundation for the specific definition that will be adopted.
The first definition of AI is from John McCarthy in 1956 with the concept of ”Thinking Ma-
chines” as: ”a sub-branch of Computer Science and Engineering that designs controlled
actions for machines and devices similar to human interpretation and replicate its char-
acteristics” (Drave et al., 2021)
From a report made to inform about AI, PwC defines AI in 2017 as ”In our broad definition,
AI is a collective term for computer systems that can sense their environment, think, learn,

and take action in response to what they’re sensing and their objectives.” (Dr. Anand S.

In an article posted by IBM called ”What is artificial intelligence” they define artificial intel-
ligence as “At its simplest form, artificial intelligence is a field, which combines computer
science and robust datasets, to enable problem-solving” (IBM, n.d.)
Stuart Russel and Peter Norvig, who wrote on the leading textbooks in the study of AI,

Finally, a definition was published in 2019 by an independent expert group set up by the
European Commission in a report called ”A definition of AI: Main capabilities and scientific
disciplines” the gave AI the definition: “Artificial intelligence (AI) refers to systems that
display intelligent behavior by analyzing their environment and taking actions – with some
degree of autonomy – to achieve specific goals [...]” (AI HLEG Group, 2018)
From the provided definitions of AI, several key themes and characteristics of AI emerge.
AI, as described by various definitions, is rooted in computer science and engineer-
ing with the goal of replicating human-like characteristics, as highlighted by John Mc-
Carthy. PwC emphasizes AI’s capability to sense, think, learn, and act based on envi-
ronmental perceptions. IBM underscores the union of computer science and datasets
for problem-solving. Stuart Russel and Peter Norvig distinguish between systems that
act like humans and those driven by rationality. Lastly, the European Commission’s def-
inition accentuates AI’s intelligent behavior and its ability to operate with autonomy.
In essence, AI combines computational techniques, human emulation, rational decision-
making, and autonomous actions to solve problems and achieve goals.
2.1.3
Definition of AI
As a combination of the different points, this thesis when referring to AI: ”A field within
computer science that aims to create systems capable of emulating human-like charac-
teristics, learning from data, making rational decisions, and operating autonomously to
solve problems and achieve specific goals”
2.1.4
AI Technology and Application
A final definition of AI have now been established, however, it is evident that even with the
established textual definition, AI remains an ambiguous and broad term, with its compo-
nents open to interpretation. This kind of textual definition serves as an excellent starting
point and provides a solid foundation for general discussions about AI. Yet, to truly deepen
the understanding of AI, it can be beneficial to define AI by also distinguishing between
the underlying technologies it encompasses. AI acts as an umbrella term, covering a di-
verse range of technologies, each distinct in their applications and underlying technology.
For instance, the AI systems designed for autonomous vehicles differ greatly from those
developed to comprehend and transcribe human speech. These systems are specialized
and tailored to their specific functions, reflecting the versatility and adaptability of AI as
a field. Understanding this, it’s important to see how AI technologies are being used all
around us today.

masters language patterns, grammar, and context.

3.5.1
Success Factor 1 - Building a Strong Business Case
3.5.1.1
Value Perception & Expectations
Before bringing AI into an SME, it is important to understand what AI can do for the com-
pany and how it fits with its goals. This process involves separating the AI hype from its
actual capabilities and requires a high-level grasp of AI’s workings and to understand how
it differs from conventional technological approaches (Bughin et al., 2017). Before starting
the implementation of new AI technologies, it is thus imperative to identify the true source
of value that AI can bring to the SME and construct a comprehensive business case for
its adoption.

Haefner and Morf (2021) highlights that AI in general can create business value in three
primary ways:
1. To be able to make Better Decisions and thus be more effective and do the right
things.
2. To create New Products and Services with data, and connection technologies to
create new sources of revenue.
3. Optimal Processes: Connected, AI-based solutions are used to optimize business
and technical processes regarding speed and cost-efficiency.
A clear business case must be established, having well-defined use cases where the
benefits of AI technology can be easily verified, detailing the benefits expected for both
the company and its customers and employees (Bughin et al., 2017). It is vital to remain
focused on the business objectives rather than getting overly captivated by the AI tech-
nologies themselves (Stowasser et al., 2023; Taulli, 2019; Bunte, Richter, and Diovisalvi,
2021). Both short- and long-term roadmaps should therefore be created, outlining the
expected accomplishments at each stage of the AI project (Taulli, 2019). This is further
fortified as research highlights that a majority of companies struggle to move beyond the
proof of concept stage to full production, primarily due to a lack of clear deployment strate-
gies (Eitel-Porter, 2021). Furthermore, by shedding light on the implementation process,
businesses can establish realistic expectations about the initial impact of AI on their oper-
ations and fully leverage the potential benefits of the technology (Akerkar, 2019). Finally,
clear-cut KPIs should be established, and their progress must be diligently measured to
track the success of the AI initiative (Taulli, 2019).
3.5.1.2
Focus Areas for Initial AI Projects
SMEs typically face constraints in terms of resources and manpower. However, their
inherent agility and swift adaptability to new technologies (Hansen and Bøgh, 2021) posi-
tion them favorably to leverage AI, often resulting in benefits that far outweigh the efforts
invested (Bughin et al., 2017). According to (Taulli, 2019) book chapter ”Implementation
of AI Moving the Needle for Your Company” when deciding on the focus areas for initial
AI projects for the SME, he suggests to consider the following approaches:
• Quick Win: Implement AI swiftly in key areas for fast benefits, building momentum
and showcasing immediate value, particularly in projects with low entry barriers.
• Meaningful: Choose AI applications that significantly enhance business growth and
efficiency, aligning with core objectives for long-term impact.
• Industry-Specific Focus: Customize AI to meet unique industry challenges, en-
suring the technology addresses specific needs effectively.
• Data: Focus on areas with quality, accessible data. Reliable data is essential for
successful AI outcomes, making data-rich domains a priority for smoother imple-
mentation.

The SME’s readiness for AI can be significantly enhanced by adopting a collaborative,

SMEs must manage expectations regarding the timeframe for adoption and the time it
takes for larger AI projects to make a significant, positive impact as the transition to
leverage self-learning AI systems thus is gradual (Stowasser et al., 2023) and should
be viewed as a long process, not a sprint (Taulli, 2019). Furthermore focusing solely
on short-term ROI can obstruct long-term growth and the realization of AI’s full poten-

AI solutions (Bughin et al., 2017).The duration of implementing end-to-end AI solutions
varies considerably, influenced by a multitude of factors, according to sources such as
Warren Cope, a renowned founder of an AI consultancy in the USA, the journey from AI
adoption to full deployment and implementation generally spans a minimum of 18 months,
followed by continues monitoring and refinement of the AI taking place afterwards to truly
leverage AI (Warren Cope, 2023). In short, a shift in leadership approach, towards pa-
tience and an appreciation of AI’s capacity to open new business avenues, is essential
(Drave et al., 2021).

3.5.2.1
Data
Data is the lifeblood of AI and analytic applications. Essentially, an organization’s readi-
ness to engage in AI projects is contingent upon its data management capabilities, and
without a focus on data, AI is simply not possible (Drave et al., 2021; Haefner and Morf,

The importance of a data-driven approach is aptly captured by a quote from Akerkar in

The importance of data in AI extends beyond its initial collection; it plays a crucial role
in the overall effectiveness of AI applications. The way data is managed and maintained
significantly influences the outcomes of AI systems. According to Polisetty et al (2023).

elements are necessary to create a robust foundation for the successful implementation
of AI technologies.
The significance of the time aspect is growing in AI and to truly grasp people’s behavior
and identify patterns in their actions, businesses need to gather a vast amount of data
over an extended period, known as longitudinal data (Akerkar, 2019). However, having a
lot of data is not enough; the quality of the data is crucial, and if it is poor, it can lead to
incorrect conclusions when utilized by AI. As Haefner and Morf (2021) emphasizes, it is
vital to ensure that the data for the AI project is not only plentiful but also of high quality.
Additionally, we need to verify that the data is free from biases. This requires a careful
and thorough approach in the preparation and management of the data.

challenges organizations face. Such as ensuring data security, governance, managing
diverse data sources, and formats, and the technicalities of data integration across cloud
platforms, these challenges are clear impediments to smooth AI adoption (IBM, 2022).
3.5.2.2
Data maintenance
Haefner and Morf (2021) states that once AI systems are up and running, it is important to
keep them accurate and up-to-date by regularly checking and maintaining the data used.
Furthermore, they state that ’Model drift’ is a term used to describe the issues that come
up when AI data get outdated due to changes in the world, like a sudden pandemic or slow
shifts in what customers want. Drave et al. (2021) further builds upon this and elaborates
that if businesses don’t keep their AI’s data current, the AI can not do its job properly.
They state that many companies do not pay enough attention to this, and as a result, they
struggle to make AI a regular part of their business and fall behind their competitors. In
short, taking care of the AI’s data is a must-do, not just to keep it working well now, but
also to make sure it helps the business succeed in the long run.
3.5.2.3
Data Analytics
While data forms the bedrock for AI, how we interpret and utilize this data determines
the insights and value derived. As Akerkar (2019) points out, there are three core meth-
ods of handling data analytics: ”Descriptive, Predictive, and Prescriptive.” Each approach
provides a unique lens through which to view and act upon the information, shaping the
trajectory of data-driven decision-making.
• Descriptive Analytics: This is the most fundamental form of data analysis and
is often the first step in data-driven decision-making. It involves using structured
data to understand past behavior and events. Analyzing past data, especially in

large volumes, can help pinpoint anomalies, patterns, and deviations. The results
are straightforward and quick to obtain which include dashboards and sales reports
with information on budget, sales revenue, etc.
• Predictive Analytics: Going a step further from understanding what has happened,

requires a lot more data as well as longitudinal data for using more advanced sta-
tistical algorithms and machine learning techniques to ”detect patterns or relation-

potential”(Akerkar, 2019) identifying future outcomes based on historical data. Pre-
dictive analytics doesn’t guarantee that these outcomes will happen, but it provides
a probabilistic view of potential future scenarios. Examples include credit scoring
and sales forecasting.
• Prescriptive Analytics: As the most advanced form among the three, prescrip-

A common application of prescriptive analytics can be seen in supply chain opti-

constraints. This form of analytics is the least used as in the past it often required
the algorithms to run for hours or days.

Success Factor 3 - Top Management and Infrastructure
Multiple of the sources found in the literature review agree that effective and visionary
leadership is crucial in driving the adoption of new technologies like AI in SMEs. Leaders
play a pivotal role in setting the tone for innovation, readiness to embrace new technolo-

3.5.3.1
Visionary Leadership and Culture
Visionary leadership and management significantly enhance the adoption of new tech-
nologies in SMEs (Wei and Pardo, 2022; Kinkel, Baumgartner, and Cherubini, 2021).
Team leaders bear the responsibility to motivate employees, inculcate organizational ma-
turity to handle data-intensive projects, and proactively clear obstacles (Drave et al.,
2021). They also need to be transparent, have the ability to effectively communicate the
benefits of AI to all stakeholders, and to create a culture that encourages innovation and
experimentation (Mishra and Tripathi, 2021). Stowasser (2023) for instance suggests that
such a culture needs to be augmented with a corporate environment that actively supports
the use of AI technologies, ensuring that the organization is primed to benefit positively
from these advancements. This could involve providing the team and or company with
clear guidelines, processes for implementing new technologies, and a distinct adoption
strategy (Akerkar, 2019; Polisetty et al., 2023). Additionally, fostering a culture that facil-
itates collaboration between humans and AI is essential, with trust playing a pivotal role
in this symbiotic relationship (Bughin et al., 2017).
3.5.3.2
Compatibility
Furthermore the organizational structure, including its technological infrastructure, plays
a crucial role in the success of AI adoption. Success in this domain is influenced by a
variety of structural prerequisites, which extend beyond the technological characteristics
of AI (Kinkel, Baumgartner, and Cherubini, 2021). Ensuring that existing systems and in-
frastructure are compatible with AI technologies is a critical success factor, as it enables
the effective implementation of AI solutions (Polisetty et al., 2023). Moreover, SMEs must
invest in robust computing power and a solid infrastructure to support AI-enabled prod-
ucts and services. This also involves establishing platforms for data organization and
integration, which are vital for the optimal functionality of AI systems (Akerkar, 2019).

Success Factor 4 - Employee Involvement
3.5.4.1
AI Pre-Phase and Faulty Communication
Effective AI implementation starts with a clear and company-wide communication of the vi-
sion and rationale behind the adoption. This should elucidate the ’why’—the core reason
for incorporating AI—while proactively addressing any potential resistance or concerns
from the workforce (Haefner and Morf, 2021). Understanding and mitigating these con-
cerns is important for creating a conducive environment for AI adoption. If they are not
dealt with at the beginning of the project they can cause confusion and become a force
of opposition to the initiative (ibid).
Miscommunication both between management and employees but also between decision-
makers and AI experts is a common pitfall in AI adoption and is a misalignment in the un-
derstanding of organizational needs and AI capabilities between decision-makers and AI
developers (Beiner et al., 2023). Decision-makers tend to focus on organizational prob-
lems, seeking AI as a solution, while developers often approach with a solution-centric
mindset, seeking suitable applications for their AI solutions (ibid). Bridging this commu-
nication gap is especially crucial for external companies, such as AI consultants, for suc-
cessful AI implementation, as often they do not have the domain expert knowledge ac-
quired by working in the SME industry, which is required to know which AI solution would
prove most beneficial to the SME (ibid).

3.5.5.1
Fostering Trust in AI Decision-Making
AI systems, have lately emerged as powerful tools in decision-making processes across
varied domains. However, for these systems to be effective and beneficial, trustworthi-
ness becomes paramount (Zielke, 2020). Trustworthiness, pertains to the user’s con-
fidence that the AI system will behave as intended. More elaborately, it encompasses
the AI’s ability to perform specific tasks (encompassing robustness, safety, reliability) and
its integrity, ensuring that information is not maliciously manipulated by the AI system,

of the AI that a compelling dilemma for businesses today revolves around the choice be-
tween crafting complex systems that harness the capabilities of deep learning, or opting
for simpler AI systems, which are typically more comprehensible and hence more trusted
(Haefner and Morf, 2021). A comprehensible AI could as an example be supported by
the use of techniques like decision trees or conventional regression (Haefner and Morf,
2021).
Several factors can contribute to building trust in AI. For instance, while reliability is often
considered vital for building trust in an AI, its absence does not always deter trust either,

ethical AI requirements, ranging from human control over AI, reliability, data protection,
transparency, and fairness to accountability. The second foundation emphasizes the ac-
tual AI implementation. According to the authors it is pivotal that the AI implementation is
spearheaded by a seasoned AI expert(s), proficient in bridging technology, business, and
human experience. This expert often deemed a ”Data Consultants,” possess rich prac-
tical insights into AI use cases, pre-assesses feasibility, and economic viability, and are
acquainted with the critical success factors for concrete AI implementations. They further

3.5.5.2
Demystifying AI: The Role of Explainability
The landscape of AI techniques has expanded, with models like neural networks gaining
prominence. These models, albeit powerful, are often intricate and lack transparency.
Coupled with legal mandates like the right to an explanation, the concept of Explainable

The importance of transparency and explainability in AI systems can not be understated.

users, these features become imperative (Stowasser et al., 2023). Key measures to en-
sure this include strengthening the explainability, ensuring transparency at varying levels,

Trust thus plays an undeniably key role in AI’s successful implementation. Humans inter-

Equally vital is the comfort in relying on the AI’s recommendations. Crucially, discerning
when to trust the AI’s recommendations and when to seek additional data to make an
informed decision is indispensable (Haefner and Morf, 2021).

3.5.7
Success Factor 7 - Risk Management
3.5.7.1
Risk of AI
AI encompasses a variety of risks that necessitate vigilant management to uphold ethical
standards and mitigate potential negative outcomes. The intricacies of these risks are
eloquently summarized by Eitel Porter (Eitel-Porter, 2021).
• Compliance and Governance: There is a substantial risk of AI applications breach-
ing regulations in areas such as employment, data privacy, financial services, and
health and safety.
• Brand Damage: AI systems that violate societal norms and taboos pose a significant
threat to a company’s reputation.
• Third-Party Transparency: The use of opaque, ”black-box” AI tools from third-party
vendors can result in transparency issues.
• Rushed Development: Hastily developed AI systems may seem functional at first
glance but could harbor underlying issues due to expedited development timelines.
• Technical Misunderstanding: A lack of comprehensive understanding regarding the
proper and ethical use of AI can hinder its development from the outset.
• Quality Assurance Challenges: Inadequate quality assurance, particularly when
performed by application developers or individuals without the required technical
expertise, can fail to identify potential issues.
• Contextual Misuse: Employing AI outside its intended context can lead to unpre-
dictable and potentially harmful outcomes.
• Data Management Risks: Improper data handling and the collection of excessive
data can expose companies to risks, including non-compliance with privacy regula-
tions like GDPR.
• Communication Barriers: A company culture that does not encourage open commu-
nication can lead to employees withholding concerns about AI systems, highlighting
the necessity for clear reporting channels and protection for whistleblowers.
Understanding and mitigating the risks associated with the outcomes it produces is crucial,
especially given the rapidly evolving legal landscape.
Further more a comprehensive report published by IEEE sheds light on this, highlighting
the European Union’s recently proposed Regulatory Framework on AI . This framework
introduces a structured approach to assess the risk levels of AI products, services, or
systems (Crockett et al., 2023). It delineates four distinct risk categories:
• Unacceptable AI Risk: AI systems that are deemed a blatant threat to people’s
safety, livelihoods, and rights fall into this category and are subject to prohibition.
• High AI Risk: This encompasses AI systems in critical areas like law enforcement,
credit scoring, and border control management. Such systems require extensive
risk assessments, effective mitigation strategies, access to high-quality datasets, as
well as ensuring traceability, clear documentation, and user-friendly explainability.
Moreover, they must demonstrate a high level of robustness, security, and accuracy.

• Minimal AI Risk: This category includes applications with negligible risk implications,

Success Factor 8 - Design and Deployment
The interplay between the design and deployment of AI soultions has emerged as a crucial
area of focus in recent literature. This concept underscores the idea that the development
of an AI model is not an isolated process but is intertwined with how it is designed for
interaction and eventually deployed in real-world scenarios.
3.5.8.1
Designing AI Systems
Designing AI solution necessitates a thorough assessment and understanding of various
aspects of the business, including technical and social impacts that the solutions may
bring, workplace perspectives on AI, and a clear scope of action for employees. Engaging
employees in the design process and ensuring a human-oriented approach can foster
acceptance and productive human-machine interaction (Stowasser, 2023).
Since end-users of AI systems are typically non-technical, whether they are employees
or consumers, it is crucial to design the AI model as user-friendly as possible (Taulli,
2019). Achieving high level of user-friendliness ensures a smoother transition and higher
acceptance rates, as users can more easily adapt to and embrace the new technology
(ibid).
Post-deployment, continuous monitoring, and evaluation of an AI project are important to
ensure its performance and to facilitate necessary adjustments. This includes revisiting
the design of applications, work organization, and employee training (Stowasser, 2023).
From Data tilsynet’s report, AI models are put into two different distinctions, static and
dynamic models. Static models, once trained on data to a satisfactory level, require no
further training—only monitoring to ensure correct output. However, these models may
become less accurate over time as input data changes, such as due to demographic de-
velopments. In contrast, dynamic models are regularly trained on new data while in use.
Consequently, they should evolve with changes in input data over time. A drawback of
dynamic models is that they necessitate more comprehensive monitoring to prevent erro-
neous evolution (Datatilsynet, 2023). Establishing a culture of continuous improvement
and regular evaluations is a must to keep AI systems up-to-date and aligned with the
evolving needs of the business and its employees.
3.5.8.2
Initial Deployment Strategies
Implementing AI in a business setting requires a strategic approach to minimize risks and
optimize benefits. The initial phase of deployment is crucial as it sets the stage for how

also emphasizes the importance of adopting an experimental approach, suggesting the
creation of a pilot program to navigate through the initial ’crawl, walk, and run phase’
of AI implementation. This phased approach allows for careful monitoring and adjust-
ment, ensuring any issues are addressed promptly and learning is maximized. Often this
means that embracing AI requires a cultural shift within the organization, and change pro-
cesses are essential to facilitate this transition. (Stowasser et al., 2023) also advocates
for the inclusion of more pilot and experimentation phases within AI change processes,
as these phases are crucial for gathering experience values, and best practice examples,

Discussion
7.1
Results
The thesis delivers two principal contributions: First, it presents a literature review that ex-
amines the main barriers, success factors, and existing frameworks created by scholars to
navigate these challenges with a focus on SMEs. Secondly, it provides five newly devel-
oped frameworks designed to improve communication between AI experts and business

In this section, I will discuss the significant insights and implications of these contributions,
examining how the identified barriers, success factors, and new frameworks interplay to
advance the understanding and practical application of AI implementation.
7.1.1
The Literature
The literature review identified six key barriers, eight success factors, and four relevant
frameworks essential for implementing AI in businesses. This information was primarily
sourced from DTU Findit and consisted mainly of English-language sources. As illustrated
in Figure 3.2 of scopus source, there has been a notable surge in publications about AI
implementation in SMEs, suggesting the emergence of new knowledge and insights that
could potentially refine the theories presented in this thesis, especially those focusing on
SMEs. However, the insights gathered in this literature review are still considered rele-
vant for AI implementation in companies. This particularly applies to more general, long-
standing theories explained in this thesis such as the concepts of; Business Cases, Data
Management & Analytics, and the Human-centric approach. These concepts, though
applied to AI, have a broader foundation and are less likely to change significantly over
time. In contrast, specific AI-related areas presented in the literature review such as AI
awareness, legal and regulatory issues, model development, and concerns about ethics
and biases are more susceptible to change. These aspects are directly tied to AI and
are more likely to be influenced or altered as our understanding of AI evolves and as
regulations are updated to keep pace with technological advancements
Much of the literature reviewed was sourced from academic channels. However, in the
context of AI’s fast-paced evolution, it can be argued that many relevant theoretical in-
sights on AI adoption also can be found through academic unconventional medias such
as LinkedIn and twitter post, podcasts, YouTube videos, and more. While these sources
can offer a more up-to-date and current perspective, thus aligning with the swift advance-
ment in AI, although they also present difficulties in terms of academic verification and
validation. This contrast underlines the dynamic and challenging nature of AI research
but also opens up for new possibilities to balance traditional academic thoroughness with
the insights obtained from more rapidly evolving platforms.

7.2
Methodology
Two primary approaches to the Design Science Research (DSR) methodology were con-
sidered in the context of addressing the research problem, a top-down approach and a
bottom-up approach.
The chosen strategy was the bottom-up approach, which begins by gathering a compre-
hensive understanding of AI implementation. The objective was to develop frameworks
that specifically address the barriers and success factors in AI implementations. These
frameworks were then to be evaluated and refined in consultation with AI experts. The
rationale behind this choice stemmed from a desire for thorough preparation before en-
gaging with experts. Without a solid grasp of the subject matter, dialogues with experts
might have been superficial and less productive. Recognizing that access to AI experts is
a scarce and also a valuable resource, the decision was made to maximize the effective-
ness of these interactions by being well-prepared, rather than risking unproductive and
general discussions.
Alternatively, a top-down approach could have been adopted, starting the research project
by directly consulting with AI experts or participating in an AI project. This method might
have expedited the project development but would have introduced a higher degree of
risk, relying extensively on initial assumptions and expert interactions.
The preference for a bottom-up approach over a top-down one was predominantly influ-
enced by considerations of resource optimization.

Limitation
The interviews conducted was geographically limited to Denmark, focusing primarily on
Copenhagen. Interviews were conducted predominantly with Danish AI professionals, al-
though one was international. This geographic limitation could impact the applicability of
these frameworks in different work cultures. However, the frameworks are designed to
be general enough that cultural differences should not significantly affect their implemen-

highlights an opportunity for further enhancements, through a broader range of academic
literature on AI implementation, which was somewhat limited at the time of the research,
as well as conducting additional interviews. These expansions in future research could
greatly deepen the understanding and add more comprehensive dimensions to the field.

that, while productive, was quite compact given the task of explaining and receiving feed-
back on five different frameworks. Acknowledging this, it’s clear that longer interviews
would have been beneficial for a more thorough exploration and understanding of each
framework. This limitation points to the need for a balance between time efficiency and
comprehensive discussion, especially when dealing with multiple and multifaceted topics
like AI implementation frameworks.
Finally the research, encompassing a broad range of literature and interviews, was not
concentrated on any specific industry or company. This approach broadened the applica-
bility of the findings across various sectors, enhancing the versatility of the insights gained.

implementation in a general context, the findings may require adaptation or further inves-

between general applicability and industry-specific relevance is a crucial consideration in
interpreting and utilizing the research outcomes.

Conclusion
As argued in the introduction, this thesis sets out to improve AI implementation, especially
among SMEs, by in-depth examination of the existing literature on AI implementation. In
pursuing this aim, this thesis sought to answer three research questions.
RQ1: What are the common barriers and success factors in AI implementation for
SMEs as identified in current literature?
My exploration of the literature has revealed six potential barriers that can hinder the adop-
tion of AI among SMEs. These barriers include: 1) Lack of qualifications and training, 2)
Limited financial resources, 3) Concerns about operational time frames & return on invest-
ment, 4) Inadequate data quantity and quality, 5) Issues related to data governance and
legal compliance, and 6) A general lack of awareness or vision concerning AI’s potential.
In addition to these barriers, I identified eight success factors that can significantly facili-
tate AI implementation in SMEs. These factors are categorized into broader concepts: 1)
Building a strong business case, 2) Data Management and Analytics, 3) Support from top
management and good infrastructure, 4) Involving employees in the process, 5) Establish-
ing trust in AI systems, 6) Adhering to AI ethics, 7) Implementing robust risk management
strategies, and 8) Thoughtfully designing and deploying the AI solutions.
It’s important to note that while these barriers are often viewed as binary obstacles, com-
pletely preventing SMEs from initiating AI projects, success factors are considered on a
spectrum. This distinction is important, as the impact of success factors is not binary but
varies in degree, depending on the extent to which they are applied within an organization.
Additionally, a significant finding was that the existing literature on AI implementation in
SMEs is quite scarce and not yet mature. Most of the sources found either adopt a very
general perspective on AI implementation or are overly specific, focusing primarily on the
implementation of AI in manufacturing and production industries. Furthermore, studies
specifically addressing AI implementation in SMEs within the European Union are no-
tably limited, with much of the research conducted in contexts are outside the EU. This
highlights a critical gap in the literature and underscores the need for more comprehensive
and region-specific research in this topic.
RQ2: What relevant frameworks already exist to facilitate AI implementation in
SMEs
There were not identified any relevant frameworks from the my academic literature re-
search, which existed to specifically facilitate AI implementation in SMEs. Most of the
current frameworks that exist today lack the specificity and practicality required for a
meaningful comparison with the frameworks I wanted to develop for AI implementation
in SMEs. Although it was possible to identify four relevant frameworks, focused only on
the AI implementation overview process and the last one focused on how to connect com-
panies with relevant AI existing AI use cases.
This absence of comparable models thus underscores the importance and novelty of the
frameworks developed in this thesis. General AI implementation frameworks found in the
literature search often offer a broad perspective, which makes them very easy to com-
prehend, but they do not sufficiently address the specific needs and challenges faced
by companies in their AI implementation efforts. This distinction highlights the innova-

tive aspect of this research, by providing a more customized and relevant approach for
companies, tailored to their unique contexts and challenges.
RQ3: How can the insights acquired from the literature review be integrated into
new practical frameworks for AI implementation?
In the field of AI implementation, this research culminates in the development of five novel
frameworks, each tailored to address distinct aspects of the AI implementation process.
Central to the five frameworks is Framework 1 - AI Process Overview, which expands
on existing frameworks by going from a single project view to incorporating a holistic view
on implementation, a novel approach scarcely found in current literature, but a perspective
that has been emphasized as being important by AI experts. This framework specifically
addresses the real-world methodologies employed by consultants in AI deployment, pre-
senting a more nuanced and detailed approach.
The remaining four frameworks have been designed to tackle specific stages within the
overarching AI implementation process, as depicted in the Process Overview Frame-
work. Framework 2 - AI Use Case Identification focuses on guiding the selection of
initial AI projects, highlighting the Analytic maturity of the company in combination with
suitable use cases. Framework 3 - Barriers and Risks, systematically compiles litera-
ture findings on barriers and risks, providing a pragmatic reference for navigating common
obstacles and risks in AI implementation. Framework 4 - AI Pilot Project focuses on the
Pilot Project process in AI implementation. This framework specifically emphasizes the
interconnection between AI model development, system design and the organisational
factors, furthermore this framework focuses on a novel exploration of different team roles
during the pilot project phase not seen in the literature. The roles are; AI ambassadors,
champions, and subject matter experts. Finally Framework 5 - AI Task Automation and
Validation Revised is a framework that explores the aspect of evaluating the appropriate
level of automation for AI solutions especially taking EU regulations into consideration.
This thesis offers a solution in filling a current gap between theory and practice in AI im-
plementation. It Introduces novel frameworks, grounded in academic literature as well
as insights gained from AI experts in the industry. These frameworks offer a structured
approach to facilitate communication and navigation in the intricate landscape of AI im-
plementation, leaving with the momentum to progress towards more successful AI imple-
mentations.

Another potential avenue to follow is to develop more detailed frameworks under the over-

implementation steps and considerations.
Furthermore, there is a significant opportunity to adapt and apply these frameworks to
specific industries or companies with unique characteristics, such as varying sizes, cul-

environments would not only validate their applicability but also potentially reveal new

English Interview Questions
We start with broader questions to understand your overall perspective on the im-
plementation of AI in SMEs.
• What is your background?
• How long have you been working with AI (how many projects)?
• What is your current position?
Introduction: I have read a lot of literature on the implementation of AI in SMEs. Based
on this literature, I have developed some frameworks that are based on the barriers and
success factors I have identified. I am now seeking empirical knowledge from the real
world as feedback on the frameworks I have developed. Feedback can be about what
works well, what doesn’t work, or what is missing.
Framework-Specific Questions: About the general process framework:
• Do you think this framework captures the implementation process for AI in compa-
nies?
• Are there any steps in this framework that you find redundant or missing?
For each diagram:
• How practical and applicable do you find this diagram in real scenarios?
• Can you suggest any improvements or additional elements to this diagram?
Barriers and Risk Assessment:
• Based on the list of barriers and risks, are there any that you think are overlooked
or exaggerated?
