is to be mounted on a Unmanned Flying Vehicle. The arm is has 2 degrees of freedom
and a remotely actuated gripper.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
A way to describe humanity is that we always have sought to become more productive so
that we may thrive. For instance we have gone from scavenging to farming and hunting
to animal husbandry. Of course this process has been helped immensely via the usage of
tools. Tools has allowed us to be more efficient or even do things not possible with human
hands. A good example of us utilizing the available resources and combining them for
even greater results is using raised horses or oxen to plow a field. This of course means
that the field can be bigger so more animals can be raised and more people fed and so on.
The process of refining, combining and utilizing available resources and tools has al-
lowed us to slowly change the overall division of labor of humanity. As our productivity
has increased more people could focus on other industries than food production, such as
producing better tools or conditions for other people. For instance a single farmer today
utilizing modern tools, such as tractors, can produce many times more food per square
meter farmland than tens if not hundreds of farmers in previous ages. All in all the
productivity of humanity has increased substantially, but it has primarily caused people
to get arguably better jobs as time passed. From farmers to mechanics to scientists
and engineers as an example. However there is a tool that has disrupted this process,
automation.
Automation has allowed us to do a task with a machine rather than a human. An
example human task replaced by automation is a governor that regulates the amount of
steam that goes into the cylinders of a steam engine. A more modern example would
be cruise control in cars, where it replaces the drivers task of maintaining the vehicles
speed. While automation has done a lot to increase productivity in our society, a more
recently invented tool has accelerated the growth of productivity even further.
The computer can be called a milestone technology of humanity. A computer allows
for a much more advanced automation and this has led to the widespread usage of robots.
A prominently known example is the usage of robots in car manufacturing. Pulling this
trend along even further in the last couple of decades electronics, batteries and especially
computers has become small and effective enough to enable the creation of mobile robots.
Also the development and usage of artificial intelligence (AI) has become prominent and
cost effective for many applications. Mobile robots and AI are in themselves very useful
tools, but in recent times combing these two has been a point of interest around the

In the first challenge one of the objectives is to obtain an object suspended underneath
a UAV that will be flying on an approximate 3D figure eight trajectory. The object will
be a soft ball with a diameter of 10cm to 15cm with a weight below 150g. The ball is
suspended via a neodymium magnet on a semi-flexible carbon fiber tube that will be
attached to the intruder UAV via a flexible joint. The magnet will have a detachment
force of less than 4N. The flexible joint can be seen in figure 1.1. Figure 1.2 shows a
drawing of the carbon fiber tube and target ball used in the challenge. The mechanism
that will capture the ball is the focus of this report. The platform this mechanism will
be mounted on is a hexacopter provided by DTU. This hexacopter and the control of it
is not part of the scope of this project.

CHAPTER 2
Problem analysis
To summarize the problem described in section 1.1, the task is to grab a ball hanging
underneath an intruding UAV using a UAV in the form of a hexacopter. More specifi-
cally the problem being tackled in this report is the mechanism used for grabbing the
ball. Now in order to start solving this problem it is necessary to expand upon the
requirements described in section 1.2.
Firstly it is important for the mechanism to be as lightweight as possible because
it will be mounted on a UAV and they do not have a lot of load carrying capacity in
general. For that reason DTU provided a total target weight of 500 grams or less for
the mechanism and ball, so as the ball weights 150 grams that means the mechanisms
targeted maximum weight is 350 grams.
Secondly as described in section 1.1 the intruder UAV carrying the target ball will
fly around in a 3D figure-8 pattern at a constant speed. This makes the task of get-
ting close to said UAV challenging. More importantly to this report is the fact that
this most likely will cause the UAV used for capturing the target ball to make a lot of
correctional movements. To ease the capturing process it makes sense to use the mech-
anism to filter these correctional movements from the part of the mechanism that will
capture the ball. For that purpose a controller is needed to counteract these movements.
Finally the actual mechanism that will fulfill these requirements needs to be designed
and build. The end point manipulator that will actually obtain and hold the ball will
need to be analysed and designed, and also the arm that will enable the end point
manipulator to do its job. Part of that analysis was done in a special course prior to
starting on the master project called Analysis of Controlled Grippers for UAV.

2.1.1
Scoring of the arm designs
In order to find which designs should be considered further a scoring system is necessary.
The scoring system will be displayed in table 2.1 with the following categories. The
score given is between 1 to 5, where 1 is the best possible score. The only exception is
in the actuators category. The categories being considered are as follows:
• Actuators: Amount of actuators needed to realize the arm and gripper.
• Actuator position: The closer the position of the actuators are to the base of
the arm, the better score is given.
• Position: Mounting position on the UAV, a central position is better.
• Arm stability: Arm stability, fewer joint are better.
• Response: Responsiveness of the arm to the correctional movements of the UAV.
• Length: Arm length required for the design, shorter is better due to torque load
on the actuators.
• Arm weight: Weight of the arm itself including end point manipulator, less is
better.
• Weight: Weight of the entire mechanism, less is better.

• Load centralization: Ability of the arm to centralize the load weight under the

• Total score: The best result is determined by the lowest total score.

2.1.3
Discussion for the arm designs
After analysing different arm designs here are the main takeaways:
• Linear actuators are not suitable for this application as they typically use more
space and are heavier than a similar system utilizing rotational joints.
• Using only winches for moving the arm is not suitable for the fast movements of a
UAV.
• Winches could be used to hold up a rod with the actual arm attached to the end
of it in order to extend the range of the arm. This however will be a heavier and
more complex system than necessary.
• Using long end point manipulators in the SL1, SL1a and SL2 designs with a large
capture zone using a net make a lot of sense for this application. Unfortunately
the hexacopter used as a platform for this project makes this impractical, as its
rotors are placed relatively close together and as such there not a lot of space for
a large system such as this. Also as the rotors are unguarded having a net close
to them could cause a premature end to the challenge. They are still good to keep
in mind as a secondary option.
In the end using rotational joints make the most sense. Also reducing the degrees
of freedom (DOF) of the arm to 2 is fine as the UAV itself can manage the distance to
the ball. Using a remotely actuated end point manipulator reduces torque load on the
actuators without adding much complexity to the system. Also they are fairly easy to
implement tactile force sensing on. So in closing the design philosophy used in the RR1,
RR2a and RR3a schematics makes most sense to pursue further, as shown in the scoring
table 2.1.

Analysing further on the results from the Analysis of Controlled Grippers for UAV
course, the arm design consisting of a two degree of freedom (DOF) joint close to the
base of the arm makes most sense for this application. The UAV can easily account for
the distance to the target and as such the last actuator needed for a 3 DOF arm will
simply be added weight and complexity. Moving on using a remotely actuated gripper
will help reduce the torque load on the actuators potentially leading to smaller and more
importantly lighter actuators. Also there is no need for the arm to be capable of holding
the target ball up against gravity, it simply needs to be captured and then the actuator
can essentially be turned off until the ball has been delivered. While the Open-Source 3D
Printed Underactuated Robotic Gripper from section 2.1.4.1 and the FinGripper 2.1.4.2
could be very good for this application, the design of these two grippers are simply too
complex with too many components. The job can easily be done with simple solid laser
cut fingers. Also for the long grippers there is simply to much risk in using a long gripper
with a loosely mounted net attached with the hexacopter used for this project, as the
rotors are fairly close. So the design that will be designed in this report is a 2 DOF arm
with a remotely actuated gripper.

3.2.1
Spring-loaded SoftPot Method
The SoftPot (Spectra￿Symbol 2019) is a linear soft membrane potentiometer that changes
resistance depending on where a physical force is being pressed on the active sensing
surface. It works the same way electrically as a standard potentiometer with a resistive
layer connected in either end by a voltage difference, a wiper connected to the output
making contact to this resistive layer will make a variable voltage division circuit. The
main difference is the construction of the wiper. In a standard potentiometer the wiper
is usually a bit of metal or similar pressing down on the resistive layer to make contact.
A fact of the SoftPot is that unless force is applied on it the output is an open terminal
not connected to anything.
Figure 3.4: Exploded view of a SoftPot potentiometer, taken from the spectra symbol
homepage (Spectra￿Symbol 2019).
The SoftPot however consists of four layers as shown in figure 3.4. First there is
the bottom layer with adhesive for mounting the SoftPot on a surface, the layer also
increases structural integrity. The second layer contains the resistive element with pos-
itive and negative traces. The third layer exists to create a space between the resistive
element and the conductive surface (collector) connected to to output, so that contact
only happens when a force is applied to the top layer towards to bottom layer. The
fourth layer contains the collector that makes contact to the resistive layer depending
on where force is applied.
In order to use the SoftPot to measure force it is first necessary to design a wiper
that can provide a constant, consistent and fairly precise pressure point on the surface.
First i designed a base to glue the SoftPot onto that is easy to mount with screws. The
base can be seen in figure 3.5. The base has been 3D printed in standard ABS plastic.

• The system does not need as much actuation as the SoftPot sytem.
• The system has worse signal quality than the SoftPot sytem.
• The resistance of the force resistor drifts over time making it unusable for precision
measurements.
• The system can be made very small and lightweight.

Comparing the two systems the SoftPot system is good for precision measurements,
but it need more actuation length for the same gripping force.
Also the system is
comparatively complex and large. The force resistor system is best used as a general
indicator of the force applied rather than a actual measurement, making only marginally
better than the microswitch system alone while making the actuation of the gripper a
good amount more complex.
In closing while the force feedback system would add
more possibilities for controlling the gripping action it will primarily add unnecessary
complexity to the arm, as both of the desired detection scenarios can be handled with
the microswitch alone. Also more importantly removing the force feedback system will
save weight and reduce mechanical complexity. Therefore the force feedback system will
not be considered further in this thesis and the tactile object detection will be handled
by the microswitch.

Gripper Design
As described in chapter 2 the gripping mechanism will be string actuated with solid
fingers. The gripper will be mounted on a carbon fiber tube with an external diameter
of 1cm, with the string used for actuating the gripper running through it. In order to
keep the design compact i decided to keep the gripper open using a spring, this also
serves to keep the string tight. The gripper is closed by pulling on the string. Another
goal is to use lasercut pieces for as many parts as possible, and especially avoid 3d
printed parts for the main stress points of the gripper construction. This is because of
the structural weaknesses native to the commonly used Fused filament fabrication (FFF)
also known as Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) 3D printers. FFF printers work by
adding layer upon layer of usually melted ABS plastic until the desired shape is reached,
as illustrated in figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Illustration of the layers used by an FFF printer to contruct an objecct
(Gringer 2018).

This limits the material strength to the adhesion between the layers. While this in
many cases will be fine, for a lightweight high stress application such as the structural
pieces of the gripper it makes much more sense to use pieces lasercut from solid plates
of acrylic. Lasercut pieces doesn’t have this inherent weakness of the 3D printed parts,
and thus they can be made with less material for the same structual strength. And in
case of the fingers it might not even be feasible to use 3D printed parts, because they
need to be as slim as possible in order to keep the weight down.

2.1
Scoring of the arm designs
In order to ﬁnd which designs should be considered further a scoring system is necessary. The
scoring system will be displayed in table 1 with the following categories. The score given is
between 1 to 5, where 1 is the best possible score. The only exception is in the actuators
category. Appendix B (chapter 6) has the diﬀerent schematics of the arm designs that are
evaluated and given a score in this chapter, where appendix A (chapter 5) describes the shapes
used.
Actuators
This category displays the amount of actuators needed for the arm, the remotely actuated
and actuated end point manipulator mechanisms will be estimated as one actuator. Scoring of
the end point manipulators themselves is in chapter 3. It is better to have the lowest amount
of servos as possible, because this will in general decrease the weight and complexity of the
mechanism.
Actuator position
This category displays the scoring for the estimated actuator positions on the arm, including
the end point manipulators actuator. The closer the actuators are placed to the base of the
arm the better score is given.
Position
This category displays the scoring for the mounting position of the arm on the UAV. The
closer the arm base is located to the center of the UAV when seen from below, the better
score is given.
Arm stability
This category displays the scoring for the estimated stability of the arm itself. The more
joints located on the arm the lower a score is given.
Response
This category displays the scoring for the estimated capability of the arm to counteract the
movements of the UAV.
==PAGE:83==
CHAPTER 2. ROBOTIC ARM DESIGNS.
Page 3
Length
This category displays the scoring of the estimated length if the arm. As the length of the arm
increases so does the torque requirement of the servo(s) holding the arm up against gravity,
this is primary scored according the part of the arm that has to use a servo to hold it up
against gravity. Therefore the better score is given to the shorter arm.
Arm weight
This category displays the scoring of the estimated weight of the arm itself including the end
point manipulator.
Weight
This category displays the scoring of the estimated weight of the entire arm mechanism.
Reach
This category displays the scoring of the estimated reach of the arm, without changing to
more exotic shapes than a straight rod between joints. Mostly the arms capability to reach
above the propellers of the UAV is considered, as it is relatively easy to increase the range
horizontally.
Load centralization
This category displays the scoring of the arms capability to have a load, in this case a ball,
centralized under the UAV after it has been picked up. This is important in order to keep
the UAV ﬂying as stable as possible after a load has been picked up. Also the ability to
counteract the weight of the arm itself if it is oﬀ-center.
Total score
The best result is determined by the lowest total score.

2.2
Robotic arm discussion
After considering diﬀerent types of arm designs there are some general takeaways. First oﬀ
for a lightweight application such as being carried by a UAV linear actuators are not well
suited, as they in general are heavier than other strategies for the same type of application.
Secondly utilizing winches solely for moving one or more axis, such as in design WA1 etc., is
not ﬁtting for counteracting the small and fast correcting movements of the UAV as winches
are simply too slow to respond well enough. They can however still make sense for assisting
against gravity such as in design WR1, unfortunately they will still add unnecessary weight
as in most cases there is little gain over simply using a more powerful servo for keeping the
arm up against gravity. To add more to that point the ball does not actually have to be held
up by the arm, as such it makes more sense to simply have the arm immediately return to
the retracted state after grabbing the ball. In this case the servo holding the arm up only has
to be capable of holding the arm itself, thus reducing weight.
Finally while the idea of long end point manipulators could make good sense for this appli-
cation, but they mostly just add either imprecision to the mechanism and/or unnecessary
extra weight and complexity. There is however one situation where the added area of the ball
capture zone that comes with a larger end point manipulator makes a lot of sense, namely in
the presence of very unsteady movement of the ball caused by wind or other factors. This will
be discussed further in chapter3. As such while they are not the primary recommendation it
is worth to keep the SL1 and SL1a in mind as a plan b.
In conclusion it makes most sense to simply utilize rotating joint only and reduce the DOF of
the arm to 2 as the UAV itself can approach the ball, it is much more important to keep the
end point manipulator at an optimal position to grab the ball. Also it makes the most sense
to use a remotely actuated end point manipulator to reduce the torque load on the servos.
Furthermore remotely actuated end point manipulators are relatively easy to implement force
sensing on.
Therefore as shown in the scoring table 1 the design types that makes most sense to further
investigate for the MBZIRC competition is the RR1, RR2a and RR3a schematics.
