Abstract
Coastal Erosion in Arctic is an ongoing phenomena which represents a relevant engineer-
ing topic for its social and environmental implications. It is the result of several processes
that highly depend on the local settings that characterize the shoreline. Foremost, the
climate conditions and the materials composition. Modelling tools are widely used to
represent such phenomena, with the purpose of giving an estimation of shoreline reces-
sion and therefore provides a future projection, useful for the engineering practise. The
objective of the study is to estimate present and future recession of an Arctic coastline
located in the Archipelago of Svalbard. This is done in connection with the preservation
of an historical and cultural remain located in the Hiorthhman shoreline: the "Taubanes-
tasjonen" (cable station). Here it is shown that the erosion rate measured in the past
is underestimated and that the future of the shoreline highly depends on future climate
scenarios. The erosion rate measured from 2011 to 2022 was found to be 2.15 m/year, his-
torical measurements done between 1927 and 2019 reported a recession of 0.76 m/year.
Future prediction using the modelling tool SCAPE, showed how, within 20 years, the
ridge recession will eﬀect the structure. When modelling future intensiﬁcation of storm
events, it is found that recession rates could have a signiﬁcant increased, by 40% in cer-
tain conditions. The results demonstrate that "Taubanestasjonen" is in serious jeopardy
and its future can be compromised in within a few years. Therefore, it is necessary to
take immediate actions to preserve the integrity of the structure.

Preface
This thesis is written to fulﬁll the graduation requirements of the Cold Climate Engi-
neering Master’s program at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and Norwegian
University of Science and Technology (NTNU), jointly with the Geotechnical Engineer-
ing Master’s program at the University of Padova (UniPd) through the T.I.M.E. program
(Top International Managers in Engineering). The study is also part of the Polar Climate
and Cultural Heritage – Preservation and Restoration Management (PCCH) program,
carried out by SINTEF.
The idea of this study was born with the need to further investigate the erosion pro-
cesses occurring in the area of Hiorthhamn (Svalbard). It was with Dr. Anatoly Sinitsyn,
a member of the PCCH, that we decided to focus on measuring past recessions and
modeling future scenarios, in order to have a precise idea about the future of "Taubanes-
tasjonen". The project, and in particular the ﬁeldwork operations were founded by the
Norwegian Research Council, in connection with the PCCH program. This study is in-
tended to be a useful source of information for the Authorities and all the parts involved
in the preservation and restoration of the cultural heritage of Svalbard.

Introduction
Coastline dynamic in the Arctic is the result of many processes. High latitude eﬀects dom-
inate and climate change poses a threat for these regions and their communities. The
present study will address a portion of the coastline located in Hiorthhamn in the Sval-
bard archipelago, situated between the North Pole and Norther Norway. The archipelago
stands between the 74° and the 81° parallels north. These areas retains archaeological
remains of coal-mining activities dating from 1917 (Hoel (1938)). One of this is "Tubanes-
tasjonen" (cable station). According to the investigations made by Nicu et al. (2020),
the area has an erosion rate of -0.76 m year−1. The cable station is under extreme risk,
and its future depends on the next 5/10 years (Rapport (2020)).
For its cultural and historical value, the protection of this remnant is vital. Therefore,
it is considered essential to further investigate the erosion processes that occur in the
shoreline that hosts "Tubanestasjonen". In particular, the prediction of future recession

useful life of the structure. To address this problem, numerical modelling was found to be
a suitable tool in order to simulate future possible scenarios. The modelling will be pre-

also in relation with its location in the Arctic.
The aim of the study is to characterized the sediments type, in order to identify
whether the erosion aﬀects a cohesive or sandy shoreline. This is followed by a direct
measurements of recession which aim to validate the results from the literature. These
information is then used to identify the most suitable model to represent the shoreline
evolution in the future. The aim of the model is to simulate a total time window of 40
years, also considering possible change in climate condition in relation to an intensiﬁcation
of storm events.

Coastal Erosion in the Arctic - Review
Coastal erosion is a natural phenomenon widely distributed along the Arctic coastal
regions and represents an engineering problem that is of high interest because of its
environmental and economic implications (Lantuit et al. (2012)). In this chapter: the
deﬁnition, causes, and implications of coastal erosion will be debated.
Furthermore,
coastal erosion hazards in Arctic environments will be investigated. Here location and
magnitude, with a main focus on the polar regions, will be introduced. Hazards and
related impacts of coastal erosion in connection to human activities will be touched in
order to highlight the major concerns for the future of coastal regions in the Arctic.
5.1
Coastal Regions in the Arctic
Generally, coastal regions are deﬁned as those areas interfacing between land and sea,
where the land is directly inﬂuenced by a variety of marine processes (ﬁg. 1). They
are generally characterized by high-energy phenomena, that contribute deﬁning a unique
and intriguing combination (Stephenson (2013)). For that reason, the study of coastal
processes and related hazards must discern speciﬁc knowledge about the area of interest
(AoI), this in order to gather localized understanding and regional background.

5.2
Coastal Erosion Processes in the Arctic
Coastal erosion is deﬁned as the net landward retreat of the shoreline with respect to
a given reference point (datum) (Stephenson (2013)). This natural phenomenon is the
result of coastal dynamics changing over time, where the temporal scale of the retreat
is greater than the singular cyclic patterns which dominate the short-term variations
(Stephenson (2013)). In the Arctic, this phenomenon is linked to three diﬀerent major
factors: sea-level rising quantity, a deﬁcit in the local sediment budget, and changes
in the hydrodynamic setting of the area due to the absence of sea ice cover (Freder-
ick et al. (2016)). All these factors are concatenated in complex feedback mechanisms
which are nothing but the result of the ongoing climate change. In the Arctic, this non-
linear combinations of factors result in an ampliﬁcation phenomenon, which is escalating
the magnitude of natural processes that facilitate the shoreline retreat (Jaskólski et al.
(2018)). Along with these causes, the so-called anthropic factors must be considered,
Page 4 of 96
==PAGE:18==
Coastal Erosion in Hiorthhamn
Carlo Antonello
where human activities and consequent demands for space induce an additional threat to
the coastline dynamic (Charlier and DeMeyer (1998)).
Within the frame of the Arctic environment, the reduction of sea ice cover poses a
great threat to the arctic coastline. Indeed, the coastline dynamic of the Arctic is mostly
controlled by the presence of shore-fast sea ice during the winter season: this limits bluﬀ
erosion, sediment transportation, and wave action (Barnhart et al. (2014)). Furthermore,
the absence of sea ice is changing the thermal properties of the Arctic Ocean. The albedo
of ice is larger compared to open water as well as the thermal conductivity. Without
sea ice, more energy is absorbed, resulting in warmer waters and stronger currents/waves
(Frederick et al. (2016)).
The presence of permafrost aﬀects the geomorphological characteristics of the shore-
lines. Warmer waters are interacting with terrestrial and submarine permafrost. Depend-
ing on the permafrost features, ice content can highly aﬀect the rate of erosion. In their
study Lantuit et al. (2012) show the positive correlation between ground ice content and
coastal erosion rate in Arctic coastlines (Figure 3).

interact with the shoreline. Arctic coastal processes are for example thermal abrasion,

infrastructure in cold climate (Sinitsyn (2019))
Thermal Abrasion
By deﬁnition, thermal abrasion is the process of erosion aﬀecting the permafrost shore-
line due to the mechanical and thermal action of the waves. Breaking waves and storm
surge causes the formation of thermo-erosion niche (ﬁgure 4). The lower bluﬀis undercut
causing high instability for the entire overlying material. The strength of the material is
mainly deﬁned by ice bonding (cohesive shoreline) (HEQUETTE and BARNES (1990)).
In this sense, ice wedges are key factors that contribute to the magnitude of the ero-
sion. Ice wedges polygons are forming due to the seasonal thawing and freezing cycles of
permafrost. During the winter, thermal contraction provokes ﬁssures, these are ﬁlled in
water during the summer which freezes back in the cold season. Consecutive cycles will
then result in the ice wedges growth. Thermal abrasion directly impacts the stability of
the wedges. Once the block collapse over the niche, the waves will erode it, and start the
process over. Figure 4 and 5 describes the processes of thermal abrasion, niche formation
and ice-wedges.
Thermal Denudation
Thermal denudation can be deﬁned as a thawing of frozen ground which then moves
downwards due to gravity.
Thermal denudation and thermal abrasion are closely re-
lated, together they are primary processes involved in coastal erosion in the Arctic. This
phenomenon is caused by subareal processes and mass movements which deprive the
shoreline reducing the stability (Anatoly O. Sinitsyn and van Oosterhout (2020)) (HE-
QUETTE and BARNES (1990)). Thermal denudation is generally greater for unlithiﬁed
ﬁne-grained soil with high ice content. In such areas where the wave action has a low mag-
nitude thermal denudation leads to relatively low rates of erosion. Whereas, when this is
combined with thermal abrasion, the shoreline retreat is ampliﬁed (Anatoly O. Sinitsyn
and van Oosterhout (2020)).

A study shows how the Arctic coastlines are on average subjected to a higher rate of
erosion compared to temperate shorelines, even if they are protected by see-ice most of

transport operated by the sea-ice must play a role (Are et al. (2008)). Nevertheless, sea-

can easily extend along with the entire sea depth. The keels, which is the portion of the
ridge below the water surface, can remove a remarkable amount of bottom sediment by
scratching it (Galloway (1975)) (Anatoly O. Sinitsyn and van Oosterhout (2020)).

Modelling the Coastal Erosion - review of existing
models
Numerical modeling is a widely used method to simulate coastal erosion processes. It is
indeed a powerful tool used to identify and quantify coastal hazards and design remedial
solutions. This section will debate upon the models that were found to be relevant for
the purpose of the project. These are divided between hydrodynamic models, intended
to model the wave dynamic aﬀecting the shoreline; and morphological models focused
speciﬁcally on the shoreline dynamic and sediment transport. An overview of the existing
models for Arctic regions is also given by (Frederick et al. (2016)). The SCAPE model
(Soft CliﬀAnd Platform Erosion) will be chosen to model future erosion in the AoI.
The information gathered in this section are referenced to the respective manuals of the
models and related article, cited along with the discussion.

Delft3D-FLOW and WAVE model®
Delft 3D is a multi-dimensional open-source hydrodynamic and transport model capa-
ble of simulating non-steady ﬂow and transport phenomena based on tidal eﬀects and
meteorological eﬀects. Delft 3D-WAVE SWAN model can be used to simulate random,
short-crested waves in coastal regions ranging from deep to intermediate and shallow
waters. The simulation takes into account wind wave, dissipation due to withe-capping,
bottom friction, wave breaking due to a change in bathymetry, and many other phenom-
ena. The output can be obtained in terms of one and two-dimensional wave spectra.
The model is based on: the Navier-Stokes equation of motion under shallow water
conditions and Boussinesq assumptions and the continuity equation to compute vertical
velocities in 3D models. The transport equation is used to simulate dissolved substances
propagation in rivers, estuaries, and coastal seas. In the vertical momentum equation, it
is assumed that vertical accelerations are negligible, this is solved using the hydrostatic
pressure equation. The set of partial diﬀerential equations is solved using a ﬁnite diﬀer-
ential grid, rectangular or curvilinear. A much more detailed description of the physics
and numerical adaptations can be found in the user manuals for FLOW model (Deltares
(2014a)) and WAVE model (Deltares (2014b)).
Thanks to its spacial discretization tools, this suite can be used to give a high-
resolution representation of complex geometries such as arctic fjords. For that, it would
be suitable to describe a complex reality like Adventfjorden. Delft 3D-WAVE SWAN
model is easily coupled with Delft 3D Flow to create a wave-induced currents simula-

6.1.3
Mike 21®: hydrodynamic module and ﬂow model
Mike 21 is developed by DHI (Danish Hydraulic Institute). The model has an important
research and application background that involves 140 countries and 50 years of history.
It is probably one of the most used commercial software in coastal and marine engineering.
Its applications range in innumerable areas: water quality management, climate change
risk assessments, coastline, marine problems, agriculture, and more.
Mike 21 is composed of a series of modules all integrated together, among these, are
the Flow Module, Spectral Waves (SW), and Hydrodynamic (HD) module (integrated
into the ﬁrst). Reference can be found in the user manuals for the two modules (DHI
(2021a)) and (DHI (2021b)).
The SW module is usually coupled with the HD model and provides a description of
wave growth due to wind, non-linear wave interaction, dissipation due to withe-capping,
and bottom friction.
The Hydrodynamic module represents the basic computational tool used in all other
modules.
It is based on the 2-dimensional shallow water equations and the depth-
integrated incompressible Reynolds averaged Navier-Stokes equations. The model con-
sists of continuity, momentum, temperature, salinity, and density equations.
The model is based on ﬂexible mesh discretization using the centered ﬁnite volume
method. The spatial discretization is performed using an unstructured grid of triangular
and quadrilateral elements.
It simulates water variation and ﬂows due to environmental and climate eﬀects. This
results in the quantiﬁcation of bottom shear stress, wind shear stress, Coriolis’s force,
momentum dispersion, wave radiation stress, etc. This model is suitable to simulate tidal
eﬀects, wind wave-generated currents and storm surges. The model output can also be
easily integrated to simulate sediment transport using the Sediment transport module.
However, this focuses on non-cohesive sediment deposits and does not capture the cutting
eﬀect of waves on the shoreline.

6.2.2
REEF3D®
Another model that is worth mentioning is REEF 3D. REEF 3D is an open-source Hydro-
dynamic model developed by Hans Bihs of the Technical University of Norway (NTNU).
A multiphysics extension allows simulating sediment transportation. This model solves
the convection-diﬀusion equation and uses a mass balance of transported sediments to
evaluate the sediment circulation based on the critical bed shear stress (more detailed
information can be found in Ahmad et al. (2015)). This model was used to estimate
coastal erosion in the Bjorndalen region at Isfjorden, Svalbard (Ahmad et al. (2017)).
The study coupled the hydrodynamic module of REEF 3D and the morphological mod-
ule. The second is based on bed shear load and stress calculation which is then used to
evaluate the morphological evolution on a sloping seabed with a seawall. The model was
found useful to evaluate the sediment rate transported from the bluﬀtoe during a storm
event. The model is more based on short temporal variation (i.e. a storm event), rather
than long-term prediction based on climate scenarios.
Overall, this model provides a good solution for hydrodynamic problems such as
free surface ﬂows, wave hydrodynamic, and structure interaction. However, the sediment
transportation module is mostly based on the cohesiveness process where thermal abrasion
is also not considered.

solves ﬁnite-diﬀerence calculation points to simulate coastal processes.
The model is
based on empirical formulations capable to make a detailed prediction of cross-shore and
long-shore coastal evolution. This can be deﬁned as a shoreline dynamic module. It
is deﬁned by a series of predictive phase and parameter description that includes (from
Nairn (1997) and Larry E. Parson (2003b)):

The model that was originally developed for sandy shore, is adapted to model down-

a thin layer of cohesionless sediment overlies a cohesive platform subjected to erosion.
Cross-shore proﬁles are generally used to model the cutting action of waves. In this case,
thermal erosion is not considered, therefore it is assumed that the cohesive deposit is not
ice-bond. In this model, the shear stress created by the orbital velocities, and wave energy
dissipation in the surf zone are the two major factors used to describe the undercutting
process. The combination of these two forces is ensured by empirical coeﬃcients.

on the reﬁnement. In many cases, COSMOS model was combined with several other
hydrodynamic models such as WaveWatchIII and Delft3D to simulate deep water wave
generation on a global scale and wave dynamic on a more regional and local scale. Due
to its versatility, COSMOS is suitable to be combined with many diﬀerent features and
models. All models and input can be integrated using MATLAB scripts.
COSMOS estimation is rather simple, but widely used in coastline management to predict
future climate scenarios for coastal planners.

Delft 3D D-Morphology model is morphological and sediment model coupled with Delft3D-

6.2.5
SCAPE
SCAPE (Soft CliﬀAnd Platform Erosion) aims to provide predictions of soft rock erosion
processes over mesoscale durations. The model was developed by Mike Walkden and Jim
Hall at the University of Bristol between 1999 and 2002. Scape was used to picture a
large spatial scale of the east and south coasts of England within the Coastal Strategy
Study program (Mike Walkden (2016)).
6.2.5.1
Model Description
Scape adopts a rather simple formulation that focuses mainly on phenomena acting on
the shore platform. In the model, the description is stated that long-term erosion is
mainly controlled by the rate of recession of the cliﬀtoe due to the cyclical action of
tides and waves on the platform (Walkden and Hall (2005b)).
This is justiﬁed by a
feedback mechanism that sees the cliﬀtoe as mainly aﬀected by stormy conditions with
steep wavefronts, this brings to episodic phenomena of rapid retreat; accordingly, the
platform becomes wider and more capable of dissipating the wave energy, leading to an
accretion of the cliﬀtoe. On the other hand, when episodes of downwearing occur, the
cliﬀtoe becomes more exposed to wave action, increasing the rate of recession until the
shore platform balance is restored. In a long-term scenario, the shore platform and the
dynamic equilibrium that is established with the cliﬀtoe is the main feature found in
Scape.
The beach volume is deﬁned by a layer of loose sediments overlying a cohesive shore
platform. The total balance of losses depends on the composition of the platform and
its tendency to erode under the eﬀect of waves. Beach volumes are therefore used to
deﬁne continuity in the total budget of sediment; the higher the amount of sediments
accumulated over the shore platform due to erosion, the higher the protection that this
gives to the shoreline, as described in the feedback mechanism. Beach volume is varied
stochastically based on average values.
The depths at which erosion occurs in breaking conditions depend on the thickness of
the beach layer, in the model it is assumed that the maximum depth of beach under which

6.3
Selection of the Model
In this Section, an overview of modeling strategies was introduced. Choosing a model
that perfectly ﬁts the purpose of this study is not obvious. There are several options to
pursue, depending on how detailed the solution must be. However, using a model that
outstands in the quality and possibilities of the data and measurements available for the
area is counterproductive.
Given the morphology of the shoreline and the description of the area in the following
Section 7, the shoreline is identiﬁed as a cohesive shore with an excess of ground ice
negligible. Other sources of sediment from the inland (river and mountains close by) are
here not considered. This is to simplify the solution. It might be that these processes play
a remarkable role in the sediment balance of the shoreline. However, where investigations
have not been done, the lack of information forbids modeling these complex phenomena.
To address this, the measurements of coastal erosion made by Nicu et al. (2020) will be
used to calibrate the model starting from the year 1927.
That being said, SCAPE model will be used to simulate the shoreline dynamic. This
model is speciﬁcally developed to simulate the evolution of cohesive shorelines on a long-
term scale. It is decided to not choose a process-based model due to the lack of data and
the required computational time to model future scenarios in a time window of 20/40
years. It is believed that the quality of the data retrieved from the Area of Interest (AoI)
is suﬃcient to be implemented and adapted in SCAPE. The measurements of recession
done in the past can be integrated into the future predictions, to reach the shoreline proﬁle
equilibrium needed for a mesoscale model such SCAPE. In total, 135 years starting from
1927 until 2062 will be modeled. In this way, the erosion process will be described by
coupling the past, present, and future of the shoreline, reaching the equilibrium over time
under the given waves action, tides, and sea-level rise rate.

7.1
Geographical, Climate and Historical Setting
The present study will address a portion of the coastline located in the Svalbard archipelago,
situated between the North Pole and Northern Norway. The archipelago stands between
the 74° and the 81° parallel north. The AoI is located in Hiorthhamn in Adventfjorden-
this is situated in Spitsbergen, the largest island of the archipelago 12. Close to the area,
ca. 3 km south is Longyearbyen: the largest inhabited area of Svalbard. With its unique
location in the Arctic Ocean, Svalbard climate is deﬁned by a variety of climate condi-
tions inﬂuenced by diﬀerent ocean currents. The climate in this area is characterized by
a mean annual temperature of -5 °C with certain variability, and a mean annual precip-
itation of ca 190 mm, of which 60% occurs as snow (Forland et al. (2011)) (Wickström
et al. (2020)). The air temperature is inﬂuenced by the West Spitsbergen Current which
provides an inlet for warmer waters in the fjord (Zajaczkowski (2008)). This causes sea
ice to be mostly absent in the southwest of the Archipelago. The dominant wind blows
from south est, along the Adventdalen valley. This area, upon the others in the Arc-
tic, has been aﬀected by a dramatic change in temperature over the last decades. The
weather station located at the Svalbard Airport recorded an increase in temperature of
7.8 °C in the periods 1917-2017 (Hansen et al. (2019)). This extreme warming as a result
of the so-called Arctic Ampliﬁcation is leading to an increase of precipitations in form of
rain, strong storm, and wave action which eventually poses an additional weakening of
the coastlines (Charlier and DeMeyer (1998)). On top of this, Adventfjorden, which once
used to be covered with seasonal sea ice during the winter, is now dominated by open
water throughout all year. The progressive disappearance of sea ice poses an additional
threat to the shoreline stability, being the ice natural protection from the wave action.

7.2.3
Shoreline Characterization
From the information gathered in this section about the sedimentology of the area, it is
now possible to make assumptions about the shoreline type that characterizes the area
of interest.
All types of coastlines found in temperate areas are also found in the Arctic (Overduin
and Wetterich. (2014)). Therefore, the main diﬀerence is represented by the average cold
temperatures and high latitude eﬀects that result in the presence of ground-ice and sea
ice which, as mentioned in Section 5, may aﬀect the shoreline dynamic.
Coastlines are generally divided into two main classes: lithiﬁed and unlithiﬁed coasts.
The ﬁrst type is representative of rocky cliﬀs, considered to be stable with generally small
or null erosion. The second type identiﬁes unconsolidated materials, usually loose clastic
and cohesive shores. Unlithiﬁed is where "depositional and erosional processes dominate"
(Larry E. Parson (2003a)). This last type is what is expected to characterize the Hiorth-
hamn coastline. Among the unlithiﬁed coasts, gravelly sandy and cohesive beaches are
found. It is here presented a summary of what are the main features and diﬀerences
identifying these types of shorelines. References can be found in the "Development of
coastal infrastructure in a cold climate: Summary Guideline" (Anatoly O. Sinitsyn and
van Oosterhout (2020)) and the "Coastal Engineering Manual" (Larry E. Parson (2003a)).
Sandy and Gravelly beaches
Cross-shore, long-shore and wind-blow sediment transport are what characterizes the
coastline process for these unlithiﬁed coasts. In the Arctic, sea-ice action is also a source
of sediment transport as described in Section 5. In this case, it is distinguished between
accumulative and abrasive clastic shores. The ﬁrst deﬁnes a deposition and therefore an
accretion of the shoreline. The second describes coastline erosion, hence an imbalance in
the sediment volumes. Erosion processes are potentially reversible for this type of shore,
depending on local settings such as waves, wind dynamics, and shoreline geometry. It is
indeed common practice to modify the cross-shore geometry using groynes and submerged
walls to change the coastal dynamic and gain accretion. In the case of sandy or gravelly
beaches, the grain size can facilitate the process of erosion, where small grains are more
eager to be transported. In the Arctic, erosion processes for this type of coastline mainly
occur during the period of open water where wave action reaches its apex. Given the
Page 39 of 96
==PAGE:53==
Coastal Erosion in Hiorthhamn
Carlo Antonello
high hydraulic conductivity of sandy and gravelly material, ground ice is generally found
in small quantities, therefore thermal erosion is not a major factor.
Cohesive beaches
The presence of a silty/clay deposit with typical glacial cliﬀas the one shown in ﬁgure
5, is generally correlated to a cohesive shoreline. In this type of shoreline coastal erosion
is an irreversible process. Here thermal abrasion and denudation are major factors, espe-
cially for those ice-rich bonded cliﬀs. Retrogressive thaw retreat is typical of these shores,
the undercutting eﬀect of waves can lead to extreme erosion (up to 10 meters per year
in the Russian and Alaska shorelines (Lantuit et al. (2012)). 65% of the Arctic coasts
is characterized by cohesive deposit. The majority of these are ice-bonded which makes
them more susceptible to coastal erosion, as already mentioned.
When it comes to the Area of Interest, the information gathered from the literature
along with the site investigations suggest that the Hiorthhamn shoreline belongs to the
cohesive type. Given the sedimentology, excess in ground ice does not play major rules,
since the ridge is relatively small in height and it interests mainly the melted active layer.
It is believed that the erosion process involves the cohesive shoreline platform underneath
the overwashed sandy deposit.

7.4.5
Rate of Sea Level Rise
The average sea-level rise for the Arctic Ocean is estimated to be 2.2 ± 1.1 mm/year
(Andersen and Piccioni (2016)). The rate of Sea Level Rise (RSLR) in the Arctic can be
extremely variable because many lands in the arctic are currently experiencing vertical
land movements. This is due to viscoelastic deformation of Earth’s surface from past
millennial ice un-loading, and contemporary elastic adjustment related to a local change
in ice loading (Ludwigsen et al. (2022)). In the case of Svalbard, the uplift rate was
measured using Global Navigation Satellite System stations located around Svalbard.
Between 2008 and 2018 Longyearbyen experienced an uplift rate of 7.2 ± 0.6 mm/year
(Hanssen-Bauer et al. (2019)).
When the RSLR is considered net of vertical land movement, its value was estimated
to be + 0.52 mm/year. This was calculated by Ludwigsen et al. (2022) comparing tide
gauges data and GNSS data. Fig.42 is shown the average sea-level rise measured in the
Arctic compared with the value of vertical land movements.

The purpose of this section is to give an overview of possible solutions used to mitigate
coastal erosion. In particular, the feasibility of these solutions in connection with the
Hiorthhamn local setting will be debated. The application of one method or the other
and its eﬃcacy depends on several considerations deﬁned in the designing phase, here
not included.
Given the extreme variability of the phenomena acting on the shoreline and the de-
pendence of these on the local setting, there is not an absolute solution to address coastal
erosion (Larry E. Parson (2003a)). In the Coastal Engineering Manual, tab.I-2-2, several
possible remedial solutions are presented, here shown in tab.14.

When designing a groin there are several factors to take into consideration. These
depend on the main parameters that govern the beach response to the groin installation.
The most important are listed below.
• Waves, wind and tides variability: in terms of magnitude direction and duration;
• Beach and sediment setting: availability, density, grain size and variability;
• Groin geometry: length, elevation, porosity, shape, orientation;
Groins usually do not oﬀer protection from storm events, hence cross-section sediment
transport will still be acting on the shoreline. Moreover, these structures, if not armored,
tend to be eroded with time due to wave action and longshore currents. For these rea-
sons, the groins will likely have to be reshaped with a certain regularity depending on
their dimensions and the type of sediments of which it is composed. Indeed, for a proper
design, it is important to correctly select the grain size of the groin material. The groin
should be permeable to allow water and sand to move alongshore and reduce rip current
formation and cross-section sediment loss. On the other hand, the groin material should
have a reasonable dimension to prevent it from being washed away, resulting in a more
frequent need for reshaping. The feasibility and eﬃcacy of a groin must include a de-
tailed analysis of longshore transport phenomena. This will help to properly design the

orientation and size of the groin.
Another possible remedial solution presented in the CEM, is the beach nourishment.
This is a non-structural solution that simply consists of the movement of materials from
one of the accumulation points to the shoreline of interest, to increase the beach width.
This solution could be implemented together with a reshaping of the shoreline proﬁle,
this to modify the long-shore sediments pathway. In particular, with a smoother shoreline
proﬁle between the accumulation area and erosional area, sediments trapped in the for-
mer would eventually be able to reach the latter, increasing the beach width over time.
However, further investigations using a processed based model for sediment transport
should be made. In normal practise, beach nourishment is also combined with other
structural solutions such as breakwaters or groins. In the ﬁrst case, this can be used to
increase the dry beach width and consequently the eﬃcacy of the breakwaters.
As ﬁnal adaptation option, retreat represents a non-structural and less invasive op-
tion. This consists simply of the movement of the structure landwards. Considering
the recession rate modeled in this study the structure should be moved about 40 meters
(or more) backward to prevent it to be submerged in the next 40 years. As already
mentioned, this considers the worst-case scenario of events, as discussed in the modeling
section (Sec.8). This solution will probably be the most eﬃcient solution to prevent dam-
age to the structure, although the environmental impact of using big machinery in the
area should be taken into consideration. Moreover, the retreat will likely be logistically
more complex than other remedial solutions like groins or beach nourishment.

Conclusions
When addressing the problem of coastal erosion in Hiorthhamn, the ﬁrst task was to pro-
vide a characterization of the shoreline in terms of sediment types and erosion processes.
This was done by consulting the literature and investigating the ﬁeld.
When it comes to the type of sediments, the initial hypothesis sees the shoreline as
being of the unlithiﬁed type, formed by cohesive materials such as clay or silt. Moreover,
it was assumed the presence of a relatively short layer of overwashed sand overlying a
cohesive shoreline platform within the tide area. The site investigation made with the use
of a permafrost drill, allowed to retrieve three samples from the tide area on which a grain
size distribution test was performed. Overall, the results of the test conﬁrm the presence
of a sandy top layer and an increase of cohesive components with depth. Indeed, the ﬁrst
sample ("sample 1") showed a content of silty/clay of less than 10 %, whereas, for sample
"3", retrieved from a depth of 1.5 m, about 45% of the mass was identiﬁed as clay or silt.
Ideally, sampling to at least 2 m below the sea bottom surface should be permitted to
conclude on the coastal type. To better characterize the area, a grab sampler was used
to retrieve samples from the sea bed while moving on the coastal area with an open boat.
The samples retrieved were mostly found of the cohesive type, with the only exception of
the shallowest one (retrieved at about 0.5 m depth). In conclusion, the initial hypothesis
is overall met, it is believed the erosion processes are aﬀecting the dynamic equilibrium
of the cohesive platform under the eﬀect of waves, tides and sea-level rise, which directly
controls the ridge recession. This conclusions point out the need to use a model handling
cohesive sediment as the ﬁrst approximation. Models using longshore sediment transport
might have been used as the next step to better clarify the erosion patterns, however the
SCAPE model was chosen for its ability to model long term scenarios.
In order to investigate the erosion processes in the AoI, direct measurements of ridge
recession were performed by using GPS instruments and orthophoto obtained with a
drone. The recession was measured by comparing the ridge position in 2011 found in
the orthophoto edited by the Norwegian Polar Institute with the new images obtained
during the ﬁeldwork. The erosion rate measured from 2011 to 2022 is 2.15 m/year with
a maximum recession of the ridge of 25.8 meters measured in front of the structure of
interest. Furthermore, during the measurements, a clear accretion area was found in the
western part of the shoreline, with an accumulation of sediments that led the shoreline

to move 23.5 meters seaward. During the measurements, it was also possible to measure
the width of the sea-ice layer and visually realize the important protective rule that this
provides during the winter season. Observations of accumulation zone (Fig.27) point out
on deﬁcit of sediment in front of Taubanestasjonen. Overall, the results of the measure-
ments were found to greatly diﬀer from the ones found in the literature. In particular, for
the same shoreline Nicu et al. (2020) report recession rates in the range of 0.19 to 0.76
m/year from 1927 to 2019. It is supposed that the diﬀerence in values could be linked
to the longer time window considered in the measurements, otherwise, signiﬁcant storm
events occurring between 2019 and 2022 result in a sharp increase in the recession.
The model SCAPE (Soft CliﬀAnd Platform Erosion) was then used to simulate fu-
ture recession of the shoreline. The model was selected based on its ability to model the
erosion of cohesive shoreline in long-term scenarios (20 to 40 years). Being a mesoscale
model, it is decided to use erosion rates measured by Nicu et al. (2020) from 1927 to 2019
for the calibration of the model, to make sure that the equilibrium between the hydro-
dynamic forces and the shoreline platform evolution is met. The model results showed
a recession of 9 meters within 20 years from the present. Being the structure of interest
a few meters from the shoreline ridge, it is expected the water to reach the structure in
about 10 years from the present. When modeling for 40 years, the recession simulated is
about 16 meters, with a rate of 0.64 m/year. This value of recession is slightly less than
the historical value, this is because when considering cohesive shoreline and a constant
rate of sea level rising, the erosion rate tends to decrease with time, without stopping.
The investigation proceeds to consider an increase in signiﬁcant wave height due to an
intensiﬁcation of storm events. To do so, increases of 10% and 20% in signiﬁcant wave
height were modeled. The results showed a signiﬁcant increase in recession, between 20
and 40 % respectively for 20 and 40 years of simulation, meaning that the shoreline stabil-
ity is indeed extremely sensitive to a change in climate conditions. This is along with the
sharp increase in recession that was measured during the ﬁeldwork, probably caused by
an intense storm event. However, it must be said that the SCAPE model is not intended
to model short-term events like storms, it is, therefore, important to address this type of
modeling with a diﬀerent approach, using for instance a processed based models that can
capture a single event and its magnitude in terms of recession.

Overall, measurements of erosion and modeling of future scenarios done in this study
provide a fairly good understanding of the engineering problem. It was proved that reces-
sion is happening, even faster than what was measured historically. Further investigation
should be done in order to develop more precise solutions of recession suitable to simulate
short storm events. Based on the measurements of erosion, it is in fact expected that
the recession could occur stronger in the future. However, the use of a processed-based
model would also require more data, that in the case of Svalbard are mostly not available
or limited.
In any case, "Tubanestasjonen" is indeed in great danger, and immediate action should
be taken to prevent it to be reached by the sea, with evident consequences for the stability
of the structure, the integrity of the materials, and foremost its cultural and historical
value. The study also reviewed some possible remedial solutions, their feasibility was dis-
cussed in terms of environmental impact and landscape preservation, however, no analyses
were made on their eﬃcacy. It appears that one possible solution would be represented
by the construction of breakwaters or groins, using as building material sentiments that
are accumulating on the western part of the shoreline. On the other hand, the retreat
of the structure backward could also represent a solution, with its technical implications
and issues.
