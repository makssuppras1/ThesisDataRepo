Abstract
Severe lower respiratory tract infections (LRTI) induced by Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)
infections in early life are characterised by a pronounced T Helper 2 (TH2) environment. This TH2
immune response enhances disease severity and augments the risk of later development of childhood
asthma or wheeze. TH2 cytokines IL-4 and IL-13 share a common receptor, IL-4R, and have been
linked to the immunopathology of RSV secondary infections in mice by promoting diﬀerentiation
of TH2 memory T cells, mucous hypersecretion, and airway hyperresponsiveness. In this thesis, we
examine the eﬀects of blocking IL-4/IL-13 signaling by using an anti-IL-4 Receptor alpha (IL-4Rα)
blockade during primary RSV infection in BALB/c mice on the lung immunopathology during
secondary infections.
We also characterise the immune response to primary RSV infection in
neonatal mice. Our results show that secondary RSV infections lead to signiﬁcant immunopathology
characterised by weight loss and enhanced cellularity, but blocking IL-4Rα during the primary
infection is not suﬃcient to attenuate the disease severity upon recall. Furthermore, we show that
enhanced illness observed during reinfections is not necessarily caused by a biased TH2 response
during primary neonatal infection.

1.2
Early detection of RSV via PRRs: RSV RNA is detected by endosomal TLRs: TLR-
7 and TLR-3 as well as intracellular RIG-I. Signalling of RNA through TLRs triggers
the NF-κB pathway and IRF pathway, which leads to the expression of Type I and
Type III interferons. The interferons then signal through IFNAR1/2 located at the
cell surface via a paracrine and autocrine feedback loop, causing the upregulation
of interferon stimulated genes (ISGs). This process aids the host by inhibiting viral
replication. Figure adapted from [2]. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

1.3
Cytokine induced diﬀerentiation of naive T cells: Speciﬁc cytokines determine the
phenotype of activated T cells. For example, IL-12 induces TH1 cells, IL-4 induces
TH2 cells, and IL-6 and TGFβ induce a TH17 response. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

1.4
The neonatal immune response to RSV: A: IL-33 signalling following RSV entry
can stimulate proliferation of ILC2s which are known producers of IL-13. ILC2s
can produce other Type II cytokines such as IL-4 and IL-5.
IL-5 signalling can
activate eosinophils. B: Neonatal pDCs have been shown to have attenuated IFN-I
production. C: Neonatal cDCs express higher levels of IL-4Rα and lower levels of
co-stimulatory molecule, CD86. Neonatal cDCs have been shown to preferentially
activate TH2 cells [3].

1.6
Type I and Type II receptor complexes of IL-4 and IL-13 signalling: IL-4 signals
through the IL-4Rα subunit of Type I and Type II receptor complexes. The Type
I receptor is comprised of the IL-4Rα subunit and common chain (γc). Signalling
through the Type I receptor is important for TH2 diﬀerentiation.
The Type II
receptor is comprised of IL-4Rα subunit and IL-13Rα1 subunit, and can bind both
IL-4 and IL-13. Signalling through the Type II receptor complex is associated with
airway inﬂammation and mucous production. Both receptors trigger the activation
of the transcription factor, STAT6. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .
12
1.7
Blocking IL-4 and IL-13 using an anti-IL-4Rα antibody: Anti-IL-4Rα binds to alpha
subunits of Type I and Type II receptors, preventing binding of both IL-4 and IL-13.
Blocking of these receptors prevents the downstream activation of STAT6, inhibiting
TH2 diﬀerentiation as well as the induction of a Type II immune response.

Introduction
Since its ﬁrst isolation in 1956, Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV) has been recognised as one of
the leading causes of respiratory tract infections and bronchiolitis during infancy [1]. For most
children, RSV will induce a milder upper respiratory tract infection. However, a small proportion
will develop severe lower respiratory tract infections which may lead to hospitalisation or even death
[1]. Severe RSV infection, characterised by the onset of bronchiolitis, has also been correlated to
the later development of asthma in children [4, 9]. Asthma is commonly associated with a Type II
immune response which includes T helper 2 cells, eosinophils, and cytokines such as IL-4, IL-5, and
IL-13 [10]. It is well known that RSV induces a Type II biased response in infancy, with elevated
IgE, IL-5, IL-4, and IL-13 found in the sera and nasal secretions of infected infants [11]. This type of
immune response leads to enhanced disease, resulting in signiﬁcant airway obstruction and airway
hyper-responsiveness (AHR) [12]. In turn, the immunopathology induced by severe RSV infections
may lead to heightened sensitivity towards allergens, and augment the risk for development of
asthma later in life.
Currently, no vaccine exists against RSV. This is partly due to an infamous clinical trial in the
1960s based on a formalin-inactivated RSV vaccine which led to the death of two infants [1]. While
vaccine research against RSV is ongoing, the trial’s tragic outcome highlighted the complexity of
the infantile immune response towards RSV infection. Humans are also susceptible to repeated
RSV infections indicating a lack of eﬀective immune memory against natural infection, creating
an additional challenge to vaccine development [13]. While there is no vaccine, a prophylactic
treatment under the name of Palivizumab is available [1]. Palivizumab is a human neutralising
monoclonal antibody against RSV F protein given to infants who are at high risk, but it is a
1
==PAGE:16==
preventative measure only and has no eﬀect during active cases [1].
While Palivizumab is an
eﬀective treatment against viral F protein, an alternative prophylactic in the form of a Type II
immune inhibitor could be administered as a method of reducing immunopathology, and reduce
the later risk of asthma in susceptible patients. Several studies have shown that blocking TH2
development using a neutralising IL-4 antibody can lead to reduced illness and improved viral
clearance [14, 15]. In this thesis, we aim to examine the impact of blocking IL-4 and IL-13 using an
anti-IL-4Rα antibody during primary infection on immune pathology after secondary RSV infection
in mice. To our knowledge, this is the ﬁrst example of using an anti-IL-4Rα antibody as a method
of modulating the immune response during RSV infections in BALB/c mice.
The remainder of this Chapter will discuss the scientiﬁc question and aims of this thesis, and
provide a general introduction to: RSV, the immune response towards RSV, long term consequences
of respiratory infections during infancy, and ﬁnally the modulation of Type II cytokines.
1.1
Scientiﬁc Question and Aims
Question
Does blocking the receptor of IL-4 and IL-13 cytokines (IL-4Rα) during primary neonatal RSV
infection have an impact on disease severity and immunopathology upon adult reinfection?
Speciﬁc Aims
• Characterise the cellular immune response upon reinfection using BALB/c mice which are
genetically predisposed to a TH2 bias. Speciﬁcally, this thesis will examine the diﬀerences
between neonatal and adult responses to RSV. The cellular immune response will be charac-
terised upon blocking IL-4Rα in the murine lung using ﬂow cytometry.
• Characterise the eﬀects of IL-4Rα blockade on a transcript level by using quantitative poly-
merase chain reaction (qPCR) to examine the impact on speciﬁc mucous related genes;
muc5ac and gob5, which are upregulated by IL-13 signalling.
• Examine the eﬀects of infection and IL-4/IL-13 blockade on the cytokine response by imple-
menting quantitative assays such as ELISA.

1.2
Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)
RSV is of the family Paramyxoviridae, order Mononegavirales, and is an enveloped, non-segmented,
negative sense single-stranded RNA virus. The viral genome is comprised of 10 genes that encode
11 proteins [1]. The following proteins are highlighted in Figure 1.1 as the main contributors to the
virulence of RSV; nonstructural proteins N1 and N2, nucleoprotein (N), phosphoprotein (P), matrix
protein (M), small hydrophobic protein (SH), attachment glycoprotein (G), fusion protein (F), large
polymerase protein (L), and ﬁnally M2-1 and M2-2 which regulate viral RNA transcription and
synthesis respectively [16].
Figure 1.1: Structure of RSV: RSV has 10 genes which encode for proteins associated with RSV virulence
and include: G protein, P protein, N protein, F protein, L protein, M protein, M2 protein and SH protein
[1].
Two major subclasses of RSV exist, subgroups A and B, which are characterised based on the
high variability of the G protein with subgroup A typically described as the more virulent strain
[17, 18]. This thesis uses the A2 strain, which has been propagated for research purposes and
is one of the most commonly used strains for studying RSV infection. RSV infects the surface
airway epithelial cells for entry via G, F and SH proteins [19]. Entry of the virus into the host cell
3
==PAGE:18==
coincides with fusion of infected cells with neighbouring uninfected cells, resulting in a cell with
several nuclei known as syncytia, giving rise to the virus’ name [20]. Histological staining from
fatal cases of infant RSV infection also revealed that RSV infected the alveolar and bronchiole
epithelium of infants [19].
Development of treatments and vaccines against RSV have primarily focused on generating
humoral immunity towards the F protein as it is typically conserved across all currently known
RSV strains. RSV G protein also stimulates neutralising antibodies, but the high variability of the
G protein between RSV A and B makes it a challenging target for therapies against RSV [16].
1.3
The Immune Response to RSV
Early Detection of RSV
The innate immune response is the ﬁrst line of defence against pathogens and is initiated by
signalling through pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) [21]. Pathogenic microorganisms are com-
posed of repetitive motifs known as pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPS), which are
recognised by PRRs. Ligation of PAMPs to speciﬁc PRRs leads to downstream events that alert
the host immune response.
Notable PRRs associated with RSV signalling include toll-like receptors (TLR)-4, 2/6, 3, 7
and RIG-I [16]. Epithelial cells as well as antigen presenting cells (APCs) such as macrophages
and dendritic cells (DCs) express PRRs. Upon entry into the airway epithelial cells, RSV triggers
several signalling cascades that will alert the host immune system. Alveolar macrophages sit below
the epithelial cells and will uptake viral particles. This will cause production of proinﬂammatory
signals that will recruit more immune cells to the site of infection. DCs will process viral proteins
and present the peptides on MHC I and MHC II, as well as migrating to the lymph nodes to
activate naive T cells.

associated with viral detection and are important for early signalling during an infection with
intracellular pathogens [21]. TLR-7 recognises single stranded RNA. TLR-3 and RIG-I recognise
double stranded RNA that is present during RSV replication within the host cell [16]. Surface
TLRs such as TLR-4 shown in Figure 1.2A is typically associated with bacterial LPS. However,

Dendritic cells are the link between the innate and adaptive immune response and are essential
for presenting antigen to naive T cells as well as the diﬀerentiation of T cell subsets. For this
reason, DCs are critical immune cells involved in mounting an eﬀective immune response against
pathogens. Shrestha et al [27] have shown that murine neonatal conventional dendritic cells (cDCs)
as well as pDCs express higher levels of IL-4Rα, the high aﬃnity receptor of IL-4, compared with
adults. Additionally, high expression of IL-4Rα on DCs was shown to inhibit their maturation,
characterised by low CD86 expression, see Figure 1.4C. This correlates with data indicating that
immature DCs are more likely to induce a TH2 response [22]. Additionally, Ruckwardt et al [28]
have shown that neonatal cDCs are less eﬃcient at migrating to lymph nodes and activating CD8
T cells which are necessary T cells involved in viral clearance. Combined, these studies reinforce
the theory that an ineﬀective innate immune response in infants may contribute to the severity of
RSV disease.
The Adaptive Response in Infants
The adaptive immune response is important for clearance of aggressive infections and development
of immunological memory. The main components of the adaptive immune response in the context
of a viral infection includes the generation of a Type I response, such as eﬀector T cells (CD4+
TH1 cells and CD8+ T cells) as well as pathogen speciﬁc antibodies secreted from plasma B cells.
A protective immune response against RSV involves the generation of IgG and IgA antibodies.
Protective maternal antibodies against RSV can be passed to infants via the placenta and milk [22].
Despite this, it has been shown that the presence of maternal antibodies may hinder the production
of host protective antibodies [29]. In addition, the infantile antibody response toward RSV appears
to be poorly neutralising and short-lived [1, 22]. This also remains true for adult infections where
antibody titres may be greater but are relatively short-lived, leading to multiple RSV infections
throughout life.
Tregoning et al [30] have shown that IFN-γ production during primary RSV
infection in neonatal mice can lead to the attenuation of an eﬀective antibody response. While
IFN-γ is an important cytokine for viral clearance, this study shows that in neonates, IFN-γ
8
==PAGE:23==
production may be a double-edged-sword by reducing the levels of eﬀective neutralising antibodies
against RSV.
Eﬀector and memory T cells are important for clearance of infections as well as preventing
reinfection. In neonatal mice, RSV infection leads to the induction of eﬀector CD8 T cells which
contribute to the enhanced disease observed in reinfection models [31]. CD8 T cells are important
producers of IFN-γ, which has been shown to be at decreased levels in neonatal mice after primary
RSV infection [31]. In general, the neonatal immune system is skewed towards the diﬀerentiation
of TH2 cells and is less likely to induce a Type I response (including TH1 and CD8 T cells) [32].
It has also been shown that adult mice induce a memory TH2 response when challenged with the
same antigen that was used during neonatal immunisations [32].
Lastly, it is important to note that the induction of a skewed TH2 response towards RSV is
also partly due to the virulence of RSV. Speciﬁcally, the viral G protein has been shown to induce
a TH2 phenotype [15].
1.4
Long-Term Consequences of Neonatal RSV Infection
RSV infection in early life, as well as other respiratory viruses such as Rhinovirus, have been
correlated to the later development of asthma and allergy [33, 31, 9]. Several murine studies have
since demonstrated the link between RSV and sensitisation towards allergens [34, 35]. The causal
relationship in humans appears to be a debatable topic with conﬂicting literature [36]. It is diﬃcult
to argue that RSV puts every child at risk for the later development of asthma when the majority of
studies aimed at identifying the link only include infants who experienced severe LRTI [37]. Even
though every child is likely to be infected with RSV, only a small percentage of RSV infections lead
to hospitalisation. For this reason, there is limited data on infants who do not experience severe
LRTI.
The mechanisms explaining how respiratory viral infections lead to asthma are poorly under-
stood [37]. It is highly likely that genetics will predispose individuals to developing severe LRTI
and asthma. Several immunological theories may also shed light on the correlation. For example, as
RSV induces a TH2 biased response during infancy, the consequences of such immunopathogenesis
may lead to lung injury, which in turn may increase susceptibility to developing asthma [37, 35].
9
==PAGE:24==
Children are also more vulnerable to chronic inﬂammation induced by some viral and bacterial
infections due to the immaturity of their immune system [4]. However, as Figure 1.5 (adapted
from [4]) illustrates, it is likely a combination of genetic, developmental, and environmental factors
contributing to the development of asthma following a viral infection. The need for continued
research in understanding the neonatal immune response to respiratory infections is reﬂected by
the lack of a deﬁnitive explanation for the risk of asthma induced by previous infections.

1.5
Modulation of Type II Responses
The goal of this thesis is to characterise the immune environment in the murine lung in response to
RSV infections. Disease severity during infancy is associated with a TH2 phenotype and IL-4 is a
key cytokine in TH2 diﬀerentiation that has also been shown to preferentially drive development of
TH2 memory eﬀector cells [32]. IL-13 has also been linked with perpetuating disease severity and
contributing to airway hyper-responsiveness (AHR) [23]. In this thesis we investigate the eﬀects of
blocking IL-4 and IL-13 signalling during primary infection of RSV. By blocking Type II cytokines
10
==PAGE:25==
linked to severe disease, the TH2 bias may be attenuated and result in reduced pathology upon
reinfection.
1.5.1
IL-4 and TH2 Polarisation
In the context of a Type II immune response, IL-4 is typically produced by eﬀector TH2 cells as well
as mast cells, eosinophils, and innate lymphoid cells (ILC2s) [38]. IL-4 binds with high aﬃnity to IL-
4Rα which is expressed on a range of leukocytes including, T cells, B cells, mononuclear phagocytes,
epithelial, endothelial, and smooth muscle cells [38]. TH2 polarisation typically occurs in the T
cell zone of lymphoid tissue when naive T cells interact with an activated dendritic cell. While
migratory dendritic cells are needed to activate naive TH2 cells, the mechanism remains uncertain
as it is not yet known if dendritic cells produce IL-4, the driving force of TH2 diﬀerentiation [39].
IL-4 signalling occurs through IL-4 receptor alpha (IL-4Rα). The receptor is comprised of Type
I and Type II receptor complexes, see Figure 1.6. The Type I receptor complex is composed of a
IL-4Rα subunit and common gamma chain (γc). It binds to IL-4 alone and is important for TH2
diﬀerentiation. The Type II receptor complex is composed of IL-4Rα and IL-13 receptor α subunits,
and bind both IL-4 and IL-13. The Type II receptor complex is expressed on both immune cells
as well as non-hematopoietic cells and signalling is associated with mucous production and airway
hyper-responsiveness during RSV infection [40].
The ability of IL-4 and IL-13 to signal through a shared receptor complex explains the common-
ality of both cytokines to induce Type II responses. Respective ligation of the cytokines to Type I
and Type II receptor complexes leads to the activation of the transcription factor STAT6, result-

Type II cytokines, and mucous production [38]. In addition, You et al [41], showed that IL-4Rα is
highly expressed on neonatal CD4 T cells and can contribute to the pathogenesis observed in RSV
reinfections.

1.5.2
IL-13 and Enhanced Disease during RSV Infection
As discussed, IL-13 is recognised as an important Type II cytokine and is secreted by TH2 cells,
ILC2s, mast cells, basophils, and eosinophils [23]. Signalling and secretion of IL-13 contributes
to goblet cell hyperplasia and mucous secretion.
Dakhama et al [42] have demonstrated that
IL-13 contributes to airway hyper responsiveness (AHR) and mucous production during neonatal
RSV infection in BALB/c mice. Inhibition of IL-13 led to attenuation of eosinophilia and mucous
hypersecretion. These results highlight the advantages of inhibiting both IL-4 and IL-13.
1.5.3
Blocking TH2 signalling during RSV Infection
It is important to note that the mechanistic role of IL-4 during RSV infection has not yet been
determined, but it can be hypothesised that IL-4 contributes to the disease pathology observed
in infants with severe RSV infection by promoting a TH2 environment. In one study conducted
by Tang et al [14], BALB/c mice were immunised intramuscularly with formalin-inactivated RSV
and treated with three successive intraperitoneally injections of anti-IL-4 (11B11, 29), before being
challenged intranasally (i.n) with live RSV 4 weeks later. The results showed reduction in illness
12
==PAGE:27==
signiﬁed by reduced weight-loss compared with control mice, as well as reduced viral replication,
and increased IFN-γ production. Within this context, it is clear that successive blocking of IL-4
from binding to its receptors promoted a more TH1 biased environment. Encouragingly, promotion
of a TH1 response did not enhance the sickness of the mice.
1.5.4
Methods of Blocking IL-4 and IL-13
There are several methods that can be used to block cytokine signalling. As mentioned, IL-4 can be
blocked directly with an anti IL-4 antibody or using a recombinant form of soluble IL-4Rα. Soluble
IL-4Rα binds to IL-4 and neutralises it, thus regulating IL-4 activity [38]. As IL-4 and IL-13 are
capable of signalling through the same Type II receptor, solely blocking IL-4 may not eﬀectively
block induction of a TH2 response. Dual inhibition of IL-4 and IL-13 can be achieved by blocking
the receptor itself. One example involves the use of antisense oligonucleotides which hinders IL-4
and IL-13 signalling by downregulating the expression of the IL-4Rα receptor [38]. Ripple et al [40]
demonstrated that multiple i.n administrations of an antisense oligonucleotide speciﬁc for IL-4Rα
during primary RSV infection reduced disease severity when mice were reinfected as adults. In
this thesis, we attempt to block the IL-4Rα chain using Anti-Mouse CD124 (clone mIL4R-M1, BD
Biosciences), see Figure 1.7 for an illustrated mechanism of action. Anti-CD124 is a monoclonal
antibody that binds to CD124, the α subunit of the murine IL-4 receptor, thereby inhibiting IL-4
and IL-13 signalling.

Analytical Techniques
In this thesis, IL-4 and IL-13 were blocked indirectly by a single i.p injection of the rat monoclonal
anti-mouse CD124 antibody (mIL4R-M1) during primary i.n live RSV infection of neonatal BAL-
B/c mice. The mice were reinfected at week 8 and the eﬀect of IL-4Rα blockade on the murine
immune system was characterised using ﬂow cytometry, ELISA, and qPCR.
2.1
Flow Cytometry
Flow cytometry is an important analytical method within the ﬁeld of immunology for the study of
single cells. The technique is based on the principle of light scattering and ﬂuorescence emission
[43]. The excitation source is a laser beam which scatters light from moving particles (cells) and
ﬂuorescence emission occurs from excitation of ﬂuorochromes conjugated to antibodies that are
speciﬁc for a distinguishing cell marker [43]. In this thesis, ﬂow cytometry was used to characterise
the cellular immune response to RSV infection as well as investigating the impact of blocking the
IL-4 receptor on the cellular immune environment. The ﬂow cytometer used in this project was a
non-sorting BDLSR Fortessa. The main components of the instrument include the ﬂuidics, optics
(excitation and collection), and detectors as outlined in Figure 2.1. The ﬂuidics system directs cells
in solution to a light source. Figure 2.1 shows cells in solution being directed to the interrogation
point where the laser beam is focused by excitation optics onto the cells while the collection optics
transfer the scattered light to the detector unit, converting the signal into a digital output [43].
As mentioned, lasers are the excitation source and lenses are used to direct the laser beam
towards the stream of cells.
When the laser beam collides with a cell, light is scattered in a
manner as depicted in Figure 2.2. Two types of light scatter occur, forward scatter (FSC) and side

2.2
Cytokine Detection using ELISA
In this thesis, cytokines from supernatant following intracellular restimulation and lung homogenate
were analysed using enzyme-linked immunosorbant assays (ELISA). ELISA is a useful for the
quantitative measurement of antigens, such as cytokines, in biological samples [44]. Used alongside
ﬂow cytometry, it provides an additional characterisation of the inﬂammatory cytokine response
towards RSV infection.
While several variations of ELISA exist, a sandwich ELISA was used for the above applications.
17
==PAGE:32==
The basic principle of a sandwich ELISA involves detection of a target molecule bound between a
capture and detection antibody that recognises speciﬁc epitopes on the target, see Figure 2.3. The
detection antibody is often conjugated to an enzyme (horse radish peroxidase (HRP)). In other
cases, the detection antibody is conjugated to biotin and a volume of strepdavidin bound to HRP
must be added in solution where strepdavidin will form a non-covalent bond with biotin [44]. In both
cases, it is the enzyme that reacts with an added chromogenic substrate to produce a colour change
from blue to yellow, which is measurable by a plate reader [44]. The colour change can be directly
correlated to the concentration of target antigen bound to the capture and detection antibodies.
The higher the concentration of antigen, the higher the signal recorded. The value obtained is
reported as the optical density value (OD). Analysis of the data is carried out by generating a
standard curve to extrapolate the unknown concentration. The general procedure involves coating
a high binding microtitre plate with a capture antibody that will recognise a speciﬁc epitope on
the cytokine of interest. The sample containing an unknown amount of cytokine is added followed
by a detection antibody which will recognise a diﬀerent epitope on the target cytokine.

2.3
Quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction (qPCR)
Quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) is a convenient method to quantify messenger RNA
(mRNA) which can indicate the upregulation of target genes. In this thesis, qPCR was used to
characterise the gene expression of RSV virus L-gene (large polymerase subunit L, a polymerase
18
==PAGE:33==
protein). qPCR was also used for the detection of mucous related genes muc5ac, which encodes
for mucin 5AC and is linked to mucous production, and gob5 which is also associated with mucous
hyperproduction and goblet cell metaplasia [45, 33].
In order to quantify mRNA associated with the genes, RNA must ﬁrst be extracted from
lung tissue and reverse transcribed to DNA sequences that are complementary to mRNA (i.e
complementary DNA, cDNA). The principle behind qPCR involves detecting ampliﬁed cDNA
targets (i.e. amplicons) in a sample by measuring the emission of a ﬂuorescent reporter dye which
emits light upon excitation [44].
The amplicon is quantiﬁed by measuring the ﬂuorescence which increases proportionally with
the ampliﬁed PCR product. Quantiﬁcation is based on comparing the number of cycles required to
reach a speciﬁc threshold of ﬂuorescence (the point at which the ﬂuorescence is signiﬁcantly above
the baseline or background) [44]. The cycles include three steps to amplify the target DNA:
1. Denaturation - DNA double strands are heated causing hydrogen bonds to break and
generating single stranded DNA.
2. Annealing - The temperature is lowered to allow primers to bind to the template DNA.
3. Elongation - The temperature increases once more and a new strand of DNA is synthesised
by Taq polymerase.
If a sample contains many copies of the target cDNA template, fewer PCR cycles are required
to pass the threshold.
In this way, higher cycle numbers indicate less target cDNA and fewer
cycles reﬂect greater cDNA. The quantiﬁcation cycle (Cq) value is deﬁned as the number of cycles
required to reach the ﬂuorescence threshold signal [44]. Using the Cq value, gene expression can
be quantiﬁed by two methods; absolute quantiﬁcation (standard curve) or relative quantiﬁcation
(delta Cq method). Relative quantiﬁcation is based on using internal reference genes such as HPRT,
which was used in this thesis. A reference gene is one that is expressed across all samples despite
treatment (e.g.
infected vs uninfected), and is used to normalise the gene expression between
samples using the delta Cq method: 2(δCq).
The detection method used in this thesis was ﬂuorescent probes based on the reporter dye, FAM
(6-carboxyﬂuorescein). Fluorescent probes bind to speciﬁc sequences within the amplicon which

products [44]. The principle as depicted by Figure 2.4 is based on a probe containing a ﬂuorescent
label on one end and a quencher molecule on the opposite end. Unbound probe molecules are
not detected as the quencher prevents emission of ﬂuorescence [44].
Once a probe binds to a
target sequence, ﬂuorochromes are released from quenchers due to degradation of bound probe
molecules by DNA polymerase [44]. Ampliﬁcation of a speciﬁc product is directly proportional to

4.1.1
RSV Induced Enhanced Disease upon Reinfection
The infection model used in this thesis is based on previous research which has shown that the
age of primary infection can lead to enhanced disease upon reinfection of adult BALB/c mice. We
use this model to capture the immunopathology that RSV infection induces in human infants, and
explore possible interventions. The timeline of the infection model is shown in Figure 4.1. Seven
day old pups were infected with RSV and reinfected 8 weeks post primary infection. To explore
the eﬀect of neonatally imprinted immune memory on disease severity, we also infected adult mice
(8-weeks-old) with RSV. To investigate the eﬀects of IL-4Rα blockade on the pathophysiology in
reinfected mice, anti-IL-4Rα was administered in a single i.p injection 4 days after primary infection
of the pups. The mice were weighed each day including on the day of euthanisation. At 5 days
post-reinfection, we euthanised the mice and evaluated the pulmonary pathology by ﬂow cytometry.
The viral load after acute adult infection and reinfections was quantiﬁed by qPCR.
Disease severity in response to RSV reinfection is characterised by signiﬁcant weight loss,
whereas acute adult infected mice exhibit minimal weight loss [46]. It has also been shown that
mice infected in the ﬁrst week of life have increased cellularity upon reinfection, which can be
correlated to weight loss and exacerbated inﬂammation [46]. The results in Figure 4.1B align with
such ﬁndings, where it is shown that mice infected as neonates in both RSV(isotype)/RSV and
RSV(anti-IL-4Rα)/RSV develop severe illness, with weight loss up to 15% on day 5 post reinfection.
By contrast, mice undergoing acute adult infection (Mock/RSV) display weight loss of less than
5% of their initial weight. During reinfection, mice infected at 7-days-old, (RSV(isotype)/RSV and

4.1.2
The Myeloid Response
Eosinophils are a hallmark of a Type II response and their diﬀerentiation is driven by the Type
II cytokine, IL-5. Upon activation, eosinophils release lipid and protein mediators which cause
mucous secretion, airway hyper-responsiveness, and airway remodeling. Evidence of eosinophils
during RSV infection includes the observation of pulmonary eosinophilia in fatal cases from the
formalin-inactivated RSV vaccine trial in the late 60s. In addition, Culley et al [46] showed that
neonatal mice infected at 7-days-old recruit more eosinophils in the bronchiolar lavage (BAL) ﬂuid
compared with acute adult infected mice. Corresponding with these ﬁndings, results in Figure
4.2A show a moderate increase in the number of eosinophils in the lung of RSV(isotype)/RSV and
RSV(anti-IL-4Rα)/RSV mice when compared with acute adult infected mice (Mock/RSV).
Neutrophils are recruited to the lung during RSV induced bronchiolitis in infancy, but their
role (protective or pathogenic) in RSV infection is not determined [50]. Figure 4.2B shows that
all infected mice experienced some level of neutrophil recruitment to the lungs, but there was no
diﬀerence between reinfected mice and acute adult infected mice. The alveolar macrophage response
was also examined as they are important producers of Type I IFNs during viral infections. It
was found that during acute adult infection and secondary RSV infections, alveolar macrophages
are signiﬁcantly depleted in the lung tissue, as shown in Figure 4.2C. There was no statistical
diﬀerence between RSV(isotype)/RSV and RSV(anti-IL-4Rα)/RSV in eosinophil, neutrophil, or
alveolar macrophage numbers. These results suggest that a single treatment with anti-IL-4Rα is
not suﬃcient to modulate the recruitment of innate inﬂammatory cells during RSV reinfection.

4.6
The Neonatal Response to Primary RSV Infection
Our results from the adult infection model correspond with literature which demonstrate that
mice previously infected as neonates experience signiﬁcant weight loss, increased cellularity, and
an enhanced eﬀector T cell presence upon reinfection. We found that blocking IL-4Rα during the
primary infection does not alter this response in vivo. To study the immune pathology of primary
neonatal RSV infection, neonatal BALB/c mice were infected at 7-days-old and euthanised 1 week
later as described in Section 3.3. The timeline for neonatal experiments is outlined in Figure 4.15.
By examining the neonatal immune response to primary RSV infection, we may identify time-points
at which blocking IL-4 and IL-13 signaling might prove more eﬀective.
The lung tissue was handled in the same manner as the adult infections.
MLNs were not
collected for the neonatal readouts. Additionally, only two antibody panels were used in the ﬂow
cytometry staining; Myeloid and T cell. ELISA was used for cytokine detection in restimulated
supernatant and qPCR was carried out on the same genes discussed throughout this thesis: RSV
L-gene, muc5ac, and gob5. The left lung lobes were also homogenised and cytokine analysis will
take place at a later date.

not lead to weight loss. Also unlike adult infected mice, alveolar macrophages remain at steady
levels, as do eosinophils, and neutrophils, see Figure 4.16B-D.

Type II bias during neonatal infection. The ELISAs will be repeated on the supernatant collected
from the repeat experiment to verify the ﬁndings shown here.

Discussion
RSV is one of the leading causes of hospitalisation and mortality due to severe lower respiratory
tract infection (LRTI) [1]. Palivizumab is an antibody against RSV F protein and is the only
available prophylactic treatment given to high-risk infants to prevent RSV infection. The absence
of an eﬀective vaccine against RSV highlights the complexity of the infantile response to RSV
infections, which is linked to a Type II immune environment. This thesis aimed to characterise
the immune response to RSV infection using TH2 biased BALB/c mice that were primed with
RSV at 1 week old, and reinfected as adults. Furthermore, as Type II cytokines IL-4 and IL-13
are linked with severe disease, an anti-IL-4Rα antibody was used in an attempt to block IL-4 and
IL-13 signalling.
This thesis is the ﬁrst example of blocking IL-4Rα with a monoclonal antibody during an
RSV reinfection model. The infection model was ﬁrst described by Culley et al [46], where it was
demonstrated that the outcome of secondary RSV infections is highly dependent on age at ﬁrst
infection i.e. mice infected as neonates develop enhanced disease upon reinfection. This model
has been used to recapitulate the severe disease often observed in hospitalised infants with RSV.
Ripple et al [40] showed that down-regulating IL-4Rα on dendritic cells and epithelial cells using
an anti-sense oligonucleotide, reduced the TH2 response to RSV and skewed the response to a more
TH1 biased environment. Further inspiration for this thesis was drawn from the dual blockade of
IL-4 and IL-13 using the human monoclonal antibody, Dupilumab, on humanised mice (Il4rahu/hu).
Dupilumab broadly blocked TH2 responses in a murine allergy model (House dust mite (HDM)
challenge). As such, we hypothesised that blocking IL-4Rα in a murine RSV model might also
attenuate the TH2 response observed in RSV reinfections.
62
==PAGE:77==
The results from this thesis align with previous research which has shown that mice exposed to
RSV infection during the ﬁrst week of life and re-exposed as adults experience enhanced disease.
The disease severity in mice is characterised by weight loss and an increase in overall cellularity in
lung tissue. The reinfected mice in both RSV(isotype)/RSV and RSV(anti-IL-4Rα)/RSV groups
used in this thesis experienced signiﬁcant weight loss during reinfection compared to acute adult
infected mice (Mock/RSV). In addition, we showed that reinfected mice have lower viral replication
compared with adult infected mice. This indicates that mice develop some level of immunological
memory to the virus, and enhanced disease upon reinfection is caused by the hosts immune re-
sponse and not viral load. While we were able to demonstrate a successful lung pathology model,
our results indicated that a single i.p injection of anti-IL-4Rα was not suﬃcient to reduce disease
severity induced by the immunopathology. The reason for such results may be explained by the
pharmacokinetics of the monoclonal antibody. More speciﬁcally, single bolus injections of antibod-
ies are often rapidly cleared from the body. A high circulating in vivo half-life is optimal to reduce
the number of times an antibody has to be administered [52]. If the antibody used for this thesis
has a short-lived serum half-life, it could be argued whether the antibody had adequate time to
arrive in the lymph nodes to successfully block cells that express IL-4Rα, before being excreted.
As pharmacokinetic data of mIL4R-M1 is not available, it is not possible to deﬁnitively conclude
that rapid clearance is the reason why the experiment failed to interfere with the immune response
in reinfected mice.
The monoclonal antibody, mIL4R-M1, has not been used previously to modulate TH2 responses
in mice. However, there are several studies which have used a neutralising monoclonal antibody
directly against IL-4 or IL-13 in order to reduce RSV associated immunopathology. As discussed
in Section 1.5.2, Tang et al [14] blocked IL-4 signalling by three to ﬁve injections of anti-IL-4
monoclonal antibody (11B11) in BALB/c mice prior to immunisation with formalin-inactivated
RSV. From these results, blocking IL-4 directly reduced weight loss upon reinfection as well as
enhancing expression of IFN-γ mRNA. IL-13 signaling has been blocked using an anti-mIL-13 Ab
by Tekkanat et al [53] to demonstrate the role of IL-13 in airway hyper-responsiveness during RSV
infection. The antibody was administered every 2 days post-infection until the day of euthanisation.
These examples enforce the idea that several injections of the blocking antibody may be required
63
==PAGE:78==
to modulate the immune response. It is conceivable that for a signiﬁcant outcome to be achieved
in our infection model, multiple injections prior to infection and/or after infection would be needed
to interfere with a TH2 environment and reduce disease severity.
Another important aspect of this thesis was to characterise the immune response to RSV infec-
tions in both adult and neonatal mice. As others have shown, reinfection in mice previously infected
as neonates lead to enhanced recruitment of immune cells in the lungs [31]. Our results also showed
a moderate increase in eosinophils and neutrophils in the lung tissue, which is commonly reported
in severe RSV disease. Less commonly reported is the signiﬁcant depletion of alveolar macrophages
during primary adult infection, and most notably during reinfection. Alveolar macrophages are
crucially located as a ﬁrst line of defense against pathogens and are a primary source of inﬂam-
matory cytokines such as Type I interferons (IFN-α/β), which limit viral replication. Kolli et al
[54] tracked the fate of alveolar macrophages following RSV infection, and also observed signiﬁcant
decrease in numbers just 6 hours after infection. Kolli et al [54] were able to determine that RSV
infects alveolar macrophages and induces a fast necrotic process, which may explain the depletion
seen in our results. In contrast to the adult infection groups, 1 week old mice appear to experience
no increase in innate cell numbers during viral challenge, and maintain steady levels of eosinophils,
neutrophils, and alveolar macrophages. This corresponds with data from Tregoning et al [31] who
have also shown neonates display minimal illness.
Tang et al [14] demonstrated that blocking IL-4 before and after RSV infection, reduced weight
loss upon secondary infection compared to control mice. By this measure, successfully blocking of
IL-4Rα with the correct dosage may have also reduced weight loss of the reinfected mice. Ripple
et al [40] showed that downregulation of IL-4Rα with an anti-sense oligonucleotide led to some
reduction of eosinophils in the BAL ﬂuid of reinfected mice. Neutrophils were not aﬀected by
the downregulation of IL-4Rα, but a more signiﬁcant decrease was observed in the lymphocytes.
Additional tests with IL-4Rα are required to determine if blocking the receptor may impact weight
loss or cellularity in lung tissue during reinfection.
In reinfected mice, there is enhanced recruitment of CD4 and CD8 T cells during reinfection.
The enhanced disease we observe may partly be caused by the CD8 and CD4 response in accordance
with Tregoning et al [31]. The authors have shown that depletion of CD8 T cells inhibited weight
64
==PAGE:79==
loss upon reinfection as well as reduced cellularity of BAL ﬂuid during secondary challenge. These
results indicate that eﬀector memory CD8 T cells contribute to the disease pathology observed here
and in the literature during RSV reinfection. Our neonatal results showed a signiﬁcant increase in
the frequency of eﬀector CD8 response during RSV infection. It is possible that there is a level
of dysregulation occuring within the neonatal CD8 response that leads to enhanced disease upon
reinfection.
While the CD8 response contributes to enhanced disease, RSV infection in mice and infants
is commonly described as a CD4 TH2 biased immune response, especially in severe cases.
In
our results, we did not ﬁnd a signiﬁcant increase in eﬀector TH2 cells, characterised by their
expression of ST2. In addition, the intracellular staining did not point to a general increase in
Type II cytokines in either the lung or MLN. The TH2 response in neonates reﬂected a similar
result, with no increase in TH2 eﬀector cells during infection, and no increase in intracellular IL-4
after restimulation. Instead, we saw that neonates are capable of producing signiﬁcant amounts
of IFN-γ during RSV infection, although they produce less IFN-γ than acute adult infected mice.
ELISAs will be carried out on restimulated supernatant from the repeat neonatal experiment to
substantiate these results. We did show that neonates have a higher frequency of ST2+ TH2 cells
compared with adults, which aligns with current literature that states neonates have a more TH2
skewed environment in the lung.
This thesis has previously discussed that RSV infections trigger IL-33 signalling, the ligand of
ST2. There is evidence which may help explain the observed reduction in ST2 upon RSV infection.
Receptor internalisation is often triggered by the binding of a ligand, and one study has shown ST2
is ubiquitinated and degraded in the proteosome upon IL-33 signalling [55]. If this is the case in
our infection model, a diﬀerent antibody may be more suited for the detection of TH2 cells within
the T cell panel. However, as Saravia et al [12] have successfully used ST2 staining in an RSV
infection model, it is important to also optimise our ST2 staining including using diﬀerent dilutions
of ST2 antibody during surface staining.
The results of this thesis also examined the presence of tissue resident memory (TRM) cells in
the lung. TRM cells are important for clearance of pathogens upon reinfection and are important
for reducing illness in RSV infections. Humans experience frequent RSV infections throughout
65
==PAGE:80==
their lifetime which hints at a lack of eﬀective immunological memory.
Our results exhibit an
accumulation in the numbers of TRMs during reinfection. Without the use of Tetramer staining,
our results cannot conclude the presence of RSV-speciﬁc memory driven T cells upon reinfection.
However, we know that mice develop some level of immunological memory as evidenced by the
reduced RSV L-gene expression shown in the qPCR results of reinfected mice.
The most signiﬁcant results to come from the experiments is the substantial depletion in the
frequency of regulatory T cells (Tregs) observed in the lung and MLN of reinfected mice. Regulatory
T cells are important for dampening immune responses and preventing autoimmunity. They also
play various roles during viral infections such as suppressing inﬂammation. Loebbermann et al
[56] have also demonstrated that Tregs are signiﬁcantly decreased in the lung tissue in response to
the formalin-inactivated RSV vaccine in mice. Despite this, the role of Tregs in RSV is not yet
determined. Possibly, Tregs become dysregulated during reinfection and are unable to attenuate
the inﬂammatory response.
As with the innate response, blocking IL-4Rα did not interfere with the T cell response upon
reinfection. Tang et al [14] demonstrated that blocking IL-4 enhanced the IFN-γ mRNA thus, by
blocking the receptor we may have seen enhanced TH1 environment or signiﬁcantly more IFN-γ
production. With that being said, our results also do not indicate an enhanced TH2 response. This
is highlighted by the insigniﬁcant changes in IL-5, IL-13, and IL-4 levels in intracellular staining and
lung homogenates. As such, it is not possible to determine the eﬀects of IL-4Rα blockade on either
IFN-γ production or Type II cytokines. However, it is important to note that the intracellular
ﬂow cytometry staining was relatively weak for the TH2 cytokines. Further optimisation of the
restimulation process could improve on our current results. It is also possible that the ELISA
kits for IL-4 and IL-5 were not sensitive enough to detect changes in the levels of cytokine in the
homogenate. We will run additional IL-13 ELISAs on the lung homogenate as well as the serum
samples to detect systemic changes during reinfection. Additionally, GATA3, the transcription
factor for TH2 cells could be added to the intracellular panel for further identiﬁcation of eﬀector
TH2 subsets. qPCR for the detection of IFN-γ and IL-4 mRNA might oﬀer more sensitive detection
of even small changes in the immune environment compared with ELISA. Age at initial infection
is important for determining the outcome of secondary infections. At post natal day 7, the murine
66
==PAGE:81==
immune environment begins to switch from a TH2 to a TH1 environment.
It is possible that
repeating our experiments with earlier primary infections may correlate with a more pronounced
TH2 recall during secondary infection that is often reported in literature. It is conceivable that
examining the lung pathology at day 5 is either too late or too early to identify peak concentrations
of Type I and Type II cytokines. Particularly as the qPCR results show that by day 5, reinfected
mice have barely detectable levels of the virus in the lung tissue. While, it is likely that eﬀector cells
continue to produce inﬂammatory cytokines even when the viral expression is low, the time-point
for examination may be critical in characterising the immune response during RSV reinfections.
We previously addressed the conﬂicting IFN-γ results from both ﬂow cytometry and intracellular
ELISA between the ﬁrst and second experiments reported in this thesis. Speciﬁcally, the IFN-γ
production in the reinfected mice appeared to be decreased in the second experiment as evidenced
by the ﬂow cytometry plots and the supernatant used for ELISA. However, Tregoning et al [31]
report increased IFN-γ production upon reinfection, and we also see this from the intracellular ﬂow
cytometry results in our ﬁrst experiment, see Figure 4.7 Lung A for reference. It is reasonable to
consider that technical error has occurred leading to misreported data and such contrasting results
between the two experimental days. In addition, the unstimulated controls had consistently high
signals. Based on this alone, it is problematic to report on the true nature of eﬀector subsets
identiﬁed in this thesis. Additional experiments are needed to verify the results.
Finally, qPCR results presented in this thesis demonstrated that in both neonatal and adult
RSV infections, the expression of muc5ac and gob5 were unaﬀected. Under homeostatic conditions,
muc5ac is barely expressed in mice which accounts for low expression levels in our results from both
Mock neonates and Mock adult mice. Over expression of gob5 is linked with goblet cell metaplasia
and mucin over production. The Type II cytokine, IL-13 is linked with mucous production by
signaling via goblet cells. As disease severity and airway obstruction has been linked with IL-13
production, a dual IL-4/IL-13 blockade such as anti-IL-4Rα may have inhibited mucous hyper-
secretion. Several studies have shown that the RSV A2 strain does not always induce IL-13 or
mucin expression in BALB/c mice. This may partly explain the unchanged expression of muc5ac
and gob5 in our results [57]. As previously discussed, PAS staining will provide further insights
into the mucous environment of RSV infected adults by conﬁrming mucin levels and goblet cell

==PAGE:82==
numbers. Furthermore, the IL-13 ELISAs on lung homogenates will provide further insights into
the lung immune environment during RSV infection.

Conclusions and Future Outlook
The response to RSV secondary infections in previously challenged neonatal mice has been com-
pared to the response in acute adult infection, as well as the primary response to RSV in neonatal
mice alone. The eﬀects of using an IL-4 and IL-13 blockade on the outcome of secondary reinfec-
tions was also examined. We have shown that secondary RSV infection results in enhanced disease,
characterised by weight loss and an inﬂux in cellularity in both innate cells and T cells. We have
also shown that neonatal mice exhibit minimal disease which is also in keeping with current liter-
ature [31]. Importantly, our results demonstrate that a single i.p injection of anti-IL-4Rα is not
suﬃcient to modulate the immune response during secondary RSV infection. However, there was
a general trend in the results that showed that reinfected mice receiving anti-IL-4Rα treatment
had consistently lower recruitment of immune cells to the lung, including eosinophils and CD4
lymphocytes. While not statistically signiﬁcant, repeating the experiments with diﬀerent doses
of antibody, as well as multiple injections of IL-4Rα may successfully interfere with the disease
outcome.
Additionally, we have taken whole lungs of neonates from 2-11 days post RSV infection and
we will run ELISAs to determine the peak concentrations of IL-4 and IL-13. This will allow us
to investigate if IL-4 and IL-13 should be blocked at earlier or later time-points. From a clinical
perspective, multiple injections of a therapeutic antibody are not desirable but experiments such
as these will provide valuable information on the role of IL-4 and IL-13 in RSV infections. We have
also shown that secondary RSV infections in BALB/c mice does not always result in an enhanced
TH2 environment. As mentioned in the Discussion, the murine immune environment begins to
switch from a TH2 to a TH1 environment at post natal day 7. The results of the lung homogenates
69
==PAGE:84==
above may identify earlier time points at which repeating the infection model may reﬂect a more
pronounced TH2 recall during secondary infection.
It would be beneﬁcial to experimentally prove that the IL-4Rα did in fact block the IL-4
receptor. One way of determining blocking could be by measuring the mean ﬂuorescence intensity
(MFI) of IL-4Rα on immune cells and epithelial cells by ﬂow cytometry. Alternatively, CD44-
CD62L[?] naive T cells could be puriﬁed and cultured under TH2 inducing conditions in the
presence or absence of anti-IL-4Rα antibody.
Matsuse et al [35] have demonstrated that multiple RSV infection in conjunction with allergen
sensitisation leads to chronic airway inﬂammation in mice. These results feed into the theory that
RSV infections in early life increase the risk of asthma and recurring wheeze in childhood. Future
work may involve investigating if dual blockade of IL-4 and IL-13 during primary RSV infection in
early life reduces sensitisation to common allergens such as house dust mite (HDM).
This thesis highlights the nuances and complex nature of the host immune response towards
RSV. While our results did not demonstrate immune modulation by anti-IL4Rα blockade, this
body of work has furthered our understanding of the immune response to RSV. We have achieved
the preliminary goals set out at the beginning of this thesis. Furthermore, we have successfully
established a lung pathology model which will enable continued research into facets of the immune
response during respiratory infection.
