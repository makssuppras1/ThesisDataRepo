Project Statement
Energy consumption related occupancy behavior is of importance regarding
building performance and of increasing interest in the literature with several
diﬀerent means of modeling occupancy behavior. We wish to investigate where
the current literature is and deﬁne a possible common framework for modeling
occupancy behavior and further how to estimate such models. The next purpose
is to use the deﬁned framework to modeling the occupancy behavior on one or
more dwellings by using a data set containing direct or indirect measurement of
occupancy behavior. The data set could contain multivariate variables regard-
ing occupancy behavior and demographic and/or socioeconomic information.
Proﬁles obtained by the modeling of occupancy behavior can enter building en-
ergy simulation tools in estimating building performance or enter as input for
energy demand forecasting.
The topics to be investigated in this thesis are listed below.
• Obtain a overview of the current literature describing occupancy behavior
in dwellings on energy consumption.
• Provide a framework for describing occupancy behavior in dwellings on
energy consumption.
– Markov chain
– Univariate measurements
– Multivariate measurements
– Demographic and/or socioeconomic information
• Model occupancy behavior in one or more dwellings.
– Estimation
– Validation
• Interpret the model and hereby give an explaination of the states of which
the occupant(s) can enter.

Abstract (English)
The residential sector is one of the most important sources of energy consumption and is greatly in-
ﬂuenced by the often under-recognized occupancy behavior. Even between identical buildings energy
consumption can vary greatly due to the occupants and their behavior. To overcome future global and
local energy issues, which is recognized in policies around the world, an accurate description of occu-
pancy behavior is vital. An accurate description of the eﬀect of occupants on the energy use will diminish
the diﬀerence between predicted and actual energy use of buildings which is of ever greater importance
in minimizing the excess energy production and maximizing the use of renewable energy sources. In
this thesis we provide a overview of the current literature regarding occupancy behavior which is often
inconsistent and lack a common language. We formulate a framework for modeling occupancy behav-
ior in which we propose a hierarchical model structure by the generalized state space model to include
population models and extending the current framework to include both multivariate and non-normal
distributional models. With readily smart metering data available as indirect observations of occupancy
behavior this framework is favorable to exploit. We investigate the use of indirect observations to describe
occupancy behavior by the use of indoor measurements of CO2 in a danish summer house. We propose
and validate a sophisticated model with 5 distinct states of occupancy behavior with time-dependent
transition probabilities. It is demonstrated that a simple 2 state ﬁrst order homogeneous Markov chain
is not suﬃcient in describing occupancy behavior and that the state vector should contain at least 4
states with time-dependent transition probabilities when using a ﬁrst order Markov chain in describing
occupancy behavior.

Purpose Statement
This Master thesis is written as the ﬁnal assignment in order to achieve the Master of Science in Engi-
neering (Mathematical Modelling and Computing) degree at the Technical University of Denmark.
The purpose of this thesis is to:
• Facilitate an overview of the current literature describing occupancy behavior in dwellings regarding
energy consumption.
• Provide a framework for describing occupancy behavior in dwellings regarding energy consumption.
• Develop a model to describe occupancy behavior in one or more dwellings regarding energy con-
sumption.
• Interpret the model and hereby give an interpretation of the states of which the occupants can
enter.
This thesis takes of in chapter 1 ’Motivation and Introduction’, were we introduce occupancy behav-
ior and the ﬁndings in this thesis. In chapter 2 ’The Model Space’, we loosely deﬁne the model space
considered of modeling occupancy behavior regarding energy consumption. In chapter 3 ’State of The
Art’, we facilitate an overview of state of the art models for occupancy behavior regarding energy con-
sumption, and compare these models to the model space deﬁned in chapter 2. In chapter 4 ’Theoretical
Approaches to Generalized State Space Models’, we deﬁne the framework for describing occupancy be-
havior in dwellings regarding energy consumption. This framework deﬁne the generalized state space
model in a mathematical manner. This leads us to chapter 5 ’Model Estimation’, where we deﬁne how
we estimate the model. Chapter 6 ’Model Validation’, concerns how to validate a considered model and
how to choose the ’best model’ among competitive models. In chapter 7 ’Data’ we present the data pro-
vided for modeling occupancy behavior. We use the data to model the occupancy behavior in the given
dwelling in a univariate setting in chapter 8 ’Modeling Occupancy Behavior - Univariate CO2 Levels’,
were we present the ﬁnal univariate model in section 8.7. In chapter 9 ’Modeling Occupancy Behavior -
Bi-variate’, we model the occupancy behavior in a multivariate setting, however fail to ﬁnd a suﬃcient
model.
We end this thesis with a discussion and a conclusion in chapter 10 and 11.
R code of implementation of models is provided in Appendix C.

Motivation and Introduction
Energy is expensive to buy and produce. If based on fossil fuels, energy production is polluting the envi-
ronment and is not sustainable since fossil fuels are not renewable. This is recognized in Danish policies
but also globally. The long-term goal for Danish energy policy1, is to be independent of fossil fuels by
2050, especially by focusing on lowering the energy consumption and increasing the use of renewable
energy.
Thus, energy optimization is of importance from both economic and environmental perspectives but it
is also important from a health perspective, which also aﬀect the energy use. Energy optimization and
hereby lowering the energy consumption, is good for both consumers and the environment e.g. price of
electricity, indoor climate, pollution and global warming. Further, in succeeding in energy optimization
we might also overcome future local and global energy issues such as providing renewable energy for the
growing population and use of electrical appliances.
The residential sector constitutes about 30% of the global energy consumption [Lopez-Rodriguez et al.,
2013], thus, a reduction in the energy consumption of the residential sector will have great impact on the
global energy consumption. An accurate description of occupant behavior is of great value in relation
to energy optimization, since the energy consumption in the residential sector is due to occupants and
their use of energy in their dwelling. The value will only increase in moving toward nearly zero energy
homes or in estimating energy demands.
In recognition of the signiﬁcance of energy use in buildings, the International Energy Agency (IEA), has
since 1977 been working on energy use in buildings.
In one of their researching programs, Annex 66, it is stated ...the inﬂuence of occupant behavior is
under-recognized or over-simpliﬁed in the design, construction, operation, and retroﬁt of buildings.2 In
the ’Annex 66 Text’ [Yan and Hong, 2014], it is found that even between identical dwellings energy
consumption show a large variability. Models often underestimate the impact of occupants in dwellings
due to simpliﬁcations used in describing occupancy behavior. This will lead to a poor performance in
prediction of energy consumption in dwellings and of the use of new technologies based on automatic
climatic resulting in an overproduction of energy and an unhealthy indoor climate.
To reasons above, like Annex 66, we are interested in understanding the inﬂuence of occupancy behavior
on building performance. In researching energy optimization we, too, work with the term occupancy
behavior. Occupancy behavior is occupants’ presence, movements and interactions with appliances in a
dwelling.
In building performance, which is energy consumption, durability, air quality, thermal comfort and mois-
ture sources, occupancy behavior plays a signiﬁcant role. If we can understand occupancy behavior
in dwellings, we can improve building performance. Thus, we are interested in obtaining proﬁles
of occupancy behavior that describes the presence of occupants and their associated activities. These
proﬁles can be used as inputs in maximizing building performance and minimizing excess energy. Know-
ing the occupancy behavior, the energy supplier, can size the energy supply and, thus, optimize the

production of energy. Further, in building houses, these proﬁles could also be used as inputs in building
energy simulation tools, in estimating building performance, leading to better performance in estimating
energy consumption or the indoor climate.
In the transition toward climate neutrality, the point in which there is balance between emissions and the
absorptive capacity of the Earth, we transition from non-stochastic fossil fuels to stochastic renewable
energy such as wind, water and solar energy. Since these energy sources are stochastic, the energy supply
can not easily be altered. Thus, the sizing of the energy demand require a more precise description of the
distribution of energy consumption which is greatly impacted by the occupancy behavior. Occupancy
behavior, thus, is as important as ever.
Approach to Occupancy Behavior
Energy consumption can occur when no occupants are present but is more probable when occupants
are present, thus, there is a relation between the presence of occupants and energy consumption. The
approaches of how to model the presence and the related energy use in dwellings are widespread in the
current literature.
In existent literature3 occupancy behavior has been divided into three categories. Purpose-driven relates
to entering/leaving a room, consuming domestic hot water, using an appliance or other related behavior
performed with a purpose by the occupant. Comfort-related is the adjustment of indoor climate for
obtaining comfort by the occupant, such as opening a window, adjusting a thermostat, turning on lights
and closing the blinds. Not all interaction between the occupant and the dwelling is intentional (purpose
or comfort) and are considered random. Even though an event is purpose-driven or comfort-related it
still has a stochastic nature as the time of event is random.
This means, that two identical dwellings would yield diﬀerent proﬁles due to the occupants behavior.
Along the same line, demographics and socioeconomic aspects could be factors in describing the variation
in occupancy behavior. The complex and stochastic nature of occupants complicates the modeling of
energy consumption in dwellings. Thus, a standardized proﬁle of occupancy behavior is not suﬃcient as
input in models on energy consumption. An accurate description of occupancy behavior is needed.
Current literature on modeling occupancy behavior is often inconsistent and lack a common language.
To the best of our knowledge there does not a framework in the current literature collecting a broad
class of models to model occupancy behavior. Thus, a key goal of this thesis is to obtain an overview of
the current approaches and provide such a framework.
Process and Applied Model
In this thesis, we suggest an approach for dealing with occupancy behavior in dwellings through utilizing
the generalized state space model (GSSM), deﬁned in chapter 4, in the strive for a common framework
for modeling occupancy behavior and to obtain proﬁles hereof.
The GSSM is an extension of the linear Gaussian state space model, [Madsen, 2007], allowing for non-
linear and non-Gaussian modeling but is still a Hidden Markov Model (HMM).
This framework can include both multivariate and non-normal distributional models simultaneously in
the modeling of occupancy behavior. For utilizing demographic and/or socioeconomic information re-
garding occupants, we have suggested a mixed eﬀect setup through the GSSM to allow for population
diﬀerences, such as random eﬀects, in modeling occupancy behavior.
This provide a way to model
the variation between groups of demographic and socioeconomic heritage. Allowing more than a small
number of random eﬀects will make the computation infeasible, hence, we have suggested to implement
this scenario through the use of the Laplace approximation, [Madsen, 2007]. We hope this give rise
to upcoming models including both a multivariate and hierarchical form in the modeling of occupancy
behavior.

It is diﬃcult to obtain direct data regarding occupancy behavior, the activity and whereabouts of oc-
cupants over a prolonged period. However, smart metering data, which are indirect measurements of
occupancy behavior, are readily available. One example hereof would be the electricity consumption
in a dwelling. In this thesis we were provided indoor and outdoor weather information along with the
indoor CO2 and noise levels in a summer house. The indoor CO2 and noise levels will serve as indirect
measurements of occupancy behavior.
We have focused on the CO2 levels as the response variable since we believe that this is the best indicator
of presence of the provided variables. One question arising here is whether or not we can use indoor
CO2 levels to classify and describe occupancy behavior. While we are not interested in the CO2 levels
themselves, we are interested in the states of the occupants. Since the states are hidden (not observable)
in a HMM the states need interpretation in order to be utilized, which is the focus of this thesis.
In our overview of the current literature, in relation to occupancy behavior, we have yet to ﬁnd the
use of CO2 levels. Neither has including more than one (indirect) response variable at each time point.
Hence, we are in possession of multivariate indirect measurements of occupancy behavior. This gives
an opportunity to model occupancy behavior through indirect measurements and classify behavior by
unobservable states in the framework of a Hidden Markov Model, on a response variable not applied
before in the occupancy behavior framework.
Findings
This thesis provides a current literature overview of modeling occupancy behavior and propose a frame-
work that collect many approaches hereof. Further, this thesis, utilize this framework and ﬁnd that at
least four states should be used in describing occupancy behavior and that time dependence is important
to account for since these probabilities change over the course of the day. We propose to use ﬁve diﬀerent
states to describe occupancy behavior based on the measurements of indoor CO2 levels in a summer
house in Denmark.
The occupancy behavior proﬁle is the conglomerate of these ﬁve distinct states and their transition prob-
abilities. The proﬁle shows which states that are probable over the course of the day. Along with the
transition probabilities of the states, they describe the probability of whether or not the occupants are
active, and at what time. These proﬁles could be used in optimization of building performance such as
energy minimization.
Further, applying global decoding, to determine the most likely sequence of states given the model, and
comparing this sequence of states with the measured indoor noise levels we have succeeded in interpreting
the states.
Thus, we have succeeded in describing occupancy behavior using the measured indoor CO2 levels and
obtained a proﬁle over the course of the day. This gives us a broader understanding of occupancy be-
havior which can be use in improving building performance.
The ﬁve distinct states used, with time dependent transition probabilities, of which the occupants can
enter are classiﬁed as
• Absence or sleeping
• Long term absence
• Outdoor interaction
• Presence with high activity
• Presence with low activity and the occupants have been present for a long term
Further, we investigated including both the CO2 and noise levels as the response variables. Propitious
results were found linking the exponential distribution to the noise levels of the above 5 states. However,
an accurate description of occupancy behavior by this approach needs further work.

presence - [Page et al., 2008]

[Page et al., 2008] formulates an algorithm to simulate occupancy behavior based on inhomogeneous
(ﬁrst-order) HMM occasionally interrupted by periods of long absence. Presence (or absence) of one
occupant is simulated independently of other occupants within a zone of a building. If more occupants
share an oﬃce each zone is multiplied or multiple simulations are accumulated. The model is validated
on private oﬃces, however, the authors claim that it will hold for any type of building.
The authors emphasize that high quality climate data is available but data linked to the occupant or the
behavior hereof is scare. They enforce the importance of the occupants within the building and the eﬀect
hereof. The inﬂuence of occupants on the building they occupy can later be broken down into several
means of interaction described in [Robinson, 2006]. The focus of the article is therefore on the presence.
The data used for estimating the transition probabilities was collected from December 2001 until January
2006 in 20 ’zones’ of an oﬃce building but only 2 years of the data was usable. Times of arrival, lunch
break and departure were drawn from the empirical distributions.
Xt is deﬁned as whether the occupant is in the zone or not at time t. In their framework they assume
independence between zones and occupants and does not model movement between zones. The authors
account for this in the paper: The sensor only recognizes two states of presence: occupied or vacant,
and can therefore not distinguish whether the zone is multiply occupied or not. This however is not a
6
==PAGE:21==
problem, since the presence of guests in an oﬃce is dependent upon whether the owner of that oﬃce is
present so that the owner-occupant presence is continuously accounted for. [Page et al., 2008]
They have a argument here but there are many situations where we believe that this is not valid. The
same occupant might be simulated to be in two or more zones at the same time. This could be their
own oﬃce, hallway and coﬀee lounge which would lead to wrong conclusions.
Their assumption makes the modeling task much easier and the assumption of independence between
occupants is likely to be suﬃcient. However, modeling/simulating with the assumption of independence
between zones seems like a dangerous choice.
The weekdays and weekends are handled diﬀerently, which is of larger importance in the case of com-
mercial buildings due the nature that most oﬃce workers work during the weekdays. This is opposed to
[Liisberg et al., 2016] where weekdays and weekends are handled the same, however in that work they
considered households, where the diﬀerence between weekdays and weekends are to be less extreme.
The authors describe that energy proﬁles of the weekday, weekends and peak load are of great interest.
These can be obtained by considering a speciﬁc type of building, including the heat gain (metabolic heat
gain, receptacle load, lighting load) by the presence of the occupant in the building. These proﬁles may
be simulated or based on data. A mentioned weakness of this is the over-simpliﬁcation which gives an
averaged behavior and does not take the seasonal variations into account.
Both Wang [Wang et al., 2005] (non-homogeneous Poisson process) and the model suggested by [Page
et al., 2008] found that intermediate presence (at work but in and out of the oﬃce) does not follow an
exponential distribution. Further, that each occupant has their own behavior.
Many of the former simulation tools base intermediate presence on the exponential distribution and the
ﬁrst arrival, lunch break and last departure on the normal distribution which led to simple and elegant
model with day to day variation, however, it does not resemble the complexity of real occupant behavior.
In [Page et al., 2008] they conclude: Periods of presence cannot be reproduced by an exponential distribu-
tion with a homogeneous coeﬃcient, and times of arrival, of departure as well as absences during lunch
breaks are not normally distributed.
[Yamaguchi et al., 2003] investigated by the use of computers a model to simulate working states to
derive the heat and electrical load. As in [Wang et al., 2005] they also assume time-independence. The
sequence is replaced by a Markov chain using the Poisson distribution, however closely connected to the
approach in [Wang et al., 2005]. A diﬀerence is that the times of arrival/departures follows an empirical
distribution instead of the normal distribution. However, they still face the ﬂaw that the time spent in a
working state is not time-independent. [Page et al., 2008] suggest instead to use a proﬁle of probability
of presence as input to the Markov chain and hereby obtain intermediate periods of presence and ab-
sence distributed exponentially with a time-dependent coeﬃcient as well as the ﬂuctuations of arrivals,
departures and typical breaks.
The authors also ﬁnd that the behavior of occupants is usually very diﬀerent at their ﬁrst arrival and
last departure than during any intermediate arrivals and departures.
One major advantage of the proposed algorithm, by comparison to the former simulation tools on the
market, is the better handling of long absence (corresponding to business trips, leaves due to sickness,
holidays, etc.) and the handling of overtime/early meet in. This leads to a better estimation of the total
yearly presence and associated energy consumption. However, even though their algorithm is superior
to the former, it still underestimates presence and absence.
In the concluding remarks the authors claim that simulation tools reproduce the deterministic physical
behavior of buildings with ever greater detail but the former simpliﬁed modeling of occupancy behavior
led to errors in predictions of the peak demand in energy resources of a building.

3.2
Concluding Remarks on State of The Art
Occupancy behavior play a signiﬁcant role in building performance and hereby energy consumption.
Advance in the modeling of occupancy behavior is ongoing, however, we found that detailed information
on the interaction between occupants and their respective dwellings is scarce.
There is great diversity between occupants and how they use their dwelling. The variation in energy
consumption is aﬀected by the occupants and type of the occupants (e.g. daily routine, time of day,
marital status, kids, employment, location, etc.). This was supported in e.g. [Haldi and Robinson, 2011],
where they found that there is an impact of occupancy behavior on buildings energy demands and even
greater impact by that of the individuals diversity. Further, in the ’Annex 66 Text’ [Yan and Hong, 2014]
it was found that even between identical dwellings energy consumption show a large variability.
Deterministic physical behavior of buildings is modeled with great detail. As also mentioned in the
introduction, occupancy behavior in dwellings is often under-recognized or oversimpliﬁed although the
signiﬁcance. It is often the case, in order to simplify and account for occupancy behavior, to use a stan-
dard proﬁle in simulations of building performance. This lead to sub optimal performance. Neglecting
the inﬂuence of occupancy behavior will lead to a poor performance in modeling or prediction of energy
consumption in dwellings.
Criticism of ﬁrst-order homogen Markov
We found that in order to describe occupancy behavior we need other tools than using ﬁxed distributions
such as the exponential and normal distribution in describing the presence, arrival and departure of occu-
pants. Also, for most cases in modeling occupancy behavior, a ﬁrst order 2-state homogeneous Markov
chain is not suﬃcient to describe the occupancy behavior. Even sometimes a 2-state inhomogeneous
Markov chain is too simple.
As described in [Page et al., 2008] and [Liisberg et al., 2016] which are summarized above, a very im-
portant notion is that time independence cannot be assumed.
Hence, the Markov chain(s) must be
time-varying and likely also depend on other variables. Further, if one model arrival, lunch or departure
times, one should not approximate these times by a normal distribution.
[Liisberg et al., 2016] found that extending the number of states led to not necessarily very diﬀerent states
but "intermediate" states the occupant had to "visit" before reaching another state. E.g. an occupant
does not go directly from inactivity (sleep) directly to absent (leave for work), or open their computer
with a cop of coﬀee and leave the next instant. There is one or more intermediate states before departure
in order to get ready to depart.
This is in coherence with the ﬁndings that ﬁxed distributions should not be used and in the work of
[Andersen et al., 2014] that the inhomogen model perform superior to the homogen model.
In general many authors have used a inhomogeneous Markov chain which seem to become consensus in
the literature. The use of a homogeneous Markov chain for occupancy behavior is subject to criticism.
The daily proﬁle of a state in a Markov chain describing occupancy behavior, e.g. presence, will vary
over the course of the day. E.g. the probability of an occupant being present at night is larger than at
the middle of the day on a work day.
[Wilke, 2013] used a higher order Markov chain since they stated activity durations cannot be captured
coherently using a ﬁrst-order homogen model. However, the inhomogen ﬁrst order model were superior
to the higher order model. They recommend to use a ﬁrst order approach iﬀ. there is no particular
importance of durantions of presence or absence but enforce that the distribution of the absence and
presence should be modeled by the higher order approach. [McKenna et al., 2015] also suggest that a
higher order model should be tried.
Opposed to the work in [Wilke, 2013], the work of [Aerts et al., 2014] insisted on transition probabilities
and duration probabilities rather than ﬁtted survival distribution. However, both authors accounted for
16
==PAGE:31==
time dependence.
Our reasoning is that the ﬁrst-order Markov approach is suﬃcient, when using more than two states,
but time dependence is critical. Further, a second order Markov chain can be written as a ﬁrst order
by extending the state vector, see section 4.2.1. Thus, using more than two states, might, correspond to
using a higher order Markov chain.
One way of solving the duration issue stated in [Wilke, 2013] might be to extend the state vector to
more than the two states of presence and absence. Thus, criticisms is raised against the homogen models
when using a ﬁrst order Markov chain with only two states. From our ﬁndings the Markov chain should
be inhomogeneous.
Zone Independence
For a simulation scheme of presence, great work is done in [Page et al., 2008]. However, we ﬁnd it doubtful
that the same assumptions of zone independence will hold for a residential building as the authors claim.
In a residential building with several rooms an occupant have a large probability of presence in, compared
to an oﬃce building where the occupants presence is most likely in their own oﬃce. Take for example
an occupant living alone (single) and consider his/hers residence. When the occupant is at home, the
kitchen and living room would have perfect or almost perfect correlation, depending on the time of day
and the size of the residence. To model the presence of the occupant in the kitchen and the living room
independently is absurd.
3.2.1
Population Models in Occupancy Behavior
Demographic and/or socioeconomic information is of importance in modeling of occupancy behavior.
Articles reviewed in this thesis, investigating the inﬂuence by demographic and/or socioeconomic in-
formation are all in coherence with the importance of demographic and/or socioeconomic information
in modeling occupancy behavior. Many authors, however, only conclude that it should be accounted
for but does not include in the modeling. [Zaraket et al., 2015] and [Wilke, 2013] are the only studies
where demographic and/or socioeconomic information is actually included. [Wilke, 2013] proposed to
apply the information in the transition probabilities through logistic regression. We did not ﬁnd a clear
description of how the information is utilized in [Zaraket et al., 2015]. Other studies often propose a
model and later hold the results up against demographic and/or socioeconomic information. They ﬁnd
room for a population models, accounting for the diﬀerent demographic and/or socioeconomic heritage
of occupants. This was suggested in both [Liisberg et al., 2016] and [Wilke, 2013] but also supported by
the ﬁndings in [Haldi and Robinson, 2011] and [Aerts et al., 2014].
3.2.2
Approaches to Modeling Occupancy Behavior
In the growing interest of modeling occupancy behavior there are many diﬀerent approaches.
Some
approaches are rather simplistic using empirical data in estimating presence and absence. [Richardson
et al., 2010] used a simple approach accumulating the empirical presence and probability of using an
appliance at a given time to describe the electrical consumption. Others use time use-data and focus on
appliances and relate those to the energy consumption.
We are heavily inspired by some of the recent studies by [Aerts et al., 2014], [Andersen et al., 2014],
[McKenna et al., 2015] and [Liisberg et al., 2016]. From their work we conclude that current state of the
art on modeling occupancy behavior use inhomogeneous Markov chains. Here the transition probability
matrix is time dependent e.g. by applying logistic regression and including variable representing time
but possibly also other variables. Further, except for [Andersen et al., 2014], more than two states were
used.
We believe this path of using time dependent transition probabilities with more than two states is highly
beneﬁcial in modeling occupancy behavior.
I.e.
inhomogeneous Markov chains with more than two
states. We are especially inspired of the work in [Liisberg et al., 2016], using indirect measurements,
which are readily available, in order to model occupancy behavior.

Implementation
Based on the work in [Zucchini and MacDonald, 2009] we have implemented the algorithms used in this
thesis in R version 3.2.1 ’World-Famous Astronaut’. For optimization of the likelihood we have used

5.5.1
Starting Values
The state dependent means are spread equidistantly over the range of the data and possible covariates
are set to zero. The standard deviation of the normal distribution has been initialized as the empirical
standard deviation.
For the homogeneous transition probability matrix it is suggested by [Zucchini and MacDonald, 2009]
to start all oﬀ-diagonal transition probabilities at small values, e.g.
0.01 or 0.05 and let the state-
dependent means be spread over the range of the observations. Using 0.01 in the oﬀ-diagonal has been
used since this lead to the least problems in convergence of the Maximum-likelihood algorithm.
For the inhomogeneous transition probability matrix we have set the coeﬃcients to zero, which yield
equal initial transition probabilities.

5.6
"Economic" Transition Probability Matrix with Covariates
In section 4.4.2 we deﬁned a transition probability matrix including covariates.
Increasing the amount of states by 1 yields an increase of 2·(m−1) transitions to be estimated. For the
homogeneous transition probability matrix this corresponds to 2·(m−1) extra parameters for each addi-
tional state. However, if we allow for covariates to enter the transition probability matrix the amount of
extra parameters needed to be estimated when an additional state is added is 2·(m−1)·p, where p is the
amount of covariates entering the transition probability matrix. This is assuming all oﬀ-diagonal transi-
tions take in the same covariates. The estimation of these parameters will quickly become infeasible to
estimate when increasing the amount of states. For example if one would use a spline with the need of 4
parameters, e.g. indicating time of day, for a model with 4 states it yields (m·m−m)·p = (4·4−4)·4 = 48
parameters. I.e. for a time of day indicator we will need to estimate 48 parameters in this example.

we propose, as in [Liisberg et al., 2016], an alternative transition probability matrix where only the di-
agonal includes the linear predictors. The transition probabilities in the row is not estimated by logistic
regression but the row-sum constraint of 1 is withheld and the computation is somewhat similar. In this
way we allow for covariates to enter the model in the transition probability matrix without increasing
the number of variables exponentially.

In this we chapter we discuss how to validate a proposed model and how to choose between competing

By increasing the number of parameters or states in the generalized state space model, one will always
improve the ﬁt and the likelihood, L. Hence, over ﬁtting is at risk when only considering the likelihood.
In order to avoid overﬁtting, we propose the following. For a broader discussion we refer to chapter 7

calibration by the model or the modeler has been performed.
The training set is used for training the model, i.e. estimating parameters.
The validation set is used to estimate the performance of the considered (competing) models in order to
do model selection.

6.2
Simulation
By the use of the stationary distribution we can compare the marginal distribution of the measured data
and the model.
It is not trivial to obtain the stationary distribution for a time dependent distribution. As discussed
in section 4.2 one can obtain a limiting distribution π, which is also a stationary distribution. If we
simulate the process for t →∞we will obtain an estimate of the long run behavior of the process. We
can use this result to interpret the states but also compare the results to that of the observed process.
The limiting or stationary distribution will be used as a validation tool in comparing the marginal
distribution to the measured data. If the model is valid, the marginal distribution should be of the same
shape as the observed.
6.3
Model Selection
Forward Stepwise Selection by AIC
In order to ﬁnd the most suitable model we would preferably apply ’best-subset-selection’ or ’backwards
selection’ see [Hastie et al., 2009]. However, for the types of models considered here the number of
parameters will increase vastly whenever a new state is added to the model and the estimation time
of these models is slow. Moreover, there is not an upper bound of the number of states since these
states are unobservable. We propose to apply forward selection to ﬁnd the best suitable model. There
is no good way to split data for cross-validation when data is time-dependent. Further, our focus is on
interpretation of the model and we will therefore stay within the likelihood setting. Thus, we will apply
forward stepwise selection using AIC. We will add one new covariate or state at each step and keep the
covariate or state that gives the largest improvement of the ﬁt of the model measured by the informa-
tion criteria AIC, until no improvement is found. We will add the constraint that a maximum amount
of states is 5. A HMM with more than 5 states in this setting will most likely lead to over interpreta-
tion but also numerical problems in estimating parameters and local minimas in the optimization routine.
Models considered will further be validated using the normal pseudo residuals (section 6.1) and comparing
the marginal distributions (section 6.2) by the model to that of the observed measurements.

6.4
Forecasting Ability
We will also evaluate models on their forecasting abilities.
The likelihood setting deals with one-step predictions and so does the information criterias. Since the
CO2 levels are correlated, the use of the information criterias AIC and BIC might not yield in the best
model. If the observations are highly correlated a good model, for one-step predictions, is a model with
the expectation of the previous observation
Yt = Yt−1 +εt
(6.10)
ˆYt = Yt−1
(6.11)
For a prediction further than one-step a model using the expectation of the previous observation would
be inferior to a model accounting for a trend. E.g. if we at 1 pm are interested in predicting 12 hours
ahead. A time indicator would likely improve the prediction that since e.g. the CO2 level would likely
be large since the occupant is home and sleeping at 1 am.
Therefore we will expect that a time of day dependence in the state dependent mean and/or in the
transition probability matrix would yield a superior prediction.
We will estimate coeﬃcients by using the likelihood setting (1-step predictions) but also evaluate on the
forecasting ability on 4, 12, 24 hours and 1 week. We note that there is a small bias since the model
coeﬃcients are estimated using future observations.
Evaluating models on their forecasting abilities will prefer models with stationary mean of the state
dependent distribution within the range of the data (see section 8.4). For a large prediction, e.g. 1 week,
the prediction of the state dependent mean will be close to its stationary mean for that state.
Using the mean squared error for evaluating the forecasting ability is not suﬃcient since the distribution
of the forecast distribution is not Gaussian. Even if each underlying state dependent distribution is
Gaussian, the (weighted) sum of the distributions is not Gaussian. We will therefore use the AIC as a
measure for the forecast ability to stay within the likelihood theory.

We expect that CO2 and noise levels are important factors of presence of an occupant. From the above

8.3
Conclusion on Conditional Independent Models
The models ﬁtted so far are conditional independent models. These models assume independence be-
tween observations when conditioned on the Markov chain.
Considering the model diagnostics, we have not been able to ﬁt a model with uncorrelated normally dis-
tributed pseudo residuals. Thus, the assumptions of the model are not fulﬁlled and we can not validate
any of the models considered so far. This goes for both the homogen and inhomogen models.
It does not seem plausible for an occupant to have more than 5 states concerning the indoor CO2 level,
however, extending the model with several states was carried out. In adding states to the model, we
observed that each additional state serve as a transient state. This was seen by ’fat’ diagonal transition
probability matrices. Thus, adding an extra state give rise to an additional transient state and in that
way obtain a better ﬁt measured by the AIC.
The better AIC value obtained by increasing the amount of states is most likely due to the CO2 levels
are highly correlated i.e. observations Yt−1 and Yt are not conditionally independent given the Markov
chain. Adding more states is a (poor) way of modeling the correlation of the measured CO2 levels.
The ’fat’ diagonal transition probability matrices yields that the state can only go up or down. In that
way the additional states are an artifact of the correlation. The models ﬁtted so far are conditional
independent models. These models assume independence between observations when conditioned on the
Markov chain. We conclude that this is not fulﬁlled and that increasing the amount of states until no

improvement measured by the AIC is not a feasible solution in this setting. We deal with the correlation
in section 8.4.

8.4
Markov-switching Autoregressive Model
In the previous sections we found, by investigating the model diagnostics, that the pseudo residuals
were correlated. The indoor CO2 levels are slowly increasing and decreasing which yields autocorrelated
observations i.e. that the observations Yt−1 and Yt are not conditionally independent given the Markov
chain, which the models in the previous sections assume.
The models are trying to model the autocorrelation which results in the addition of transient states.
Thus, increasing the number of states will provide a better ﬁt since the transient states are accounting
for the autocorrelation. Modeling the autocorrelation in this manner is beneﬁcial to the model with the
greater amount of states but this is inappropriate and will lead to an overﬁt by the amount of states
needed in the model. Thus, the model comparison using AIC/BIC values in the previous section is not
appropriate.
Instead of increasing the amount of states in the model we will account for the correlation between
neighboring values.
One option to account for the observations Yt−1 and Yt not being conditionally independent given the
Markov chain is presented here. A Markov-switching AR(p) (Markov switching autoregressive process
of order p) switches between autoregressive (AR) processes according to a Markov chain. Conditioned
on the state, this model allow for additional dependence between the observation Yt and backwards in
time until the observation Yt−p. By using this model one will obtain a distinct AR(p) process for each
state.

8.7.1
Interpretation of The States
We have identiﬁed 5 states which we have classiﬁed as
Interpretation of the states
• State 1: Absence or sleeping
• State 2: Long term absence
• State 3: Outdoor interaction
• State 4: Presence (high activity)
• State 5: Presence (long term, low activity)
We see that state 1 and 2 are describing a form of absence or sleeping, where state 4 and 5 are describing
presence with high and low activity, respectively. Finally state 3 is representing an interaction with the
outdoor environment such as opening of a window or a door. Further, we have found that ’State 2: Long
term absence’ is a state of absence with transition probabilities almost not aﬀected by which state the
process is in or the time of day.
If we add the probability for an occupants being present by state 4 and 5. We see, by the stationary
distribution over the day, 2 maximas during the morning and evening hours. This correspond well to
a working schedule or enjoying the summer; eating breakfast and dinner at home but enjoying the day
outside.
Arguments of the classiﬁcation of each state, based on the above plots and tables, are given below.
• State 1: Absence or sleeping
In this state we found that the CO2 level is dropping. This is seen by the model coeﬃcients and the
global decoding. The marginal distribution for this state showed that the entire range is used but
this state tend to favor the lower CO2 levels. Further, there is no noise in this state. If we ignore
state 2 ’long term absence’, then this is the state the occupants are most likely to be in during the
night. All of these attributes could indicate that the occupants are sleeping.
There is a peak right after lunch in the stationary distribution and the probability of being found
in this state at dinner is low. This correspond well to that the dwelling recently has become absent.
Further, entering this state almost only occur from state 4 and 5, the presence states. This supports
that when in this state the occupants has either left or are sleeping.
From the transition probabilities, given that you are in state 1 ’absence or sleeping’, there is a
large probability at night of staying in this state. We have the following interpretation. Given that
you sleep, there is a large probability that you will keep sleeping. The stationary distribution and
the probability of staying in this state has a local max at noon. This is interpreted as if you have
left in the middle of the day then there is a large probability that you will stay as absent in the
middle of the day. The stationary distribution of this state goes down until around dinner, where
the occupants enter one of the presence states.
With the above in mind, we classify this state as absence or sleeping.
One ﬂaw of this interpretation could be that the activity level is low and the occupant is far away
from the measurement box. This could cause the CO2 level to fall slowly. However, we are fairly
certain that classifying this as absent or sleeping is valid since there is no noise in this state. Thus,
this state is at worst a misinterpretation of ’low activity’.
• State 2: Absence (long term)
The change in CO2 levels is slow and dropping towards a low CO2 level. The transition probabilities
are constant and there is no noise when the occupants are in this state. This yields a absence for
a longer period. The mean time spent in this state is also by far the largest of all of the states.
We also see that global decoding suggest this state in the long periods with low CO2 level, again
supporting that this state is a state of absence for a longer period.
83
==PAGE:98==
• State 3: Outdoor interaction
The time spent in this state is very short and the state dependent variance is large. We found that
in this state the CO2 levels ﬂuctuate between high and low levels and that it is very unlikely to be
entered from state 1 ’absence or sleeping’ or from state 2 ’long term absence’. It is only probable
to enter state 4 ’presence (high activity)’ from this state.
This state account for a great and sudden change in the CO2 levels, both up and down. We ﬁnd the
most plausible explanation of this sudden change in the CO2 level is due to the indoor environment
is interacting with the outdoor environment. This could be the arrival/departure of occupants or
opening or closing of doors and windows. The stationary distribution is rather constant but also
low over the course of the day. It is, however, larger during the day than at night. That there is an
almost equal chance of e.g. opening a window during the day seems plausible.
That the state is a transient further support the interaction with the outdoor environment. E.g.
opening a window would give a sudden change of the indoor environment but only for a short
amount of time. Further, departure of guests would (most likely) require the opening of a door
which would cause the CO2 level to drop drastically. Afterwards the CO2 level would rise again
since the occupants (’owners’ of the dwelling) are still home. Also, the arrival of occupants would
make the CO2 level rise. We found that the distribution of noise in this state is close to ﬂat. Thus,
there is almost never quiet when in this state. This yields presence and activity, possible by many
occupants. For example greeting with guests or high activity level in the process of opening/closing
the door or windows. Both cases correspond well to that this state can almost only enter state 4
’presence (high activity)’.
Whether this state is arrival/departure of guests or opening a window or a door, all require an
interaction with the outdoor. Thus, we have chosen to classify this state as ’outdoor interaction’.
• State 4: Presence (high activity)
We found that this state yields a fast upward erratic drift of the CO2 levels and a great amount of
noise. It is the most likely state to go from all other states than state 2 ’absence (long term)’. This
yields that if the occupants have been absent/sleeping, relaxing or an interaction with the outdoor
environment has occurred, we will expect for the occupants to enter this state. The occupants will
almost always enter this state after state 3 ’outdoor interaction’. Hence, a typical trend is to enter
this state when the CO2 level is (relative) low (absence, outdoor interaction or presence but around
dinner) and letting this state boost the indoor CO2 level upward. This gives rise to the hypothesis
that this state correspond to high activity, presence of many occupants or that occupants has just
has arrived.
The rising stationary distribution over the day yield that more occupants are home at dinner. This
makes sense, since on average all members of a household has arrived at that time and people come
together at dinner. Thus, we have chosen to classify this state as ’presence (high activity)’.
• State 5: Presence (long term, low activity)
In this state we found that when the CO2 level is large then the CO2 level is steady. The marginal
distribution for this state is distributed around large values (just above 6 and relative narrow).
When the occupants are in this state the CO2 level is steady and large, and there is some noise but
not much. This indicates low activity.
The state is most likely entered from state 4 ’presence (high activity)’ which indicates that there
has been presence for a while and the occupants have settled down. The state can also be entered
through state 1 ’absence or sleeping’. This could be explained by that the occupants are sleeping
uneasy (noise and higher CO2 level), has just woken up or the occupants has been absent for a
short time.
This state work as transient state during the work hours but there is a large probability of staying
in the state when outside of the working hours. This hour is normally an easing time for people
(sleeping, easy morning at breakfast or after dinner) which ﬁts well with this state. Thus, the
occupants are probably relaxing which we classify as low activity.

Discussion
In this thesis we have investigated the current literature and formulated a framework for modeling oc-
cupancy behavior in dwellings. Further, we wished to investigate whether we can use measurements of
indoor CO2 levels as indirect observations to describe occupancy behavior. In doing so, we succeeded
in ﬁnding 5 distinct states to describe the occupancy behavior in a danish summer house. Not only
have we succeeded in describing presence. We have also succeeded in describing the activity level of the
occupants by interpretation of the 5 distinct states.
The model diagnostics suggested no indication that contradict the model assumptions and we were fur-
ther able to validate the model by simulation.
We conclude that indoor CO2 levels can be used for describing occupancy behavior.
To describe the behavior of occupants through a Markov chain using only two states of presence and
absence is not suﬃcient. That, for some purposes, one is only interested in presence and absence of
occupants is ﬁne. However, if applying a Markov chain in the modeling of occupancy behavior, one
should include more than two states of presence and absence and relate the states in the Markov chain
to presence and absence. Further, one should account for time dependence in the transition probabilities.
In the delineation of this work we focused on the parameter driven model given by Equation (4.15). We
found that the model needed to account for correlation of the measurements. In doing so we extended
the observation equation, such that the future observation depend on both the current state and the
current observation.
10.0.1
Evaluation of Findings - Pursuit of an accurate description
Findings
The initial homogen 2-state conditional independent model provided a description of presence of occu-
pants. If one is only interested in presence this model was provided in section 8.1.2. This model was
not validated and neither were conditional independent models with additional states. The conditional
independent model had to be extended in order to have a chance at modeling the occupancy behavior
and the likelihood kept improving drastically when adding states to the model. Thus, we found that this
type of model is not suﬃcient in describing occupancy behavior.
A distinguishing feature compared to other data sets considering occupancy behavior in chapter 3 ’State
of The Art’, were that the observations of the indoor CO2 levels were extremely correlated. That led to
the Markov switching autoregressive model which later was validated when the transition probabilities
were time dependent (inhomogeneous) and at least 4 states were used. Thus, we found that the model
had to be time dependent and contain at least 4 states, otherwise the pseudo residuals showed non-
normality and correlation. The proposed model was the Markov switching autoregressive model with 5
states. This is a sophisticated description of the occupancy behavior by the dynamics of the measured
CO2 levels. This model gives a better description of occupancy behavior than a model with only two
states of presence and absence. Compared to current literature, to the best of our knowledge, this is the
90
==PAGE:105==
most sophisticated model in describing occupancy behavior in one dwelling.
If presence is only of importance, we suggest, that one use the sophisticated (5-state inhomogen Markov
switching) model and relate the states to presence or absence. By the model proposed in this thesis, we
would accumulate state 4 and 5 as presence and state 1 and 2 as absence and further, let state 3 act as
a change in the environment but belong to presence since we found that this state could (almost) only
enter state 4 which is presence.
The marginal distribution found by simulation of the proposed model correspond well to the measured
marginal distribution, however, not perfect. The 5-state model yielded a better AIC and the marginal
distribution also yielded a better ﬁt than the 4-state model which was the simplest model we have
validated by the use of pseudo residuals and the ﬁt of the marginal distribution.
We mentioned that in the work in [Andersen et al., 2014] the authors suggested using several states of
to describe diﬀerent durations of absence. In the model proposed in this thesis, 2 out of the 5 states are
related to absence which improved the ﬁt, as suggested in [Andersen et al., 2014].
Best Model
There is a diﬀerence in modeling for prediction and for describing a process. In this thesis we are in-
terested in interpreting the states and describing the variation in the occupancy behavior. We therefore
have relied on likelihood techniques and not on prediction performance.
In the search for a ’best model’ we did not ﬁnd an optimum by the AIC. Thus, we might need more
than 5 states, as proposed by the ﬁnal model in this thesis, to describe the occupancy behavior in the
given dwelling. By adding more states we might obtain a better description of the occupancy behavior.
For example it would be interesting to see if state 1, which was classiﬁed as to describe both absence
and sleeping, could be split into two distinct states of absence and sleeping.
Also, the marginal distribution by the simulation of the proposed model is close compared to that of the
measured CO2 levels but there is still room for improvement. Even though there is some randomness to
the behavior, and hereby the marginal distribution, extending the model in some way may improve the ﬁt.
Extending the model is attractive. However, adding another state would increase the amount of param-
eters to estimate from the current of 55 parameters to 72 parameters. 55 parameters is already a very
large amount of parameters to estimate. With a model containing that many parameters one is subject
to both numerical diﬃculties in estimating the parameters but also an over interpretation of the model.
For the very least, in extending the model, one would have to come up with another way to parameterize
the model, such that the amount of parameters would not be as large.
One option, which we have not dealt with in this thesis, is to switch the framework from discrete time
to continuous time. In continuous time we can save many parameters since only three transition rates of
staying, increasing and decreasing will be needed for each state. Hence, the transition matrix will only
contain non-zero elements in the diagonal and oﬀ-diagonal.
For time dependence in the transition probabilities we used splines.
We used three periodic splines
with two knots were the knots were chosen at equidistant percentiles. Both the number of splines and
knots were chosen as a tradeoﬀof being able to model the time dependence but keeping the number of
parameters to a minimum. However, the impact of the number of knots and splines were not investigated.
This would be interesting to investigate. To see how or if the proﬁle, number of states and interpretation
of the model would change.
Also, we smoothed all variables onto a interval of 15 minutes since the sampling was sporadic. We took
a fairly simple yet eﬃcient approach in smoothing these variables. It is open for discussion of how to do
this in a ’best’ manner which, we in this thesis, have not put overly eﬀort in to.
State of The Art
Some authors, see chapter 3 ’State of The Art’, have used fairly simple methods in modeling occupancy
behavior. Some have used a ﬁrst order homogen HMM with two states for presence and absence. This
was subject to criticism by the later work on occupancy behavior.
91
==PAGE:106==
In this thesis, we also ﬁtted both a two state homogen and inhomogen HMM. This type of model was
discussed in section 8.3.1, were we found that the marginal distribution does not ﬁt the measured distri-
bution. Also the non-normality and correlation by the forecast pseudo residuals showed that the model
is not capable of fully describing the occupancy behavior. While this type of model does provide infor-
mation about the presence of an occupant, it is not suﬃcient, at least not when considering CO2 levels
as the indirect measurements of occupancy behavior.
One thing left unsolved in chapter 3 was the claim by the work in [Wilke, 2013]. They enforce that
the distribution of the absence and presence should be modeled by a higher order approach, not the
ﬁrst-order Markov chain. We reasoned that the ﬁrst-order Markov approach is suﬃcient, when using
more than two states, but the time-dependence is critical. We suggested that addition of states in the
state vector we might solve this, such that there are more than the two states of presence and absence.
From our model, this was validated. The duration of time spend in each state were given in Table 8.13
and corresponded well to that of the observed.
We conclude, that in our case, adding states managed the problem of the distribution of presence and
absence. Extending the amount of states led to diﬀerent states of presence and absence. As found by
the proposed model in this thesis, there is a state of absent (and sleeping) but also a state where the
occupants are absence for a long period.
By the modeling of occupancy behavior in this thesis, we have not answered the question of whether
there is a need for population models, since we have only considered one dwelling. In the framework we
have proposed a way of incorporating both ﬁxed and random eﬀects. The data used in this work did not
contain demographic or socioeconomic information. However, in chapter 3 ’State of The Art’, we found
that there is a need for population models and we have suggested a framework to include population
models.
Future work would consist of allowing demographic and/or socioeconomic information to enter the model.
One diﬃculty of adding demographic or socioeconomic information is that the number of parameters
will increase and it is not immediately clear how to deal with this. We have proposed to use the Laplace
approximation to minimize the computational eﬀort, however, it does not solve the above.
10.0.2
Modeling Perspectives
We ﬁtted the model based on only one time series regarding measured CO2 levels. The measurements
contain data for less than one year and we therefore need further studies in order to validate the ﬁndings in
this thesis. This could be either by obtaining several years worth of data and/or by similar measurements
in a diﬀerent dwelling.
However, we found that indoor CO2 levels are extremely correlated and we needed an extended model
in order to account for this correlation. This might be the nature of the variable that CO2 is building
up over time. However, by the latest work on occupancy behavior we found that activities are correlated
and there is time dependence on the probability of performing an activity. Hence, accounting for the
correlation by the use of a Markov switching AR(1) process might also relate to other smart metering
data, as e.g. [Liisberg et al., 2016], and hereby increase the ﬁt in that setting.
Unobservable states
The states in the Markov chain of the ﬁtted model are unobservable. While the states are based on a
statistical framework, the interpretation of the states is subjective. Various plots have been investigated
in order to obtain a valid interpretation this is, then, a heuristic technique. The interpretation of the
states did not contradict the information provided of the use of the summer house.
One ﬂaw of our interpretation is that we are not able to give an estimate of how many people that are in
the summer house. We suggested that state 3 and 4 might relate to several occupants present, however,
this is not clear.
The summer house is used for vacation and by many diﬀerent families. Thus, it is not clear whether the
model type is incapable of obtaining an interpretation of the amount of occupants in the dwelling.

Multivariate State Dependent Distribution
We investigated to utilize both the indoor CO2 levels and the indoor noise levels, in the strive for a
better description of the occupancy behavior in the summer house. However, we have at the time of
writing not yet succeeded in utilizing this bi-variate setting.
We found that it was inappropriate to apply the normal distribution, since the data in the variable
containing the noise average is skewed. Other transformations of the noise levels, than the one proposed,
could be considered in applying a multivariate normal distribution. However, given the skewness, it does
not seem plausible that this is a good path to follow.
From the states, found by the proposed model on the CO2 levels, there were indications that the dis-
tributions of the noise levels follow exponential distributions. We found, when ﬁtted by a bi-variate
distribution assuming independence, using the exponential distribution on the noise average, that the
exponential distributions had almost the same rate for each state. This might be due to the skewed
distribution of noise and the assumption of independence. Hence, this approach did not increase the
description of the occupancy behavior.
The great diﬀerence between the noise distributions for the states by the proposed model indicate a link
between the proposed states and the distribution of noise. Thus, we are not done in the modeling of
the bi-variate setting and we have listed this as further work. One option would be to parameterize
the rate of the exponential distribution through the state dependent distribution of the CO2 level, such
that there is no assumption of independence. Another option would be for the noise levels to be time
dependent which we found plausible when we investigated the data in chapter 7.
Other Variables
In the framework and by the code used in this thesis, it is rather easy to include other variables.
In the data provided for this thesis there was also information regarding the in and outdoor temperature.
We had hoped to also include these variables in modeling of occupancy behavior.
Especially, if state 3, is indeed a state with ’outdoor interaction’ it would have been interesting to see
how the model would change with information regarding the temperature.
Inclusion of in and outdoor temperature were incorporated in the state equation through the transition
probability matrix. However, severe optimization problems occurred which are outside the scope of this
thesis to fully investigate.
We found, that there is a daily trend in the proﬁle of the occupancy behavior in the provided summer
house and that the time of day play a major role. It would be interesting to see if the addition of a weekend
or a weekly trend would increase the explanatory power of the model. It is probable that occupants in
general behave diﬀerently in the weekends than during the week. However, since the considered summer
house is purely used for vacation it is not likely in this case.
Minimization - Estimation time
To ensure ﬁnding a global minimum it would be preferable to chose e.g. 200 diﬀerent starting values and
check the convergence of a model. However, some of the models in this thesis have an estimation time
about 10 hours on the computer used. Hence, it is not feasible to use many diﬀerent starting values and
check for convergence. For competing models or models which we know should have a greater likelihood
(nested models), we have tried diﬀerent starting values to check for convergence. Much eﬀort has not
been put on this part. We have accepted that some lower order models did not ﬁnd a global minimum if
it is not important for the overall goal of modeling occupancy behavior. I.e. in Table 8.9 we see that the
inhomogen 3-state model does not have a greater likelihood than the homogen model. This is of little
concern since the likelihood is far better of the 5-state model than the 4-state model. However, there is
no guarantee, that any of the models have reached a global minimum in the optimization.
This also means, since the 3-state model did not converge, that we can not reject that a 3-state model
might be valid in describing the occupancy behavior in our setting.
We mentioned that the proposed model contain many parameters (55) and including more parameters is
infeasible. If one is greedy for eliminating parameters, one could parameterize the transition probabilities
such that the transient states are not time-dependent. E.g. it might not be crucial to have time of day
93
==PAGE:108==
variation for the transient state, state 3, since the probability of staying in this state is relatively low. The
error by eliminating the time-dependence for a transient state will likely not be of signiﬁcance. Further,
we found that for state 2, the probability of staying in the state or leaving to another state is almost
constant over the course of the day. Thus, it might be favorable to let some transition probabilities be
homogeneous.
Same Coeﬃcients AR(p)
In modeling the correlation of the CO2 levels, we used a Markov switching AR(1) model. One reason for
this was to keep the number of parameters to a minimum and to retain model interpretations. Another
option would be to allow for diﬀerent intercept, for each regime (state), but enforce that the coeﬃcients
are the same for each state. This would yield a model in the form of
Yt = ci +φ1Yt−1 +φ2Yt−2 +···+φpYt−p +εt
for i = 1,2,··· ,m
(10.1)
In general, for an AR(1) process the model can be written as Yt −µ = +φ(Yt−1 −µ). Hence, the coeﬃ-
cient φ determines the ’speed’ the process reaches its stationary mean.
This generalizes to the p coeﬃcients determining the ’speed’ in the AR(p) process of reaching its sta-
tionary mean.
For the state dependent processes to have the same coeﬃcients except for the intercept yields that all of
the processes would have the same (relative) ’speed’. Investigating the plots of the indoor CO2 levels, we
see that sometimes the level is approximately constant and sometimes it rises/falls greatly. If the model
has the same coeﬃcients except for the intercept, this will cause the stationary mean to lie outside the
measured CO2 levels for some of the states. Hence a model using the same coeﬃcients was not pursued.

Conclusion
In this thesis we have investigated the current literature of modeling occupancy behavior. We found many
approaches hereof and we have formulated a framework for modeling occupancy behavior in dwellings,
collecting many of the approaches in a framework we see best ﬁt.
We have applied the proposed framework to model occupancy behavior in a Danish summer house based
on indoor measurements of CO2. Using CO2 as an indicator of occupancy behavior has to the best of
our knowledge not been done before. We found ﬁve diﬀerent states of occupancy behavior with diﬀerent
activity levels. By validating the model we conclude that Indoor CO2 levels can be used as indirect
measurements to describe occupancy behavior. Further, we found that to describe occupancy behavior,
using CO2 levels as indirect measurements of occupancy behavior, time dependence is of signiﬁcance
and more than two states are needed to suﬃciently describe occupancy behavior. We found the need of
at least 4 states to obtain a suﬃcient description. Finally, we used 5 states to most accurately resemble
the occupancy behavior. The model proposed is more sophisticated than any of the models suggested
beforehand in the sense that it involves 5 states.
We have proposed a model to describe and hereby help us understand occupancy behavior where the
framework can be used in other settings as well.
There is no guarantee that the ﬁndings in this thesis, using the indoor CO2 levels will apply to smart
metering data such electrical consumption. However, the approach taken in this thesis, is similar to
that of [Liisberg et al., 2016], which give reason to believe that the approach taken should be utilized in
future modeling of occupancy behavior. Even though these ﬁndings are based on CO2 measurements, we
conclude that in future development of modeling occupancy behavior, several states with time dependent
transition probabilities should be used to describe occupancy behavior.
Thus, neglecting the inﬂuence of occupancy behavior will lead to a poor performance in prediction or
simulation of energy consumption in dwellings. The activities of occupants are time dependent which
should be acknowledged whenever one touch upon the subject of occupancy behavior and building per-
formance.
With focus on lowering the energy consumption, e.g. in moving toward nearly zero energy homes, an
accurate description of occupant behavior is of great value. By the proposed framework and the proposed
model we can improve building performance e.g. this can be part of the estimation of the energy use of
buildings in the work of Annex 66.
Energy eﬃcient technologies and buildings will need a description of occupancy behavior in order to be
eﬀective such that the diﬀerence of the design and the actual energy use in buildings is small.
Thus, applying new technologies in the design of buildings and the implementation of smartgrid will
not be integrated to the fullest if the design is not appropriate for the user. E.g. if we know that an
occupant is the state of ’long term absence’ it might be advantageous to lower the temperature in the
house and close the windows. However, doing so, if the occupant is only gone for a short amount of time
will likely cause the occupant to turn up the heat to a maximum. In this scenario, there is unnecessarily
movement of heat and loss of energy. This scenario is easily transferred to other setting such as cooling
and ventilation.

==PAGE:110==
Indirect measurements of occupancy behavior are e.g. smart metering data as used in the work of [Li-
isberg et al., 2016] or indoor CO2 levels as used in this thesis. Exploiting indirect measurements of
occupancy behavior is a relative easy way to obtain data regarding occupancy behavior, which is oth-
erwise diﬃcult to obtain. Indoor CO2 measurements are strong indicators of when energy consumption
is ongoing, however, it is not directly linked to energy consumption. It would be very interesting to
be in a possession of both measurement of CO2 levels and some other measure of energy consumption.
Simultaneous use of both measurements would hopefully give a better description of occupancy behavior
and the related energy use.
That indirect measurements of occupancy behavior are readily available is an opportunity to rapidly
advancing the modeling of occupancy behavior. As we move forward, we hope, that demographic and
or socioeconomic information will be connected with smart metering data and data of the type in this
thesis. The framework in this thesis will hopefully give a rise to advancing the modeling of occupancy
behavior by including populations as an integrated part of the modeling.
Given there is a mapping from occupancy behavior to energy consumption we can connect energy con-
sumption onto the activity level, which is described by the states in the proposed model in this thesis.
The proﬁles could be used as inputs in a model for forecasting the energy consumption to be used by a
supplier of energy. Example hereof are the use of domestic hot water, lighting or electric appliances and
cooking. Hence, in this way we would obtain an energy proﬁle over the course of the day, which would
help in sizing the energy supply and, thus, optimize the production of energy.
Especially if we included smart metering data with the proposed model in this thesis we would possibly
obtain an even better description of occupancy behavior and their use of energy.
With this in hand, we can use the proposed framework and model in future building performance and
in energy optimization of both local and global energy issues. The proposed model, can e.g. be used
as input in a simulation tool for summer houses. With an understanding of occupancy behavior we
can better plan how to implement the use of renewable energy and this model is an addition in the
steps toward becoming independent of fossil fuels.
Hence, the description of occupancy behavior is
important and will only increase in the shift toward climate neutrality. In general we hope this will
help to lower the global energy consumption, and to beneﬁt in an economic, environmental and health
perspective.
