Abstract 
There exist different approaches and methodologies to assess the robustness of a concept or a 
finished product. When a new product is developed, the robustness is usually analysed on the 
concept level and later. A new technology therefore needs to be implemented as a concept before 
its robustness can be assessed, which eventually leads to multiple iterations before the most robust 
technology is found. This work tries to help to avoid this by providing a methodology for how 
the robustness can be analysed on the technology level in an early stage of the design process. 
The basis for the methodology is the analysis of the product category wherein the product is 
developed. This analysis leads to the customer requirements, the customer satisfaction and a 
Product Robustness Index for the analysed products. For a specific product, that uses a certain 
technology, some of the requirements’ performances are affected by external influences, the noise 
factors. To increase a products robustness, the robustness against these noise factors needs to be 
improved. The methodology helps to find the noise factors and shows which ones can be 
controlled on the technology level. Their impact on the most important requirements, the Key 
Characteristics, is measured. The result leads to the conclusion on the robustness of a technology 
and allows the comparison between different technologies’ robustness. The methodology 
generates a framework that allows a later assessment of new technologies for the given product 
category. 
This work uses a case study on battery free bicycle lights with power generation technologies that 
are alternative to the conventional dynamo. The analysis and the resulting Product Robustness 
Index are done for three products, which use two different technological solutions to generate 
power. The two technologies are analysed on their robustness and compared to a third, new 
technology. The result gives a suggestion for the most robust technology and a prospect on to 
what extend it can improve the robustness when it is implemented in a product, compared to other 
technologies.

1. 
Introduction and methodology 
1.1 Context of the work 
The motive is to develop a new product within a defined product category. The new product 
should be based on either an in this category already existing technical solution or a new one. The 
main requirement for choosing the technology and its implementation is to improve the 
customer’s satisfaction significantly, compared to the other products within the category. 
The product category is characterized by a set of common functionalities. The similarity of the 
products should be high enough to allow the following assumptions: 
1. The customer group is the same for the whole product category. 
2. Same application; the scenarios of use for the products are the same. 
3. In use, the products are exposed to the same external (environmental) influences. 
Taking these assumptions as given, the question is; how do the external influences affect the 
products? And thereby; how much does this affect the customer satisfaction for the different 
products? Using the concept of robustness, these questions can be expressed as; how robust is 
the design against the external influences’ impact on the customer satisfaction? 
It can be analysed what makes the implementations of the different technologies more or less 
robust against the most critical external influences. The insights will guide us towards a new 
technical solution or a new concept for an implementation of an existing one.

14 
 
1.2 Robust Design 
Robust Design focuses on developing engineering designs which functional performance is 
insensitive to use and production caused variations of design parameters. The sources for 
variations in the design parameters (the geometrical dimensions) can be categorized as [1]: 
• 
Production tolerances (e.g. due to variation in shrink percentage, process parameters etc.) 
• 
Assembly tolerances (e.g. due to clearance around mounting screws) 
• 
Load deformations (e.g. due to user loads, wind loads, gravity, etc.) 
• 
Variation due to ambient conditions (e.g. due to change in temperature, humidity, etc.) 
• 
Variation over time (e.g. due to wear, creep, swelling) 
The design parameter and the functional requirement are connected by the transfer function. The 
transfer function translates the geometrical dimensions of the design to its functional 
performance. The gradient of the transfer function represents the sensitivity to changes of the 
input parameter; the design parameter. The so called robust design is defined by its insensitive 
transfer functions. Or ideally, a perfect robust design’s functional requirements should show no 
variation if the design parameters are varied [1]. 
 
Figure 1.1: Variation Management Framework 
The design parameters are not only input variables for the functional requirements, they are also 
an output of the production variables. And the functional requirements again are input variables 
for the customer attributes, related by the quality loss function. The quality loss function 
represents how the customer perceives different performances of the functional requirement. This 
mapping of the production variables (PV) to the design parameters (DP), further to the functional 
requirements (FR) and finally to the the customer attributes (CA) can be visualized in the 
Variation Management Framework (Figure 1.1) [2]. The framework is a simplified model, 
because in most cases one output variable (e.g. a functional requirement) depends on multiple 
input variables (e.g. design parameter). And beside the input variables, noise factors can have a 
significant impact, too (Figure 1.2). For example, in case of the functionality of a belay device 
for rope climbing, besides the design parameters, a couple of external factors (noise) come into 
play. These are the rope thickness, the climbers weight, the carbine’s dimensions, the belaying 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
15 
 
person’s skills and other factors. The products design also has a certain sensitivity for each of the 
noise factors. That means the input variables for a functional requirement are a set of design 
parameters and noise factors with an individual sensitivity each. These sensitivities depend on the 
engineering design. Because the customer satisfaction results from the functional requirements, 
the design needs to be robust against noise factors as well in order to achieve a high customer 
satisfaction. In the example of the belay device, the design could be made in a way that makes it 
insensitive to variations in the user’s belay skills and thereby getting a higher customer 
satisfaction. 
 
Figure 1.2: Design parameters and noise factors as input variables for the functional requirements 
1.3 Analysing the robustness 
If a product’s functionality is highly dependent on noise factors, they might have a larger impact 
then the production based variations of the design parameter. Therefore, it makes sense to analyse 
the design’s robustness against the noise factors separate from the design parameters. Especially 
when taking customer feedback as a basis for the analysis, the design parameter’s variation’s 
impact on the satisfaction can be neglected, compared to the impact of the noise factors (Figure 
1.3). This can be done due to the following reasons: 
1. The design is exposed to many noise factors. 
2. The noise factors have a much higher impact on the functional requirements than the 
variation in the design parameters. 
3. The purpose of the analysis is to determine the design sensitivity for the noise factors. 
4. The analysis is based on customer feedbacks/reviews and therefore the variation in the 
related products’ design parameters is unknown. 
5. The variation in the design parameters can be assumed as similar among the set of 
analysed products. 
 
Figure 1.3: Design parameters and production variables are black boxed as product features 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
If the design parameters are assumed to be constant at their nominal value, their variations are 
neglected, thus the remaining input variables for the transfer functions are the noise factors 
(Figure 1.4). The goal of the analysis is to compare the robustness of different designs within the 
same environment in which they are exposed to the same noise factors and characterized by the 
same functionalities (functional requirements). This is possible now, because the noise factors are 
global within one product category (the products are exposed to the same environment and used 
by the same customer group) and only the individual designs with its features change for each 
product. Thus, the focus of this thesis is to make conclusions on the robustness of a product against 
noise factors using customer satisfaction as a measurement. 
 
Figure 1.4: With the design parameters set to constant, the noise factors are the remaining input 
variables for the transfer functions. 
1.4 Key functional requirements and Key Characteristics 
The analysis of the customer satisfaction not only enables to make conclusions on the robustness 
of the design against noise factors, it also gives an indication of how important each functional 
requirement is for the customers. The functional requirements are the same for each product 
within the product family. Therefore, the customers’ frequency and manner of mentioning the 
functional requirements are evaluated for all products combined. Additional knowledge about the 
products and the product family helps to identify the most important functional requirements for 
the customer. These key functional requirements are the same for all the products within the 
product category. However, they are based on product features that are individual for each product 
and noise factors for which each product is individually sensitive. The most influential product 
features and noise factors are called Key Characteristics. The Key Characteristics are the features 
of a product that need to be maintained most carefully. Or when redesigning a product, it should 
be made robust against these key characteristics. Further more, the insights into the Key 
Characteristics can guide towards new technical solutions or a new implementation of an existing 
technical solution in a new design. Anna C. Thornton provides the following definition for the 
Key Characteristics [3]: 
“Key Characteristics are the product, subassembly, part, and process features that significantly 
impact the final cost, performance, or safety of a product when the KCs vary from nominal. 
Special control should be applied to those KCs where the cost of variation justifies the cost of 
control.” 
First, the high-level/product-level Key Characteristics are identified and then traced down to the 
feature-level (Figure 1.5 shows an example of a Key Characteristics Flowdown). Usually, design 
parameters are chosen as the Key Characteristics to be observed and worked with. Since it is 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
17 
 
easier to monitor few Key Characteristics on the feature-level, only the most significant 
contributors to the high-risk Key Characteristics are chosen [4]. 
 
Figure 1.5: Key Characteristics Flowdown for the features of a car door [3]. Above, it is referred to the 
customer requirements as the functional requirements. 
In the explanation of the methodology in this chapter, only the functional requirements are 
mentioned. They can be measured in a quantitative way and are therefore more convenient for the 
use together with the quantitative variation of design parameters. However, besides the functional 
requirements, also qualitative requirements exist. In the following case study, some of the 
requirements are qualitative requirements. Therefore, to cover all sorts of requirements, the term 
customer requirements is used.

2. 
Analysis of bicycle lights 
2.1 Introduction to the case 
The range of bicycle lights is wide; starting at conventional rim dynamos, to hub dynamos, all 
different kinds of bright high-end models to disposable battery lights, to rechargeable ones, 
Additionally, there are many other solutions and designs in different price segments. Bicycle 
lights, using conventional dynamos, are well established. They are usually installed on the bicycle 
by the manufacturer or later by a bicycle mechanic and only in few cases by more experienced 
customers. The dynamo lights are mounted permanently and can usually be switched on and off 
by the user. However, battery solutions are often installed by the dealer or the customer him or 
herself. Their key functionality includes being removable and attachable from the bicycle. 
Bicyclists without preinstalled lights often prefer to install self-purchased battery lights, due to 
their convenience and low price. However, these lights have two major drawbacks. First, they 
need to be maintained; the user needs to remember to mount them on the bicycle or carry them 
with him for the case that they are needed. Second, new batteries need to be purchased when they 
run out, which adds to the overall expense. Recently, rechargeable lights have become more 
popular as a substitute for these. They can often be charged with the same USB charger as used 
for smartphones. This eliminates the extra expenses caused by switching batteries and makes the 
lights more sustainable. However, the most convenient kind of bicycle lights is still rare; the ones 
that can be installed independently by the user, are mounted permanently, have an integrated 
power generator and thereby require much less maintenance. 
In Denmark, a bicycle light produced by Reelight has become increasingly popular. It uses 
induction to generate power, whereby the magnets are mounted on the spokes of the wheel and 
the light unit with the coil is mounted on the wheel shaft. Following the introduction of the 
Reelight induction light, a similar solution was a light developed in Germany which instead uses 
eddy currents to generate power. This light consists out of one unit that is mounted close to the 
rim of the wheel and its strong magnets inside the unit induce eddy currents in the conductive rim 
when driving. These currents result in a magnetic field that causes a rotation of the magnets and 
thereby induce a current in the coils placed next to the magnets. Both the Danish and the German 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
produced solutions have in common that they can be mounted on the bike permanently and 
relatively easy by the customer. They can be left installed on the bike and do not require similar 
maintenance as the traditional battery lights do. Furthermore, one of the main advantages, offered 
by these technologies, is the very low resistance they cause when cycling, compared to 
conventional rim and some hub dynamos, which can often be felt by the cyclist [6]. 
There is a high potential for possible solutions in this range of bicycle lights which use a 
technology to generate power that is alternative to the conventional dynamo. Both the induction 
and the eddy current solutions for generating power can be implemented in many other designs. 
Besides these two technologies, there are other solutions that can be developed and implemented. 
This is the motivation for working with this case; the potential for new and even better bicycle 
light designs within this product category. 
2.2 Design brief 
There are only a few existing bicycle lights that do not use a conventional dynamo, but are 
nevertheless generating power while the bicycle is driven. Thus, there is a high potential for new 
designs and technical solutions in this field. A solution for power generation has to improves the 
robustness against external influences when in use, whether they are caused by the environment, 
the user or the bicycle design. Furthermore, it has to be implemented in an affordable design, 
which increases the value to the customer through its reliability, satisfying brightness, ease of 
mounting and adjustment, low need for maintenance and robustness against variations of bicycle 
designs, driving speed, shocks and other environmental influences. 
2.3 Definition of the product category 
The methodology, explained in the previous chapter can be applied to products in the same 
product category that are used in the same context by the same user group. The following list 
characterizes the products contained by the category analysed: 
• 
Battery/accumulator free 
• 
No conventional dynamo used for power generation 
• 
Independent installation of the light; no integration in the bicycle design 
The customer group has the following characteristics: 
• 
Daily commuters and others who use the bicycle as a main mean of transportation 
• 
Parents who want a reliable and low maintenance bicycle light for their children 
• 
Persons and bicycle rentals that need an inexpensive bicycle light that is mounted on the 
bicycle permanently 
These factors describe the environment that the products are exposed to: 
• 
A large variety of bicycle designs 
• 
Mainly night conditions in populated areas with street lighting 
• 
Four seasons outdoor weather conditions 
• 
Load and shock impacts caused in normal use or accidents 
• 
Bicycle driving speeds from very slow (walking) to fast (racing) 
• 
A user group with no expertise to high expertise in bicycles

2.5 Review Analysis 
For the robustness analysis, three products are chosen (Figure 2.1). They represent the most 
successfully implemented technologies in this product category. The Reelight SL100 series is the 
most popular light using induction with separate magnets mounted on the spokes. The specific 
model, SL150, is chosen, because it provides a steady light, like the other two analysed products, 
and not only flashes. The Magnic Light was the first model to generate power through eddy 
currents, which are induced in the rim of the wheel. However, this light is a high-performance 
product with an almost four times higher price point and it is not as common on the streets yet, 
compared to the SL150. Therefore, as the third light and a complementation to the Magnic Light, 
the Reelight City is taken. It uses the eddy current technology, too, but lays in the same price 
range as the SL150. 
 
Figure 2.1: Reelight SL150, Magnic Light and Reelight City (FLTR) 
The robustness analysis, carried out in this work, is based on the customer satisfaction. In a first 
step, the customer’s satisfaction with each requirement (see chapter 2.4) for each of the three 
bicycle light models is assessed. The most reliable data could be gained by doing a survey among 
the customers. If it was built up in the same way for all three products and answered by a 
statistically significant amount of people, it would serve as a good basis for the comparison. Due 
to the focus and the limits of this thesis, a different source of data is chosen. The two Reelight 
models are sold on the online sales platform Amazon [7][8][9]. There, the customers have the 
chance to write a review about the product. The Magnic Light is not sold on Amazon. But there 
is a number of very detailed customer reviews listed on the Magnic Light website [10]. All 
reviews are text based with an additional five-star rating for the Amazon ones. The analysis is 
carried out in a text coding method according to these steps: 
1. Read review and search for topics related to the requirements in Table 2.1 
2. If the satisfaction regarding a requirement is expressed in a positive way it is rated with 
a 3, if it is expressed in a negative way the rating is a 1 and if is mentioned, but neither in 
a clearly positive nor in a clearly negative way the rating is neutral with a 2 
3. Additionally, the requirement is marked as important (1) or not important (-1) if the 
customer expresses some kind of subjective weighting. 
4. For each review, its link, the rating and subjective importance for the requirements 
mentioned and the five-star rating (for Amazon reviews only) are filled in the Excel 
template. 
The Excel template calculates the mean rating for each requirement. It counts how often a 
requirement is mentioned and adds up the subjective importance. Table 2.2 shows the Excel table

2.6 Requirement ranking 
As mentioned above, the requirements are not all as important to the customer. Some are more 
important, others less. But it is difficult to determine the exact importance for each of them. An 
indicator is how often a requirement is mentioned in the reviews. Another is the subjective 
importance, expressed by the author of a review. 
The chosen methodological approach, to assess the importance, is to rank the requirements. 
Inspired by the Design Structure Matrix [5], all requirements are compared to each other. Based 
on certain criteria it is distinguished if a requirement is more, less or similar important than an 
other one. Doing this for all requirements leads to an order.

An example for the applied methodology is shown in Table 2.3. Four requirements A, B, C and 
D are compared. In the row for requirement A, its importance compared to the other requirements 
is stated. The first entry (in column A) is of course 0. The second entry (column B) is -1. This 
means that requirement A is less important than requirement B. But it is considered as more 
important than requirement C, therefore the 1 in column C. The last entry is a -1 for the 
comparison with requirement D. If a requirement is considered to have a similar importance, 
compared to an other one, it is stated with a 0; for example, for requirement B and D (row B/ 
column D). Finally, the values in the columns are added up and noted in row I. And the values in 
the rows are added up, too, subtracted by the corresponding sum in row I and noted in column II. 
This is the ranking value. In this example, requirement B and D are both most important with the 
rank 2. Requirement C is least important with -3 and A lies in between with -1. 
The comparison of the two requirements in the bicycle case is based on two criteria and the 
subjective opinion/knowledge of the person who carries it out. First priority has the subjective 
importance, expressed by the customers. Second is the number of times the requirement is 
mentioned. And at last, if required, the subjective opinion comes into play.

2.8 Product Robustness Index and Customer Satisfaction 
It is desirable to compare the robustness of different products. For example, it can be interesting 
for the customer to know a product’s robustness benchmarked against the robustness of other 
similar products [11]. A labelling system could be introduced, similar to the EU Energy Label 
[12]. For this kind of comparison, a product’s average variation from nominal for a defined set of 
functional requirements, after a defined reference time of use, has to be measured. But only 
functional requirements with a quantitative performance can be measured and compared this way. 
Thus qualitative requirements’ performance must be evaluated differently. 
The Product Robustness Index (PRI) developed for this purpose is based on the customer 
satisfaction. An importance weighted average of the requirements’ grade from the requirement 
analysis is calculated. And the importance weighting factors are determined on the requirement 
ranking. It would be possible to use each requirement’s rank and translate it into a weighting 
factor. But it is considered more accurate to use the requirements’ relative rank (column II in 
Table 2.4). These values reflect the requirements’ importance more precisely. 
The importance weighting factors are distributed from between a minimum weighting factor, for 
the lowest relative rank, and a maximum weighting factor, for the highest relative rank:

Analysis of the technologies’ robustness 
In this chapter, the robustness of the induction, the eddy current and a third technology is analysed. 
In a first step, the Key Characteristics Flowdown for the induction and the eddy current 
technology is carried out. This method helps to identify the noise factors and see on which design 
level they can be controlled. In a second step, theoretical models are made for the two first 
technologies. They are used to analyse the impact of the noise factors on the Key Characteristics’ 
performance. The third step contains the introduction of a new technology and its analysis. In the 
last step, the robustness of the three technologies is compared. 
3.1 Key Characteristics Flowdown 
The Product Robustness Index allows the comparison of the products’ over all robustness against 
the influence of noise factors on their performance. The noise factors’ influences are controlled 
on different design levels. E.g. the vibrations and shocks affecting the position of the light can be 
controlled on the level of the mounting design. To distinguish between the different levels and 
their features, the following four are introduced:

For the comparison of the power generation technologies, the technology level is the relevant one 
and only the noise factors that are controlled on the technology level need to be taken into account. 
It has to be analysed how these noise factors affect the products performance for different 
technologies. In this work, the products’ performance is measured by the customer satisfaction 
and the moist influential requirements are the three Key Characteristics, determined in Chapter 
2.7. It is considered that an improvement of the KCs’ performance will improve the customer 
satisfaction, too. This means that the different technologies impact on the KCs’ robustness against 
==PAGE:30==

the noise factors need to be evaluated, in order to choose the most robust power generation 
technology. 
A KC’s performance is based on multiple design features. These features are defined on different 
design levels; the levels of the work principle, the concept/ structure and the detailed design. The 
technology level is present as the relation, the physical phenomena, between those features that 
are related to the technology. These features are usually on the work principle level. Additionally, 
to the design features, some external features impact the KC’s performance. These are the noise 
factors. 
 
Figure 3.1: Section of the KC Flowdown for the Brightness with the eddy current technology 
A method to determine the design features, noise factors and their relations is the Key 
Characteristics Flowdown. Usually, the KC Flowdown is used to identify the high-risk KCs, on 
the product, subsystem, part and process level [3]. Here, the Flowdown is structured according to 
the features functionality and their functional groups. The Flowdown is drawn as an Ishikawa 
diagram [13] (fishbone diagram) because it provides a good overview for cause-effect trees with 
functional groups. The diagrams are drawn for each KC and for both technologies (induction and 
eddy current). The features are marked in the colour of the design level in which they are 
defined/designed. The noise factors are marked in red. And the functional group impacted by the 
noise factors are marked bold. Figure 3.1 shows a section of a KC Flowdown as an example. All 
six diagrams can be found in the Appendix A.3.

3.5 New technological solution and its analysis 
The eddy current technology gives the insight that it is fortunate to have a magnetisation on both 
the generator unit and the moving part. The eddy currents generate a magnetic field that leads to 
an increased attraction between the magnet and the conductor. This allows greater distances 
between the two. But the eddy current technology also requires a continues conductor on the 
wheel. The rim is an in the bicycle design existing one. But using it, binds the light to a radial 
position that is equal to the one of the rim. However, an additional conductor could be placed at 
other radii on the wheel. But this does not lead to a set up that is as flexible as the one of the 
induction technology with single magnets. 
A new technology is chosen, based on these insights. It is a combination between the induction 
and the eddy current technology. There already exist concepts of that kind. Here, one magnet is 
placed on the wheel (M2) and an other one (M1) on an oscillator in the generator unit. The oscillator 
is able to rotate around its centre point, has the height h and a mass m. When the moving magnet 
M2 passes the magnet M1 it accelerates the oscillator. The resulting motional energy can be used 
to generate electric power. The oscillator could additionally be equipped with a torsion spring or 
a small magnet besides it that rotates it back to the starting position and leads to an oscillation. In 
the model set up below, no torsion spring or additional small magnet are implemented.

3.7 Effect on the Product Robustness Index 
For products with different technologies, it has been analysed which noise factors affect the Key 
Characteristics. These noise factors can be controlled on different levels of the product’s design. 
The ones that can be controlled on the technology level are analysed on their impact on the related 
Key Characteristics performance. This allows the conclusion on the technologies robustness 
against the different noise factors’ impact. Knowing this, it can be compared for which technology 
the Key Characteristics’ performances are affected the least by the variations of the noise factors. 
And further it shows which technology leads to the highest performance of the Key 
Characteristics, for a given set of nominal values for the noise factors and the other parameters. 
The Key Characteristics are those requirements which have the highest impact on the Product 
Robustness Index and the customer satisfaction. Therefore, the technology with the highest 
robustness and that causes the highest performance will lead to at largest improvement of the 
Product Robustness Index and the customer satisfaction. Ideally, this technology should be 
implemented in a new product. 
A technology has of course also an individual robustness for all the other requirements (besides 
the Key Characteristics) against the impact of noise factors. In an extended analysis, they could 
be taken into account, too. This would lead to a more detailed picture of the technologies’ total 
robustness. But for only to choose the most robust technology, they Key Characteristics should 
be sufficient.

This work shows how the robustness can be analysed on the technology level. This allows the 
choice of the most robust technology for a certain application. But during the design process, the 
robustness of the other levels’ solutions should should be assessed too. There exist different 
methodologies in the Robust Design theory for this purpose. 
 
 
==PAGE:49==
 
 
49 
 
Conclusion 
For the comparison of a set of products within the same product category, but with different 
technical solutions, a common level of performance needs to be found. The customer satisfaction 
is ideal as a common level of performance and is chosen in this work. The interest lays in the 
robustness of the different technologies against the impact of external factors on the performance, 
the customer satisfaction. These factors of external influences that the product is exposed to are 
the so-called noise factors. The products within one product family are exposed to the same noise 
factors. From measuring the customer satisfaction for the product, the products’ robustness 
against the noise factors can be found. 
The customer satisfaction can for example be measured with help of a survey. For the case of the 
battery free bicycle lights with alternative power generation technologies, a review analysis is 
carried out. The analysis leads to: 
1. A list with customer requirements; the requirement list 
2. The customers’ satisfaction concerning each requirement 
3. The requirements importance for the customer 
The requirements are ranked according to their importance. To determine the importance, three 
factors are taken into account in the following order: 
1. The importance, expressed by the customer 
2. The number of times a requirement is mentioned 
3. The over all knowledge, gained from the review analysis and the insights into the product 
category 
The importance ranking leads to the requirements’ importance weighting factors. These factors 
are the same among the product category. 
The requirements can be categorized as robustness related and non robustness related. The 
robustness related ones are those which are affected by one or several noise factors. The 
requirements which are ranked as important and are robustness related are the Key 
Characteristics. The Key Characteristics need to be maintained most carefully when redesigning 
a product or developing a new one. Because if the robustness against a noise factor that affects a 
Key Characteristic is improved, the products performance is improved significantly. 
For the comparison of the analysed products’ robustness, a Product Robustness Index is 
developed. The Product Robustness Index is based on the robustness related customer 
requirements. Each of these requirements is multiplied with its importance weighting factor and 
the mean of the weighted requirements is calculated. In the case of the three bicycle lights, the 
Magnic light is considered as the most robust one against the influence of noise factors. 
The Product Robustness Index defines the robustness of the products entire design. But to 
determine a technologies robustness, it needs to be analysed which noise factors are controlled by 
the technology and which on other design levels. The product’s design can be split up into four 
levels; the technology, the work principle, the concept/ structure and the detailed design. The 
technology level concerns the physical phenomena. 
First, the Key Characteristics Flowdown is carried out for each technology and Key 
Characteristic. This leads to the noise factors that affect the Key Characteristics and visualize 
which factors are controlled on the technology level. These are taken and their impact on the Key 
==PAGE:50==
50 
 
Characteristics’ performance is calculated. For this purpose, theoretical models of the 
technologies are made. The models describe the relations between the technologies’ performance 
and the different parameters they depend on. Some of these parameters are the noise factors and 
how they affect the performance of a technology can be seen in the model of the technology. 
In a next step, for all technologies similar nominal values are chosen and the quantitative impact 
of the noise factors’ variations on the performance of the Key Characteristics calculated.  The 
smaller the impact, the more robust a technology is. Because the Key Characteristics have the 
largest impact on the Product Robustness Index and the customer satisfaction, a technology that 
is more robust will increase the Product Robustness Index and the customer satisfaction 
significantly. The result shows which technology is the most robust one and should be chosen for 
the implementation in a new product.  
The process and results for the case of the bicycle light explain the methodology and proof its 
concept. But for a more accurate result of the case study, the analysis should be based on a more 
representative survey to gain more representative data for the customer satisfaction as well as the 
requirements’ importance. The importance ranking should not depend on the subjective opinion 
of the editor. Further on, the models for the technologies are much simplified. They are not 
accurate enough to receive detailed results for the technologies’ robustness. An other work could 
be to gain a deeper understanding of the physical phenomena on which the technology is based 
and based on that, to develop more exact theoretical models. This could eventually be obtained 
with the help of experiments.
