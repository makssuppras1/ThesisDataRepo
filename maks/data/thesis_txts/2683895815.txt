Abstract
The supply of freshwater and heat to Northeast Greenland fjords and coast are changing
owing to climate change induced acceleration of Greenland Ice Sheet mass loss, dimin-
ishing Arctic sea ice cover and anomalous warm water pulses propagating through the
Nordic Seas. Despite its node location as a recipient of these changes, relatively little is
known about the hydrographic response of the Northeast Greenland region. The purpose
of this study is to investigate hydrographic changes in Northeast Greenland fjords and
the adjacent coastal region. To this end, a vast collection of previously unsynthesised
observational data, going back almost one hundred years, was compiled. Analysis of
core water mass properties revealed substantial Polar Water freshening and Atlantic Wa-
ter warming across the entire study region highlighting the interconnected nature of the
systems in question. One exception hereto was the southernmost fjord system, Scoresby
Sound, which did not show a clear freshening trend. Furthermore, a profound vertical
redistribution of water masses was found, reflecting increased AW presence and thinning
of the Polar Water layer. These findings reflect ongoing changes in neighbouring regions.
Further studies are needed to establish causal relationships and constrain uncertainties
over how the changing Northeast Greenland hydrography contributes to and accompa-
nies future change.

Introduction
The Arctic Ocean has undergone substantial change in the recent decades. It is currently
warming (Polyakov et al. 2012), accumulating freshwater (Haine et al. 2015) and losing
sea ice cover (Spreen et al. 2020). Concurrently, mass loss from the Greenland Ice Sheet
(GrIS) has accelerated supplying an anomalous freshwater flux to the ocean (Bamber et
al. 2018). Located on one of the major outflow shelves of the Arctic Ocean, and in direct
contact with the GrIS, the Northeast Greenland fjords and coast are vulnerable to these
changes. In this context, the fjords of Greenland have received increased attention as
they serve two important roles in the communication of Arctic climate change. Firstly,
fjords are the main gateways through which GrIS melt enters the ocean. Secondly, fjords
facilitate propagation of warm oceanic waters to marine terminating glaciers accelerating
mass loss through submarine melting and undercutting (Straneo and Cenedese 2015).
Both GrIS melt and Arctic Ocean change have potential to affect the hydrographic prop-
erties of the region with consequences for its ecosystems and cryospheric components,
including sea ice and glaciers. Increased freshwater input may e.g. alter surface stratifi-
cation thereby impacting vertical mixing and nutrient availability. Furthermore, continued
southward transport along the coast returns the coastal waters and their properties to the
Subpolar North Atlantic. This has invoked speculation on the potential fate of freshwater in
deep convection sites leading to increased stratificaiton and slow down of the large-scale
Atlantic meridional overturning circulation (Rahmstorf et al. 2015; Böning et al. 2016).
Relatively few studies have been conducted on the Northeast Greenland region. At-
tention has been focused on Southeast and West Greenland fjords, with exception of the
Young Sound-Tyrolerfjord system (74°N), which has been consistently monitored since
2003. Efforts from this system have contributed to the understanding of seasonal changes
in fjord hydrography, circulation and the role of sea ice cover (Bendtsen et al. 2014; Kirillov
et al. 2015; Boone et al. 2018). Additionally, this program has revealed decadal fresh-
ening of both fjord and adjacent coastal waters (Sejr et al. 2017). Other studies have
concerned themselves with warm water pathways to the 79 North Glacier, demonstrating
ocean induced melting of marine terminating glaciers highlighting the coupled nature of
ice-ocean interactions (Wilson and Straneo 2015; Schaffer et al. 2017). By consolidating
previously unsynthesised observational data from the Northeast Greenland region, the
current study provides regional insights to hydrographic change in selected fjords and
the adjacent coast. Additionally, the hypothesis that uncovered changes have implica-
tions for upper ocean nutrient availability and accessibility of Atlantic Water as a marine
heat source is explored. The remainder of this report is structured as follows; section 2
is intended as an expanded introduction giving relevant background on the hydrographic
setting. In section 3 the metrics and used to characterise change are detailed. These
include core temperature and salinity properties of Polar and Atlantic Water, Polar layer
thickness and depth of Atlantic Water. These will be referred to continuously throughout
the results section 4, starting with a review of fjord hydrography, followed by coastal hy-
drography and finally stratification. Section 5 discusses the main findings and a summary
is given in section 6.

Background
The Northeast Greenland fjords and coastal region are located along one of the major
outflow shelves of the Arctic Ocean (Michel et al. 2015). As a conduit for waters and sea
ice exported from the Arctic Ocean, the region is directly influenced by ongoing changes
in the Arctic Ocean. The region is also influenced by the Nordic Seas in the east bringing
warm waters to the East Greenland Shelf, and continental Greenland to the west supply-
ing the fjords and coast with fresh runoff from the GrIS. The Arctic Ocean, Nordic Seas and
GrIS all have potential to affect the properties of coastal Northeast Greenland with conse-
quences for its ecosystems and cryospheric components including sea ice and glaciers.
Furthermore, continued southward transport along the coast returns the coastal waters
and their properties to the Subpolar North Atlantic, where they have potential to influence
deep convection and possibly the large-scale Atlantic meridional overturning circulation
(Holland et al. 2001; Jahn and Holland 2013; Böning et al. 2016). The following sec-
tions give a brief introduction to the main pathways to, and major water masses of, the
Northeast Greenland coastal region. Later, characteristics of fjords and some important
forcings are introduced.
2.1
Oceanographic setting
2.1.1
Principle water masses and currents
Fram Strait is the major gateway through which Arctic waters and sea ice enter the Nordic
Seas region. The East Greenland Current (EGC) originates in the Arctic and is responsible
for the southward transport though Fram Strait. Cold and relatively fresh Polar Surface
Waters and Arctic Halocline waters constitute the upper part of the EGC. These waters
originate from within the Arctic Ocean and have been formed via interactions between ice
melt, shelf waters primarily consisting of Eurasian river runoff and low salinity Pacific Water
entering the Arctic Ocean through Berring Strait (Rudels 2009). The Polar Surface Water
is a low salinity mixed layer influenced by sea ice melt in the summer and homogenised by
sea ice formation and brine rejection in the winter. The salinity of this layer differs laterally
across the Arctic. It can be as fresh as 30 psu to 32 psu in the Canada Basin and ranges
between 32 psu and 34 psu in the Eurasian basin (Rudels 2009). Below the PSW a steep
salinity gradient indicates the Arctic halocline layer, composed largely of waters of Atlantic
and Pacific origin. This layer is complex and typically consists of several different water
masses (Polyakov et al. 2020). Several mechanisms have been suggested to maintain
the halocline. These include salinification of the cold surface waters via brine rejection
overlain with fresher cold water and lateral advection of cold brine rich shelf waters (Steele
and Boyd 1998; Aagaardt et al. 1981).
The distribution and pathways of the surface waters are largely controlled by winds and
atmospheric pressure systems. A high pressure cell over the Arctic creates the anticy-
clonic Beaufort Gyre in the Canada Basin and a cyclonic circulation in the Siberian Arctic
(Carmack et al. 2016). These converge to form the Transpolar Drift, which advects fresh-
water and sea ice directly across the Arctic Ocean towards Fram Strait (Rudels 2012). The
EGC also caries Atlantic Waters which enter the Arctic Ocean through Fram Strait and
the Barrents Sea. During circulation around the Arctic Ocean as topographically steered
subsurface currents the Atlantic Water is modified via cooling and becomes Arctic Atlantic
Water (AAW).
Circulation in the Nordic Seas influences the Northeast Greenland region from the
east. Atlantic Waters enter the Nordic Seas with two branches of the Norwegian Atlantic
Current. One component follows the Norwegian continental shelf to converge with the

West Spitsbergen Current and continue north towards the Arctic Ocean as subsurface
current. A part of this flow branches off westward as Recirculating Atlantic Water (RAW,
Håvik et al. (2017)). Having continuously lost heat to the atmosphere during transition
through the Nordic Seas, RAW is several degrees colder than when it originally entered
the region (Blindheim and Østerhus 2005). RAW merges with the Polar Water and AAW
of the EGC at the western boundary of the Nordic Seas. The main core of this current
follows a path along the slope of the Northeast Greenland Shelf, dividing the cold and
comparatively fresh waters of Arctic origin from the warm saline Atlantic origin waters
(Håvik et al. 2017). Eddies and rim currents transport parts of the southward flow into
the interior basins (Blindheim and Østerhus 2005). The rest of the EGC continues to
the southern tip of Greenland. Here, a part branches of to enter the Subpolar North
Atlantic whilst the rest propagates northwards with the West Greenland Current. During
its transit, the waters of the EGC are modified via sea ice interactions, heat exchange with
the atmosphere and mixing with surrounding waters (Jones et al. 2008).

The East Greenland Current
The Northeast Greenland coastal region is dominated by the waters carried with the East
Greenland Current (Rudels et al. 2002). The East Greenland Current is characterised by
a persistent three-layered hydrographic structure. The upper 150 m – 200 m are occu-
pied by fresh Polar Waters comprised of Arctic Polar Surface Water and Arctic Halocline
waters. Atlantic origin water is found below the Polar Water and is defined as all interme-
diate waters with temperature above 0°C. This layer has contributions from Recirculating
Atlantic Water of the West Spitsbergen Current as well as Arctic Atlantic Water. Below the
Atlantic Water layer Greenland Sea deep water can be found. Based on velocity transects
Håvik et al. (2017) identified three branches of the EGC; the outer EGC, the shelfbreak
EGC and the Polar Surface Water jet. The shelfbreak EGC is the most prominent of these
and responsible for the majority of the southward transport. It flows along the shelfbreak
and upper continental slope with velocities ranging between 0.2 and 0.4 m/s. The outer
EGC is found further offshore over the mid to deep continental slope. Finally, the Po-
lar Surface Water Jet flows closer to the Northeast Greenland coast. It is weaker than
the shelfbreak EGC, with highest velocities about 0.2 m/s, but still an important part of the
current system responsible for transporting approximately 35% of the freshwater exported
from the Arctic via Fram Strait (de Steur et al. 2018).
2.1.2
Sea ice along the Northeast Greenland Coast
Another characteristic feature of the Northeast Greenland coast is the steady inflow of
sea ice it receives from the Arctic Ocean (Michel et al. 2015). Between 85% and 90%
of Arctic sec ice is exported through Fram Strait (Spreen et al. 2020). The sea ice is
directed towards the northeast Greenland coast, where it forms a dense ice pack in the
north (Bacon et al. 2014). Additionally, land-fast ice formed locally during winter, is present
along the coast. The maximum sea ice extent occurs in April, at which time sea ice covers
the Greenland coast and shelf from Fram Strait to Cape Farewell at the southern tip of
Greenland. By the time of the minimum sea ice extent in August the sea ice has retreated
to ~74°N (based on median sea ice extent (1981-2010), Fetterer et al. (2017)). In more
recent years the minimum sea ice extent has retreated further north, e.g. all the way to
~78°N in September 2019. Melting of sea ice represents a major source of freshwater to
the region.
2.2
Northeast Greenland Fjords
Fjords facilitate communication between the ocean and GrIS in two major ways. Firstly,
fjords are transport pathways through which runoff from the GrIS can enter the ocean and
potentially reach deep convection sites. Secondly, fjords are gateways for warm deep
waters to reach marine terminating glaciers and potentially accelerate mass loss through
submarine melting and undercutting (Straneo and Cenedese 2015; Wood et al. 2021). For
these reasons interest in Greenland’s fjords has peaked in recent decades. A lot of the
attention has been directed to Southeast and West Greenland fjords. Less has been said
on fjords in the northeast. An exception hereto is the Young Sound – Tyrolerfjord system
at 74°N, which has been monitored systematically since 2003 as part of The Greenland
Ecosystem Monitoring Programme. As such, the Young Sound - Tyrolerfjord system has
been the subject of many previous ecological and physical studies (eg. Bendtsen et al.
(2014), Sejr et al. (2017), and Boone et al. (2018)). The topic of ocean – fjord – glacier
interactions involves a wide range of spatial and temporal scales. The largest scales are
those associated with regional oceanographic circulation (> 1000 km) and the smallest
are those associated with exchange through the ice-ocean boundary layer (millimetre
scale). The fjord scale modification of water mass properties and fjord-shelf exchange
are of particular concern. The processes thought to be dominant in Greenland’s glacial

2.2.1
Characteristics of Northeast Greenland Fjords
The fjord systems of Northeast Greenland are commonly long, narrow and several hun-
dred meters deep. They typically have steep ridges and can have complex geometries
with several interconnected fjords (Cottier et al. 2010). Many also have one or more sills
that act as topographic barriers hindering free exchange of waters with the adjacent shelf.
Another important characteristic, unique to Greenland’s fjords, is the seasonal surface
melt from the GrIS they receive as discharge. For fjords with marine terminating glaciers
the freshwater is released at grounding line depth and may persist in the subsurface de-
pending on the stratification of the fjord waters. Fjords influenced by land terminating
glaciers will, on the other hand, receive a large seasonal freshwater input to the surface
(Straneo and Cenedese 2015). Combined with seasonal ice cover, this produces a pro-
nounced seasonal cycle in fjord properties (Straneo and Cenedese 2015; Boone et al.
2017).
Stratification and water masses
Greenland fjords typically have a three-layered structure comprising of a surface, inter-
mediate and deep layer. The surface layer is highly variable and strongly influenced by
local winds, summer insolation and discharge (Bendtsen et al. 2014). The intermediate
layer is advected from the shelf and therefore derived from the Polar Water layer of the
EGC. Mixing within the fjord modifies the PW properties to varying degrees. AW will oc-
cupy the deep section of the fjord, if the sill bathymetry is sufficiently deep. In shallow sill
fjords, where inflow of AW is hindered, the bottom waters are typically waters that have
been locally modified. Wind mixing, cooling and brine rejection act to break down the
stratification through winter and spring. This three-layered structure is therefore typically
only fully developed during summer (Cottier et al. 2010).
Rotational effects
The effects of planetary rotation on fjord hydrography and circulation depend on the width
and stratification of the fjord as well as the Coriolis parameter (Cottier et al. 2010). Since
the Coriolis parameter depends on latitude and stratification varies seasonally, the influ-
ence of rotation will vary seasonally and spatially. The balance between stratification and
rotation is often evaluated with the Rossby Radius of deformation, which together with the
fjord width gauges the importance of rotation in a given system. If the radius is smaller
than the width of the fjord, rotational effects are expected, and manifest themselves as
cross fjord gradients and deflection of flow to the right of the direction of motion (Cottier
et al. 2010). For east facing Greenland fjords, this means inflow is concentrated along
the northern coast and outflow along the southern coast. Due to the narrow character
of many Greenland fjords rotational effects are generally considered negligible (Suther-
land et al. 2014; Sciascia et al. 2014; Straneo and Cenedese 2015). However, rotational
effects should be expected for some of the broader systems.
2.2.2
Circulation and fjord-shelf exchange
Fjord circulation is influenced by local winds, tides, shelf variability, buoyancy forcing from
glaciers and rivers, as well as surface fluxes (Jackson et al. 2018). These external forces
will modify, mix or exchange fjord waters. Several of these are likely to be present in North-
east Greenland fjords, however, only a few are summarised below. Particular attention
is given to buoyancy and shelf driven circulation as these are thought to be dominant in
Greenland’s glacial fjords (Straneo and Cenedese 2015).

Estuarine circulation is common in many fjords (Bendtsen et al., 2014). It arises as a
response to surface discharge at the head of the fjord which entrains water from the layer
below as it flows downstream. This motivates a compensatory inflow of denser water
in the opposite direction. An analogy hereto in fjords influenced by marine terminating
glaciers, is the buoyancy driven flow. This mode of circulation involves the release of
freshwater at depth, which subsequently rises as a plume to the depth of neutral buoyancy.
Ambient waters are entrained as the plume rises, drawing deep waters in close to the
glacier. Cowton et al. (2016) has argued that buoyancy driven circulation is the main
conveyor of heat to glaciers for this reason. Owing to the strong stratification common in
Greenland fjords, the plume may not reach the surface, but rather intrude between the PW
and AW layers. This implies that a three- or four-layered description of Greenland’s fjords
is necessary, in contrast to the two-layer description commonly used to explain classic
estuarine circulation (Straneo and Cenedese 2015).
Another important mode of circulation is shelf-driven flow also known as intermedi-
ary circulation. This circulation may be initiated by along-shore winds or advected density
anomalies, establishing density differences between the shelf and fjord. The resulting ho-
risontal pressure gradient forces a flow within the fjord (Jackson et al. 2018). Observations
from two Southeast Greenland fjords showed that shelf driven flow was the dominant cir-
culation mode between September and May and commonly associated with downwelling
wind events (Jackson et al. 2014). The specific fjord response to shelf-driven flow will
depend on the fjord geometry (width, length and sill depth) as well as the forcing ampli-
tude and period (Jackson et al. 2018). Shelf-driven flows have been shown to drive rapid
(3-10 days) fjord water renewal in Kangerlussuaq fjords and Sermilik fjord on the South-
east Greenland coast (Jackson et al. 2014). Timescales of water renewal are important
as they set a boundary for the timescales over which external forcing may be transmitted
to through a fjord. However, as Straneo and Cenedese (2015) point out, large discrepan-
cies in renewal timescales exist, highlighting the knowledge gap in fjord-shelf exchange
processes.
2.2.3
Coloured dissolved organic matter as a tracer
Coloured dissolved organic matter (CDOM) is a heterogeneous mixture of organic com-
pounds small enough to be considered dissolved (< 1 µm). The “coloured” part of CDOM
refers to the material’s optical properties, which include absorption of light over a range of
visible and UV wavelengths (Coble 2007). CDOM is derived from biotic and abiotic degra-
dation of organic matter. Autochthonous production is the dominant source of CDOM in
the open ocean releasing dissolved or particulate organic matter at every level of the
food web. Rivers are also considered important CDOM sources as they supply terrestrial
organic matter to the coastal environment (Coble 2007). A fraction of CDOM emits a flu-
orescence signal that can be measured in situ with a fluorometer. Fluorometers typically
measure at single excitation and emission wavelength pairs providing information on the
concentration of CDOM (Gonçalves-Araujo et al. 2016).
Both of the major CDOM sources are represented in the Arctic Ocean. Pacific derived
Halocline waters pick up CDOM from autochthonous production as they pass over the
Chukchi Shelf, and CDOM associated with Atlantic derived Halocline water is ascribed
runoff from primarily Siberian rivers (Dmitrenko et al. 2019). CDOM is incorporated into
the halocline layers as both salt and CDOM are rejected during sea ice formation leading
to an accumulation of brine and CDOM on the shelves, which is eventually advected into
the interior. Fluorescence measurements can therefore be used to trace the high CDOM
contents of Arctic Halocline waters. Furthermore, owning to variable optical properties of
CDOM from different sources, fluorescence measurements can be used to trace Arctic
river CDOM in the Polar Water of the EGC (Amon et al. 2003).

Data and methodology
3.1
Data
In situ hydrographic observations from the East Greenland shelf, coast and fjords were
consolidated for the purposes of the current study (Table 3.1). The data set consists of
publicly available data from large oceanographic databases, including the ICES oceano-
graphic database, the World Ocean Database (WOD), the Global Temperature and Salin-
ity Profiling Programme (GTSPP), and PANGAEA. Only profiles identified as ”accepted
value”, ”element appears to be correct” or ”element appears to be probably good” by the
WOD and GTSPP quality controls, respectively, were downloaded. Additionally, data
provided by personal contacts are included. To further ensure data quality, all profiles
were passed through a value range, depth inversion and gradient check. Expected value
ranges were set based on literature values (Bourgain and Gascard 2012; Pawlowicz
2013): The temperature range was defined between −2 ◦C and 20 ◦C, salinity range from
0 to 42 g kg−1 and pressure range from 0 dbar to 4000 dbar. The gradient check was done
to avoid erroneous increases or decreases in temperature or salinity with depth. A gradi-
ent was deemed unreliable if it exceeded 0.7 ◦C m−1 for temperature and 3 g kg−1 m−1) for
salinity (Garcia et al. 2018). Measurements that fell outside of the expected ranges were
discarded. Data points that fell below the freezing temperature of sea water were likewise
removed. Finally, any duplicate profiles were identified and removed. This resulted in a
total of 14,077 profiles collected in the summer months (June - October), spanning almost
a hundred years from 1923 to 2019. Prior to 1990, the majority of profiles were sampled
using bottles to measure temperature and salinity at standard levels. Modern profiles were
primarily sampled with CTD instrumentation, typically with an accuracy between 0.001 ◦C
and 0.005 ◦C for temperature, 0.003 psu to 0.01 psu for salinity and ± 2.4 dbar for pres-
sure. Few profiling floats contribute to the data set between 2016 and 2018 with accuracy
similar to that of CTD casts. Additionally, fluorescence measurements of coloured dis-
solved organic matter were available for some profiles. These were collected with in-situ
fluorometers, operating on the excitation 350 nm and emission 450 nm wavelength pair.
These were calibrated to laboratory measurements of DOM fluorescence made using a
Horiba Aqualog fluorometer, and pure water as a blank and reference (Lawaetz and Sted-
mon 2009).
In-situ temperature and practical salinity were converted to conservative temperature
and absolute salinity, respectively, using the Gibbs-SeaWater (GSW) Oceanographic
Toolbox built for MATLAB by McDougall and Barker (2011). Subsequent analysis and
computation of derived variables was done using these parameters, from hereon referred
to as ”temperature” and ”salinity” unless otherwise specified.
Defining the study area
In order to focus the study a coastal region is defined by a polygon extending 50 km
from the coastline. This definition is drawn based on observations of the East Green-
land Coastal Current on the Southeast Greenland shelf as far as 30 km offshore (Bacon
2002; Sutherland and Pickart 2008). Here, a broader region is defined to ensure a certain
amount of data. The northern and southern sections of the East Greenland coast are de-
lineated by Denmark Strait. This resulted in a total of 2283 profiles between 1925 to 2019.
Spatial coverage is limited up until the 1970’s, and improves thereafter (Figure A.1).

3.2
Characterising hydrographic change
Warm subsurface water of Atlantic origin, here simply Atlantic Water (AW), is charac-
terised by temperature > 0 ◦C and salinity > 34.4 g kg−1. Polar Water (PW) is defined by
temperature < 0 ◦C and salinity < 34.4 g kg−1 (following Sejr et al. (2017)). Hydrographic
changes of PW and AW are characterised by the core temperature and salinity properties
of the two water masses as well as by their vertical positioning and extent. Each metric
is described in the following.
Core temperature and salinity
PW and AW cores are identified by the temperature minimum and maximum within the
two water masses, respectively (Figure 3.2). These features are identified for each pro-
file after linear interpolation onto a 1 m spaced grid from 0 m to 600 m. Only profiles with
depths larger than 100 m were considered. In the absence of a pronounced temperature
minimum, the mean properties between 40 m and 60 m were used to characterise the
profile. This was particularly relevant for shallow sill fjord systems such as Young Sound.
Identification of the AW core can likewise be problematic if the sampling depth does not
penetrate adequately into the AW layer. To this end, the temperature of the subsequent 3
depth-levels were compared. If the temperature deviated by more than 0.01 ◦C between
subsequent depth-levels, the core property estimates were deemed unreliable and dis-
carded (following Lind et al. (2016)). A final concern regarding the ability of historical data
with coarse vertical resolution, to accurately estimate core properties was addressed with
a small experiment. In this experiment the procedure was run on high resolution profiles
sub-sampled at typical bottle depths (5 m, 10 m, 25 m, 50 m, 75 m, 100 m, 150 m, 200 m,
300 m, 400 m). Core properties identified using the sub-sampled and full resolution pro-
files were then compared. Differences in temperature and salinity properties were found
to be statistically insignificant (maximum p-value = 0.05). However, the experiment indi-
cated that depth of the AW core requires higher vertical resolution to be reliably estimated.

3.3
Time series construction and trend analysis
Regional time series of each of the parameters described above were constructed. Given
non-random and often opportunistic sampling, simple averaging is not sufficient to pro-
vide an accurate spatial representation (McGrew et al. 2014). Spatial sampling bias is
accounted for by computing declustered statistics, whereby extensively sampled regions
do not dominate the time series. This is achieved by assigning each data point a weight
proportional to the area it represents, as determined by Voronoi polygons. With this ap-
proach, a polygon is constructed about each sample point. The distance between any
location within a given polygon and the sample point it represents, is shorter than the dis-
tance to any other sample point. Voronoi polygons are constructed using the VoronoiLimit
function for MATLAB by Sievers (2020). Weights, w, are then calculated as the proportion
of polygon area to the entire area of interest:

century, this approach can only reasonably be applied for the period after 1970. Earlier
data is, however, included to provide context. The resulting statistics and final time se-
ries are thus area-weighted spatial representations that account for the heterogeneity of
sparse data coverage.
Linear trends are computed using least squares regression and statistical significance
is evaluated with the Student’s t-test.

Fjord Hydrography
Hydrographic observations are presented for each fjord individually. Due to limited data
availability in some of the fjords the results are presented in a qualitative manner. Statisti-
cal measures are stated explicitly when relevant. A summary of fjord findings is provided
at the end of the section.
Dijmpha Sound
The vertical temperature and salinity distributions in Dijmpha Sound reveal a stably strat-
ified hydrography showcasing the three layered structure typical for Arctic fjords (Fig-
ure 4.1). A fresh surface layer overlies the PW layer with characteristic sub-zero temper-
atures and relatively low salinity. Whilst a pronounced temperature maximum is absent,
positive temperatures of the deep waters reveal the presence of AW in the fjord.
The earliest available observations from within Djimpha Sound stem from 1997. Data
from three subsequent years reveal changes to the AW core in terms of both warming and
salinification through the observation period. More noticeable, however, is the vertical
displacement of the 0 ◦C isotherm from a mean depth of 232 m in 1997 to 156 m in 2017.
A reduction in the PW layer thickness is likewise evident and accompanied by shoaling of
the PW core. The PW layer additionally experiences substantial freshening (−1.02 g kg−1)
and moderate warming (0.25 ◦C) from 1997 to 2017. This is, however, not true for 2016
where the PW is both more saline and colder than in 2012. These changes are reflected in
the density profiles, for which the net effect is an exaggeration of the pycnocline associated
with PW. The resemblance of average density and salinity profiles showcases the strong
saline control on the vertical density structure - an unsurprising feature for Arctic systems.

Young Sound
A shallow (45 m) sill at the entrance to Young Sound hampers free inflow of AW (Sejr
et al. 2017). The hydrographic structure of Young Sound is therefore markedly different
than that of Dijmpha Sound. In Young Sound, a thin fresh surface layer caps the strong
gradients that occupy the upper 10-30 m. Below this, PW dominates and temperature and
salinity change minimally with depth (Figure 4.2). The entire subsurface water column
shifts towards warmer temperatures and lower salinities between 2003 and 2014. This
is seen as a drastic deepening of the 32 and 33 isohalines from 30 m and 47 m to 50 m
and 223 m, respectively. Sejr et al. (2017) determine the rate of freshening and between
2003 and 2015 to −0.099 g kg−1 y−1 (p-value < 0.01). The freshening trend is reversed
in 2014, as the following years are characterised by increasing salinity. This change is
most pronounced in the 30-50 m depth range, where salinification occurs at a rate of
0.057 g kg−1 y−1 (p-value < 0.01). The temperature trend of the 30-50-m depth range
likewise reverses in 2014, cooling at a rate of −0.083 ◦C y−1 (p-value = 0.03). The net
effect of the described temperature and salinity changes is a deepening of isopycnals.
Consequently, the waters of Young Sound have become lighter in accordance with finding
reported by Boone et al. (2018).

Godthåb Golf

presence of a warm source prior to 2019. Consequently, the deep waters experience
a temperature change of 1.25 ◦C between 1931 and 2019. The 50-70 m depth range

Kong Oscar and Kaiser Franz Joseph Fjords
The hydrographic structure of Kong Oscar and Kaiser Franz Joesph fjords is that typical of
deep sill Arctic fjord systems: Warm, saline AW under cold, fresh PW, capped by a warm
and fresh surface layer. These fjord systems are remarkably homogeneous throughout
their outer sections. However, spatial variability within the narrow inner arms of the fjord
systems is strong (Figure 4.10). As such, the following is based on properties of the outer
sections of the fjord systems.
PW core temperature has remained fairly constant through the observation period in-
creasing by only 0.102 ◦C between 1931 and 2018. Significant freshening of the PW core
was however observed. Core salinity dropped from 33.84 g kg−1 in 1931 to 32.31 g kg−1
in 2018, yielding a total difference of 1.53 g kg−1. Most of this freshening occurred be-
tween 1930 and 2013, decreasing further by only 0.04 g kg−1 between 2013 and 2019.
Salinity of the AW core increases by 0.08 g kg−1 through the observation period. Consid-
ering the vertical redistribution of water masses, considerable salinification occurs from
150 m to the bottom. Consequently, both the halocline and pcynocline have become
more pronounced. AW core temperature shows large long and short term variability, e.g.
a 0.78 ◦C increase was observed between 1931 and 1932 - a scale of change which is
similar to that observed over entire observation period (0.92 ◦C).
Perhaps even more impressive than the changes of core properties are the changes
in the vertical distribution of the water masses. The vertical extent of the PW layer has re-
duced dramatically through the observation period. The PW layer occupied almost 200 m
in 1931 and only 90 m in 2018 (Figure 4.4). In concert, with a correspondingly substantial
shoaling of the AW upper boundary, this represents a huge change of fjord hydrography
and e.g. entails increased fjord heat content by volume alone. This will be elucidated in
the discussion.

Scoresby Sound
PW and AW characteristics are well reflected in the hydrography of Scoresby Sound. PW
is typically found in the upper 250 m, and most pronounced between 50 m and 100 m.
Salinity and temperature increase steadily below the PW layer, in some years reaching
temperatures above 1 ◦C at the AW core, and thereafter decreasing slightly towards the
bottom. Despite the considerable length of Scoresby Sound (~195 km) AW characteristics
are largely unchanged from the fjord mouth and through the outer section. PW on the other
hand, shows steady warming with increased distance from the mouth. This warming can
be as large as 0.65 ◦C resulting in a relaxation of the steep transition between AW and
PW.
In light of the extreme width of Scoresby Sound (~40 km), it is reasonable to assume
that fjord circulation is subject to rotational effects. Based on hydrographic observations
from 2016, Seifert et al. (2019) estimate the Rossby Radius of deformation to be 6 km in
the main trunk of Scoresby Sound. Given the much larger fjord width, horisontal circula-
tion patterns, boundary currents and cross fjord gradients are likely present. Under the
influence of rotation the inflowing waters are deflected to the right of the current direction,
corresponding to the northern part of Scoresby Sound. Correspondingly, the outflow is
concentrated along the southern coast. Fjord hydrogrphy and circulation can therefore not
be represented in a two dimensional manner. In the absence of cross fjord transects and
velocity data, the representativeness of the observations acquired for the current study
cannot be assessed. The reader is invited to keep these effects in mind, when considering
the findings presented below.
The AW core temperature is observed to vary considerably through the observation
period. It is particularly high (1.21 ◦C) in 1933 and markedly lower in 1959 (0.31 ◦C).
AW core temperature remains stable until 1990, at which time it increases at a rate of
0.03 ◦C y−1 (p-value = 0.04) to a mean value of 1.47 ◦C in 2018. The same temporal pat-
tern is present in AW core salinity, which increases at a rate of 0.0032 g kg−1 y−1 (p-value

Whilst the magnitude and timing of the uncovered changes vary between the studied
fjords, the general direction of change is similar. That is, all fjords experience warming
and substantial freshening of the PW layer. The exception hereto being Scoresby Sound,
where no trends in PW were found. Warming and salinification of AW is likewise observed
in all fjord systems (where AW is present). Additionally, the vertical distribution of PW and

from Young Sound, Godthåb Golf, Kong Oscar and Kaiser Franz Joseph fjords indicate

Comparison with coastal observations
Four sub-regions (A1 - A4) going from north to south along the coast were defined to
enable comparison of fjord and coastal observations (A.4). Results from the comparison
are shown in T/S space on figure Figure 4.6.
Migration of PW and AW core properties are evident. Three of four regions show a
shift in PW towards fresher and warmer properties. In the northernmost region this is
seen as progressive migration down the salinity axis. This shift amounts to freshening
of −1.67 g kg−1 between 1979 and 2019. Temperature increases gradually parallel to
the freezing temperature line until 2012, at which time deviation from freezing increases.
Apart from 2018, T/S properties are fairly well confounded within years. The large variabil-
ity seen in 2018 is driven by observations collected at the Nioghalvfjerdsfjorden Glacier
front. 2016 deviates from the general pattern as it is both colder and more saline than the
immediately surround years. On Figure 4.6 it is seen as part of a concentration of data
points centered around 32.5 g kg−1 and −1.7 ◦C. Freshening also dominates the second
coastal region, where PW salinity decreases by −1.58 g kg−1 between 2003 and 2014.
Furthermore, a slight, yet noticeable, migration upwards in T/S space reveals coastal
warming over the same time period. In 2014 the trends begin to reverse and movement
is now towards more saline properties. Only few observations are available from the third
coastal region. This region represents the waters outside Kong Oscar and Kaiser Franz
Joseph fjords. Figure 4.6 visualises the −1.53 g kg−1 drop in fjord PW core salinity previ-
ously presented. The available coastal stations support this finding. PW core properties in
the final region immediately outside Scoresby Sound, are found to be highly variable and
scattered. No dominant PW pattern can be discerned. AW core properties of the north-
ernmost coastal region show migration towards warmer and saltier properties. In total
salinity increased by 0.22 g kg−1 between 1979 and 2019 and temperature by 1.2 ◦C over
the same period. A similar magnitude of AW core temperature change was observed in
the southernmost coastal region, where temperatures warmed by 1.42 ◦C between 1990
and 2018. Data were deemed too scarce for comparison in the third coastal region.
The characteristics of the fjord waters closely resemble those of the coastal water
masses. This is important as it speaks to the connection across systems, and shows that
fjord properties are susceptible to coastal variability. As such, the hydrographic changes
observed in the coastal sub-regions correspond well with fjord changes. The emerging
picture is one of a fresher, warmer PW layer and a warmer AW layer.

Coloured Dissolved Organic Matter
The presence of Arctic CDOM on the East Greenland shelf has been documented by
several previous studies (Amon et al. 2003; Granskog et al. 2012; Gonçalves-Araujo et al.
2016). Granskog et al. (2012) demonstrate that high CDOM levels on the East Greenland
shelf are associated with PW exported from the Arctic via Fram Strait. This chemical
signal is not limited to shelf and coastal region, but further advected into fjords. Figure 4.9
shows elevated CDOM levels in the PW of the coastal region, Dijmpha Sound, Kong Oscar
fjord and Kaizer Franz Joesph fjord. CDOM decreases towards the surface indicating
dilution by precipitation and/or sea ice melt. Note, CDOM is essentially absent in AW.
The presence of CDOM in East Greenland fjords proves that PW originating in the Arctic
retains its properties during its southward propagation, thus making it possible to trace
these water masses for more than 1000 km from their place of origin on the Siberian
shelves. This speaks to a connectivity between East Greenland fjords and the Arctic
Ocean and its shelves.

4.4
Stratification
Spatial patterns of stratification are evaluated over the large scale latitudinal dimension
of the study region. Two periods, 1980-2010 and 2010-2019, are defined to facilitate the
analysis. The periods were defined in an effort to ensure similar spatial coverage and
number of observations in each period. Stratification is evaluated over i) the surface to
200 m interval to summarise hydrographic findings, ii) the surface to the depth of the
0.75 ◦C isotherm to asses the accessibility of AW as a marine heat source and iii) from
the surface to 50 m to evaluate potential nutrient limitation in the euphotic zone.
4.4.1
Stratification, surface to 200 m
Upper ocean stratification shows a general pattern of decrease along the coast Figure 4.12.
This is particularly apparent in the second period, whereas a region of low stratification
between ~76°N and ~74°N interrupts the signal in the first period. This spatial pattern can
be understood from the vertical distribution of density (Figure A.6). High stratification in
the northernmost part of the study region is explained by relatively low density of water
between 50 m and 125 m, broadly corresponding to the PW layer. The density of this
depth range subsequently increases, causing the integrated density difference of the up-
per 200 m to decrease, as reflected the low APE between ~76°N and ~74°N. The effect
of increasing subsurface density is somewhat dampened by decreasing surface density
over the same spatial range. South of ~74°N, stratification increases again. This cannot
be explained by subsurface layers as these remain largely unchanged. However, since
surface density continues to decline, the increase is attributed to hereto. Thus, the overall
pattern of upper ocean stratification between 1980 and 2010 follows that of PW density
along the majority of the coast, insinuating a central role of PW. This is confirmed by a
high negative correlation (R = -0.79, p-value < 0.01) between PW density and APE across
the coastal region.
In the second period subsurface density distributions mirror that of the surface, only
with smaller latitudinal gradients (Figure A.6). Consequently, the water column becomes
more homogeneous along the coast and the highest values of stratification are found to
the north. Strong relationships with APE are found for both surface and PW density (R =
-0.60 and R = -0.73, p-value < 0.01). In summary, the stratification of the upper 200 m is
dominated by the density of the 50 m to 125 m depth range north of ~74°N. This broadly
corresponds to the PW layer. South hereof, the influence of PW decreases and eventually
the pattern of stratification follows that of the surface density. This is, however, not the
case in the second period, where both PW and surface density are important across the
entire length of the coast.
Stratification is stronger along the majority of the northeast coast in the second period.
This is explained by an exaggeration of the density contrast between the upper 75 m and
lower 125 m of the water column. As such, the the upper 75 m have become lighter.
This is most pronounced at the surface, which experienced a two unit decrease in density
between ~80°N and ~75°N. Consequently, the latitudinal surface density gradient has
reversed between the two periods. The depth range occupied by PW has likewise seen a
decrease in density, consistent with effect of freshening reported in above sections. The
100 m to 125 m depth range has become denser, which is attributed shoaling of AW. Little
to no change has occurred between 150 m and 200 m. Thus, despite volumetric effects
of AW replacing PW, the decrease in surface and PW density results in an increasing net
change in stratification north of ~71°N. Interestingly, all subsurface layers experience an
increase in density in the area immediately outside of Scoresby Sound, explaining the
observed decrease in stratification.

Stratification, surface to 0.75°C degree isotherm
The temporal and spatial patterns of stratification from the surface to the 0.75 ◦C isotherm

of the patterns uncovered for the general upper ocean stratification. Since the 0.75 ◦C lifts
between the two periods, the temporal change in stratification is not as drastic as for upper
ocean stratification. Nevertheless, strengthened stratification is the dominant signal north
of ~71°N.
4.4.3
Stratification, surface to 50m
A different pattern emerges when considering stratification of the upper 50 m. Figure 4.14
shows a prominent pattern of increasing stratification along the coast between 1980 and
2010. This is consistent with decreasing surface density, and hence a more homoge-
neous upper water column in the north. Density at 50 m varies between 1026.1 kg m−3 at
80°N to 1026.3 kg m−3 at 70°N, with an intermediate maximum of 1026.6 k m−3 at 75°N.
This variability can, however, be considered small relative to the 1.2 unit drop in surface
density. The latitudinal density gradient at 50 m is more pronounced in the second pe-
riod, increasing from 1025.67 kg m−3 to 1026.58 kg m−3 along the coast. Owing to similar
surface and subsurface spatial density patterns, period two shows relatively uniform strat-
ification along the coast without a dominant pattern or direction.
Stratification increases north of ~75°N between the two periods. The region south
hereof, experiences a reduction in stratification. These difference are attributed to the
reversal of the latitudinal surface density gradient previously mentioned and seen on Fig-
ure A.6.
The 0 m to 50 m depth interval is too shallow for a considerable influence of PW,
thereby explaining the discrepancy in spatial patterns of stratification when evaluated over
the upper 50 m and 200 m. The stability of the upper 50 m is thus largely controlled by
surface density and only to a lesser degree by the density distribution of the subsurface.
This implies a central role for processes acting on the surface and their ability to modulate
nutrient availability in the euphotic zone.

Discussion
5.1
Main hydrographic changes
5.1.1
Sources of PW freshening
Substantial local and regional freshening of PW was found. The main potential sources
of freshening are GrIS melt exported at the surface or subsurface via fjords, or freshwater
exported from the Arctic via Fram Strait in either liquid or solid state. The relevance of
these potential sources of freshening is discussed in the following.
The Northeast GrIS is thought to have been in equilibrium up until the early 2000’s
(Mayer et al. 2018). Speed up and thinning at the ice sheet margins has since then ac-
celerated mass loss to the coastal environment (Khan et al. 2014). Bamber et al. (2012)
estimated the terrestrial freshwater flux from Northeast Greenland, including GrIS and
tundra runoff, to 125 ± 9 km3 y−1 between 1992 and 2010. This value increases to 240
km3 y−1 when evaluated over the 2007-2016 period (Bamber et al. 2018). Even if this dis-
charge is assumed to occur over only the summer period, it is still an order of magnitude
lesser than that of the sea ice flux through Fram Strait, for which the most recent estimate
is 2400 ± 640 km3 y−1 (mean 1992 - 2014, Spreen et al. (2020)). Note, that in order to
convert a sea ice flux into a freshwater flux, a reference salinity of ambient water and
sea ice salinity must be assumed. Regardless of the choice of these values, the sea ice
freshwater flux will remain much larger than the terrestrial flux from Greenland. If fresh-
ening were to originate from GrIS mass loss, the signal would be expected to strengthen
along the coast as GrIS run-off (as well as other terrestrial sources) is concentrated from
upstream areas. No evidence to support this was found in the current study, where the
largest freshening was found in the northernmost coastal region and freshening seemingly
dampened along the coast. Furthermore, the non-linearity of the freshening signal in both
fjords and the coastal region opposes GrIS melt as the dominant source of freshening,
as it is expected to increase gradually. If, on the other hand, sea ice from Fram Strait is
the dominant source, we need an explanation as how the signal is communicated to the
Northeast Greenland coastal region and fjords. Up until relatively recently the existence
of the East Greenland Coastal Current (EGCC) north of Denmark Strait was debated (the
EGCC is known as the Polar Surface Water Jet on the northeast shelf). However, hydro-
graphic transects from the summer of 2012 reveal its presence as far north as 74°N (Håvik
et al., 2017). More recently, it has been documented already in Fram Strait (deStruer et
al., 2018), and in a study by Foukal et al. (2020) a connection between the two coastal
currents, i.e. the EGCC and Polar Surface Water Jet was established. Differentiating be-
tween which outflow the sea ice follows, i.e. the Polar Surface Water Jet or the shelf break
EGC is important as sea ice exported with the latter is readily mixed off the shelf into the
Greenland Sea (Foukal et al. 2020). As such sea ice export must follow the Polar Water
Surface Jet to be of relevance to the coastal region. Based on a combination of ice drift
speeds, radar imagery and sea level pressure across Fram Strait, Smedsrud et al. (2017)
constructed an 80 year time series of sea ice area export through Fram Strait. From this
the authors show increased annual sea ice area export initiated in 1979. Considerable
interannual variability is associated with this signal. Particularly low export is noted in the
years 2003 and 2005, and particularly high export is noted in 1995, 1999 and 2011-2013.
Conversely, by taking ice thickness into account Spreen et al. (2020) argue that sea ice
volume export through Fram Strait has been declining over recent years. Clearly the rel-
evance of sea ice as a freshwater source necessitates melting along the northeast coast.
The energy available for sea ice melt must therefore be evaluated alongside trends in sea

==PAGE:35==
ice export. This is beyond the scope of the current study. Here, it is simply noted that
summer export is likely to be of greater importance to coastal freshening as winter export
is transported further south before melting (Königk 2005).
Finally, liquid freshwater export via Fram Strait must be considered. PW in the EGC
comprises Arctic Halocline and Polar Surface Waters, which in turn are influenced by
Arctic river runoff, net precipitation and Pacific Water. Positive freshwater anomalies north
of Denmark Strait have previously been linked to enhanced Pacific Water export via Fram
Strait (de Steur et al. 2015). However, whilst the composition of the Fram Strait outflow is
associated with large variability (Dodd et al. 2009), the overall variability is much less. This
implies that other freshwater sources compensate when e.g. Pacific Water is absent. In
line with this, no trend in freshwater transport was found between 1997 and 2009 (Haine et
al. 2015). The annual mean freshwater flux was estimated to 2800 ± 420 km3 y−1 for the
2000-2010 period, which is very similar to the 1980-2000 estimate of 2700 ± 530 km3 y−1
(Haine et al. 2015). This does not hold for the subsequent period (2010-2015), over
which an additional 3106 km3 freshwater, relative to the 2003-2009 mean, was exported
(de Steur et al. 2018). The same study notes low freshwater export from 2009-2010,
high export between 2010 and 2013 and again decreased export from 2014 to 2015. The
relative stability of the freshwater export prior to 2009 argues against liquid freshwater
as the main source of coastal freshening, as freshening was already well underway by
this point. Furthermore, the volume of the 2010-2013 fresh anomaly was more or less
conserved during its transit from Fram Strait to Denmark Strait. This implies that the
majority of the freshwater anomaly was confined to the shelf break EGC, out of coastal
influence.
The above discussion is based on few data with limited temporal and spatial cover-
age. Nonetheless, the main potential sources of freshwater have been put into context of
the freshening observed in the current study. Based hereon, I surmise that local melting
of sea ice exported through Fram Strait is the dominant source of PW freshening. Arc-
tic freshwater, precipitation and melt of icebergs and not least GrIS are all contributing
factors. The importance of latter is expected to increase in the coming decades as mass
loss of the Northeast GrIS accelerates (Mouginot et al. 2019) and sea ice export via Fram
Strait declines (Haine et al. 2015; Spreen et al. 2020).
5.1.2
AW warming and shoaling
In addition to PW freshening, the AW core temperature was found to have increased over
the study period. This result is consistent with Schaffer et al. (2017), who found average
shelf temperatures at 200 m depth to be 0.5 ◦C warmer in the 2000-2016 period compared
to the 1979-1999 period. In a later study by Schaffer et al. (2020) AW was observed to
shoal by over 50 m in the 2016/2017 winter. This implies that vertical displacements of
AW may occur over seasonal timescales.
AW warming and shoaling are well documented features of change in the Arctic Ocean,
where enhanced presence of AW in later years is tied to on-going sea ice melt (Polyakov
et al. 2017). Time series from a mooring array in Fram Strait has recorded warming of
AW entering Fram Strait since 1997 (Beszczynska-Möller et al., 2011). The authors also
note substantial warming of the AW component that recirculates in Fram Strait before
merging with the EGC and turning southward. In addition to this general warming trend,
the volume of AW present in or near Fram Strait has increased. For example, de Steur
et al. (2009) showed a nearly 6 Sv increase in the flux leaving Fram Strait between 2001
and 2007 and related this to enhanced flux of the deep waters (< 700 m). Despite poor
understanding of the mechanisms bringing AW onto the shelf (Harden et al. 2014), this
indicates increased AW presence in the region.
Sutherland et al. (2013) observed that AW could penetrate onto the Southeast Green-
28

==PAGE:36==
land shelf at shallower depths during summer and autumn compared to winter and spring.
Harden et al. (2014) suggests that the larger AW volumes at these times force the less
dense PW to shoal. This observation is tied to seasonal fluctuations in AW presence,
whereas the current study observes AW shaoling over several years. Furthermore, the
AW shoaling reported by Schaffer et al. (2020) occurred over the 2016/2017 winter, indi-
cating that the trend is not limited to the summer period. However, following from Suther-
land et al. (2013), the question as to whether a similar mechanism over the Northeast
shelf could explain PW thinning and AW shoaling is posed. Clearly AW pathways across
the shelf would modulate this effect, whereby bathymetry becomes central. Due to the
spatial paucity of the data set used in the current study, observing such a situation is
highly unlikely. This discussion therefore remains speculative.
Warming AW in concert with its increased presence thus emerges as a possible expla-
nation for the dominant AW trends uncovered in the current study. Furthermore, it poses
as a potential contributor to melting of sea ice as it is exported from Fram Strait. The
question remains as to why the vertical displacement of AW is much more pronounced in
the fjords.
5.2
Implications of uncovered change
Available potential energy calculations revealed change in the large scale spatial pattern
of upper ocean (0-200 m and 0-50 m) stratification. As outlined in section 4.4.1 PW plays
a central role in maintaining water column stability. This is true for both of the consid-
ered periods, implying that the density decrease of this layer has offset the effect of its
volumetric reduction. Alongside an increased role of the surface layer, the net effect is
strengthening of upper ocean stratification. The implications of this trend on the accessi-
bility of marine heat and nutrient replenishment of the upper 50 m will be discussed in the
following.
5.2.1
Marine heat
Increasing temperatures and upward migration of AW have been shown to influence sea-
ice in the Eurasian Basin of the Arctic Ocean, Kara Sea and Barrents Sea (Polyakov et al.
2017; Ivanov et al. 2016; Lind et al. 2018). Owing to the similar trends in AW uncovered in
the current study, the question as to whether fjord and coastal sea ice has become more
susceptible to basal melting by AW was posed. However, as shown in section 4.4.2 AW
as a marine heat source has become increasingly inaccessible within the studied fjords
and along the majority of northeast coast of Greenland. This was also shown to be related
to the density of both PW and surface water. Whether isolation of AW heat will hold in the
future depends on the ability of upper ocean density to offset the effect of a thinning PW
layer.
For deep sill fjord systems with marine terminating glaciers, warming and shoaling of
AW has a further implication, namely increasing the heat supply to glacier fronts. This
has been the subject of many previous studies. E.g. Wood et al. (2021), who used in-situ
and model data to link increasing ocean temperatures on the continental shelf to glacier
retreat along the entire coast of Greenland. While the timing of deep ocean (250 m) tem-
perature change on the Northeast Greenland shelf generally agrees with the findings of
the current study, Wood et al. (2021) fail to recognise the presence of AW in several fjord
systems owing to a lack of observations. This calls for increased availability of existing
data to facilitate improved understanding of shelf-fjord communication. Straneo and He-
imbach (2013) further highlight that increased fjord temperatures do not simply translate
to increased submarine melt rates. They emphasise the inefficiency of glacier-ocean heat
exchange facilitated through the thin ice-ocean boundary layer, which in turn is dominated
Hydrographic Changes of Northeast Greenland Fjords and Coast

==PAGE:37==
by plumes. Furthermore, the vertical position of the PW/AW interface is particularly im-
portant in the context of ocean heat supply as it controls cross-sill flow of AW (Straneo
and Cenedese 2015). The shoaling of AW observed in the current study, therefore has
potential to increase heat supply to the marine terminating glaciers along the northeast
coast of Greenland. This effect has already been observed at the 79NG ice tongue, where
increased heat supply in the winter of 2016/2017 was linked to thickening of the AW layer
on the continental shelf (Schaffer et al. 2020). This speaks to the central role of fjord
bathymetry in constraining ocean heat. Improved understanding of processes that facili-
tate exchange and modify water masses as they propagate through the fjord systems is,
however, essential for constraining the implications of these AW changes on the ocean
heat delivery to the GrIS and fjord ecosystems.
5.2.2
Nutrient availability
Vertical mixing supplies nutrients to the upper water column where they support primary
producers (Henley et al. 2020). Stratification is fundamental in this context as it poses
as a direct barrier to vertical mixing. A latitudinal organisation of stratification over the
northeast coastal region is apparent in the 1980-2010 period and was shown to be strongly
related to surface density. An accumulation of less dense surface waters along the coast
resulted in stratification more than twice as strong in the south compared to the north. The
consequence of this is increased resistance to vertical mixing, implying that more work
is needed to bring nutrients to the euphotic zone. By the second period this ”fresh cap”
has spread over the entire coast insinuating that nutrients have become more difficult to
access.
Change in surface density can be driven by advection of waters with different tempera-
ture and salinity characteristics, atmospheric forcing in terms of precipitation, evaporation,
heating, cooling and wind induced mixing, as well as brine rejection in connection to sea
ice formation (Henley et al. 2020; Randelhoff et al. 2020; Li et al. 2015). These processes
are all associated with seasonal cycles. Interaction between the processes and their sea-
sonal cycles determines the timing, magnitude and distribution of stratification. The lack
of seasonal observations is a major limitation for the current study as the importance of the
aforementioned processes can hardly be assessed based solely on summer data. The
results of this study instead speak to the energy required to resupply subsurface nutrients
through the summer and autumn period and how much work must be done to erode the
density gradients between growing seasons. Even so, the implication of enhanced ver-
tical stratification on nutrient availability is difficult to asses as vertical mixing via break
down of stratification is just one of many potentially important nutrient transport modes.
Upwelling of nutrient rich deep water motivated by buoyant melt water plumes has been
used to explain high productivity of fjord systems with marine terminating glaciers (Meire
et al. 2017). Similarly, it has been argued that upwelling along coasts and shelf breaks
may play a role (Michel et al. 2015; Henley et al. 2020). Seifert et al. (2019) further point
out that boundary currents in broad fjord systems such Scoresby Sound likely contribute
to sustaining high productivity. Clearly, there are many differences between and within
systems, highlighting the difficulties associated with making future projections regarding
primary productivity. Furthermore, with ongoing changes in the spatiotemporal distribu-
tion of water masses and sea ice, limitations on primary productivity are likely to change
(Randelhoff et al. 2020).

5.3
Perspectives
The current study is based solely on summer observations and has not dealt with sea-
sonal variability in the hydrographic properties. This is a major limitation as it impedes our
ability to resolve the timing and during of freshening. Year round observations are needed
to uncover potential change in the background salinity on which summer/seasonal fresh-
ening is imposed. This is important in terms of constraining uncertainties over the total
southward freshwater delivery, as a large proportion of the EGC freshwater transport is
thought to occur over the winter period (Harden et al. 2014). Winter observations from
the Northeast Greenland fjord and coastal region are, however, severally limited due to
the remoteness of the region and logistical challenges associated with data collection in
ice covered environments. Incorporation of other observational platforms such as moor-
ings and autonomous systems may provide aid in this regard. Tagged ring seals have for
example successfully supplied oceanographic data from Kongsfjorden in Svalbard over
both summer and winter seasons (Everett et al. 2018).
Future work should additionally address fluctuations in AW height, the timescales over
which this variability occurs and the drivers involved. Improved understanding of AW
variability and will be instrumental to efforts on constraining the ocean heat supply to
glaciers in future climates.

Conclusion
This thesis has explored the hydrographic properties of Northeast Greenland fjords and
coast. Based on in-situ observational data, consistent cross-system changes have been
uncovered. The PW of Dijmpha Sound, Young Sound, Godthåb Golf, Kaiser Franz Joseph
and Kong Oscars fjords was shown to have decreased by more than a unit in salinity. The
adjacent coastal region has likewise experienced significant PW freshening, underscoring
the coupled nature of the fjord-coast system. Constraining the precis onset of this change
is challenging given the spatial and temporal paucity of the data set. However, there is
some indication that freshening coincided with an increase in sea ice export through Fram
Strait. More work, including consideration of melt dynamics of sea ice, is required to con-
firm the plausibility of sea ice as the primary source of freshening. AW properties were
also revealed to have undergone change. Critically, the AW layer has shoaled over the
last two decades. In concert with the warming trend found over the same period, this rep-
resents a change in the oceanic boundary conditions at fjord entrances. The implications
of this on the marine heat supply to the GrIS depends on how the signal is modulated
during transit through the fjords. The findings in the current study offer perspective on the
hydrographic response of the Northeast Greenland fjord and coastal region to changes in
freshwater release from and sea ice and GrIS as well as increased AW presence.

A.2
Stratification index comparison
The three stratification indices presented in section 3 are quantitatively compared via their
correlation coefficients and normalised standard deviations. These metrics compare the
linear pattern and variation of the stratification criteria. A qualitative comparison is also
included, by which the similarities of their spatial patterns are assessed. The buoyancy
frequency and density contrast are very alike as seen by their high correlation coefficient
(0.98) and similar development through time and space (Figure A.3). Available potential
energy has a different latitudinal development than the two other criteria. Both N2
z2 and
∆σ increase with decreasing latitude between 1990 and 2010, whereas APE decreases
between 82 °N and 72 °N. This can be attributed to the fact that APE is an integral indicator,
and hence provides information on the vertical density structure between the depths of
interest. There is general agreement amongst the three criteria between 2010 and 2019.
