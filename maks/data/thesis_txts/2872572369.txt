Abstract
Ferroresonance is a complex, non┬¡linear phenomenon that can lead to severe overvolt┬¡
ages and distorted waveforms in electrical power systems, posing significant risks to
equipment integrity and operational reliability. This thesis investigates the occurrence and
mitigation of ferroresonance in the context of large┬¡scale photovoltaic (PV) solar parks,
focusing on the case of the Kass├© installationÔÇöScandinaviaÔÇÖs largest utility┬¡scale solar
power plant. Following a catastrophic transformer failure suspected to be caused by fer┬¡
roresonance, a detailed analysis and simulation┬¡based investigation was undertaken us┬¡
ing PSCAD. The study includes the development of accurate transformer and cable mod┬¡
els, including a UMEC┬¡based transformer model and frequency┬¡dependent geometric ca┬¡
ble representations. A variety of simulation scenarios were analyzed, including asymmet┬¡
ric switching events and varying loading and network topologies. Key factors influencing
the onset and severity of ferroresonance were identified, such as transformer core charac┬¡
teristics, cable capacitance, neutral grounding, and switching practices. The thesis also
evaluates several mitigation strategies, including passive damping, improved switching
procedures, and active loading practices. Simulation results confirm that under specific
conditions, ferroresonance can be initiated and sustained, leading to voltages exceed┬¡
ing equipment ratings. The work concludes by offering practical design and operational
recommendations to reduce the risk of ferroresonance in future PV installations.

Introduction
For over one hundred years, ferroresonance has been a known phenomenon plaguing
power engineers and electrical installers worldwide, and was first observed by the J.
Bethenod in 1907 [1]. The term ÔÇØFerroresonanceÔÇØ was coined by P. Boucherot in 1920
[2], and has since been the subject of several studies. Ferroresonance is a complex,
non┬¡linear resonance that can occur in power systems under specific conditions involving
non┬¡linear inductive and highly capacitive components[3]. Unlike classical resonance, fer┬¡
roresonance is characterized by its unpredictability, potential for over┬¡voltages, and highly
distorted waveforms, which can lead to equipment damage, incorrect operation of protec┬¡
tive equipment, and power quality issues. Understanding and mitigating ferroresonance
is therefore essential for power engineers tasked with designing and maintaining resilient
systems.
In the year 2021, construction of the largest utility┬¡scale photovoltaic installation ever
planned in Scandinavia, the Kass├© solar installation, began. Before June of 2022, a
catastrophic failure in one of the distribution transformers took place, and resulted in its
explosion. Studies conducted at the site by external experts highlighted the possibility
that it could have been caused by ferroresonance. After replacing all the distribution
transformers, no more explosions took place, though it became apparent that a much
deeper analysis of the distribution grid was needed, which culminated in the creation of
this project.
To aid in the analysis of the solar distribution grid, a high accuracy model of the it is
developed in the EMTP (ElectroMagnetic Transients Program) PSCAD and analysed at
length.
To qualify the readerÔÇÖs understanding of the phenomenon, an introduction to ferroreso┬¡
nance is provided in the next section.

Theory of Ferroresonance
Ferroresonance can be described as a non┬¡linear oscillation between a saturable inductor
and a capacitance [3]. During a ferroresonant mode of operation in an electrical power
network, the voltages and currents will oscillate at a frequency close to the power fre┬¡
quency (50 or 60 Hz), though with very large magnitudes and a high harmonic distortion.
It is caused by the network experiencing the magnetic saturation of an iron core inductor
(usually a transformer core), and is often initiated by asymmetric operation of the 3 phases.
Ferroresonance differs from linear resonance in that it is not limited to a single natural fre┬¡
quency. Let us illustrate this on a typical series LC oscillator, where the impedance of
each passive component is linearly dependent on the frequency of the excitation:

state. If there is a small decrease in current, the inductive voltage decreases less
than the capacitive voltage, and the system shifts toward normal operation. In both
cases, the system cannot return to the unstable equilibrium point, as there is no
restoring force.

2.2
Modes of Ferroresonance
Up to this point, ferroresonance has been referred to as a singular phenomenon of which
only a single type exists, though in reality, it is not a single mode that can easily be iden┬¡
tified. There exist 4 distinct modes of ferroresonance, the first two being periodic, and
the others being aperiodic. The two periodic modes are known as ÔÇØFundamentalÔÇØ and
ÔÇØSub┬¡harmonicÔÇØ, the others as ÔÇØQuasi┬¡staticÔÇØ and ÔÇØChaoticÔÇØ. Whilst they are all caused by
a magnetic saturation causing the exchange of power between a magnetic and electric
field, they differ significantly in time and harmonic content. The following image, taken
from [1] shows some typical ferroresonant waveforms and their harmonic contents:
Figure 2.7: Modes of Ferroresonance
A fundamental ferroresonance (a) will have a strong fundamental component as well as
higher order harmonics. The sub┬¡harmonic type (b) will consist of a strong fundamental
component as well as lower and often also higher┬¡order harmonics (These higher┬¡order
terms are omitted in the above graph, though due to the magnetic saturation are still very
much present). The quasi┬¡static ferroresonant mode (c), which has distinct components,
will appear somewhat periodic when observed in time but contain both higher and lower
order harmonic components, where the fundamental component is not necessarily the
strongest ┬¡ though often is. Chaotic ferroresonance, which all ferroresonant oscillations
start as during the transient initiation phase, is represented by a continuous spectrum with
peaks at frequencies that rarely correspond to the fundamental. They very rarely persist,
and most ferroresonances typically decay to a periodic mode after a few seconds.

This illustrates why a delta connection is more susceptible to ferroresonance than a star

winding impedances, so a greater capacitance to ground is needed to bring the natural
frequency of the resulting single┬¡phase equivalent circuits to power frequency. The delta

To understand how switching transients relate to the initiation of ferroresonance, it is nec┬¡
essary to understand what they are and the underlying mechanisms that cause them.
Switching transients in power systems refer to sudden, short┬¡duration disturbances that
occur when elements such as circuit breakers, disconnect switches, or protective relays
alter the topology or operating state of the network. These transients are characterized by
rapid changes in voltage and current, driven by the systemÔÇÖs inherent inductance and ca┬¡
pacitance interacting in response to abrupt changes in circuit conditions. The frequency
content of switching transients is typically broad, often containing components at both
fundamental and higher harmonic frequencies. Switching transients arise in various sce┬¡
narios, including capacitor bank switching, transformer energization/de┬¡energization, fault
clearing, and reclosing operations.
The amplitude and frequency of these transients
depend on the point on the voltage waveform at which switching occurs, the system
impedance, and the characteristics of connected equipment.
If we assume we have an inductive load that is suddenly disconnected, due to the induc┬¡
tance and sudden, sharp drop from non┬¡zero to zero current in a short time, a large tran┬¡
sient recovery voltage will appear at the terminals. Any parasitic capacitances connected
to this inductance will then begin to oscillate the energy contained in this sudden burst
between the the magnetic and electric fields involved, but will typically decay very quickly
due to the short time constant in an LC circuit with a very small capacitance. Therefore,
should we wish to avoid generating a large transient recovery voltage, we must make
sure the current is very low when the switching operation takes place, the resulting drop
in current is much less sharp and will therefore not generate such a large transient over┬¡
voltage. However, in the case of ferroresonant circuits, the reality of switch timing is more
complex. Switching at the current zero┬¡crossing in this ÔÇØstillÔÇØ mostly inductive grid (Not yet
saturated) will introduce a DC on the interrupted phase that will take up to several cycles
to decay. Should a sufficient potential across phases develop before this DC can decay
to a safe level, the resulting voltage difference can also cause the core to saturate and
initiate a ferroresonance.
However, if this hypothetical inductance is replaced with a saturable inductance, and the
capacitance connected is relatively large, then the frequency of the decay can dramat┬¡
ically increase. The non┬¡linear behaviour in our case (a utility┬¡scale photovoltaic distri┬¡
bution grid) arises primarily due to the saturable magnetic characteristic of transformer
cores, where the inductance varies significantly with flux level. When the core saturates,
the effective inductance (and thereby the winding reactances) decreases sharply, allowing
higher currents to flow and facilitating energy exchange between inductive and capacitive
elements at other frequencies than the system frequency (Though in the case of ferrores┬¡
onance, these other frequencies are typically very close to the power frequency).
In the instant that the inductance falls and the current draw spikes, a large amount of
electrical energy flows into the transformer core and begins to oscillate between the trans┬¡
former and cable. This exchange in energy causes the voltage to spike again, and thus
allowing more energy to be drawn by the core. Should the natural frequency of the circuit
approach the power frequency, then this ferroresonance can be sustained and catas┬¡
trophic failure can become a serious risk.

Simplified Ferroresonance Simulation
As mentioned in the theory, a sustained ferroresonance requires the presence of a voltage
source. In a utility┬¡scale photovoltaic installation, this can be one of two candidates: either
the external grid connected to the installation through the point of connection (POC), or
the solar inverters on the low┬¡voltage (LV) side. For both cases, the other source must
be disconnected or have low load to eliminate significant damping. Due to the typical
energisation sequence of these plantsÔÇöwhere the POC is energized first, followed by the
high┬¡voltage (HV), medium┬¡voltage (MV), and finally the LV sectionÔÇöa grid┬¡fed ferrores┬¡
onance is considerably more likely. Such a scenario is often associated with normal op┬¡
erating procedures like plant energisation or de┬¡energisation. In contrast, an inverter┬¡fed
ferroresonance would typically require abnormal conditions such as asymmetric loading,
load rejection, or faulting.
Though it is theoretically possible for inverters to act as a source for ferroresonance,
in practice this is highly unlikely. The cabling between the inverter and the distribution
transformer is typically very short to minimize Ohmic losses (as the voltage is low here),
resulting in significantly reduced cable capacitance. This makes it difficult to achieve the
conditions necessary for ferroresonance. Additionally, most inverters have a substantial
inductance at their output to smooth their internally switched voltage into a usable 50 Hz
sinusoidal waveform. This inductance would require much greater cable capacitance to
lower the natural frequency into a range where ferroresonance could occur. Furthermore,
the vast majority of commercially available solar inverters are grid┬¡following rather than
grid┬¡forming, meaning they rely on the presence of an external voltage reference and are
not designed to sustain voltage independently. The energisation process is also typically
initiated from the grid side first, while de┬¡energisation occurs from the inverter side. At low
load, inverters also operate with lower efficiency, introducing internal damping that further
reduces the likelihood of ferroresonant conditions. For all these reasons, this study will
focus exclusively on grid┬¡fed ferroresonance.
If we observe the distribution grid of a typical utility┬¡scale photovoltaic installation, there
are some typical components that we expect to see. Going from the POC to the grid, we
would expect to see:
ÔÇó High voltage cables or overhead lines (To transport large amounts of energy at high
voltage with very low losses, they will be inductive in nature)
ÔÇó A power transformer (Stepping up the voltage from MV to HV)
ÔÇó MV cables (MV for minimizing losses in the distribution grid)
ÔÇó Distribution transformers
ÔÇó LV cables that connect the inverters to the distribution transformer
However, since ferroresonance is a highly localized phenomenon, this project will not
consider the influence of the power transformer on the behaviour of the grid. It is sufficient
to study the interaction of the distribution transformer with the MV cables, as these are
the components most likely to interact during ferroresonance. This is a product of the
MV cablesÔÇÖ significant capacitance (due to their length) and the fact that the distribution
transformer is connected to these cables in a delta configuration.

UMEC Transformer Model
After the preliminary simulations produced the expected response, it became necessary
to model the transformer more accurately. Until now, a simple transformer using just
winding resistances, leakage inductances, a magnetizing branch and a parallel nonlinear
inductance (for modelling saturation) was used. As several simulations would have to be
conducted later, it was deemed valuable to make a more realistic representation now, to
apply the POW analysis and risk assessment to.
To do this, a choice had to be made on the transformer models available in PSCAD to be
applied. The choice was between a duality┬¡based model (i.e. gyrator┬¡based, that uses
both nodal equations like the basic model, as well as magnetic dual┬¡variables), or a unified
magnetic equivalent circuit (UMEC) transformer model (state┬¡space/hybrid based). Whilst
both present more accurate representations of zero┬¡sequence, mutual coupling effects,
inrush currents as well as providing greater numerical stability, the UMEC equivalent has
the most robust handling of deep saturation effects and the highest numerical stability.
This comes at a cost of increased computational complexity, but since it was relatively
easy to apply and the author had access to sufficient computational resources, the UMEC
was ultimately chosen.
Unlike the other models in PSCAD that use methods such as Jiles┬¡Atherton or Preisach
models to approximate a BH curve based on some real┬¡world or numerical constants, the
UMEC model requires the user to directly input an IV curve. The magnetic characteristic
curves of the actual transformers were available to the author, and 10 points (with a focus
on those near the knee point) were chosen to represent the magnetic behaviour. The
resulting graphs are shown in figure 5.1═¥

Further Work
Much research remains to be done, to better understand the mechanisms that allow fer┬¡
roresonance to propagate. Whilst EMTP simulation tools and empirical conclusions have
been used to formulate the boundaries found in this report, there do exist other methods
