Abstract
The building industry needs to optimize the use of load­bearing materials for a sustainable
future. With the continuous advancement of computation capabilities, new calculation
methods emerge. This thesis applies the principle of Minimum Complementary Energy to
create a non­linear finite element program for reinforced concrete with bi­linear material
curves. A triangular mixed linear strain membrane element is used.
The genre of this program is Finite Element Serviceability Analysis (FESA). It can be
considered a counterpart to Finite Element Limit Analysis (FELA) that cannot model de­
flections or cracks needed in Serviceability Limit State design.
The method relies heavily on mathematical optimization. More specifically, on Second
Order Cone Programming, a type of convex optimization. It is critical to express the opti­
mization as a well­scaled problem for effective and correct optimization. The constitutive
model is limited to those that yield a convex expression for the Minimum Complimentary
Energy.
The program models simple non­linear stress states perfectly and shows good results for
non­linear behavior in load­displacement curves of deep beams. The results compare well
to other similar works, and the chosen element for the modeling shows good performance
in regard to stress continuations between elements.

Introduction
The creation of a sustainable building industry will require an optimized use of load­
bearing materials. Traditionally, the field of structural engineering has been quite con­
servative in regards to designing load­bearing structures. However, with the continuous
advancement of computation capabilities, more options for efficient and accurate calcu­
lation tools have emerged and will continue to expand.
Concrete is one of the most widely used materials in structural engineering due to its
strength, durability, and versatility. It’s relatively inexpensive and readily available, mak­
ing it a popular choice for construction projects. However, concrete has a significant
contribution to carbon emissions. In fact, cement, the most polluting ingredient in con­
crete, is responsible for about 8% of the total CO2 emissions in the world, and emissions
from this will have to decrease significantly to fulfill the Paris Agreement for 2030 (Lehne
and Preston 2018). As a result, there is a growing need to develop methods for utilizing
concrete better, thereby, reducing the amount of concrete used.
This project will utilize mathematical optimization algorithms to formulate a method for
calculating stress states and displacements of a reinforced concrete wall using non­linear
material models.
1.1
Background
1.1.1
Structural behavior of reinforced concrete
In construction, reinforced concrete is used for many types of elements: Beams, columns,
walls, and foundations. Since concrete is brittle, the steel reinforcement ensures the de­
formation capacity, also called ductility, of the structure. Ductility in structures is important
because it is the ability of a structure to undergo deformation without collapsing.

1.1. Background
1.1.2
Limit states
The two most common design checks are the ultimate limit state (ULS) and the service­
ability limit state (SLS). Each limit stage has certain load combinations and design require­
ments. Where ULS design ensures the safety of a structure in case of extreme loads, the
SLS design should ensure that the structure will remain functional under the loads of nor­
mal use conditions. The requirements of reinforced concrete for both limit states are found
in EN 1992­1­1. The SLS requirements are categorized into three:
• Stress limitations
• Crack control
• Deflection control
1.1.3
Finite elements analysis
The most straightforward finite element method is linear elastic finite element. Here, ma­
terials are assumed to be linear elastic, and forces are distributed according to a stiffness
matrix for each element. Used correctly, the method can be used for estimating both the
stresses and deformations. However, by using non­linear methods to make elastic­plastic
material analysis, a larger load capacity of a structure can be calculated, thereby decreas­
ing the material required for a specific load.
Finite Element Limit Analysis (FELA) is a type of non­linear finite element method that
assumes rigid, perfectly plastic material i.e. infinite material stiffness before yielding and
zero stiffness after yielding. FELA is a reliable way to calculate the Ultimate Limit State
of an RC structure. However, since the method assumes infinite material stiffness, FELA
does not provide results for the size of displacements, which is needed for design checks
in the Serviceability Limit State.
An alternative to FELA is Finite Element Serviceability Analysis (FESA). FESA is a fairly
new method and investigations by Daniel Vestergaard in Vestergaard 2022 is a source of
inspiration for this project. Both FELA and FESA use the lower bound theorem: ”The load
determined from a statically admissible and safe stress field will be smaller or equal to
the actual collapse load of the structure.” FESA uses the mathematical branch of convex
optimization to find what is called Minimum Complementary Energy of a system bounded
by lower bound equilibrium.
These finite element analysis methods can be applied to different types of elements. In
this project, FESA is first applied to a bar element to gain insights into the method and then
applied to a membrane element for analyzing walls. Structurally, the walls are described
as disks: A 2D structural member mainly subjected to in­plane forces. The thickness of a
disk is small relative to its length and height. Disks are calculated under the assumption of
plane stress, meaning that the stress perpendicular to its own plane is zero. A membrane
element is a finite element type used for calculating structures with plane stress. Many
types of membrane elements exist, including rectangular and triangular elements seen in
fig. 1.2.

Theory
This chapter presents the theory required to understand how a finite element program
based on Minimum Complimentary Energy functions. For this, the formalities and use of
convex optimization are given. Furthermore, the derivation of Minimum Complimentary
Energy for structures is explained and expressed in the convex optimization format.
2.1
Convex optimization
Convex optimization is a mathematically defined method of optimizing convex functions
in a convex set. Contrary to non­convex problems, a local minimum for a convex problem
is always the global minimum, which makes these problems easier to solve (Jorge and
Stephen 2006). The complexity varies depending on the problem and can be placed into
subcategories.
Linear Programming (LP) is the simplest form of convex optimization. LP is a subcategory
of Quadratic Programming (QP) and can subsequently be expressed by Quadratic Pro­
gramming problems. Likewise, QP is a subcategory of Second Order Cone Programming
(SOCP) and can be expressed as such. SOCP is the required optimization complexity to
touch the full scope of the program presented in chapter 4. Quadratic programming can
be used for more simple cases like the bar program in chapter 3.

optimization of this set is also referred to as the feasible region.

2.1.5
Scaling
The scaling of the problem is important in optimization. Scaling is when the units repre­
senting a variable are changed (e.g., from N to kN). Balanced scaling is when variables
have equal influence in the optimization algorithm, e.g., the order of magnitude of the
variables is close together (Jorge and Stephen 2006).
Poorly scaled problems can result in errors or performance problems. While many opti­
mization algorithms are made to be robust against scaling, they still require the user to be
take care that the problem is well­scaled.

Duality with Minimum Potential Energy
Duality is a concept that comes from mathematical optimization. For each minimization
problem (called the primal problem), a unique dual counterpart exists. The duality princi­
ple states that the objective values of the primal and dual problems are equal in terms of
solution.
The dual problem to the Minimum Complementary Energy problem is the Minimum Po­
tential Energy problem. Rather than the equality conditions that come from static admis­
sibility, the dual problem is set up using kinematic admissibility.

5.4. Efficiency and Scaling Sensitivity
5.4
Efficiency and Scaling Sensitivity
It has already been stated that appropriate scaling of the optimization problem is impor­
tant for the good performance of numerical optimization algorithms. Throughout mm and
kN has been used for the distance and force unit used for defining material parameters,
topology, and loading. This has been done, as it was noted early on that the usual SI
units m and N did not create correct solutions and, thereby, did not result in well­scaled
problems for the MOSEK optimization algorithms.
The numerical problems can give wrong results or require many iterations to converge,
so a well­scaled problem not only ensures the correct solution but also can reduce the
computational efficiency significantly.
5.4.1
Scaling sensitivity - Simple stress states
The scaling sensitivity is investigated by running a scaled problem and comparing the
results to known solutions for simple stress states. The simple stress states tested are
the ones described in section 5.1 and are loaded to their limit load.
The error of the program is calculated as the relative error of the stress state as stated in
eq. (5.16). The relative error for all elements is calculated, and the mean of this represents
the error of the scaled problem

Chapter 6
6
Conclusion
The application of Minimum Complementary Energy for RC structures is effective and
yields good results for non­linear deflections and stress states. The program models sim­
ple stress states perfectly and shows ductile behavior and stiffness close to zero when
reaching the ultimate limit load.
The analyzed structures show results similar to those of similar works by Vestergaard
2022. Load­displacement curves for the modeled geometries of Deep Beam and Deep
Beam with openings are replicated and are only slightly different from the results mod­
eled by Vestergaard. While Vestergaard experienced problems with spurious modes of
the deformations, the mixed linear strain element used in this project is defined from de­
formations of global nodes, just like traditional Finite Element Modelling, and does not
run into deformation­related issues. Furthermore, stress seems to flow better between
elements than in Vestergaard’s work.
The principle of Minimum Complementary Energy shows promising results for both Ul­
timate Limit State analysis and Serviceability Limit State analysis. As Finite Element Limit
Analysis gives results for the ultimate limit state but not any information on deflections, this
method with Minimum Complementary Energy can be considered a serviceability counter­
part to FELA. Modeled geometries showed good convergence and gave deflection close
to the converged deflection even with a very coarse mesh. However, loads close to the
limit load require a finer mesh than at lower loads.
It is found that it is critical to express the optimization problem such that it is well­scaled. It
has been shown that inconsiderate scaling can lead to wrong answers and longer compu­
tational time. The formulation with convex optimization also limits the possible constitutive
models. A positive, non­increasing material stiffness is required for a convex formulation
of the Minimum Complementary Energy. This means that it is not possible to model the
strength loss in materials that occurs after reaching the ultimate load. Therefore, the re­
inforced concrete material is modeled with infinite hardening of steel and concrete, and
the concrete is modeled without a tensile strength.

6.1. Further Works
6.1
Further Works
This project can be the groundwork for many future investigations and improvements.
Improvements to the program include implementing negative stiffness for modeling the
breakage of materials, but it would not be straightforward to keep the convexity of the
optimization problem.
It would be interesting to implement specific Eurocode requirements of the serviceability
limit state in the program and investigate what impact the analysis results of this problem
could bring regarding design adjustments and material usage. Also, the implementation
of the effectiveness factor could bring value to the accurate imitation of real RC structures.
The membrane system presented in this project only works with in­plane forces, and ex­
panding the program to a shell element program would open up the possibility of looking
at stability problems like buckling and eccentric loading. Regarding the issue of scaling
sensibility, an important investigation will be to create a systematic scaling method to en­
sure that the problem is always well scaled, thereby creating a more reliable finite element
analysis.
