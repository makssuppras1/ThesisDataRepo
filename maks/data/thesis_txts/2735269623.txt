Abstract
This thesis investigates the formulation of surfactantВӯfree Pickering emulsions for sunВӯ
screen applications, with an emphasis on enhancing physical stability and examining
UV protection efficacy. Utilising organoclay and polymeric particles as stabilisers, the
research delves into the rheological properties, stability mechanisms, and transparency
of mineral sunscreen formulations. Rheological analysis indicates that Pickering partiВӯ
cles significantly elevate the viscosity of emulsions, demonstrating nonВӯNewtonian shearВӯ
thinning behaviour typical of weakly flocculated systems. Moreover, the study elucidates
the impact of droplet size on the emulsionsвҖҷ stability and effectiveness, revealing that
larger droplet sizes, predominantly stabilised by Pickering emulsifiers, exhibit improved
stability and a particular вҖқwaterВӯdropвҖқ effect.
Stability assessments underscore the importance of steric repulsion and pinpoint depleВӯ
tion and bridging flocculation as pivotal factors affecting emulsion behaviour. Despite the
theoretical SPF values derived from inВӯsilico modelling being lower than anticipated, the
potential of Pickering emulsions to enhance the sensory attributes and user compliance
of sunscreen formulations is acknowledged. The analysis further suggests that additional
optimisation of organoclay Pickering particles is required, alongside a thorough examinaВӯ
tion of the environmental sustainability and UV protection efficacy of the UV protective
emulsions.
The findings propose that Pickering emulsions offer a promising path for the development
of novel sunscreen products that meet the increasing demand for highВӯefficacy sun proВӯ
tection. The emulsionsвҖҷ ability to be coldВӯprocessed at low shear allows for the integration
of temperatureВӯ and shearВӯsensitive active ingredients, improving both the efficacy and
user experience of sunscreen products. By serving as a foundation for potential future
innovations, this thesis underscores the importance of sunscreen formulations that offer
not only effective UV protection but also enhance sensory attributes, aiming to improve
the cosmetic liabilities of mineral sunscreens and user experience.

Mineral vs Chemical UVВӯFilters: Characteristics, Challenges, and

Chemical UV filters, like Octinoxate, Homosalate and Avobenzone, to name a few, contain
aromatic carbons and/or electronВӯdense bonds in their chemical structure, which allows

called вҖқnew generationвҖқ chemical UV filters, like TinosorbВ® M/S and MexorylВ® SX, have
also been designed to absorb UV energy in the UVA spectrum (315Вӯ400 nm). [3] As most
individual UV filters do not absorb UV light across the whole ultraВӯviolet range, sunscreen
formulas will typically contain two or more different UV filters (mineral, chemical, or both)
to obtain synergistic UV energy absorption across the ultraviolet wavelength range. A

1.1.2
Emulsions in Cosmetics
Emulsions play a vital role in formulating and enhancing the functionality of products
across a diverse range of industries, like pharmaceutical and food production, as well
as in the cosmetics industry. Emulsions as disperse systems consisting of two immiscible
liquids. In these systems, liquid droplets, known as the dispersed phase, are distributed
within a liquid medium, referred to as the continuous phase. There are various classes of
emulsions, typical oneвҖҷs being oilВӯinВӯwater (O/W), waterВӯinВӯoil (W/O), and waterВӯinВӯoilВӯinВӯ
water (W/O/W), each distinguished by the nature of the dispersed and continuous phases
involvedНҫ as displayed in fig. 1.1. OilВӯinВӯwater (O/W) emulsions, or reverse emulsions,
consist of oil droplets that have been dispersed into a continuous water (aqueous) phase.
WaterВӯinВӯoil (W/O) emulsions, are emulsions where water droplets have been dispersed
into an oil continuous phase. WaterВӯinВӯoilВӯinВӯwater (W/O/W) emulsions, also known as
complex or multiple emulsions, consist of an W/O emulsion which has been dispersed
into a continuous water phase.[6]

1.1.3
Exploring The Alternatives
The shift towards surfactantВӯfree formulations in skin care lotions and sunblock has heightВӯ
ened the focus on emulsions stabilised by particles, commonly referred to as вҖқPickering
emulsions.вҖқ[11] The emergence of Pickering emulsions, named after the British scientist
Percival John Pickering, represents a significant development in emulsion stabilisation
science. Although the concept of particleВӯstabilised emulsions dates back to before 1910,

the novelty lies in the contemporary ability to customВӯdesign nanoparticles for the explicit
purpose of emulsion stabilisation.[11] The surface wettability of the particulates can then
be tuned by modifying the surface chemistry of the particles, which opens up a much
greater range of flexibility in emulsion formulation than for traditional emulsion formulaВӯ
tions. The stabilisation mechanism behind Pickering emulsions relies on the irreversible
adsorption of particles at the oilВӯwater interfaces, forming a protective layer around the
droplets, preventing coalescence.[12]. The characterisation of the amphiphilicity of the
particulate material used in Pickering emulsions is typically accomplished by employing
surface wettability, which can be quantified by measuring the threeВӯphase contact angle,
Оё, of particles adsorbed at the oilВӯwater interface. This provides a reliable measure of the
ease with which the material can adsorb at the interface. Thus, surface wettability is an imВӯ
portant parameter to consider when selecting particulate materials for Pickering emulsion
stabilisation, as it can significantly influence the emulsionвҖҷs stability and, consequently, its
shelfВӯlife.[13] This unique mechanical stabilisation mechanism offers several advantages
to Pickering emulsions, including enhanced longВӯterm stability and increased resistance
to coalescence.

surfactants, which are often considered to represent a more environmentally detrimental
group of materials, as their limited flexibility in formulation does not always allow for the
more environmentally friendly choice of surfactant to be made. Pickering emulsions are
easier to formulate since they are stabilised by a packed layer of particles adsorbed at the
oilВӯwater interface, making the particles less mobile and HLB less important. This offers
formulators more latitude and versatility, especially in stimuliВӯresponsive compositions
that are storageВӯstable but become unstable and release their internal phase upon appliВӯ
cation to the substrate. [15] Pickering emulsions find applications across a wide array of
sectors, from oil recovery to tissue engineering and food technology. The pharmaceutical
industry is exploring these emulsions for creating innovative drug delivery systems. FurВӯ

1.2
Objectives
The investigation explores the domain of Pickering science, with a particular emphasis
on high internalВӯphase Pickering emulsions (HIPEs), comparing them to traditional emulВӯ
sions. The research seeks to elucidate the overarching role of emulsions in sunscreen
formulation, highlighting the limitations of conventional surfactants in terms of longВӯterm
stability and environmental impact. A significant focus is placed on particleВӯcontaining
emulsions, specifically Pickering emulsions, to understand the stabilisation mechanism
through the irreversible adsorption of particles at liquidВӯliquid interfaces.
An extensive review of the literature on recent advancements in the sun protection indusВӯ
try, surfactantВӯstabilised emulsions, polymerВӯcontaining emulsions, and the role of solid
particles in emulsion stabilisation precedes the practical investigation. The study narrows
down to stabilising emulsions using organoclay particles and studying the effects of the
addition and variation of biodegradable polymeric emulsifiers. Beyond the literature reВӯ
view, the destabilisation mechanisms and rheological properties of Pickering HIPEs will
be analysed to elucidate how their composition might be optimised for sunscreen products.
Effects of the particulate matter on the emulsionsвҖҷ rheological parameters, integrating a
brief overview of applicable phenomenological theories, their stability, and how their comВӯ
position may be optimised for sunscreen products, is studied.
Seven Pickering HIPEs, varying in coВӯemulsifiers, organoclay concentrations, and the
ratio of the dispersed phase, will be synthesised to achieve a comprehensive understandВӯ
ing of their internal structures. From rheological measurements and numerical modelling
based on phenomenological theories to an exploration of their destabilisation kinetics utilВӯ
ising wellВӯestablished mathematical expressions is aimed at shedding light on the comВӯ
plex interplay between the surfaceВӯstabilising compositions and the stability, viscoelastic
properties, and functional performance of these emulsions.
The ultimate goal is to contribute insights for future advancements in the field. This inВӯ
cludes discussing ongoing research into Pickering emulsions, spotlighting efforts to inВӯ
vestigate novel particle systems, understanding new stabilising mechanisms, and idenВӯ
tifying innovative applications for future advancements. So, how do the incorporation
and variation of organoclay particles and inherently biodegradable polymeric emulsifiers

2.1.1
Stability
The characteristics of the interfacial film and the adsorption mechanism of surfaceВӯactive
agents largely govern the stability of traditional emulsions stabilised by surfactants and/or
emulsifiers. Emulsions can be stabilised through four primary mechanisms: (1) electroВӯ
static repulsion, (2) steric repulsion, (3) the MarangoniВӯGibbs effect, and (4) stabilisation
via thin films/interfacial membranes.[6] The presence and concentration of surfaceВӯactive
agents are crucial for emulsion stability, as these agents enhance stability by surrounding
water droplets at waterВӯoil interfaces with films. The formation of these films contributes
to emulsion stability by increasing interfacial viscosity and reducing interfacial tension.[6]
Interfacial stabilisation via the utilisation of solid colloidal particles will be discussed in the
next section, SurfactantВӯFree Systems.

2.1.1.1
Electrostatic Repulsion
Electrostatic repulsion arises from the interactions between the electrical double layers
enveloping charged droplets, and thus resulting in the droplets repelling each other, as
shown in fig. 2.3. This repulsion is primarily facilitated by the adsorption of ionic surfacВӯ
tants. [9] The generation of electrostatic forces is linked to the inherent charge of particles
within a medium, notably in polar substances like water, which exhibits a high relative perВӯ
mittivity. The source of this surface charge is multifaceted, involving various mechanisms
such as the adsorption of ions from the solution or the dissociation of surface groups.
However, its significance is diminished in waterВӯinВӯoil (W/O) emulsions due to the lower
dielectric constant of the oil phase, making electrostatic forces less effective in stabilising
these systems. Instead, the stability of W/O emulsions is mainly due to the strength of
the interfacial film that surrounds the water droplets. [9, 6]
The equilibrium between attractive van der Waals forces and repulsive forces, such as
the electrostatic forces, is pivotal for colloid stability. Repulsive forces must outweigh the

2.1.1.3
MarangoniВӯGibbs Effect
Surface elasticity, known as the вҖқselfВӯhealingвҖқ phenomenon, is predominantly driven by
surfactants through the GibbsВӯMarangoni effect. [9] This process aids in the stabilization
of emulsions by inhibiting the continuous phase from draining while the droplets converge.
As droplets draw near, their surfaces align in parallel, resulting in a shift in their surface
area вҖ“ which in turn, attempts to drain the interfacial film.[6] At this point, the adsorbed
surfactants facilitate surface diffusion that is significantly more rapid than diffusion from
the bulk solution. As surfactant molecules diffuse across the droplet surface, they not only
fortify the interfacial film but also drag the underlying liquid layers back together, effectively
вҖқsealingвҖқ the thin regions of the film, represented schematically in fig. 2.4. [9] This dynamic
surfactantВӯinduced surface elasticity, ensures that the integrity of the interfacial film is
safeguarded and the emulsionвҖҷs structural integrity is maintained. [6]

Polymeric surfactants have demonstrated their superiority over their monomeric counВӯ
terparts with respect to stabilising emulsionВӯbased formulas, as the multiple anchoring
points of polymeric surfactants facilitate a stronger interaction with the dispersed phase.
The concentration of polymer segments situated between dispersed droplets creates an
osmotic barrier, which induces the attraction of water and effectively keeps the droplets

The aforementioned abilities of polymers as surfactants provide them with a particular

Additionally, polymers have proven themselves as great textural enhancersНҫ specifically
amphipathic oligomers block copolymers. This makes polymers particularly advantageous
in mineral sunscreen emulsions, as high levels of mineral UV filters lead to thick and sticky
emulsions, which are an aesthetic liability and difficult to apply and spread. These specific

the same time, maintain high levels of inorganic particles in a sunscreen.[15]

2.3.1
Rheology & Sunscreen Efficacy Вӯ Why Does it Matter?
The rheological properties of sunscreen formulations are paramount in determining their
efficacy and user satisfaction. Sunscreens, by design, should offer a uniform protective
layer over the skin, which necessitates an optimal rheological profile to ensure ease of apВӯ
plication, sufficient coverage, and durable protection. The consistency, spreadability, and
filmВӯforming ability of sunscreens are largely governed by their rheological characteristics,
making the study of these properties a crucial aspect of formulation development.
Rheology directly influences sunscreen efficacy through parameters such as viscosity (О·),
thixotropy, and the elastic (GвҖІ) and viscous (GвҖІвҖІ) moduli. The OstwaldВӯde Waele power
law model, represented by the equation eq. (3.1), provides a fundamental understanding
of sunscreen flow behaviour under shear [19]. For example, the power law exponent, n,
depends strongly on the range of repulsion and describes the shearВӯthinning behaviour
of a fluid. Thixotropy, a timeВӯdependent shear thinning property, is particularly important
for sunscreens, enabling them to be easily spread during application and quickly regain
viscosity to form a uniform film that adheres to the skinвҖҷs microВӯtopography [37].

A flow sweep, or shear rate sweep, is a fundamental rheological test that characterises the
flow behaviour of materials by systematically varying the applied shear rate over a wide
range. This type of test is crucial for understanding how materials respond to different

This wide range of shear rate was to ensure that the materialвҖҷs response is observed

flow behaviour.
The primary objective of the flow sweep experiment was obtaining the viscosity curve,
which plots viscosity as a function of shear rate. The viscosity describes a materialвҖҷs
resistance to flow, where the internal friction due to molecular cohesion is the source of
that resistance. The viscosity curve is also important to observe whether Newtonian or
nonВӯNewtonian flow behaviour is present. Newtonian fluids display constant viscosity,

3.3.2.4
Accelerated Aging
All samples underwent an accelerated ageing process in a Heraeus lab oven on day four
postВӯsynthesis for 13 consecutive days at 50В°C.
Accelerated ageing of emulsions is a process designed to simulate the longВӯterm physical
and chemical stability of emulsionВӯbased formulations over a relatively short period. The
primary function of accelerated ageing is to induce and accelerate the processes that lead
26
Development of Novel Sunscreen Products
==PAGE:37==
to the degradation or destabilisation of emulsions, including phase separation, creaming,
sedimentation, coalescence, and chemical decomposition. These processes are naturally
slow at room temperature, making it impractical to rely on realВӯtime ageing studies for
timely product development. The method utilised here involved three factors:
вҖў Temperature вҖ“ The use of elevated temperatures accelerates the kinetic processes
that contribute to emulsion degradation. Heat increases the molecular motion, poВӯ
tentially leading to a faster breakdown of the emulsifier system and changes in the
emulsionвҖҷs physical propertiesНҫ
вҖў Duration вҖ“ The period of 13 days at elevated temperature is chosen to represent a
significantly longer period under normal storage conditions.
вҖў Closed Sample Cell вҖ“ Keeping the samples in a closed cell helps maintain constant
humidity and prevents the evaporation of the water droplets from the emulsion. This
arrangement aims to reduce the likelihood of observed changes in emulsion propВӯ
erties resulting from accelerated ageing instead of evaporation or external contamiВӯ
nation.
Analysing the stability of emulsions under stressful conditions and comparing them makes
it easier to identify formulation parameters that may enhance the stability of emulsions at
ambient temperatures. This technique is very useful in the cosmetics, paints and food
industries, where the stability of emulsions significantly impacts their efficacy, safety, and
quality. By subjecting the emulsions to elevated temperatures, manufacturers can identify
potential stability issues, and evaluate the efficacy of various formulation components
under aggravated conditions.

Stability & Stabilisation Mechanism
5.1.2.1
Steric Repulsion
All the samples in this investigation contained the same concentration of NaCl in the
aqueous phase, i.e. the same bulk ionic concentration, in order to negate the possibility
of variations in stability due to electrostatic repulsions between the droplets. This was
done in order to accurately account for the predicted repulsive interactions due to the
presence of the polymeric coВӯemulsifiers and Pickering particles, along with their effects
on the stability of the emulsions.
According to the DLVO theory, when the nearest neighbour distance, dnn is comparison
to the adsorbed layer on the droplets, h, is greater than about twice the adsorbed layer
thickness (h) then there is little or no interaction at all. However, when dnn < 2h, an
interpenetration of the adsorbed layers occurs, which results in repulsive (entropic) interВӯ
actions. [9] This is true for all samples, thus, we can assume that in all samples there is
some level of repulsive interactions between the dropletsНҫ but in various degrees. Since
the assumption earlier, in the calculation of eq. (A.10), was that all droplets adsorb the
same amount of nanoplatelets, ranking the samplesвҖҷ strength of repulsive interactions
by their nearestВӯneighbour distance, dnn ( table 4.6) should be the followingНҫ Thus, imВӯ
plying that samples S3, S5, and S6 вҖ“ which contain the polymeric coВӯemulsifiers, have
the strongest repulsive (entropic) interactions). However, when looking at the TSI valВӯ
ues of the samples, which indicate their ability to resist sedimentation and creaming, the
samples rank in overall stability as soНҫ This tells us that the samples with the polymeric
coВӯemulsifiers (S3, S5, S6) exhibit the most prominent steric repulsion due to the presВӯ

5.1.2.2
Depletion & Bridging Flocculation
Depletion flocculation happens when nonВӯadsorbing polymers are added to the continuВӯ
ous phase, leading to flocculation when their concentration exceeds a certain level.[25]
This occurs because the polymers can not fit in the narrow spaces between particles,
causing the dispersion medium to be depleted out by osmotic pressure, creating a weak
attraction. This effect significantly increases the viscosity at low shear and introduces an
apparent yield stress in concentrated dispersions. Depletion strongly affects the phase
behaviour of concentrated dispersions resulting in a strong increase in lowВӯshear viscosВӯ
ity and the occurrence of an apparent yield stress.[25] The intensity of this attraction is
influenced by several factorsНҫ
вҖ“вҖў The concentration and molecular weight of the free polymer
вҖў The size of the droplets
вҖў The overall volume fraction of the emulsion, П•.
Surprisingly, an increase in the molecular weight of the polymer or the volume fraction
of the emulsion leads to a reduction in П•c. [10] Bridging flocculation, however, can ocВӯ
cur when only partial coverage of the droplets is obtained due to insufficient amounts of
an adsorbing polymer being present in the emulsion.[9, 25] When the droplets are only
partially covered, the polymers absorb onto more than one droplet at a time as shown in
fig. 5.3, especially at high molecular weightsНҫ thus forming вҖқbridgesвҖқ between droplets.
Thus the most probable cause of these very pronounced differences in stability between
S5, and S6 is that sample 5 was exposed to either depletion flocculation or bridging flocВӯ
culation. It is hard to tell from the conditions of the conducted experiments in this paper,
as the critical polymer concentration was not established. Though the polymers in the coВӯ
emulsifiers for S5 and S6 were only present in very small amounts, <2%, the rheometry
data for S5, where it has a high yield stress at high modulus values, is indicative of depleВӯ
tion flocculation. Thus, the sorbitan monoester in S5 negatively influenced the PGPRВӯ3вҖҷs
contribution to sterically stabilising the dispersed water droplets in the emulsion.

5.1.4
Final Remarks
вҖқHowever beautiful the strategy, you should occasionally look at the
resultsвҖқ
вҖ“ Winston Churchill
The findings suggest that Pickering emulsions could enhance the physical stability and
potentially the SPF of sunscreen formulations. However, the actual impact on UV protecВӯ
tion, especially in realВӯworld conditions, remains insufficiently explored. The optimisation
of organoclay Pickering particles ought to be further investigated, in order to get a more
holistic overview of their sustainability profile. Moreover, the environmental impact of
nanoparticle use over all, largely due to potential toxicity to aquatic life, is still a matter
of debate. With that said, it is necessary that a higher loading of UV particles into the
emulsions is studied, as the low SPF values of the samples used in this study were far
lower than a commercial sunscreen product would be.
The studyвҖҷs insights into the rheological properties and вҖқwaterВӯdropвҖқ effect of sunscreen
formulations highlight the importance of sensory attributes in user compliance. While
the study indicates that certain formulations improve sensory attributes, translating these
findings into enhanced user compliance remains speculative. The complex relationship
between rheological properties, sensory perception, and user satisfaction would necessiВӯ
tate some inВӯdepth user studies.
The trend towards using environmentally sustainable products, such as reefВӯsafe sunВӯ
screens, is a positive move. However, the research done so far on the subject has not
fully considered the entire life cycle of sunscreens, from production to disposal. Future
studies should take into account the impact of raw materials and manufacturing processes
on the environment beyond just the biodegradability of the emulsifiers. There are many
opportunities in the development of novel sunscreen products, including increasing UV
protection, enhancing the user experience, and promoting environmental sustainability.
Additionally, W/O Pickering emulsions can be coldВӯprocessed at low shear, which opens
up an array of loading temperatureВӯ and shearВӯsensitive active ingredients into the aqueВӯ
