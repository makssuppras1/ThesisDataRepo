Preface
The thesis came through after the idea of writing about the relationship between architecture
and engineering emerged. The idea of engineers coming into the project earlier was my own
opinion at ﬁrst, but was found to be a clear need and problem in reality as well. After talks
with supervisor Tim McGinley the agreement of the thesis came through, and later developed
into visualization and measurements as part of the early stage implementation in collaboration
between architects and engineers.
The relationship between architecture and engineering has always been a large interest and
focus throughout my entire education in civil and architectural engineering. The industry today,
as one of the least eﬃcient industries in the world needs to change. It is not putting the con-
struction industry in the good light it should be, and is somewhat embarrassing as it is one of
the biggest industries as well. I believe the thesis can contribute to more focus on good collabo-
ration, and that the tool can be, if not used, at least developed into something that can help make
all collaborations in the industry better. Better collaboration means better eﬃciency leading to
good projects.
Throughout the thesis, I hope to learn more about how the diﬀerent sectors within the con-
struction industry work and how the relationship between the diﬀerent parties is. To map out
the collaboration and to get insights into exactly how it is done today, and what needs to change
in order to ﬁx what is wrong will be challenging, but interesting. It is clear that there are prob-
lems and to see what the industry’s general opinion on what the problems are will be interesting.
This thesis is submitted as a partial fulﬁllment of the requirements for the degree of Master
of Science in Civil Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The project is credited
to 30 ECTS points. And I want to thank my supervisor Tim McGinley, Rambøll Copenhagen,
friends and family for the contribution to the thesis.

Abstract
The thesis is tackling the problems and investigating solutions in the collaboration between
the architects and engineers in the construction industry. Particularly early implementation of
engineers and how to do that is a focus point of the thesis. The thesis’ problem statement and
topics are based on case study analyses which gave insight on how the industry works with col-
laboration and how the synergy between architects and engineers are today. The case studies
tell the story about what works and what needs to change in order to make the collaboration
better.
The ﬁndings where that engineers are generally included too late in the design process of a
project, and that there is a lack of collaborative thinking in project delivery. Based on the ﬁnd-
ings in the case studies and state of the art research, three major factors to collaboration are
discovered, people, processes and products. These are the three factors of the BIM ecosystem
which are transferred over and put in a collaboration perspective. The thesis goes into detail
on these three factors which are including, how should a collaborative relationship be? Can
the way projects are delivered be more integrated and collaborative? And what products and
tools are used in order to make the best possible collaboration? Other focus points to the the-
sis were the lack of good measurements and visualization of good collaboration. For a manager
to know where the problems in the collaboration are is very powerful and needs to be developed.
The ﬁndings through the case studies and the BIM ecosystem research on people, processes
and programs lead to a tool that is to be used for measuring collaboration. The measurement is
survey-based and will be able to identify where the collaboration is good, where it is bad and in
what areas it should be better. The collaboration is visualized with a developed tool that shows
alignment and visualizes how the collaboration between architecture and engineering is work-
ing. By using this tool, the project management has a much better idea of how the collaboration
is working and how it can be made better.
The measurement is tested through demonstration to architects, which together with the
engineering bias held developing the tool, give an all-around consensus that the tool and the
topics the thesis takes up are important. There is a big problem with engineers being involved
too late in the process and there is a general collaboration problem that can be ﬁxed by being
able to identify problems and obstacles in the collaboration. Fixing these problems will lead to
more eﬃcient, less costly and all over better projects in the construction industry.

The engineering and architecture ﬁelds are areas in constant change, and in fast and revolu-
tionary development over the last 30 to 40 years. This is in large part due to the introduction of
3D drawings, digital calculation software and higher demand in functionality, sustainability and
economy. However, the methods are not keeping up with this evolution and the traditional roles
of what an architect is and what an engineer is, are still more or less the same as it was 50 years
ago. New tools call for new methods and new ways of collaborating between the ﬁelds. The old
and outdated solutions are the ones that are implanted in the back of the mind of many architects
and engineers, and can be seen as one of the major problems in today’s collaboration[1].
The negative stereotype for an architect is still to have a very optimistic approach to what
a building or a structure can look like, with the mindset of "if it can be drawn, then it can be
built". The thought of the structure being "in the way" of the aesthetic and the architecture is
still there[2], and makes the distance between the architect and the engineer larger. On the other
hand, the engineer has the stereotype of being narrow-minded and not open to new solutions.
Engineers are typically looked at as pessimists with only one solution to problems and the need
for straight lines and right angles. These are obviously stereotypes, but does still bring some
truth to it. The goal and optimal solution for both parties is to meet more in the middle and have
a more realistic approach.
Illustrated in Figure 1.1, there is a certain area that the architect and engineer are willing to
be within in terms of the design. The engineer will for example like as small spans as possible
between columns in order to have the least amount of stress possible, while the architect, on the
other hand, might want large open areas with much air that will help the architectural expression.
Both will be willing to compromise as they can see the other party’s point, but will often not give
in 100% because it is either not structurally possible or does not work with the architecture[3].
It is therefore important to ﬁnd this illustrated middle ground in between what is illustrated as
an optimistic architect and a pessimistic engineer.
The middle ground can be seen as a realistic approach. This crossover area (marked in red)
will vary from person to person, project to project and even from component to component.
The important and crucial thing is that both parties some times are willing to make sacriﬁces
and collaborate in order to make a realistic and optimal design. Sometimes the compromise
area is large, obvious and easy to ﬁnd like in Figure 1.1a, while it can other times be very small
and signiﬁcant work and collaboration might be necessary in order to ﬁnd the optimal solution
together like in Figure 1.1b. Figure 1.1 is a visualization made from the authors perspective of
this middle ground.

before the introduction of computer programs and later BIM programs that today characterize
the industry. However, this old-fashioned disciplinary distance is still implemented in ﬁrms and
education, and is not keeping up with the fast evolving industry.

1.2
Problem Statement
The collaboration process between the architects and the engineers needs to be reworked to
keep up with the ever changing industry. It is not good enough to work on a project one after
one with the architect doing the full design before the engineer is asked to make the design work
structurally and practically. To make the project process work as well as it should, the collabo-
ration between the parties must be enhanced, imposed and executed in a way that connects the
parties and that suits today’s industry better.
This thesis will focus on the collaboration between architects and engineers, the structural
engineer in particular and how this collaboration can be optimized. The collaboration between
the two is important in terms of the structure being a part of the architecture, or vice versa.
When implementing early stage collaboration, the engineer will be included right away and be
part of the architect’s design process. This will allow the engineer to follow the architectural
design process while calculating, and to have the opportunity to shut down or change the design
early. Through simultaneous design, the amount of rework will decrease and the collaboration
between the diﬀerent ﬁelds will be developed for the better.
Early stage collaboration can be done in several ways. The thesis will focus on how this
can be implemented and what options there are. To implement and enforce early collaboration,
there must be an attitude change not only in the industry but in the mindset of the people col-
laborating. People in general are set in their way of working and doing things. Changing this

Collaboration principles
The collaboration itself means how the companies interact, work and complement each other.
There are several factors to it, but this section will mainly focus on collaboration in a more gen-
eral way. Why is Collaboration so important and how does good or bad collaboration aﬀect the
ﬁnal outcome of a project? Why is there a need for collaboration? And how is collaboration

An Investigation of Structural Engineer-Architect Collaboration[1], is a survey conducted with
speciﬁc questions to experienced and practicing engineers and architects. The goal was to map
out the quality of the interdisciplinary collaboration and the relationship between the parties.
The survey asks questions about the attitude towards the other, how satisfactory the collabo-
ration is on average and some more speciﬁc questions about the collaboration process for the
individual.
The results indicated a fairly positive attitude towards the other party and that the collab-
oration process seemingly is going as it should, even though the responses are generally more
positive from the architects than the engineers. On a scale of one to nine, the architects are on
average one point more positive than the engineers. This scale is however not very solid and
has no scientiﬁc data to it. This is interesting in the way that there seems to be more satisfac-
tion from the architects. This is reﬂected in other parts of the survey where architects believe
that the existing collaboration with engineers are more than satisfactory, while the engineers
on the other hand hopes for more and earlier collaboration. This might be a cause of the strong
architectural bias the author has.
The survey does however indicate that the stereotype about the lack of interest and under-
standing for the other ﬁeld is present. Structural engineers are critical of the architects’ lack of

Chapter 15 in "BIM in current and future practice"[6] is the one to be used in this thesis and

give everyone on the project the opportunity to contribute.

People
Relationships are an important part of a successful collaboration. To be able to work together
with understanding, trust as well as with joy is crucial when designing and delivering a satisﬁ-

mutual trust is one of the most important things about any relationship.
The heart of the paper is the management of the trust-building loop. The cyclical trust build-

Trust in this paper is divided into three diﬀerent categories depending on the relationship,

develops, and often the contractual trust which relies on the other party staying to the signed
contract, goes over to become goodwill trust because of the ongoing relationship. This process
is much like any other relationship between people.

future of collaboration and is using this contractual framework in many of their relationships

By products, it is meant speciﬁc tools, equipment or programs that will help the collabora-
tion either by enhancing the communication, bringing people together or by sharing the work
and project more suﬃcient. Earlier tools that were found obvious like fax machines and even
telephones can enhance the collaboration, but today more eﬃcient and modern tools like BIM

"Visualisation in architecture, engineering and construction (AEC)"[12] is a paper focusing on
the concept of using visualization as a communication tool. Visualization can in some cases
cover the entire lifecycle of a project, from the conceptual stage and all the way to production
and maintenance. The paper covers various methods of visualization from simple old school
hand drawings to new modern renderings and virtual reality (VR). Visualization as a way of
communicating design is ever increasing as it is intended to make the evaluation and comparison
easier for both client and designer. The paper looks at how it is applied in today’s industry by
looking at three case studies that used three diﬀerent approaches.
Architects have through 3D modeling and VR been allowed to visualize and show their design
much clearer and understandable than before. By doing walkthroughs using VR glasses that
give a clear indication of how the building is going to work and be like, or with renderings
to show a more realistic picture of the structure, the client along with the designers can have a
better understanding. They can say what needs to change and what aesthetic factors the building
should get. This way of communication is today also very much used as a marketing tool as it
can improve the way building designs are developed.
The three case studies represent three diﬀerent projects where the communication and col-
laboration are done through visualization. One is focusing on the conceptual design stage, the
other is on the usage in the housebuilding sector and the last one is on how visualization can be
used on a site-level. These represent both diﬀerent technologies and diﬀerent stages of a project
to give the article depth.

2.2.3
Processes
The process of delivering a project is important to how the entire project is set up. Every
project has a delivery method, but some projects are more focused on the process than others.
In this thesis processes that support collaborative work and also early stage collaboration, will
be investigated and mapped out for use in the collaborative set up of a project. Tried and tested
methods such as the conventional Design-Bid-Build (DBB) and Design-Build (DB) along with
new up and coming methods will be investigated with the collaboration in mind.
"Your Essential Guide To Collaborative Project Delivery Models"[16], is a short and compact
guide to delivery methods made by Autodesk, where the use of BIM is in focus. The guide goes

3.1.1
Identify Problem and motivate
The problem statement in section 1.2 identiﬁes the problem and the reason why the thesis
is ﬁlling holes in the existing industry. The collaboration in the construction industry between
architecture and engineering today is lacking a lot. The engineers typically come into projects
too late and are unable to make their mark on the design, and are not a part of the initial design
process. Through more focus on early stage collaboration and inclusion of engineers in the
design process early enough to make a diﬀerence, the problem can be coped with. Visualizing
and measuring collaboration is a way of doing this. It will give companies the opportunity to see
how their working method is aﬀecting the collaboration and what factors should be bettered in

the collaboration, either within a company or between two companies. The focus should be

Products are as described by the BIM ecosystem [6] how the tools and aids are being utilized.
Tools for improving collaboration is fast evolving and is starting to really take over how projects
are executed. Collaboration tools are getting more and more related to project delivery methods
and are in many instances part of the contractual agreement. To have collaboration tools as part
of the project gives huge advantages compared to not using them, and is therefore seen by many
as the future of the construction industry. This statement is supported by many project case
studies that show how collaboration tools of a diﬀerent kind lead to more economical, predictable
and faster deliveries[4, 22].
Collaboration tools can have multiple functions, some tools are speciﬁc to one task, while
others will have a more total project approach. Looking at the most obvious tool used today,
BIM is a tool that will help with everything from architectural and technical design to detailed
scheduling and cost estimation. It is a tool very much used for larger projects with multiple
parties involved. Other collaboration tools can be speciﬁc to communication, planning or pro-
curements in the project. Projects usually use multiple of these specialized tools in order to get
the best delivery and most detailed project execution.
All in all collaboration tools seek to better the communication either through direct commu-
nication tools, or by using BIM programs or other information sharing softwares. Even visual
representation is often used as a tool of communication by sharing ideas, design and renderings
to other participants. Tools for collaboration is changing the industry and saves time and cost
through understanding and doing mutual work. The construction industry is today one of the
least suﬃcient industries due to its time consumption and low eﬃciency. BIM, cloud sharing and
other tools, seek to better the understanding and execution of collaboration, communication and
coordination.
4.2.1
Building Information Modeling
When thinking about tools in civil engineering, most people will think of Building Informa-
tion Modeling (BIM). BIM gives project teams the option of making detailed and comprehensive
shared digital models, making the project more collaborative. By using BIM as much a possible
in the project and sharing it across the team, every decision and design is made by the person
most suitable for the job. Accordingly, the person making the design can follow the feedback and
the development of it. Due to these obvious advantages, BIM is getting more and more popular
in the construction industry and is today used in more than 75% of all projects[42].
BIM is by many viewed as a pure 3D modeling tool, but the 3D modeling is only the top
of the iceberg in the abilities that BIM provides. BIM in total can combine "among other things,
the design, fabrication information, erection instructions and project management logistics in one
database"[21]. BIM gives a platform for collaborative work across all disciplines including builders
and engineers and will make way for early inclusion of engineers in the design. Through BIM,
information can be transferred more smoothly (see section 4.2.3), model sharing will be more
eﬃcient due to the common model with shared access and the project management will be much
more eﬃcient as it can be based around a common model with common features that include all
members.

Interdisciplinary advantage
The use of BIM has skyrocketed recently and it is due to its eﬃciency and advantages in
collaboration. Much of the industry has converted its working practice to include BIM at least
partly. BIM provides unique eﬃciency in terms of production and development of construction
drawings, ﬂoor plans and building details. These can be automatically produced and sent over to
partners. This enables for example the engineer to work on the architect’s plan early in the pro-
cess, and can then again deliver the verdict on buildability or structural advantages right away,
to give the architect feedback on the work as soon as possible. This will have huge implications
on the amount of rework necessary when the technical partners can unwrap ﬂaws in the design
early on. With the ﬂexibility and transferable nature of today’s BIM programs engineers and ar-
chitects can get direct feedback on their design, which again leads to early design decisions that
work. Many feel that some decisions in terms of ﬁnal design and material should not be done too
early. With BIM this is made possible through direct calculations, built in material characteristics
which will save tons of time, by making these decisions early and not going backwards in the
design process.
The focus on interdisciplinary in the BIM programs are getting more and more appreciated. It
increases the level of development for the model as it can include almost all the components that
the building or construction consists of. Softwares such as Revit oﬀers both a structural plugin
and an MEP plugin that can be transfered into the architecture model which is the program’s
primary function. This combination is making the BIM community grow and the communication
and visualization through a 3D model are getting more realistic and detailed, as all the factors
that are going to be in the ﬁnished building are present. See section 4.2.4 for elaborated use of
3D models.
Buildability
The buildability factor in BIM is often an underestimated asset. With buildability, it is meant
how easy and seamlessly the building process goes. That there is an easy and intuitive building
plan and drawings. Clashes and complicated plans often lead to misunderstandings and mistakes
on the construction site. This means there can be a deviation from the initial plan, which again
adds up to more deviations in the construction, which can have disastrous results. BIM provides
exact and detailed building details that are intuitive and easy to understand for the builder. In
addition are many software compatible with crash test programs that identify when components
overlap each other. By doing this the model can be much more exact, and the details in it will be
more accurate. Typical clashes can be that two walls do not connect correctly or that columns
go straight through walls or slabs.

Information Sharing Tools
Information sharing is very important in the collaboration process. To make sure everyone on
the project team is on the same page, sharing of important information is crucial. The information

align what the diﬀerent people on the project are doing.
Information sharing can happen in multiple fashions, the traditional way of emails or other

opportunity for multiple people to work on the same model or document in order to have instant
information sharing, which is extremely time and eﬀort saving is emerging. Projects typically
involve more than just one software tool, diﬀerent ones perform diﬀerent tasks like architecture,
structure, MEP, project management, etc. This means the information sharing between the disci-
plines is even more important to execute. Without the common model or software, it is important
for all parties to have knowledge both in diﬀerent systems, and how to share and combine them.

Cloud Sharing
Cloud sharing is used to have a common saving platform where all work and models used
in the project are saved in the same place, where everyone has access to them. The clouds use a
central web-based database to support the common ﬁle saving platform[14]. Through the clouds
and the shared access, the teams are able to share ﬁles easily and update the ﬁles in order to make
sure everyone has the latest updates. The clouds which have several opportunities in terms of
the provider, has become increasingly popular in the last ﬁve years and are usually user friendly.
Some cloud services oﬀer instant saving, meaning all parties will be up to date on meeting reports
or schedules it, all in all, makes the teamwork much more collaborative.
Model Sharing
A model says almost everything there is to know about the design process and if BIM is
utilized perfectly, it can say almost everything about the entire project. By looking at a project
model and all the information that is put into it, the reader can get information about the place-
ment and size, the cost and the plan for every component. In addition, a model can include cost
estimates, analysis and even expected life cycle of the components, and the entire structure in
general. Because of this, sharing of the models in the project can be extremely beneﬁcial.
How the model is shared can vary from project to project. Many BIM programs give the
opportunity for multiple participants to work on a model at the same time. By doing this ev-
ery change made in the model will automatically be updated to the other participants, and is
collaborative in that sense. The problem with this way of sharing the model, is the fact that it
is necessary that the partners work in the same software. Many architects like to work in pure
architecture programs, while structural engineers often swear to computational programs such
as ﬁnite element method programs or programs with structural analysis automated into the pro-
gram. Many softwares do oﬀer both opportunities within the same program such as Rhino or
Revit, but it is still necessary for both to have a common knowledge in it.
The solution to ﬁrms not using the same software is the mentioned cloud sharing (see section
4.2.3). Through the cloud, it is made possible to save the model, and for every time a change is
made and saved it is updated in the shared cloud folder. This is however not the full solution.
Softwares typically save ﬁles exclusive to that program and is therefore not possible to open with
other softwares. This problem was identiﬁed early in the BIM era and it is called the Industry
Foundation Class (IFC). IFC has a target of making a comprehensive description of the building
and the construction site[14]. In other words, IFC will gather details and descriptions of the build-
ing and put it together in a 3D model. An IFC model includes the conceptual model, information
about the construction site, details in the model (walls, stories, ﬂoors, etc.), structural elements,
MEP and electrical systems [14]. By using IFC the design team has the opportunity of working
on the model in diﬀerent software based on the personal preference, software speciﬁcations or
sharing opportunities. A normal way of using it can for example be if the model is designed in
Revit, it can be transfered via IFC and then crash tested in Solibri.

Visualization
The value of a visual representation of a structure is powerful and can be used for multiple
purposes. First of all visualization and especially 3D modeling when designing is helpful to see
the result of what you are designing at all times. Something that might look good in 2D on paper,
might not look as good when the full picture is seen in 3D. It will be instantly generated and will
give a more realistic picture of the model. Seeing the complexity and detail level it provides is
crucial in communication and project development through the whole project, and will produce
diﬀerent representations depending on the receiver. Diﬀerent people might want diﬀerent views,
for example will a true rendering be interesting to an owner or client as it depicts the ﬁnal result.
Engineers will on the other side be more interested to see wall detail or spacing for ducts and
structural elements.
Earlier standard 2D drawings were the main source for design communication, where it was
either faxed or hand delivered. Today when the technology is allowing more detailed and faster
deliveries, the models are getting more and more realistic and detailed and will soon be able to
depict more or less the entire visual part of the project. This development leads the way for new

tool from an engineer or structural designer to the builder or construction manager in order to
transfer ideas and design in a good and understandable way.

In this thesis, the delivery methods in focus are methods with a large collaborative focus and

method.

4.3.1
Design-Build
Design-build is a delivery method that has been around for a long time, but has long been in
the shadow of other more traditional delivery methods such as DBB and CMR. However the last
twenty years DB has emerged and is increasingly starting to be one of the most popular delivery
methods in the building industry. DB seeks to make the gap between architects and contractors
smaller. This is done by having both on the same contract from the owner, and thereby take
inspiration from constructions from hundreds of years ago where all these roles were held by
the same person, that being the concept of the Master Builder. DB is a method that transforms
the industry which today can be described to be ineﬃcient, wasteful and slow, to what was "a
culture of cooperation, teamwork, trust and mutual respect"[43]. With this as the basic idea DB
was a development from the traditional DBB by removing one contract for the owner, making it
much more predictable.
With only one entity for the owner to handle as a single point of responsibility the role of
architect and contractor are integrated into one unit, making it virtually like the old way of doing
projects. From the beginning, the contractor and architect work together on the project design
and development, and together work to make the most out of the owner’s schedule, budget
and project demands[44]. Any occurring problems will go through the team before the owner,
meaningless trouble for the owner, through what would be a more back and forth distribution
job if DBB were used. The partnership made between designers and builders who are obviously
also on the same contract, nurtures collaboration and teamwork, further the integrated teams
will work together to successfully complete and deliver the project faster, with higher quality
and as eﬃcient as possible. Even though the biggest and most critical diﬀerence between DB
and DBB is the single-source contract, DB is also a way of making a collaborative culture and
mindset of the ﬁrms using the method.

Concept
The DB concept is that the owner is having one single entity to deal with. The owner has a
single contract with both the architect and the contractor, that both have subcontractors beneath

middleman. DB also put much more trust in the employed ﬁrms, as the owner is putting the
risk on either the architect’s or the contractor’s shoulders. By doing this the owners risk will
obviously go down, but the reward will not[45]. At the same time, the risk also includes respon-

4.3.2
Integrated Project Delivery
Integrated Project Delivery is a delivery method that has collaboration and teamwork in the
front seat. It works to align all parties on a project to be on the same page, and to have equal
importance to the project. More collaboration is slowly changing the industry, the need to have
cost eﬀective, time eﬀective, sustainable and rigid structures have never been bigger. IPD is a
reaction and is by many seen as the answer to all these needs. IPD brings a stable economic and
structural basis for large projects with the need for large performance [20]. Through using an
IPD contract, the parties in the project align to the same level with as much business interest, and
are in general all treated as equals. IPD strives to motivate workers through equal reward and by
having the attitude of stakeholder success equals project success, meaning all parties takes pride
in the full project and not only their own personal success[22].
All parties in an IPD project will put the project’s interest in front of their own, the mindset is
always what is best for the team and the ﬁnal result, which in turn will beneﬁt themselves in the
long run. The delivery method is there mainly to support and encourage the collaborate thinking,
and get the most out of every person on the team, "IPD generally favors a ’carrot’ approach,
whereas, traditional delivery methods generally us a ’stick’ approach"[16]. Through common goals,
shared values and fellowship, IPD seeks to attack projects together and by that identity get good
results.
Concept
The general concept of IPD is that all key participants are in it as equals, which means they
have shared risk and reward on the project’s outcome, that there are a transparent accounting,

One of the large things about IPD is the use of tools and aids in the process of working, de-

has a say in early design decisions to limit rework and unchangeable errors. In case studies done
by AIA [22], it was clear that teams including all key participants from the beginning generally
enjoyed more success in the project than the ones that failed to do so. Participants of the case
studies talks a lot about how early constructability help from engineers, and cost estimation
reviews with architect and contractor, was a huge help in both scheduling, time saving and total
cost. Most commonly is the contractor included in the brief stage of the project, but owners
are often served better with inclusion already at the strategy stage. Projects with contractor
involvement in the deﬁnition stage or later, often suﬀer from this even though this is the more
traditional way of doing it from DB and DBB.
Through common communication platforms and in an optimal case co-location, the early
involvement can be implemented as smoothly as possible, with as little distraction from a regular
work environment for the architect[22]. Through regular meetings, often weekly, either through
communication tools or in person meetings the project can be performed correctly from the early
going. With BIM tools and BIM clouds, the contractor can follow the project design from the ﬁrst
line and make sure errors are found earlier and in turn corrected correspondingly.
4.3.3
Joint Venture
Joint Venture is an older delivery method and can be dated all the way back to the early
thirties when it was used in the Hoover dam project[47]. It has not been much used as many ﬁrms
grow bigger and bigger, but JV has enjoyed a boost in the last 30 years due to it is collaborative
advantages, which provides greater opportunities for smaller more specialized ﬁrms. Because
the core of JV is for two or more ﬁrms to come together and bid and deliver projects jointly.
With separate skills, expertise and experience it gives ﬁrms the opportunity to deliver larger and
better projects than what they would be able to on their own. In other words, it is similar to DB
and IPD in the way that the collaborating ﬁrms, for example a contractor and an architecture
ﬁrm has shared risk and reward and will go into the project as equals like in IPD.
JV requires openness, trust and willingness to collaborate to succeed. A normal and recom-
mended structure choice is to make a separate limited ﬁrm that can act on its own, where the
two companies have a 50/50 ownership or an ownership distribution that reﬂects the eﬀorts and
work put into the project[16]. To have the contract agreed on beforehand is in many cases abso-
lutely crucial, as JV is the most fragile out the three delivery methods. The partnership is reliant

Concept
JV is, in general, an arrangement where two or more parties commit funds, services and fa-
cilities to an enterprise in the interest of both parties, with mutual beneﬁts. With the help and
partnership of other ﬁrms in a similar position, the ﬁrms are able to ﬁll each other’s gaps and bid
on larger projects and compete with larger ﬁrms with broader knowledge and expertise[48]. JV
will decrease the risk a ﬁrm goes into a project with, while of course also decreasing the reward
by transferring this over to the collaborating partner. The separate enterprise the two parties go
together in is legally separate from the two ﬁrms and is a mutual platform for decision making
and sharing. In the UK, JV is deﬁned as its own contractual pattern as: "a partnership between
two or more companies covering building, mechanical and electrical engineering, or other specialist
services for the purpose of tendering a building or civil engineering contract, each of participating
companies having joint and several liability for their contractual obligations to employer"[48].
Figure 4.12 shows the way a JV delivery is set up. The general set up is a hierarchy with

architect. They are followed by supporting subcontractors and subconsultants who are hired by
the companies. This hierarchy will make sure there is a clear diﬀerence for the main participants
with the shared risk and reward, and the ﬁrms hired underneath them again. This can lead to a
less collaborative eﬀort, but will at the same time give less room for lengthy decisions and time
waste. This system is not to be confused with DB which on paper looks similar. In JV the two
collaborating ﬁrms are a lot closer, and is on paper the same limited company instead of two
separate bounds by a contractual agreement.

In general, there are two diﬀerent categories of JV in the building industry, there is integrated

and the project is therefore jointly managed. Non-integrated however is more similar to DB

Advantages
As a smaller company, JV gives advantages and opportunities that other delivery methods
do not. Even small companies can compete for the biggest and most valuable projects with help
from other companies of similar size, but with complementary attributes. Shared risk and reward
is an advantage for the smaller ﬁrms in a big project, as it will be expensive to make mistakes and
it will require a longer pre-bid process, which can serve costly for smaller companies with limited
resources. The shared risk and reward will limit this risk as well as giving ﬁnancial security[16].
JV projects build trust, build relationships and can in many cases form long time partnerships.
If the right partner is found, it can expand the opportunities for any ﬁrm. The relationships
made by JV is in most cases inﬂuenced by the structure. To have long time success using JV,
the structure needs to be right, which again can make the relationship itself better due to the
enhanced trust earned from a successful project.
Restrains
JV relies a lot on communication, the relationship between the parties and often the success
of the project. A common reason why JV projects fail is insuﬃcient communication, insuﬃcient
knowledge sharing and collaboration[16]. If the contract agreements are not understood and
executed properly, it is a big chance for misunderstandings and issues that should have been dealt
with at an earlier time. Contract misunderstandings are often due to not accepting obligations,
changes made by the owner or conﬂicts in interests.
A JV agreement is only as strong as its weakest link. Failing to ﬁnd a suitable partner that
does not match the standard of the companies standard, can impair the actual good work is done
which again will weaken the total quality of the delivery and weaken the trust. All decisions
done will have to be made as early as possible, failing to do so will lead to misunderstandings, and
disagreements are bound to happen. As in IPD, there is a constant conﬂict within the company
whether to put the JV or the company ﬁrst when decisions are made, it requires a mindset and
culture to go for the win-win situations, which often is diﬀerent when leading a JV rather than
a single company[49].

Summary
Delivery methods will guide the project delivery in the right direction by implementing con-
tractual obligations to collaborate and to collaborate as early in the process as possible. The
delivery methods will often act as a guideline for participants in a project in terms of how the
project should go, and how the process ultimately should be. Through the contracted factors in
the delivery method, the participants are obliged to collaborate, have relationships with other
participants and be told when the tasks are to be done. It is clear that the sooner the delivery
method is decided and utilized the better. To have everyone on a project including engineers,
contractors and builders in early has proved to be vital for projects investigated and will, in the
long run, save cost and time.
Choosing the correct delivery method is extremely important for the project result, as it will
have an impact on the entire process and system used in the delivery. Many might want to go for
the tried and true delivery methods like DBB or CMR, but going for more collaborative methods
like the ones described in this thesis can beneﬁt the project greatly, especially for larger more
complex projects. The requirements and the goals set for the project will form the basis for the
project, and sometimes the traditional delivery methods can be perfect, and tools like AHP can be
the way to make a decision with the analytical way of thinking and weighing the pros and cons
of every method. When choosing the method, multiple factors count as there are advantages and
disadvantages to all of them. Factors should include cost, schedule, complexity, risk, reward and
what the level of the leaders of the projects are, some delivery methods do for example rely more
on the owner than others[16].

Comparison
The three delivery methods describe in this thesis are all collaborative delivery methods that

with the partners. The three delivery methods are based on the same ideas but are diﬀerent in

with two or more companies underneath the owner, but these are more collaborating for the same
project. Only a collaboration contract bounds the companies together, and DB can therefore be
looked at as a more simple delivery method, and is for that reason also more used. The similarities
lay in the advantages, which for both are more direct contact, more collaboration and early
involvement of key participants. JV is a more structured way of working, but will require much
more commitment, but will if successfully executed end in a more predictable project delivery.
Many of the delivery methods are also somewhat combinable. The commitment to collab-
oration and shared risk and reward that IPD brings to the table are for many scary, and it is
therefore common for projects not to be all in on IPD, but will be either a DB or JV project with
implemented IPD ideas. Things like shared risk and reward can be implemented to a DB contract
to make it more collaborative, the owner inclusion is possible to add to both DB and JV. On the
other hand, the joint venture principle of a mutual company can be used in IPD to force even
more trust in the partners.

People
Working interdisciplinary will for many be a big challenge. Diﬀerent background, diﬀerent
thinking processes, diﬀerent focus and sometimes even diﬀerent language can lead to misunder-

inﬂuential as it says something about who is actually doing the work[6]. The relationship be-

a relationship during construction projects are inevitable, and the task is therefore to make sure
the relationship is as good as it can be. Many occupations have diﬀerent disciplinary languages
and might therefore struggle to communicate, but in architecture and engineering, the language
and project tasks are somewhat overlapping[4]. In order to collaborate, this overlap will be big
or small depending on the partners involved and their attitude going into the project. The best
relationships that will last for a long time are the ones that are successful in combining the
participants’ skills, perspectives and knowledge in their respective ﬁelds. This is called part-
nership synergy and can be crucial in enjoying a good relationship and delivering satisfactory
projects[11].
Relationships are found everywhere in the industry with many of the largest architecture or
engineering ﬁrms having reoccurring partnerships with the same ﬁrms and will opt to collaborate
with earlier acquaintances. Architects, in particular, tend to be self-centered in the design of
their building so when they are able to ﬁnd a partner with similar ideas and a shared vision, who
understand their goal and design, they often end up partnering for a long time on more than just
this one project[4]. It is therefore not uncommon to see the same engineering ﬁrm repeatedly
on the most famous projects. The same goes on the other hand with architects understanding
structural principles and appreciating the glory of structural elements. Structures like the Eiﬀel
Tower or the Brooklyn bridge are not possible without an architect who can see the value in
structural elements.
Today more and more ﬁrms appreciate the concept of interdisciplinary work and collabo-
ration within the company’s four walls. To include engineers early in the process and to have
engineers as advisors in the design and architectural work, can be hugely beneﬁcial and it has
been recognized by architect ﬁrms. In-house collaboration is more and more common and a
study made by AIA in 2012 shows that six percent of all employees in American architecture
ﬁrms are engineers[51]. This shows that the big architect ﬁrms are willing to focus resources on
close collaboration and early technical involvement in the design. Firms like SOM (see section
4.1.4) and Fosters+Partners are going long ways in this new trend and are enjoying large success
with it.

Trust
Trust building in relationships is essential and will mean a lot for the eﬃciency of the project.
Often for new partners who are yet to gain each other’s trust nor trusting the other party, a
cultural clash happens. With one of the parties having a more artistic ﬂoating mindset of an
architect, while an engineer typically has a more scientiﬁc approach. For this reason, a mutual
understanding and acceptance of the other party are important in trust building and collabora-

tion. Trust is something that ﬁrst of all needs to be developed over time, naturally there can be
some kind of existing trust do to previous work done by the partner, but the mutual trust com-
ing from a continuous relation and cooperation gives something extra to the relationship. On
a survey done in New Zealand on what managers saw as most and least important in business
relationships and collaboration, "mutual respect and trust" came out on top with 60 percent of
the managers having it of "vital importance" to the relationship [8].
Trust is a variable of diﬀerent factors in the project, contract and collaboration. There are in
general three types of trust, "contractual trust", which are related to the contract and the trust
in the other person honoring it. "Competence trust", which is based on the trust in ability and
previous performance. And there is "goodwill trust", which is about the commitment to the
partner and the relationship [52].
Goodwill Trust
Goodwill trust is the type of trust people will use time to develop and that in many cases
needs to be earned by the participants. If goodwill trust is present in a partnership it can be the
start of a long standing relationship that can produce more than just the project in focus at the
time. Eliezer Geisler states that relationships "will be more likely to survive over time, the more
there are initial assets of good will, trust, favourable prior beliefs, mutual psychological commitment
and prior relations between the parties"[53], meaning that without a certain level of goodwill trust
there will be hard to remain in the same state and remain acquaintances.
One of the problems with goodwill trust is the lack of measurements and the possibility
to have a rigid proof and something to show for. While competence and contractual is easily
determined through the contract and work, goodwill trust is more elusive and is more based on
"feelings of faith, conﬁdence, expectation, reliance and security"[54]. From this the perhaps most
inﬂuential factor in building goodwill trust is time. The trust must evolve, and over time there
will be developed a trust in the way the partners know each other’s way of work, communication
and production.
Contractual Trust
There is no need for trust if there is no risk involved. In construction and project management
there is of course always some kind of risk involved in the process. This is where the contract is a
way to help reduce the risk for a company in a project, and hereby also building contractual trust.
The contractual trust comes around when most contracts are signed and it reduces the potential
for any misbehavior from the other party. A common way to enhance the contractual trust is
setting a schedule with milestones, objectives and continuous goal setting that both parties are
obliged to follow[8].
Contractual trust is for several the start of many relationships in the construction indus-
try. The goal of contractual trust is often for it to evolve into goodwill trust. This is often the
case of repeated contractual agreements over multiple projects. In long standing relationships,
contractual trust is often excessive as the goodwill trust should take over.

Competence trust
Competence trust is as the name indicates the trust a person has due to the other person’s
competence, skills and experience. Going into a new relationship there should be some sort of
faith in the partner, this faith should over a short period transition into competence trust, which
symbolizes that the other person knows what he or she is doing, and that the eﬀect it will have
on the project will be positive. The competence trust will as goodwill trust develops over time.
The more the parties work together, the work will be more and more clear and the possibility
of proving themselves to the other party arise. The competence will develop over time as the
parties learn to work with each other, and will hopefully turn into a combination of competence
and goodwill trust [8].
Summary
Trust is important for a relationship to work, diﬀerent types of trust are based on what is
trustworthy in the partnership. Collaborative professional relationships are dynamic processes
that require every part of the project doing their job. At the same time, everyone needs to trust
that the other party does their job. Trust in the other party makes sure that everyone does their
part without the interaction of others, while at the same time being open for questions, helping
each other and having the partnership that is required for a successful project.
The combination of the three trust categories described is a way of developing and using
trust as a mechanism to make the relationship better. With a supportive relationship, with a plan
contributing to good contractual trust and competence trust, which over time will hopefully
lead to goodwill trust, can over time establish a long standing partnership for the companies.
The trust will with this long standing relationship develop, and can prove to be a strength in the
project[8].
On the other hand, the lack of trust can be straight up destructive for the relationship and
weaken the project in general. Single projects can work with the lack of trust, but will prove to
be tiresome for the people involved and hereby being destructive for both the project and the
relationship. It is said that over time the trust can develop into goodwill trust, but time can also
be destructive for the trust building. If trust is not present from the beginning, time can make
the trust worse and worse and will end up with the opposite of goodwill.
4.4.2
Partnership
What can almost be regarded as the deﬁnition of a relationship, is the partnership and how
the synergy between the parties work. Partnership synergy is describing to what degree a part-

success[11]. As described in the processes (section 4.3) many of them include a partnership
between two companies in order to get a better project delivery. To gain an advantage over
competing single companies, it is necessary to develop good partnership synergy, which will
combine the diﬀerent skills and experience in order to get the best team together. The goals as
well as challenges the partnership will face, is ﬁnding the best way to merge their knowledge,
experience, skills and information, and to keep the relationship at the highest possible level.

Finding the Right Partner
To ﬁnd the right partner is perhaps the most important in collaborative work. A partnership
formed by two or more companies will set the standard for how the project will go. In the sense
of the diﬀerent delivery methods, a partner will ﬁll the holes and contribute with complementary
skills and resources. The most important is to have a partner you need, and that also needs you.
As long as the need for each other is there, the partnership will survive. When there no longer
is a need for each other there is no need for the partnership[50]. Often a partnership can also
be between two architecture ﬁrms and not necessary between architects and engineers. These
partnerships are often short lasted as they usually partner up for niche projects that can beneﬁt
from a wider range of architects or engineers.

side to asses these criteria [50]:
• Do the strengths of each partner fulﬁll the needs and expectations of the other?
• Are the combined resources of the partners suﬃcient to achieve their expectations?

partnership and it should not be a partnership. A partnership where the need for each other is
not there or the expectations are unlikely to be met, is not a healthy partnership. It will likely

4.4.3
Duration
The length of the collaboration can have a major impact on the collaboration and the general
relationship between the companies. The duration has implications on all the other relationship
aspects as the length of a relationship will develop more trust and people will know each other
better with time. Typically as mentioned in section 4.4.2 relationships that work well and ﬁll the
partnership criteria listed, will have a longer duration than the ones that do not. Most relation-
ships will obviously have the goal of lasting as long as possible, and will try through nurturing
and continuous collaboration to make it stick. Some collaborations and partnerships are made
to last only a limited time, for example, are collaborations between competitors like two archi-
tecture ﬁrms put together to have more people on the project, but are unlikely to continue their
constant relationship.
New Relationships
New relationships are the case for most relationships. Through either common interest in
a project where ﬁrms come together to form a joint venture or through winning separate com-
petitions and then forming the partnership, the relationship should develop and be formed as
described earlier in this chapter. New relationships typically face the get-to-know-you phase
and will rely on contractual trust, to begin with, before ultimately going over to competence and
goodwill trust (see section 4.4.1). It will often be clear early if the partnership has the potential
to be long lasting or not. If the right partner is found it will be evident through the information
exchange and combined knowledge. However, it is important to know when a relationship does
not work. Forcing a long standing relationship is not good for anything. Sometimes ending a re-
lationship is contracted in terms of how it is done, this is especially for joint ventures [50](section
4.3.3), but in most cases the relationship simply does not bid for new projects together.
Long Standing Relationships
The value of a long standing relationship can not be underestimated. To have experience
of working together previously and doing so multiple times is extremely valuable, it eliminates
a lot of problems that come up in many new relationships. Companies with successful previ-
ous encounters tend to get together over and over again to continue working together on new
projects. This is a good advantage for them as the trust is already built and working methods
are established. The fact that these factors are already present is intriguing for owners, who can
then put some extra competence trust in the appointed companies as they have previous work
to show for.
Many big architecture and engineering ﬁrms have their go to partner that they tend to go to
when the stakes are high and the work needs to be done to perfection. Examples are many includ-

4.5.
Objectives
4.5
Objectives
The tool will need to evaluate diﬀerent aspects and objectives of the collaboration in order to
ﬁnd out in what areas the collaboration could be better. The objectives will be based on what has
worked in other projects, and what is important to in a good collaboration. The objectives can be
diﬀerent from project to project, but should generally be based on the process of collaboration
and the factors that make up a collaboration that works across the team. The function of the
objectives is that they will form the basis of what should be investigated in the three factors of
people, processes, products. These three factors are obviously diﬀerent, but the objectives will
form a common theme and topic within collaboration in general.
Eight objectives are mapped out that can be regarded as the most important factors in col-
laboration between architecture and engineering. The objectives are based around the analysis
done on case studies on both projects that have worked and not worked in "Collaboration in
Architecture and Engineering"[4] and "IPD: Case Studies" [22] respectively. The objectives form
a base on some things that are important in collaboration and cover typical stages of collabora-
tion and will together with the BIM ecosystem factors people, processes, products form a good
specter of measuring the collaboration between the parties in a construction collaboration.
4.5.1
To Learn From Each Others Field
Working collaboratively is not a single project or task process, it is a learning process that
should make engineers better engineers and architects better architects as well. Knowing the
partner and learning from them is essential in this process as it will lead to more understanding,
help the communication process, enhance the eﬀect the collaboration has, and give the engineer
or architect a much better reputation as someone to work with. To have a focus on learning from
each other gives a sense of camaraderie and will make the collaboration process more interdis-
ciplinary. Building under this principle is Hans Schober from SBP in the Berlin Hauptbahnhof
project stating " it stimulates the engineer to innovate. In turn, when the architect takes input and
learns from the collaboration about good structural ideas, the architect is also stimulated by the
engineer".
4.5.2
To Take Responsibility
When thinking about collaboration it is easy to think about everyone being the same and
everyone is equal. This is true, but it is important to take responsibility for your work and to get
on the right side of the job. Do your job and make sure it is done for the sake of the project. To
take responsibility does not mean collaboration is not a factor, it only means that it everything
needs to be done properly and that does not happen if nobody steps up and makes sure it is
completed the right way. Leadership and eﬃciency are important in any collaboration. Buro
Happold from the Artic case study in "Collaboration in Architecture and Engineering" uses this
principal heavily in their team building, with Greg Otto, founder of their Los Angeles oﬃce
stating "We look for people that are gung-ho to take responsibility, take a bit of risk and challenge
convention"[4].

4.5.3
To Share Credit
Collaboration is a big group delivering something together and it is therefore important to
share the credit gotten for the job. Any team eﬀort no matter if it is in construction or any other
industry is not better than the weakest link. Everyone on the project is contributing to what
is to become the ﬁnal result. To have an equal credit system enhances even the intern to do
their best, as the reward and treat are at the end of the tunnel. In the B2 BKLYN project being
a collaboration through and through this was even more important, with Arup principal David
Farnsworth stating: "one needs to be able to collaborate, be quick to get on the right side of a job,
have a willingness to take responsibility, but also know when to share credit"[4].
4.5.4
To Have a Back and Forth Process From the Beginning
Almost all the case studies described their collaboration work as a back and forth process that
stimulates both the engineer’s and the architect’s working process. The back and forth process
more or less means that one party does their design, before the other reviews and expands. It
gives both parties the opportunity to be a part of the design at all times even if the changes
form the engineer might not be major at every step. It is really a process of true collaboration,
where the architect gets the opportunity to do their design, hand it over to the engineer that
does the technical analysis followed by the architect maybe needing to revise the design. SOM
being an in-house collaboration uses this factor to fullest with architect Ben Mickus describing
the process: "Based on the results of that analysis they come back to us perhaps with a revised model
or we make revisions to the model based on what they found". Guy Nordenson from the "Simmons
Hall" project enjoys this working process stating "it is a process of true collaboration where the
give and take continues through the entire process"[4].
4.5.5
To Enjoy Working With the Other Party
More important than anything else in a job is to thrive and be happy at work. One of the key
features of a successful collaboration is therefore to enjoy work and to look forward to every day.
No one is served with unhappy workers that are not motivated for the job. Enjoyment and fun
at work are aﬀected by the relationship with the other party, what the working process is and
how the project is working. Enjoying work will enhance collaboration and can make or break
the relationship. Neil Denari can see the value of appreciation for the other ﬁeld by stating "I
always enjoy the interaction with the structural engineers because I think it’s at the heart of making
a project great rather than simply disciplining it"[4].
4.5.6
Working to Make the Other Party’s Job Easier
A collaboration is all about making the project the best it can possibly be with the prerequi-
sites it has. Every decision should be made with the project as the main priority. It is therefore
essential to think about the greater good when working towards the project goal. Working to
make every person on the team’s job easier is important and shows dedication to the project in-
stead of the more self-centered "me-ﬁrst" approach which is a trap that is easy to fall in to. In the
"Cooper Square" project, Engineers from IBE was clear about the importance of understanding in

4.5.7
To Have Interest and Knowledge in Integrated and Interdisciplinary De-
sign
If the knowledge and understanding in interdisciplinary work and design is strong it makes
the process of communication and collaboration much more intuitive and fruitful. To understand
each other’s language and being able to see things from an other side is important in the process
of ﬁtting architectural and structural design together. Interdisciplinary knowledge will make it
easier to fulﬁll the other objectives as well as a broader and better knowledge and interest help
to make more feasible solutions and is making it easier to share understandable and relatable
solutions and designs. Interdisciplinary experience can also be helpful for the other party as
the engineer can work on the architecture team and vice versa without bringing confusion or
destructive ideas. In "Collaboration in Architecture and Engineering" all ten case studies focused
on the importance of integrated design and how interdisciplinary knowledge was crucial to the
collaboration[4].
4.5.8
To Use BIM to Help Each Other
As described in detail in section 4.2.1, BIM is soon becoming a necessity in every construction
project. It is setting the standard for how projects are delivered and how the design and planning
process is done. In collaboration, it is extremely important that both parties are on the same
page in this area, to understand the programs, how to work with each other’s models and how
to implement their own work in the diﬀerent programs. If both parties are not on the same page
here, it can lead to big mistakes and confusion in the design. BIM use was evident in the "Keelung
Harbor Project" (section 4.1.5) where Rhino Grasshopper was used by the engineers to catch up
as they came in late in the design process.

4.7.
Tool Requirements
4.7
Tool Requirements
The tool formed and generated is a tool that should show how the collaboration between
architects and engineers presumably structural engineers is working. The tool must follow the
following three requirements in order to be as informative and easy as possible.
4.7.1
Requirement 1: Compare the Perspective of the Architect and Engineer
In an optimal collaboration, the entire team has the same goal, shares the same preference
in the process of getting there and generally has the same attitude toward the project. The more
equal the easier the project will go, as the chance of major disagreements are lowered. The tool
should be able to visualize if the partners are on the same wavelength in these aspects or not. If
the parties have a diﬀerent perception on for example BIM or credit sharing it is a problem that
should be possible to see just from a simple characteristic in the model.
4.7.2
Requirement 2: Easy and Intuitive to Read
The tool is there to help, and to help everyone on the project. Therefore it must be an intuitive
visualization that anyone should understand early without too much explanation. Once the tool
is understood which should not take long, it should be really easy to understand all the other
models that visualize the other objectives and ecosystems.
4.7.3
Requirement 3: Show the Attitude Towards Both Disciplines
There is a large diﬀerence between an architectural approach and a structural approach.
Depending on the project the diﬀerent focus will be on either the structural design or the ar-
chitectural design. By nature, the architect will care mostly about the aesthetic look, while the
structural engineer obviously will have the structural system as the primary focus. This is in-
tuitive, but there should be a mutual understanding of what will be the project’s ultimate focus.
Will the structure be prioritized or should the architecture be prioritized? The tool must show
the attitude for both parties and should in some way show how the two attitudes compare.

the tool has diﬀerent characteristics for every requirement, even though they overlap in form of
the visualization.
The numbers that the tool is visualizing is through surveying the diﬀerent people working
on the architectural and engineering team respectively. To get an idea on how a survey can look
like, the tool presentation includes the survey that is presented when the collaboration between
the architects and the engineers is in focus. It consist of 48 statements for the architects and the
engineers, and is presented in form of tables based on how they ﬁt into the evaluation table (see

5.1.1
Requirement 1: Compare the Perspective of the Architect and Engineer
The tool has a clearly stated direction, depending on if the focus is more on the architecture
or on the structure. The diﬀerence in these two will show the perspective of the person or
company in focus. By looking at the diﬀerence it can be read if the company is project-oriented
by having a healthy and short diﬀerence between structure and architecture. Comparing the two
discovered mindsets, the general collaboration can be read to some extent. The closer these two
lines are together the more likely the collaboration is to have success. If the two lines are exactly
aligned the more similar are the perspectives of the collaborating teams, which again mean that
the relationship through communication and philosophy, has a better chance to be a good one.
The idea of alignment is easy to compare. In many situations, it is eﬃcient just to compare
the angle of the slope, while at the same time having the easy to read diagram. However, in a
building project, it is important that every person on the team does their job, and will have that
as a primary focus. The angle will therefore automatically be tilted towards a structural focus for
the engineer and an architectural focus for the architect. To measure the perspective is therefore
hard, but the more positive the points are, the more interdisciplinary is the mindset of the person.
The angle will though show the relationship between the structural and architectural attitude.

have a good collaboration both parties should be as closely matched as possible even though this
in most cases is unrealistic. Almost no collaboration will have both parties as positive in one

from both the architecture team and engineering team in question. In the form of the numbers
on the level of agreement the person has on the diﬀerent statements it will form the basis for
the presented evaluation tool. The survey will give a visualization for every cell in the table (see
section 4.6), each of which will tell how the collaboration is working for that speciﬁc objective,
in that speciﬁc ecosystem factor. This result will give the management the opportunity to see
how exactly the team is thinking on the diﬀerent topics, in addition to how it compares to the
other team’s survey results.
Then there is the added "total" part of the table. The three statements for the diﬀerent objec-
tives are diﬀerent, but it is not by much. The idea is that the three questions are similar so that
the average of the three gives the full picture of each objective. One question might be misunder-
stood or overlooked, but with three questions that essentially tell the same story, they will even
out each other and give a realistic picture of the opinions of the two parties. The same will be
done for the three diﬀerent ecosystem factors that will show the total for the three factors. This
part however, will not be as informative as the questions are not designed the same way to make
the answers even each other out. With these total results, the manager will get a more realistic
picture of how the collaboration works for the diﬀerent objectives set and ﬂaws will hereby be
easy to identify.

6.2
Setting Up Good Collaboration
Setting up a collaboration team requires much from both a management perspective and
from a participant perspective. During this thesis, multiple factors in a good collaboration are
found and discussed, and is the main objective when setting up a team that will collaborate as
good as possible. Often the ﬁrst thing to be decided by the management which is generally led
by the owner is what delivery method the delivery should be built upon. This choice is impor-
tant to how the project is going to pan out. It says something about how all of these described
factors are utilized, and when the collaboration is ultimately starting. To have a focus on what
project delivery method that is being used is important, but really depends on what project it
is, the identity of the collaborating ﬁrms and how big of a part the owner wants to play. If the
project is large and complex with a knowledgeable owner, IPD would be the preferred method.
If the project is large and technical without an informed owner, DB would be preferred, while
JV is optimal for two companies that ﬁll out each other and want to bid on a project together.
Alternatively, for more simple and low detailed projects, more traditional and less collaboration
oriented deliveries such as CMR and DBB can be just as good.
Crucially to any relationship and any collaboration is ﬁnding the right partner. ﬁnd a partner
whom can be trusted, that has the required skills and expertise, and that gets along well with the
employees. The partnership should be one that potentially can develop over time into something
bigger and get results down the line. Finding a partner that potentially can be a long standing
relationship does not mean it has to be forced, but can mean something for the chemistry and bear
fruits throughout the collaboration. Get a partner that has the potential of giving a back and forth

learning, for the engineers to learn from the architect and vice verse. This will make both the
engineer and the architect better, as interdisciplinary knowledge is one of the most underrated
traits in today’s construction industry and will be extremely beneﬁcial for the ﬁrms long term.
Interdisciplinary knowledge brings a mutual language that takes away many misunderstandings
and ﬂaws in the collaborations. If succeeding in ﬁnding a partner that brings these traits, that will

general need for each other the partnership has a chance of working well.

necessity in today’s industry.

How To Implement Engineers Early
Introducing engineers to the project early is an important part of optimizing the collaboration
in a construction project. The engineers can provide technical knowledge that will ﬁll the gaps
of the architects, and implementing the engineers as early in the project as possible will only be
positive from a collaboration perspective. The cons from resistors of early stage collaboration
are all from an economic point of view. Studies like the mentioned case studies do show however

that the advantages and help the engineers can provide is beneﬁcial in the long run through more
eﬃcient project deliveries, time-saving and less rework leading to lower project cost.
The advantages of implementing engineers as early as possible are evident, the biggest chal-
lenge however is the process of implementing this. Many companies swear to the old DBB like
process of the architect more or less ﬁnishing the design early before the engineers come in and
"make it work". The same factors that help to enhance the collaboration can also help to imple-
ment the engineers early. The collaboration tools will help with introducing the engineers early,
especially through communication tools will the engineers get access to group chats and to the
shared clouds. Sometimes also the model sharing is utilized early and shared with the engineers
from the early design process. These things are helpful for the engineers who can then follow
the design at all times.
This access triggers the back and forth, give and take process that is the way to go for the
mutual design process. Early stage implementation of engineers does not necessarily mean all
technical details need to be worked with right from the start of the project, and that the engi-
neer is co-located in the oﬃce at all times. It means that the engineers should be included in
the process to make sure obvious design and planning ﬂaws are discovered as early as possible,
in order to prevent irreversible mistakes that can escalate down the line. Include the engineers
in the communication and early planning, make sure technical knowledge is available so that
if there is anything, they will be reachable, have knowledge in the project and can come with
insight and do controls early in the architectural design process.
The timing of collaboration is contracted. The contract or the delivery method says some-
thing what role the diﬀerent people on the project will have, what they will do and ultimately
when they will do it. The owner has the power to decide these things, especially if he or she
has good knowledge of construction and project scheduling. Early stage collaboration is in other
words ultimately up to the owner and what delivery method is chosen. If any of the three meth-
ods from this thesis is chosen, early stage collaboration will happen almost automatic. But even
though it is contracted it is still unless contracted or forced by the owner, up to the partners to
decide how the communication and collaboration are happening.
Based on the previous relationship and the existing partnership between the companies the
implementation will be either hard or easy to implement. If the relationship is existing and long
standing the early implementation will be smoother as the architects will have trust in the engi-
neering team, and can trust the process and their skills, both in engineering and in architectural
design. To have this existing trust gives the architects an opportunity to start using the engineers
in the design process, obviously depending on the engineer’s knowledge and interdisciplinary
skills. With the engineer on the team, the early stage implementation is obviously done. How-
ever, with a new relationship, there is a need to build up a relationship between the parties, and
the engineer might have to "earn their place" at the table of architectural design.

have been elaborated more to show what can go wrong if the things talked about in this thesis

8. Conclusion
8 | Conclusion
The thesis set out to demonstrate what collaboration in architecture and engineering is, how
it is executed today and how it can be improved. The initial hypothesis of engineers being imple-
mented in a project too late to do a diﬀerence and that the implementation should happen from
the beginning was met with substantial agreement. Interviews and case studies with architects
and engineers all said the same thing. Projects with engineers on the team from the beginning
are more successful than the ones that do not have early stage implementation. The same answer
was received in a group meeting with Rambøll architects, who also enjoyed more success with
early stage collaboration.
Collaboration has several factors on what it consist of and what has to be part of good col-
laboration. The identiﬁed factors were taken from the BIM ecosystem and are deﬁned as people,
processes and products. The people factor connects people together and tells us how people are
working together. It tells how the relationship between the people and the ﬁrms have an impact
on the collaboration and that a good solid relationship leads to great projects. The processes
factor tells the story of how the project is developing, how it is delivered and the whens, wheres
and hows of a project. By choosing the proper delivery method for the project, the project will
have a greater chance of being collaborative through contracted scheduling, tool use and re-
sponsibilities. Lastly, the products factor is how collaboration is enhanced by the use of tools
and methods. Sharing and communication tools enhance collaboration by shortening the dis-
tance between people and makes it easier to be on the same wavelength. BIM is becoming a
must use tool in today’s industry and to have full BIM implementation has proven to be an enor-
mous asset, and must be a part of any project with the ambition of being collaborative.
From the case study analysis and working on the principles of AEC collaboration, the objec-
tives of collaboration are deﬁned with backing from interviews with architects and engineers.
The objectives are together with the three ecosystem factors as the most important aspects to be
considered when collaborating in the team. The eight objectives serve as guidelines for work-
ing collaborative and is made into a cheat sheet from a request by Rambøll architects(see Ap-
pendix B). Together, the objectives and the three factors make up the evaluation table that be-
comes the basis for the evaluation survey.
To measure the collaboration, a survey-based system is made that is able to uncover bad
collaboration and the areas of the collaboration that should be enhanced. The system is a step
in the direction of measuring and visualizing the collaboration in an intuitive way, that helps
the project managers to see exactly where the collaboration is well functioning and where it has
room for improvement. The combination of the evaluation table along with the visualization that
is able to show through alignment how the two parties stand, makes up for both a readable and
precise measurement. This combination can be regarded as a feasible method to implement, as
it does not include any fancy numbers or ﬁgures.

8. Conclusion
8.1
Further Work
The topic of collaboration, especially early stage collaboration in architecture and engineer-
ing, is always going to be a hot topic and should continue to develop. This thesis brought out
some deﬁnitions on what exactly is AEC collaboration all about and what does it consist of.
However, this deﬁnition should continue to develop into something more concrete that can be
used in daily work, in education and something that should be common knowledge for everyone
in the industry.
In order to further develop the deﬁnition made with people, processes, products, it has to
be brought out into the industry and researched in the ﬁeld. The current research is done by
analyzing case studies, interviews and observations done by others. To see if this deﬁnition and
this deep dive into all of them is how AEC collaboration works it has to be brought out to the
public and seen from a professional perspective. Does the management of a small architecture
ﬁrm agree with the deﬁnition? Does the board in large construction companies agree? And does
architects and engineers working every day in collaborative groups share the same opinion of
this being a good deﬁnition of AEC collaboration? These are questions that need to be answered
before two lines can be put under it.
The major action that needs to be taken is to test the tool. It has to be veriﬁed through using
it in the industry and testing it on an actual project. Through testing on large projects with both
an architecture team and an engineering team, the tool will either be approved or disapproved by
the management of the particular project. This will give the tool much more feedback by people
actually using the tool, which is important for the further development. Most likely the tool
will need more development and more revision as it is still raw and has not been through much
testing. The tool will also need some more programming in order to make it more automatic as
it is manually made at the moment. When programmed properly, the survey conducted should
automatically generate the visualization and a full table with all the data surveyed. This would
make it much easier for the management to conduct the collaboration evaluation, as they can
use as little time as possible on getting the data, and use more time on processing it. Through
less time conducting the data, more time can be spent on repairing with the ﬂaws identiﬁed by
the tool.
Using the tool can mean a lot for any project if it is done properly. It is not only for the
construction industry, but can be transferred into other collaboration projects like in hospitals
or collaboration directly towards clients. To be able to do this, research must be conducted on
the speciﬁc ﬁeld and new objectives need to be found. The people, processes, products aspect of
this thesis is a strong aspect that can also be transferred into most other industries, it will only
mean something else to a doctor than it will to an engineer or an architect. The visualization
itself works well for other industries, but must be redesigned for the speciﬁc collaboration it is
visualizing.

B. Cheat sheet
B | Cheat sheet
To Learn From Each Others Field
Working collaboratively is not a single project or task process, it is a learning process that
should make engineers better engineers and architects better architects as well. Learning from
the other ﬁeld will ease up collaboration and make every project better.
To Take Responsibility
Do your job and make sure it is done for the sake of the project. To take responsibility does
not mean collaboration is not a factor, it only means that everything needs to be done properly
and that does not happen if nobody steps up and makes sure it is completed the right way.
Leadership and eﬃciency are important in any collaboration.
To Share Credit
Collaboration is a big group delivering something together and it is therefore important to
share the credit gotten for the job. No team is better than the weakest link. Share the glory and
share the pain, everyone is contributing to the greater good and deserves credit for it.
To Have a Back and Forth Process From the Beginning
Early collaboration is extremely important, the back and forth process more or less means
that one party does their design, before the other reviews and expands. The architects get the
opportunity to do their design, hand it over to the engineer that does the technical analysis,
followed by the architect maybe needing to revise the design.
To Enjoy Working With the Other Party
More important than anything else in a job is to thrive and be happy at work. Make sure to
look forward to every day, it will enhance the collaboration and can make or break a project.No
one is served with unhappy workers that are not motivated for the job.
Working to Make the Other Party’s Job Easier
Every decision should be made with the project as the main priority. It is therefore essential
to think about the greater good when working towards the project goal. Working to make every
person on the team’s job easier is important and shows dedication to the project instead of the
more self-centered "me-ﬁrst" approach which is a trap that is easy to fall in to.
To Have Interest and Knowledge in Integrated and Interdisciplinary Design
If the knowledge and understanding in interdisciplinary work and design is strong it makes
the process of communication and collaboration much more intuitive and fruitful. To understand
each other’s language and being able to see things from an other side is important in the process
of ﬁtting architectural and structural design together.
To Use BIM to Help Each Other
BIM is soon becoming a necessity in every construction project. Make sure you understand
the relevant tools and programs. BIM speeds up the project delivery and will bring more continu-
ity and predictability to the design process. Utilizing all possibilities within BIM limits confusion
and mistakes and brings new solutions to the table. Using it to help each other makes the process
more collaborative and integrated.
