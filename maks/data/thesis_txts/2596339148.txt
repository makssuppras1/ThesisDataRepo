Summary (English)
Type 2 diabetes is a growing global health problem. Despite advancements of
medication research there is still a large proportion of people with diabetes who
do not reach treatment targets. Pharmaceutical companies have developed long-
acting insulin where injections are only needed once a day. Researchers now look
to develop new ultra long-acting insulin where injections are only needed once
a week.
To aid in the research of once-weekly insulin we implemented models which de-
scribe insulin-glucose dynamics in people with diabetes. We simulated a virtual
patient undergoing once-weekly insulin treatment from these models.
Using
simulations we suggested two linear regression models which could be ﬁt to
data containing information of insulin doses and glucose measurements. These
models were converted into dose prediction models which could be used to sug-
gest insulin doses needed to reach a target glucose level. The prediction models
were tested with regards to safety and accuracy by comparing them to dose
suggestions from standard of care treatments. An important aspect of this work
was to investigate how number of glucose measurements aﬀected the prediction
models’ ability to predict safe and accurate doses.
The results show that a dose prediction model can suggest safe and accurate
doses in almost all cases using only 3 glucose measurements per week. This
could save on time initiating insulin treatment for people with newly diagnosed
T2D.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Type 2 diabetes is a growing global health problem.
In 2018, 34.2 million
Americans, or 10.5% of the population had been diagnosed with diabetes. The
number of those diagnosed is increasing by 1.5 million each year. Approximately
90-95% of those diagnosed have type 2 diabetes [10]. In 2017, The American
Diabetes Association (ADA) estimates that diagnosed diabetes cost $327 billion
in the United States. Around 72% of that amount can be attributed to direct
medical costs such as hospitalisation, medication and other diabetes supplies.
The remaining 28% can be attributed to reduced work productivity or early
mortality [2]. The need for development of diabetes medication and treatment
practises is immensely important.
1.2
Diabetes treatment
There are two main types of diabetes. Type 1 diabetes (T1D) is usually devel-
oped early in life and requires patients to get lifelong insulin injections. Type 2
diabetes (T2D) usually develops later in life and is related to unhealthy lifestyle
==PAGE:14==
2
Introduction
such as being overweight, lack of physical activity and unhealthy eating habits
with genetics also playing a part[28]. These factors can cause deﬁciency in the
glucose-insulin balance such as decreased sensitivity to insulin which can cause
damage to many organs and tissues. Eventually this can cause the patient to
have reduced insulin secretion from the pancreas, which along with decreased
insulin sensitivity further disrupts the glucose homeostasis[18]. Maintaining this
balance between glucose and insulin is therefore vital for a healthy life[20].
T2D may be treated with lifestyle changes and weight loss, oral medication or
insulin injections [27]. Untreated diabetes can have serious consequences. The
absence of insulin or the lack of ability to respond to insulin causes elevated
blood glucose concentration, referred to as hyperglycemia. If a patient experi-
ences long periods of hyperglycemia it can have a damaging eﬀect on the heart,
blood vessels, eyes, kidneys and nerves and can in extreme cases lead to a coma
or even death. On the other hand if blood glucose levels are too low, i.e. if the
patient has received too much insulin it can cause what is called hypoglycemia
which may cause a variety of symptoms such as confusion, clumsiness, seizures
or even death.
The ADA recommends starting T2D treatment with extensive lifestyle changes
and insulin sensitivity increasing oral medication [3]. If blood glucose levels re-
main high, the patient will eventually need insulin injections. This initial period
of stepwise introduction to glucose-lowering treatment can take a long time and
the patient would start insulin therapy 10-15 years after diagnosis[26]. Insulin
treatment usually starts with low doses and are increased based on pre-breakfast
self measured blood glucose (SMBG) values until blood glucose concentrations
reach clinically recommended levels. This process of slowly increasing or de-
creasing insulin doses is called titration.
Titration is a delicate process and
there are obstacles preventing T2D patients from managing to get adequate
glycemic control. These obstacles include lack of self-conﬁdence to manage in-
sulin therapy, fear of hypoglycemia, need for frequent glucose monitoring, fear
of needles and more. Many automatic algorithms have been developed to help
with titration [6]. These titration algorithms suggest changes in doses based on
SMBG values. If the blood glucose values are high the algorithms suggest an
increase in insulin and if blood glucose is low a decrease in insulin is suggested.
These algorithms come with challenges as they are sensitive to high variation in
glucose levels which can arise from many factors such as missing measurements
and a change in lifestyle which can aﬀect insulin sensitivity.

1.2.1
Digital Devices for Insulin Treatment
Diabetes has become a serious problem and a large burden on the health care
system. This has resulted in increased research into diabetes treatments. Many
digital solutions and personalized diabetes medicine have emerged[12]. One such
solution is the produced by Glooko which has developed software which allows
people with diabetes to upload data from blucose measurement diabetes devices.
The Glooko developed Mobile Insulin Dosing System (MDIS) can use this data
to suggest treatment adjustments for both the patient and clinician[1].
Other titration systems include the Intelligent Dosing Systems (IDS) by Cook
et al. [11], which uses glucose measurements and insulin data to calculate a next
dose to reach a desired blood glucose level for T1D patients.
Solutions using continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) have been shown to be
beneﬁcial in treating T2D and have seen an increase in usage. They have been
shown to improve glycemic control and treatment adherence[29][7]. The CGM
measures interstitial blood glucose interstitial, usually every 5 to 15 minutes
which gives a clearer picture of how blood glucose progresses throughout the
whole day instead of the traditional once-daily pre-breakfast SMBG measure-
ments. The use of CGMs have suggested reduced fear and risk of hypoglycemia
in patients[18].
The need for the development of safe and eﬀective treatments for T2D is of
greater importance than ever for the growing problem of type 2 diabetes.
1.3
Thesis objective
Researchers are now focusing on developing insulin treatments with longer last-
ing insulin so that patients need to only inject once a week instead of every
day. Compared to once-daily insulin injections, this new long lasting insulin
will reduce the treatment burden on the patient and may improve adherence as
the number of injections is reduced from 7 to 1 per week. It is important to
understand the dynamics of once-weekly insulin so that the initiation of insulin
therapy can be robust and patients are not at risk of over- or under dosing which
could have disastrous consequences. This project is an exploratory research into
how ultra-long acting insulin aﬀects patients with T2D and what methods can
be applied to set a safe dose for those patients. We intend to research how once-
weekly insulin aﬀects blood glucose proﬁles of a virtual patient with T2D using
an established insulin-glucose physiological model. We also wish to propose a

dose prediction model which can estimate the insulin need of a patient based
on as few blood glucose measurements as possible.
The objectives of this project are
• Implement mathematical models used for simulating patients with T2D
• Modelling diﬀerent drug proﬁles and titration algorithms and evaluating

• Using the insulin eﬀect proﬁles to propose dosing strategies for long acting

These titration algorithms need to be safe for patients even if they do not follow
the treatment exactly. To minimize the burden on the patient, we strive to
develop dosing strategies based on 1-3 blood glucose measurements per week.

Chapter 2
Background
In this chapter we provide an overview over some of the existing work which has
been done in developing physiological models which emulate diabetes patients.
We start by describing a few models which have been developed. We present
how two of these models have been augmented to simulate patients with T2D
as they originally were developed to simulate people with T1D. We then present
some insulin dosing strategies which are used in real-life treatment of T2D.
2.1
Insulin-glucose dynamics
Blood glucose regulation in people with type 1 diabetes has been extensively
researched.
This research has lead to the development of many mathemati-
cal models which aim to mimic the insulin-glucose dynamics in humans [21].
These include the Bergman minimal model [8], the Hovorka model [14] and
the Medtronic Virtual Patient model [17]. These models are usually a system
of coupled ordinary diﬀerential equations (ODEs). Most of these models are
divided into submodels which describe physiological functions involved in the
glucose-insulin dynamics in the human body. The submodels vary in size and
complexity and can include a few compartments to describe the insulin absorp-
tion, glucose absorption and the metabolic eﬀect. In the following sections we

2.1.3
The Medtronic Virtual Patient Model
The Medtronic Virtual Patient model (MVP model) ﬁrst described in Kande-
rian et al. [17] builds on preexisting models for example the Bergman minimal
model [8] and the Hovorka model [14]. The MVP model was developed using
data from a study of 10 adults with type 1 diabetes. The participants were
observed for 28 hours where blood samples were taken approximately every 20
minutes. The model is divided into three submodels which describe meal absorp-
tion, insulin absorption and metabolic eﬀect. Since the model was developed to
simulate a person with T1D undergoing insulin treatment with fast-acting in-
sulin it needs to be augmented to model long-acting insulin and the endogenous
insulin production found in people with T2D.

2.2
Common dosing strategies
Before investigating new diabetes treatments we will brieﬂy discuss the standard
of care treatments put forth by the American Diabetes Associations (ADA).
Numerous treatments guidelines exist for T2D. Arnolds et al. [6] did a study
where a few of these guidelines were investigated. Among these are treatments
by the American Diabetes Association (ADA), the European Association for
the Study of Diabetes (EASD), the Internation Diabetes Federation (IDF),
World Health Organization, National Institue for Health and Clinical Excel-
lence (NICE), and the Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network (SIGN) [6].
Type 2 diabetes treatment usually starts with lifestyle changes and insulin sen-
sitivity increasing oral medication [9]. If these methods do not suﬃciently lower
fasting blood glucose levels an insulin treatment will be needed eventually. In-
sulin is given in small doses to start with and are reevaluated based on blood
glucose measurements until the clinically recommended fasting blood glucose
target is reached. The glucose targets for diﬀerent guidelines vary. The general
recommended target for fasting glucose measurements by the ADA is between
4.4 and 7.2 mmol/L [3]. This target can vary between patients depending on
personal risk factors such as hypoglycemia, age and body weight [3]. Insulin
is often begun at a low dose, approximately 0.1-0.2 U/day/kg, although these
doses can be much larger, 0.3-0.4 U/day/kg for patients experiencing more se-
vere hyperglycemia[16]. But many algorithms start titration with a dose of 10
U/day[6] with weekly dose adjustments. This starting dose can then be titrated
using simple algorithms. The ADA recommends using a simple titration algo-
rithm to drive glucose into the target glucose interval. One such algorithm can
be seen in table 2.2 which we will call the simple titration algorithm. The simple
algorithm adjusts the doses with ±2 U/day if fasting glucose levels are outside
the target glucose interval.

3.1.1
Stochastic formulation
In real-life diabetes patients there is always some random variability in measure-
ments. This variability can be the results of many factors and can be broadly
divided into drug related variability and variability of the physiological state of
the body due to stress, weight loss and exercise to name a few [5]. In this section
we aim to simulate this variability by transforming the Aradóttir model from
a system of ordinary diﬀerential equations (ODEs) to a system of stochastic
diﬀerential equations (SDEs).
The Aradóttir model is a system of ﬁrst order ODEs which can be written on
general form
dx
dt = f(x(t), u(t), θ)
(3.1)
where x(t) is the state vector containing information about insulin and glucose
concentrations, u(t) is the insulin input, θ are the parameters and f describes
the glucose-insulin dynamics. The glucose variability can be simulated by trans-
forming the ODE system to stochastic diﬀerential equations (SDEs) by adding a
diﬀusion term in the form of a Brownian motion, κdw(t). This SDE formulation
can be written as
dx(t) = f(x(t), u(t), θ)dt + κdw(t), 0 < t < T
(3.2)
where w(t) is a standard Brownian motion and the solution x(t) is a random
variable for each t, κ is a tuning parameter which describes how large the vari-
ability factor of the Brownian motion is. SDE systems can be solved using the
Euler-Maruyama method from Higham 2019 [13], where the solution can be
written as
x(τj) = x(τj−1) + f(x(τj−1), u(τj−1), θ)∆t + κ∆wj
(3.3)
where ∆wj = w(τj)−w(τj−1) and ∆wj is normally distributed stochastic vari-
able with mean 0 and standard deviation of I∆t, ∆wj ∼N(0, I∆t).

The biggest problem for using the normal equation in practice is computing the
inverse of XT X. There are methods which can be used to compute this inverse

QR decomposition is a factorization of a square matrix A into a product of

3.2.3
Performance Measures
Having come up with two dose prediction models we want to set up a metric
which can be used to assess how good the dose suggestions are. We have chosen
to investigate two aspects of the dose suggestions, these aspects are safety and
accuracy. A safe dose should be low enough so that the patient does not enter
a hypoglycemic state. An accurate dose should lower blood glucose to within
the target glucose interval recommended by the American Diabetes Association
of between 4.4 and 7.2 mmol/L. In this section we will introduce an important
concept which we will use to assess the safety and accuracy of the dose predic-
tions.
We want to investigate the interval of dose sizes which are safe and accurate. In
Aradóttir et al. [9] a so called target dose interval is deﬁned. The target dose
interval is the interval where doses are deemed safe and accurate, and blood
glucose measurements are within clinically recommended targets. We use the
simple titration algorithm to assess the lower and upper limits of the target dose
interval. For the lower limit we ﬁnd the size of a target dose needed to bring
blood glucose below 7.2 mmol/L. For the upper limit we ﬁnd the target dose
which is needed to bring blood glucose down to 4.4 mmol/L. A target dose is
identiﬁed when three consecutive dose changes are equal to or within ±2 U/day
and blood glucose lies within the target glucose interval
A dose is assessed to be safe if it is below the upper limit of the target dose
interval. These doses should not make the patient hypoglycemic but they are
not necessarily large enough to bring the glucose into the target glucose inter-
val. An accurate dose is a dose which lies within the target dose interval. These

doses should lower blood glucose to within the target glucose interval and are
less likely to make the patient either hyperglycemic or hypoglycemic.

3.2.5
Eﬀect of Steady-state
When once-weekly insulin is administered it lowers blood glucose levels over
approximately one week. When the next weekly dose is administered there are
still some traces of the previous dose in the system. This means that a dose can
aﬀect blood glucose measurements long after it has been administered. When
new doses are administered, the insulin is added to the amount already present
in the body and this results in a higher eﬀect over time even when the dose size
is not increased. After some weeks, this stacking of doses stabilizes and the full
eﬀect of a dose size has been obtained. In this section we will discuss how the
delay to reach steady state aﬀects the data we collect.
We need to come up with a method for measuring how far towards steady-
state blood glucose the patient has reached after initiating insulin therapy. The
steady-state blood glucose is deﬁned as the blood glucose level which the patient
will maintain if he receives the same amount of insulin at the same time each
week. We want to ﬁgure out a way to measure the steady-state glucose level
and then use that value to measure how far towards steady-state the glucose
has reached after each week.
We investigated two aspects of the steady state blood glucose. Firstly we will
measure how the size of the dose aﬀected steady state and secondly we want to
see how the blood glucose gradually reaches steady state over time. We set up

4.2.8
Safety
We have looked at how accurate the dose prediction models are, that is to say
how many predictions lie within the target dose interval. We also looked into
where the predictions which lie outside the interval are. For real-life clinical
situations it is safer to give smaller insulin doses than larger so we looked at
how many dose predictions were below the target dose interval and how many
lie above.
The result was that every single dose prediction which did not lie inside the
target dose interval was above the upper limit of the interval. This is not a
great result for the prediction model as it is better to keep the doses lower.
==PAGE:60==
48
Results
However given how well the linear model predicted doses inside the interval it is
no surprise that no dose predictions were under the lower limit. The non-linear
model is more troubling as it did not predict many doses inside the interval and
when it predicted outside it the doses were too large.
These results suggest clearly that the linear ﬁt is a safer dose predicting model. It
predicted more safe doses and had a much smaller deviation in dose predictions.
With regards to how many data points per week were used it was apparent that
the more data points were used the better the predictions up to 5 points per
week. For the diﬀerent times between dose changes there was some diﬀerence
going from changing doses every week to every other week, but it did not seem
to have a notable diﬀerence making the period between dose changes longer than
2 weeks. This lengthening of the initial dosing period could have a detrimental
eﬀect to reaching the target insulin dose. If the initial doses are much lower than
the target dose of the patient it can lead to extended periods of hyperglycemia
which negatively aﬀects the health of the patient. It would be advantageous
to ﬁnd a way to keep the initial dosing period as short as possible and predict
doses that are as safe.
4.3
Summary
In this chapter we have shown the results of our various simulation scenarios.
We started by showing results from the MVP model where we observed that
the meal compartments had only a small eﬀect on the fasting glucose of the
patient. We then moved on to the Aradóttir model where we simulated blood
glucose proﬁles which included a stochastic process to mimic glucose variability.
We showed how the model reacted to some standard of care treatments. We
showed that it takes 6 weeks to see the full eﬀect of the insulin to lower blood
glucose. We measured the target dose interval which was an important metric
in comparing the dose prediction models.
We then investigated how well the linear regression models represented a large
data set with a wide range of insulin doses and blood glucose values. The results
were that the non-linear model gave on average a better estimate of the data.
The dose prediction models were then tested in practice where the linear model
proved to be better at predicting target doses in almost all cases.

Discussion
The work done in this project was an exploratory investigation of how the
insulin-glucose dynamics of a T2D patient might behave when given once-weekly
insulin. The work was centered on investigating how dosing might be adjusted to
reach glycemic control. We investigated how standard of care dosing strategies
for once-daily insulin would fare when used on a patient undergoing once-weekly
insulin treatment. We also developed two models which could be used to suggest
dose sizes to bring the patients blood glucose levels to clinically recommended
targets.
Both the simple titration algorithm and the stepwise algorithm proved to be ad-
equate to bring glycemic control to the patient. The stepwise algorithm brought
glucose to lower levels which is expected given that its target glucose range is
lower than that of the simple algorithm. This results in the stepwise algorithm
generally suggesting larger doses. This may be dangerous in real life situations
as clinicians and patients are very careful not to take too much insulin as this
increases the risk of hypoglycemia greatly. The insulin sensitivity of people is
not a ﬁxed number as it is in our modelling. Changes in lifestyle such as weight
loss, more healthy eating habits and less smoking has been shown to increase
insulin sensitivity and thus lowering the persons need for insulin. Once-weekly
insulin doses are much larger than once-daily doses and so patients could be
more sensitive to large doses. The modelling in this work could be taken fur-

With regards to the dose prediction models we found that they performed simi-

clinical scenarios. The linear dose prediction model performed very well when
given only a few data points. Almost all dose suggestions landed within the
target dose interval. The non-linear model fared much worse when given only a
few data points. Its dose suggestions were often outside the target dose interval.
More importantly it was very unsafe with most of the dose predictions outside
the target dose interval being too large.
When comparing the dose prediction models to standard of care treatments we
mostly look at the time it takes to reach a target dose. The standard of care
treatments reached glycemic control after approximately 6-10 weeks depending
on the target glucose interval of the titration algorithm used. For the prediction
models the length of the period before a dose was predicted was varied. Results
showed that after 3 weeks the linear model could predict 98%+ safe and accu-
rate doses with enough data points.
Comparison of the two methods, standard of care and our dose prediction mod-
els, shows that some time can be saved reaching a target dose by using dose
prediction models. However if the safe and accurate doses are much larger than
the doses used in the initial dosing period it could be dangerous to increase
them drastically in one go. Some sort of algorithm could be needed to gradually
increase doses until the predicted dose is reached.
The implementation could be improved in some ways for a better representation
of real-life people with T2D. The physiological models we have used to simulate
these patients were based on clinical trials of people with type 1 diabetes and
had to be augmented to represent T2D patients. The augmentation chosen was
the simplest one suggested by the literature and choosing a more complex T2D
augmentation could have an eﬀect on how well the model represents T2D. The
model parameters used to simulate a patient were the average of the parameters
found in the original article. This average patient does not necessarily represent
how a real person would react to once-weekly insulin. The prediction models
could be tested on patients with diﬀerent parameters. The target dose interval
is speciﬁc to the patient and would change by changing parameters. This could
result in smaller target dose intervals which could further cause the dose predic-
tion models to predict outside the interval. It would be interesting to see how

Chapter 6
Conclusion & Future Work
This work proposes an approach to insulin initiation for people with T2D using
ultra-long acting, once-weekly, insulin. We aimed to investigate the potential of
this new generation insulin in T2D treatment through the three following steps;
1) to implement physiological models which describe insulin-glucose dynamics
in people with T2D 2) to use these models to simulate standard of care treat-
ments for virtual T2D patients with once-daily and once-weekly insulins, and 3)
to develop prediction models which could suggest weekly insulin doses to bring
blood glucose measurements into the target glucose interval. We aimed to be
able to predict insulin doses using only a few fasting blood glucose measure-
ments inorder to lessen the burden on the patient and speed up the titration
process.
We implemented two diabetes models, the Medtronic Virtual Patient (MVP)
model and the Aradóttir model. The MVP model includes a meal compartment
which describes the eﬀect meals have on blood glucose. This was deemed un-
necessary for this work as meal induced glycemic spikes had only a small eﬀect
on fasting blood glucose. The Aradóttir model is based on the MVP model but
does not include a meal compartment. We used the Aradóttir model to simulate
a patient undergoing once-weekly insulin treatment.

Conclusion & Future Work
We researched how the eﬀect of once-weekly insulin is not fully observed until a
few weeks after starting treatment. The true eﬀect of the insulin was only clear
after 6 weeks. We proposed two linear regression models to ﬁt to insulin-glucose
data, one linear model where the decrease in glucose was linearly proportional
to increases in insulin and a non-linear model where the decrease in glucose was
proportional to increase in the square root of insulin. Results showed that the
non-linear model better represented the data when given a wide range of insulin
and glucose values. These regression models were converted to dose prediction
models. The linear prediction model proved much more adept at predicting safe
insulin doses in all cases. It suggested safe doses in all cases when using only 4
blood glucose measurements per week. The non-linear model made poor dose
predictions when fewer data points were available.
6.1
Future Work
In this work we simulated a patient using the Aradóttir model. This patient
was deﬁned by the average values of parameters given by the MVP model. The
modelling could be improved by implementing more patients using a diﬀerent
set of parameters to give new glucose proﬁles to further test how accurate the
dose prediction models can be.
In this work we assumed that during the initial dosing period the patient would
adhere completely to the treatment. It would be of interest to investigate how
missing insulin injections or missing blood glucose measurements would aﬀect
blood glucose proﬁles and the data gathered for the prediction models.
Modelling of how the eﬀect of insulin doses is not fully observed there could be
improvements made to the prediction model where instead of giving the patient
the same dose for more weeks an estimate could be used to predict the steady-
state value of the glucose instead of giving the patient the same dose for many
weeks in a row.
All modelling and simulations were done using a patient not undergoing in-
sulin treatment. Many people with T2D currently undergoing once-daily insulin
treatment would switch over to once-weekly treatment. Modelling how a pa-
tients blood glucose would behave when the insulin medicine is changed would
help in the development of once-weekly treatment.
