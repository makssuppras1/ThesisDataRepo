Abstract 
The energy landscape is changing fast all over the world. A transformation 
toward decarbonization, the integration of renewable energy sources (RES), 
and the development of efficient energy storage technologies have become 
critical to secure the energy systems and offer affordable renewable energy. 
Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cells (SOECs), particularly when integrated as 
reversible Solid Oxide Cells (rSOCs), are a promising solution that is under 
investigation and shows good performance so far. On this basis, a general 
steady-state SOEC model is designed at the beginning, and then model 
validation is conducted using experimental data from multiple sources, with 
strong agreement in voltage–current (i–V) characteristics and overpotential 
trends. The steady-state model was followed by the development and 
simulation of a dynamic, spatially resolved model for Solid Oxide Electrolysis 
Cells (SOECs), focusing on plug-flow behavior and frequency-domain 
performance. The cell is discretized into ten slabs, each modeled as a 
continuously stirred tank reactor (CSTR), allowing for the analysis of gas 
composition, electrochemical potential, and impedance characteristics along 
the flow direction. The model integrates key physical phenomena, including 
Nernst voltage evolution, gas conversion resistance, temperature effects, and 
fuel utilization sensitivity. Sensitivity analysis in the plug flow model shows how 
temperature and utilization impact local performance: higher temperatures 
improve conductivity and voltage efficiency, while high utilization increases 
impedance and response time. These results highlight critical trade-offs in 
SOEC design. Both steady-state and transient behaviors are captured, 
enabling a detailed study of the low-frequency arc in Nyquist plots, commonly 
attributed to gas conversion impedance. 
The model's strengths include modularity, frequency response analysis, and 
extensibility to system-level integration. Limitations such as simplified thermal 
modeling and neglected electrode microstructure are noted, with future work 
proposed to incorporate thermal stress, degradation, and control strategies. 
The outcomes contribute to the design of efficient, reliable, and controllable 
SOEC systems for sustainable hydrogen production.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
As the global energy landscape undergoes a profound transformation toward 
decarbonization, the integration of renewable energy sources (RES) and the 
development of efficient energy storage technologies have become critical to 
ensuring a sustainable and resilient energy system. Among the promising 
solutions addressing these challenges are Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cells 
(SOECs), particularly when integrated as reversible Solid Oxide Cells (rSOCs). 
These systems offer a pathway to high-efficiency, low-emission energy 
conversion and storage, aligning with international efforts to achieve carbon 
neutrality. 
SOEC electrolyzers exhibit several significant advantages over conventional 
low-temperature electrolysis technologies such as Proton Exchange Membrane 
(PEM) and Alkaline Exchange Membrane (AEM) systems. Operating at 
elevated temperatures (typically 600°C to 900°C), they enable enhanced 
reaction kinetics and superior electrical efficiencies. They can convert surplus 
renewable electricity—often generated intermittently by wind and solar—into 
hydrogen, thereby facilitating large-scale, long-duration energy storage. 
Moreover, stored hydrogen can be utilized directly or reconverted into electricity 
through fuel cell operation, as seen in dual-mode rSOC systems. This 
bidirectional functionality results in enhanced round-trip efficiency, flexibility for 
grid balancing, peak shaving, and seasonal energy storage. 
The compact and integrated design of rSOCs, which combine Solid Oxide 
Electrolysis Cells (SOECs) and Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (SOFCs) within a single 
system, further reduces installation footprint and enhances system-level 
efficiency. These features render rSOCs particularly well-suited for 
decentralized applications, including small islands, remote residential 
communities, and industrial sites with steady energy demand and local RES 
generation. Additionally, their compatibility with thermal and hydrogen storage 
systems positions them as multifunctional assets capable of delivering 
electricity, heat, and fuel [1], [2]. 
From a strategic perspective, the deployment of SOEC-based systems aligns 
with multiple regional and global energy goals. The European Union’s 
REPowerEU strategy and Hydrogen Strategy explicitly identify rSOC 
technologies as key enablers for achieving climate neutrality. They set a target 
of 500 MW electrolyzer capacity by 2030, with up to 20% expected from 
reversible systems. Globally, initiatives such as the IEA’s Net Zero by 2050 
Roadmap and the U.S. Department of Energy’s Hydrogen Shot recognize the 
crucial role of high-temperature electrolysis in transitioning to clean hydrogen 
economies [3-5]. 
European authorities have responded to this pressing need by actively 
supporting the deployment of rSOC technologies. The REPowerEU plan not 
only sets ambitious capacity targets but also allocates €3 billion from the 
NextGenerationEU fund to accelerate clean hydrogen deployment, explicitly 
==PAGE:9==
including rSOC pilot projects [3], [4]. Several recent EU-supported initiatives 
exemplify the technological readiness and diverse applications of rSOC 
systems: 
• 
REFLEX (CORDIS): A 10 kW rSOC system in rural France achieved 
62% round-trip efficiency and over 1,000 successful mode-switch cycles, 
supplying power and heat with minimal degradation and optimized 
performance through model predictive control [5-7]. 
• 
NAUTILUS (CINEA): A hybrid SOFC–battery system developed for 
maritime decarbonization demonstrated >30% CO₂ reduction and 85% 
combined heat and power efficiency in sea trial simulations, aligning with 
IMO 2030 targets [8-9]. 
• 
HyLink: Sunfire’s 100 kW rSOC in Saxony integrates wind and solar 
inputs, producing 284 kg/day of hydrogen with >65% efficiency and 
delivering grid services like peak shaving and frequency regulation [9]. 
• 
ELCOGEN Project: Elcogen's scalable rSOC stacks demonstrated 
>85% efficiency and low hydrogen production costs, supported by 
substantial EU funding and private investment to scale production 
capacity to 360 MW/year [10]. 
• 
VTT Smart Energy Hub: A 5 kW rSOC system integrated into a Finnish 
building reduced primary energy use by 18% and achieved an energy 
utilization factor of 0.85, leveraging adaptive digital control to extend 
system lifespan and ensure economic viability [11]. 
Together, these projects underscore the versatility, scalability, and growing 
commercial readiness of rSOC systems across diverse sectors—from 
microgrids to maritime applications—reinforcing their role as pivotal enablers of 
Europe’s clean energy transition. Technological advancements in materials 
science have also significantly improved SOEC durability and performance, 
making them increasingly viable for real-world applications. As public and 
private funding accelerates and demonstration projects proliferate across the 
EU and beyond, SOEC technology continues to mature into a commercially 
attractive and technically robust solution. Its potential for integration with grid 
services and industrial processes firmly positions SOECs at the forefront of the 
global clean energy transformation [12],[13]. 
1.2  Research Objective 
It is an urgent need to act and create new and more advanced and efficient 
technologies, like rSOC, to help in decarbonization, in storing energy, and to 
solve environmental issues. Although, EU, with its foundation programs and 
private funds, is funding the research and the investments around the rSOC 
field, it is not enough to establish the technology as the main solution. There 
are still many issues that need to be solved by researchers across the world. 
So the purpose of this master's thesis is to improve the overall research and 
understanding around the rSOC technology.  
==PAGE:10==
This master’s thesis focuses on the modeling and analysis of Reversible Solid 
Oxide Cell (rSOC) systems, with an emphasis on their role in supporting the 
green energy transition and sustainable energy applications. Specifically, the 
work integrates recent scientific and experimental findings from the literature to 
develop a comprehensive and advanced steady-state model. This model is 
subsequently validated against both experimental and simulated data to ensure 
accuracy and robustness.  
A second key contribution of this thesis is the design of a dynamic-state model 
for the investigation of the capacitive behavior of the SOEC cell, segmented 
into ten slabs, and its influence on overall cell performance. The capacitive 
effects, particularly relevant at low frequencies, are modeled to enhance the 
understanding of alternating current (AC) versus direct current (DC) operational 
characteristics. 
Based on an extensive literature review, current research in the rSOC field can 
be broadly categorized into three main areas: 
• 
Thermochemical modeling, which addresses both operational modes 
(SOFC and SOEC) and their integration with balance-of-plant systems. 
• 
Dynamic modeling, focusing on the transient behavior of the system in 
both modes. 
• 
Optimization and management modeling, which includes component 
sizing and the development of efficient operational strategies. 
In this context, a dynamic rSOC model is developed using MATLAB/Simulink to 
simulate the transient performance of the system under SOEC mode. The 
results provide critical insights for future research and development efforts, 
particularly in the areas of control design, operational optimization, and grid 
integration of rSOC systems. 
1.3  Thesis Structure 
This thesis is organized into five main chapters. Chapter 1 introduces the 
research background, motivation, and objectives of the study. Chapter 2 
reviews the latest advancements in Reversible Solid Oxide Cell (rSOC) 
technology, highlighting current challenges and the key areas that require 
further development to enhance its performance and applicability. Chapter 3 
details the modeling methodology of Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cell (SOEC) and 
presents a validation of the developed model using experimental and literature-
based data. Chapter 4 focuses on the analysis of the capacitive behavior of the 
SOEC stack, examining its impact on system performance at low-frequency 
operation. Finally, Chapter 5 summarizes the main findings and offers 
recommendations for future research directions in the field of rSOC systems.

2. Literature Review 
2.1  rSOC Operational Principle 
2.1.1 rSOC Stack Level Operation 
Reversible 
Solid 
Oxide 
Cells 
(rSOCs) 
leverage 
high-temperature 
electrochemistry to both generate and store energy within a single device, 
operating interchangeably as a Solid Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC) and Solid Oxide 
Electrolysis Cell (SOEC). In SOFC mode, they oxidize fuel (e.g., H₂, CO, CH₄), 
producing electricity, heat, and reaction products. In contrast, in SOEC mode, 
they consume electricity and heat to split steam (or CO₂/H₂O mixtures) back 
into hydrogen (or syngas) and oxygen. High operating temperatures (600–900 
°C) enable rapid ion transport through a dense ceramic electrolyte, commonly 
yttria-stabilized zirconia (YSZ), and allow for internal heat integration, yielding 
round-trip efficiencies of 60–70% under optimized conditions [15-17].  
In the anodic reaction of the SOFC operation, the H2 or other fuel is oxidized, 
releasing electrons into the external circuit: 
 
H₂ + O²⁻ → H₂O + 2 e⁻  (anode)  
(2.1) 
 
While in the cathodic reaction, the O2 from air gains electrons at the cathode 
and incorporates into the electrolyte as oxide ions [14]: 
 
½O₂ + 2 e⁻ → O²⁻  (cathode)  
(2.2) 
 
The O²⁻ ions migrate through the YSZ electrolyte from cathode to anode, 
sustaining the redox reactions and closing the electrical circuit via external 
electron flow, delivering power to the load.  
During the cathodic reaction of the SOEC mode, the steam is reduced, and 
electrons are consumed to generate H2:  
 
H₂O + 2 e⁻ → H₂ + O²⁻  (cathode)  
(2.3) 
 
Oxide ions are formed at the cathode and migrate to the anode, where they 
release electrons to form O2 gas [15]: 
 
O²⁻ → ½O₂ + 2 e⁻  (anode) 
(2.4) 
 
It has been supported by many researchers that, by harvesting waste heat 
from the SOFC mode, residual heat can supply the endothermic component 
of the SOEC reaction, reducing supplemental electrical energy by up to 30% 
[40].

The following schematic illustrates the Balance-of-Plant (BoP) and reactant 
flow paths for a reversible solid oxide cell (rSOC) system operating in both fuel-

rSOC unit, and the way they are connected, is also shown. 
The system consists of the fuel tank (bottom left) and the exhaust tank (top left). 
The first tank stores H₂, CO, or CH₄ for SOFC mode. When operating as a fuel

The cooler HX (middle left) chills the high‐temperature SOFC exhaust before it 
enters the pump, protecting downstream components, while the preheater HX

reducing auxiliary heating requirements.

regulated with upstream and downstream valves of the pump and enables 
purge or bypass operations during mode switching.

fuel at the anode and reducing oxygen at the cathode (air enters via the dashed‐

electrolyzing steam back into hydrogen (blue fluid stream), which can then be

2.2 rSOC Systems Categorization 
As described above, multiple commercial rSOC projects have been built in 
different ways. rSOC systems differentiate in many critical parameters that can 
affect the total performance of the system, such as the cell geometry, the 
switching mode, and the feedstock flexibility. The available options for each of 
the above parameters are presented. 
2.2.1 Cell Geometry 
Reversible solid oxide cell (rSOC) systems encompass a diverse range of cell 
architectures and operational schemes tailored to specific applications, each 
presenting unique advantages and engineering challenges. Planar cells—
characterized by thin, flat electrolyte layers sandwiched between ceramic 
electrodes—can achieve very high-power densities (up to 1–2 W/cm² in SOFC 
mode) due to their large active surface area and short ionic transport distances. 
However, the large, flat geometry demands exceptionally reliable sealing 
technologies (e.g., glass or mica seals) to prevent gas cross-leakage at 
temperatures above 700 °C. Planar designs also facilitate the use of advanced 
coating techniques for electrode materials, enabling fine-tuning of catalytic 
activity and ion conduction paths [18]. 
In contrast, tubular cells feature a cylindrical electrolyte tube surrounded by 
porous electrode layers, inherently sealing the reactant streams within the cell. 
While tubular designs typically operate at lower power densities (0.2–0.5 
W/cm²), their built-in mechanical robustness and uniform thermal expansion 
make them highly tolerant of the thermal cycling stresses encountered during 
frequent mode switches [8]. Tubular modules have therefore found favor in 
applications that demand extreme reliability over thousands of thermal cycles, 
such as remote off-grid power systems or industrial backup systems [19]. 
==PAGE:14==
2.2.2 Switching Mode 
Beyond cell geometry, rSOC systems are differentiated by their mode-switching 
schemes. In batch-switch configurations, the entire stack transitions between 
SOFC and SOEC operation in discrete steps, often with a brief purge and 
thermal soak between modes to ensure residual gases and temperature 
gradients are managed safely. This approach simplifies control logic but incurs 
downtime during transitions, which can range from 10 to 60 minutes depending 
on stack size and thermal management capability.  
Conversely, continuous dual-mode or co-stack operation leverages sub-stack 
segmentation, where one portion of the cells operates as a fuel cell while 
another operates as an electrolyzer simultaneously. By carefully balancing 
current distribution, gas flow, and thermal conditions, continuous dual-mode 
systems can maintain uninterrupted power or hydrogen production, offering a 
fast response to grid signals or market prices. This architecture requires 
sophisticated power electronics and advanced thermal integration to prevent 
internal heat imbalances, but can dramatically improve overall system 
availability and round-trip efficiency [20]. 
2.2.3 Feedstock Flexibility 
A third axis of categorization arises from feedstock flexibility and chemical 
integration. Traditional rSOCs handle pure steam and hydrogen, but co-
electrolysis variants incorporate CO₂ into the feed, converting H₂O/CO₂ 
mixtures directly into syngas (a mixture of H₂ and CO) in SOEC mode. This 
syngas can then be processed via Fischer–Tropsch or methanation reactors to 
produce liquid fuels, synthetic natural gas, or value-added chemicals, enabling 
carbon capture and utilization pathways within a single modular unit. Co-
electrolysis not only broadens the fuel options for SOFC mode—allowing the 
system to operate on biogas or even flue gas mixtures—but also tightly couples 
power, heat, and chemical production in a flexible “power-to-X” strategy.  
By scaling these modular stacks from a few kilowatts (suitable for residential 
combined heat and power, or CHP) up to several megawatts (for grid-scale 
energy storage or industrial clusters) rSOC technology can address a wide 
spectrum of energy and sustainability challenges, from decarbonizing small 
communities to stabilizing large-scale renewable generation [21]. 
2.3 rSOC Challenges 
The modeling of rSOCs is a significant scientific and technical concern. Various 
modeling approaches can be implemented, ranging from rSOC stack 
simulations to their integration within broader systems. These modeling 
approaches can be categorized into: 
• Thermochemical modeling, which addresses SOEC and SOFC 
operational modes and the balance of plant (BoP). 
• Dynamic modeling in both operational modes (SOFC and SOEC). 
==PAGE:15==
• Optimization and management modeling, focusing on the optimization 
of the system component sizing and operation strategies in reversible 
mode.  
Although interest in this technology is growing, numerous research studies and 
experiments are ongoing, and academic papers are being published. Several 
research gaps can be identified from the literature review that need further 
investigation.  
Firstly, current studies have quite limited consideration for the mode switching 
process of rSOCs when implementing system-level operation for microgrids. 
Although several publications focused on building detailed dynamic models to 
characterize the transient performance of rSOCs during mode switching, these 
models primarily address component-level modeling rather than system-level 
operation [27], [23]. 
Furthermore, few studies consider the heat recovery potential of rSOCs in 
optimal operation for microgrids. While some researchers attempt to utilize 
waste heat during rSOC operation, they primarily focus on waste heat at the 
fuel cell mode, neglecting broader heat recovery opportunities, such as those 
in the electrolysis mode with exothermic reactions [24-26]. 
Additionally, the fitted energy conversion models used in these studies do not 
adequately capture the real thermal dynamics and struggle to evaluate the 
waste heat released during different operation modes accurately. Therefore, 
there remains a lack of comprehensive operation models [22], [28]. 
2.4 Academic Research 
Literature review has yielded many integrative modeling frameworks. For 
example, in the studies [29],[30], different models were created to accurately 
predict the thermo-electrochemical behavior under dynamic loads, enabling 
digital-twin approaches that cut peak temperature gradients by up to 50 %. An 
advanced nonlinear model-predictive control (MPC) scheme that reduces 
mode-switch times from ~15 min to under 8 min while slashing degradation 
rates by 25% over hundreds of cycles was created by 2 different researcher 
groups, [31], [32].  Multi-stage start-up/shutdown protocols have achieved 15 
% lower energy consumption and 20 % longer cycle life through staged heating 
and gas purges [33]. According to literature, heat-recovery strategies 
leveraging exhaust recuperators and phase-change materials (PCMs) now 
supply up to 30 % of SOEC heating needs, stabilizing temperatures within ±5 
degrees. At the same time, materials innovations—such as ceria-based 
composites and graded electrodes—have extended stack lifetimes beyond 
10,000 h under reversible cycling, reducing coking and sulfur poisoning [27]. 
Finally, techno-economic studies show that optimized dispatch and control 
algorithms can cut the total cost of ownership by 15–20 %, improving revenue 
from electricity and hydrogen markets. A detailed literature review is presented

2.4.5 Degradation Mitigation Strategies 
Material innovations have focused on developing electrodes capable of

phase transitions. These electrodes demonstrated high performance and 
durability in reversible solid oxide cells, attributed to their tailored 
nanostructures that facilitate efficient oxygen ion transport and accommodate 
redox-induced structural changes. 
Collectively, these studies underscore the critical role of material engineering in 
enhancing the durability and performance of rSOC systems. By addressing the 
challenges associated with redox and thermal cycling through innovative 
electrode designs and materials, these advancements pave the way for more 
robust and commercially viable rSOC technologies [48].

3.2  Modeling Validation 
The theoretical electrochemical equations described in the preceding section 
were employed to simulate experimental results, serving as the foundation for 
model validation. The validation process incorporated three distinct data 
sources: two peer-reviewed research studies and one set of industrial 
simulation data provided by a commercial technology developer. This multi-
source validation approach ensured both scientific rigor and practical 
relevance. 
The first validation was conducted using experimental data from [57], who 
investigated the current-voltage (i–V) characteristics of hydrogen production in 
a solid oxide electrolysis cell (SOEC) with a planar, electrolyte-supported 
configuration. The cell architecture included an electrolyte composed of yttria-
stabilized zirconia (YSZ), a nickel–YSZ (Ni–YSZ) composite cathode, and an 
anode made from a mixture of YSZ and strontium-doped lanthanum manganite 
(LSM–YSZ). The respective thicknesses of the electrolyte, anode, and cathode 
were 1000 μm, 100 μm, and 100 μm. The experiments were performed at a 
constant pressure of 1 bar, with a steam molar fraction of 60%, and over a 
temperature range of 1173–1273 K. The hydrogen production results reported 
in this study were cross validated using the methodologies described in the 
published work by [58]. 
The second validation utilized the modeling framework presented by [59], which 
was implemented in MATLAB R2020b. This model captures both the 
electrochemical energy demands of the SOEC and the energy requirements of 
the balance of plant (BoP). The cell configuration consists of a perovskite-based 
==PAGE:23==
oxygen electrode using La₀.₆Sr₀.₄Co₀.₂Fe₀.₈O₃–δ (LSCF), supplemented by a 
Gd₀.₂Ce₀.₈O₂–δ (GDC) barrier layer. The electrolyte is made of 8 mol% YSZ 
(8YSZ), while the hydrogen electrode is a supported structure containing both 
an active layer and a diffusion layer, composed of Ni–8YSZ and Ni–3YSZ, 
respectively. The volume fractions of nickel in the active and diffusion layers are 
assumed to be 0.35 and 0.5. The model assumes an active cell area of 0.04 
m², which lies between current state-of-the-art configurations (0.02 m²) and 
projected values for 2050 (0.1 m²).

Danish commercial technology developer company, Dynelectro. This industrial

3.2.1 1st Model Validation 
To verify the accuracy and robustness of the developed SOEC model 
implemented in MATLAB/Simulink, a comprehensive validation procedure was 
undertaken. This involved benchmarking the simulation results against the 
experimental data presented in the first academic reference listed in the 
validation literature table, specifically the work by [57]. The model was carefully 
tuned to reflect the operating conditions, geometric dimensions, and material 
parameters used in that study. 
Following the experimental setup described in [57], the electrolyte, anode, and 
cathode were modeled with thicknesses of 1000 µm, 100 µm, and 100 µm, 
respectively. The simulations were conducted under atmospheric pressure (1 
bar) with a steam molar fraction of 60% in the fuel feed stream. The operating 
temperature varied between 1173 K and 1273 K to investigate the thermal 
influence on SOEC performance. The model's validity was assessed by 
comparing the simulated current–voltage (i–V) characteristics with those 
reported in the literature, focusing on both qualitative trends and quantitative 
accuracy. 
The i–V plots generated from the model showed a strong correlation with the 
experimental curves presented in the reference study. The shape and position 
of the curves, particularly the starting and ending voltage values for a given 
current density range, closely matched the reported data. For instance, at an 
operating temperature of 1273 K, the modeled cell voltage begins in the range 
of approximately 0.85 V to 1.0 V at open circuit (0 A/m²) and rises to above 1.6 
V at a current density of 6000 A/m², which is consistent with the published 
results. This strong agreement across the operating temperature range 
confirms that the implemented physical phenomena—such as activation 
losses, ohmic resistance, concentration overpotentials, and reversible Nernst 
potential—have been correctly modeled. 
An important observation from the results is the temperature-dependent 
behavior of the cell voltage. As the operating temperature increases from 1173 
K to 1273 K, the overall cell voltage for a given current density decreases. This 
is consistent with electrochemical theory: higher temperatures enhance ionic 
conductivity in the electrolyte, reduce activation energy for electrode reactions, 
and improve mass transport phenomena. Consequently, operating the SOEC 
at higher temperatures has the following advantages: 
• lead to reduced voltage requirements,  
==PAGE:25==
• improved electrical efficiency and  
• lower irreversible losses.  
These attributes are highly beneficial for the energy balance of high-
temperature electrolysis systems, especially in large-scale hydrogen 
production applications [61]. 
However, the advantages of high-temperature operation must be balanced 
against the associated challenges. While lower operating voltages at elevated 
temperatures are desirable, maintaining temperatures above 1173 K places 
significant thermal and mechanical stress on system components [62]. Such 
as: 
• material degradation, such as sintering of electrodes,  
• thermal expansion mismatch, and 
•  increased susceptibility to redox cycling and delamination.  
Furthermore, the energy cost and engineering complexity of achieving and 
sustaining such high temperatures can reduce the net system efficiency, 
particularly if the heat is not available from waste sources or integrated high-
temperature processes. 
Thus, model validation not only confirms the correctness of the electrochemical 
representation but also provides a valuable platform for conducting sensitivity 
analyses, optimizing stack design, and evaluating trade-offs between thermal 
input, cell performance, and long-term durability.

3.2.2 2nd Model Validation 
The second validation step of the developed SOEC model was based on the 
study conducted by [59], in which a comprehensive techno-economic and 
environmental assessment of solid oxide electrolyzer cell (SOEC) systems was 
==PAGE:28==
presented. The authors investigated the life cycle and material-criticality 
implications of hydrogen production via SOEC technology, focusing particularly 
on the structural and functional components of the cell, such as the electrolyte, 
anode, and cathode. Their work detailed the impact of different material 
properties—specifically thickness, conductivity, and durability—on the 
electrochemical performance, cost, and sustainability of the overall system. By 
highlighting the role of each material layer, they provided a valuable benchmark 
for validating computational models of SOEC operation. 
To replicate and compare the findings of [59], the simulation model developed 
in MATLAB/Simulink was adapted using similar parameter values. The values 
can be seen in the above table. These conditions closely follow the 
assumptions in the referenced study, ensuring a fair comparison of 
electrochemical behavior under steady-state operation. 
The simulation output includes the cell voltage, individual overpotential 
components (activation, ohmic, and concentration), and the thermoneutral 
voltage, which is critical in evaluating the thermal self-sufficiency of the 
electrolysis process. As shown in the generated plots, the cell voltage increases 
monotonically with current density, ranging from 0 A/cm² to 2 A/cm² (or 0–
20,000 A/m²), while the voltage spans from approximately 0.8 V to between 
1.25 V and 1.5 V, depending on the level of the value of the current density at 
every time. 
The Nernst or the so-called reversible voltage (𝑉𝑛𝑒𝑟𝑛𝑠𝑡= 𝑉rev), indicated by the 
black line in the simulation graph and the yellow area in the research paper, 
remains relatively constant around 0.8 V. This aligns with thermodynamic 
expectations for steam electrolysis at high temperatures, where the standard 
Gibbs free energy of reaction governs the theoretical minimum voltage 
requirement.  
The overpotentials in the simulation model and the research paper follow the 
trends. More specifically, the activation overpotential increases gradually with 
current density due to the inherent sluggishness of the electrode kinetics, 
particularly at the electrode/electrolyte interfaces. This is followed by the ohmic 
overpotential, which exhibits a linear trend consistent with Ohm’s law and 
reflects ionic and electronic resistance across the SOEC layers. Lastly, the 
concentration overpotential becomes more prominent at higher current 
densities, indicating limitations in mass transport of the reactant and product 
gases—a common phenomenon in SOEC operation under high load [67]. 
The thermoneutral voltage (𝑉thn), indicated by a dashed blue line in the 
simulation plot and black in the research paper plot, serves as an indicator of 
whether the system operates in an endothermic or exothermic mode. Below 
𝑉thn (~1.28 V), external heat input is necessary (red colored area). However, 
once the cell voltage exceeds this threshold, observed at a current density of 
==PAGE:29==
approximately 1.475 A/cm² in the referenced paper, the system becomes 
exothermic. This behavior reflects the enthalpic balance of the electrolysis

4. Dynamic State Modelling 
4.1 Introduction 
In a dynamic model simulation, there are parameters that are changing through 
time, and they affect the overall behavior of the current and the voltage. As 
shown in the above section in a steady state model the voltage and the current 
==PAGE:35==
are in the same phase, which means that they follow the same pattern or trend. 
This section will try to investigate, analyze and describe how the parameter that 
is called Gas Conversion Impedance affects the behavior of the current and 
voltage.  
The gas conversion impedance is a crucial parameter that affects the overall 
performance of an SOEC cell, stack, or unit and  reveals how fast chemical 
gases are reacting. Additionally, the simulation and analysis of the gas 
conversion impedance help to optimize the flow rates of the input gases, the 
operating temperatures, and the gas compositions, resulting in better fuel 
utilization and more stable operation. For the above reasons, the designed and 
verified model, by 3 different sources, was used for the modeling of the gas 
conversion impedance. 
This section presents the simulation and analysis of the gas conversion 
impedance and the Nernst voltage (𝑉nernst) within a segmented solid oxide 
electrolysis cell (SOEC), specifically the Elcogen E350 cell, divided into ten 
equal segments as used in Dynelectro’s simulations. 
Simulating both 𝑉nernst and gas conversion impedance provides a dual 
perspective on cell performance. The Nernst voltage captures the equilibrium 
potential locally in each slab, reflecting variations in gas composition and 
temperature. In contrast, gas conversion impedance represents the dynamic 
resistance arising from reactant depletion and product accumulation, 
particularly under transient conditions such as AC perturbations. 
This dual modeling approach is essential for SOECs, where significant spatial 
gradients and slow gas-phase kinetics influence both steady-state and transient 
behaviors. The model incorporates a spatially resolved mass balance in each 
segment, which dynamically updates the local gas composition. As water is 
consumed and hydrogen is produced, the resulting change in partial pressures 
alters both the 𝑉nernst profile and the impedance response. 
Importantly, gas conversion impedance helps differentiate between purely 
kinetic resistances and those induced by mass transport limitations. For 
instance, in electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), the low-frequency 
arc is often attributed to gas conversion processes—an insight that becomes 
evident through this coupled simulation framework. 
In summary, 𝑉nernst defines the local thermodynamic drive (static), while gas 
conversion impedance captures the system's dynamic response to 
perturbations. Modeling both in 
segmented architecture enables a 
comprehensive analysis of electrochemical and transport limitations, informing 
strategies such as increased flow rates or cell segmentation to optimize 
efficiency and transient performance.

4.1.2.2

From a dynamic standpoint, gas conversion impedance displays as a distinct 
low-frequency arc (semicircle) in the electrochemical impedance spectrum 
(EIS). This impedance arises due to the spatially distributed and time-delayed 
response of the gas-phase composition during an AC perturbation. When the 
current fluctuates, the corresponding changes in gas concentrations cannot 
instantaneously propagate along the cell length due to finite flow rates and 
conversion kinetics. The following equation describes the gas conversion 
impedance, and the parameters that it consists of will be analyzed in the 
modeling section.

At high frequencies, the gas composition is effectively "frozen," and the 
impedance response is dominated by charge-transfer and double-layer effects. 
However, at lower frequencies, the AC perturbation interacts with the gas 
conversion dynamics, leading to a frequency-dependent impedance, 
specifically attributable to composition redistribution delays. 
Gas conversion impedance should not be considered as overpotential in the 
traditional (static) sense but rather a geometry- and flow-induced transient 
resistance. As first noted by [68], [69], longer cells or segmented geometries 
==PAGE:38==
exhibit this low-frequency feature, which diminishes in shorter or better-mixed 
systems [70]. 
4.1.2.3 
Interdependence and Mitigation Strategies 
The gas conversion impedance is directly influenced by the non-uniform 𝑉nernst 
profile. During low-frequency excitation, outlet regions, having lower hydrogen 
partial pressures, cannot respond as rapidly as inlet regions, inducing phase 
lag and energy dissipation observed as impedance. In a hypothetical case with 
perfectly uniform gas composition and constant 𝑉nernst, this impedance 
component would vanish. 
Mitigating gas conversion impedance can be achieved by increasing reactant 
flow rates, reducing channel length, or enhancing mixing, all of which reduce 
spatial gradients and allow the system to behave more like a well-stirred reactor 
[68]. 
4.1.3 Model – Methodology - Parameters 
In [70], the gas conversion impedance for a CSTR model was described. The 
segmentation of a cell into “n” parts transforms the system into a plug-flow 
model. The impedance in a plug-flow model is half of the resistance in a CSTR 
model.  
The characteristic time for the gas to pass from the inlet to the outlet is 1/𝑓𝑠, 
where 𝑓𝑠 is the summit frequency of the gas conversion impedance arc. In other 
words, 𝑓𝑠 is the frequency for the impedance at the top of the gas conversion 
impedance semi-circle. 𝑓𝑠 is typically around 3-8 Hz, and depends on gas flow 
rate, geometry, and other parameters. 
So, for further simulation, it was decided to segment the cell into 10 equal slabs, 
a series of CSTR models. Each CSTR model represents a slab, and all slabs 
have the same overpotentials. At the beginning, the Nernst potential was 
calculated using the equation as before, and the operating voltage was decided 
to be 1.29 V, slightly above the thermoneutral voltage at 1.283 V. 
After that, gas entered all the slabs, and the model evaluated how the gas 
conversion and, in turn, the Nernst voltage changed over time. In that way, the 
designed model simulated the capacitive part of the SOEC stack. A temperature 
dependency was also added to the initial guess of the resistance for each slab 
to model the temperature increase and losses for each slab over time. The 
temperature change on each slap for dT is calculated by assigning a heat 
capacity to the slap and the gas. For that purpose, it was assumed that the 
stack was made from steel. 
The model was simulated for steady operation at the beginning, and then a 
sensitivity analysis was performed for different temperature values, utilization 
factors, and gas compositions.

5. Conclusion 
At the beginning, a literature review was performed to identify the current 
challenges and the scientific research around the rSOC technology. Examples 
of operational rSOC units were presented, and the fields of current research 
about the rSOC were analyzed. 
Following the literature research, a well-performed rSOC model was created on 
MATLAB/Simulink, and then the model was validated against 3 different sets of 
parameters. The i-V curves and the additional plotted results verify the 
performance, the robustness, and the adaptability of the model. More 
specifically, in the 3rd  chapter of the research project was performed model 
validation against experimental data from multiple sources ([57], [59], 
Dynelectro) confirmed the fidelity of the general designed model after several 
simulations. The confirmation of the general’s model fidelity was based on the 
visual comparison of the i–V curves characteristics, Nernst voltage trends, and 
overpotential breakdowns. Discrepancies in overpotential magnitudes were 
attributed to assumed constant properties (e.g., diffusion coefficients, 
conductivities). Nonetheless, overall trends and behaviors match reported 
experimental results, especially under high temperature and low-flow 
conditions. 
The 3rd chapter was focused on the simulation and design of a segmented plug-
flow model that captured the spatial and dynamic behavior of the SOEC system 
effectively. The simulated impedance behavior, particularly the emergence of a 
dominant low-frequency arc, confirmed that it matches literature descriptions of 
gas conversion impedance performance. The simulations showed increasing 
impedance and longer time constants under high fuel utilization, consistent with 
experimental observations. Similarly, the temperature sensitivity analysis 
revealed that higher temperatures improve ionic conductivity, reduce gas-
phase resistance, and enhance the Nernst potential, leading to improved 
dynamic response and performance. 
The model’s strength lies in its spatial resolution, segmenting the cell into slabs 
to track the local electrochemical conditions and dynamics. This design 
captures plug-flow characteristics and allows realistic frequency-domain 
analysis. It combines steady-state and transient behavior, integrates 
thermodynamics and impedance models, and produces results that correlate 
with literature and experiments. However, assumptions such as uniform intra-
slab mixing and constant parameter values limit realism had to be made. It must 
be noted that the model does not include any porous transport or degradation 
effects. The thermal gradients are simplified, and charge-transfer or gas 
diffusion impedance arcs are lumped. Introducing intra-slab diffusion, real gas 
flow dynamics, degradation kinetics, and thermal stress models would enhance 
accuracy. 
==PAGE:60==
The general model and the plug-flow segmented model could be improved 
further in the future. For example, in general modeling, some key improvements 
include modeling the gas-phase diffusion explicitly (e.g., Warburg impedance), 
incorporating dynamic thermal behavior, and extending to full stack and 
balance-of-plant simulations. The model could also support predictive control 
or degradation forecasting, helping to optimize operation under realistic, 
varying conditions. Testing these enhancements against impedance 
spectroscopy (EIS) data from long-term experiments would strengthen 
confidence in predictive capability.
