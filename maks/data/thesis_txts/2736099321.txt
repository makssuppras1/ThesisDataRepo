Abstract
The adoption of Electric Vehicle (EV)s has increased significantly over the past years im-
posing a great demand for charging services and infrastructure. The EV charging ecosys-
tem is investigated in this work, identifying its current state, future opportunities and cur-
rent challenges. The investigation is mainly focused on the role of the Charging Point
Operator (CPO) in this ecosystem, identifying common trends in their strategies, pricing
structures and smart charging implementations across Europe. This study also considers
the effect of electricity prices, transmission and distribution grid tariffs, payable taxes and
local policies on the CPO business models and value propositions.
This work also highlights the importance of CPOs for the full integration of EVs into the
grid, particularly identifying the smart EV charging potential for ancillary and flexibility ser-
vices provision. In a practical view, a simple optimization model is formulated to simulate
real-time CPO operations on charging infrastructure considering the potential of flexibil-
ity provision during peak demand hours. This model aims to tackle two problems: the
maximization of social welfare and the maximization of CPO profit. A case study has
been used to test the implementation of this optimization model on a CPO’s operations in
Denmark.
From the outcomes of this thesis, the CPO is highlighted to play a pivotal role in the full grid
integration of EVs. Its strategies and developments highly impact various stakeholders
such as the E-Mobility Service Provider (EMSP), the Distribution System Operator (DSO),
the Transmission System Operator (TSO), the aggregator and last but not least the EV
user. In the future, further collaboration and development within this network of stakehold-
ers will enable smart EV charging to overcome economic, technical, social and market
barriers.

1.1
Background and Motivation
Rising concerns about climate change have encouraged countries worldwide into action,
leading to the adoption of ambitious policies aimed at mitigating Green House Gases
(GHG) emissions. The European Union (EU) stands as a major contributor to the global
efforts to fight climate change effects. Climate targets for various economic activities’

3.2
EV Charging Ecosystem
The transition to electric mobility is not solely about the vehicles themselves but also
about the many different stakeholders working together to create a robust EV charging
ecosystem. This ecosystem involves a diverse range of players, each with its unique role
and responsibilities. The most relevant stakeholders for this study are illustrated in Figure
3.1 and will be further described.
Figure 3.1: EV Charging Ecosystem. Author’s own elaboration.
Transmission System Operators (TSOs): TSOs play a pivotal role in ensuring the effi-
cient and reliable flow of electricity across high-voltage transmission networks. The TSO
is the entity that owns, maintains and manages the transmission grid which means assets

with voltage higher than 100 kV [5]. TSOs are responsible for maintaining grid stability
and capacity planning always ensuring the balance between production and consump-
tion. Both TSO and DSO (Distribution System Operators) cooperate to ensure security
and quality of supply, increase flexibility, investments of infrastructure and better utilization
of Distributed Energy Resources (DER)s [6].
Distribution System Operators (DSOs): DSOs own, maintain and manage low and
medium-voltage distribution networks, which means grid infrastructure below 100kV. DSOs
are connected to the end consumers and producers with limited production capacity [5].
They are also responsible for integrating DERs into the grid, more specifically distributed
generation, such as small-scale behind-the-meter batteries, and controllable loads, such
as smart EV chargers, heat pumps, or demand response assets [6]. They are crucial for
integrating EV charging infrastructure into the grid. DSOs must assess and upgrade their
networks to support the increased load from EVs and work closely with TSOs to ensure
grid resilience.
Charge Point Operators (CPOs): CPOs are at the forefront of the EV charging ecosys-
tem. They own, operate and maintain charging infrastructure, both their hardware and
their software components. They are responsible for ensuring the operation and expan-
sion of their charging network. They also focus on providing a positive charging experi-
ence to the customers and different ways of payment alternatives adjusting to the cus-
tomer preferences. They often collaborate with EMSPs to provide value-added services
to the users [7]. Nowadays, CPOs are also interested in participating in grid services
provision as part of their business strategies [5].
E-Mobility Service Providers (EMSPs): While CPOs’ primary role is the back-end oper-
ation, EMSPs offer a range of front-end operations solutions to the CPOs based on their
needs to increase value-added to their charging service and provide better customer sat-
isfaction [7]. These services can include apps, where public stations can be located and
reserved, billing systems, such as subscriptions and payment systems, roaming opportu-
nities and load management tools [5].
EV Users: At the heart of the EV charging ecosystem are the EV users themselves.
They rely on the infrastructure and services provided by CPOs and EMSPs to charge
their vehicles. EV users aim to maximize their utility when charging, having a charging
service for a fair price. Every user has different needs and CPOs try as much as possible
to capture needs and add to their portfolio to increase their preference with respect to
their competitors. EV charging behaviors in terms of location of charging, the quantity
of energy required, charge duration, the preferred times of charging, and their preferred
mode of charging impact greatly the overall EV charging ecosystem [8].

Pricing Structures
CPOs offer several pricing structures in their portfolios and business strategies intending

Figure 3.6: Most common pricing schemes adopted by CPOs in Europe. Author’s own

It is essential to recognize that the pricing structures for EV charging are strongly linked to
the charging stations’ power capacity. The review into Europe’s most predominant CPOs,
has shown that there exist pricing gaps between different power capacities. Charging
prices can, in reality, be expressed in euros per kilowatt (€/kW) per kilowatt-hour (kWh).
This reflects the direct relationship between power capacity and charging prices. The
price for EV charging can differ significantly between stations offering 22 kW or 50 kW.
Energy-based pricing:
This pricing structure, denominated in €/kWh, is the most commonly utilized model by
CPOs in Europe. Energy-based prices are based on the amount of power withdrawn by
the EV, and can either be flat or real-time. A flat price can be set by the CPO based on
the power level of the chargers or type of customer subscription. The price can also be
set to follow the electricity prices meaning that they fluctuate depending on the time of
day and the demand during peak hours.

Time-based pricing:
Time-based pricing is another strategy for EV charging, where the cost is solely deter-
mined by the amount of time the EV is connected to the charging station. This pricing
model is rarely found in CPOs, however, observed a slightly predominance in countries
such as France.
Fixed rate/subscription pricing:
CPOs often offer subscription plans, where for a fixed monthly fee, EV owners can either
enjoy unlimited charging or benefit from reduced charging costs across different pricing
models like energy, time, or session-based schemes. This approach provides the user
with flexibility in line with their charging needs, as well as protecting the user from sudden
high charging prices during peak hours. Additionally, the use of charging tags, typically in-
cluded in subscription plans, simplifies the process by allowing users to activate charging
at designated points, enhancing convenience and offering competitive rates compared to
one-time-payment services.
Session-based pricing:
The session-based pricing structure essentially charges users based on the number of
charging sessions they undertake. Each session is billed independently, providing a clear
and straightforward billing approach for the customers. It is a very uncommon trend in the
CPOs but it is still present in bigger cities in countries such as France.
3.4.2
One-time-payment vs. Subscription-payment
Energy-based is the most commonly used scheme for the CPOs in Europe and therefore,
it will be the only scheme this study will focus on. As can be seen from Figure 3.7, the
most common EV payment modalities are either a one-time payment or a subscription
payment.
Figure 3.7: Final price components. Author’s own elaboration.
Choosing between one-time payments and subscription-based payments for EV charging
depends on user needs and driving habits. One-time payments offer flexibility for irreg-
ular users, allowing charging as needed through various methods like Apps, contactless
options, or QR codes. Subscription payments provide frequent EV users with predictable
costs and can be more economically favourable for customers with high charging needs.
They merge a fixed monthly fee with constant energy rates. This protects users from price
fluctuations. However, this convenience may come at a higher price rate. Subscriptions
might include additional benefits, such as reserved charging spots or off-peak discounts.
Ultimately, the choice hinges on personal usage patterns: occasional drivers may benefit
from the pay-per-use model, whereas regular commuters might find subscriptions eco-
nomically more beneficial.

ergy market, specifically in the Day-Ahead (DA) electricity market. Here, large quantities
of energy are traded within bidding zones, and the market operates purely on a finan-
cial basis, meaning energy quantities are bought and sold at specific prices. The hourly
price of electricity in this fully competitive market is determined by the merit order supply
and demand curves [18]. Producers’ capacity offers per unit of cost are organized from
the lowest to the highest, while demand bids, reflecting the price consumers are willing to
pay, are arranged from highest to lowest. The intersection of these curves establishes the
market-clearing price, as illustrated in Figure 3.10. Noteworthy is the clear impact of the
rapid growth of renewable energy, leading to the phasing out of conventional generation
units and subsequently lowering the market-clearing price. It is important to recognize that
while the DA market sets the stage one day before the energy delivery day, other mar-
kets such as the Intra-Day (ID) and balancing markets operate closer to the actual delivery
time, contributing to the overall efficiency and adaptability of the electricity market.
Figure 3.10: European Wholesale Electricity Market. Author’s own elaboration.
Day-Ahead (DA) Market
The DA market is a forward market where electricity is bought and sold for delivery on the
following day. It allows market participants, such as generators and retailers, to secure
or sell electricity in advance, providing a degree of certainty in managing their supply and
demand. Market participants submit bids and offers based on their anticipated electric-
ity needs or generation capabilities. The market clearing price is then determined, and
transactions are settled [18].
Intra-Day (ID) Market
The ID market operates closer to real-time, allowing market participants to adjust their
electricity positions on the day of delivery. It facilitates changes in supply and demand,
providing flexibility for market players to readjust their positions based on closer to real-
time conditions. Participants can buy or offer quantities of electricity in this market gen-
erally at a higher price in case they have excess or lack power based on DA agreements
[18]. This market enhances the overall system flexibility by accommodating unforeseen
changes from generation and demand sides.
Balancing Market
The balancing market is designed to address short-term imbalances between electric-
ity generation and consumption. It ensures grid’s stability and reliability by addressing

Static Time-Of-Use (ToU):
The Time-Of-Use (TOU) tariff rates under this scheme are fixed for every hour, meaning
that the network operators will charge their customers at a fixed energy-based rate hourly.
In case of an anticipated peak period, this tariff design will react triggering peak pricing
rates, such as CPP and CPR. The demand is then informed 1-2 days prior the activation
of such mechanisms [21]. However, one can argue that this scheme lacks real-time re-
sponsiveness as it cannot be readjusted to sudden peak demand periods or a large share
of renewable production. This is why it is denominated as a static tariff.
Dynamic Time-Of-Use (ToU):
On the contrary, the dynamic TOU tariff rates vary along the day. This rate follows the
state of the grid to a better extent than the static tariff, meaning that during peak hours,
the rate will be significantly higher than in off-peak hours [16]. This scheme has predeter-
mined peak and off-peak hours to encourage customers to decrease their consumption
during peak hours, shifting their consumption to off-peak hours. In Denmark, this was
implemented with the new Danish Tariff Model 3.0 [22]. This tariff design also tries to
capture the seasonal variations of peak load hours for the winter and summer.
Real-Time Pricing (RTP):
Unlike TOU tariffs, Real-Time Pricing (RTP) tariffs can vary in their rates every hour or
even more frequently [16]. This can change depending on the DA market prices, strongly
dependent weather forecast, grid congestion, and peak demand hours. In other cases,
less often seen, RTP tariffs can also be based on real-time parameters such as carbon
intensity, encouraging EV users to adapt their charging behavior.
Critical Peak Pricing (CPP) and Critical Peak Rebates (CPR):
These two schemes are often combined with the static TOU tariff [16]. Critical Peak Pricing
(CPP) describes the higher prices set for critical hours during a day. By critical hours, it
is often referred to as the hours when there is a high energy price or high demand for
network capacity. The scheme encourages flexible demand to shift their load at those
hours, hence helping the system. On the other hand, Critical Peak Rebates (CPR) instead
of increasing the prices, this scheme apply a discount to customers who can reduce their
load at those certain hours.

4.3
EV Charging Modalities
4.3.1
Home Charging
Dominating the charging landscape, home charging is the preferred method for approxi-
mately 88% of EV users, as outlined by [33]. It allows for overnight charging, taking advan-
tage of lower electricity rates and off-peak hours, thereby providing both convenience and
cost efficiency [34]. Most home charging stations are Level 1, offering a slower charging
speed. A notable advantage of home charging is its compatibility with renewable energy
sources, such as Photovoltaic (PV) cells. This integration supports the concept of EV
owners as prosumers, who not only consume but also produce energy, further enhancing
the sustainability of EVs [35].
4.3.2
Businesses Charging
Accounting for 8.8% of EV charging, business solutions are increasingly adopted by com-
panies aiming to incentivize sustainable practices to their employees and customers [33].
These stations, typically Level 2 or 3, are located at workplaces and commercial points,
accessible through a charging tag or alike [36]. Despite their semi-public nature, they
often offer exclusive access to company customers and/or employees.
Fleet Charging Solutions
Customized for business use, fleet charging solutions are offered to companies operating
multiple EVs, such as delivery companies, taxi services, car-sharing companies and pub-
lic transportation. These solutions blend semi-fast and fast-charging infrastructure. They
are used during both day and night times depending on the business. If not managed
properly, EV fleets can cause significant strain on the grid system [37].
4.3.3
Public Network
Public charging networks, vital for EV adoption, represent 3.2% of EV charging prac-
tices. Fast charging, especially along highways, plays a significant role, with around half
of the sessions at these stations, facilitating convenient long-distance travels [33]. The
expansion of these networks, featuring Level 2 and 3 chargers, is critical in supporting the
growing EV market [36].

4.4
Smart Charging and Grid Services Provision
Smart charging represents an advanced method of charging EVs that goes beyond rapid
and immediate replenishment of an EV’s battery but focuses on optimizing the charging
process itself [10]. This approach takes into account the flexible nature of EV charging
demand and the increasing variability of renewable energy sources due to their integration
into the energy mix. It is crucial to note the strong dependency between smart charging
and electricity prices and grid tariffs [10]. They serve as crucial signals that reflect the
system’s current state, enabling smart charging strategies to provide efficient charging
solutions for EV users while simultaneously offering benefits to the electrical grid [38]. Key
to the successful implementation of smart charging is the enhancement of communication
pathways between the EV, charging stations, and the grid, urging the deployment of more
complex control and management systems.
4.4.1
Bidirectional Charging
Bidirectional charging encompasses both the charging and discharging capabilities of
EVs, positioning them as dynamic energy storage systems from a grid’s perspective
[38]. This method leverages Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) technology, which transforms EVs
into portable energy storage units. V2G facilitates the transfer of surplus electricity from
EVs back to the power grid, enhancing grid stability and alleviating peak demands [39].
Essential to this concept are bidirectional chargers and sophisticated vehicle-to-grid com-
munication interfaces, which together allow EV owners to monetize the excess energy
their vehicles supply to the grid during periods of high demand. This not only offers a
potential revenue source for EV owners but also supports the provision of power to micro-
grids or aggregators/energy communities. By enabling such interactions, V2G charging
creates a strong relationship between EV users and the electrical grid, promoting the con-
cept of consumers as producers, or ’prosumers’ [35]. However, the widespread adoption
of V2G technology faces significant challenges, primarily due to the large investments
required in new infrastructure and major updates on the existing electrical grid [39]. Its
implementation currently faces technological and market barriers. The main obstacle is
the large expenditure to accommodate the bidirectional flow of energy infrastructure in
the grid and the charging infrastructure. Thus, the commercialization of this technology
presents complexity.
4.4.2
Dynamic Load Management (DLM) Implementation
The efficacy of smart charging is inherently linked to the intelligent management and
strategic utilization of the charging infrastructure [38]. DLM systems provide real-time
monitoring and control in charging infrastructure operations and facilitate the even distri-
bution of the available power within a station. This will prevent the charging infrastructure
from potentially being damaged and avoid undesired operational ranges. Furthermore,
such management systems contribute significantly to the grid’s stability by mitigating over-
loading and congestion issues. The application of this management system also ensures
the most efficient charging possible for the vehicles connected, thereby enhancing both
the grid and the users [14].
The deployment of DLM strategies is particularly pertinent to chargers of higher capacities,
specifically those at charging levels 2 and 3. It is crucial to acknowledge that semi-public
and public charging stations frequently incorporate multiple chargers, thereby featuring
the relevance of DLM principles in such contexts. DLM adoption is most relevant within
the AC and DC charging infrastructure networks. Although implementing DLM strategies
at a few charging stations might appear to have little impact on the overall infrastructure,
the significance of these strategies greatly increases as they are applied to larger numbers
of stations.

Ancillary services are essential for supporting the operational pillars of the grid, which in-
clude (i) the adequacy and security of supply, (ii) power system stability, and (iii) power
system resilience. These services, originally designed for a centralized grid, face chal-
lenges in adapting to the increasing prevalence of decentralized generation and flexible
demand [41]. The evolution of ancillary services to accommodate the needs of TSOs is
a pressing issue that CPOs are actively exploring. In Europe, the activation of ancillary
services varies significantly across different synchronous grid areas [42]. As illustrated
in Figure 4.3, these grid areas, each with its designated ancillary services, demonstrate

part of both the Continental European and Nordic synchronous areas, corresponding to
market bidding zones DK1 and DK2 respectively. This strategic positioning offers service
providers in Denmark, a broader spectrum of service provision opportunities.

1 MW, whereas for DK2, it is significantly lower at 0.1-0.3 MW. Pricing models for these
services range from Pay as Bid (PaB) to Uniform Pricing (UP), with remuneration struc-
tured either as reserve (R) payments, activation (A) payments, or a combination of both.
Reservation auctions for these services can also differ, varying from month-ahead (M-1)
to day-ahead (D-1) schedules [44]. In the special case of EVs, participation is currently
not possible for all ancillary services mostly due to market and technology barriers. Ad-
ditionally, minimum bid size, reaction time or duration time requirements can become a
challenge for the provision of services from flexible demands. However, EVs participation
is on a commercial stage and currently more applied for ancillary services: FCRs in DK1
and FCRs-N and FCRs-D for DK2 [42]. This will be discussed further in Chapter 7.
4.4.4
Provision of Flexibility Services on Distribution Grids
While ancillary services for TSOs have more of a system-wide perspective and clear
framework and market structure, flexibility on the DSO operational level considers a more
local view, however, remains deficient in a clear framework and implementation of mar-
kets. EV smart charging can provide a flexible service to the distribution grid by adjusting
their consumption on their chargers. This can be implemented in different ways, such
as (i) scheduled flexibility, where CPOs have previous knowledge of how much support
they will provide, (ii) natural flexibility or so-called demand response, where flexibility is
provided without any agreement beforehand, and (iii) conditional flexibility, where, grid
operators can request for flexibly in case of need [41].
In [41], the exploration of all possible services for flexibility are explored, and the most
suitable flexibility services, for this thesis work, have been chosen and described in the
following Table 4.2. This provides an overview of the most relevant services that EVs,
implementing smart charging, can participate in. These services, depending on their pur-
pose, are clustered in different categories which reflect the key missions of distribution grid
operators. Congestion management services, employed by grid operators to maintain
optimal operational loading on their components such as transformers and medium/high
voltage lines. Grid stability services cover the stability, adequacy and security of supply
of the system outside of the electricity markets mechanisms. Voltage regulation services
are used to maintain voltage stability and avoid short-circuit faults. Lastly, power quality
services are aimed to improve supply quality and reduce grid losses [41].
Most of the flexibility services found in the literature support DSO operations, but some
of them also support TSO operations. The services presented can be further categorized
into the three flexibility service approaches:
• (i) Scheduled flexibility: Services 2 and 7.

• (iii) Conditional flexibility: Services 3, 4, 5, 8, 9 and 10.

4.5
Final Remarks
This chapter reveals the wide range of opportunities for EVs to be part of ancillary and
flexibility services provision. It is clear that EVs can make a difference, regardless of their
usage or scale. Among the various charging modalities discussed on the chapter, home
charging is the predominant choice over business and public charging. The integration
of EVs with PVs could transform regular users into ’prosumers’—both consuming and
producing energy. This is based on the idea of using EV batteries as an alternative en-
ergy storage solution. However, this application requires V2G technologies, which are
currently pricy and demand significant upgrades to the existing grid infrastructure. Thus,
the widespread adoption of V2G infrastructure poses considerable challenges for a large-
scale application.
On a broader scale, business and public charging stations offer notable advantages to
the users, providing flexibility and faster charging. The concept of CPOs providing grid
services is particularly relevant for these stations because of their semi-public or public
nature and their large installed power capacities. It is also crucial to highlight that grid
service provision goes together with the implementation of smart charging technologies.
Smart charging presents an innovative approach for CPOs to enhance a better charging
service and achieve operational efficiency. Energy management tools, like DLM, facilitate
a control system designed to optimize charging power across stations without exceeding
any capacity limits or causing overloads on the grid. This tool also enables quicker adjust-
ments and control over charging power at the stations, ideal for grid services provision.
Additionally, the chapter delves into the potential for EV charging to participate in the an-
cillary services market, focusing on frequency regulation for the transmission grid and
flexibility services for the distribution grid. In the ancillary services field, providing fre-
quency regulation services is competitive and market-driven. The operational charac-
teristics of EVs make them suitable to participate mostly in FCRs services in DK1 and
both FCRs-D and FCRs-N services in DK2. However, the primary limitations for EV par-
ticipation in other service markets lie in the specific requirements for each service, as
well as, economic feasibility and technological developments. On the contrary, flexibil-
ity services currently exist outside a market-based implementation. Yet, both consumers
and producers connected to the distribution grid can offer services related to congestion
management, grid stability, voltage regulation, and power quality. These services can be
categorized into scheduled, natural and conditional flexibility services. Natural flexibility
is encouraged to be maximized, as scheduled and conditional flexibility is more efficiently
employed within a market framework, which remains to be fully established.
In summary, this review highlights the existing capabilities of EVs to support grid ser-
vices and underscores the significant path ahead to enhance EVs’ contribution to the
grid. The role of CPOs has been identified as crucial being the direct link between EVs
and aggregators/energy community formations for grid service provision to DSOs and
TSOs. It becomes evident that stronger collaboration among relevant stakeholders in the
EV charging ecosystem is needed. The implementation of smart charging applications,
the incorporation of EV charging into aggregators and energy communities, and the de-
velopment of flexibility services within a clear market-driven framework, will broaden the
pathway for EV charging to become a key player in grid regulation services.

5.1
Chapter Introduction
This chapter consists of a simple optimization model formulation aiming to optimize EV
charging station operations.
Building on the literature reviewed in previous chapters,
Chapters 3 and 4, the optimization problem will focus on a CPO-level context where part
of its business model and value proposition includes (i) offering dynamic charging prices
schemes to their customers, (ii) providing smart charging solutions in their chargers, and
(iii) analyzing the potential of flexibility services provision to the DSO. This chapter aims
to collect the most relevant findings from the literature and implement them into a mathe-
matical model which will be further tested in the following chapter.
5.2
Assumptions
The model is developed under several assumptions to ensure a simple and clear appli-
cation while following the project scope. Here are the assumptions made for the different
relevant stakeholders considered in this study:
Time-frame and perspective: The model is designed to optimize real-time operations
from a CPO perspective on an hourly basis throughout the day.
EV users: Users are considered flexible and not in urgent need of charging, therefore the
State-Of-Charge (SOC) of all vehicles is assumed to be 50%. The user’s utility will not
depend on the vehicle’s SOC but rather on the power rate at which it is being charged.
This means they are willing to accept instances where vehicles may not be charged at full
capacity during the charging session.
CPOs Infrastructure: It is assumed that all chargers considered to operate within the
same distribution network, are equipped to perform smart charging, and can handle sud-
den power shifts. The hourly power demand for each charger is assumed to be known
and remains constant at every hour. Information regarding the maximum power capacity
of each charger and electrical connection setup is also assumed to be known.
CPOs Pricing: The CPO’s charging prices to their customers are assumed to be following
a dynamic behavior. The CPO’s cost for electricity, which includes spot prices, grid tariffs,
and payable taxes, is also considered to be known for the model.
Distribution Grid Status: The distribution grid status, specific hours throughout the day
where grid overloading becomes an issue for the DSO, is presumed to be given as input.
This input serves as a flexibility signal that is price-driven. It is important to stress that the
price paid for flexibility services is assumed to be known and it will always be paid at a
significantly high rate. In previous similar works [50], transformer capacities information
is considered as a signal for requesting flexibility. Other studies review a direct flexibility
request from the DSO considering a penalty for any mismatch on the request [51] and
[52].
These assumptions are critical for the development of the model to simulate the operations
of EV charging stations and their interaction with the electrical grid in the most accurate
way possible. The optimization model will be developed in Python using the Gurobi solver
package.

7.2
Pricing Mechanisms Adopted by CPOs
In the investigation of the EV charging ecosystem, it becomes clear that it is a network in
current development and improvement characterized by the complex interdependencies
among the various stakeholders involved such as EMSPs, DSOs and TSOs. This network
is heavily influenced by the level of adoption of such technologies within every country. In
the case of the Nordic countries, policies and initiatives have ensured a faster adoption of
EVs than in other countries, intrinsically related to their economic wealth. The rapid growth
of the charging ecosystem can be attributed to the popularity of EVs in the market, marking
it as a profitable business for CPOs. Charging prices within the European landscape
predominantly comply with an energy-based pricing model, aligning the charging cost
with the corresponding consumption. This pricing model not only offers more simplicity
and transparency to the users but also complies more effectively with the energy prices,
grid tariffs, and taxes that are on an energy basis. The CPOs offer different payment
modalities to their users, the two most popular ones being one-time, or on-the-go payment,
and subscription formats. Figure 7.1 and 7.2 illustrate the different modalities of payment
an European CPO offers to their customers and their associated cost of electricity for both
AC and DC stations.

7.3
Smart Charging Strategies & Grid Regulation Provision
CPOs and EMPs have increasingly directed their focus towards the provision of grid reg-
ulation services as an essential value proposition. In addition to their core value proposi-
tions of clean transport and charging services, CPOs are the key communication pathway
for enabling EVs to provide flexibility to the grid. Certain charging infrastructure and sys-
tems must be implemented before considering any grid service provision from EVs.
The ’smart charging’ term implies an upgrade on charging infrastructure to provide higher
real-time feedback and coordination, as well as rapid and smart control mechanisms for
EV charging. This has been already implemented by the incorporation of smart meters
in connection points and the adoption of dynamic TOU tariffs, but also by more efficient
control management systems such as DLM. DLM systems allow a more efficient way to
monitor, operate, and control charging infrastructure remotely, in addition to becoming
more cost-efficient in regard to electricity costs. The controllability features of such tech-
nology provide an even distribution of the available power within a station to maximize
the charging power for the EVs without exceeding undesired limits. Synergies between
DLM implementation and dynamic TOU tariff adoption can be identified. The integration
of both can result in a more responsive and flexible participation of EVs. However, the
crucial gap between them lies in the customer engagement. The effectiveness of such
incentives strongly depends on whether the users are encouraged enough to shift their
consumption to off-peak hours, and being compensated with low charging prices.
This study also delved into the exploration of EV participation in grid regulation services,
assessing their current status and future opportunities. The study focused on ancillary
and flexibility services (covered in Chapter 4). For ancillary services, the main focus of
the exploration was given to the frequency regulation services for Denmark. The geo-
graphic location of Denmark, being part of both European and Nordic synchronous grid
areas, seems to be beneficial to foster EV participation. Each grid area has a different
way of tackling imbalances. The Nordic grid offers two more additional services than the
continental European. In the investigation of the frequency regulation services available
in Denmark, it is found that inertia response services are not commercially available for
EVs, mainly because they require an extremely fast response of 1-second [41]. Nonethe-
less, flexible consumption, including EVs, will play a more significant role in considerably
fast services such as FFRs services in DK2 [64]. Additionally, the provision of aFRRs
and mFRRs services from EVs has been investigated by different pilot projects but it is
not economically feasible for EVs as of now. The reason primarily lies in the long duration
time support those services require. In the current stage of development, most commonly
EVs provide services for FCRs services. Those services vary in requirements according
to the synchronous area. For the case of bidding zone DK1, FCRs services require a
minimum bid of 1 MW, while for bidding zone DK2, FCRs-N and -D require a minimum
bid of between 0.1-0.3 MW. The provision of FCRs services becomes more complex in
DK1 than in DK2, as larger bids are required. However, smart sharing technologies will
allow EVs be easily integrated into aggregators/energy communities where they can offer
a higher available capacity in the market. As the adoption of EVs rapidly advances along
with their technologies, EVs will most likely be able to be market participants in more
ancillary services markets in the future.
On the other hand, EVs have a larger range of opportunities for flexibility services than
ancillary services (see Table 4.2). It was identified that flexibility can be provided for

congestion management, grid stability, voltage regulation, and power quality purposes.
Within those services, different ways of requests can be observed: scheduled, natural,
and conditional flexibility. The main challenge for flexibility provision is the lack of a clear
market-based framework where players can offer their services to the DSO. The literature
reveals that natural flexibility has no need for a market framework and therefore the uti-
lization of those services should be maximized [41]. This does not imply that scheduled
and conditional flexibility services are considered of less importance. But generally, in
those types of services, there will be more benefits through a market mechanism imple-
mentation. This is because it will be a competitive market where the lowest price offer will
predominate, significantly reducing grid operators’ operational costs.
It should be noted that natural flexibility services, relevant for the EV charging ecosystem,
mainly consist of reactions on capacity features stipulated in the grid codes and demand
response mechanisms, including the adoption of dynamic tariffs. Thus, EV users play a
vital role in flexibility provision due to the large influence that their charging behavior has
on the overall system’s stability.
7.4
Model Limitations
The optimization model implemented in Chapter 5 aims to apply the findings in the liter-
ature in a real-time view of CPO operations providing flexibility. It focused on assessing
two different problems: the maximization of social welfare and the maximization of CPO
profit. Although the optimization model implemented provides relevant outputs on flexi-
bility provision from EV charging, some limitations can be identified as described below.
• Input data accessibility: The input data given by the CPO solely consisted of the
average power consumption recorded in some of the CPO’s chargers from their
network. However, this data does not capture some important aspects of the users.
A more extensive data collection could have included the amount of EVs visiting
the charging stations, the duration of their charging session, and the initial SOC at
the start of the charging session. This could have impacted considerably how the
optimization model was formulated, providing a more accurate representation of the
energy demand and utility of the individual user.
• Utility function assumptions: The utility function of both AC and DC users has
been modeled by a quadratic function, concave at the maximum charging power
of the charger. The calibration of α and ω parameters have been calibrated so the
user’s utility increases as charging power increases, reaching its maximum point on
the maximum charging power possible. However, it can be argued from the litera-
ture that as the charging power increases, there exists a point where utility remains
equal or decreases, though not necessarily at the maximum point of charging. A bet-
ter modeling of the user utility would have provided more realistic outcomes. Other
applications for utility functions, such as exponential trends, were found in the lit-
erature but some implementations of such functions were not compatible with the
optimization solver, Gurobi in Python.
• Flexibility provision assumptions: The flexibility provision assumed by the model
has been implemented by defining a total consumption threshold. For hours where
the DSO’s hourly settled consumption was higher than the set threshold, the model
would assume flexibility was most likely needed by the system. It should be men-
tioned that the hourly settled consumption covers a big picture of the DSO dynamics.
However, there might be locations, within this DSO’s operational range, that present
more overloading and congestion than others. There was no easily accessible data
62
Dynamic load management and pricing of electric vehicle charging point operators
==PAGE:75==
for the demand of this specific DSO in the location considered, like for instance,
transformer overloading historical data.
• Time-frame and approach: The model aims to portray the real-time operations
of the CPO. The model lacks the implementation of a forecast-driven approach as
charging patterns can vary depending on various factors such as seasonal variations
in prices, charging demand, and flexibility needs. The Stochastic approach might
represent this problem more realistically as randomness is present in the context of
this study.

7.6
Social Acceptance
The integration of EV users into the grid highly depends on social acceptance. The under-
standing of user needs is the first step when trying to incentivize charging behavior shift-
ing. Some key insights into EV users’ needs and behavior have been obtained through
the conduction of a semi-structured interview with some EV users in Denmark. With the
input of the users interviewed and the main findings of this thesis, some general remarks
can be made in regard to user behavior and social acceptance.
Based on the interviews conducted, the users expressed that EVs are more seen on the
roads than before. Ten years ago, having an EV was considered not only an alternative
but a luxury. Nowadays, EVs have become a more attractive and affordable high-tech
transport solution. Additionally, the users indicate overall satisfaction with charging ser-
vices provided by CPOs. However, some aspects have room for improvement. The users
mentioned that it has become easier to find an available charger on a station with easy
payment methods and Apps where information and/or errors on the session are displayed.
On the other hand, the users have experienced some downsides when charging their EVs.
The biggest downside is the fact that charging stations are frequently installed in big cities
but when travelling long distances, it becomes more challenging to find an available sta-
tion. The users also express an overall dissatisfaction with the unappealing environment
where charging stations are located. This makes the users opt for more fast chargers if
possible, aiming to spend the least amount of time on the station. The users were also
asked which aspects are considered when looking for a charging station. The availability
of the charging station, the distance from the station and the speed of charging provided
were the highlighted parameters mentioned. They expressed that they rarely consider the
price for charging when the need to charge is urgent. They were also asked if they would
switch the charging to cheaper hours, assuming the prices for charging were significantly
higher on peak hours. The users stated that Apps and platforms only display the price
for charging for the current hour. There is a lack of an overview of the hourly charging
prices for the current day and perhaps the following day. If they had a better overview of
the prices, they would identify the most cost-effective hour that suits their routine.
Some interesting links can be made between the interviews’ outcomes and some of the
thesis findings. First, it was revealed that demand shifting is fairly acceptable among EV
users. However, there is a lack of understanding from users on how impactful uncontrol-
lable charging can be to the system. Secondly, it can be suggested from the interviews,
that more clarity and transparency on the hourly charging prices is missing from the CPO
side. It is also observed from the interviews that some of the users do not have sufficient
knowledge of DSO dynamic tariffs and their variation throughout the day. Moreover, it
is also evidenced that monetary remuneration for users’ flexibility in their consumption

In conclusion, the user’s behavior is the main driver for successfully implementing flex-
ibility provision to the grid from EVs. Economical remuneration seems to be a way to
encourage users to change their charging behavior. Nevertheless, a lack of information
is still present among the users causing less involvement from them. The effectiveness of
dynamic charging prices and dynamic grid tariffs is highly linked to the knowledge and un-

a clearer, more transparent and informative approach, CPOs can significantly reduce the
current gap between the users and the grid. This can lead to users having a more signifi-
cant impact on preventing grid overloads and congestion. It becomes evident that a high
rate of flexible demand adoption among users is rather preferred over a high infrastructure
capacity expansion rate.

8.2
Concluding Remarks
- What synergies exist within the CPOs and the rest of the EV charging environment? and
what are the current strategies employed by CPOs to achieve economical and operational
efficacy?
The role of the CPO plays an essential role in the EV charging ecosystem. The CPO is not
only responsible for providing EV charging services to users, it is the one maintaining and
expanding the network from large cities to remote areas. CPOs work hand in hand with
grid operators, mostly DSOs, to ensure grid access and safe operational levels from their
charging infrastructure. In the competitive EV charging services landscape, the CPOs re-
quire business models, value propositions and pricing structures that capture and adapt
to the preferences and needs of the users while maintaining financial feasibility. CPOs
offer diverse products such as home, business and public charging solutions with charg-
ing levels ranging from 3.7 to 350kW depending on the users’ needs. Moreover, CPOs,
together with EMSPs, aim to provide an accessible and appealing charging experience
for the users via virtual platforms or Apps. These user interfaces facilitate accessibility to
the infrastructure, displaying real-time status on charging, among other features. CPOs
adopt on their prices an energy-based structure for their most common payment modali-
ties such as subscription plans and one-time payments. This is due to the preference for
a volumetric tariff on the users, but also to align charging prices with the cost of energy
which is also energy-based. EV charging prices have evolved to dynamic rates leaving
behind the traditional flat rates. Additionally, CPOs are currently working on better sustain-
able EV charging practices. For example, the implementation of dynamic charging rates,
from CPOs and dynamic TOU tariffs, from DSOs, aim to encourage users to shift their
demand to off-peak hours. Other incentives are also being investigated from CPOs and
EMSPs such as smart charging solutions on their portfolio to ensure more cost-efficient
operations, satisfactory user charging experiences, and favourable grid interactions.
- How can DLM strategies be effectively integrated into charging stations to ensure that
charging operations do not impose excessive loads on the distribution grid?
From this thesis, it is suggested that smart charging implementation into the existing and
future charging infrastructure is of crucial importance. Smart charging technologies are a
great cost-effective alternative to ensure EVs’ full integration into the grid. Smart charging
applications such as DLM, allow CPOs to improve their overall operations. DLM mech-
anisms enable CPOs to monitor, operate and remotely control charging in their assets
more effectively. Through the implementation of DLM, the charging power is evenly dis-
tributed across the charging station, leading to optimized overall operations and users’
70
Dynamic load management and pricing of electric vehicle charging point operators
==PAGE:83==
experience satisfaction. The control features of this technology prevent the charging sta-
tions from exceeding operational limits. This enables EV charging to become a solution
rather than a cause for grid congestion and overloading. While a few EV chargers cannot
provide a significant service to the grid, smart EVs charging can be integrated into ag-
gregators/energy communities where several assets are grouped offering flexibility to the
system. CPOs and EMSPs play an important role as they are the communication path
between the users and the rest of EV charging ecosystem. Without further development
on this matter from their side, EVs will continue being a source of congestion and overload
during peak hours for the grid.
- What are the operational and economic opportunities and challenges associated with
the adoption of DLM mechanisms for the CPOs? And what is the impact of this imple-
mentation on the other stakeholders?
Smart charging adoption for CPOs rather than an additional feature, is a necessary im-
plementation for EVs grid integration. In Europe, several CPOs have implemented smart
charging by DLM applications and demand-response mechanisms in their infrastructure
and operations. The widespread adoption brings opportunities but also poses challenges
for all stakeholders in the EV charging ecosystem. Smart charging applications offer a
more dynamic charging service to the user and a more cost-efficient operation for the
CPO. Additionally, this technology provides real-time management and control in the
charging infrastructure, opening the opportunity for EVs, with their flexible nature, to con-
tribute to aggregators/energy communities. Incorporating so, EVs will be able to provide
significant services for grid stability to the TSOs and flexibility to the DSOs. However, the
widespread adoption of smart charging faces several challenges:
• Economic Barriers: The incorporation of smart charging in CPO and EMSP oper-
ations implies high investments in both time and resources.
• Technology and Market Barriers: It should also be highlighted that smart EV
charging is not technologically mature enough to exploit the full potential in trans-
mission (TSO) and distribution (DSO) grid services. Additionally, a market-based
structure for flexibility provision lacks support, development and implementation.
• Social Barriers: The EV users become the main players in this entire problem due
to the great impact caused by their charging behavior. Flexibility by smart charging
has a real impact only if users shift their charging behavior.
In the future, EV market is expected to expand, therefore enabling smart charging tech-
nologies to overcome the various economic, technology, market, and social barriers present
in today’s energy system.

8.3
Future Work
Based on the topics covered in this thesis, several insightful directions for future work can
be considered. Some of those will be mentioned in this section.
• Further research could explore the effects of the implementation of dynamic TOU
tariffs, focusing on future scenarios on a system with full EV integration.
• While this thesis concentrated on semi-public and public EV chargers, considering
home chargers can also bring light to a broader range of opportunities EVs can
provide to the grid.
• This thesis work considers only the investigation of EV’s potential for flexibility pro-
vision. An interesting future research can also analyze the role of the aggregator in
flexibility services considering more flexible assets.
• A more in-depth consideration of the distribution network operations could achieve
more accurate and impactful results on the model optimization. For instance include
loading on transformers, lines and other components where charging stations are
located within the data collection.
• Charging patterns and prices can always vary causing uncertainty on the optimiza-
tion results therefore considering stochastic optimization would become relevant to
tackle the problem with a more probabilistic-based approach.
