Abstract
Knowledge graphs (KGs) encode structured information across domains, providing rich
context for tasks such as relationship prediction, link inference, and knowledge discovery.
However, extracting relevant and interpretable information from largeВӯscale KGs remains
challenging, particularly for large language models (LLMs), which are designed to operate
on natural language rather than structured graph representations.
This thesis presents KВӯPaths, a generalВӯpurpose, modelВӯagnostic retrieval framework that
extracts diverse and meaningful multiВӯhop paths from KGs to support prediction and reaВӯ
soning tasks. KВӯPaths is trainingВӯfree and uses a diversityВӯaware adaptation of YenвҖҷs alВӯ
gorithm to retrieve concise, nonВӯredundant paths between query entities. These retrieved
paths can be used in two ways: they can be assembled into compact, queryВӯspecific subВӯ
graphs suitable for graph neural networks (GNNs), or verbalized into natural language
descriptions that serve as input to LLMs. This approach improves predictive accuracy on
tasks involving unseen or emerging entities, increases interpretability by providing explicit
reasoning traces, and reduces computational cost by restricting inference to informative
graph regions.
While this work is motivated by biomedical applications, such as predicting drugвҖ“drug
interactions and identifying drug repurposing opportunities, KВӯPaths is also applicable to
other domains beyond biomedicineНҫ in this thesis, it is evaluated on citation intent classiВӯ
fication tasks over academic knowledge graphs. Overall, the framework enables efficient
and interpretable knowledge graph retrieval that supports both symbolic reasoning with
LLMs and neural reasoning with GNNs.

Introduction
Extracting actionable insights from large, interconnected datasets remains a fundamental
challenge in many scientific and industrial domains. This is especially true in settings such
as drug development and safety assessment, which have traditionally been timeВӯintensive
and costly, often spanning years and requiring billions of dollars [1, 2]. Recent advances in
computational power and the availability of structured data sources are transforming this
landscape, enabling faster, more costВӯeffective knowledge discovery [3, 4]. For example,
deep learning models trained on structured and semiВӯstructured data can now achieve
high predictive performance in specialized tasks, from drug toxicity to material design [5].
However, the volume and heterogeneity of such datasets pose integration and reasoning
challenges, making it difficult to isolate the most relevant information [6, 7].
Knowledge graphs (KGs) provide a structured representation for integrating diverse relaВӯ
tionships among entities, including compounds, genes, diseases, authors, and research
concepts [8, 9]. Despite their expressive power, largeВӯscale KGs often contain millions
of edges, making it difficult to extract the most relevant subgraphs for a given task [10].
Although only a small portion of the graph is typically needed for a specific prediction
or analysis [11], most models consume the full graph or rely on handcrafted sampling
strategies, which can be computationally expensive or fail to generalize. Graph Neural
Networks (GNNs) have shown strong performance in leveraging KG structure for tasks
like link prediction and classification [12, 13, 4]. However, they often incur high computaВӯ
tional costs on largeВӯscale graphs [14] and critically lack the ability to generalize to unseen
or emerging entities [15], a limitation in many realВӯworld applications beyond biomedicine.
Large language models (LLMs) offer a complementary paradigm: they excel in zeroВӯ
shot reasoning and inductive generalization [16, 17, 18]. Recent works demonstrate that
grounding LLMs in KGВӯderived context improves factual accuracy and reduces halluciВӯ
nations in specialized domains [18, 19, 20, 21]. However, LLMs cannot directly traverse
graph structures, and converting KG information into a format suitable for natural language
inference remains an open challenge [22, 23].
ChainВӯofВӯThought prompting has recently been proposed as a way to improve reasoning
consistency by encouraging models to generate explicit intermediate steps before predictВӯ
ing [24]. While effective in tasks such as arithmetic and question answering, its integration
with structured KG retrieval is still underexplored. Current KG integration methods either
focus on narrow tasks such as question answering or binary classification [25, 26, 27],
or are tightly coupled to GNN architectures and thus hard to extend to LLM settings [28].
Overcoming these limitations is essential for building generalВӯpurpose, scalable systems
for knowledge discovery.
This work introduces an inВӯdepth evaluation of KВӯPaths [29], a modelВӯagnostic retrieval
framework designed to extract highly relevant and interpretable reasoning chains from
large KGs. KВӯPaths retrieves structured, diverse, multiВӯhop paths between entities, supВӯ
porting both LLMВӯbased and GNNВӯbased inference. These paths are particularly useful in
inductive settings, where the goal is to predict unobserved relationships involving new or
sparsely connected nodes. Unlike prior systems, KВӯPaths operates without modelВӯspecific
training: it adapts YenвҖҷs algorithm [30] to prioritize biologically or semantically meaningful
paths while removing redundant ones.

Related work
This chapter reviews prior research across the domains of KGs, GNNs, and LLMs, with a
focus on their applications to drug discovery and academic datasets. The chapter is orgaВӯ
nized into two main parts: biomedical KGs and academic KGs. For each domain, existing
methods for reasoning with GNNs and LLMs are examined, along with their limitations in
inductive settings involving unseen or emerging entities. These insights motivate the deВӯ
sign choices behind KВӯPaths.
2.1
Biomedical KGs
Biomedical KGs have been widely used to model the complex relationships among drugs,
diseases, genes, and other biological entities, enabling dataВӯdriven advances in drug disВӯ
covery [13, 4, 11, 31]. These largeВӯscale KGs are often curated for specialized purposes
such as drugВӯdisease interactions [32, 8], geneВӯdrug interactions [33], and proteinВӯprotein
interactions [34]. This work focuses on drugВӯdisease interactions and drugВӯdrug interacВӯ
tions as they play a critical role in drug discovery, clinical decisionВӯmaking, and patient
safety. A key limitation of biomedical KGs is that they can be incomplete in several ways.
They may lack observed interactions between existing entities (e.g., drugs or diseases)
or fail to capture interactions involving emerging entitiesвҖ”those newly introduced or unВӯ
derrepresented in the KG. The focus is on predicting unobserved interactions among exВӯ
isting and emerging drugs or diseases, as well as interactions involving emerging drugs
or diseases. This setting requires inductive reasoning, where a model must generalize to
unseen nodes [28], as opposed to transductive reasoning, which assumes full knowledge
of the graph at training time. The inductive nature of this challenge reflects realВӯworld
biomedical discovery, where new drugs and diseases are continuously introduced.
2.2
GNNs for drug discovery
GNNs, including variants of graph convolutional networks, have demonstrated strong perВӯ
formance in modeling the structure of biomedical KGs and predicting unobserved interВӯ
actions between entities [28, 31, 35]. These models often leverage external KGs such as
Hetionet [8] to learn richer representations of biomedical entities. However, training on
largeВӯscale KGs is computationally expensive. To address scalability, recent methods like
[31, 11] extract fixedВӯsized subgraphs before passing them to the graph network. While
effective, these approaches are typically designed for transductive settings, where both
nodes involved in a prediction are observed during training. This limits their ability to genВӯ
eralize to emerging entities, a critical need in drug discovery. In this work, we operate in
the inductive setting, aiming to predict interactions involving unseen drugs or diseases.
Our work is closely related to EmerGNN [28], which augments the training network with
Hetionet and uses a flowВӯbased model to extract paths and construct subgraphs for GNN
processing. However, EmerGNN requires a separately trained model and relies on beam
search to generate paths tailored for graph architectures. In contrast, KВӯPaths is trainingВӯ
free, retrieves diverse and biologically meaningful paths with minimal computational overВӯ
head, and supports modelВӯagnostic integration with GNNs and LLMs. This flexibility enВӯ
ables KВӯPaths to support both supervised and zeroВӯshot learning. Furthermore, in chapВӯ
ter 5, when KВӯPaths is integrated into EmerGNN improves efficiency and performance
compared to using the entire KG. KВӯPaths also significantly improves LLM zeroВӯshot perВӯ
formance without any parameter updates.

2.3
LLMs for drug discovery
LLMs have emerged as powerful tools in biomedical research, with applications ranging
from conversational agents for drug discovery [36], and drug repurposing [37], to molecВӯ
ular understanding [38]. A key factor in their success is inВӯcontext learning [39], which
enables LLMs to incorporate new information, such as retrieved examples or structured
facts, at inference time without fineВӯtuning. This ability is particularly valuable for multiВӯhop
reasoning, where models synthesize information across multiple steps or sources, such
as KG paths, to derive accurate conclusions [26]. Recent studies show that LLMs can
reason faithfully over KG paths to answer complex factual queries [26, 25]. However, this
potential remains underexplored in the context of biomedical KGs, particularly in inducВӯ
tive settings, where multiВӯhop reasoning could improve tasks like predicting interactions
involving novel drugs or emerging diseases.
GraphВӯAugmented LLMs:
KВӯPaths builds upon emerging research at the intersection
of retrievalВӯaugmented generation (RAG) and graphВӯbased reasoning [25, 20]. Existing
approaches construct synthetic KGs from unstructured text using LLMs [20, 21] or rely
on generalВӯdomain KGs like Freebase [25] for question answering. In contrast, KВӯPaths
leverages curated biomedical KGs to enable precise, domainВӯspecific inference. MoreВӯ
over, existing systems like RoG [25] and GNNВӯRAG [26] require trained planning/retrieval
modules or exhaustive path enumeration (e.g., KGВӯLLMвҖҷs [27] depthВӯfirst search), which
are computationally expensive and difficult to scale to dense KGs. KВӯPaths circumvents
these limitations by using a trainingВӯfree, heuristic adaptation of YenвҖҷs algorithm, with a
filtering step for efficiency and relational diversity.
Design TradeВӯoffs:
RoG, GNNВӯRAG, and KGВӯLLM represent three distinct paradigms
for pathВӯbased LLM reasoning, but each faces limitations for fullВӯgraph biomedical inВӯ
ference. RoG and GNNВӯRAGвҖҷs retrieval modules operate on queryВӯspecific subgraphs
generated via PageRank [40] and assume answers exist within the local subgraph. This
assumption may break down in inductive settings, where testВӯtime entities may not appear
during training. Additionally, their reliance on preВӯgenerated subgraphs limits scalability
to large biomedical KGs. Similarly, KGВӯLLM is limited to a binary setup, and its exhaustive
path enumeration over the full KG becomes infeasible in highВӯdegree biomedical graphs.
In contrast, KВӯPaths enables scalable, explainable inference by retrieving diverse, bioВӯ
logically meaningful paths, without training or exhaustive search. Therefore, these apВӯ
proaches are analyzed conceptually rather than as direct baselines because they differ
significantly in reasoning scope, scalability, and modeling assumptions.

2.4
Academic KGs
Academic knowledge graphs (KGs) capture structured relationships among entities such
as papers, authors, venues, institutions, and research topics. These graphs enable largeВӯ
scale analysis of scientific activity and are widely used in applications including citation
prediction, expert finding, and discovery of emerging research trends. A prominent reВӯ
source is the Open Academic Graph (OAG) [41, 42].
2.5
GNNs for Academic Discovery
GNNs have been widely applied to academic KGs for tasks such as paper recommenВӯ
dation, author disambiguation, and citation forecasting. These graphs are inherently hetВӯ
erogeneous, encoding diverse relations (e.g., cites, writes, affiliated_with) that are
critical for accurate modeling [42]. Techniques such as metapathВӯbased GNNs and atВӯ
tention mechanisms have demonstrated strong performance [43]. However, challenges
remain in scaling to large datasets, generalizing to newly introduced entities, and producВӯ
ing interpretable outputs. Most existing GNN pipelines assume static graphs or require
complete training data, which limits their effectiveness in settings where publications, auВӯ
thors, or research topics are continuously evolving. KВӯPaths complements these methods
by extracting informative, queryВӯspecific subgraphs and pathВӯbased contexts that can be
directly integrated into GNN architectures, improving inductive generalization and comВӯ
putational efficiency.
2.6
LLMs for Academic Discovery
LLMs have been applied to academic KGs to enable scalable, interpretable reasoning
over academic entities. Systems such as LocalВӯGlobal Graph RAG [44] and RoG [25]
perform multiВӯhop reasoning over citation and collaboration networks.
However, they
typically depend on static heuristics or computationally expensive subgraph extraction
pipelines, which can limit adaptability to dynamic graphs. In contrast, KВӯPaths retrieves
diverse, semantically rich paths without requiring training or preprocessing, making it well
suited to academic KGs where entities and relations evolve over time.
The trainingВӯfree design of KВӯPaths makes it wellВӯsuited for citation intent classification
and other academic graph tasks. Its ability to integrate with both GNNs and LLMs enables
scalable and interpretable knowledge discovery.

3
Approach
This chapter presents the design and components of KВӯPaths. The first section defines
the problem setting. The remainder of the chapter describes the core stages of the frameВӯ
work: graph augmentation, diverse path retrieval, and integration with downstream modВӯ
els (LLMs and GNNs).
3.1
Problem Definition
The task involves predicting unobserved relationships between pairs of entities in a knowlВӯ
edge graph (KG), particularly in inductive settings where entities may be absent during
training. This challenge is common in areas like biomedical discovery and academic
knowledge graph analysis, where reasoning over structured, multiВӯrelational graphs supВӯ
ports tasks such as interaction prediction and citation intent classification.
Formally, a knowledge graph is defined as G = {(u, r, v) | u, v вҲҲE, r вҲҲR}, where u
and v are entities and r is a relation type. Given a query involving entities u and v, the
objective is to predict whether a relation r exists between them. These relations may not
be explicitly observed in the graph but can be inferred through multiВӯhop reasoning across
existing connections.
A computational model П•(G) is used to perform this prediction, leveraging both zeroВӯshot
LLMВӯbased reasoning and supervised GNNВӯbased classification.
3.2
KВӯPaths Framework
3.2.1
Overview of the Framework
KВӯPaths is a trainingВӯfree, modelВӯagnostic framework designed to retrieve diverse and
informative reasoning paths from largeВӯscale KGs. These paths facilitate downstream
prediction tasks by serving as structured input for either LLMs or GNNs.
The framework includes three components:
1. Augmented KG Construction: Combines domainВӯspecific interaction data with a
broader external KG to yield a rich, interconnected structure.
2. Diverse Path Retrieval: Extracts multiple, semantically diverse paths between
query entities using a filteringВӯenhanced variant of YenвҖҷs algorithm.
3. Path Integration Module: Transforms the retrieved paths into formats suitable for
inference (natural language for LLMs or subgraphs for GNNs).
A highВӯlevel schematic is shown in fig. 2.1.
3.2.2
Augmented KG Construction
The augmented knowledge graph G is formed by integrating observed interactions from
the task dataset with a domainВӯspecific external KG (e.g., Hetionet, Open Academic
Graph). This approach increases coverage by incorporating intermediate nodes and adВӯ
ditional relational context.
To support bidirectional traversal and reasoning, inverse relations are added to all edges,
following established practices [28]. The resulting graph enables reasoning over both obВӯ
served and emerging entities and serves as the foundation for all subsequent processing

4.1

This chapter investigates the performance and design choices of the proposed KВӯPaths

by the following research questions:
вҖў RQ1: Can language models accurately perform multiВӯclass classification by reasonВӯ

вҖў RQ2: How do different path selection strategies (e.g., shortest paths vs. diverse

вҖў RQ3: How does using queryВӯspecific subgraphs constructed via the KВӯPaths frameВӯ

the model is encouraged to articulate intermediate reasoning steps before producing a
final answer. This process can help the model better organize and synthesize the inforВӯ
mation contained in the paths.
For example, given a query about whether a compound is indicated for AlzheimerвҖҷs disВӯ
ease, CoT prompting might produce an explicit reasoning chain such as:
вҖңDonepezil binds CYP2D6, which is associated with AlzheimerвҖҷs disease, so
it is likely to have an effect.вҖқ
In contrast, direct KВӯPaths retrieval provides a concise factual statement like:
вҖңDonepezil binds CYP2D6, an enzyme linked to AlzheimerвҖҷs disease progresВӯ
sion.вҖқ
CoT prompting helps the model articulate intermediate steps and can improve interpretabilВӯ
ity by making reasoning explicit. However, it may also introduce speculative or inconsisВӯ
tent connections. In contrast, KВӯPaths provides deterministic, verifiable evidence grounded
in the knowledge graph, supporting more concise and reliable predictions. When predictВӯ
ing whether a compound is indicated for a disease, direct KВӯPaths supplies factual paths
describing binding events, regulatory effects, or structural similarities, which the model
interprets directly. CoT over KВӯPaths further encourages the model to enumerate these
connections step by step, clarifying their relevance to the prediction.
Overall, this comparison highlights complementary strengths. CoT reasoning over KВӯ
Paths can enhance interpretability and sometimes improve accuracy by guiding the model
to connect evidence to conclusions explicitly. Direct KВӯPaths reasoning yields shorter,
more focused outputs while retaining the benefits of structured retrieval.

The effect of the number of retrieved paths (K) and the path selection strategy on Llama
3.1 8BвҖҷs prediction performance is examined using the validation set. The following strateВӯ
gies are considered:
1. Base setting (K = 0), using no retrieved paths.
2. Diverse paths from KВӯPaths (K = 1, 5, 10, 15, 20)Нҫ increasing K leads to longer
paths and more hops.
3. Shortest paths, retrieved without applying diversity filtering.
4. Local neighborhood edges, retrieving 5 direct neighbors per entity.

improve performance. On DDInter, F1Вӯscores increase from 12.66% (K = 0) to 46.73%

Discussion & limitations
6.1
Discussion
KВӯPaths introduces a modelВӯagnostic framework for augmenting machine learning models
with structured, multiВӯhop relational evidence derived from knowledge graphs. Its core
component is a diversityВӯaware adaptation of YenвҖҷs algorithm that retrieves paths that are
both topologically valid and semantically diverse, enabling richer relational context and
improved interpretability.
These retrieved paths serve as explicit reasoning chains for two main applications: enВӯ
hancing zeroВӯshot inference for LLMs and improving supervised learning for GNNs. In
LLMs, KВӯPaths supplies grounded, contextВӯspecific input that helps the model predict
interactions between previously unobserved entity pairs, such as drugвҖ“disease associВӯ
ations or citation links. In GNNs, KВӯPaths generates compact, queryВӯspecific subgraphs
that maintain predictive performance while substantially reducing input size and training
time.
KВӯPaths also achieves strong results on citation intent classification tasks in academic
knowledge graphs. This crossВӯdomain performance demonstrates that structured, multiВӯ
hop retrieval can be a broadly effective strategy for supporting knowledge discovery.
6.2
Limitations
KВӯPaths has the following key limitations:
вҖў Dependence on graph topology: KВӯPaths relies on the existence of informative
and wellВӯconnected paths in the KG. When entities are sparsely connected, the
framework may fail to retrieve meaningful reasoning chains.
вҖў Static retrieval strategy: Path retrieval is performed independently of the downВӯ
stream model. This separation limits opportunities for endВӯtoВӯend optimization or
joint learning between retrieval and prediction.
вҖў Path informativeness is not guaranteed: Although semantic diversity helps genВӯ
eralization, some retrieved paths may be noisy or irrelevant.
Developing better
methods to score and filter path relevance remains an open problem.
вҖў DomainВӯspecific tuning: Although KВӯPaths is generalВӯpurpose, some tasks may
require customized heuristics for defining semantic similarity or relevance. These
must be manually designed or learned, depending on the domain.
To summarize, KВӯPaths provides a modular approach to augmenting LLMs and GNNs
with structured relational context. It is particularly effective in settings where symbolic
reasoning and generalization over unseen entities are critical. By combining algorithmic
retrieval with neural prediction, KВӯPaths advances the integration of knowledge graphs
and foundation models in a range of scientific and academic domains. Its limitations open
promising directions for future work.

7
Conclusion
This thesis presents KВӯPaths, a unified, modelВӯagnostic framework that augments LLMs
and GNNs with structured reasoning paths from knowledge graphs. The main idea is that
semantically diverse, multiВӯhop paths provide valuable context that improves generalizaВӯ
tion, interpretability, and efficiency, especially in tasks that rely on explicit relationships.
Experiments in biomedical and academic domains show that KВӯPaths significantly imВӯ
proves zeroВӯshot prediction in LLMs by supplying relational evidence the models would
otherwise lack. In supervised GNN training, KВӯPaths produces compact subgraphs that
reduce input size and training time while maintaining accuracy. These benefits are consisВӯ
tent across different datasets and models. Finally, this work demonstrates that combining
structured retrieval with CoT prompting can improve predictive accuracy.
The frameworkвҖҷs diversityВӯaware path selection is modular and adaptable, making it suitВӯ
able for a range of applications. Its performance in multiple domains demonstrates the
value of combining symbolic retrieval with pretrained models for knowledge discovery.
KВӯPaths contributes to bridging structured knowledge and neural methods, enabling more
transparent and efficient AI systems. This work lays the foundation for future systems that
can jointly retrieve, reason, and learn, supporting interpretable applications in biomedical
research and other fields.

Effect of Textual Definitions on
Performance
We analyzed the impact of combining textual definitions with KВӯPaths in zeroВӯshot settings.
Although this approach increases input context length, it does not consistently improve
performance. For example, on PharmacotherapyDB with LlamaВӯ3.1Вӯ70B, the F1 score
drops slightly from 67.46 (KВӯPaths) to 66.00 (KВӯPaths + Definitions). On DDInter, perforВӯ
mance remains effectively unchanged (46.19 vs. 46.18). The added context also leads
to higher inference time and computational cost, making largeВӯscale fineВӯtuning more exВӯ
pensive. These observations motivate prioritizing pathВӯonly retrieval as a more scalable
and costВӯeffective strategy.

D
QLoRA FineВӯTuning
For the supervised LLM experiments, Llama 3.1 8B Instruct was fineВӯtuned using QLoRA.
Training was performed under two distinct scenarios across the datasets. In the first
scenario, the model was trained using the retrieved paths corresponding to each training
query. In the second scenario, training was conducted using text definitions of the drugs or
diseases, with definitions capped at 200 tokens to reflect the average length per dataset.
In both settings, training ran for 10 epochs using the default QLoRA repository configuВӯ
ration, with two modifications: a learning rate of 1eВӯ3 and a maximum input length set to
the datasetВӯspecific average token length. Training was executed on 8 A100Вӯ80G GPUs
and typically completed within 24 hours, depending on the dataset.
During inference, reasoning paths were first retrieved using KВӯPaths. These paths were
then appended to the original query and passed to the fineВӯtuned LLM to produce the final
prediction.

E
GNN Baselines
E.1
Relational Graph Convolutional Network
The Relational Graph Convolutional Network (RGCN) [12] is implemented to operate on
the augmented graph containing multiple relation types. It uses message passing to propВӯ
agate structured information across nodes. The implementation uses PyTorch and PyВӯ
Torch Geometric.
Node feature initialization: For biomedical datasets, drug and disease nodes are iniВӯ
tialized using RoBERTa embeddings [53] derived from PubMedВӯsourced descriptions. In
the academic domain, paper nodes are initialized using embeddings extracted from abВӯ
stracts. All other entity types (such as genes, anatomical terms, paper authors etc.) are
assigned random initial embeddings, enabling the model to learn taskВӯspecific represenВӯ
tations during training.
Training setup: The inductive setting for dataset splitting, as described in section 4.2 is
followed. Training follows a link prediction framework, where training nodes are sampled
with all their relations observed. Test nodes are introduced to evaluate generalization.
Two training settings are considered: (1) Training on the entire augmented KG (Complete
KG) and testing on test nodes along with their retrieved test KG. (2) Training on the diverse
retrieved train paths and testing on test nodes along with their retrieved test paths.
Model architecture & training details: A threeВӯlayer RGCN is trained using the Adam
optimizer with a learning rate of 1eВӯ3 and a scheduler based on validation loss. CrossВӯ
entropy loss is used for predicting drugвҖ“drug or drugвҖ“disease interactions. Training runs
for up to 1,000 epochs with early stopping. To ensure class balance, stratified sampling
selects up to 1,000 samples per epoch and 10 per class. Each GCN layer is followed by
ReLU, projecting node embeddings into a 128Вӯdimensional space with batch normalizaВӯ
tion, ReLU, and dropout (rate 0.5). An edge classifier predicts interaction types using the
final embeddings of entity pairs. Basis decomposition with two bases is used to improve
computational efficiency.
E.2
EmerGNN
EmerGNN [28], a GNN designed specifically for emerging drugвҖ“drug interaction preВӯ
diction, is evaluated alongside RGCN for comparison.
The official implementation is
used without altering the model architecture or training pipeline. While RGCN relies on
RoBERTaВӯbased embeddings for initializing drug and disease nodes, EmerGNN incorpoВӯ
rates molecular features, capturing structural and chemical properties to enhance node
representations.
The two models are compared based on interaction prediction accuracy, highlighting the
effects of different node initialization strategies and the use of augmented knowledge
graphs.

This thesis explores how large language models (LLMs) can be augmented with knowledge graphs
(KGs) to improve performance and interpretability across scientific domains. It introduces KВӯPaths,
a modelВӯagnostic framework that retrieves diverse, multiВӯhop relational paths from large KGs to
support reasoning. Applied to both biomedical and academic datasets, KВӯPaths improves zeroВӯshot
LLM inference and enhances the efficiency of graph neural networks (GNNs) by focusing training on
compact, taskВӯrelevant subgraphs. The findings suggest that structured retrieval can bridge the gap
between symbolic knowledge and neural models, offering a scalable path toward more explainable
and generalizable scientific AI.
