Abstract
In this thesis, a non-toxic very-long-read protocol for Nanopore MinION has been devel-
oped using a triple isopropanol precipitation approach. The developed protocol is simple,
cost-eﬀective and eﬃcient. The protocol was established using a laboratory strain of E. coli
transconjugated with a Citrobactor freundii to obtain the plasmid pT1.
At ﬁrst, a dialysis based protocol was developed. However, it proved diﬃcult to work with
and did not yield the desired amount of genomic DNA, so ultimately it was exchanged with
the triple isopropanol precipitation protocol.
The eﬃciency of the protocol was established using MinKNOW data as well as analysis with
CLC Genomic WorkBench Version 11 and NanoPlot data.
The results were then compared to that of the standard recommended protocol, using
Gtip/500 with standard amounts of genomic DNA and fragmentation mix. Here the novel
triple precipitation protocol proved to yield longer reads and have a higher N50 value than
that of the standard Gtip protocol.
The developed protocol was able to yield reads up to 473kb and had several reads in full
plasmid size, ∼154kb, and give an N50 value of ∼26kb. The percentage of reads >20kb was
found to be ∼12%, compared to 4% for Gtip/500 protocol.
The novel protocol was also the only protocol tested, which proved able to provide several
reads with the length of the full plasmid that mapped to the known pT1 sequence using
the BLAST function in CLC Genomic WorkBench. Other protocols tested were either not
capable of providing reads long enough to close the plasmid with one read, or only provided
a single read of the full plasmid length.

Introduction To This Work
The ﬁeld of Deoxyribonucleic Acid(DNA) sequencing has been expanding rapidly since 1975
with the development of Sanger Sequencing, which is known as a ﬁrst generation sequencing
method. In 2005 second generation sequencing was established, followed by third generation
sequencing in 2011[1]. The main problem with ﬁrst generation sequencing was the cost per
base, the speed of sequencing and short read fragments. While second generation sequenc-
ing(SGS) provided sequencing at a lower cost, then full genome assembly was often poor due
to the short reads, and the process was still a slow one. Third generation sequencing(TGS)
is focusing on providing longer reads to meet this issue. However, there is a higher error
rate with TGS compared to SGS. SGS has an error rate below 2%, where a TGS such as
Paciﬁc Biosciences has an error rate from 10%-15%[2],[3].
While short sequences obtained from SGS might have a lower error rate, a SGS and Sanger
Sequencing can rarely be used to sequence and assemble novel complex genomes on their
own. Complex genomes can be genomes containing transposons, genomic duplication, as
well as complex chromosomal rearrangements which is often a feature of tumor cells[3].
This is one of the areas, which are overcome by longer reads in TGS, along with limiting
the sequencing bias from Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) ampliﬁcation and lowering the
sequence time from days to hours[2].
5.1
Project background
The MinION is the ﬁrst release of a TGS platform from Oxford Nanopore Technologies and
was released in 2014. It was able to sequence both Ribonucleic Acid(RNA) and DNA by
measuring the individual changes in electrical current caused by the DNA or RNA molecules
via a single-molecule nanopore. This technology can now sequence DNA fragments of vari-
ous length, from a 100base pairs(bp) to over a 1,000,000bp[4].
To date, it is only recommended to use high-quality Genomic DNA(gDNA) with the Min-
ION, and the existing standard protocols only promise reads above 150kilobases(kb)[2],[5].
Protocols which provides longer reads contains the use of health-damaging reagents such as
Phenol[6].
Phenol can cause severe illness both from exposure via inhalation, dermal contact, and
oral ingestion, and can cause death if taken orally[7]. Avoiding the use of phenol is the
main cause for creating an alternative path to obtaining very long reads with the Nanopore
MinION.
5.2
Objective
The main goal in this project was establishing a protocol, which can provide very long
reads, at over 50,000bp, using the Nanopore Technologies MinION. This was desired so
1
==PAGE:10==
that it could be possible to sequence an entire bacterial plasmid in just one, or near one,
read. This will limit the risk of obtaining misassemblies due to repetitive sequences, such as
insertion sequences(ISs) and tandem repeats, which are present in both chromosomes and
plasmids[8],[9].
The size of microbial plasmids varies in size, from under a 1kb to over 1 million base
pairs(Mbp)[10],[8]. Therefore, it was desired that the protocol would be able to produce
reads which were over 100,000bp in every run, to accommodate a big portion of the plas-
mids.
Not only is it desired to be able to assemble the plasmid using just one or near read, but
also to assemble the full bacterial chromosome while using ultra long reads.
A protocol capable of continuously producing long reads would have a positive impact on
the diagnostic medical ﬁeld, epidemiology and disease control. It will provide much-needed
information on plasmids’ virulence, antibiotic resistance, and bacteriocins, and be able to
pinpoint exactly where in the plasmid these genes are located. If the plasmid is split into
several shorter contigs, then it is not always possible to tell which plasmid a certain gene be-
longs to, and at times, a gene, like say a resistance gene, is not spotted, because it became
fragmented during the assembly. An assembly of the plasmid could show this resistance
gene, but it is not always a hundred percent correct, and sometimes important information
gets lost.
Futher, more complex genomes containing several tandem repeats and ISs, both in the chro-
mosomes and the plasmids, can also prove problematic when using an assembly of short
reads alone. Here assembly with long reads would be more likely to assemble the genome
with the correct orientation and sequence order.
Gaining longer reads will also immensely impact the epidemiology of diseases, and make it
easier to track diseases and ﬁnd the possible source of an outbreak.
Ultra long reads had been achieved before using the MinION[6][11], and it was known
that the nanopores did not have a limit to what length of fragments they could sequence.
Therefore, it was hypothesized that if the DNA fragments could remain as intact as possible
during the extraction and puriﬁcation process, then it should yield a larger fraction of long
reads. This was thought possible by creating a protocol with limited handling of the DNA.

Introduction to the domains of life
There are three cellular domains today recognized, bacteria, and archaea. This division was
ﬁrst suggested by Carl Woese and is based on the unique Ribosomal RNA(rRNA) from each
of the three domains[12].
Figure 1 depicts a simpliﬁed version of what is commonly known as the tree of life. It shows
the relation of archaea, bacteria, and eukaryotes, and is often used to describe how the three
domains have a common ancestor, LUCA(Last Universal Common Ancestor).
Figure 1: Tree of life. Simpliﬁed version of the tree of life, showing the three domains and
their relation to each other. LUCA(Last Universal Common Ancestor) and FUCA(First
Universal Common Ancestor) are also shown. Branch length and sizes have no meaning in
this tree. The tree was developed using information from [13].
The development of an rRNA based phylogenetic tree was made possible through the ad-
vances in sequencing techniques, though it was not until 1977 that archaea were ﬁnally
established as a domain alongside eukaryotes and bacteria[14]. The tree is often depicted
with archaea having a closer relation to eukaryotes regardless of its similarities to bacteria
in cell structure. The conclusion that archaea are closer related to eukaryotes stems from
their cellular functions. Bacteria are, for example, sensitive to streptomycin, where archaea
and eukaryotes are not. Further, archaea and eukaryotes are both sensitive to aphidicolin,
which is an inhibitor of DNA polymerase, whereas bacteria are not. Archaea and eukaryotes
also share several DNA replication components, which are absent in bacteria. These include
TATA box-like binding sites and other transcription factors[15].
Figure 1 shows the main diﬀerences between the three domains of life, with a focus on their
genome size. To underline the diﬀerence between them, info on their cell structure have also
been included.

From table 1 it seems that archaea and bacteria have more in common than archaea and

As it is today, viruses do not have a place in the domains of life, as they are not cellu-
lar. However, the role of viruses in terms of evolution has been studied, and in 2010, it was
suggested by P. Forterre that viruses are complex living beings and that they have had a
role in the biological evolution[17]. Further, in 2012 it was suggested that viruses made up
the fourth domain which was suggested to either have predated or co-existed with LUCA.
Furthermore, it was proposed that viruses had lost most of their genes in the early stages
of evolution and that this explains their parasitic life style[18].

7.1
Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria
Bacteria can be divided into two main forms, Gram positive and Gram negative, where
the main diﬀerence is in their cell barrier. Figure 2 shows an overview of the two types of
cell barriers. For Gram-positive, the cell barrier consists of one plasma membrane which is
covered with a thick layer of peptidoglycan. In contrast, Gram-negative bacteria have two
membranes, one plasma and one outer membrane consistent of lipopolysaccharides and pro-
teins, and a thinner layer of peptidoglycan between them[8],[16]. The two types of bacteria
became distinguishable by Gram-staining, a method invented by Hans Christian Gram in
1882[19].
Figure 2: Gram positive and Gram-negative cell wall The cell barrier of a Gram-
positive and Gram-negative cell. A Gram-positive bacteria cell barrier consists of an inner
plasma membrane and an out peptidoglycan layer separated by periplasmic space. A Gram-
negative bacteria have two membranes, a plasma membrane and an outer membrane contain-
ing lipopolysaccharides and proteins. A layer of peptidoglycan, surrounded by periplasmic
space separate the two membranes. Images from Image from Wikimedia Commons[20].
A well-known example of a Gram-positive genus is Staphylococcus.
This genus contains
both pathogenic and non-pathogenic species. However, the main concern with this genus
is the species Staphylococcus aureus(S. aureus) as many of them develops or acquires re-
sistance to methicillin and becomes what is known as methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus
aureus(MRSA)[21]. With a bacteria such as S. aureus, and in particular MRSA, or another
Gram-positive bacteria as Listeria monocytogenes, WGS can be a great tool to control
MRSA outbreaks and limit the transmission, by correctly identifying strain relatedness and
ﬁnding the source of the outbreak[22],[23].
E. coli is an example of a Gram-negative bacteria, and as it is the bacteria used to generate

has multiple cellular functions, one of which is a virulence function, allowing the bacterium
to attach itself to human uroepithelial cells and cause a urinary tract infection[26]. Other
functions of the pili include genetic exchange, communication, and twitching motility[27]
7.2
Chromosomes
The chromosome is the main carrier of genetic material in prokaryotes. Along with plas-
mids and transposable elements(TEs) they make up the sum of the genetic elements in the
bacteria.
TEs do not exist as separate molecules but are inserted into other DNA molecules[8]. TEs are
important as they have been shown to impact the genomes structure, the genome size, and
genome rearrangement[28]. Therefore, TEs are an important focus point of whole genome
sequencing with long reads.

7.3
Plasmids
Plasmids exist freely independent of the chromosome in the bacterial cell, and are unre-
lated to the chromosome, although, their replication does rely on chromosomal encoded
enzymes. The number of plasmids found in a bacteria varies, and over 300 types of plasmids
have been isolated from the genus E. coli alone[8]. Commonly, plasmids consist of double-
stranded DNA, and are circular in structure, although a few plasmids are linear, these are

Replication
The origin of replication(oriC) on the chromosome, see ﬁgure 4, is where the DNA replica-

E. coli contains 5 DNA polymerases, out of which DNA polymerase III is the main enzyme
used to replicate chromosomal DNA. These enzymes are what catalysis the addition of De-
oxyribonucleotide Triphosphate(dNTPs) in DNA replication. A primer, called primase, is
needed for DNA polymerase to be able to attach a nucleotide.[8].
In order for DNA polymerase to replicate the DNA, the DNA strand will have to be un-
wound.
This is done at a site called the replication fork.
The enzyme responsible for
separating the double-stranded DNA into single strands is called a DNA Helicase [8]. To
break the hydrogen bonds between the base pairs in the double-stranded DNA Helicase uses

7.4.2
Transcription
During transcription, mRNA is synthesized from a DNA template by the RNA polymerase
enzyme.
Transcription is started at a promoter site on the DNA. , and the transcription of mRNA
from the DNA starts from this region. The DNA contains several promoter sites, which
proceeds genes that can be transcribed by RNA polymerase. A transcription terminator
follows the promoter after one or more genes have been transcribed. This cluster of genes
is called an Operon, and the resulting mRNA is called a Polycistronic mRNA if it encodes
for two or more genes[8].

Translation is carried out by the ribosomes found freely in the cell’s cytoplasm, see ﬁgure 3.

towards these types of antibiotics revolves around protecting the ribosomes[45].

The three-dimensional structure of DNA was solved in 1953 by James Watson and Francis
Crick by building upon previously discovered facts concerning DNA. Their discovery rested
upon the early discovery of ’nuclein’ found inside the nuclei of a human white blood cell
by Friedrich Miescher in 1869. ’Nuclein’ later became what we know today as DNA. Fur-
ther studying DNA and its structure, Erwin Chargaﬀnoticed in 1950, almost a hundred
years after the discovery of DNA, that the amount of Adenine(T) was similar to that of
Thymine(T), and the same could be said for the amount of guanine(G) and cytosine(C).
From this, Chargaﬀ’s rule was established, saying that the total number of purines(A &
G) always equals the total number of Pyrimidines(T & C)[1],[61],[62]. Figure 11 shows the

three dimensional DNA Helix correctly identiﬁed by Crick and Watson in 1953, by using

8.1
First generation sequencing
Although ﬁrst attempts of sequencing were focused on RNA, since RNase enzymes capable
of cleaving RNA chains at speciﬁc points were well known, then sequencing of DNA from
bacteriophages became possible by the use of radioactive nucleotides in the 1970s[1].
In 1975, a method to discern DNA’s sequence was developed by F. Sanger and colleagues,
using DNA polymerase[63],[1].
F. Sanger continued to work on the sequencing of DNA, and in 1977 published two papers,
one describing a ”plus and minus” method and a ’chain-termination’ technique, making
using of the 2’,3’-dideoxy and arabino nucleoside analogs of dNTPs[64],[65].
This chain-termination protocol is what is commonly reﬀed to as Sanger Sequencing, and
alongside the ”plus and minus” protocol, it can be considered the as the start of FGS. Both
methods use polyacrylamide gels to identify nucleotides[1].
Later, ’shotgun sequencing’, PCR and ﬂuorescence detection was developed, to enable longer
reads from the Sanger method.
’Shotgun sequencing’ enabled the fragments to be put
together in one long continuous sequence, also called a contig, by cloning the DNA fragments
and sequencing them separately. PCR ampliﬁcation also allowed for higher concentrations
of pure DNA for sequencing[1].
In 1986 a partial automatization of DNA sequencing method was developed by L. Smith and
colleagues, which provided the sequence of the DNA by the use of ﬂuorescence detection. A
diﬀerent ﬂuorophore was used for each of the bases to give a signiﬁcant ﬂuorescence signal
detected by the computer[66].
Figure 12: Sanger Sequencing Workﬂow for Sanger Sequencing. The ﬁrst step is PCR

base, and separating the DNA by electrophoresis, was used to sequence the ﬁrst human
genome, during the Human Genome Project which lasted from 1990 to 2003[67].

8.3
Third generation sequencing
TGS, contrary to SGS and FGS, utilizes single molecule sequencing(SMS), real-time se-
quencing and, perhaps most importantly, requires no ampliﬁcation of the samples. With
the elimination of ampliﬁcation, a lot of bias and errors in genome assembly is removed[1].
The ﬁrst company to oﬀer TGS was Helicos BioSciences, founded in 2003. They commer-
cialized the SMS method developed by Stephen Quake. The HelicosTM SMS was the ﬁrst
sequencing method to oﬀer non-bias sequencing with no ampliﬁcation needed and used the
HeliscopeTM Analysis Engine to provide data analysis in real-time. However, the company
went bankrupt in 2012, leaving the market of TGS open for other companies[73],[74],[1].
To date, one of the more widely used TGS technology is the Single Molecule, Real-Time(SMRT)
platform oﬀered by Paciﬁc Biosciences. It takes advantages of the DNA polymerase kinetics
and uses it to allow direct detection of DNA nucleotides[75],[1].
In ﬁgure 16, the principle of direct detection of DNA methylation, can be seen. The SMRT
sequencing is performed in a SMRT cell, which contains several nanostructures dubbed zero-
mode waveguides. Attenuated light from an excitation beam penetrates the lower 20-30nm
of each zero-mode waveguides, essentially creating a microscope, capable of measuring tiny
volumes of samples. A complex of DNA template and polymerase is then immobilized at
the bottom of the zero-mode waveguides, and the wells are ﬂooded with Phospholinked
nucleotides, each labeled with a unique colored ﬂuorophore. A light pulse is produced each
time a base is held in the detection volume, and after incorporation, the dye is cleaved,
ending the signal[1],[73][38].

9.1
Cell Lysis
In this section, the focus will be on the lysis of the cells, in a way that preserves the DNA’s
integrity. For simplicity, the puriﬁcation and extraction of DNA will be explained in the
individual method chapters.
Common for the three methods is the cell lysis step. The lysis step was adapted from The
QIAGEN Genomic DNA Handbook[83], and an explanation of the steps and their function
will be explained in this section as well as the choice of isolate.
E. coli
J53 is a laboratory mutant strain derived from E. coli K-12 and its plasmid-free
genome is well known, making it a good candidate[30][8].
To be able to conﬁrm that the developed method is capable of sequencing the whole plasmid
from only one, or near one, read, the plasmid pT1 was transferred to the laboratory strain
via conjugation.
The isolate is deﬁned as AMA1442 and is a transconjugated E. coli, which has been de-
rived from AMA332(Citrobacter freundii with pT1) conjugated with E. coli J53 Azid resis-
tant[33][30]. The isolate is grown overnight at 37oC in LB broth, the following day the cell
culture is then pelleted.
After pelleting, the culture was re-suspended in a mixture of Ribonuclease(RNase) A and
a buﬀer(B1), which contains 50mM Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid(EDTA), 50mM Tris·Cl
at pH 8, 0.5% Tween®-20 and 0.5% Triton X-100. See Appendix 14.1 for the recipe.
Buﬀer B1’s function is to break down the cell barrier of the bacteria and is an important
ﬁrst step.
Trixton X-100 is the reactant used to break down the cell membrane proteins and solubilize
the bacteria cell.
Tween®-20 has the same function as Triton X-100. Both detergents can denature proteins
and macromolecules, as well as lipid-lipid, protein-lipid and protein-protein interactions,
making them ideal for cell lysis. They are both non-ionic, which is important as ionic de-
tergents could interfere with the function of other reactants[85].
Tris·Cl is a buﬀer solution intended to keep the pH at an optimal level for DNA stability,
as the cellular reactions can inﬂuence the pH[86].
If working with Gram-positive bacteria, Lysozyme should be added to ensure membrane
breakage, and it was recommended in the QIAGEN Genomic DNA Handbook to add it[83].

Lysozyme, an enzyme produced by animals as part of the innate immune system, cleaves
1,4-beta-linkages between N-acetylmuramic Acid and N-acetyl-D-glycosamine of peptidogly-
can[41]. As can be seen in ﬁgure 2 it is Gram-positive bacteria that would be most aﬀected
by lysozyme, and it would have limited eﬀect on Gram-negative bacteria, since it does not
break down the outer membrane. It would not harm the lysis of the cell to add lysozyme,
however, in order to limit the number of components to remove in the later DNA puriﬁcation
steps, it was left out of this Gram-negative based protocol.
The next step is to get rid of any unwanted components in the lysed cell. RNase A is a
nuclease that degrades RNA into smaller components[87]. EDTA is a chemical which is
used to deactivate enzymes dependent on divalent ions, such as DNase, which is unwanted
as they degrade the DNA to smaller components[88].
Proteinase K and buﬀer solution B2 was added to ensure protein degradation.
Proteinase K is an endopeptidase capable of cleaving peptide linkages in proteins[89].
Buﬀer B2 contains 3M guanidine hydrochloride(GdnHCl) and 20% Tween®-20. GdnHCl is
a strong denaturant that aﬀects protein stability and is essential in the complete denatura-
tion of the proteins in the bacterial cell[90]. For recipe see Appendix 14.1.
After these steps the DNA is ready to be extracted and puriﬁed.
For full lysis protocol see Appendix 14.2.
9.2
Qubit
To measure the amount of input gDNA Qubit was utilized.
Qubit is capable of measuring the amount of DNA in a sample (in ng/µl). Assay dyes bind
selectively to double-stranded DNA, and the Qubit 4 Fluorometer then measures it via UV
absorbance[91].
The Qubit dsDNA broad range assay kit was used throughout this protocol.
The Working Solution Mix is made by adding 1µl Qubit dsDNA broad range reagent to
199µl Qubit dsDNA broad range buﬀer. This is done for each sample that is to be read,
including the two standards.
For example, six samples, plus the two standards, equals
6x199µl(1592µl). This is then combined with 8µl of dsDNA broad range reagent.
190µl of the Working Solution Mix is added to each tube. Then in a tube marked S1 10µl of
Qubit Standard 1 is added. In another tube marked S2, 10µl of Qubit Standard 2 is added.
Then 10µl of a sample is added in another tube, marked appropriately for the number of
samples run. A smaller volume can be used if needed. For most of this thesis, only 1µl
sample was added to conserve the DNA.
The tubes are then vortexed for 2-3 seconds and placed on the table for 2-3 minutes before
the Fluorometer reads them. Here it is important not to exceed the time limit, as the dye
is light sensitive, meaning that the absorption may be low if too much time passes. On the
other hand, if not enough time passes, the dye may not have interacted with the sample,
and the correct amount of gDNA may not be measured.

==PAGE:34==
The two standard DNA samples are used to calibrate the Fluorometer, using the dsDNA
setting and following the instructions of the screen.
Then each sample is loaded, and the volume of input DNA is stated before the sample is
read.
9.3
NanoDrop
To measure the purity of a sample NanoDropTM 1000 Fluorospectrometer was utilized. Using
a broad spectrum ﬂuorescence analysis, it can quantify nucleic acid concentrations[92].
Absorbance at 260 is DNA, 230nm is peptides and soaps, 280nm is aromatic amino acids,
which is a sign of protein contamination in the sample. The ratio between 260/280 and
260/230 can then be used to determine the purity of the sample[93],[94].
2-3µl of deionized water is loaded onto the lower surface of the Fluorospectrometer, and this
is done to clean the upper and lower optical surfaces. After, 1µl of resuspension buﬀer is
used to generate a blank measurement. Then ﬁnally, the samples can be loaded onto the
Fluorospectrometer and measured[94].

9.5
DNA Dialysis
To generate longer reads by keeping the DNA strands as intact as possible, DNA Dialysis
was proposed as a possible solution.
27
==PAGE:36==
DNA dialysis works by separating molecules depending on size. The size of the pores re-
lates to a molecular weight cutoﬀ(MWCO), and 1kDA is around 1.3nm in membrane pore
size[97].
In ﬁgure 21 the general principle of DNA dialysis can be seen. Here, it can be seen that
molecules with a molecular weight lower than the MWCO pass through the semi-permeable
membrane, while larger molecules are retained on the membrane[98].
There are six bi-products from the cell lysis, which needs to pass through the ﬁlter, to
leave the pure gDNA. These are EDTA(2.9224kDA), Guanidine HCL(0.9553kDa),Tween®-
20(1.22754kDa), Triton X-100(0.647kDa), RNase A(13.7kDa) and Proteinase K(28.9kDA).
These means that the used ﬁlter should have a MWCO well above 28.9kDA, or 37.57nm
pore size, to ensure full removal of Proteinase K.
Figure 21: Dialysis Membrane Function Molecules which are smaller than the pore size
will pass through the ﬁlter, and larger molecules will be retained on the surface. Figure from
[98].
The membrane ﬁlters were procured from Merck Millipore [99] and had a size of 0.45µm(450nm).
Therefore, Proteinase K and shorter reads should diﬀuse through the membrane, leaving only
the longer fragments behind.
9.5.1
Cell Lysis
The best volume of cell culture was found to be 400µl for this experiment. To obtain enough
DNA, three copies needed to be made for each dialysis ﬁlter. Less would not yield enough
gDNA for further analysis.
The Cell Lysis was performed as stated under section 9.1, using Qiagen buﬀers, RNase A
and Proteinase K. For buﬀer recipes see Appendix 14.1, and for the description of the buﬀers
see 9.1.
After the cells have been lysed the DNA is precipitated using Isopropanol. Isopropanol is
added at room temperature, and the sample is then centrifuged at 15000x g for 15minutes
at 40C. Then the supernatant is removed, and the resulting pellet is then washed with

410 µl 70% cold ethanol, before being centrifuged at 15000x g for 10minutes at 40C. The
supernatant is again removed, and the pellet is allowed to air dry to allow the evaporation of

9.5.2
Dialysis
The following is performed on three sets of samples from the lysis.
A petri dish containing 30ml distilled water was prepared. Then a membrane ﬁlter with a
0.045µm pore sized and 25mm diameter is placed carefully on the surface, making sure that
no water spills onto it.
30µl of sample is then carefully deposited on the center of the ﬁlter. The sample is then left
on the ﬁlter for 1 hour.
After the hour has passed, the ﬁlter is moved to another petri dish containing 30ml distilled
water and left for another hour. A theoretical 1,000,000 dilution is reached following this
step.
The ﬁlter is moved onto a clean surface before the sample is removed using a wide-tipped
pipette. All three samples are then added to a 1.5ml DNA LoBind Eppendorf Tube.
The DNA quantity of the sample can now be measured via Qubit and is ready to be used
for a library.
A Protease Activity Assay was utilized to ensure the removal of Proteinase K. Since the
other molecules that need to pass through the membrane were much smaller than Proteinase
K, then no detectable Proteinase K in the sample should mean that the rest of the molecules
had diﬀused through the membrane as well. This way, the Protease Assay would be able to
state the purity of the sample more eﬃciently than a NanoDrop.
See Appendix 14.4 for full protocol.

QIAGEN Genomic-tip 500/G
QIAGEN Genomic-tip 500/G DNA puriﬁcation was performed to establish a baseline for
what could be obtained in terms of read length and quality, using the recommended and
standard method of DNA extraction. It should be able to yield reads at 150kb eﬀectively[5].
However, this is when using the recommended amount of fragmentation mix and amount of
gDNA from Nanopore[106]. If these volumes were to be adjusted, it might result in longer
reads.
The recommended volume for the Rapid Library Kit preparation of Fragmentation mix is
2.5µl to 400ng gDNA.
To test the theory that less Fragmentation mix would lead to longer reads; four diﬀerent
setups were created. Some of which also tested to see whether an increase in the amount
of input gDNA would lead to longer reads. In theory, the ratio between the transposome
complexes(fragmentation mix) and gDNA would increase, and therefore lead to less frag-
mentation, see ﬁgure 17.
Barcode01, was used for the standard and as a positive control, using 2.5µl Fragmentation
mix and 400ng gDNA.
Barcode02, tested whether longer reads could come from using more input gDNA. The
amount of fragmentation mix remained 2.5µl, but the input gDNA was increased to 1600ng.
Barcode03, tested if less fragmentation mix could lead to longer reads, using only 1µl of
fragmentation mix to 1600ng of gDNA.
Finally, barcode04 was used to see how much the system could handle, by using 3200ng
gDNA to 1µl of fragmentation mix.
Using 1.5µl of fragmentation mix had previously been performed successfully by Josh Quick,
using a Phenol based extraction[6]. However, as phenol is highly toxic, it was desired to
create a protocol that did not utilize this method[7].
In the protocol by Josh Quick, it was stated that 1.5µl of fragmentation mix was the
maximum amount of fragmentation mix that could be added, without resulting in over-
fragmentation[6]. Therefore, 1µl could in theory also give favorable results, where 2.5µl
should result in over-fragmentation.

10.2
DNA Dialysis
A main problem with the QIAGEN G-tip protocol is that it is not designed to keep the
DNA as intact as possible, as several handling steps can fragment the DNA.
To address this, the possibility of using Dialysis ﬁlters were examined.
Here, the only
handling of the DNA is when it is removed from the ﬁlter. A Protease Activity Assay was
performed alongside the dialysis, to ensure the full removal of Proteinase K. This was done
because the presence of Proteinase K in the sample will damage the protein nanopores in
the ﬂow cell.
10.2.1
Dialysis & Protease Activity Assay
Before running the DNA library in the MinION, the purity of the DNA had to be estab-
lished. This was done with a Protease Activity Assay, to ensure that the levels of Proteinase
K left in the sample would not aﬀect the library. To ensure this, four control samples(P1,
P2, P3 & P4), which had previously been used in libraries and run through the MinION
were used.
Two variations of the test samples were made in two copies. The ﬁrst was using one Iso-
propanol Precipitation followed by dialysis. Here the ﬁlter has dialyzed a total of three times,
in 30ml of Milli-Q water. The second variation was washing the ﬁlter with tris buﬀer after
the DNA had been removed. This was recommended in the original dialysis protocol[108].

Discussion
In this section, the results for the Gtip protocol will be compared to the results obtained
from the ﬁnal Isopropanol precipitation.
The DNA dialysis will not be compared to the isopropanol precipitation protocol, as the
protocol proved to be ineﬃcient for this type of DNA extraction and puriﬁcation. However,
the DNA dialysis, alongside the protease assay did prompt the idea that isopropanol precip-
itation alone could be used to purify the DNA. Isopropanol along with Ethanol is commonly
used to precipitate the DNA after puriﬁcation [83],[6], but from the data obtained from the
protease assay, it was seen that the precipitated DNA had a Proteinase K activity of the
close to the DNA samples from a Gtip protocol after two consecutive precipitations. This
lead to the conclusion that three precipitations would provide DNA of high enough quality
to run through the MinION, and due to the elimination of several handling steps, which
potentially can damage the DNA, the DNA obtained from this precipitation should also
yield longer reads as it, in theory, remains more intact.
To test whether or not the triple precipitation protocol provided data which were better than
using the standard Gtip protocol, a normalization of the data from the two precipitation
trials was performed, as assembly eﬃcacy does to some extent depend on not only the length
and quality of the data but also on the quantity. The two precipitation trials were compared
to both the Gtip data obtained from using the standard and recommended volumes of DNA
and fragmentation mix, and second, there were compared to the Gtip data obtained from
using more input gDNA and the same recommended amount of fragmentation mix. The
two precipitation protocols were not normalized to the Gtip data for 1µl of fragmentation
mix, as these yielded very little data, and the normalization of the precipitation protocols
compared to these would not testify to their eﬃcacy.
First, the two precipitation trials were compared to the data obtained from the Gtip/500
protocol using the recommended amount of gDNA and fragmentation mix, namely 400ng
to 2.5µl. Since a total of 450MB of data was gathered from the Gtip run, the data from
the two triple precipitation protocols had to be normalized, before a fair comparison could

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to create a non-toxic very-long-read protocol for Nanopore Min-
ION. It was hypothesized that keeping the DNA as intact as possible during extraction
and puriﬁcation would lead to longer reads. Alongside this, it was also thought that if the
amount of gDNA used in the library were increased, it would also result in longer reads.
Based on these hypotheses, a triple isopropanol precipitation protocol was established, based
on ﬁndings from a Dialysis protocol paired with a Protease Assay.
This protocol eﬀectively removed many of the otherwise damaging handling steps of the
DNA and also used a signiﬁcantly higher amount of input gDNA than that of the standard
recommended protocol. This protocol yielded the long reads which were desired, as well as
reads of a high enough quality to be used in an assembly of the full genome.
Importantly, the protocol resulted in reads of full plasmid size, making it possible to cover
the entire plasmid of AMA1442 in just one read.
A potential problem with the triple precipitation protocol was also discovered when con-
ﬁrming the results. Although the conﬁrmation trial did provide long reads and had a high
N50 value, the read lengths were in general shorter than that of the ﬁrst trial, and only one
read was the full length of the plasmid, versus the seven reads obtained from the ﬁrst trial.
However, this does not entirely discredit the protocol, as other factors could have aﬀected
the results, such as the ﬂow cell being used twice and still containing leftovers from the ﬁrst
trial. Therefore, it was found that a ﬂow cell should not be reused after a precipitation
protocol has been run through it.
In conclusion, the goal of the thesis was met, and a protocol capable of generating very
long reads in a non-toxic way was created.

Future Perspectives
While the goal of creating a non-toxic-very-long-read protocol was met, then improvements
to the protocol and further testing is needed prior to future use.
The protocol, while yielding long reads, also yielded a high percentage of short reads. This
could be overcome by adjusting the precipitation protocol, to get rid of some of the shorter
reads during the precipitation steps. A possible way of doing this is to perform the ﬁrst
precipitation step, as usual, washing with ethanol and then resuspending in 10mM Tris-
pH8+0.02%Triton X-100 for just a few minutes and then remove the supernatant. Then the
sample would once again be resuspended for one hour and precipitated twice more, before
the ﬁnal resuspension. In theory, this could get rid of some of the shorter reads as the
shorter DNA fragments resuspend faster than, the longer fragments. However, this is purely
theoretical, and it would require further testing to conﬁrm the theory.
Another future step is to enable the use of multiple barcodes so that multiple samples
can be analyzed with this protocol. Since the ﬂow cell can only be used one time per library
generated from the triple precipitation protocol, then the expense per sample would be quite
high if solely one sample can be analyzed per run.
It was found that a total of six individual tubes was the max for one person to physically
handle in the laboratory while carrying out this protocol due to time restrictions. More
samples would lead to the pellet separating from the side of the tube and getting into the
supernatant. This means that a total of three samples could be sequenced and analyzed
from this protocol if it is possible to use multiple barcodes.
From performing a DNA library after the triple precipitation extraction, it could be seen,
that the DNA visibly fragmented after the addition of fragmentation mix. Therefore, it
should be possible, to pool three separate DNA libraries before the addition of RAP.
Cost wise, it would also be advantageous to ﬁgure out the viability of the triple precipitation
protocol when using a used ﬂow cell from a ligation library. If this yields similar results as
seen in the ﬁrst trial, then this would signiﬁcantly lower the cost of the protocol and could
lead to its implementation in a day to day practice.
During this thesis, the protease activity assay was used to measure the Proteinase K activity
in the gDNA sample after two precipitations. This result led to the belief that if three pre-
cipitations were performed, then the DNA should be as free of Proteinase K as the samples
obtained from the Gtip protocol.
This seemed to be the case when using the DNA in the library. Nevertheless, it would
be beneﬁcial to perform a protease activity assay on the gDNA obtained after three pre-
cipitations to conﬁrm this belief. This was not performed during this thesis, due to time
restrictions.

When performing the Gtip/500 protocol, the ratios of fragmentation mix versus gDNA
was not ideal for the sample containing 400ng and the one containing 3200ng. It would have
been more prudent to have one with 1µl fragmentation mix and one with 2.5µl for both of
the variations, as it was for the sample with 1600ng gDNA. As it were, it was desired to
keep the maximum number of barcodes to four to get the highest throughput possible for all
four barcodes. However, it is not ideal when comparing ratios not to have the same variable
for all samples. It would have been interesting to see how the sample with 3200ng gDNA
would have performed when given 2.5µl of fragmentation mix, and it would most likely have
given a better output for this sample.
Another possibility for the Gtip/500 protocol is to perform it once more, and consequently
use 2.5µl fragmentation mix to four diﬀerent concentration of gDNA, where the fourth is as
high as possible from the obtained gDNA.

Incubate the reaction of 5min at RT.
• Mix SQB and FLB tubes by vortexing, spin down and return to ice. Spin down the FLT
tube, mix by pipetting, and return to the ice.
• Open the primer port on the ﬂow cell
• Prepare the ﬂow cell priming mix by adding 30µl of FLT directly to the tube of FLB and
mix by pipetting.
Remove air bubbles from the priming port by inverse pipetting.
• Load 800µl of priming mix into the ﬂow cell via the priming port. Avoid air bubbles. Wait
5 min.
• Thoroughly mix SQB by pipetting.
• In a new tube, add 34µl of SQB, 30µl NFW, and 11µl DNA library. Mix by ﬂicking.
• Gently lift the SpotON sample port before loading the 200µl priming mix
• Load 200µl of priming mix into the ﬂow cell via the priming port. Keep it open, until
everything is loaded.
• Mix the prepared library gently just before loading. Load the sample to the SpotON
sample port in a drop-wise fashion.
• Gently replace the SpotON lid and close the priming port, then replace the MinION lid.
