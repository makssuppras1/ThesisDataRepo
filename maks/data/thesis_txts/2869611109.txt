Motivation and Introduction
Motivation
In 1980 Miles L. Loyd published a paper conceptualising a large­scale kite­based wind
generator [1], however, during his time, drone technology was not as well developed as it
is now, meaning the kite concept was not possible to create. Now, in 2025 development
into renewable energies is greater than ever as as wind turbines grow larger and larger,
further developments are being looked into to improve technologies and bring down the
cost.
Airborne wind energy systems (AWES) potentially offer tangible advantages over tradi­
tional wind turbines:
• Material cost: The kite does not require a tower or nacelle, these two can combine
to over 1000 tons[2] of material savings. Offshore stability is less of a requirement
so savings can be made in the floating platform too, potentially bringing costs down
by as much as 60% [3]
• Better wind resources: As altitude increases, the wind resource gets better. Kites
are not limited by tower height but by their tether length and as such, these better
resources are much easier to reach.
• Selective generation: Traditional turbines are constantly exposed to the environ­
ment and as such the slow, constant fatigue damage will degrade the turbine, even
outside of production periods, meaning the lifespan for a turbine is fixed. A kite can
be flown or pulled in whenever the user wants, meaning they can release it when
electricity prices are high and pull it in if they are low. This means the fatigue of the
Kite is determined by the user depending on how and when they want to use the
Kite.
• Transportability: Due to the reduced size of the system, moving, installing and main­
taining these systems is much easier.
A drawback of the kite­based wind generator is the requirement to get the kite into the air
for generation to occur. This necessitates a method for propulsion to move the kite into
the sky. This simplest idea is to use the rotors themselves as propellers but this would
require an investigation into whether the rotor can perform both roles.
Introduction
Airborne wind energy moves wind energy generation into the sky. Replacing the tower of a
traditional turbine with a tether connected to a kite can reach higher more bountiful energy
resources. Airborne wind energy systems (AWES) come in a variety of designs and ideas.
Ground­based systems, that use traditional kites tethered to a spool, can generate power
using a pumping action by using wind to slowly reel out a tether and spinning a generator
on the ground. Once the kite has reached its maximum distance, the kite is stalled and
the tether is reeled­in, using some power in the process. Crosswind on­board generating
kites are much more interesting. These use a fixed length tether attached to a kite which
has turbines mounted on the device to generate power.

Mohammad Hossein Kazemi wrote his thesis on Aerodynamic dual performance rotors
for use in airborne wind energy systems[4] under Mac Gaunaa in 2020 with the aim of op­
timising the efficiency of the kite in both the turbine and propeller model. His investigation
showed that blade element theory was valid at lower tip speed ratios by using the vortex
method as a comparison. His optimisation framework maximised the efficiency turbine
and hover scenarios in a dual optimisation, however, the stresses endured by the rotor
weren’t investigated as a possible problem. Confirming that BEMT is possible for this
application, how can this be scaled to other models and are their any physical limitations
to the given designs?
A case study was required to compare how the optimisation design compared to some real
results. The case study chosen was the Makani M600 kite which was a large, high power
kite design that ultimately failed. Within their reports, the rotor design plays a very minor
part with the focus being solely on the kite design. This posed an interesting question,
what is the optimal rotor for this specific test case?
The primary objective of this thesis is to optimise rotor aerodynamics for onboard genera­
tion in AWES, with a focus on maximising the coefficient of power over thrust and treating
the hover case as a minimum requirement constraint. This is achieved through BEMT­
based modeling, optimization and parameter studies.
The structure of this thesis is as follows:
• Chapter 2: Provides an in­depth review of BEMT and its application to AWES.
• Chapter 3: Details the methodology used for rotor modeling and optimization as well
as its validation.
• Chapter 4: Presents a case study on the Makani M600 system and applies the
optimization framework.
• Chapter 5: Discusses the results, including performance improvements and practi­
cal feasibility.
• Chapter 6: Concludes the study with key findings and recommendations for future
research.

Methods and Theory
Blade element momentum theory (BEMT), originally developed for propellers, has been
used as a simple way to model the operation of rotors, furthermore, the model is also valid
for turbine modeling. Due to its validity in both areas and simplicity, it meant that it could
be used for both the hover and turbine generation.
2.1
Blade Element Momentum Theory introduction
A mathematical model needs to be created for the rotor design that can be considered
compatible with reality. Blade element Momentum Theory mixes both the 1D momentum
theory and blade element theory which results in a simple and fast way to calculate the
expected outcome of a given design whilst also being accurate enough to be considered
useful. In the following section the model will be described and validated using similar
models and results from different sources. [5]
2.1.1
1D momentum theory
1D momentum theory is considered to be one of the simplest of models in rotor models.
The model assumes an incompressible, uniform flow through a rotor disk as well as full
conservation of energy and momentum. The rotor disk decelerates the incoming flow,
and the momentum is transferred into the rotor.

The optimiser brings all of the theory together to produce an optimal design. The Matlab
function fmincon was used for this project with the Sequential Quadratic Programming
algorithm. The SQP algorithm solves a quadratic approximation to nonlinear problems
by iteratively solving the a Hessian approximation and Lagrange Multipliers until conver­
gence. This was originally used because analytic gradients can easily be applied to the
model, which would have accelerated its convergence and produced more robust results.

4.1.3
Adding Propeller constraint
Optimising for the turbine portion only gives an incomplete picture for the purpose of the
rotor. For the kite to produced power, it needs to be in the air first and to do that, it needs
the rotor to function as a propeller as well. To implement this into the optimisation, a
further constraint is added, this constraint runs a propeller BEMT to calculate the thrust
produced by a given design. The total thrust is then compared to the weight of the turbine.
This means a new design variable is required, the rotational speed of the propeller. There
were several ways that the design could be constrained, for example a maximum power
of the propeller could have been selected such that the propeller needed to stay below a
rated power, however, leaving the maximum power open means the designs can poten­
tially stay unchanged meaning the turbine is unaffected.
The constraint was set such that the kite was in hover phase, meaning the inflow velocity
would be zero, however, the BEMT code would not find a result and requires a slight inflow,
for this reason, the inflow velocity was set to 0.1ms−1. The tip speed of the propeller
needed to be limited by an upper bound, was initially chosen as 0.8 Mach which is around
of 239 rads s−1 since the aerodynamic model starts to break down at these high values.
In their report Manaki mention that they have no pitch controller on their rotors due to
O&M reasons[3].

Conclusion
Makani initially estimated that their system could generate a total of 410 kW with a wind
speed of 8 m/s. However, using Lloyd’s ideal Kite equilibrium model reveals that this is
not the case, even before considering the rotor design. The rotor input parameters further
highlight this discrepancy, as the number of blades and turbine specifications produce
unrealistic results. Specifically, the bending stress exceeds safe limits for a reasonable
selection of materials.
To address this, a centered parameter study was conducted, using Makani’s kite design as
a basis, to explore different rotor configurations and identify potentially feasible solutions.
While Makani’s airborne system employs 8 turbines, this setup results in excessively high
bending stress, making it impractical.
The parameter study revealed that increasing both the tip speed ratio and the number of
blades improves the power efficiency coefficient CP/CT, but also leads to thinner, weaker
blades. In contrast, decreasing the number of turbines raises the chord length, thus en­
hancing the strength of the blades. Ultimately, a balance between these three variables
was needed to optimise turbine design. The most viable solution identified involved 3
blades, 6 turbines, and a tip speed ratio of 3.
Future work and missed opportunities
Analytic Gradients were a major time sink for this project and unfortunately they had to be
removed due to time constraints. This meant that the optimisation code took much longer
than what was ideal. By integrating the analytic gradients, the code could be substantially
improved and more testing could have been done. Furthermore, due to the issues from
the analytic gradients, focus was misplaced in areas which wouldn’t have had substantial
impact on the results within this paper. If tests which were implemented after the optimi­
sation such as changing blade number, number of rotors, blade length and constarints
such as bending limits could have been applied to the code to give more accurate results.
Other areas could also have been investigated such as the affect of the generator or
control system as they will have a substantial affect on the performance of the kite.
