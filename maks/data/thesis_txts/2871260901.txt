2. 
Abstract 
This thesis investigates the passive fire protection of wooden facades, focusing on the effect of sun shading sys-
tems to prevent vertical fire spread. The study is motivated by the increasing use of wood and other bio-based 
materials in Danish construction, which presents unique fire safety challenges. The research aims to evaluate the 
performance of a sun shading system designed to act as a flame barrier, preventing fire spread to untreated 
wooden facades. The sun shading system was originally scheduled to be investigated using five different perfo-
rated steel plates with varying the perforation hole diameter and the opening ratio, designed to quench the 
flame. The experimental schedule was evaluated during the experiments, which resulted in only three different 
solutions being investigated. One of the solutions had a hole size smaller than the pre-determined quenching 
diameter and the other two solutions had a larger hole size. The solution with the smallest hole size performed 
very well, but over time, the flame propagated through the perforated steel plate. The solution with the hole 
size that was slightly larger than the pre-determined quenching diameter, proved to be effective in preventing 
vertical fire spread for a short period of time by quenching the flame. The solution with largest hole size did not 
quench the flame and could not prevent the vertical fire spread. 
The study shows that a sun shading systems can be used as measure to obtain passive fire protection, but it 
needs to be improved before it can be used to ensure the fire safety of wooden facades. Therefore, future re-
search should be done regarding the perforation hole size and the opening ratio. Furthermore, the performance 
needs to be evaluated according to a large exposure façade test.

3. 
Introduction 
There is currently great interest in the use of wood and other bio-based materials in Danish construction. 
Whether for load-bearing structures, interior surfaces or exterior cladding, including the outermost cladding, 
also known as the rain screen. With projects such as TRÆ [TRAE, 2024], Mini-CO2 Etagehus TRÆ [Realdania, 
2024], and Sundby Skole [DBI, 2024], shows that building are constructed as high-rise, mid-rise, and low-rise 
buildings where the primary building materials are including wooden- or bio-based materials. 
This development in Danish construction is assumed to align with the national requirements being gradually 
implemented towards 2030, aimed at ensuring Denmark reduces its CO₂ emissions by 70% compared to the 
1990 level [BPST, 2021]. 
The geometries of buildings and the choice of materials are relatively complex from a fire safety perspective. 
This report will primarily focus on the use of wood as the outermost cladding of buildings, referred to in this 
context as the rainscreen, which is designed with a ventilated cavity behind it, as well as the design of passive 
fire protection. The rainscreen in this study is an untreated wood cladding so the wood are not expected to per-
form better than the natural properties regarding reaction to fire. 
The high complexity of this topic has been examined in both national and international studies in recent years. 
Several fire tests have been conducted where the flame barrier consisted of a metal plate with a depth of ap-
proximately 20-30 cm [Thomas Engel, 2021] [ref. Engel-Werther]. This type of flame barrier has also been used 
in at least one specific Danish construction project and in a series of demonstration tests [DBI, 2025] conducted 
by the DBI – The Danish Institute of Fire and Security Technology (hereafter referred to as DBI). In buildings 
where the flame barrier is used to protect the wooden cladding and where the fire safety design of the façade is 
verified/documented through fire testing, these buildings are considered highly complex in relation to the Dan-
ish Building Regulations (Building Regulations 2018, hereafter referred to as BR18) [BR18, 2025]. Buildings con-
structed in Denmark must comply with the requirements set forth in the building regulations (BR18, effective 
from January 1, 2025), which allow performance-based design concerning the fire safety requirements. BR18 
includes the following requirements regarding façades in relation to fire [BR18-5, 2025]: 
§87  Materials, structures and building parts intended to contribute to the fire safety of the building must be used 
and carried out with due consideration of their fire properties such as heat development, flame spread, 
smoke generation, production of burning droplets and particles, falling building parts and fire resistance 
and load-bearing capacity. 
§104 Buildings must be planned and erected to ensure that material spreading of fire or smoke does not occur in 
case of fire. This must be done with due consideration of the following: 
 
1) The risk of fire is limited. 
 
2) Spreading of fire and smoke is limited to the fire unit where the fire started. 
 
3) Spreading of fire and smoke to other fire units is prevented for the time necessary to carry out evacuation 
and rescue. 
 
4) Spreading of fire to other buildings on the plot is limited. 
 
5) Fire does not spread to buildings on other plot.

§117 Outer walls and roofs must be planned and constructed in a way which ensures that:  
 
1) Spreading of fire in and on outer walls and roofs is limited; 
 
2) in buildings consisting of more than one fire safety unit, spreading of fire does not occur between the var-
ious fire safety units via outer walls and roofs for the time necessary for evacuation and for rescue personnel 
to carry out their work; 
 
3) outer wall do not develop unacceptable levels of burning droplets and particles; 
 
4) parts of the wall does not fall down and constitute a risk of injury to persons. 
These fire safety requirements are further outlined in several guidelines and some specific solutions (ap-
proved solutions for different use). Additionally, the Danish Building Regulations specify that for new 
constructions or refurbish of buildings that alter fire safety conditions, a certified fire safety consultant 
must be involved. For buildings with high complexity or where complex methods are used to document 
fire safety – such as verifying the fire performance of wooden façades through fire testing – a certified 
fire safety consultant (level 3/4) must be involved, along with a third-party reviewer (also a certified fire 
safety consultant). In cases where the fire performance of wooden façade is demonstrated through fire 
testing, the acceptance criteria for the fire test must be determined by the certified fire safety consultant 
(level 3/4), who prepares the documentation. However, the acceptance criteria must also be approved or 
aligned with the third-party reviewer. This allows for the establishment of acceptance criteria where fire 
spread on the façade may extend across multiple fire-separating building elements. The fire safety re-
quirements in the building regulations are performance based which gives the opportunity to design a 
flame barrier that prevents flames from passing and causing fire spread within 60 minutes, in accordance 
with the assessment method for medium-scale façade fire testing – based on draft version 8 of Assess-
ment of fire performance of façades using medium fire exposure. 
In this study, the flame barrier will be integrated into a sun shading system positioned above windows, 
which may be necessary in some buildings to mitigate sun heating and to avoid mechanical cooling. 
3.1 
Flame barrier 
The previously mentioned solution regarding flame barrier generally consists of a horizontal oriented metal 
plate with a depth of approximately 20-30 cm along the length of the façade. The solution are assumed to orig-
inal have a purpose as a drip board and are extend to prevent flame spread as a flame barrier. 
In this case, we look into a sun shading system and give this building element a multi-purpose function as a 
flame barrier, so the passive fire protection is hidden in other already used elements. The sun shading system is 
generally consisted of a perforated steel plate which is fixed to a steel frame. The design of the perforated steel 
plate is based on principles as a miners safe-lamp or Davy safe-lamp for miners [Newman, 1836]. The Davy 
safe-lamp is invented by Sir Humphry Davy and is shown on Figure 3.1 and a similarly safe-lamp by George 
Stephenson is shown on Figure 3.2.

The safe-lamp are designed as an oil lamp with a cylindrical cage of wire gauze woven of iron. The wire gauze 
was creating small meshes or squared spaces between the wires so the greatest quantity of light is achieved 
with a satisfactory safe level [Newman, 1836]. Thus, the wire gauze manufactured with a specific mesh size and 
a given diameter of cylindrical, then the wire gauze will prevent the flame propagate through the wire. This phe-
nomenon is also known as flame quenching which was investigated by J. M. Holm which provide to give a 
quantitative measure for quenching distance or quenching diameter for several combustion gaseous mixtures 
[Holm, 1932]. 
3.2 
Approach 
In this study the sun shading system will be investigated several times with different meshes or holes in 
the perforated steel plate. The designed of the sun shading system is kept very simple so the different 
designs can be easier evaluated. 
In section 4 is the test method and the theoretical approach described and in section 5 is the experi-
mental method and the assumption described. 
The main purpose of this study is to investigate the performance of sun shading systems as a passive fire 
protection for a wooden façade. The sun shading is therefore used as a flame barrier. The criteria for suc-
cess is completely to avoid fire spread on the façade. 
3.3 
Hypothesis 
The planned experiments are expected to provide another passive fire protection solution to avoid fire spread 
on the façade to incorporate multi-purpose elements in the building industry than horizontal oriented metal 
plate that protrudes. The passive fire protection solution is expected to increase the use of wood with its natural 
properties regarding reaction to fire and thus not apply any chemical or other components which need to be 
maintained in its lifetime. 
The statement of intent is: 
Can the sun shading system prevent vertical fire spread to an untreated wooden façade by incorporation 
flame quenching theory as a design parameter?

4. 
Theory 
4.1 
Façade test  
If the building design is consisted of a wood façade on a taller building than described in the Danish Building 
Regulations and the guidelines, then the performance can be documented by the fire safety engineer, which will 
state the criteria of performance but only if it is in conformity with the third party fire safety engineer. 
In already constructed buildings that comply with the Danish Building Regulations (BR18), where the height ex-
ceeds the limits set by the guidelines and if the building regarding to fire safety are high complexity, then some 
building façade has been documented through fire testing following the principles of the upcoming method 
“Assessment of fire performance of façades using large fire exposure” [J. Anderson, 2024] which is based on BS 
8414 [BS8414-2:2020, 2020]. In buildings where the complexity are much lower, then the fire safety engineer 
can use a simplified method to document the fire safety solutions. 
In connection with the work on the upcoming fire performance of façades, a medium-scale method has also 
been developed. The method called “Assessment of fire performance of façades using medium fire exposure” 
[J. Anderson, 2024]. 
The fire performance of façade and sun shading design will be in accordance with the principles in the draft re-
vision 8 of “Assessment of fire performance of façades using medium fire exposure” [J. Anderson, 2024]. In the 
following subsections, the most important parts of the method are described.

Test equipment 
The test equipment – as mentioned in the method – consists of the following subsections.

construction is design with openings in the main face for the combustion chamber and a secondary opening. 
The secondary opening shall be 1200 mm in the width and height. The position shall be located 1000 mm 
above the combustion chamber and 500 mm from the finished corner. 
The supporting construction shall consist of masonry, e.g. aerated or light weight concrete blocks or slabs, and

mock-up of the materials.

6.2.1.5 
Discussion 
The wood crib developed a smaller flame exposure beneath the sun shading concerning the area. The equa-
tions concerning HRR (4.1) including the equations to determine the MLR, are developed with assumptions that 
not are similarly to the experimental method. The assumptions for the equations are in this case: 
- 
The wood crib is ignited by a small amount of liquid positioned beneath the wood crib (center-ignited). 
- 
The effective heat of combustion is taken to be 12 ⋅103𝑘𝐽 𝑘𝑔−1. 
The wood crib was ignited at the end of the stick which resulting in a slower fire propagation compared to the 
expected assumptions prior to the equations. The specific effective heat of combustion was not determined and 
the mass loss rate for the wood crib was not measured during the experiment. These circumstances can affect 
the determination of the heat release rate. 
Furthermore, the moisture content for the wood crib on the experimental day was not determined prior to the 
experimental and the gaseous composition was neither deemed during the experiment. 
The observed flame length or the flame extension beneath the sun shading was similarly to the determined val-
ues. The determined temperature on the sun shading was relative comparable for the points 1.25 m and 0.45 m 
(524-538°C), 1.45 m and 0.65 m (425-452°C), 1.45 m and 0.85 (359-397°C), respectively. 
The measured temperature on the sun shading was in the experimental period in generally higher than deter-
mined and the temperature exceeding 538°C after 227 seconds of testing at thermocouple SU4.1. The devia-
tions are assumed to be the difference between the prerequisites for the equations and the experimental condi-
tions. The assumptions for the equations are e.g.: 
- 
Mean flame (4.5): is depended on the HRR equation (4.1), developed for circular fuel sources (“best fit” 
for pool fires) and free-burning fire. 
- 
Flame extension (4.6): is based on equation (4.5) and developed to unconfined ceiling. 
-

6.2.2.5 
Discussion 
The wood crib developed a smaller flame exposure beneath the sun shading concerning the area and a shorter 
flame length or the flame extension beneath the sun shading compared to the determined values. These cir-
cumstances are assumed to be possible due to the vary materials in the wood crib and as mentioned in section 
6.2.1.5 concerning the assumption for the equations. As mentioned in section 6.2.1.5 the specific effective heat 
of combustion, the mass loss rate, the moisture content and the gaseous composition for the wood crib was 
not measured. 
The measured temperature on the sun shading was in the experiment period in generally higher than deter-
mined and the temperature exceeding 538°C after 211 seconds of testing at thermocouple SU1.2. The measure-
ments are assumed to be representative and comparable with the collected data from experimental 1. The tem-
perature development are also expected to increase further if the experiment not was been stopped. 
The wood crib was extinguished immediately after flame propagation to prevent the flame spread to the clad-
ding why the experimental period was relatively short. 
The perforated steel plate had a slightly larger diameter than the determined quenching diameter, but the 
flame exposure was still continued in the horizontal direction for a short period before the flame propagate the 
steel plate. The determination of the quenching diameter are based on simplified method where the gaseous 
mixture are assumed based on theory with pyrolysis of wood in controlled environmental – in a reactor – and 
by an expected weighted average (4.10). Therefore, the quenching diameter are determined with uncertainty 
close to this hole size. The temperature reached high temperature within a short period of testing. This indicates 
that the amount of energy on the upper side of the sun shading probably exceeding the minimum ignition en-
ergy [Babrauskas, 2016b] for an unidentified gas, which propagates the perforated steel plates. This led to the 
gas being ignited, probably due to the gas exceeding the lower flammability limit (LFL) [Zabetakis, 1964]. This 
phenomenon is expected when the steel plate exceeds the ability to obtain the heat. 
The experiment was not video recorded due to the battery ran out at the commencement of the experiment.

7. 
Conclusion 
This study was made at the Technical University of Denmark and with experiments conducted at Nordsjællands 
Brandskole (fire fighter school) with the view to determining how a sun shading system can prevent the vertical 
fire spread to an untreated wood façade. This passive fire protection has become relevant as wooden façades 
are increasingly being used and expected to be used more in the building industry in mid-rise and high-rise 
buildings. 
The wood crib performed very consistent throughout all three experiments; however, the theory and the equa-
tions were modified to the specific set-up. The prerequisites in the equations were not aligned with the experi-
mental conditions, but with the mentioned assumptions, regarding the flame length or extension was also con-
sistent throughout all three experiments. The prerequisites in the equations regarding the determined tempera-
ture in the centerline were lower than measured, which indicates that the plume region probably could be de-
termined as continuous flame region instead of an intermittent region. The measured temperatures are as-
sumed to be valid for each experimental result regardless of the discrepancy regarding how to determine the 
temperature in the centerline. 
The performance of the perforated steel plates in experimental 1 (type 1 – R0.75T1.5) is proven to be effective 
in preventing vertical fire spread where the ability to quench the flames was demonstrated. The measured tem-
perature on the unexposed side of the perforated steel plate shows that the temperature was exceeding 600°C 
after 273 seconds of testing, which is equivalent to a period of approximately 14 ½ minutes at high tempera-
ture. 
The performance of the perforated steel plate in experimental 2 (type 2 – R1.5T2.5) is proven to be effective in 
preventing vertical fire spread for a short period of time, probably due to the fact that the hole size quenched 
the flame but not the unburned gaseous mixture. The measured temperature on the unexposed side of the per-
forated steel plate shows that the temperature was exceeding 600°C after 245 seconds of testing which was 28 
seconds earlier than experimental 1. 
The performance of the perforated steel plate in experimental 3 (type 3 – R2T3.5) is proven not to prevent verti-
cal fire spread, probably due to the fact that the hole size was larger than the quenching diameter for the un-
burned gaseous mixture. The measured temperature on the unexposed side of the perforated steel plate shows 
that the temperature was exceeding 600°C after 256 seconds of testing which was 11 seconds later than experi-
mental 2. 
For all three experiments the period of high temperature indicating that the amount of energy on the upper 
side of the sun shading was exceeding the minimum ignition energy for an unidentified gas, which propagates 
the perforated steel plates. This led to the gas being ignited, probably due to the gas exceeding the lower flam-
mability limit (LFL), and the vertical fire spread. 
The performance of the perforated steel plate type 4 – R2.5T4 and type 5 – R3T5 was not investigated in this 
study since the ability to prevent vertical fire spread was not possible with smaller holes. 
The perforated steel plates that were investigated could not prevent the vertical fire spread for the whole exper-
imental duration. All three experiments were stopped before the wood crib was self-extinguished. The sun 
shading system in experimental 1 (type 1 – R0.75T1.5) could quench the flame and prevent the vertical fire 
spread after approximately 18 minutes of testing. 
The investigated solutions was exposed to the medium fire exposure, which is a smaller wood crib with a short 
test duration compared to the assessment method with a large fire exposure. Thus, the sun shading system 
must probably be redesigned before the solution is exposed to a larger exposure.

Outlook 
This study had been comprehensive but the method of quenching flames could be relevant concerning prevent 
fire spread in relation to the building industry. 
As mentioned in this study several assumptions are assumed. These assumptions could have influence on the 
expected experimental results and could be future studies. The future works could possible be: 
- 
Determining the gaseous mixture when pyrolysis wood used to wood cribs. 
- 
A comprehensive investigation of burning wood cribs combustion chambers. 
- 
A different design of the sun shading system, e.g. two layers of perforated plates or a solution with in-
tumescent paint. 
In addition to this topic it could be relevant to look into prevent the vertical fire spread on a wooden façade 
could be incorporated in already used building elements, so we develop multipurpose building elements.
