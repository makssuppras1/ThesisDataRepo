Abstract 
Bladder cancer is a major global health concern, ranking as the tenth most common malignancy 
worldwide. Non-muscle invasive bladder cancer (NMIBC) accounts for approximately 75% of 
cases, with Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) immunotherapy as the gold-standard treatment. 
However, BCG therapy faces significant challenges, including resistance, adverse effects, leading 
to discontinuation and a relapse rate of 40%. Additionally, global supply shortages have further 
limited its accessibility. These limitations highlight the need for alternative or complementary 
immunotherapeutic strategies. This MSc thesis explores the potential of Mycolicibacterium vaccae 
(M. vaccae) as an immunotherapeutic agent in contrast to BCG, with a specific focus on cancer 
immunotherapy. M. vaccae is a non-pathogenic mycobacterium that has gained clinical interest 
due to its immunomodulatory properties and favorable safety profile. Unlike BCG, which induces 
a strong Th1-mediated response, M. vaccae elicits a weaker but more controlled immune 
activation, modulating the tumor microenvironment with reduced inflammatory intensity. These 
characteristics suggest that M. vaccae may offer advantages over standard BCG, positioning it as 
a promising combination therapy. This study proposes a clinical trial framework to evaluate M. 
vaccae in combination with BCG for NMIBC treatment, assessing its safety, efficacy, and feasibility 
in reducing BCG dosage while maintaining therapeutic outcomes. By addressing the limitations of 
BCG therapy, this research contributes to the development of novel mycobacterial-based 
immunotherapies, potentially improving bladder cancer treatment strategies and overcoming 
BCG’s current challenges.

VIII 
 
Study Rationale and Hypothesis 
Bladder cancer is a significant global health concern, with non-muscle invasive bladder cancer 
(NMIBC) accounting for most cases. Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) immunotherapy has been 
the gold standard for high-risk NMIBC treatment for decades, effectively reducing recurrence and 
delaying progression post transurethral resection of the bladder tumor (TURBT). However, its 
limitations, including resistance, adverse effects, and global shortages, necessitate alternative or 
complementary immunotherapeutic strategies.1 
Mycolicibacterium vaccae (M. vaccae) is a promising candidate due to its immunomodulatory 
properties and favorable safety profile, demonstrated in clinical trials2. This study hypothesizes 
that M. vaccae can serve as an alternative or be used in combination with BCG by activating 
immune pathways with reduced toxicity, enhancing efficacy when co-administered, and providing 
a viable framework for clinical application in NMIBC. 
Objectives and Methodology  
This thesis evaluates the immunotherapeutic potential of M. vaccae compared to BCG. Through a 
comprehensive literature review, this research aims to: 
• 
Examine the mechanisms of action of both BCG and M. vaccae to gain a deeper understanding 
of their immunomodulatory properties and role in cancer immunotherapy. 
• 
Assess the efficacy of M. vaccae in cancer treatment, exploring its potential as an alternative 
agent for BCG immunotherapy. 
• 
Investigate M. vaccae’s role as an adjuvant in cancer therapy, evaluating its immunological 
advantages and potential synergies with BCG. 
• 
Develop a clinical trial framework to assess the safety and efficacy of M. vaccae in NMIBC. 
• 
Identify future research directions, addressing key gaps, challenges, and broader applications 
beyond bladder cancer. 
This study systematically reviews preclinical and clinical data, regulatory guidelines, and peer-
reviewed literature from databases such as PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science, accessed via 
DTU Findit. By providing an evidence-based assessment of M. vaccae’s existing studies, this 
thesis supports the development of novel mycobacterial-based immunotherapies for NMIBC

1 BCG in Cancer Immunotherapy  
BCG is a live attenuated strain of Mycobacterium bovis (M. bovis) initially developed as a vaccine 
to protect against tuberculosis (TB). Attenuation was achieved after 13 years of serial culture 
growth, which removed virulence genes, making it safe for human use. Developed by Albert 
Calmette and Camille-Guérin, the BCG vaccine stimulates the immune system without causing the 
disease. Introduced in 1921, it remains widely used, especially in countries with high TB incidence3.  
BCG’s ability to elicit a strong immune response has extended its application in oncology. Cancer 
immunotherapy involves various strategies that activate the immune system of patients to 
recognize and attack tumor cells. While tumor cells are usually recognized as foreign, they often 
evade the immune system's detections by inducing immunotolerance, allowing cancer 
progression4. 
Due to its immunostimulatory properties, BCG is currently considered an effective cancer 
immunotherapeutic agent, specifically for bladder cancer. Intravesical administration of BCG is the 
gold-standard treatment for high-risk NMIBC5 following TURBT. Clinical trials have reported 
response rates of approximately 65% and recurrence-free survival rates around 71.8%6.  
1.1 History of BCG as an immunotherapeutic agent 
Cancer immunotherapy originated in the 19th century, when observations revealed that infections 
like gas gangrene or erysipelas could lead to tumor regression4. In 1893, William Coley, known as 
the father of cancer immunotherapy, detected that bacterial infections could reduce tumor size and 
started systematically treating cancer patients with bacterial products, later known as Coley’s 
toxins. Although Coley’s toxins did not contain mycobacteria, his findings inspired future research 
into testing live bacteria in cancer treatment, including BCG.  
In the early 20th century, it was observed that there was a lower incidence of cancer among TB 
patients, however, the underlying mechanisms were unknown. This observation led to the 
development of BCG vaccine against TB in 1921, inspiring researchers to investigate its potential 
use in cancer treatment. BCG was first reported as successfully used in cancer in 1935, when 
Holmgren treated gastric cancer patients with repeated BCG doses. 
In the 1950s, studies on mice demonstrated that administering BCG prior to tumor exposure could 
prevent cancer development. Later, in 1969, Mathé et al. conducted a clinical trial using BCG for 
acute lymphoblastic leukemia in humans with promising results5. By 1970, BCG vaccination was 
associated with lower leukemia mortality in children under 15, with incidence twice as high in 
unvaccinated children, suggesting its immune-stimulating effects against cancer7. However, 
limitations in trial designs, variability in outcomes, and the widespread adoption of chemotherapy 
and radiotherapy in the mid-20th century curtailed further research into BCG as a cancer treatment 
4,5,8.   
Zbar and Rapp's 1974 study demonstrated that BCG-tumor contact, and delayed hypersensitivity 
could regress tumors and prevent metastases, identifying favorable conditions for success using 
a guinea pig model. In 1976, Morales et al. conducted the first human trial using BCG for superficial 
bladder cancer treatment. They showed that injecting BCG directly into the bladder effectively 
reduced the recurrence of NMIBC. In their study, patients received 120 mg of BCG diluted in saline 
over a two-hour retention period, repeated weekly for six weeks. This regimen left seven, out of 
ten patients, tumor-free at follow-up, with favorable outcomes in nine patients. Although 
maintenance schedules, extending over months or years, have been added to improve results, 
this induction therapy remains the basis of BCG treatment5,9,10,4.

1.2 BCG and Bladder Cancer 
1.2.1 Bladder Cancer Overview 
Bladder cancer is the 10th most prevalent cancer worldwide and ranks as the 13th leading cause 
of cancer-related deaths, according to GLOBOCAN 2020 estimates of cancer incidence. It is the 
most common malignancy of the urinary system, with rising incident rates, particularly in 
industrialized countries and predominantly among men. The main risk factor is smoking, 
responsible for approximately 90% of cases. While advancements in early detection, robotic 
surgery, and immunotherapy have improved outcomes, bladder cancer remains a significant and 
increasing global cancer burden, especially in high-income countries, with the highest lifetime 
treatment costs among all malignancies11–13. 
1.2.1.1 Epidemiology 
Bladder cancer ranking as the 10th most frequently diagnosed cancer globally, presents 
approximately 573,000 new cases and 212,536 deaths reported in 2020. It accounts for 3% of all 
cancer diagnoses and 2.1% of cancer-related mortality. Bladder cancer occurs nearly four times 
more frequently in men compared to women. Southern and Western Europe, along with North 
America, have the highest incidence rates, with Greece and Hungary leading for men and women, 
respectively, while Middle Africa and South-Central Asia report the lowest rates.  
Survival outcomes vary significantly by stage, with 5-year survival rates of 96% for in situ disease, 
69% for localized cases, 36% for regional spread, and just 4% for metastatic disease12.

1.2.1.2 Etiology and Risk Factors 
The bladder wall consists of four layers: mucosa, submucosa, muscularis, and serosa. The 
mucosal urothelium is a stratified, non-squamous epithelium approximately seven cells thick, 
topped by large umbrella cells. Most bladder cancers originate from these urothelial cells and are 
classified as urothelial carcinoma (also transitional cell carcinoma). Less common subtypes include 
squamous cell carcinoma, adenocarcinoma, small cell carcinoma, and mixed histologies. 
 
Smoking along with exposure to chemicals such as aromatic amines, arsenic-contaminated water, 
and chronic bladder irritation. Urothelial carcinoma, which accounts for 90% of cases, is strongly 
linked to tobacco smoke and chemical exposure due to the direct contact of urothelial cells with 
environmental toxins. In contrast, squamous cell carcinoma is more prevalent in regions such as 
Africa, where schistosomiasis, a parasitic infection causing chronic bladder inflammation, is 
endemic12. 
Chronic inflammation plays a pivotal role in bladder cancer pathogenesis, acting as both a risk 
factor and a driver of tumor progression. Several studies indicate that inflammatory processes 
contribute to carcinogenesis by promoting cell proliferation, angiogenesis, and immune evasion. 
Persistent inflammation, whether triggered by urinary tract infections, environmental toxins, or 
immune dysregulation, has been linked to an increased risk of bladder cancer development15. 
Bladder cancer has few hereditary links and predominantly affects older individuals, with >90% of 
diagnoses occurring after age 55. This highlights the extended process of carcinogenesis, driven 
by prolonged toxin exposure and the disruption of tumor-suppressor genes12.  
1.2.1.3 Histological Types of Bladder Cancer 
Urothelial cells line the bladder and urinary tract (including the urethra, ureters, and renal pelvis), 
adapting to fluctuations in urine volume through their stretchable structure. The smooth muscle 
layer facilitates bladder function by relaxing for storage and contracting for urine expulsion through 
the urethra.

As outlined above, bladder cancer is primarily categorized as: 
• 
Urothelial carcinoma or Transitional cell carcinoma (90%): comprises most cases and is 
linked to environmental exposures.  
• 
Squamous cell carcinoma (5%): often linked with chronic irritation or infection, such as 
schistosomiasis. 
• 
Adenocarcinoma (<2%): originated from mucus-producing glandular cells. 
• 
Small cell carcinoma: considered a rare subtype, originated in neuroendocrine cells12.  
Bladder cancer is further categorized by its progression within the bladder wall: 
• 
NMIBC: confined to the bladder lining and the most common form, accounting for about 70% 
of cases. 
• 
Muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC): penetrates the muscle wall or extends beyond it, 
posing a higher risk of metastasis and a poorer prognosis11,16. 
1.2.1.4 Diagnosis 
Bladder cancer diagnosis often begins with detecting painless hematuria, prompting clinical 
evaluation, including medical history, physical examination, upper urinary tract imaging, and 
cystoscopy, the gold standard for initial diagnosis. Traditionally, cystoscopy uses white light that 
enables direct tumor visualization, biopsy, fulguration, or resection, providing essential staging 
information for treatment planning17. 
Recent advancements like blue light fluorescence cystoscopy and narrow-band imaging have 
improved tumor detection, particularly for carcinoma in situ (CIS), a high-grade, flat, non-invasive 
lesion confined to the urothelium. These new technologies exploit metabolic and vascular 
differences in cancerous tissues, increasing diagnostic accuracy, though cost and false-positive 
rates are limitations17,18. 
Additionally, imaging methods like computed tomography (CT) urography and magnetic resonance 
imaging (MRI) play a vital role in detecting metastases.  Adjunct tools, including urine cytology and 
biomarkers (e.g., NMP22, UroVysion), offer additional diagnostic data but have limitations for low-
grade tumors detection. Emerging non-invasive biomarkers like circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA), 
show promise in early detection and recurrence monitoring.For high-grade tumors, repeat TURBT 
within six weeks ensures complete resection and correct tumor staging. This, helps to guide 
NMIBC management while improving outcomes18. 
1.2.1.5 Risk Stratification  
Not all bladder cancers are the same, differing in biological behaviors, likelihood and risk of 
progression. Risk stratification is an important tool for achieving optimal patient outcomes while 
avoiding overtreatment. It involves categorizing bladder cancer based on clinical and pathological 
factors such as tumor stage, grade, size, biomarkers, among other factors.  
For NMIBC, given the wide range of treatment possibilities for surveillance to radical cystectomy, 
risk stratification guides whether to monitor or intervene aggressively. For MIBC, advanced 
diagnostics and molecular profiling has enabled personalized care beyond the historical “one-size-
fits-all” (standard) approach17. 
CIS, considered a different subset of NMIBC, often presents a worse prognosis due to its biological 
heterogenicity and the difficulty of complete resection. It can manifest as a primary, simultaneous, 
or secondary disease, which increases the complexity of its clinical outcome19.

1.2.1.5.1 Tumor Stages  
Bladder cancer is stratified using the Tumor-Node-Metastasis (TNM) system to assess tumor 
expansion, lymph node invasion, and the presence of metastases. This guides a critical treatment 
strategy choice. NMIBC includes Ta (tumors confined to the urothelium), T1 (tumors invading the 
lamina propria), and CIS (flat lesion frequently associated with T1 disease). MIBC includes T2 
(tumors invading the muscularis propria), T3 (extension into perivesical fat), and T4 (invasion into

1.2.1.5.2 Tumor Grading 
Tumor grading further refines the classification, assessing differentiation and malignant potential, 
and is most relevant in NMIBC. The WHO classification for NMIBC has undergone two major 
refinements, in 1973 and 2004/2016, both of which remain in use. Each system employs a three-
tier grading approach. The WHO 1973 classification assigns tumors to Grade 1 (low-grade), with 
well-differentiated cells and minimal progression risk; Grade 2 (intermediate-grade), showing 
moderate abnormalities and an intermediate progression risk; and Grade 3 (high-grade), 
characterized by poorly differentiated cells with a higher risk of recurrence, invasion, and 
metastasis.

1.2.1.6 Prognosis 
 
At the initial diagnosis, around 75% of patients present with NMIBC. Amont those treated with 
TURBT but without additional treatment, about 50% experience progression to MIBC or develop 
metastases. Among patients with MIBC, 10–15% exhibit metastatic disease at diagnosis. Despite 
radical cystectomy, approximately 50% of MIBC patients experience disease recurrence, with one-
third developing local recurrences and the remaining two-thirds progressing to distant metastases.

1.3 BCG as the Gold Standard Treatment for NMIBC  
Compared to TURBT alone, BCG therapy significantly reduces recurrence and progression risks 
in NMIBC patients and remains the only treatment proven to lower progression risk. AUA and EAU 
guidelines for NMIBC recommend an induction course of BCG for high-risk disease consisting of 
six weekly instillations, starting a few weeks after tumor resection. This means the treatment is 
administered once per week for six consecutive weeks without interruption unless complications 
or patient-specific adjustments arise. Following induction, a maintenance schedule is 
implemented, with additional BCG instillations every three to six months for one to three years. 
The duration of the maintenance therapy can vary depending on the clinical practice. The AUA 
recommends three years of maintenance BCG for high-risk patients, while the EAU suggests one 
to three years for both intermediate- and high-risk groups19. In the maintenance course, BCG can 
be administered full dose or reduced by 1/2 or 1/3 to minimize toxicity.  
The therapy has demonstrated complete response rates of 55–65% for high-risk papillary tumors 
and 70-75% for CIS. However, 25-45% of patients do not benefit from the therapy, and about 40% 
experience a relapse despite initial success. Adverse effects are not uncommon, leading to 
intolerance in 20% of patients, which can alter treatment schedules5.

1.3.2  Follow-up after BCG Therapy 
The EAU guidelines recommend regular follow-up for NMIBC patients receiving BCG therapy to 
monitor recurrence and progression. All patients undergo an initial cystoscopy at three months 
post-TURB, which helps assess treatment response and predict future recurrence. 
For intermediate- and high-risk patients, surveillance involves periodic cystoscopy and cytology, 
with additional imaging, such as CT urography, for high-risk cases to detect upper urinary tract 
involvement. Techniques such as blue-light cystoscopy and urinary biomarkers are being 
investigated to enhance detection, though cystoscopy remains the gold standard. The intensity 
and frequency of follow-up are tailored to the patient's risk profile, with high-risk patients requiring 
closer monitoring27.

1.3.3 BCG Strains, Manufacturers and Shortages 
BCG strains have diverged over decades through serial subculturing in different laboratories, 
leading to genetic and phenotypic variations that affect immunogenicity. Early strains, such as 
Russia, Moreau, and Japan, and later strains, including Tice, Connaught, and Danish, exhibit 
distinct genetic and antigenic profiles that influence their therapeutic efficacy. 
 
Manufacturers of BCG include Organon-Merck (OncoTICE®), Medac GmbH (BCG-
Medac/Vejicur®), Japan BCG Laboratory (Immunobladder®), Green Signal Bio Pharma 
(Urovac™/BCG-Onco), and the Serum Institute of India (SII-Onco BCG). However, BCG 
production remains highly complex due to strict biosafety measures. Additionally, as a slow-
growing mycobacterium, its prolonged bacterial growth cycle limits scalability28. Supply disruptions 
have been exacerbated by regulatory barriers, facility closures, and the high demand for NMIBC 
treatment, leading to periodic shortages worldwide1.

flexibility, and continued research into alternative therapies1.  
 
1.3.4 Limitations and Challenges of BCG in NMIBC. 
Despite its historical success, BGC faces significant challenges, primarily due to variability in 
patient responses and associated complications. Approximately 30-40% of patients do not achieve 
a satisfactory response within two years of BCG treatment, which often results in disease 
recurrence or progression in high-grade T1 and carcinoma in CIS cases, without a standardized 
treatment strategy post-failure32,33.  
Factors such as the biological tumor characteristics, including grade and stage, and the patient's 
immune status, can contribute to this lack of response to BCG. As noted, the ability to generate an 
effective immune response against BCG antigens is critical for its efficacy; therefore, 
immunocompromised patients may not respond adequately. This issue particularly affects 
individuals with health problems or those undergoing immunosuppressive therapies, as the risk of 
severe side effects can outweigh BCG’s potential benefits34.  
Additionally, a significant portion of patients experience side effects, contributing to therapy 
discontinuation. For instance, only 16% of patients on a maintenance BCG regimen completed the 
full therapy due to complications, underscoring the need to carefully balance therapeutic benefits 
against adverse effects35.  
Therefore, while intravesical BCG therapy has been the standard for high-risk NMIBC patients for 
over 30 years, its challenges underscore the need for alternative or complementary approaches to 
improve outcomes and reduce side effects.

1.3.4.1 Complications of Intravesical BCG Therapy 
Approximately 69.5% of patients undergoing BCG therapy report complications, which can be 
classified as local or systemic. Local complications, such as cystitis (the most common), dysuria, 
and bladder irritation, affect over 80% of patients and can significantly impact the quality of life33,36. 
In contrast, systemic complications, though less frequent, can be severe, including disseminated 
BCG infections (BCGosis), which may progress to BCG sepsis, often requiring hospitalization and 
intensive treatment37.  
Many complications appear months or even years after treatment, making early diagnosis difficult. 
Given that a significant proportion of patients cannot tolerate these side effects, their occurrence 
contributes to poor compliance with BCG therapy, leading to treatment discontinuation in 
approximately 7.8% of patients. Furthermore, the lack of standardized guidelines for managing 
severe reactions exacerbates clinical challenges35,37. 
At the same time, BCG's relationship with TB is a concern. Derived from a strain of M. bovis, BCG 
can induce TB-like infections, especially in immunocompromised patients or those with a TB 
history. Therefore, intravesical BCG instillation poses a risk of renal TB, and careful patient 
screening and monitoring is needed38. Additionally, the occurrence of asymptomatic bacteriuria in 
patients receiving BCG therapy further calls into question its safety39. 
1.3.4.1.1 Reducing adverse effects  
To mitigate BCG-related complications, efforts have focused on modifying formulations, optimizing 
treatment regimens, and reducing toxicity. One approach is lowering the BCG dose, which has 
been shown to reduce adverse effects while maintaining therapeutic efficacy in selected patients. 
Other strategies include purified mycobacterial antigens (e.g., MPT-64, Ag85, and mycobacterial 
DNA) and reformulated BCG cell wall extracts, such as liposomal delivery systems, which aim to 
enhance stability while minimizing toxicity. Additionally, non-viable mycobacteria (e.g., heat-killed 
and γ-irradiated preparations) have demonstrated the ability to retain antigenic profiles while 
eliciting weaker inflammatory responses4,35.

1.3.4.2 Failure of BCG Therapy 
To improve BCG's therapeutic efficacy and minimize toxicity, it is essential to understand its 
immunological mechanisms. BCG is believed to induce an anti-tumor response by recruiting 
immune cells to the tumor site, creating localized inflammation. However, the exact pathways 
involved in this process remain unclear. Variability in immune responses contributes to BCG 
resistance and treatment failure. In BCG terminology, treatment failure can be classified into the 
following categories40:  
• 
BCG-unresponsive (as per FDA guidelines) occurs when at least one of the following criteria 
is met:  
o

completion of adequate BCG therapy 
o

therapy

•

o

o

o

• 
BCG-relapsing is defined as recurrence within six months after achieving a disease-free 
period, including the recurrence of G3/high-risk tumors after BCG maintenance despite initial 
success. 
 
• 
BCG-intolerant describes treatment discontinuation of a full induction course, due to adverse 
side effects.  
These definitions play a pivotal role in determining the next steps in management, including options 
like continued BCG therapy, pursuing alternative treatments, or considering a radical cystectomy 
based on the classification of BCG failure33.  
Bladder-preserving strategies, including immunotherapy, chemotherapy, device-assisted 
therapies, radiotherapy, and experimental approaches like genetically engineered adenovirus and 
non-live BCG, show promising clinical trial results but lack sufficient long-term data. Therefore, 
radical cystectomy remains an important strategy for patients who do not respond to these 
therapies, due to its proven oncological effectiveness. However, while this surgical option offers 
potential survival benefits, it also carries significant risks and morbidity, particularly for older or frail 
patients33,40.

also includes Gemcitabine and Taxanes (such as Docetaxel and Paclitaxel), which directly target 
cancer cells within the bladder, minimizing systemic side effects. These drugs can be administered

1.3.5.2 Device-Assisted Therapies  
Electromotive Drug Administration (EMDA) is a technology that improves MMC absorption in the 
bladder, achieving satisfactory recurrence-free intervals when combined with BCG. Additionally, 
chemo-hyperthermia is a technology that encompasses systems like Synergo and HIVEC

respectively, to increase chemotherapy absorption in bladder tissue. Synergo uses microwave-
induced hyperthermia, while HIVEC maintains a heated MMC solution via a recirculating system. 
Studies indicate these approaches produce higher recurrence-free survival rates, often around 
50% over several years, with better outcomes than standard intravesical chemotherapy33,40. 
 
1.3.5.3 Viral-based agents 
Viruses act as vectors for delivering genetic material into tumor cells with high specificity. This 
promising approach allows efficient intravesical administration and demonstrates higher efficacy 
than BCG. Their targeted nature enables selective cell entry, potentially reducing adverse effects. 
Recent clinical trials highlight viral vectors as a viable alternative or complement for NMIBC 
immunotherapy, particularly in BCG-unresponsive patients1. 
1.3.5.4 Bacterial-based agents 
To mitigate BCG-related adverse effects, safer immunotherapeutic options include non-viable 
mycobacteria, other bacterial species, or bacterial components. Salmonella has shown strong 
immune activation with fewer doses than BCG and is in clinical trials (e.g., NCT03421236). Other 
bacterial-based approaches, such as Lactobacillus and Pseudomonas aeruginosa-derived 
vaccines, are also investigated. Among mycobacterial alternatives, Mycolicibacterium phlei Cell 
Wall-Nucleic Acid Complex (MCNA) is being tested for BCG-resistant patients, while 
Mycolicibacterium brumae (M. Brumae) has shown promising antitumor effects in preclinical 
studies. This thesis will evaluate the role of Mycolicibacterium vaccae (M. vaccae) as a potential 
immunotherapeutic agent1,41,42. 
1.3.5.5 Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors  
Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) provide a promising and non-surgical alternative for cancer 
treatment, by blocking proteins that inhibit immune activation. Particularly, ICIs that target PD-
1/PD-L1 and CTLA-4 (Cytotoxic T-Lymphocyte Antigen 4) pathways enhance T-cell immune 
responses and have shown effectiveness in various cancers. Given their success in treating 
metastatic urothelial carcinoma, ICIs are currently being tested for NMIBC, especially for BCG-
resistant patients33,40,43.  
Pembrolizumab, the only FDA-approved ICI for BCG-unresponsive NMIBC, demonstrated a 40.6% 
complete response rate at three months with durable effects in the KEYNOTE-057 trial. Other 
investigational ICIs include Atezolizumab, Avelumab, Durvalumab, and Nivolumab. Ongoing 
clinical trials are exploring their safety and efficacy alone or in combination with treatments like 
radiation and BCG 33,40,43.

1.3.5.6 Gene Therapy 
Nadofaragene firadenovec (Adstiladrin®) is an intravesical gene therapy also showing promise. It

cells' ability to produce interferon-alpha (IFN-α), leading to a stronger immune response against 
the tumor. This therapy has been approved by the FDA for treating BCG-unresponsive NMIBC33,40.

26.6-month median response duration. Treatment follows an induction and maintenance schedule 
for up to 37 months, depending on patient response. Reported side effects include urinary 
symptoms and immune-related reactions. 
Nogapendekin alfa inbakicept is also being investigated for other indications such as non-small-

Ongoing trials aim to expand its applications and optimize its therapeutic strategies44,45.

1.3.6 Future Directions for NMBC Treatment 
BCG immunotherapy remains a cornerstone in the treatment of high-risk NMIBC and has 
demonstrated improved overall patient survival. However, significant challenges persist, such as 
clinical complications and patient compliance issues. A deeper understanding of BCG´s 
immunological action mechanism is essential to improve clinical responses and enable tailored 
NMIBC treatments. Future research should focus on elucidating these mechanisms, optimizing 
treatment protocols, and mitigating adverse effects.   
Reliable biomarkers for predicting BCG response remain lacking. Several candidates, including 
Ki67 (a proliferation marker), IL-8 (a pro-inflammatory and angiogenic factor), and PD-L1 (an 
immune evasion marker), are under investigation, but none have received FDA approval due to 
challenges in standardization and clinical validation. Some biomarker panels, such as CyPRIT, 
have shown promise in experimental settings, offering potential tools for patient stratification. As 
personalized treatment approaches, such as gene therapy and ICIs, gain traction in clinical trials, 
ongoing research into predictive biomarkers in NMIBC remains essential33. 
BCG could be improved by designing more effective BCG strains or by exploring synergistic 
combinations of BCG with other treatments. To address these limitations, researchers have also 
explored other non-pathogenic mycobacterial species with immunostimulatory properties as 
potential alternatives to BCG.  
1.4 BCG and Immune Activation in Cancer Therapy 
1.4.1 Anticancer Responses  
The approved use of BCG in cancer immunotherapy underscores the critical role of the immune 
system in cancer control. Cancer cells express a variety of tumor-associated antigens (TAAs), 
some of which are uniquely recognized by the innate immune system, while others share structural 
similarities with bacterial antigens and are detected by the adaptive immune system. 
The immune system can recognize both endogenous and exogenous danger signals. Endogenous 
signals, such as stress proteins and heat shock proteins (HSPs), are produced in response to 
tissue damage or tumor-associated abnormalities. These alarm signals trigger immune responses 
through Pattern Recognition Receptors (PRRs) such as Toll-like receptors (TLRs) and Nucleotide-
binding Oligomerization Domain (NOD)-like receptors (NLRs). Dysregulated immune regulation is 
thus a key factor in cancer pathogenesis. 
TLRs, widely expressed in urothelial and immune cells, including dendritic cells (DCs) and 
macrophages, initiate intracellular signaling cascades that promote cytokine secretion (IL-12, IL-8, 
and IFN-γ), recruit immune cells, and enhance antigen presentation. Among them, TLR2, TLR4, 
and TLR9 play a key role in urothelial immune surveillance, detecting bacterial components and 
maintaining bladder homeostasis. 
BCG and other mycobacterial antigens exhibit potent immunomodulatory properties, influencing T 
cell differentiation and activation. These antigens stimulate the release of pro-inflammatory 
cytokines through Pathogen-Associated Molecular Patterns (PAMPs), such as lipoglycans and 
lipopeptides, as well as Danger-Associated Molecular Patterns (DAMPs), such as HSPs, which 
activate TLRs and foster a tumor microenvironment favorable to anticancer responses. This 
immunomodulation plays a crucial role in the development of distinct T-helper (Th) and cytotoxic 
T cell (Tc) populations, which can have antagonistic roles in antitumor immunity.

Most cells undergoing malignant transformation are detected and destroyed by the immune 
system. However, some malignant cells can evade this immune surveillance, through mechanisms 
that allow them to escape immune responses repeatedly, ultimately leading to tumor development. 
This process is known as immunoediting.  
Therefore, immunotherapeutic interventions should not only activate immune responses against 
tumor-specific epitopes but also redirect immune regulation pathways. One promising approach is 
to combine tumor-specific epitopes, recognized by the adaptive immune system, with 
mycobacterial adjuvants that modulate the innate immune system's regulatory functions, thereby 
enhancing antitumor immunity2,46. 
1.4.2 Mechanisms of Mycobacterial Antigen Immunostimulation 
While the specific molecules in BCG responsible for its anti-cancer response are unknown, 
researchers have identified multiple mycobacterial antigens with immunostimulation capacity. The 
mycobacterial cell wall is rich in lipids, glycolipids, glycans, and proteins, that are recognized by 
the immune receptors. Beyond cell wall components, other fractions have been shown to induce 
Th1 activity and stimulate cytotoxic activity against T24 bladder cancer cells, a human bladder 
carcinoma line largely used in cancer research.  
Molecules such as lipoarabinomannan (LAM), phosphatidylinositol mannosides (PIMs), and HSPs 
are detected by surface receptors of Antigen Precenting Cells (APCs) such as TLR 2 and TLR4, 
or mannose receptors. Additionally, HSPs act as critical alarm signals, binding to TLR4 and NLR 
receptors. Antigens like trehalose mono- (TMM) and dimycolate (TDM), are recognized by C-type 
lectin receptors (CRLs), which further enhance the immune response. 
Other mycobacterial antigens, upon internalization into the cell, interact with specific intracellular 
immune receptors. For instance, unmethylated cytosine-guanosine nucleotide (CpG)-rich DNA 
motifs, muramyl dipeptide (MDP), and cytosolic DNA bind specifically to TLR-9, NOD2, and cyclic 
GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS), respectively. Once being processed inside the cell, some antigens 
are presented to T cells via CD1 receptors, such as mycolic acids (MA), trehalose, and glucose 
mycolates. The signaling through these receptors promotes the release of cytokines and 
chemokines, which leads to a pro-inflammatory response that supports antitumor activity within 
the tumor microenvironment4.  
Not all mycobacteria possess the same antigens, and there are significant variations in antigen 
structures across species. While LAM, TDM and MA are common to all mycobacteria, structural 
differences (such as the presence or absence of mannose residues in LAM or variations in the 
length, saturation, and oxygenation of lipid chains in TDM and MA) can significantly influence their 
interactions with the immune receptors and the resulting immune responses. For example, BCG 
predominantly induces the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, whereas other non-
pathogenic mycobacteria, such as M. vaccae, exhibit a more regulated immune profile, balancing 
inflammation with regulatory effects. 
Mycobacteria have an important antigenic complexity, with strains of the same species often 
exhibiting different antigenic profiles. A notable example is the BCG vaccine, which originated from 
a seed strain that, after decades of subculturing in various countries, has undergone substantial 
genomic variations and resulted in numerous distinct BCG strains. Today, approximately ten BCG 
strains are used worldwide, primarily for the treatment of bladder cancer and TB vaccination2,4. 
Some BCG strains contain antigens such as phenol glycolipids, phthiocerol dimycocerosates, and 
the MBT64 protein, while others differ in their MA profiles, possessing either alpha-, methoxy-, and 
keto-MA or only alpha- and methoxy-MA. However, despite extensive research, the specific 
antigens responsible for BCG's efficacy as an antitumor agent or TB vaccine remain unidentified4.

1.4.3 T Cells in Antitumor Immunity 
T cells play a crucial role in cancer immunity by recognizing and killing tumor cells. They mature 
into different cell populations that produce various types of cytokines. Specifically, Th cells can 
mature into Th1 or Th2 populations, secreting Type 1 cytokines (e.g., IL-2, IFN-γ) or Type   
cytokines (e.g., IL-4, IL-10). Similarly, CTLs differentiate into Tc1 and Tc2 populations, producing 
the same cytokine profiles.  
Type 1 cytokines, mainly produced by Th1 and Tc1 cells, are critical for effective antitumor 
responses, driving pro-inflammatory and cytotoxic pathways. In contrast, Type 2 cytokines, 
produced by Th2 and Tc2 cells, can suppress antitumor immunity and allow tumor progression.  
Advanced cancers often exhibit a Th2/Tc2 shift, which is correlated with poor prognosis and 
immune escape. Mycobacterial antigens, including those from BCG, can reverse this shift by 
stimulating Th1-dominant immune responses via TLR and NLR signaling pathways. 
Simultaneously, regulatory T cells (Tregs) can prevent excessive immune suppression while 
maintaining anti-tumor activity.

1.4.4 BCG’s Immunological Mechanism in Bladder Cancer  
The exact BCG’s mechanism of action in bladder cancer therapy remains unclear. Its efficacy is 
attributed to a combination of direct tumor cell interactions and a robust immune response. 
Intravesical BCG administration following TURBT exposes residual tumor cells to high 
concentrations of live mycobacteria, initiating a complex sequence of immune events.  
These mechanisms involve several interdependent phases: 
1. BCG attachment and internalization: upon entering the bladder, BCG adheres to the 
urothelium by binding to extracellular matrix components, particularly fibronectin. Fibronectin 
links  ibronectin attachment protein (FAP) on BCG to integrin α5β  on urothelial cells, 
facilitating adhesion on the bladder wall 5,9,33,47.  
 
BCG is then internalized by both malignant urothelial cells and resident immune cells. 
Macrophages and DCs internalize BCG through phagocytosis, enabling subsequent antigen 
presentation to T cells. Bladder cancer cells also actively internalize BCG via oncogene-driven 
macropinocytosis, but their ability to present antigens is limited. Variability in BCG uptake 
among patients may influence therapy responsiveness48.

2. Induction of immune responses: once internalized, bladder cancer cells increase the 
expression of MHC class II and ICAM-1 and secrete cytokines. The production of cytokines, 
along with the involvement of DCs, leads to the infiltration of immune cells into the bladder 
lumen4.  
 
o 
Innate immunity activation: BCG triggers an innate immune response through the 
recognition of its PAMPs by PRRs, including TLR2, TLR4, and TLR9, complement 
receptors, NLR (e.g., NOD2), CLRs, and dendritic cell-specific intercellular adhesion 
molecule-3-grabbing non-integrin (DC-SIGN). Activation of TLRs triggers the MyD88 
signaling pathway, leading to the production of proinflammatory cytokines such as IL-6, IL-
8, granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF), and TNF-α, which recruit 
immune cells into the bladder.  
 
Neutrophils, the main infiltrating cells post-BCG instillation, drive tumor destruction through 
TNF-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL) secretion. TRAIL is a key cytokine that 
induces apoptosis in cancer cells via TNF-family death receptors while largely avoiding 
normal cells.  
 
Macrophages phagocytose BCG and tumor debris while releasing cytokines that shape the 
immune response. DCs capture BCG antigens and present them to T cells, serving as a 
key link between innate immune activation and subsequent adaptive immunity. NK cells 
exhibit cytotoxicity against BCG-infected cancer cells, and T cells contribute to the adaptive 
response. Repeated BCG instillations amplify this response through a "prime-boost" effect, 
enhancing antitumor activity5,47,48,48. 
 
o 
Adaptive immunity activation: BCG activates adaptive immunity by stimulating CD4⁺ Th 
cells and CD8⁺ CTLs, both essential for tumor cell elimination. It induces a Th1-dominant 
response (IFN-γ, IL-2, and IL-12), enhancing CTL cytotoxicity against bladder cancer. 
Additionally, studies indicate that BCG activates peripheral blood Vδ + γδ T cells,  urther 
contributing to Th1 cytokine production and increased cytotoxic activity against tumors49.  
Conversely, a predominant Th2 response (IL-4, IL-10) is linked to poor BCG efficacy.   
 
The interplay between innate and adaptive immunity is therefore essential for achieving a 
robust and sustained antitumor activity5,47. 
 
3. Tumor-Specific Immunity: unlike general inflammation caused by BCG exposure, tumor-
specific immunity requires the immune system to recognize bladder cancer cells as persistent 
targets. A key factor in this process is the modification of the tumor microenvironment (TME). 
BCG induces epigenetic changes in innate immune cells (monocytes and macrophages), a 
phenomenon known as trained immunity, which enhances their tumor-recognition abilities 
even after BCG is cleared. These trained immune cells continue to produce cytokines and 
present antigens, ensuring long-term immune surveillance and tumor control5, 9.  
 
Additionally, APCs cross-present tumor antigens, allowing CTLs to develop a more precise 
immune response, beyond the initial inflammation triggered by BCG. Repeated BCG 
instillations strengthen adaptive immunity through a "prime-boost" effect, reinforcing immune 
memory against bladder cancer. However, patient responses vary due to differences in tumor 
antigen expression, immune evasion strategies, and individual variations in trained immunity, 
which makes BCG’s efficacy unpredictable48.

1.4.4.1 Immunological Factors contributing BCG Failure 
Several mechanisms contribute to BCG therapy failure, including deficits in immune modulation, 
an immunosuppressive TME:  
Deficits in immune modulation 
Bladder cancer cells exhibit a general downregulation of TLRs compared to normal urothelial cells. 
TLRs, particularly TLR2 and TLR4, play a key role in initiating the inflammatory cascade necessary 
for BCG-induced immune activation. Low TLR activity hinders BCG internalization, limiting cytokine 
production crucial for DC maturation and T-cell recruitment. Notably, low-grade tumors show better 
response rates than high-grade tumors, suggesting an inverse correlation between tumor severity 
and BCG efficacy33,46. 
Immunosuppressive TME 
The composition of the TME can significantly influence BCG responsiveness. A TME composition 
abundant in exhausted T cells and Tregs is associated with BCG failure, as these immune cells 
suppress antitumor activity. Additionally, non-responding patients often exhibit high levels of PD-
L1 expression, which further inhibits CTLs activation and prevents an effective immune response. 
This has led to an increasing interest in combining ICIs with BCG therapy to counteract immune 
evasion33.

1.5 Alternative Mycobacterial Species than BCG in Cancer 
Immunotherapy 
Other non-pathogenic or less pathogenic mycobacterial species and their components are being 
explored for treating various cancer, urological and non-urological, as they present similar immune-
stimulatory properties than BCG but with different benefits and potentially with fewer side effects 
and broader therapeutic applications.  
Examples of them include Mycolicibacterium smegmatis (originally Mycobacterium smegmatis), 
Mycolicibacterium phlei (originally Mycobacterium phlei), Mycolicibacterium vaccae (originally 
Mycobacterium vaccae), Mycolicibacterium obuense (originally Mycobacterium obuense), 
Mycobacterium indicus pranii, Mycolicibacterium brumae (originally Mycobacterium brumae), as 
well as more recent candidates like Mycobacterium paragordonae and an attenuated strain of 
Mycobacterium tuberculosis. 
• 
Mycolicbacterium phlei (M. phlei) is a non-tuberculous mycobacterium usually found in the 
environment. It was one of the first species considered for cancer treatment.  In cancer 
immunotherapy, M. phlei is used in a non-viable form, meaning only components, like cell wall 
fragments, are used. The cell wall-nucleic acid complex from M. phlei (MCNA), commercially 
known as Urodicin®, effectively stimulates the immune cells without requiring live bacteria and 
reducing infection risk.  
 
• 
Mycolicibacterium smegmatis (M. smegmatis) is a non-pathogenic mycobacterium with a 
safety profile, studied for its potential clinical applications in non-urological cancers, specifically 
melanoma. This bacterium can be genetically engineered to express tumor-specific antigens, 
enhancing the immune response against cancer cells. Studies involving melanoma have 
shown that combining M. smegmatis with immune-stimulatory agents like monosodium urate 
crystals can delay tumor growth. 
 
• 
Mycolicibacterium obuense (M. obuense) is a non-pathogenic species phylogenetically 
related to M. vaccae and is available in a commercial heat-killed form known as IMM-101 with 
the NCTC 13365 strain. It is considered a promising agent and is being studied in clinical trials 
for non-urological cancers, such as pancreatic cancer and melanoma, demonstrating no toxic 
effects.  
 
• 
Mycobacterium indicus pranii (MIP) was initially developed as an immunotherapeutic agent 
for leprosy but is being investigated for other infectious diseases. The strain DSMZ 45239T is 
recognized for its strong ability to trigger immune responses without causing infection.  
 
• 
Mycolicibacterium brumae (M. brumae) is a non-pathogenic and fast-growing 
mycobacterium with a safe profile. Studies using preclinical models indicate that it can inhibit 
bladder tumor growth thanks to its local immune-stimulating properties, making it a good 
alternative to BCG for BC.  
 
• 
M. vaccae: will be further evaluated in this MSc thesis. The M. vaccae strain typically used for 
cancer treatment is the R877R (NCTC 11659), a selected rough colony type from Uganda.  It 
is presented in a heat-killed form, commercially known as IMM-201 or SRL172. It has been 
developed to treat various cancers such as pancreatic, lung, prostate, and melanoma, and has 
shown potential as combination therapy alongside treatments like chemotherapy51.

2.1 History of M. vaccae as an Immunotherapeutic Agent 
M. vaccae is a non-pathogenic species commonly found in soil or water environments. It was first 
described in 1964 by Bonickse and Huhász and its name, derived from the Latin word for cow, 
“vacca”, refers to the first strain isolated from bovine sources in Austria. This strain is referenced 
in the literature under designations such as American Type Culture Collection (ATCC) 15483T, 
Deutsche Sammlung von Mikroorganismen und Zellkulturen GmbH (DSM) 43292T, and National 
Collection of Type Cultures (NCTC) 10916T 50,52.  
In 1971, a different strain of M. vaccae was isolated in Uganda during a BCG trial for leprosy. In 
1980, Stanford and colleagues first explored its potential as a TB vaccine. Following its isolation, 
it was discovered that exposure to M. vaccae followed by BCG vaccination, could provide 
enhanced protection against several pathogenic mycobacterial species. This finding led to the 
exploration of a killed M. vaccae suspension as an adjuvant to the BCG vaccine in leprosy patients. 
Based on its ability to stimulate a protective immune response to mycobacterial antigens, further 
testing of M. vaccae preparations for TB treatment was carried out 50,52–54.  
In 1971, a different strain of M. vaccae was isolated from Lake Kyoga mud in Uganda during a 
BCG trial for leprosy. This strain, NCTC 11659, was later explored by Stanford and colleagues in 
the 1980s for its potential as a TB vaccine. The following studies found that exposure to M. vaccae 
before BCG vaccination could enhance immune protection against pathogenic mycobacteria. 
These findings led to clinical trials testing the suspension of heat-killed M. vaccae NCTC 11659 as 
adjuvant therapy in leprosy and TB treatment, with a stable variant with a rough colonial 
morphology name R877R selected for use50,53,54.  
One of the first human trials, conducted in the UK in the 1980s, showed that M. vaccae combined 
with BCG elicited a long-lasting immune response lasting over a decade in leprosy patients55. 
Subsequent international trials across diverse populations reported improved bacterial clearance, 
symptom relief, and immune enhancement, though a South African study showed no significant 
benefit, likely due to environmental, genetic, or methodological differences53.  
This thesis focuses primarily on M. vaccae NCTC 11659, given its extensive research history and 
documented immunoregulatory properties in cancer immunotherapy studies.  
2.2 M. vaccae NCTC 11659: General Information  
M. vaccae NCTC 11659 is a rapid-growing bacterium, aerobic, gram-positive, acid-alcohol-fast, 
and rod-shaped forming rough, yellow-pigmented colonies when cultured. It is cultivated on various 
growth media, including GYM agar, Middlebrook 7H10, and PMG agar, with an optimal growth 
temperature of 37°C. M. vaccae NCTC 11659 has been identified in municipal water systems and 
within the human oral cavity and upper respiratory tract, with studies showing that the airway 
microbiome of rural children is more abundant in mycobacteria compared with rural children52.  
Although it has not been associated with human pathogenicity, rare cases of opportunistic 
infections in immunocompromised individuals have been reported, though not specifically linked 
to this strain56. Despite being non-pathogenic, M. vaccae is typically heat-killed in clinical research 
to ensure safety and to facilitate regulatory approval for therapeutic use.

2.3 M. vaccae and Immune Modulation 
M. vaccae NCTC 11659 belongs to a group called "old friends", environmental mycobacteria that 
have co-evolved with humans and have immunomodulatory properties57. This concept, also known 
as “trained immunity”, can be de ined as any contact with microbial stimuli that can induce long-
lasting epigenetic changes in innate immune cells, which can not only result in an enhanced 
response to a second challenge by the same microbial antigen (immunological memory), but also 
to unrelated microbial attacks. The immune response elicited by M. vaccae involves the modulation 
of various immune pathways, including the activation of DCs and macrophages PRRs such as 
TLRs, NLRs, and CLRs. This interaction promotes immune modulation, particularly by inducing 
Th1 and Tregs responses50. 
2.3.1 Trained Immunity 
Unlike adaptive immunity, which relies on antigen-specific memory, trained immunity enhances 
innate immune responses through metabolic and epigenetic reprogramming of monocytes, 
macrophages, and NK cells. BCG and environmental mycobacteria, including M. vaccae, 
contribute to this process by promoting M1 macrophage polarization and generating trained 
inflammatory monocytes, which exhibit heightened responsiveness to subsequent pathogen 
encounters. 
 
BCG, a well-characterized inducer of trained immunity, enhances cytokine production, particularly 
type 1 responses (IFN-γ, TNF-α, and IL-6), leading to macrophage activation, improved antigen 
presentation, and stimulation of CD8+ T cells and NK cells. This mechanism supports BCG’s 
effectiveness in NMIBC and has been suggested to provide cross-protection against viral 
infections, including coronaviruses. While M. vaccae shares some immunostimulatory properties 
with BCG, its specific contribution to trained immunity remains less defined57. 
 
2.3.2 Th1 and Regulatory Responses Dynamics  
Studies have shown that M. vaccae induces a robust early Th1 response, which declines over 
time. Zhang et al. compared the immunomodulatory effects of live M. vaccae and BCG in a mouse 
model, demonstrating that M. vaccae initially promotes a strong Th1 response, marked by 
increased CD3+CD4+IFN-γ+ T cells and elevated IFN-γ, TNF-α, and IL-12 levels. This response 
effectively counteracts Th2 polarization, which is linked to allergic and non-protective immunity. 
However, by week 4 of the study, the Th1 response declined, coinciding with a significant increase 
in regulatory T cells (CD4+CD25+FoxP3+, CD4+CD25+CD152+) and immunosuppressive 
cytokines such as IL-10 and TGF-β, suggesting a shi t toward an immunoregulatory phase. This 
transition came with a resurgence of Th2 cytokines (IL-4), indicating that M. vaccae does not 
sustain prolonged Th1 activation. In contrast, BCG induced a more stable Th1 response, with a 
slower decline in IFN-γ and TNF-α and less pronounced regulatory or Th2 activation. 
Interestingly, both M. vaccae and BCG activated DCs, with M. vaccae especially stimulating 
plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs), which are key producers of type I interferons (IFN-α/β). This uni ue 
biphasic modulation of M. vaccae: initially enhancing Th1 responses, followed by regulatory 
immunosuppression, suggests a potential role as an immune adjuvant rather than a standalone 
immunotherapy. Although the transitory Th1 response can seem limiting, M. vaccae’s regulatory 
shift could benefit cancer immunotherapy and inflammatory disease modulation. Early IFN-γ and 
TNF-α activation suggests M. vaccae is a priming agent, enhancing tumor recognition while 
preventing excessive immune activation. Its capacity to stimulate DCs and modulate adaptive 
immunity without inducing chronic inflammation makes it a promising candidate for controlled 
immunotherapy.

2.3.3 Th2 Suppression via Dendritic Cells Priming  
One of the primary immunotherapeutic effects of M. vaccae is the promotion of a Th1-type immune 
response while suppressing Th2 pathways. This dual mechanism is key for combating cancer, 
infections, and allergic diseases, among others. Le Bert et al. (2011) investigated how heat-killed 
M. vaccae NCTC 11659 influences Th polarization, demonstrating that it inhibits Th2 responses 
through DC priming. 
M. vaccae modulates immune responses in a dose-dependent manner. In vitro studies by Le Bert 
showed that heat-killed M. vaccae NCTC 11659 promoted DC maturation, as evidenced by the 
upregulation of CD86 and CD83, key co-stimulatory and maturation markers. This effect was most 
pronounced at a 100 µg/mL dose, which also resulted in the greatest reduction in IL-4+ T cells, 
indicating strong Th2 suppression. 
M. vaccae interacts with TLR-2 on DCs, leading to maturation and transcriptional changes. Unlike 
other TLR-2 agonists, M. vaccae-primed DCs inhibited Th2 differentiation, as shown by a reduction 
in IL-4-producing T cells. While M. vaccae may also engage DC-SIGN, CCR5, dectin-1, NOD2, or 
the mannose receptor, its primary effects appear to be mediated via TLR-2. However, specific 
TLR-2 ligands alone (e.g., Pam3CSK4, a synthetic TLR2/TLR1 ligand) failed to induce the same 
Th2 suppression observed with M. vaccae, indicating that TLR-2 signaling alone is insufficient to 
mediate this effect. 
Th2 suppression occurs through a distinct intracellular signaling pathway, where M. vaccae 
selectively activates the CREB (cAMP response element-binding protein) pathway within DCs. 
This activation antagonizes NF-κB, a pro-inflammatory transcription factor, and is known to 
upregulate IL-10 in certain contexts. However, no direct IL-10 secretion was observed in M. 
vaccae-primed DCs, suggesting that Th2 inhibition occurs independently of IL-10-driven regulatory 
mechanisms. Additionally, CREB activation correlates inversely with Th2 polarization, indicating 
that higher M. vaccae-primed DC numbers lead to stronger Th2 suppression. 
While M. vaccae-primed DCs induced CD25high/FoxP3high Tregs, their presence did not correlate 
with Th2 suppression, and no evidence of IL-10 production was found. This suggests that Th2 
inhibition by M. vaccae occurs independently of Treg-mediated mechanisms59. Instead, an 
increased DC-to-T cell ratio was correlated with higher IFN-γ production, indicating a shi t toward 
Th1 differentiation through enhanced antigen presentation. Additionally, TLR-2 activation by M. 
vaccae led to IL-12 secretion, further supporting Th1 polarization59.  
However, according to Zhang et al. (2016), M. vaccae initially induces a strong Th1 response (high 
IFN-γ, TNF-α, and IL-12) while suppressing Th2 activity. Then, after week 4, this Th1 response 
declines as regulatory Tregs expand, increasing IL-10 and TGF-β levels. This shi t promotes a Th  
resurgence, likely as a feedback mechanism to control inflammation and prevent prolonged Th1-
driven immune activation58.

2.3.5 CD8+ CTLs Activation 
Skinner et al. investigated the adjuvant properties of heat-killed M. vaccae ATCC 15483 in 
promoting CD8+ CTL responses. M. vaccae was co-administered with the soluble protein 
ovalbumin in mice and, unlike other heat-killed mycobacteria (including M. tuberculosis, BCG, M. 
smegmatis, and M. phlei) it uniquely induced an OVA-specific CD8+ CTL response. This suggests 
that M. vaccae possesses distinct immunostimulatory components that enhance CD8+ T cell 
priming. 
A key finding of the study was that M. vaccae’s 65 kDa HSP (Hsp65) played a central role in 
stimulating CD8+ CTLs against unrelated proteins. This response was likely mediated by 
enhanced antigen presentation, possibly through cross-presentation via MHC class I. Notably, M. 
vaccae successfully induced CD8+ CTL responses when administered intranasally, demonstrating 
its potential as a mucosal adjuvant.  
Furthermore, M. vaccae was shown to increase IL-12 secretion, a cytokine crucial for priming 
CD8+ T cells and promoting Th1 responses. The study also suggested that M. vaccae might 
facilitate cross-presentation of exogenous antigens via MHC class I, enabling CD8+ T cell 
activation even with soluble proteins like ovalbumin.  
These findings highlighted the potential of M. vaccae as an immunomodulatory adjuvant requiring 
CD8+ T cell activation, positioning it as a promising candidate for cancer immunotherapy and 
mucosal vaccine development62. 
2.3.6 Monocyte Tumor Interaction  
M. vaccae has been studied for its effects on monocyte-tumor cell interactions, compared to BCG 
strains. Monocytes exposed to tumors typically become deactivated, with reduced TNF-α and IL-
12 production and increased IL-   secretion. A study by Jarosław Baran et al. demonstrated that 
pretreatment with three M. vaccae strains (SN 920, MB 3683, B 3805) partially reversed this 
deactivation by enhancing TNF-α production. In the study, SN 920 and BCG improved IL-12 levels, 
while only BCG effectively reduced IL-10.

2.4 Immune Modulation: M. vaccae vs. BCG 
BCG and M. vaccae exhibit different immunomodulatory profiles that influence their therapeutic 
potential in cancer immunotherapy. BCG primarily induces a Th1-skewed immune response, 
marked by IFN-γ production, which is crucial  or intracellular pathogen clearance and anti-tumor 
immunity. However, depending on strain and dosage, BCG can also trigger a mixed Th1/Th2 
response, which may influence its therapeutic applications. Th1 responses promote cellular 
immunity essential for targeting malignant cells, while Th2 responses are more associated with 
humoral immunity and anti-inflammatory effects57. 
 
In contrast, M. vaccae initially induces Th1 activation but also strongly suppresses Th2 cytokines. 
While BCG sustains a prolonged Th1-dominant response, M. vaccae shifts into an 
immunoregulatory phase, promoting Treg expansion and immune homeostasis. This dual action 
allows it to reduce chronic inflammation while preserving immunity, as its DC-driven IL-12 
production and antigen presentation may help sustain controlled Th1 activity. This makes M. 
vaccae particularly useful for allergies, autoimmune, and inflammatory conditions.

2.5 M. vaccae as a Potential BCG Alternative 
Several studies have demonstrated the potential of M. vaccae immunotherapy to enhance the 
safety and efficacy of various cancer treatments, while also contributing to an improved quality of 
life.  
2.5.1 IMM-201 in Cancer Immunotherapy 
IMM-201, formerly SRL172, is an immunotherapeutic preparation derived from M. vaccae, 
formulated as a heat-killed suspension to ensure safe use without causing infection. Heat-killed 
preparations of M. vaccae are produced by autoclaving at 121°C for 15 minutes. 
This preparation was specially designed to harness the immune-stimulatory properties of the 
bacterium. Initially, it was investigated due to its potential to shift immune responses from Th2 to 
Th1, thereby promoting anti-tumor activity 65. It consists of the R877R strain, a refined subline of 
M. vaccae NCTC 11659, selected for its stable rough colony morphology - a trait associated with 
enhanced immunogenicity. The R877R strain was developed for consistent immunological effects, 
making it reliable for clinical applications 66.  
 
IMM-201 has been widely explored for its immunomodulatory effects in both infectious diseases, 
such as TB, and non-infectious conditions, including autoimmune disorders and certain cancers. 
In cancer immunotherapy, it has been investigated for malignant melanoma, prostate, renal, and 
lung cancer across various phase I, phase II, and phase III clinical trials.  
The following section presents these studies, in which M. vaccae was originally referred to as 
SRL172. However, for consistency, the term IMM-201 will be used in all references to this 
preparation, considering both terms synonymous. 
2.5.1.1 Prostate Cancer  
A 1998 study published in the British Journal of Urology investigated the immunomodulatory effects 
and safety of IMM-201 in a phase I/II trial involving ten patients with advanced hormone-refractory 
prostate cancer. IMM-201 was administered intradermally at regular intervals, with patient 
responses monitored for 3 to 14 months. The main aim of the study was to investigate if IMM-201 
could promote a beneficial shift from a Th2 to a Th1 immune response, characterized by increased 
IL-2 and IFN-γ production and reduced prostate-specific antigen (PSA) levels, a key biomarker of 
prostate tumor activity. 
In this study, five of the ten patients showed reduced PSA levels, with two achieving this only with 
IMM-201 treatment, without additional therapies. Flow cytometry revealed that PSA responders 
typically had increased IL-2 production by PBMCs, while IL-4 levels, associated with the Th2 
response, were reduced. Although IFN-γ production was inconsistent, the  indings suggest a link 
between IL-2 production and PSA decline after IMM-201 vaccination. These findings suggest the 
potential of IMM-201 as an immunomodulatory treatment for prostate cancer by shifting the 
immune response to combat tumor progression67.  
2.5.1.2 Renal Cancer 
As BCG has been historically used in immunotherapy, IMM-201 has also been explored for renal 
cancer treatment like renal cell carcinoma (RCC). This is a condition that has limited therapeutic 
options and an unfavorable prognosis in advanced metastatic stages.

To investigate the clinical potential of IMM-201 in RCC, Patel et al. (2008) conducted two phase II 
clinical trials. Their findings revealed that administering intradermally IMM-201 alone provided 
comparable survival outcomes to traditional biological therapies, such as IL-2 and IFN- α, but with 
significantly fewer and more manageable adverse effects. IMM-201 demonstrated good tolerability 
in both trials, with only mild side effects.  
Additionally, this research suggested that combining IMM-201 with IL-2 may reduce IL-2-
associated toxicity, highlighting IMM-201’s promise as a supportive therapy for patients with 
advanced RCC, especially for those who cannot tolerate standard treatments’ side e  ects. 
Therefore, this study supports IMM-201 as a good candidate to be further evaluated to assess its 
impact on the survival and quality of life, ideally in larger controlled trials, in combination with newer 
immunotherapies or with other cancer types65.  
2.5.1.3 Lung Cancer 
2.5.1.3.1 Non-Small-Cell Lung Cancer 
Among all lung cancer types, NSCLC is the most common, comprising 80-85% of cases. Typically 
diagnosed in advanced stages, NSCLC has limited surgical options and poor prognosis. 
Conventional treatment is platinum-based chemotherapy, with frequent side effects that impact the 
quality of life of patients. For this reason, the potential of IMM-201 has been explored in addition 
to chemotherapy for NSCLC, intended to improve immune function.  
 
O’Brien et al. conducted a phase III clinical study to assess whether combining intradermal IMM-
201 with conventional chemotherapy could enhance outcomes for patients with advanced NSCLC. 
The primary goal was to investigate overall survival, with secondary objectives focused on quality 
of life and treatment tolerance. While IMM-201 did not improve overall survival compared to 
chemotherapy alone, patients reported significant improvements in quality of life. Enhanced Global 
Health Status scores and chemotherapy-associated symptoms like nausea and peripheral 
neuropathy were observed. These findings suggest that IMM-201 holds potential as a supportive 
therapy to improve quality of life in advanced NSCLC patients, even though does not benefit overall 
survival68.  
2.5.1.3.2 Small-Cell Lung Cancer  
Harper-Wynne et al. conducted a randomized study to evaluate whether adding intradermal IMM-
201 to standard chemotherapy could improve outcomes in small-cell lung cancer (SCLC). The 
main goal was to assess survival, while secondary objectives focused on symptom control and 
safety. Although IMM-201 did not improve survival compared to chemotherapy alone, patients in 
this group showed significantly better symptom control. These results aligned with O’Brien et al.’s 
findings in NSCLC, where IMM-201 improved quality of life. The authors suggest that a placebo-
controlled trial is needed to confirm its role in symptom management69.  
2.5.1.3.3 Adenocarcinoma 
More specifically, J.L. Stanford et al. evaluated the impact of IMM-201 on survival outcomes among 
different histological subtypes of NSCLC, including adenocarcinoma and squamous carcinoma. 
This phase III clinical trial compared the combination of chemotherapy with IMM-201 injections vs. 
chemotherapy alone in patients with advanced NSCLC. Survival rates and quality of life were 
assessed using the EORTC Quality of Life Questionnaire. 
 
The results demonstrated a significant survival benefit for adenocarcinoma patients who received 
IMM-201 as part of their treatment, with a mean survival increase of approximately 135 days 
compared to chemotherapy alone (357 vs. 222 days, p = 0.0009).

The survival benefits were strongly correlated with adherence to the full IMM-201 treatment

treated with IMM-201, which may be explained by differences in tumor biology between

IMM-201 exhibited a dual role as both an antigen and an immune modulator, enhancing immune 
activation in adenocarcinoma. It also showed potential as a combination therapy with 
chemotherapy for adenocarcinoma patients. Additionally, the treatment was well-tolerated, with 
minimal side effects and minor injection site reactions, further supporting its clinical use70. 
2.5.1.4 Melanoma 
In 1999, a phase I/II study by Maraveyas et al. evaluated IMM-201 in 24 patients with stage IV 
metastatic melanoma, administering it intradermally at 15-day intervals for three doses, followed 
by monthly injections. The results showed that 39% of patients exhibited sustained IL-2 induction 
in peripheral blood lymphocytes (PBLs), correlating with a median survival of 59 weeks, compared 
to 31 weeks in non-responders. The treatment was well tolerated, with only mild local and flu-like 
symptoms. M. vaccae was considered a non-specific immunological adjuvant for metastatic 
melanoma, warranting further clinical investigation71.  
A phase II clinical trial in 2013 evaluated the long-term outcomes of administering intradermal IMM-
201 injections in patients with stage IV metastatic melanoma. Seventy-two patients were treated, 
and the disease-specific survival rates were 29.6% and 23.9% at three and five years, respectively, 
exceeding historical controls for advanced melanoma. The overall response rate was 12,1%, with 
28.8% achieving stable disease after six months of treatment. Notably, M. vaccae was effective 
combined with surgery and radiotherapy, suggesting increased survival rates in patients with lower 
tumor burdens.  
 
The vaccine functioned as a non-specific immune modulator, suppressing Th2 and restoring Th1 
responses improving tumor targeting, even in non-resectable cases. It also enhanced the 
effectiveness of chemotherapy and radiotherapy. Toxicity was minimal, with mild flu-like symptoms 
and localized reactions being the most common side effects, contrasting with the significant 
toxicities of treatments like ipilimumab. Further research is needed to explore M. vaccae’s potential 
as a low-toxicity complement or alternative to existing melanoma treatments72.  
2.5.1.5 Insights and Limitations of M. vaccae Clinical Trials in Cancer Immunotherapy 
Cancer immunotherapy has had an impact on the treatment of solid tumors. M. vaccae has been 
investigated in various cancer vaccine approaches, showing clinical benefits in specific contexts. 
While some clinical trials showed promising effects, particularly in melanoma and lung cancer, the 
results have been inconsistent. 
Although IMM-201 demonstrated immune activation and some survival benefits, outcomes varied 
across studies, with no clear, reproducible advantage over existing treatments. This inconsistency, 
combined with a limited understanding of its mechanisms, likely contributed to declining 
pharmaceutical interest. 
Consequently, despite initial interest, pharmaceutical investment in M. vaccae for cancer 
applications declined, with SR Pharma discontinuing its development. Positive survival outcomes 
in melanoma patients prompted the UK-based company Immodulon Therapeutics to focus on its 
immunomodulatory properties but later redirected efforts toward tuberculosis immunotherapy, 
further limiting its clinical exploration in oncology73.

2.5.1.6 Potential Role of M. vaccae in Bladder Cancer Therapy 
Various bladder cancer cell lines have been studied for their growth inhibition in vitro using heat-
killed and γ-irradiated M. vaccae, among other preparations, demonstrating inhibitory capacities 
like live mycobacteria.  
However, cytokine production by bladder cancer cells infected with non-viable mycobacteria is 
lower than that observed in cells infected with live mycobacteria. Notably, among the non-viable 
preparations, γ-irradiated mycobacteria elicited similar cytokine levels than live mycobacteria. 
Additionally, studies revealed that immune activation was higher in response to live mycobacteria 
compared to non-viable mycobacteria4.

2.5.2 IMM-101 in Cancer Immunotherapy 
Later research has demonstrated that M. obuense is superior to M. vaccae in terms of immune 
modulation and is easier to produce under Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP). The known form 
IMM-101 contains heat-killed M. obuense. In a phase II randomized clinical trial, IMM-101 was 
evaluated in combination with gemcitabine as a first-line treatment for advanced pancreatic ductal 
adenocarcinoma. The results showed good tolerance of IMM-101, with prolonged progression-free 
survival rates compared to patients receiving gemcitabine alone, and with beneficial survival 
effects in patients with metastatic disease73,75.  
IMM-101, regarded as a broad-spectrum immunotherapeutic agent, is being developed by 
Immodulon Therapeutics. Based on compelling Phase II IMAGE-1 Pancreatic Cancer data, 
Immodulon is preparing to launch a Phase III Bayesian adaptive platform study for IMM-101 in 
pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma. This upcoming study will include multiple parallel pathways to 
assess IMM-101's effectiveness not only in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma but also in other 
immunologically "cold" tumors, which are cancers characterized by a weak immune response76.

3.2 Potential of BCG Therapy in Combination with M. vaccae 
BCG alone is the standard of care for high-risk NMIBC following TURB, effectively reducing 
recurrence and progression rates. However, some patients do not respond to BCG, and others 
experience recurrence or progression despite adequate treatment. Additionally, significant side 
effects and complications often lead to compliance issues95,96. One strategy to mitigate BCG-
associated toxicity is dose reduction35. While novel therapies are emerging, their widespread 
adoption requires further validation of long-term efficacy and safety. 
M. vaccae, has been studied as an immunotherapeutic agent in various diseases, including cancer. 
This agent has demonstrated the potential to enhance Th1-dominant immune responses 
progressively. Therefore, its ability to enhance systemic immunity makes it a promising candidate 
for combination therapies to address the limitations of BCG. IMM-201, a heat-killed preparation of 
M. vaccae, may synergize with BCG by enhancing efficacy, reducing toxicity, and improving patient 
compliance as well as quality of life2,50.  
In cancer therapy, adjuvant therapy is administered after a primary treatment. However, this differs 
from combination therapy, where agents are used simultaneously to improve efficacy, sustain 
immune responses, and reduce resistance. While BCG induces a localized Th1 activation, 
intradermal IMM-201 broadens systemic Th1 responses, potentially enhancing efficacy while 
reducing toxicity. Therefore, administering IMM-201 alongside BCG aligns with combination 
therapy rather than adjuvant therapy77.  
3.3 Clinical Trial Hypothesis and Objectives  
The hypothesis of this study is that IMM-201 administered intradermally, in combination with BCG, 
could synergistically strengthen the anti-tumor immune response in NMIBC. BCG primarily acts 
locally by inducing cytokine release and recruiting immune cells to the bladder wall, while 
intradermal IMM-201 enhances systemic Th1-dominant immunity through antigen presentation 
and immune activation. Together, these mechanisms (local via BCG and systemic via IMM-201), 
could create a synergistic antitumor effect50.  
Primary Objective: evaluate recurrence-free survival (RFS) across study groups. 
Secondary Objectives: assess immune biomarkers (IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-10), reduction of adverse 
effects, and quality of life (QoL) improvements (along with other patient-reported outcomes).  
3.4 Clinical Trial Scope and Design   
A clinical trial is a prospective study designed to compare the effects and value of one or more 
interventions against a control group in humans. Conducting a clinical trial requires meticulous 
planning to ensure the study is well-designed and compliant with ethical and regulatory standards, 
including FDA78, European Medicines Agency (EMA)79 requirements and International Council for 
Harmonisation of Technical Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for Human Use (ICH) guidelines 
such as ICH E6, ICH E8 and ICH E980. The planning process starts with creating a clinical study 
plan, establishing re uirements, and dra ting a protocol that outlines the trial’s objectives and 
methodology. This protocol is submitted to an Institutional Review Board (IRB) or Ethics Committee 
(EC) to ensure participant protection and ethical integrity.

The next stage involves practical implementation, including identifying and qualifying trial sites, 
obtaining informed consent from participants, registering the trial, and developing a monitoring 
plan. Public registration of the trial, for example on ClinicalTrials.gov, promotes transparency and

Regulatory approvals, such as those from the FDA, are obtained by submitting detailed preclinical

Pharmaceutical trials are traditionally classified into Phases I, II, III, and IV. However, trials 
involving behavioral, lifestyle, or surgical interventions may not align completely with these phases, 
and even drug trials may not always fit neatly into a single phase. 
Phase I provides early data on safety, tolerability, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, and 
dosage optimization. These trials often involve 20-50 participants, typically healthy volunteers or 
oncology patients who may benefit from the treatment.  
Phase II evaluates the biological activity, effect, and safety of a drug in fewer than 100 participants 
with narrower inclusion criteria. These trials often explore biomarkers, early clinical outcomes, and 
safety indicators to inform Phase III design. 
Phase III trials confirm therapeutic efficacy, safety, and clinical value in several hundred to a few 
thousand participants, representing broader patient populations. This phase is critical for 
establishing the intervention’s real-world applicability and forms the basis for regulatory approval. 
Finally, phase IV trials are post-approval studies conducted in larger, more diverse populations, 
potentially involving millions of participants. These trials aim to identify rare adverse effects and 
assess long-term safety and effectiveness. Therefore, phase IV data provide crucial insights into 
real-world applications and safety81.

phase III trial can provide robust safety data, as it benefits from a larger and diverse population 
that enables long and rare adverse effects detection.

3.6 Randomization and Stratification Strategies 
Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) ensure unbiased treatment allocation by giving each 
participant an equal chance of being assigned to intervention or control groups. This process 
balances both known and unknown confounding factors, such as disease severity, and facilitates 
valid statistical analyses. For testing new therapies in patients with high-risk NMIBC BCG-exposed, 
only RCTs are accepted.  
As a large clinical trial, this study will use computer-generated randomization for its accuracy, 
reproducibility, and ability to eliminate human bias81. Stratification will be applied to enhance 
validity by ensuring balanced allocation of participants based on key prognostic factors:  
• 
Presence of CIS: stratification by the presence of CIS (CIS ± Ta/T1 vs. Ta/T1 only), ensuring 
a balanced representation of different disease profiles. 
• 
Time of last BCG therapy: stratification based on the interval since the last BCG therapy (<6 
months vs. ≥6 months) accounts  or di  erences in disease progression and treatment 
response. 
• 
Adequacy of prior BCG therapy: stratification based on whether participants received 
complete or incomplete BCG therapy, addressing variations in treatment history. 
 
To account for these key prognostic factors, this trial employs stratified randomization, combined 
with blocked randomization, which ensures that participants are uniformly distributed across the 
three study arms. A fixed allocation ratio of 1:1:1 is used, meaning each participant has an equal 
chance of being assigned to one of the three cohorts (standard BCG, standard BCG + IMM-201, 
or low-dose BCG + IMM-201)81,83.

3.6.1 Blinding 
Given the distinct administration methods (intravesical BCG vs. intradermal IMM-201), this trial is 
conducted as an open-label study, where both participants and investigators are aware of the 
assigned treatments. However, to minimize bias in key outcome evaluations, outcome assessors 
responsible for analyzing biomarker changes and QoL evaluations are blinded to treatment 
allocations. This ensures that the interpretation of results is not influenced by the knowledge of the 
intervention group. 
 
3.7 Clinical Endpoints 
3.7.1 Primary Endpoints 
The primary endpoint of this study is two-year RFS, defined as the time from randomization to the 
first recurrence of NMIBC (including high-grade lesions such as Ta, T1, and CIS) or progression 
to MIBC. This endpoint reflects IMM-201’s e  icacy in enhancing the therapeutic e  ects o  BCG 
and preventing NMIBC recurrence or progression. Given that the majority of NMIBC recurrences 
occur within the first two years33, this timeframe aligns with established clinical guidelines. 
 
For patients with CIS (with or without concurrent papillary disease), the complete response (CR) 
rate at six months will be evaluated as a key secondary endpoint. Since CIS is not fully resectable 
through TURBT, CR - defined as the absence of visible disease on cystoscopy, negative urine 
cytology, and, if indicated, negative biopsy results - serves as the primary efficacy measure for this 
subgroup. In contrast, for patients with papillary-only tumors (Ta or T1), which are fully resected 
before treatment, RFS remains the primary measure of efficacy88.  
3.7.2 Secondary Endpoints 
In this trial, key secondary points will include:  
• 
Progression-free survival (PFS): defined as the time until disease progression to MIBC or 
distant metastases. This endpoint evaluates the intervention’s e  icacy in delaying disease 
advancement. 
• 
Cystectomy-free survival: time until radical cystectomy is required due to recurrence or 
progression. This is a critical outcome given the significant morbidity and impact on QoL 
associated with cystectomy 77,78,82.  
• 
Immune biomarker changes: assessment of IFN-γ, TNF-α, and IL-10 levels to evaluate 
systemic immunological responses and their role in enhancing BCG. This reflects the Th1/Th2 
balance and provides insights into the mechanisms driving the therapy's efficacy. 
• 
QoL: measured using the EORTC Quality of Life Core Questionnaire (EORTC QLQ-C30) a 
30-item instrument designed to measure multiple aspects of cancer patients' well-being89. The 
questionnaire will be administered electronically at baseline and at regular intervals throughout 
the study. 
• 
AEs: recorded and graded using the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events 
(CTCAE, version 6.0), with serious adverse events (sAEs) monitored for 90 days post-
treatment90. AE assessments will include incidence, causality, and outcomes, along with 
changes in key laboratory values and vital signs. Long-term safety, particularly immune-related 
adverse events (irAEs), will be analyzed separately91.

3.9 Statistical Considerations 
Statistical planning is a cornerstone of clinical trial design, ensuring that the study is adequately 
powered to detect meaningful differences while maintaining scientific and ethical rigor. For this 
Phase III randomized, open-label, three-arm trial in patients with NMIBC, statistical considerations 
are aligned with established methodologies from previous trials, such as KEYNOTE-676 
(evaluating pembrolizumab with BCG)91 and ALBAN (assessing atezolizumab with BCG)97, while 
addressing the specific needs of evaluating IMM-201 as an adjunct to BCG therapy. 
 
3.9.1 Sample Size Estimation 
Accurate sample size estimation is critical to ensure that the trial has adequate power. The primary 
objective is to demonstrate a clinically meaningful improvement in 2-year RFS, which is the primary 
clinical endpoint of this study. While specific historical RFS rates vary across studies, incremental 
improvements of 15% in RFS have been identified as clinically meaningful in prior NMIBC trials 
involving combination therapies29. 
This study hypothesizes a 15% absolute improvement in 2-year RFS for the experimental arms 
compared to standard BCG monotherapy. Assuming a hazard ratio of 0.70, an alpha level of 0.05, 
and 80% power, the sample size required to achieve statistical significance is estimated at 600 
participants (200 per arm). To account for an anticipated 10% dropout rate, approximately 660 
participants will be enrolled. 
CR, defined as the absence of detectable disease on cystoscopy, cytology, and biopsy, is a critical 
secondary endpoint for participants with CIS. CR, is achieved by approximately 70% of patients 
following BCG induction therapy83. This study hypothesizes that IMM-201 will further improve CR 
rates, consistent with results observed in combination trials like KEYNOTE-67629,83,91. 
3.9.2 Statistical Analysis Plan  
The primary endpoint, RFS, will be analyzed using Kaplan-Meier survival curves, with between-
group comparisons performed via the log-rank test. Cox proportional hazards regression models 
will adjust for confounders such as tumor stage, prior BCG adequacy, and CIS presence.  
Secondary endpoints, including CR, PFS, and QoL, will be evaluated using appropriate statistical 
methods. CR rates will be analyzed via logistic regression, while mixed-effects models will analyze 
longitudinal changes in QoL and immune biomarkers. To reduce potential bias, outcome assessors 
for QoL and biomarker analyses will remain blinded to treatment allocation29.

4 Discussion 
This study explores the immunotherapeutic potential of M. vaccae as an alternative or adjunct to 
BCG in NMIBC. The findings highlight that M. vaccae exhibits distinct immunomodulatory 
properties that could address some of the limitations associated with BCG therapy, including 
treatment toxicity, resistance, and global supply shortages. This discussion critically evaluates 
these findings in relation to existing research, considers their clinical implications, and outlines 
areas for future investigation. 
 
The need for BCG alternatives 
While BCG has been the gold standard for NMIBC treatment for decades, its limitations, such as 
treatment failures and adverse effects, remain significant. Its toxicity, primarily driven by 
inflammatory mechanisms, can cause both local and systemic adverse effects, that may 
necessitate treatment discontinuation (Section 1.3.4.1). This underscores the need for additional 
or alternative immunotherapeutic strategies. Furthermore, periodic global shortages have 
disrupted BCG availability, reinforcing the importance of identifying viable alternatives (Section 
1.3.3). One approach to addressing these challenges is dose reduction, which has been shown to 
lower toxicity while maintaining efficacy in some studies35. Additionally, reduced dosing could 
extend BCG availability, mitigating supply shortages. However, the long-term efficacy of lower-
dose regimens, particularly in high-risk patient populations, remains a concern. 
Comparison between BCG and M. vaccae 
BCG induces a strong and sustained Th1 immune response, critical for its therapeutic efficacy in 
bladder cancer. However, excessive inflammation contributes to toxicity and treatment intolerance 
(Section 1.4.4).  
In contrast, M. vaccae appears to generate a more balanced immune response, selectively 
enhancing CD8+ cytotoxic T cells and γδ T cells, key immunological mechanisms for effective 
cancer immunotherapy, while mitigating excessive inflammatory reactions (Section 2.3.4). This 
controlled immune modulation may reduce adverse effects and improve patient tolerance. 
Nonetheless, M. vaccae exhibits a weaker induction of IL-12 and a regulatory influence on IL-10 
(Section 2.3.6), suggesting that while it may not be as effective as a standalone therapy, it could 
serve as a valuable combination to BCG. A comparative analysis of immune modulation between 
M. vaccae and BCG, summarized in Table 4, supports this hypothesis.  
Other non-tuberculous mycobacteria, such as M. brumae, and M. obuense74,75, have 
demonstrated even stronger immunostimulatory effects and should be considered in future 
research as alternative or adjunct immunotherapeutic agents. 
Potential clinical applications 
M. vaccae has been investigated for its potential in treating distinct cancers, including melanoma, 
prostate cancer, and lung cancer, but noy yet in bladder cancer (Table 5). Clinical trials involving 
heat-killed M. vaccae (IMM-201) have reported promising results; however, some studies have 
reported no significant improvement in overall survival. Given that NMIBC is a cancer driven by 
inflammatory processes15, IMM-201 represents a promising candidate for further investigation in 
this context. M. vaccae’s immunoregulatory properties suggest potential therapeutic roles in 
autoimmune diseases, allergies, and neuroinflammatory conditions. Given its broad 
immunomodulatory potential and relatively low production costs, M. vaccae represents a feasible 
candidate for further development in NMIBC treatment. Future research should prioritize rigorous 
clinical trials to evaluate its safety, efficacy, and optimal integration into existing treatment regimens 
(Section 2.5.1.6).

Proposed clinical trial framework 
To evaluate the potential role of IMM-201 in NMIBC, a Phase III randomized multi-center clinical 
trial is proposed to assess its efficacy combined with BCG (Section 3). The study focuses on BCG-
exposed patients, who are more likely to benefit from additional BCG treatment. RFS and CR are 
prioritized clinical endpoints, alongside key methodological considerations such as patient 
stratification, safety monitoring, and interim analyses to ensure clinical applicability. If successful, 
this trial could, for the first time, establish M. vaccae as a complementary treatment, potentially 
reducing BCG toxicity while improving NMIBC outcomes. These findings could not only inform 
clinical guidelines but also shift funding priorities in cancer research.  
 
Despite its potential, this trial design poses several challenges. Variability in biomarkers, imaging 
differences, and patient heterogeneity across centers require rigorous standardization. The open-
label, three-arm design may introduce bias and complicate statistical analyses, while recruitment 
and retention challenges (particularly concerns over adverse effects) could impact statistical 
power. Logistical and financial constraints, including multi-center coordination and large-scale 
IMM-201 production costs, further affect feasibility.  
Reasons for limited Investigation of M. vaccae in NMIBC 
The limited investigation of M. vaccae in NMIBC is mainly due to inconsistent clinical outcomes in 
cancer immunotherapy (Section 2.5.1.5), the lack of direct comparisons with BCG, and the focus 
on more targeted immunotherapies like ICIs. While M. vaccae has shown immunomodulatory 
effects, studies in other cancers have had mixed results, with no clear survival benefits, which has 
lowered research and commercial interest.  
Unlike BCG, which triggers a strong local bladder immune response, M. vaccae has been primarily 
tested intradermally, making its NMIBC efficacy uncertain. Though generally well-tolerated, its 
long-term safety in cancer patients remains underexplored. As a rapidly growing bacterium, it offers 
cost-effective large-scale production5. However, regulatory requirements, preparation methods, 
and market dynamics ultimately shape its cost-effectiveness. Given its immunomodulatory 
properties, further research into its role as a combination NMIBC therapy remains valuable. 
 
Future directions 
Future research should prioritize rigorous clinical trials to evaluate M. vaccae’s sa ety, e  icacy, 
and optimal integration into NMIBC treatment regimens. Studies should focus on determining the 
most effective dose, route of administration (e.g., intravesical vs. systemic), and treatment 
schedule to maximize its immunotherapeutic potential. Exploring its combination with ICIs, gene 
therapy, or IL-15 antagonists is particularly promising, given the ongoing success of these 
strategies with BCG (Table 7). Additionally, identifying biomarkers predictive of patient 
responsiveness to M. vaccae could facilitate a more personalized approach to NMIBC treatment. 
 
M. vaccae's immunomodulatory profile suggests its potential for use in combination with BCG 
rather than as a replacement. Exploring its synergy with phosphoantigens or other immune 
stimulators could enhance its antitumor effects. Notably, recent research highlights M. vaccae’s 
anti-inflammatory properties, mediated by its 10(Z)-hexadecenoic acid derivative (Section 2.3.7), 
which may help reduce chronic inflammation and improve TME modulation. Investigating this 
pathway in NMIBC could inform new combination strategies for immunotherapy. By addressing 
these areas, future studies can clarify M. vaccae’s role in NMIBC treatment and establish its place 
within evolving immunotherapeutic protocols, potentially expanding the scope of mycobacterial-
based therapies.

5 Conclusion 
This thesis confirms that M. vaccae exhibits distinct immunomodulatory properties that differentiate 
it from BCG and confirms the initial hypothesis that M. vaccae can be used in combination with 
BCG. Its biphasic immune response, initially inducing a Th1-dominant activation followed by a 
regulatory phase, contrasts with BCG’s sustained pro-inflammatory profile. This modulation 
suggests that M. vaccae may contribute to antitumor immunity while mitigating BCG-related toxicity 
and adverse effects.  
 
The use of heat-killed M. vaccae preparations, such as IMM-201, further supports its potential 
clinical applicability due to an improved safety profile. Unlike live-attenuated BCG, M. vaccae does 
not carry the risk of systemic infection, making it a safer alternative for immunocompromised 
patients and those who are unable to tolerate BCG. These characteristics conclude that that M. 
vaccae may not replace BCG in NMIBC but could suit as a complementary agent to optimize 
treatment efficacy while reducing toxicity. 
 
Despite its advantages, M. vaccae requires further clinical validation before being integrated into 
NMIBC treatment. Key considerations include optimizing dosage, refining treatment sequencing, 
and implementing immune monitoring. Additionally, challenges such as regulatory approval, 
pharmaceutical investment, and demonstrating long-term efficacy must be addressed. 
 
Future studies should focus on refining its clinical application, particularly in combination with other 
immunotherapies such ICIs, gene therapy, IL-15 antagonist or chemotherapy, to determine the 
most effective strategy for enhancing anti-tumor immunity. Beyond bladder cancer, its role in 
malignancies associated with chronic inflammation warrants investigation. Additionally, its 
immunoregulatory properties may offer therapeutic benefits in autoimmune and inflammatory 
disorders. Large-scale clinical trials will be essential to validate its efficacy in NMIBC and broader 
immunotherapy settings while determining its optimal integration into existing treatment regimens. 
 
While there is much more to explore, the findings presented in this thesis contribute to a deeper 
understanding of M. vaccae as a potential immunotherapeutic agent in cancer treatment and 
strengthen the interest in mycobacterial-based immunotherapies. By addressing some of the 
limitations of BCG and providing a novel approach to immune modulation, M. vaccae could expand 
treatment options for bladder cancer and other diseases. However, further research is required to 
fully assess its long-term clinical benefits, potential limitations, and the best strategies for 
integrating it into current treatment protocols.
