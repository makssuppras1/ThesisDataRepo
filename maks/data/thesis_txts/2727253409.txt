Abstract
Gaussian boson sampling is a promising platform for quantum computing that uses
the correlation between squeezed states to form the computational resources needed
for quantum computing. In this thesis, I will be working with squeezed states of light.
I will explore the theory behind the generation and measurement of continuous wave
squeezed states and also perform simulations of pulsed squeezed states. I will provide
a comprehensive theoretical background on coherent and squeezed states, the optical
components used, and optical cavity dynamics and stabilization. Furthermore, a detailed
analysis of the optical parametric oscillator theory and how to measure squeezing.
The main results of this thesis come in the form of measurements of squeezed noise
powers for different local oscillator phases and squeezed noise power as a function of
pump power, one using a polarizer to ensure polarization in the local oscillator and the
other without it. The difference between them was approximately 1 dB, allowing us to
reach 3.5 dB of phase noise which is a reduction of more than 50% with respect to shot
noise.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Quantum phenomena and their applications have been an area of scientific research and
technological advances for decades. In particular, quantum computing is a promising
field where quantum systems can perform some tasks more eﬀiciently than classical com-
puters do. Among the different approaches to quantum computing, boson sampling is
one of the most intriguing concepts. This technique uses the behavior of indistinguish-
able particles known as bosons.
The concept of boson sampling originated from the work of Scott Aaronson and Alex
Arkhipov in 2011 [1]. They proposed a theoretical work for a computational problem that
cannot be treated in classical computers but can be solved eﬀiciently using a quantum
setup. Identical particles, such as photons, inspired their idea and can exhibit quantum
interference, leading to complex constructive and destructive interference patterns.
When we send a set of bosons through a network of optical elements, such as beam split-
ters, their interactions lead to interference patterns. Simulating the output distribution
of these particles is computationally ineﬀicient but quantum eﬀicient. Therefore, it gains
valuable insight into the computational power of quantum systems. The main idea of
boson sampling is that after measuring, it is possible to associate a permanent matrix
describing the associated quantum system, which is computationally hard for classical
computers to calculate.
Recently, a variant of boson sampling known as Gaussian boson sampling emerged
as a promising platform for quantum computing. The original boson sampling deals
with single-photon states, while the Gaussian boson sampling uses continuous-variable
states, whose quasi-probability Wigner distribution function describing the amplitudes
and phases of the electromagnetic field is a Gaussian one, and the states have correlations
between them, such as squeezed states or entangled states.
The theoretical groundwork for Gaussian boson sampling was laid by Hamilton et al. in
2017 [2]. They demonstrated the diﬀiculty of computing the output probabilities of a
Gaussian boson sampler using classical computers. This result indicated that Gaussian
boson sampling could offer an advantage over classical computers in some scenarios.
One critical aspect comes into play in the pursuit of eﬀicient Gaussian boson sampling:
squeezing. Squeezed states of light, a well-known quantum resource, play a vital role
in Gaussian boson sampling protocols. Squeezing reduces the quantum fluctuations in
a particular quadrature, such as the ˆX or ˆP quadrature of the electromagnetic field,

resulting in increased fluctuations in the complementary quadrature. This squeezing
effect can be achieved through various techniques, including parametric down-conversion.
The importance of squeezing in Gaussian boson sampling stems from its correlations. By
harnessing squeezed states, we aim to overcome certain limitations of classical simulation
algorithms, leading to quantum computational advantages.
The field of squeezing dates back to the early days of quantum optics in the 1960s when
it was first proposed as a means to manipulate the quantum properties of light and first
generated in 1985 by Levenson et al. [3]. Since then, numerous advancements have
been made in generating, manipulating, and detecting squeezed light, paving the way
for its use in diverse areas ranging from quantum information processing to precision
measurements.
This thesis corresponds to the initial part of the Innovation Fund Denmark project Pho-
toQ (Photonic Quantum Computing). This project aims to create a fully configurable
and cloud-accessible boson sampling system. We will contribute to this project by gen-
erating and characterizing continuous wave squeezing and doing theoretical research on
pulsed squeezing.
The thesis is structured in the following way:
Chapter 2 gives an introduction to quantum optics theory. It starts by explaining two
of the most critical quantum states and operators and then discussing some optical
components that will be used during the experimental part of the project.
In Chapter 3, I dive into the theory behind optical cavities, explaining one of the essential
locking techniques for stabilization and characterizing the laser beam inside.
Chapter 4 is all about the experimental setup.
There are two main parts, one for
distributing the light through all the optical fibers to reach the eight squeezers and
another for the free space setup. It also includes essential calculations like mode matching
and intra-cavity loss.
Chapter 5 introduces the non-linear crystals used to generate squeezing and the theory
behind the optical parametric oscillator for continuous wave and pulsed light.
Chapter 6 includes some theoretical background on how to measure squeezing, consisting
of a gain measurement and a squeezing measurement for different pump powers.
Finally, Chapter 7 wraps up the thesis by summarizing the main findings and discussing
their implications.

2.5.2
Beam Splitters
Beam splitters are a critical element in any optical system, particularly in fiber optic
systems. They allow the splitting of light between optical fibers. During this project, we
will use beam splitters designed specifically for use with coupled fiber systems. The light
is transmitted through the fiber via the core, encased in a low refractive index layer.
Light splitting is achieved via evanescent coupling, where a portion of the light is trans-
mitted into a secondary fiber while the remaining power stays in the first fiber. When
two fibers are brought closer, the evanescent fields that extend beyond their boundaries
interact. The fibers are taped to ensure precise alignment and mechanical stability. It
also allows flexibility in adjusting the coupling strength and can give a variety of beam
splitters with different power-transmitted ratios. This project will use beam splitters
with transmittances 99:1%, 75:25%, and 50:50%.

2.5.3
Fiber Strecher
A fiber stretcher is a device used in fiber optics to slightly change the phase of a fiber by
applying a strain to it. This strain is applied with a piezo-electric as shown in Figure 2.9.
Carefully rolling the fiber into an elliptical shape part and using the piezo-electric to
increase the perimeter of this ellipse will lead to a change of phase of the form ∆ϕ = k∆L.
Figure 2.9: Fiber Strecher with a piezo-electric (red).
In the context of a laser operating at a specific wavelength, such as λ = 775 nm, achieving
a change of 2π in phase requires a corresponding change in optical path length, which, in
this case, would be ∆L = 775 nm. This control over the phase is essential, particularly
when measuring squeezing, as we must act on the phase between the squeezing and the
local oscillator.
2.5.4
Electro Optical Modulator
An electro-optic-modulator (EOM) or phase modulator (PM) can be integrated into
fiber components, enabling control of the light’s phase propagating to the fiber much
faster than the fiber stretcher. When an electrical signal is applied to the EOM, a change
in the refractive index causes a phase shift in the light passing through the fiber.
One of the main goals of using a phase modulator is to create sidebands. When an optical
signal is phase-modulated, sidebands around the original signal are created.
These
sidebands will carry information that will be used for locking the cavity using the Pound-
Drever-Hall locking technique, which will be explained in Section 3.2.

CHAPTER 3
Optical Cavity
Optical cavities are a fundamental tool in optical research. They are used to confine
light enhancing the light-matter interaction in this region. There are different types of
optical cavities, depending on the number of mirrors they have.
The one used in this project is a Fabry-Pérot cavity or a standing wave cavity. They
consist of two mirrors parallel to each other, and the distance between them must be a
multiple of half the wavelength of the light to produce resonance. The light is reflected
back and forth between the mirrors, and it is possible to observe an increase in the
intensity of the light in the resulting standing wave pattern.
This chapter will start by explaining the dynamics of the cavity and the physics behind
a cavity lock and then the characterization of the laser beam inside the cavity.

3.2
Pound-Drever-Hall
The Pound-Drever-Hall (PDH) technique is a well-established and widely used method
for stabilizing the frequency of a laser or for stabilizing a cavity. This technique was
developed in 1983 by R. V. Pound, R. W. P. Drever, and J. L. Hall [8], and since
then, it has become an essential tool in many fields. The PDH method works phase
modulating a reference beam in a way sensitive to the laser frequency. The resulting
signal is then demodulated, low-pass filtered, and fed back into the laser or cavity to
correct the frequency or the phase difference. In our experiment, we will act on one of
the mirrors of the cavity and correct the phase difference to match the frequency of our
laser.

the refractive index from the center to the edges, allowing for compact and versatile
optical designs.

After characterizing the complex beam parameters after the GRIN lens qGRIN and inside
the cavity qcavity, we must design the optical setup to achieve the desired complex beam
parameter when the beam reaches the cavity.
While various lens configurations are
possible, we have determined that using two lenses offers the optimal solution.
Using a single lens should provide a solution since we have four degrees of freedom: the
distance from the GRIN lens to the first lens, the distance from the first lens to the
cavity, and the two lenses, for matching two parameters (beam size and beam position).
However, the lenses do not offer a full degree of freedom as we have only a finite number
to choose from. This approach is impractical because the distance between the GRIN
lens and the cavity will need to be modified. Therefore, we have opted for a two-lens
configuration where there are five degrees of freedom: the distance from the GRIN lens
to the first lens, the distance between the two lenses, the distance from the second lens
to the cavity, and the two lenses.
Additionally, we have restricted the setup by fixing the distance between the GRIN lens
and the cavity to 350 mm reducing one degree of freedom. This constraint helps simplify

CHAPTER 5
The theory behind Optical
Parametric Oscillator
This chapter will provide a detailed theoretical analysis of optical parametric oscillators
(OPO) for continuous wave (CW) and pulsed light together with an analysis of non-linear
crystals and phase matching technique.
An optical parametric oscillator is an essential tool in optics with various applications,
from quantum information to imaging and spectroscopy. They work by using the nonlin-
ear optical properties of certain materials, which allow the generation of new frequencies
by a process known as parametric down-conversion.
5.1
Nonlinear crystal
Nonlinear crystals play a crucial role in OPOs. These are responsible for the parametric
down-conversion process that generates new frequencies through the interaction of the
pump laser. There are different types of crystals, such as beta-barium borate (BBO),
lithium niobate (LiNbO3), or the one used in this project, potassium titanyl phosphate
(KTP).
Furthermore, not only the choice of the crystal is essential, but also the design of it. This
crystal must be carefully oriented and cut to ensure eﬀicient parametric down-conversion
and sometimes must be temperature controlled to maintain this optimal performance.
In addition, periodic poling is often used in the design of the crystal to enhance the
parametric down-conversion process. This periodic poling alternates the direction of the
crystal’s polarization domains and enables quasi-phase-matching, as later explained in
Section 5.2. Therefore, careful crystal design and periodic poling techniques are essential
for an eﬀicient and controlled parametric down-conversion.

6.1
Homodyne Detection
Homodyne detection serves as a well-established approach for the detection of squeezing.
The fundamental principle involves the mixing of a robust local oscillator (LO) with
the signal field under consideration. The methodology and derivation presented here
closely align with the approach outlined in Gerry and Knight’s work [4]. Theoretically,
we adopt a balanced homodyne detection system, where the beam splitter is expected
to exhibit a balanced 50:50 division. However, in practice, this is not true, and a fiber
attenuator will be used to attenuate one of the ports after the beam splitter to ensure
this 50:50% ratio.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this thesis, I have studied both theoretically and experimentally the generation of
squeezing. First, I started with a general view of quantum optics theory in Chapter 2,
where I explained the theory behind coherent and squeezed states together with some
of the most important components and some theoretical aspects of optical cavities later
in Chapter 3, including a study of one cavity stabilization technique.
In Chapter 4, the initial experimental setup for a future Gaussian boson sampling was
presented. We characterize all the possible beam and lens positions and measure the
intra-cavity-loss to determine the maximum possible squeezing.
In Chapter 5, I have studied the theory of optical parametric oscillator, presenting
results for the well-known continuous wave squeezing and giving a detailed analysis
of pulsed squeezing. In this analysis, I have calculated some of the temporal modes
and simulated the squeezing and anti-squeezing for different pump power and pulse
length. Also, I have conducted a small analysis when considering more than one pulse,
although experimentally, it may be observed a different result, as the simulations are
computationally hard.
Finally, in Chapter 6, I have discussed homodyne detection as a way of measuring
squeezing and implemented it in the laboratory. This allows us to measure squeezing for
different pump power and give us an estimation of some important parameters such as
eﬀiciency and phase noise. We improved a bit the eﬀiciency of the homodyne detection
by increasing the visibility using a polarizer in the beam splitter, which holds special
interest because it seems that in PM fibers, some undesired polarization is also traveling
through.
Future work may include improving the overall eﬀiciency for continuous wave squeezing
and reducing phase noise. Furthermore, start measuring pulsed squeezing and compare
the results with the background theory presented in this project.
