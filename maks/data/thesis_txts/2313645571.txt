Abstract
Two photon ﬂuorescence microscopy is a superior method for detecting tumour tissues
due to its ability of proving cellular and subcellular resolution images. Implementation of
this method through optical ﬁbers is a crucial step for the development of next generation
biomedical endoscopes.
We present two novel conﬁgurations of delivering ultrashort
pulses with high peak powers and free of dispersion at the microscope. In the ﬁrst case,
two-photon ﬂuorescence images(TPFI) have been retrieved by delivering 20.3fs pulses
through a single-mode ﬁber(SMF) with the method of pre-chirping. In the second case,
we demonstrate an all-ﬁber-based conﬁguration combining a SMF with a high-order
mode ﬁber(HOMF), where TPFI have been obtained by focusing down the LP02 mode.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
It has been over two decades since two-photon microscopy(TPM) has been introduced.
Speciﬁcally, the concept of two photon absorption was ﬁrst examined and published by
Maria Goppert in 1929[1]. However it was in 1990 when this idea was ﬁrst developed
by Denk et.al[2], where they managed to focus infrared light through an objective lens
so as to excite ﬂuorophores(a ﬂuorescence molecule). Few details would be necessary
to understand the working principle and the main characteristics behind this speciﬁc
technique.
TPM is a nonlinear technique, at which two photons can be absorbed simultaneously by
a molecule and to result in a ﬂuorescence signal. In contrast to one photon absorption
which is linear process and the amount of absorbed light is proportional to the intensity
of incident light, in the case of two-photon absorption, the amount of absorbed light
is proportional on the squared of incident light intensity[3]. Figure 1.1a shows the so
called Jablonski diagram at which two photons of near infrared light are incident at the
same time in a ﬂuorescence molecule, exciting it from the ground state to a higher en-
ergy states. It is worth to mention that, the sum of the energy of two incident photons
should be larger than the energy diﬀerence between the ground state and excited state
in order to get absorbed. Eventually the molecule decays to a vibrational state emitting
a ﬂuorescence signal.
Regarding tissue optics, light Propagation is restricted due to absorption and scattering
eﬀects, which are necessary to overcome in order to increase the penetration depth. Pre-
cisely, light is absorbed by the water containing in the human body at wavelengths above
1100nm and below 200nm. Moreover, from 200nm to 600nm light absorption occurs due
to proteins, haemoglobin and melanin. A range from 600nm to 1100nm is the so called
Biomedical Window where the light can propagate deeper in the tissue, deﬁning this
region as the most preferable for biomedical applications.
Although absorption is signiﬁcantly reduced on biomedical window, scattering on the

Two-photon ﬂuorescence occurs at a small volume of the focal point of microscope ob-

One method for two-photon absorption, is to focus the light from a Continuous Wave(CW)
laser. However, in order to achieve this, it needs high average power at the focal volume
which is undesirable due to the fact that it causes photo-damage and photobleaching on
the tissue.
The second and most preferable method is to use a mode-locked laser such as Ti:Sapphire,
which is able to provide high intensities by generating ultra-short pulses. Furthermore,
using ultrashort pulses in combination with a high Numerical Aperture(NA) objective,
the ﬂuorescence signal can be further increased.
The above consideration deﬁnes ultrashort pulses as a key characteristic of TPM. Pulses
in a femtosecond regime can provide high peak power which results to increase the ex-
citation eﬃciency and at the same time to reduce photo-damage and photobleaching,
since the average power exposed on the tissue is low[3].

TPM oﬀers a number of advantages compared to other imaging techniques such as,
wide-ﬁeld microscopy[4] and confocal microscopy[5]. As was mentioned above, in TPM,
excitation occurs only at a small volume of the focal point of microscope objective, in
contrast to wide-ﬁeld microscopy where light excitation occurs out of focal point result-
ing to an unclear image. Furthermore, it is not using any pinhole in order to reject
out of focus light as in confocal microscopy. Additionally in two-photon, there is a big
separation between emission and excitation spectrum which makes it feasible to reject
the excitation light while ﬁltering out only the useful ﬂuorescence signal.
Moreover,
two-photon process suﬀers from less scattering in the tissue than wide-ﬁeld microscopy,
since the wavelength of light is larger[6].
Today, there is a large amount of research topics and a huge interest on two photon
microscopy for cancer detection since the technique can provide 3D in vivo image of cells
and tissues. Additionally, a powerful method with a promising future for two photon
microscopy would be, to deliver ultrashort pulses through a ﬁber, permitting the method
to be used for endoscopic applications. However, there is an issue when such short pulses
propagate through glass materials such lenses and ﬁbers. The optical properties of glass
make the pulses broader in time and space due to linear and non-linear eﬀects introduced
by these components, as a consequence to increase their duration and making them in-
appropriate for two-photon microscopy. Thus, it is more than necessary to compensate
the ultrashort pulses and to deliver them through a ﬁber and free of dispersion at the
microscope.
1.2
Purpose
The purpose of the thesis is to demonstrate a ﬁber-based scheme of delivering ultrashort
pulses for TPM, so as to be used for bladder cancer detection. Two novel techniques
have been implemented and presented below.
The ﬁrst one, introduce a pre-compensation scheme at which negatively chirped femtosec-
ond pulses are entered into a single-mode ﬁber(SMF) and delivered free of dispersion at
the microscope.
The second conﬁguration is an all-ﬁber-based compensation scheme
where two-photon ﬂuorescence images have been obtained combining together a SMF
and a High-Order-Mode-Fiber(HOMF).
Both techniques can be used for endoscopic applications and to eﬃciently detect tumour
tissues facilitating their treatment.

2.1
UltraShort Pulses
An ultra-short pulse, is characterized by its small duration which is in the range of
femtoseconds to few tens of picoseconds. Mode-locked lasers such as Ti: Sapphire are
used to generate pulses as short as 6.5 femtosecond[7]. The method of mode-locking is
achieved by coupling the modes together inside the cavity and locking their phases which
results in a pulse train at the output of the resonator[8, p. 608]. At this point it is worth
to mention that, the number of longitudinal modes is determined by the length of the
cavity and the wavelength of light . However not all of these modes are oscillating, this
depends on the gain medium which also determines the bandwidth of a laser[9]. In our
case, Ti: Sapphire can provide very large bandwidth at 800nm.
This large spectral bandwidth of ultra-short pulses is related to the pulse duration by
the following expression [9]:
∆ν∆t >= k.
(2.1)
Where ∆ν is the FWHM spectral bandwidth, ∆t is the pulse duration and k is the
shape of the pulse. For a Gaussian shape k = 0.441[3]. Equation 2.1 it is known as
Time-Bandwidth product. When equality to k is reached on the above equation, the
pulse is called transform limited which means that the pulse has its minimum duration
for a given spectral bandwidth.

Dispersion can be normal or anomalous. In the case of normal dispersion, parameter
D < 0 and parameter β2 > 0, while in the case of anomalous dispersion, D > 0 and
β2 < 0. Furthermore another eﬀect of dispersion, is that it can increase or decrease

(upchirped) where the lower frequencies will travel faster than higher frequencies . In
contrast, if the ﬁber has anomalous dispersion, the high frequencies will travel faster
than lower as a result the negative chirp of a pulse(downchirp). Thus, positive chirp
induced by a material on a pulse can be eﬃciently compensated by introducing negative
chirp.

2.3
Linear Compensation Mechanisms
As it was mentioned above, chromatic dispersion can be compensated by using appro-
priate techniques which can alter the sign of the eﬀect. For instance, when a dielectric
material such as optical ﬁber adds positive or normal dispersion on the pulse, then com-
pensation can be done by introducing negative or anomalous dispersion on the pulse and
the opposite. This section introduce some basic mechanisms for dispersion management
on ultrashort pulses from a Ti:Sapphire laser source. It should be mentioned that, there
are also other techniques[13][14] for compensating dispersion at longer wavelengths, how-
ever these are beyond of the scope of this work since we are only interest for ultrashort
pulses at 800 nm operational wavelength.

more, the amount of dispersion can be controlled by the number of bounces between the
dielectric materials.
2.3.2
Angular Dispersion
Angular dispersion is another technique which leads to negative GDD dispersion. Angu-
lar dispersion can be introduced by a pair of prisms where the light spreads into diﬀerent
wavelengths when is incident at the so called Brewster angle. Under this speciﬁc angle
parallel polarization light will be refracted with minimum reﬂection losses. It is well
known that the refractive index of glass varies with the wavelength of light as a result
the light entering the prism, separates in diﬀerent wavelengths and diﬀerent angles at the
exit of the prism. Brewster angle can be achieved by controlling the minimum deviation
of the angle that exits the prism according to its incident path(ﬁgure 2.2)[12].
Moreover the amount of GDD can be controlled by changing the separation distance
between the prisms and the position that the beam enters in the material, since a beam
heating at the middle of the prism has to travel through more glass than a beam heating
at the apex of the prism.
Another source of angular dispersion is provided by diﬀraction gratings. Such a com-
ponent consists of grating lines or grooves which the separation distance between them
determines the grating period. Grating period plays a crucial role for disperse the light
at diﬀerent angles. The light that emerges from the grooves have diﬀerent path length
and diﬀerent phase, as a result to create an interference pattern. Now, when two waves

have the same phase, they constructively interfere each other and on the other hand,
when two wave have a diﬀerent phase, then interfere destructively. In the latter case one
of the two waves travels, half a wavelength further that the other one[15].
2.3.3
High-Order-Mode Fiber(HOMF)
An alternative method for compensating dispersion is given by high-order-mode ﬁbers.
The idea of using HOMF as compensating module, is that light can propagates in a mode
diﬀerent than the fundamental mode, exhibiting diﬀerent dispersive characteristics.
A large amount of research about linear and nonlinear properties of HOMF has been done
by Ramachandran et.al[16][17]. Furthermore, the ﬁrst scheme of delivering ultrashort
pulses through a HOMF with anomalous dispersion at 800 nm, has been implemented
by Jespersen et.al[18].
The information included in this section are mainly from the above two authors, since
our idea of using HOMF as a compensation module for TPM based on their research.
Therefore, it would be noteworthy to say few words about the dispersive properties of
these speciﬁc ﬁbers.
To begin with, a refractive index of a ﬁber consists of three diﬀerent layers. The ﬁrst layer
is the core, the second is lower index and is called ring and the third one is a doped-trench.
The dispersive properties of each mode depends on power distribution from the high
index core to the ring with respect to the wavelength. For the fundamental mode(LP01),
this power distribution results only when the entire mode is expanding from the core
index to the ring, introducing bend and radiation losses. On the other hand HOMs and
speciﬁcally LP02, exhibits a portion of power in low index rings while maintaining the
well-guiding behaviour in the core. This reveals that for the same values of refractive
index, LP02 is more dispersive than LP01[17]. Another advantage of using HOMs is that,
such modes consists of larger Aeff than LP01, making them more sustainable to nonlinear
eﬀects. Consequently, exploiting the dispersive properties of LP02, the mode can be used
9
==PAGE:20==
to compensate the dispersion of LP01. However, higher order dispersive eﬀects should
be taken into serious consideration for eﬃciently compensation. This is accomplished by
the so called dispersion slope of the ﬁber[10,p.43]:
S = (2πc
λ2 )2β3 + (4πc
λ3 )β2
(2.10)
S is the dispersion slope parameter, and β3 is the third order dispersion parameter. The
slope has units of ps/nm km. After all, for eﬃcient compensation of dispersion between
two modes, their slope values needs to be equal or at least as much closer to each other.
Here a critical question rises on how can someone select only the LP02 mode of HOMF.
The answer is given by a module called long period grating(LPG) which is used as mode
converter.
A LPG[19] is a piece of ﬁber which is exposed to UV light in order to induce period per-
turbation on the core’s refractive index. Coupling between two modes is achieved when
their phases are matched together. The eﬃciency of matching procedure depends on the
grating period, the diﬀerence in the propagation constants between these modes and the
wavelength of light. Furthermore, the bandwidth of LPG is inversely proportional to
the diﬀerence in the group delay(GD) between the modes. For short wavelengths, the
LP01 and LP02 modes are well guided in the ﬁber’s core, however the group delay(GD)
of LP02 is larger than the one of LP01 because the LP02 reﬂects more times between the
index boundaries. On the other hand for long wavelengths, the GD of LP02 is smaller
than LP01 since the energy of the ﬁrst is located mostly at the lower index rings. Thus,
at an intermediate wavelength, the GD of both modes are matched together providing a
maximum bandwidth. Moreover, when the GD’s are equal, the bandwidth is determined
by the dispersion properties between these modes and when the dispersion properties
are also equal then the bandwidth is determined by the dispersion slopes of the modes.
Hence, a HOMF which have all the above parameters equal each other, can exhibit large
bandwidth and retains the phase matching condition over a large wavelength region[17].
However, an issue with HOMFs arises when part of light from other modes interfere with
the signal of the desired mode causing a phenomenon called multipath interference(MPI).
This is more evident at the points where the HOMF needs to be spliced with a SMF,
which has as a result the decrease of the eﬃciency of mode conversion. According to
Jespersen et.al[18], 30 dB conversion eﬃciency means that 99 per cent of the mode has
been converted.
To highlight some key points of this section, HOMF can be used to compensate the
dispersion induced by SMF due to their advantage of propagating light into modes with
higher dispersive properties than the fundamental. Furthermore, splices between ﬁbers
should be almost perfect in order to minimize the losses and the MPI of the ﬁnal module.
Last, a major component of implementing a compensating scheme is the LPG, which can
selectively choose which of the HOMs needs to propagate separating it from the others.

2.4
Non-linear Eﬀects on Ultra-short Pulses
Although dispersion is a linear process in optical ﬁbers,there are also non-linear eﬀects
which can alter and distort the pulse as it propagates in a medium. One mechanism
that cause non-linearities is the so called Kerr eﬀect, at which under intense electric
ﬁeld, the refractive index of a medium changes.
The intensity dependent refractive
index has as a consequence a non-linear phase shift which varies with the time and adds
chirp to optical pulses. The phenomenon is known as Self-Phase Modulation(SPM).This
non-linear phase-shift of the refractive index can be described by[20]:
n(t) = n1(t) + n2I(t)
(2.11)
where n1 is the linear refractive index, n2 is the non-linear refractive index and I =
P/Aeff is the optical intensity. It can be clearly seen by equation 2.11 that non-linear
index part is intensity dependent. Moreover, an important parameter for estimating
nonlinearities is the so called non-linear length which indicates that, nonlinear eﬀects
become crucial above a certain length in ﬁber[10,p.270].
Lnl = 1
γP
(2.12)
Lnl is the nonlinear length, P is the peak power and γ is the nonlinear parameter given
by[10,p.64]:
γ = 2πn2
λAeff
(2.13)
Here, n2 is the nonlinear coeﬃcient, λ is the wavelength of light and Aeff is the ﬁber’s
eﬀective area.
The eﬀect of SPM on pulse bandwidth is that, it can increases the bandwidth of a
positive chirped pulse or to decrease it, if the pulse is negatively chirped as a result to
broad or narrow its spectrum[21]. Furthermore, it should be also noted that, when SPM
coexists with GVD can lead to additional pulse distortion.
Other nonlinear eﬀects which can arise by intensity variations of refractive index are the
cross-phase modulation[10,p.65] and four-wave mixing[22,p.235].
It is obvious that nonlinearities can cause a signiﬁcant distortion on ultrashort pulses,
making them unsuitable for biomedical applications.
2.5
Non-linear Compensation Mechanisms
Methods should be taken into account so as to overcome or reduce nonlinear eﬀects that
occur in ﬁbers. One method is to reduce the average power. Since the refractive index
changes due to high intensity(Power/area), reducing the average power that coupled into
the ﬁber can result to decrease the eﬀect. Moreover, light intensities are higher for Single
11
==PAGE:22==
mode ﬁbers than for multi-mode ﬁbers due to smaller eﬀective areas(Aeff). Therefore,
increasing the eﬀective area of the ﬁber in order to carry more energy in the core can
lead to reduce the eﬀect of SPM.
An additional technique to compensate SPM, is to control the peak power carried by
the pulse until the point that the eﬀect of SPM becomes equal with the eﬀect of linear
dispersion. On that occasion, the two eﬀects have opposite chirp properties and their
interaction lead to the formation of a soliton pulse[23][24]. In addition to above methods,
there are also other techniques such as phase modulators[8,p.834] which can also be used
to reduce the non-linear eﬀects.

Discussion
5.1
Pre-compensation
Theoretical calculations revealed a pulse broadening of 2.79 ps after 25 cm propagation
inside the SMF. Such a long pulse was impossible to measure it with the speciﬁc au-
tocorrelator since the component was not able to measure optical pulses above 300 fs.
Furthermore, a time domain representation of the pulse was also not feasible due to slow
electronics in the detector. Therefore, theoretical calculation point out that even for
few cm propagation in the ﬁber, the GVD can cause a severe broadening on the pulse
making it inappropriate for two photon microscopy.
On the other hand, the impact of SPM on positively chirped pulses with energies
(> 0.4nJ) has as a result to broaden their spectrum by 20 ± 2 nm as was shown in
ﬁgures 4.3a and 4.4a. A critical question could be, whether SPM distort the pulse more
than GVD. To answer on this question, it needs to solve equation (2.5) and (2.12) for
dispersion and nonlinear length respectively. Results show that nonlinear length is much
larger than dispersion length, indicating that GVD is the most dominant eﬀect in the
ﬁber and should be of more concern than SPM.
The insertion of the pre-compensation module, result to deliver a pulse of 20.3 fs at the
output of SMF as shown in ﬁgure 4.4b. Such a pulse duration is a remarkable result
verifying that second and third order dispersion have been eﬃciently compensated by
the pair of prisms and dispersive mirrors respectively. Furthermore, a part of dispersion
has been compensated by SPM since the nonlinear eﬀect has an opposite chirp factor.
Particularly, the optical pulse after the prism pair, acquire a negative chirp at which the
high frequency components are moving faster than the low ones, in contrast to positive
chirp which introduced on the pulse when enters the ﬁber and at which the high fre-
quency components are moving slower than the low ones, as a consequence both eﬀects
to cancel each other.
However as has been depicted in ﬁgure 4.3b, the interaction of SPM with the negatively
chirped pulses result to narrow the spectrum by 15 ± 5 nm verifying what has been
34
==PAGE:45==
discussed in the theory part of section 2.4. Since SPM depends on the power induced in
the ﬁber, for low power levels (< 2mW) almost all spectrum is available. In contrast for
high power levels and pulse energies larger than 0.026 nJ, the bandwidth is reduced by
the nonlinear eﬀect.
The noise at the autocorrelated signal of ﬁgure 4.4b, is due to the low average power (8
mW) inside the autocorrelator. Increasing the power at around 20 mW, a clear auto-
correlated signal can be visualized. However this would be risky for the ﬁber itself, due
to the fact that its input power also increases signiﬁcantly.
The delivery of femtosecond pulses at the microscope is of signiﬁcant importance since,
such pulses have high peak powers which in turn lead to very high intensities on the ex-
cited volume, increasing the number of generating two photon absorption events. Indeed,
the number of such events has been increased resulting to the two photon ﬂuorescence
image of ﬁgure 4.5a. Although the average power exposed on the sample was low(5mW),
the ﬂuorescence ring and the throughout are depicted very clear on the image indicating
that, two photon microscopy is a technique that allows imaging with low power levels,
minimizing the probability of photodamage and photobleaching in the tissue. Moreover
the image shows that, the existing ﬁber based conﬁguration provides micrometer reso-
lution which is useful for imaging process on living cells.
The values of minimum and maximum ﬂuorescence emission shown at the histogram of
ﬁgure 4.5b, includes a small error rate since they will vary with repeated measurements.
The interpretation of this variation depends on the portion of the background signal that
is detected each time. Thus the lower the background signal, the higher the ﬂuorescence
signal.
The inﬂuence of dispersion has as a result to increase the pulse duration and to decrease
the ﬂuorescence emission as has been shown by ﬁgure 4.6b.
Maximum ﬂuorescence
emission occurs at the shortest pulse duration(20.3fs), while it decreased as the pulse
duration is slightly increased. Furthermore, it can be clearly seen by the ﬁgure 4.6a that
even for very small diﬀerences in the pulse duration, the image of ﬂuorescence bead with
the brightest ring is given by the one with the shortest pulse. This result highlight the
fact that dispersion can signiﬁcantly reduce the amount of ﬂuorescence emission.
An example regarding the importance of ultrashort pulses on TPM is given by ﬁgures
4.7 and 4.8. Comparing the histograms of both ﬁgures one can observe the diﬀerence in
the ﬂuorescence emission. Speciﬁcally, even for low power levels(0.5mW) the maximum
intensity of ﬂuorescence signal is almost double when the laser is mode-locked in ﬁgure
4.7, in comparison to ﬁgure 4.8 at which the laser operates in CW mode. At 4.8mW
the diﬀerence in the maximum intensity between the two ﬁgures is increased by 1058 for
mode-locked operation. Moreover it can be clearly seen by the images that the beads are
depicted much more bright in mode-locked operation than in CW specially in the case
of 4.8mW. These results point out again that ultrashort pulses with high peak powers
results to increase the exited ﬂuorescence intensity while at the same time the average
power can be kept in low levels. In contrast the image formation on CW operation, it
35
==PAGE:46==
is not a two-photon absorption process but probably a result of scattered photons that
managed to pass from the shortpass ﬁlter and detected by the PMT. Thus, for generating
high intensities in CW-mode, the average power should be increased enough with a risk
of causing photobleaching in the molecule and photodamage in the tissue.
The two photon ﬂuorescence image of a BPAE cell in ﬁgure 4.9a, displays very clear
the nucleus part while the less bright microtubules are also depicted. Additionally, the
F-actin is not observable. A possible reason that the last two parts of the cell are not
depicted so clear, could be due to the low detection sensitivity of the PMT. This issue
can be overcome by increasing the voltage level of the PMT which result to increase
its gain and as a consequence to increase the detection sensitivity level. Moreover, the
image is not colourful due to the short pass ﬁlter that is used in the microscope setup.
Hence, replacing the existing short pass ﬁlter with a bandpass that allows only a certain
wavelength to pass (for instance at 461 nm), the visualization of a blue nucleus would
be feasible.
Additionally, the proof of concept about two photon absorption process is demonstrated
in ﬁgure 4.9b. The graph shows a nonlinear power dependence of ﬂuorescence emission
of Rhodamine B dyes with a slope of 1.86 verifying that two photons have been absorbed
by a ﬂuorescence molecule.
The above two-photon ﬂuorescence images, reveal the eﬃciency of the ﬁber-based conﬁg-
uration with cellular and subcellular resolution making it appropriate tool for endoscopic
applications.
5.2
All-Fiber Based Compensation
Since the Ti:Sapphire laser did not provide any functionality to tune it in a wavelength
diﬀerent than 800nm, the bandpass ﬁlter utility was to eliminate all the unwanted out of
band radiation and at the same time to allow high transmission in the region of 770nm.
This was performed mainly because the LPG provides eﬃcient mode conversion from
LP01 to LP02 at this speciﬁc wavelength. A center wavelength diﬀerent than 770nm will
have as a result to reduce the coupling behaviour between these modes and therefore to
lead in ineﬃcient mode conversion which in turns leads to ineﬃcient dispersion compen-
sation. On the other hand, measurements of pulse duration before and after the ﬁber
module were not feasible and a possible reason for this, could be due to the low average
power inside the autocorrelator(recall that the autocorrelator works better at average
power levels around 20mW).
As has been discussed in the theory, for eﬃcient dispersion compensation, the dispersion
slopes of both ﬁbers should have diﬀerent signs and needs to be as close together as
possible. Since the dispersion slopes are not exactly equal(0.59 ps/(nm2km) for SMF
and −2.54 ps/(nm2km) for HOMF), the contribution of third order dispersion has a
signiﬁcant role on a pulse broadening. Thus, the length of the module was decreased
down to 1.76m so as to minimize this contribution. Furthermore, regarding second order
36
==PAGE:47==
dispersion compensation, both ﬁbers were tolerated to appropriate lengths where their
GDDs are almost equal but with diﬀerent signs.
The theoretical calculations shown that after propagation in 0.815m of SMF, the pulse
duration increases from 40fs to 2.7ps taking into consideration only the GVD. Since
both ﬁbers have the same GDDs but diﬀerent signs, an eﬃcient dispersion compensation
is expected at the output of the HOMF. It is worth mentioning again that the average
power at the exit of the module is very low(1.6mW) making impractical the measure-
ments of pulse duration inside the autocorrelator.
Even for low input power levels, the inﬂuence of SPM results to broaden the spectrum
by 5 ± 1nm. However to examine whether SPM is the dominant eﬀect, the dispersion
length(LD) and the nonlinear length(LNL) were calculated and their results determine if
there is linear or nonlinear pulse propagation. Particularly, for linear pulse propagation
the LD should be smaller than LNL and the ratio of these two should be less than one.
Figure 4.11 shows this ratio as a function of average power where the black dashed line
indicates the limit under which linear pulse propagation occurs. It can be clearly seen
that for the case of SMF, dispersion is the most dominant eﬀect at average power lower
than 25mW. On the other hand, for the case of HOMF the limit is reduced to 15mW
underling the fact that, nonlinear eﬀects and speciﬁcally SPM is dominant and should
be much more of concern than the dispersion. This is due to the fact that the HOMF
exhibits smaller values of Aeff for LP02 mode than the SMF. Moreover, an interesting
point is when the LD = LNL giving a ratio of one, meaning that, a soliton is generating
at this speciﬁc position. However, we should be careful about the above ratios, since
these results have been obtained assuming 40fs initial pulse duration.
The two photon ﬂuorescence image of a ﬂuorescent bead in ﬁgure 4.13 depicts very clear
the dark red ring and the stain throughout. Furthermore, the histogram shows high
ﬂuorescence intensity with only 1.3mW average power at the sample. Additionally, it is
worth mentioning here, that the image was achieved by focusing down the LP02 mode
rather than the fundamental, increasing further the image resolution. As has been shown
by Smith et.al[39], the Lp01 mode is focusing better than the LP02 in a linear process,
however in nonlinear process the outer ring of the LP02 is cut-oﬀand only the inner
part is present at the focus point of the microscope objective, resulting to higher lat-
eral resolution since the intensity distribution of LP02 is higher than Lp01. Moreover as
ﬁgure 4.14 shows, the ﬂuorescence intensity is high enough so as to depict the image
of a ﬂuorescent bead at even for a very low average power(0.4mW). Additionally, the
ﬂuorescence intensity increases rapidly as we increase the average power, resulting to a
more bright image.
In ﬁgure 4.12b the nonlinear power dependence of ﬂuorescence emission for Rhodamine B
dyes revealed a slope of 2.11. The small aberration from 2.0 is probably due to the error
that might exist from the background signal such as in the case of pre-compensation
scheme where the slope value was lower than 2.0. On the other hand, the sample with
the BPAE cells was placed at the microscope for two-photon imaging, however we did
37
==PAGE:48==
not achieve to obtain any image. A possible cause of this issue might it be due to the low
average power exposed at the sample(1.3mW). It should be reminded that, in the case
of pre-compensation scheme, cell image was obtained with an average power of 7mW at
the sample. Since the cells are not so bright as the beads, more power could be solve
that issue.
For that occasion, the above assumption was based on the calculations of equation from
Denk at.al[2,ref.9] about the number of photons absorbed per ﬂuorophore per pulse(na).
The two-photon absorption cross section of DAPI was found 18GM[40][41] at 770nm. A
pulse duration of 150fs was also assumed and for an average power of 1mW, calculations
shown na = 8.9 ∗10−4 which is very low in comparison with the pre-compensation case
where na = 0.2. Thus, we conclude that for the same number of absorbed photons(na)
in the all-ﬁber based scheme, an average power of around 12mW needs to be delivered
at the microscope.
5.3
Comparison Between Two Schemes
Results of both schemes have been compared together so as to highlight any similari-
ties and diﬀerences that exist. It is worth mentioning again, the comparison based on
the assumption and the theoretical calculation of pulse duration for the all-ﬁber-based
setup, since we could not measure it experimentally. Hence, a comparison regarding the
eﬃciency of both set-ups would not be proper, since the above assumptions should be
replaced or validate by experimental results.
In terms of pulse duration, both schemes have shown that pulses in the regime of fem-
toseconds have been delivered at the output of the ﬁber, indicating that material dis-
persion has eﬃciently compensated. Moreover, the eﬀect of SPM was evident in both
schemes by narrowing and broadening the spectrum accordingly. However SMP did not
cause any implications on the pulse, since dispersion was the dominant eﬀect in both
cases. On the other hand, the graph of ﬁgure 4.11 points out that for linear propaga-
tion, average power below 15mW should be considered for the all-ﬁber-based scheme.
This is because the total ﬁber length of the speciﬁc conﬁguration is longer than in pre-
compensation scheme and due to the smaller Aeff that the LP02 mode in HOMF consists
of.
Comparison between the images of ﬂuorescence beads obtained by both setups would
not be workable, since the bead images taken by the pre-compensation scheme does
not refer on the same bead as the images taken by the all-ﬁber-based case(each bead
exhibits diﬀerent ﬂuorescence intensity values). However one can observe that, in the
second setup the bead image of ﬁgure 4.13 is depicted more intense and possibly with
better lateral resolution than in the pre-compensation case, due to LP02 mode.
As was mentioned in section 5.2, cell image was not feasible in the all-ﬁber-based con-
ﬁguration and a potential reason could be, due to low average power which has as a
result to reduce the na. On the other hand, if that is the case and according to the bead
38
==PAGE:49==
images, a better image of the cells is also expected by the second scheme.
After all, in terms of complexity and applicability, the all-ﬁber-based scheme is more
favourable than the pre-compensation where in the last occasion more optical compo-
nents are needed which makes the conﬁguration more severe for endoscopic implemen-
tation.
5.4
Comparison With Other Relevant Studies
During the past few years, several research topics about dispersion compensation on
ultrashort pulses at near infrared wavelength have been published. Le et.al[42] managed
to deliver 25fs pulses over a 1.6m large mode area (LMA) photonic crystal ﬁber using a
pair of dispersive mirrors and four prisms. Moreover Lefort et.al[43] have demonstrated
a scheme with a pair of grisms at which 45fs pulses have been delivered after 2m
propagation inside a SSMF. Additionally, the same method was used by Kalashyan
et.al[44] for eﬃcient delivery of 29fs through LMA-SMF and SSMF.
Furthermore, other relevant studies[45][46][25] have shown the advantage of ﬁber-based
ultrashort pulse delivery on TPM using either a LMA-SMF or Multimode ﬁbers.
In addition to all these methods, a new approach was presented by Jespersen et.al[18],
where they managed to deliver femtosecond pulses through a HOMF at 800nm and free
of dispersion.
Although the dispersion have been eﬃciently compensated by the above methods, we
report the shortest pulse delivery (20.3fs) at the output of the ﬁber that is in our
knowledge so far. Moreover, we show that TPFI are also feasible through a SSMF and
using a pair of dispersive mirrors and two prisms.
On the other hand, TPM through an all-ﬁber-based scheme was implemented without
the need of pulse pre-chirping. Despite the fact that experimental measurements of pulse
duration was not feasible, the TPFI of the beads revealed that this method is the most
appropriate for endoscopic applications, since the ﬁbers can replace successfully all the
free space optics proving high eﬃciency and less complexity. Furthermore, the technique
of using LP02 mode to compensate the dispersion and for the image acquisition, was
implemented for the ﬁrst time in TPM with very promising results.
5.5
Conclusion
In conclusion, we demonstrate two diﬀerent techniques of delivering ultashort pulses
through a ﬁber for TPM. In the ﬁrst technique, 20.3fs pulses have been delivered at the
output of 25cm SSMF with the method of pre-chirping using a pair of dispersive mirrors
and two prims. In the second technique, ultrashort pulses have been delivered at the
output of 1.76m all-ﬁber-based conﬁguration which consists of a SSMF-LPG-HOMF.
In both cases, images of ﬂuorescence beads with diameter of 6um was observed and
39
==PAGE:50==
analysed, pointing out that both schemes are capable of providing cellular resolution
which is an important asset for detecting cancer cells.
Last but not least, the all-ﬁber-based conﬁguration presented in this work, is a very
powerful tool and could potentially be used for endoscopic bladder cancer detection. It
needs only few seconds to retrieve an image and to reveal tumour tissues, giving doctors
the opportunity to facilitate their treatment in an early diagnosis process. However,
more research is needed to validate or to oppose the assumptions that was expressed
during the experimental work.
5.6
Improvements and Features
Regarding the pre-compensation scheme, improvements can be done in terms of the
ﬁber length by replacing the two prisms with a pair of reﬂection gratings. The amount
of negative group delay dispersion that gratings can oﬀer is much larger than this of the
prisms, giving the possibility to increase the length of a ﬁber. However, the drawback of
using such a component is the high reﬂection losses that introduces.
Furthermore, improvements are also feasible regarding the simplicity of the existing
setup. For instance, the pair of prisms and the chirped mirrors can be replaced by a pair
of grisms which is a combination of a grating and a prism in one component. Grisms
are able to provide adequate both second and third order dispersion compensation. On
the other hand the alignment of such a component is more challenging than the above-
mentioned.
In the all-ﬁber-based scheme, the most important improvement is to increase the average
power delivered at the output of the ﬁber so as to enable eﬃciently pulse measurements.
This could be achieved by writing a LPG for 800nm center wavelength.
Since the
Ti:Sapphire has its most power at this particular wavelength and can not be tuned down
to 770nm, a LPG at 800nm would give the possibility to remove the bandpass ﬁlter from
the setup, as a consequence to permit more average power at the input of the ﬁber and
at the same time to provide eﬃcient mode conversion. Alternatively, a diﬀerent light
source with a center wavelength at 770nm would be also suitable.
Improvements are also feasible in terms of the ﬁber length, where the total 1.76m could
be reduced to 1m as a result to decrease the amount of accumulated high order disper-
sion and making it more compatible for endoscopic applications.
Eventually, after increasing the average power and the successful measurement of pulse
duration before and after the module, the all-ﬁber-based conﬁguration should be test
it again on the image acquisition of ﬂuorescence beads with more power(approximately
4.5mW) as well on the BPAE cell.
As a next step, a method of collecting the back-reﬂected ﬂuorescence signal inside the
ﬁber module should be implemented. This could be achieved using a double cladding
ﬁber where the transmitted signal will propagate in the ﬁber’s core and the ﬂuorescence
signal will collected in the ﬁber’s cladding region.

==PAGE:51==
Although the TPM is a powerful technique for visualizing cellular and subcellular sur-
faces, a combination with other modalities such as Optical Coherence Tomography(OCT)
would enable the endoscopic applications to provide also structural tissue information.
In that occasion, it would be more convenient to use a ﬁber with a double or a triple
core structure so as to propagate the OCT light from the one core, the two-photon signal
from the other core and the third core will be used to collect the back-reﬂected light from
both modalities.
Last, apart from biomedical applications, the all-ﬁber based scheme could potentially
be used as a dispersion-compensation module on telecommunication industry, providing
large amounts of dispersion compensation.
