ABSTRACT SUMMARY
In recent years, technological progress driven by both environmental and safety
incentives has pushed development of autonomous vehicles into new territory. In
the coming years, autonomous vehicles will bring a paradigm change in the way
automobiles are operated, not only in traditional urban areas, but also in other
environments. With the propagation of more self-driving vehicles navigating in rural
areas, new challenges arise, and thus new, innovative solutions are needed.
This thesis addresses the challenges of navigating a racetrack without barriers on
the side of the track. Navigating a racetrack with barriers has been investigated
and solved in previous projects. A racetrack with no barriers is a very good model
of a traditional country road in rural areas, thus is solves the need for a safe test
environment when developing new solutions, and at the same time resemble the
real-world scenario for which solutions are needed.
The initial part of this project analysed the existing platform (the DTU Dynamo)
and its capabilities and concluded that it was possible to add new functionality
to the existing platform without changing hardware and compute capacity. A key
element was to enable the car to decide what was the road, and what was not, when
no barriers existed.
The existing perception capabilities was enhanced with roughness estimation and
reﬂectivity based on data from the 3D VLP16 Velodyne LiDAR sensor used on the
platform. This enabled the car to correctly track the road in the local environment
with no prior knowledge of the area.
Then a corner detection algorithm was implemented with the purpose of optimizing
the driving strategy, in order to drive as fuel-eﬃcient as possible, around the track
without sacriﬁcing safety.
The solutions developed in this project are tested and investigated in various
environments. Test drives at the Roskilde Racing Center track without barriers,
showed that the solution is robust and capable of driving several laps around the
track autonomous and based only on data from the LiDAR sensor. Other rural areas
were tested successfully as well.
The DTU Dynamo platform now has the foundation for autonomous and fuel-
eﬃcient driving on a barrier less track. That ﬁts the expanded requirements of the
autonomous competition and is much closer to real-world scenarios and the future of
mobility.

Background
Technical University of Denmark [DTU] Roadrunners is a student-driven project
with focus on research and construction of extremely fuel-eﬃcient ecocars. The
Roadrunners team consist mainly of mechanical and electrical engineering students.
The motivation behind the DTU Roadrunner team has been participating in the

The Shell Eco-marathon competition is a global program where students from all
over the world design and build ultra-energy-eﬃcient cars. The vehicles are driven
around a track at the competition, pushing for innovative solutions to improve energy
eﬃciency.

1.2
Motivation
Over the last years, autonomous vehicles have become a popular subject of discussion.
Large companies spend a vast amount of money on developing autonomous cars, and
for a good reason.
Autonomous vehicles could bring about one of the biggest
revolutions when considering our transportation system.
Even as self-driving
technology is becoming increasingly common, a major challenge in the area of
autonomous vehicles is trust. It is important to get this technology out into controlled
environments to inspire the engineers and scientists of the future to be taking on
challenges such as developing and reﬁning the autonomous technology to create this
trust. Closed-oﬀareas such as tracks, roads, and race tracks are good opportunities
for safe and controlled testing to speed up the process. In an article by Oxford
University, Norman Koch, Technical Director of Shell Eco-marathon, said:
“By presenting this concept vehicle we aim to inspire our participants and keep the
competition exciting, challenging and relevant for the future of mobility.”3
For these reasons, the Shell Eco-marathon competition has provided safe environments
for universities to extend their UC cars with autonomous capabilities for the last two
years. Both years, DTU Dynamo participated and proved that it is capable of driving
autonomously at a track with barriers on each side of the road. Concerns about
the use of barriers were raised. It is diﬃcult for the current autonomous system to
navigate if larger gaps in the barriers are present. Driving with barriers does not
mimic real-world scenarios and is not seen as the future of mobility; furthermore,
setting up barriers is not practical. Looking into new and diﬀerent methods for
navigating tracks with or without barriers will provide the future competitions with
new and exciting possibilities to extend the challenges to demonstrate better the
progress made with the technology of autonomous driving that will revolutionize the
worlds of mobility and transport.
An additional beneﬁt of testing on race tracks is the opportunity to implement new
navigation strategies that closer mimics the manual racing driver. The developed
car is a capable fuel-eﬃcient race car holding world records. Therefore, this could
be an intriguing step forward in implementing autonomous driving challenges that

1.3
Problem Statement
The following problem statement was established as this basis for the master’s thesis:
“The aim of the project is to design, implement, and test optimized navigation for
autonomous track driving with the DTU Dynamo ecocar. The thesis will focus on the
route planning aspects and the necessary methods of constructing an environment
map. The thesis’s outcome will facilitate the DTU Roadrunner project by extending
the DTU Dynamo with optimal route planning and navigation for autonomous track
driving.”
As stated, the focus of this thesis lies in research, design, and implementation of
adequate algorithms onto the DTU Dynamo ecocar, to solve the task of navigating
autonomously around a race track without the need for barriers. From this, several
project objectives are formulated:
• Deﬁne the conditions needed in order to detect track limits without barriers
through LiDAR perception
• Develop a method to detect a track through LiDAR perception autonomously
• Deﬁne what an optimal route based on Dynamo’s platform is
• Develop a method to build an optimal route based on Dynamo’s platform
1.4
Assumptions, conditions and limitations
Several projects have been conducted on the DTU Dynamo platform since the
introduction of the autonomous challenge. Steering, acceleration, communication,
and fusion of odometry and LiDAR data are vital parts of the core platform that
were introduced by previous projects.
Based on these projects, it is assumed that the current hardware platform on the
DTU Dynamo car is adequate to fulﬁll the objectives of the project. Researching
and developing new software is enough to successfully implement new solutions for
autonomous driving.
However, it is essential that the algorithm is capable of running real-time on the
current hardware platform. Especially since the current platform has proven capable
of handling the autonomous challenges at the competition even though the platform
is a low power system with limited computational power. This must be taken
into consideration when developing new solutions. Therefore, this thesis will not
investigate nor explain all known autonomous driving methods but focus on methods
that can be implemented with the current platform.

Safety is the most important condition when testing an autonomous solution. Thus,
the solution is not intended for public roads, nor is it expected to be tested in
such situations. Testing in closed areas without the risk of oncoming traﬃc or

2.1
Background analysis
Prior work by DTU
This section addresses some of the prior work executed by students from DTU on
the Dynamo autonomous platform. Overall, three masters, one bachelor and six
synthesis projects, have worked on diﬀerent areas of the autonomous capabilities
of the DTU Dynamo car over the years. All were contributing to the autonomous
platform as it is today. Culminating in a vehicle capable of executing the speciﬁc
challenges within the ﬁeld of autonomous navigation, set at the autonomous Shell
Eco-Marathon competitions. Several other projects have been conducted on the
mechanics and design areas, such as the ICE, chassis, and aerodynamics, but they
are not the focus of this research.
The ﬁrst of the master’s theses was: Platform Integration for Autonomous Self-
Driving Vehicles, written by [Hø17]. He worked on designing the foundation of the
autonomous platform by integrating the necessary hardware and software into the
DTU Dynamo car. In his words, sensors and actuators designed, implemented, and
tested, "augmenting the car with autonomous capabilities". Some of the fundamentals
include input from sensors such as LiDARs, GPS, IMUs, and wheel sensors. Together
they form the basis for the car’s ability to sense its surroundings. Combined with
closed-loop control of the actuators allows the car to drive a predetermined path
and will, with further improvements, enable DTU Dynamo to achieve full autonomy
and participate in the expected upcoming autonomous competitions.
In preparations for the 2018 autonomous Shell Eco-marathon challenge, two M.Sc
projects worked concurrently in close relation with each other. The M.Sc thesis
executed by [Top18] focused on situation awareness for autonomous vehicles by
extending the perception capabilities of the existing autonomous platform built by
[Hø17], primarily through a Velodyne VLP-16 LiDAR. Based on the 3D LiDAR

output, the project’s goal was to detect the expected barriers at the competition
robustly. He worked on processing the raw LiDAR data combining it with the
odometry and IMU, resulting in an output, which he refers to as sensor fusion. From
the fused LiDAR data, a graph-based ground detection method is investigated. This

The method has been implemented and tested in various environments, and he
concluded that it was functional. Overall, the high amount of raw data provided
by the 3D LiDAR has been successfully ﬁltered and fused with odometry and IMU
into essential information for situation awareness. It also serves as a foundation for
future navigation and planning in regards to the autonomous challenges.
The other M.Sc. thesis written by Jesper Martin Christensen and Lars David Aktor
focused on Sensor-Based Navigation for Autonomous Car [AC18]. Based on the fused
3D LiDAR sensor input from [Top18], it investigated and implemented a method
called Voronoi, to calculate a route in the middle of the road based on the detected
barriers. It was deemed as the safest strategy for the competition as the method will,
at all times, try to ﬁnd the route furthest from any classiﬁed obstacle. This route
is used as a reference signal to the various controllers for the steering, speed, and
brake power. Both M.Sc projects had algorithm-execution time as a priority since
the system is meant to function in real-time. The various controllers for physical
control are proposed, implemented, and veriﬁed.
Together these M.Sc projects provide a functioning autonomous vehicle and a
foundation for further development and research.
Prior work general autonomous driving
Existing methods for autonomous driving based on perception, localization, and
segmentation algorithms can be separated based on whether they require access to
prior knowledge of the speciﬁc environment and the degree of information available.
Even within the domain of LiDAR-based localization, several techniques exist. Widely
used methods in urban areas include so-called mobility-on-demand systems that

2.2.1
Autonomous vehicles features
The main features of an autonomous vehicle (or mobile robot) can be divided into
seven categories according to [RR04]
1. Self-steering - the ability to set and move the steerable wheels within the
available degrees of freedom.
2. Braking - the ability to utilize the brakes in a controlled manner.
3. Acceleration - the ability to operate the engine within its parameters.
4. Perception - the ability to detect obstacles in the predicted path.
5. Localization - the ability to determine the pose and velocity of the vehicle
by use of sensors.
6. Navigation - the ability to navigate in the environment trough use of sensors.
7. Planning - the ability to calculate the required path to move from its position
to a speciﬁed location incorporating perception to avoid obstacles.
A vehicle or robot capable of performing all seven would be widely accepted as
a fully autonomous vehicle.
The deﬁnition of a fully autonomous machine or
platform can be discussed widely. A commonly used classiﬁcation for autonomous
vehicles standardized by SAE International [Int16] can bee seen on ﬁgure 2.1. It
helps categorizes diﬀerent levels of automation based on its capabilities.

SAE level is relatable to the seven categories outlined by [RR04]. The ﬁrst three
categories, self-steering, braking, and acceleration of the Roland Siegwart and Illah
.R Nourbakhsh list, ﬁt one of the four essential groups called the execution of steering
and acceleration/deceleration.

2.2.2
Considerations about track driving
All racetracks can, in essence, be boiled down and sectioned into a unique set of
straights and corners. In racing, the racing line is the route that the car takes around
the track. For each track, there is an inﬁnite number of possible lines. The optimal
race line typically identiﬁes the trajectory that a car should follow to achieve the
best lap-time on a given track. Contradicting there are also events or circumstances
where lap time is not the main objective. The following is some of the major factors,
racing line depends on.
Car speciﬁcs
• Propulsion power
• Vehicle dynamics
Track speciﬁcs
• Corner layout
• The position and direction of the next corner
Such a clear distinction is not feasible as many factors directly intertwine. As
mentioned, there is an inﬁnite number of possible lines, and one line is rarely the
optimal line for all circumstances. For the environment side of things or also referred
to as the track speciﬁcs, track layout, track surface, and weather all play a role in
ﬁnding an optimal line. For the speciﬁc car, propulsion refers to the power levels or
the ability or rate of acceleration and speed it can provide. Vehicle dynamics spans
over areas such as tiers, aerodynamics, and mechanical design. They all inﬂuence
the car’s ability to drive around a racetrack. Consequently, it is not easy, and it is
time-consuming to determine or outline the ideal path around a speciﬁc track. Even
if a static optimum is found, other parameters, like the grip level, are dynamic. Over
time, during racing scenarios, ﬂuctuations or decreases aﬀects the optimum and a
reason that race car drivers are an essential part of the overall performance; hence
the car will not be able to follow the same line nor strategy over time.
A feature that is present in almost all racing lines is referred to as the apex or
clipping point. If a corner is simpliﬁed into 3 phases (more phases exist but for the
sake of simpliﬁcation only 3 is mentioned here). All corners have the following three
sections.
• First - entrance to the corner
• Second - middle of the corner
• Third - exit from the corner

The entrance is self-explanatory but still has several actions and parameters. It
marks the transition from either a straight section or another corner to a new corner.
Approaching at too high speed requires braking. What is too high? Moreover, how
to know in advance? The chain of actions necessary depends on the vehicle dynamics,
current state, and the corner layout. The middle part is where most drivers and
racing strategies aim for the apex of the corner. Apex is the point at which the car
is closest to the inside edge of the corner and typically at maximum steering angle.
Once at the apex, the driver and or car should be able to reduce the steering lock
and increase the throttle and focus on the exit. There are diﬀerent ways to deal
with apex and corner strategy in general; two commonly used terminologies are the
geometrical apex and the racing apex. In theory, the optimal route around a given
corner seen as 2D is the geometric line navigating the corner with the largest radius.

and many more, all serves a purpose. All have their advantages and drawbacks and
can oﬀer beneﬁts in certain dynamic situations. Often optimizing for each corner
individually, without taking the next transition or section into account, will hurt or
limit the overall performance.

The 3D LiDAR sensor used in this project, is the Velodyne VLP-16 seen in ﬁgure 3.3,
and was implemented in a previous project. It is a small active exteroceptive sensor,
with a low power consumption running on 9-18 V, and it is capable of measuring both
the distance to objects and the reﬂectivity of the object. An array of 16 infra-red (IR)
lasers paired with IR detectors spin within its ﬁxed housing. It provides a scan of
its surroundings, ﬁring each laser approximately 18.000 times per second, providing,
real-time, detailed 3D point cloud.As mentioned earlier, this sensor is assumed to
meet the requirements needed to build a road detection algorithm for navigating
around a race track.
A channel is a laser emitter and detector pair. Each channel is ﬁxed at a particular
elevation angle relative to the horizontal plane of the sensor, as seen in ﬁgure 3.4.
The vertical ﬁeld of view is 30◦, −15◦to 15◦with an angular resolution (the elevation
angle (ω)) of 2◦. Each laser channel is given a Laser ID number, which can be seen
in appendix D. In this project, only channel 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 14 are used because
only these channels emit laser towards the ground. It is suﬃcient for road detection
because looking for objects above the ground, such as branches from trees or road
signs, is not part of this project’s scope. As a result, only half of the data gathered
by the sensor needs to be processed, reducing the execution time of the solution.
Figure 3.4: VLP-16 - 30 degree ﬁeld of view of the 16 individual lasers with 2 degree
increments shown in a 2 dimensional view.
It spins around its vertical axis covering 360◦in the horizontal ﬁeld of view with an
angular resolution (Azimuth (α)) of 0.1◦−0.4◦. The angular resolution is the angle
between two ﬁring sequences, where a ﬁring sequence occurs when all the lasers in a
sensor are ﬁred. The resolution depends on the rotation frequency, which can be
set between 5-20Hz; the higher the frequency is, the lower is the accuracy. In this
project, it is set to 10Hz (resolution of 0.2◦) as it was in the previous projects seen as
a good trade-oﬀbetween speed and accuracy [AC18]. The scan rate of 10Hz limits
the execution time of the road detection algorithm to 100 ms.

the information of data points equal to 24 ﬁring sequences (384 points). Each packet

Reﬂectivity
The VLP-16 LiDAR sensor can measure the intensity of a returned laser beam.
Therefore, it can recognize diﬀerent objects based on the reﬂectivity of the scanned
surface. The reﬂectivity of an object is independent of laser power and distance.
For each laser measurement, a reﬂectivity value is returned in addition to distance.
These values are based on laser calibration against NIST-calibrated reﬂectivity
reference targets at the factory. Reﬂectively values are segmented into two ranges to
distinguish between diﬀuse reﬂectors (e.g., tree trunks, road) in the low range from
retroreﬂectors (e.g., road signs, license plates) in the high range. The values can be
seen in table 3.2.
Retroreﬂectors reﬂect light to its sources with a minimum of scattering, where the
separation between the transmitting laser and the receiving detector is negligible.
As stated in the user manual, "retroreﬂecting surfaces pop with reﬂected IR light
compared to diﬀuse reﬂectors that tend to scatter the reﬂected energy."
Additionally, it is stated that the reﬂectivity is ideal for detecting retroreﬂectors like
street signs or lane markings but using it for road detection is not mentioned.

Ground Detection
Graph-based ground detection was implemented by [Top18] and used to achieve
robust obstacle detection by separating obstacles and ground points. Regions of
ground points are seen as driveable points, which the graph-based ground detection
solution removes. The remaining points are grouped into obstacle regions where the
car is not allowed to drive. The classiﬁcation starts by looking at points belonging to
the ring closest to the car. If a point in this ring lies approximately within the ground
plane as the car, then it is ground; if not, then it is an obstacle. The remaining points
are evaluated based on the slope change between a point and its lower neighbor. A

4.3
Road Detection
Separating road and ground points is the next step of the road detection algorithm
and will be an extension of the previous solution. Instead of removing all ground
points as the previous method, this solution aims only to remove points found on
the road’s surface. The remaining ground points and obstacle points are combined
into obstacle regions. This distinction between road and the surrounding areas is an
essential requirement to classify drivable and non-drivable regions from the point
cloud. It is subsequently enabling the car to identify the track without depending
on barriers.
4.3.1
Roughness
The method for robustly detecting the road at the race track or rural areas from
LiDAR measurements is based on roughness estimation. The general assumption
is that in a given environment, the car must interpret and react autonomously
based on LiDAR perception. The road in the measured environment will repeatedly
appear with a smoother texture relative to surrounding areas based on the distance
measurements.
These points lie approximately within the same ground plane,
therefore classiﬁed as ground points. However, since ground points are a broad term
that includes both road and the surrounding areas, a new classiﬁcation is required.
If the assumption regarding the variation in the distance measurement of the local
environment is not met, then there is no further need for another classiﬁcation as it is
not suitable for navigating based solely on a 3D LiDAR. However, if the assumption
is met, then introducing a new classiﬁcation is needed to navigate the environment.
Roughness estimation is a measurement of the smoothness of the ground and provides
a tool to separate the road from ground points. It shows how much points within
the ground plane vary from their neighbors; smaller variations indicate smoother
textures like asphalt than more substantial variations that indicate rougher and more
disruptive regions such as wild vegetation, grass, gravel. At an early stage of the
project, it was assumed that these variations could be used to distinguish between
drivable and non-drivable areas. To test, and support the assumptions held, early
LiDAR test was conducted at the bus track to retrieve raw data output from the
LiDAR that visually show if roughness estimation is a viable method worth further
investigation.

and the diﬀerence between rough and smooth can be used to locate the edges of the
road. The idea is that the edges can be located by using the standard deviation to
search for the transition between smooth and rough textures. This is illustrated in
ﬁgure 4.12, where the vertical red lines mark the position of the road edges.

Introducing a method that locates the right and left edges of the road can be used
to prevent errors where points otherwise would be wrongly classiﬁed as the road.
The right side of the road is located by ﬁnding the n’th point in each ring, where
the standard deviations are larger than x.
x and n are tunable parameters used to choose the sensitivity of edge detection. n
should be above a certain threshold to ensure that noise is not seen as an edge.
Also, the position of the right edge in a ring must not diverge more than a certain
degree from the right edge located in the previous scan to ensure that noise does not

OPTIMAL ROUTE PLANNING ALGORITHM
The following section sums up the part of the deduced solution outlined above in
chapter 3 referred to as "Route planning" in section 3.2. It describes the integration
of the various methods and solutions that lays the foundation for the route planning
algorithm. It is the second of the two requirements mentioned in section 3.1. The
chapter is partitioned into several sections focusing on the speciﬁc areas, in order to
ease the understanding.
6.1
Driving strategy
Robustness is still the primary goal and more important than being able to improve
fuel eﬃciency. A previous thesis stated that due to hardware and steering wheel
speed limitations, the optimal position of the car is in the middle of the road; furthest
from every object [AC18]. Doing so minimizes the risk of crashing into the side
of the road or other detected obstacles. However, when driving on a track such
as the RRCs without barriers, the consequence of driving oﬀthe track is minimal.
This allows, inspired by the motivation in chapter 1, section 1.2, for an extension of
the driving strategy. As the UC platform is designed and built with fuel eﬃciency
in mind, implementing robust route planning methods with eﬃciency in mind to
incorporate the goal of DTU Dynamo, which is still energy-eﬃcient driving.
Additionally, this thesis aims to navigate based on the local environment without
relying on predetermined routes based on detailed 3D maps or GPS coordinates.
Thus, having a map of the location of the corners at the track and a predetermined
plan of how to drive through each corner is not within the scope of this thesis.
Instead, the extension will be based on detecting corners and planning the route
while driving through it. If the car drives at a straight section or its uncertain if it is
driving straight or through a corner, then driving in the middle of the road is seen
as the optimal route. If, however, the car detects a corner, then the new strategy
aims to enable the car for a more eﬃcient way of driving through corners.
As mentioned in section 2.2.2, there are a great number of possible lines through a
corner. The optimal lines for this platform depend on the detection range of the 3D
LiDAR sensor, the speed, and the allowable turning radius for a given speed. The
detection range, performance of road detection, and corner classiﬁcation are crucial.
Driving closer to the track edge increases the risk. The optimal route planning must
66
==PAGE:75==
6. Optimal route planning algorithm
consider the potential risk to reward ratio. As previously mentioned, the following 3
line strategies have been considered for the ecocar when driving through detected
corners.
• SP (Shortest Path)
• MCP (The Geometric Racing Line)
• Late Apex
Planning the route around the RRC track based on these strategies puts high
demands on the detection ranges of the 3D LiDAR, especially since it is not part of
this thesis to use a predetermined plan. The VLP16 sensor must be no more than
approximately 20.99 m from the apex for the apex to be within the detection range
of ring 7, and even closer for the car to detect the corner. None of the three strategies
can be implemented for the entire track without a 3D map, adding landmarks or
GPS. They require the ability to detect the entrance to the corner, apex, and exit
from the corner robustly to plan the route as seen in ﬁgure 2.3, beyond the detection
ranges of the VLP16 LiDAR sensor.
However, a dynamic solution will be investigated where the car drives closer to the
inside of the corner as soon as the corner is detected. The car should return to the
middle of the road when the corner is no longer detectable. The goal is to reduce
the distance traveled, which is assumed to increase the fuel-eﬃciency when driving
autonomously.
The following strategies will be further investigated:
• If the car detects the corner before entry, then the car will drive close to the
inside of corner before entry; mimicking the SP strategy
• If the car detects the corner at the entry, then the car will drive close to the
inside of corner; mimicking the MCP strategy
• If the car detects the corner late or the system fails to detect the corner, the
car must stay in/drive back to the middle of the road
These strategies are more suitable for this system and can be applied while driving
through a corner.
The strategies are based on the interpretation of the local
environment and require lower detection ranges than SP and MCP. An additional
beneﬁt of only reacting in detected corners reduces the overall duration of higher
risk navigation.
Driving through a corner similar to the SP strategy puts higher demands on the
car’s kinematics and limits the possible cornering speed and potentially increases
the friction loss. Even though this is the case, the speed in which the car is driving
autonomously is within the limits of the corners at the RRC track. The car can
turn with a radius of 8m while driving 7m/s [AC18]. For this reason, the proposed
strategies are deemed plausible.

The output from chapter 5, as illustrated in ﬁgure 5.8, can be described as a set
of sorted discrete 2-D points with corresponding x and y values. The same can be
said for the points illustrated in ﬁgure 5.3, 5.4, 5.5 and 6.1. A Voronoi diagram of
such a set of points will section a region surrounding each point individually so that
any arbitrary point will belong to the speciﬁc region of the discrete point closest to
it. These regions are referred to as a Voronoi region or cells, and together from all
the discrete x,y points form a Voronoi diagram. The method is easiest to use and
understand in a 2-D space.
The Voronoi regions can be seen in ﬁgure 6.1 outlined by the blue lines referred to
as "Voronoi edges." The green circles called Voronoi vertices are located in corners
wherever three or more Voronoi regions connect. The proposed method for "extracting
the middle line" by [AC18] works by clustering the Voronoi regions to either the left
or right edge by distance. Instead of ﬁltering and handling all the raw vertices, the
search can be limited to looking for edges and vertices that are placed between the
left and right cluster or, as they call it, "boundary-lines between cells of diﬀerent
clusters."
As described and mentioned in both ﬁgure 6.1 and 6.2 a Matlab prototype was
created as a step of determining whether the existing methods for route planning
can be used together with the new Road detection method. The Matlab method
was developed not as a complete copy but rather as a general method to test general
limitations and downsides of the Voronoi and hull method. In general, a partial
conclusion is that even though [AC18] mentioned limitations or downsides of Voronoi
being sensitive to too few data points. Research and test with a Matlab prototype
using data from the bus track and RRC give a strong indication that both the left
and right method or hull method is still useful or applicable together with Voronoi
even though the points are spread out further by not having the barriers present.
Even though the sequence in ﬁgure 6.2 is only a still image, the vast majority of
RRC data gives a similar output.
As the default of the proposed optimal navigations strategy requires a method to ﬁnd
and navigate the middle path, no compelling arguments are found not to continue
with the Voronoi method. It has several advantages both in obstacle avoidance and
computation time.

This method uses the drive points to determine whether the car is driving towards

6.4
Route planning
As mentioned in 6.1, robustness is more important than being able to drive eﬃciently.
If the car is driving at a straight section or cannot detect the corner, it must follow
the middle of the road.
With this in mind, calculating the bounding box of the driving line is deemed a
good solution for the proposed driving strategy. The driveline and its size are based
on how well the navigation algorithm can interpret the course of the road from the
3D LiDAR measurements of the local environment. The driveline is categorized as
74
==PAGE:83==
6. Optimal route planning algorithm
either long or short, depending on how many points the driveline consists of. It is a
long driveline if it consists of more than certain amount of drive points. This value
was found through testing as a lower value occurs when the car is unsure of the
surroundings. Thus, the bounding box ratio must only be able to detect roads if
the drive point vector includes more than 10 points. If this is not the case then the
data is considered insuﬃcient for corner classiﬁcation, and the car should remain
in/return to the middle of the road.
The cornering driving strategy’s goal is to drive closer to the inside of the corner
by oﬀsetting the driveline when the bounding box method detects a left or right
corner. By using an integrator for each iteration to move the driveline closer to the
inside of the corner until a corner is no longer detected or the oﬀset has reached the
maximum value. This value is a tunable parameter, and choosing how far the drive
points are allowed to be oﬀset depends on the width of the road. How aggressive
the integrator moves the drive points is based on how large the bounding box ratio
is. The sharper the corner, the more signiﬁcant is the ratio. The driveline position
is updated each time the navigation algorithm is executed, which is equal to 20Hz.
When the corner is no longer detected, the integrator will move the driveline towards
the middle of the road, where it is safer to drive.
The corner detection algorithm is sensitive to high-frequency noise. The integrator’s
inﬂuence on the strategy is limited, but as a precaution, the return to the middle
integrator is more aggressive. The oﬀset of the car will be relatively small due to
high-frequency noise while driving at a straight section. Only if a corner is repeatedly
detected will the car drive closer to the edge of the road.

DISCUSSION
In this section the results of the tests will be discussed with respect to the problems
formulated in chapter 1. The goal of this project was to make the car capable of
navigating based on sensor data obtained from the local environment, without the
need for barriers along the track, and at the same time implementing a solution that
optimizes the route around the track.
The previous platform relied on barriers on each side of the car to drive around
a track autonomously. The point cloud was divided into obstacles and ground
points. However, ground is a broad term that includes both the road and the areas
surrounding it. This diﬀerentiation is seen as insuﬃcient to detect the road.
First, it was required to deﬁne which conditions are needed to detect the track
limits from the ground points without barriers through LiDAR perception. Based on
the test at the beginning of the project, it was clear that diﬀerent textures can be
distinguished from one another if the diﬀerence in roughness is signiﬁcant enough.
As a result, the roughness estimation was used for road detection. Several methods
were implemented to improve the robustness of the solution while lowering the
execution time. The solution proved to be robust, especially when the surrounding
grass grew wildly. However, this is not always the case, as was seen on the third
test at the RRC track. The grass was newly cut, which proved to be a challenging
environment for the car to navigate in. The car was capable of driving one full
lap, but it was far from robust. The car was oscillating throughout the entire lap
as a result of a road detection algorithm that could not robustly track the road’s
boundaries.
Thus, it was decided to look into the reﬂectivity values provided by the 3DLiDAR
sensor.
It has not been researched nor investigated thoroughly, but it was
implemented and showed promising results when combined with the existing road
detection algorithm. This combination made it possible for the car to drive eight
laps with breaks in between, without driving oﬀthe track or below the minimum
speed. Two of these laps (2.5 km) were without breaks. The car could have driven
several laps without breaks, but in order to gather as much test data as possible the
laps had breaks between them to oﬄoad data and start again.
The 2 laps in a row is the longest distance that the car has driven continuously and
autonomously. And this was achieved in a challenging environment without barriers

CONCLUSION
This thesis has provided the existing autonomous platform of the DTU Dynamo
ecocar, with new, robust navigation capabilities. The system now has a robust and
fault-tolerant solution through LiDAR perception using a Velodyne VLP-16 LiDAR
to run entirely autonomously on a barrier less racetrack. The new autonomous
capabilities of the DTU Dynamo was demonstrated to DTU Roadrunners and the
press at Roskilde racing center.
The solution has been implemented into an existing urban concept car and tested in
various environments. Results have been very satisfactory, and the conclusion is that
the stated requirements and goals are met. The task of navigating autonomously
around a racetrack without the need for barriers are clearly solved.
All the objectives formulated in Section 1.3 have been accomplished as part of this
thesis:
• The environmental conditions needed for the current hardware platform to
classify and detect track limits has been deﬁned, tested and veriﬁed.
• A method has been developed which make the system autonomously ﬁnd and
classify drivable regions such as racetracks, rural roads, roads surrounded by
barriers etc. based on LiDAR perception.
• Optimal route planning has been researched, investigated, and a deﬁnition is
formulated, and principles from it, is implemented based on the limitations of
the car and platform.
• Tests were conducted at a racetrack, and the car could navigate fully
autonomously with no prior knowledge of the track, without crashing.
• Extensive track testing data was collected.
• The goal of the autonomous competition is to mimic the challenges in making
real-life autonomous cars. During the project, it has been veriﬁed that the
solution is not only capable of navigating in scenarios that mimic the real
world, but has actual navigated successfully on a public road.
As promised in the problem statement, the results from this project will facilitate the
DTU Roadrunner project by extending the DTU Dynamo capabilities with solutions
and algorithms to navigate without the need for barriers and to optimize route
planning. A robust method of detecting corners and identifying the optimal route
through a given corner are also included.

The car can now perform robustly in environments with an increasing amount
of uncertainties and adequately take countermeasures for system failures without

Taking the project further, “full autonomy” in many environments is desired. This

10FUTURE WORK
10.0.1
LiDAR resolution
Detecting the road puts higher demands on the vertical resolution of the sensor,
since there are blind gaps between each channel. Especially the blind gap between
LiDAR ring 7 and 8 is big. To mitigate that issue, upgrading the resolution of the
LiDAR could be beneﬁcial. This can be done by upgrading to a LiDAR sensor with
more channels. Another way of upgrading the resolution is to mount a mirror on the
LiDAR to point more lasers on the ground without having to buy a new sensor.
Left and Right points
If the LiDAR resolution is upgraded, then the implemented right and left edge
method can be beneﬁcial since they require less computational power than the
current obstacle method.
10.0.2
Reﬂectivity
As mentioned in chapter 4, reﬂectivity has been implemented to improve the
robustness of the road detection algorithm by combining it with roughness estimation
based on the data from the LiDAR sensor. The implementation is simple, yet it has
proven to perform well at the RRC track. It could most likely be developed further
and end up being a very strong tool for navigating autonomously.
10.0.3
Adding simple topologic input
As mentioned in related word, chapter 2, MIT uses GPS and a sparse topological
map for global navigation and combines it with a sensor-based perception system for
local navigation. Combining the GPS that DTU Dynamo used in 2017 for navigation
and the road detection in the local environment could enable the car to navigate
even more robustly and help in predicting ahead of time.
10.0.4
Lidar and camera fusion
Detecting the side roads at the RRC track proved to be diﬃcult with the 3D LiDAR
sensor. Using the camera to detect these side roads could remove the need for using
97
==PAGE:106==

any sort of barriers to stop the car from turning. Additionally, a fusion with camera
opens up the opportunity of dynamically limiting or specifying where the LiDAR
should look based on color or other known image analysis methods.
10.0.5
LiDAR misclassiﬁcation
As mentioned in 7.1.1, errors on the left side of the road occurs during left corners.
Initial research and analysis of the data suggest is could be the large vibrations
when the engine is initially started, or possible a defect or other damage or foreign
objects on the LiDAR lens. The root course of this error has not been identiﬁed.
Solving this will improve the robustness of the road detection algorithm and the
corner detection algorithm.
10.0.6
Dynamic corner integrator
Unfortunately, the integrator used, resulted in a too slow reaction, compared to the
actual speed of the car. This inﬂuenced how the car drove through corners at the
ﬁnal demonstration at RRC. How aggressive the integrator moves the car towards
the inside of the corner should not only depend on the sharpness of the corner but
also be based on the speed of the car. Implementing this will enable the car to drive
through corners at diﬀerent speeds and follow the path as seen in ﬁgure. 7.17.b.
