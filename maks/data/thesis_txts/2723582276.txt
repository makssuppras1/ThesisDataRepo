Abstract
This Thesis proposes a methodology for the prediction of the indoor temperature in a Dan-
ish school. The parameters of four black-box ARX models, proposed by gradually adding
the measured ambient temperature, the set-point temperature, the forward temperature
and the solar radiation to each subsequent model, are initially estimated for a total of 26
days. The predictions are compared against the experimental and the set-point temper-
atures. The performance of each model is evaluated using the root mean square error
(RSME). Simultaneously, a white-box model of the same room has been implemented in
Open Modelica from parameters found in the literature. Successively, the simulated data
from the white-box model have been used as inputs in the proposed ARX models, and the
thermal dynamics of the room has been accurately captured. However, the estimation of
non-linear parameters failed when a small amount of Gaussian noise was added to the
simulated data. Future research is recommended to improve the RMSE of the ARX mod-
els followed by more advanced white-box models to predict additional relevant thermal
dynamics variables.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The rapidly increasing volume of data from different advanced sensors has dramatically
changed the manufacturing industry, leading to the creation of ”digital twins” of complex
physical systems. These virtual systems are continuously interconnected with the actual
system, enabling a bidirectional flow of information that can be used to perform changes
in the physical model through simulations and predictions developed by the digital twin.
In the framework of net zero greenhouse emissions by 2050, digital twins play a vital
role in the development of the next generation of smart cities, where urban data from
vehicles, buildings, and individuals can be used together with algorithms to control the
desired output according to some pre-defined constraints.
Since buildings in cities are responsible for a large amount of energy consumption, it is
of paramount importance that new buildings are heated by renewable energies. In doing
so, the new buildings need to estimate and forecast the different sources of renewable
energies - solar, geothermal, and wind,- according to what is needed by the occupants.
Many estimation techniques for the prediction of the thermal dynamics of buildings, based
on how the physical model is represented, exist in the current engineering community.
Statistical models based on experimental data are fast in the prediction but they don’t
take into account the physics of the building. Physical models, on the other hand, are
accurate for thermal dynamic prediction but they require large computational time.
Therefore one way to circumvent this problem is to create a digital twin which is able to
generate physics-based predictions in a fast way.
1.2
Aim and objectives
The aim of this is this MSc. Thesis is the development of a methodology that will be
able to generate a digital twin of a Danish building school using experimental as well
as simulated data. In particular, the final objective is the development of statistical ARX
models where the original experimental data are replaced by white-box simulation data.
These ARX models will be used for the predictions of relevant thermal properties in one
of the building rooms.
1.3
Structure of the Thesis
This thesis is composed of the following chapters:
Chapter 1 is an introduction to the background of the problem investigated.
Chapter 2 presents a literature survey of the current state of the art of smart energy sys-
tems within urban districts, outlining how these systems are important to meet the require-
ments for a complete transition to renewable energies by 2050. After an introduction to the
district heating technology for space heating of buildings, a description of the different es-
timation techniques of the thermal dynamics is presented. The chapter is concluded with
a review of the previous thermal dynamic estimations for the Danish school investigated
in this project.

Literature Review
2.1
The need for smart energy systems
Within the current energy and climate policy intended to achieve the transition from fossil
fuels toward a more sustainable and efficient energy system, new energy grids with higher
flexibility, defined as the capacity to match the supply and demand of energy fluxes con-
tinuously, are needed. In this regard, it is necessary to replace the traditional electrical
grid with a system able to sustain the fluctuating power generated by variable renewable
energies (VRE) such as wind and solar. Smart Energy Systems [1, 2] have been identified
as a promising new energy approach where flexibility is achieved through the integration
and simultaneous coordination by information and communications technology (ICT) tech-
nologies of multiple energy vectors (e.g. electricity, heating, cooling). This new approach
has been developed at the national level in countries such as Denmark [3], Finland [4]
and Germany [5] where the complete national energy supply from res renewable energy
sources (RES) by 2050 is physically possible.
Smart energy systems can be relevant for the development of the next generation of cities.
Here, different energy sources are in close proximity and could be integrated together
with machine learning algorithms to coordinate and optimize the distribution of different
energy systems [6]. It is expected that by 2050 66% of the world population will live in
urbanized areas, and in the European Union (EU) urbanization is expected to rise from a
current 75% to 85% by 2050 [7]. This growth in urbanization has the effect of concentrat-
ing around 60–80% of the global greenhouse gas emissions in a small, highly populated
area. To meet the objectives of the EU heat Energy Roadmap 2050 [8], building heating
in highly populated areas should be delivered by District heating (DH) infrastructures. DH
is a technology that has evolved over time from late 1800 when steam was the main heat
carrier until reaching the current fourth generation, designed with the purpose to aban-
don the dependency on fossil energy. The integration of the heating with the electricity
sector as well as the transport sector could also be developed in the future [9]. District
heating is based on the distribution of heat as hot water from a combination of different
heat generation sources through a network of pipelines that connect the central heating
plant to substations that are interconnected with the buildings. Since the development
of pipelines contributes to higher maintenance costs and higher heat losses, DH is gen-
erally more convenient in dense cities. The traditional heat sources are combined heat
and power (CHP) plants, and more recently from geothermal energy biomass energy, so-
lar and wind power, and also from recycling the industrial excess heat [10, 11, 12]. Two
pipeline temperatures are essential for the DH. The temperature of the water that brings
the heat from the source to the building, the supply temperature, and the temperature
in the pipes carrying the heat back to the source, the return temperature. The reduction
of those two temperatures is one of the main objectives for the forthcoming 4th Gener-
ation District Heating (4GDH) [9] that will increase the efficiency of the next sustainable
energy systems for new buildings as well as for already existing buildings. This will be
accomplished by a reduction of the heat losses throughout the network [13], and by the
incorporation of the excess heat waste from common industrial places such as data cen-
ters and supermarkets. The 4GDH aims to achieve the supply and return temperature
to around 50◦C and 25◦C, respectively, and it has been estimated that with such low
temperatures, it is possible to save 10 EUR per MWh [14].

2.2
Smart Building Management System
The demand for energy efficiencies in flexible energy systems requires a new form of
energy management in buildings where ICT devices are used to provide automatic regu-
lation and control of many building operations (heating, air conditioning, lighting) with the
lowest possible energy consumption. Those operations are accomplished by the Building
Management Systems (BMS), a computer-based system able to regulate building energy
consumption. Through a large amount of data from sensors throughout the building, it is
possible to reduce the energy consumption by 15% [15].
2.3
Building energy forecast and estimation
Smart buildings can be achieved by control algorithms such as model predictive control
(MPC) [16, 17]. In this type of algorithm, the readings of the smart sensor are used to
predict the consumption of energy based on a physical model that describes the dynamics
of the system under investigation. The obtained predictions are subsequently used to
lower the building energy consumption subject to constraints such as human comfort,
building security, and operational costs. In the literature, a vast amount of statistical and
mathematical models exist for the control and estimation of the heat dynamics inside
buildings. Many of those are classified as white-box models, black-box, and grey-box
models, depending on how the information from the physical system is correlated with
the information from sensor data.
White-box models [18, 19, 20] rely on the numerical solution of the heat transfer con-
servation laws. Although this approach is the most accurate from a physical standpoint,
it requires high computational power and therefore the simulations are time-consuming.
Examples of white-box models are found in simulation software such as Modelica [21],
EnergyPlus [22] or TRNSYS [23].
Black-box models [24, 25], rely on data-driven statistical models to estimate the unknown
function between the input data (e.g. solar power, external temperature) and the out-
put energy consumption. Based exclusively on data, these methods are not backed by
any theoretical or physical law [26]. Examples of black-box models are linear [27] and
non-linear [28] AutoRegressive with eXogenous input (ARX) time series models, Artificial
Neural Networks [29], and Long short-term memory models [30].
A combination of the simplified governing equations with the information from sensor data
using statistical methods leads to the grey-box models [31, 32, 33]. The physical equa-
tions describing the dynamics of the systems are expressed as a set of continuous in-time
stochastic differential equations (SDE). The observation equation for the output variable
investigated (i.e the temperature inside the building) is expressed by a discrete in-time
measurement equation, whose unknown parameters are calculated from data using esti-
mation techniques.
2.4
Heat dynamics estimation of buildings with district
heating
The estimation of the thermal dynamics of buildings with water-based heating has been
source of relevant research works in the past few years, all aimed to achieve the flexibility
needed for the future smart energy system. Some of the works relevant to the current
project are related to the development of accurate predictions of the thermal dynamics
inside building such as schools.
A non-linear grey-box model based on SDEs of a Danish building school has been pre-
sented by Thilker et al. [33] and validated using experimental data for one week. In this

article, the radiator thermostatic valve which controls the water flow inside the radiator has
been included for the first time in a grey box model as a non-linear sigmoid function to
open and close the valves proportionally to the indoor air temperature. The results of this
work - indoor air temperature, return temperature, and heat load - predicted with the max-
imum likelihood estimator from time series data, were in agreement with the one-week
experimental data. A following study on non-linear MPC to predict the optimal radiator
set-points has been addressed in [33]. The authors, taking into account the solar irradi-

In a new research article, Thilker et al. [28] proposed a non-linear third-order ARX model

a one-day simulation. The time-dependent solar gain effects has been estimated in each
room of the building using de Boor B-splines [34] basis expansion from solar power exper-
imental data, achieving predictions in agreement with the physical diurnal solar variation.
The authors also improved the previous sigmoid function using cubic Hermite polynomi-
als, capturing sudden changes in thermal dynamics. Compared to previous works found
in the literature [35], they reduced the root-mean-square-error (RMSE) of the temperature
predictions.

3.1
The Modelica framework
Modelica [36] is an object-oriented language used to model multi-component (e.g heating,
mechanical, electrical) of complex cyber-physical systems such as those found in build-
ings. It is used in the simulation process of engineering design by describing the rela-
tionships between systems in a convenient mathematical formulation such as differential,
algebraic, and discrete equations. The model generated in Modelica is called Functional
Mockup Unit (FMU), and in Figure 3.1 one example of Modelica instance with different
components is shown.
Figure 3.1: Example of a Modelica system [37]
3.2
The Danish building school
The building used in this project is a Danish school with three floors, one basement, and
a total of 10 classrooms. District heating is used for space heating, domestic hot water,
and the ventilation system that causes the air to circulate in the classrooms. Each room
is heated by a water-based radiator where a thermostatic valve regulates the water flow
to keep a pre-defined set-point temperature. A heat exchanger is used as the interface
between the space heating and the district heating system.
3.3
The experimental data
The experimental data used in this work for the room C0.01 of the school are taken from
the previous work of Thilker et al. [28]. In particular, the ambient temperature Ta, the
indoor C0.01 air temperature Troom, the set point temperature Tset, the solar power Ps
and the forward temperature Tfor of the water in the radiator have been used. All the
temperatures are expressed in [◦C], while the solar power is expressed as [kW/m2]. The
total number of observations is 1294, each sampled every 30 minutes, from 2021-03-01
to 2021-03-27.

While the results from the parameters are reasonable, the model’s performance is poor

5.7
Discussion
5.7.1
ARX based on measured data
The results of the ARX model selection with experimental data as input showed that the
M1 model with only the ambient temperature as input achieved the highest RSME error
(1.71) of all the models considered in this project (1.71 against 0.85 of the model with the
solar radiation with the B-splines polynomials). The poor thermal dynamics performance
of this model can be explained by the fact that it’s based only on one linear measurement
data (the difference between the ambient temperature and the room temperature) without
taking into account other heat thermal sources such as the heater inside the room and
the solar radiation entering from the windows. When the thermostatic valve function,
estimated with the non-linear numerical optimization solver, is included in the model, the
performances of the M2 model’s predictions are drastically improved, reaching an RSME
of 0.92. The reduction of the RMSE (and therefore the improvements on the predictions)
for the solar radiation models are obtained only by expanding the solar inputs with the
B-splines interpolating functions (0.94 and 0.85 for the M3 and M4 models, respectively),
although more accurate ARX models with lower RSME are still present in the literature,
30
Digital twin experiments for room temperature prediction
==PAGE:39==
(0.6 ◦C in the previous ARX work [28] or 0.77 ◦C with Artificial Neural Networks [29]).
In all the models investigated in this project, including the M4 model, the temperature
predicted at the end of the simulation does not correspond to the measured temperature.
Moreover, the exponential decay and the increase of the room temperature when the set
point temperature is changed are captured only up to a certain level of accuracy, with
improvements significantly made by the thermostatic valve function.
5.7.2
ARX based on simulation data
Due to software licensing issues, a decision was made to use a simpler Open Modelica
FMU model for the white-box model instead of the originally planned Modelica model. As
a result, all the ARX models based on simulated data have shown an RMSE of 1.089
◦C compared to the true measured data. Nevertheless, the resulting ARX models are
able to replicate quite well the fast dynamics of the building heat transfer, and also in
the final part of the simulation the measurements are well predicted. It seems however
that such models are not able to predict the low temperature reached by the white-box
simulation data. The majority of the parameters estimated with the simulation data were
in agreement with the parameters found in the ARX models with the experimental data,
with few variations for the solar radiation parameter (β3) in both the simple model and
the one with the splines. In addition, the offset parameter in the sigmoid function for the
thermostatic valve (α2) was different from the ARX estimated parameters. When some
noise is included in the simulation data, the estimated parameters are altered and some
variations are visible in the predictions. For the model with the solar radiations expressed
with B-splines, the effect of a σ2 = 0.1 noise is so intense that the non-linear parameters
are particularly negative, and most likely those estimated values are erroneous, although
there is no evidence of this behavior on the predictions.

6
Conclusion
A methodology for the prediction of the indoor temperature for one of the rooms in a Dan-
ish school building has been proposed in this work. In the first part of the Thesis, the
parameters of four different black-box statistical ARX models based on measured data
have been estimated in a Python environment according to the linearity of the param-
eter, followed by a comparison between the model predictions and the real measured
temperature for a total of 26 days.
The ARX models were proposed by gradually incorporating additional data and param-
eters into each subsequent model. The initial model solely incorporated ambient tem-
perature as an input. Subsequently, a second model was developed that introduced the
thermostatic valve of the radiators as a non-linear sigmoid function to simulate the valve’s
opening and closure effect. The third model included linear solar gain input from the win-
dows. The final model enhanced the solar gain estimate by employing B-splines polyno-
mials. All the linear parameters in the proposed models were estimated through a closed-
form solution, whereas the non-linear parameters were transformed into a new linear re-
gression and estimated through the Sequential Least Squares Programming (SLSQP)
optimization function present in the SciPy library.
The results from the predictions show that the ambient temperature alone is not able to
accurately predict the room temperature as well as the variability of the temperature on
an hourly basis. A strong improvement in the predictions is made by the inclusion of the
thermostatic valve expressed as a non-linear sigmoid function, while the effect of the solar
radiation is captured with good accuracy only when the solar radiation is expanded with
B-splines coefficients.
A simpler white-box model than what originally planned was executed with the parame-
ters found in the literature. The resulting temperature was utilized as input for the ARX
predictions, substituting the original measured temperature.
In general, the ARX predictions using simulation data were capable of accurately track-
ing the dynamics of the simulated temperature and the thermostat set-point temperature.
However, the non-linear parameter estimation fails when noise is introduced into the sim-
ulated data.
6.1
Future work
In terms of future research, it is suggested to enhance the ARX models using measure-
ment data until the root mean square error is equivalent to that reported in the literature.
More advanced white-box room models should be used, including state-of-the-art mod-
els for the solar radiation effects. Also, the hydronic system, i.e. the radiators, should
be included. Furthermore, effects from occupants, like window opening, and ventilation,
should be included in the white-box model. The impacts of such different effects on the
predictive performance of the ARX models should be studied in future research. With a
more advanced white-box model, new simulations should be run. New methods for de-
veloping optimal learning test sequences should be developed. The effect of including an
MA term in the ARX models should be investigated, and how it can improve the predictive
performance.
