Summary
Calibration of microphones is a fundamental step to provide comparability when cap-
turing and reporting sound pressure levels. It relates the frequency behavior and level-
dependent voltage output of a microphone to the input sound pressure. A secondary
calibration is performed to compare the device under test against a reference micro-
phone, determining the corresponding properties through comparison with the known
microphone. This has to be done for every microphone intended to comply with inter-
national measurement standards, and can be a very time-consuming and many times
manual process for the microphone manufacturer. This calibration has to be done es-
pecially eﬃciently, when the microphone manufacturer has to calibrate dozens or hun-
dreds of microphones each week in its production line. The calibration method should
be as quick as possible but at the same time precise and stable in terms of measurement
uncertainties and repeatability. The aim of this work is to ﬁnd such an eﬃcient and
robust calibration procedure especially for high-sensitivity condenser microphones.
The problem with high-sensitivity microphones in particular is that in order for them
to capture very low sound pressure levels, the microphone diaphragm has to be very
loose. This creates a very low acoustic impedance of the microphone which means that
it cannot easily be corrected for in an actuator or coupler measurement. The main
reason for this is that the resonance frequency of this type of microphone changes with
changing radiation impedance and this prohibits the standard actuator or pressure
calibration for high-sensitivity microphones.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Calibration is a standard procedure for microphone manufacturers and most micro-
phones are calibrated with an electrostatic actuator measurement. This measurement
is corrected for the ﬁnal purpose of the microphone, i.e., pressure ﬁeld, free-ﬁeld or ran-
dom incidence. Unfortunately, this does not work with high-sensitivity microphones.
High-sensitivity microphones, also called low-noise microphones, are designed to cap-
ture sound pressures of very low levels, for example, below the threshold of human
hearing. To achieve this comparably high microphone sensitivity, the microphone’s di-
aphragm needs to be easily excited by the sound ﬁeld, which is achieved by designing
it with a rather loose diaphragm. This low-damped diaphragm, however, introduces a
prominent resonance peak in the frequency response that has to be compensated for
with an electrical ﬁlter to ﬂatten the response again. Due to the low diaphragm ten-
sion, the spread of resonance frequencies within production of these microphones can
be quite large and this is why the electrical ﬁlter has to be adjusted individually for
each specimen.
The process of adjusting the ﬁlter is performed in combination with estimating the
ﬁnal response of the microphone with the ﬁlter. This happens in parallel by measuring
the frequency response of the microphone to check for a ﬂat response and adjust the
ﬁlter correspondingly, meaning that many measurements and adjustments are needed
before the desired response is achieved. Due to the low acoustic impedance of the
microphone, these measurements cannot be accurately performed with an actuator or
a coupler, so these are conducted under free-ﬁeld conditions, e.g. an anechoic room.
All this makes the manufacturing and calibration process of low-noise microphones
considerably cumbersome and the time spent on producing one such microphone is
signiﬁcantly more than for a regular microphone.
==PAGE:12==
2
1 Introduction
Unfortunately, hardly any literature on high-sensitivity condenser microphones is avail-
able, and even less is described about the calibration of these. Of course, many text-
books discuss condenser microphones in general, but low-noise microphones are mostly
mentioned with only a paragraph, if at all. The aim of this work is to investigate pos-
sible alternative calibration procedures, where an anechoic room is not needed any
more. This hopefully reduces production and calibration time, as well as space require-
ments, eases practicalities of the whole process, and frees the anechoic room for other
purposes, e.g. research and development.
To illustrate the eﬀect of diﬀerent sound ﬁelds on the radiation impedance of the micro-
phones, diﬀerent approaches will be used. These include analytical, electroacoustical,
and numerical solutions. As will be discussed in this work, one critical unknown in
all the modeling approaches is the diaphragm tension. Especially in typically manual
manufacturing processes, where the diaphragm is pressed onto the microphone car-
tridge by a skilled person, the eﬀective tension can only be known with large eﬀort, or
has to be estimated. More than 800 measurements have been conducted in this work
to analyze and predict the behavior of 5 high-sensitivity microphones of type 40AH in
diﬀerent conditions.
The structure of the presented work is as follows: After this introduction, the basic prin-
ciple of operation and important properties of condenser microphones will be outlined
in chapter 2. In chapter 3 the theory behind some other acoustic properties and meth-
ods will be presented and a short introduction to electroacoustical lumped-parameter
modeling will be given. In chapter 4 there will be some details on high-sensitivity mi-
crophones in particular and the preparations needed for the following investigations.
Then chapter 5 will discuss the many design parameters that are used in constructing
and modeling a condenser microphone and will highlight the diﬀerent methods that
are available for predicting the frequency response. The investigations on diﬀerent cal-
ibration setups are then explained and evaluated in chapter 6. Based on these ﬁndings
chapter 7 suggest an improved and eﬃcient calibration procedure. The work is brieﬂy
discussed in chapter 8, before chapter 9 will ﬁnally give a summary. Additionally, rel-
evant technical standards are supplementary found in Appendix A.

CHAPTER 2
Basics of Measurement
Condenser Microphones
Condenser microphones are known for their high stability and accuracy[1]. They are
the only types of microphones that can be calibrated with an accuracy of less than
0.1 dB uncertainty[2, p.3], which is equal to less than 1.0 %. They are therefore used
to do measurements that have to be very reliable, as with the absolute quantiﬁcation of
sound pressure levels, for example, for validation of sound emissions. This means that
for every measurement microphone a calibration has to be performed to make sure that
what is measured with the microphone is actually really representing the conditions of
the sound ﬁeld. Therefore a back-tracing of the individual microphone to a deﬁned
standard has to be established, and this is why calibration processes have to be as
accurate as possible, i.e., with the smallest possible uncertainty in the measurement
results.
Condenser microphones are the best choice for calibration and measurement purposes,
because they have a robust mechanical stability and their frequency response is usually
very ﬂat over a wide range of frequency[3]. Due to their apparent simple geometry
and construction, their behavior is also comparably easy to be modeled and predicted,
which also helps, for example, to apply corrections for diﬀerent sound ﬁelds.
The following chapter will give an introduction to the condenser microphone as such,
its history, the principal of operation and the most common condenser microphone
properties.

2.1
A Brief History of the Condenser Microphone
The AIP Handbook of condenser microphones. Theory, calibration, and measurements
provides a short introduction to the history of the condenser microphone, which the
following section is based upon [2, ch. 1]. In general, electroacoustical transducer
development started in the 1870s when the invention of the telephone led to the need
of sound transmitting and receiving transducers. One of the ﬁrst microphones was the
carbon microphone, however, this was not a reciprocal transducer, it could not reproduce
sound, only capture it. Soon the discovery of the piezo-electric eﬀect in the 1880s gave
rise to electromechanical transducers, but the application of piezoelectric transducers
was only widely possible after the development of electrical ampliﬁers in the 1900s.
For similar reasons, the development of the condenser microphone was ﬁrst possible in
1917, when E. C. Wente could amplify the low voltages created by the microphone with
electrical ampliﬁers [4]. He also introduced the idea of a standard source to calibrate
the microphones to an absolute sound pressure, using the pistonphone, a mechanical
device that creates a known sound pressure at the microphone diaphragm by coupling
it to a small air volume, at one particular sinusoidal frequency (usually 250 Hz). Shortly
after, he also introduced the concept of pressure calibration, where the microphone is
calibrated over a wider frequency range by its pressure sensitivity in a small coupler.
It was then the Western Electric Company that started producing the ﬁrst unof-
ﬁcial standard microphones used for primary standard calibrations. Soon, the electro-
static actuator, the reciprocity method and the free-ﬁeld calibration were introduced.
During World War II, the demands to measurement microphones increased due to the
heavy use of communication equipment, and comparison calibrations were used to ac-
complish this. Meanwhile, condenser microphones have become a very reliable and
time-stable product, so international standards for acoustical measurements adopted
the concepts. The Western Electric Company then agreed with Brüel & Kjær A/S
to have them produce and further develop the standard microphones, which they do
till this day. GRAS Sound & Vibration A/S is one of the few competitors, that can
deliver such high-precision microphones nowadays, too [5].

2.2
Principle of Operation
The condenser microphone’s principle of operation — as the name suggests — is based
on forming an electrical capacitor, consisting of two metallic plates, in this case a mov-
ing diaphragm and a rigid back-plate. The diaphragm movement is caused by the pres-
sure change of incident sound waves and this movement results in a change of distance
between the diaphragm and the back-plate. This creates a change in capacitance of
the condenser, which can be observed as a change of voltage, which is proportional to
the diaphragm movement and thereby to the sound pressure.
Behind the diaphragm is the back-chamber, which is enclosed by the housing and ter-
minated by the insulator, that holds the back-plate inside of the back-chamber, see
Figure 2.1. A spacer is put between the insulator and the part of the housing that lim-

The microphone diaphragm can be considered a mass-spring resonator, which means
that it has a resonance frequency at which it is very easy to excite the diaphragm, and
its displacement is maximum at this frequency. Putting the back-plate close to the
diaphragm to form the condenser will create some damping due to the fact that a small
layer of air is somehow “trapped” between the two. This reduces the displacement at
and around the resonance. By drilling holes into the back-plate some control over this
damping can be gained as this inﬂuences the resistance of the air layer. Many physical
and material properties determine the microphone’s behavior, and some of them will
be further explained below, while others are covered later on in the electroacoustical
section, see section 3.11.
2.3
Measurement Microphones
Measurement microphones are divided into two categories, namely the laboratory stan-
dard (LS) and working standard (WS) types. Laboratory standard microphones are
intended for calibration purposes only, therefore they are very stable and precise but
also very fragile. For example, they have no protection grid in front of the diaphragm,
but a surrounding ring with a protrusion to ensure a certain spacing of the diaphragm.
Working standard microphones are designed for every-day measurement use, there-
fore they are more robust and they have a protection grid to prevent destruction of the
microphone diaphragm.
Depending on the size of the microphone, an integer number is added to the type
designation, indicating the diameter: LS1 and WS1 types are of 1′′ diameter, LS2 and
WS2 are 1⁄2 ′′ microphones, and for working standard microphones there is an additional
category, WS3, for 1⁄4 ′′ diameter microphones. Figure 2.2 shows some dimensions for
LS2 and WS2 microphones as speciﬁed in [7, 8]. The main diﬀerence is the protection
grid that can be attached to the WS microphones.

2.3.1
Mechanical Properties
Ideally, a measurement microphone would capture all sound pressure waves that are
arriving at its diaphragm without altering these incoming waves. However, there are
limitations that restrict frequency range, dynamic range, sound ﬁeld inﬂuence, fre-
quency response and directionality. In the design process of developing a condenser
microphone it is therefore necessary to consider the following factors, that all have a
signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the ﬁnal behavior of the microphone.
For example, a large microphone creates more diﬀraction, reﬂection and shadowing,
thereby reducing sound coming from the side or behind the microphone. Smaller mi-
crophones have less of these eﬀects, are therefore more omni-directional for higher
frequencies. Smaller microphones are also better suited for higher sound pressures, as
their displacement is lower, as well as their non-linear distortion[9]. For even higher
sound pressures, it is also important to not exceed the critical displacement that would
make the diaphragm touch the back-plate, which might destroy the microphone perma-
nently. Figure 2.3 displays an overview for diﬀerent microphone sizes and some typical
dynamic ranges. In contrast, larger microphones are less prone to inherent noise (both
electrical pre-ampliﬁer noise and thermal noise of the diaphragm) and therefore the
better choice for very low sound pressures.

As already mentioned, the diaphragm can be considered a damped mass-spring res-
onator, so mass, tension, and stiﬀness altogether determine the diaphragm’s resonance
frequency. This means, that larger or thicker diaphragms have a lower resonance fre-
quency. The stiﬀness of the diaphragm (which can be modiﬁed through the tension)
also inﬂuences the resonance frequency, but the resonance damping as well. And the
distance between diaphragm and back-plate not only inﬂuences the damping, it also
has a big inﬂuence on the sensitivity, because a smaller distance creates more output
voltage, i.e. a higher sensitivity.
2.3.2
Frequency Properties
For low frequencies a larger diaphragm provides a possibly higher SNR than a smaller
diaphragm[6] because it is easier to move the large diaphragm simply due to the larger
surface area aﬀected by the incoming sound pressure. The low frequency limit (usu-
ally given as the -3 dB frequency) is mainly determined by the size of the atmospheric
pressure equalization vent which is needed to accommodate the pressure in the back-
chamber to the atmospheric pressure to avoid an increased static displacement; a larger
vent gives a quicker adaptation to static pressure changes, but also gives a higher low-
frequency cut-oﬀ. However, larger diaphragms also have a lower resonance frequency,
which means that their upper frequency roll-oﬀis earlier, resulting in a lower SNR for
these high frequencies compared to a smaller microphone. The upper frequency limit

2.3.3
Sound ﬁeld properties
The purpose of a measurement microphone is to capture the sound ﬁeld as it would
be without the presence of the microphone. Due to reﬂection and diﬀraction eﬀects
introduced by the geometry of the microphone itself, the sound ﬁeld is changed by
introducing the microphone. This eﬀect is dependent on the size of the microphone,
which means that a larger microphone will have a larger inﬂuence on the sound ﬁeld,
especially for higher frequencies. It diﬀracts and reﬂects sound waves as soon as the
dimensions are comparable with or larger than the wavelength.
A larger diaphragm creates more reﬂections in front of the microphone, thereby inﬂu-
encing the sound ﬁeld more than a smaller microphone. This change of behavior in the
sound ﬁeld, created by the acoustic impedance now loading the diaphragm, is called
==PAGE:20==
10

radiation impedance [2, p.237] and will be of great importance when discussing the
diﬀerent calibration techniques.
Due to the mere presence of the diaphragm in the sound ﬁeld, the microphone will
create an additional pressure accumulation in front of the diaphragm, because the
impedance of the microphone is much higher than that of air, which creates reﬂec-
tions. This means that the pressure representation that is about to be captured is only
true, when this pressure accumulation was already present before the introduction of
the microphone. For example, this can be the case when one is interested in the sound

2.3.4
Other Microphone Properties
There are other parameters that furthermore determine the microphone sensitivity, but
are less important in the scope of this work. For example, the polarization voltage
has a major inﬂuence on the sensitivity. Usually it is provided and kept at a rather
constant level by two methods, the pre-polarized microphone with an electret, or the
externally polarized microphone with power supply. For pre-polarized microphones
the polarization voltage is provided by an electret, which is a permanently charged
material, so there is no need for an external polarization voltage[9]. This is more
convenient in the case of, for example, hand-held devices, where an external power
supply would be rather annoying. In the case of an externally polarized microphone
the polarization voltage is delivered by an external power supply, which is usually the
cheaper solution, as this requires less production eﬀort. This is more cost-eﬀective, for
example, in the case of large microphone arrays, where multiple microphones can be
operated with a single power supply[9].
Microphone preampliﬁers are used to impedance-match the cartridge to the equipment
connected to the output and put gain on this voltage to make it easier to be processed
and analyzed at later stages[6, sec.2.4]. The preampliﬁer and the cartridge together
usually determine the low end of the dynamic range.
There also is a small pressure equalization vent in the cartridge that allows pressure
exchange between the air volume in the capsule behind the diaphragm and the out-
side ambient pressure. This is important to avoid static deﬂections of the diaphragm
which would alter the diaphragms’ stiﬀness and displacement, thereby inﬂuencing the
sensitivity, too. Diﬀerent ambient pressures would create diﬀerent sensitivities other-
wise. However, the low frequency limit of the microphone is inﬂuenced by this vent,
see subsection 2.3.2.

Additionally, there are coeﬃcients for describing the inﬂuence of aging over time, changes

3.1
Open Circuit Sensitivity
The open circuit sensitivity of a microphone describes how the incoming sound pressure
is converted into electrical voltage. Obviously, higher sound pressures create more
displacement, and therefore more output voltage from the condenser. But diﬀerent
designs of microphones will also give diﬀerent output voltages for the same input sound
pressure, simply due to their electrical and mechanical construction. The open circuit
sensitivity of a microphone is the output voltage of the microphone when a certain
sound pressure is applied to its diaphragm, and when the microphone is disconnected
from any electrical circuit. This is the sensitivity of the microphone cartridge alone,
irrespective of the microphone preampliﬁer in use. Because it is impractical to measure
the voltage without connecting the microphone electrically, it is captured with the insert
voltage method, described in the following section.

3.2
Insert Voltage Method
The insert voltage method is used to obtain the open circuit sensitivity of a microphone.
The standard IEC 61094-2[10, sec.5.3] describes the demands as follows. The micro-
phone cartridge is connected in the ground shield conﬁguration[7, sec.7.2] in series
with an electrical reference impedance, a calibrating voltage is applied across the refer-
ence impedance. When the applied voltage is equal to the open-circuit voltage created
by a known sound pressure at the diaphragm, the open-circuit sensitivity is found.

3.4
Radiation Impedance
The radiation impedance is the eﬀect of a source (or receiver) on the sound ﬁeld in
which it acts as a transmitter. It is in a way still an acoustic impedance, but more
speciﬁc to sources coupled to a sound ﬁeld[12, sec.10.9]. Diﬀerent sources see a dif-
ferent load from the sound ﬁeld, caused by mechanical and acoustical properties that
determine e.g. the stiﬀness of the radiating surface, but also by diﬀraction, reﬂection
and shadowing eﬀects from the geometry[13, sec.4.5]. This coupling creates diﬀerent
sound pressures for diﬀerent frequencies. This is easy to imagine with a loudspeaker
of a given size, which radiates higher frequencies with more eﬃciency, because there
is less resistivity in the sound ﬁeld at these frequencies (the wavelength is small com-
pared to the diaphragm size, so this frequency is easy to excite). Although the term
might seem to be a little misleading at ﬁrst, it also applies for the eﬀect of a receiving
microphone on the sound ﬁeld it is inserted into. Recalling that the condenser mi-
crophone is a reciprocal device, the impedance of the microphone radiating is equal to
the impedance of the microphone receiving a sound pressure. The radiation impedance
comes especially into play when the microphone is used in free-ﬁeld conditions, because
it creates an increase of sensitivity in the high frequencies. The free-ﬁeld correction is
used to correct for this eﬀect, if needed.
3.5
The Different Sound Fields
There are three sound ﬁelds that are commonly distinguished. Those are pressure ﬁeld,
free ﬁeld, and diﬀuse ﬁeld. A pressure ﬁeld is apparent when there are small enclosing
dimensions compared to the wavelength, as for example in couplers[3]. Especially for
microphones, a pressure ﬁeld is deﬁned as ”the sound pressure uniformly applied over
the surface of the diaphragm”[7, sec.3.4]. Basically, the assumption is that in a pressure
ﬁeld the sound pressure is equal at any point.
Free-ﬁeld conditions are found when there are no reﬂecting waves interfering with
the sound ﬁeld[14, sec.3.5]. This is often also called an anechoic condition and can
be achieved by acoustically absorbing material at the walls, as in anechoic rooms and

3.9
Zoom-FFT
Zoom-FFT is a special case of the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), which is capable of
providing a much higher frequency resolution on a particular band of the frequency
range than an ordinary FFT. This technique is very helpful for analyzing frequencies
when very high precision is needed, for example ﬁnding small variations of a resonance
frequency. Several methods exist[21], but the general approach is usually a regular FFT
with some modiﬁcations. These modiﬁcation consists of a frequency shift of the signal
followed by a low-pass ﬁltering and a downsampling[22].
First, a frequency band for the zoom has to be deﬁned, whose center frequency fc will
be shifted down to 0 by multiplication with a complex unit vector e−j2πfc t. The signal
is then low-passed at the upper band-limiting frequency to avoid aliasing. Finally the
signal is resampled at a lower rate, which is deﬁned by the decimation factor D. This
provides the same amount of FFT bins, N, but over a much smaller frequency band with
bandwidth bw, which is dependent on the sampling frequency fs and the decimation
factor D,
bw = fs
D .
Some other practical restrictions arise from the method. The product D × N, of the
decimation factor D and the FFT size N, must be equal to or greater than the input
frame length L. As FFT size and input length are usually given, D should be derived as
an integer multiple of L/N, because the number of samples in the input signal must be
a multiple of the decimation factor.

3.10
Removal of acoustic reﬂections
Reﬂections can disturb acoustic measurements because they interfere with the direct
sound wave. This interference creates unwanted changes in the sound ﬁeld at the
observation position and it is advisable to avoid reﬂections when their inﬂuence is sig-
niﬁcant. Acoustic reﬂections are created wherever there is a sound wave encountering
a change in acoustic impedance, which of course happens at boundaries like walls or
other obstacles. Even in very good anechoic rooms these reﬂections exist because the
intended continuous change of impedance (by the acoustic absorbing wedges at the
walls) is never perfect. The result is a superposition of direct wave and reﬂected waves
at the point of observation, with periodic peak-notch-patterns in the frequency domain
due to addition and subtraction of the two coherent waves. Furthermore, it is likely to
create standing waves between source and receiver transducers. Often, these eﬀects
are neglected in good anechoic rooms, but for high-precision measurements, these have
to be taken into account.
To remove these reﬂections from the measurement, a time-selective technique can be
applied[23]. In the paper, the technique is applied to electrical transfer impedance
measurements, but the procedure should be equally valid for any measurement dis-
turbed by reﬂections, such as frequency response measurements. The procedure is
to transform the measured frequency response into the time-domain via an inverse
Fourier-transform, giving the impulse response of the captured system. This includes
the direct wave, the standing wave, and the reﬂections. It is now possible to apply a
window on the impulse response, which will only allow the direct wave information
to persist. After the time-window has been applied, the cleaned impulse response is
Fourier-transformed into frequency domain and a cleaned frequency response is ob-
tained.
However, there are some precautions to be considered and these are explained in great
detail in the PhD thesis[24] accompanying the paper. The time-window, for example,
should not be a rectangular window because this introduces a large disturbance in
the frequency domain[25], also called spectral leakage — due to the multiplication of
the window in time domain, this results in a convolution in the frequency domain
==PAGE:37==
3.10 Removal of acoustic reﬂections
27
(convolution theorem), creating the ripples known from the sinc-function. It is instead
suggested to use a Tukey-window[23].
The frequency response used for the transformation into impulse response must also
have enough information in the low frequency end. This means, that if only measure-
ment down to, for example, 250 Hz are captured, the low-frequency end has to be
extrapolated in a reasonable manner. On the other end of the frequency range, the re-
sponse’s decrease to zero can be artiﬁcially enforced by a low-pass ﬁlter. This low-pass
ﬁlter introduces a delay in the impulse response, and a linear-phase FIR low-pass ﬁl-
ter is recommended to avoid non-causalities (for example, in MATLAB: fir1() ). The
length of the ﬁlter has a similar eﬀect on the impulse response, again following the
convolution theorem.
Similar consideration apply to the length of the time window. The shorter the time-
window, the more ripples will be introduced in the resulting frequency response. This
is a consequence of the Fourier transform of the window, because the truncation of
the time signal with a short window creates broad side-band ripples in the frequency
domain[24, sec.3.3.2]. This means, that at some point the improvement through the
removal of reﬂections will be counterbalanced by the introduction of those ripples. This
eﬀect is especially apparent at the extremes of the frequency range, so it is advised to
use a high sampling frequency (e.g. 100 kHz). In the implementation this still had an
eﬀect in a range of up to 5 kHz, i.e. with 100 kHz sampling frequency, results between
0-5 and 45-50 kHz were contaminated.
In general, this method is a very delicate procedure — especially when the time-window

3.11
Electroacoustical modeling
Electroacoustics are a common method to model a complex system’s behavior by cre-
ating an electrical analogous circuit that approximates the true system behavior. Of
course, assumptions and simpliﬁcations are necessary to do so, and describing one par-
ticular physical system or device (for example a condenser microphone) with high pre-
cision is extremely diﬃcult, because many parameters have to be known or determined.
Still the concept is very powerful in describing the trend of a more general behavior, e.g.
predicting the overall system performance if one parameter is changed; for example the
change of microphone sensitivity after altering the microphone diaphragm thickness.
The following explanations and equations are more deeply discussed by Leach[11], on
which this section is heavily based upon. Please note that some notations might be
diﬀerent however.
3.11.1
General concept of electroacoustics
The underlying idea of electroacoustics is to convert a system transfer function into
a collection of equivalent electrical components with similar behavior in frequency.
These components usually correspond to frequency independent components, which are
modeled as resistors, proportional to frequency-components like coils/inductances, and
inversely proportional to frequency-components such as condensers/capacitors.
There are two analogies used for the conversion into electroacoustics, namely mobility
analogy and impedance analogy. The choice of analogy is usually arbitrary, but handling
of solutions can be easier (or more intuitive) in one or the other analogy depending on
the system that is to be modeled. The analogies diﬀer by their representation of the
electrical ”across” and ”through” variables, voltage U and current i respectively. For
acoustical systems these variables are sound pressure p and volume velocity Q; for
mechanical systems these are mechanical force F and velocity v. From the complex
generalization of Ohm’s law, Z = U/i, the analogous acoustical impedance is Za = p/Q,
and the mechanical impedance is Zm = F/v. Conversions between the two analogous

4.1
High-Sensitivity Microphone Peculiarities
The terms high-sensitivity microphone, low-noise microphone, and low-level microphone
all describe the same type of construction: microphones that are designed to capture
very low sound pressure levels, possibly below the human threshold of hearing. They
need a rather high sensitivity to give valuable output even at low sound pressures,
therefore they are also called high sensitivity microphones. To achieve this, the inherent
noise of the microphone needs to be very low to not mask the signal of interest; this is
why it is also called a low-noise microphone.
To capture very low sound pressures, the diaphragm must be easily excited by the
sound waves, which means that it needs to be moved more easily than for a regular
microphone. This also means that a very high open circuit sensitivity is needed, to
give satisfactory output voltage for even very low sound pressure levels. This can be
achieved by diﬀerent factors in the design stage of the microphone. The distance be-
tween diaphragm and back-plate should not be reduced too much to avoid contact
between the two, which might cause destruction of the microphone. A higher polar-
ization voltage instead causes more electrical noise, which would be counterproductive
when the aim is to construct a low-noise microphone. A larger diaphragm is only an
option when the measurement application allows for example switching from a WS2
to a WS1 microphone. So the “easiest” way to create a low-noise microphone would be
to reduce the diaphragm tension, and this is usually how it is done in practice.
But the reduction of diaphragm tension gives rise to another problem: a less damped
diaphragm. This low damping creates a severe peak in the frequency response around
the resonance, thereby disqualifying the microphone from regular measurement appli-
==PAGE:44==
34

Calibrations and measurements at GRAS are implemented through National Instru-

As mentioned in the appended section on measurement uncertainty (see Appendix sec-

repeatability, but usually it also means a lot of eﬀort. So for a microphone manufac-

the accredited calibration accuracy. For GRAS Sound & Vibration A/S the pressure
ﬁeld calibration via actuator is certiﬁed to an uncertainty of 0.3 dB for 1⁄2 ′′ microphones.
This provides a good indicator of how accurate the investigated calibration method for
the free-ﬁeld procedure should be, preferably less of course.
4.5
Free-ﬁeld validation of the anechoic room
In order to conduct measurements under free-ﬁeld conditions as demanded by e.g.
some parts of IEC 61094, usually an anechoic or hemi-anechoic room is needed. A
sound ﬁeld is considered anechoic, i.e., free-ﬁeld, when there is free propagation of
sound waves possible without disturbance due to reﬂections [3, p.98].
Most commonly, anechoic conditions are created by gradually changing the acoustic
impedance towards the terminating wall, by for example, placing acoustically absorb-
ing material in shape of wedges perpendicular to the outside walls. This is often called
an anechoic room, or in smaller scales, an anechoic chamber. Hemi-anechoic rooms
are diﬀerent from regular anechoic rooms because they have one reﬂecting wall, where
the sound source is ﬂush-mounted to approximate a source in an inﬁnite baﬄe. This
reduces diﬀraction eﬀects from the edges of the source.

Modeling the Frequency
Response
Modeling the sensitivity of a condenser microphone is a crucial step in the design pro-
cess to avoid unnecessary prototyping. By knowing the basic behavior of the individual
design parameters, it is possible to estimate the total sensitivity of the microphone.
However, this is only a generalized model, because due to production tolerances, the
individual microphone sensitivity might diﬀer signiﬁcantly. Instead, the purpose of
modeling can be to predict the behavior of a microphone or a microphone parameter
before changing it.
Several methods will be explained below. First, a simpliﬁed analytical solution will be
presented, which is based on a similar approach as the lumped-parameter electroacous-
tical model shown thereafter. Another presented prediction method is the numerical
Finite Element Method (FEM). Then a list of needed microphone parameters will be
presented and the derivation of these will be explained. The inﬂuence of the used de-
sign parameters on the ﬁnal response will be estimated using the Monte Carlo method.
Finally some approximations to unknown design parameters will be given.

5.1.3
FEM simulations
The above mentioned prediction methods are based on sometimes very wide assump-
tions and, for example, often disregard the perforation of the backplate as one part
of the electrical condenser. Also, the movement of the diaphragm is usually consid-
ered to be like a piston, which is obviously impossible because the diaphragm has ﬁxed
boundary conditions on the circumference. Results obtained from these methods are
expected to deviate from true measurements, and more detailed procedures would ide-
ally overcome those assumptions and incorporate some of these peculiarities.
Including some of the above mentioned issues and for providing a better understand-
ing of the microphone parameters, a numerical analysis was performed using the ﬁnite
element method (FEM). A detailed model of the geometries of the GRAS microphone
capsule type 40AH has been implemented in a commercially available numerical anal-
ysis software, COMSOL [37]. The numerical model was based on an example for the
Brüel & Kjær type 4134, which is given as a detailed modeling tutorial within the soft-
ware and the instructions are freely available online [38].
The model includes individual physics components for each the pressure acoustics,
thermo-viscous acoustics, membrane tension and deﬂection, and electrostatics.
Of
course, the geometries have been changed to match the speciﬁcations of the 40HL
type. Due to symmetry reasons, a quarter of the total geometry was modeled and in
the post-processing expanded into a full 3D geometry, see Figure 5.3.

in the anechoic room to check if the method is still valid. These reduced setups include
several setups in the anechoic room, a smaller anechoic chamber, a small test box and
ﬁnally coupler measurements to see if a method can be derived to express the free-ﬁeld
sensitivity from a pressure calibration.

Free-ﬁeld sensitivity in anechoic room
The most obvious choice to measure the free-ﬁeld response is to conduct these mea-

most likely to match the concept of free-ﬁeld, which by deﬁnition is a sound ﬁeld with-
out any reﬂections, see section 3.5. Unfortunately, this is also by far the largest setup
presented and ideally, the anechoic room would be emptied from all equipment before

The room has inner dimensions 4.40×4.22m and its walls, ﬂoor and ceiling are treated

In-house procedure
The current calibration procedure at GRAS Sound & Vibration A/S is considered
the reference measurement that all following measurements will be compared to. The
exact procedure was already presented in section 4.2. The procedure is a stepped sine
comparison measurement, where ﬁrst a secondary calibrated microphone is put into
the position of the microphone under test. A 1′′ microphone is used as a transmitter to
act as the acoustic source of the measurement. Stepped sines from 250-25,000 Hz are
presented from the source and the output voltage of ﬁrst the reference microphone is
measured and later the microphone under test is changed into place. The frequency
response of the microphone under test is then derived from the ratio of the output
voltages of both the reference and the test microphone.

6.2
Pressure sensitivity from actuator
measurements
A common practice in microphone manufacturing is to calibrate the microphone with an
electrostatic actuator measurement and then correct this measurement to the wanted
sound-ﬁeld purpose, i.e. free-ﬁeld, pressure, or random incidence ﬁeld.
This method is usually plausible because the actuator response is very close to the pres-
sure response, where it is assumed that a constant pressure-distribution is achieved
over the whole microphone diaphragm[43]. Especially at lower frequencies the dif-
ferences between the diﬀerent sound-ﬁeld responses are almost negligible; less than
0.1 dB below 500 Hz for WS1 microphones for example, and even less for smaller mi-
crophones[32].
That is why after capturing the actuator response, a correction for the free-ﬁeld re-
sponse can be applied, because the radiation impedance is mainly determined by the
microphone’s geometry and this is almost perfectly identical from one specimen to the
other within the same type[13]. Ideally, such correction curves would be measured
once for the type of microphone and remains valid as long as the geometries and mi-
crophone properties do not change. The free-ﬁeld sensitivity is measured and the dif-
ference to the actuator response is called the free-ﬁeld correction.
The free-ﬁeld sensitivities of the microphones under consideration are already known
(Figure 6.1), so the free-ﬁeld correction could be determined after capturing the actua-
tor response. The results for the electrostatic actuator measurements of the type 40AH
high-sensitivity microphones are shown in Figure 6.2.

6.6
Pressure sensitivity in a small coupler
The setup presented and validated in section 6.4 seems an appropriate alternative so
far. However, some more time was spent on trying to predict the high-sensitivity mi-
crophones’ free-ﬁeld sensitivity from pressure-ﬁeld measurements. As seen from the
actuator measurements and their free-ﬁeld “correction” curves in Figure 6.3, there is
no easy way of correcting from one sound ﬁeld to the other. The problem seems to be
that the acoustic impedance of the high-sensitivity microphones is so low that already
the introduction of an actuator will change the resonance frequency and quality fac-
tor of the microphones. The same must be expected to occur in small cavity couplers,
where otherwise the pressure sensitivity could be obtained.
A more sophisticated model must take this acoustic impedance into account. Assuming
that the most contributing part in the microphone sensitivity change between the sound
ﬁelds is the diaphragm tension, and assuming that the tension can be derived from
the resonance frequency, it should be possible to correct a coupler measurement with
the known acoustic impedance of the coupler cavity in combination with the acoustic
impedance of the microphone (derived from the resonance frequency = tension).
However, the procedure is not trivial and relies on many assumptions. The idea could
be to measure the pressure sensitivity of the high-sensitivity microphone under test by
means of an electrostatic actuator or a coupler comparison to a reference microphone.
Assuming that it is possible to estimate the diaphragm tension from the resonance fre-
quency captured in the pressure sensitivity, an estimate of the acoustic impedance of the
microphone could be possible. With this acoustic impedance, a model for the free-ﬁeld
sensitivity could be fed. After this, the electrical ﬁlter to equalize the resonance could

Another, hypothetical method could be considered if something like an impedance
matched source could be constructed. The problem with high-sensitivity microphones
is their low acoustical impedance, which means that they are easily inﬂuenced by the
surrounding sound ﬁeld. But if a sound ﬁeld could be created that has the same acous-
tic impedance as the microphone, then it should be possible to measure the pressure
sensitivity of these microphones without the need for any correction. The problem is
that such a source probably does not exist.
One approach could be derived from the concept of probe microphones, which are reg-
ular microphones with a closed cavity in front of the diaphragm that has a very narrow
duct with an opening to the outside. This probe microphone is often used in small enclo-
sures or very near to sources where the inﬂuence of the microphones must be as small
as possible. Usually, the tube to the outside sound ﬁeld has a diﬀerent impedance than
the microphone diaphragm. This creates reﬂections at the diaphragm which introduce
standing waves in the duct and therefore the sensitivity of the microphone system has
many notches. To balance these reﬂections, a second tube is put inside the microphone
and this tube is so long that it basically acts as an inﬁnite tube, i.e. without creat-
ing reﬂections. This secondary inside tube is impedance matched to the microphone
diaphragm, so that there will be no reﬂections and no standing waves.
With a similar method it should be possible to construct a coupler with a tube impedance-
matched to the test microphone impedance. The problem is possibly, that the impedances
of the high-sensitivity microphones have a too large spread and that an impedance
ﬁne-tuning would be necessary for each calibration. Without further investigation, this
seems a very complicated setup and not necessarily more time-eﬃcient than the current
one and aﬄicted with a high uncertainty. This might be of interest for future work.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
Most results have been discussed when they were presented. Hence, this chapter will
give a more general, very brief discussion about the applied methodology and their
limitations.
In general it seems that for high-sensitivity microphones some of the assumptions, that
especially pressure sensitivity and actuator calibrations rely on, do not hold. This is
because the acoustic impedance of the microphones is so low that they are too much
inﬂuenced by the pressure ﬁelds, so that a correction to free-ﬁeld is not possible any
more. The microphones behave diﬀerent due to the change in radiation impedance.
This means that if corrections cannot be predicted, each sound ﬁeld correction must be
measured for each single microphone, especially if the spread among microphones is
as large as observed in this case.
In most of the measured free-ﬁeld sensitivities there was a small step upwards in mag-
nitude around the resonance frequency. The main contribution to the deviations in
these sensitivity measurements seems to be the shift of the resonance frequency peak,
possibly due to changing ambient conditions. A small change in resonance frequency
will already introduce a large deviation between two measurements, because suddenly
two narrow peaks overlap at diﬀerent frequencies, creating a step-like response around
that frequency. Possible causes are probably the environmental conditions, such as tem-
perature and ambient air pressure, as these have an inﬂuence on the microphones’ di-
aphragm by changing the mechanical and acoustical damping. This will slightly move
the resonance frequency, creating aforementioned eﬀect. To avoid this, measurements
must be conducted under the same environmental conditions when compared with
each other. Otherwise, the uncertainty around the resonance frequency will create
unreliable results.
==PAGE:96==
86
8 Discussion
In the electroacoustical modeling approach many components are low frequency ap-
proximations, according to Leach[11]. The analytical solution is based on the same
derivation, therefore the same limitations apply. This gives way to discussing if these
models can be used for predicting the resonance frequencies, or if the simpliﬁcations
do not hold in this frequency range.
For the FEM simulation many coupled parameters are considered (coupled physics), but
a simulation with high mesh resolution would take many hours, even days in extreme
cases. Still it is based on parameters that are only estimates, for example the tension
and this makes the method only as good as the estimate of those parameters.
As can be seen from the serial number, microphone 81157 is the oldest of the investi-
gated devices, which could be a plausible cause for its response to diﬀer so much from
the others. With age, the diaphragm tension will decrease and so will the resonance
frequency. Both facts are reﬂected in the measurement data. But this could be sim-
ply coincidence. Of course, more data could provide more information in terms of the
spread of the production tolerances and thereby hopefully reduce the uncertainty.
Furthermore, it could be worth specifying smaller tolerances in the production line to
make sure that all microphones have more or less the same diaphragm tension. This
way, modeling and correcting becomes much easier, if the spread of the parameters can
be reduced. A rerun of the modeling approaches could be more promising then.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The focus of this work was aimed at ﬁnding an eﬃcient secondary calibration tech-
nique for high-sensitivity microphones. The current procedure at GRAS is very time-
consuming and taking up a lot of time in the anechoic room, which was intentionally
designed to host research and development work. An alternate procedure should avoid
the anechoic room.
As was shown, the problem with high-sensitivity microphones is that due to their low-
damped diaphragm their acoustic impedance is very low and coupler or actuator mea-
surements are therefore not possible. The diaphragm has a prominent resonance fre-
quency that changes too much when introduced into another sound ﬁeld. This reso-
nance peak is caused by the low diaphragm tension. The reason for the low diaphragm
tension is to allow more displacement and thereby create more output voltage, which
is the major purpose of a high-sensitivity microphone. One of the main problems in
production is that the diaphragm tension is unknown and underlies relatively large vari-
ations. It might be worth trying to solve the problem on the production side, reducing
those variations because then corrections could possibly be generalized.
To provide a more eﬃcient calibration technique, several setups have been investigated.
First, the anechoic room was tested for its free-ﬁeld conditions. Then the free-ﬁeld sen-
sitivities have been captured in the anechoic room to serve as the reference sensitivity.
After this, the setup was more and more reduces in size, ﬁrst into an anechoic chamber,
then an anechoic box and ﬁnally into couplers. The anechoic chamber was combined
with a time-selective technique to remove reﬂections and the inﬂuence of the standing
wave between the two microphones. This proved to be almost equally accurate as the
free-ﬁeld conditions in the anechoic room, and the reductions in the physical setup are
already signiﬁcant. The anechoic box setup was already to small to really fulﬁll the
==PAGE:98==
88
9 Conclusion
free-ﬁeld conditions. Although the results were improved using a radical time-selective
window, this method’s uncertainty is too large. The same holds for the coupler setups,
which were hoped to deliver some sort of more or less general correction from the pres-
sure sensitivity to the wanted free-ﬁeld sensitivity. These more theoretical concepts
have been investigated for a long time, however without delivering any such correc-
tions in a reliable fashion.
The space needed for all these alternatives to the anechoic room is of course much
smaller than using a whole room. So already the anechoic chamber is a worthy alter-
native. It can easily be handled by one person at one desk and there is no need for
occupying the anechoic room with this type of setup. However, the time-selective tech-
nique is key to this setup to be comparable to the free-ﬁeld conditions and needs to be
implemented carefully.
Many suggestions on possible improvement of the current or a future calibration setup
were given and the preferred alternative method is the anechoic chamber in combina-
tion with the time-selective technique.

Calibration is the process of ensuring that measured quantities are comparable. This
is possible through traceability of calibration processes, i.e. that each calibration can
be routed back towards a well deﬁned standard, e.g. the kilogram or the meter. For

national metrology institutes. International standards make sure that measurements
that comply with its regulations, deliver a certain quality.

The reciprocity calibration method is a primary calibration, which means that it can
be used to deﬁne the unit of the pressure, Pascal, which is N/m2. National metrology

laboratories are able to perform this primary method. The primary calibration has
the highest precision, i.e. the lowest measurement uncertainty, which also means that
equipment used for these procedures has to fulﬁll the highest demands. In the case
of microphone calibration, the primary calibration can be conducted both in pressure-
and in free-ﬁeld. This type of measurement is suited to provide microphone sensitivities

unlike for example the actuator calibration, the frequency response is already at the
right levels. Also, the reciprocity calibration gives absolute sensitivities without the
need of an additional reference, i.e. the sensitivities can be determined from only a
set of three reciprocal microphones[46]. The reciprocity calibration is based on the
reciprocity theorem, which states that the sensitivity of a condenser microphone is the
same if used as a receiver or a transmitter.

A.3.3
Relative Uncertainty
The relative uncertainty u(x)
X
is the standard uncertainty u(x) of the quantity x with
respect to its observed physical measure X. Its dimension is unitless but the ratio can
be expressed as a percentage. The relative uncertainty puts the absolute uncertainty
in relation to the actual measurement value and thereby makes it easily possible to
compare diﬀerent results’ uncertainties with each other; standard uncertainties are
absolute values and therefore depend on their measures’ size.
For example, the uncertainty of a weighing machine might be ±0.1 g, which is insignif-
icant when measuring 15 kg boxes; the relative uncertainty of this scenario would be
0.1g
15kg = 6.67e−6. But using the scale for checking the weight of small electronic pieces
gives a much higher relative uncertainty, for example when weighing 3 g components
the relative uncertainty is now 0.1g
3g = 3.33e−2, although the absolute uncertainty is still
the same, ±0.1 g.
