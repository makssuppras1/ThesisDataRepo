Abstract
Minimizing noise and vibrations due to dual-engine beating vibrations in ships present a serious
challenge in the marine industry.
Current solutions to vibration dampening of ship motors usually include the installation of large
mechanical vibrational compensators, which add cost and complexity to the ship.
The principle of Synchrophasing engines for the purpose of vibrational reduction has proven to be
an effective solution, with applications in the aerospace, industrial and maritime industries.
With the dawn of new, electronically controlled engines, a solution to this vibrational problem
could potentially be solved far more cost-effectively than current, mechanically implemented,
Synchrophasing solutions.
This thesis describes the design and implementation of a Synchrophasing system within MAN
Diesel & Turbo’s ME-Class electronic engine controllers, using only existing hardware, with the
purpose of minimizing engine vibrations.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In 1940, Albert Kalin was one of the ﬁrst to patent a method of reducing engine vibrations by
synchronizing the speeds and positions of the main engines of steam ships 1. Using this method,
he achieved synchronous engine speeds and could keep a desired phase angle constant between
engine positions through interconnected mechanical speed governors.
Since then, the concept of Synchrophasing has been further developed, and proven essential in
propeller aircraft where it is used extensively in both civilian and military applications 2 3. With
the dawn of modern software controlled marine engines, Synchrophasing has however yet to see
much use in modern times.
The use of twin-engine ships has seen an increase in popularity over the last years, partly due to the
the ﬁnancial crisis of 2008 giving fuel efﬁcient dual engine ship and advantage over single engine
solutions4. Currently, 80% of the 150 fuel tanker ships operating in Norway are dual engine ships
and around 90 dual engine ships around the world use engines developed by MAN Diesel & Turbo.
This year, around 43 dual engine ships have been sold by MAN Diesel & Turbo, most of them
being 5 cylinder ships, which are particularly prone to engine vibrations.
Since the hardware used in the maritime industry requires extensive testing and certiﬁcation, the
primary challenge of this thesis is the creation of a solution compatible with the Engine Control
System developed by MAN Diesel & Turbo (MDT), without the need for costly hardware additions.
The analysis of this thesis will describe the ship dynamics and vibration sources that are the
basis of this project, as well as the viability of Synchrophasing. The architecture of the currently
implemented systems aboard ME-Class ships from Man Diesel will be described, together with
speciﬁc implementation of the Multi-Purpose Controller (MPC) and its subsystems. Furthermore,
the embedded software platform will be described and the simulation tools used. The necessary
precautions considering power-train dynamics and existing controllers will be discussed, together
with a description of the scope of the project.

2.1
Introduction
Several things must be considered when designing and implementing a Synchrophasing system
for maritime use. For one, controlling a 30.000 kW heavy fuel engine at sea is very different than
controlling a DC motor on a lab bench 1 .
Some of the biggest differences are the dynamics when changing to the engine speed, which can
only be performed via cylinder driven fuel injection, leading to a relatively long time interval
between inputs. While the power output of the engine is unlinear, the load seen by the engine
can also be expected to be highly varying, due to mechanical couplings and feedback from the
propeller.
All in all, this adds to a highly dynamic and complex mechanical system and thereby understanding
the system is essential for a successful implementation in real life applications.
This chapter includes an analysis of the vibrations experienced on board a ship caused by both
engine and propeller dynamics. It will also describe the mechanical and electrical hardware
components of the Man Diesel & Turbo ME-ECS engine relevant to this project. Finally, a
description of the software foundation built for the electronic engine controllers, as well as the
simulation environment used to verify the behavior of the engine controllers.

2.2
Background
In Sydhavn, Copenhagen, the Danish branch of MAN Diesel & Turbo, often called MAN B&W, is
tasked with building engines for large cargo ships such as container ships and fuel freighters.
A number of Dual engine ships experience strong vibrations while sailing at different speeds, felt
mostly on the deck of the ship. Causing not only a high amount of discomfort among the crew,
but also leading to metal fatigue in the hull of the ship. Needless to say, it is in the interest of both
MAN Turbo & Diesel and their customers to get rid of these vibrations.
Within MAN B&W, Department LDC1 is specialized in working with the electro-hydraulical
control of the ME class engines, which are designed for control of valve operation without a
camshaft. LDC1 presented us with the vibration problem experienced on Dual engine ships which
could likely be solved via electronic synchronization of the engine speeds and positions during
cruising, dubbed Synchrophasing.
The effects of Synchrophasing had been investigated by the vibration department in Sydhavn, and
shown to be viable (further explained in 2.4). However, the functionality of Synchrophasing on the
engines had not been implemented yet.
2.3
Vibration sources and derived issues
This section describes the forces acting upon maritime craft and the type of vibrations they may
cause. It presents the reader with the fundamental forces Syncrophasing has to cope with and has to
avoid. There are many types of vibration sources which cause vibrations on board a ship, even the
oceans waves can hit the hull and cause oscillating rolling of the ship see Figure 2.1. In this report
the three main vibration sources and two controller derived issues will be explained and taken
into account. Some types of vibrations can be dealt with using Synchrophasing and the potential
vibration sources induced by the controller can be handled by limiting the fuel throttling at speciﬁc
speeds.
Figure 2.1: Vertical oscillating of the hull
The ﬁrst source of vibration is categorized as unbalanced forces. These can be classiﬁed as un-
balanced vertical 1st, 2nd, 4th and 6th -order moments, and as horizontal ﬁrst order moments see
Figure 2.2. They are well known and become a problem when the motor runs at speeds near the
hull resonance frequency or when the hulls natural frequency become and even composite number
of the engines speed. These vibrations can be very strong and can in some cases cause structural
damages and make the work environment for the ship crew unbearable. Effectively making the ship

limited to sail either slower or much faster than desired, and therefore delaying or causing wasteful
fuel economy. Any of the previous complications can be induced by any of the ﬁve sources of
vibrations.
Experience at MDT has shown that only ship engines with seven or less cylinders encounter these
vibration issues. Another factor is that the ship hull resonance frequency could be located at speeds
faster than, or sufﬁciently lower than, the maximum speed of the ships motor and can therefore not
induce a strong enough oscillation. If this is the case, the following two types of motor vibrations
won’t cause oscillation. The sheer size and multitude of sub-elements of the ships in question
means that, despite most theories describing vibration problems are well understood, it is not
currently considered feasible to design ships that do not risk having these issues. Many known
vibrations problems are therefore practically dealt with when encountered.

A common method of moving a resonance frequency of the hull is by making it more or less stiff,
effectively moving the resonance frequency. This is done by adding or thinning metal beams within
the hull. This process adds a lot of extra costs and causes delays in the construction process. Simply
copying an already existing ship without vibration problems is therefore also a common practice.

that can accure are devided into two categories: H-type guide force moments and X-type guide

The third relevant type of vibration source is axial vibration, much of the vibrational description
comes from the book Two-stroke Engines (2005). When the crank throw see Figure 2.5 is loaded
by the gas pressure through the connecting rod, the arms of the crank throw deﬂect in the axial
direction of the crankshaft, exciting axial vibrations. Through the thrust bearing, the system is
connected to the ship’s hull.
Figure 2.5: Axial vibrations
Generally, only zero-node axial vibrations are of interest. Thus the effect of the additional bending
stresses in the crankshaft and possible vibrations of the ship’s structure due to the reaction force
in the thrust bearing are to be considered. An axial damper is ﬁtted as standard on all engines,
minimizing the effects of the axial vibrations. Axial vibrations are relevant to the project because
speed controlling and Synchrophasing are achieved by deducting or adding fuel in each cylinder.
A very aggressive speed controller could generate axial vibrations by adding a lot of fuel at once.
Therefore, the design of the controller has to take these vibrations into account by having strict fuel
limiters to avoid generating vibrations see Figure 2.5.
The fourth type is torsional vibrations, see Figure 2.6. The rotating masses of the engine including
the crankshaft, the thrust shaft, the intermediate shaft(s), the propeller shaft and the propeller are
for calculation purposes considered as a system of rotating masses (inertias) interconnected by
torsional springs. The gas pressure of the engine acts through the connecting rod mechanism with
a varying torque on each crank throw, exciting torsional vibration in the system with different
frequencies. In general, only torsional vibrations with one and two nodes need to be considered.
Figure 2.6: Torsional forces acting on main shaft
The main critical order, causing the largest extra stresses in the shaft line, is normally the vibration
with order equal to the number of cylinders. For example, six cycles per revolution on a six cylinder
engine. The resonance happens at the engine speed corresponding to the natural torsional frequency
divided by the number of cylinders. The torsional vibration conditions may require a torsional
vibration damper.

Ships with a controllable pitch propeller, a so-called QPT (Quick Passage of a barred speed
range Technique) is an alternative to the vibration damper. The QPT would be implemented in
the governor in order to limit the vibratory stresses during the passage of the barred speed range.
Because Synchrophasing is implemented by a separate phase and speed controller that alters the
fuel index of the governor, the QPT could end up working against Synchrophasing. It is likely
that these two systems would have to be able to coexist, because ships with a controllable pitch
propeller could be exposed to other types of vibrations as well. These vibrations would have to be
tackled by Synchrophasing. The Synchrophase controller would have to modify the fuel index of
the governor, while still respecting the QPT’s garred speed range.
The ﬁfth and last discussed are propeller induced hull vibrations. As the propeller pushes the ship,
a continuous force of water is pushed vertically up against the hull. The inertia forces origination
from the propeller rotation and reciprocating masses of the water hitting the hull create unbalanced
external moments although the external forces are zero see Figure 2.7.

the hull’s rigidity and distribution of masses wich can due , whereas the vibration level at resonance

In dual engines, the only system where Synchrophasing is possible, the possibility of counterphase

systems such as vibration compensators (RotComp).

2.4
Viability of Synchrophasing in twin engine ships
While Synchrophasing has proven its worth since the 1920’s using mechanical solutions, it has
yet to see much use in the world of electronically controlled ship engines. To investigate this, the
effectiveness of Synchrophasing was tested in 2007 by MAN Diesel in sea trials (Preem (2007)),
where the twin engines of a 137.5 kiloton LNG (Liquid Natural Gas) tanker were brought in a
Synchrophased state, through manual control of its dual 6S70ME-C engines. The 6S70ME-C
engine is a 6 cylinder “Super long stroke” electronically controlled engine, with a piston diameter
of 70 cm. It develops 19.620 kW of power.
2.4.1
Tests
The tests done by MDT were designed to investigate the effectiveness of mechanical top bracing on
the vibration level of the ship. Also, the effect of Synchrophasing was tested, while maneuvering
and at different speed sets. The primary speed chosen for the test was 87 RPM, due to the 6th order
H-mode resonance at that speed, according to previous testing done by DSME.

2.4.3
Vibration testing conclusion
The results of the investigations showed that Synchrophasing at the critically resonant speed
of 89 RPM, it was possible to reduce the amount of vibrations from 25mm/s to 5mm/s on the
engine cylinder frame transverse direction of the ship. The damping of these vibrations are higly
dependant on the phase angle between the engines, which must be a controllable parameter for the
Synchrophasing system.
Additionally, it showed that the electronic control unit is able to control the engine speed accurately
enough to allow for an implementation of a Synchrophase system without the need for additional
specialized hardware.
As expected, keeping the engines Synchrophased during maneuvering will pose a challenge, due
to the different dynamics imposed on the engines during a turn. The test was inconclusive in
disproving the viability of Synchrophasing during maneuvering, since the ship was operating close
to the limiter (at MCR speed) and thus would not be able to control the engine fully.

System Redundancies and fault response
When Synchrophasing is enabled it is always during long hauls, for example on a two week ocean
crossing at some set cruising speed. It is at such crossings that the ship crew carry out their
maintenance work which could involve replacing control modules (MPC’s) which amongst other
things are used for Synchrophasing. Since the ships already require protection against single point
of failure – multiple MPC’s are always racked in redundancy system. The same precautionary
actions are therefore taken for the Synchrophasing system and in addition – cross cabling is also
done with a backup cabling. The conﬁguration of the current cabling does however carry a few
issues. For example – the position encoder of the motor shaft does not have its own power supply
(yet).

Ideally in order to have a smooth transition between the regular governor control and the Syn-
chrophasing control, a “bumpless transfer” from the bog Gene F. Franklin (1991) feature should be
used. However, the available RAM & processor power of the MPCs is limited, and the "bumpless
transfer" feature would cost too much of its processing power. A short description of what a
"bumpless transfer" can be seen in ﬁgure 2.14 and ﬁgure 2.15.
Figure 2.14: Standard implementation of bumpless transfer set to governor
Figure 2.15: Bumpless transfer set at Synchrophasing governor
A standard way of implementing it would be to run both controllers, but only using the current
controllers’ output for the actual regulation. This means that when you switch the other controllers
integrator values, they are already in sync, thus enabling a “bumpless transfer”. Instead of this the
master engines integrator value is send to the master governors integrator when the two engines
speed difference is small enough. This means that the ﬁrst fuel index calculated afterwards will
only deviate from the previous active governors’ index value with the phase difference. There will
be no change in terms of calculating the fuel index for the speed set points, necessary to keep up
the speed.

2.7
Adaptive Filter and Gain Scheduling
Both single engine and dual engines ships have a non-linear dynamic. There are three causes
for this dynamic, the ﬁrst one is the engine power curve, the second one is the propellers’ water
resistance, and thirdly there is the discontinuous rotation with decreasing speed.
2.7.1
Discontinuous rotation with decreasing speed
When a ship with one or multiple engines sails at a very low speed, it will not be able to sail at a
constant speed. The movement will become unsteady, similar to the movement of an old locomotive
starting to move. This unsteady wobble is diluted with increasing the speed. The cause of this
wobble can be explained by looking at how the engine runs. When sailing at a higher speed, the
speed with which the piston moves up again after an explosion is relatively similar to the speed it
had when coming down. As soon as the engine is told to slow down, fewer and smaller explosions
occur, and the speed with which the piston gets pushed down by the explosion is now much higher
than when its moving back up. Imagine the piston being pushed down rapidly by an explosion,
then slowly coming up again almost at point of stand still, reaching top point when fuel is injected
again and then accelerating rapidly by the explosion, as seen in Figure 2.16.
Figure 2.16: Crankshaft revolution - illlustration simpliﬁed for the sake of point.
Therefore, the crankshaft and accordingly the propeller will rotate with a variable speed. This effect
is weakened as the number of pistons on the crank shaft increases.

For example, a one cylinder motor, would theoretically have to use the same amount of fuel as a 8
cylinder motor, but would have to deliver one strong explosion all at once to rotate the crank shaft.
An 8 cylinder motor would distribute the same power, but divided in 8 equal portions. This would
rotate the crank shaft more evenly. In order for the ECU to know when to inject the cylinders with
fuel, it would need to know the relative positions of the individual cylinders. These positions are
known by reference to the position of the crank shaft. A so called “Activation table”. is used to
keep track of this. The table lists the position of the cylinders in relation to the rotation of the crank
shaft. The rotation of the crank shaft is expressed in degrees, in which 0 degrees is deﬁned by
the position of the crank shaft, when the piston in cylinder one is at its top position. Therefore, it
sufﬁces to sample the rotation of the crank shaft in order for the ECU to know the position of each
cylinder. The position of the crank shaft is deﬁned by the position of cylinder one. This means that
when cylinder one is at its top position, the crank shaft is at 0 degrees. When cylinder one is at its
lowest position, the crank shaft is at 180 degrees.
When designing a controller, it is undesirable to use extra fuel or slow down the motor when the
crankshaft’s average speed is constant.
A fuel index is calculated when crank shaft position corresponding to the Activation table values.
Without a speed ﬁlter to see the average speed, a smaller amount of fuel is calculated for the
accelerating point of pistons after fuel explosion. The Error is repeats when controller calculates to
much fuel for the pistons slowing down due to compression see Figure 2.16.
Propellers water resistance
When the engine is running , there will come a varying resistance from the propeller. If the ship
is accelerating, the propeller has to push the ship through the water see Figure 2.17. The water
surrounding the propeller gives resistance, which in turn creates a resistance to the ships crank
shaft.

the ship see Figure 2.18. But when the ship needs to decelerate, the propeller gets dragged through
the water by the massive ship see Figure 2.19. At this point, the propeller is moving the crankshaft,

2.11
Encoder ring
The encoder/trigger ring (often simply called "the Tacho") is one of the most important measurement
components in the MDT ME-Engine program, since the ME engine has no mechanical connection
between crankshaft and fuel injection/exhaust valves as an engine with a mechanical camshaft.
The engine design is implemented with an electronically measured positional feedback, for software
control of the engine. The trigger ring assembly can be seen mounted to an engine on ﬁgure 2.24.
The measurements are based on a trigger ring with a total of 360 teeth as well as a semicircular
ring, used for absolute positioning. The relative movement of the crankshaft is measured by four
sensors, called the quadrature sensors, since they deliver a positional update each 1/4 period of the
encoder. Each ECU receives four quadrature inputs (e.g ECU A is only connected to Q1A and Q2B
as shown in ﬁgure 2.25).
In order to ﬁnd the absolute position of the crankshaft, the measured quadrature positions are
referenced to the measurements of a semicircular ring with two sensors named Marker Master and
Marker Slave, as shown on ﬁgure 2.25. Four of these sensors exist, placed at 45 degree increments
of the crankshaft. This means that the accuracy of the crankshaft can be evaluated to a precision of
±22.5 degrees at system start-up.
Figure 2.24: Tachometer encoder ring with sensors mounted (on the upper left of the image) on
the a 6S70ME-C engine
As mentioned in section 2.10, the TPU is tasked with the constant monitoring of the encoder. In the
embedded software application code, there are two different events that trigger a positional update
from the TPU, time based triggering or angle based triggering.

Entry Point of the Synchrophasing system
The fuel index is a name of a unit-less factor used in MAN diesel’s engine controllers. In principle,
it deﬁnes a quantity of fuel delivered to the engine cylinder, effectively a measure of energy. The
fuel index is deﬁned as a ﬁxed point value between 0 and 1, corresponding to zero fuel injection
and maximal fuel injection respectively. While zero fuel index will cause the engine to hault, the
maximum Fuel Index is calculated as the amount of fuel injected when running at the engines
Maximum Continuous Rating (MCR) speed.
As described in section 2.8, the primary role of the ECU governor is the calculation of the desired
fuel index of the engine with regards to the desired engine speed.
Within BES, the governor component is responsible for the calculation of this Fuel Index, called
by a task within the RTOS. This task is named the Primary Governor task, or "Pritask" and its
execution is triggered by the TPU each time an engine piston reaches TDC, calculating the required
amount of fuel for the cylinder.
This primary governor task will be the primary entry point of the Synchrophasing system. The
ECU governor class will calculate the fuel index ﬁrst and the contribution from the Synchrophasing
system (based on speed and phase error) will be added to the fuel index, before limiter checks will
be performed.
Also, since this project requires simulation with two ECU Governors, a method for realistic
execution of the primary and secondary tasks must be implemented in simulation.

2.14
Scope
The primary goal of this project is the implementation of a solution for reducing ship vibrations via
Synchrophasing. A list of general requirements have been formulated for the implementation:
• The system should be able to bring the engines to a pre-speciﬁed phase offset within a
reasonable time span.
• It must be able to accept disturbances from external forces such as ocean currents or waves
• Must be implemented within the current platform without impacting the performance of the
existing ECU
• The required hardware for implementation should not need additional components
• Controller implementation must obey limits/QPT
• The System will bench-marked in the Dynamic Simulation Environment, before moving on
to tests on the hardware wall.
Features that will not be implemented in this project are:
• The Synchrophasing system will not be expected to perform in maneuvering situations, since
maneuvering requires the engines to operate at different speeds.
• The controller will only be tuned via the relatively simpliﬁed Ziegler-Nichols tuning method,
since the ship model that will be used for ﬁrst sea trials is unknown at the moment
• Analysis of the ship/engine system in order to ﬁnd the optimal phase offset will not be
performed.
• While the ﬁnished system should be user controllable from the MOP, a polished GUI
implementation for controlling the Synchrophasing system will not be implemented
• Since the main purpose of this project is to create a system that synchronizes two engine
control systems, the detailed emulation of low level I/O’s on the secondary engine systems
are deemed unnecessary since the primary system is used as the reference for veriﬁcation of
the system behavior.
• Noise from ADC converters, as well as speed measurement noise caused by the engine
dynamics have not been modeled, due to limitations in the DSE model. The robustness to
noise will tested once the project reaches the MDT Test Wall.

3.3
Controller design
This section describes the proposed solutions for implementing phase and speed controllers at a
theoretical level, with a short description of the controllers, as well as thoughts that were behind
the selection of the implemented controllers.
During synchrophasing, both engines will not be able to be phase locked while following their
speed setpoint accurately, unless these speed setpoints are exactly the same. Since functionality to
synchronize speed setpoints was deemed outside the scope of this project (described in chapter
6), methods were devised to work around this problem. While the speed set points could be
synchronized, the phase difference corrections were deemed too time critical to be synchronized
between the two system. Because of this, integration of the phase error is only performed in the
slave engine.
The principle of Synchrophasing is two engines’ crankshafts having a constant position relative
to each other. The two engines of the ship are of the same type, yet not exactly identical, as there
are differences in friction in cylinders, friction in the propeller, the fuel injection, etc. Therefore
it is impossible for the two engines to have the same crank shaft position while keeping their
Speed setpoint. A design decision was made to split up the engines into a Master and a Slave
engine. The Slave engine follows the Master engines’ speed setpoint. Two controller designs were
conceptualized, both using the Master-Slave principle.
The two designs both make the two engines aware of the other engines’ speed and position. This is
executed by adding two P controllers to each system and giving the ﬁrst P controllers the speed
difference and providing the second P controllers with the phase difference. This system with only
P controllers, would result in a steady-state phase error. This is solved by adding an integrator to
the slave engines’ P phase controller, but hereby a new problem is introduced, the slave engine now
has two integrators. Both controllers solve this problem in a different way.
3.3.1
Synchrophasing controller with deadband
The ﬁrst controller concept, illustrated on ﬁgure 3.3, is based on the principle of a P speed- and PI
phase- controller on the slave engine, with the addition of a deadband around the existing ECU
governor speed controller error, to accommodate the two integrators in the slave engine as explained
in the introduction. The master engine contains a P controller for the phase controller, but with
no deadband in the ECU governor. The deadband is not necessary since the master only has one
integrator in its system. Therefore the desired speed setpoint can still be met.
The advantage of using a deadband on its speed controller, is that it allows the slave to deviate
from the speed setpoint without saturating its governor integrator, as well as allowing the phase
controller to adjust the speed without integrating up against the governors integrator.

4.3
Extended Phase Error
Even though we can read the current position of the crankshaft from both engines, the dynamics of
the ship and engine may prevent the phase synchronization controller from achieving zero error
without overshooting by one revolution.
If this is not taken into account, as soon as the phase error exceeds ±2π the resulting inversion of
the error signal could lead to constant oscillations around the set point.
A ﬁgure illustrating the problem and the implemented unwrapping functionality can be seen
in ﬁgure 4.1. The ﬁgure shows the primary engine position rotating relatively to the (linearly)
illustrated secondary engine position. The intersection between the two indicate the phase error.
Since the position of a crankshaft can be deﬁned as going between ±π, the maximum error between
them is ±2π, as illustrated by the blue area.
In order to combat this, the extended phase error class keeps track of the change in phase error
between each measurement, creating a ﬁxed phase reference point across multiple revolutions of
difference, as shown in the green area.

As explained in the analysis, calculation and ﬁltering of the secondary engine speed is highly

Two ﬁltering classes are implemented:
Simple ﬁlter
Implemented as a simple speed estimation, using the "time since last activation" value of the tacho

Adaptive ﬁlter
The adaptive ﬁlter is implemented using a different approach, based on a patented algorithm
owned by MDT, speciﬁcally made for ﬁltering out the dynamics of a ship engine. A more detailed
description of the original ﬁlter from 1994 can be found in Appendix A.8
The implementation is based on a counter and three vectors. The counter "measurement point"
is incremented at each call, going from zero to the number of cylinder activations because the
adaptive ﬁlter needs information from exactly one revolution earlier as seen in Section 3.4.
At initialization, "measurement point" is set to zero. The three vectors used in the class, "TimeS-
tamps", "lastCalcSpeed" and "FilterDeviationSignalsSpeed", are initialized with a length equal to
the number of activations.
Each call to the ﬁlter trigger an accumulation of a variable storing the total revolution time, while the
discrete intervals "timeSinceLastActivation" value is stored in the "TimeStamps" vector according
to the "measurement point" on the crankshaft.
This way the vector stores each delta time corresponding for each activation angle of the crankshaft.
This way a rolling average of the revolution duration is calculated, since the old delta time is
retrieved from the vector and subtracted from the total revolution time, every time a "measurement
point" is hit.
Entries for the vector "FilterDeviationSignalSpeed" are set according to the "lastReadingAtPoint"
counter, and represents a weighted deviation from the mean speed, for each individual activation
angle. The deviation from the mean speed at a speciﬁc activation angle is implemented as:
FilterDevSignalsSpeed[i] = FilterDevSignalsSpeed[i] + (lastCalcSpeed[i] −meanspeed)
NoOfActivations
where
lastCalcSpeed[i] = rawspeed −FilterDevSignalsSpeed[i]
This calculation is performed at each iteration of the ﬁlter, with regards to one revolution earlier of
the crankshaft, and the deviation is subtracted from the raw speed value received from the tacho
telegram, resulting in a ﬁltered speed. An illustration of the implementation of the algorithm as it
goes from one crankshaft activation to the next, can be seen on ﬁgure 4.3

4.7
State machine
The State Machine contains the primary logic for controlling the behavior of the Synchrophasing
system during cruising. The main purpose of the state machine is to handle special conditions
during running, such as a too large engine speed difference as well as the prevention of wrapping
of the phase error.
The class contains 3 timers, used for timeouts, to signal the end of a fall-back state.
The system can be in a total of 8 different states, where 3 of these can be categorized as fall-back
states.
The "Inactive speed diff" state is used to signal to the crew that the difference in engine speeds is
too high, as deﬁned by the Speed diff upper and lower limit parameters.
Most of the state-machine logic is carried out in its own RTOS thread, the statemachinetask.
The state machine is instantiated keeping a reference to the governor speed error as a parameter,
and otherwise uses its component input variables (speed error, phase error, enabled) to determine
the next state.

• Inactive Timer

to give the system a certain amount of time to correct the speed error, before Synchrophasing
is disabled.

4.9.1
Master Controller Implementation
The master is implemented as a p speed and p phase controller:
FuelIndexContribution = KpPhase · Ephase + KpSpeed · ESpeed
(4.1)
In order to reduce the stationary error of the P controller implementation, a specialized condition
has been implemented. If the phase error is decreasing while Synchrophasing, the FI contribution
from the speed controller is decreased.
This is necessary because when the phase error is moving towards 0, the contribution from the
speed controller (which is trying to reduce speed difference to 0) would work against the phase
controller in achieving its set-point.
To achieve this a condition is added:
If the last calculated phase error is less than current phase error (if we are moving away from
set-point)
FuelIndexContribution = KpPhase · Ephase + KpSpeed · ESpeed
(4.2)
if the last calculated phase error is larger than the current phase error (moving closer to setpoint):
FuelIndexContribution = Kpphase · Ephase
(4.3)

Controller tuning via Ziegler Nichols
test2 In this part Ziegler Nichols closed lope tuning methods have been used to ﬁnd the initial coarse
tuning parameters. This classical tuning method is usually only used for a single regulation system.
The relevant system in this case is however of the Multiple Input Multiple Output type (MIMO),
see ﬁgure 3.4. In order to tune the Synchrophase controller system, one of the two controllers has
been turned off (not running syncrophase). This effectively means that the two controllers will still
have the same speed set point but only one system will attempt to syncrophase. Since the engine
controllers are set up as master and slave, only the slave controller contains an integrator on the
phase.
To begin Ziegler Nichols closed loope tuning the two governers are treated as system noise, meaning
that we do not touch the parameters of either engine governers. Then the slaves PI controller is
reduced to a P-type controller by setting the integrator to zero. Syncrophasing begins 55 seconds
after the simulation is initiated. After this point the parameter of the p-gain is slowly increased
until a minimal stable system is achieved. See ﬁgure 5.11 (Engiene speed scaled 0-1 MCR).
Figure 5.11: P-gain for minimal stable system Engiene speed scaled 0-1 MCR
The P-gain reached is 2.5 and a minimal stable system can be seen from roughly 65 seconds and
onward into the simulation. The time constant, T0, is found from the plot and calculated to 2.43
The P-gain and time constant is then multiplied with the corresponding coefﬁcients as seen in table
5.7. As previously mentioned, normal usage of Ziegler-Nichols method is for single regulators.
Since the syncrophasing uses it for two, and both systems has 2 set parameters, a total of four
systems should be taken into account. This means that all regulator parameters are divided by four
before their ﬁnal implementation in order to keep their total value within the tuning parameters of
the Ziegler-Nichols method.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
6.1
Choice of controller
As an alternative to the Master/Slave controller architecture, a completely different path could be
taken in engine control.
Via the creation of a "virtual" master reference for speed and phase, both engines could effectively
function as slaves, leading to a more symmetrical implementation. This method has been used
and patented in the aircraft industry (patent US 3460012 A). However, since the architecture dual
engine electronic control system in the ME class ships is inherently constructed as two separate
entities, a centralized, symmetrical implementation of the Synchrophasing system did not seem
like a straight forward solution, and would likely incur additional hardware additions.
6.2
High speed cruising
The Synchrophasing system would be unsuitable when operating at speeds close to MCR, since
any correction to the engine speeds would be limited by the ecu governor limits. This would
result in unstable performance and should be communicated to the crew via the MOP GUI. In the
implementation of the system, the state machine ensure disabling of the Synchrophasing system,
unit the speed difference between the engines have been reduced by the ECU governor.
6.3
Determining optimal phase offset
During the construction process of a ship, analyses of the vibration characteristics of the hull and
engine are usually performed by the shipyard. The results of the analyses are already utilized, as
described in the Analysis chapter, for vibration dampening, via passive or active methods. This
same method would also be used in determining the optimal phase offset for the Synchrophasing
phase offset set point.

CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
6.4
Limitations of the Microprocessor/RTOS
As mentioned in the implementation introduction, the current software release running on the ECU
MPC is already running close to capacity with regards to memory and operating system limits.
During development of this project, a number of hard-coded limits of the RTOS and BES framework
were reached, related to the maximum number of I/O objects, application components and parame-
ters. This was a particularly big problem when running in the Dynamic Simulation Environment,
since it in principle require the simulation of two concurrent ECU and powertrain models.
The solution to this was to increase the hard-coded limits deﬁned for the compiler, which has
allowed us to simulate the systems with no apparent problems.
As for the embedded system, initial testing on the test wall showed that the Synchrophasing
system was able to run without problems. Since department practices dictate that dynamic memory
allocation should only be done during initialization of the MPC, we (and other developers at MDT)
believe that the hard coded RTOS limits may have been chosen a bit too conservatively.
6.5
Alternative solutions to the software implementation
As for the entry point of the Synchrophasing software application, the speed/phase controller
calculations were inserted just after the ECU governor calculation of the Fuel Index, which was
then adjusted by the Synchrophasing system. Alternative implementations could be conceived, but
our solution was chosen in order to keep in spirit with the Object Oriented programming paradigm.
With the current solution, the Synchrophasing system can be seen as a separate entity, and should
make it robust to changes in the existing ECU governor.

CHAPTER 7
Future Work
7.1
MODBUS
Before sea trials can be performed, the MODBUS functionality would need to be implemented, in
order to ensure safe operation of the synchrophase system.
Since the MPC platform already supports MODBUS, it would be ideal for use everywhere in this
project, but because of the high amounts of data overhead on the bus, it was not guaranteed to
deliver data faster than 2Hz. This made it unsuitable for transmission of time-critical data such as
engine speed and position. For less time-critical tasks however, it has many beneﬁts over using
analog signals.
The future implementation of MODBUS would be used in facilitating the communication of speed
setpoint between engine systems. This would be useful in eliminating ADC errors in setpoints
from the bridge.
As mentioned in chapter 2, the cross cabling only transfers the position of the sister engine, but not
the direction, which opens the potential for accidental enabling of synchrophasing when running at
inverse speeds on the engines, this could also be eliminated by MODBUS.
Additionally, it could be possible that the TPU/MCU would evaluate the speed error relative to its
sister engine, possibly resulting in unwanted behavior.
7.2
GUI
With the modbus communication functionality implemented between ECU’s, the speed setpoints of
the engine could be transmitted directly as well as the ﬁltered speed. When both speeds are within
range, a simple GUI would need implementation to allow for control and to indicate the state of the
synchrophasing system.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
With the rise in production of dual engine ships, a similar rise is seen in dealing with the problem
of 2nd order vertical hull vibrations, caused by propeller interaction and beating engine frequencies.
Currently, no solution exists to eliminate these vibrations.
This thesis addresses the implementation of a software upgrade in to the Man Diesel & Turbo’s
Electronic Engine Control system, enabling synchronization of the engine speeds and relative
phase, in order to eliminate these vibrations.
This upgrade, dubbed the Synchrophasing system, has been created using object oriented design
methodology and includes customizable parameters and scheduling, allowing a modularized
integration taking into account engine dynamics, as well as differences in engine size or type.
The implemented P and PI controller implementations allow for "bumpless switching" in and out
of Synchrophasing and complies with existing engine limitations.
The implementation was successfully tested on the MDT Multiple Purpose Controller, both in
simulation and on hardware, without the need for any new components. Due to the placement of
the Synchrophase controller, all Fuel Index calculations are also ensured to be within system limits.
For performance veriﬁcation, a novel simulation method was developed in MDT’s Dynamic
Simulation Environment, enabling twin engine simulation, useful not only in this project but
enabling future developers in implementing dual engine simulations.
Within the simulation model, the functionality of the controller has been veriﬁed in reaching a
pre-speciﬁed phase-offset between two engines within 10-35 seconds.
The Synchrophasing system has the potential for use in eliminating vibrations as a retroﬁt solution
to existing ships, or reducing the cost of implementing vibration dampening solutions in newly
constructed ships.

9.2. CHANGES DURING THE PROJECT
93
9.2
Changes during the project
At the kick-off of this project, we were highly optimistic. This was mainly due to the outcome of the
analysis work done beforehand by the vibration group at MDT, who proved that Synchrophasing
would work in reducing vibrations, and the existing governor of the ship was precise enough to
keep a steady phase between both engines. Because of this, the goals for the project were very
ambitious.
However, as in most engineering projects, unexpected delays were encountered. These delays
caused us to postpone some of the goals and ultimately lead to some features being dropped from
the project in order to reach completion in time, namely the MODBUS functionality and the GUI
application.
The original time plan can be seen from the Gantt chart on ﬁgure ??.
Mainly, the process that took the longest amount of time was the implementation of the system
within the dynamic simulation environment. This was due to the difﬁculty in creating an entire
parallel engine system (in an environment designed to simulate a single MPC), as well as creating
the specialized classes for tacho signal generation and simulated engine. The large amount of
spent time on implementing this was justiﬁed by the high importance for veriﬁcation of the system
performance, before the system was allowed on the test wall.
Due to the delays, the time window necessary to perform the sea trials in December had passed,
and with that the Modbus functionality and GUI implementation, as they would only be necessary
for the sea trials.
While the documentation of the project was updated concurrently during development, some time
needed to be set aside for ﬁnalization of the report. Originally, 4-5 weeks were planned this process,
but since the test-wall was available between 24 dec - 2 jan, due to holidays, it was an ideal time
for us to test the ecu software in a realistic environment. Unfortunately, running the software on the
test wall required a great deal of specialized knowledge about the testing system, and since little
help was to be found during the holidays, most of the time was spent debugging the simulation
hardware.
All in all, we feel that we came a long way in the project. We developed a working controller with
almost all of the wanted functionality. We feel conﬁdent that the Synchrophasing system can be
brought to a working state on a ship within a relatively short amount of time, and we hope that
MAN Diesel & Turbo will continue development of the system.
In terms of setting goals and deadlines throughout the creation of this project. Mortan kept an
overview of the daily tasks that needed completion, as well weekly and longer-term goals. With the
regards to the Report, Mortan also layed out the ambitions of the report, structurized the chapters,
and delegated the speciﬁc tasks.

DUAL ENGINE DIFFERENCES 2 
Required accuracy for filtered speed 
As explained in the adaptive filter chapter, tacho readouts are triggered by the primary engine position at fixed 
positions of the crankshaft. Since the adaptive filter needs a fixed number of evenly spaced samples of the engine 
position to estimate the fluctuations in a rotation, both engine speeds need to be synchronized in order to ensure that 
the samples are taken at the right intervals.  
This parameter defines the phase difference threshold where the adaptive filter will be used, when the phase 
difference becomes lower than this parameter, secondary engine speed input will be switched from using the raw 
speed, to using the adaptive filtered speed in calculate the speed difference, allowing for higher accuracy. 
Desired Phase offset 
Allows for selection of the desired phase difference between the two engines (in degrees) 
Speed filter 0,1,2 
Selection of the filter used for the secondary engine speed, this can either be: 
0 
-  raw speed directly from the tacho telegram 
1 
– filtered speed from the adaptive filter implemented in AdaptiveFilterSecondaryengineclass 
2 
– speed with the “simple” low pass filter implemented in SpeedFilterSecondaryEngineClass 
Enable Error Resetting 
Toggle the functionality of resetting the extended phase error. With this parameter enabled, the last calculated (raw) 
phase error will be compared with the unwrapped extended phase error. If there is a disparity, the extended phase 
error will be reset to the raw value.

Defines the numbers of revolutions of phase error tracked by the class, defaults to 4 revolutions e.g -4pi to 4pi

Defines the maximum phase change per calculation of the extended phase error, if the phase error changes more than

Evaluated in “inactive speed diff”, to see if the speed error is small enough to transition to “inactive_timer”.  
Lower hysteresis/upper hysteresis (% of limit value) 
the speed diff limit with added hysterises values are used during “active_timer” and “active”.  
If the speed error exceeds the limits + hysteresis, the state machine will go to inactive_speed_diff.  
Phase error reset limit, lower, upper (degrees) 
Will be checked during active mode, triggering transition to “Phase Error Reset” if the limits are exceeded. 
Reset timer Timeout Value 
Used to define the time spent in the “phase error reset” state. 
activation and de-activation Timeout Value 
Used to define the time spent in the “active_timer” and “inactive_timer” states respectively. 
Gov Speed Error Limit 
Value used for evaluating the speed error of the ECU governor, during “active” and “active_timer”. Will cause a
