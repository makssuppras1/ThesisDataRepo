Abstract
As a result of the growing need for renewable energy-based electricity generation, more
converter-interfaced generation (CIG) units are connected to the power grid. As the
share of CIG units becomes larger and the share of conventional generators smaller, the
CIG units need to ensure a stable and robust power system. This task is now covered
by conventional generators. The method proposed for CIG units to perform this task
is to use grid forming (GFM) control. GFM control directly regulates the voltage and
frequency, whereas the CIG units traditionally follows the frequency and voltage of the
grid formed by conventional generators.
In the investigation of GFM control, the renewable energy source is often neglected.
However, research has suggested that this simplification is inadequate in studying the
stability of GFM CIG.
Therefore, this thesis models a GFM offshore wind power plant (OWPP) considering
turbine dynamics. The OWPP modelled is made up of 30 type IV wind turbine gener-
ators (WTG) with a rating of 14 MVA. The type IV WTG implemented in this project
connects to the electrical grid via a back-to-back converter and a permanent magnet
synchronous generator (PMSG). The rotating masses of the wind turbine are modelled
with a two-mass model. The power plant and the grid side converter controller are mod-
elled in a synchronous reference frame (SRF). The benefit of modelling the electrical
components in an SRF is that the models become linear, making linearisation of the
electrical components a simple task.
The GFM controller used for the GSC is the virtual synchronous machine (VSM), which
replicates the swing equation to generate the reference frequency. Furthermore, a re-
active power droop controller and casceded voltage and current controllers are used to
provide the voltage references. The machine side converter (MSC) regulates the torque
on the rotor based on reference given from the DC link voltage controller. Finally, the
power reference for the GFM controller is the maximum power point tracker (MPPT),
which ensures that the maximum available aerodynamic power is extracted by the wind
turbine.
When simulating grid events in the grid voltage, it is shown that wind turbines have an
oscillatory response with low damping. The oscillations within the drive train are then
reflected back into the electrical grid in the form of power oscillations. These interac-
tions are also confirmed by small signal analysis. The GFM WPP model considering
turbine dynamics is linearised and its state-space model is obtained with the use of
MATLAB/Simulink. With the state space state matrix it is possible to get the eigenval-
ues of the system and evaluate the interaction between states with the participation
factors. The participation factors show that states originating from both the GFM con-
troller and the turbine dynamics participate in the same eigenvalues. With the turbine
dynamics incorporated, it is also possible to show that the GFM WPP is capable of pro-
viding an inertial response. But not a lasting response that brings the frequency back
to its nominal value by itself.

Introduction
In 2015, 196 countries adopted the Paris Agreement, a legally binding international
treaty on climate change. The general goal of the treaty was to limit the increase in the
global average temperature to well below 2°C [1]. Following the adoption of the Paris
Agreement, the three largest economic areas (EU, USA, and China) have since then
within their regions followed up with legislation that binds them to meet the goals of the
Paris Agreement [2]–[4].
The International Energy Agency highlights in its roadmap to net zero emissions by
2050 that a majority of the reductions in greenhouse gas emissions needed will con-
tinue to come from deploying more electricity generation from solar and wind power,
displacing fossil fuels in the power sector [5].
To reach climate neutrality, the EU has declared that it wants 420 GW wind installed
by the year 2030, where the installed capacity in September 2023 was 205 GW [6].
Wind turbines are often installed in large power plants because a single unit has a low
power rating. Furthermore, their performance is location-based, and for wind turbine
generators (WTG), the assumed best location is either on large open fields or at sea.
1.1
Background
The state-of-the-art wind turbine generator (WTG) consists of a synchronous generator
driven by a wind turbine interfaced to the grid through a back-to-back converter coupled
by a DC link. The converter unit is rated at the same capacity as the generator unit. This
type of WTG is described in the industry as a Type IV WTG [7] and shown in Figure
1.1. We refer to turbine, drive train, generator, machine side converter (MSC), and DC
link as the backend, and the converter connected to the grid as the grid side converter
(GSC).

CIG control voltage, frequency, and power through high-frequency switching of semi-
conductors, which have a minimal ability to conduct currents above nominal values,
and the temporary energy storage in converters is often very small. This combination
of characteristics makes CIG able to respond quickly to energy imbalances, but only
for a very short time. Although these characteristics may not be favourable, the ability
to have full control over the generation unit to optimise power production outweighs the
disadvantages.
The typical converter used in CIG is voltage source converters (VSC). However, syn-
chronising a VSC to the grid is considerably different from synchronising a synchronous
generator (SG), which is used in conventional generation, to the grid. An SG’s mechan-
ical torque is physically coupled to the electric signals at the SG terminals. In contrast,
when the generator is connected to the grid through a VSC, the power transferred is
synchronised with the grid voltage signal based on measurements processed in a digital
controller.
One of the highlighted challenges is the effects of replacing synchronous generation
with non-synchronous generation. This results in the loss of inherent mechanical in-
ertia and robust control of voltage and frequency present in the conventional power
system [9], because non-synchronous generation synchronises to the grid digitally and
the mechanical energy is decoupled from the grid. To combat this challenge, research
and development into converter control is needed to ensure that the new generation
units can ensure the same level of system stability as the old generation units. The pro-

1.2
Literature Review
In the literature, two general synchronisation methods for converters can be classified,
grid-following (GFL) control and grid-forming (GFM) control [11]. A GFL converter is
synchronised to the grid by measuring the voltage at the PCC. Based on this measure-
ment, the converter can regulate the currents to achieve the desired active and reactive
power exchange with the grid. A GFM converter aims to directly regulate the PCC volt-
age and frequency based on active and reactive power exchange. Therefore, it is said
that for GFM converters, synchronisation is power-based [11].
Generally, the GFL control is approximated as a controlled current source with a high
parallel impedance, and the GFM control as a voltage source with a low series impedance,
as shown in Figure 1.3. This leads to differences in the response to a grid event. During
a grid event, a GFL converter terminal voltage will change to maintain the injected cur-
rent, however, its response is relatively slow due to the need for a new measurement
of the grid voltage. On the contrary, a GFM converter maintains its terminal voltage,
leading to a near-instantaneous change in the output current [12].

More advanced control techniques have also been used to formulate GFM control meth-
ods, such as model predictive control (MPC) [17] and dispatchable virtual oscillator con-
trol (dVOC) [18], which are multiple input multiple output (MIMO) control techniques.
MPC for power electronics determines the next switching action based on the opti-
mal switching strategy when evaluating N possible switching sequences. The optimal
switching strategy is found by minimising an objective function while including limits
and constraints. The MPC for the GFM control shows great performance in [17], when
it replaces the cascaded inner loop voltage and current controllers.
dVOC builds upon a virtual oscillator control (VOC), which uses a synchronisation
method inspired by coupled oscillators and relies solely on local measurements to gen-
erate the frequency [19]. dVOC then expands on VOC by reformulating the control law
to be able to receive power and voltage set points while maintaining droop character-
istics during small power imbalances [18].
Due to the ambition of regulators to raise WPP to eliminate emissions from electricity
generation [6] and the expectation that WPP will have to implement GFM control [8][10],
studies of GFM control integrated in WPP have received much attention [20]–[23]. In
[20], the fault ride through capabilities of GFM wind turbines are analysed and [21]
investigates GFM control strategies for black start by offshore WPPs. Because WPP
plants are often electrically distant from consumption centres, their connection can be
considered weak. Therefore, [22] analyses a wide variety of GFM control methods for
weakly connected WPP, concluding that among the studied GFM control methods, VSM
shows the best performance for the given conditions. Another aspect to consider is
the limited overcurrent capability of CIG. This is why [23] analyses the implications
of current limits in a GFM WPP. In this analysis [23] also investigates whether a fully
aggregated WPP captures the same instabilities as a WPP aggregated in clusters.

1.3
Problem Statement
In the literature on GFM WPP, the dynamics of the backend in a wind turbine are often
neglected, and studies where they are incorporated show that the backend dynamics
have an influence on stability. Furthermore, studies show that fully aggregated mod-
els of a GFM WPP fail to capture instabilities at expected average operating points.
Therefore, this project aims to extend the research within GFM WPP by analysing the
stability of WPP where backend dynamics are incorporated while utilising vectorised
simulation. As a consequence of the formulated model and the stability of the WPP the
following research questions will be addressed.

1.3.1
Research Questions
• How is a GFM WPP with backend dynamics modelled with a synchronous refer-
ence frame?
• How does the backend of a WTG interact with grid side converter control and
electrical components of a WTG?
• How does backend dynamics affect the GFM operation of WPP during grid events?
By including backend dynamics in studies of GFM WPP it is possible to identify how
well an isolated WPP can maintain stability and how well it can respond to grid faults
without the need for backup storage or flexible AC transmission system components,
which often comes without cost [16]. Thus, maturing the knowledge within GFM control
and contributing to the goal of maintaining a stable power system with robust control,
when deploying more emissions-free CIG.
1.4
Methodology
The type of WPP utilised in this project is an offshore WPP (OWPP). To this end, an
electrical network used in OWPP will be defined. To derive the model of the electrical
network and other electric components, Kirchhoff’s circuit laws will be used. The con-
troller uses voltage and current signals and products thereof. In order to control voltage
and current signals with the classic control theory for SISO systems, the voltage and
current signals are transformed to a synchronous reference frame. Classic control the-
ory is also used to derive the parameters for the GFM controller along with the swing
equation of motion.
To evaluate interactions between the grid and the GFM WPP with backend dynamics,
small signal stability is used. Small signal models are obtained by linearising the electric
network model along with the WTG model. By modelling both the WTG controller and
the electrical grid in a synchronous reference the electrical network equations nonlin-
earities are removed.
To evaluate the effects of incorporating backend dynamics during grid faults, time-
domain simulations in MATLAB/Simulink will be performed. An added benefit of syn-
chronous reference models is that it allows for vectorised simulation, and thus the eval-
uation can happen with a partially aggregated model and uneven wind speeds.
1.5
Scope and Limitations
This project is focused on the electrical dynamics of power systems and therefore also
on the electrical dynamics of the wind turbine. This means that only mechanics that
directly influence electrical dynamics will be considered and not how electrical dynamics
influences the mechanical loads on the wind turbine.

For the control structure of the wind turbine, there are no implemented or tested protec-
tion systems, such as reactive power injection or chopper activation, that are commonly

Due to the primary tasks being to incorporate backend dynamics into a GFM WPP

The high switching frequency nature of the switching elements in CIG will not be con-

Modelling and Validation
Besides the benefits of controlling steady state values, modelling the entire electrical
network in syncrhonous reference frame also has some advantages.
With dq modelling, it is possible to implement vectorised simulations in matlab/simulink,
which is not a possibility when using MATLAB/Specialized Power System components.
E.g, this allows for detailed modelling of individual wind farm strings, making much of
the aggregation process unnecessary. The aggregation process can remove some of
the internal resonant modes within a wind farm or alter their characteristics [26].

Verification of DQ modelling
The verification process consists of implementing both the stationary and synchronous
reference frame models in Simulink and comparing the results.
3.2.1
Phase Shift
Both models are initialised with a wind speed vwind = 0.84 pu and a drive train speed
ωWTR = ωgen = 0.66 pu, which is the minimum rotor speed. This allows the system to
reach a steady state output power at the grid side converter of Pvsc = 0.58 pu, while
maintaining a VDC without large deviations. The response of the two models will be
tested by applying a phase shift in the external grid voltage of −π/40 radians. Here,
the grid angle is moved back in time, meaning it is a negative phase jump. When the
phase jump happens, the converter is ahead of the grid, thus a negative angle shift in
the grid means that the voltage angle difference between the receiving and sending
end becomes larger. Looking at (1.22) this gives a rise in the active power demanded
by the grid. Selected metrics in the GFM-WTG module are shown in figure 3.2.
The resulting power mismatch in the VSM controller causes a rapid power increase,
after which the controller acts to resynchronize and settles at the angle that gives the
desired reference power again shown in figures 3.4a and 3.4b. In the DC link the in-
creased power output at the grid side converter causes a drop in DC voltage, shown
in figure 3.2c, where the DC voltage controller responds by increasing the power from
the machine side. This translates into an increase in electric torque in the generator
in the wind turbine causing the rotor to slow down. Although the rotational speed de-
crease is minimal, the increased torque induces electromechanical oscillations in the
wind turbine drive train, shown in Figure 3.4d. Because the oscillations are still present,
the MPPT output is also oscillating, which becomes visible in the GSC output power.

Small Signal Analysis
To perform the sensitivity analysis of the eigenvalues to the state of the system and
selected system parameters, the DQ-model is linearised. For the same reasons that
power system signals are regulated in a synchronous reference frame (SRF), when
modelling entire electrical networks in SRF, all component models are linear. The non-
linear modules are the transformation operators, power measurements, and drive train
and aerodynamic models, which need to be linearised. Due to the model’s complexity
and scale the linearised modules will be implemented in Simulink and with the help of
the MATLAB Control System Toolbox their state-space representation will be obtained.

and measurement modules, followed by linearisation of the backend dynamics. Lastly,

4.3
Sensitivity Analysis
With the small signal model it also possible to analyse the variation due to specific
parameters. The sensitivity to a specific parameter changes the eigenvalues, and by
plotting the eigenvalues, it can be determined whether the changes in a parameter
provide more damping or enhance oscillatory behaviour.
4.3.1
Turbine Inertia
The first parameter to test is the inertia of the turbine rotor. Although it is more com-
mon to control parameters instead of physical values, the turbine rotor provides some
information on how the size of the turbine affects the system. When the dimensions of
the wind turbine were introduced in section 2.2, some values were taken from [42] and
some values from [28]. The main difference between the two references is the size of
the WTG, [42] is 15 MW and [28] is 5 MW. Figure 4.3 shows the change in eigenvalues,
when the rotor inertia is increased from that of [28] towards the values mentioned in
[42].
The results of Figure 4.3 show that as HWTR is increased the eigenvalues closest to
the right half plane move closer to it. The result is a longer decay, but as the complex
eigenvalues move closer to the real axis, the frequency of the oscillatory response
decreases. The aforementioned eigenvalues correlate with λ71,72 and λ74. The other
set of eigenvalues shown in Figure 4.3 corresponds to λ73, which is dominated by the
GFM controller. The effect of the higher turbine rotor inertia is a more damped response.
This can be interpreted as the GFM controller becomes less sensitive to system events,
as the turbine rotor inertia increases.

5
Discussion and Conclusion
5.1
Discussion
This section summarises the analysis performed and the results obtained and dis-
cusses them with respect to the methods used in the thesis.
5.1.1
OWPP Dimensions
Choosing to implement a WPP with a very long export cable inherently provided a weak
connection to the grid of each wind turbine. The export cable is also dimensioned at a
length and capacity, which would most likely be impractical. In practice, the length limit
of a 220 kV AC cable compensated at both ends is 110 km [48].
Although vectorised programming was not used in the analysis, the methodology for
aggregating the collector network has proven useful in other works with vectorised pro-
gramming [23]. An intermediate result of the aggregation methodology is the aggre-
gated string impedance. Thus, by not moving on to aggregated paralellel strings, the
aggregated string impedance is readily available.
5.1.2
Converter controller
With the implementation of cascaded inner-loop controllers, there was an opportunity
to implement specific current limits on the references. As it was not a focus point of
the thesis, current limiting controls were omitted. However, it was observed whether
the current magnitude at the converter terminals rose to unacceptable limits during
simulation.
During the simulations, the inner-loop controllers performed well with the chosen tuning
strategy. A common method to select PI controller gains for LC converter filter is to use
the symmetric optimum method [13]. The symmetric optimum method provides an ana-
lytic solution to obtain stable controller gains. Furthermore, it is also common to provide
feed forward terms, which were not found necessary in this system. Feed forward terms
are often seen to provide more controllability and better disturbance rejection [13].
The GFM controllers, the reactive power droop and the VSM are two simple methods to
implement GFM capabilities. They provide the core functionalities that describe the volt-
age and frequency of the network. Although more sophisticated GFM control strategies
have been proposed, a WPP is of considerable size and often has centralised control.
Therefore, it could be beneficial to use simple control strategies, for which the dynamics
are well known. A reason why modified VSM control strategies for CIG is needed could
be to better use the fast response that converters are capable of.

5.1.3
DC link
The component interfacing the GSC and the MSC is the DC link capacitor. It provides a
small energy storage, but due to the high integral gain of the controller that regulates the
DC link voltage, the energy storage is not utilised in this project. The effect of the high
integral gain is that the DC link never deviates a lot, almost instantaneously translating
the active power effects on the grid side to the machine side. A major drawback of high
integral gain is the risk of instability. However, during the tuning process an objective
was to have enough phase margin, and instabilities from the DC-link were not seen.
5.1.4
Wind Turbine Generator Model
The WTG model can be divided into three parts; the aerodynamic model, the turbine
controllers, and the drive train. Due to the differences in references used for dimension-
ing the three parts, synergy between them could not be guaranteed. However, when a
time series of wind speed steps was applied, the performance resembled results found
in the literature [45]. The WTG performance showed an aggressive pitch controller,
which could have been tuned to have less overshoot. Since the pitch controller also
functions as a safety measure, its aggressive tuning was considered acceptable.
A great simplification was made by using the reference signal for the MSC as the elec-
trical torque on the turbine generator. Electrical connections between MSC and gener-
ator are possible to model. However, these dynamics were not of interest in this project,
while not disregarding that they could have an influence.
5.1.5
WPP Models considering Turbine Dynamics
With the simplification of the MSC the integration of the WTG into the converter model,
both the abc and the DQ WPP model could be evaluated. Both models showed the same
behaviour during the applied phase shift and voltage sag events in the grid voltage. In
each case, it was visible that the dynamics of the turbine and drive train were also seen
as oscillations on the grid side.
During the ROCOF event, it could be seen that a WPP with an MPPT providing the ref-
erence for the GFM controller, could not sustain the necessary power ouput during the
frequency deviation. It revealed that the controller combination is capable of providing
the inertial response but not the power to maintain the frequency. The simulation also
showed how the response was without considering turbine dynamics, where the WPP
was able to maintain the power output.
5.1.6
Eigenvalue Analysis
The linearised DQ model shows acceptable response when compared to the nonlin-
ear model. Differences may be attributed to the question of whether the disturbance
used for the testing is of a small signal nature or not. Due to the limited availability

of wind turbine data, the linearisation of the highly nonlinear coefficients used in the
aerodynamic model, may also contribute to difference between the nonlinear and the
linearised model when comparing their response to a disturbance.
However, when using the linearised model for eigenvalue analysis, it revealed that
both states originating from the WTG and the GFM controller participate in eigenvalues
together. This result amplifies the importance of considering turbine dynamics when
analysing GFM WPPs.
In the sensitivity analysis it is revealed how increasing either virtual inertia or turbine
rotor inertia, decreases the drive train system damping. Furthermore, the sensitivity
to the SCR has also been shown to decrease overall damping of the system. These
results are in line with the analysis of the single machine infinite bus system in [30] and
the derivation of the GFM controller in section 2.1.4
The sensivity analysis can potentially also be used to determine how plausible it has
been to use a relatively small inertia constant for the turbine rotor. However, no signif-
icant changes were observed in the eigenvalues. Based on the eigenvalue sensitivity
to the rotor inertia constant, it would be expected that an increase in rotor inertia would
cause larger oscillations in the grid side during faults.
A missing point in the sensitivity analysis is the validation of the conclusion made based
on the parameter variations. The typical method is to vary the same parameters during
nonlinear time domain simulations.

5.2
Conclusion
A common simplification when analysing the stability of GFM CIG is to neglect the
nature of the energy source. It has been proven that the dynamics of the energy source
has an influence on the stability, this thesis has modelled a GFM WPP while considering
the turbine dynamics.
In Chapter 2, the WPP dimensions are outlined and the GFM converter and backend
dynamics are modelled. The backend comprises the DC link, MSC, drive train and
aerodynmics. In addition, the controllers for the turbine are introduced. All parts are
combined to model the entire WPP and it is connected to an external grid through a
dimensionable grid impedance. When modelling GFM WPP with backend dynamics a
clear strategy for distributing controller responsibilities is needed. Since a GFM CIG unit
needs to respond to grid events inherently, it is decided that the MSC gets its power
reference from the DC-link voltage controller and the GSC receives its power reference
from the MPPT. A side effect from this distribution of roles, is that the turbine dynamics
become readibly available at the GSC, becuse the MPPT uses the rotor angular speed
as input.
The components of the model relying on sinusoidal signals are then transformed to an
SRF in Chapter 3. These components are WPP electrical components as well as the
controller for the converter. The backend dynamics are used as is. The SRF model is
tested against the stationary reference frame model by evaluating their responses to
phase shift and voltage sags. The results are two highly identical models, only seeing
a steady state offset in the converter reactive power. Another result of the simulation
performed is that it is verified that during grid events, the drive train experience oscil-
lation, which can be seen in the converter’s active and reactive power. Another grid
event that is performed is a ROCOF, resembling a load event. This simulation reveals
that the GFM WPP is able to respond initially with inertia and synchronise at the new
frequency, but not keep an increased power output for longer periods if necessary.
In addition to the fact that modelling electrical networks in an SRF gives the possibility
for vectorised programming, in Chapter 4 SRF modelling is used in this thesis for small
signal analysis, as electrical models in an SRF are inherently linear. The linear model is
used to obtain a quantitative measure of how much the GFM controls and the backend
dynamics interact. By finding the eigenvalues of the linear model, the participation fac-
tors can be used to show how much different states participate in an eigenvalue. The
participation factors show that there indeed is an interaction between GFM controller
states and turbine states.
This thesis has shown how to model a GFM WPP in a SRF while considering the dy-
namics of the wind turbine. The result confirms that there are interactions between the
GFM GSC and the backend dynamics. Furthermore, it also found that in a ROCOF
grid event, the turbine dynamics can enforce constraints on the WPP to have GFM
capabilities.

5.2.1
Future Work
The model developed and the framework outline for system analysis provides a good
basis for further analysis regarding GFM WPP when considering turbine dynamics. The
imidiate work not in this report is the validation of the small signal sensitivity analysis by
using the parameter variation in nonlinear time domain simulations. Furthermore, the
small signal analysis was performed only at one operating point. As the wind turbine
rotor speed increases and decreases depending on the operating point, performing
the linearisation at different operating points can provide a clearer view on when the
dynamics of the machine side and the grid side interact.
In this work, the GFM WPP showed that it performed well under voltage sag and phase
shift events. A more concerning issue is whether a GFM WPP can support the power
system and maintain its stability under ROCOF events. These types of events really
puts the turbine under pressure, as they ideally should deliver more power. However,
this not a possibility. The model in this work can be used to test different strategies to
provide grid support while not depleting the energy in the wind turbine rotor. Moreover,
a clear solution on how to control both the GSC and MSC is needed. As could be seen
from the ROCOF event performed in this thesis, the MPPT failed to go back to prefault
levels. A possible solution could be to make a spinning reserve mandatory for all GFM
WPP.
A driving factor behind all decisions is the monetary value of the strategy selected to
operate a WPP. A necessary investigation when researching opportunities where re-
newable energy is curtailed is if it makes less money for the operator. Besides the sta-
bility analysis, an investigation into the operating cost of choosing one control strategy
over the other should be evaluated.
