Abstract
Reshoring has an upward trend worldwide since the industry sector presents an in-
creasingly number of companies which are implementing offshoring and then, reshoring.
The purpose of this thesis is to provide some recommendations for companies which
have in mind either to offshore or reshore. In order to do this, it is essential to iden-
tify which factors were taken into account when offshoring and which ones were not
considered as it should that caused reshoring. There is a gap between the reasons why
a company decides to offshore and then, after a while, decides to come back to the
same or closer country. The research gap is tackled through the use of case studies
within the reshoring topic choosing two Danish companies as a sample.
It has been quite difficult to obtain information since reshoring is an intense topic
that can damage to the corporate image of the companies. For this reason, apart from
all the reshoring literature gathered from research papers and books, the information
extracted from the interviews and corporate documentation have high value. Once
all the information was gathered, it has been selected two methods to analyse the
reshoring cases to be able to obtain some patterns looking at the missing factors and
the factors considered when offshoring and reshoring.
The Knoster model for managing complex change has been used to analyse the start-
ing point for reshoring. It has been used to deduct which could be the missing main
factors when the offshoring strategy was implemented. Then, the Systems Dynamics
Method has been used to visually analyse the relation between the primary factors
and the secondary factors. The main factors are used to be the same the companies
take into consideration when the risks analysis is performed. However, most of them
(included the two cases presented in this thesis) misunderstand the complexity of
the process and do not value the secondary factors as much as are needed. In some
cases, the secondary factors can influence the facts changing the trend of the situation
and creating a turning point in the strategy implemented. Due to this fact, lots of
companies implementing ofsshoring had to reconsider the first decision and change it
completely.
Finally, this thesis aims to provide some recommendations to take into account when
a new offshoring and reshoring strategy is going to be implemented. Companies which
are planning to offshore or reshore could be interested in take into consideration these

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
In recent years, Western firms started sending manufacturing work abroad to low-cost
countries on a large scale. This was mainly due to pressures for low cost production,
and secondly for the need of shorter time to market/fast innovation and access to
new markets [1]. By the 90s, this offshoring trend was well established, resulting in
companies with increasingly more globally disposed manufacturing activities [2]. The
movement was overwhelmingly in one direction: away from rich countries to places
where overall production expenses were much cheaper [3].
Deciding where to locate manufacturing facilities in an international context is widely
considered a key strategic decision that has a wide-ranging impact on the business
as a whole [4][5][6]. The international location decision (ILD) of manufacturing is a
macro-level decision made by a firm to pinpoint the geographic location of a partly or
fully owned facility from a set of international alternatives. It consists of six categories
of factors which are cost, labour and social characteristics, infrastructure, politics and
regulations, economics, and markets and resources [7].
Nevertheless, some offshore projects do not reach most of the goals expected and tend
to be unsuccessful [8]. Some of those causes are that more overhead causes higher
costs [9], labour forces are as quickly lost as acquired [10], new entrants from the
offshore countries increase competition [11], quality is not as high as intended [12],
and projects take more time than was expected [13].
Thus far, however, little discussion exists about why these plans for offshoring are at
times reversed and not well-predicted and companies have to invest money moving
back to the origin country, e.g. reshoring. Was offshoring never the correct decision
or has factors changed, in the offshore location or at home, which makes moving
activities back more attractive?
Not long ago, reshoring has become an increasingly important option for manufac-
turing firms. Reshoring is defined as the decision to relocate production or supply
previously offshored back to the firm’s home country [14]. Therefore, it can be viewed
as the reverse decision of a previous decision to offshore, it can refer to all or only a

part of previously offshored activities and finally, it is irrespective of the ownership
mode in the offshore country [2][15][16][17].
The concept of reshoring is quite diffused. The definition of “both large multination-
als companies and small enterprises operating in different industries have decided to
reverse their previous manufacturing offshoring decisions and have brought their pro-
duction activities back home” has often been referred to as manufacturing reshoring,
although other terms have been used as well (e.g., backshoring, back-sourcing) [18].
Besides, this term is often adopted to indicate different concepts, either as an ad-
justment to changed location advantages, or as a correction of a previous erroneous
location choice [15][5].
Generally, as for any new and multifaceted concept, with lack of clarity about “what”
production relocation to the home country is, and “which forms” it can take. It is
relevant that these definitions be bought to unity in order to remove potential lexical
ambiguities [16].
As it was aforementioned, the term “backshoring” was the first academic definition
proposed by Holz, 2009 [19]: “the geographic relocation of a functional, value creating
operation from a location abroad back to the domestic country of the company”. In [5]
backshoring is used to mean the “re-concentration of parts of production from own
foreign locations as well as from foreign suppliers to the domestic production site
of the company”. The former description points out that firms can repatriate only
part of their foreign production, however, the latter definition extends the concept to
include also the repatriation of production activities of foreign suppliers.
Nevertheless, there is a lack of research on why companies have to re-visit their
offshoring decisions and end up reshoring. For this reason, there is an important
research gap between the reasons why a company decides to offshore and then, after
a while, decides to come back to the origin country. There are a large number of
papers that explain the reasons and the decisions taken by a company to offshore
its production [20]. However, there is no much information about the reasons why
offshoring might not be successful and which data managers should consider when
offshoring.
The fact that reshoring is a reversal of a decision of offshoring, indicates a dynamic
element to this process.
Therefore, in this thesis it will be investigated the type
of data in connection with reshoring decisions in more depth. Dynamic data refers
to the use of knowledge about the behaviour of the mining algorithm in order to
choose a sample size [21]. Looking to this data, managers could have a much broader
vision when deciding to offshore since it is crucial to assess the information over
time when making managerial decisions. For instance, companies should not gloss
over cultural implications, and could obtain the percentage of quality satisfaction in
low-cost manufacturing countries in the automotive sector.

Static data, on the other hand, is obtained by applying some fixed criterion in which
the context is not involved [21]. An example is the calculation of the number of sales
in a specific moment. This type of information does not include the context in which
the company is involved, as could be the country or the political situation.
1.2
Problem Statement
In the last few years, both large multinational companies and small enterprises op-
erating in different industries have decided to reverse their previous manufacturing
offshoring decisions and have brought their production activities back home [18].
Interest in manufacturing reshoring rose initially among practitioners; more recently
it has gained momentum among scholars [14] and policy makers [22][23][24]. In light
of the rapidly increasing amount of publications on the topic, literature reviews have
been recently conducted, though they have only been taking into account the motiva-
tions driving the phenomenon [2]. A broader and more “comprehensive” examination
of the extant knowledge is currently missing [18].
Much of the existing body of reshoring literature has focused on why firms reshore
[5][25]. It arguably treats the reshoring decision as a discrete event; it typically focuses
on a snapshot in time and on an ex-post analysis of what drove a firm to repatriate
[25]. Most of the literature on reshoring is thus far conceptual. Only a very limited
number of empirical studies have been conducted, with the most prominent being
survey-based [26]. While there are some recent case study examples [27][28], there
is a need for more in-depth case work to further the understanding of the reshoring
process [29].
In addition to filling the literature gap about the concept of reshoring, the analysis
and systematic classification of motivations for reshoring is of relevance for managers.
A better understanding of the drivers that lead companies to repatriate their off-
shore operations can help managers to address the “location decision dilemma” more
effectively [14].
1.3
Purpose and Research Questions
Previous Sections 1.1 and 1.2 briefly pointed out some of the gaps about the term
reshoring and its fundamental causes. Therefore, the purpose of this qualitative study
is to analyse the factors that were not take into account when offshoring and then,
caused reshoring. As it was mentioned before, in some cases reshoring is considered
as a discrete event, but actually, is not.
The phenomenon of reshoring could be

of a previous erroneous location choice [15][5].
The outcome of this study is to provide some recommendations to companies that

Therefore, this thesis seeks to address the research gap mentioned above by answering

1.4
Thesis Structure
This thesis is structured as follows. First, a theoretical framework is presented, show-
ing the latest research on corporate reshoring trends and the definition of relevant
terms for the case.
All of this theory is extracted from the papers and books to
provide an overview of the term reshoring and to show how far the existing theory
is currently explaining. Then, the research methodology is introduced in which the
Research Onion Model is used to present an structured way to solve the research
problem. It also includes the data collection, data analysis and data validation. After
that, the following section shows the empirical findings, that presents an in-depth
explanation about the data found regarding each of the two case studies and the way
it has been analysed in order to make a decision to offshore and then reshore. Then,
an analysis is performed in which the Knoster model for managing complex change
and Systems Dynamics Method are applied in order to draw conclusions about the
information used or not used in each case when making a decision of reshore. These
two methods are not specifically extracted from the reshoring literature, but I have
considered that are the most adequate to analyse the two company cases. For these
reason, they are not included in the theoretical framework.
Finally, some conclu-
sions are provided in which, at the end general recommendations are pointed out to
companies involved in offshoring and reshoring processes.

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical Framework
The methodology of this thesis is a structured literature review [31]. This methodol-
ogy results in a base data of existing and relevant scientific literature about reshoring
and important related terms, such as offshoring, insourcing and outsourcing. This
information of secondary sources is then analysed following an inductive process, for
the identification of themes and patterns in the data, with the aim of constructing a
list of recommendations, which might be generalizable to other cases not included in
this research [32].
This Section points out the theoretical foundation in which the thesis is based on.
First, the concept of reshoring is presented and causes of why reshoring is an im-
portant fact for the companies are provided. Then, some recent studies that outline
reshoring are commented. It is remarkable that scholars have paid far too little atten-
tion to the reasons of why reshoring appears and has an upward trend in the present
years.
2.1
Reshoring as a Concept
Reshoring [15][16][14] is a company’s location decision which refers to the relocation
to the home country of formerly offshored production or service activities based on
some premises [2][17]:
• It is the reverse decision of a previous decision to offshore.
• It can refer to all or only a part of previously offshored activities.
• It is irrespective of the ownership mode in the offshore country.
Besides, a great part of the scholars point out that the concept of Reshoring can
also be further differentiated into Backshoring and Nearshoring [2].
Backshoring
is the decision to partially or fully relocate value creation to the home country of
the firm’s headquarters [14][5]. The term Backshoring specifically refers to moving
task production all the way back to the home country [33].
Nearshoring denotes
repatriating value creation tasks from the foreign host country to a location closer to,
but not within, the borders of the home country[14].
==PAGE:22==
8
2 Theoretical Framework
However, the term Reshoring is agnostic as to whether the manufacturing being
brought home occurred in a wholly owned facility in an offshore location or in the
factory of an offshore supplier. Reshoring as such, is fundamentally concerned with
where manufacturing are to be performed, independent of who is performing the man-
ufacturing activities in question [15]. Therefore, four possible manifestations or types
of reshoring (see Figure 2.1): (a) in-house reshoring, in which a firm fulfils demand
in its local market by relocating manufacturing activities being performed in wholly
owned offshore facilities back to wholly owned facilities; (b) reshoring for outsourc-
ing, in which a firm fulfils demand in its local market by relocating manufacturing
activities being performed in wholly owned offshore facilities back to onshore suppli-
ers; (c) reshoring for insourcing, in which a firm fulfils demand in its local market
by relocating manufacturing activities being performed by offshore suppliers back to
wholly owned onshore facilities and (d) outsourced reshoring, in which a firm fulfils
demand in its local market by relocating manufacturing activities being performed
by offshore suppliers back to onshore suppliers [15].
Figure 2.1: Reshoring options. Source: Gray et al. 2013 [15].
On the other hand, one of the most common topics in reshoring studies is based on
the identification and analysis of the reason firms decide to repatriate manufacturing
activities.
While the vast array of motivations identified in the literature suggest that reshoring
decisions can originate for several reasons, some authors have argued they can be
ultimately intended as either a deliberate strategy or a reaction or a reaction to off-
shoring failure [34]. This view of reshoring combines two different interpretations of

reshoring proposed in the extant literature, i.e., either a mere correction of a prior mis-
judged decision [15][5] or a deliberate response to exogenous or endogenous changes
[14][27]. Grandinetti (2015) [35], specifically referred to changes in a firm’s business
strategy consistent with the idea that reshoring is “more than just a geographical shift
of operations. It is also a reconfiguration of systems” [36]. In this respect, it must
be noted that while manufacturing offshoring decisions are often motivated by cost
elements (especially the labour ones)[37], reshoring strategies seem to be undertaken
also on the base of strategic elements, such as “made in effect”, vicinity among RD,
engineering and production, responsiveness to customer demand [14].
2.2
Reshoring Process
Benstead et al. [25] provide a framework comprised of three key elements: reshoring

the implementation considerations can be determinant on the process as well as the

2.2.1
Reshoring Drivers
Firstly, the reshoring drivers are the factors that motivate a firm to reshore, thereby
reversing a prior location decision. This may include changes, misjudgements, draw-
backs and unexpected consequences of being located in the foreign country; advan-
tages to be gained by being located in the domestic country; and pressures to reverse
the location decision [25].
This thesis divides the drivers behind the reshoring decision into four categories:
(a) Risk, uncertainty and easy of doing business; (b) Cost-related; (c)Infrastructure-
related; (d) and competitive priorities.
The first category includes the drivers that motivate a firm to move onshore to re-
duce risk, uncertainty and allow efficient operations to be run in the domestic location.
There can be a drive to reshore to reduce the supply chain disruption risk that is in-
herent to dispersed and extended global networks [38][17].
The cultural distance
between offshore and domestic locations can also make it difficult to operate offshore
and incentivise reshoring [15][17]. Meanwhile, reshoring has been linked to offshore
legislation that complicates business transactions and operations [27]. Unpredictable
global economic conditions can influence reshoring; Kinkel (2012), [26] suggested that
companies are more likely to re-concentrate production domestically when the global

economy is unstable. A related factor is currency exchange rates and variability. The
strengthening of the Chinese currency, for example, has increased the cost of imports
[27][39], while the risk of currency fluctuations increases the appeal of domestic lo-
cations [17][40]. Reshoring can also be motivated by a desire to reduce the risk of
environmental and social issues [15][17][41], which can impact firm reputation.
Secondly, although the reshoring decision is dependent on multiples variables, costs
that were unexpected, hidden or greater than expected can make a domestic loca-
tion more attractive. Labour costs are a key issue with a closing wage gap between
domestic and offshore locations [42][43]. Additionally, labour productivity improve-
ments may also be available in the domestic location and offset any remaining foreign
wage differentials [42][17][44]. Meanwhile, a desire to reduce duty costs can influence
reshoring. In addition, domestic production lowers transportation costs [38][17] while
attractive energy prices have been highlighted as lowering both transportation costs
and non-labour production costs [42].
According to Maloca (2009)[5] and Kinkel (2012) [26], the high coordination and
monitoring costs of offshore locations that increase overheads (e.g. travel costs) is a
fact, particularly for captive offshore firms [45]. Offshoring can also result in higher
working capital/pipeline costs, including high inventories in transit or distribution
centres [17]. Martínez-Mora and Merino (2014) [27] highlighted how switching to
domestic suppliers can create smaller, more frequent orders that reduce inventory
costs. Finally, Zanker (2013)[46] found that reshoring improves domestic capacity
utilisation thus lowering overheads. This may be particularly relevant to firms that
have retained a domestic presence when offshoring.
Then, the third category of reshoring drivers is the infrastructure-related drivers. The
motivation for firms to reshore is based on the possibility to obtain an improved infras-
tructure in the domestic location of if there are issues in the offshored infrastructure.
Several authors identified on-site infrastructure issues offshore as a potential reshoring
driver [5][46]. Firms can find it difficult to establish a reliable raw material supply
network offshore [5]. Meanwhile, it should be highlighted the influence skilled hu-
man resource availability can take in the reshoring process. This encompass a lack of
availability offshore [43][29] and concerns about the deskilling of domestic labour due
to extensive offshoring [38]. Indeed, it is noted that high doestic unemployment and
union pressure is also driving reshoring [17][14]. Finally, the discussion about the ben-
efits of the automated machinery use in factories are broad. While some researchers
[33] argued that the increase reliance in this type of machinery could negate higher
onshore labour costs, Dachs and Kinkel (2013) [47] point out that there are several
benefits like the increase of the product quality and the flexibility of automation.
Finally, companies can be motivated to reshore in pursuit of competitive priorities
which are frequently non-cost related. Survey evidence has highlighted that reshoring
can improve flexibility and dependability [5][26][46], reducing the risk of late delivery

Implementation Considerations
The second main factor composing the reshoring process is the implementation con-

approached; that facilitate reshoring; and steps to be taken to smoothly embed the
activity back in the domestic location [25].

needs to be considered [14].
On the contrary, Bailey and De Propris [38] discussed barriers that must be overcome
to operationalise the reshoring decision, including gaining access to labour and finance.
Besides, Shih [54] emphasized the importance of in-house training to improve skills
and employee retention. The author also recommended building strong relationships
with suppliers and improving information sharing with suppliers.

Research by Murat (2013)[56] suggests that three main levels of offshoring can be dis-

determinants are mentioned as “push factors”. Examples could be the importance
of intellectual property, cultural and communication issues, additional costs related

a stronger supply chain and better-quality control are some examples that are more
present.
The next dimension is the risk assessment. Kumar et al. (2009) [57] shows in their
research different risk that should in analysed; (1) Operational risk, (2) Strategic risk,
(3) Compliance risk, (4) Reputation risk, (5) Conformity risk, (6) Country risk and,
(7) Financial risk.
Finally, the last step consists of pointing out which were the benefits of the previous
offshoring. Examples could be the friendly governmental policy and legislation, the
access to new technologies and knowledge and the access to new markets among
others.
Then, the new decision has to be made. Depending on the main benefits and hurdles
resulting from the initial offshoring process, firms can decide to (a) further offshore
part of their production activities, (b) maintain the part of offshored activities at a
constant level, or, (c) reshore part or all of the previously offshored manufacturing
activities [56].
Regarding the offshoring, according to Bellego (2014)[58], three different strategies
can be implemented. The tactical reshoring consists of looking for the best interna-
tional locations for high added-value activities. The development reshoring is done
by companies after considering the total costs of offshoring to low-wage countries.
These companies return to their home countries to more upmarket goods. And the
last strategy is home reshoring which consists of doing the reshoring in the home
country by companies following a launch phase in a low-wage country. This strategy
is usually implemented by companies that have been disappointed by their previous
offshoring experience.

Offshoring
Much is at present being said about reshoring, however, offshoring has not been deeply
defined yet.
According to McKnight (2012)[66], the term offshoring is defined as the relocation of
the whole factory to another country. The function is still performed in-house, but
in another country with operational or economic advantages such as cheaper wages
or legal advantages. Therefore, it can be said that offshoring is the execution of a
task or work in a country different to where the firm’s headquarters are located [67].
However, the management and ownership are kept in-house [68].
The phenomenon of offshoring has and continues to receive increasing attention
[69].The start of the term itself took place in the early 1990s under the guise of
re-engineering and later in the early 2000s, in a similar trend of reshaping business
processes with the goal of reducing costs [70]. Today, organizations try to reduce
costs by transferring low-skills jobs to low cost or low-wage countries [71].
Increasingly complex transactions which require a higher degree of qualification are

2.3.2
Outsourcing
The basic definition of outsourcing is to source materials or services from outside of
the company [76]. Over the years, new outsourcing concepts arose and the definition
was extended.
According to McKnight (2012)[66], the adapted definition of outsourcing is the act
of having another company performing a specific, limited function, which was per-
formed in-house before. Hereby it is important to mention that when a company is
outsourcing a task, the ownership and control will be transferred to a third party
[33].
However, Srinivasan (2004) [77] has two different perspectives of outsourcing. On
the one hand, he states that in the 1980’s it was referring to firms expanding their
purchases of inputs from outside the company rather than making them internal. On
the other hand, he points out that since 2004, the meaning of outsourcing changed
and is now referring to a specific segment, which is the growing international trade.
So, it can be mentioned that there has been a change in the nature of the concept of
outsourcing and so, a change in its understanding within the business world.

Insourcing

been outsourced. Osborne (2016) [78] confirms this definition adding that insourcing
is an activity which is performed in-house instead of being handed over an outside
party.
It should be added that the ownership and control are going back to the
company when performing insourcing [33].

Research Validation
Once all the research has been done, for the proper validation of qualitative studies,
the triangulation method can be applied. Validity, in qualitative research, refers to
whether the findings of a study are true and certain; “true” in the sense that research
findings accurately reflect the situation, and “certain” in the sense that research
findings are supported by the evidence [91].
Triangulation is a way of increasing
confidence in research data, creating innovative ways of understanding a phenomenon
and revealing unique findings [92]. Using interviews for example, is a way of increasing
the utility of the results.
Four types of triangulation are usually used in the literature [93][94]: Data triangu-
lation, Investigator triangulation, Theory or Theoretical triangulation and Method
or Methodological triangulation. However, in [91] a fifth method is described, the
Environmental triangulation.
• Data Triangulation entails the collection of data from different people or sources,
aiming to gain multiple perspectives. The mentioned data can vary from inter-
views, observations and written material to photographs, videos or music doc-
uments. The observations and personal notes taken during the interviews are
also a way of triangulating data. These observations are not limited to verbal
activities [93], but non-verbal language, body gestures, surroundings, etc, can
also be taken into consideration.
• Investigator Triangulation involves two or more researchers to interpret the
same study and have different points of view, and draw some conclusions. It is
a complex triangulation because it takes a lot of effort. The best way is to work
in a team [93].
• Theoretical Triangulation uses different perspectives or theories to analyse and
interpret the data. In this triangulation, different theories or hypotheses can
assist the researcher in supporting or refuting findings [94].
• Methodological Triangulation involves the use of multiple methods to study a
specific problem. It is usually used in qualitative methods such as interviews.
The use of several methods lower the possible bias produced by a single method.
If the conclusions from each of the methods used in the triangulation are the
same, then validity is established [91].
• Environmental Triangulation involves the use of different locations, settings,
and other key factors related to the environment in which the study took place,
such as the time, day, or season [91].
Some environmental factors may be
influencing the information received. Making a sensitivity analysis and varying
some of them, one could check which factors affect more to the study.

5.1
Knoster Model for Managing Complex Change
5.1.1
The Model Explanation
The Knoster model for managing complex changes, also called VIRSA model, con-
sists of a five-part approach to organizational change management aimed to provide
guidance for those companies undertaking a service management journey, as it can
be observed in Figure 5.1.
Many companies face the organizational change by getting staff trained on the new
tools but most of them do not take into consideration that the work of service man-
agement is an ongoing endeavour.
Frameworks and methodologies for service management have been around for years,
however many organizations are failing when applying them. This phenomenon is
usually caused by the fact that companies usually mistake the tool or the training
and miss the most important part, the people properly aligned in the process.
Most of the companies experimenting this issue follow the same pattern. They change
the technology and the process by applying a new methodology and then, they start
the purchasing of a new tool that is verified to work with the processes of that
methodology. However, even though they expect to success in this process, the truth
is that after large sums of money and lots of hours of time, these organizations are
==PAGE:66==
52
5 Analysis
not providing adequate value to the business, they are not producing real customer
satisfaction and they are experiencing the same issues they had before deciding that
a new methodology would help.
The conclusion is that service management is not something it can be bought, it
should be implemented in the correct way. In order to have success, it should be
aligned with the culture of the organization; the way people act and behave.
Figure 5.1: Knoster model for managing complex change. Source: Knoster et al.
2000 [95].
The VIRSA model is composed by five key features that if they are perfectly accom-
plished the successful change is produced. These are the vision, incentives, resources,
skills and action plan. When one of each factor is missing, the outcomes differ.
• Missing vision: The outcome is confusion; people is confused about the direction
and reasons of the change process.
• Missing incentives: The outcome is resistance; people is reluctant to the change
that has no clear benefits for them.
• Missing resources: The outcome is frustration; people do not have the where-
withal to make the change.
• Missing skills: The outcome is anxiety; people do not have or feel that they do
not have the abilities needed to fulfill the change.
• Missing action plan: The outcome is false starts; people do not know the route
of the change plan.

5.1.1.1
Vision
Knoster et al. [95] states that vision can be defined as the imagined view of the end
state of change. As a view of the outcome of the specific organizational change under
consideration, it should be general enough that it does not hinge on small details, but
specific enough that it does not imply universal applicability.
In order to define an adequate vision, it should be attainable with consistent and
directed effort and realistic but challenging to be clear and easily communicated.
Therefore, an effective vision must provide a perspective, an ambition of how the
people in the organization should operate to fulfill the shared company goal.
The vision for organizational change can begin anywhere in the organization, but to
be successful, it must be shared by those capable of driving the change [95]. Therefore,
the initiative could arrive from any person working for the organization no matter
what position the employee has, but the important is to be able to transmit this vision
to the management layer, who are the ones capable of pushing for the initiative. In
general, the wider the change, the higher in the organization it must be developed
and adopted [95].
Once the change leader is defined having the appropriate standing within the organi-
zation, he/she should be fully involved in the project and transmit this involvement
to his/her team. The road map should be clear and organized so as to the project
team has defined instructions to work on. The project manager should approve each
of the steps of the change project to be sure that are completed as expected.

When the vision for organizational change is missing, the result is confusion.This is
represented by Figure 5.2. The project team needs to understand the reason for the
change announced and the next steps. If those two failed, the team stars to create
different interpretations for the change and to modify the desired outcome of the
organizational change.
5.1.1.2
Incentives
Incentives occupy the second position in the model since after the definition of the
vision, the people involved on it want to know what’s in it for them. It is important
to not confuse incentives with rewards. The former are enticements to accomplish,
however, the latter are given for accomplishments [95].

The types of incentives required to produce cultural change are those that impact
the work itself. Resistance, frustration and anxiety will increase when the change is
perceived as an increment in the burden on individuals for an extended period. That’s
why, the change should be transmitted as a way to easier the tasks to accomplish the
work. Making work-life better and easier is the best incentive [95].
To define the incentives, change leaders should take into account what benefits of the
change will be felt by individuals, what kinds of incentives people in the organization
respond to and what types of incentives can and should be offered in the short-,
medium- and long term.
Organizational change is essentially cultural change, which demands reasons to change,
intellectual excitement and opportunities for collaboration in planning and achieving
the new objective.
Change leaders should be aware the complexity of the change and offer incentives
according to the level of change demanded. To be mistaken in offering short-term
incentives when the complexity of the change high could lead to generate motivation
only for a short period of time.
In order to face the change, change leaders should asses the impacts on individuals
and groups either positive or negative ones. Besides, they should create incentives
that reinforce the positive and counterbalance the negative [95].

People will revert to old habits given the opportunity. For this reason, one of the
key ways is to ensure that the project team in charge of the change have the required
skills to face the change and then, to accomplish their work in the new environment.
One of the most common ways to eliminate the reluctance to the change is providing
education and training. The change leader should transmit the importance to work

the goal, an indication of who’s responsible for achieving the objective and a timeline
for achieving the objective. Finally, all of these should be aligned with the vision of
the change plan in order to accomplish the desired change.

5.2
Systems Dynamics Method
In a world of accelerating complexity and change, thoughtful leaders increasingly
recognize that the tools that had been used not only failed to solve the persistent
problems we face but may in fact be causing them [96]. All too often, well-intentioned
efforts to solve pressing problems create unanticipated side effects. The result is policy
resistance, the tendency for interventions to be defeated by the response of the system
to the intervention itself [96].
There are a lot of examples that shows policy resistance [97].
• Road building programs designed to reduce congestion have increased traffic,
delays, sprawl and pollution.
• Antibiotics have stimulated the evolution of drug-resistant pathogens, including
virulent strains of TB, strep, staph and sexually transmitted diseases.
However, the issue is to know how to understand the whole systems and avoid policy
resistance [98]. Some scholars point out that the solution lies in systems thinking,
which is the ability to see the world as a complex system, to understand how every-
thing is connected to everything else [99].
Thus, to be effective, engineering must consider the social, political, ecological and
other impacts of proposed technical solutions [100]. Failure to do so leads to unan-
ticipated side effects that damage human welfare, and more often than not, to the
failure of the engineering solution on its own terms [96].
==PAGE:73==
5.2 Systems Dynamics Method
59
In 2000, Sterman [96] published a paper in which he described how policy resistance
arises from the mismatch between the dynamic complexity of the systems have been
created and the cognitive capacity to understand that complexity. The research shows
that policy resistance most often arises not because of the lack of technical knowledge
and tools to model the problem, but because the mental models systematically lead
people to incorrect inferences about the dynamics of even the simplest systems [96]).
The idea that there is an enduring structure to business and social systems, which
somehow predetermines achievable futures, is not necessarily obvious [99]. People in
general face the facts in a different way, and it is due to the existence of different
perspectives. Depending on some factors such as the personality, ambition, chance
and circumstances, everyone interprets the world with his own perspective [101].
Thus, systems dynamics try to provide a more broadly and systemically way of think-
ing about the people related to organizations and the intentions, goals and actions of
those organizations [102].
The basis of systems dynamics is composed by two main points [103]:
• System structure leads to patterns of behaviour which lead to the events which
are visible to people. For example, the decision to reshore. Following a systems
dynamics approach, there should be a search for the underlying organizational
structure that led to offshoring, and later, reshoring decision.
• Circular causality thinking, which indicates that any system actions on its en-
vironment will have an effect on this environment, and thus, will also influence
this same system’s decisions on future actions on the same environment. Also, in
complex systems, some actions have “unintended consequences” because some
of these circular causality effects were not identified in time. For instance, a deci-
sion to reshore because of low labour cost, might stimulate the local production
of similar products with the expertise that is obtained creating competition for
the offshoring company which was not there before the offshoring was started.
As an example, this will eventually increase the labour costs in the offshoring
location.
In order to analyze a different way of thinking, the feedback systems thinking is
presented.
A feedback approach is different from event-oriented thinking because
it strives for solutions that are “sympathetic” with their organizational and social
environment. Problems and solutions coexist and are independent [104].
A causal loop diagram is a method used to identify the factors that affect to one
situation and the relation between them. It is a matter of judgment and experience
to know where the boundary on the factors is to include since each situation is different
from the other [102].
==PAGE:74==
60
5 Analysis
People responsible for strategy development and facing problematic situations often
have in mind partial and conflicting views of these situations [104]. It is therefore, well
worth spending time to capture their individual perspectives, develop and overview,
share the big picture and thereby try to anticipate the ramifications, knock-on conse-
quences, and side effects of strategic change [99].
One cause of policy resistance is the tendency to interpret experience as a series
of events, for example inventory is too high, or sales fell last month.
People has
in their mind that every event has a cause, which in turn is an effect of some still
earlier cause. The event-oriented, open-loop worldview leads to an event-oriented,
reactionary approach to problem solving [96]. It reflects a belief that problems are
sporadic, stemming from uncontrollable events in the outside world.
The typical
thinking style here is linear – from problem-as-event to solution-as-fix [99], as shown

• Congestion →Build new roads

Likewise, real systems react to interventions and they generate feedback since the
results of the actions define the situations in the future [96]. The new situation alters

that difficulted the communication for several reasons, one of them, the time zone dif-
ference. This influence the risk of the business, since it provokes having less flexibility
and then, more stock due to longer lead times.
Company 2 had to face with less customer satisfaction since some of their clients
did not receive the products on time.
This fact accelerated the decision to move
from China to Poland. It was also directly affected by the European growing Market.
Therefore, as the market had an increasing trend, the sales were also higher.
Besides, one of the main reasons of leaving China was the upward standard of living
which increased the purchasing power and then, the production costs due to the
increase of the labour quality.
As the raw material from the polish suppliers was better, the product quality im-
proved. With all of this, the sales increased due to the client satisfaction. As was
aforementioned, the client satisfaction is directly influenced by the lead times, the
flexibility and the risk assumed. Working with polish suppliers improved all of these
three aspects, so the reshoring strategy was properly defined.
As it can be seen in the feedback loop of Figure 5.12, it contains two reinforcing loops
and one balancing loop. This shows the reinforcing trend for this sequence of facts.
That is to say, if Company 2 decided to stay in China and not to move to Poland,
the situation was continuing being worst and Company 2 had to close the factories
there in a worst economic situation.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
Reshoring has an upward trend worldwide since the industry sector presents an in-
creasingly number of companies which are implementing offshoring and then, reshoring.
The purpose of this thesis is to provide some recommendations for companies which
have in mind either to offshore or reshore. In order to do this, it is essential to iden-
tify which factors were taken into account when offshoring and which ones were not
considered as it should that caused reshoring. There is a gap between the reasons why
a company decides to offshore and then, after a while, decides to come back to the
same or closer country. The research gap is tackled through the use of case studies
within the reshoring topic, that helped to address the following research questions
(RQ):
• RQ1: What were the factors taken into account when offshoring? and which
were not considered?
• RQ2: What are were the factors that caused reshoring?
• RQ3: What factors should managers take into account when deciding to off-
shore?
In order to broadly understand in a practice way the reshoring concept two company
cases have been selected to analyse their situation and to determine which factors
are involved in the offshoring and reshoring decisions. The company cases have been
selected among Danish companies which have been implemented offshoring and then
reshoring. The two companies are named Company 1 and Company 2 for confiden-
tiality reasons.
6.1
Conclusions company 1 case
As it was aforementioned, in an attempt to introduce their products in the Asiatic
market, Company 1 first started to sell their products through local partners in 2000.
The pharmaceutical company supplied them with finished and bought products and
then, the local partners distributed themselves using their infrastructure.
After some years, Company 1 decided to change the strategy by controlling their
supply chain and sales themselves. For this reason, they decided to move part of

presented an increasing growth trend from 2000 achieving the 10,3% GDP in 2010.
Company 1 encountered legal difficulties. The Chinese government was exigent and
the company had a lot of trouble to obtain the license to operate in their new packag-
ing plant. Besides, another exigency was to reduce the prices in exchange for speeding
the licence documentation. The time was running and without the license, Company
1 could not completely use their packaging facility to they were wasting money each
day. So, they decide to close the facility and move back to Denmark again.
After the implementation of the Knoster model for managing complex change, Com-
pany 1 was missing a clear vision of the change project they were facing. First, they
only thought about to have an increased presence in China since it was a potential
market but they did not research enough about the legal exigencies and the complica-
tions it could cause to them. The fact that this was not researched enough forced to
Company 1 to change their road map several times. First, they considered the selling
strategy through local partners, then, a new facility where they started to package
and later, they were considering to start producing. All of these changes without
a clear vision of the strategy change and lacking a proper research of the Chinese
business legislation promoted an offshoring failure caused from the confusion of the
people involved in the project.
Then, as it can be seen in the implementation of the systems dynamics method (See
Section5.2.1), Company 1 was affected by lots of factors not considered in their first
research when offshoring. From all the information extracted from Company 1, it
can be concluded that they only took into account some of the factors found in the
analysis and they did not considered the relation among them. That is to say, they
evaluated the factors in a static point of view when they should be considered in a
dynamic way.
For example, the Chinese Government control is influenced by commercial interests.
At the same time, these interests are fostered by the manufacturing presence in the
country which is regulated by the licenses the government give to the companies in
the same sector. Besides, there are more legal exigencies to give these licenses, for
example the cost reduction of the products and product volume manufactured in
China.
To conclude, Company 1 determined their offshoring strategy by the upward trend
in the Chinese economy and the pharmaceutical presence in that country in a static
point of view. Nevertheless, although these factors are also important, they should
be considered in a dynamic point of view since they change through the time and
there are influenced by other secondary factors that drive the facts in one direction
or another. So, the relation between the primary factors and the secondary factors
should be taken into account by considering the option that even though the trend

6.2
Conclusions company 2 case
Company 2 do not have own production facilities. It has established several ware-
houses in Europe from where it distributes the articles worldwide. It has two ware-
houses in Germany, one in Sweden, Poland and Denmark.
However, the 46% of the stores are located in the Scandinavian countries, Eastern
countries and China. Then, the 48% are located in Central-South Europe and finally,
the 6% in Canada and different countries in Asia.
For this reason, Company 2 decided to invest in new offices in China from where
they could increase their presence there gaining new Asiatic clients. Therefore, they
changed all their Danish suppliers for Chinese suppliers. However, they needed to
fulfil also the requirements of their suppliers around the world. There was when the
difficulties appeared.
Company 2 had to face with too much longer lead times and a low quality, so their
clients were not satisfied. Besides, their market share in China was not as expected.
The turning point was when they run out of stock of some of their garden products in
their warehouses and the order arrived from China to Europe when the summer season
was already finished. In that moment, Company 2 decided to move the production
management department to Poland, where they have a warehouse, and to change
from the Chinese to the Polish suppliers.
After the implementation of the Knoster model for managing complex change, Com-
pany 2 was lacking an action plan that is totally necessary when a new strategy is up
to implementation. They had very clear what they wanted to do; to increase presence
in the Asiatic market since their competitors were doing it and at the same time, to
reduce costs by changing from Danish suppliers to Chinese suppliers.
However, they did not plan enough the implementation steps to apply in a successful
way the strategy change. Company 2 did not perform a complete research of the
Asiatic market and all the factors affecting when a new company is introduced phys-
ically in the sector. They state in the interviews that Company 2 followed a trend
from their direct competitors to move to China, and therefore, they decided to do the
same. Nevertheless, without a complete analysis of the implementation plan, a false
start was produced and therefore, after some year they had to reshore.
Then, as it can be seen in the implementation of the systems dynamics method (See
Section 5.2.1, several factors are affecting to the offshoring and reshoring process.

Recommendations
In order to conclude this thesis, some recommendations are constructed to guide
companies which have in mind either offshore or reshore. So as the RQ1 and RQ2
are answered in the first part of the conclusion, this part aims to answer the third
research question (RQ3) in which are pointed out what factors managers should take

To be able to state some recommendations for companies, the valuable data extracted

companies make when changing their strategy implementing offshoring or reshoring.
As it was stated before, the most common reshoring drivers are differentiated in two
main groups; the managerial mistake and strategic decisions. The former includes
three which are applicable to the company cases analysed in this thesis; miscalcula-
tion of the actual cost, lack of knowledge on the host country and lack of systematic
location planning. The latter also includes some of them that are identified in the
company cases; miscalculation of the costs, customer related issues and an unstruc-

the two company cases are shown in Section 5.2.1.
However, the drivers commented by the interviewees of each company case were to
reduce costs and access to new markets for offshoring and quality problems and
maladjusted opportunities analysis for reshoring. This means that the companies are
not fully aware of the factors that actually caused reshoring.
For this reason, in order to mitigate this phenomenon, some recommendations are
presented to guide companies which are considering to implement either offshoring
or reshoring. The aim is to inform companies of the most common missing factors to
have them in consideration for their strategy change plan.
Companies considering offshoring:
• Identify the primary factors that can be involved in the offshoring project
• Perform an in-depth research of this factors through the time. Considering past
decades and forecasting the coming ones.
• Identify the secondary factors affecting the primary drivers.

• Based on the complete analysis, decide if it is worthy to offshore, not only
looking at the numbers, but also paying attention to the trend of the relation
of the factors, if it is balancing or reinforcing.
• Communicate the final decision to the team in charge of driving the project;
