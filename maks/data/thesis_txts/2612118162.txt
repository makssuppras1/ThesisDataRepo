Abstract
The following thesis focuses on the field of damage detection. In particular, it aims to
assess the structural damage occurred during a dynamic event.
A literature review on the current state­of­the­art of the field of damage detection is pre­
sented and the main methods developed in the research papers are described. Several
investigations including the use of the linear Kalman Filter (KF) and the Unscented Kalman
Filter (UKF) are conducted, in order to replicate and test two methods for damage detec­
tion found in the literature. The KF and the UKF are used in order to estimate the state of
the structure, the input ground motion or time invariant parameters of the structure.
An innovative method for damage detection is proposed. It is based on the state esti­
mation with the UKF and the model updating performed in OpenSees (FE software). Its
performance is tested on 2D steel structures with the application of several ground mo­
tions.
The proposed method is simulation­based and makes use of noisy measurements taken
by a limited number of sensors during a dynamic event. The elements of the structures
are modelled in OpenSees with nonlinear elements and fiber sections, in order to account
for the nonlinear behaviour of the structure.
The method is tested for 36 strong ground motions and the results are presented and
discussed. The issues found in the proposed method are investigated and a potential
solution is proposed and discussed.
The main advantages of the proposed algorithm are short computational time and inde­
pendence of the type of input load. Its application is therefore suitable for different types
of dynamic events, including ground motions, wind and wave loading.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The field of damage detection in civil structures has caught the attention of many re­
searches over the last decades. Knowing the damage and current state of a structure is
crucial for determining its level of safety and its expected performance when subjected to
different types of loading.
This especially applies to old structures, which have been exposed to long term loads, dif­
ferent types of loads, fatigue problems, as well as environments that can be aggressive
for the materials.
More recent structures however, even though they have been designed according to the
most recent building codes, can also suffer from structural damage if they are subjected
to very strong events, such as an earthquake or a strong wave loading.
The information that is usually available for detecting and assessing the current state of
a structure comes from the noisy measurements of a few sensors that are placed in the
structure. From this information and from the model of the structure, it is up to the engi­
neers to find the potential locations and the magnitude of the structural damages.
The need of more research in this field is due to safety and economic­related reasons.
Structures need maintenance, and assessing the structural damage can be an expensive
process if the structure, or parts of it, are not easily accessible. Therefore, automatic
damage detection, with only a model of the structure and some measurements, would be
extremely beneficial.
The above mentioned reasons, together with the fact that the field of damage detection is
in an advancement stage, are the main motivations behind this work, which aims to give
a contribution to this research field.
1.2
Structural damage
In order to formulate the problem to be solved, a definition of structural damage is neces­
sary.
Structural damage can be defined, in the broad sense, as a change of the properties of a
structure and, therefore, a change of its behaviour. The sources of structural damage can
be very different, and the damage itself can take place during a short and strong event,
like an earthquake or a fire, or it can happen in a long and slow process, like corrosion of
steel due to environmental conditions or fatigue.
From the moment that a structure has been built, only a certain amount of information can
be extracted from it in order to assess its condition and current state.
The real state of a structure is always subjected to a degree of uncertainty. Small imper­
fections and defects are not avoidable when building a structure. On top of that, the exact
material properties are unknown and various external loads or conditions can cause small
changes. These changes are defined as damage.

1.3
Main focus of this project
This work focuses on structural damage, damage of non­structural elements has not been
considered.
A damaged structure can experience a permanent change of mass, stiffness, a loss of
strength or a loss of capacity. The consequences are different for different types of dam­
ages. A brief explanation is provided hereafter.
Change of mass of the structure: this is the least common type of damage. An example
of this is ice accretion on wind turbine blades, which causes a change of the mass and a
change in aerodynamic properties ([10])
Change of stiffness: this is usually the most relevant type of damage. It can be caused
by a strong event like an earthquake or an intense wave loading.
Loss of strength: this is mostly the case for concrete structures, which have a softening
behaviour when subjected to large displacements.
Loss of capacity of the structure: residual displacements can cause a loss of capacity of
the structure. This can be seen comparing a pushover curve performed before and after
the structure has residual displacements.
The structure can also have other types of damage due to fatigue, corrosion of reinforce­
ment bars or steel elements, change of aerodynamic properties, damages due to a fire
etc.
The type of damage considered in this work is only related to a change of stiffness of
the elements. The damaged elements cause a change in the dynamic properties of the
whole structure. The damaged elements need to be identified, and the magnitude of the
damage needs to be assessed.
1.4
Objective of this work
In this project several investigations are conducted in order to find a reliable way to es­
timate the state of the structure during and after a strong dynamic event. It is assumed
that the only information available comes from the measurements of a limited number of
sensors, during the event.
The main objective is to develop a method to assess the damages that the structure has
undergone during a specific event, without the need to physically visit the structure after
the event.

2.1
Damage detection in the literature
Damage detection is commonly subdivided in 4 levels([2]):
1) determination of damage occurrence;
2) determination of damage location;
3) determination of magnitude of the damage;
4) decision making.
This work and the related literature only focus on levels 1 to 3.
A formal definition of structural damage can be the following: damage is defined as
changes introduced in a structure which adversely affects the current or future perfor­
mance of that structure [25]. As mentioned in the previous chapter, this work and the
reviewed literature only focus on changes in the stiffness of the structure.
Damage identification can take place at two different moments of life of a structure. One
of them is when the structure is subjected to a strong event/loading: the evolution of the
state of the structure can be studied during that particular event. Otherwise, it can take
place at present time, in order to assess the current state of the structure.
If the evolution of the structure during an event is studied, the main objective is to find
the history of the behaviour of structure and of its damage during the event. Bayesian
inference, in particular Kalman Filter, can be used for this purpose.
On the other hand, if the structure has already been damaged and the damage needs to
be identified in the current state of the structure, the history and evolution of the damage
is not relevant.
For this last case vibration­based techniques are broadly used. They are discussed in the
following section, whereas the first case is treated subsequently.

2.2
Vibration­based structural damage identification
A lot of researchers (for example [19], [21], [16], [23], [9]) focused their attention on esti­
mating the damage of existing structures assuming their current dynamic properties (fre­
quencies and mode shapes) are known. In this branch of damage detection, the main
strategy is to treat the problem as an inverse problem, for which an optimization is per­
formed.
Knowing the properties of the structure when it is undamaged, and knowing the dynamic
properties of the damaged structure, the optimization aims to identify the damage dis­
tributed in the structure as well as its magnitude.
The damages are mostly modelled as a reduction of stiffness of certain elements, and the
structures are usually simple.
In order to perform the optimization, heuristic methods (Genetic Algorithm, Particle Swarm,
and many more) or Machine Learning (artificial neural networks) are used.
Machine learning and heuristic methods can be very powerful tools for damage detection
and very accurate results can be obtained in detecting the structural damage([2],[4]).
Pioneering works in this area started in 1979 [19]. In 1997 Ruotolo and Surace [21] made
a study that aimed to assess the structural damage in a multi­cracked cantilever beam,
using a Genetic Algorithm to perform an optimization. The objective functions used in the
optimization included the natural frequencies and the mode shapes of the structure.
A similar methodology is used in several research works done over the years, with differ­
ent heuristic methods such as Simulated Annealing ([23]), Ant Colony Optimization ([16]),
Particle Swarm Optimization ([5]), Multiuniverse Optimizer ([9]) and other methods, some
of which are hybrid and enhanced versions of the ones mentioned above.
Machine learning has been used and proved its efficiency in this type of problems ([27],[8]).
As mentioned before, the methodology is very similar in the mentioned works. The algo­
rithms (heuristic methods or neural networks) are able to identify the damage locations
and magnitude with satisfactory accuracy, even in presence of noise. However, the struc­
tures used when applying these methods are simple (cantilever beam, shear frame, 2D
and 3D trusses). Additionally, the number of elements is limited and they are modelled
only considering their cross sectional area, second moment of inertia, young modulus E.
These methods have not been pursued in this work.
From the cited papers, the advances in heuristic methods and machine learning are what
affects the performance of damage detection the most. Focusing on these methods was
considered to give this work a small potential.
On top of that, it has been preferred to consider more complex structures and their non­
linear behaviour, which was not considered in the cited papers.

Kalman Filter in structural damage identification
Kalman Filter is an adaptive filter that makes use of Bayesian filtering, or Recursive
Bayesian estimation. Its function is to estimate the most probable state of the system
in a certain time instant, knowing the state of the system in the previous time instant (re­
cursive filter).

In order to make the estimation, the Kalman Filter makes use of measurements, and of
the uncertainties associated with the model and with the measurements.
The Kalman Filter is one of the main tools used in this work, and a section is dedicated to
this filter in the next chapter.
The use of Kalman Filter allows to consider the nonlinear behaviour of the structure dur­

Some of the uses of the Kalman Filter in the field of damage detection in civil structures

Different types of Kalman Filters have been used such as: linear Kalman Filter, Aug­

Estimation of ground motion
In 2017 Astroza et al. ([20]) used the Unscented Kalman Filter to estimate the ground
motion in a nonlinear steel structure and a nonlinear concrete structure using the mea­
surements from a limited number of sensors. Together with the ground motion also some
time invariant parameters were estimated. An investigation about this methodology has
been conducted in this work, in which the use of the Unscented Kalman Filter allowed to
estimate the input ground motion. Once the ground motion has been estimated, a dy­
namic analysis can be performed, and all the needed information can be extracted from
the model.
Estimation of time invariant parameters
Similar studies on the estimation of time invariant parameters using the Unscented Kalman
Filter can be found in [22],[3], [29]. The estimation of time invariant parameters (such
as the yielding strength of the steel, or the strain­hardening ratio (ratio between plastic
modulus Ep and elastic modulus E0) are relevant in order to have a model that is as
representative as possible of the real structure.
State estimation of linear systems
The state estimation of linear systems using the Kalman Filter can be found in [26], where
a Dual Augmented Kalman Filter has been used in order to estimate the external input
more accurately.
As mentioned before, an accurate state estimation of the system can provide enough in­
formation in order to calculate the structural damage. For this reason, state estimation is
used in the method proposed in this thesis, which is applied to nonlinear systems. If the
state estimation is accurate, it can provide enough information to calculate the location
and the magnitude of the damages occurred in the structure during a specific event.
Self­tuning Kalman Filter
The process of choosing the parameters and calibrating the noise in the Kalman Filter
is called tuning. An Adaptive Kalman Filter has been used in [18] in order to estimate
recursively, additionally to time invariant parameters, the covariance matrices R and Q,
related to the noise of the measurements and to the uncertainty of the model.
The Adaptive Kalman Filter does not need an accurate choice for Q and R, which can
have a big influence on the performance. The covariance matrices Q and R, are updated
automatically during the analysis, and this can improve the performance of the estimation.

3.3
Unscented Kalman Filter
The Kalman Filter discussed so far is limited to estimating the most probable state of the
system in case the probability distribution remains Gaussian and the system is linear. If
the system with a Gaussian distribution goes through a nonlinear function, the distribution
of the new system is not, in general, Gaussian.
Since that is one of the hypothesis of the Kalman Filter, in case of a nonlinear system,
some change needs to be implemented.
The most natural change is to locally linearize the problem calculating the partial deriva­
tives, therefore computing the Jacobian. This principle is used in the Extended Kalman
Filter (EKF) for making estimations of nonlinear systems. The EKF has not been imple­
mented in this work.
Another way of using the Kalman Filter for estimating a nonlinear system is to make use
of the Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF) ([13]), which is based on the unscented transfor­
mation, explained in this section. The UKF is used in this work for nonlinear systems.
The algorithm of the UKF is different from the linear KF, but they share the main steps.
Figure 3.2 shows the algorithm for the UKF.

The prediction process of the UKF now includes the computation of sigma points. The
unscented transformation (UT) is then used to calculate the predicted state, predicted
measurements and the corresponding error covariances.
In the estimation process a cross covariance term is calculated in order to compute the
Kalman Gain. Finally the estimate of the state and of the error covariance are computed
in the same way as in the linear Kalman Filter.
3.3.1
Unscented transformation
The unscented transformation characterizes the UKF and makes it very efficient in terms
of computational cost. An explanation is given below.
Given a state variable x that follows a Gaussian distribution, has a mean ¯x and covariance
Px, and given a generic function f, it is required to compute the mean and covariance of
the transformed variable f(x).
x ∼N(¯x, Px)
(3.38)
If f is a linear function, then the mean and covariance of f(x) are easily computed from
the mean and covariance of x, since f(x) would also have a Gaussian distribution. The
linear Kalman Filter discussed previously is based on this concept.
If f is a nonlinear function, then the statistical characteristics of the transformed variable
f(x) are not easily calculated. In this case a very large sample of values of x can be used
to compute a very large amount of values of f(x), then the statistical characteristic can

be calculated. This is at the base of the Monte Carlo method, which requires to calculate
a lot of points in the transformed variable and is very computationally expensive.
A way to overcome this is by using the unscented transformation, which consists of a
selection of a few so called ”sigma points”, which capture the main statistical characteristic
of the new distribution of f(x).
Thus, a few values of the state variable x are chosen and are transformed with the function
f, and from these few points of f(x) the main statistical characteristic of f(x) can be
obtained.
A total number of 2n+1 sigma points are computed, where n is the dimension of the state
variable x. A weight is assigned to each sigma point. These few sigma points are enough
to capture the first and the second statistical moments of the distribution of f(x). The first
and second statistical moments of a distribution are the mean and the covariance.

3.3.2
Unscented Kalman Filter algorithm
Having presented the main formulas of the UKF, an explanation of the algorithm is pre­
sented in this section. As an explanatory case, the state estimation of the system is used.
For the purpose of an easier understanding of the functioning of the algorithm, the fol­
lowing oversimplification is provided. It can be thought as if the algorithm was exploring
different values of the state of the system x (sigma points). For each of them the re­
sponses of the monitored DOFs are extracted. The responses are then compared with
the measurements from the sensors, and from this comparison and from the covariance
matrices, the algorithm provides an estimate of the most probable state of the structure,
x.
Nonlinear Response and Damage Detection of structures subjected to time­varying loads
19
==PAGE:32==
Prediction process
For a generic time step k, 2n + 1 sigma points are first computed using the previous esti­
mate and error covariance xk−1 and Pk−1. The previous estimate xk−1 is also the mean
value of the sigma points. The sigma points will be further from the mean if the error co­
variance is large.
Each sigma point is transformed with the functions f and h, which in case of the state
estimation are the matrices A and G described previously.
As explained before, the system matrix A relates the state of the system in the previous
time step k −1, to the one in the current time step k. G extracts from the state of the
system the responses of the monitored DOFs, in the location of the sensors.
Since the covariance of the process noise (Q) and of the measurements (R) need to
be included, they are added to the error covariance (Pk + Q) and to the measurement
covariance (P y
k + R).
Estimation process
Like in the linear Kalman filter, the Kalman gain is computed in order to update the pre­
diction. The form of the Kalman gain is different, but its role is the same as before.
Finally, the prediction can be updated, and the final estimate of the state and of the error
covariance can be computed. As it was the case in the linear KF, the final estimation
depends on the initial prediction, on the Kalman gain, and on the difference between the
predicted response and the measurements.
3.4
Summary of Kalman Filters used in this work
In this work the Kalman Filter has been primarily used for the estimation of the state of
the system. The state of the system is defined as the velocities and displacements of all
DOFs of the structure.
First, a state space formulation of the system had to be made, which is explained in section
3.1. The formulation has additionally been made in modal coordinates. In the Augmented
Kalman Filter the state space formulation has been slightly changed in order to include
the state and the external input into an augmented state vector.
The formulation that was needed in the Kalman Filter is in a discrete and recursive form.
In this way, the state at time step k can be estimated knowing the state at time step k −1.
There are three types of Kalman Filters used in this work: (linear) Kalman Filter, Aug­
mented Kalman Filter and the Unscented Kalman Filter.
The linear Kalman Filter allows to estimate the most probable state of a linear system at
time step k, knowing the state of the system at time step k −1.
The needed inputs are:
• the system matrices A,G;
• the covariance of the process noise, Q;
• the covariance of the measurement noise, R;
• the (noisy) measurements from the sensors, u.
20
Nonlinear Response and Damage Detection of structures subjected to time­varying loads
==PAGE:33==
The system matrix A relates the previous state of the system with the current state.
The G matrix selects the responses from the system that will be compared with the ones
from the measurements.
Q and R represent the uncertainty of the model (Q) and the measurements (R). Calibrat­
ing Q and R can be very important for the performance of the Kalman Filter, it is called
tuning of the Kalman Filter.
If the state space model formulation has been made with modal coordinates, the Kalman
Filter works exactly in the same way.
If the load needs to be included, an Augmented Kalman Filter can be used. The system
matrices change in order to include the external loading into the system model.
An important additional input in this case is the covariance of the external load, S. This
needs to be chosen in advance, like Q and R. For the implementations used in this work,
the estimation of the load was not satisfactory, but using the Augmented Kalman Filter
improved the performance of the estimation.
The last type of Kalman Filter used in this work is the Unscented Kalman Filter. It is a
nonlinear Kalman Filter, and it needed to be used for the nonlinear analyses made in this
work. For a state estimation, the input matrices are the same as for the linear Kalman
Filter, even though the internal operations of the UKF are different from the ones of the
linear KF. The augmented formulation can also be used in the UKF.
Other inputs are the choice of the parameters for the unscented transformation (α, β, κ),
which are stated in each case the UKF has been used in this work.

5.2
Estimation of the ground motion with Unscented Kalman
Filter
For the following investigation, as well as for the investigation in section 5.3, the method
proposed by Astroza et al. ([20]) was applied.
The aim is to estimate the input ground motion, having the measurements from a limited
amount of sensors. After estimating the ground motion, a dynamic analysis can be run
and all the needed information can be extracted from the simulation.
Given the nonlinearity of the problem, this method makes use of the Unscented Kalman
Filter (UKF). The UKF is coded in MATLAB. In the MATLAB program, OpenSees is used
inside the loop of the UKF.
Structure and sensors
The structure used for this investigation is the two­story steel frame (chapter 4.1).Figure
5.18 shows the placement of the sensor in the structure.
Figure 5.18: Placement of the sensor in the structure
The sensor measures the horizontal displacement of one node in the first story.
Ground motion
The ground motion of El Centro (figure 5.1) has been used for this investigation.
Parameters of the Unscented Kalman Filter
The (scaled) Unscented Kalman Filter has been used with the following choice of param­
eters: α = 0.1, κ = 0, β = 2.
The noise covariance matrices have been chosen as Q = x · 10−8 and R = 10−4. Finally,
the initial value of the covariance for each ground acceleration (P0) is 2 · 10−4 · g, where
g is the gravity acceleration 9.81m/s2. These parameters are the same as the ones used
in [20].
Another important parameter used in this method is the memory length L = 20. The mem­
ory length L defines the dimension of the time window in which the ground acceleration is
being estimated. This means that, for L = 20, the ground acceleration of the last 20 time
steps is being estimated by the UKF.
The measurements are compared with the estimated responses for the same time steps
for which the load is being estimated.
At a generic time step ˆn of the analysis, the ground acceleration of the time steps ˆn−20+1
42
Nonlinear Response and Damage Detection of structures subjected to time­varying loads
==PAGE:55==
up to ˆn is being estimated. This means that the ground acceleration of every time step is
estimated and adjusted 20 times. It also means that, for computing the Kalman gain, the
last 20 responses are compared to the measured ones.
A larger value of L would result in a higher precision, but the time of the analysis would
be very long.
For this analysis MATLAB was used together with OpenSees tcl. The MATLAB program
interacts with OpenSees by modifying one of the input files for the OpenSees model.
Afterwards, Opensees is run through MATLAB and the results are written on a ”.out” file.
Finally, MATLAB reads the results, processes the data and proceeds to the next time step.
5.2.1
Explanation of the method
Figure 5.19, taken from [20], shows the algorithm used in the article and in this investiga­
tion.
Figure 5.19: Algorithm used in [20]
In this section a brief and qualitative explanation of the method is provided. However,
a complete explanation can be found in [20]. Additionally, the UKF is only explained in
qualitative terms, for a more detailed explanation about it, see chapter 3.
For each time step of the analysis, a total of 2 ∗L + 1 ( 41 for L = 20) sigma points are
computed, and for each of them one dynamic analysis is run.
For a generic time step ˆn each sigma point has different values for the last L (20) values
of the ground acceleration, whereas the preceding values have already been estimated.
For example, at time step ˆn = 400, it is assumed that the ground acceleration up to time
step 380 has already been estimated, and the 41 (if L = 20) sigma points all have a set
of different values for the ground acceleration at time the steps 381 to 400.
Nonlinear Response and Damage Detection of structures subjected to time­varying loads
43
==PAGE:56==
For each sigma point the analysis in OpenSees is run, up to time step ˆn. Then, using the
monitored displacements, the Kalman gain is computed.
From the comparison between measurements and estimated responses and from the
Kalman gain, the UKF can ”choose” the most probable values of the ground acceleration
for the last L (20) time steps.
This repeats until the end of the ground motion.
The number of analyses (41 per time step) is very large. In the ground motion used for
this investigation the total number of time steps is 7800.

6
Proposed method for damage detection
In the following chapter the main method for damage detection developed in this work
is presented and discussed. The method is tested on a simple structure (two­story steel
frame described in section 4.1) and the results are shown.
In chapter 7 the results of the application of several ground motions are shown for a bigger
structure.
This method aims to find the displacement time histories of all the DOFs of the structure.
It does not serve the purpose of directly defining where the damages occur.
Knowing all the displacements time histories, it is possible to run a simulation and find all
responses of the structure. For example, the moment curvature history of all cross sec­
tions can be plotted, and it can be assessed which sections have experienced a nonlinear
behaviour and are therefore damaged.
Thus, no definition of damage or direct assessment of the amount of damage has been
included. This information is considered available after the simulation.
The (scaled) Unscented Kalman Filter has been used for the estimation of the state of
the system (velocities and displacements of all DOFs). The UKF has been augmented in
order to include the external input. This was done in order to improve the performance of
the algorithm.
A state space formulation has been used in the UKF, both considering all the DOFs and
using the modal coordinates.
A finite element model in OpenSees has been used in the algorithm in order to update
the structural properties at each time step.
This proposed method requires a short computational time, depending on the type of dy­
namic event. The time for analyses ranged from 40 to 120 seconds, in the tests performed
in this work.
The method has been set up in Python, using OpenSeesPy.
6.1
Main objectives and available information
It is assumed that a limited number of sensors is placed in a structure, and the sen­
sors measure the responses during a dynamic event (an example is shown in figures
6.3,6.4,6.5). Only ground motions have been considered, however it can be applied to
any type of dynamic loading.
As mentioned before, the proposed method aims to find the displacement time histories
of all DOFs of the structure. The final outcome is the model and all displacements. With
this information, a simulation can be run in order to find all responses. It is equivalent
to having a model of the structure and the external loading and performing a dynamic
analysis in order to find all the needed information.
In the final section of this chapter it is proven that having the correct displacement time
histories of all DOFs of the structure is equivalent to knowing the input load(s) that caused
those displacements.
52

6.2
Main assumptions
The following assumptions were made in this work: the model corresponds to the real
structure; the structure is initially undamaged.
The first one in particular, is a strong assumption to make in real life, for a nonlinear
behaviour of a structure. In a real­life scenario, the model of the structure can be tuned
and refined in order to have the same properties as the real structure, however it is only
valid when the structure remains elastic.
Therefore, it is possible to have a model with the same elastic characteristics as the real
structure, but it is not possible to know the exact values of the parameters which only arise
when the structure is damaged. For example, it is not possible to know the exact yielding
strength fy of the steel in different elements, before yielding has occurred.
Due to the scope of this work, both assumptions have been kept throughout.
6.3
Proposed method
The basic idea that has been applied in the following method is the same as the one ap­
plied for a state estimation with the linear Kalman Filter (chapter 5.1).
At each time step the previous state is used in order to estimate the state in the current
time step. The system is represented in a state space formulation using the mass, damp­
ing and stiffness matrices.
In addition to this basic idea, the following concept has been implemented in the proposed
method.
Together with the state estimation, at each time step a static analysis is run in OpenSees.
The analysis in OpenSees imposes the displacements that have been estimated with the
UKF to the structure at the same time step.
After the analysis the stiffness matrix is extracted from the OpenSees model. In case
of damage or any nonlinear behaviour (including geometric nonlinearities), the stiffness
matrix is changed.
The stiffness matrix is then used to update the damping matrix. Both matrices will update
the state space formulation of the system. In case of modal coordinates, the modes are
also computed with the new stiffness matrix and used for the estimation.

the ground motion. In consequence, the time of the analysis for each time step would
increase, resulting in a total time of dozens of hours.
Lastly the values for Q, R and S (that are related to the uncertainties of the model, of the
measurements and of the input load, needed in the Kalman Filter) need to be chosen.
After several tests it has been found that in order to have a better performance, the value
of R should be smaller than the value of Q. This means that the measurements are
considered more ”reliable” than the system model. This is mainly because the external
excitation is missing in the system model (state space formulation). Regarding the choice
of S, a very large value can help the estimation of the state of the system. The values
chosen for S range from 1011 to 1014.
At this point the algorithm can start with the estimation and the update of the stiffness
matrix.
An important point is that the mass matrix cannot have ”0”’s in the diagonal, because the
state space formulation would not work. Therefore, in case of lumped mass, a rotational
and vertical nodal mass must be assigned to all nodes. It has been chosen to arbitrarily
assign 1 kg of rotational and vertical mass to all nodes. This value is extremely small and
does not affect the results, however it is necessary for running the analysis.
Measurement noise
The noise added to the measurements is expressed in percentage. The percentage in­
dicates how the standard deviation of the noise applied to one measurement relates to
the standard deviation of the measurement itself. The noise is white Gaussian noise, with
mean equal to ”0”.

6.3.3
State estimation with the Unscented Kalman Filter
Once the state space formulation has been set, the system matrix A, together with the
measurements, G, Q and R matrices can be used in the UKF. The functions f(·) and h(·)
introduced in chapter 3, are equal to the matrices A and G, for the state estimation.
The initial value of the state of the system (displacements and velocities) is set to ”0”. The
initial value of P, P0, is arbitrarily set to ”10”. However it will not affect the performance of
the method.
The UKF will compute a total number of 2n + 1 sigma points, where n is the number of
parameters to estimate.
For example, if the modal responses of 2 modes and the ground acceleration need to
be estimated, the total number of parameters n is 5 (2 modal displacements, 2 modal
velocities, 1 ground acceleration), resulting in a number of sigma points of 2n + 1 = 11.
This number would be much higher if the responses of all DOFs were estimated, instead
of the modal responses.

values of these parameters have been tested, all leading to similar results.
The output of the UKF is the estimation of the state of the system and, in case of aug­

The relevant output of the estimation are the displacements.

6.3.4
Application of the displacements in the OpenSees model
After the displacements have been estimated they can be applied in the OpenSees model.
The method used for this purpose is explained in section 6.6.
The displacements estimated with the UKF are imposed to the model, through a static
analysis in OpenSees.
This step is crucial to the method, and makes it innovative. In this step the damages are
potentially introduced in the structure, and they result in a change of the stiffness matrix.
Since the state space formulation assumes a linear system, the displacements that are
imposed to the structure in this step of the algorithm, need to be small. Small steps will
provide a good approximation of the nonlinear behaviour of the structure.
From Opensees the current stiffness matrix is extracted with the command printA. The
current stiffness matrix includes geometric nonlinearities such as P­Delta.
After this step the wipeAnalysis command is used to wipe the analysis (but not the entire
OpenSees model). Wiping the analysis will keep the current state of the structure, in terms
of displacements and damages. In the next time step, the estimated displacements will
be applied to the structure starting from the previous state.

Repetition for all time steps
After extracting the stiffness matrix, the results are stored, and the loop is run for all time
steps.

6.5
Divergence issue
The proposed method has been tested several times, and it shows a recurring problem
when the ground motion is very strong for the structure.
In this case, the UKF estimates very big (and unreasonable) displacements in the time
steps of the analysis in which the damages are very extensive. These displacements
are then imposed in the OpenSees model, which does not reach convergence and the
algorithm stops.
A potential solution is proposed for this issue. However, due to the scope of this work,
the solution has not been automated, but it has been implemented with a trial and error
process.
70
Nonlinear Response and Damage Detection of structures subjected to time­varying loads
==PAGE:83==
The proposed solution consists of suspending the model update for a limited number
of steps, in order to avoid divergence. This means that for the ”skipped” time steps, the
displacements estimated with the UKF are not imposed in the OpenSees model, therefore
the stiffness matrix remains the same for the ”skipped” steps.
An investigation has been conducted and presented at the end of the next chapter, for
one case where divergence occurred.
6.6
Application of the estimated displacements in the FE
model
In the following section it is explained how the displacements estimated with the UKF are
imposed in the OpenSees model.
Once the displacements of all DOFs of the structure are known, a one­step static analysis
can be run in OpenSees in order to force those displacements in the structure.
It can be achieved in three different ways, as OpenSees allows three different types
of static analysis. The three types are: load­controlled displacements, load­controlled
forces, displacement­controlled forces.
The one that has been considered optimal for this method is the ”load­controlled forces”
type of analysis. The same type is used when applying the gravity load.
The reason for the choice of this type is that the required displacements are applied with
high precision, and the stiffness matrix can be directly extracted from OpenSees with the
command printA.
Imposing the displacements with a ”load­controlled displacements” analysis would result
in the application of the exact displacements. However, the imposed displacements are
treated as constraints by OpenSees, therefore the stiffness matrix is no longer available.
An additional step needs to be added in order to extract the stiffness matrix.
The last type of static analysis, ”displacement­controlled forces”, which is the same one
usually chosen for a pushover analysis, would ideally get very close to the results obtained
with the ”load­controlled forces” analysis, however it is considerably less precise.
6.6.1
Calculation of the forces to be applied to the structure
The current state of the structure, the desired target displacements and the current stiff­
ness matrix are known.
First a ∆­displacement vector needs to be computed as the difference between the target
displacements and the current state of the structure. The target displacements are the
ones estimated with the UKF, while the current state of the structure can be extracted from
OpenSees with the command nodeDisp (in OpenSeesPy), for all DOFs of the structure.
Once the ∆­displacement vector is known, the forces that will cause the target displace­
ments are calculated as follows:
F = K · u
(6.14)
where F is the load vector, K is the stiffness matrix and u is the ∆­displacement vector.
This is the main equation of the stiffness method.
F is a vector of dimension nx1, n being the number of DOFs of the structure. In order to
get the desired target displacements, each force in the F vector needs to be applied to
the corresponding DOF.

6.6.2
Investigation on equivalence of imposing the displacements and
applying a dynamic load
In the following section it will be demonstrated that imposing the displacements in the
structure is equivalent, in terms of behaviour of the structure, to a dynamic analysis.
The FE software OpenSees is used for this purpose.

8
Conclusions and further work
8.1
Summary and discussion
The main objective of this work was to explore the field of damage detection in structures.
Several investigations were conducted in order to test the performance of different meth­
ods found in the literature.
After conducting the investigations, a method has been proposed for indirect detection of
structural damage in structures subjected to a strong dynamic loading.
The proposed method aims to estimate the displacement time histories of all DOFs of the
structure, under the assumption that knowing all displacements is sufficient in order to
accurately assess the structural damage.
The main idea of the method is based on the implementation of the Unscented Kalman
Filter (UKF) in order to estimate the displacements of the structure. The estimated dis­
placements are then imposed to the FE model in order to update the stiffness of the
structure at each time step. The potential structural damage occurring in each time step
results in a change of the stiffness.
The method has been tested on steel frames and with several strong ground motions.
It provided good results for the majority of tested ground motions. However, the algorithm
failed when stronger ground motions were applied, due to divergence of the FE analysis.
The main issue of the method encountered during the analyses was the above mentioned
divergence of the algorithm when the behaviour of the structure was highly nonlinear. In
that case, the UKF tends to estimate unreasonable displacements for the structure at a
few time steps, which causes the analysis of the FE model to diverge.
A solution for this issue is proposed, and it consists of suspending the imposition of the
displacements in the FE model for a limited number of time steps.
8.2
Conclusions
The methods tested during the investigations showed a good performance in the estima­
tion of the state of the system in a linear case (with the linear Kalman Filter), the ground
motion and time invariant parameters (using Astroza et al.’s method ([20]), with the UKF).
The estimation of the ground motion, in particular, was extremely precise when noise is
not applied to the measurements. Once the ground motion is known, a dynamic analysis
can be run and all structural damage can be assessed. The downside of this method is
the very long computational time (approx. 50 hours in the investigation, which can be
reduced in case of parallel analyses).
The investigation of a method for estimating structural damages using the same algorithm
as in Astroza’s work, lead to a wrong damage assessment. However, the estimated re­
sponses were almost identical to the measured ones, which demonstrates that different
damage scenarios can lead to the same responses.
The method proposed following the investigations performed well on the tested steel struc­
tures for the majority of selected ground motions. It showed poor performance for the
Nonlinear Response and Damage Detection of structures subjected to time­varying loads
97
==PAGE:110==
stronger ground motions.
The potential solution that has been proposed in order to overcome this limitation per­
formed well.
All structures used in this work were steel structures. Concrete structures present more
issues due to their highly nonlinear behaviour. However, the method can potentially be
extended to concrete structures.
It has been showed that estimating the displacements of all DOFs of the structure, pro­
vides all the information on the structural behaviour during a dynamic event. Therefore
the structural damage that has potentially occurred, can be assessed.
The estimations obtained with the method showed different levels of accuracy:
the horizontal displacements were estimated accurately; the vertical displacements were
not estimated accurately, however they are irrelevant for the behaviour of the structure in
case of ground motions;
the rotations at the lower floors were estimated with good precision, whereas the estima­
tion of rotations at the top of the building was less accurate.
The moment and the curvature of the monitored cross sections were estimated with sat­
isfactory accuracy in case each quantity was considered separately, throughout the time
of the analysis. However, the moment­curvature plot showed a discrepancy between the
actual and the estimated values in case of highly nonlinear behaviour.
It should be stressed that the proposed method has a very short computational time com­
pared to the method for estimating the ground motion used in the investigations (approx.
1 minute as opposed to approx. 50 hours in the analyses in this work).
A short computational time allows to run several analyses with different settings and to
post­process the estimation in order to make it more accurate.
Finally, the proposed method for damage detection can provide additional information to
the assessment of existent structures and the damage occurred during dynamic events.
8.3
Further studies
Divergence issue
The proposed solution can be implemented in an automatic way. This solution could
also improve the performance of the method even in case of no divergence, because
the UKF tends to overestimate the displacements when there is a peak response in the
measurements.
Other types of Kalman Filter
In this work only the scaled Unscented Kalman Filter has been used. The parameters of
the UKF could be changed and possibly improve the performance.
An Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) could also be used in place of the UKF.
Additionally, a Dual Kalman Filter and an Adaptive Kalman Filter can be implemented. The
Dual KF would make two estimations at each time step, and it can be used for estimating
the ground motion. The Adaptive KF is a Kalman Filter that tunes the Q and R matrices
during the analysis. In this way, the performance of the algorithm would be less affected
by the initial choice of R and Q, and the estimation would be improved.
Not only simulation based
In this work all analyses were simulation­based. The algorithm should be tested using
data from real structures. Modelling errors, which were not included in this work , would
also be relevant in this case.

==PAGE:111==
Extend to 3D
All the simulations have been done on 2D models. The work could be continued using
3D models.
Different materials
Only steel was used in this study, which behaves in a more predictable way compared to
concrete. The method should be extended to concrete structures, and possibly to other
materials.
Sensors and modes
Investigating different arrangements of the sensors in a structure can be very important.
Different arrangements of sensors can make a big difference. Additionally to the arrange­
ment, the quantities (acceleration, velocity of displacement) measured by the sensors can
change the performance.
Increasing the number of modes used for the estimation did not improve the performance
of the method, however finding a way to include more modes can potentially improve the
estimation.
