Abstract
Diesel engine emissions constitute a signiﬁcant health hazard and are pollutants. The Diesel Oxidation
Catalyst (DOC) in current exhaust treatment system is responsible for oxidation of CO, hydrocarbons
and conversion of NO to NO2.
Today, legislation limits are satisﬁed, however the DOC contains
expensive noble metals. Further development of the DOC should involve a decrease in noble metals
without compromising activity. A 1 wt% Pt/Al2O3 catalyst from Sigma Aldrich performs well for CO
oxidation (Light-oﬀtemperature for heating: 116◦C and cooling: 99◦C). Therefore, this report puts
focus on modiﬁcation of Pt/Al2O3 to yield more eﬃcient catalysts for low temperature CO oxidation.
In this report, two types of modiﬁers have been tested, i.e. iron oxide and potassium. Potassium
should decrease electron density of platinum thereby reducing Pt-CO bond strength. Iron oxide is
assumed to act as oxygen provider. The modiﬁers are impregnated on 1 wt% Pt/Al2O3 by incipient
wetness impregnation in two diﬀerent ratios each. The resulting catalysts are tested by temperature
ramp experiments on a small scale plug ﬂow reactor with regards to CO oxidation. Both types of mod-
iﬁed catalysts outperform the plain Pt/Al2O3, and K-Pt/Al2O3 results in the light-oﬀtemperatures
of 58◦C for heating 72◦C for cooling curve. To yield high activity, the potassium modiﬁed catalysts
should be reduced prior to use. This is not necessary for the iron oxide impregnated catalyst. During
experiments, hysteresis is observed, however hysteresis decrease for the Fe-Pt/Al2O3 catalyst.
In-house catalysts are prepared for comparison by wet impregnation from γ-Al2O3 and H2PtCl6·
6 H2O.
Modiﬁers are co-impregnated and the ﬁnal catalysts are reduced.
These catalysts show
similar trends as the catalysts from Sigma Aldrich, however the catalysts based on 1wt% Pt/Al2O3
from Sigma Aldrich exhibit higher activity. Dispersion is measured by pulse titration experiments,
and the in-house catalysts show signiﬁcantly higher dispersion (82 % versus 21 % for Sigma Aldrich
catalyst).
Two models are developed with rate expressions for one or two rate determining steps, and ﬁtted
to experimental data. Both models perform well, and only one rate determining step is needed to
predict CO conversion and propose kinetic parameters. The CO adsorption enthalpy was predicted at
-119 and -144 kJ/mol, which corresponds well with values obtained from the literature (-121 kJ/mol
at 0.11 ML). The model predicts conversion at too low temperatures and further development of the
model should include adsorption enthalpies as a function of surface coverage.

Resumé
Udstødningsgasser fra dieselmotorer udgør en betydelig sundhedsrisiko og de er skadelige for miljøet.
Diesel Oxidation Catalyst (DOC) er en del af efterbehandlingen af udstødningsgasser i diesel biler og
den er ansvarlig for oxidation af CO, kulbrinter og omdannelse af NO til NO2. Grænseværdierne for
diesel emissioner er i dag opfyldt, men DOC’en indeholder kostbare ædelmetaller. Derfor bør videreud-
viklingen af denne enhed omfatte en reducering af ædelmetaller uden at kompromittere katalysatorens
aktivitet. En 1 wt% Pt/Al2O3 katalysator fra Sigma Aldrich har vist gode resultater for CO oxidation
(50% omdannelse ved ↑116 ◦C og ↓99◦C). Derfor fokuserer denne rapport på additiver til Pt/Al2O3
som giver mere eﬀektive katalysatorer for CO oxidation ved lave temperaturer.
I denne rapport er to forskellige additiver blevet testet (Fe2O3 og K). Disse er imprægneret på
en 1 wt% Pt/Al2O3 i to forskellige vægtforhold for hvert additiv. Kalium skulle bevirke at elek-
trondensitet af platin reduceres hvorved Pt-CO-båndet bliver svagere.
Jernoxid antages at levere
oxygen til reaktionen med carbonmonoxid. Katalysatorerne er blevet testet for CO oxidation på en
stempelstrømsreaktor i laboratorieskala. Begge typer af modiﬁcerede katalysatorer præsterer bedre
end katalysatoren kun med platin. Især den kaliummodiﬁcerede katalysator viser lave temperaturer
ved 50% omdannelse (↑58◦C og ↓72◦C). Den høje aktivitet ved lave temperature opnås kun hvis
katalysatoren har været reduceret. Dette er ikke nødvendigt for jernoxidimprægnerede katalysatorer.
Under forsøgene er hysterese blevet observeret, dog er hysterese mindre udpræget for de jernoxidim-
prægnerede Sigma Aldrich katalysator.
Til sammenligning med Sigma Aldrich katalysatoren er lignende katalysatorer blevet lavet fra γ-
Al2O3 og H2PtCI6·6 H2O, og lignende modiﬁcerede katalysatorer er lavet ved samimprægnering. De
endelige katalysatorer reduceres. Disse interne katalysatorer viser lignende tendenser som katalysator-
erne fra Sigma Aldrich. De udviser dog ikke lige så høj aktivitet. Dispersion er målt ved pulstitrerings-
forsøg og de interne katalysatorer har betydeligt højere dispersion (82% mod 21% for Sigma Aldrich
katalysator).
To modeller er udviklet med hastighedsudtryk for en eller to hastighedsbestemmende trin. Disse
modeller er ﬁttet til eksperimentelle data.
Begge modeller klarer sig godt og kun et hastigheds-
bestemmende trin er nødvendigt for at forudsige CO-omdannelse og ﬁnde kinetiske parametre. CO
adsorptionsenthalpi blev estimeret til -119 og -144 kJ/mol, hvilket stemmer godt overens med værdier
fundet i litteraturen (-121 kJ/mol). Modellen forudsiger omdannelse ved for lave temperaturer, og
videreudvikling af modellen kan omfatte adsorptionsentalpier som funktion af dækning af platin.

Background
Increasingly stringent regulations on diesel vehicle emissions motivate further development of more
advanced diesel exhaust treatment systems, targeting regulated pollutants such as hydrocarbons, CO,
NOx, and particulate matter. Currently, a standard diesel exhaust system consists of four diﬀerent
units. The Diesel Oxidation Catalyst (DOC) is responsible for oxidation of hydrocarbons and CO.
Furthermore, an optimized DOC unit would generate NO2 from NO as this species is more reactive,
and can be used as reactant in subsequent exhaust treatment components. NO2 can be used for soot
oxidation in the Diesel Particulate Filter (DPF), and for fast Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR).
The DPF ﬁlters particulate matter from the exhaust gas, while NOx is reduced to N2 using ammonia
as reducing agent in the SCR component. Ammonia is supplied prior to the SCR through controlled
injection of urea solution. As ammonia is a pollutant itself, the last unit in the diesel exhaust after-

treatment system consists of an Ammonia Slip Catalyst (ASC).
Scope of Thesis
This project will focus on preparing and testing alternative formulations of the Diesel Oxidation
Catalyst. Currently, the DOC is an expensive after-treatment component as it contains noble metals.
It is commonly made of 1 wt% mixture of Pt and Pd on Al2O3. To create a competitive product,
further development of the DOC could involve a decrease in noble metals without compromising the
eﬃciency. Therefore, this project will consist of identiﬁcation, synthesis and testing of alternative
DOC formulations, which are modiﬁed from the current DOC using less expensive materials. To
better understand the results, a kinetic study of a speciﬁc DOC formulation will be conducted.
The work will comprise the following tasks:
• Literature review regarding basic theory of the after-treatment system and DOC chemistry,
with emphasis on identifying potential alternative DOC formulations
• Introductory measurements on CO oxidation using a commercial Pt-based catalyst
• Synthesis of alternative DOC formulations, selected based on the literature review
• Testing of alternative DOC formulations on a small scale plug ﬂow reactor emulating the on-
board DOC unit.
• Kinetic study of a speciﬁc DOC formulation, resulting in proposed kinetic parameters for the
reaction.
This thesis is part of a larger research project at the Department of Chemical and Biochemical Engi-
neering about next generation exhaust gas cleaning technologies for diesel vehicles.
Learning Objectives
Competences within analysis, synthesis and evaluation of test results will be used and developed while
working with the master thesis. The following objectives will be achieved:
• Reﬁned skills within state-of-the-art research and application of advanced theory will be a
result of the research, which are to form basis for identiﬁcation of possible alternative DOC
formulations.
• Competences within analysis are developed when interpreting test results and evaluating the
plug-ﬂow reactor set-up.
• General laboratory skills are learned during the experimental phase. This includes planning,
executing and managing experimental test set-up.
• Understanding and critical evaluation of the ﬁeld of study will be applied throughout the project.
This project will contain elements of both literature research, experimental work and mathematical
modelling and therefore several diﬀerent learning objectives are to be achieved.

Introduction
In the recent decades extensive focus on reducing environmentally harmful emissions has led to in-
creasingly stringent regulations on diesel vehicular emissions. A signiﬁcant increase in automotive
emission in the developed countries causes smog and awareness of the potential environmental impact
and adverse health eﬀects. Therefore, the Clean Air Act was introduced in USA in the late 1960s
and the ﬁrst European regulations were made in 1970 [1, 2]. Both Europe, USA, and to some extent
Japan, have been regulating emissions for several decades and are leading the trend of more strin-
gent regulations. Since the 1990s, the emission legislation has become signiﬁcantly more stringent as
technologies have been developed, allowing for reduced emission. European emission standards since
1993 for diesel passenger cars (category M) are displayed in Table 1.1.

Table 1.1 represents European emission standards for diesel passenger cars (category M), however
similar tendencies are seen for more heavy duty vehicles. Countries all over the world are increasingly
concerned about limiting emissions, and some countries base their emission regulation on the earlier
European and American emission standards [5]. Comparison of emission standards is very compli-
cated since emission regulation in the various countries are often based on diﬀerent test cycles [1]. In
the coming years, legislations are expected to become even more stringent, and a leading edge in the
diesel exhaust after-treatment technology would result in commercialisation and proﬁt.
Diesel after-treatment systems consist of four diﬀerent units. These are designed to reduce some of
the pollutants and hazardous compounds in raw diesel exhaust. This includes hydrocarbons, carbon
monoxide, oxides of nitrogen and particulate matter. The ﬁrst diesel after-treatment unit is the Diesel
Oxidation Catalyst (DOC), responsible for decreasing carbon monoxide and hydrocarbons to meet
legislation limits. Furthermore, this unit should oxidise nitrogen monoxide to nitrogen dioxide, which
is beneﬁcial for subsequent unit. The main focus in this report is a DOC unit for next generation
exhaust gas cleaning technologies for diesel vehicles.
Currently, well-functioning DOC catalysts contain relatively high amounts of expensive noble met-
als (∼1 wt% mixture of Pt and Pd). The aim of this report is to reduce the amount of noble metal
by additives or modiﬁers, while maintaining high eﬃciency to comply with the stringent legislation.
The ﬁnal product is analysis and test data of potential new generation DOC catalysts.
The thesis includes of a literature review to determine state-of-the-art DOC units. Furthermore, po-

2.1
Diesel Engine Fundamentals
A diesel engine is powered by a cycle consisting of intake, compression, expansion and exhaust. The
cycle begins with admission of air into the combustion chamber where the air is compressed to ∼4.0
MPa [6]. Due to compression, the air temperature is increased to ∼550◦C. After compression, a
small amount of fuel is injected into the hot combustion chamber [6]. When mixed with hot air, the
fuel evaporates and subsequently auto-ignites thereby releasing energy stored within the fuel. Finally,
the exhaust is released to the after-treatment system. Since highly compressed air in the combustion
chamber causes the fuel to ignite, diesel engines are also named compression-ignition (CI) engines.
Diesel engines use a cylinder and piston arrangement, where released energy is converted to work as
the piston is moved by the expansion of the gas [1].
Advantages of diesel engines over gasoline engines include superior fuel economy, superior per-
formance, and generally higher longevity [1, 7]. Since the diesel engine provides lower speciﬁc fuel
consumption, these are often advantageously selected over gasoline engines for large vehicles, especially
trucks and ships.

2.2.1
Formation and Characteristics of NOx, HC, CO, and PM
All of the regulated species are minor constituents in diesel exhaust gas, however they pose signiﬁcant
health and environmental hazards. The origin of each species is explained in this section.
Nitrogen oxides, NOx
Nitrogen oxides are formed in the diﬀusion region of ﬂames, and are a result of side reactions in
the combustion chamber. The following reaction is thermodynamically favoured in the combustion
chamber, where high oxygen concentration and temperatures prevail [9]:
N2 + O2 ⇀
↽2 NO
(2.2)
The equilibrium for reaction (2.2) moves far to the left once the exhaust gas continues to the after-
treatment system and temperature and pressure decrease. This should lead to decomposition of the
formed nitrogen monoxide. The reaction rate for decomposition, however, is very slow and therefore
nitrogen monoxide poses a serious concern when dealing with exhaust gas treatment. After com-
bustion, nitrogen dioxide is formed from oxygen and nitrogen monoxide according to the following
equilibrium:
NO + 1
2 O2 ⇀
↽NO2
(2.3)
Usually, nitrogen monoxide is most abundant with only a small part of the nitrogen oxide emissions
consisting of nitrogen dioxide or other similar species (<30%). However, nitrogen dioxide is highly
toxic and extremely reactive. All nitrogen oxides are active ozone precursors, and as of year 2009,
49% of all NOx pollution originates from traﬃc sources. Therefore, NOx is considered one of the most
critical pollutants found in diesel exhaust [1, 8, 10].
Hydrocarbons, HC
Hydrocarbon emissions are mainly a result of incomplete combustion as ideal mixing is not obtained
in the combustion chamber. The hydrocarbons consist of a mixture of many hydrocarbon species,
including partially decomposed fuel molecules and recombined intermediate compounds such as alde-
hydes and alcohols [9, 10]. The various hydrocarbons have diﬀerent properties and present diﬀerent
hazards. Some of them, including benzene and formaldehyde, are toxic and/or carcinogenic. Further-
more, hydrocarbons are ozone precursors, as they undergo photochemical reactions with NOx. The

hydrocarbon species originates from diesel fuel or lubricating oil and typically, hydrocarbon emission
is measured as total hydrocarbon emission (THC). Methane exhibits diﬀerent atmospheric activity
compared to longer chain HCs, and therefore methane is sometimes excluded by setting regulations
for nonmethane hydrocarbons (NMHCs).
Hydrocarbons in diesel exhaust exist in two phases - gaseous and particulate. In the latter the

volatile hydrocarbon species are regulated as particulate matter, though no clear distinction between
volatile and non-volatile hydrocarbons exist [1].
Carbon monoxide, CO
As for the majority of hydrocarbon emissions, carbon monoxide is a result of incomplete combustion
due to lack of oxygen or low temperatures [10]. CO emission from diesel engines today are relatively
low due to high Air-to-Fuel ratios (A/F) of more than 22:1, but emission increases during transient
operating behaviour such as start-up and acceleration [7, 8, 11].
Particulate Matter, PM
One of the major harmful emissions from diesel exhaust is particular matter (PM). PM does not
consist of well-deﬁned species, but is deﬁned as any matter in the exhaust that can be trapped on
a sampling ﬁlter medium at 52◦C [10]. PM mainly originates from organic and inorganic substances
inducted to the engine and are a result of the liquid fuel injection. Particulate matter emissions are
very harmful, and they are considered one of the major disadvantages of diesel engine exhaust [1].
Traditionally, particulate matter is divided into three main categories forming the total particulate
matter (TPM) [1]:
• Solid fraction (SOL)
• Soluble organic fraction (SOF)
• Sulphate particulates (SO4)
SOL primarily consists of elemental carbon formed during combustion, but also includes ash. Ash
originates from combustion of lubricating oil additives and metal oxide impurities from engine cor-
rosion. As explained in a previous section, liquid or absorbed hydrocarbons are considered PM and
constitute the category, SOF. SOF hydrocarbons typically originate from lubricating oil or high boil-
ing fuel hydrocarbons. At the diesel exhaust temperatures, most of the SOF is in vapour phase, and it
liqueﬁes only after cooling below 52◦C. The last category, SO4, mainly consists of hydrated sulphuric
acid, but may also include sulphate salts [1]. The majority of sulphur in raw diesel exhaust exists as
gaseous SO2 which is not regulated.

to possible adverse health eﬀects from exposure to the smaller particles, PM2.5. The particle size
distribution by number is bimodal with two peaks representing nuclei and accumulative mode. The

2.3
Diesel Exhaust Treatment System
Today, diesel vehicles are equipped with extensive after-treatment systems to reduce regulated emis-
sion species (NOx, CO, HC and PM). Such systems consist of a series of monolithic catalyst units.
An illustration of a standard diesel exhaust treatment system is displayed in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Illustration of standard diesel exhaust after-treatment system
The raw exhaust gas leaves the combustion chamber, and is directed to a Diesel Oxidation Catalyst
(DOC) that treats HC and CO and oxidises a part of NO to NO2. The latter oxidation is required
since NO2 is highly reactive and therefore useful in the subsequent units. After the DOC, particulate
matter is ﬁltered in a Diesel Particulate Filter (DPF). Following the DPF, NOx is reduced to N2 via
Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR). Ammonia is used as reducing agent, and is injected into the
exhaust gas stream through controlled injection of a urea solution. Finally, excess NH3 is oxidized to
N2 by an Ammonia Slip Catalyst (ASC) [12].
In this report, the main focus is the DOC unit. Therefore, the other three components are only
brieﬂy reviewed in the following sections while the DOC will be thoroughly reviewed in Section 2.4.
Diesel engine modiﬁcations to achieve emission reduction, as e.g. Exhaust Gas Recirculation (EGR),
will not be discussed in this report.

2.3.3
Ammonia Slip Catalyst (ASC)
Most of the injected ammonia is converted to nitrogen and water by reaction with NOx in the SCR.
Ideally, the NH3/NOx ratio is 1, however high NOx conversion is often achieved by increasing this
ratio, leading to ammonia slip. The excess ammonia is removed downstream the SCR by an ASC via
selective oxidation to nitrogen [9, 11].
4 NH3 + 3 O2 ⇀
↽2 N2 + 6 H2O
(2.7)
The reaction presented is an overall reaction and actually the ASC is dual layer catalyst consisting
of a lower platinum based oxidation layer and an upper SCR layer. Ammonia is stored in the upper
layer, and upon diﬀusion to the lower layer the platinum based catalyst oxidises NH3 to NOx species.
As NOx passes through the upper layer SCR occurs where stored ammonia converts NOx to N2 [15].
Eﬃcient ASC should achieve high oxidation activity and low formation of NO and N2O. Commercial
catalysts achieve well over 90 % conversion of NH3 to nitrogen depending on the operating conditions
[16].

The DOC unit is based on a monolithic support - typically made from ceramics, however metal

The monolith is cylindrical and has a honeycomb structure, with a multitude of small linear channels.
Advantages of this design over regular catalyst pellets include minimal pressure drop, high geometric
surface area and excellent attrition resistance. Most commonly, the ceramic monolith is made from
porous cordierite. This material consists of 2 MgO · 2 Al2O3 · 5 SiO2, but trace amounts of NaO2,
Fe2O3, and CaO are also present. The porosity of the walls is in the range 20-40 vol%, and mostly
originates from macropores [11, 17].
Several diﬀerent types of monolith are on the market, and the conﬁguration and geometry should
be chosen carefully as this aﬀects diﬀerent aspects of the catalyst performance. The cell density is
deﬁned as the number of cells per unit frontal surface area, and this has great impact on fouling and
catalyst eﬃciency. Lower cell densities are usually preferred as the wider channels prevent plugging
due to fouling by particulate matter. The DOC can therefore operate for extended periods at relatively
low temperatures (∼150◦C). However, increasing the cell density will result in larger surface area
and therefore higher eﬃciency. Another factor to consider is the pressure drop. This factor decreases
with large open frontal area, implying that the wall thickness should be as thin as possible. The wall
thickness is however limited by factors such as ﬂow distribution and mechanical strength. Furthermore,
the monolith should be light weight and resistant to rapid temperature change [11, 14, 17].

ing in Reynolds numbers in the range of 100-400 depending on the operating conditions. Although
this is higher than for equivalent gasoline systems, the ﬂow through the monolith is still in the laminar

2.4.2

The monolithic base is covered with a thin washcoat layer, mainly to ensure high surface area. The
majority of the washcoat consists of a carrier, usually inexpensive highly porous materials such as

in higher conversion. The carrier material should be chosen well as it has an impact of the DOC
eﬃciency. E.g. a study on hydrocarbon oxidation by Haneda et al. shows that Al2O3-based platinum
catalysts ages better than TiO2-, ZSM-5- or ZrO2 based platinum catalysts [20].

catalytic promoters and catalytic modiﬁers. These additives have very speciﬁc tasks, e.g. preven-

Alumina
A variety of diﬀerent alumina exists, diﬀering in structure, surface area and composition. The main
component of alumina is of course Al2O3, but it can be modiﬁed to contain small amounts of other
substances, e.g., Fe2O3, Na2O, and SiO2. Alumina exists in several polymorphous, and for DOC

application, usually a mixture of porous amorphous γ−, θ−and δ−alumina is used. These polymor-
phous can be used as catalysts themselves due to Brønsted bases/acid sites. γ-Al2O3 show instability
at high temperature, where it is converted to the crystalline α-phase. This is managed with stabilizer

Properties of alumina include high thermal, mechanical stability, and several other properties,

tend not to sit on the surface, and thermal degradation of the catalyst is inhibited. Furthermore,

the most common carrier material in the DOC industry [14].
Zeolites
Zeolites are inorganic compounds based on a three-dimensional lattice structure of silicon and alu-
minium, tetrahedrally bound to oxygen. Neutral zeolites are entirely based on silicon and oxygen, but
an overall negative charge is generated for each silicon atom replaced with aluminium. This excess of
electrons is counterbalanced by charge-compensating cations placed on the zeolite surface. Similarly
to alumina, zeolites also have very high speciﬁc surface area, stemming from pores and cages in the
structure. Zeolites are built from 18 diﬀerent units called secondary building units (SBU) that, put
together, create various uniform channels and cages [22]. The large number of building units means
that the zeolites can be very speciﬁcally designed. For the DOC component, zeolites are very useful in
storage of SOF at low temperatures when conversion is less eﬀective. The zeolite adsorbs the gaseous
pollutants, and retain them until the temperature increases enough to ensure conversion. This is
especially useful for transient operation [9].
Metal oxides
Metal oxides are numerous and can be found in a large variety of structures. For the DOC technology,
TiO2 can be used as a support to yield sulphur resistant catalysts. The disadvantage of this type
catalyst carrier is however smaller speciﬁc surface area and poor thermal stability [23]. Research have
shown that the lower activity can be increase with addition of yttrium [23].
In DOC’s, metal oxides are often used as additives rather than main carrier material as they can
potentially enhance catalytic features and improve catalysis. E.g. a series of patents propose to use
cerium dioxide (CeO2) as a division layer between two diﬀerent catalyst (e.g. Pt and Pd) to ensure
that they do not poison or counteract each other [24]. Furthermore, cerium dioxide can improve
tolerance towards sulphur [9].
Another metal oxide additive is zirconium oxide, which enhances
stability and prevents catalyst degradation and deactivation. Promising catalytic supports are made
from a mixture of cerium oxide and zirconium oxide providing a combination of sulphur tolerance and
high stability [25].

2.5.1
Sintering
Thermal degradation is a physical process leading to catalyst deactivation.
The most signiﬁcant
form of thermal degradation for the diesel exhaust gas operating conditions is sintering. Sintering
is a process which leads to catalyst deactivation due to structural modiﬁcation. Many factors aﬀect
sintering e.g. pressure, porosity, atmosphere, etc., and the sintering process is highly temperature
dependent, with sintering rates increasing exponentially with temperature [28, 29].
Initially, the active phase is present as small clusters, but when exposed to high temperatures,
these clusters may grow. Sintering of supported metals is very complex and diﬀerent mechanisms for
the process have been proposed. Atomic migration involves detachment and migration of a single
surface atom, which is then captured by larger crystallites.
The second mechanism is crystallite
migration, where entire crystallites migrate, collide, and coalesce with other aggregates.
A third
mechanism involves vapour transport, but this only occurs at temperatures much higher than the
DOC temperatures and is not relevant for these studies. Both of the former mechanisms cause the
total surface area of the metal particulate to decrease and ultimately leads to fewer active sites. If the
catalytic activity is structure sensitive, the eﬀect of sintering can be enlarged, and the loss in activity
can be greater than the corresponding surface area loss [28–30].
Sintering is irreversible or diﬃcult to reverse, and should be prevented rather than cured. Struc-
tural promoters can inhibit surface migration and reduce sintering. Alumina and silica are considered
very thermostable supports in oxidizing atmospheres. Well-chosen additives or impurities may also
be used to prevent migration as these interact with and ﬁx the metal clusters [28, 29].
Most thermal deactivation in the DOC is caused by agglomeration of HC and PM on the catalyst
surface, causing local hotspots when oxidized [9, 27]. New and promising DOC catalysts must be
thermally stable as even small thermal degradation may over time lead to violation of emission limits.

2.5.2
Poisoning
Poisoning is the loss of activity as a result strong chemisorption of impurities on otherwise available
catalytic sites. Usually, a distinction between inhibitors and poisons are made. Whereas the former
only creates weak bonds with the surface and is reversible, the latter interacts strongly with the sites
and creates irreversible bonds. A direct impact of a chemisorped poison is blockage of active sites,
however some poisons also cause electronic modiﬁcation to adjacent sites, rendering these inactive.
Therefore, poisons may cause more severe deactivation than what is anticipated [30].
Poisons are classiﬁed as selective, anti-selective, or non-selective. Selective poisons tend to interact
with the most active sites ﬁrst, while anti-selective poisons attack the weakest sites. Non-selective
poisons do not diﬀerentiate between sites and are chemisorped uniformly on the catalyst surface.
These three kinds of poisoning behaviour cause diﬀerent rates of deactivation. This is displayed in
Figure 2.9. Here it is seen that the activity loss is proportional to the concentration of adsorbed
non-selective poison. The deactivation rate is slower for anti-selective poisons, and faster for selective
poisons [29, 30].
Figure 2.9: Three kinds of poisoning behaviour plotted as normalized activity as a function of nor-
malized poison concentration [29].
Typical poisons are Pb, P, Zn, and S. Trace lead may be present in the diesel fuel, but today it
does not generally cause a problem, though lead reacts irreversibly with Pt to form an inactive alloy.
Phosphorous is a contaminant present in lubricating oils and it binds strongly with alumina. This
anti-selective poison does not cause any signiﬁcant immediate deactivation, but can eventually block
active site surfaces. Zinc has limited inﬂuence on the deactivation as it only absorbs on the outer
surface of the washcoat. Sulphur (present as SO2) is a selective poison of concern regarding noble

metal catalysts [17, 21, 30].
As previously mentioned, some carrier materials have aﬃnity for the poisons and adsorb them.
This relieves catalytic sites and extends catalyst lifetime. Especially metal oxides-based catalysts
can be very resistant to poisons. Furthermore, selective poisons are used to intentionally alter the
selectivity of catalyst in order to repress conversion of unwanted by-products. For the DOC technology,
V2O5 is used as a selective poison to ensure minimal selectivity and oxidation of SO2 [30, 31]. Poisons
can accumulate on the catalyst surface and over time, even trace amounts of poison in the feed stream
can cause signiﬁcant deactivation of the catalyst, and thereby aﬀect the catalyst durability [30].
2.5.3
Fouling
Fouling is deﬁned as physical deposition of molecular species on the catalyst surface and in the
catalyst pores.
The species do not interact chemically with the active sites.
Instead they limit
the pore area, creating diﬀusion resistance or block entire pores leaving the active sites inaccessible,
ultimately resulting in activity loss. The deposits are either residues from mechanical wear, dust and
impurities in feed, or by-products from the catalytic reactions, implying that the catalyst itself is
responsible for fouling. A typical fouling substance for the DOC is coke, produced by polymerisation
and dehydrogenation of organic molecules, originated from fuel hydrocarbons and lubricating oil
[9, 28]. Fouling and coke deposition is best prevented by an appropriate washcoat pore structure,
designed to tolerate large amounts of fouling species [32].
2.5.4
Other Types of Deactivation
Several other mechanisms can lead to catalyst deactivation, one of which is vapour-solid reactions.
This involves chemical modiﬁcations of the active site causing activity loss due to formation of a new
phase. Examples hereof include oxidation, sulphurization, or carbidation of the metal clusters.
DOC catalysts are made from monoliths and deactivation can occur by irreversible mechanical
breakage of the monolith pore structure. This is mainly due to the fact that exhaust gas treatment
system is situated on-board the vehicle and may be susceptible to attrition. The monolith washcoat
layer itself can also be lost due to high linear gas velocities or thermal stress. Proper adhesion of the
washcoat to the monolith is an important issue, which should be considered upon design of the next
generation DOC [29, 30].
2.6
Reaction Mechanisms and Kinetics
In heterogeneous catalysis the reaction takes place on active sites on the catalyst surface and involves
adsorption of reactants. For DOC chemistry all reactions are bimolecular, and two diﬀerent reaction
mechanism theories exist, Eley-Rideal and Langmuir-Hinshelwood.

==PAGE:33==

Eley-Rideal Mechanism
The Eley-Rideal mechanism proposes that one reactant reacts directly out of the gas phase without
adsorption. This mechanism is described in Figure 2.10 for the oxidation of CO.
Figure 2.10: Eley-Rideal reaction mechanism of CO oxidation [33].
According to the Eley-Rideal mechanism, three main steps occur for heterogeneous catalysis of CO
oxidation. First, oxygen chemisorps to the catalyst surface while undergoing dissociation. The disso-
ciated oxygen atom then reacts directly with the gaseous CO by replacing the bond to the catalyst
surface with a stronger C-O bond. Finally, the product is released [21].
Langmuir-Hinshelwood Mechanism
For the Langmuir-Hinshelwood mechanism it is assumed that all species are adsorbed on the catalyst
surface before reaction takes place. This mechanism is described in Figure 2.11 for oxidation of CO.
Figure 2.11: Langmuir-Hinshelwood reaction mechanism of CO oxidation [33].
Similar to the Eley-Rideal mechanism, ﬁrst an oxygen molecule is dissociatively chemisorped. The
second step consists of adsorption of CO on a diﬀerent active site than oxygen. CO does not dissociate,
but creates a bond (Pt-CO) over a hollow space between metal atoms. The two absorbed species
migrate near each other, and then create a more stable C-O bond, were oxygen detaches from the
catalyst surface and attaches to the CO molecule. Finally, the product is released. The mechanism
for oxidation of HC is identical, except for the adsorbed reactant specie [21].
The Langmuir-Hinshelwood mechanism is more prominent in heterogeneous catalysis, and cur-
rently this is also the case for diesel oxidation catalysis.
Many observations favour this reaction
mechanism, and at high temperature, experimental works support the Langmuir-Hinshelwood mech-
anism. However, some observations contradict the opinion of only Langmuir-Hinshelwood kinetics.
A catalyst surface completely covered with adsorbed oxygen will still result in conversion of CO to

Several diﬀerent mechanisms have been proposed to explain CO oxidation at low temperatures. One

2.8
Carbon Monoxide Oscillations
For the catalytic oxidation of CO over PGMs, spontaneous oscillations in CO2 conversion is often
observed even under ﬁxed reaction conditions. The oscillations can be long (up to 1 h), and this
suggests that kinetics alone cannot account for the phenomena. Sales et al. suggest that the oscil-
lations are due to slow and reversible modiﬁcation of the catalyst surface, e.g. alternate oxidation
and reduction of the metal surface [39]. This behaviour is coupled with shifts in catalytic activity
of platinum [39]. Stable and pronounced oscillations mostly occur for highly dispersed catalysts, im-
plying that the oxidation takes place on low-coordinated corner and edge Pt-sites. The oxidation is
more prone to occur in high temperature environments. Vendelbo et al. propose that the oscillations
are due to adsorbate-induced restructuring at low pressures and switch between metallic and oxidized
platinum at higher pressures (> 1 mbar) [40]. The latter is dominant at diesel exhaust conditions.
Two branches for the reaction rate exist for the two diﬀerent states (oxidized or metallic). The switch
between the two branches results in an oscillation [41].

Proposed Modiﬁers to Enhance Performance
In DOC technology, the primary catalytic component is often platinum, and for this report modiﬁca-
tion of platinum catalysts will be of main focus as a commercial 1 wt% Pt/Al2O3 has shown promising
activity. Although the benchmark catalyst perform well, platinum is an expensive resource, and for
new generation DOC’s, it is desired to enhance or maintain the performance, while eliminating some
of the highly expensive platinum metal. Diﬀerent modiﬁers for Pt/Al2O3 have been proposed in the
literature, and some are presented in this section. The most promising catalytic modiﬁers should be
further investigated and tested in the micro-reactor set-up.

is formation of hydroxyls in proximity or on the active sites. These hydroxyls may react with carbon
monoxide and form the oxidation product more easily. A similar mechanism is assumed to form basis
for the promotional eﬀects of metal oxides. The eﬀect of the alkali ion promoters is very dependent
on the amount added. Based on the results presented by Minemura et al., the optimal promoting

2.9.5
Acid/Base Promoters for NO Oxidation
Pt/Al2O3 catalysts are used in DOC’s for their high activity and stability towards NO oxidation.
The formation of NO2 decreases when the catalyst is oxidatively pretreated. This is presumed to be
due to the presence of platinum oxides. Some properties of the support can suppress the formation
of the platinum oxides. An acidic support is electrophilic, and will cause the platinum to be electron
deﬁcient, which will make it less susceptible towards formation of platinum oxides.
A study by

2.9.6
Dual Layer Catalysts
In the previous sections modiﬁers to a commercial DOC catalyst 1w% Pt/Al2O3 have been discussed.
The commercial catalyst has proven very eﬃcient. However, for the next generation of DOCs, a more
novel catalyst should also be considered. Several patents are made, which include multiple layers of
catalyst, each attending to a certain function. For example, ceria could be used for oxygen storage,
while zeolites would be responsible for storage of SOF at low temperatures. One patented example
of a layered catalyst (year 2012) consists of a layer consisting of palladium (platinum is optional)
impregnated on a support comprising ceria in an amount of at least 45 w%. The second layer is
located on the ﬁrst layer and consists of a metal oxide support with platinum and palladium. The
weight ratio of platinum to palladium should be higher than the ﬁrst layer [55].
This type of dual layer catalyst could also be used to reduce platinum content in DOC catalysts.
Based on results presented in Section 2.9.2, large amounts of platinum can be exchanged with palla-
dium without compromising catalytic activity. Presently, this will however result in an unacceptable
reduction in NO oxidation. A solution could be to mix a catalyst with high palladium content with
a catalyst suitable for oxidisation of NO to NO2.

2.10
Discussion on Literature Review
The ﬁnal aim for this report is to develop a new generation DOC, using less of the expensive platinum,
while maintaining or enhancing catalytic performance. The Literature Review is performed in order
to gain knowledge of potential catalyst formulations, which will result in enhanced low temperature
catalytic activity.
Several diﬀerent options for modiﬁcation of the commercial 1 w% Pt/Al2O3 catalyst were pre-
sented in the previous sections. First, it was shown that a Pt/Al2O3 is structure-insensitive and as
a consequence, activity is proportional to Pt dispersion, which should be as high as possible. Plat-
inum is not thermally stable and is susceptible towards sintering, therefore modiﬁers should account
for this thermal instability. Another possibility to enhance both performance and protect against
sintering is to replace some of the platinum with the less expensive palladium. These types of DOC
optimizations has undergone extensive research and well-functioning stabilizers and Pt/Pd catalysts
are already commercially available. Therefore, these type of modiﬁers will not be further investigated
in the experimental section.
Based on the diﬀerent articles on enhancing CO oxidation studied for the Literature Review, the
main goal is to weaken the CO-Pt bond, to hinder the formation of platinum oxides or to provide sites
for oxygen supply. The former two can be achieved by causing electron deﬁciency in the platinum
metal. On basis of the studies presented in the previous section, alkali metal ions and acidic oxides
will result in electron deﬁcient platinum, and NO, CO and HC conversion will all occur at lower
temperatures. Of particular interest for the next generation DOC’s are potassium ions that show
high activity towards CO conversion. The eﬀect of alkali, magnesium and acidic oxides should be
further investigated and compared with the benchmark catalyst. Even though many metal oxides are
basic, they promote CO oxidation. This is thought to be due to non-competitive dual site adsorption,
where a metal oxide provides the oxygen atom used in the oxidation reaction. Especially, iron oxide
is of interest as it enhances oxidation signiﬁcantly. The metal oxide does however, aﬀect the platinum
surface electron density and experiments will show whether NO and HC oxidation are aﬀected. The
results obtained from the Literature Review have been obtained for a variety of reaction and pre-
treatment conditions. Many of the experiments have been performed for PROX under hydrogen rich
conditions, and it should be tested whether the results can be replicated for CO oxidation under diesel
exhaust gas conditions.
This report will only focus on the DOC, and treatment of CO, HC and conversion of NO to NO2.
SOF is considered included in the HC and will not be investigated as such. The removal of PM,
and reduction of NO and NO2 will not be considered. The objective for the following experimental
section is to ﬁnd necessary reaction conditions and further investigate whether the modiﬁed catalyst
performs better than the benchmark. A well-functioning catalyst could potentially be part of the
next generation of DOCs.

3.1.1
Impregnation Procedure
Diﬀerent impregnation procedures are applied for preparation of the catalysts presented in Table 3.1.
The benchmark catalyst is already prepared with platinum and subsequent impregnation of iron and

Incipient wetness impregnation
The benchmark catalyst is supplied with platinum, and subsequent impregnation of the modiﬁers
are applied using incipient wetness impregnation. An impregnation solution is prepared from the
respective nitrate, and the incipient wetness method utilises capillary forces to draw the solution into
the catalyst pores. This process is much faster than the comparable diﬀusion process. The following
steps describe the incipient wetness impregnation method used in this study.
• Estimate pore volume of support by dripping solvent until slight ﬁlm formation.
• Prepare aqueous solution containing nitrate of modiﬁer to exactly match the loading needed.
• Drip exact volume onto support while stirring/shaking.
• Dry moist catalyst at 80◦C for ≈3 h.
The disadvantage of incipient wetness impregnation is that the maximum metal loading is limited
by the solubility in the solvent. If higher loadings are required, the impregnation process should be
repeated. Drying of the catalyst is quick as only a small amount of ﬂuid is applied. Potassium is highly
mobile and these molecules are presumed to easily disperse on the catalyst surface. The potassium
impregnated catalyst samples are therefore dried in a regular oven in stagnant air. Iron does not
disperse as easily as potassium. An article by Munnik et al. suggests that drying in nitrogen ﬂow

is to be tested. Even though the ABB continuous gas analyser does not measure hydrocarbons, the
hydrocarbon conversion can be determined indirectly from a mass balance as all carbon dioxide is
obtained from either carbon monoxide or hydrocarbons. For this project, mainly carbon monoxide
conversion is to be tested, however the possibility for addition of propene is present.

which exceed the limits of the continuous gas analyser. The GC-MS is from Thermo Scientiﬁc and is

3.3.3
Sources of Error
Several diﬀerent sources of error for the 037-03 Selective Oxidation set-up exist, most of which do not
inﬂuence the results of screening signiﬁcantly, however they can be responsible for small ﬂuctuations
and deviations from experiment to experiment.
One of the most diﬃcult tasks when running the micro-reactor set-up is ensuring similar reactor
packing for each experiment. Diﬃculties occur as the catalyst and inert material are used in small
quantities, and to compare results, the quantities must be the same for each experiment. This is not
exactly true and small variations are to be expected. If the variations are too large, the diﬀerence in
catalyst weight must be accounted for. Furthermore, the particles tend to adhere to the weighting
boat due to friction forces. An anti-static fan is used to cancel the static electricity and get all particles
transferred to the reactor.
Another inconsistency is the mixing of inert material and catalyst. Ideal mixing is not obtained,
which is visualised by slight colour shade diﬀerences in the bed. This is also due to the fact that
48
==PAGE:63==
Chapter 3. Experimental Procedure
smaller particles tend to settle on top of the bed.
The mixing is diﬀerent for each experiment;
however utmost care is taken to ensure as good mixing as possible. Discrepancies with respect to
void fraction is another source of error. To make this factor less signiﬁcant the reactor is tapped a
few times. The diﬀerence in void fraction is diﬃcult to measure, as the height of the catalyst bed
is measured with only one signiﬁcant digit. The void fraction discrepancy is also inﬂuenced by the
catalyst and inert size fraction. Both are sieved into the desired size fraction, however the average
particle size is not known and could be diﬀerent for the catalyst and inert material.
Furthermore, the sieved catalyst and inert glass beads are stored in larger quantities. Therefore,
care must be taken to use a mix of particles and not only the top layer as this is likely to consist of
the smaller particles. The catalyst particles are very delicate and attrition could be a factor during
weighing and transferral. During the actual packing of the reactor, the bottom layer of quartz wool is
to be completely level. This is diﬃcult to obtain and may disrupt the catalyst bed layer. All of these
factors leads to potential violation of ideal assumptions and entails slight diﬀerences for each reactor.
Sources of error also exist for the actual micro-reactor set-up, 037-03. The oven temperature has
a large overshoot of ∼20 ◦C, due to the relatively fast ramp time and the controller settings. This
overshoot is similar for each run and occurs after full conversion and should therefore not pose a
signiﬁcant error. The bed temperature is measured by a thermocouple immediately after the bed and
the actual temperature of the bed is unknown. The concentration of CO in feed gas is very low (<250
ppm), and the feed ﬂow rate is high (300 NmL/min), resulting in large heat transport. Therefore,
the bed temperature can be assumed equal to the temperature immediately after the bed.
The system is thoroughly leak tested, but a small diﬀerence in ﬂow rate before and after the
reactor is observed (∼-1-2 %). Furthermore, the ﬂow rate is very dependent on room temperature
and pressure. It is only calibrated once, and these conditions vary daily. The ﬂow rate must be
at least 300 NmL/min as this is required by the continuous gas analyser, otherwise, measurements
are slightly delayed and inaccurate.
The continuous gas analyser is calibrated before every new
experiment, but calibration is not possible during the several day long experiments. Any discrepancy
should be discovered as the CO2 concentration is diﬀerent from zero, and this can be accounted for
during data processing. Estimation of water content in the gas is diﬃcult, but has been made easier
with the new humidity sensor. The ﬂow is set prior to addition of water, and when the feed gas
contain vapour, the actual ﬂow is diﬀerent from speciﬁed. This again should be accounted for during
the data processing.

Experiments Without Catalysis
A number of experiments are performed to investigate the responds without any catalyst present

For empty reactors with and without thermocouple, close to complete conversion of
both CO and C3H6 is observed at temperatures > 400◦C. CO oxidation could be caused by trace

change the outcome. The conversion sets in ∼350◦C, and any result using a packed reactor will yield
full conversion at much lower temperatures. Therefore, this phenomena is assumed irrelevant for the
purpose of screening the diesel oxidation catalysts.
Previously, SiC has been used as inert ﬁller material, however this material cannot be ground,
and tests are made to determine whether SiC can be replaced with solid glass beads. Experiments
with inerts only, resulted in larger pressure drop, which is expected since the density of glass is
approximately 20% less than the density of SiC. This should be considered when using glass beads
instead of SiC. Based on the results and previous experience, the bed is diluted 1:5 with glass beads
ground to same size fraction as the catalyst particles.

for the initial deactivation is oxidation of metallic platinum.

oscillations could also be due to slow and reversible modiﬁcation of the catalyst surface as suggested

less than 5 min for the heating curve but ∼2 h for the cooling curve. During this extended period,
the slow surface changes have better time to occur.
The other phenomena observed in Figure 4.1 is hysteresis, as there is a clear diﬀerence in light-oﬀ
temperature depending on the run history. The cooling runs exhibit lower light-oﬀtemperature at
103 ◦C compared to 115 ◦C for the third, degreened heating run. Subbotin et al. report that this
phenomenon is also observed for CO oxidation over a variety of other catalysts [64]. The multiplicity
can be explained by a number of diﬀerent reasons [65]. Abedi et al. specify that one possibility is
occurrence of local hotspots as heat created by the exothermic reaction (∆Hr = 283 kJ/mol) is larger
than the rate of dissipation. During the cooling phase, the inside particle temperature can be warmer
locally than measured [64, 65]. Local hot spots could also be the reason for the small ﬂuctuations in
the cooling curves.
Carlsson et al. report that during cooling, partially oxidized platinum is reduced by chemisorbed
carbon monoxide. The metallic platinum is more active compared to the oxidised catalyst, and the
light-oﬀtemperatures reduces [66]. Carlsson et al. furthermore report that the width of the hysteresis
loop appears to a function of particle size, which can be attributed to diﬀerent oxidation and reduction
rates of platinum at diﬀerent particle sizes [65, 66]. Hysteresis can also to some extent be caused by
strongly adsorbed carbon monoxide on the catalyst surface during heating. At low temperatures the
surface is assumed completely covered with carbon monoxide, and reaction does not take place until
oxygen can be accommodated on the catalyst surface. For the cooling curve, the active sites are
initially available.

5.4
Further Discussion of Results
As seen from the preliminary and the screening experiments, many factors eﬀect the outcome of
CO oxidation experiments.
The catalyst pre-treatment procedure is important and the platinum
catalyst should be reduced before usage. In this study, the pre-treatment was conducted at 550◦C
for 8 h. The actual DOC can peak at temperatures up to 700◦C and since deactivation is a concern
for especially the iron impregnated catalysts, the pre-treatment procedure could aﬀect the results.
Another inﬂuencing factor on light-oﬀtemperature is number of runs and whether heating or cooling
curve light-oﬀtemperature is chosen.
The latter is due to hysteresis and the number of runs is
important as the ﬁrst and second run can have reversed hysteresis probably due to sintering or initial

Chapter 6
Testing Criteria for Packed Bed
Reactor
During testing and screening of the catalysts, it is important to limit non-ideal reactor behaviour as
these phenomena can disguise the intrinsic catalytic activity [70]. The assumptions and simpliﬁcations
should also be valid to allow appropriate modelling of the reactor kinetics. The following assumptions
are made:
• The reactor can be approximated by a plug ﬂow reactor
• Axial dispersion and wall eﬀects are neglected
• Pressure drop over the bed is neglected
• External and internal mass and heat transfer limitations are neglected
• Ideal mixing of catalyst particles and inert material is assumed
• Catalyst particles are assumed spherical
• The reactor is assumed isothermal
• Any eﬀect of bed dilution is neglected
The validity of several of the assumptions presented above can be supported by testing diﬀerent
criteria.
Heat transfer limitations are not examined as the concentration of CO is very low (250
ppm), the bed is diluted (1:5) and the ﬂow through the catalyst bed is high (300 NmL/min).
6.1

Both external and internal particle mass transfer can be limited, but deviations from ideal transfer
conditions may cause unreliable results and the following criteria must be satisﬁed if mass transfer
limitations may be neglected.

individual parameters have been made. Firstly, the concentration of carbon monoxide on the catalyst
surface is assumed equal to the bulk concentration, which is estimated as an average between inlet
and outlet concentration of carbon monoxide. Actually, the carbon monoxide concentration is higher
at the beginning of the bed, while catalyst particles further down experience lower concentration.
The Weisz-Prater number is rather sensitive towards changes in the surface concentration and once
conversion sets in and the catalyst bed experience large concentration gradient, some internal mass
transfer is expected in the lower part of the catalyst bed.
In this report, the reaction rate is estimated from the amount of carbon dioxide in the outlet gas at
each data point. These data points are based on non-steady state measurements. As steady state has
not been reached, the actual reaction rate could be diﬀerent than calculated during transition between
complete conversion and no conversion. This eﬀects only the steepness of the step in Weisz-Prater
number between full conversion and no conversion, and the Weisz-Prater number is not very sensitive
to uncertainties in reaction rate.
Another assumption is estimation of the internal diﬀusion coeﬃcient based on transportation in
macro pores where Knudsen diﬀusion can be neglected. The sensitivity to changes in the eﬀective
diﬀusion coeﬃcient has been tested by researching eﬀects of a 30 % decrease. This new eﬀective
diﬀusion coeﬃcient results in small violation of the Weisz-Prater Criterion for the middle particle size
fraction (106-150 µm). Ultimately, the Weisz-Prater number is estimated based on many uncertainties
and to ensure internal diﬀusion can be neglected, the Weisz-Prater number should preferably be a lot
smaller than 0.6.

ature and pressure. Since the mass transfer coeﬃcient increases more than the surface concentration
decreases, the result is a slightly decreasing curve once the maximum reaction rate has been reached.
Similar trends are observed for the Weisz-Prater Criterion.

Future Studies
This report has focused primarily on ﬁnding, preparing and screening new catalyst formulations with
the purpose of reducing platinum content in the new generation DOC. The initial screenings have
proven successful, and the potential alternative DOCs should be further tested and developed. A
number of observations were made from the processed data, which should also be further investigated.
8.1
Further Testing with Regards to Catalysts Formulations
Impregnation with either potassium or iron has increased low temperature activity of platinum on
alumina. Both modiﬁed catalysts have been prepared with two diﬀerent ratios, however an optimal
ratio for both is yet to be found. Experiments with the catalyst with high content of potassium
(unreduced BK10) showed almost no conversion, probably due to blockage of active sites.
This
implies that some blockage does occur, and the optimal ratio is found where the enhancing eﬀect
outweighs the small decrease in number of active sites.
The 1 wt% Pt/Al2O3 from Sigma Aldrich performs signiﬁcantly better than the equivalent catalyst
prepared in-house (0.922 wt%Pt/Al2O3). Even though the in-house catalyst contains less platinum,
this is not enough to explain the large diﬀerence of 81 ◦C in light-oﬀtemperature for the cooling curves.
γ-alumina with a surface area of 153 g/mol was used for preparation of the in-house catalyst samples.
Other types of alumina should be tested for comparison. Furthermore, other types of supports show
potential for DOC purposes. TiO2 is very sulphur resistant and it would be of interest to test the
performance of 1 wt% Pt/TiO2 versus 1 wt% Pt/Al2O3.
The modiﬁers, which have successfully
improved the performance of 1 wt% Pt/Al2O3 may not function equally well on other supports.
Based on the results for CO oxidation, the highly dispersed catalyst performs worse compared to
the benchmark. To research whether the low dispersion is a contributing factor to the high activity
of the benchmark, similar catalysts are to be prepared with diﬀerent dispersions. This could be done
by impregnating the catalyst carrier with the cationic [Pt(NH3)4]2+. This ion will not be dispersed
as easily as the anionic chloroplatinate complex [PtCl6]−2 due to the point zero charge of alumina.
Therefore, the result should be lower dispersion.

Iron oxide and reduced potassium show potential for increasing low temperature CO oxidation.

Due to stringent legislation, deactivation is of concern when designing new catalysts for diesel

8.2
Further Analyses and Tests on Reactor Set-Up
To ensure comparable screening results, several factors were excluded from the experiments. Most
importantly, the synthetic diesel gas did not match real-life conditions as HC, NOx, H2O, impurities
and poisons were not present. The potential new generation DOC should be tested under presence
of C3H6, NOx, and H2O on laboratory scale as the DOC should be able to oxidise HC and to some
extent convert NO to NO2. Especially, conversion of NO to NO2 is important, as this has proven
diﬃcult for many DOC catalysts. The conversion is necessary to create optimal conditions for the
downstream exhaust treatment units.
The presence of C3H6 and HC will increase light-oﬀtemperature as both of these compounds act
as inhibitors. Water in the feed gas has the opposite eﬀect, and light-oﬀtemperature will decrease
as water is a promoter in CO oxidation over platinum-based catalysts. Furthermore, the eﬀect of
the modiﬁers (Fe2O3 and K) could be further enhanced or decreased in the presence of water. The
Literature Review showed that the enhancing eﬀect of water on a magnesium modiﬁed catalyst is
more than the added eﬀect of magnesium and water (Figure 2.16, page 31).
Finally, when the potential DOC has been optimized and successfully tested on laboratory scale,
response to large scale experiments should be tested. The synthetic diesel exhaust gas is simpliﬁed
even when HC, NOx and water have been included. In reality HC consists of more than 200 species
and only the response to C3H6 is tested in laboratory scale. Furthermore, the synthetic diesel gas
does not contain the impurities which are present in raw diesel exhaust gas.
For the actual 037-03 Selective Oxidation micro-reactor set-up, several testing criteria have been
calculated to estimate whether the plug ﬂow assumption is valid.
A tracer experiment could be
performed to measure plug ﬂow and degree of back mixing at diﬀerent operating conditions.
To gain knowledge of the oxidative state of the platinum surface, an EXAFS spectroscopy could be
performed. This provides structural information about the sample by analysis of an X-ray absorption
spectrum.
Hysteresis behaviour can be researched, and furthermore it can be estimated whether

8.3
Optimizing Kinetic Model
For future kinetic experiments, more accurate results are obtained if steady state measurements with
low pressure drop over the bed are used for estimation of kinetic parameters. Low conversion could
furthermore ensure Mears and Weisz-Prater Criterion ≪0.15 and 0.6, implying no mass transfer
limitations.
If pressure drop over the reactor approaches 0.2Ptot, an equation of state should be
incorporated in the plug ﬂow model to account for the diﬀerence in carbon monoxide concentration
and ﬂow.
For this project, kinetic, temperature ramp experiments have been conducted for ﬁve diﬀerent CO
concentrations, while keeping all other conditions constant. The rate expression is also dependent on
the oxygen concentration, and further kinetic experiments could be made varying this parameter in
order to investigate dependence on oxygen. The reaction order of oxygen is positive, and an increase
in oxygen concentration should decrease light-oﬀtemperature.
The desorption of CO becomes easier when the catalyst surface is covered with CO due to repulsive
forces. Therefore the adsorption enthalpy is a function of surface coverage. Oxygen desorption is also
a function of surface coverage and this eﬀect could be included when further developing the model for
optimization of kinetic parameters. This could potentially decrease the predicted conversion at low
temperature resulting in better ﬁt to experimental data.
The rate expression developed for this report is a function of carbon monoxide and oxygen only.
When inhibitors are added to the feed gas, expressions for these are to be incorporated in the rate
expression. The compounds compete for the available sites and the site balance should include both
HC and NOx. The model could also include the state of the platinum surface as the metallic surface
performs diﬀerently compared to the oxidised platinum surface.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
Currently, diesel oxidation catalysts (DOC) contain expensive noble metals, and new generation DOCs
should be less expensive without compromising eﬃciency. The main focus of this report was to identify
and synthesise alternative DOCs, and to test their performance on a small scale plug ﬂow reactor.
A Literature Review was conducted in order to gain general knowledge on DOC composition and
operation conditions.
Furthermore, reactions occurring in the DOC were investigated.
Based on
elementary reactions, diﬀerent Langmuir-Hinshelwood rate expressions were derived for one or two
rate determining steps. A 1wt% Pt/Al2O3 from Sigma Aldrich exhibited low light-oﬀtemperature
during testing of CO oxidation (↑116◦C and ↓99◦C ). Based on this, the aim of the literature study
was to identify modiﬁers, which could improve low temperature performance of this catalyst. Metal
oxides and alkali and alkaline earth metals showed potential towards increasing activity of platinum
catalysts for CO (and HC) oxidation under PROX operating conditions. Of the metal oxides, iron
oxide was superior to the other metal oxides tested in the articles, while potassium outperformed the
remaining alkali metals. Iron oxide should act as an oxygen provider, while potassium should decrease
electron density of the platinum metal resulting in weakened Pt-CO bond.
Seven diﬀerent alternative DOC catalysts were synthesised. Incipient wetness impregnation was
used to add the modiﬁers to the Sigma Aldrich catalyst (B). Each modiﬁer was impregnated at
two diﬀerent weight ratios (BK5, BK10, BFe1, and BFe2). For comparison, a 1 wt% Pt/Al2O3 (H)
was synthesised by wet impregnation from γ-Al2O3 and H2PtCl6·6 H2O, and modiﬁed catalysts were
prepared similarly by co-impregnation (HK3, BFe2).
Prior to the testing of the alternative DOC formulations, preliminary experiments were performed
in order to determine reaction conditions during screening. Each run were conducted as temperature
ramp experiments. Initial deactivation was observed and it was decided to pre-treat all catalysts
at 550◦C for 8 h under air ﬂow.
Another observed phenomenon was hysteresis, with a ∼20◦C
diﬀerence between heating and cooling light-oﬀtemperature. This was mainly assumed due to change
in oxidation of the platinum surface. The Literature Review revealed that CO, NOx and HC inhibits
oxidation of each other, and to ensure comparable results it was decided to research CO oxidation only.
The remaining constituents and impurities were excluded from the reactor feed gas. In preliminary
103
==PAGE:118==
experiments with water, a decrease in light-oﬀtemperatures of ∼26◦C was observed. As water highly
aﬀects the results, this is also excluded from the screening gas feed. The light-oﬀtemperatures proved
very dependent on operating conditions, pre-treatment and run history. Therefore direct comparison
of results obtained from the literature is diﬃcult.
The synthesised catalysts were tested on a small scale plug ﬂow reactor, with a ﬂow rate of 300
NmL/min and a feed composition of 250 ppm CO, 10 vol% oxygen and balance nitrogen. The iron-
impregnated Sigma Aldrich catalysts performed signiﬁcantly better compared to the plain platinum
catalyst, because light-oﬀtemperatures of BFe2 have been reduced to ↑79◦C and ↓89◦C. Repeatability
tests showed a signiﬁcant decrease in hysteresis for BFe2 to ∼3◦C. Improvement is not initially seen for
the potassium modiﬁed catalyst, however the experiments were repeated after the catalyst had been
reduced in hydrogen ﬂow. This signiﬁcantly changed the outcome, and the potassium modiﬁed Sigma
Aldrich catalyst (BK5) showed notably lower light-oﬀtemperatures (↑58◦C and ↓72◦C) compared to
both plain platinum and iron impregnated platinum catalysts.
Initially the in-house catalysts show poor CO oxidation performance, but this improves upon
reduction.
Therefore, reduction of platinum catalysts is necessary.
The in-house catalysts show
similar tendencies as the impregnated Sigma Aldrich catalysts, and the potassium modiﬁed catalyst
(HK3) display lowest light-oﬀtemperatures (↑75◦C, ↓99◦C). All of the in-house catalysts performed
worse compared to the equivalent Sigma Aldrich impregnated catalyst. Dispersion is much higher
for the in-house catalysts, and research whether dispersion inﬂuences the catalytic activity at low
temperatures should be undertaken. Reverse hysteresis is observed for all the modiﬁed catalysts.
From experiments performed on the small scale plug ﬂow reactor, it can be concluded that both
iron oxide and reduced potassium modiﬁed catalyst show potential as alternative DOC formulations.
Both of these catalysts improved light-oﬀtemperature during heating signiﬁcantly, which is positive
as this will increase conversion during engine start-up. The iron and potassium modiﬁed catalysts
should be further researched with regards to NOx and HC oxidation. Furthermore, the response to
sulphur impurities in the feed should be measured as well as thermal stability.
To investigate whether the small scale plug ﬂow reactor exhibited ideal plug ﬂow behaviour, a
number of testing criteria were estimated. Based on these, the screening experiments could not be
considered ideal above light-oﬀtemperatures. For kinetic experiments, the particle size fraction and
the amount of inert were reduced (106-150 µm, 1:2). A model for the plug ﬂow reactor was developed
for both 1 and 2 RDS, and the model was ﬁtted to experimental data while optimizing kinetic
parameters. Both models performed well and only 1 RDS is needed. The optimized 1 RDS model
is able to predict the point of full conversion, however it overestimates low temperature conversion.
Further model development could involve inclusion of adsorption enthalpies as a function of surface
coverage.
The calculated adsorption enthalpies for CO is in the range -(119-144) kJ/mol, which
corresponds well with values obtained from the literature (-121 kJ/mol at 0.11 ML).
Finally, further studies regarding the DOC candidates have been proposed. More experiments are
needed in order to better understand the eﬀect of the modiﬁers and to ensure the potential DOCs
perform well, also regarding the other aspects of the DOC technology. Based on this study, Fe2O3
and K both increase catalytic activity of Pt/Al2O3 and thereby reduce DOC content of noble metals.
