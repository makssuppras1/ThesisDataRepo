ABSTRACT
For the next ten years, Denmark has several construction projects coming up, such as hospital
buildings, the Femern Bælt connection, light railways, new motorways, metro connections, and
more. The forecast for 2019 stated that 997 construction projects were planned for 42.3 billion
Danish Kroner in the Capital Region of Denmark in 2019.
It is important to have eﬀective
project management as billion of Danish Kroner are spent on construction projects, and because
Denmark has several upcoming construction projects.
The purpose of this study is to assess whether or not Agile Project Management can be incor-
porated as a part of MT Højgaard’s current management approach. MT Højgaard is one of the
leading contractor companies in Denmark with a hundred years of experience in the construction
ﬁeld.
Throughout the purposes of this study, data were collected from 16 MT Højgaard project man-
agers, and 17 project managers from LinkedIn were purposefully selected to respond to the
quantitative study. Three out of these 17 project managers, selected from LinkedIn, have fur-
ther participated in a qualitative survey, where they have been interviewed about how they have
implemented Agile Project Management and its frameworks.
This study shows that Agile Project Management can bring more ﬂexibility to the project team,
and at the same time, improve productivity and quality, and increase customer business satisfac-
tion. The study also showed that various Agile frameworks had been implemented, and Agile
Project Management can be incorporated into the current approach to project management that
is being used in today’s construction projects.
This study concluded that Agile Project Management not only belongs in the software industry
but can be adapted to ﬁt diﬀerent projects and diﬀerent industries. Lastly, there is a possibility
that a hybrid of Traditional Project Management and Agile Project Management can complement
each other.

This Master thesis was prepared by Mint Rasmussen, a Master student at the Department of

1.1
Problem Statement
For the next ten years, Denmark has a lot of major construction projects such as hospital buildings,
Femern Bælt connection, light railways, new motorways, metro connections, etc (Harboe, 2017).
MT Højgaard establishes a variety of construction projects, ranging from bridges to homes, to
super hospitals and much more. They have hundreds of years of experiences in the construction
ﬁeld (MT Højgaard, 2017a). MT Højgaard has left its mark on the history of Denmark throughout
the twentieth century. They have built the Øresund and Little Belt Bridge, the Great Belt Tunnel,
the Moesgaard Museum, the Black Diamond and a number of other well-known construction
projects in Denmark, as well as in the Nordic region and the rest of the world (MT Højgaard,
2017a).
In addition, the forecast for 2019 states that 997 construction projects are planned for 42.3
billion Danish Krone in the Capital Region of Denmark in 2019.
In particular, new housing
and commercial construction represent a signiﬁcantly greater portion (Gram, 2018). Municipal
constructions are often delayed. The Capital Region of Denmark is behind on their construction

1.2
Delimitation of the subject
This thesis’ focus is primarily on Agile Project Management. Diﬀerent Agile frameworks will be
mentioned. However, it does not delve deeply into how these frameworks can be fully imple-
mented in the construction industry.
Besides, literature regarding Agile Project Management compared to Traditional Project Manage-
ment has been investigated. Various project management certiﬁcations such as IPMA, PMBOK,
and PRINCE2 will also be presented. Here the emphasis will only be on IPMA, as this is the
course MT Højgaard oﬀers to its employees. The diﬀerent certiﬁcations are referred to as a
comparison and should not be more discussed.
The investigation will, as mentioned, only focus on Agile Project Management in general and
how it can improve the management of construction projects. In construction projects as well
as all other projects, the collaboration between individuals in the team must work, and those
common goals are achieved successfully. Thereby, even though the research is also based on
the team openness to changes, the topic of organisational structure, change management, and
communication approaches will not be included. However, it is noted that change management
is an essential and exciting topic, as there are many parties involved in a construction project.

1.3
Methods
The project’s methodology has been based on literature research, quantitative, and qualitative
studies.
To better understand this study and the context of it, research in literature was done early on.
Besides, updating the collected materials is essential to avoid the material not being exceeded
by recent data or information. For a more in-depth and broader understanding of the subject,
data collection was carried out in two phases with a quantitative survey in the ﬁrst phase and
purposeful sampling in the second, qualitative phase.
The qualitative study was performed
based on quantitative research, and the participants were purposefully selected. The collected
theoretical material provides the basis for the theory of this project. All studies have been based
on questionnaire data and interview statements with diﬀerent project managers. Furthermore,
research papers have been used to compare with the collected questionnaire data and interview
statements.
1.3.1
Literature Research
A literature search aims to collect relevant materials for the study. The content is restricted,
concentrating on Agile Project Management in general, to prevent unnecessary topics. However,
the search for literature could be further limited, as there are many methods in Agile Project
Management. The focus is primarily on Agile Project Management and not how precise each
framework can be applied in the construction ﬁeld.
Also, literature regarding Agile Project
Management compared to Traditional Project Management has been investigated. These topics
have helped build more knowledge in the ﬁeld of modern project management compared to
traditional project management.
1.3.2
The Quantitative Approach
The quantitative approach produces a questionnaire that is sent to various project managers in
the construction ﬁeld, both with and without the knowledge or experience of Agile Project Man-
agement. Most of the questions in the questionnaire apply to Agile Project Management and
its advantages and challenges. The main ﬁndings can be interpreted based on these results and
then elaborated with the theory. The response rate from MT Højgaard was at 16 percentage,
handled by the HR department while the response rate from other companies was at 56 per-
centage, handled by emailing to various project managers on LinkedIn, which were purposefully
selected for the survey.
1.3.3
The Qualitative Approach
The qualitative approach provided more in-depth data, better understanding, and insight. As
a result, questions were based on quantitative research. The questions for the interviews were
prepared to gain a better understanding and insight into the thinking of project managers. Three
interviews were conducted and were based on the theories used in this study and should be a
source of contributing to the problem statement. The three project managers were purposefully

2.1
Project Management Methodology
Since the 1960s, project management has been developed as a scientiﬁc discipline (Spalek, 2016).
(Project Management Institute, Inc., 2019) deﬁnes project management methodology as a set
of methods, techniques, procedures, rules, templates, and best practices used on a project.
Another commonly used methodologies are the "structured project management method," which
is described by PRINCE2 and composed partly of speciﬁed processes and techniques.
Some
deﬁne the project management methodology as (Spundak, 2014):
■a set of guidelines and principles that can be tailored and applied to speciﬁc situations,
where guidelines could be as simple as a task list, or it could be a speciﬁc approach to the
project with deﬁned tools and techniques,
■the knowledge set about tasks, techniques, deliveries, roles, and tools used during the
project paired with knowledge about adjusting all of that to a speciﬁc project,
■a structured set of techniques and tools used for solving speciﬁc problems,

■a structured way to manage projects consisting of rules and directions and is based on a
speciﬁc form of thinking.
According to (Spundak, 2014), the broadest deﬁnition of project management methodology is
given by Cockburn. Cockburn deﬁnes it as any principle a project management team relies on
to deliver project results successfully. A proper project management methodology will guide the
project manager through a controlled, managed, and visible set of activities to achieve the results
of a project.
2.2
Project Management Approaches
The term project management approach is most often used as a set of principles and guidelines
that deﬁne how a speciﬁc project is managed. While the term framework, which has almost a
similar meaning, is used for an operative set of rules, processes, methods, and templates to be
used during the project life cycle (Spundak, 2014).
2.2.1
Traditional Project Management
Traditional methods of project management are well established. They have been developed over
a long period, but it appears that they are no longer suﬃcient to overcome the challenges that
companies are facing today, regardless of their country of origin.
The Traditional Project Management method focuses on work breakdown structures (WBS),
Gantt charts, and detailed budgeting, including earned value technique (EVT) (Spalek, 2016).
(Project Management Insitute, 2013) describes the structure of work breakdown as a fundamental
component for initiating, planning, executing and monitoring, and controlling processes that are
used for project management.
A Gantt chart is a project management tool that helps plan
and schedule projects of all sizes, although it is especially useful to simplify complex projects
(Association for Project Management, 2020b). Earned value technique is a technique used to
measure progress objectively (for Project Management, 2008).
Traditional Project Management is primarily based on a mechanical, mono-casual, non-dynamic,
linear structure, and a discrete view of human nature and societies and their perceptions, knowl-
edge, and actions (Bergmann and Karwowski, 2019). In addition, the iron triangle (time, resource,
scope) is prevalent in the practice of managerial projects (Spalek, 2016). The ultimate goal of
this approach is optimisation and eﬃciency in an initial detailed project plan, or, to ﬁnalise a
project within planned time, budget, and scope (Spundak, 2014).
The PMBOK guide deﬁnes Traditional Project Management as "a set of techniques and tools
that can be applied to an activity that seeks end producer outcomes or a service." Also, project
managers should monitor and control the results against the plan in the 10 Knowledge Areas, as
illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1 (Project Management Institute, Inc., 2019). The 10 Knowledge Areas
acquire speciﬁc skills and experiences to achieve the goals of the project. Each area of knowledge
consists of a set of processes, each with inputs, tools, techniques, and outputs. Together, these
processes perform proven project management functions and drive the success of the project
(Visual Paradigm, 2019).

3.1.1
A Brief History of Agile
It all started in the early 1990s with the so-called "Application Development Crisis." During this
crisis, there was a considerable lag time of about three years between the business need for an
application and the actual software delivery. Those long lead times led to frustrations among
leaders in the software development industry. They began organising informal meetings between
themselves, determined to ﬁnd a way to develop software solutions more quickly and eﬃciently.
It started with a gathering of 17 software development leaders at the Snowbird Ski resort in Utah
between 11-13 February 2001. This gathering produced the "Agile Manifesto" that changed the
way projects are managed today (Kanbanize, 2019b).

requirements. This helps the client and development team to agree on what will be provided
early in the project and gives the client a feeling of certainty on what the project’s results will
look like. Planning and design phases simplify as the requirements are set early in the project
and because it allows an inclusive design to cover the full scope of the project. Progress can be
measured more quickly as the project’s scope is known at the beginning of development. The
involvement of the clients can be limited to a key milestone, such as reviews, approvals, and
status meetings.
Early planning has the advantage that the order in which the product is produced can be arranged
based on integration, key, dates, and other variables. Through careful estimation and planning,
work on project resources can be planned in such a way as to minimise the idle time of the project
and allow it to focus on other tasks when not focusing on project-related activities.
One of the key areas where the waterfall methodology falls short is how requirements and changes
are managed. The client often realises that what they asked for is not really what they envisioned
or needed. With this in mind, the costs are less expensive, and it is also less risky when changing
the scope early, such as in the revision or planning phases. However, changes during design,
development, or testing increase risk and cost signiﬁcantly (Croitoru, 2018).

techniques that best suit their project, the project environment, and the contracting parties
focusing on behaviours, leadership and governance instead of methods that create the best op-
portunities for successful project delivery (Association for Project Management, 2019). Figure

Scrum is a process framework that has been used to manage complex projects since the early

4.1.1
Scrum Roles
There are three diﬀerent roles in the Scrum framework. These three roles are the Product Owner,
Scrum Master, and Development Team, and they all consist of the Scrum Team. The Scrum
teams are self-organising and cross-functional. Self-organising teams choose the best way to do
their work, rather than being led by others outside the team. Cross-functional teams have all
the skills needed to complete the task without having to rely on others who are not part of the
team. Scrum teams are designed to optimise ﬂexibility, creativity, and productivity.

Scrum Teams deliver products iteratively and incrementally, maximising feedback opportunities.
Incremental deliveries of the "Done" product ensure that a potentially useful version of the
working product is always available.
"Done" is deﬁned as a shared understanding of what it means for work to be complete to ensure
transparency. The deﬁnition of "Done" is used to determine when work on the Product Increment
is complete (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017).
Product Owner
As the Product Owner, one has the responsibility to have the overall vision and authority to
represent the customer’s interest (Croitoru, 2018). It is the responsibility of the Product Owner
to maximise the value of the product produced by the Scrum Team. The Product Owner is the
sole person responsible for the management of the Product Backlog (an ordered list of all known
requirements in the product) which includes (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017):
■Items of Product Backlog are clearly outlined,
■Items in the Product Backlog are ordered to achieve the goals and missions best,
■Optimising the value of the work performed are by the Development Team,
■Ensuring the Product Backlog is visible, transparent and clear to everyone, and show what
the Scrum team is going to work on next,
■Ensuring that the Development Team understands items in the Product Backlog to the
level required.

Development Team
The Development Team consists of experts working to deliver a potentially releasable Increment

4.1.2
Scrum Events
There are ﬁve diﬀerent events in the Scrum framework. These events are the Sprint, the Sprint
Planning, the Daily Sprint, the Sprint Review, and the Sprint Retrospective. All these events are
time-boxed events, which means every event has a maximum duration (Schwaber and Sutherland,
2017).
The Sprint
The heart of Scrum is the Sprint, which has a duration of one month or less, where a usable
and potentially releasable product is created. A new Sprint starts immediately after the "end" of
the previous Sprint. Sprints consist of the Sprint Planning, Daily Sprints, the work, the Sprint
Review, and the Sprint Retrospective (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017). This periodic pattern
helps maintain a sense of routine and reduces the amount of change in the way the team works
(Croitoru, 2018).
==PAGE:40==
4.1 Scrum
30
Each Sprint within a project should not be longer than a one-month horizon. When the horizon
is too long, the deﬁnition of what is being built may change, complexity may occur, and risk may
increase. Sprints allow predictability by ensuring inspection and adaptation of progress toward a
Sprint Goal at least every month (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017).
Sprint Planning
The tasks to be done in the Sprint is planned at the Sprint Planning. Sprint planning is created
by the collaborative work of the three roles mentioned earlier.
The maximum duration of a
Sprint Planning is eight hours for a one-month long Sprint. If the Sprint is shorter, the event will
usually be shorter as well (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017). Sprint Planning is performed once
per sprint cycle, usually 1-3 days before the start of the Sprint. The team chooses an item from
the Product Backlog into the next Sprint and determines how many items they can complete
in the Sprint. The items have their prioritisation set by the Product Owner. The Development
Team will determine whether or not the items are Done. Otherwise, the items will need to be
redeﬁned to prepare for future sprints (Ellis, 2016). It is important that each Sprint has a goal
as these goals will provide the stakeholders with a high-level overview of what the team will be
working on in the upcoming sprint (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017).
Daily Sprint
The Daily Sprint has a duration of the 15-minute event. The meeting is held every day of the
Sprint. The Daily Sprint is held at the same time and location each day to reduce complexity.
For the next 24 hours, the Development Team plans the work that needs to be done. This
optimises team collaboration and performance by inspecting the work since the last Daily Sprint
and forecasting upcoming Sprint work (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017). A typical agenda is to
request that each participant spend 2-3 minutes answering these questions (Ellis, 2016):
1. What tasks what done yesterday?
2. What is today’s plan?
3. What are the barriers that could prevent progress?
The team utilises the Daily Sprint to monitor progress towards the Sprint Goal and to check
how progress towards completing the Sprint Backlog work is moving forward. The Daily Sprint
optimises the team’s likelihood of meeting the Sprint Goal. Every day, the team should understand
how it aims to work together as a self-organising team to achieve the Sprint Goal and generate
the expected increment by the end of the Sprint (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017).
The main reason for Daily Sprints is to ensure that everyone on the Scrum team is aware of the
work that has been completed and what work remains on the iteration (Croitoru, 2018). Daily
Sprints improve communications, eliminate other meetings, identify obstructions to development
for removal, highlight and encourage rapid decision-making, and enhance the knowledge level of
the team (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017).

Sprint Review
The Sprint Review shall be held at the end of the Sprint to inspect the Increment (the sum of all
completed items in the Product Backlog, in the Sprint) and, if necessary, to adjust the Product
Backlog. The Scrum Team and stakeholders collaborate on what was accomplished in the Sprint
during the Sprint Review. On that basis, during the Sprint, any changes to the Product Backlog,
participants work together on the next items that could be Done to optimise the value (Schwaber
and Sutherland, 2017).
During this Sprint Review meeting, the participants (typically Product Owner, Scrum Master,
Scrum team, and anybody who has a vested interest in the project) have an opportunity to
review the delivered work during the iteration and provide comments on it (Croitoru, 2018).
The meeting is at most a four-hour meeting for a one-month long Sprint. The Scrum Master
guarantees that the event occurs and that the participants understand its purpose. The Scrum
Master teaches everyone involved to maintain it in time. This Sprint Review outcome is a revised
Product Backlog that describes the likely Product Backlog items for the next Sprint. Overall,
the Product Backlog can also be adapted to satisfy or meet new opportunities (Schwaber and
Sutherland, 2017).
Sprint Retrospective
The team meets at the end of each Sprint to discuss what they have performed well, and what they
can improve for the future (Ellis, 2016). Sprint Retrospective meetings are "working sessions"
to allow team members to talk out and share their ideas on cooperation and consistency and
working within teams. The meeting is at most at a three-hour meeting for one-month Sprints
(Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017). At the end of each Sprint, the team meets to discuss what
they did well and what they can learn for the future. This is an excellent way to reset the team’s
expectations based on the information collected during the meeting and concentrate on ways to
enhance how everyone works together.
The Scrum Master should ensure that the team remains focused on discussing matters that can
be controlled by the team and prevent blaming individuals, leading to a split team (Croitoru,
2018). The Scrum Team should have recognised changes to be implemented in the next Sprint
by the end of the Sprint Retrospective. Implementing these improvements in the next Sprint is
adapting to the Scrum Team’s inspection (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017). The main advantage
of running these retrospective sessions after each sprint is that members of the Scrum team have
an opportunity to enhance how they function together early and iterate throughout the project
(Croitoru, 2018).

Scrum Artifacts
There are three diﬀerent artifacts in the Scrum framework. These artifacts are Product Backlog,
Sprint Backlog, and Product Increment.
The artifacts of Scrum represent work or value to
ensure transparency and inspection and adaptation possibilities. Artifacts deﬁned by Scrum are
speciﬁcally intended to maximise important data and transparency so that everyone has the same
artifact knowledge (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017).

Product Backlog
The Product Backlog is the ordered list of everything the product needs (Schwaber and Suther-
land, 2017). The Product Backlog contains all known user stories for the full product. The user
story is a description of the product requirements from an end-user point of view. Stories can
come into the Product Backlog in many ways but usually from the Product Owner. There is a
limit to how many entrances, but stories are given priority by the Product Owner (Ellis, 2016).
The Product Owner is responsible for the Product Backlog, including its contents, availability,
and ordering (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017).
A backlog of products is never completed. The earliest development sets out the demands that
were initially recognized and best understood. The Product Backlog evolves as the product and
environment evolve. The Product Backlog is dynamic as it continually changes to identify what
the product needs to be appropriate, competitive, and useful. If a product exists, its Product
Backlog also exists (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017.
The Product Backlog lists all features, functions, requirements updates, and improvements that
represent the changes to be made in future releases of the product. Product Backlog items have
a description, order, estimate, and value characteristics (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017
The Product Backlog becomes a more signiﬁcant and more comprehensive list as a product is
used and gains value, and the marketplace provides feedback. Product Backlog is a living artifact
as requirements never stop changing. Changes in business requirements, market circumstances,
or technology may result in changes in the Product Backlog (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017.
Adding detail, estimates, and order to items in the Product Backlog is the act of Product Backlog
reﬁnement. During the reﬁnement, items will be reviewed and revised. The Scrum Team decides
how and when to reﬁne. Usually, reﬁnement consumes no more than ten percent of the team’s
ability. Product Backlog items may, however, be updated by the Product Owner at any time or
the Product Owner’s judgement (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017.
Usually, higher-ordered Product Backlog items are clearer and more comprehensive than lower
ordered items. More accurate estimates are produced based on enhanced clarity and detail, the
smaller the order, the less detail (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017).
Sprint Backlog
The Sprint Backlog is a subset of the Product Backlog. The Sprint Backlog carries only items
that can be Done for the Sprint, plus a plan to deliver the Product Increment and achieve the
Sprint Goal. The Sprint Backlog is a plan with suﬃcient detail to allow the Daily Sprint to
understand changes in progress. The Sprint Backlog is an extremely visible, real-time image of
the work planned by the team during the Sprint (Schwaber and Sutherland, 2017).
Product Increment
The Product Increment is the amount of all the Product Backlog items that were completed
during a Sprint and the value of all prior Sprints’ increments. The new Product Increment must
be "completed" at the end of a Sprint, meaning it must be in useable condition. A Product

Product Increment is a move in the direction of a vision or goal. The Product Increment must

4.1.4
Burn-Down Chart
One tool to present the reader with is the Burn-Down Chart. It is a graphical representation
of work left to do versus time. Burn-down charts can be applied over time to any project which
involves measurable progress. Usually, in the chart, the work is often on the vertical axis and
time along the horizontal. It is useful for predicting when all of the work will be completed. It
helps determine when all the work is done. The Development Team updates the Sprint Burn
Down in the Daily Sprint and schedules the remainder of the day’s work (Visual Paradigm, 2018).
The Sprint Burn Down is a technique for a clear display of the current Sprint’s remaining work.
It displays the work which remains in a Sprint on the vertical axis, and the horizontal axis it
shows Sprint’s working days (wibas GmbH, 2015). For the following main reasons, a burn-down
chart is almost "must" have for a Scrum team (Visual Paradigm, 2018):
■monitoring the project scope creep (features that are unapproved),
■keeping the team running on schedule,
■comparing the planned work against the team progression.
4.2
Kanban
Kanban is an approach to software development based on Lean’s principles (Hammarberg and
Sundén, 2014). The word Kanban is Japanese and translates as a signboard or signal card. Car
manufacturer Toyota initially used this signal card as a mechanism to help them ensure that they
received the required components just in time.
A physical card was delivered to the supplier as a signal that the plant needed more of a certain
component. Without a formal decision, the number of cards cannot be reduced or increased. This
system ensures that the amount of unused components is maintained at a level that maximises
ﬂow while minimising manufactured but unused components of the vehicle. Components of the
vehicle that have not yet been mounted on a vehicle body are considered as waste (Björkholm
and Björkholm, 2015).
4.2.1
The Four Foundations Principles of Kanban
The method of Kanban is described by the four foundation principles, as illustrated in ﬁgure 4.4.
Kanban’s four principles show that it is a method to drive improvement, and it starts with the
processes the team already has. As principle one and three clearly state, do not initially make
any changes either in the process or in roles. Principle two and four set the mind to involve
everyone in taking small and incremental steps towards change and improvement (Björkholm
and Björkholm, 2015).

Practice 1 - Visualising the work: The ﬁrst practice is to visualise what one is doing. The
visualisation involves both the process steps and the work one is presently doing in each phase.
Use an electronic board or a physical whiteboard to visualise their work. It is great to have an
electronic tool, but nothing is better than a big whiteboard. A big whiteboard will give a better
overview of the status and is more ﬂexible as one can add new lines and add text wherever it is
needed. The entire team can stand around it, gain a good knowledge of the project or process,
and simultaneously update the status (Björkholm and Björkholm, 2015).
Practice 2 - Limiting work in progress: The second practice is to limit work in progress (WIP).
By setting limits, one is not allowed to bring more work in than they can handle. The task cannot

Practice 4 - Making process policies explicit: The fourth practice is to be clear about the
process and the policies and principles behind it. The fourth practice is to ensure that everyone

is diﬃcult to discuss and improve the process unless the current process is understood (Björkholm

Practice 5 - Implementing feedback loops: It is not all about measuring and optimising the

the revenue and well-being of the company. This is where practice number ﬁve comes into the
picture; the need for getting feedback from people outside of the system. Feedback loops within
a system are also required to ensure that the desired function is implemented at the appropriate
level. Here, diﬀerent kinds of tests are described. Automated experiments are always favoured
because feedback loops are shortened (Björkholm and Björkholm, 2015).
Practice 6 - Improving collaboratively, evolving experimentally: The sixth practice is the one
that makes Kanban both more interesting and more complex. This practice informs the use of
all ﬂow theories that can be applied to the system to fulﬁl practice number three (optimising
lead-time). These theories can be Lean, queuing theory, chaos theory, gaming theory, the theory
of constraints, and many more. This will take a while to learn, but in the long run, it will be
worth it (Björkholm and Björkholm, 2015).
The Kanban board

work-in-progress, and maximising ﬂow or eﬃciency. Kanban board incorporates cards, columns,
and continuous improvement to assist the team in getting the right amount of work Done. Teams
write all of their activities on the cards, one activity per card. Once these cards are on the board,
it helps everyone on the project and stakeholders understand easily and quickly what the team
is working on. The columns represent a speciﬁc activity that creates a workﬂow. The columns
can be as simple as: "To Do," "Doing," and "Done" (Rehkopf, 2020).

Scrumban is used for maintenance projects, which combines the best features of both Scrum and
Kanban (Savita Pahuja, 2019). Scrumban is a simpliﬁed version of Scrum, maintaining the daily
meeting, and the Kanban board, but eliminating the planning activities and measuring speed.

team works in ﬁxed-length iterations, reviewing the release after each iteration, optimise the
release plan, and update priorities in collaboration with the customer; optimising the process by
having a retrospective after each iteration. In Kanban, it is important to visualise the workﬂow;
split the work into pieces, and write them on a card and place them on the board; use named
columns to illustrate where each item is in the workﬂow. Also, it is essential to limit the Work in
Progress. Lastly, measuring the lead-time, and optimise the process to make lead time as small
and predictable as possible (Savita Pahuja, 2019).

4.4
Lean
Lean is a management philosophy developed by Toyota. It is described in 14 principles on how
to think long-term and to focus on the quality and smoothness of the process (Björkholm and
Björkholm, 2015). Maximising customer value while minimising waste is the key concept of Lean.
==PAGE:49==
4.4 Lean
39
Lean is to create more value for customers with fewer resources (Lean Enterprise Institute, 2019).
The 14 principles are as follow:
Long-term philosophy:
1: Base the management decisions on a long-term philosophy, even at the expense
of short-term ﬁnancial goals,
The right process will produce the right results:
2: Create continuous process ﬂow to bring problems to the surface,
3: Use the "pull" system to avoid overproduction,
4: Level out the workload (work like the tortoise, not the hare),
5: Build a culture of stopping to ﬁx problems to ensure quality right from the start,
6: Standardised tasks are the foundation for continuous improvement and employee
empowerment,
7: Use visual control so no problems are hidden,
8: Use only reliable, thoroughly tested technology that serves your people and pro-
cesses,
Add value to the organisation by developing your people and partners:
9: Grow leaders who thoroughly understand the work, live the philosophy, and teach
it to others,
10: Develop exceptional people and teams who follow your company’s philosophy,
11: Respect your extended network of partners and suppliers by challenging them
and helping them improve,
Continuously solving root problems drives organisational learning:
12: Go and see for yourself to thoroughly understand the situation,
13: Make decisions slowly by consensus, thoroughly considering all options; imple-
ment decisions rapidly,
14: Become a learning organisation through relentless reﬂection and continuous
improvement.
Apart from the 14 principles, Lean is a way of thinking and an attitude. It is about challenging
problems, continually improving, and constantly striving for perfection. With a Lean attitude,
the competitors are known to have the same problems, and one has to be a little better oﬀthan
the competitors who deal with the same problems, then one is ahead of them. There is also a
way of handling visions that are overwhelming in the Lean attitude. It is a simple way and can
be described as follows:
1. Deﬁne your vision even if it feels impossible to achieve,
2. Deﬁne where you are now,
3. Deﬁne the next goal to be reached in the right direction,
==PAGE:50==
4.5 Danish guidelines related to Agile Project Management
40
4. Interactively make improvements until the goal has been reached.
Steps two to four should be repeated forever, as long as the vision remains valid. If this is not
the case, one will need to go back and ﬁnd a way to redeﬁne it. Lean also aims to ensure that
employees are encouraged and expected to continuously suggest and implement improvements
in the way they work. Everyone needs to get the full picture to be able to contribute. That is
why visualisation is so important to Lean (Björkholm and Björkholm, 2015).

4.5.1
Guideline to the involvement of subcontractors
VærdiByg, also known as Construction to Create Value, focuses on maintaining the values of the
construction throughout the construction project - from the construction plan to the operation
of the completed construction. They have developed common solutions to common problems
for the beneﬁts of the entire construction value chain. They ﬁnd and transmit the industry’s
best experiences for eﬃcient construction processes, collaboration, and value creation (Simonsen,
2013).
The guideline from VærdiByg, The Involvement of Subcontractor, is based on various possibili-
ties to actively involve the subcontractor’s competences in the construction process, from how
contractors can involve their subcontractor early in the process to the various planning methods
(Simonsen, 2013).
There is a need for coordination, continuous communication, and clariﬁcation of tasks within
a construction project. This is most commonly done at various types of meetings (Simonsen,
2013), as ﬁgure 4.8 illustrates. Early in the project, the meeting to be held is deﬁned, including
their content, participants, and frequency. The duration, scope, and content of the meetings will
depend on the size of the project and, in particular, on the complexity of the project. Complex
construction projects also need more on-going communication between the participants.
Start-up (Kick-oﬀ) workshop
Start-up workshop is for everyone involved in the execution of the project. Introduction to the
project and the client’s criteria for success are laid down at this meeting. The tasks and ground
rules are agreed upon for future work by the participants in the project.
==PAGE:51==
4.5 Danish guidelines related to Agile Project Management
41
Figure 4.8: Overview of the various meetings (Source: Simonsen, 2013).
Project Review meeting
A Project Review meeting is where the project design team hands the project over to the con-
tractors. The meeting will be used to tackle all kinds of project issues and uncertainties.
Phase Schedule meeting
Phase schedule meeting is where the subcontractors review the project together, prepare the
activity plan, and coordinate interfaces and sequences of activities where several activities are to
be carried out in the same area. Phase scheduling is aimed at ﬁnding agreement on the upcoming
process and deﬁning and reviewing the sequence of activities across the sector. The completion
of the phase scheduling usually takes 2-3 hours and can proceed as follows:
1. Introduction to phase schedule.
2. Each sector writes on coloured post-it notes its activities within the project. Each sector
has its colour.
3. When the activities are noted, the post-its are put on the wall in the order where the
participants think the activities should be carried out.
4. They discuss the sequence and the placement of the post-its while doing it. Sectors are
only allowed to engage in their activities, and other sectors are not allowed to move the
post-its. If someone believes that the activity needs to be moved, he or she will get a hold
of the person involved in the sector and agree on this.
5. The phase scheduling is complete when all the post-its have been placed, and everyone
agrees on the sequence in which the activities are to be carried out.
Besides this, there is the main timetable for the project, which is often based on signiﬁcant
milestones and the contract. Participants will be more motivated to adhere to a plan that they
have contributed to themselves than to one that construction management has made.
==PAGE:52==
4.5 Danish guidelines related to Agile Project Management
42
The contractor and sub-contractors will estimate how long each activity will take after the
scheduling meeting. Based on this, the plan can be entered into, for example, MS Project, with
the duration. The sectors may need to meet again (1-2 weeks later) to complete or reﬁne the plan
e.g., by changing the resource or conducting multiple tasks in parallel, enabling the negotiated
plan to be executed within the deﬁned deadlines/milestones. The phasing schedule is completed
when everyone thinks they will adhere to the plan and the deﬁned deadlines.
Client meeting
Client meeting involves the client, the safety coordinator, consultants, and relevant contractors
and suppliers. The project-speciﬁc challenges can be discussed at this forum to address them
eﬃciently and create the best possible value creation for the project. The meetings are often
held when necessary, usually on large and complex projects.
Site meeting
A Site meeting is typically held every or every second week to let the chairmen of the diﬀerent
subcontractors discuss the project, agreements, responsibilities, and ﬁnances. If there are other
types of planning meetings that take place concurrently, you can have fewer site meetings, or
you can move the discussions to other meetings.
In the Look-ahead Plan meetings, participants from each (sub-)contractor or sector will regularly
discuss the activities and future challenges and interfaces for the coming week. The look-ahead
plan outlines the activities, e.g. the next six weeks, and is used to evaluate the activities for
potential issues. If something stands in the way of future activity, then it is agreed who should
be responsible for removing the barrier, and a deadline for this. The look-ahead plan and the
upcoming activities are usually discussed by chairmen or managers from each sector at the regular
site meetings.
Coordination meeting
Coordination meeting is also known as a weekly meeting, lean meeting, chairmen’s meeting,
or meeting of team leaders. The Weekly Work Plan is a detailed work plan for each sector
on-site. The sector chairmen will have arranged the activities they are to perform during the
coming week and the order of these activities for the weekly meeting concerning space, health,
and safety, machinery, and sequence of activities. The interactions between the activities are
explained to avoid multiple professions being simultaneously in the same place. The planning
and coordination involve the subcontractors and their capabilities in the project. The planning
and coordination give the project and work a sense of ownership, which makes activities more
eﬃcient. The weekly work plan, which is a simple schedule, is the foundation for the weekly
work plan coordination and dialogue.
Safety meeting
Safety meetings should be held every other week and should be attended by all contractors doing
work during the time-frame.
The contractor and safety representatives evaluate and discuss
the current safety and working conditions on the site. Safety meetings and inspections can be

combined into a walkabout meeting scheduled before the site meeting can be a good idea. This
will bring both meetings up to date and ensure that the coordinating working environment issues
are identiﬁed and discussed.
4.5.2
TrimByg
TrimByg is MT Højgaard’s version of Lean Construction. To optimise the construction process,
MT Højgaard has developed several rules and tools for eﬀective construction management. It
creates beneﬁts for all parties involved. They refer to it as TrimByg, Trimmed Construction (MT
Højgaard, 2017b).
TrimByg helps ensure less waste, greater eﬃciency, and increased value (Højgaard, 2017).
TrimByg’s goal is to create the most productive construction process. A construction project
is a huge logistics challenge, and TrimByg focuses on trimming the evolving and complicated
construction processes by a continuous planning operation. TrimByg identiﬁes deviations on the
occurrence and takes immediate action and follow-up on necessary initiatives. Ir also ensures
production maintains a steady rhythm in this way. TrimByg is putting people and connectivity
at the forefront to create an overview of the entire production process. A construction project
involves many people who need to communicate about everything from resource tasks, on-site
logistics, and the weather to ensure that everyone has the necessary information to meet the
expectations of the builders. TrimByg has developed concrete solutions, tools, and principles,
all of which support communication between the construction process partners and ensure an
eﬃcient construction process through proper organisation, timely planning, joint coordination,
ongoing monitoring, and ﬁnal evaluation. TrimByg is a concept in process management that
provides less waste, more productivity, and greater value. Across ﬁve phases, it aims to create
the most eﬀective construction process for all involved parties (MT Højgaard, 2017b).
1. Organisation - requiring collaboration: At the construction site, the distribution of roles
are identiﬁed early in the process and ensure a clear division of responsibilities and tasks
between the key players. And then, central process management is introduced.
2. Planning - focusing on the prerequisites: At the start of the construction project, the focus
is on the prerequisites and thoroughly planned activities.
Afterward, a short planning
horizon is applied to the construction site to improve predictability and ensure healthy
activities.
3. Coordination - ensures the best possible working conditions: The process management
supports the collaboration among the construction site participants and ensures that the
individual tasks are constantly cross-coordination. In this way, it creates a smooth workﬂow
at the construction site and provides the contractor with the optimal result.
4. Follow-up - so mistakes will not be repeated: There is a continuous record of the construc-
tion, whether or not it follows the plans so that the process can be optimised. If something
goes wrong; identify the cause; evaluate the incident, and ensure that the mistake does
not occur again.
5. Evaluation - with a focus on knowledge sharing: Between the projects, the collaboration,
the construction process, and the experience of each project are evaluated. The evaluation
==PAGE:54==
4.5 Danish guidelines related to Agile Project Management
44
ensures that the good experiences are passed on so that the same mistakes are not repeated
in the next project.
TrimByg has integrated the use of Location-Based planning, also known as cyclogram planning.
Location-Based planning gives a better overview of the project early in the construction project.
It is a planning method that has three dimensions: activity, time, and location (see ﬁgure 4.9).

In addition to scheduling, it can show in which areas the individual work takes place so that
you can coordinate and optimise the work about time and activity. The eﬀect of TrimByg and
Location-Based planning allows us to build more eﬃciently. By planning in three dimensions,
one has the opportunity to have (Højgaard, 2017):
■an entire project scheduled for a major building or construction project down on one page,
■a clearer schedule and improved ﬂow,
■a proactive rather than a reactive schedule,
■an overview of optimisation opportunities in the construction process, for example where
several activities collide,

■an improved communication with subcontractors,

In addition to Location-Based planning, they also have diﬀerent types of meetings, that also
follow the guideline from VærdiByg; Kick-oﬀworkshop with subcontractors; Phase scheduling
meeting; Subcontractor meeting; Chairmen meeting; Time-out meeting; and Final evaluation
(Højgaard, 2017).
Kick-oﬀwith subcontractors
After engaging with the subcontractors, the kick-oﬀmeeting is held as early as possible.
A
kick-oﬀmeeting aims to create a common understanding of the project and to get an idea of the
scope of the diﬀerent sectors. A presentation of the construction project will give subcontractors
an idea of what generates value for the customer by inviting the client and consultant. The
kick-oﬀmeeting is an opportunity to create more ownership of the project with all parties. The
goals and success criteria of the parties should be set at the meeting (Højgaard, 2017).
To ensure a clear and accurate organisational plan for the project, issues such as role allocation,
procurement relations, and communication channels need to be discussed at the kick-oﬀmeeting.
It is useful to talk about mutual expectations, like generally good behaviour and communication,
respect for each other, and how to manage any diﬀerences.
When the topics are addressed
in a joint forum, it ties the participants together and commits everyone to the collaboration
(Højgaard, 2017).
The kick-oﬀmeeting ends with an agreement on when the next evaluation meeting should be.
This evaluation looks back at the commitments and success criteria agreed upon at the kick-oﬀ
meeting. There, the individual actions are adjusted, and an action plan is drawn up for the
activities needed to proceed as smoothly as possible with the project (Højgaard, 2017).
Phase scheduling meeting
A phase scheduling plan is not a schedule but a plan in which the most appropriate sequence
of tasks is drawn up for the construction process. The work consists of creating activity cards
in the order they consider to be the most appropriate. This is an ongoing process throughout
the construction phase, and the plan is drawn up in close cooperation with the parties involved,
including the subcontractors, in-house production, and any suppliers and consultants to ensure
it is realistic and focuses on problem areas promptly. Working on the process plan often reveals
a lack of decision making and project clarity (Højgaard, 2017).
It is the responsibility of the process manager to arrange phase scheduling meetings with the
involved parties, where the relationship between the individual activities are deﬁned, and the
prerequisites for the activities are carried out. The project begins by breaking down the project
into manageable areas and thus creating zoning (a sub-area, part of a building, ﬂoor, or duration).
Based on the zoning, process planning must identify (Højgaard, 2017):
■The key activities of the contractor,
==PAGE:56==
4.5 Danish guidelines related to Agile Project Management
46
■Order of activity,
■The connection with activities with other contractors
The process manager or construction manager puts the duration of each task based on contrac-
tors’ information, and through this, the execution schedule is updated. The construction process
is managed according to the execution schedule and can make extracts for both period schedule
and weekly schedule (Højgaard, 2017).
Subcontractor meeting
The subcontractor meeting is an eﬀective tool for managing and organising the work of various
subcontractors on the site and also for ensuring the exchange of knowledge and information. It
is important to note that the subcontractor meeting is not used to discuss ﬁnances. The meeting
must focus on future production and on making the collaboration work. At the subcontractor
meetings, it is important to ensure clariﬁcation/agreement on the following (Højgaard, 2017):
■Progress for individual contractors, particularly any dependencies to or from other contrac-
tors,
■Safety and occupational health and safety,
■Looking ahead plan for 5-8 weeks and discovering some inaccuracies in the project material
that need clariﬁcation through either consultants or client and adding this to the list of
obstacles,
■The future look-ahead plan. Execution schedule, which should be detailed if activities
lasting longer than ten business days exist within the ﬁrst 5-8 weeks of the plan,
■Any changes to the project material,
■Any major transports to the construction site or other changes to the construction site
layout that may aﬀect the work,
■Possible visits to Work Environment and Quality
Minutes must be taken oﬀthe subcontractor meetings. The minutes are a legal document that
can or will be used in case of a dispute. A complete and correct record is, therefore, important
(Højgaard, 2017).
A look-ahead plan is a 5-8 weeks section of the execution schedule. The look-ahead plan expresses
what can be done during this period. A period of 5-8 weeks has been selected because the
uncertainty of when to carry out the activities will be too signiﬁcant for a longer horizon. Agree
with the participating subcontractors about what should happen 5-8 weeks ahead. Take a look
at the 8th week and note what the possible barriers for the production to work without being
stopped are. Obstacles can occur in one of the seven streams. These obstacles are written on
the obstacle list with a recent clariﬁcation date (Højgaard, 2017).
1. Prior activities (Is the previous activity ﬁnished?),
2. Materials (Have the materials arrived, should they be delivered, and what about delivery
times?),

4. Crew (Is there enough crew?),
5. Stock (Is there the right stock and everything is ready for it? use?)
6. Space (Is there room to do the work?)
7. External conditions (Conditions you are not master of but can try to take your precautions
about, e.g., weather or approvals).
Chairman’s meeting
The Chairman’s meeting is used to manage and coordinate the work of the various subcontractors
on the construction site. The meeting should take no more than 30-45 minutes. A 1-2 week
section of the execution schedule supplemented with input from the chairmen is called the weekly
schedule. The plan is bought to the chair meeting and agree upon (Højgaard, 2017):
■What will happen next week (who, what, where, and for how long)?
■Coordination of joint weekly schedule,
■When can the next subject come in (handover business)?
■Next week’s material deliveries to the construction site are coordinated (delivery schedule),
■Construction site/ﬂoor plan update. Where materials are stored (area management scheme)
and possibly record who works where and when,
■Working environment conditions on the site.
During or after the meeting, the process manager updates the weekly schedule with the changes.
Thus, the updated weekly schedule is also minutes of meetings. Throughout this manner, it will
be the schedule of the chairmen and not one decided by the construction management. The
weekly schedule sets out what will be done (Højgaard, 2017).
Time-out meeting
The process manager is responsible for holding time-out meetings at appropriate intervals, 1-3
times, on a construction case. The meeting should be seen as an opportunity to learn from the
process along the way and improve or change it based on input from the participants. Participants
may vary as needed, but the focus will typically be on construction management, subcontractors,
and craftsmen. The agenda may be (Højgaard, 2017):
■Focus on ﬂawless delivery,
■The ﬁnal step with craftsmen,
■The ﬁnal process planning,
■Evaluation of process and collaboration at the construction site,
■Proposals for adjusting and improving/optimising the construction process,
■A joint event

as well as a sense of ownership based on their way of being self-organising.

value to the project itself, and the team in it.

How Agile is the construction industry?
The focus of the project has been on Agile Project Management and its mindset in the construc-

the Agile Manifesto? It can be discussed whether or not the construction industry is focusing on
the "individuals and interaction over processes and tools" or not. MT Højgaard has taken in the
lean concept, the basis on which they have developed the TrimByg concept, used to optimise the
construction process. Furthermore, planning is derived from Location-Based planning. There is
no doubt that the right side is tended to.
At the same time, it can be discussed whether some things to the left side are being neglected
to some degree. For instance, Location-Based planning is a great example of a good process

Although the focus is dominant on the processes and tools, some good initiatives towards the
Agile are also in place. For instance, MT Højgaard focuses on the team and a decent start on
collaborations. TrimByg also ensures that all of the parties involved communicating together to

The general tendency, however, indicates that the right side is more in focus. This might have

With respect to the second manifesto value, it can be discussed how the construction industry

Collaboration with the clients is a diﬃcult task in the construction industry, as there are many

6.2
Agile Project Management frameworks and the
Danish Guideline for construction industry
The focus of the study has been on Agile Project Management; it can be debated whether or
not its frameworks are similar to the existing Danish guideline for the construction industry and
MT Højgaard’s TrimByg.
The Sprint Planning Meeting can be compared to the project kick-oﬀmeeting, which refers
to VærdiByg’s Start-up (kick-oﬀ) workshop or TrimByg’s Kick-oﬀwith subcontractor. Sprint
Planning meeting is the ﬁrst meeting of a new Sprint, and this is somehow similar to a kick-oﬀ
meeting, where the relevant parties meet up and are introduced to the project and its success
criteria to create a common understanding of the project. However, Sprint still diﬀers, as new
Sprints constantly comes, whereas Kickoﬀmeeting most likely is not used several times during
the project, this could be reconsidered. The Sprint Planning meeting can also be referred to as
VærdiByg’s and TrimByg’s Phase Scheduling Meeting. These are remotely similar, as the Phase
Scheduling meeting intends to set: the key activities of the subcontractor, the prioritisation of
the tasks, and the connection with tasks and subcontractors in order.
==PAGE:80==
6.2 Agile Project Management frameworks and the Danish Guideline for construction industry70
The Daily Sprint is another event within Scrum that is similar to some of the already existing
meetings such as, VærdiByg’s Site meeting and Coordination meeting, and TrimByg’s Subcon-
tractor meeting and Chairmen meeting. Coordinated meetings and Chairmen meetings are held
weekly and are meant to manage and coordinate the work of various subcontractors on the site.
Site meetings and Subcontractor meetings are held every week or every second week. And they
intend to ensure knowledge sharing and information sharing on the site. A 10-15 minutes daily
meeting could give beneﬁt to the contractors, as they will know the progress of each subcontrac-
tor.
Further, it would give the subcontractors the motivation to work eﬃciently as there is a meeting
every morning. These meetings can be where they agree upon which activities and tasks are
going to be done within that day and what kind of challenges could arise throughout the day, and
what they could do about it. With the site, coordination, subcontractor, and chairmen meetings,
the contractor would only get insights every week or every second week. The daily meetings
will create value, as the time between updates can generate a bigger mistake and can cause the
schedule delay.
The Sprint Review resembles VærdiByg’s Client meeting, as this is where the work can be
reviewed and if there are any issues or challenges can be discussed how to meet them. The
project manager from the third interview mentioned that he managed to apply a Scrum Master
for his project. However, it seems more like a new coordinator is chosen for every week than a
Scrum Master. The reason behind this is that the "Scrum Master" that has been chosen for that
week’s task was to arrange a meeting and make sure that the team understands the intention of
it. This is not what a Scrum Master does. A Scrum Master is responsible for coaching the team
and make sure that the team is following the Agile mindset.
Further, the Scrum Master ensures that the project runs smoothly with the right tools. They
are also responsible for motivating the team and solving conﬂicts that could arise within the
team. The most important task for a Scrum Master is to make sure that the Product Owner
and Development Team are enabled to maximise the value of the product. This means that
the Scrum Master needs a particular skill-set, and random participants of the team cannot just
become one.
The Sprint Retrospective resembles TrimByg’s Time-out meeting, as it is perceived as an oppor-
tunity to learn from the process throughout the project and to improve or change it based on
participants’ input. It also resembles the Final evaluation, where the participants discuss good
and bad experiences of the project. Every project has its own way of evaluating the good and bad
experiences, and what could be apart of the next project, and what could be better for the next
project. The Sprint Retrospective is after every Sprint, where the time-out meeting is 2-3 times
throughout a project, and the ﬁnal evaluation is for the completed project. Having evaluations
every 2-3 weeks or months could help each team or subcontractor, as everyone can address and
discuss their good and bad experiences, evaluate their positions, and what kind of challenges
they have met.
The similarities between these events or meetings should make it easier for the project manager
to implement Agile Project Management, as the team is already familiar with these meetings.
The team needs to know what value these new management tools can bring to the project and
them. All these meetings might seem like a complicated process in the short-term, as they have

During the Phase scheduling

project. But perhaps, one should consider whether one can "dig deeper" and make things more
transparent for each subcontractor.
With the Kanban board, every subcontractor can get an overview of the project, such as: what
needs to be done, which activities are in the making, what is done and what is to be reviewed. This
resembles the Location-Based planning, where all the activities are illustrated on the cyclogram.

causing the contractor to create yet another schedule for the subcontractors. If the contractor is

it more accessible and more transparent with a Kanban board? Make it simple, so everyone
understands and avoids later conﬂicts and challenges that may arise. It could be that some
subcontractors are unexpectedly in the same location, even though they planned it together
through Phase scheduling meeting, but they forgot it, or they could not remember what the
intention of the plan was. Therefore, the subcontractor and also the contractor have an advantage
from the Kanban board, as there will be consensus on what is going on, on the site, and what
activities are upcoming.

The Traditional (waterfall) Project Management and Agile Project Management both have their
advantages and disadvantages, and each has its own set of principles, processes, and workﬂows.
Construction projects are clearly deﬁned in advanced, which is typical in Traditional Project
Management, and it focuses on having structured processes and comprehensive documentation,
with minimal customer involvement. Construction projects depend heavily on proper planning,
and the team works with relatively low guidance unless a challenge arises. However, it gives a
clear direction, as the team knows its responsibilities and the requirements of the project, and
who is in control. So why apply a new way of working, if the current process seems to work? But
does it, though? As mentioned in section 1.1, billions of Danish Kroner are spent on construction
projects, and some projects have diﬃculties keeping within the timetable and budget. It is not
only about learning a new way to manage projects but also to make it more manageable and

What kind of opportunities/beneﬁts can be gained?
First, let’s conclude with what opportunities/beneﬁts Agile Project Management and its frame-
works can contribute. In the quantitative and qualitative results and analysis, section 5.1 and
section 5.2, it was found that Agile Project Management contributes to a reduction in cost,
improved productivity, improved quality, increased business satisfaction and more ﬂexibility.
Besides that, Agile Project Management has also contributed with a better overview because
everything becomes more visible and transparent. The communication within the construction
site has proven to be better, and the Scrum meetings have contributed to a better overview and
communication as well. The tasks and activities have been clear to everyone, as to who is doing
what.
The team is planning, working, and leading the project together, which contributes to a greater
sense of ownership. Another thing that Agile Project Management contributes is the synergy

eﬀect, motivation, and team spirit. The team becomes more ﬂexible as well since everyone is
close to each other’s tasks; this means that the support and help come faster if it is needed.

than working on one task, and if that task cannot be completed, they would not be able to go
further as they would not know what to work on, or what to do, or if anyone is working on that

What kind of challenges arise? According to the research, the biggest challenge is the team’s
willingness to change. Generally, the construction industry is very conservative and has a highly
conservative view of development and change. It seems that the construction industry cares too
much about old habits and is thereby resistant to changes. The research showed that many
project managers have a diﬃcult time implement changes as the team would say that they
"usually" do this and that. It is the attitudes and habits of the team that are hard to change,
and that is why education and training in change management could be needed.
The costs of implementing the actual Agile Project Management can be costly, according to
the research.
However, it does not have to be if it is only some frameworks that are being
implemented. Agile Project Management is very customised; it is about taking diﬀerent tools
from the frameworks and apply them to the project such as meetings and Kanban board, like
the project managers from the qualitative study have done. A simpliﬁed version of Agile can still
beneﬁt the projects.
Can Agile Project Management contribute to IPMA certiﬁcation/course with changes or exten-
sions?
It can be concluded that Agile Project Management can contribute or combined to the IPMA
course that MT Højgaard provides their employees. Agile Project Management can also con-
tribute and combined with Traditional Project Management that the construction industry is
using.
Agile Project Management, in its full context, might be challenging to implement to the con-
struction industry as the research has shown. There might be cultural barriers to incorporate
Agile Project Management in the industry. However, a hybrid approach, with Agile frameworks
and tools, can still beneﬁt the industry.
An example is the use of the Scrum Meetings, where the team meets regularly to discuss the
progress and achievements of the previous day, what the day’s activities and goals, and what
could prevent them from doing their activities. The Daily Sprint should help identify and minimise
obstacles so that no signiﬁcant errors or shortcomings arise.
The Sprint Planning will help create improved communication and motivation within the team, as
they work closely together on the process planning until an agreement has been reached between
them. This is considered to provide a better understanding of the task and a clearer picture of
the overall tasks.
Another example is the use of a Kanban board, or a simpliﬁed version of it, as this visualise all
the tasks and make it more transparent and visible for everyone on the project.

begins with the decision to try. If one does not try, there would not be a beginning. If there is
not a beginning, one will not get wiser or more experienced. It is about taking small steps at a
time and try to prove the value of Agile frameworks. Small steps are better than no steps, and
slow progress is better than absolutely no progress.

8.1
Hybrid Approach
The ﬁrst perspective reﬂects on the already written and how the results can have signiﬁcance
and relevance in a broader context, such as hybrid project management of Lean Construction
and Agile Project Management.
The construction industry works with Traditional Project Management. Recently, in the con-
struction industry, concepts of the construction process have changed signiﬁcantly. Nowadays,
Lean Construction has been embraced by many construction companies, as the industry has
recognised the need to be lean.
Lean Construction, or TrimByg, is already a part of MT Højgaard. Therefore it would make
sense to look into the hybrid of Lean Construction and Agile Project Management, or TrimByg
and Agile Project Management. As mentioned in section 4.4, Lean is a philosophy, while Agile
is a conceptual framework. Customer satisfaction through continuous collaboration is the main
focus of Agile, while Lean works at minimising waste and maximising customer value with fewer
resources.
==PAGE:90==
8.2 Higher Knowledge Level
80
One of the most eﬀective ways to increase the eﬃciency of the construction industry is to improve
the planning and control process (Aziz and Hafez, 2013). Last Planner System’s purpose is to
create better predictability and reliable schedules that keep in practice, and this involves the
people who do the work. The result is better communication, overview, and ﬂow, which in turn
means better compliance with time, better ﬁnances, better security, and greater job satisfaction.
The Last Planner System breaks down the planning of work on the construction project into three
levels: the Phase Scheduling plan, the Period plan, and the Weekly plan. (Lean Construction -
DK, 2015). Last Planner System is similar to Agile as both approaches interact with the client.
The last Planner adjusts and improves the plan based on feedback; the same goes for Agile.
The hybrid approach of Lean and Agile is where the Last Planner System is used to create a
master plan for the project and where the Location-Based Planning is for high-level planning.
Whereas, Agile can bring more detailed planning and a better overview of the project with simple
visualisation such as Kanban board.
Agile could bring more regular meetings to discuss the
progress of the project, such as Daily Sprint. A Retrospective meeting could be implemented
regularly as well, for example, a weekly evaluation, as lessons learned concept, instead of only
using the evaluation meeting at the end of the completed project.
If the Lean Construction or TrimByg can minimise waste, and maximising customer value with
fewer resources in construction projects, then why can Agile not make the construction project
more ﬂexible as well? Lean Construction and Agile’s hybrid version could open up the opportunity
for the construction industry to save time and cost while at the same time satisfying the client
and thereby making proﬁts from their projects.
8.2
Higher Knowledge Level
This perspective takes into account that the knowledge level could be higher at the execution
level. It should be considered whether the level of knowledge is high enough at that level about
the way construction project develops.
For example, when introducing new work approaches or processes, it is essential to consider
whether these changes involve the need for enhancing a higher level of knowledge and training
of the subcontractor and their craftsmen.
Nowadays, there are many foreign craftsmen at a lower cost than Danish craftsmen. The con-
struction industry should rely on well-trained craftsmen that can produce a high-quality product.
A higher level of knowledge may increase the likelihood of developing new and innovative solu-
tions that will eventually mean improvements in the industry’s productivity. By enhancing each
craftsman level of knowledge, more can be created and thus increased productivity.
Within the new initiatives, continuous education should be given the highest priority. Danish
construction craftsmen are well qualiﬁed and have acquired many skills and abilities that allow
them to deliver a product with good quality. Nevertheless, there is still a need for craftsmen to
be educated in communication, collaboration, coordination, leadership and management skills,
quality control, and planning.

8.3 Future Study

8.3
Future Study
An idea for further study of the topic has also arisen after the thesis was written. The Agile
frameworks are quite interesting, especially the Scrum approach. Look into how Scrum could be
implemented through the design and planning phase of a construction project. Also, consider
how the Scrum Roles, Artifacts, and Events can incorporate in a construction project. Scrum
has great potential for the construction industry.
Scrum could be implemented as a way to
communicate between diﬀerent contractors and subcontractors to reduce waste of time, i.e.,
Daily Sprint could be beneﬁcial to inform the subcontractors about the work progress and the
daily goal of other subcontractors that are on the site.
Another idea for further study is to compare an IT company that uses Agile Project Management
and Scrum approach, with a construction company that uses Traditional Project Management.
In doing so, the two companies could be compared to how agile they are in relation to the Agile
Manifesto and the twelve principles. Throughout this, diﬀerences and similarities between the
two companies could be identiﬁed.
