Abstract 
Laser surface treatment is an effective method to improve the surface hardness and wear 
resistance of titanium and its alloys. Laser remelting in the open atmosphere was performed on 
bare beta titanium alloy with different a duty of cycles (40-60-80-100). Considering that all the 
laser treatment parameters were the same, variable duty of cycle can significantly affect on 
surface quality and thickness of treated layer. In this work, the composition, microstructure, 
micro-hardness, surface morphology and tribological properties, specially coefficient friction 
(COF) and size distribution of wear debris (DLS) were carefully characterized and compared. 
The experimental results showed that laser treated samples and bare beta titanium have 
considerable differences in the microstructure, and surface color and morphology. Between 
laser treated samples, by increasing duty of cycle, mechanical behavior due to increased 
thickness of the treated layer is improved. Moreover, tribological properties were strongly 
affected by the hardness and microstructure of laser treated beta titanium samples and laser 
remelting led to remarkable progress in their wear resistant properties. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Keywords: Laser surface remelting, beta titanium alloy, tribological properties, duty of 
cycle, hardness, wear resistance

1. Introduction 
 
The emergence of new philosophical theories about the human relationship with the world, as well 
as the remarkable advances in medical technology in the twentieth century, have allowed us to 
dream more about humankind and provide us the opportunities to bring these dreams closer to 
reality. With regard to these principles, the restoration and reproduction of human organs have 
become one of the main and important scientific and research issues of recent decades. As an 
example, global demand for orthopedic implants has been ever increasing due to rapidly aging 
population, road accidents, and sports injuries [2]. 
On July 26, 2017, the BIS Research Center published a report titled "Global Big Data in Healthcare 
Market- Analysis and Forecast, 2017-2025" which showed that the global market for orthopedic 
devices is valued 40.20 billion in 2016 and is expected to reach 60.02 billion by the end of 2023 
[3].On the other hand, Europe always plays an important role in the global medical industry, and 
many European countries are exporting medical devices to other markets. Meanwhile, Denmark 
is also one of the emerging countries in the field of orthopedic implants [4]. 
Surface treatment of biomaterials is critical as interaction with biological environment such as 
bone occurs at the surface of biomaterials. So, properties of surfaces are main factors that 
determine rejection or acceptance of biomaterial in the body [5]. Titanium alloys are extensively 
used as the most prominent bimetallic material in medical device and orthopedic implants, but 
they are not used as bearing materials for orthopedic implants mainly because of the poor wear 
resistance [6].Therefore, improvement of this material in terms of wear resistance is highly 
important. Laser surface remelting in the presence of atmospheric gases is one of the surface 
modification methods that produce hardened layer at topmost surface that improved the wear 
resistance and mechanical properties of surface [7]. This project deals with laser surface remelting 
of beta-titanium alloy to improve wear resistance and hardness of surface by formation of nitride 
and oxide layers on top of titanium surfaces as bearing beta-titanium alloy for prosthetic articular 
joints.

after tribostress. It was endeavored to explain influential parameters in the better tribological 
performance of the treated beta titanium samples as compared to untreated beta titanium.

2. Literature Review 
 
2.1. Tribology  
Tribology is the science of studying surfaces moving relative to one another for better performance 
such as bearings [8]. Fundamentally, the tribology is the art of providing solutions to deal with the 
economically significant issues, especially in the maintenance and repair of equipment exposed to 
wear [9]. Tribology is very important in the modern world because of the great energy loss caused 
by friction and wear of the mechanical systems consisting of one or more moving parts. Generally, 
the tribology consists of three main subjects: friction, wear, and lubrication [10]. Friction is 
defined as resistance to relative motion, wear is a loss of material due to motion, and lubrication 
is an application of fluids or coatings to reduce friction and wear [8]. 
The tribology plays an important role in the function of artificial joints too [11]. Artificial joints 
are exposed to a dynamic force far greater than body weight during walking, which is accompanied 
by relative motion at the bearing parts. The friction and wear are crucial parameters in the design 
of orthopedic parts because particles which resulted from wear contribute to harmful bio reactions. 
Thus, it is important to reduce friction and wear by proper lubrication [8]. Hence, we give a brief 
description of these parameters. 
The friction is resistance to relative motion. The amount of friction is related to factors such as 
material properties, surface features, component dimensions and environmental conditions. In 
most cases, lower friction is desirable in order to obtain the highest efficacy of operation. Overall, 
the friction increases with a loaded force and a rough surface but can be decreased by use of a 
lubricant. In fact, the friction depends not only on the loaded force but also on the complex 
functionality of the materials and properties of their surfaces that are in contact with each other 
[9]. A most conventional instrument to measure friction forces is tribometer. 
The wear is defined as the loss of material, which is usually due to slipping at the interface. In 
total, the wear is undesirable and causes increased friction and ultimately fracture in the piece. 
Like friction, a lubricant can be used to minimize the wear and to avoid direct contact with each 
other [12].  
There are two common types of wear: abrasive wear and adhesive wear. In the abrasive wear, 
harder materials scratch and remove softer materials. In the adhesive wear, the two bodies are 
adhered together and start to wear from further relative motion [9]. In the artificial joints, wear 
==PAGE:19==
 
4 | P a g e  
 
causes not only the loss of materials, the loss of precision of bearing dimensions, and the lowering 
of tribological characteristics and performance, but also renders adverse biological reactions and 
effects. Recent researches show that the wear particles in the artificial joints cause sudden tissue 
reactions and bone inflammation. Therefore, understanding the wear mechanism can help to 
design more suitable orthopedic implants [8]. Usually, the wear of artificial joints can be studied 
through simulated experiments using pin-on-disc tribometer. 
In short, the tribology is an interdisciplinary science as a combination of mechanical engineering, 
materials science, and chemical engineering. The most critical section in the tribology is the study 
of surfaces moving relative to each other. Therefore, topography, surface and texture profiles and 
surface properties are very important, which is vital for orthopedic implants. It is quite obvious 
that it is challenging to achieve the normal joint function in the design of artificial joints. Hence, 
designing the surfaces of the artificial joint bearings involves two fundamental goals: reducing 
wear and sufficiently low friction. 
 
2.2. Metallic biomaterials 
Metallic biomaterials are used extensively to construct orthopedic and surgical implants [13]. Two 
important factors of high strength and fracture resistance providing by this category of materials, 
assuming the proper manufacturing process, resulting in the production of long-lasting implants 
with good performance in the main load-bearing situations. On the other hand, there are very 
extensive and somewhat easy techniques, including casting and machining, for the fabrication of 
simple and complex forms, increasing the use of metal in the field of orthopedics [14]. 
It should be noted that the metal processing (cooled molten metal for solid formation) determines 
its microstructure and the properties of metal [14]. Hence the understanding of the properties of a 
metal and its processing during the production process of a metallic piece is particularly important 
in the orthopedic implants in order to achieve a proper function [15]. 
 
In continue, it will be discussed about the metallic biomaterials and their properties, their history 
of progress and limitations, especially in terms of titanium which the main metal in this thesis. 
However, it is necessary to give a brief explanation about the metal structure beforehand.  There 
are three types of atomic bonds: ionic, covalent and metallic. Metals are characterized by a metallic 
interatomic bond that determines many of the physical properties of the metal such as strength, 
==PAGE:20==

flexibility, and thermal resistance. In addition, most of the metals used in the implants consist of 
three crystal lattice structures of fcc, hcp, and bcc (see figure 1). In reality, these crystalline 
structures have defects in the arrangement of their atoms compared to the ideal layout, which is 
called a lattice defect and has a very strong influence on the physical, mechanical and chemical 
properties of the metal [14].

It is worth noting that elastic moduli (Young's modulus and shear modulus) are intrinsic properties 
of the materials and are affected very little by the metal processing. In contrast to elastic moduli, 
other properties of metal such as structural strength are sensitive, meaning they are affected by 
metal processing. Hence, the most important point in the orthopedic implants or essentially 
artificial joints in contact with bone is that most of the metals have a very high elastic constant

the bone compared to the metal implants. As a result, the contact of the metal implants and bone 
and their interactions induces stress due high modulus difference and the creation of "shilling 
stress" in the host bone(see figure 2), which causes serious bone damage over time, including 
significant bone loss, inflammation, and osteomyelitis [14].

The first titanium used as biomaterial was commercially pure titanium (Cp Ti). The commercially 
pure titanium is classified into four grades of 1-4, where the concentration of impurities such as 
nitrogen, oxygen, and iron increases as the grade rises. It should be noted that the commercially 
pure titanium strength enhances with increasing concentrations of impurities and, conversely, 
decreasing flexibility. Subsequently, a titanium alloy called Ti6Al4V was used. The recent 
discovery of the toxicity of vanadium (V) has suggested the use of other types of titanium alloys, 
including Ti5Al2.5Fe and Ti6Al7Nb [19]. Considering the detrimental effects of the aluminum on 
the organs of the body, the next generation of titanium alloys has emerged as an example of Ti-
15Sn-4Nb-2Ta-0.2Pd [20]. It should be mentioned that all of these alloys are α-type Ti, except for 
the pure titanium [21]. 
The development of titanium alloys for applications as biomaterials requires not only non-toxic 
but also non-allergic elements. In this regard, based on the findings, the elements such as Nb, Ta, 
Mo, and Zr are suitable for titanium alloys due to their non-toxic and non-allergic properties [21].

2.3. Surface Treatment 
The structural and surface compatibility are the functional requirements of orthopedic implants. 
Structural compatibility means shape, size and bulk mechanical features of an implant, such as 
elastic modulus and strength. Surface compatibility means the surface specifications of an implant, 
including surface topography, tribological and corrosion properties, and wettability, affecting 
directly or indirectly orthopedic implant function and bone healing. Therefore, not only good 
materials are required for the functioning of the implants, but also implant surface must meet the 
requirements [30]. There are different surface modification technologies such as mechanical, 
chemical and physical methods that play a significant role in improving biocompatibility, 
hardness, and wear of titanium and titanium alloys [31]. 
Some conventional mechanical surface modification methods include machining, grinding, 
polishing, forming or removing material surface. The mechanical modification targets specific 
surface roughness and topography, removal of contamination and adhesion enhancement at next 
bonding stages [31]. 
==PAGE:24==
 
9 | P a g e  
 
There are some chemical surface treatments including electrochemical treatment (anodic 
oxidation), sol-gel, chemical vapor deposition (CVD) and biochemical modification. Chemical, 
electrochemical or biochemical reactions occur at the interface between titanium surface and a 
solution during chemical processes, electrochemical treatment or biochemical modification. 
Chemical vapor deposition refers to the chemical reaction between the chemicals in the gas phase 
and the sample surfaces that leads to a non-volatile compound deposition on the substrate. The 
chemical reactions of the sol-gel process occur in solution, not at the interface between the sample 
surface and the solution [31]. 
Physical vapor deposition (PVD) and thermal spraying are good examples of surface modification 
processes that do not cause chemical reactions. In these processes, electrical and kinetic energy, 
as well as heat, are considered the main tools to modify surface layer or form the coatings or films 
on the alloys of titanium or titanium itself. In the process of thermal spraying, liquid droplets of 
the coating materials are obtained with the help of heat, and then the substrate is instantly (kinetic 
energy) covered by these droplets. In the process of physical vapor deposition, ions, molecules or 
atoms of the coating material are formed by the chemical reaction between neighboring vapor and 
a substrate surface; these particles are made in a target and transmitted to substrate surface to react 
with the atoms of the surface lattice and perform condensation, which results in film growth. 
Electrical discharge in a vacuum, electron beam, resistance heating or laser, as well as ion 
implantation and glow discharge plasma treatment, are examples of the means to form these ions, 
molecules or atoms [31]. 
 
2.3.1. Laser surface modification methods 
The principles of these approaches embrace the induction of controlled alterations of the surface 
physically, chemically and mechanically with the aid of direct interaction of the laser beam and 
the working material. The positive properties of these methods are flexibility of working with a 
versatile range of materials, feasibility of modifying a selected area, remote non-contact 
processing, and improved metallurgical microstructures due to very high cooling rates. Surface 
hardenings, remelting, texturing, alloying and cladding are the mechanisms by which lasers can 
modify the implant surfaces. The addition of exogenous materials to the surface layer occurs in 
laser surface cladding and alloying strategies. The surface heating over optimal temperature using 
laser followed by cooling-down are considered in the laser surface hardening and remelting. The 
surface characteristics are modified through imparting microstructural changes in laser surface 
==PAGE:25==
 
10 | P a g e  
 
hardening way. The surface topography, roughness, wettability, wear, hardness and corrosion 
resistance are altered via surface remelting, and this affects the surface biocompatibility. Melting, 
evaporation and rapid solidification are main reasons for these changes within laser surface 
remelting [30]. 
One workpiece is exposed to a laser beam through an appropriate optical system in laser surface 
remelting and presents the relative motion between the beam and the workpiece relying on the 
purposes. This relative motion may occur due to robotic controllers, CNC systems, and 
galvanometric scanning mirrors. Optical components such as collimators, optical lenses, 
homogenizers, masks, refractive elements, and dynamic optical elements can control the shape 
and size of the beam [30]. 
The main processing factors in laser surface modification can be laser power, beam spot size and 
interaction time. The product of laser power and interaction time divided by beam cross sectional 
area is defined as the energy density delivered to the surface. The beam spot diameter/the scanning 
speed ratio means the interaction time for a continuously moving laser beam, but zero or negligible 
beam displacement during a single pulse leads to the interaction time to be equal to the pulse on 
time in the pulsed interactions [30]. 
Previous studies have reported the effects of various process parameters on the physical, 
mechanical and metallurgical properties of the remelt surface of Ti alloys. However, many of these 
reports are on the laser processing conducted in an N2 rich environment, appearing nitride surfaces 
through the strong affinity of Ti towards Nitrogen in the presence of higher thermal ranges. These 
interactions are classified in laser surface alloys. This nitriding undergoes poor reproduction and 
develops uncontrollable cracks in the surface and weakly non-homogeneous layers due to the 
complexity of controlling the dissolution of the alloy element in the melt pool [7]. 
According to Langlade et al. [32] and Ageev et al. [33], Ti and other alloys oxides are produced 
because of remelting in air. TiO2 predominantly can be found in the surface oxide layer, followed 
by TiO, Ti2O3, and Ti3O5. The brittleness is elevated and lowers the fatigue resistance is reduced 
due to oxygen diffusion into the surface [34]. The fatigue strength is decreased up to 20% for 
Ti6Al7Nb through partial surface melting during laser marking of prostheses in air following the 
oxygen diffusion or the cracks in the remelt surface [35]. These events are restricted in case of 
doing the process in an inert atmosphere by Argon or other shielding gasses. Accordingly, the 
monitoring process parameters, including laser power, beam spot diameter, duty of cycle, 
==PAGE:26==
 
11 | P a g e  
 
frequency and laser scanning speed, can manage and reproduce the obtained surface characteristics 
[36]. 
The processes can strongly adapt to industrial environment through a simple set-up and the least 
number of process factors. Moreover, the cost of processing can be lowered in the case of failing 
elaborate set-up and reduced material and energy consumption. Hence, the processes of cladding 
and alloying can be replaced by laser surface remelting in the construction of biocompatibility-
improved orthopedic implants [31]. 
However, there is limited information on the laser surface remelting of Ti and its alloys for 
orthopedic implant application in spite of multiple reports on the roles and the effects of these 
process parameters on implant surface compatibilities [31].  
 
2.3.2. Duty of cycle  
Beta titanium alloys were treated with laser technique at the Queen’s University of Belfast (Dr. 
Chi-Wai Chan’s Laboratory) according to the previous methods. Technical details are available in 
literature [6], [2].The major parameter in this study was the variation of duty of cycle. Duty cycle 
is directly related to the pulse duration and pulse width laser parameters. It is basically the ratio of 
the pulse width over the time between pulses (see below figure 3 for image representation). A duty 
cycle equal to 1 defines a CW laser (continuous laser operation). The average laser power is 
highest in CW mode. It is also the most heat sensitive regime (i.e., you can imagine it is more heat 
transfer in the laser-material interaction zone). 
 
Figure 3: Duty of Cycles 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
12 | P a g e  
 
 
 
When the duty cycle of laser is less than 1, the laser is operated in pulsed mode. The average laser 
power is reduced, so do the amount of “heat transfer in the interaction zone” and “the interaction 
time between the laser and material”. The rule of thumb is that shorter the duty cycle, shorter the 
interaction time interval and smaller the heat transfer. In the laser nitriding process, the width and 
penetration of each nitride laser track, surface features (roughness/topography) and microstructure 
are directly controlled by the heat transfer in the laser-material interaction process. Too much heat 
transfers and/or too long interaction time in CW mode can result cracks in the nitride layer. The 
motive of nitriding the materials in pulsed mode (i.e., reduces the duty cycle) is to find out an 
optimum laser setting that can retain the positive nitride features (i.e., very hard and lower friction) 
and minimize the negatives (i.e., heat deformation and cracks in nitride layer).

3.1.1. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) 
Images taken with scanning electron microscopy contain remarkable details and high resolution. 
Superficial parts of the specimens or cross-sectional layers could be observed by SEM, and it is 
known as a non-destructive method. In a vacuum chamber, a specimen of interest is hit by beams 
with high levels of energy coming from an electron gun that accelerates them. Amplifying the 
emitted electrons makes it possible to detect them and produce an image after processing the 
obtained signals. The main parts of SEM are shown in Figure 6. Electron gun emits electron to the 
objective lens, which could be collimated by the help of the apertures and electron lenses. 
Afterward, detector captures the resulting signals. Electrons emitted and interacted with the object 
could be categorized into two groups of backscattered electron (BSEs) and secondary electrons 
(SEs) with varied levels of energy [44]. As shown in Figure 5, the energy level of SEs is less than 
50eV and is lower in the depth of its penetration.  SEs has great application for morphological 
analysis due to its characteristics. BSEs can penetrate more deeply, which makes it appropriate to 
perform cross-sectional analysis [45]. Components with a higher atomic number are illustrated 
brighter by BSEs.

3.1.4. Glow discharge optical emission spectroscopy (GD-OES) 
Glow Discharge Optical Emission Spectroscopy (GDOES) provides rapid, direct bulk analysis 
and depth profiling analysis of solids: metals, glasses, and ceramics. The speed of analysis makes 
it particularly interesting in the context of process development and quantity control.  
The anode is a copper tube with a diameter of, as a rule, 4 mm. Located in front of the anode and 
separated from the anode ceramic with an O-ring seal, is the sample, a cathode. The sample is 
mounted on the source by applying to the O-ring. The space inside the anode and from the cavity 
between the sample and the ceramic is evacuated, after which argon enters the tube. Under the 
influence of an electric field, argon ions are accelerated to the sample with sufficient energy to 
spray the surface of the sample. Sputtered particles, in turn, enter the plasma and are excited 
through collisions. The emerging optical emission characterizes the elemental composition of the 
sample. The spectrum is recorded on an optical spectrometer.GDOES analysis can be performed 
on both conducting and non-conducting surfaces. It is also possible to measure curved 
surfaces.  All relevant elements including the light ones as O, N,  are measured quantitatively. [48]

3.1.5. Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) 
Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) is a method used to determine the distribution of particles in 
solutions and suspensions, which is a non-destructive and fast technique for detecting the particle 
size in the range of nanometers to microns. In recent technologies, the particles with a diameter of 
less than nm can also be measured by this approach. This method depends on the interaction of 
light with the particle. The light scattered by the nanoparticles in the suspension is varied with 
time, which can be attributed to the particle diameter [49]. 
 
3.1.6. Pin-on-disk tribometer 
Pin-on-disk tribometer is a method of characterizing the frictional and wear properties between 
two sliding materials. In this setup, a pin is loaded against a rotating disk. The shape of pin end is 
typically round. However, it could be designed in any other shapes based on the properties required 
for specifically desired contact simulation. The ratio of the frictional force to the loading force on 
the pin is used to calculate the friction coefficient [50].

Based on the results of surface roughness before and after pin-on-disk tests, it can be concluded 
that surface roughness (Ra) has remarkably increased after the laser treatments of beta-titanium. 
In addition, surface of roughness value (Ra) increase with increased DoC. This increase can be 
rationalized as follows. Firstly, the increase of DoC makes the temperature of heat transfer zone 
higher. Then, this rise in temperature consequently results in making the bigger melting pool and 
generating larger turbulence. [54] Therefore, surface deformation occurs due to flow of melting 
pool (liquid) and turbulence effect, possibly leading to increases in surface roughness. Moreover, 
when the temperature of heat transfer zone goes up, the remelted zone (RZ) increases, causing the 
formation of the dendrite structure in created TiN layer. Overlapping also can increase surface 
roughness value. The microstructure images, EDX and XRD data support this process. (see figure 
31)

4.3. Mechanical Properties  
4.3.1. Hardness test  
As it can be seen in figure 47, the Vickers hardness tests for all samples have been conducted and 
analyzed in two different forms. This is done based on the characteristics of TiN layers of samples, 
which are recognized in OM images.  In the first step, the hardness profile was acquired along 
three vertical lines (from the surface to bulk of the samples). In the second run, hardness was 
measured in a lateral direction again depending on the microstructure of laser-treated sample 
surfaces. The untreated beta-titanium base metal had a hardness of 250 ± 10 HV. Further, the 
hardness in the nitrided zone and HAZ of samples were observed to vary along with varying DoC. 
There is a large difference between laser-treated and untreated samples, and the nitrided area of 
beta-titanium was harder than untreated beta-titanium. But, hardness values varied significantly 
according to the microstructure of laser-treated samples too. As shown in the figures (48-51), 
hardness showed the maximum values in remelted zone and then gradually decreased in the base 
metal zone. In other words, the hardness of RZ is higher than that of HAZ.   
The results from the second experiment revealed that the hardness of the layer right below the 
laser-treated layer of sample of the lowest DoC (40%) is very low in some parts and similar to the 
hardness of base metal.

5. Conclusion 
 
In this project, an investigation has been conducted to determine the effect of laser surface 
remelting on structure, composition, mechanical properties and finally tribological properties of 
Beta-Titanium alloy. The results were compared with bare beta titanium alloy. Main conclusions 
are as follows. 
a. Different duty of cycle in laser surface remelting process can make a difference in 
surface colour, morphology, as well as the size and distribution of dendrite structure 
in TiN layers formed on top of Ti surface. 
b. During laser surface remelting, the surface roughness of beta titanium increased 
significantly. 
c. The hardness of treated layers depends on the volume fraction of TiN dendrite 
structures. By increasing DoC parameter, hardness remarkably increased. However, 
imperfect linearity of laser tracks affected on the homogeneity of the mechanical 
behavior of samples.   
d. Tribological properties of laser treated of beta titanium such as wear and friction 
strongly depend on the hardness and microstructure of beta titanium.
