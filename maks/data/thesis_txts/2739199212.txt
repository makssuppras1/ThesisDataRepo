Abstract
Objective: Poor mental wellbeing among young adults is an increasing problem
throughout the world. In Denmark, 34% of women in the demographic scored
low in mental health analysis, while 20% of their male counterparts scored low
as well, making the need for focus on the subject higher than ever. Following
an adapted double diamond approach, this project aims to research the prob-
lem statement of how to design a solution for a technological intervention, and
if such a solution would be feasible to promote mental wellbeing in young adults.
Methods: The PRISMA model was used to gain insight into the subject of
mental health in relation to technological intervention.
In addition, market
analysis of state of the art was made to investigate whether a new product is
needed on the market.
Low and high fidelity protoyping and testing thereof, as well as a usability study
was conducted to ensure user-centered development. With a user-centered and
evidence backed foundation, an android application was developed. With the
android application, a feasibility study was completed.
Results: The PRISMA model resulted in 33 highly relevant papers. Further-
more, the market analysis found that a new product was needed.
The usability study deemed the prototypes to be usable and intuitive with minor
adjustments. Lastly, the feasibility study found that all testers had generally
positive attitudes toward the application, indicating that the system is feasible.
Conclusion: Key features and implementations to promote mental wellbeing
through technological intervention, along with both major and minor pitfalls
were found.
Although the feasibility study indicates the system to be feasible, not enough
testers participated in the study. Therefore, a feasibility study should be con-
ducted once more to confirm the indications.

Introduction
"It is going in the wrong direction with the mental health of the youth." was the
title of an article authered by The Dansih Health Authority in March 2022[1].
The article takes it basis in the 2021 danish Healthprofile, conducted every four
years, which is produced by widely distributing questionaires to danish citizens.
The report concerns itself with both physical and mental health of the entire
population divided into several groups.
The groups consist of, among other
things, age, gender and education. Most spectacular of the group divisions is
the numbers present in the different age categories. More than 20% of men in
the age groups 16-24 years old and 25-34 years old score low in mental health
analysis. Similarly, young women in the age group 25-34 years old, 25% score low
in mental health. Remarkably a whole 34% of danish women in the age group
16-24 also score low on mental health. These results show an increase of low
mental health of 5-10% across all discussed groups in just four years. Declining
mental health has been a trend for the last decade according to these studies,
but rather than the decline being linear, it seems to be nearing an exponential
development. This trend is not reserved to the confines of Denmark. Several
modernly developed countries report a decline of mental health omung their
young citizens. To name a few, Germany, The United States of America and
France are all experiencing this development through recent years.
Mental health is a complex, multifactorial topic, which is not easily diagnosed,
nor treated. In addition, determining the root cause of an individuals mental
health issues is a cumbersome task. Both research and specultaion point to
several aspects of the average, modern young adult. Among these aspects are
the use of mobile devices and social media. Another article from The Danish
Health Authority [2] suggests that social media is both a good tool and a source
of stress for the young adults. It quickly becomes apparant that mobile devices
and social media is not going anywhere. It is also evident that social media
has an ambiguous impact on young adults, despite its negative reputation. but
also that they do not only impact the youth negatively despite the reputation
given. The problem and solution may even be found in the same place. One of
differentiating factors of social media and mobile application having a negative
or positive impact is debated to be found in the usage of said tools.
One method of deriving a positive impact from mobile device usage is the adop-
tation of one or more mental health applications. These applications are often
refered to as mHealth applications. Along with an increase in mental health
issues, an increase in mental health application has also taken place.
These
applications all share the goal of improving their user’s mental health. The far
majority of these application cater to mild mental health issues such as feeling
overwhelmed or mild sadness. This is often a calculated decision in ordrer to not

replace clinical help. Instead, the application are more of a preventative tool to
be used prior to the user needing professional help. The problem with a lot of
these applications is, according to numerous research papers, that they are often
not based on research. Keeping up engagement and generating revenue without
inconveniencing the user is not easily achievable. Moreover, a desire to imple-
ment the newest technologies, such as chatbots, is present in most application
development, and this could end up hurting the user in the end.
These applications, successful or not, often focus on specific aspects of mental
health, aspects such as mindfulness, sobriety, stress relief and motivation. The
focus of this project will be to cater to the habitual nature of human beings. To
instill habits good for mental health and nudge users to do tasks that will in turn
clear up mental clutter or directly boost their mood. This focus will be backed
by a well established basis of knowledge gathered from research on the matter
of relevant mHealth. The demand for mental health solutions are growing and
the market is vast and filled with many differnet niches and gaps to be filled.
Innovation and research based development is needed to mitigate the damages
to come. Therefore, this project aims to answer the problem statement: How
can we promote mental wellbeing through Technological Intervention.

Related Work & Background
2.1
Background knowledge
Although the concept of therapy within mobile applications is a relatively new
one, there is a lot of research, existing applications and critique available online.
In 2010 the arguably first widely accepted mental health mobile application
Headspace was founded. Since then, more than 10.000 mobile applications deal-
ing with mental health has been released on various application platforms. Some
of these applications are developed on a foundation of research, and some are
more liberal in their development. The mobile mental health app developed
in this project is rooted in research largely gained from utelizing the PRISMA
model, and analysis of other popular mental health and mindfulness options on
the market. In order to have the best preconditions for the chapters ahead, a
walkthrough of relevant litterature and mental health options currently on the
market will be made in this chapter.

2.2

2.2.1
Research approach
With an increase of tools which are aiming to alleviate the burden of mental
health issues, research and knowledge in the field is also expanding. This project
aims to build upon this growing foundation of knowledge and utilize it to steer
the direction in which mobile mental health should move. In order to do this,
extensive research is needed. All of the litterature research was done by following
the PRISMA model.
As will be explained in the next section as well, the PRISMA model is a method
of gathering an often unusably large amount of research papers and then boiling
down the material into a few highly relevant papers, usable for the project
at hand.
Four main stages of the model is traversed in order to obtain the
most relevant papers; identification, screening, eligibility and the model then
concludes in the included papers.
Two platforms for paper gathering were used. Base-search.net and findit.dtu.dk.
Base search was used because of its wide inventory of available papers and the
helpful parameter options for directed searches.
Findit.dtu.dk was used be-
cause of similar reasons and the group and project’s close relation to DTU.
Several search phrases were used to obtain the optimal search results. Ulti-
mately, the search phrase "Mental health mobile application" was used as both
mental health and mobile applications are highly relevant for the project. The
same phrase was used for both of the search engines. Below in figure 1 the
results of the different steps of the PRISMA model is shown.

2.4.1
Happify
Happify is a relatively simple mobile mental health application. At Verywell
Mind the app is marketed as the best available tool for positive thinking. This
is reflected in the applications main feature, positive games. Researching the
application, a clear flow of the application was found after the profile creation
was complete. The user is prompted to choose a track. These tracks determine
the games and the positive themes in which the user will partake. Referring to
figure 4 below, on page one, the Aspire category of game is chosen (the blue
mountain icon), in this game, the user will pop moving balloons containing
inspirational words in regards to aspiration. This is one of several categories
of games and positive themes the user can engage in. On page number two, a
statistics or proggresion page is shown. Here, the user can view what they have
completed and what individual categories they have progressed in. On the third
page, a page for making achievements public is shown. Here, the user may show
friends within the application what they have done and in return recieve likes
and comments from those friends.

These aspects are the essential functionalities of the Happify application. Ac-
cording to research, letting the user explore their own mind in their own pace and
order is often a good idea when developing mobile mental health applications.[4]
Furthermore giving the user a sense of accomplishment and proggression are
both methods which are often used in mobile applications. This is partly be-
cause they give the user a temporary ’feel good’ and gives them a reason to
come back and use the app again later to further their progression. On the
other hand, letting users in potentially fragile mental states engage in posting
their accomplishments for their friends to see may not be a net positive. Users
are likely to view their mental health application as a safe space which gives
them a positive impact on their mental health. However, when the expectation
of likes and comments become larger than what is being given, users may feel a
lack of validation, stress and lowered self worth. [5] [6]
From Happify, two of three aspects would be good in a new solution. The option
for users to steer their own usage of the application with multiple activities to
choose from. And the progression or statistics page for the user to follow their
own achievements and progression. What should not be implemented is the
social media like environment seen on page three of figure 4.

Headspace
Headspace is one of, if not the most known mental health application on the mar-
ket currently. The application is subscription based with a free trial. Headspace
lays a heavy focus on mindfulness and meditation. In this application there are
no games, but instead exercises where the user often times has the goal of ob-
taining a calmer state of mind. Headspace is traversed with bottom navigation
and three main pages; Home, Explore and a profile site seen below in figure 5.
On the homepage a greeting is shown at the top, an exercise is suggested by the
application below it. At the bottom of the page, a tutorial on how to use the
application is provided. Next is the explore page on page two. Here, a bunch of
exercises are suggested as well as podcasts and special exercises made by public
authors. Additionally, the profile page shows statistics and progression as well
as an option to navigate to settings. Lastly, the application shows the user a
streak, which can be upheld by using Headspace multiple days in a row.

Worry Watch
Worry Watch is an application specializing in anxiety according to the Verywell
Mind website. This application is completely free to use. Initially, the appli-
cation provides a survey inquiring about what the user seeks in their mobile
mental health application. It does not seem that there is any personalization
based on what the user inputs. Instead the survey teases what kind of appli-
cation the user is about to engage with. From that point on, the user is free
to explore the features within the application. This app is also navigated by
bottom navigation, but this time with a total of five main pages.
On the first page in figure 6 a notes feature is present. Alternativ: Here, a
couple of related note-taking methods are present. From the top of the page
and down, the user is supposed to do the following. Record an episode of anx-
iety. Identify the reasoning behind the anxious episode. Ponder what can be
done in such a situation where anxiety occurs and lastly reflect on everything.
On page two a meditation feature is provided. Here, the user again has the
option to select slightly different methods of engaging with the feature. Varia-
tions of meditation such as breathing exercises, meditation and visualisation is
available at the top of the page. Page three serves as a mood log. Here, the
user can log their mood on a scale from one to five, using emojis. The user
can then reflect upon their selflogged data and hopefully learn from it. This
method inspired by personal informatics, and it can be highly valuable for men-
tal health self evaluation. Page four is a page dedicated to motivational quotes.
Again, several variations of the feature is available for the user at the top of the
screen. Contrary to popular belief, motivational quotes are not necessarily corny
or pretentious. A study from the Americian Library of Medicine published in
2018 suggests that motivational quotes are effective in raising self esteem if the
individual reading it has general control of their life and the quote is not overly
positive.[7] Meaning that motivational quotes are less effective for cronically ill
individuals with little to no control over their situation for instance. Further-
more, quotes like "Life is pure joy, be grateful for every minute" are ignoring
the difficult times of life. This can wake emotions like anger and disgust in the
mind of the beholder as it is a clear false representation of life. Instead, quotes
aknowledging, explicitly or implicitly, the harder times of life and focusing on

3.4
PRISMA model
Research can be a daunting task for any project based on previous findings and
litterature. Unstructured research is likely to result in an unstructured knowl-
edge base. Furthermore, it becomes increasingly hard to trace the the papers if
strict notes are not kept. The PRISMA model offers structure and a streamlined
approach to gathering and filtering papers based on several phases.
Initially, a large amount of papers are found through one or more relevant search
engine(s). This phase is the identification phase. Here, it is up to the user to
filter out papers based on a specific author, type of paper or some other general
metric relevant for rough filtering.
Next, the screening phase is undergone for filtering of the initially identified
papers. Here the papers are filtered based on practical metrics: Is the piece of
litterature a complete paper? Is it scientific? etc.
Page 28 of 122
==PAGE:30==
Methods
Following the screening phase is the elligibility phase, here all titles of all papers
are read and deemed relevant or not for the project at hand. When the titles
are read and the papers are filtered based on them, the same thing is done for
the abstracts. When this is done, all the remaining papers, deemed relevant
based on title and abstract, the same thing is done with the entire text of the
papers. This approach ensures that the most timeconsuming filtering tasks are
done on the least amount of papers.
When these phases are complete, the model concludes in the included papers
that are relevant for the project at hand.
Variations in the metrics of filtering can vary from project to project.
The
above runthrough of the PRISMA model was used in this project with slight
alterations in the screening phase.
By using the PRISMA model, this project was able to structurally boil thou-
sands of papers down to a mere 33 relevant and traceable papers.

3.6
Cognitive behavior therapy
Cognitive behaviour therapy (CBT) is a highly aknowledged therapy method,
which has been found to be effective for various mental problems.[11] Among
these problems are anxiety, addiction and depression. Advances in CBT are
made through both research and clinical trials. The therapy method is build
around three core principles:
• Mental health issues are in part caused by unhealthy thought patterns.
• Mental health issues are in part caused by learned unhealthy behaviours.
• People can alleviate their mental health issues with the right coping meth-
ods.
CBT usually revolves around changing thinking patterns and recognizing un-
healthy behaviours and patterns in order to then alter those behaviours and
patterns for the better. Methods of doing that could include:
• Realizing one’s own destructive way of thinking and then reevaluate.
• Understanding others motivation and healthier way of thinking.
• Developing confidence in one’s own capabilities.
• Learn how to calm one’s mind and body.
This is traditionally done with a clinical psyciatrist able to guide the patient. In
more recent iterations of CBT the user can be given a form of CBT treatment
through digital mediums. This form for CBT is sometimes reffered to as IM-
CBT [12]. The key difference is the treatment being delivered through digital
channels opposed to face to face interaction. Some studies claim IM-CBT to
be as effective as traditional CBT. The level of interaction between the patient
and the clinical psychyatrist can vary a lot based on what type of program is
provided. A self-guided treatment has little to no direct interaction between
patient and clinician.
This project utilizes aspects of a self-guided IM-CBT treatment program. While
the user may not consciously realize that they are undergoing treatment, nor
Page 30 of 122
==PAGE:32==
Methods
consciously identify bad thinking patterns and altering them, this project in-
coorporates other aspects of treatment. The user will understand others moti-
vation through motivational quotes, develop confidence in ones own capabilities
through completing tasks and learn to calm ones mind and body through mind-
fulness tasks.
It is also the aim to use these aspects of CBT to unconsciously prepare and
familiarize the user with CBT concepts. This is done with the aim of the user
being more comfortable and equipped to undergo a clinical CBT treatment,
should it become relevant for the user in the future.

should be expected that this option would be priced at up to 100$ for the
scale fo this project. However another candidate is manually distributing
the installation file via a third party service.

installation file used for Android applications are of type .apk which is
almost always flagged as harmful or decietful by mail or chat providers.
In recent years it has even come in violation with service terms for cloud
storage providers such as Google Drive and Dropbox.

guide-videos. To ensure best possible success rate the .apk file is stored at
WeTransfer with a download link, which was then included in a customized

The situation with mobile mental health options today
Research has shown that there definitely is a lot of different ways that companies
are trying to adress the current problem with large amounts of young people

that could potentionally worsen the situation. This extends to various facets
of the solution such as monitization, individual application features, behavior

clinical help, but rather as a supporting tool for mental health therapy.
Following the addapted Double Diamond framework (3.2) the problem space
therefore resulted in a clear set of do’s and don’t’s, as well as an overview of
the current mobile applications which together with the background knowledge
accumulated about the very early stages about mental health and treatment,
all provided a foundation for which the solution space could begin.

4.7.2
The selection algorithm / Task adaptation model
A total of 45 individual tasks are available within the functional prototype for
the user to complete.4 These tasks are divided into nine relevant categories.
4The workbook tasks in this project are created with the help of ChatGPT, to facilitate
an orderly use of the allocated time. The prompt in its entirety is added in appendix on page
108
Page 60 of 122
==PAGE:62==
Results
In each of the nine categories, five tasks reside. These tasks are means to the
end of the user picking up new healthy habits by continuously completing tasks
offered by the application. As mentioned in section 2.3, research showed that
providing the user with options is very important for various reasons. It was
hereby decided to provide the user with the choice between five tasks each day.
The user can complete as many or as few of the offered tasks as they like. It
should be worth noting that the application will not offer more than five tasks a
day, regardless of how many has been completed. The reasoning being that of-
fering more tasks may unnecessarily increase the user’s screentime, which most
likely have more negative effects contrary to positive ones.
Early implementations offered the tasks at random.
While this approach is
not necessarily bad, a few desirable functionalities would not be available. As
mentioned, the goal with these tasks are to instill some positive habits into the
user’s everyday life. While getting into the habit of using the application con-
tinuously ensures the user being offered some healthy or productive tasks, being
able to create habits on a lower level would likely be beneficial for the user.
Therefore, an adaptive task offering model was developed. What this results in,
is every task category having a weight, used to determine whether a task from
a certain category should be offered. The greater the weight, the greater chance
of the category being chosen to provide tasks. A couple of metrics were used
for the model. Including a base weight. A metric for whether a task from this
category completed last time the app was used? What is the cumulated value of
how many times the user has rated their day positively after having completed
a task within this category? How many times has the user completed a task
from this category? With all of these metrics added together for each of the
categories, a final weight is achieved for every category. Refer to figure 39 on
the next page for a visual representation of the weight calculation.
Page 61 of 122
==PAGE:63==
Results
Figure 39: Example of a visual representation of how a category’s weight is calculated
Page 62 of 122
==PAGE:64==
Results
The base weight is initially added to the category, from where the cumulative
value for each time the user has completed a task within the category and then
reiewed their day as "Okay" or above is added. Now a substantial amount is
substracted from the weight if a task from the category was completed last time
the application was used. This is done to make sure the user is not offered
the same category all the time and that all categories have the chance to be
offered. If this measure is not taken, one can imagine that the first completed
task category will be offered more often, then completed more often, resulting
in the category weights being increasingly skewed as the app is used.
The next operation is regarding how many times the user has completed a task
within the category. If the user has completed a task in the category less than
30 times, a value is added to the weight based on the rating of the day the last
time a task within the category was completed. The value is not added if the
rating was "Not sure" or below as the rating may be an indication of the users
enjoyment of the task category. As the application is a mental health applica-
tion, it would be inappropriate to increase the chance of negatively impacting
tasks to be offered to the user.
If the user has completed a task within the category between 30 and 60 times,
no value is added. The reasoning behind that, is that research suggest that it
takes 30 to 60 days to break a habit, and roughly the same time to create a
new one [16]. When the user has completed a task from a category less than 30
times the value is added so the user can be offered the category more often and
be more likely to form a habit. When the user is between 30 and 60 comple-
tions, the likelihood of the category plateaus until the 60th completion mark.
At 60 completions the application determines that the user has formed a habit
of doing some task within the category, and no longer needs to be reinforced
by the app. Therefore the chance of the category being offered is reset, making
room for other categories to be offered and new habits to be formed.
When all weights are calculated, the adaptation model normalizes them, adds
them together in a stack and acummulates their weights. From there, a random
number from zero to one is generated. Based on what category in the stack the
random number falls in the interval of, said category is then chosen. Figure 40
shows a visual representation of this process.
Page 63 of 122
==PAGE:65==
Results
Figure 40: Visual representation of how a category is chosen based on normal-
ized weights and a randomly generated number R
This is repeated four more times so that five category entries are available for
task selection. It is possible for the same category to be chosen multiple times,
making it even more likely that one or more tasks are chosen from the category.
When the categories have been chosen, five tasks are chosen at random. Here,
it is not possible for the same task to be chosen more than once.
The adaptive task selection model ultimately culminates in five tasks being
offered to the user each day they log in. These tasks are based on how many
times the user has chosen the task category previously and the user rating of
the days where said category was chosen. Ensuring that the application adapts
to the perceived preference of the individual user.

Discussion
This section will discuss findings from various sections of the project. As the
previous section described, during the course of this project, a wide range of
results have been produced, ranging from smaller subdeliverables such as iden-
tifying specific pain points a user experiences in a Figma prototype, to bigger
deliverables such as the fully implemented Android application.
Other achievements included a comprehensive research following the PRISMA

A Figma prototype was developed through several iterations, and was used as

actual implementation of the application and its features was developed with

While these achievements were presented in the previous section, it was also

5.1
Limitations
While being able to complete subdeliverables as mentioned, it was also apparent
that during the course of the project several challenges was encountered. The
following will present some of the limitations which would be relevant to discuss.
5.1.1
Testing
Testing is essential in developing a user-centered product.
Having potential
end-users test and give feedback on the development of the product, allows the
developers to steer the product in a direction where it is useful to someone.
However essential testing is, there are some very well-known obstacles to tackle.
The testers need to be recruited, engaged throughout the testing and give gen-
uine feedback. To some extend it is the developers responsibility to ensure these
obstacles are overcome, or at least addressed.
Page 74 of 122
==PAGE:76==
Discussion
In the case of this project, measures were taken to overcome these obstacles.
Firstly, a plethora of prizes were obtained from various generous sponsors. Some
of these prizes were of high value, for instance the minicruise giftcards. These
were used to gain the interest of potential testers, so that they may enlist for
testing if the concept alone was not convincing enough. Furthermore, when the
testers enlisted in the testing, in order to be eligible for prize winnings, they
would have to complete the entirety of the provided task. Studies have found
that lotteries and monetary gain significantly improves the response-rate. [17]
So in theory, testers should be more likely to complete the test provided due to
the prizes offered in the test.
Based on these points, it is believed that the offering of prizes had a positive
impact on both the recruitment obstacle and the engagement obstacle.
In the case of genuine feedback, a couple of measures were taken to provide
the best possible framework for the testers to provide genuine feedback. All
sensitive notes and data, which were a direct product of the testers engagement
with the application was encrypted and ensured to be readable only to the device
on which the user was created as a software privacy measure. Furthermore, pin-
code for the application was implemented as a hardware privacy measure. The
aim of this was to enable the tester to test genuinely and in turn provide genuine
feeback. However, the trust for software solutions in terms of privacy has only
declined with the increasing news of data leaks and data brokers throughout
recent years. And the very nature of a mental health mobile application could
be very sensitive for the users. So even if the privacy measures are secure, there
is no way to convince the tester/user of that if they believe otherwise. This
could establish a basis for artificial use of the application and in turn result in
inaccurate feedback.
Another concern for the integrity of the feedback is the offering of prizes. While
the prizes are good for other obstacles, the prizes may attract testers who do
not wish to provide meaningful feedback. A tester only attracted by the prizes
may provide the fastest possible feedback or even overly positive feedback in an
attempt to sway the lottery in their favor. The occurance of this would natu-
rally influence the test results and negatively impact the integrity of said results.
Even with these measures in place and concerns adressed, meaningful results
cannot be drawn from a too small amount of testers. This project managed to
recruit almost 100 testers, but only six completed the usability testing and only
three completed the feasibility testing. There are several possible explanations
for the high attrition. Among these are simple loss of interest, forgetfulness,
thinking the sent emails are scams and the fact that most testers were DTU
students and the mails were sent around exam periods and later on vacation
periods.

it could be an option to redo the studies with more testers. This would likely
improve the accuracy of the findings and provide more precise results. Espe-

For this feasibility study redo, it may be a good idea to provide testers with

Keeping on the subject of the technology limitations, the choice of cloud storage
for the database was also a decision which would be a point of discussion.
The Firebase solution was chosen because of it being a Google product which
itself provided complete compatibility with Android and the Android Studio
IDE. As mentioned briefly ealier, Google Firebase has several solutions within
the Firebase solution space. One of them being the Firebase Firestore (from now
on just Firestore), which is a NoSQL database for serverless solutions. Along
with the other solutions in the Google Firebase, Firestore has its own user au-
tentication module which is available to developers across multiple platforms.
This build-in authentication provides separate security rules as well as an ac-
companying console interface for developers to manage created users and more.
This would provide a ready-to-go user management and authentication for our
Page 78 of 122

Discussion
solution, however it became obvious that for this to work, the user had to sign
up with their email adress (or Google account, Facebook, etc.) which conflicted
with the purpose of the near total anonymity we wanted to provide to the users
of the application.
Instead the solution was expanded to include several layers of security while
working around the already available user management as mentioned. The spe-
cialized solution was created with a user having a userID (1-3 characters) and
an accompanying pincode to access their user. While this combination would
not hold up to the security standards we would like this project to adhere, an-
other layer of security was therefore added. This consisted of a encryption key
being created upon user creation. This encryption key is then stored locally on
the device via SharedPreferences. Said key is then used to encrypt and decrypt
personal data being stored and retrieved via the Firestore Cloud storage. This
way, in a case of userID and pincode combination (or cloud database access)
somehow being shared unauthorized with another party, they would not be able
to read any personal data without using the exact mobile device from which the
user was created originally.
While this solution will work for the purpose of completing a controlled feasi-
bility study, it would not be sustainable in the long run. As an example, for
this solution a user would not be able to access their data via another device.
They would be able to log in, but the data retrieved from the cloud would not
be decrypted correctly and they would instead encounter an error message due
to the failed decryption. However as expected, this scenario was not reported
during the course of the feasibility study.
5.1.3
Monetization
Whether the proposed solution would be feasible for the users is one thing.
Whether it would be feasible for the facilitator to offer the solution is another.
The development of a mobile application, regardless of it being centered around
mental wellbeing, has costs. Moreover, updates, maintenance and fixes along
with potential new features has to be developed, tested and released in the fu-
ture. It becomes clear that there is a bunch of expenses related to developing
and maintaining a mobile application and as of right now, the proposed solution
has no revenue streams.
The subject of monetization of a mobile application is usually a relatively
straightforward obstacle to tackle. Usually an application costs money to down-
load, contains microtransactions or is a facilitator itself of advertisement for
other companies. But in the case of mental wellbeing applications, the obstacle
becomes harder to handle. As mentioned by several papers in section 2.2.2,
Page 79 of 122
==PAGE:81==
Discussion
accessibility ranked among the highest for what users want in a mobile mental
health application. Furthermore, accessibility was found by several other papers
in the same section to be one of the greatest strengths of mobile mental health
apps. One might realize that putting a price on downloading a such application
would harm its accessibility significantly.
Similarly, hiding features behind microtransactions will undoubtedly either hin-
der the user in their mental health betterment or intrigue them to pay for non-
essential features and the users will ultimately be taken advantage of. Users,
where some might be in a vulnerable state.
On the topic of microtransactions. This form of monetization is already ethi-
cally questionable and can very quickly become predatory as well. An example
was made previously in this paper on how a well-known application offers its
users to regain their lost streak for a small payment. This form of monetization
would go against the core principle of a mobile mental health application. The
user is supposed to feel safe in a space where they can explore and better their
own mental state, not be taken advantage of.
Advertisement within the application may be the most harmless form of moneti-
zation, but the implementation has to be carefully thought through and testing
would be necessary. If the application is simply plastered with advertisements,
the user’s potentially fickle mental state may be disturbed so much that they
will not make genuine progress. Furthermore, the user may not perceive the
application as a serious health tool.
Gathering and selling the users data may be of the worst form of monetiza-
tion possible in a mental health application. In this project it was made clear
to the testers that no data would make it out of the confines of testing and sen-
sitive information would be visible to no one but themselves. Even then, among
the highest concerns were data confidentiality and privacy. These findings are
also supported by several papers in section 2.2.2. Even with genuine assurément
that data will not be sold to third parties, a good amount of the population will
not use the application without restraint, if they will even use it at all. One can
only imagine how few users will use an application that openly sells user data
to third parties.
Perhaps the best form of monetization in an application such as the one de-
veloped in this project, would be to work with clinicians. If users were directed
to clinicians through the application through some sort of contact feature, the
application developers could get a cut of the revenue the clinician gained from
the referral.
This way the user has the option to easily contact a clinician
and gain clinical help, the clinician gains patients and the app developers gain
revenue. The implementation would require testing and a graceful method of
referral would be needed, but this method could ensure that the user is not

taken advantage of, nor disturbed in their self betterment. However this ap-
proach could also imply that developers would gain value from even wrongfully
facilitating contact from a user to a clinician, which again would be troublesome.

5.2.2
Use of AI to generate content
Generative artificial intelligence has become a popular topic in recent times. Its
use has been discussed in terms of ethics and the problems it entails. On the
other hand, tools like ChatGPT are great for generating relevant and coherent
text for especially students. This project has used ChatGPT exclusively to cre-
ate task objects. More specifically, the name and a short description for 36 of
the 45 tasks present in the application.
While this particular use of generative AI is very time efficient and convenient
in development, various aspects are up for discussion.
Ethical concerns of generative AI can be discussed endlessly, but the fact of the
matter is that the tasks created by the AI may be inaccurate. As ChatGPT has
access to immense amounts of data, some of this data is bound to be inaccurate,
misleading or even wrong. Therefore it is extremely important to go over the
generated results and be critical in the implementation of said results. In this
project, the results were analysed and only relevant task generations were im-
plemented and in some cases altered to fit the context of the application better.
Even with these precautionary measures, the tasks would undoubtedly be very
different if the project group alone came up with all 45 tasks. However, with
limited relevant knowledge within the project group, it is hard to say if the result
would have a better or worse overall impact if the tasks were instead exclusively
created by the project group.
Ideally, workbook tasks would have been created in collaboration with relevant
experts or clinicians.

5.3.1
Additional features and adjustments
While the application embodies a relatively complete application, several fea-
tures and small adjustments could be made.
First for the minor adjustments. The prototype contains some spelling mistakes
and small errors in especially the task texts. It was mentioned in direct feedback
from testers that user-made notes should be editable and individually deletable,
which should definitely be implemented in the future. UserID would of course
become personal in a real release of the application along with the option of
the app remembering login information. Additionally, on the subject of data
security mentioned in 5.1.2, it would be more secure to have a password instead
of a pincode, so that is also a point of future adjustments.
A tester also pointed out that on the profile page, it was not clear what the
"Best rated activities" section was, and that should also be made more clear.
The algorithm picking which tasks to offer the user, could even have its very
own testing period. While much thought has gone into the different factors and
their weights, not much evidence supports them. An overhaul of the algorithm is
undoubtedly necessary, but until extensive research has been carried out, most
aspects of the algorithm remain relatively arbitrary.
To improve technical feasibility, the overall stability of the application should
also be improved.
One tester experienced a crash in their testing endevors,
however they did not provide the details of the actions leading up to the crash.
One way of avoiding this is in the future, could be to implement relevant logs
to be distributed in the event of any future crashes. This being said - a single
crash reported is not deemed to be sufficient proof of the application not being
technically feasible at this time.
Now for the more significant future changes. Both for monetization purposes
explained in section 5.1.3, but also for the sake of the user’s mental wellbeing, a
feature putting the user in contact could be a great addition to the application.
This would require support from clinicians and maybe even the danish govern-
ment if implemented in Denmark. Furthermore, direct involvement of clinicians
will undoubtedly have an impact on the development of the application.

6
Conclusion
Reflecting back on the final problem statement which was presented on page 20,
this project aimed to investigate if a proposed technological intervention - an
application featuring streaks, motivational quotes, habit- promoting tasks and
self-reflective note-taking, would be feasible for the goal of promoting mental
wellbeing. In the course of the last almost six month this venture has been
aimed towards finding an answer to that exact problem.
During this time comprehensive research was made, which included over 1771
reports which went through several screenings based on title and abstract and
concluded in 33 relevant studies which were used to provide knowledge on how
other studies have tried to answer the similar question of how technological
intervention can be used in the improvement of mental wellbeing of users across
the world.
The insights gained from the major amount of research paved the way for this
project group to create a list of requirements for a new solution which would
combine all the lessons learned and taught in previous research and solutions.
Based on the requirements, created was then a low fidelity prototype which
would eventually evolve through several iterations to become a high fidelity
prototype, from which a usability study could be produced. The usability study
itself succesfully identified several pain points for the user, which would make
for the final adjustments before the actual implementation and development of
an Android application could commence.
While the choice of development framework and cloud storage for data would
each bring their own limitations to testing and data security, the application
would end up being succesfully developed with all the features included from
the high fidelity prototype which came before it.
To finally be able to answer the question of whether the resulting mobile ap-
plication would serve to prove that such a technological intervention would be
feasible to promote mental wellbeing, a feasibility study was constructed. The
feasibility study would conclude that while the response was positive in terms
of proving the undertaking as feasible, it would be advocated that the feasibility
study should be redone due to a too small foundation of data.
Although not being able to complete the goal of confidently conclude the solution
as feasible or not, the realization of the project offers two distinct takeaways.
An indication that the proposed solution could be feasible if properly tested as
well as the importance and complications of recruiting and retaining testers.
