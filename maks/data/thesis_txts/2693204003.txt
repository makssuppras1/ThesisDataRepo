Abstract
The use of renewable or green energy sources is growing. While the renewable energy
sources are needed and desirable in many aspects, sources such as windmills and so-
lar panels are inherently unreliable. Hence, to rely on these sources effective energy
conversion and storage is needed. One way to store energy is through electrochemical
batteries, such as lithium-ion batteries. These batteries are widely used in wearable and
mobile applications. However, these batteries degrade over time. To best utilise the bat-
teries it is paramount to accurately gauge there condition. Electrochemical Impedance
Spectroscopy (EIS) is widely used as a diagnostic tool for electrochemical devices such
as lithium-ion batteries. EIS can be used to monitor the impedance spectrum of a battery,
and hence its condition. It is desirable to perform these measurement doing the normal
operation of the batteries, hence ”on-line” EIS while charging the batteries is of great in-
terest. To conserve energy as best as possible high efficiency converters, for instance
for battery charging applications, are desired. Partial power processing provides a way
to use a novel DC/DC converter in a configuration insuring very high efficiency.
In this thesis an existing DC/DC converter in a partial power configuration is modified to
work with a low voltage supply and load, designed to charge two lithium-ion batteries in
series. The advantages and disadvantages of partial power processing and electrochemi-
cal impedance spectroscopy are discussed in detail. A theoretical model and a simulation
is created to provide a better understanding of the system, and is used to formulate the
system specifications to provide adequate conditions for battery charging. However due
to time constrains and safety concerns EIS was not performed on lithium-ion batteries. A
new current measurement setup is implemented, and adequate software is developed to
enable semi-automatic EIS through open-loop control. The needed modifications to safely
perform EIS on lithium-ion batteries, such as closed-loop control and external fail safes,
are discussed. Finally an EIS measurement on a resistive test load was performed, to
compute the full impedance spectrum of said load, and validate the used sinusoid based
EIS approach.

1
Introduction
As the use of renewable energy grows so does the need for energy storage. Energy
sources such as wind-turbines and solar power are inherently unreliable, and hence en-
ergy storage is needed to make these energy sources reliable. Energy storage can take
many forms: Physical storage such as gravitational hydro-pumped, compressed air and
rotational energy storage, electrical storage such as superconductive magnetic energy
storage systems (SMES) and capacitive systems, and electrochemical systems such as
batteries and electrolysis systems [1]. Electrochemical batteries are well established as
energy storage systems. They are most prevalent in mobile applications such as electric
cars, cell-phones and wearable electronics. Most prevalent of all, are lithium-ion batteries
[1]. Batteries degrade over time. This degradation can be separated in three categories:
Phenomena affecting electrodes, phenomena affecting the composition of the substituent
parts and mechanical degradation [2]. To best utilise batteries accurate monitoring of bat-
teries condition are needed.
This thesis focuses on the use of a partial power converter in charging of lithium-ion bat-
teries and the implementation of Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) as an
”on-line” condition monitoring tool.
1.1
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy is widely used in areas such as corrosion tech-
nology, materials science, conducting polymers, ceramics, coatings, semiconductors,
solid-state, and energy storage [3]. EIS, as a condition monitoring tool for electrochemical
devices, is less invasive than other diagnostic tools, can differentiate between different
kinds of faults, and can distinguish between causes of deterioration. Most prevalent of
its advantages though, is that EIS can be performed during operation of an electrochem-
ical device, for instance during the charging of a battery [2]. However, EIS does have
drawbacks. The most interesting in context of this project is that the use of conventional
sinusoid based EIS can be time-consuming to such an extent, that it is inappropriate for
online condition monitoring [2]. EIS on electrochemical devices is usually performed us-
ing one of two methods: Sinus waves as the excitation signal [4], or a Pseudo-Random
Binary Sequence (PRBS) as the excitation signal [2]. Both methods consist of injecting an
excitation signal, and measuring the current and voltage responds from the load. PRBS
inherently provides a shorter measurement time, but the measurement time of the sinus
waves method can be shortened by stacking up to five sinusoids into a single excitation
signal [4]. A PRBS like approach was adopted in [5] where a step was used to perform
EIS on a series connection of batteries using them as the input of a DC/DC converter
connected to a load. This approach provides information at the stepping frequency and
its odd higher harmonics in a single perturbation step. However, since the harmonics
decrease in size, a larger step is needed if higher harmonics are desired.
1.2
Partial Power processing
Partial power processing (PPP) does not require a new, different or specialised DC/DC
converter. It is rather a use case of a novel DC/DC converter in a different configuration
than usual. The first publication to propose PPP is [6] from 1996, where the ”partial power

converter” is denoted ”The Series Connected Boost Unit (SCBU)”. In this early publica-
tion a DC/DC converter is used in series with a voltage supply to drive a load. Partial
power processing has since 1996 been used in space application, wind power genera-
tion, energy storage, and electrical vehicle fast-charging applications [7]. Partial power
processing is the use of a DC/DC converter to process only part of the power delivered to
a load. Since the majority of the load power comes directly from a source the efficiency
of such a system can become extremely high even for converters with low efficiency [4],
[7]. A series connection with a converter is not the only way to drive a load while only pro-
cessing a part of the power. If a series connection of loads is driven by a supply, DC/DC
converters can be put across each load to perform Differential Power Processing (DPP).
DPP is used in data-center, battery management, and photovoltaic applications [8]–[10].
DPP like PPP only processes a part of the power transferred to the load, and hence an
argument can be made that DPP is PPP. In the literature however, the two terms seem to
be distinguished by whether a converter is connected in series with the load for PPP, or
in parallel with the load for DPP. It should be noted that if EIS is performed through DPP a
voltage and current measurement for each load in the series is needed. If PPP is used to
perform EIS on a series connection of loads they inherently have the same current, and
hence only one current measurement is needed instead.
1.3
Thesis objectives
The objectives of this thesis are the following:
• Modify the converter made in [4] to function with a low voltage load and supply.
• Simulate the converter in a partial power system configuration.
• Use said converter to perform Electrochemcial Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) mea-
surements on a test load.
• Perform EIS on two lithium-ion batteries in series.

2
Theory
The theory relevant for the project can be split into two main topics: Partial Power Pro-
cessing and Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy. While circuit theory and state
space modelling are also used, these topics are assumed to be familiar to the reader and
hence are not discussed in detail.
2.1
Partial power processing
Partial power processing refers to a configuration of a supply, DC/DC converter, and
load, where the load is connected in such a manner that is is partially powered through
the converter and partially powered directly from the supply. As opposed to the normal
configuration, referred to as full power processing for the sake of clarity, where the load
is powered solely through the converter as seen on Figure 2.1. Since less power is fed
through the converter, any loses in the converter must have less effect on the overall
efficiency of the system. The two possible configuration of a partial power system, using
bidirectional DC/DC converters, as identified in [4], can be seen on Figure 2.2. In the
literature no general unified drawing approach to describe partial power systems exist,
and hence many different ways of drawing the possible configurations has emerged. The
style used in this publication is drawn to emphasise the series connections of the input
side of the converter and the load. It should be noted that throughout the report the two
sides of the converter will be denoted the ”input” and ”output” side, as shown in Figure
2.2, even though this places the voltage supply on the output of the converter and the load
in series with the input. To remain consistent throughout the report the input side of the
converter will in all drawings be shown as the left side of the converter, with the voltage
placed to the left of the input side of the converter in an attempt to relate the drawings as
best as possible to the full power converter schematic.
In a partial power systems, the current through the load is the sum of the current drawn
directly from the supply as well as the current being fed back from the output side of the
converter, hence the load current will exceed the supply current. Furthermore, since the
input side of the converter is in series with the load, the input current of the converter is
equal to the load current. The output voltage of the converter is the supply voltage, this
means that, contrary to most applications, in this case the input voltage will be controlled
instead of the output voltage. A more detailed version of Figure 2.2 (a) with current and
voltage denominations can be seen on Figure 2.3. Lastly since the supply voltage is
effectively in parallel with the series connection of the load and input side of the converter,
the following equation can be made:

From the shown equation and figures general observation as to the limitation of partial
power systems can be made: Firstly, the load cannot be electrically isolated from the
voltage supply even when using an isolated converter. Hence, if electrical isolation is
needed between the load and supply, additional components are needed. Secondly,
electrical isolation between the input and output side of the converter is not needed for
PPP, however it limits the configuration to one akin to the one shown on Figure 2.2 (b).
This type of configuration would need the load to float. Similarly, if a grounded load is
required only the configuration shown in Figure 2.2 (a) can be used, requiring electrical
isolation between the input and output of the converter, because the input side of the
converter needs to be floating. Thirdly, Since the supply voltage and current directly
affect the load voltage and current, the system has a feed-forward component, not usually
present in converter systems. Finally, since the output (supply) voltage always is larger
than the input voltage, only converters with boost functionality from the input to output can
be used for PPP. It is feasible to use a buck-style converter as long as the input and output
are reversed compared to the system described here. However this converter would need
to be functional with a negative input and output current using the conventional current
direction denomination.
2.2
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) requires an excitation of some kind and
two measurements. Put simply, a signal is imposed on a load, and the response is mea-
sured through a current and voltage measurement. To do ”on-line” EIS means that the
EIS is performed during normal operation of the load, hence the excitation signal has to be
imposed on the load voltage or current by the converter. Since the objective of this project
is to perform EIS on multiple batteries in series, the excitation must be on the current dur-
ing the charging of the batteries. One way to perform EIS is to impose a sinusoid on the
current and measure both the current and the voltage of the load. When performing EIS

frequency domain. This is done through Fast-Fourier Transformation (FFT). To improve
the FFT, processing of the signal is necessary. The FFT expects a whole number of peri-

sinusoids are known, it can easily be accounted for by configuring the number of samples

Current Measurement
When testing the converter several issues where discovered with the current measure-
ment. When no current is running through the circuit, the voltage at the ADC-input of
the control card was 840 mV, which is only 40 mV higher than expected. However, when
current higher than approximately 0.8 A is running through the circuit, 1.25 V is measured
at the ADC-input. As stated earlier the expected value at the ADC-input is 800 mV ∓
20 mV/A, hence the voltage change is not only magnitudes different than expected, it is
also an increase when a decrease in voltage is expected. When the current is increased
further, to approximately 1.5 A, the voltage jumps to 1.365 V. Furthermore, the voltage
measured just before then lowpass filter, directly at the output at the opamp, seems to
work as expected. This means than when current was running, an additional 400 to
500 mV was present across the lowpass filter. This behaviour was most likely due to, too
high impedance at the ADC-input. From the datasheet and the ADC note for the control
card it was found that the ADC-input should be low-impedance, 50 Ωat most, for proper
functionality, supporting these findings [12], [15]. A new current measurement setup was

5.2
Imposing multiple sinusoids
To best impose multiple sinusoids, it is preferable to use static phase changes so that
the overall amplitude becomes as low as possible, to keep the current as close to the
operating point as possible. In [4] it is stated that the Matlab function fminsearch can be
used to minimise the amplitude of the overlapping sinusoids by choosing appropriate φ
values in equation 2.2. However, how the functions is used is not described in detail. The
Matlab function can only minimise a function of a set of variables. Since the sinusoids
depend on the time t, as well as the static phase changes φ, it cannot be directly used.
It can only be used to minimise the function for a set time t0. Since the amplitude of
the sinusoids all can be 0 at a given t, as well as cancel each other’s contribution to the
sum, the amplitude of the sum of the sinusoid can be 0 at any given t. Hence simply
optimising for an appropriate t = t0 cannot be trusted to provide a valid result. To combat
this, two different approaches where tried: Iteratively using the fminsearch function at
random times and choosing the φ with the lowest amplitude for an entire period of the

6
EIS Measurements and Processing
Each measurement is done by configuring the controller to loop through the sinusoid LUT
at a given frequency to match one of the five measurement groups defined in Table 2.1.
The DAQ is then configured to the same frequency and the measurement is done auto-
matically by the script as will be explored later. Each measurement takes 5 full periods
for each group, meaning that group 5, the quickest, is done in 20 ms while group 1, the
slowest, takes 200 s. Before the measurements were done, the test load was measured
to be 4.32 Ω, while slightly different from the value used in the calculations, it makes little
difference to the validity and quality of the measurements.
6.1
EIS setup
The EIS voltage and current measurements are obtained using the USB-6003 DAQ from
National Instruments. The DAQ can be interfaced with using Python with the NI-DAQmx
package. Hence, any data processing can be done through Python. The USB-6003 has
eight analog inputs, configurable as four differential inputs if needed [17]. This means that
up to three series connected loads can be measured at the same time using the USB-
6003. To test the the synchronisation between the differential inputs, a sinusoid from
a signal generator was fed to two differential inputs of the DAQ. Hence, any difference
between the measured signal has to come from the USB-6003 or the cables used. The
five base frequency sinusoids in Table 2.1 where used as the test signals. The measure-
ments where transformed to the frequency spectrum and a pseudo impedance spectrum
was created to show the phase difference between the two signals. The measurements
and the resulting frequency spectra for the 250 Hz sine can be found on Figure 6.1. All
the measurements and resulted frequency spectra can be found in Appendix C. From
the testing it is found that the phase difference between the measurements at the base
sinusoid frequencies are
[θ1, θ2, θ3, θ4, θ5] = [0.9018◦, 0.9021◦, 0.8996◦, 0.8984◦, 0.8997◦],
(6.1)
where θ1 through θ5 is the phase difference for each of the base frequencies correspond-
ing to group 1 through 5. This small phase disparity can by accounted for by subtracting
it from the EIS measurements, giving a more accurate phase measurement of the load.
The data processing was separated into two Python scripts: A measurement script and
a processing script. The measurement script is structured so that by inputting the cur-
rent measurement group of frequencies from Table 2.1, all the relevant parameters are
changed to fit the measurement. Since one period of the group always consist of 200
points from the LUT, the needed sampling frequency can be found as 200 times the base
frequency. Taking 1000 measurement points hence insures five full periods of the group.
Afterwards the data is saved to a CSV file consisting of a time, current and voltage column.
It should be noted that the current measurement is saved as the raw voltage measurement
from the DAQ with no post-processing. The saved file is named so that the measurement
group and the time of the measurement distinguish the files from each other, insuring that
no files are overwritten. This approach allows for multiple measurements in quick succes-
sion. After the measurements are done, the processing file is used to read the CSV files
generated by the measurement script, and generate the frequency spectra for the current,
voltage and impedance. Firstly the current is converted to its correct value. Afterwards

Future Work
If one were to continue working on this project two main objectives should be a priority:
Closed loop control to enable safe EIS of the batteries, and communication between the
DAQ and the converter controller to enable fully automatic EIS.
7.1
Closed loop control
The software implementation of closed loop control is already present in the code of the
controller. However, the current reference signal is to noisy to implement the control. The
final EIS current measurement seen in Figure 5.4 could easily be configured to fit within
the 0 to 3 V range of the controller’s ADC. Connecting the output of the INA163 instrumen-
tation amplifier to the input of the buffer in Figure 5.1, would hopefully provide a current
measurement usable as a reference. However, additional lowpass filtering at the output
of the INA163 might be beneficial, both to improve the reference signal, but also since the
non-buffered signal should be used for the EIS measurement to keep the delay between
the voltage and current measurement as low as possible. It might also prove beneficial to
use another INA163 for the voltage measurement with unity gain, under the assumption
that the delay caused by each INA163 is equivalent.
With the current reference successfully implemented, an appropriate control algorithm
would have to be implemented. The controller has build in PID-functionality, so imple-
menting the closed-loop control can be done by choosing appropriate P, I and D values
for the system. A PID-controller has been shown in [4] to be adequate for EIS use. How-
ever if active filtering is desired more complex control is needed. Using closed-loop control
is paramount to safely charge the batteries, since any change in state of charge needs to
be compensated for by the control, to keep the average current constant. Furthermore, if
the current cannot be controlled directly, the imposed sinusoids might be distorted, espe-
cially for the slower frequency measurements. In practice surrounding circuitry to charge
the batteries safely should be implemented in case of the converter failing. This circuitry
could be an external shutdown if the current or voltage exceeds a limit. A programmable
voltage supply and a fuse would most likely be sufficient.
7.2
Communication between the DAQ and converter
controller
Between the controller and the DAQ, doing the EIS measurement comes down to man-
ually changing three values: The current measurement group needs to be configured in
both the DAQ and controller, and the imposition needs to be enabled in the controller.
To fully automate the EIS measurement, communication between the DAQ and controller
is needed. It is feasible to start the converter by itself charging the batteries with pure
DC. The DAQ could then initialise the EIS measurement and send an update signal to
the controller every time the measurement group needs to change. The communication
could either be done through a hardware connection, since both the DAQ and controller
has digital I/O capabilities, or completely through software. Any given I/O port can be used
since synchronisation is not needed. The DAQ can simple request a change in measure-
ment group, and then the controller can send a confirmation signal when the update has
gone through. Any delay caused by this approach will be small compared to the length

delay, with five periods measured, the full measurement would take 222.22 s. Since both

facing with Code Composer Studio through Python to change the appropriate variables
on the converter side. This should be possible using DSS and Jython.

Conclusion
The existing DC/DC converter from [4] was modified to function with a low voltage supply
and load in a partial power configuration, and was successfully used to perform elec-
trochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) on a resistive test load. An ideal theoretical
model and PLECS simulation was created to attain better understanding of the system.
These models were used to formulate the system specifications needed for the change to
low voltage operation. The system was designed to perform EIS on two series connected
lithium-ion batteries but due to time constrains and unforeseen challenges, EIS was not
attempted due to safety concerns. One of these unforeseen circumstances was the non-
operational current measurement circuitry which had to be corrected. Three different
approaches were tried, ultimately using a 50 mΩsense resistor and a INA163 instrumen-
tation amplifier with a 500 V/V gain was implemented, to get a current measurement for
the EIS.
The EIS implementation is two sided: Imposing a group of sinusoids on the current using
the converter controller, TMS320F28335, and measuring the current and voltage using
the USB-6003 DAQ. To impose the five groups of five sinusoids needed for the EIS, a
single 200 point look-up table (LUT) was implemented to impose the sinusoids directly on
the duty cycle of the converter by cycling through it. Hence, to change the frequency of the
imposed sinusoids, one simply has to change the speed of the LUT cycle. The converter
is driven in open-loop, hence a conversion between the wanted amplitude of the imposed
sinusoids on the current, has to be manually related to the amplitude-change needed on
the duty cycle. If closed-loop control is desired it should be possible by tweaking the
current measurement and connecting it to the converter controller through the on-board
ADC. The USB-6003 was used to get the needed voltage and current measurement for
EIS. It was driven by a Python script, which saves the measurement directly to a CSV file,
encoding it with the needed measurement information, such as the measurement time
and type. This CSV file is then processed using a separate Python script, which performs
the fast Fourier transform and division needed to compute the impedance spectrum. The
Python implementation of the EIS measurement and processing provides the infrastruc-
ture needed for semi-automatic EIS, with little manual input.
This project provides a starting point for fully-automated EIS using a DC/DC converter in a
partial power setup. While challenges were plentiful, it has been shown that the concept
of using a low voltage partial power converter to perform EIS on a low-voltage load is
sound.

schematic of the converter, the steps from the schematic to the final state space schematic
for each of the two different periods is shown here. For convenience the timing diagram
for the switches is repeated in Figure A.1.
Period 1 and 3:
The first step is to change the MOSFETs to open or closed switched depending on their
state in period 1 and 3. The result can be seen on Figure A.2. From Figure A.2 it can be
seen that the transformer is shorted and hence no current is running through it. Removing
all nonconducting elements from the schematic yields Figure A.3.
Redrawing Figure A.3 yields the final state space schematic for period 1 and 3 seen on
Figure 4.1 (a) and repeated in Figure A.4 for convenience.
Period 2 and 4:
Just like for period 1 and 3 the first step is to change the MOSFETs to open or closed
switched depending on their state. However, in this case different switches are on or off
for period 2 and 4. The equivalent circuit for period 2 can be found on Figure A.5 and
the equivalent circuit for period 4 can be found on Figure A.6.
From the two figures it
is seen that they are equivalent and hence both can be redrawn as shown in Figure A.7.
Changing the transformer to a controlled voltage and current source, on the input and
output respectively, yields Figure A.8. The controlled current source can be neglected
since it is in parallel with the voltage source, providing an unknown current. Redrawing
Figure A.8 with this is mind yields the final state space schematic for period 2 and 4 seen
on Figure 4.1 (b) and repeated in Figure A.9 for convenience.
