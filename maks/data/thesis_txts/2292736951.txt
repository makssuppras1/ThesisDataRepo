I
 
- Metabolic: Boosting Sustainability, Science & Technology in Young Minds -
The number of Dutch children 
entering science and technology 
fields is steadily decreasing [1]. This 
number must improve if technical 
sustainability challenges are to be 
effectively solved. 
These facts motivated the 
following research question as basis 
of this master thesis.
‘How can we make an impact at 
an early age in order to stimulate 
understanding and interest, and 
foster values in sustainability, science 
and technology?’
In order to tackle this problem a 
co-design approach was adopted 
from the beginning, based on 
contexmapping methodology and 
creative sessions. That entailed the 
involvement of the end user, in 
this case children, in all the phases 
throughout the whole project by 
giving insights, generating ideas 
and concepts, and testing the 
prototypes.
 
The outcome of the project is 
Eat the Beat, an interactive toy 
that approaches urban agriculture 
through microgreens in a playful 
manner. It consists in three 
interconnected play modes: 
painting, music and cooking.
The painting play mode consists of 
a especial pen which allows children 
to draw with seeds thanks to a 
gelatin ink matrix.
The music play mode connects 
the grown microgreens with a 
capacitive sensor. With it children 
can play music by touching the 
greens with their hands.
The cooking play mode involves a 
cookbook that provides recipes to 
prepare dishes with the previously 
painted, 
grown 
and 
played 
microgreens.
With these three play modes Eat 
the Beat aims to engage children 
with urban agriculture from 
different perspectives:
•	 Hands-on experience learning.
•	 Gamification.
•	 Connecting sustainability with 
other topics such as painting 
and technology.
•	 Leveraging the five senses in 
order to create positive and 
last longing bonding with the 
topic.

moments—maybe a teacher, maybe 
an experience, maybe a toy—that 
changed the way they think about 
their future.

The standpoint of this project is 
based on collective creativity along 
the design process. It is grounded 
on the conviction that involving 
actively 
users, 
stakeholders 
and even non-experts in the 
topic throughout the process is 
necessary in order to create last 
longing change and innovation. This 
approach in literature can be found 
under several names: Participatory 
design, co-creation and co-design. All 
the terms are related but no clear 
agreement on the precise definition 
withstands amongst them. Here it 
will be referred to as co-design.
In co-design participants play a 
large role by contributing in all 
the phases of the development 
process: research, ideation and 
conceptualization [2]. Participants 
are the experts of their own 
experience which makes them 
beneficial contributors for their first 
hand knowledge [3]. The designer 
within the co-design approach 
has the responsibility to empower 
participants 
and 
facilitate 
the 
expression of their thoughts, needs 
and ideas. Also, the designer has 
the expertise to convey and shape 
all the information gathered into 
specific solutions [2].
To 
specifically 
face 
co-design 
in the project, contextmapping 
methodology and creative sessions 
were used. Contextmapping was 
used in all the phases of the project; 
analysis, ideation, conceptualization 
and testing. It involved the end user, 
the children.
Contextmapping is a methodology 
that aims to explore user insights in 
his or her own contexts. It makes 
the users aware of their own 
experiences which later are used 
to generate ideas in collaboration 
with them [4]. It enables them to 
express their goals, motivations, 
meaning, latent and tacit needs, and 
practical matters [5].
Contextmapping 
combines 
several methodologies such as 
interviews, observations, generative 
techniques and probes [6]. For 
further explanation about it refer 
to Appendix A.
The creative sessions were done 
in collaboration with Metabolic 
and with an external creative 
team 
encompassing 
varied 
disciplines 
(design, 
architecture 
and engineering). The generative 
session with Metabolic aimed for 
the research and first stage of the 
ideation. The one with the creative 
team fully focused in the ideation 
and conceptualization phase.
Creative sessions involved several 
generative tools and techniques 
(brainstorm, 
sensorial 
stimulus, 
bisociation, lateral thinking, etc.) in 
order to trigger participants latent 
knowledge and imagination [7].

Experiential learning theory is 
defined by David A. Klob (1939) 
as ‘the process whereby knowledge 
is created through transformation of 
the experience. Knowledge results 
from the combination of grasping and 
transforming experience’ [8]. 
It is also known as learning by 
doing or hands-on experience 
learning. Several other educational 
psychologist developed learning 
theories 
focused 
on 
learning 
through experience. They all share 
several commonalities. 
 
Experiential learning parts from 
the base that people are naturally 
motivated to learn. A basic principle 
is to involve students in deciding 
the learning content in order to 
spark personal motivation [9]. 
The content must trigger interest 
in order to create engagement 
throughout the learning process; 
otherwise, if no intrinsic motivation 
is present, one might neglect the 
whole experience and thus not 
nurture from the learning activity.
Experiential learning is conceived 
as a process thus, even more 
important than the learning goal, is 
the process that leads to it. Through 
the process one learns to critically 
analyse problems, synthesize what 
one has learnt and reflect from the 
outcome [10].
Experiential learning aims to create 
a transaction between person and 
environment [10]. The acquired 
knowledge have to be able to be 
related to real world problems 
forming 
the 
basis 
for 
future 
experiences.
Experiential learning understands 
knowledge 
as 
a 
transformation 
process which is created and 
recreated, instead of absolute and 
transmitted [10].
The 
education 
system 
for 
experiential learning also includes 
semi-structured activities where 
teacher’s 
role 
is 
to 
facilitate 
instead of direct, pulling apart 
from traditional learning. Besides, 
it 
promotes 
cooperation 
and 
collaboration amongst pupils to 
build up on knowledge. [9]
Kolb defines a cycle of four stages 
within experiential learning. [8][9]
 [10] 
Concrete Experience
Direct hands on experience on 
a topic. No previous instructions 
or information is given (except 
the beforehand knowledge of the 
student). One experiments and 
explores the new problem laid 
before him.
The aim of this first phase is not the 
quality of the end result, but rather 
the importance of the experience 
by 
itself; 
observations, 
results, 
feelings, discoveries, etc. [9] [10]
 
Reflective Observations
The discoveries of the concrete 
experience are processed and 
shared.
This enables an inner and outer 
discussion of the similarities and 
differences of the experiences 
creating a base for reflection on the 
activity. [9] [10]
Abstract Conceptualization
Once the experiences are analysed 
and internalized they can be 
extrapolated into theories and 
principles. Thus, these principles 
and trends can be identified and 
translated into other experiences. 
[9] [10]
 
Active Experimentation
The 
insights 
and 
knowledge 
absorbed along the previous steps 
are used to face coming similar or 
new situations. [9] [10]

Intrinsic 
Motivation
Intrinsic motivation refers to a 
behaviour that is self driven without 
any kind of external reward. The 
motivation arises from him or her 
self just and only for the pleasure 
of performing a specific activity. [11]
Malone
Thomas W. Malone (1952) studied 
the taxonomy of computer games 
in order to understand what 
makes them so captivating. By 
understanding these principles he 
could relate them to the nature 
of intrinsic motivation and design 
engaging instructional environments 
[12].  In his dissertation about 
intrinsic motivation he differentiates 
between 
individual 
and 
interpersonal motivations [13].
Individual motivations
Challenge
Challenge is a driving force for self-
improvement. In order to create 
challenging environments clear and 
specific goals, uncertain outcome, 
performance feedback and control 
are needed. [13]
In order to stimulate intrinsic 
motivation, it is important to 
calibrate challenge; activities that are 
trivially easy or impossibly difficult 
will be of little intrinsic interest. [14] 
Fantasy
Malone defines fantasy contexts as 
scenarios that evoke parts of the 
real or imaginary world that are not 
present. Create imaginary situations 
can help people to assimilate and 
understand a topic which later can 
be identified and applied in the real 
world. [12][14]
Curiosity
Curiosity is the most direct self 
driven action for learning. Malone 
identifies two different types of 
curiosity: sensory and cognitive. 
Sensory curiosity is related to the 
stimulus of the five senses and 
how they spark human attention. 
Cognitive curiosity applies to the 
human impulse to understand 
and fulfill partially incomplete, 
inconsistent 
or 
unparsimonious 
knowledge. [12][14] 
Control
The power to influence the course 
of events is part of the intrinsic 
motivation. Factors that define 
control are contingency, choice and 
power. It is important to understand 
that it is the sense of control, not 
the real control, of a situation which 
triggers intrinsic motivation. [13]
Interpersonal 
motivations
Cooperation & Competition
Cooperation and competition are 
two sides of the same coin. One 
can either work together or against 
others in order to instigate the 
achievement of a goal.
Usually competition is seen as bad 
and cooperation as good, however 
both are powerful to stimulate the 
purpose of learning. [12]
 
Recognition
Having efforts and accomplishments 
recognized and ap­preciated by 
others is enjoyable.
In order to foment motivation 
through recognition it is critical to 
make the activities visible for the 
others. [12]

Fontijn
W. Fontijn studied the source of fun 
by seeking into evolution theory 
in his paper Functional Fun with 
Tangible User Interfaces (2007).  He 
highlighted three aspects to ensure 
survival: skills, knowledge and social 
cohesion.
In translation to learning these 
aspects become: developing skills, 
gaining knowledge and bonding. 
As learning costs time and effort, it 
could benefit from some stimulation. 
Therefore, Fontijn proposes fun as 
tool to make learning intrinsically 
motivating [15]. The infographic 
below shows the interpretation of 
Fontijn between fun and its sources 
and the evolution theory.
Core Sources
Fontijn claims that for a game or toy 
to be fun it has to include at least 
one of the following core sources. 
Accomplishment
Accomplishment is related to the 
challenge of achieving goals. When 
activities have clear goals and there 
is an influence over the progress, i.e. 
feedback, people tend to engage. 
The challenge puts at stake the skills 
of the player who tries to master 
them in order to accomplish the 
goal. 
The accomplishment is determined 
by the balance between challenge 
and control. If the task is too 
hard, the player tend to quit. 
If the task is too easy that 
the outcome is predictable, people 
tend to get bored and quit. [15] 
[16]  
Accomplishment is closely related 
to what Malone describes as 
challenge.
Discovery
Discovery is triggered by human 
curiosity, the attraction to the 
unknown, the thirst of knowledge. 
It has its core in the unpredictability 
of the outcome and also sparks 
creativity.
Discovery requires the balance 
between ambiguity and consistency. 
If events are arbitrary and have no 
relation, the player cannot follow 
the task and decreases the overall 
fun. [15]
Discovery is related to what Malone 
describes as curiosity.
Bonding
Bonding involves the feeling of 
being accepted as part of a group. 
It deals with feelings of recognition 
and 
affirmation. 
Cooperation 
and competition are part of the 
bonding. While cooperating people 
work together and support each 
other creating a sense of belonging. 
While competing people raise their 
level of engagement with the task.
For a task to be fun it has to balance 
cooperation and competition [15] 
[16].
Bonding 
is 
related 
to 
what 
Malone describes as interpersonal 
motivations, 
which 
embraces 
cooperation, 
competition 
and 
recognition.
Enhancement Sources
Around the core sources of fun 
there are the enhancement factors. 
The enhancement factors do not 
provide fun by themselves like the 
core source. However, they intensify 
and support the core sources. [16] 
Three different sources can be 
identified: fantasy, aesthetics and 
physicality.
Fantasy
Fantasy provides games with themes 
and situations out of the normality. 
The player can experiment other 
realities and become someone else 
in a safe and controlled environment.
Aesthetics
Aesthetics refers to the appearance. 
It deals with how appealing the 
game or task is to the user. Factors 
like colours and shapes are of 
relevance.
Physicality
Physicality deals with the tangible 
interfaces 
and 
how 
engaging 
they are. The relation to fun 
enhancement has not yet been 
found. It may not be an 
independent factor.[15]

2.2.1 Child Development
Learning 
and 
information 
processing 
are 
part 
of 
the 
cognitive 
development. 
Many 
different descriptions and theories 
can be found on what cognitive 
development comprehends. Two of 
the most important theorists in the 
field of development psy­chology 
are L. Vygotsky and J. Piaget.
Vygotsky and Piaget are two 
contemporaneous with opposite 
views in development psychology. 
Piaget believed in the individual as 
source of the learning process, while 
Vygotsky saw the core of learning 
process in the social interrelations. 
[20]
Vygotsky
Lev Vygotsky (1896 - 1934) 
theory of learning development 
has 
its 
foundation 
on 
social 
interaction. 
Vygotsky 
claimed 
that social relations lead mental 
development. The first stage of 
the learning process starts through 
interpersonal interactions, which 
later are internalized and assessed 
in order to achieve a complete and 
deeper understanding. 
Vygotsky distinguished between 
three different kinds of learning 
processes called speeches: social, 
private and internal. Social speech 
refers to the instructions that 
adults give to children. Private 
speech alludes to children’s internal 
assimilation process of the adults’ 
instructions and application in similar 
situations. Inner speech is thinking 
in pure meanings, where words 
die as they become thoughts. For 
Vygotsky development of mental 
concepts and cognitive awareness is 
intimately related to language. [21]
Vygotsky defined the process of 
learning with his theory of Zone 
of 
Proximal 
Development. 
In 
Vygotsky’s words Zone of Proximal 
Development 
is: ‘the 
distance 
between the actual developmental 
level as determined by independent 
problem solving and the level of 
potential development as determined 
through problem solving under adult 
guidance or in collaboration with more 
capable peers’ [22]. 
In other words it means that what 
children can learn or achieve 
by competence guidance and 
collaboration today, they will be able 
to do by themselves tomorrow.
Piaget
Jean Piaget (1896 - 1980) theory 
of learning has its foundation on 
individuals. Each individual has to 
adapt to the environment that 
surrounds him. In order to adapt, 
he describes two complementary 
processes; 
accommodation 
and 
assimilation.  Accommodation refers 
to the process of adjustment of the 
inner world to the outer world in 
order to accept the surrounding 
reality. 
The assimilation works the other 
way 
around; 
transforming 
the 
environment so it matches the 
inner and already existing cognitive 
structures. [22] [23]
For 
Piaget 
the 
balance 
of 
accommodation and assimilation 
lead to  the ideal  development. 
When 
disequilibrium 
 
occurs 
children 
are 
facing 
a 
new 
environment which one does not 
know. This motivates a change of 
mental structures which is translated 
into the process of learning. [20]
Piaget describes four stages during 
cognitive development. [19] [23]
Sensori-motor
(birth - 2 years)
Children learn through the five 
senses and motor responses. They 
do not understand the concept 
of object permanence (when the 
object is hidden it stops existing). 
Children experience egocentrism 
as they can not see others point of 
view.

Preoperational
(2 - 6 years)
It is defined by solving one-step 
logic problems, develop language 
and complete simple operations. 
Children continue being egocentric.
Concrete Operational
(7 - 11 years)
Children 
conserve 
and 
think 
logically but they are bounded to 
the physicality of objects, i.e. no 
abstract thinking. Children are not 
anymore egoistic.
Formal Operational

Children can achieve logical and 
abstract thinking in hypo­thetical 
situations. As children pass through 
these 
stages, 
their 
cognitive

become 
increasingly

Play
Since the introduction of these 
theories by Piaget and Vygotsky 
the educational value of play was 
recognized. Play has a cognitive 
purpose. 
For 
Piaget 
play 
is 
characterized by the principle 
of 
assimilation. 
The 
subject 
incorporates new structures and 
behaviours to the preexisting ones 
[23]. He identified several stages of 
play which coincide with the stages 
of the development process [24].
Functional Play
(0 – 2 years)
It consists of a repetitive use of 
motor, physical and sensorial skills 
usually centered in the use of 
objects. It has no specific objective 
apart from the self-mastery of the 
activity. [24]
Symbolic Play
(2- 6 years)
In this stage children use fantasy 
to role play and create stories and 
tales. Objects symbolically represent 
other object which usually have 
similar shapes or colors (a banana 
becomes a phone).
By interacting with others in play 
settings, children learn social rules 
such as give and take, reciprocity, 
cooperation and sharing. Children 
will try out new roles and possible 
situations and will develop flexible 
thinking. They will learn to create 
beyond the here and now and 
stretch their imaginations in a risk-
free environment. [24]
Games with rules
(7 - 11 years)
Play becomes structured and with 
clear and specific goals. Children 
accept and understand  pre-
arranged rules and how games 
cannot work without them. The 
success and team play concept 
teaches children that the game of 
life has rules (laws) and that we all 
must follow them to live in society.
When a child grows older and 
functions at a higher level, it can fall 
back on previous play type phases, 
but applied differently. Play types do 
not disappear but often come back 
in a more complex form later on. 
[24]
Relevant 
considerations
For the aim of the project it seems 
adequate to focus the target in the 
operational stage (age 7 to 11). At 
this stage children develop their 
logical, deductive and objective 
thinking and can operate according 
to those. Nonetheless children 
do not yet show abstract thinking. 
Children are able to learn from 
concrete experiences and build 
meaning out of it, as well as following 
and understanding rules. 
These stage is a sweet spot to foster 
values and trends, as children are 
able to perform more complicated 
tasks but still do not elaborate on 
abstract concepts.

Children’s perception of science 
and technology is strongly defined 
at an early age. Positive contact with 
science and technology at this stage 
has a critical influence in children. 
Thus, it can create enthusiasm and 
stimulate a last-longing commitment 
to it. On the contrary, a negative 
experience 
with 
science 
and 
technology can affect dramatically 
children’s opinion having direct 
effect on their future career choices 
[1].
A study from OECD confirms that 
children age 8 to 11 start to have 
a decreasing interest in science and 
technology, and it reaches its critical 
point at the age of 15 [4].
Children age 8 to 9 have a more 
positive attitude towards science 
and technology than children 10 
to 11 years old, which show less 
interest in topic. However, 10 to 11 
feel more confident [35].
Age is more important than gender. 
Girls and boys at a young age show 
similar fascination in science and 
technology. The difference reside on 
the topic of interest; whereas girls 
tend to prefer life sciences, boys 
lean towards physical sciences [35].
Theory indicates several reasons 
for this detachment. For starters, 
current 
school 
approach 
to 
science and technology is heavy 
focus on memorizing theory and 
complicated terminology, which fails 
to be attractive to children [36].
Students 
request 
hands 
on 
experience. Children’s quotes say: 
‘I like practical science because it’s 
a better way to understand things 
rather than just writing them down’
‘I like the experiments because you 
get to do the things yourself. This helps 
me remember new things.’
‘Experiments are fun, you can talk 
and discuss what you are doing. In 
technology you get to build and take 
something home that works.’ [35]. 
Science and technology taught at 
school has barely any connection 
with children’s daily life. Students 
cannot relate what they learn with 
the real world, making it more 
difficult for them to refer to and 
thus internalize it. There is a huge 
gap between today’s technology 
application and what is taught at 
school [37].
Prospect carriers of science and 
technology are neither presented 
nor of easy access to students 
until they become 14 years old. 
That creates distorted views and 
stigmas that can influence children’s 
motivations [1].
On top of it certain careers bare 
stereotypes, which are enhanced 
by the social environment (parents, 
tutors, friends, etc.). In the case of 
girls this is more critical and hard to 
overcome due to the lack of role 
models (women are a minority in 
science and technology) [1].

theoretical approach at school, lack 
of relation with the real world and

This section digs into the theory of senorial

The five senses are the human 
interface to the world. They are the 
tools to explore and understand 
our surroundings. They create our 
experiences which may have an 
impact on our perceptions in the 
future. The five senses are crucial in 
early ages of development; some of 
our most powerful impressions are 
formed in childhood. [38]
 
Branding has put its attention to 
the five senses in order to engage 
and create an impact on customer 
behaviour. Brands are evolving 
into holistic sensory experiences 
and demonstrated that the higher 
the number of sensory memories 
activated, the stronger the bonding 
between brand and consumer. [38]
 This perspective on how to create 
an impact is a powerful tool that 
can be translated to many fields. 
In this particular case it is aimed 
in boosting interest in science, 
technology 
and 
sustainability 
amongst children. Thus, children can 
be stimulated to follow a career 
regarding these topics in the future. 
A more thorough study of sensory 
branding is presented to better 
understand its ground.
 
The studies of the company 
Millward Brown show the relative 
importance of each sense separately 
represented in the infographic 
below. [39]
It can be observed that sight is the 
most powerful of the senses which 
is why traditionally branding has put 
mainly its efforts on it. Nonetheless 
all the others also have their 
importance. Hereunder there is 
a brief explanation on why each 
sense is important and what is its 
influence.
 
Sound
 
Sound is tightly bound with our 
mood and emotions [40]. A clear 
example is the movies where part 
of the emotional load comes from 
the music. Even mute cinema had 
accompanying piano music.

Sound can be perceived in a 
conscious and unconscious way 
which makes it a powerful asset 
in marketing. Hearing is passive 
while listening is active therefore it 
is equally important to target the 
two aspects in order to influence 
customer behaviour. [38]
For example in a study published in 
the Journal of Consumer Research, 
Ronald E. Millman demonstrated 
that the pace of music playing in 
the background affected service, 
spending, and traffic flow in stores 
and restaurants [38]. Furthermore, 
music 
makes 
new 
memories, 
evokes the past and instantaneously 
can transport you to another place.
 
The sound of the product enhances 
the values and can convey standards 
such as trust, luxury or safety that 
may benefit the brand. [38]
 
Sight
Vision is the most powerful of our 
senses.  Understandably, traditional 
branding has spent most of its time, 
budget and efforts on it. However, 
nowadays the visual information 
received is overwhelming which 
may lead to the palliation of its 
effectiveness as a brand builder. 
Despite these facts, its reliability has 
been proven many times. Take as 
example the ‘Toblerone’. Its shape is 
highly recognizable; triangular shape 
emulating the Matterhorn. It actually 
stands out for its distinctive shape 
rather than its taste. [38]
 
Unique shapes and colours can 
create a strong and solid foundation 
for brand building. Shapes are easily 
recognizable 
and 
remembered 
which may account for long-
standing brands. [38]
  
Touch
 
The sense of touch uses the largest 
interface to gather information from 
the world; the whole body.  One 
can perceive external forces and 
object through contact. Material, 
temperature, weight and shape have 
an impact on the touch experience.
The quality of a brand is closely 
related with how it feels and 
therefore it depends on the touch 
attributes of the product. A good 
example is the cork in wine bottles. 
They give a hint of quality that 
plastic screw tops cannot. [38]
 
Smell
 
Smell is in the air and one of the 
most subliminal senses of all. It is a 
powerful reminder which triggers 
memories, sensations, images and 
associations long ago forgotten. 
Smell even has an impact on 
the mood. Tests show that when 
exposed to a pleasant fragrance the 
mood improves 40% if it recalls a 
good memory. [38]
Smell involves a 45% of brand 
recognition, right after sight [40]. 
Nonetheless, smell is usually very 
much underestimated.
 
Taste
 
Taste together with the smell 
is known as the chemical sense 
because one can sample the 
environment with them. In fact taste 
is very much depending on smell. 
However, taste is bound to a much 
closer interaction, which makes 
it difficult for plenty of brands to 
address it, except for the food 
industry. [40]
Above it has been explained how 
each sense can influence customer 
behaviour separately. However, the 
real potential comes when all five 
senses work in synergy in order to 
create a sensory consistency.
With the synergy of the five senses 
one can climb what M. Linström 
calls the sensory pyramid; going 
from stimulate, through enhance 
in order to achieve bond with a 
specific brand. [38]
Relevant 
considerations 
Sensory branding is a powerful 
tool to create bonding between 
consumer 
and 
brand. 
The 
hypothesis here is to translate this 
idea to the purpose of the project; 
create an impact, bounding and 
a positive long-standing relation 
with sustainability, science and 
technology.
This strategy can be very influential 
at an early age where the senses 
are crucial in the development. This 
could create a positive influence 
regarding science, technology and 
sustainability in children.
In the solution of the project, as it 
will be further developed in next 
chapters, all five senses are present 
through the tree different play 
modes. Painting includes sight. Music 
touch and sound. Cooking smell 
and taste.

DIY Toy Kits
DIY toy kits are hands on experience 
toys which usually teach about 
only one, very specific topic such 
as wind and solar energy. Children 
aim to build the specific technology 
and make it work. With it children 
learn the fundamental principles 
of a specific technology in an 
interactive way and throughout the

Usually most of these kits are

Mainly, 
the 
gameplay 
focuses 
on the building up phase and 
verification of principle. These type 
of products usually do not envision 
further gameplay. 
Generally DIY toy kits are targeted

producer exponents within the 
market are; Kosmos and Thames, 
and 4m. These two companies have 
a large and varied range of DIY toy 
kits, which embrace several fields. 
Both have a section dedicated only 
to sustainability.

The observations above can be 
linked with aspects of the theory 
from previous sections.

and

Accomplishment is achieved by 
setting clear goals (build up the

a certain level of difficulty and 
challenge to surpass. Discovery is 
embedded within the curiosity of 
proving the veracity of the theory. 
Other elements from the theory 
do miss in such type of product, 
for instance bonding and fantasy. 
These missing aspects open a 
window in the solution of space 
for novel products with similar 
characteristics.
DIY toy kits are also closely related 
with experiential learning. In fact, 
the fundamentals of DIY toy kits 
are based on it.

Probes is a technique for learning 
about 
intended 
users 
in 
an 
inspirational way based on their 
self-documentation. Probes are a 
collection of evocative tasks that 
the user has to complete in his 
daily context. It is a non intrusive 
explorative tool to gain insights and 
inspiration through the user. [5] [42]
Within 
the 
project 
probes 
were both part of the initial 
research as well as first step 
of the contextmapping design 
methodology, the sensitization.
Sensitization helps the user to 
familiarize with the topic of the 
project. It can be considered as a 
warm up previous to the ideation 
session, the following stage of the 
contexmapping process.
Objectives 
The probes were designed to 
gather insights about children’s 
interests, passions and vision of 
the future. At the same time the 
topic of science, technology and 
sustainability was at focus together 
with their educational context and 
the awareness of the five senses. 
The probes were designed to be 
ambiguous and open-ended in 
order to trigger unexpected results.
Set Up
The probes designed for the 
project consisted in a booklet with 
six different tasks to complete over 
a week, one each day.

technology and science

to…
•	 Task5: When I grow up…
•	 Task6: Five senses exploration

returned which 8 were totally 
complete.

Gamification
Gamification refers to the use 
of game mechanics in non-game 
contexts. In this particular case it is 
referred to learning.
It is a valuable tool because it pokes 
into the intrinsic motivation of 
children and places learning outside 
the school/obligation context.
Experiential learning
Experiential learning is a hands on 
experience approach to learning.
The students learn from a more 
practical and proactive perspective 
directly related to the real world. 
Both goal and process of the study 
are significant during the education, 
thus creating a deeper and lasting 
knowledge. 
Clean-technology
It is the meeting point between 
science, technology and sustainability.
It is a suitable field for experiential 
learning because usually it is a hard 
topic to understand exclusively 
from the literature.

Target
The chosen target group for the 
project are children from 8 to 11 
years of age, both boys and girls.
The reasons behind this specific 
target are:
(1) children still do not have 
preconceived ideas and there is still 
margin to foster values. [44] 
(2) Children begin to accept and 
understand activities that follow 
rules, structure and goals. [24]
(3) Previous to puberty, children, 
independently of gender, enjoy 
similar 
activities, 
show 
similar 
behaviours (social and emotional) 
and also share similar physical 
characteristics. [28][29][30]
(4) The Dutch educational system 
creates an accentuated segregation 
at age of 12. Children choose 
depending on their interests and 
academic performance either a 
university or a vocational studies 
path. [31]
(5) 
Hobbies 
and 
activities 
performed at an early age (7-11 
or even younger) have last longing 
effects and help to develop skills 
and preferences that last until and 
during adulthood. [27]
 
Context
The context  includes the intended 
gameplay and the place where the 
solution will be used and sold.
 
The product is aimed to have an 
extended gameplay. This means 
that the product is intended to 
be used repeatedly over the time. 
Thus, it diverges from the classic 
technology DIY toy kits which focus 
mainly in the building part and not 
on the later gameplay.  
The product is aimed to be used 
at home. Absences and advantages 
of such environment should be 
considered during the development 
phase. The product has to be 
tailored to suit this scenario. 
 
The product will be sold in the 
Metabolic Lab. Metabolic lab is an 
education and showcase center 
regarding sustainability owned by 
Metabolic. To make the product 
more vendible it would be advisable 
to make it enticing and interactive 
within the Metabolic Lab, using the 
showcase as a teaser.     
Five senses
Sensorial Branding has proven to 
be effective in order to create an 
impact, as well as bonding between 
brand and consumer. [38]
The hypothesis considered is to 
convey this concept into a learning 
context.
It is considered that leveraging 
the five senses in the product 
would create a deeper experience 
on children that could last until 
adulthood.
Approach
Due to the complexity of each of 
the elements that form the design 
frame and time constraints of the 
project a decision had to be taken 
in how to approach the design; 
either focus on a few elements of 
the design frame in depth or try to 
incorporate all of them at once in a 
more elementary way.
As all the elements were considered 
equally important and worked in 
synergy it was decided to take the 
second approach and embed all the 
elements at once in the design.

Outcome
The major and most promising 
and enticing clean technologies 
identified during the session were:
Algae
Algae have a huge potential 
regarding sustainability and currently 
there is a lot of research invested 
on them. Algae exchange CO2 for 
O2 during the photosynthesis and 
can be grown massively almost 
anywhere and with an incredible 
rate of reproduction.  A type of 
bioluminescent algae exists for 
which their brightness is a water 
pollution tester. Green algae are a 
great source for biofuels. Spirulina 
is an algae type which can be 
cultivated for vitamin-rich human 
or animal food.
Bioplastics
Bioplastics are based on starch. 
Starch together with glycerol form 
a polymer which is biodegradable. 
Currently there is on going research 
in how to improve the properties 
of these bioplastics. Nowadays few 
commercialized products in the 
market are made out of bioplastics.
Smart grids
A smart grid is a modern monitoring 
and automation of the electric 
grid. It automatically shares and 
regulates the electricity between 
different kind of generators (if 
possible renewable energies) and 
consumers, in order to make it 
more efficient and effective.
Biofuels
Biofuels are energy sources made 
from living things or living things 
waste, called biomass. Defenders 
of bio-fuels argue that the green 
gas emissions are highly reduced 
compared to current fuels. 
Upcycling
Upcycling is about updating waste 
into new products or materials with 
a higher value or better quality.
Biofertilizers
Biofertilizers are the alternatives 
to synthesized chemicals used in 
plants. They are substances that 
contain living things which stimulate 
plants growth.
 
Urban agriculture
Urban agriculture is the practice of 
cultivating as local as possible within 
cities; in the kitchen, on the terrace, 
in a balcony, in parks, etc. 
The movement tries to reduce 
‘food miles’ (food transportation 
effects), eat season products and 
even turn the cities greener.

with three play modes: painting,

Drawing
What if instead of just planting

children to draw with them? 
Children are able to create their

The different colours and textures

Children can observe their creations

Children’s aim is not anymore to

that usually are disconnected.
Cooking
What if children could prepare

children during the cultivation and 
preparation of meals will spark their 
interest in eating a variety of foods. 
Children could discover different 
taste and texture through the game.

greens which hopefully could be 
scaled to other vegetables.

Circular play 
In the previous section the different 
play modes that conform the whole

circle is the cooking. Finally, greens 
are ready. They can be harvested 
and one can prepare a meal with 
them. By eating the greens children 
close the circle and add the last 
dimension to the circle. 
Closing the cycle does not mean 
the end of the gameplay. Children 
can start again the whole process 
and try new variations throughout 
the process which will create new 
outputs.

Tools
In order to create simultaneously a more painting like 
experience with the seeds and more accuracy several assisting 
tools were proposed.
Tool 1 - spring
The tool consists on a spring injection device. It releases a 
small amount of seeds every time one presses the piston. This 
device gives accuracy. However, it neither fixes the seeds in 
the medium nor creates a continuous way of painting. The 
device used for this experiment was called a mini-seed master 
available at stores.
Tool 2 - clutch
The tool is based on the clutch pencil mechanism. Each time 
one presses the top of the tool, it releases a small amount 
of seeds.  In this case the painting is more fluent since the 
clutch can remain open, however it is very hard to control the 
amount of seeds released.
 
Tool 3 - air pressure
The mechanism to release the seeds, as its name indicates, 
works with air pressure. This mechanism provides a more 
continuous lines, which makes it more similar to painting but 
does not provide accuracy since seeds are expelled with too 
much power. The mechanism does not fix the seeds on the 
medium neither.
Tool 4 - gelatin ink
The tool was the result of the combination of tools and 
mediums. The idea behind is to use gelatin (tissue culture) as 
matrix for the ink that contains the seeds. Air pressure is used 
to eject the gelatin. The ink (gelatin) creates a seamless flow 
of lines. Also, the sticky consistency of the jelly ensures that 
the seeds get fixed the medium. Furthermore,  the agar agar 
gelatin is completely innocuous and it does not influence the 
growth of the microgreens. It could even be beneficial during 
the germination if some nutrients are added. Two aspects are 
to be taken in account for this tool:
•	 The density of the gelatin has to be liquid enough to be 
easily spread but not too much so it works as ink.    
•	 The rate of seeds in the gelatin has to be enough for 
growing into recognizable lines but not too much to 
compromise seed growth.
Choice 
The Gelatin ink pen was the most promising method. It provides 
a more similar painting experience, has better precision and 
control over the painted lines and the seeds get stuck in the 
medium instantly.

Music
The music play mode with plants is 
based on capacitive touch sensors.
The principle behind capacitance 
and capacitive touch sensors is 
explained hereunder. 
A basic capacitor is a device which 
consists of two conductors or 
electrodes, e.g. metal plates, and an 
insulator in between these two. The 
insulator medium is called dielectric 
and can be made out of different 
materials such as air and ceramics. 
When the capacitor is connected to 
a battery the plates are able to store 
the charge until reaching the same 
voltage as the source. Capacitance 
is the electrical property which 
measures the amount of charge 
that a capacitor can store given a 
certain voltage It can be formulated 
as follows: [51]
C : capacitance
: relative permittivity
: permittivity of free space
A : area of the plates
d : distance between the plates
From the equation the capacitance 
depends on the area of the plates, 
the distance between plates and 
the dielectric material.
A capacitive touch sensor consists 
of only one of the electrodes. 
The hand and the surrounding 
environment  become the other 
electrode.
A measurement unit is taking 
samples of the capacitance of the 
first electrode periodically. When 
the capacity of the plate changes 
due to the contact of a conductive 
material, in this case a hand, the 
measurement unit detects the 
variation and converts it into a 
trigger signal. [52]
When translating the capacitive 
touch sensor into the project, 
plants become the first electrode 
connected to the measurement 
unit. When the plants are stroked 
by a hand the capacitance change 
is measured by the unit. These 
difference of capacity is translated 
to an output signal sent to the 
speaker which transforms it into 
sound. 
In order to build up a capacitive 
touch sensor with  plants the 
key  element of it was an Arduino 
microcontroller.
In the Appendix L can be found 
the detailed components, hardware 
and software used.

Proposal
	
The name chosen for the proposal 
design was Eat the Beat. 
Eat the Beat offers all three modes 
in a compact way. Its triangular 
shape is meant to express the 
three different play modes, i.e. a play 
mode per corner, and the circularity 
amongst them.
The casing is made entirely out of 
laser cut plywood. It is designed 
for assembly in order to facilitated 
the construction by means of 
interlocked panels. It can be 
observed in the explosion view of 
the model on the next page.
Three 
clear 
areas 
can 
be 
distinguished in the embodiment 
including each play mode: painting, 
music and cooking.
The central part contains all the 
necessary items for the painting 
play mode. It consists in: gelatin ink 
pen, three different packages of 
seeds, a package of agar and several 
coconut fiber canvases. 
The Gelatin ink pen is placed on 
one lateral. On the other, each seed 
package has its own compartment 
as well as the agar package.
Underneath those a compartment 
can be found for storing the 
coconut coir canvases.
Furthermore the lid of the casing 
becomes an easel for the coconut 
coir canvases when fully opened.
On one edge of the casing the music 
play mode can be found. It contains 
the 
Arduino 
microcontroller, 
the circuit, the speaker and the 
correspondent cabling. The holes on 
the side allows the sound to come 
out. There are also two buttons to 
calibrate the capacitive sensor.
Two different cables come out of 
the compartment. On the exterior 
side there is the power cord for 
easy plug in. In the interior side the 
anode of the capacitive sensor is 
placed, as it is easier to reach the 
microgreens on the easel from 
there.
On the other edge of the casing 
there is a small compartment, the 
cooking area. This compartment 
contains a cookbook with several 
recipes that children can prepare 
with the microgreens they cultivated 
previously.
The 
next 
pages 
include 
the 
exploded view of the model and 
pictures of the model in detail. The 
technical drawings can be found in 
the Appendix O.

5. CONCLUSIONS &
FUTURE PROSPECTS
This chapter synthesize the overall project and 
expresses the results, the successes and limitation 
of the project. At the end it leaves some open 
questions for further research and verification. 
It also contains the recommended further steps to 
follow in the design process. It focuses both on the 
improvements on the short term as well as a long 
term plan.

Eat the Beat is the result of the 
research question: ‘How can we 
make an impact at an early age in 
order to stimulate understanding 
and interest, and foster values in 
sustainability, science and technology?’
Eat the Beat is an interactive toy 
which aims to stimulate urban 
agriculture in children aged 8 to 
11 years old. It consists in growing 
microgreens through the use of 
three interrelated play modes: 
painting, music and cooking. They 
evolve one after each other.
The painting mode includes a 
gelatin ink pen. The ink matrix of 
gelatin contains seeds. By pressing 
the gelatin ink out, children are able 
to draw their own patterns and 
paintings. The gelatin ink pen allows 
continuous lines of seeds that get 
immediately ‘glued’ in place keeping 
the sense of the composition. 
Children have to water and take 
care of the seeds in order to see 
them grow.
When 
the 
microgreens 
have 
grown enough they are able to be 
connected to the music play mode. 
When plugged, this play mode turns 
the greens into an instruments, 
thanks to a capacitive sensor, that 
when touched produces sounds. 
Hence children can play music by 
interacting with the plants. 
The final play mode is the cooking 
one. When the microgreens are 
ready they can be harvest. Eat 
the Beat provides a cookbook 
which offers several recipes with 
microgreens as ingredients. Hence, 
children can prepare dishes with 
their own cultivated greens.
The research question can be 
deconstructed in several key points 
which Eat the Beat addresses 
simultaneously. 
Early age.
During 
childhood 
children 
development is constant and fast 
paced. Eat the Beat due to the 
complexity of the topic (science, 
technology 
and 
sustainability) 
targets children age 8-11 years. 
At that age children can already 
understand complicated issues but 
yet do not posses abstract thinking. 
Thus, it is a sweet spot for Eat the 
Beat to approach complex subjects 
and foster values within science, 
technology and sustainability.  
Science, technology and sustainability. 
Eat the Beat converges these three 
topics effectively through urban 
agriculture together with a more 
technical perspective of electronics 
within the music play mode.
Stimulate understanding and spark 
interest.
Eat the Beat avoids traditional 
learning 
by 
approaching 
the 
topics from two different fronts; a 
practical and hands on experience 
perspective (experiential learning) 
and introducing game mechanics 
through the process of learning 
(gamification). 
Eat 
the 
Beat 
pushes children to explore urban 
agriculture by using any of the 
modes (painting, music and cooking) 
which add the playfulness.  This in 
combination with the unification of 
non related subjects like agriculture 
and music helps to reinforce the 
engagement. Learning in a fun and 
practical manner can dissipate fears 
attributed to science, technology 
and sustainability. 
  
Create a long lasting relation.
By the leverage of the five senses, 
Eat the Beat is intended to create a 
fully sensorial experience. Children 
can 
explore 
the 
five 
senses 
consciously creating a powerful 
experience. With it users will be 
able in the future to relate sensory

SHORT TERM 
PROSPECTS
The user test helped to arise 
satisfactory aspects as well as flaws 
of Eat the Beat. Each one of the play 
modes could be improved.
Painting
The 
following 
aspects 
were 
considered for further improvement 
within the painting play mode. 
•	 Adapt size of the Gelatin ink 
pen to children’s hands.
•	 Easier reload process of the 
Gelatin ink pen.
•	 Improve the distribution of 
seeds within the jelly matrix.
•	 Easier identification of seed 
and microgreens colours.
The difficulty of reloading is critical 
for two reasons. On one hand 
the reload takes quite some time 
breaking the creative spell of the 
moment. On the other when 
reloading it is hard to create an 
even distribution of seeds within 
the matrix of jelly affecting the 
outcome of the composition.
A possible solution would be to 
create cartridges that can be load 
into the pen. Therefore, children can 
prepare several cartridges before 
starting to paint. Also it would be 
convenient to design the cartridges 
in such a way that allows simple and 
even load of jelly and seeds. 
In order to identify better the 
color of the seeds, the jelly could 
be coloured as well. Thus, children 
can immediately recognize which 
colour is used. A suggestion would 
be to pack together the exact 
quantity of seeds and agar (with the 
correspondent colour) per each 
load.
Music
The following aspects were 
considered for further 
improvement.
•	 Dissipate fear towards the 
electronics.
•	 Create more control over the 
sound.
•	 Provide information.
Children was very skeptical about 
this play mode. In fact, they thought 
it was a trick. In order to remove 
this feeling more information could 
be given about the principle behind.
Also it would be interesting to 
let them investigate with more 
materials than just the plants. Thus, 
the music play mode could be 
detachable and work with batteries 
independently from the grid.
Another important factor observed 
was that lack of control over the 
music which diluted the fun of the 
experience over the time.
To overcome this pitfall further 
development of the code would be 
needed.
Also to offer more gameplay it 
would be interesting to be able to 
connect more than one plant with 
different sounds each.
Finally, in order to be able to have 
more control and quantity of sound 
a more powerful speaker would be 
needed.
Cooking
The following aspects were 
considered for further 
improvement.
•	 Explore creativity.
•	 Recognition of their 
achievements.
•	 Increase complexity within 
reasonable limits.

An option to tease their creativity 
could be to add empty pages in 
the cookbook. There they can write 
down their own dishes. 
Another option would be to 
add some recipes with missing 
ingredients which they have to 
choose to complete the dish. 
It would at the same time add 
some unexpected results, boost 
their creativity and increase the 
complexity of the dishes.
A missing aspect of Eat the Beat 
is a social aspect. Children love to 
share with their friends and learn 
together. In order to create this 
bonding a proposal would be to 
create an Internet platform to 
share their culinary inventions. On 
it, children would be able to upload 
their recipes and pictures as well 
as learn from the others. In order 
to gamify it a voting and ranking 
system could be added.
Embodiment
First of all, the proposal for the 
embodiment together with other 
proposals should be presented 
to the users. The users could give 
feedback and insights for further 
improvements. 
Due 
to 
time 
constraints the prototyping of more 
proposals was not possible. Also, In 
order to follow with the co-design 
approach of the project, users could 
design their own mock ups which 
could be incorporated into the final 
design.
On the other hand from the 
previously presented embodiment 
several difficulties arose during 
the actual prototyping which had 
potential to be improved:
•	 Use of two many parts increases 
the building time and influences 
precision.
•	 The use of glue increases the 
production time and there is a 
risk of detachment if the process 
has not being done properly.
•	 The use of hinges increases the 
production time and number of 
parts.
•	 Some necessary angles are hard 
to produce and need of extra 
tools that have to be expressly 
produced.
The most critical problem is the use 
of a large number of parts. By using 
certain laser cut patterns it is possible 
to bend wood. Thus, most of the 
embodiment would consist in only 
one part which would reduce the 
number of parts, avoid the need of 
hinges and eliminate the complicate 
angles. Check the picture below for 
better understanding.

LONG TERM 
PROSPECTS
The previous sections stated the 
immediate steps to follow within 
the product development process. 
However, to bring the product 
into the market there is still a long 
journey. An overview proposal for 
a future plan could be the one 
presented below.
User Test.
After realizing the improvements 
stated, the product should be 
validated again by the users. This 
user test could be longer and 
extended to the children’s daily life 
and context by giving them a fully 
working prototype to play with at 
home. 
The user test is determinant. If it is 
satisfactory the process can move 
to the next stage otherwise it 
would require another iteration for 
further refinement. 
Final Design.
Adjust the last bits and pieces of 
the design and make the detailing. A 
final and fully functional prototype 
should be made to have a clear 
view and understanding of the final 
product.
Strategy.
In order to introduce the product 
into the market different strategies 
could be adopted to gain the most 
profit. These have to be developed, 
analyzed and chosen in order to 
ensure the success of the product.
Production. 
Prepare the product for production: 
technical drawings, list of materials, 
contact suppliers and send to 
production. In order to see the 
results a small batch can be 
produced before starting with 
bigger quantities.  
Market.
The product is ready. It is time 
to get in contact with potential 
customers, set reliable networks, 
prepare distribution channels and 
the logistics to face the market.
