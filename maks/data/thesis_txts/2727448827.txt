Abstract  
This thesis seeks to investigate the ability of δ-cyclodextrin (δ-CD) and its three derivatives (permethylated- 
(PMe-), 2-hydroxypropyl- (HP-), and sulfobutylether-δ-CD (SBE-δ-CD)) to function as carriers for a range of 
flavor and aroma compounds (Figure 1). Cyclodextrins (CDs) are, among others, used in the food and cosmetic 
industries to encapsulate flavor and aroma compounds to prolong shelf-life and modify fragrance-release for 
volatile aroma compounds1. By using 1H NMR spectroscopy, two aromatic flavor compounds, thymol and 
isoeugenol, was found to exhibit the largest changes in chemical shifts in presence of δ-CD and its three 
derivatives. Subsequently, the binding strengths between thymol and δ-, PMe-δ-, and HP-δ-CD were 
determined which indicated that a relatively weak 1:1 binding event took in all three cases. The binding 
interaction between thymol and SBE-δ-CD was found to be more complex, where both scenarios of a 1:2 and 
2:1 binding event showed to be realistic options.  
In the following part of the thesis, a hydrolysis rate study is presented where hydrolysis profiles for δ-CD and 
its three derivatives are investigated in presence of human saliva α-amylase. The results suggested that δ-CD 
can act as a fast-releasing carrier, PMe-δ-CD as a flavor-masking carrier, while HP-, and SBE-δ-CD can 
function as slow flavor-releasing carriers.  
The final part of the thesis presents the development of a UV-vis displacement assay that aims to function as 
a tool in the discovery of molecules that bind to δ-CD. The assay relies upon a change in the absorbance of the 
pH indicator, Bromothymol blue (BTB), in presence of δ-CD. However, the assay showed to depend on an 
optical change that were too small to be reliable and the assay needs further optimizations to be a useful tool 
in the future.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Supramolecular chemistry  
Supramolecular chemistry is the study of molecular assemblies formed by intermolecular interactions between 
two or more molecules. Supramolecular assemblies are held together by noncovalent interactions that among 
others includes hydrogen bonding, van der Waals forces, electrostatic interactions, π-π interactions, and 
hydrophobic effects. The focus in supramolecular chemistry relies upon how these molecular assemblies 
interact and recognizes each other as well as how they fold and self-assemble into larger complex structures 2. 
Some key supramolecular concepts concern host-guest complexes, molecular recognition, and self-assembly. 
Several supramolecular systems can be found in Nature, for example the double-helix structure of DNA that 
is formed due to the two nucleotide strands that self-assemble through hydrogen bonds and π-stacking 
interactions. The supramolecular phenomena are also observed during the translation of RNA into proteins, or 
when an enzyme and a substrate recognize each other forming a host-guest complex. Therefore, the design of 
novel supramolecular complexes draw inspiration from biological systems2,3 
The field of supramolecular chemistry has been fast-growing the past decades and in 1987 and 2016 the field 
achieved scientific recognition. Jean-Marie Lehn, Donald J. Cram and Charles J. Pedersen received the Nobel 
prize in chemistry in 1987 for their work on cryptands and described the area as “chemistry of molecular 
assemblies and of the intermolecular bond”2. Later, in 2016, the Nobel prize in chemistry was awarded Jean-
Pierre Sauvage, Sir J. Fraser Stoddart and Bernard L. Feringa for their work on “the design and synthesis of 
molecular machines”4.  
1.1.1 Systems chemistry 
Systems chemistry is the study of complex mixtures, or networks, of interacting molecules with roots of 
supramolecular principles. It has previously been difficult to study systems of complex mixtures and the focus 
in chemistry has primarily been on isolated species. The development of modern analytical techniques, such 
as high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), has changed the situation and allowed for the study of 
complex systems. The focus in systems chemistry relies on studying the functions and behavior of the whole 
system rather than the isolated molecules. In addition, such systems respond to an external stimulus which 
alters the composition and lead to amplification of a specific molecule. Several described systems are 
thermodynamically stable such as Dynamic Combinatorial Libraries (DCLs) (Figure 1a) which will be 
explained in detail in section 1.3. Other systems that operate under kinetic control (Figure 1b) are also studied. 
They exist transiently as trapped in a local energy minimum. Furthermore, systems that operate far from 
equilibrium exist (Figure 1c). They require a continuous energy supply to push the system into an energy state 
where unpredictable emergent properties might occur5–7.

1.2 Cyclodextrins 
Cyclodextrins are a family of cyclic oligosaccharides formed by d-glucopyranose units that are linked together 
by α-1,4-glycosidic bonds. They are commercially produced from the enzymatic breakdown of starch or 
amylose by cyclodextrin glucanotransferase (CGTase). The catalytic action of CGTase takes place on α-1,4-
glucans where it both catalyzes reversible transglycosylations and slow hydrolysis (Figure 4). The 
transglycosylation reactions can take place as both an intramolecular and intermolecular reaction. During 
intramolecular transglycosylation, a single linear α-1,4-glucan undergoes cyclization which leads to formation 
of a cyclodextrin and a shorter linear α-1,4-glucan. However, CGTase does not catalyze intramolecular 
transglycosylation on linear α-1,4-glucan with less than seven glucose units. In the intermolecular 
transglycosylation, two linear α-1,4-glucans react to form a shorter or an elongated linear α-1,4-glucans also 
called disproportionation. A new reducing-end glucose unit is formed in the hydrolysis and cannot be 
reincorporated in the formation of a cyclodextrin. The hydrolysis is slow meaning that all α-glucans will 
eventually be converted to glucose8.

The most common and well-described cyclodextrins are α-, β-, and γ-cyclodextrin, which are composed of six, 
seven, and eight α-1,4-linked glucose units, respectively2. As seen in, they exhibit truncated cone structures 
with a hydrophilic exterior and hydrophobic interior as a result of the glucose units exhibiting 4C1 
conformation. This gives rise to all secondary hydroxyl groups (2-OH and 3-OH groups) are placed on the 
wider edge of the cyclodextrin while the primary hydroxyl groups (6-OH groups) are placed in the narrower 
edge. The C-H hydrogens on position C3 and C5 forms the hydrophobic interior together with the glycosidic 
oxygen bridges9. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The water solubility of the three native cyclodextrins α-, β-, and γ-CD varies and are 14.5, 1.85 and 23.2 
g/100mL, respectively, at 25 ⁰C10. The surprisingly low solubility of β-CD may be explained by its high 
symmetry giving rise to seven internal hydrogen bonds between the hydroxyl groups on C2 and C3. The rigid 
structure of β-CD decreases the possibility of hydrogen bond formation with water which lowers its solubility. 
Oppositely in α-CD, two glucose units have a distorted orientation and therefore only four hydrogen bonds are 
present explaining its higher solubility. For γ-CD, the greater aqueous solubility is probably due to its higher 
flexibility and noncoplanar geometry11. δ-CD consists of nine α-1,4-linked glucopyranose units and has a water 
solubility of 8.19 g/mL at 25 ⁰C. In similarity to β-CD, the solubility of δ-CD can be explained by the formation 
of intramolecular bonds between the secondary hydroxyl groups10.  
The larger cyclodextrins (δ, ε, etc.) are very unexplored due to their synthetic inaccessibility. Therefore, the 
existing knowledge regarding their properties, host-guest chemistries and their possible applications is 
limited12. Previous methods to synthesize and isolate large ring cyclodextrins have been to isolate them from 
complex mixtures which requires extensive chromatography and are therefore time-consuming and costly13. 
In a study conducted by Ueda and coworkers14, δ-CD was isolated from a commercial mixture of CDs and

as they might offer potential applications in situations where the native cyclodextrins are inadequate. 
 
 
 
 
 
1.2.1 Applications of cyclodextrins and their derivatives 
The use of cyclodextrins and their derivatives in pharmaceutical applications has widely increased in recent 
years. Their ability of enhancing solubility, stability, and bioavailability of guest molecules makes them useful 
as carriers18–20. As of September 2022, the total count of marketed active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) 
formulated with cyclodextrins was 129. Among these, nearly half of them were formulated with modified 
cyclodextrins, of which sulfobutylether- (SBE) and 2-hydroxypropyl- (HP) derivatives of β-CD were 
dominating21. To a lesser extent, randomly methylated (RM) derivatives of β-CD are also used for 
encapsulation of APIs 22. The molecular structures of these modifications are seen in Figure 7. As mentioned 
earlier, native β-CD has a relatively low aqueous solubility, which is significantly enhanced when it is 
modified, as its intramolecular hydrogen bonds breaks apart23,24.  Furthermore, the cavity size of β-CD is 
suitable for a wide range of APIs, resulting in the formulation of numerous marketed APIs with β-CD and its 
derivatives. The cavity size of α-CD is insufficient in many cases, and there are no reported pharmaceutical 
applications for derivatives of α-CD, while a few examples of HP-γ-CD have found applications. In the 
pharmaceutical industry, CDs and their derivatives are generally used for enhancing solubility, improving 
bioavailability or masking the bitter taste that many drugs have1,25.

Besides their applications in the pharmaceutical industry, cyclodextrins and their derivatives are also useful in 
the food- and cosmetics industry. They are widely applied for flavor and aroma compounds as these are volatile 
species which are prone to degradation by heat, oxygen, and light induced reactions1. However, their stability 
and shelf life can be extended when forming an inclusion complex with a CD2. In addition, they can be used 
to mask or eliminate unpleasant flavors and odors1,26. This is for example applied in the food industry for goat 
milk, where β-CD is used to eliminate the “goaty flavor” by complexing with branched-chain fatty acids (e.g., 
4-methyloctanoic acid) which gives rise to the unpleasant flavor. In general, CD derivatives shows better as 
stabilizing carriers than the natural CDs1.  
Between 2000 and 2004, α-, β-, and γ-CD was included in FDA’s list of ingredients that are generally regarded 
as safe (GRAS) for use as food additive. The two widely used derivatives of β-CD, SBE-β-CD and HP-β-CD, 
has been included in Food and Drug Administration’s (FDA) list of inactive pharmaceutical ingredients23.  
 
1.2.2 Hydrolysis of cyclodextrins 
The initial breakdown of starch in humans is carried out by salivary α-amylase, which is the most abundant 
protein found in human saliva. This is responsible for catalyzing the hydrolysis of α-1,4-glycosidic linkages to 
produce maltose, maltotriose or larger oligosaccharides. The digestion of starch continues in the stomach by 
the action of pancreatic α-amylase which completes the hydrolysis27. The action of α-amylase does not take 
place on maltose to form glucose, as this reaction is catalyzed by maltase enzymes28,29. Cyclodextrins, which 
are composed of glucose units linked together by α-1,4 glycosidic bonds, can serve as substrates for the 
catalytic hydrolysis by α-amylase. In literature, hydrolysis studies on α-, β-, and γ-CD as well as CD derivatives 
are described, however, inconsistent results on the hydrolysis of α-, and β-CD by human salivary α-amylase

are reported. A study by Marshall et al.30 reports that γ-CD is slowly hydrolyzed by human saliva α-amylase, 
while α-, and β-CD are not. In a more recent study by Harangi et al. 31, it is reported that the hydrolysis of α-,

The rate of hydrolysis for CDs and their derivatives becomes crucial in their role as carriers for flavor and 
aroma compounds. CDs that are hydrolyzed fast by α-amylase can function as flavor-releasing carriers while 
acting as stabilizing agents during storage. Alternatively, CDs that are slowly or not hydrolyzed by α-amylase 
can serve as flavor-masking carriers for unpleasant tastes25,32,33.   
1.3 Dynamic Combinatorial Chemistry  
The way that δ-CD is produced in our group is by isolating it from an enzyme-mediated dynamic combinatorial 
library. Therefore, a brief introduction to the concept of Dynamic Combinatorial Chemistry DCC is given as 
it underpins the only efficient way of producing δ-CD.  
DCC refers to the supramolecular approach that concerns self-assembly of oligomers under thermodynamic 
control. The approach involves small molecular building blocks that react reversibly with each other and forms 
a dynamic equilibrium mixture of new covalently linked molecules. This interconverting mixture is called a 
dynamic combinatorial library (DCL)34,35. The distribution of the products in the DCL is influenced by the 
intrinsic stabilities of the library components, which means the composition of the library can be altered when 
exposed to an external stimulus such as changes in pH, temperature, light, concentrations, or solvent. The most 
explored approach to change the distribution of the DCL relies upon molecular recognition. This is where the 
DCL is exposed to a template, which is a compound that selectively interacts non-covalently with one library 
member that will be stabilized and amplified in the mixture35–37. The concept of DCC is illustrated in Figure 
8. The library distribution is dependent on the total thermodynamic stabilities of all library members in the 
system. This implies that the amplification of a specific library member will affect the stability of the other 
library members as they are in equilibrium. Therefore, situations can occur where the amplified specie is not 
the one with the greatest binding strength to the template5,35.  
==PAGE:22==

Figure 8: Schematic representation of DCC. A template drives the DCL towards the selective production of the bound library

DCC is a powerful tool when fast generation of dynamic complex mixtures is desired making it possible to 
rapidly screen many compounds for a desired activity. Hereby, it is possible to discover and access complex 
architectures that are normally inaccessible36. The approach is among others of interest in drug-lead discovery. 
DCC has been used to synthesize effective ligands for biomacromolecules, catalysts, sensors, dynamic 
materials and to study the structural arrangement of foldamers such as peptides, nucleic acids, and synthetic 
polymers35.  Furthermore, the templated approach in DCC, has made it possible to synthesize complex 
structures, among others linear receptors39, macrocycles40, cages41, catenanes, and knots42. 
 
1.4 Enzyme-mediated DCC 
Limited research has been carried out on the use of enzymes in DCC. Enzymes are suitable to use in DCC as 
they can catalyze reversible reactions under mild conditions such as physiological pH and aqueous media 
which resembles a biologically relevant environment. In addition, they are easy to denature in order to render 
the DCL static for analysis or isolation of library members.  To date, only a limited number of enzyme-
mediated DCLs have been reported, all describing the formation of relatively small DCLs43. An example of 
enzyme-mediated DCC was reported by Brown et al. (2004)44,45 who showed the formation of a DCL with 
sialic acid analogues using the enzyme N-acetylneuraminic acid aldolase (NANA aldolase EC 4.1.3.3). When 
the DCL was exposed to wheat germ agglutinin as a template, the system was driven towards the synthesis of 
a specific library member. Another, more recent, example of enzyme-mediated DCC was reported by Ulijn 
and coworkers, that showed the generation of a DCL of short aromatic peptide derivatives using the enzyme 
thermolysin (EC 3.4.24.27)46–48. This enzyme both catalyzes amide bond formation and hydrolysis. The formed 
of Fmoc-peptides were shown to self-assemble into antiparallel β-sheet structures under thermodynamic 
control46. The authors showed in a later study48, the formation of a DCL of unprotected peptides. The product 
distribution was manipulated by various environmental conditions to give different peptide nanostructures.

1.4.1 Recent work on enzyme-mediated DCC within the group 
An earlier study within the group by Larsen et al. (2019)43 showed the formation of a DCL of interconverting 
cyclodextrins and linear α-1,4-glucans under pseudo-thermodynamic control. The DCL was formed starting 
from an α-1,4-glucan source in presence of the enzyme cyclodextrin glucotransferase (CGTase). The DCL was 
generated at neutral pH and at room temperature and consists almost exclusively of the native small-ring 
cyclodextrins (α-, β-, and γ-CD). The DCL was formed in the initial phases of the reaction as part of a 
kinetically trapped subsystem that transiently operates under thermodynamic control as depicted in  Figure 9. 
The system is called pseudo-thermodynamic since CGTase, as previously described, catalyzes slow and

(G1), as the real thermodynamic product of the reaction.

By exposing the transient DCL to different templates, the authors showed that β- and γ-CD is exclusively

two strong binding constants, while β-CD binds strongly to SDS in a 1:1 stoichiometry. As the entropic penalty 
is larger in a 2:1 binding compared to 1:1 binding and the entropic contribution is depending on temperature, 
it was hypothesized and shown that α-CD was favored even more when lowering the reaction temperature.

1.5 Thermodynamics of binding interactions for supramolecular systems 
The formation of a host-guest complex occurs, like any other spontaneous binding event, when the overall free 
energy of the binding interaction has a negative Gibbs free energy (ΔG⁰)50. The Gibbs free energy is dependent 
on the two energy contributions, enthalpy and entropy, at a given temperature (T) as defined in equation 1.   
∆G° = ∆H° −T∆S°
(1) 
The change in enthalpy (ΔH⁰) is defined as the total amount of energy that is either lost or gained upon 
complexation, while the change in entropy (ΔS⁰) is defined as the level of disorder within the system. A 
spontaneous complex formation occurs when the overall change in Gibbs free energy is negative, which can 
be obtained when the binding is either entropically (0 < ∆S°) or enthalpically driven (∆H° < 0), or both. A 
binding event is considered both enthalpically and entropically favorable when the complex formation is 
exothermic and there is an overall increase in the level of disorder.  An enthalpically favorable binding event 
takes place when there is a total increase in the number and strength of the intermolecular interactions within 
the system. This is a balance between bonds formed (∆H° < 0) and bonds broken (∆H° > 0) upon 
complexation51. An entropically favorable binding is more clearly explained by the hydrophobic effect. In 
aqueous solution, the hydrophobic cavity of CDs is occupied with highly ordered water molecules resulting in 
energetically unfavorable apolar and polar interactions. By encapsulating a hydrophobic guest inside the cavity 
of a CD, a more energetically stable inclusion complex is formed due to the hydrophobic effect2. The 
hydrophobic effect is predominantly entropy driven as the water molecules are released into the bulk solvent 
upon complexation. This release allows the water molecules to move more freely increasing their degrees of 
freedom2,11. When an inclusion complex is formed between a host and a guest, the binding is most often 
associated with a large negative enthalpic contribution and a minor either positive or negative entropic 
contribution2.

1.6 Aim of the project  
The aim of this thesis was to synthesize δ-CD and three of its derivatives. The derivatives of δ-CD were chosen 
to be SBE-, RM-, and HP-δ-CD as these, as earlier mentioned, are some of the derivatives that are utilized in 
the industry for the native cyclodextrins. 
After having δ-CD and three of its derivatives in hand, it was the aim to investigate their complexation 
properties with a range of flavor and aroma compounds using 1H NMR spectroscopy. In addition, the project 
aims to study the rate of hydrolysis of δ-CD and its three derivatives by human salivary α-amylase. This is of 
interest in order to investigate their potential as carriers to either mask unpleasant flavors or release nice flavors 
on the mouth.  
The purpose of the project further intends to develop a UV-vis displacement assay, to aid in the discovery of 
molecules that bind to δ-CD and three of its derivatives.

After having δ-CD and three of its derivatives in hand it was of interest to explore their complexation properties

The compounds were analyzed with 5.6 mM excess of δ-CD, PMe-, and HP-δ-CD while an excess of only 2.7 
mM was possible for SBE-δ-CD due to its lower solubility. The measured samples contained 90% H2O and 
10% D2O as the stock solutions of the flavor compounds were prepared in water while the stock solutions of

spectra were analyzed for interactions by looking for changes in chemical shifts for either the flavor compound 
or the CD upon complexation. It showed to be more challenging than expected to analyze small changes in 
chemical shifts as the external calibrant signal often gave a doublet or multiplet instead of the expected singlet. 
This was discussed to be either due to the water suppression method applied or an instrumental shimming 
error. Therefore, the overlapping spectra were additionally analyzing for changes in chemical shifts by 
overlapping an identical peak and see whether the remaining signals had changed.

The flavor screenings presented in section 2.2, revealed large changes in chemical shifts for the two aromatic 
compounds, thymol and isoeugenol, when they were analyzed for interactions with all four δ-CDs. It was 
therefore of interest to investigate the binding strength of thymol and isoeugenol with δ-CD and its three 
derivatives (PMe-, HP-, and SBE-δ-CD). However, isoeugenol, that appears as a thick and sticky oil, proved 
to be challenging to dissolve in water as it was only possible to obtain a suspension of this compound. 
Therefore, 1H NMR spectroscopy titrations were only carried out for thymol with δ-CD and its three 
derivatives.  
The initial titration carried out was between thymol and δ-CD, where increasing equivalents of thymol (0-40 
mM) were titrated into a fixed concentration of δ-CD (1 mM). The titration was performed by having thymol 
as the guest and δ-CD as the host to minimize the amount of δ-CD used. However, it was not possible to 
quantify a binding constant in this titration, as linear binding isotherms was obtained (see appendix 6.5).

δ-CD (0-35 mM) into a fixed concentration of thymol (1 mM). Partial 1H NMR spectra obtained from this 
titration is depicted in the top of Figure 29, while two binding isotherms for peak 1 and 4 are plotted in Figure 
30. It was seen that the curve begins to stabilize late in the titration indicating a relatively weak binding 
interaction. The two peaks were fitted to equation 12 using non-linear curve fitting in Origin, where a weak 
binding constant of 33.8 ± 0.7 M-1 was found. Since a binding constant successfully was determined between 
δ-CD and thymol in this way, the remaining three titration experiments were performed in this way.

added in aliquots to a fixed concentration of thymol (1 mM). Partial 1H NMR spectra obtained from these

binding isotherms of peaks 1 and 4 were plotted for HP-δ-CD as displayed in Figure 30. Compared to the 
titration with δ-CD, smaller changes in chemical shifts are observed for the titration with PMe-δ-CD and larger 
changes in chemical shifts are observed with HP-δ-CD. Additionally, it is seen that the curves stabilize later 
for PMe-δ-CD while the curvatures stabilize sooner for HP-δ-CD when the binding isotherms are compared 
to the titration with δ-CD. The binding constants was found to be 19.2 ± 0.6 M-1 between thymol and PMe-δ-

2.3 Investigation of the hydrolysis of δ-CD and its derivatives by α-
amylase 
After investigating the complexation properties between δ-CD and its three derivatives with a range of flavor 
and aroma compounds, it was of interest to explore their potential to either disguise bitter flavors or release 
nice flavors in the mouth. Therefore, a hydrolysis rate study was carried out using human salivary α-amylase. 
The first study conducted aimed to investigate the hydrolysis rate of δ-CD in comparison to α-, ꞵ-, and γ-CD. 
The anomeric protons of the four CDs are distinguishable in a 1H NMR spectrum making it possible to study 
their rate of hydrolysis simultaneously by analyzing the peak intensities.  
The experiment was carried out in sodium phosphate buffer in D2O at pH 7.0, with all four CDs at a 
concentration of 1 mg/mL. The reaction was initially started by addition of 10 µL/mL saliva to explore the rate 
of hydrolysis. Over a period of 40 minutes, the resulting NMR spectra showed slow hydrolysis of primarily δ-
CD, but also some hydrolysis of γ-CD. The concentration of α-amylase was therefore increased, hence a new 
sample was prepared with 40 µL/mL saliva. Figure 34 depicts some of the 1H NMR spectra obtained within 
24.5 h for the experiment with 40 µL/mL of saliva. It is seen that the anomeric proton peak from δ-CD 
decreased rapidly within approximately 30 minutes compared to α-, ꞵ-, and γ-CD. Simultaneously, a new peak 
builds up corresponding to the anomeric reducing end proton of linear α-1,4-glucans at 5.27 ppm along with a 
mixture of anomeric non-reducing protons from linear α-1,4-glucans at 5.45 ppm. It was observed that δ-CD 
is hydrolyzed to near-completion within 30 min, while γ-CD is hydrolyzed to near-completion within 24.5 h, 
whereas α- and ꞵ-CD were not affected by the enzyme.

2.4 UV-vis assay for investigation of supramolecular complex formation  
A part of the aim in this project was to develop a UV-vis displacement assay to aid in the discovery of 
molecules that bind to δ-CD. The concept behind the assay relies upon an indicator that has a UV spectrum 
that changes when it complexes with δ-CD, as illustrated in Figure 39. When a competitive binder is introduced 
to a solution of this complex, it will displace the indicator that is bound inside the cavity of δ-CD. As a 
consequence, the UV-vis spectrum will return to the unbound form of the indicator.  
 
Figure 39: Schematic representation of the concept behind the UV-vis displacement assay. 
Advantages of this assay includes that very small amounts are required to test competitive binders with the 
assay, as micromolar concentrations can be detected with UV-vis spectroscopy using highly absorbent dyes. 
Additionally, the assay can function as a fast high-throughput method to identify binders.  
The first indicator tested was bromothymol blue (BTB), as it was suggested by PhD student Kasper Hansen, 
that it binds to δ-CD in a 1:1 binding with a binding constant of 11 × 103 M-1 by NMR. As seen on Figure 40, 
BTB exists in its ring-closed form under acidic conditions, where it absorbs blue light at 433.0 nm and therefore 
appears yellow63. Under alkaline conditions, BTB exists in its ring-opened conformation, where is absorbs 
yellow light at 615.5 nm63 and therefore appears blue. The pKa of BTB is 7.563, meaning that at this pH, BTB 
is present equally in both of its acidic and basic forms.

2.4.1 Investigation of BTB in presence of δ-CD and its three derivatives 
The initial experiment carried out aimed to investigate whether the UV-vis spectrum of BTB changes in 
presence of δ-CD and its three derivatives (PMe-, HP-, SBE-δ-CD). The absorbance of BTB was measured in 
presence of each of the four CDs at five pH values (pH 3, 5, 7, 8, and 10) to cover a broad range of the pH-

possible changes in absorbances. The measurements were performed in 50 mM ammonium acetate and 
phosphate buffer with 500 µM of CDs and 25 µM of BTB. The results of this experiment are seen in Figure

is clearly seen in presence of δ-CD at pH 7.0 with an absorbance increase of ~0.15 at 620 nm. Furthermore, it 
is seen at pH 7.0 that BTB has a higher absorbance around 440 nm, compared to its absorbance at this 
wavelength in presence of the CDs. This suggests that the presence of CDs pushes the acid/base equilibrium 
of BTB towards its opened deprotonated form and stabilizes BTB in this form. It is also clear to see changes 
in absorbance at pH 8.0 in presence of δ-CD and HP-δ-CD, with increase around 0.1 and 0.05, respectively, at

chemical shift changes as depicted in Figure 46.  
Looking at the stereochemistry of BTB, as illustrated in Figure 47, it could be that BTB exists as two 
enantiomers due to steric clashes causing slow rotation of aromatic moieties. One hypothesis is that each set 
of peaks corresponds to the situation where the enantiomers of the acid and base forms do not interconvert 
with each other. Another hypothesis is the situation where the interconversion between the acidic and basic 
forms is slow in the presence of δ-CD, but the interconversion of enantiomers is rapid. Upon addition of base 
in the end of the titration it was observed that the spectrum changed to only one set of peaks, corresponding to 
the presence of the ring-opened (basic) form of BTB. The UV-vis titration suggests that the ring-opened form 
of BTB binds preferentially as a larger absorbance was seen at 620 nm in presence of δ-CD.

2.4.4 Screening of competitive binders  
Despite the interaction between δ-CD and BTB showed to be more complex than anticipated, the change in 
absorbance at 620 nm can still serve as an indicator in a qualitative displacement assay. Therefore, the final 
experiment aimed to test the developed UV-vis assay with a handful of molecules that are known within the 
group to bind to δ-CD. The purpose was to verify the functionality of the assay as a decrease in the absorbance 
of BTB at 620 nm will indicate the competitive binders displaces the BTB that binds to δ-CD. By observing 
the UV-vis spectrum return to the unbound form of BTB, it will indicate a new supramolecular complex is 
formed. The experiment was carried out in sodium dihydrogen phosphate buffer at pH 7.40, with a 
concentration of 1 mM for the competitive binders, while δ-CD were present in a concentration of 500 µM 
and BTB in 25 µM. Two reference specta were also measured which included BTB (25µM) in buffer and BTB 
(25 µM) in buffer with δ-CD (500 µM). 
The competitive binders that were screened can be seen in Figure 48  along with their binding strengths with 
δ-CD. They included the earlier described boron-chloride cluster (Na2B12Cl12, 2) along with the boron-bromide 
cluster (Na2B12Br12, 3), which both have been reported to have very high binding affinities to δ-CD49. 
Furthermore, five bola amphiphile templates (molecules with two hydrophilic head groups in either end of a 
hydrophobic aliphatic chain) were screened. Three of them (4, 5, 6) were synthesized by Dr Andreas Erichsen. 
Compound 4 are known to bind in a 1:2 binding with δ-CD, compound 5 is known to bind in a 1:1 binding 
with δ-CD while compound 6 is known to bind in a 1:3 binding with δ-CD 12. The other two bola amphiphiles 
(7, 8) are synthesized by Dr Mads Georg Rasmussen, which both are found to bind to δ-CD in a 1:1 binding 
event. The final competitive binder that was screened was thymol (1), as it was earlier found in this study that 
it binds weakly to δ-CD in a 1:1 binding. The UV-vis spectra of BTB obtained in presence of competitive 
binders and δ-CD is seen in Figure 49.

To conclude, the developed UV-vis displacement assay can be used as a tool to aide in the discovery of 
molecules that bind to δ-CD. However, one should be aware of its limitations as minor deviations for certain 
binders were observed. In addition, screening of competitive binders that contains acid groups showed to be 
challenging. Therefore, a further optimization of the assay would involve investigating an indicator that, in 
contrast to BTB, is not pH sensitive.  
 
Summary of the UV-vis assay 
The UV-vis spectrum of BTB was found to change significantly at 620 nm with addition of excess of δ-CD at 
pH 7.0. A slight change in the BTB absorbance at pH 8.0 was observed in the presence of HP-δ-CD while no 
clear change was observed for PMe-, and SBE-δ-CD at any of the screened pH levels. The largest change in 
BTB absorbance with an excess of δ-CD was subsequently found to be at pH 7.41. The UV-vis titration showed 
different curvatures of binding isotherms, which suggests a more complex binding occurs than anticipated to 
begin with. The NMR titration data additionally suggest that δ-CD stabilizes both the acidic and the basic 
forms of BTB, albeit it seems to interact more strongly with the basic form. At last, the UV-vis assay was 
tested with a handful of competitive binders, which showed the optical range is too small to be reliable. This 
was seen as some of the competitive binder deviated from the optical range while others showed absorbances 
in between the range. It was confirmed that the presence of acid groups in the competitive binders was found 
to decrease the pH of the measured solution leading to false positive results. To assess the functionality of the 
assay, it would be necessary to repeat these measurements. In addition, future studies would involve testing a 
binder which is not a pH indicator and exhibit a larger optical change in presence of δ-CD.

3 Conclusions and further work 
In this thesis, δ-CD and its three derivatives (PMe-, HP-, SBE-δ-CD) were successfully synthesized. HP-CD 
and SBE-δ-CD were synthesized as a mixture of products where the average degree of substitution was 10.7 
and 4.7, respectively. In the future, the synthesis of modified δ-CDs could be tested in an enzyme-mediated 
DCL using modified α-CD as staring material and the dodecaborate cluster ion as template. This might enable 
the synthesis of RM-δ-CD, as conditions that allow for this product are yet to be explored.  
After having δ-CD and its three derivatives in hand, their binding properties with a range of flavor and aroma 
compounds were investigated using 1H NMR spectroscopy. The largest change in chemical shifts were 
observed when δ-CD and its three derivatives were analyzed for interactions with the two aromatic flavor 
compounds, thymol and isoeugenol. Subsequently, the binding strength between δ-, PMe-δ-, and HP-δ-CD 
and thymol were successfully determined. The association constants for a 1:1 binding system indicated in all 
three cases that at relatively weak interaction were present. The binding constant between thymol and δ-, PMe- 
δ, and HP-δ-CD were found to 33.8 M-1, 19.2 M-1, and 47.1 M-1, respectively. The binding interaction between 
thymol and SBE-δ-CD was found to be more complex than a 1:1 binding event, where the obtained data 
showed that scenarios of a 1:2 or 2:1 binding event both showed to be reasonable possibilities. In the future, 
δ-CD and its three derivatives should be analyzed for interactions with a diverse range of flavor and aroma 
compounds that have structurally different properties in order to find stronger complex formations. However, 
it would be ideal to focus on aromatic flavor and aroma compounds. In addition, the titration between SBE-δ-
CD and thymol should be carried out with a higher concentration of SBE-δ-CD to estimate the binding 
constants.  
A hydrolysis rate study was carried out with 1H NMR spectroscopy using human saliva α-amylase. It was 
discovered that δ-CD was hydrolyzed more rapidly than γ-CD while α- and ꞵ-CD were not hydrolyzed in the 
measurements. After 6 days in presence of α-amylase, α- and ꞵ-CD were still present to the same extent even 
though the activity of the enzyme was unchanged. In addition, the hydrolyzed linear α-1,4-glucans were 
determined to consist almost exclusively of maltose. It was furthermore discovered that SBE-δ-CD and HP-δ-
CD were hydrolyzed at a slower rate than δ-CD. In contrast, PMe-δ-CD was not observed to hydrolyze at all 
under the applied conditions. The results of the hydrolysis rate study suggested that δ-CD can act as a fast-
releasing carrier, while PMe-δ-CD can function as a flavor-masking carrier. The results additionally suggested 
that SBE-δ-CD and HP-δ-CD, can be used as slow flavor-releasing carriers. In the future, a hydrolysis rate 
study should be carried out in a quantitative setup where it is possible to determine hydrolysis rate constants 
for δ-CD and its three derivatives. This could for example be achieved using isolated human saliva α-amylase, 
which will enable a greater degree of control of the enzyme concentration.  
A UV-vis displacement assay was developed with the purpose of aiding in the discovery of molecules that 
bind to δ-CD and its three derivatives. The UV-vis absorption of BTB was found to change significantly at 
==PAGE:69==
 
54 
 
620 nm in presence of δ-CD and the optimal pH at which the change was most pronounced was found to be at 
pH 7.41. No clear changes for the absorbance of BTB was found in presence of the three derivatives of δ-CD. 
The binding between BTB and δ-CD was found to be more complex than anticipated, as it stabilizes two 
species in solution, most likely the basic (ring-opened) and the acidic form (ring-closed). When the assay was 
tested with a handful of competitive binders, the optical range showed to be too small to be reliable. 
Additionally, the assay was found to give false positive results with competitive binders containing acid 
groups. In the future, the assay should be tested with another indicator that exhibit a larger optical change in 
presence of δ-CD and its derivatives. Furthermore, one should be aware of not choosing a pH indicator at or 
near its pka value. Suggestions of interesting indicators to test could, for example, be crystal violet or 
solvatochromic dyes such as Reichardt’s dye or nile red.

transferred to the cuvette using an Eppendorf pipette and a UV-vis spectrum was measured. Hereafter, aliquots 
of the guest stock solution (12.5 µM-1 mM/0.5-40 eq.) were added to the cuvette and UV-vis spectra were 
recorded. After each aliquot addition, the solution in the cuvette was thoroughly mixed by pipetting up and 
down using the same pipette tip throughout the entire titration to avoid unwanted changes in the total volume.  
 
4.4.5 UV-vis screening of competitive binders 
This experiment was carried out in phosphate buffer at pH 7.40. First, a solution of phosphate buffer (50 mM 
NaH2PO4 ∙ 2H2O) in MilliQ water was prepared. A part of the phosphate buffer solution was adjusted to pH 
7.40 by the addition of 12 M NaOH. This solution was used to prepare the stock solution of the competitive 
binders (1-8) (1 mM). The stock solution of BTB (50 µM) was prepared in the other part of the phosphate 
buffer. Subsequently, this solution was adjusted to pH 7.40 by the addition of 12 M NaOH. The stock solution 
of δ-CD was prepared in 500 µM using the BTB stock solution. The competitive binders were screened by 
transferring 1300 µL of the δ-CD stock solution to the cuvette along with 1300 µL of one of the competitive 
binder stock solutions. Each measurement contained BTB (1 eq. / 25 µM), δ-CD (10 eq. / 250 µM) and 
competitive binder (20 eq. / 500 µM). Two reference spectra were also recorded that contained 1300 µL of the 
BTB stock solution and 1300 µL of either the δ-CD stock solution or the pure pH adjusted phosphate buffer 
solution. The two reference measurements contained BTB (1 eq. / 25 µM) and δ-CD (0 or 10 eq./0 or 250 µM). 
Each solution measured was thoroughly mixed in the cuvette by pipetting up and down with an Eppendorf 
pipette before the UV-vis spectra were recorded. The resulting UV-vis spectra were analyzed.

4.4.6 NMR studies (general) 
New NMR tubes and lids were used for all NMR studies carried out.  
For the flavor screenings, a lock tube containing fumaric acid (5 mM) in D2O was used to reference the 
obtained spectra as a hard water suppression with a fixed receiver gain was used to record the spectra.  
For the titration experiments, acetonitrile was used as reference. An aliquot of MeCN was added to the D2O 
that was used to prepare the stock solutions.  
For the hydrolysis experiments, the residual water signal was used to reference the obtained spectra. For each 
experiment carried out, a new batch of salivary α-amylase were used. All NMR samples were prepared in 50 
mM sodium phosphate buffered D2O at pH 7.0. The 1H NMR spectra obtained in the experiment described in 
section 4.4.7, were acquired with a water suppression method using 16 scans. The 1H NMR spectra obtained 
in the experiment described in section 4.4.8, were acquired with a water suppression method using 128 scans.    
 
Preparation of 50 mM sodium phosphate buffer in D2O at pH 7.0  
The two hydrolysis experiments were carried out in sodium phosphate buffer, which was prepared from stock 
solutions of 0.5 M Na2HPO4·2H2O and a stock solution of 0.5 M NaH2PO4·2H2O in D2O. These were 
combined and diluted with D2O to obtain a concentration of 50 mM and pH 7.0.  
 
4.4.7 Hydrolysis of α-, ꞵ-, γ-, and δ-CD 
The stock solutions of α-, ꞵ-, γ-, and δ-CD were prepared in phosphate buffer in 4 mg/mL. 125 µL of each CD 
stock solution was transferred to a clean NMR tube to obtain a concentration of 1 mg/mL of each CD in the 
tube and a 1H NMR was measured. 5 µL (10 µL/mL) of undiluted saliva was added and a new 1H NMR 
spectrum was measured. This experiment was followed every 5 minutes over a period of ~40 minutes. It was 
observed that the hydrolysis of δ-CD was slow but present, so the experiment was repeated but with a greater 
concentration of α-amylase. 20 µL (40 µL/mL) of undiluted saliva was added a new NMR tube prepared in 
the same manner as above and a 1H NMR spectrum was measured. This experiment was followed every 1 
minutes for a period of ~40 min. Hereafter, measurements were made every ~10 minutes for ~18 hours. Final 
measurements were taken after 20 h, 24.5 h and ~6 days. 50 µL (96 µL/mL) of the δ-CD stock solution (4 
mg/mL) was added after ~6 days to see whether the enzyme was still active. 
 
4.4.8 Hydrolysis of δ-CD and its three derivatives 
The CD stock solutions (δ-CD, PMe-, HP-, and SBE-δ-CD) were prepared in phosphate buffer in 1 mg/mL. 
500 µL of each CD stock solution was each transferred to clean NMR tubes and a 1H NMR spectrum was

4.4.9 NMR screening of flavor compounds  
This experiment was carried out in MilliQ water with 10% D2O. A total of nine flavor compounds (depicted 
in Figure 25) were each screened with δ-CD and its three derivatives (PMe-, HP-, and SBE-δ-CD). The flavor 
stock solutions were prepared in 1 mM in MilliQ water, while the cyclodextrin stock solutions were prepared 
in 50 mM in D2O for δ-CD, PMe-, and HP- δ-CD and in 24 mM for SBE-δ-CD.  
For each screening, the following volumes were transferred to an NMR tube:

 450 µL of a flavor stock solution and 50 µL of a CD (δ-CD, PMe-, HP-, or SBE-δ-CD) stock solution  
Each screening contained a cyclodextrin stock solution in 5.6 equivalents excess for the screenings with δ-CD, 
PMe-, and HP-δ-CD. For the screenings with SBE-δ-CD, an excess of only 2.67 equivalents was possible. 
Each sample was prepared by first transferring 50 µL of CD stock solution followed by 450 µL flavor stock 
solution to ensure proper mixing. References of CD and flavor stock solutions were also measured which

4.4.10 NMR titration experiments  
Four titration experiments were conducted, where increasing equivalents of a guest solution (δ-, PMe-, HP-, 
or SBE-δ-CD) where added to a fixed concentration of host (thymol). A stock solution of thymol (1 mM) was 
prepared in D2O. This solution was used to prepare all the guest solutions in 50 mM for δ-CD, PMe-, HP- δ-
CD, and in 20 mM for SBE-δ-CD. To acquire a 1H NMR spectrum of the host solution, 500 µL or 450 µL was 
transferred to an NMR tube for δ-CD, PMe-, HP-δ-CD or SBE-δ-CD, respectively. For each titration 
experiment, aliquots of the guest solution were added to the NMR tube to gradually increase the concentration 
of the guest. The NMR tube was thoroughly mixed before a 1H NMR spectrum was recorded. For each titration, 
the guest solutions of δ-CD, PMe-, HP-, or SBE-δ-CD were added in 0.25-35 mM, 0.25-25 mM, 0.25-28.5 
mM or 0.25-8 mM, respectively. The resulting 1H NMR spectra were analyzed and binding isotherms for 
specified proton signals were plotted by the change in chemical shifts on the y-axis and the corresponding 
concentration of guest solution on the x-axis. The binding isotherms were fitted to equations 12, (section 1.5) 
using the data analysis software OriginPro (2023b, OriginLab Corporation, Northampton, MA, US)  or 
supramolecular.org.
