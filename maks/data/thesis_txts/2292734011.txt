Abstract
Life outside the Earth is a big topic in the current era of space exploration. From
studies on life on Earth, water has been identiﬁed as a key component in both
the creation and preservation of life. Therefore the search for extraterrestrial life,
begins with the search for water. Strong indications, of subsurface oceans, existing
on some of the icy moons in the solar system, have been found. Such oceans are
particularly interesting, because of the possibility of very large amounts of liquid
water. Furthermore such oceans would be shielded from harmful radiation from space,
by the icy crust that covers the surface. In this study the detection possibilities of
such oceans, by measurements on induced magnetic ﬁelds, is studied. The aim is not
to process existing data and draw conclusions on whether oceans exist or not, but
rather a study of a method that can be used to process such data, and how it can
assist in determining the design requirements of future missions. The study focuses
on the Jovian system.

Resume
Dette studie omhandler magnet felter, induceret i himmellegemer, i vores solsystem.
Inducerede magnet felter dannes som reaktion på, at et ydre tids varierende magnet
felt, integrere med ledende materialer i legemet. Studiet tager udgangspunkt i en
model fra litteraturen, der kan bruges til at beregne det teoretisk induceret felt, for
en bestemt struktur. Ved at benytte en metode kaldet Monte Carlo, kan denne model
inverteres, så strukturen af legemet kan beregnes ud fra et induceret magnetfelt. Disse
værktøjer bruges til at studere hvordan støj, på en eventuel satellits instrumenter
og placering, vil påvirke muligheden for at detektere markante egenskaber, ved den
ledende struktur af himmellegemet. For at gøre dette udarbejdes en simulations
algoritme i Matlab, der kan bruges til at danne kunstige data set. Ved at simulere
data med forskellige støjtyper og størrelser, kan dette bruges til at analysere hvordan
disse påvirker muligheden, for at detektere forskellige typer strukturere.
Arbejdet går i dybden med to speciﬁkke legemer, der begge er måner tilhørende
Jupiter. Den første er Europa og den anden er Ganymede. Begge disse måner har
mulighed for at have et stort hav, under en overﬂade af is. Sådan et hav vil have en
stor ledeevne og derfor genere et induceret magnet felt. Det undersøges hvorledes
sådan et magnet felt, kan bruges til at bekræfte et skjult hav og eventuelt afsløre dets
tykkelse. Til sidst laves et kort studie af forskellige kredsløb parameters indvirkning,
på hvordan støj forplanter sig fra instrumenter til model parametre.

1.
Introduction
Several stellar bodies in the solar system have shown to generate induced magnetic
ﬁelds. The source of such induced ﬁelds, is the interaction of a time varying outer
magnetic ﬁeld, with conducting materials in the stellar body. Therefore these induced
ﬁelds oﬀer a unique opportunity, to study the structure of the stellar bodies. Here
the structure refers to the conductance or resistivity of the objects interior.
In the literature models of how the conducting structure aﬀects the induced ﬁeld,
have been done. This type of model can be refereed to as a forward model. Using
such a model as a starting point in this study, a method called a Monte Carlo analysis
is used to invert the forward model, into an inverse model. An inverse model, in this
context, is a model that maps the conducting structure based on measurements of
the induced ﬁeld.
The aim of the study is to use this inverse model, to investigate how noise and
errors on the instrumentation, of a potential satellite, will aﬀect the determination
of the conducting structure. To achieve this a simulation method is developed in
matlab. This simulation method can generate artiﬁcial data for a given orbit around
a stellar body. The artiﬁcial data consist of times, positions and magnetic ﬁeld
measurements. By adding noise on the artiﬁcial data, it is studied how diﬀerent noise
levels and types aﬀect the ability to detect the conducting structure. The noise on a
measurement is varied both in the form of normal distributed noise and calibration
noise. The study includes noise on the magnetic ﬁeld and position parameters.
Because this type of study is highly dependent on the actual case, two diﬀerent
cases are studied in detail. These cases are that of Europa and Ganymede. Both are
Galilean moons and orbit in the strong magnetic ﬁeld from Jupiter. These two cases
are chosen, because they both have a possible subsurface ocean. Such an ocean will
be conducting and generate strong induced ﬁelds. Even though the origin of the
induced ﬁeld is similar in both cases, they are far from identical. This is due to their
diﬀerence in size and placement in the Jovian magnetic ﬁeld. And Ganymede has
strong internal dipole ﬁeld which doesn’t seem to be the case for Europa.
The third and ﬁnal case of the study involve a more general study of, the eﬀect
of orbital parameters.
The study is highly relevant, because oceans on icy moons around Jupiter is an
open debate. Oceans on such moons could form a basis for life outside the Earth,
because water is believed to be a key ingredient.

2.
Overview
The main subject of this study are induced magnetic ﬁelds in stellar bodies. A stellar
body is in this context a planet or moon in our solar system. In order for such a
stellar body to produce an induced magnetic ﬁeld, it must contain some degree of
conducting material, which can therefore be studied through the induced ﬁeld. The
induced ﬁeld is a direct consequence of the interaction between a time varying, outer
magnetic ﬁeld and the conducting material, in the stellar body. Therefore such an
outer magnetic ﬁeld must exist for the cases investigated in the study. The outer
magnetic ﬁeld is in this study referred to, as the primary ﬁeld, but in the literature
it is also often denoted the inducing ﬁeld.
The Primary focus of the study, is to investigate how noise on measurements,
will aﬀect the ability to derive knowledge, on a stellar objects conducting structure.
This conducting structure is closely linked to the physical structure of the stellar
body. To solve this task, a forward model from the literature is used, which is based
on a three layer spherical structure. This forward model describes the induced ﬁeld,
generated by a speciﬁc structure and primary ﬁeld. Based on this forward model, an
inverse method is developed, which can calculate physical parameters, based on the
parameters for the induced ﬁeld, described in the forward model. Using this inverse
model it is possible to calculate how noise on the induced ﬁeld parameters transfers
into noise on the structural parameters.
The noise on the induced ﬁeld parameters are linked to instrumental noise by
performing a simulation. This simulation generates an artiﬁcial data set, that would
be similar to possible measurements. From this artiﬁcial data set, the induced ﬁeld
parameters are calculated. By adding noise to this simulation, the transfer from
instrumental noise, into noise on the induced ﬁeld can be mapped. This complete
the link from direct measurement noise, into noise on structural parameters.
Since the actual restrictions, that would apply to a speciﬁc mission, depends on a
large number of variables, no general results can be calculated. Instead the method
developed is demonstrated for a number of diﬀerent cases that illustrates, how it
works and main features that can be relevant.

Previous Missions

4.1.1
Quasistatic approximation
Since the subject of the study are induced ﬁelds, the ﬁelds will vary over time.
In general the electromagnetic ﬁelds from a time variable source, will not change
instantly in every point. Instead the change will propagate with the speed of light,
meaning that an observer who is far away, from the changing source, will see the
change at a delayed time. However since the speed of light is very large and the time
periods which are relevant for this study, are large, all electromagnetic ﬁelds can be
considered to be in equilibrium at every given time. This approximation is called
the Quasistatic approximation and allows the use of magneto-static equations such
as Biot-Savart’s law. [10].
4.1.2
Permeability
The permeability is a measure of a materials ability to become magnetized. Therefore
it’s a measure of how a material reacts in the presence of magnetic ﬁelds. It is deﬁned
as the level of magnetization generated by a material in response to a magnetic ﬁeld.
[10].

are discussed in the end of the report.

4.5
Monte Carlo Analysis
A Monte Carlo Analysis is a type of computer algorithm that uses large amounts
of randomly sampled models, to generate numerical results. In this study such an
analysis is used on the inverse problem, to generate model parameters from simulated
data. The beneﬁt of a Monte Carlo analysis, is that it can be used on problems that
are very complicated or near impossible to solve. It is used in a very wide amount of
ﬁelds and span a wide amount of speciﬁc algorithms.
The general concept is to generate a large amount of possible models in the
allowed model space and calculate the corresponding errors relative to measured
data. The generated models, are generated based on their likeliness, meaning that
more models are present in areas of the model space, where the corresponding data
error is low. Therefore the concentration of these models can be used to calculate
the possible model parameters and their errors. This means that the distributions
of models must satisfy a probability distribution that is transferable into the data
space. In this study the algorithm of choice is the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm,
which is a Markov chain Monte Carlo method. This method ensures that the models
have the correct distribution. In the following section, this algorithm is explained
step by step.

Step size
The step size of the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm can in principle have any value,
but it has a large eﬀect on the acceptance rate of new models. Therefore it will
aﬀect how many models the algorithm must calculate to cover the model space. This
means that if the step size, s, is very large, very few models will be accepted and it
will need many models. However if s is small, then it takes a large amount of steps to
move around in the model space. Therefore s should be chosen so the acceptance rate
is in the vicinity of 50-75 %. To ensure this, the acceptance rate can be evaluated
continuously and s can be adjusted accordingly during the analysis. Adjusting s
won’t aﬀect the distribution density.

The Forward Problem
A forward problem is in principle a model that can calculate the theoretical data, that
would be measurable, by a certain conﬁguration. In a very general way a forward
problem can be represented by the equation.
¯d = M( ¯m)
(5.1)

The forward problem represent the theoretical problem of constructing M. In this
study the model parameters, ¯m, must cover the primary ﬁeld, and the conducting
structure of the stellar object. Using these the model M must then create a theoretical
induced ﬁeld, deﬁned for every point, above the surface, of the stellar object. The
forward problem must have a unique solution which can be found through the
laws of physics. The solution to the forward problem is useful to generate data for
simulations and can also be used to check possible conﬁgurations with actual data.
However the forward problem can not be used to calculate the model parameters
directly from measured data.

the induced ﬁeld
After the induced ﬁeld has been calculated the fraction Bi
Be is known for each of the
present frequencies. The problem of determining the the conducting structure from
the induced ﬁeld, is a diﬃcult inverse problem because it is nonlinear and because
the parameters in the forward problem are coupled. The approach which has been
chosen in this study, is a Monte Carlo analysis. This is a numerical method that
is very well suited in solving problems with many coupled variables. The analysis
is performed by the use of the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm. The principle of a
Monte Carlo analysis are described in chapter 4.5.
When running the Monte Carlo method it is important to limit the span of the
physical parameters, allowed by the model. This can be done by using information
of the system, known from prior measurements, or by simply considering physical
limitations. Such limitations could be layer thickness, or restrictions on the allowed
conductance. The results of such an analysis will in many cases yield several possible
results. In many cases the extra results can be discarded as non-physical.

8.
Noise Propagation
The primary focus of the study is the instrumentation requirements needed to detect
the induced ﬁeld and to derive useful information on the conducting structure of
the stellar body. Such requirements will include the noise level on the magnetic
ﬁeld detector and the noise level on the position determination. One of the main
focuses must therefore be the propagation of noises from the detected signal, to the
ﬁnal knowledge on the conducting structure. This process can be divided into two
steps. The ﬁrst step is the propagation from direct measurements, to the model
parameter Bi from formula 5.2, for each present frequency. The second step is the
noise propagation from this model parameter, to the physical model parameters of
the actual object studied. By using these two steps in reverse it will be possible to
set requirements to the instrumentation.
8.1
Measurement noise to model noise
The measurement noise is the noise on the actual measurements and their standard
deviation can be denoted as σ(r, θ, φ, B, Bφ). One for each of the three coordinate
parameters and two for the magnetic ﬁeld strength and orientation. These ﬁve
standard deviations, will induce a noise on the parameter Bi. This parameter is
complex and contains information on both the amplitude and phase, of the induced
signal. Therefore the noise induced on Bi can be divided into to parameters σphase
and σamplitude, referring to the standard deviation of the phase and amplitude.
This noise propagation from measurements to the model parameters, is very
dependent on the actual case considered, meaning the actual orbit, time period and
structure. The parameters are also strongly coupled, meaning that the eﬀects of
noise on a single parameter can not be studied separately form the others. Therefore
a general model for the noise propagation is not derived, but instead studied for each
case individually.
The method used for this study is the simulation described in chapter 6. By
running this simulation a large number of times, a large number of values for the
phase and amplitude can be calculated. These can then be used to calculate the
standard deviation, for the noise on the amplitude and phase, for the speciﬁc case.
This requires some calculation time.

Eﬀect of calibration errors
The instrumental noise studied above is normal distributed around the correct value,
in the studied dimension. This means that if an inﬁnite number of measurements

addition to the Gaussian noise. This type of error can be directly linked to the error
in the calibration of the instrument. In the following, the eﬀect of such calibration
errors on the ﬁve diﬀerent instrumentation parameters is studied. This is done
using the same method as for the Gaussian noise but with a constant value for each

Conclusions of case A
The study of Europa has shown that two main time varying signals are present
around the moon. The study has focused on the detection of a subsurface layer of
salt water. Forward modeling have been used to show the noise limitations, on the
induced ﬁeld parameters, required to detect the layer.
A simulation has been used to model how instrumental noise in position and
detectors, will propagate into the calculation of the induced ﬁeld parameters. This
provides the link from detectors to the forward model.
Finally inverse modeling has been used to show how the noise on the induced ﬁeld
parameters, will propagate into the detection of the actual layer thickness. Especially
attention should be directed towards the noise generated in ﬁgure 9.12 b). This
shows that the relative phase must be determined rather accurately. Comparing to
ﬁgure 9.5 and 9.6 this results in the fact that the colatitude and attitude must be
well determined.

12.
Approximations Revisited
This chapter provides a small elaboration on some of the approximations in this
study.
12.1
Spherical symmetry
The conducting structure of the stellar objects were assumed to have spherical
symmetry. This approximation, was needed to make the forward model suﬃciently
simple. The approximation limits the study, to involve global eﬀects, that are present
equally around the entire stellar object. In the case of a subsurface ocean, this means
that the thickness must be equal at every position on the stellar body. However in
reality it could be very plausible that this depth, is dependent on parameters such
as the colatitude. An example of such a geometry, could be a subsurface ocean with
frozen poles or perhaps a frozen equator.
12.2
Permeability
The permeability is assumed to be equal to that of free space in the entire report.
The permeability only appears in the forward model in the calculation of the wave
numbers, ki = √iσiωµ0. These wave numbers are used in equations 5.3, 5.4 and
5.5. In reality the permeability is not equal to that of empty space. And it can be
rewritten by deﬁning the relative permeability, µ = µrµ0. Because the permeability
appears as a product with the conductivity, this relative permeability is equal to a
relative conductivity factor. Therefore the eﬀect of the relative permeability would
seem as a change in conductivity.
For materials like water and ice the relative permeability is very close to one and
the approximation is within the error margin of the conductivity. But for materials
which are highly ferromagnetic, the relative permeability can be very large. The
permeability depends both on the magnetic ﬁeld strength and frequency.

13.
Future Works
This chapter is intended to give an overview of the possible directions, further work
in the ﬁeld of study could take. The suggestions presented here, were never intended
to be covered in this study, but could be very interesting for studies in the future.
13.1
Improved forward model
The forward model used in this study has several limitations. First of all it assumes
a spherically symmetric conducting structure. This assumption limits the possible
models that can be studied. It would be very interesting to make a forward model
that, allows for other symmetries. For example in the case of a subsurface ocean, a
model with frozen poles, like on Earth, could be very interesting.
Changing the forward model would not require changes to the methods used
in the rest of the studies, since neither the simulation or the inverse model require
any limitations on the used forward model. This makes each part of the study very
general and allows for extensions in the forward model to be easily implemented.
Another possible improvement of the forward model would be to extend it to
more layers than three. This can be used to study the eﬀect of several ocean layers,
separated by ice. Such models have been suggested for Ganymede, discussed in case
B.
13.2
Flyby missions
For both case A and case B, several ﬂyby missions have been made. It would be
interesting for a future study, to use some of the data gathered during these, to
further verify the methods demonstrated in this study. From case C it was indicated
that such ﬂybys could be modelled by orbits with large e values (close to 1).

14.
Conclusion
In the beginning of the report a three layer forward model, assuming spherical
symmetry was introduced. This model was taken from [23]. Using this forward
model, a simulation method to generate data for speciﬁc orbits, around speciﬁc stellar
bodies was developed. The forward model was also used together with a Monte Carlo
analysis, to create a method for inverse modelling, from the simulated data to the
conducting structure. The simulation and inverse modeling were used on two speciﬁc
cases. In both cases the main focus was to investigate how instrumentation noise
would aﬀect the possibility to map the conducting structure of the stellar body.
The ﬁrst case considered was that of Europa, an icy moon around Jupiter. This
study focused on the detection of a subsurface ocean. The general study of this
case identiﬁed two primary signals, one with an origin in rotation of Jupiter, and
one with an origin in the orbit of Europa. The ﬁrst part of the study showed how
instrumentation noise, could be mapped into noise on the induced ﬁeld model. Then
it was investigated how the uncertainty of the induced ﬁeld model, would transfer
into noise on the ocean thickness. Because of skin eﬀects it was clear that for oceans
thicker than approximately 100 km, only the slow varying primary signals would
penetrate deep enough to resolve the thickness.
In the second case the stellar object was Ganymede. Here the focus was also a
subsurface ocean, but this time the ocean could possibly be much deeper and covered
by a very thick layer of ice. Also the magnetic ﬁelds around Ganymede is much
diﬀerent, compared to Europa. The case study shows that one primary ﬁeld, that
is easily detectable, is present. It varies with a period similar to the orbit period.
The skin depth can penetrate oceans to a depth of 400 km, so below this value the
thickness is resolvable. The Monte Carlo analysis showed that two possible solutions
could exist, but that only one would be physically possible. Therefore both the ocean
thickness and the thickness of the top layer, can be determined by measuring the
induced ﬁeld relative to the primary signal.
In the end of the report, the eﬀect of orbital parameters was studied. This
study showed that with increasing orbital radius, the eﬀect of the noise level would
also increase. This is in some sense trivial as increased radius result in weaker
induced ﬁelds. A similar result was seen for eccentricity, as increased eccentricity
results in a larger mean radius. The results from high eccentricity are however good
approximations to ﬂybys. As a last result from the orbital parameters study, the
eﬀect of inclination was studied. From this study it was seen that the methods used
had an optimal inclination of roughly 60◦, for circular orbits.

%% Calculates the Bi constant in units of the Be constant.
% sigma must be a vector with 3 conductivities
% omega must be a scalar with the angular frequency of the external
% field
% r must be a vector with tree radiee following the syntax ([r1,r2,r3]
% with r1>r2>r3>0
function [Bi] = BidBeCal(sigma,omega,r,n,mu)
% Detects the number of time varing signals
Steps=length(omega);
Bi=zeros(Steps,1);
for i=1:Steps
% wave numbers
k=sqrt(j*sigma*omega(i)*mu);
% Bessel functions
[F1_r3k2,F2_r3k2,F1n_r3k2,F2n_r3k2] = Bessel(r(3)*k(2),n);
% r3k2
[F1_r3k3,F2_r3k3,F1n_r3k3,F2n_r3k3] = Bessel(r(3)*k(3),n);
% r3k3
[F1_r2k1,F2_r2k1,F1n_r2k1,F2n_r2k1] = Bessel(r(2)*k(1),n);
% r2k1
[F1_r2k2,F2_r2k2,F1n_r2k2,F2n_r2k2] = Bessel(r(2)*k(2),n);
% r2k2
[F1_r1k1,F2_r1k1,F1n_r1k1,F2n_r1k1] = Bessel(r(1)*k(1),n);
% r3k1
% G/E
GE=F1_r3k2/F2_r3k2*(F1n_r3k2-F1n_r3k3)/(F1n_r3k3-F2n_r3k2);
% D/C
DC=F1_r2k1/F2_r2k1*(F1n_r2k2-F1n_r2k1+F2_r2k2/F1_r2k2*GE*(F2n_r2k2-...
F1n_r2k1))/(F2n_r2k1-F1n_r2k2+F2_r2k2/F1_r2k2*GE*...
(F2n_r2k1-F2n_r2k2));
% Bi/Be
Bi(i)=n/(n+1)*(F1n_r1k1-n-1+F2_r1k1/F1_r1k1*DC*(F2n_r1k1-n-1))/...
(F1n_r1k1+n+F2_r1k1/F1_r1k1*DC*(F2n_r1k1+n));
Bi(i)=(abs(0.5-Bi(i)))*exp(j*(angle(Bi(i))-pi));
end

function [output] = SinusFit(t,y,T)
% Range of y
yRange = (max(y)- min(y));
% Estimate of phase
ym = mean(y);
% Define the function to fit
fitFunc = @(b,x)
b(1).*(sin(2*pi*x./T + 2*pi/b(2))) + b(3);

% Set the number of function evaluations
options = optimset('MaxFunEvals', 10000);
% Minimise Least-Square
s = fminsearch(leastSqure, [yRange; -1;
ym],options);
% Output strength and phase
