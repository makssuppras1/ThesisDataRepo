Abstract
Network devices are integral to the success of high speed between networks. This has
mainly been achieved due to the ability of network devices to process a large amount
of packets per second. An important role which is located in the control plane of
each device is the packet parsing. Although space consumption on chips for packet
parsing is minor, the more flexibility is given to packet parsing the more is the risk
in hindering the device from reaching its full potential.
In the current thesis, an effort is made to design, implement and synthesise a
fixed and programmable packet parser written in VHDL language for FPGAs that
are reflecting today’s standards. Moreover the trade-offs will be analyzed between
the different implementations as well as an evaluation will be conducted for each
architecture in the following areas: operating frequency, area consumption of chip,
performance.
The proposed designs demonstrate achievable speeds of more than
400MHz using less than 1% of the total chip area.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Since the commercialization of the Internet back in the late 1980s, the need for higher
speeds was unquestionably one of the pivotal issues concerning the Internet commu-
nity. However, in comparison to previous years, the current performance of networks
has been accelerated while at the same time a variety of protocols are flooding the
Internet.
Having said that, one of the most crucial components that drove this evolution to
come to fruition is the ever-developing infrastructure, and more precisely the devices
that are able to route traffic (packets) throughout the network at very high speeds.
1.1
Packet Parsing
Despite the diversity in terms of functionality that exists among network devices,
a fundamental process that all devices share, is the processing of header fields per
packet e.g. in order to make a forwarding decision. This requires each device to
identify the suitable header(s) related to the OSI layer they operate and extract the
relevant fields.
The above-mentioned procedure is defined as packet parsing and it can be to
a large degree challenging when performed by devices located closer to the access
network where a greater amount of protocols must be processed by a single device.
What’s more, packet parsing will always be a subject under investigation as hardware
evolves and needs to be addressed properly in order to avoid situations like being the
critical path in the design and thus limiting the system to reach its full potential.
1.2
Objectives
Before delving into the theory, it is important to give an insight into the objectives
of this project. First of all, an architecture for a fixed parser (coded at compile time)
will be designed that is compliant with the Comcores ApS requirements and later on
tested in Vivado Suite for FPGA use. The fixed parser should be able to process data
in a streaming manner which means without having to wait for the whole packet to
be received. The streaming concept is further analyzed in 2.2.
==PAGE:14==
2
1 Introduction
With regard to performance requirements, the goal is to achieve an operating
clock frequency around 400MHz while at the same time power and space on-chip
consumption should be kept at reasonable levels. This means that by instantiating
in a switch a single parser instance, it should be able to process ”400 * data-width”
bits/second. Of course, by generating multiple instances of the parser and processing
packets in parallel, we can have an aggregated throughput of ”instances * frequency
* data-width” bits/second.
The second and more challenging objective is to design and implement a pro-
grammable parser. By the term programmable, it is implied that the component
should be able to support new header formats on run time, namely without needing
to shut down the FPGA. Similar to the fixed parser, the programmable should be
capable of processing data without needing to store the whole packet first. Lastly, as
far as performance is concerned, the initial goal was to reach 300MHz.
1.3
Related work
Most related researches on this field especially the recent ones are reporting remark-
ably high speeds [BPK16; Cab+18; Koz+10]. However, most of these works imply a
”fixed” implementation. In other words, a predefined parse graph 2.1 is used and if
at some point new headers need to be supported into the system, re-coding while the
FPGA is turned off should take place, thus jeopardizing the network performance.
In addition as discussed in 2.4, the ways for implementing a packet parser use
either a software or hardware approach. By using a software approach, the parallel
assumption of headers is cumbersome in comparison to hardware.
Having said that, the architecture proposed by Kozanitis et al. [Koz+10] for a
programmable parser reports an achievable line rate at up to 40Gbps. Packets are
first stored in a memory in a non-streaming manner and a TCAM is used to store
keywords such as Ethertypes along with data to identify packet formats and process
associated data. Moreover, a presumption of headers is used so as to process more
than one header in one clock cycle.
Gibb et al. [Gib+13] conduct a thorough analysis to indicate the key differences
and the trade-offs between fixed and programmable parsers. Furthermore, they pro-
pose the principles behind the designs of these two cases. While the details of the
sub-blocks are not disclosed, the generic concept that have been used is similar to
the present study. Nevertheless, on the grounds that TCAM consumes a lot of power
and generates a high level of heat, it is replaced by a LUT 4.5.
While not directly related to our work, other approaches include domain-specific
languages such as P4 [Cab+18; BPK16] for mapping logic to HDL design and the use
of HLS systems to translate software logic into hardware.

2.1
Parsing methods
A prerequisite when designing a parser is to define a parse graph. Parse graphs as
illustrated in figure 2.1 are representations of the system’s supported headers with a
successive order from top to bottom as observed by network devices. Parse graphs
are acyclic while each node represents a protocol (header located in a packet) and
arrows indicate the next possible header encounter of the system. That being said,
parsers are classified into:
• Fixed,
• Programmable
Fixed parsers consider a single parse graph defined when designed, thus flexibility,
namely moving from figure 2.1a to 2.1b, is not supported when new headers need to
be considered. In fact, for the parser to be in compliance with a new parse graph
that involves these new headers, a new design at compile time must be determined.
In figure 2.1c a big union of graphs is depicted while the highlighted protocols are
used in the implementation of the fixed parser analyzed in chapter 3.
Regarding the programmable option, the architecture should be engineered in
such a way so as new parse graphs can be defined at run-time. One way to carry this
through is by combining an FSM style approach along with a memory element where
instructions can be loaded and processed by the FSM. Figure 2.1c reflects the parse
graph defined for the programmable parser in chapter 4.
2.2

Two types of operations, as defined in [Gib+13], can be found in parsers. The first
one is the streaming operation that takes place as soon as the packet is being received
by the system. Typically a small circular or shift buffer can be used to store data
temporarily until header identification and field extraction is completed.
On the
other hand, in a non-streaming procedure, after the header-related data is captured

2.3
Hardware Anatomy
In today’s world, ASIC’s and FPGA’s chips are used predominantly for implementing
hardware. Both technologies are integrated circuits configured by designers to per-
form certain tasks. However, as the name field-programmable gate array implies, the
main difference between those two is that FPGA’s can be reprogrammed after man-
ufacture as they consist of programmable switches that interconnect cells across the
chip. This provides the flexibility to design prototypes and reconfigure a particular
design that can be used later for application-specific chip implementation (ASIC’s).
Although ASICs have their advantages as well, such as achieving better performance
and lower power consumption, some extra benefits of using FPGA’s over ASIC’s are
listed below [Bar05]:
• Quicker time-to-market
• Perfect for smaller designs
• Remodification diminishes risk, cost e.g when new revisions are needed or when
flaws are identified in the implementation
==PAGE:20==
8
2 Theory
• Reduced NRE (non-recurring costs) costs
• Suitable for applications including digital signal processing, software-defined
radio, aerospace and defense systems, medical imaging, computer vision, speech
recognition, cryptography, bio-informatics, computer hardware emulation and
a growing range of other areas.
• Ideally for research and testing.
It is also worth mentioning that apart from FPGA’s and ASIC’s, processors can
be programmed using some software to achieve the aimed functionality. Nonetheless,
the main drawbacks of using processors are that they consume a greater amount of
power and execute their tasks in a sequential manner as compare to FPGA’s and
ASIC’s where multiple processes can be executed in parallel [CH99].
2.4
On Chip Implementation
Currently, a large amount of engineers use HDL languages such as VHDL and Verilog
for Electronic Design Automation(EDA) logic synthesis. On the other side, engineers
alos have the ability to use High level Synthesis (HLS) tools. HLS is the transforma-
tion of a description of a particular design to an RTL list and thus to hardware. The
abstractions that can be used to describe a design varies. As a result, engineers can
describe algorithms or FSM’s in a more abstract way by using software languages
like C, C++ along with libraries such as SystemC [Ass+12] or Xilinx Vivado’s HLS
[Xil14] that convert a C specification into an RTL level.
The above-mentioned methods have their pros and cons. One of the main ad-
vantages of HDL languages over HLS is the flexibility to modify the system at the
Register Transfer Level for optimization purposes. Further analysis between the pros
and cons among the different techniques can be found in [Bai15; Pel+16].
2.5
Project tools
By having analyzed the existing options and technologies in 2.3 and 2.4 that can be
used for packet parsing, in this subsection, a brief account is given as to which tools
have been used to achieve the predefined in 1.2 objectives.
Firstly, VHDL code is generated that describes the hardware behavior of each of
the deployed architectures. The synthesis and all analysis deduction related to timing
,area on chip and power consumption have been conducted through Vivado Design
Suite tool.
Moreover, the Vunit framework [Asp14] has been used for test verification in
conjunction with ModelSim [Cor14] that provides a GUI environment. The afore-
mentioned frameworks are further described in chapter 5.

Architecture
Starting from the fixed parser’s architecture as illustrated in 3.1, it can be observed
that it is constituted by four sub-blocks. The first sub-block, where the identification

header is recognized, two internal multiplexers are involved.

The first multiplexer is responsible for constructing the descriptor. More specifi-
cally, the rest of the system can identify which headers have been found in the packet
by looking at the raised bits of the descriptor. Each bit position corresponds to a
header, is predefined in table 3.5 and should be known to the rest of the system.
The second internal multiplexer is responsible for activating the ”Marker Update”

Lastly, the ”Bytes and cell” procedure is accountable for enumerating the bytes

3.4
Identification Block FSM
As mentioned, the core functionality of the system is taking place in the Identification
Block in an FSM style.
The FSM as illustrated in figure 3.2 consists of 4 states.
Apart from its main functionality, it manipulates the rest sub-blocks with appropriate
control signals.
Figure 3.2: Finite State Machine
3.4.1
IDLE
The first stage is responsible for sensing the beginning of a valid packet in order to
transit to the CORE HEADERS state for packet investigation. In case this criterion
has not met the system remains in this state. Additionally, the system is always
transitioning to IDLE regardless of the state of play if a new packet is to be examined
or an error has occurred.
==PAGE:26==
14
3 Fixed Parser
3.4.2
CORE HEADERS
Upon entering this state the parser always assumes that the Ethernet header exists
and tries to identify the next header based on the Ethertype field located in the
Ethernet header.
In case one of the predefined headers is recognized, the system
transits to FRER state while the respective tag is constructed for the descriptor.
Furthermore, if none of the predefined headers is found, only the default Ethernet
tag is created for the descriptor and the system moves to WAIT_FOR_EOP stage.
3.4.3
FRER
The current state is introduced in order to check whether the FRER field exists in the
packet. This can only happen if the VLAN header is previously found in the packet.
In a different case, the system transits to WAIT_FOR_EOP without waiting for
potential FRER fields to be received. In general, the VLAN fields are shimmed in
the Ethernet fields between the source mac address and the Etherype, thus headers
encapsulated in VLAN can be processed here.
3.4.4
WAIT_FOR_EOP
When the whole procedure is done, the system will be located in this state and remain
here for two clock cycles. This is due to delay added to ensure that all information is
passed into the hvct_o output.
Based on the configured generic “MODE” in 3.2, flags are raised appropriately
and inform the rest of the system about the process completion. Regardless of the
MODE and due to the added feature for counting both bytes and cells of a packet,
the system must wait until the last byte is received for raising the corresponding flag
that indicates the completion of the counting procedure to transit to IDLE again.
3.5
Signal Timing Diagram
Before starting the timing analysis it must be said that both inputs and outputs are
attached to registers. The reason for placing registers on the inputs is to ensure that
by instantiating the parser component multiple times in a switch, logic primitives
that may occur on the path between adjacent components would not affect timing,
namely leading to a situation where propagation delay is longer. On the other hand,
outputs retrieve the values from their internal registers only when their associated
flags are driven high in order to ensure stability.
Having said that, in 3.4 and 3.3, the timing diagram of the parser is illustrated
for both cases of the generic MODE in case DATA_WIDTH is 8 bytes.
In 3.3, when the system receives a new SOP signal along with data, it needs t1
time to calculate where the payload starts and raise the pay_ptr_valid_o flag. T1
has a fixed delay of 4 clock cycles plus the varied time to process the underlying

4.1
Introduction
The following chapter is focused on the detailed implementation of the programmable
parser. Moreover, the main reasons behind the modifications (transitions) undergone
by the architecture in reaching its final state are also highlighted and reviewed in
section 4.2.
Similar to the fixed parser, the processing of packets follow a streaming manner
and the targeted supported parse graph is the big union illustrated in 2.1c. This
means that all depicted headers should be able to be identified, and as described in
2.1, both dynamic addition and subtraction of new and old headers in the parse graph
tree should be feasible.
The key factor to the previous statement is to deploy techniques similar to in-
struction decoding as described in [Koz+10; Gib+13]. More specifically in our case,
an FSM is used in conjunction with a memory component and based on the memory
inputs if a match is found appropriate data are sent back to the FSM, driving the sys-
tem to perform the appropriate updates on its registers. In this way, while the FPGA
is running, new data (Instructions) can be loaded to the memory for supporting new
headers without compromising the system’s performance.
4.2
Implementation phases
As far as the temporary storage of input data is concerned, analogous techniques
to fixed parser were attempted for the programmable one. Although in the former,
the same buffer is used to temporarily store data for header identification and field
extraction, this is not the case for the latter. In fact, two cascaded shift buffers were
used. As depicted in 4.1, data enters the fist buffer in order for headers to be identified
and by an overflow event, up to 8 bytes are passed to the next one. The reason for
using a shift instead of circular buffers is to narrow down inter-wiring connectivity at
the connection points between the buffers when bytes are conveyed.
==PAGE:36==
24
4 Programmable parser
Figure 4.1: Shift buffers
Furthermore, the identification buffer is connected to a memory and provides to
that memory with a ”key”. If a match is found, appropriate data are sent back e.g
to build a descriptor that indicates existing headers in the packet. The length of the
identification buffer depends on the clock cycles needed to match fields in memory,
extract and process data for header identification before overflow happens. On the
other hand, the size of the extraction buffer should be as in the fixed parser’s case
equal to the path with the longest header fields.
4.2.1
Identification buffer variations
With regard to memories used, data can be output every other clock cycle by the time
an address is given. Therefore the ideal case would be to provide an address every
clock cycle. Nonetheless, until the final step of the programmable implementation,
this was not possible on the grounds that the memory was holding information related
to the next ”key” field encounter.
That being said and without considering MPLS headers, a 4*8 bytes size window
was needed in case 2 VLAN were located in a packet. Despite being fully functional,
the component could only achieve around 125MHz. Amelioratory approaches were to
either change the processing logic or pipeline the system. The latter requires at least
two pipeline stages in order to achieve a frequency close to 300 MHz while at the same
time scalability was limited. Were the two pipeline stages implemented, 4 clock cycles
would be needed to provide an address to the memory-making the buffer up to 3 times
bigger. This would have led to a more complex design with higher power consumption
since large multiplexers would have been introduced to keep track of data in every 8
bytes window, compromising the initial statement for better performance.

4.3
Top level Architecture
The final top level architecture for the reprogrammable parser is illustrated in 4.2.
The Programmable logic entity is the core component designed in an FSM style
that constructs the desirable outputs in conjunction with a RAM that contains infor-
mation for the IPV4,6 protocol fields and a LUT responsible for storing information
related to different ethertypes or key fields of 16 bits wide.
As far as the RAM component is concerned, it is a dual-port Block RAM. Each
address holds information about the next protocol after IPv4,6 along with its length
and a unique numeric value that is assigned to the descriptor. Based on [Rey02] a
subsequent header defined in the protocol field has a unique value between 0 and 255.
Therefore, to support all of them, a RAM of 255 depth has been instantiated and by
storing them in incremental order, this integer value can be used as an address to
access the data.
Moving to the LUT, a component that so far has not been discussed, is simply a
match table consisting of a 2 dimensional-array of registers grouped together in the
FPGA and mimics the behavior of TCAM. One of the main reasons for using it to
store data is the expedited data access and retrieval. In fact, the whole LUT table can
be examined in one clock cycle by comparing stored ethertype fields with an input. On
the other hand, increased area and power consumption are some of the disadvantages
of implementing this component. A key factor to keep the aforementioned drawbacks
at reasonable levels is to minimize the depth and width of the LUT table.
Lastly, for data entries, VHDL records are used. Records are akin to structures in
C for signal clustering. In 4.2 the record format for each entry in RAM and LUT is
illustrated. The only difference between the two formats is the added ethertype field
that is used for comparison in the LUT.

4.6.2.1
SOP
Similarly to the fixed implementation, the initial stage is responsible for commencing
the parsing when a valid packet along with the SOP signal is received. In a different
case, the system maintains this state. Note also that regardless of the state the system
might be at a given timestamp, if an error occurs, the process is terminated forcing
the system to transit to this state.
4.6.2.2
CORE HEADERS
In the current state, the system waits for the ethertype to be received without consid-
ering VLANs. Upon retrieval of the ethertype value, if it does not match MPLS, the
value of which is stored internally and is always accessible by the FSM, it is routed to
the LUT for identification while the system transits to the ASSUME_INNER_VLAN
state. Otherwise, a transition to the MPLS_LABELS_1_2 occurs for determining
the number of stacks in the packet.
4.6.2.3
ASSUME_INNER_VLAN
In the following state, it is presumed that the previously encountered ethertype is
actually the TPID field of an inner VLAN, thus the new location of the ethertype
field is sent to the LUT for inspection. Given that no error occurs, the system transits
in the next clock cycle to the ETHERTYPE state.
4.6.2.4
ETHERTYPE
By the time this state is reached, data from the LUT are present at the Programmable
logic entity. Based on that, the system is able to distinguish whether an outer or
inner VLAN exists and appropriate control signals are manipulated so as to acquire
the location of the next key (IP) and feed it to BRAM if necessary. In case of an
outer VLAN the shifted location of the ethertype field is sent for identification in the
LUT as well. The possible transitions from this state are listed below:
• OUTER VLAN, when an outer vlan is found,
• INNER VLAN, when an inner vlan is found,
• MEMORY HEADERS, when an IP header is found and further identification
is required,
• EOP, when an error occurs or an ethertype is found and further identification
is not required,

4.6.2.5
MPLS_1 _2
All MPLS related states are introduced to handle the uniqueness of this header.
In the current state, it is examined based on the stack bit in the MPLS header
whether it is constituted by one or two stacks. If that’s the case, a transition to
the IPV4_IPV6_INQUIRY is taking place while the information regarding header
length and descriptor’s value, otherwise retrieved from the LUT table, is constructed.
In a different case, a transition to MPLS_3 _4 is taking place.
4.6.2.6
MPLS_3 _4
Similar to the previous state, it is examined whether three or four stacks comprise
the MPLS header. Since more stacks are not supported the process is terminated if
that’s the case, otherwise the system transits to IPV4_IPV6_INQUIRY.
4.6.2.7
IPV4_IPV6_INQUIRY
Owing to the fact that no information is present in the MPLS header for the subse-
quent header, this state is introduced to extract that information from the Version
field located in IPv4,6 (A.1c, A.1d). In addition, the length of IPv4 and IPv6 are not
alike. Therefore control signals are deployed as with the VLAN case to acquire the
location of the next key responsible for the next header identification. Lastly, this
key is fed to the memory and the system transits to MEMORY_HEADERS state.
4.6.2.8
MEMORY_HEADERS
At this point, the system has a clearer view about headers up until the header after
the IP stack. If the process has not already been terminated by the en_of_search
in 4.2, it is implied that an encapsulated header follows (e.g NVGRE). Thus the
appropriate field is sent to the LUT for identification and the system transits to
IDENTIFY_ENCAPSULATION state. It is worth mentioning that the key-field is
located either in the GRE protocol type field or UPD’ destination port field (A.1b,
A.1a), and due to its width(16 bits), it is stored in the LUT table.
4.6.2.9
IDENTIFY_ENCAPSULATION
This penultimate state of the FSM is introduced to add a delay of one clock cycle
required to recover data related to the encapsulation header from the LUT and process
them before moving to the EOF state.
4.6.2.10
EOF
Lastly, in the EOP state, the rest system is informed about the completion of the
parsing.

all outputs are valid. T1 range is between 6 to 13 clock cycles and depends on the
amount of identified headers.

Analysis of outputs
Akin to the fixed implementation, the same outputs are necessary for characterizing
the packet. Starting from the hvctr_o, it should be large enough to store the data
for the longest possible header fields. Although a shift buffer is used and the buffer
is constructed in a different manner compare to the fixed parser, the same alignment
as in figure 3.7 is attained when the parsing is ready on the grounds that incoming
data are shifted in the buffer from right to left. An example of how the shift buffer
with 24 bytes size is constructed while data are received, is illustrated in figure 4.9.
Functions for extracting specific fields are not defined due to the great amount of
fields. In comparison with the tailored functions in the fixed parser however, there
is a need for a generic function that based on dynamic inputs e.g from a memory,
different fields can be extracted. The arising problem of the previous statement is
that in hardware, for dynamic value assignment between different clusters of registers,
exhaustive inter-wiring is required.
As far as the pay_ptr_o is concerned, the value that would be present at the
completion of the process is similar to the fixed parser, the accumulated number of
all identified header’s length (table A.1).

CHAPTER6
Conclusion and
future work
FPGAs, primarily owing to their prototyping nature, gave an opportunity to design
and study different parsing blocks for network devices. As a result, two architectures
have been developed and anatomized that cover the existing principles of parsing.
Taking the synthesis results into consideration, it is confirmed that both implementa-
tions in modern FPGA’s, that are suitable for large architectures, can achieve close
to 500 and 400MHz for the fixed and programmable parser respectively. This means
that by instantiating a parsing block between input/output ports, a speed up to
42Gbps and 25Gbps can be achieved respectively.
Each of these implementations has its own set of trade-offs as described in 2.1.
The biggest advantage of the programmable over the fixed parser is the ability to
define and deal with nodes in the parse graph 2.1c at compile time. This leads to
higher computational logic between registers and thus making the path delay longer
as compare to the fixed parser. The research reveals also the impact our design has on
different FPGAs and discloses that the overall chip area is dominated by the circular
buffer for fixed parsers and both the shift buffer of the header vector and LUT for
programmable parsers.
While fixed parser’s implementation is straightforward since the fields subjected to
investigation are always known to the designer and with proper pipelining maximum
speeds can be reached, there is still room for improvement in the proposed architecture
for the programmable parser. For example, include a separate dynamic identification
process to existing and future-emerging nonuniform headers such as MPLS without
the need to define them at design time and with the help of control signals driven by
a memory to disable or enable that feature.
Another issue that has been identified through this research lies in the extraction
of fields. Particularly, all fields of an identified header are stored in the header vector,
however, in reality only a few are later used by the rest of the system. By storing
only the necessary fields the buffer’s size accommodating the header vector can be
shrunk, thus facilitating the field extraction.
