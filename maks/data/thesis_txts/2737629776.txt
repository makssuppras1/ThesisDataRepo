Abstract
This thesis project aims to improve user interaction with the Ecorustics rental platform by using AI
models to suggest improvements to rental listings.
The project involves a comprehensive approach
that encompasses data collection from rental listings, the training or ﬁne-tuning of AI models on this
data, and utilizing the models to generate improvement suggestions to platform users. While existing
literature predominantly concentrates on the application of Natural Language Processing (NLP) to
customer reviews, one paper[1] aligns closely with our approach. This particular study investigated the
typical aspects found in descriptions, utilizing statistical models to measure their impact on performance
across diverse markets.
The project comprises two major components. The ﬁrst will be utilizing NLP models to extract fea-
tures from listing descriptions such as topics and grammar. The second involves exploring features
extracted from listing thumbnails through methods such as computer vision and using AI models. By
incorporating features from textual descriptions, images, or other tabular data, a model will be trained
to evaluate the performance of rental listings. The model should output a prediction of the performance
of a listing. Post-training, the model will be employed to assess the impact of diﬀerent enhancements
(e.g., grammar correction, textual adjustments, image modiﬁcations) on the overall score. Generative
AI models will be used to explore diﬀerent improvements. These improvements will be used to prioritize
enhancement suggestions based on their potential impact. This will be utilized to generate personalized
suggestions meant to be sent to the listing host, proposing speciﬁc improvements to elevate the listings
performance.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Ecorustics
Ecorustics1 is an online marketplace for renting long- or short-term accommodations in Portugal. The
focus of Ecorustics is to revitalize the rural areas of Portugal and is described as ‘an initiative that aims
at transforming the abandoned and remote villages in Portugal into hubs for remote working, recreation
activities and revived spaces for sustainable living’2. Stays are, therefore, primarily oﬀered in less densely
populated areas around Portugal. There are currently around 37 active accommodations available for
rent and more than 100 if inactive accommodations are included. The inactive accommodations are in
a hidden part of the website since they are not being updated or taken care of3. The hosts of inactive
accommodations are informed of this.
The main alternatives to Ecorustics are Booking.com4 and Airbnb5, which oﬀer stays around the world
including Portugal. However, the higher commissions of these platforms can make it unattractive for
hosts in rural areas, where demand is generally lower.
1.2
Problem Statement
A typical way to promote a rental platform is to present the most attractive stay in banner ads or clips.
It is, therefore, in the best interest of the platform to ensure the listings are as appealing to customers
as possible.
However, not all hosts are good at presenting their rentals, and it is not always clear
what would grab a potential client’s attention. It, therefore, makes sense to explore the possibility of
enhancing hosts’ listings either automatically or through suggestions. Furthermore, some hosts can be
careless with their listings. A small percentage does not update the listening availability or description.
The pictures are either bad quality or not enough to provide an idea of the space. Fixing this issues is a
full time job. The alternative is to contact the hosts with suggestions to improve their listening which
is also very time consuming.
The aim of this project is to create personalized suggestions for hosts on how to improve their listings.
By utilizing AI models, we can ﬁnd trends in listings that do well and then present those trends to
hosts of less attractive rentals in a user-digestible way.
1.3
Research Objectives
The main objective of the project is to explore diﬀerent areas of AI and AI technologies with the goal
of trying to apply such techniques on our use case of rental platform listings. More speciﬁcally, We will
address the following research objectives:

1.4
Scope and Limitation
In this project, we are mainly focusing on sharing economy type of rentals. This means we will not be
exploring traditional hotel listings. For AI models, we will mostly be using existing pre-trained models
and not developing any models from scratch. Moreover, while the Ecorustics platform is specialized for
rural accommodations in Portugal, we explore all types of accumulations mainly English listings. So,
our results might not generalize well to the listings on the platform. Finally, time and computational
resources limit the number of trained models and experiments.
1.5
Methodology Overview
The initial part of our project involves gathering a dataset. The data from Ecorustics is not enough as
the platform is still new and has fewer users. The dataset will be selected based on its total number of
listings and the features available for exploration. Afterwards, the dataset will be analysed to understand
the problem better and identify keys features to be used.
The second part of the project involves the usage of AI models on chosen features. This will include
extracting features using these models as well as training or ﬁne-tunning models for feature extraction.
In this part of the project, we will iterate through multiple models by evaluating the results of one
model to create a better one in the next iteration.
The ﬁnal part of the project involves the creation of listings’ improvement pipeline. In this pipeline,
we use generative AI models with our designed queries to modify the listing. Afterwards, we will use
previously trained models to evaluate the performance of modiﬁed listings. Depending on the results,
either we will accept the query as a recommendation or reject it. Finally, we evaluate the eﬃcacy of
the pipeline against human feedback collected through a survey.

2.1
Rental Platform Listing Improvement
Existing literature predominantly concentrates on applying Natural Language Processing (NLP) to cus-
tomer reviews. In such studies, customer reviews are analysed and used to predict listings performance
[2]. A noteworthy paper[1] aligns closely with our approach of using listing to predict its performance.
This study investigated the typical aspects or topics found in the descriptions. There aspects are then
used to create statistical models. This helps to measure the impact of the topic on performance of
listing across diﬀerent markets.
Leveraging ChatGPT and Digital Marketing for Enhanced Customer Engagement in the Hotel Indus-
try[3] is another interesting paper that looks into how generative AI models like ChatGPT can be used
to enhance customer engagement within the hotel industry. This study shows that the integration of
AI in the hotel industry has favorable inﬂuence on customer interactions, availability, eﬃciency and
cost-eﬀectiveness.
The paper Optimization Method of Advertising Texts Based on Generative Models[3] has a close ap-
proach to our project. While the paper applies AI models primarily on social networks advertisements,
the methodology is similar. The paper uses a large corpus of successful advertising text for training the
model to general successful texts. The paper also shows successful results through increase of view-rates
with a lower average cost per click.
2.2
NLP
Natural language processing is essential for analysing textual data. The ﬁeld initially relied on statis-
tical models, symbolic AI, and rule-based systems. Nowadays, the ﬁeld heavily utilises deep learning.
Most state-of-the-art NLP systems today rely on the transformer architecture, especially it’s scalability
properties, which allow it to train billions of parameters. Most large models depend on a pre-training
step where the model is trained on an extensive corpus of data. Afterwards, the model is ﬁne-tuned for
speciﬁc tasks like sentiment analysis, translation, etc.
2.3
Transformer
Transformers are the newest type of architecture that had a huge success. They are the main building
block of large language models like ChatGPT3 .

2.3.1
BERT/Distilbert
BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) is an encoder-only model based on
the transformer architecture.
It leverages the attention mechanism to achieve state-of-the-art per-
formance in various NLP tasks.
It was developed by Google and introduced in the paper BERT:
Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding[5].
==PAGE:11==
2.3 Transformer
5
The following diagram provides an overview of the BERT model:
Figure 2.2: BERT Architecture[5]
From the ﬁgure, we can see that BERT only uses the encoder component of the transformer. With the
110M parameters model, 12 layers of encoders are stacked to create BERT. The key feature of encoder-
based models is that the model considers the whole context around a word to derive its meaning. BERT
processes the text input from both left-to-right and right-to-left, which provides a good understanding
of the context of each word. Hence, it is called bidirectional.
In addition to this, BERT is pre-trained on a large corpus of text. The pre-training is done using two
unsupervised learning tasks:
• Masked Language Modelling (MLM): the main idea is to randomly mask a word in a text and
train the model to predict the masked word
• Next Sentence Prediction (NSP): the main idea is to provide the model with two sentences A and
B and train it to predict if B follows A.
After pre-training, the model is capable of transferring its knowledge to a multitude of speciﬁc tasks.
This allows the ﬁne-tuning step to require less training data and time.
BERT is capable of performing text classiﬁcation tasks with Fine-Tuning. This requires the addition
of a classiﬁcation layer on top of the BERT output.
DistilBERT is a distilled version of BERT. The model is introduced by Hugging Face. It is 40% smaller
and 60% faster version of BERT that maintains 97% of the performance. DistilBERT is also an encoder-
based transformer, but it has fewer layers than BERT. It is trained with a special knowledge distillation
method where BERT is considered a teacher, and DistilBERT is considered a student. The student’s
loss function is inﬂuenced by the similarity between the embeddings produced by the student and the
teacher.

2.3.2
Gemma
Gemma is a family of recently open-sourced large language models. It is a model developed by Google
and released in March of 2024. The model is more similar to the GPT family of models. It is also
based on the transformer architecture. Unlike BERT, Gemma is a decoder-based model instead of
an encoder-based model. This allows the model to have generative properties like GPT for question
answering and content creation.
The following diagram gives an overview of the architecture of Gemma:
Figure 2.3: Gemma Architecture[6]
From the diagram, we can see that the architecture is similar to that of BERT. The main diﬀerence is
that the transformer layers are decoder-based. With decoder-based models, the attention mechanism
is modiﬁed to use a mask. This mask ensures that only the left context is being used. Unlike encoder
models that use both right and left context, decoder models can only use the previous words in a
sequence to gain context. This makes decoder models especially good at predicting the next token and,
by extension, content generation.
2.3.3
Low-Rank Adaptation
Low-rank adaptation (LoRA) [7] is a ﬁne-tuning method designed to be eﬃcient for large language
models. Instead of modifying all the model’s weights during ﬁne-tuning, it is suﬃcient to introduce
new weights to the model, which would be updated during training while leaving the original weights
unchanged.
==PAGE:13==
2.4 Image Processing
7
The following diagram provides an overview of how LoRA works:
Figure 2.4: Low-Rank Adaptation[7]
The weights A and B are introduced in the ﬁne-tuning step, and the predeﬁned weights W are frozen.
Given that ∆W is the accumulated gradient update during ﬁne-tuning. According to the paper, after
ﬁne-tuning:
∆W = AB[7].
This means all the knowledge updates are captured in the weights of A and B. This way, the output of
the ﬁne-tuned model is computed as:
h = Wx + ∆Wx = Wx + BAx[7]
The matrices A and B have a lower rank (dimensions) than the original weights W. This makes ﬁne-
tuning A and B much more eﬃcient. Moreover, the same model can be ﬁne-tuned on multiple tasks,
and only the matrices A and B have to be changed.
2.4
Image Processing
Image processing is the ﬁeld of manipulating and analysing images. It’s primary goal is to enhance
images or extract meaningful information. Like NLP, the image processing ﬁeld has been revolutionised
with advances in machine learning. First with the introduction of convolutional neural networks (CNN)
and later with transformer based models (vision transformers).
2.4.1
Convolutional Neural Network
CNNs are a class of deep learning models. They are designed to learn spacial features from input images.
Their main key components that diﬀerentiate them for multi-layer perceptrons is their convolutional
layers and pooling layers.
2.4.1.1
Convolutional layer
The convolutional layer is the component that automatically extracts features from images. It involves
the usage of ﬁlters which are trainable matrices that are used to detect features like edges and textures.
This ﬁlter is then applied on parts of the image depending on parameters like stride and padding.
This process is done across channels of the image as we would typically have 3 channels for RGB
representations of images with each channel representing a color channel. Finally an activation function
can be used to discover non-linear complex patterns.

2.4.1.2
Pooling layer
The pooling layer is another crucial component of CNNs that is mainly used for reducing dimensions of
the features to increase the eﬃciency of the model. There are diﬀerent types of pooling operation that
can be used like max pooling or average pooling. The operation will be used to reduce the size of the
features depending on stride and window size such that for the window size maps into one aggregated
value.
2.4.1.3
Architecture
The following provide an overall view of a simple CNN architecture for classiﬁcation:
Figure 2.5: CNN Architecture[8]
After applying the convolutional layer, we create more channels than our original image input. This is
the case when we apply multiple ﬁlters and each ﬁlter will produce a channel. Next, the pooling layer
aggregates the information thus reducing its dimensions. We can apply more convolutional layers and
pooling layers. Finally, we use a fully connected layer to rearrange our features into a single vector and
use it for the downstream task.
2.4.2
Vision transformer
Vision transformers are an application of the transformer model on the image input. It leverages the
same architecture as for the NLP based models. First, the image is split into patches. Then, it is
ﬂattened into a ﬁxed-dimensional embedding space.
This is the equivalent of tokenization in NLP.
Moreover, position embeddings are added to the encoding to each patch to keep information about the
positioning of each patch. Then, the attention mechanism gives the model a global context where each
patch of the image can related to any other patch.
In this project, we use the vision transformer BEiT which was introduced in the paper BEiT: BERT
Pre-Training of Image Transformers[9]. BEiT is pretrained in a self-supervised way similar to BERT.
While the pre-training of BERT involves hiding text tokens and training the model on predicting the
missing tokens, BEiT is pre-trained by hiding patches of the image and training the model to predict
the missing patch. This gives the model all the beneﬁts of NLP transformer based models namely
handling long ranged dependencies and a global context.

Methodology
This project utilises a number of diﬀerent methods to reach our goal of enhancing rental listings using
artiﬁcial intelligence models. We had to take many steps to achieve this goal:
• First, we need to ﬁnd a suitable dataset. The more data the dataset contains, the more options
we have to explore.
• Second, we train models to predict a listing’s performance. This is done to be able to distinguish

• Finally, we use a generative AI model to modify the listings using our trained model to check for

In the next chapters, we will explore the analysis and results in more details
3.1
Data Collection
The Ecorustics platform currently shows around 37 long-term stays. Given that this is not a large
enough dataset for a machine learning model to be trained on, we instead opted to ﬁnd a dataset with
more samples. The initial idea was to scrape Airbnb since it is a rental platform similar to Ecorustics.
However, the Terms of Service from Airbnb’s website prohibits this1. It was, therefore, decided to
ﬁnd an existing dataset. A dataset originating from Inside Airbnb2 was agreed upon. This dataset
can be found at https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/joebeachcapital/airbnb/data. Inside Airbnb
collects publicly available information from the Airbnb website. Airbnb data is more suitable for the
model we want to use on Ecorustics than data from another rental platform like hotel websites or rentals
for extended periods.
The main advantages of this dataset are its size and the variety of its features. Training machine learning
models requires a large amount of data. We have almost 500,000 data points. The more data a model
is trained on, the better the performance. Other sources would have fewer data points, and getting
data from diﬀerent sources oﬀers challenges as the features can not be easily merged. The features of
our dataset contain text, tabular, and images.
The goal is to improve the presentation of Ecorustics listings rather than improve the actual rental
itself. This would hopefully entice more potential users to engage with the website. The ideal metric to
optimise for would be eg. click-through rate or how many times a place has been rented. This would tell
us something about how attractive a listing is. However, since these metrics are not publicly available
from the website, it becomes impossible to scrape. Therefore, the metric "Reviews per Month" has
been chosen instead since it implicitly tells us how many people have used the rental. There are certain
drawbacks to using this metric. "Reviews per Month" does not directly correlate to how attractive a
listing is, the same way the click-through rate would. Instead, whether a place gets a review depends
more on the stay at the rental. There could also be other reasons a listing has more reviews, such as
location or bad/good service.

4.3
Target metric
In the data, there are multiple metrics that could be used to assess the performance of a listing. There
are numerous review scores, the total number of reviews and the number of reviews per month. The
issue with using the review scores is that all listings have a very high review score. The average score
is around 4 stars out of 5. This holds true for all score variants. It is also possible to have a high score
simply because you have a few high ratings. This could be biased towards newer listings which have a
single high or low rating.
Given that the majority of reviews are positive, we assume that having a lot of reviews is equivalent to
having a good listing. The Number of Reviews column is therefore a potential target metric. However,
there is still an issue that old listings that assemble 2 reviews per year with an total number of reviews
of 10 would be considered better than a new listing that has only been up for less than a year with a
total of 5 reviews. We can therefore not rely only on the total number of reviews and must look into
how often a listing is reviewed. Reviews per Month is therefore a better metric. In the end Reviews per
month is chosen and will be used as the primary metric for measuring popularity, since it explains the
popularity of a listing without too much bias.

5.3
DistilBERT Models
For this project, we have used 2 diﬀerent DistilBERT model variations. The ﬁrst model is pre-trained
but has a limited context length. The second model has more context length but is not pretrained.
5.3.1
DistilBERT 512
The ﬁrst DistilBERT 5 model we have ﬁne-tuned has a maximum of 512 tokens in its context length.
This means the model has a limit on how large the text it can process. While our dataset contains
multiple text descriptions, our model can only handle a maximum of 3 columns. This poses a question
about which sections of the description we want to train our model on. For the ﬁrst iteration of our
models, we only ﬁne-tuned on the Summary.
The used model has also been pre-trained. This means that the model can already perform some tasks
with limited performance. For our task, we introduce a linear layer on top of the hidden state output
of the DistilBERT model. Then we ﬁned-tune the whole model. This means both our newly added
classiﬁcation layer and the DistilBERT model weights are ﬁne-tuned.
5.3.2
DistilBERT 1024
This variation of the model is capable of handling 1024 context tokens. This means it can process longer
text. Luckily for our use case, the combination of all textual descriptions ﬁts within the 1024 context
length. The main advantage is that our model can be trained on the full listing description and not
just a subset, which should give the model more information to learn any trends and relations between
the text and the predicted class the listing belongs to.
The downside to this model is that it has not been pre-trained. This means the model will require a lot
of data if we want to see any improvement in the performance. During the pre-training step, the model
learns how to interpret words in diﬀerent contexts. It is equivalent to the model learning a vocabulary
that it can used to further understand the text. However, with a not pre-trained model, the model will
need a lot of data to be able to achieve greater performance. There is another downside. Once we train
our model, it will mainly be good for the classiﬁcation task we trained it for and will perform badly on
any other task. This is not a problem for us as we only need to train the model for one task.
We train the model on about 80.000 data points.

CHAPTER 8
Improving Listing Descriptions
To ﬁnd out what improvements can be made to a listing, and how eﬀective it is, we need to ﬁrst change
to description and check whether the trained model considers it improved. To do this, we ﬁrst decide on
some potential improvements. The improvement is then turned into a prompt, which is sent to an AI
language model alongside the description. The idea is to then take the improved description, check the
new rating using the trained NLP model, and if the listing receives a higher rating, the improvement is
sent to the host as a recommended improvement.
For the AI language model Groq’s1 API is used due to it’s fast performance and ample free usage
limits. When making requests to Groq multiple AI language models can be chosen. For next sections
the llama3-70b-8192 model is used.
8.1
System prompt
There are multiple types of prompts which can be given to Groq when making a request. The system
prompt typically contains context, the model’s role, and rules it must adhere to. The user prompt
contains the actual content or instruction. When improving the listing descriptions the system prompt
is used to provide context telling Groq the purpose of the prompt as well as the format for the input and
output. To create an eﬃcient prompt OpenAI’s prompt engineering guide is used2. The main struggle
is to ensure Groq only returns the output without prefacing it with a line such as "Here is the modiﬁed
output:". Even when explicitly telling Groq not to do this, it will still append it randomly. The only
consistent way of getting around it is to tell Groq to always append it, give it an example, and then
manually removing it from the output. The resulting system prompt is seen in ﬁgure 8.1.
You have received the following rental listing description and have to correct
it based on the given prompts, of which there can be 1 or more prompts.
Keep all the information of the original description.
The input will be ’[[[Description]]] [[[Prompt1]]] [[[Prompt2]]] [[[Prompt3]]]...’.
Start the output with ’Here is the revised description:’.
Here is an example of an input prompt and expected output:
Input:
[[[Located in the heart of the Down Town Los Angeles our place offers
endless choices and convenience!]]]
[[[Make the post more negative]]]
Output:
Here is the revised description: Situated in the congested and noisy heart
of Downtown Los Angeles, our place is surrounded by chaos and inconvenience.

8.2
Improvement prompts
After choosing an appropriate system prompt, the next step is to decide on prompts for testing improve-
ments to a listing description. By using the explainability tool mentioned in section 5.6.4 it is possible
to ﬁnd which words most often contribute to making a listing popular, and which words make it less
popular. A snippet of these words are found in appendix A.1 and appendix A.2. The most signiﬁcant of

8.2.1
First experiment
The ﬁrst experiment is simply to test the performance of applying each individual prompt. A subset of
500 of the listing descriptions are taken and the prompts are individually applied to each description
by sending them to Groq. The DistilBERT model is then applied to the modiﬁed description and if

the results of the ﬁrst experiment. The "Improved" column shows the percentage of descriptions which
were improved.
We can see that "Make it more negative" is the most inﬂuential prompt, with around 50% of the
descriptions improving when this prompt is applied. This seems counter-intuitive as it is not expected
that a more negative listing would perform better. However, one theory is that the prompt performs so
well because the DistilBERT gets confused by the negativity, since the listings it has been trained on
rarely speak negatively. Another theory is that it performs well because it changes the description more
drastically than the poorer performing prompts. Since the DistilBERT model is not very accurate,
drastic changes is enough to change a rating completely. There is some indication that this is true,
since most of the well performing prompts are more general, while the under-performing prompts are
more speciﬁc. For example the prompt "Make it sound more personal" allows Groq to be creative and
change the description more than the prompt "Call them ’roommates’ instead of ’guests’", which merely
substitutes a word.
The rightmost column "Avg. score change" shows the average change of the DistilBERT score after
applying the prompt. We can see that the lowest performing prompts on average decrease the score,
rather than improving it. Especially of note is "Call them ’roommates’ instead of ’guests’" which does a
simple word replacement, yet still manages to decrease the score. ’Roommates’ is generally considered
a good word according to the explainability tool, while ’guests’ is considered a bad word. This indicates
that context matters a lot to the DistilBERT model, and simply changing one word to another will not
always give the expected results.

Another way we can help the hosts is to provide the best and worse sentences in their description
according to our model. For this, we use the explainability tool transformers-interpret on our trained
model. While the tool can provide an attribution score for each token, we can compute the attribution
of a sentence by averaging the attribution of all tokens in each sentence. We then ﬁnd the original
sentences instead of tokenized sentences. Afterwards, sentences with the highest and lowest attribution
can be provided in the feedback to show the host which part of their description is great and which
part needs improvement.

CHAPTER 9
Future Improvements
In this chapter, we will delve into some of the work and experiments that can be used to enhance the
results of this study.
9.1
Data
One of the hurdles has been that the dataset does not contain data to accurately determine the popu-
larity of a listing. Data such as click-through rate or rental count would likely have been a better metric
to optimize for, rather than reviews per month. For improving the models in the future, it might be
possible to reach out to Airbnb, Booking.com, or other online marketplaces to potentially gain access
to their internal data.
Another interesting idea we have considered is to synthesis mock or training data using generative AI.
Especially for generating examples of bad listings given that our model considers negative listings as
good listings. This problem stems from the lack of very bad listings in our dataset as Airbnb most
likely just delete or blocks listings that are overly negative. Generative AI could be used to create such
synthetic data the same way it was used to modify the listings by making them negative. The main
drawback to such a method would be labeling such synthesised data as it would require human to label
them. It might even require more averaging label given by multiple people to avoid having biased labels.
9.2
NLP
For the NLP based models, the token representing empty section had a high attribution score to good
listings. This might be due to some section not being necessary and that customer genuinely prefer the
absence of such sections. One way to verify that is for each section we would train a BERT based model
to extract features from one section speciﬁcally. Then we could combine all extracted features into a
classiﬁer. Such a classiﬁer will have a better understanding of which section are missing and whether
that will have a positive or negative eﬀect on the listings performance. Another simpler idea would be
to improve the encoding of missing but this might require more experimentation.
Another improvement could be to further experiement with the Gemma models. While this will require
more computational and time resources, we have not fully explored the limits of such a model with
diﬀerent LoRA conﬁgurations.
9.3
Image processing
The image models are trained using the thumbnail of each listing. However, on Airbnb’s website a
listing can have multiple photos attached. If multiple photos could be incorporated into the model,
it might be able to more accurately rate a listing. This is because it will it have a more complete
representation of the rental by having visuals of multiple of the essential rooms, and it could compare
those between each rental. There are generally always photos of the bedroom, bathroom and exterior
of the rental. So if all of these were consistently present for each rental during training, the model could
==PAGE:56==

eg. compare the bedrooms of each rental and base it’s score on that, rather than comparing bedrooms,
bathrooms and exteriors, since those happened to be the thumbnails.
9.4
Multi-modal
A multi-modal model could be trained using combinations of textual, tabular and image data. While
we did not have time to experiment with more than just the textual and numeric data, it would be
interesting to try out other features especially combining textual and image data. We do not expect
the performance of such a model to be much greater than our current model given that we only have
access to the thumbnail. However, if there is a dataset that contains more images per listing, such a
multimodal model would be interesting to study.
9.5
Provided Feedback
After ﬁnding the suggestions for improvements the idea is to send them to the host in a personalized
message. This message could eg. be an email referring to the listing in question and what improvements
could be made. Creating this email could be done either using email templates or utilizing Groq. To
properly set up the email chain it would be necessary to access the Ecorustics database to ﬁnd the host
associated with a listing and their email.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
In conclusion, by using a dataset containing scraped Airbnb data it has been possible to train several
models that try to predict a listings performance based on it’s textual description and thumbnail. The
metric used for this was "Reviews per month" which seemed to correlate poorly with both the image
and text data, hence the models’ performance suﬀered and ended up not being very accurate. For the
text model the DistilBERT transformer model had the best performance with an accuracy of 0.45 when
trained on four classes. For the image model the BEiT vision transformer model performed better
than convolutional neural network models, ending up with an accuracy of 0.35 when trained on four
classes. A multi-modal model was also trained, which uses both the listing description and tabular data.
However this model ended up performing about as well as using only the textual data.
A pipeline has been created which takes in listing description and outputs suggestions for improvement.
To ﬁnd out possible improvements to a listing, the explainability library called transformers-interpret
has been used alongside the DistilBERT text model.
Using this it has been possible to ﬁgure out
what features most prominently aﬀect a listing descriptions score. Groq has then been used to modify
listings based on those features, and the results have been evaluated to determine which improvements
would provide the most beneﬁt to a descriptions performance. Finally, we use a survey for validating
the results with some human input. The survey showed that the chosen prompts were able to improve
listing descriptions according to users, and that the trained DistilBERT model and users generally agree
on how much the descriptions are improved by.
Overall most of the research objectives have been reached. The pipeline ends when the best performing
improvement suggestions have been found. The next step would be to take the suggestions and send
them to the host in a user-friendly way (eg. an email).
