Abstract
This project presents a methodology for describing biochemical pathways, specifically
metabolic processes, by using mathematical modeling. Glycolysis and the citric acid
cycle will be used to present this methodology. Furthermore, the project investigates
the role of mathematical modeling and simulation in biological research. By combin-
ing knowledge from previous studies, equations for glycolytic reactions in yeast cells
were used to model the individual reaction rates and the production rates of each
metabolite. Additionally, simulations based on a whole-body model were performed
to demonstrate the potential for improving the understanding of the behavior of bio-
logical processes, particularly how simulation can be used to mimic the concentrations
of metabolites over time. The analysis shows the potential of combining biology and
mathematical modeling, and how simulations can complement traditional laboratory
experiments and clinical trials.
The future application of this project include im-
proving existing models by combining the proposed methodology with whole-body
models.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Metabolic processes can be modeled in a multitude of ways depending on the aim of
the research question asked. The aim of this project is to examine how mathematical
modeling can be applied to gain a better understanding of the human metabolism. In
particular, this method is applied to the metabolic pathways, glycolysis and the citric
acid cycle. By combining data found through thorough literature search, I suggest a
methodology for using the knowledge of glycolysis to strengthen the use of already
whole-body models.
1.1
Motivation
Background
The human body has been studied almost as long as the human has been on earth.
The motivation for understanding the human body is often centered around the health
and diseases that can affect us. Since the early days, many new fields of study have
been introduced such as microbiology, biochemistry, and systems biology to name
a few. However, despite new areas of research appearing, a common denominator
between the very early and the current research of the human organism, is the use
of experiments of real organisms. This technique is still in use today where both
living and dead, human and animals are used to better understand the human body.
However, these methods have disadvantages. Specifically when using living organ-
isms, be it humans or animals there are a multitude of disadvantages, namely ethical
considerations of the experiments. As for the animals, there are also considerations
to be made on the matter of whether the experiments are representative to if the
experiments were performed in humans. As for the cadavers of human, there are
both a natural limited supply but also ethical considerations to be made. Therefore,
in recent times there has been a trend toward complementing traditional experiments
with computer based experiments, in order to simulate the outcomes of different ex-
perimental strategies. This new tendency where biological research is performed in
the interdisciplinary field between statistics, computer science and biology leads to
new exciting fields such as systems biology and bioinformatics. The general intention
of these fields is to look at biology in new ways. One of the areas that has benefited
from the more mathematical approach is the study of human metabolism and its
many processes and pathways.

1.1 Motivation
2
The human body is extremely complex, and biochemical reactions occur in our bodies
without our notice. A constant formation and breakdown of metabolites enables the
body to adapt to different stimuli, and the metabolic processes are organized and de-
pendent on each other. Whole-body modeling uses the organization of the processes
in order to describe biological concepts by the use of mathematical models.
The approach of using mathematics to describe biological systems is an upcoming
way of looking at these processes within the human body, and these whole-body mod-
els, or virtual metabolic human dynamic models, can be used in making pathological
analyses or therapy design for some diseases. Kurata [1] describes how these virtual
models can be used to complement clinical studies and experimental biology. The
ability to create a virtual metabolic human being is therefore of great interest as
it can enable advances to medicine and life science in general through more precise
examination and less time consuming processes.
The metabolism is a crucial part of both human health and disease and has been
studied for many years. Both the human body and the metabolic processes are quite
complex. Therefore, it is a very cumbersome process to figure out how to model and
simulate the body correctly.
A more systematic approach is necessary to interpret the metabolic dysfunctions and
to propose new and improved treatment strategies[1, 2, 3]. Many researchers and pa-
tients could benefit from using virtual simulations as a part of the treatment strategy
for diseases caused by metabolic dysfunctions. Diabetes mellitus is an example of a
metabolic dysfunction where a better understanding of the metabolic processes could
lead to an improvement of the current treatment. For a long time, medical doctors
have been able to diagnose and treat patients with both diabetes mellitus type 1 and
type 2 fairly easy. For many patients with diabetes mellitus type 1, the treatment
consists of a continuous control of blood glucose concentration, often followed by an
injection of insulin or glucagon. Even though the treatment does not require going
to the doctor or making huge changes to the every day life, it can still result in a re-
duced quality of life, and it can complicate the life of especially kids or young adults,
who have not yet learned to manage their disease. By making virtual simulations
of patients with diabetes, there is a possibility of developing an improved treatment
strategy. This could be done by making a device that distributes insulin and glucagon
according to a personalized virtual simulation of the biochemical system. This de-
vice would act as an artificial pancreas, and could improve the daily lives of patients
suffering from diabetes.

happen inside the mitochondria. A schematic overview of an eukaryotic cell and the

2.1 The Structures of the Human Body

2.1
The Structures of the Human Body
The biochemical processes are vital for any living organism, including the human.
The processes in themselves are complex, but when considering how the processes
interact with each other and their environment, and their need to be highly organized,
the complexity truly unfolds. The structure of a human body is made by billions of
smaller structures, the four major groups of such structures being cells, tissue, organs,
and systems. Especially cells are considered to be the building blocks for our body,
and unique, highly specialized cells need to work together to form our 79 organs, 206
bones, and all of our tissue.
2.2
Biochemical Processes
Biochemistry is the study of the chemical processes that takes place within a liv-
ing organism. Even simple living organisms, such as the bacterium Escherichia coli
have more than a thousand of these chemical reactions[6]. A fundamental part of
biochemistry is the production of energy, and the synthesis and breakdown of macro-
molecules. Here, metabolites, the intermediate or end products of the metabolism,
are converted by a variety of integrated reactions, constructing a large network of
chemical processes. The products of these reactions fit into one of the three main
purposes of metabolism [6]:
• Converting energy from food to energy that is available for cellular processes
• Converting food to the building blocks for nucleic acids, lipids, proteins, and
some carbohydrates
• Eliminate metabolic waste products
In order to make the reactions occur, enzymes are needed as catalyzers.
Some
metabolic pathways require energy, anabolic reactions, and others produce energy,
catabolic reactions. Sometimes, a third kind of reactions are seen, where the pathway
can be either anabolic or catabolic depending on the conditions in the cell, and these
pathways are said to be amphibolic pathways.
In order to have a functioning pathway, the entirety of the reactions must be
thermodynamically favorable, implicating that they have to go from a less stable to
a more stable product. In order for a reaction to occur spontaneously, the change in
free energy, ∆G, must be negative. Nevertheless, an unfavorable reaction can happen
spontaneously, if it is coupled to a favorable reaction, and if the overall free energy
is negative. ∆G is dependent on the standard free energy change, ∆G◦′, as well as
the concentration of the reactants and products. In general, formation of metabolic
pathways require coupling of enzyme-catalyzed reactions and an overall negative free
energy.

CHAPTER 3
Modeling and
Simulation
The field of systems biology combines biology with computational and mathematical
analysis, and the interest in this field has been increasing the last two decades. Fur-
thermore, systems biology is often used interdisciplinary with areas such as statistics,
computer science, and informatics. By combining these disciplines, scientists from
different fields can collaborate and contribute with diverse knowledge.
3.1
Metabolic Networks
One of the main purposes of systems biology is to model and characterize how cells,
tissues, and organs function as systems. This enables us describe to specify biological
networks, and use data sets to understand the complexity of biological systems [3,
7, 8]. These data sets are often categorized as omics data, such as metabolomics,
proteomics, genomics, or transcriptomics. The different omics data types allow for
the reconstruction of biological networks which can shed a light on how cellular pro-
cesses operate in relation to each other. This way of describing the relations is called
network reconstruction [7, 9], and it is a way of organizing the biological informa-
tion in a formal, mathematical manner.
The networks are composed of chemical
compounds, the nodes, and of reactions, the links. Biochemical equations can be
used to describe these biological networks, and based on these equations, a stoichio-
metric matrix, the S-matrix, can be computed. This will be elaborated in Section 3.2.
Metabolic networks are often depicted as maps, and these can illustrate the regulatory
interactions that takes place between different metabolic pathways. The knowledge of
the interactions can be used to identify comorbidities, as defect enzymatic reactions
often create a cascading effect that can couple more metabolic diseases. Thus, these
maps can be used to determine if two disorders are connected by correlated reactions
[10]. Furthermore, the networks can be used as the basis of developing mathematical
models that ultimately can be used in improving the treatment of a wide variety of
diseases. The metabolic pathways can get very complex, resulting in the so-called
metabolic map of the entire human metabolism becoming comprehensive and diﬀicult

Stoichiometry and Matrices

products in biochemical reactions, i.e. what molecules are produced and consumed
during a biochemical reaction. The stoichiometry can be defined for all reactions
where both the reactants and products are known. An example of how this method
can be used to describe reactions will be elaborated in Section 4.1 and 4.2, where
glycolysis and the citric acid cycle are described using stoichiometry.

3.3 Mathematical Modeling

3.3
Mathematical Modeling
An important part of research is to produce and collect data, followed by statisti-
cal analysis.
But now, the quantity of collected data is beyond the capability of
humans to analyze, still the amount of data is steadily increasing, making existing
techniques, such as statistical analysis, suffer [12]. In this data-rich environment, it
has proven essential to complement ”real” experiments with computer-based models
and simulations, to explore the world around us to its fullest.
All sciences use models to describe the complex reality in a more condensed form.
Models are most often incomplete, and only take the most essential aspect of a system
into account. This is also true when modeling biological pathways, as these can be
very complex and interdependent. Therefore, one has to select only the most essential
aspects, often a subset of interactions, or a certain behavior.
Models can both be physical and virtual/mathematical. Physical modeling is rep-
resented by model organisms that has been genetically selected for.
A frequently
used model organism is the fruit fly, Drosophila melanogaster, as it has a short gen-
eration time, allowing the scientists to examine many generations in the course of
an experiment. The same applies for the yeast, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, which will
be explored more in the coming sections. Sometimes, other strengths than a short
generation time are needed in a model organism.
For instance, when performing
experiments prior to a drug release, animal models that resembles humans are used
to determine possible side effects, before conducting clinical trials on humans. The
second type of model, mathematical modeling, tries to describe a subject in a quan-
titative manner, by defining mathematical models for e.g. the biological processes.
The following sections will describe and discuss mathematical modeling, and how it
can be used in biology.
3.3.1
Whole-Body Models
When looking at every organ, tissue, or metabolite individually, there is a chance
of missing important links or interactions between these structures. If we examine
the human body as one collective unit these interaction can be taken into account,
thereby giving us a better understanding of how the human body works as a system.
When this approach is applied to mathematical modeling of the human body, it is
referred to as whole-body modeling, and it can provide important insight into how the
metabolites might work and interact differently in different organs. The human body
is very complex, and there is a long way to go before we are able to model a complete
virtual human being. However, by making some generalizations and simplifying some
concepts, we might gain a better understanding of how the human body works as a
whole. Depending on the modeling objective, the complexity of the model may differ.
==PAGE:20==
3.3 Mathematical Modeling
13
In academic literature, researches have proposed several models, some complex and
some simple, used to describe the metabolic processes in man. In 1985, Sorensen [13]
described the interaction between glucose, insulin, and glucagon, by using a simple
whole-body model. In more recent years, more complex models have appeared, and
in 2020, Panunzi et. al [14] suggested a revised and refined version of Sorensen’s
model, where food-intake was added as a parameter, by using the already existing
SIMO-model, which is a simple interdependent model describing the relation between
glucose and insulin. Later on, a modified version of the SIMO-model has been used
to also take glucose, amino acid, and lipid uptake into account, when modeling the
metabolism in man [2].
Yet other authors combine whole-body models with sto-
ichiometry of the biochemical reactions, and furthermore expand their models to
include more than one metabolite [1, 15, 16].
It is expected that when the number of parameters in the model is increased, the
simulations of the biochemical processes will converge toward the actual biochemical
processes the the human body. But there is a risk of focusing too much on getting a
lot of information incorporated into the model, risking spending a lot of time getting
the data, and not enough time on actually testing and fine-tuning it. It will always
be a balance between having enough information in the model, and only having what
is needed, in order to be able to use the models going forward.
3.3.1.1
Why Use Whole-Body Models?
The study of metabolic processes require performing in vitro and in vivo experiments,
and as the processes cannot be seen with the naked eye, the experimental setups can
be very advanced and require a large amount of sample material. As in vitro and
in vivo experiments take place in a laboratory, there is a risk of technical problems
as well as human errors, which give rise to several sources of error. Research solely
dependent on laboratory experiments are often very time and resource consuming as
they require careful planning, specialized skills, and waiting for incubation, followed
by a careful analysis of the data to ensure any errors originating from the experiments
are not mistaken for actual evidence. This renders a long time from the beginning of
an experiment before conclusions can be drawn. Further, when using sample material
from humans, ethical considerations must also be taken into account. The ethical
considerations will be discussed in Chapter 6.
In vitro and in vivo experiments cannot be replaced by computational models, but
models can complement experiments. For example, the use of a whole-body model
can complement the current practice by including simulations in the design of the
experiments, thereby reducing the time spent and increasing the precision of the re-
search. With the use of whole-body models, the experiments can be designed in a
way that, based on the model and simulations, seems to be more effective. This can
reduce time spent on performing experiments, both the successful and the unsuccess-
ful experiments. Additionally, mathematical models give the opportunity to perform
and adjust virtual experiments more easily compared to experiments in a laboratory,
where the supply of samples and incubation times might be limiting factors.

3.4
Computational Models and Simulations as a Tool
in Biological Research
Simulation is the practice of using mathematical equations, i.e. mathematical mod-
eling, to describe one or more processes [18]. Modeling combined with simulations is
a great way of saving time and resources compared to performing traditional experi-
ments in a laboratory, by utilizing the strength of mathematical models. Simulations
cannot replace experiments, but they can assist in getting results and data faster,
which can enable the scientists to optimize their experiments.
3.4.1
The Role of Simulations in Biology
As mentioned in Section 3.3.1, mathematical models can be used to describe inter-
actions in the body, by mimicking the processes occurring and interacting. These
models often consist of systems of differential equations, but they can be diﬀicult to
interpret, especially for people who are not trained in mathematics or similar subjects.
This is where simulations can be useful, as they can often give a more intuitive and
maybe even visual description of the given prediction. Simulations can be used in
different ways, depending on the purpose. For instance, simulations can be used to
optimize experimental conditions, to analyze an output dependent on the effect of
different input, to test experimental design ideas, to investigate treatment designs, or
perhaps to troubleshoot an already known problem. The success of a simulation is
based on the model it is made from, thus making modeling a prerequisite for simula-
tions [12, 18].
Simulations can be used to examine the behavior of a variable of interest at dif-
ferent parameter values [19].
In order to fully take advantage of the potential of
simulations, the model has to have some level of accuracy. Since models are mathe-
matical representations of a process, we are not yet able to fully describe the exact
behavior of the biochemical processes, as these are often very complex and entan-
gled with other biological processes, but the models should at least include some of
the interaction between networks and physical structures, in order to be sure the
computations show the correct tendencies[3]. Therefore, it is important to note that
simulations cannot stand alone, and should always be complemented by experiments
and analysis of experimental data.

3.4.2
Pros and Cons of Biological Modeling and Simulation
Mathematical models and simulation can complement laboratory experiments by help-
ing researchers identify the most likely experimental setups to investigate a hypothe-
sis. Moreover, the outcome or results can be explored by examining the predictions
of models based on different hypotheses. After performing simulations, experimental
setups can be designed to target the desired hypothesis, but also to refine one’s un-
derstanding of the subject of matter [12].
However, there are some downsides of using these mathematical models, and at least
some considerations need to be made to get a useful outcome. Enzymatic reactions
are often very complex, and one have to select an appropriate level at which to ana-
lyze the processes. This also applies for the biochemical reactions in glycolysis. For
example, in order to properly describe the glycolytic reactions associated with phos-
phofructokinase, the enzyme responsible for step three in the glycolysis, would require
a system of at least 15 nonlinear differential equations to get an accurate description
[13]. One could imagine the same kind of complexity for the other enzymes, and even
if suﬀicient information about these reactions were obtainable, a complete model of
glucose metabolism will require a complex system of differential equations.
This
might not in itself be a problem, but if the goal is to describe all metabolic pathways
in such detail, such systems of differential equations will become immense.
Of course, the ultimate goal is to be able to describe the human metabolism com-
pletely, using these mathematical models, as this would enable the making of a com-
plete virtual human being.
But until this has been accomplished, some level of
simplification is needed. If the right simplifications and assumptions are made, it is
possible to get a model describing the overall tendencies in the human metabolism.
Furthermore, making models is an iterative process requiring the researcher to have
been able to identify the most important variables, but the necessary quantitative
data has to be obtainable to describe those variables [12].
Conclusively, network reconstruction can be used to understand how the metabolic
pathways are connected and how the metabolites interact. By using this approach,
the information contained in the metabolic pathways has be organized in a formal
and mathematical manner. For this purpose, stoichiometric matrices can be useful as
a way of illustrating the relation between the reactants and products of a biochemical
reaction. Furthermore, these stoichiometric matrices can be incorporated as a param-
eter in mathematical modeling, when seeking to describe the complexity of metabolic
pathways.

Metabolic Processes
When studying anatomy and physiology, it can be diﬀicult to determine where to
start, as everything is connected in some way. The same applies when describing
the human metabolism. There is not one right or wrong pathway to start with since
metabolites that are a product of one process might be a substrate of another, and
vice versa. This chapter focuses on describing glycolysis and the citric acid cycle, as
these pathways are vital in converting glucose to energy.
4.1
Glycolysis
Glycolysis is a metabolic pathway that takes place in a vast variety of living organisms.
No matter whether this process is happening in yeast, bacteria, or animals, the reason
for the process is the same; breakdown of glucose to produce energy. This process
is carried out in the cytosol in eukaryotic cells, Figure 2.1. Complex carbohydrates
have to be broken down for us to benefit from them, and this applies to for instance
the starch from our diet, and the glycogen stored in the liver and muscle tissue.
These have to be converted to simpler carbohydrates before we can absorb them in
the intestines and transport them to the blood stream. The breakdown is done by
cleaving starch and glycogen to maltose and maltotriose. This is done by α-amylase
from the pancreas, and to some degree also α-amylase from saliva. Maltose is cleaved
by the enzyme maltase into two molecules of glucose, whereas maltotriose is digested
by α-glucosidase. Both maltase and α-glucosidase are located as surface enzymes on
cells lining the intestines. Glucose can also be obtained from the milk sugar, lactose,
or from sucrose in vegetables. The enzymes for the degradation of these sugars are
lactase and sucrase, respectively [6].
Glucose is a monosaccharide, and it can be
transported from the intestines, over the intestinal cells and into the blood stream,
where it can reach all parts of the body.

One molecule of glucose reacts with two inorganic phosphates (Pi), two adenosine
diphosphates (ADP), and two nicotinamide adenine dinucleotides (NAD+), yielding
two pyruvate molecules, two adenosine triphosphates (ATP), two reduced molecules
of NAD+ (NADH), two hydrogen molecules (H+), and two molecules of water (H2O).

4.2
The Citric Acid Cycle
When glucose has been converted into pyruvate through the steps of glycolysis, the
pyruvate continues into the citric acid cycle.
The citric acid cycle is also known
as Krebs cycle or tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, and will be abbreviated TCA in
the following sections. In order for the pyruvate to enter TCA, it has to enter the
mitochondria where it is converted into acetyl co-enzyme A (acetyl CoA). This step
is not considered to be part of either glycolysis or TCA, as it only happens under
aerobic conditions. In anaerobic conditions, the pyruvate is converted to either lactate
or ethanol. But this irreversible conversion of pyruvate to acetyl CoA is necessary for
the TCA to occur, as it is the direct link between glycolysis and cellular respiration,
Figure 4.2. The reaction is catalyzed by three enzymes, which make up the pyruvate
dehydrogenase complex. This process is called pyruvate decarboxylation, and the
products are acetyl CoA, CO2, NADH, and H+. The synthesis of acetyl CoA from
pyruvate is rather complex as it consists of three enzymes and five co-enzymes, and
it will be simplified by the following equation.
Pyruvate + CoA + NAD+ →acetyl CoA + CO2 + NADH + H+
As one glucose molecule yields two molecules of pyruvate, the citric acid cycle occurs
twice for every molecule of glucose. The starting point for TCA is acetyl CoA, which
can be obtained from pyruvate, or alternatively from fatty acid oxidation. The prod-
ucts of TCA can be defined in three ways, depending on how many steps are included.
An overview of the products can be seen in Table 4.3, which has been divided by the
steps occurring in TCA only (A), TCA and the conversion of pyruvate (B), and the
above mentioned including glycolysis (C), respectively.

Modeling in Practice
By combining the knowledge from the previous sections, we are now are to describe
the biochemical processes of glycolysis in more detail. This is done by finding the

5.1
Mathematical Description of Glycolysis
It has proven a challenge to find suﬀicient data on glycolysis in human cells. In many
cases, only some of the metabolites where measured, making it diﬀicult to model the
entire process. In other cases, the focus has been on how glycolysis differs in cancer
cells, where pyruvate is often is converted into lactate, instead of entering the citric
acid cycle [21, 22, 23]. Therefore, the following section will be based on the work
on living cells of the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae done by Hynne et al. [24] but
the methodology can be converted to glycolysis in human cells, if the necessary data
is available. The reasoning behind the use of yeast will be discussed in Chapter 6,
where I will go into further detail on how this can yield useful results, and what some
of the consequences of using yeast cells instead of human cells are. Further, it is im-
portant to note that the purpose of this chapter is to showcase how the methodology

5.1.1
Definitions, Assumptions, and Notations
An important assumption made by Hynne et al. is that the processes happen in a
stationary state, making it simpler to define a model, as the state will not change over
time [24]. This is, of course, not necessarily the case in most biochemical processes,
and the assumption along with the consequences of the assumption will be discussed
in Chapter 6.
Some research have been conducted prior to Hynne et al.’s work, and their data is
based on biochemical studies and experiments of the particular reactions. This is
true for the rate expressions, and many of the kinetic parameters. Those, that have
not been determined by experiments have been estimated or are left unknown [24].
The experimentation consists of continuous-flow stirred tank reactor (CSTR) mea-
surements, where a suspension of starved yeast cells have been mixed with glucose
and cyanide. Here, the mixture flow into the reactor at a constant rate.
Hynne et al. propose a model based on substrate concentrations measured in steady
state. This general model can be used in cases, where the kinetic parameters are
lacking, as long as the biochemical pathway is well known [24]. The model describes
each reaction of the glycolysis as a rate equation. Some of the reactions are reversible,
which renders both forward and reverse reaction, others are irreversible giving them
only a forward reaction. These will be denoted as r+ for the forward reaction and r-
for the reverse. In the Matlab scripts (Listing 5.1 and 5.2), the denotation is rp and
rm, respectively. In total, there will be 24 reactions describing glycolysis in yeast,
and these will be saved in the reaction vector, r, as the net reaction for each step is
r = r+ −r-.
When writing out the rate equations, the classic bracket-notation for concentrations
is used, e.g. [Glc] for the concentration of glucose. The enzyme concentrations are
considered as fixed, why concentrations will refer to the metabolites.
The ratio between the extracellular volume and the total volume of intracellular cy-
tosol is denoted yvol. This is set to a fixed value of 59, which is based on experimental
data from Hynne et al.
The following parameters are used to describe the processes: S, the stoichiometric ma-
trix; C, a vector with the metabolite concentrations in mM; V, maximum velocities
in mM min−1; K, Michaelis-Menten constants; k, rate constants in min−1; yvol, ratio
of extracellular volume to the total volume of intracellular cytosol. The parameters
defined in the article are ultimately used to define the reaction kinetics, r, and the
production rates, R, given by R = S′ · r.
An overview of the reactions and the corresponding parameter values can be seen in
Tables B.1 and B.2 in Appendix B, and the reaction rate equations are described in
the following section.

5.2
Simulation with Existing Whole-Body Models
The methodology used in Section 5.1 can be further adjusted to contain more metabo-
lites or other factors that affect the pathway. For instance, if the necessary data was
available, one could rather easily expand the model to also contain the citric acid
cycle, thereby being able to describe the energy production of the cells in more detail.
Another approach would be to include more processes but less information of each
process, is what can be seen in the paper by Carstensen et al. [2], where they seek to
model and simulate the metabolism of man. As mentioned in Section 3.3.1, one has
to decide the level of complexity for one’s model, in order to achieve the best results.
A way of simplifying the human body is to only include the organs that are expected
to be the most important for the specific research question. This method can be seen
in the work by Carstensen et al. [2], where they define the body as consisting of 5 key
organs; brain, heart and lungs, liver, gut, kidney. Further, the muscle tissue and the
adipose tissue are each simplified as one compartment, considering them as one organ
each. They choose these organs, as they are seen as the key organs in insulin and
glucagon metabolism. Additionally, they specify that all these organs are connected
directly through blood vessels. In their model, they use these 7 organs as a simplified
body. Besides simplifying the body, they also simplify some of the metabolic pro-
cesses occurring, e.g. glycolysis is simplified to 3 reactions. Furthermore, they use
the parameters r and R, similr to what was shown in Section 5.1.
The following section will examine the whole-body model defined by Carstensen et

Simulating Glycogen Concentrations For 14 Days
The CWB model can also be used to simulate how metabolite concentrations will
change during a longer period of time. Carstensen et al. suggest a similar setup to
that shown in Figure 5.1, where the in silico patient is fasting every other day, but
continues for a longer period of time. Once again, the in silico patient will start with

longer period of time, where four meals will be ingested every other day, resulting

fat [2], and the simulation is run for 14 days. In this way, we can investigate how
the concentration of metabolites are affected in both well-fed and fasting conditions.
From the graph in Figure 5.2 it can be seen, how the concentration of glycogen
develops over time in the liver and muscle tissue.
Figure 5.2: Concentration of glycogen over a period of 14 days. Every other day,

by fasting for 33 hours.
Glycogen is stored glucose which can be converted from glycogen to glucose by the
liver. From Figure 5.2, we see how the concentration of glycogen in the liver drops
after the last meal of day one. This is due to the fact that glycogen initially will be
stored as glucose in the liver, but during the fasting at day two, this energy storage
will instead be used in the lack of glucose. Furthermore, the glycogen concentration
in the muscle tissue is stable throughout the simulation as the patient is at rest. One
could imagine, using these types of predictions of the essential metabolites, or perhaps
hormones, in diseases such as diabetes.

Discussion
6.1
Future Work for the CWB Model
As mentioned earlier, it has been diﬀicult to find suﬀicient data on human glycolysis,
for which reason the model in Section 5.1 is based on data from Saccharomyces cere-
visiae. Since we do not know the exact metabolite concentrations in human glycolysis,
it is diﬀicult to foresee, whether the model can be used to describe the breakdown
of glucose in human cells. Furthermore, before the model can be incorporated in the
CWB model or a similar model, data on the metabolite concentrations in the different
organs is necessary.
As future work, I suggest two different approaches to what should be researched
next. The focus should either be on obtaining the required data, or accepting the
nature of the Saccharomyces cerevisiae data, and then incorporating it into the model
keeping the possible source of error in mind.
In the first approach, the next step would be performing in vitro laboratory experi-
ments on human cells. To add the parameters to the data that is needed in the CWB
model, experiments on cells from the different organs should be performed, meaning
cells originating from the brain, heart, gut, liver, kidney, muscle tissue, and adipose
tissue. Since cells from some of these organs can be diﬀicult to obtain, e.g. brain and
heart, I would suggest to use an animal model, e.g. mice or pigs. Both mice and pigs
are widely used as animal models for studying human health and disease, as they are
anatomically, physiologically, and genetically similar and can get exposed to many
of the same diseases. An advantage of choosing mice is they are cheap to look after,
making them cost effective, and as with the fruit fly mentioned in Section 3.3, the
generation time is short, which allows the scientists to use more generations over the
course of an experiment.
Another approach is to base the model on the already available data, as this can
still yield useful results. By doing so, the next step could be to define the reaction rate
equations for the TCA to expand the model further. It is, however, important to take
into account what assumptions are made to justify the use of yeast data, and further
to describe how these assumptions would influence the accuracy of the model. One
must assume, that at least the ratios between the different metabolites are somewhat
similar in yeast and human cells, as yeast has been wildly used as a model organism for

studying cellular processes in humans, due to its genetic similarity as well as its rapid
growth [26]. The advantages of these traits can be used in the study of metabolic
reactions, as the rapid growth enables for easy cultivation in a laboratory as well as
the opportunity to research, for instance, how mutations are passed on to through
generations.
Yeast is an eukaryotic organism, and therefore share organelles and
other biological properties with human cells. Further, genetic manipulation is easy in
yeast cells, and mutated cells containing human genes can be used to help researchers
understand the role of these genes in humans. Furthermore, it is important to note
that some assumptions have been made by Hynne et al. [24] prior to developing their
model. First of all, to simplify the reaction rate equations, the intracellular metabolite
flows are disregarded. Secondly, they acknowledge a potential problem in ensuring
that all reactions are stopped immediately and at the same time. The authors used a
technique, where the samples were sprayed into -40◦C cold methanol, which according
to the authors, limited the problem. This fact should also be taking into account, if
one choose to perform experiments on human cells. If this technique does not stop
the reactions at the same time as intended, the concentrations measured afterwards
may be higher than in reality, and the ratios between the metabolites might therefore
be skewed.
Regardless of what approach is chosen as the next step, the final step before being
able to make modeling a part of any sort of treatment of disease, is that it needs to
be tested, validated, and compared to both patients and healthy people.
6.2
Examples on Variations in the Methodology
There are many ways to model the human metabolism, and choosing which way to go
depends on the modeling objective. This thesis has focused on the work by Kurata
and Carstensen et al. [1, 2], among others, where the body is seen as a system of
organs and blood vessels, resulting in a whole-body model. In both articles, only the
organs that are seen as the most important organs for their models are chosen, but
since they only use some organs in their models, the ability to show the complexity
of the human body is lost. Additionally, interactions between metabolites and their
surrounding environments can not be explored fully. It is a choice between accurately
describing a metabolic pathway and all its processes, resulting in a simpler but more
precise model, or choosing to model more metabolic pathways, making the system
more complex but simplifying the individual processes. Furthermore, it might not
always be most optimal to choose a whole-body model to describe certain processes.
One could start by describing a metabolic pathway as a one-cell model instead, being
able to describe the individual processes in more detail. There is not one right way to
do this, it is largely dependent on the research question and the resources available.
Therefore, it is also diﬀicult to be critical to the approaches that different researches
have chosen. Kurata define his model with a focus on improving the pathological
analysis and treatment design for diabetes, which is why he includes the pancreas as
one of the key organs in his model. Carstensen et al. seek to make dynamical modeling

of the human metabolism, and include the effect of both insulin and glucagon, yet they

6.3
Potential of In Silico Patients
There is a great potential to use in silico patients, or virtual human beings, in the
search for a better understanding of our body and its metabolic processes. In the fol-
lowing, I will discuss some of the advantages and disadvantages of using mathematical
modeling and simulation in general, compared to strictly performing experiments in
a laboratory.
When performing experiments in a laboratory, time and money are always factors
that one has to consider. Applicable to both mice and pigs, both time and resources
will be spent on waiting on the animals to be ready and for animal care. Further-
more, scientists have to decide beforehand, how they want to use their samples, as
they might be a limiting factor. Modeling and simulation in itself is cheaper and faster
than laboratory experiments, as these are not factors that need to be considered. Of
course, one should always have some hypotheses prior to an experiment, but when
using simulations, it is easier to change the variables, or even change more variables
at a time. An example could be to change the meal sizes that were used in Figure 5.1
and 5.2. In this rather simple model, it does not take more than a couple of minutes
from changing the values until a new simulation of the breakdown of the metabolites
over e.g. 72 hours or 13 days can be seen. If this was to happen in experiments, one
would have to wait the 72 hours or 13 days, before being able to interpret the results.
In line with being able to change more variables at one time, it is also possible to test
situations that would not be possible in other types of experiments. For instance, if
a disease occurs in only 1 in 1 million people, it is almost impossible to get samples
enough to investigate the disease. If we where to have a complete model of the human
being, scientists would be able to simulate what would happen to the given patient,
and perhaps also what types of medicine would be most effective. Moreover, other-
wise unethical hypotheses could be examined, for instance the interactive effects on
multiple known toxins, or how our cells would react after 30 years of toxin exposure.
The same is true for experiments that would otherwise be very expensive to perform.
These could either be converted into complete in silico simulations, or simulations
could be used to find the optimal experimental conditions, reducing the risk of using
insuﬀicient experimental setups and preventing unethical experiments.
==PAGE:54==
6.3 Potential of In Silico Patients
47
An obvious issue when modeling is the potential lack of necessary data, as mentioned
in the section above. For a model to be useful, it has to have some input data, and
if the data is either not suﬀicient or too diﬀicult to obtain, the resulting model can
turn out poorly. Also, it is diﬀicult to investigate for instance the interaction be-
tween different metabolites, if the processes between them are not known beforehand.
Therefore, it might not only be diﬀicult to obtain the wanted data, there is also a
possibility that since we still do not know every aspect of every process occurring in
the human body, the model will lack important aspects in order to be precise.
In the scientific and medical world, ethics is always a subject that has to be con-
sidered when wanting to develop something new. Proposals for new experimental
setups or new drugs have to be approved by an ethics review committee beforehand.
This can potentially prolong the process of developing new treatments. This is true
for all sciences and drug developments, but there are also ethical considerations for
the use of modeling in medicine. First and foremost, one have to be sure that the data
used in the model live up to either the European General Data Protection Regulation
(GDPR) or the equivalent in other parts of the world. This include making sure that
the data is collected, processed, and distributed in accordance to GDPR, and these
requirements are even more strict when working with sensitive data, such as health
data. Moreover, this might complicate the use of data, since GDPR applies to all
residents of EU, irrespective of where the data are used. Therefore, the collection of
data from different parts of the world can be challenged [27, 28].
In addition to GDPR, doctors might meet other challenges when incorporating mod-
els and simulations into their treatment designs. Some examples have been seen in
other fields, where professionals start being overconfident in the model, thereby not
knowing whether to trust themselves and their experience or the models more. Also,
models used in non-medical fields have shown to discriminate against e.g. race and
age, due to biases in the data that was used to develop the model and train the algo-
rithms [27]. There is a possibility that such biases could inadvertently be built into
models and algorithms used in health care, since we sometimes have to distinguish
between factors such as age, gender, or race.
Another important thing to note is the trust and confidence patients have in their
doctors. Doctors presumably have to be more transparent and communicating, espe-
cially with some patient groups, when using mathematical modeling in the treatment.
Since many people are used to trusting medicine and more traditional treatment
strategies based on a doctor’s experience and different examinations, some might find
it diﬀicult to trust or understand. Moreover, the doctors have to have at least some
understanding in the results from the predictions to use them correctly. Therefore,
there have to be communication between the developers and their end users - both
doctors and patients [28].

6.4
The Future Perspective of Biological Modeling
There are great possibilities for using virtual human beings in both research and in
treatment of diseases. There are, however, still a long way before we have a model
that can accurately describe the human body, the biochemical processes, and their
interactions. The final goal of this type of research could be having a complete virtual
human being, with all of the known processes being incorporated in the model. The
development of a virtual human body is an iterative process alternating between gain-
ing a better understanding of the human body’s biochemical processes and improving
the model itself. Therefore a model can be useful even while it is being improved.
For instance, one could imagine defining a model, that could be used for a specific
disease, where only the processes known to affect said disease where incorporated. In
this way, one could simulate both patients and healthy individuals, as a way of vali-
dating the model. Furthermore, with the right parameters, clinical doctors could be
able to simulate how a patient with said disease should react on a specific treatment.
This allows using virtual clinical trials to investigate and improve development of e.g.
treatment strategies as well as the development of medicine. Since more parameters
can be investigated at once, there is potential of tailoring treatment strategies to
individual patients based on factors such as gender, age, and comorbidities [1, 2, 17],
a practice known as precision medicine.
The aim of precision medicine is to tailor treatment to the individual patients, or
patient groups, instead of administering the same treatment for all. Often, patient
groups are defined based on genomics and their genomes are used to study the re-
sponse to different drugs. This area is called pharmacogenomics, and it ensures that
both acquired and inherited genetic variations are taken into account when looking at
a specific drug response. At the time being, this concept is mostly used by dividing
patients into patient groups based on either diagnostic testing or their genomes [17,
29]. There is a huge potential in improving treatment for a lot of patients, if pharma-
cogenomics and mathematical modeling of the human being are combined. In this
way, doctors would not be limited to administering the treatment most optimal for a
given patient group, but would instead be able to design a treatment specifically to
one patient, based on model predictions. Though this could potentially save resources
and perhaps even lives, it is not necessarily possible in the current age. On top of the
fact that there is still a long way before we have a model that can be used in precision
medicine, another aspect that needs discussing is the ethical issues that may occur.
In the longer run, this new method of simulation may pose new ethical dilemmas.
These dilemmas are handled by ethics review committees, who have to approve both
experimentation and drugs beforehand. But since the predictions based on mathe-
matical models are neither an experiment nor a new drug that needs approval, some
issues may arise in determining when ethical review committees need to approve of
the predictions before incorporating the results in a treatment design.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This chapter will conclude the work performed in the project by outlining the key
findings. Additionally, it will identify the limitations and propose future works.
The overall aim of this project was to demonstrate how biochemical pathways, specif-
ically metabolic processes, can be described using mathematical modeling. During
the project, the two major metabolic pathways, glycolysis and citric acid cycle, was
described biochemically and their stoichiometric matrices, S, were defined. Further-
more, a model to describe the reaction rates and production rates of yeast glycolysis
was presented, as well as the potentials for the use of such model.
The human body is made up of different structures, such as organs, tissues, and
cells. Within our cells, a vast amount of biochemical reactions occur, some of which
are defined as the metabolic pathways. In order for a biochemical reaction to take
place, it has to be thermodynamically favorable.
The metabolic pathways make up a network of reactions, and to better understand
how the metabolites interact, we can use network reconstruction. The key element
of network reconstruction is to organize the biological information of the interactions
in a formal, mathematical manner. Furthermore, the networks can be described by
biochemical equations, where the relation between the reactants and the products are
explained. The stoichiometry of a reaction can be used to determine the quantitative
relations between products and reactants. The biochemical equations can be used to
define the stoichiometric matrix for a metabolic pathway. The matrices can be used in
combination with mathematical modeling to describe the often very complex reality
in a more condensed form. An example of a type of model is the use of whole-body
models. Here, the human body is seen as a unit, where interactions of metabolites
in different organs and tissues can be investigated. Several examples of whole-body
models been elaborated in this project.
Laboratory experiments can be complemented with modeling and simulation.
This approach give the ability to, among other things, optimize experimental setups,
analyze output based on the effect of different input, investigate treatment design, or
to test experimental design ideas. Since simplifications and assumptions often have
to made to describe the overall tendencies of e.g.
the metabolism, modeling and
simulation can only be used as a supplement to experiments and clinical trials, and
cannot stand alone.

7 Conclusion

Two important pathways in the conversion of glucose to energy have been de-
scribed in this project.
The first part, where glucose is converted to pyruvate is
called glycolysis. Here, one molecule of glucose is converted into 2 molecules of pyru-
vate in a series of 10 reactions. When pyruvate has been produced, it is converted
to acetyl CoA, and can then enter the citric acid cycle which consists of 8 reactions.
Both glycolysis and the citric acid cycle are essential in the production of ATP, the
main type of energy in our cells.
Next, available data in the literature describing the glycolytic reactions occurring
in the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae was used to define the reaction rates of 24 reac-
tions and the production rates of the 22 metabolites. The rates were saved in Matlab
as vectors r and R, respectively. All rate equations have been described in this project
along with the corresponding parameter values. The potential of such modeling was
explored by performing simulation using a whole-body model, the CWB model, de-
fined by other authors. Here, it was shown how the possibility of changing parameters
in the model can be used to predict metabolite concentrations over time. Further-
more, it was discussed how the model described in this project could be integrated
in the whole-body model used for simulations or any similar models. Additionally,
two approaches to how one could proceed from here was outlined, focusing on either
performing laboratory experiments on human cells to obtain the required data, or
integrating the model in the CWB model.
Lastly, the potentials in addition to the disadvantages of using in silico patients
were discussed. The discussion included how simulations can be used to save both time
and money compared to laboratory experiments or clinical trials, how scientists or
doctors could have the possibility to investigate otherwise impossible or very expensive
experimental setups, how the lack of suﬀicient data can affect the usage of modeling
and simulations, and lastly how some ethical issues may arise when using model-based
predictions in the medical world.
Future work would involve choosing one of the proposed approaches to either
obtain the necessary data on glycolysis from human cells based on experimentation, or
to include the described methodology in whole-body models defined by other authors.
It is expected that the methodology presented in this thesis can be used to explain
the reactions of glycolysis as well as other metabolic pathways in human cells.

APPENDIX D
Project Plan
Project plan
This is the preliminary project plan composed within the first month of the master’s
thesis project Modeling and Simulation of Metabolic Processes in Man. The project
plan was constructed within the first month of the project. Changes have been made
as the project proceeded, and a revised Gantt chart can be seen in Appendix E.
Project purpose
The project is conducted in order to better the possibilities for disease modeling
and simulation, specifically in relation to diabetes. The outcome of the project will
be relevant for DTU and researchers looking into the subject, companies which has
diabetes patients as their target group (such as Novo Nordic), and also people with
diabetes.
The project relates to SDG 3 - Good health and well-being.
Aims
The overall aim is to investigate how biochemistry and mathematical modeling can
be combined in order to better understand the metabolism of man. The expected out-
come of the project is a whole-body model describing selected biochemical processes
and organs. This model can be used for simulating different scenarios relevant for
understanding the metabolism in the healthy man as well as a person with diabetes.
Time plan
A preliminary time plan for this MSc project can be seen below in a Gantt chart.
The tasks in the Gantt chart are based on the activities mentioned in the project
description. The different tasks has been elaborated to the extent that is possible at
this stage of the project, and a more detailed Gantt chart is handed in as an Excel
sheet.
