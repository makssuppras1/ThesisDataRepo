Abstract 
Modelling of an existing WWTP consists of an indispensable part of the advanced 
management of the pant. It is an exceptionally useful tool for development of control 
strategies, process performance optimization and calibration (Rieger, et al., 2012). The 
reliability of each model is intrinsically interlinked with the quality of the model input which 
could include either historical, experimental data or generated data. Although, experimental 
data ensures model reliability and hence outmatch to the other data categories (Marsh K., 
1994). In literature, there are many reports regarding the use of ASM models for performance 
prediction of pilot and full-scale wastewater treatment, although there are only few reports 
on rigorous calibration of these models to real WWTP performance data.  
The objective of this thesis was to perform thorough characterization of the influent 
wastewater of Bio Denipho process at Lynetten WWTP according to Activated Sludge Model 
2d (ASM2d) and propose a calibration procedure. In order to determine diurnal variations of 
influent and effluent flows and their nutrient concentrations at Lynetten wastewater 
treatment plant, two intensive measurement campaigns were planned and performed 
according to the STOWA protocol. . The influent wastewater to the BioDenipho process was 
fractionated into model state variables according to ASM2d and Analytical analyses, used for 
the fractionation, were performed according to standard methods..  A simplified model of the 
full-scale BioDenipho process was established in WEST and the process performance of the 
simplified model was assessed using the produced data sets from the measurement 
campaigns. Finally, a calibration procedure of the full-scale BioDenipho process at Lynetten 
WWTP was proposed according to Good Modeling Practice (GMP). 
Characterization of the influent wastewater was performed for steady state and dynamic 
conditions. Fitting of the measured data for COD, TN, TP and TSS with the generated data by 
Langergraber model reveals good agreement. Subsequently, since the Langergraber model 
produces realistic influent data, many of the time consuming and expensive measurements 
performed  to produce the 24h fractionated data set could be avoided. A simplified model of 
the BioDenipho process performance was established in WEST. The model consists of one 
anaerobic Bio-P tank, three pairs of anoxic-anaerobic tanks in series, a secondary clarifier, a 
hydrolysis tank, internal recirculation (IR), sludge recirculation, and return sludge loops.   The 
three anoxic tanks are identical with a total volume of 1/3 of the total BioDenitro system. 
Thus, the three aerobic volumes which  also are identical have a total volume equal to 2/3 of 
the total BioDenitro system. The SRT of the system was found to be 29,2 d. Simulations shows 
that  the model achieved efficient organic and nitrogen removal of 90,6% and 98,7 %.  
However, optimization of the modelled enhanced biological phosphorus removal is required 
since achieved phosphorous removal only corresponded to approximately 33%. Finally, a 
calibration procedure for ammonium and phosphorous removal was proposed where S_NH4, 
S_NO and  S_PO4 are the target variables for the calibration procedure.

Chapter 1: Introduction 
 
After the initiation of the first mathematical modelling of the activated sludge process in 
1960s, various models for carbon, nitrogen and phosphorous removal have been developed 
(ASM1, ASM2, ASM2d and ASM3).  Their contribution to the  design, optimization and 
development of the wastewater treatment plants is unambiguously recognized. (Henze, 
Gujer, Takashi, & Van Loosdrecht, 2000) 
Modelling of an already existing wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) is an essential tool in 
the advanced management of a wastewater treatment plant. In particular, it can be used as a 
tool to investigate design alternatives, to implement control strategies, to improve plant 
operation and performance in terms of cost and effluent quality.  In other words, it is a well-
structured storage of  process knowledge (Rieger, et al., 2012). Subsequently, modelling of a 
treatment plant consists an indispensable part of  the process design and development. 
However, the output of any mathematical model can only be as good as the assumptions and 
input to the model. Therefore, the quality of the model input data is a critical factor during 
modelling work. Historical, generated or experimental data could be used as input of the 
model. Although, experimental data ensures model reliability and hence outmatch to the 
other data categories (Marsh K., 1994).  
Typically both influent and effluent values for Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD), Chemical 
Oxygen Demand (COD), Nitrogen (N), Phosphorous (P) and Solids fractions are used as input 
for model calibration. During calibration process, several iterations of the model parameters 
are conducted until the simulated data will be similar with an experimental data set. According 
to good modelling practice, a calibration procedure consists of four main steps: a) 
Identification of the target, calibrated variables and the stop criteria b) Running of  several 
simulations and checking  if the stop criteria are fulfilled c)Adjust calibrated parameters and 
compare simulated and experimental data until an agreement is reached d) Model validation. 
When stop criteria are not reach (step c) adjustments of the hydrodynamics, influent recycle 
stream characteristics, settling, aeration model and biokinetic parameters are performed 
(Rieger, et al., 2012). It is obvious that the reliability of the model output is proportional to 
the reliability and the accuracy of the experimental data. Hence, it is important that the 
experimental data set must be correctly determined before any model assessment  or 
calibration is performed.  
In literature, there are many reports regarding the use of ASM models for performance 
prediction of pilot and full-scale wastewater treatment, although there are only few reports 
on rigorous calibration of these models to real WWTP performance data. 
This master thesis project will focus on good modeling practice step (ii) to achieve correct 
characterization of the influent wastewater. In accordance with guidelines for modelling of 
activated sludge models two comprehensive measurement campaigns of minimum 2x24 hour 
sampling of 2 hours composite samples will be performed. Following, assessment of the plant 
process performance will be conducted by simulating the plant under steady states and 
dynamic conditions in DHI’s modelling software WEST.  Finally, calibration of the plant 
according to GMP Unified protocol will be attempted.

The Master Thesis project is realized within the LaGas project funded by the Danish Council 
for Strategic Research (http://www.lagas.dk/). The project is focusing on diagnostics, 
monitoring and mitigation  of the laughing  greenhouse gas (N2O) emissions from wastewater 
treatment plants. N20 is a greenhouse gas 300 times stronger than carbon dioxide (CO2) and  
ozone destroying chemical. In particular, the master thesis will be used in WP2 of the project. 
This project includes five PhD students and one Post Doc with the final and overall scientific 
objective to develop tools to estimate, model and predict N2O emissions from full-scale 
WWTPs and to find operation strategies to mitigate N2O emissions. 
1.2 Objectives 
In order to fulfill the scientific challenges of model calibration this MSc thesis aims to: 
 
(i) 
Plan and perform several intensive measurement campaigns (2-4) to determine 
the hydrodynamic characteristics and collect influent, effluent samples to and 
from a well-defined set of tanks from the full-scale BioDenipho process at 
Lynetten WWTP.  
(ii) 
Perform analytical analyses according to standard methods to fractionate 
influent, effluent, and reactor COD, N, P, and X into suitable components, and 
translate them into ASM2d compatible state variables with due attention to 
variability and precision.  
(iii) 
Provide high quality data set for ASM2d modelling of the BioDenipho process. 
(iv) 
Establish a simplified model of the full-scale BioDenipho process at Lynneten 
WWTP in WEST.  
(v) 
Evaluate the performance of the BioDenipho process model in WEST. 
(vi) 
Use the produced data set to calibrate an established simulation model of the 
full-scale BioDenipho process at Lynetten WWTP – focusing on agreement with 
solids retention times, solids inventory, and C, N, and P removal. 
(vii) 
Additional tests will be performed if default stoichiometric and biokinetic 
parameters for target processes do not predict effluent values within the 
specified error range for each variable.  
 
At the end of the report a comparative study   will be conducted with the performance data 
from Lynetten and the respective data from our home countries (Greece and Ghana) 
wastewater treatment plants.

2.1 Lynetten WWTP 
Lynetten WWTP is the largest wastewater treatment plant in Denmark located in Copenhagen 
with a catchment area of 76 km2. It is operated since 1980. In the beginning, it was a landfill 
in the north part of Refshaloen Island which was removing organic waste in the sewage 
through oxygenation. In 1987, the Aquatic Environment Plan specified the need for the plant 
expansion. Finally, in 1997 a modernised plant which was removing nitrogen and phosphorous 
removal, through biological and chemical process was put into operation. Gentofte, 
Frederiksberg, Gladsaxse, Lyngby-Taarbæk and Copenhagen are the five municipalities which 
are owned in Lynnetten WWTP. The treated wastewater is discharged at Øresund. Table 1 
shows the design characteristics of the plant (lynettefællesskabet, n.d.).

2.1.2 Wastewater treatment plant process  
2.1.2.1 WWTP  layout and flow diagram 
Figure 2 displays Lynetten WWTP flowgram. The system includes a primary mechanical 
treatment and a secondary biological treatment for nutrient removal. The primary one, 
consists of a sand gritting, sand filters and a primary clarifier. Thus, the secondary comprises 
of a Bio-P tank, a Biodenipho system and a secondary clarifier. It is important to mention that 
before and after the BioDenipho system, phosphorous removal is enhanced through iron salt 
addition (lynettefællesskabet, n.d.) . 
 
Figure 2: Flow diagram of Lynetten WWTP (lynettefællesskabet, n.d.). 
Primary treatment: grid chamber, sand and grease trap, primary clarifier 
In the primary treatment, particles larger than 6 mm are removing out of the system in the 
grid chamber where the screened material is collected and transported in 
Amagerforbrænding incinerator. Following, the sand and the grease are collected through the 
sand and grease trap, where the sand is dewatered and transported for external re treatment 
and reuse, while the grease is transferred to the digester. In the last step of the primary 
treatment, finer suspended material is settled in the bottom of the primary clarifier. 
Afterwards, the primary sludge which is removed with scrappers from the bottom, is 
thickened and  sent to Lynetten digester for biogas production.

Secondary treatment: Bio-P tank, Chemical salt addition, BioDenipho proceess, secondary 
clarifier 
The secondary treatment  consists of an anerobic Bio-P tank, a Bio Denipho process and a 
secondary clarifier. 
Bio-P tank 
The first part of the secondary treatment consists of an anaerobic Bio-P tank were hydrolisis 
of COD to volatile fatty acids (VFAs) is performed. The produced VFA are consumed by 
phosphorous accumulating organisms (PAOs) which are conevrted into intracellular poly- β-
hydroxyalkanoates (PAHs). The energy demanded for this reaction is ensured by the use of 
the intracellular stored polyphosphates into orthophosphate which have been released as 
orthophosphate. Subsequently, the PHA storage in the bacterial cell is augmenting as the 
polyphosphate concentration diminishes in the Bio-P reactor. Hence, an enhanced biological 
phosphorous removal is achieved since the effluent ortho phosphorus concentration in the 
anaerobic zone is 2-3 times higher than the respective one in the influent (Metcalf & Eddy., 
2003).  
BioDenipho process 
In the BioDenipho reactors, actual phosphorous uptake is taken place under interchanged 
aerobic and anoxic conditions where the PAOs metabolize their PHAs storage used for cellular 
growth. The releasing energy during PHA oxidation is used to form intracellular polyphosphate 
bonds which afterwards they removing the soluble phosphate (PO4-P) from the water phase. 
Hence, by the sludge recirculation in the anaerobic Bio-P tank, the stored Poly-PO4 is used 
again for VFA storage. Finally, the stored phosphate is removed from the system by the 
wastage in the secondary clarifier (Water, 2014)  
Simultaneously, organic carbon and nitrogen biological removal is achieved through 
nitrification and denitrification. Figure 3 describes how nitrogen removal is achieved through 
the BioDenitro process. Interchanged nitrification and denitrification. According to figure 3 an 
operation cycle following the flow direction is performed where nitrification and 
denitrification are interchanged between the two identical tanks. During aerobic conditions 
autotrophic nitrification is taken place in two steps t by two group of autotrophic bacteria 
called nitrifiers (Metcalf & Eddy., 2003):

Secondary clarifier 
After the secondary clarifier, the sludge  produced is recirculating  in the beginning of the 
anaerobic Bio-P tank where the stored Poly-PO4 is used again for VFA storage.  While, the 
wastage is dewatered and sent to the Lynetten digester for biogas production. Finally, the 
treated wastewater is released one kilometer from the coastline into the Oresund. 
Sludge treatment 
Both primary and activated sludge are thickened and stored before being digested. After the 
digestion, the sludge is  firstly dewatered and then mixed with biosolids from Damhusåen 
WWTP  before the incineration. The sludge treatment consists of threem main parts:  


during the specific time period. Table 3 illustrates experimental methods which are used for

The reliability of a plant model depends from two factors: the influent wastewater 
fractionation and the model calibration. In particular, these two factors are as critical for the 
system performance as the design of it. The detail of wastewater fractionation could be low 
or high depending on the purpose of use. Generally a more detailed fractionation increases 
the reliability of the model output. Subsequently, the use of the model (e.g. teaching, design, 
control strategy or optimization) defines the complexity of the wastewater characterization 
procedure (Henze, Gujer, Takashi, & Van Loosdrecht, 2000), (Rieger, et al., 2012).

soluble and particulate is through micro - filtration with 0.1 μm (Roeleveld P.J, 2002) or with

2.2.3 Suspended solids (SS) fractionation 
Total suspended solids is a significant variable consisting of an inorganic part (ISS) and 
volatile (VSS) one. Figure 8 illustrates the theoretical SS fractionation approach. Generally, 
three modelling approaches have been developed for TSS (Rieger, et al., 2012): 
 COD based modelling approach where TSS is determined based on the particulate COD 
concentration and a measured conversion factor of TSS/COD ratio. This modelling 
approach has an application in ASM1. 
 TSS is used as a state variable during the simulation. ASM2d and ASM3 are examples of 
implementation of this modelling approach. 
 ISS is used as state variable during the simulation. TSS concentration is defined based on 
the ISS concentration and the measured influent COD and VSS concentrations. It is 
important to mention that ISS accumulation is analogous to the sludge age of the model.

2.4 Calibration methodologies for activated sludge models 
A number of systematic protocols for Activated Sludge Model (ASM) used for calibration, 
incorporating wastewater characterization protocols have been developed based on the 
prerequisites of mathematical modelling of activated sludge systems. Model calibration is the 
process of modifying a set of input parameters until a simulated set of data and a measured 
one are varied in an acceptable error range.  Broadly, five main protocols were established 
and used by researcher around the world: the STOWA protocol (Hulsbeek et.al., 2002) the 
WERF protocol (Melcer et al., 2003), the BIOMATH protocol (Vanrolleghem et al., 2003), and 
the Hochschulgruppe guidelines (Langergraber et al., 2004). 
The use of each protocols depends on the modelling purpose. Some of them are more 
practical or simplified, thus some other are more complex and academic orientated. Hence, 
there is not a consensus one used. 
 
==PAGE:25==

2.4.1 STOWA protocol 
An unambiguous protocol has been developed by the Dutch Foundation of Applied Water 
Research (STOWA) in 2002. The objective of this protocol was to provide standardized 
guidelines for dynamic modelling of the activated sludge system in a way that quality control 
is ensured. Based on the ASM 1, the protocol mainly focuses on the influent characterisation, 
model structure and the calibration process of a full scale WWTP. Figure 1 illustrates the main 
structure of the protocol.  It consists of nine different phases.  While the protocol structures 
varies according to the objectives and the type of study. In particular, three main model 
studies are distinguished: Optimisation studies of the current WWTP, development of control 
strategies for current and new WWTPs and determination of the most suitable system choice 
during the design of a new plant (Hulsbeek et.al., 2002). As it is expected, the monitoring 
frequency and the level of performance of the different units that should be satisfied, are 
higher in case of control strategies.

The alkalinity of the wastewater is defined by a titration with sulphuric acid. During this

the water sample is titrated with a sulphuric acid with an addition of indicator changing colour 
at pH 4.5 (more detailed description in appendix A). It is important to mention that for the 
manual titration the limit of detection for alkalinity ranges between 0.1 and 10 mmol/l.

3.2.1.4 BOD-Automatic measurement system –Oxitop 
An automatic measurement system called Oxitop is used for the experimental determination 
of BOD concentration over time. The system includes a series of measuring heads which 
register oxygen depletion from a sample due to the aerobic degradation of the carbonaceous 
substances. This method analysis generates a BOD degradation curve which is used afterwards

out suppressed ion exclusion chromatography. 
3.2.2 Sample preservation and storage 
Glass bottles are preferred over plastic ones for chemical analyses and plastic bottles for 
biological analyses. To minimize potential for volatilization or biodegradation between 
sampling and analysis, samples must be kept as cool as possible without freezing.  Samples 
must be packed in crushed or cubed ice or commercial ice substitutes before transportation 
to the laboratory. Dry ice must be avoided because it will freeze samples and cause glass 
containers to break. It might also affect the pH of samples. Samples must be stored at 4℃. 
Furthermore, it is important that the bottles are totally filled with no air inside in order to 
prevent alkalinity changes when sample is mixed in the laboratory (American Public Health 
Association, American Water Works Association, Water Environment Federation, 1999)

3.4 Wastewater characterization according to ASM2d. 
ASM 1 model does not include the relationship between nitrogen and biological phosphorous 
removal. Hence, ASM 2 model has been developed including the chemical and biological 
phosphorous removal. However, ASM2 does not include the fact that PAOS organisms can 
store internally products used for denitrification. For this reason an extension of ASM2, 
ASM2d has been developed including the anoxic uptake of phosphorous.  Subsequently, since 
BioDenipho process at Lynetten WWTP includes organic, nitrogen and phosphorous removal, 
the fractionation  was performed according to ASM2d. Please refer to appendix II for the 
matrix of COD, N and P fractionation according to ASM2d (Henze, Gujer, Takashi, & Van 
Loosdrecht, 2000). 
3.5 Langergraber model 
Langergraber model is used to generate diurnal variations of wastewater concentrations and 
flows.  Using input data from measurement campaign in BioDenipho process, diurnal 
variations are generated which are compared with the respective measured values 
(Langergraber G., 2006). Subsequently, a first calibrating approach of the BioDenipho process 
model is achieved.

4.2.1 Langergraber model for the BioDenipho system 
Dynamic simulations are important for the design or the optimization of the activated sludge 
systems.  The input variables demand information about  influent flows,  concentrations of 
COD, TSS, TKN from 24hour composite samples. However, the collection of these information 
demand measurement campaign which is the most time and resources consuming part of a

4.5 Calibration method   
According to  Good Modeling Practice described in subchapter 2.4.5, the data collection and 
the  model set- up phases, are followed by model calibration and validation, which is the last 
step regarding the modeling of a WWTP.  
The first three steps, planning and  performing of two measurement campaigns (data 
collection) and setting up of the WEST model were conducted. Regarding the last step, the 
calibration procedure was also planned based on the GMP Unified Protocol,  but it could not 
be completed due to the limited time schedule and lack of specific, yet necessary, data ( WAS 
mass/load, TPWAS, TNWAS e.t.c).  
In particular, the sludge characteristics were not available. According to the plant operators,  
Lynetten WWTP did not have a database for those characteristics. Subsequently, further  
sludge measurements were demanded, and the conduction of such extra measurements 
would have dropped the current thesis out of time schedule. Nevertheless, a detailed 
calibration procedure is described in the following scheme for future use. Based on the 
available data, process performance of the BioDenipho process plant  is evaluated which is 
elaborated in the following subchapter. 
Calibration of BioDenipho process model at Lynetten WWTP is performed based on good 
modelling practice described meticulously in subchapter 2.4.5. According to GMP Unified 
Protocol, an iterative procedure for BioDenipho process model at Lynetten WWTP is 
developed and presented in figure 35. This approach aims to firstly calibrate the sludge 
production. The target parameters for the calibration process are the Seffluent_NH4, Seffluent_NO and 
Seffluent_PO4 whereas the stop criteria are displayed in figure 35.  
 Firstly, it is important to establish a solid balance in the system in order to obtain the correct 
effluent TSS concentration, COD, Nitrogen and Phosphorous removal processes. Then, 
calibrating the nitrogen removal of the process by changing either the DO set points or the 
volumes of the aeration tanks until there is a match between the measured and the simulated 
ammonium and nitrate concentrations. Finally, calibrating of phosphorous removal by 
changing the hydrolysis constant rate.

4.4.1 Correlation of the biomass to process performance during steady states 
Figures 40 and 42 illustrate the bacterial growth of autotrophic and heterotrophic bacteria. 
According to the literature the autotrophic bacteria which are considered slowly growing have 
a maximum growth rate of 0,44 g VSS/ g VSS at 12oC. While the heterotrophic bacteria have a 
maximum growth rate of 3,49 g VSS/ g VSS at 12oC (Metcalf & Eddy., 2003).  
The autotrophs growth rate in  figure 40 is in accordance with the literature. The bacteria have 
a lag phase of around 100 days before they start growing and reach steady states 
concentration at around 123, 5 mg/l. While the heterotrophic bacteria have an exponential 
growth rate until they reach a concentration of approximately 1695 mg/l within 30 days. 
Afterwards, they decreased exponentially and stay in a lag phase for around 50 days. Finally 
they start growing exponentially again and reach steady states concentration of 1495 mg/l at 
around 20 days. It is important to mention that the fact that  autotrophic bacteria needs more 
time to growth in the aerobic reactors  explains why the sludge retention time is calculated 
based on the aerobic reactor.  
Correlating figures 40 and 41 it is obvious that the ammonium concentration is decreasing  at 
the end of the autotrophic growth lag phase. Autotrophs are growing by consuming 
ammonium. Nitrification is taken place. In particular, ammonia is oxidized into nitrite and 
nitrate which is  also mirrored in figure 43 where nitrate production take places right after the 
ammonium oxidation. Afterwards at around 50 days both concentrations reach steady states.

4.5.1 Correlation of the biomass to process performance during dynamic 
conditions 
Figures 49, 51, 53 and 54 display the bacteria growth during dynamic conditions. It is evident 
that when rainfall occurs, hydraulic load is increasing and the bacteria concentrations are 
decreasing. It is important to mention, that the overload of the secondary clarifier is caused 
by the augmenting hydraulic load. Hence, retention time is reducing. 
Correlating figure 42 with figure 43 it can be seen that the autotrophs reductions during the 
rain event results in an inefficient ammonium removal. Before the rainfall (from t=0 d until 
around t=0,6 d) there is a good correlation between the ammonium growth and the 
autotrophs bacteria. In particular, ammonium is oxidized to nitrate when autotrophs are 
growing. As can be seen, when there are enough autotrophs bacteria, nitrification is taken 
place. Subsequently, ammonium is nitrified. However, when rainfall occurs many if the 
biomass is lost with obvious consequence in the plant removal efficiency.  
The effects of biomass loss during the rain event and the impacts in nitrogen removal are also 
mirrored in figures 50 and 52. Specifically, before the rainfall event the denitrification seems 
to be efficient as the heterotrophs growth coincides with nitrate removal.  But after the 
rainfall, denitrification capacity is reducing.  
Generally, increasing of heterotrophs results in a decreasing of nitrate in the anoxic tanks. 
However, since heterotrophic bacteria are also removing carbon from the system there is not 
direct correlation in the dynamic phase between the nitrate and heterotrophs concentrations 
after 0, 7 days.

Chapter 5: Conclusion 
Two intensive measurement campaigns were successfully conducted.  Statistical analysis of 
the data and plausibility checks shows that the obtained data are accurate and reliable. 
Furthermore, an efficient laboratory schedule was organized in order to handle the high 
amount of samples and analysis which could be used in the future in other measurement 
campaigns where the amount of samples is huge.  
Experimental assessment of the Bio Denipho process plant shows that the performance of the 
plant is satisfied for organic, nitrogen and phosphorous removal and meet the Danish 
discharge criteria. Total COD effluent concentration is found 79,1 ±67 mg/l , TN 5,42 ± 4,5  and 
TP 1,21± 0,5 mg/l. Generally, almost all concentrations are in accordance with the values 
found in literature.  
Correct characterization of the influent wastewater of BioDenipho process plant at Lynetten 
WWTP was achieved according to ASM2d model. While comparing the measured influent data 
and the generated data based on Lagergraber   
Fitting the Langergraber model to two experimentally obtained 24-hour profiles has revealed 
good agreement between modelled and measured parameters, Subsequently, since 
Langergraber model produces realistic influent data, many of the time consuming  
measurements performed for collection could be  influent data could be eliminated. 
A simplified model of the BioDenipho process performance was established and assessed in 
WEST using the results of the measurement campaign. The model consists of one anaerobic 
Bio-P tank, three pairs of anoxic-anaerobic tanks in series, a secondary clarifier, a hydrolysis 
tank, internal recirculation (IR), sludge recirculation, and return sludge loops.  Evaluation of 
the performance of the simplified model show that the model achieved efficient organic and 
nitrogen removal of 90,6% and 98,7 %.  However, optimization of the Enhanced Biological 
Removal is demanded since phosphorous removal is around 33%. 
 Finally, an iterative calibration procedure is proposed according to Good modelling practice. 
In particular, ammonium, nitrate and phosphate concentrations are the target variable. Firstly 
well establishment of the sludge production is proposed and then nitrogen removal 
calibration by manipulating either DO concentrations in the aerobic tank or the volume tanks.  
To sum up, this thesis is a contribution to the ongoing La Gas project funded by the Danish 
Council for Strategic Research. During the thesis N2O measurements were performed at the 
same time. The influent fractionation will be used to calibrate the BioDenipho and afterwards 
a calibration including N2O measurements will be performed.

4.   Concentration range / limit of detection

5. Principle of analysis: 
Alkalinity:  
The ability of water to react with hydrogen ions. The alkalinity is determined by a titration 
with diluted sulphuric acid. During the titration the present weak bases are transformed to 
the corresponding weak acids.  
Under normal conditions, the major part of the bases will be the ions from the carbonate 
system: 
pH 6 –10:    HCO3- 
pH  > 10:    CO3-- 
 
Total alkalinity:  
The amount of acid, which will be needed to change the pH-value from the present pH to pH 
4,5. It is measured in milli equivalents/ litre H3O+ (meq/l) and at the endpoint  pH 4,5 all the 
ions from the carbonate system are at CO2-form. 
 
Determination of total-alkalinity (TAL): 
In this schedule, there are 2 different methods to determinate TAL. The determination can 
be a simple manual titration with an indicator or a potentiometric end-point titration: 
 
1. Manuel titration with an indicator: 
The sample is titrated with sulphuric acid with an added indicator with a colour change at pH 
4,5. This method will often do, but if there are many samples or the sample is turbid or 
coloured, it is not suitable. 
==PAGE:72==
Technical University of Denmark – Department of Environmental Engineering 
[15] 
 
 
2. TAL after the Gran-method: 
 
When the TAL level is very low < 0,1 mmol/l , a titration after the Gran-method is the most 
precisely method. After titration to pH 4,5 the H+-concentration will increase after a linear 
function, with the added ml acid. From this linear function the equality point can be 
determined more precisely. 
 
6.   Interference / precision / restrictions / suitability. 
Other bases which can react with H +-ions will contribute to the TAL result, fx ortho-
phosphate, ammonia, borate, acetate, sulphide, humus.

3. Principle of analysis 
This automated procedure for the determination of nitrate and nitrite, uses the procedure 
whereby nitrate is reduced to nitrite by a copper-cadmium column redactor coil at a pH at 
8,0. The nitrite ion then reacts with sulphanilamide under acidic conditions to form a diazo 
compound. This compound then couples with N-1-naphthylenediamine dihydrochloride to 
form a reddish-purple azo-dye, which is colorimetric measured at 550 nm. 
 
A dialyzer for the high range eliminates interference from coloured samples and suspended 
solids.
