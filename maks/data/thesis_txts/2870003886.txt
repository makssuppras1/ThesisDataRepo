Abstract
Advancements in clinical sequencing have led to the identification of genetic variants
at an unprecedented rate, resulting in the accumulation of numerous variants of uncertain
significance (VUS) or conflicting interpretations. However, determining their functional con-
sequences remains a major challenge. The BCDX2 tumor suppressor complex—comprising
RAD51B, RAD51C, RAD51D, and XRCC2—serves as a key example due to the large num-
ber of uncharacterized variants reported in its components. This complex plays a pivotal
role in homologous recombination (HR), a pathway critical for maintaining genomic stabil-
ity, and its disruption has been associated with increased cancer susceptibility. This study
integrates structural dynamics, stability assessments, and protein-protein interaction (PPI)
analyses to elucidate the functional impact of emerging BCDX2 clinical variants.
To achieve this, the analysis centered on two key factors: stability and PPIs. Both
aspects were systematically assessed using MAVISp, a structure-based framework developed
by the Cancer Structural Biology group. Stability predictions were refined through molecular
dynamics simulations, which enabled the characterization of dynamic patterns essential for
understanding protein function. Capturing conformational flexibility is crucial for assessing
structural integrity, as proteins exhibit inherent motion that can influence stability. Char-
acterizing these dynamic behaviors enabled a more precise evaluation of stability changes
induced by variants.
While stability plays a crucial role in protein function, understanding PPIs is equally
essential, as proteins rarely act in isolation. Despite their importance, PPIs are often over-
looked in computational variant effect predictions, even though disruptions in these networks
can significantly alter biological function. Enhancements to MAVISp have been introduced
to facilitate interactor selection and streamline the subsequent analysis of local interac-
tions. The developed workflow was applied to BCDX2 components to dissect the complex’s
inter-chain interactions. Analysis revealed several mutations clustering in critical regions
mediating inter-subunit interactions.
Several of these mutations have been associated with cancer, reinforcing the importance
of incorporating PPI analyses in variant effect predictions. However, findings also demon-
strate that this facet alone is insufficient for a comprehensive assessment of variants. Instead,
PPI assessments should be integrated with stability and other functional layers to establish
a more complete and accurate assessment of uncharacterized reported variants.

Introduction
Advancements in clinical sequencing have dramatically increased the identification of
genetic variants, including those associated with cancer[34].
However, while sequencing
technologies have progressed rapidly, our ability to interpret their functional consequences
has lagged behind[34]. This discrepancy has resulted in a surplus of detected variants clas-
sified as Variants of Uncertain Significance (VUS) or exhibiting conflicting interpretations
in clinical databases, posing a major challenge in structural biology and the identification
of clinically relevant mutations [13]. To bridge this gap and manage the vast amount of se-
quenced data, computational frameworks integrating structural and functional annotations
are essential for systematically predicting the effects of the reported genetic variations.
1.1
MAVISp Framework
One such approach is MAVISp (Multi-layered Assessment of Variants by Structure for
Proteins)[5]. MAVISp is a modular framework developed by the Cancer Structural Biology
group to evaluate the effects of amino acid substitutions on protein function and structural
stability, aiming to link missense mutations to molecular mechanisms. It operates in two
modes: the Simple Mode, which uses a single conformation of the protein, and the En-
semble Mode, which analyzes conformational ensembles generated through methods such
as molecular dynamics and mitigates the inherent limitations of static structures. In either
mode, the MAVISp framework is structured in a modular manner, enabling users to perform
independent predictions across distinct aspects of protein behavior. Each module is designed
to assess the impact of a specific amino acid substitution on a different dimension of protein
function and structural stability.
The framework integrates two categories of modules: structure selection modules,
which assist in identifying and preparing input structural data, and structural analysis
modules, which assess the effects of mutations on protein properties (Figure 1). From the
former category, the Interactome Module is of primary interest in this study, while from
the latter, the Stability and Local Interaction modules will be thoroughly discussed.
1. Interactome Module: This Module identifies protein interactors for a target protein
and retrieves structural models of their complexes. It integrates data from sources such
as the Mentha database [14], the Protein Data Bank (PDB) [8], and AlphaFold (AF)
[73] models that have experimentally been validated.
2. Stability Module: This Module evaluates the impact of mutations on protein folding
stability through Gibbs folding free energy changes (∆∆G). Gibbs folding free energy
quantifies the thermodynamic stability of the fold of a protein and is defined as:
∆G = ∆H −T∆S
where ∆H is the enthalpy change, and T∆S represents the entropy change at temper-
ature T. The ∆∆G is calculated as the difference in Gibbs free energy between the
mutant and the wild-type protein:

Other modules include the Post-Translational Modification (PTM) module, which in-
vestigates the impact of mutations on phosphorylation sites considering structural, func-
tional and regulatory consequences, the Long-Range Effects Module, which predicts allosteric
changes and distal mutation effects and the quantitative analysis of ensembles of structures
referred to as Functional Dynamics Module. In addition to structural analyses, MAVISp pro-
vides annotations from pathogenicity predictors, including AlphaMissense[18], and predictors
for loss/gain of function such as DeMask[57] and GEMME[49]. These annotations, combined
with structural metrics, secondary structure information, side-chain solvent accessibility, and
early folding predictions, provide a comprehensive framework for prioritizing variants for ex-
perimental validation. Additionally, they offer a systematic approach to addressing the large
number of Variants of Uncertain Significance (VUS) and conflicting variant interpretations,
aiding in their functional characterization and potential clinical reclassification.
1.2
BCDX2: A Case of Highly Uncharacterized Variants
One example that manifests the challenge of unclassified variants is the BCDX2 com-
plex, a tetrameric protein assembly involved in homologous recombination (HR) for DNA
repair [37]. MAVISp [5] curation of the four RAD51 paralogs that constitute this complex—
RAD51B, RAD51C, RAD51D, and XRCC2 [63]—revealed that the vast majority of single-
substitution missense variants recorded in ClinVar, a public database that aggregates in-
formation about genomic variation and its relationship to human health [50], remain vari-
ants of uncertain significance or exhibit conflicting evidence. Specifically, as shown in Fig-
ure 2, RAD51B (78%), RAD51C (98%), RAD51D (99%), and XRCC2 (96%) possess an
overwhelming proportion of unclassified or ambiguously annotated variants. Mutations in
the genes encoding RAD51B, RAD51C, and RAD51D are commonly identified in cancer
patients[62, 35], while variants in RAD51C and XRCC2 are linked to Fanconi anemia, a
cancer-prone syndrome[43, 60]. The combination of their established links to disease and
the high burden of unclassified variants in these genes renders the need of structural and
functional assessments paramount.

Understanding the biological function and structural organization of BCDX2 is essential
for deciphering the potential impact of its numerous reported variants. BCDX2 functions

as a tumor suppressor[37], a class of proteins that prevent uncontrolled proliferation and
genomic instability by regulating DNA repair, cell cycle checkpoints, or apoptosis. Loss of
tumor suppressor function due to mutations can result in defective DNA repair, accumulation

BCDX2, in particular, plays a critical role in Homologous Recombination (HR), the
primary pathway for repairing double-strand breaks (DSBs), one of the most severe forms of
DNA damage [63]. During HR, DSB ends are processed to generate long 3’ single-stranded
DNA (ssDNA) extensions, which serve as a platform for the recruitment of repair machinery.
Initially, replication protein A (RPA) coats the ssDNA to stabilize it and prevent secondary
structure formation [59]. Subsequently, the recombinase RAD51, recruited by BRCA2, re-
places RPA and assembles into a presynaptic filament that facilitates homology search for
accurate repair [59].
The RPA-to-RAD51 exchange, crucial for HR and replication fork
protection, is significantly mediated by the tumor suppressor BCDX2 [37] (Figure 4).

Structurally, the BCDX2 subunits-RAD51B, RAD51C, RAD51D, and XRCC2-share
conserved RecA-like folds, similar to other RAD51 paralogs[37]. These folds contain three
nucleotide-binding motifs: the Walker A and B motifs and a "lysine finger" Adenosine

RAD51B, RAD51C, and RAD51D possess a conserved globular N-terminal domain (NTD)
with an α-helical bundle structure, which is notably absent in XRCC2. Cryo-Electron Mi-
croscopy (Cryo-EM) studies have resolved the structures of these NTDs (RAD51B, RAD51C,
and RAD51D), along with three of the four C-terminal folds in the complex (RAD51C,
RAD51D, and XRCC2) (Figure 5a) [37, 63].

1.4
The Need for Structural and Dynamic Analysis
Further experimental evidence supports the highly mobile nature of RAD51B. Specifi-
cally, negative-stain electron microscopy (NS-EM) 2D class averages revealed a mobile feature
associated with a structured core [37]. Limited proteolysis with chymotrypsin resulted in the
complete loss of RAD51B detection by Western blotting, coinciding with the disappearance
of the mobile density in NS-EM 2D class averages. These findings indicate that the mobile
density corresponds to the RAD51B C-terminal domain (CTD), suggesting a high degree of
conformational flexibility [37].
Proteins are inherently dynamic macromolecules, and their conformational flexibility
is crucial for biological function[48]. This dynamic behavior significantly influences protein
stability and protein-protein interactions (PPIs), which must be considered when assessing
the impact of genetic variants. However, conventional structural biology techniques, such as
X-ray crystallography and Cryo-EM, provide only static snapshots of protein structures[42],
often failing to capture conformational heterogeneity-particularly in highly flexible proteins
like RAD51B.
To address these limitations, Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations provide a powerful
computational approach to study protein motion at atomic resolution[42]. MD simulations
model protein behavior over time by numerically solving Newton’s equations of motion for
each atom in the system[42]. By incorporating realistic biophysical conditions, these simu-
lations allow for the exploration of protein flexibility, conformational changes, and transient
structural states that may be critical for function.
Given the high flexibility observed in RAD51B[37], MD simulations provide a valuable
tool to complement structural studies by capturing its dynamic nature and comparing it to
the seemingly more rigid subunits of BCDX2, such as RAD51C. More broadly, integrating
MD simulations into variant effect prediction frameworks, such as MAVISp, enhances struc-
tural stability assessments by overcoming the limitations of static conformations[5]. This is

particularly relevant since MAVISp’s Simple Mode assumes limited backbone movement in

1.5
Protein-Protein Interactions in BCDX2 Variant Identification
Given that the BCDX2 complex functions as a tetramer, the stability of interactions
between its subunits is critical for its role in homologous recombination (HR)[63]. Disruptions
at these interfaces could compromise complex formation, ultimately impairing HR efficiency.
Therefore, a key aspect of this study is to determine whether missense variants alter these
interactions and, by extension, affect the overall stability and function of the complex.
Proteins rarely act in isolation; instead, they engage in extensive networks of protein-
protein interactions (PPIs) that regulate essential cellular processes, including DNA repair,
signal transduction, and metabolic regulation [53]. Beyond their functional associations,
such as participation in the same metabolic pathway, proteins also interact physically, either
directly or indirectly [69]. These physical interactions can be transient, modulating enzy-
matic activity and signaling cascades, or stable, forming molecular assemblies that persist
throughout a protein’s functional lifetime [53]. Regardless of their nature, characterizing
protein complexes at the structural level is critical for understanding their biological roles
and evaluating how genetic alterations may influence their function.
Despite the fundamental importance of PPIs, the number of experimentally resolved
protein complex structures remains limited. Fewer than 5% of known human PPIs have
been structurally characterized [12], making it challenging to evaluate how genetic variants
affect binding affinities and interface stability. This scarcity of high-resolution structural
data necessitates computational approaches to retrieve high confidence interaction data and
model good quality protein complexes in silico.
To mitigate these constraints, this study aims to enhance the coverage and confidence in
retrieved interaction datasets by expanding the scope of the existing Interactome Module of
MAVISp through the incorporation of additional PPI data sources. While the current imple-
mentation relies on the Mentha database [14], this work will integrate data from STRING [68]
and develop a workflow to streamline the retrieval and analysis of protein complex structures.
The following section provides an overview of the two PPI databases that will be em-
ployed in this workflow.
1.6

1.6.1
Mentha Database
Mentha [14] is a comprehensive resource for protein-protein interaction (PPI) data, in-
tegrating manually curated interactions from multiple primary databases that adhere to the
International Molecular Exchange (IMEx) consortium standards. These include MINT [16],
IntAct [40], DIP [74], MatrixDB [17], and BioGRID [66]. By following IMEx guidelines,

Mentha ensures consistency in data integration through standardized interaction vocabular-
ies (PSI-MI 2.5 [39]) and the PSICQUIC framework [4], allowing seamless access to all the
aforementioned molecular interaction repositories.
Each interaction in Mentha is assigned a confidence score ranging from 0 to 1, reflecting
the cumulative experimental evidence supporting the interaction.

Mentha compiles its data into a structured dataset, where each row represents a unique
binary interaction. When multiple sources provide evidence for the same interaction, identi-

codes), and redundant entries are merged into a single record with all supporting evidence
[14].
1.6.2
STRING Database
STRING is a large-scale PPI database that consolidates interaction data across func-
tional and physical associations. It integrates information from multiple sources, catego-
rized into seven evidence channels: genomic neighborhood, gene fusion, co-occurrence, co-
expression, experiments, databases, and text-mining [69].
The first three channels (genomic neighborhood, fusion, and co-occurrence) primar-
ily identify conserved functional associations in prokaryotic organisms. The co-expression
channel infers regulatory relationships based on correlated expression patterns[69]. The re-
maining three channels-experiments, curated databases, and text-mining-provide evidence
of direct or indirect molecular associations, making them particularly relevant for the study
of physical interactions [69].
STRING’s physical subnetwork isolates interactions where proteins are likely to form
complexes, offering a refined dataset suitable for the structural and functional studies within
MAVISp [5]. This dataset is derived from three key evidence channels:
• Experiments: This channel compiles experimental interaction data directly from pri-
mary sources such as BIND [7], DIP [74], BioGRID [66], HPRD [61], IntAct [40],
Page 9 of 61

MINT [16], and PID [64] [67]. To ensure specificity, STRING excludes genetic interfer-
ence studies, which indicate functional rather than direct molecular associations, and
applies statistical corrections to mitigate biases from large protein complexes.
• Database: This channel integrates high-confidence interaction data curated from ded-
icated knowledge resources such as Biocarta [58], BioCyc [47], Gene Ontology (GO)
[6], KEGG [46], Reactome [44], and the Complex Portal [55] [67].
• Text-mining: This channel employs deep learning-based natural language processing
(NLP) to extract physical interaction evidence from scientific literature (OMIM[38],
SGD[19], PMC, PubMed[54]). The model is trained on PubMed abstracts and full-text
articles, to evaluate contextual information. Unlike simple co-mentioning of proteins,
this approach considers sentence structure and linguistic patterns to assess the likeli-
hood of physical interaction [68].

For each protein pair, STRING aggregates interaction probabilities from the individual
sources into a combined confidence score. These scores are benchmarked against a reference
dataset of experimentally validated protein complexes in yeast and cross-validated with
known human interactomes, ensuring robustness in predicting physical associations[69].

Aims
This study aims to bridge the substantial knowledge gap surrounding the numerous
uncharacterized RAD51 paralog variants in ClinVar and establish potential molecular mech-
anisms linking them to pathogenicity. By integrating computational and structural biology
approaches, we seek to determine whether these variants impact protein stability and inter-
actions within the BCDX2 complex. Ultimately, the goal is to identify functionally relevant
variants, prioritize them for experimental validation, and uncover the underlying molecular
mechanisms.
A key objective is to investigate the structural dynamics of RAD51B, which has been
experimentally suggested to be highly mobile, though its precise behavior remains unclear.
To address this, Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations will be employed to assess RAD51B’s
flexibility and determine whether RAD51C, a representative component of the structured
core, exhibits similar intrinsic dynamics. By capturing a broader conformational spectrum of
these subunits, MD simulations will enhance stability predictions, particularly for RAD51B,
where static models may fail to account for its dynamic nature.
Beyond monomer stability, this study will examine whether ClinVar variants disrupt
local interactions within the BCDX2 complex.
To support these investigations, a com-
putational workflow will be developed to enhance interaction data retrieval and structure
identification. Specifically, the existing Interactome Module of MAVISp will be expanded
by incorporating STRING alongside Mentha. As a case study, this workflow will be applied
to BCDX2 to facilitate the analysis of local interactions between its subunits and determine
whether reported ClinVar variants destabilize key interaction interfaces, potentially impair-
ing complex assembly. By identifying regions where destabilizing mutations cluster, we aim
to highlight sites of functional importance.
Through the integration of these analyses, this study will evaluate the extent to which
stability and local interaction predictions explain the functional relevance of uncharacterized
ClinVar variants. For variants that do not significantly alter these properties, their spatial
localization within the complex will be examined to explore potential alternative functional
roles they might affect.

Methods
3.1
Molecular dynamics simulations
Two key components of the BCDX2 complex, RAD51B and RAD51C, were individually
subjected to molecular dynamics simulations to investigate the experimentally suggested
dynamic properties of RAD51B and compare them to its neighboring subunit, RAD51C,
which is considered part of the structured, more rigid core of BCDX2 [37]. The procedure
followed for these simulations is described below.
3.1.1
Systems preparation
The first step involved preparing the structures of the two selected proteins to be sim-
ulated. Both structures were generated using AlphaFold2 (AF2) and underwent trimming
to remove regions with low predicted Local Distance Difference Test (pLDDT) confidence
scores. For RAD51B, a 34-residue-long C-terminal domain was discarded, retaining the first
350 residues of the protein. In the case of RAD51C, trimming was necessary at both ends,
resulting in the retention of the region spanning residues 13 to 350 of the initial protein
chain. Once the trimmed structures were defined, the topology for GROMACS[2] was pre-
pared. The selected force field was CHARMM36 [9] and the capping of the molecules was
dictated by the trimming and was therefore done with NH2 and COOH for the trimmed ends
of RAD51C and with NH2 COO−for RAD51B, adherent to the assumption that trimmed
termini are expected to be neutralized by the missing peptide bond. Furthermore, to deter-
mine the tautomeric state of the present histidines, GROMACS[2] and H++[36] predictions
were consulted, for both proteins. In cases of discrepancies of the two predictions, epsilon-
protonated histidine was established, as it is the most abundant protonation state at neutral
pH. The detailed predictions and final assignments are presented in Tables 4 and 5 in the
Appendix for the proteins RAD51B and RAD51C, respectively.
Each system was solvated using the Three-Point Transferable Intermolecular Poten-
tial (TIP3P) water model, in a dodecahedron box (10.201nm for RAD51B, 10.599nm for
RAD51C) with water molecules, and periodic boundary conditions were applied. The box
with the protein was afterwards energy minimized. Counterions such as chlorine and sodium
ions were added to neutralize the charges. A series of steps for system energy minimization
and equilibration followed and the procedure was completed by simulating for a short dura-
tion of 5 ns. The resulting trajectory was in each case examined in terms of the observed
radius of gyration (Rg), Cα root-mean-square deviation (RMSD), and, most importantly,
the minimum distance (mindist) between the macromolecule and its periodic image to en-
sure that the box size was appropriate (mindist > 0.9 nm) and to confirm the absence of
potential periodic boundary artifacts. Since these requirements were met in both protein
cases, we proceeded to the actual productive runs.

==PAGE:20==

3.1.2
Productive run
The productive runs were carried out using GROMACS 2024[2] under the CHARMM36
[9] force field with a time step of 2 fs for a total duration of of 1µs. Hydrogen bonds were
constrained using the Linear Constraint Solver (LINCS) [41] algorithm. Long-range electro-
static interactions were calculated using the particle-mesh Ewald (PME)[23] method, while
Coulomb and Van der Waals interactions were truncated at 0.9 nm.
Periodic boundary
conditions were applied in all three dimensions with the Verlet cutoff scheme for neighbor
searching.
The trajectories obtained for RAD51B and RAD51C were subsequently utilized in the Sim-
ulation Trajectories Analysis described in the following paragraph, aiming to uncover the
dynamic patterns of the two proteins in a comparative manner. Additionally, these trajec-
tories were later on employed in the Ensemble mode of MAVISp to complement the findings
from the Simple Mode of the framework regarding the mutational effects on the folding of
the two proteins.
3.1.3
Simulation Trajectories Analysis
The post-processing of simulation trajectories involved centering the protein atoms
within the simulation box to ensure proper alignment and continuity of the trajectory. Key
structural properties were then calculated to provide insights into the system’s behavior.
The root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) of the Cα atoms was computed to evaluate
the structural deviation of each protein over time relative to its initial conformation. Ad-
ditionally, the radius of gyration (Rg) was determined to monitor the compactness of the 2
systems throughout the simulations. The root-mean-square fluctuation (RMSF) of the Cα
atoms was also analyzed to estimate per-residue flexibility, averaged over 10-ns time win-
dows. This metric refers to the average deviation of each atom from its mean position during
the simulation. To ensure the absence of boundary artifacts, the minimum distance between
atoms across periodic boundaries was verified to remain above 0.9 nm. All the aforemen-
tioned calculations were conducted using GROMACS[2] tools, specifically rms, rmsf, gyrate,
and mindist, within the in-house script Mol_An [27].

3.2.1
Local Interactions Applications
The primary objective of employing MAVISp Simple Mode for the BCDX2 components
within the scope of this thesis was to explore inter-subunit local interactions that character-
ize the tetrameric complex. To achieve this, interactors and available structural models of
dimeric complexes were identified through the enhanced Interactome workflow, the develop-
ment of which is detailed later in the Results 4.4. The methodology for assessing the quality
of the retrieved structures and calculating binding free energy is outlined below.
Structural Confidence Metrics
The quality of predicted structural complexes was as-
sessed using multiple confidence metrics, primarily from AlphaFold but not limited to it.
Among them, the Predicted Aligned Error (PAE) estimates the positional uncertainty be-
tween residue pairs in a structure, reflecting the reliability of the reciprocal arrangement of
its different domains [45]. The Predicted Local Distance Difference Test (pLDDT) assesses
local confidence at the residue level, providing insight into the accuracy of individual atomic
positions. To evaluate complex-wide accuracy, the AlphaFold Multimer-derived Predicted
Template Modeling Score (pTM) provides a global measure of structure reliability, while the
interface predicted template modeling score (ipTM) assesses the accuracy of inter-subunit
interactions [32]. However, ipTM can be affected by disordered regions or those with low
pLDDT, even if they are not part of the interface of interest [26].
To complement the AlphaFold-derived metrics and achieve a more robust assessment,
docking-specific confidence scores were incorporated to refine model selection. The pDockQ
metric estimates the reliability of docking models by integrating the number of interface
contacts and the average residue-level confidence of them (pLDDT) [11]. Extending this ap-
Page 14 of 61
==PAGE:22==
3.3
MAVISp Ensemble Mode
proach to multimeric complexes, pDockQ2 incorporates PAE to provide per-chain confidence
scores, allowing for the detection of partially correct models within complex assemblies [75].
Structure Selection and Quality Filtering
Using these confidence metrics, retrieved
structures were subjected to a structured filtering protocol, established by the group, to
ensure reliable and complete in terms of interface complexes. High-quality PDB complexes
were directly retained, while incomplete structures were replaced, when available, with ex-
perimentally validated models meeting the threshold pDockQ > 0.3. If no experimental
structures were available, AF3-predicted complexes were considered, provided they met the
thresholds pTM > 0.5, ipTM > 0.6 [3], and pDockQ2 > 0.3 [75]. If pDockQ2 fell below 0.3,
models were retained only if their interface RMSD was within 1Å compared to experimental
structures.
Binding Free Energy Calculations
For complexes that passed these filtering criteria,
interactor-specific mutation lists were generated for binding free energy calculations. The
MutateX [72] and RosettaDDGPrediction [65] pipelines were employed, restricting muta-
tions to those within 10Å of the binding interface, a threshold determined based on prior
benchmarking. This accounts for the local sampling limitations of these methods and their
reduced sensitivity to long-range effects. MutateX[72] calculations included saturation mu-
tagenesis, whereas RosettaDDGPrediction[65] calculations were restricted to disease-related
mutations due to their higher computational cost.
3.3
MAVISp Ensemble Mode
To extend the analysis, Ensemble Mode was applied to the two key subunits of BCDX2,
RAD51B and RAD51C, for which MD simulation data were available. This mode is partic-
ularly useful for classifying variants whose effects remained unresolved in Simple Mode or
for confirming destabilizing effects suggested by Simple Mode. Unlike Simple Mode, which
relies on a single static 3D structure, Ensemble Mode incorporates multiple conformations
sampled from MD trajectories, capturing the proteins’ dynamic behavior-especially relevant
for RAD51B.
For FoldX[25]-based calculations, 25 structural frames were uniformly sampled every
40 ns to ensure an even representation of the conformational space. In contrast, RaSP[10]
predictions utilized structural clustering of the RMSD matrix using the GROMOS[24] al-
gorithm, with the cutoff set to the average RMSD for each MD system. The three largest
structural clusters, representing the most dominant protein conformations, were selected,
and their representative structures were used for energy calculations.
While stability results were obtained for RAD51B and RAD51C using both Simple
Mode and Ensemble Mode, only Ensemble Mode results are presented, as they provide a
more comprehensive assessment. In contrast, Simple Mode results were used for RAD51D
and XRCC2, where MD data were unavailable.

4.1
Structural Dynamics of Molecular Simulations
Inspired by experimental data demonstrating the mobility of RAD51B within the BCDX2
complex, as revealed by negative-stain electron microscopy (NS-EM) 2D class averages and
limited proteolysis experiments [37], molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were conducted
to further explore its conformational flexibility.
Since RAD51C is the adjacent subunit
within the BCDX2 complex and, therefore, one of RAD51B closest interacting partners, the
analysis was also extended to this tumor suppressor component, to investigate whether it
presents similar inherent dynamics or if its behavior differs significantly.
While a comprehensive investigation of all BCDX2 complex components would have
been ideal, the computational demands of such an approach necessitated focusing on RAD51B
and RAD51C. Their structural dynamics are analyzed in the following sections.
4.1.1
Root-Mean-Square Deviation
The RMSD profiles of RAD51B and RAD51C exhibit distinct trends in their struc-
tural dynamics, as shown in Figure 7a. Specifically, RAD51B reaches a maximum RMSD
of approximately 1.1 nm and exhibits pronounced fluctuations throughout the simulation,
while RAD51C RMSD maintains a more stable trend with its values ranging predominantly
between 0.4 and 0.7 nm, reaching a maximum of 0.8 nm. Both proteins show an initial
sharp increase in RMSD within the first 0.1 µs, suggesting structural adjustments. How-
ever, in RAD51C RMSD stabilizes more quickly, with less pronounced oscillations compared
to that of RAD51B. The smaller deviations observed in RAD51C RMSD values indicate
that the protein sampled more similar conformations to the starting structure, as opposed
to RAD51B.
4.1.2
Radius of Gyration
Having examined the Radius of Gyration (Rg) trends of RAD51B and RAD51C as shown
in Figure 7b, it is evident that the proteins display different profiles of structure compactness.
Both proteins exhibit initial oscillations within the first 0.1 µs of the simulation. Between
0.1 µs and 0.6 µs, their Rg values diverge: RAD51B shows a gradual increase, fluctuating
predominantly between 2.30 and 2.40 nm, suggesting increased structural extension, while
RAD51C exhibits a slight decrease in Rg, ranging between 2.20 - 2.30 nm. Beyond 0.6 µs, the
Rg values of both proteins begin to converge, with RAD51C maintaining its compactness and
RAD51B showing an abrupt decline in Rg values. The sustained higher Rg values of RAD51B
align with its increased RMSD fluctuations, reflecting greater structural rearrangements over
time, as opposed to the lower and more consistent Rg values of RAD51C.

4.4
MAVISp Interactome Enhancement
Building on the investigation of ClinVar variant effects on the stability of BCDX2
monomers, the next step was to examine their potential implications for interactions among
the four paralogs. To accomplish this, an automated workflow was developed to retrieve
reliable protein-protein interaction data and collect complex structures suitable for local
interaction analyses.
The first step in developing this workflow was to enhance the Interactome Module within
the MAVISp framework, ensuring more accurate and comprehensive retrieval of protein
interaction networks for each target protein.
Currently, the Interactome Module of MAVISp integrates interaction data by querying
the UniProt accession code (UniProt AC) of the target protein against a locally installed
Page 28 of 61
==PAGE:36==
4.4
MAVISp Interactome Enhancement
Mentha database [14], using the in-house script mentha2pdb [29]. This script also retrieves
complex structures from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) [8] that involve the target protein
and its interactors. To ensure data reliability, only interactors with a Mentha score of at
least 0.4 are considered for further investigation, as those below this threshold typically lack
sufficient published data to support modeling.
To supplement Mentha-based data and further enhance interactome exploration, the
STRING database [69] was incorporated as an additional resource. To achieve this, a custom
Python script, string2pdb, was developed as part of this work and is available on GitHub
[30]. This script employs STRING API v12 [21] and processes the input UniProt AC of the
target protein through the following steps (Figure 20):
1. Mapping of Input ID: The input UniProt AC is mapped to the corresponding
STRING identifier using the "Mapping Identifiers" API method. In cases where mul-
tiple IDs are returned from STRING, the one whose UniProt AC matches the input is
retained (validated using string2upac, described below).
2. Retrieval of Interaction Data: The identified STRING ID is queried for interactors
using the "Getting the interaction partners" API method, with the following filters
applied:
• STRING Score > 0.7: Ensures high-confidence interactions, as defined in the
database’s documentation [67].
• Database Score > 0: Includes interactions supported by at least one curated
database.
• Physical Subnetwork: Restricts results to interactions forming physical com-
plexes, aligning with MAVISp’s objectives.
Following the retrieval of interaction data, the resulting STRING IDs of interactors are
converted into UniProt ACs using the auxiliary script string2upac [31], also developed as
part of this work. This script preprocesses the STRING human protein alias file [22] to retain
only primary UniProt entries, ensuring consistency with Mentha, where protein interactions
are annotated using UniProt ACs.
Finally, the converted UniProt ACs are used to query the RCSB REST API [8] for PDB
IDs of complexes involving both the target protein and its interactors. The RCSB queries
return additionally experimental details of the retrieved complex structures, including the
method and resolution.

MAVISp Interactome Streamlining
Following the enhancement of the Interactome Module by integrating STRING-derived
interaction data, the second computational step in the workflow was to aggregate all col-
lected PPI data for a specific target into a single, comprehensive data frame. This step was
designed to reinforce the informed selection of interactors for further study and to facilitate
the identification of complex structures suitable for binding free energy-based investigations.
To achieve this, the outputs of the string2pdb and mentha2pdb scripts-the two main
components of the Interactome Module-were merged into a unified data frame. This ag-
gregation step was implemented in the custom script aggregate, developed as part of this
project and available on GitHub [28]. The workflow of this process is illustrated in Figure
21, detailing how interactors retrieved from Mentha and STRING are ranked and stored in
one comprehensive interaction database.
Additionally, the resulting data frame is further enriched with structural information
retrieved directly from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) using the in-house tools PDBminer and
PDBminer_complexes.

as described in Section 3.2.1.

the recommended thresholds for structural reliability, as defined in the AF3 documenta-

Discussion
5.1
Insights from RAD51B and RAD51C MD simulations
The MD simulations conducted on RAD51B and RAD51C, two closely interacting part-
ners within the BCDX2 complex, revealed notable structural dynamics. Global metrics such
as RMSD and Rg showed clear differences, with RAD51B simulation exhibiting greater
fluctuations in RMSD values, indicating that it sampled a wider range of conformations
compared to its initial structure than RAD51C. Notably, RAD51B displayed a peak in both
metrics at 0.6 µs, potentially attributed, upon examination of the pdb movie, to the exten-
sion of a long loop in its CTD.
In contrast, per-residue fluctuation analysis revealed a strikingly similar pattern in both
proteins, with two distinct regions of high flexibility: one between residues 50-100 and an-
other between 250-300. The key difference was that RAD51B flexible regions were more
extended, as reflected in broader RMSF peaks, likely contributing to its higher RMSD and
Rg values. Interestingly, the highly flexible region detected in the RAD51BCTD corresponds
to the loop contributing to the RMSD and Rg peaks at 0.6 µs.
The undeniable similarity in the RMSF patterns between RAD51B and RAD51C likely
stems from their evolutionary relationship as RAD51 paralogs, sharing structural and func-
tional features essential for homologous recombination [20]. The observed flexibility in these
regions may enable conformational changes crucial for effective complex formation and func-
tion, particularly in mediating interactions within the BCDX2 complex. Meanwhile, the
consistently low RMSF values across the rest of their sequence suggest a highly conserved
and stable core structure, further supporting their common structural ancestry.
To further investigate the functional implications of the observed flexibility, the detected
flexible regions of RAD51B and RAD51C were mapped onto the AF3-generated model of the
BCDX2 assembly. This analysis revealed that the mobile parts, specifically those localized
in the CTDs of both proteins, are positioned adjacent to each other within the tetrameric
complex (Figure 10c).
At first glance, this mobility could suggest a role in facilitating
RAD51B-RAD51C interactions within the complex. However, a structure-based sequence
alignment of the BCDX2 components with RAD51 from Rawal et al.[63] indicated that these
regions fall within conserved ssDNA-binding loops. This finding implies that their flexibility
may primarily serve DNA-binding rather than protein-protein interactions.
To assess whether this flexibility manifests in experimental structures, all available Cryo-
EM data of the BCDX2 complex (8FAZ, 8GBJ [63], 8OUZ, 8OUY [37]) were examined. In
all these structures, RAD51B is only resolved up to residues 73-75, despite its full sequence
spanning 384 residues. Notably, this retained region covers only the RAD51BNTD, extending
up to the highly mobile linker identified in our MD analysis, which implies that the flexibility
of this segment might prevent structural resolution of the remaining polypeptide chain in
Cryo-EM studies.
In contrast, RAD51C exhibits a significantly larger structural coverage in the experi-
mental data. While both of its highly mobile regions (67-83 and 291-300) are absent, the
majority of the protein, including both the RAD51CNTD and nearly the entire RAD51CCTD,
is resolved. This difference in structural representation indicates that, despite displaying sim-

According to Rawal et al. [63], RAD51B is positioned at the bottom of the BCDX2 com-

5.2.1
Localization of destabilizing variants
Among the four investigated proteins, RAD51B had the fewest reported variants in
the disease-related databases integrated into the MAVISp workflow, with approximately 300
mutations, compared to RAD51C (919), RAD51D (778), and XRCC2 (529). Unlike the
other proteins, where the focus was placed on ClinVar-annotated variants, for RAD51B, the
MAVISp Stability predictions were examined for all reported variants. This decision was
necessary due to the limited number of ClinVar entries for RAD51B (only 32 out of 292
reported variants), which would have significantly restricted the analysis.
Among these reported variants, 14 mutations were predicted to destabilize RAD51B
structural integrity according to the Ensemble Mode of MAVISp. Their localization anal-
ysis revealed that all destabilizing mutations were concentrated within the RAD51BCTD.
Furthermore, when considering the entire set of saturation mutagenesis-derived variants, a
notable clustering of destabilizing mutations was observed in the β-sheet secondary structure
in the core of the RAD51BCTD. The pathogenicity of these mutations is further supported by
the supplementary predictors incorporated in MAVISp (GEMME, AlphaMissense, and De-
MaSk), suggesting that this region plays a critical role in maintaining the structural integrity
of RAD51B.
Expanding the analysis to the remaining BCDX2 paralogs (RAD51C, RAD51D, XRCC2),
despite their large number of variants reported in ClinVar, the vast majority (96-99%) remain
unclassified in terms of their functional impact. This reflects a major gap in the interpreta-
tion of BCDX2 clinical variants, emphasizing the need for further studies to provide a more
complete understanding of their consequences.
Focusing specifically on RAD51C, the mapped ClinVar and saturation mutagenesis-
derived variants (Figure 14) reveal a strong over-representation of destabilizing mutations in
the β-sheet and surrounding helices of the C-terminal region, highlighting the significance
of these areas in RAD51C folding.
A notable observation was that, out of the seven pathogenic ClinVar variants, only one
was predicted to destabilize the monomer, supporting its pathogenicity from a structural
stability perspective. This enabled further investigation into the remaining six pathogenic
variants (Figure 15). Among these, R258H-reported in all considered cancer databases-is
located within one of the protein’s DNA-binding loops, as identified by Rawal et al[63], sug-

5.2.2
Mutation patterns of destabilizing variants
Beyond their localization, valuable insights can also be gained from the types of mu-
tations observed to destabilize the monomers comprising the BCDX2 complex. A common
trend across all paralogs was the enrichment of destabilizing mutations to proline, detected
mainly along the α-helices of the structures. This observation aligns well with established
knowledge in structural biology, as proline residues are known to act as helix-breakers due
to their rigid and unique structure, which can impair proper folding when introduced into a
helical secondary structure.
Another noteworthy observation was the spatial distribution of destabilizing mutations
involving glycine. When glycine was replaced by another residue, these mutations were found
at the edges of β-strands (G125D/V in RAD51C; G107R in RAD51D; G48D in XRCC2) and
α-helices (V142G in RAD51C, G162D in XRCC2). Conversely, when glycine was introduced
via mutation, these substitutions predominantly occurred within the structured regions, par-
ticularly in β-strands (R307G in RAD51B; E122G, V156G in RAD51C; V102G in RAD51D).
This trend reflects glycine’s critical role in protein architecture. Due to its small size and
inherent flexibility, glycine is often found in regions requiring local conformational adapt-
ability, such as helices and strand termini and loops. However, within well-ordered elements
like β-strands, its insertion is likely to compromise structural integrity.
5.3
Insights from Interactome data extraction
As part of the computational aspect of the present project, interaction data from the
STRING database were incorporated into the group’s workflow for the first time and com-
bined with previously retrieved Mentha data to construct a more comprehensive interac-
tome for each target protein (Figure 21). This enhancement necessitates an evaluation of
the retrieved interaction data to determine whether it effectively meets this objective or if
adjustments to the search parameters are needed to optimize interactome coverage while
maintaining confidence in the extracted interactions.
A comparative analysis of interactome data from the BCDX2 subunits reveals notable
differences in the number and type of interactors reported by STRING and Mentha (Tables
7, 2, 8, 9). These differences might align with the distinct curation methodologies of the
two databases. Mentha exclusively integrates experimentally validated interactions curated
from IMEx-associated databases[14]. In contrast, STRING aggregates interaction data from
multiple sources, including curated databases, literature mining, computational predictions,
and orthology-based transfer across species [69]. As a result, Mentha prioritizes specificity,
while STRING aims for broader coverage by incorporating interactions with varying degrees
of experimental support.
Page 51 of 61
==PAGE:59==
5.3
Insights from Interactome data extraction
To align STRING data with the objectives of the MAVISp workflow, STRING inter-
actions were filtered to include only those forming physical complexes and meeting high-
confidence thresholds (STRING score > 0.7, database score > 0, physical interactors), as
detailed in the Methods section 4.4. Even with these refinements, however, an important
distinction remains: Mentha continues to provide only experimentally validated interac-
tions, whereas the filtered STRING dataset includes interactions supported by experiments,
curated databases, or text-mining evidence. This difference is particularly relevant when in-
terpreting interactions that are exclusive to STRING, as they may lack direct experimental
validation but still have support from the remaining resources.
The effect of these filtering parameters was assessed using the interactome of RAD51C
as a representative case, as similar patterns were observed across all BCDX2 components.
Many interactors were commonly identified in both databases, including XRCC3, RAD51B,
RAD51D, XRCC2, RAD51, PALB2, and BRCA2, which are all well-established members
of the DNA repair mechanism (Table 2). However, a subset of interactors was found ex-
clusively in one of the two databases.
Specifically, Mentha uniquely reported SWSAP1,
HELQ, DDX3X, HRNR, SPACA1, and SPG21, while STRING exclusively retrieved SWI5
and SFR1.
Further examination of STRING’s scoring system clarifies the underlying reason for
these discrepancies. All Mentha-exclusive interactors failed to meet the 0.7 STRING con-
fidence threshold, with STRING scores ranging from 0.165 to 0.370.
These interactions
were supported only by weak experimental evidence and had no curated database references
(database score = 0), which led to their exclusion under the applied STRING filtering crite-
ria. In contrast, SWI5 and SFR1, which were absent from Mentha but retained in STRING,
lacked direct experimental evidence but had support from curated databases, particularly
Gene Ontology (GO) annotations. Additionally, SWI5 had some interaction evidence de-
rived from homologous proteins in other organisms, slightly elevating its confidence score
compared to SFR1.
These observations reveal an unintended consequence of the applied STRING filtering
parameters: although STRING is inherently a broader resource than Mentha, the current
workflow seems from this example case to have effectively restricted STRING data retrieval
even further than Mentha’s, primarily due to the high confidence threshold and stringent
database score requirement. As a result, interactions that are present in Mentha but weakly
supported in STRING appear to be only reported by the former, in the final data frame.
To account for this, further adjustments to the STRING filtering criteria could be con-
sidered. First, lowering the STRING confidence threshold below 0.7 could allow the inclusion
of additional interactions, even if they lack extensive literature validation. Second, modifying
the database score criterion to retain interactions with either a positive database score or a
positive experimental score would ensure that experimentally validated interactions are not
excluded solely because they are not yet incorporated into curated pathways or complexes.
These refinements would help restore STRING’s intended broader coverage while maintain-
ing a high-confidence dataset, ensuring that STRING-derived interactomes complement the
ones gained from Mentha.

5.4.1
RAD51C-RAD51D Interaction Insights
The pattern of observed destabilizing mutations highlights key structural regions es-
sential for RAD51C-RAD51D interaction (Figure 40). One such region is the inter-NTD
interface, where destabilizing mutations were identified in both RAD51C and RAD51D. In
RAD51C, the mutations E37K and E37G are positioned at the inter-NTD contact site, while
in RAD51D, the R5G and R5S mutations occur in a corresponding region. These mutations
introduce charge changes that, especially in the case of R5G/S, may disrupt electrostatic
interactions (Figure 36), thereby weakening inter-domain binding. Such perturbations could
impair the tight stacking of the RAD51B, RAD51C, and RAD51D NTDs, that has been
observed in the BCDX2 atomic model by Greenhough et al. [37].
Another region of interest is the ATP-binding site, where destabilizing mutations were
identified in both RAD51C and RAD51D. In RAD51C, the H307R mutation is located near
the ATP-binding site of RAD51D, potentially disrupting ATP binding by destabilizing the
adjacent protein-protein interface. Similarly, in RAD51D, the G110D mutation is positioned
near its ATP-binding domain, where the charge change it introduces may interfere with ATP
binding. Since the current protocol does not account for ligands, the precise impact of these
mutations remains speculative.

5.4.3
RAD51B-RAD51C Interaction Insights
The investigation of this specific dimer interaction was primarily exploratory, as the
AF3-generated structure, based on its confidence metrics, did not fully meet the quality
criteria established by our group.
Consequently, a more thorough literature review was
conducted to determine whether prior studies support the RAD51BCTD-RAD51CCTD con-
tact suggested by the AF3-derived structure. Since the RAD51BNTD-mediated contacts of
these two proteins have already been resolved in available Cryo-EM structures from the PDB
(8FAZ, 8GBJ [63], 8OUZ, 8OUY [37]), the literature search specifically focused on validating
the inter-CTD interaction.
Recent ATPase assays have shown that RAD51B hydrolyzes ATP only following RAD51C’s
catalytic ATP hydrolysis, indicating a coupled ATPase activity in which RAD51B activation
is RAD51C-dependent [37]. Further structural insights from hydrogen-deuterium exchange
mass spectrometry (HDX-MS) revealed that during RAD51C ATP hydrolysis, the RAD51B
lysine finger K324 engages with RAD51C, resulting in the allosteric activation of RAD51B
ATPase and the placement of the CTDs of both partners in contact [37]. Additionally, the
interactions between RAD51BCTD and RAD51CCTD were found to be ATP-dependent, as
they were specifically stabilized in the ATP transition state but not in the pre-hydrolytic
state, as evidenced by comparative HDX-MS experiments [37].
The AF3 prediction of BCDX2 in our study revealed direct interactions between RAD51BCTD
and RAD51CCTD. Additionally, in the AF3 model, RAD51B K324 is positioned directly
below the RAD51C ATP-binding site, underpinning the previously observed RAD51BCTD-
RAD51CCTD engagement, as shown in Figure 42. This suggests that the predicted structure
likely corresponds to the active intermediate during the ATP hydrolytic cycle. Given these
insights, it would be valuable to examine the results that the Local Interactions Module
yielded on this model, as described below.

Figure 42: AF3 model of the RAD51B (blue) - RAD51C (gray) dimer. The ADP and Mg2+ in the RAD51C
ATP-binding site are shown in magenta, and the L324 residue of RAD51B is highlighted in yellow. Image

From the perspective of RAD51B, the unique interface destabilizing mutation site
(E247K) resides within its CTD and appears to mediate inter-CTD interactions within the
complex. The destabilizing effect may stem from the local charge change introduced by the
substitution of glutamic acid (E) with lysine (K), which likely disrupts existing interactions
between the two binding chains (Figure 43).
In contrast, RAD51C carries multiple variants predicted to destabilize the interaction.
The clustering of destabilizing mutations within the RAD51CCTD (P127L, P127R, G128R,
G162E, R212L, R212S, R319P) highlights its central role in mediating inter-CTD interactions
between subunits, further supporting our hypothesis of RAD51CCTD-RAD51BCTD contacts.
Additionally, two NTD mutations (S20F, S20Y) verify the importance of inter-NTD contacts
in stabilizing the RAD51B-RAD51C interaction.
The RAD51CCTD mutations A221V, Y224C, and L225P localize to a key interface me-
diating RAD51CCTD-RAD51BNTD interactions, suggesting that the NTD of RAD51B may
interact transversely with both domains of its adjacent subunit. This mechanism resembles
the interactions observed between the RAD51CNTD and both RAD51D domains.
Figure 43 further illustrates how these interactions contribute to structural stability. The
absence of an adjacent NTD above RAD51BCTD allows for greater mobility of the latter,
whereas RAD51CCTD appears more stabilized due to its interactions with RAD51BNTD.

5.4
Insights from Local Interactions Investigation
Figure 43: Visualization of destabilizing mutations in the RAD51B-RAD51C interaction. RAD51B is shown

Additionally, the L205I mutation, which affects the RAD51CCTD interaction with the
α-helix of the RAD51B linker, further underlines the role of this structured linker region in
stabilizing inter-subunit positioning within the BCDX2 complex. This finding is consistent
with previous observations in the other dimeric interactions (RAD51C-RAD51D, RAD51D-
XRCC2), where the linker appeared to be a key stabilizing element.
Lastly, the proximity of mutations P127L, P127R, and G128R to the ATP-binding
site mirrors destabilizing variants identified in other BCDX2 dimers (RAD51C-RAD51D,
RAD51D-XRCC2). This raises the possibility that dissociation in this region could indi-
rectly affect nucleotide binding. However, since the current computational protocol does not
account for ligands, the precise impact of these variants remains speculative and should be
interpreted with caution.
Among the variants that appear to destabilize RAD51B-RAD51C contacts, numer-
ous RAD51C variants have been identified in cancer-related conditions, as provided in
ClinVar[51]. More specifically, G12E, P127L/R, G162E, and R212L/S have been reported
in Fanconi anemia, while S20Y/F, G162E, and R212S have been associated with heredi-
tary cancer-predisposing syndrome. Additionally, G162E has also been identified in breast-
ovarian cancer.

5.5
Insights from non-benign ClinVar Variants Localization
Summary of BCDX2 dimeric Interactions
Overall, the dimeric interactions between neighboring subunits within BCDX2 appear
to follow similar structural principles, relying on stabilization through both NTD and CTD
contacts, with the NTD engaging both domains of the adjacent subunit. These interactions
are further reinforced by linker-mediated anchoring and contacts proximal to ATP-binding
sites.
5.5
Insights from non-benign ClinVar Variants Localization
The analysis of non-benign ClinVar-reported RAD51B mutations that were not pre-
dicted to affect monomer stability or interactions yielded interesting findings. As shown in
Figure 37, eight out of nine variants classified as neutral regarding monomer folding and
local interactions with RAD51C were found in key structural regions, predominantly within
the two DNA-binding loops [63]. Their positioning suggests a potential impact on ssDNA
binding, which could compromise RAD51B’s role in the HR repair pathway. Additionally,
three variants are located near the ATP-binding site of RAD51C, indicating a possible effect
on this function.
Overall, nearly all variants whose effect was not identified through the Local Interactions
and Stability modules—and remain of unknown significance in ClinVar—are positioned in
regions crucial for ssDNA or nucleotide binding. This stresses the importance of integrating
multiple functional assessments when evaluating variant pathogenicity. However, these inter-
pretations rely solely on positional analysis, and further in silico or experimental validation
is required to determine the actual effects of these variants.
The pattern described above was not observed in the other BCDX2 components, where
the number of ClinVar-reported variants without an effect on stability or protein-protein in-
teractions (PPI) was significantly higher (Figure 38). In RAD51C, these variants were widely
distributed, whereas in RAD51D, approximately one-third were located near nucleotide- or
DNA-binding sites. In XRCC2, just under half exhibited this localization. Therefore, unlike
RAD51B, the remaining variants do not cluster in significant functional regions, making it
impossible to infer their potential effects.
These observations emphasize the importance of tailoring functional predictions to the
specific role of each target protein. For instance, in the case of BCDX2, assessing DNA-
and nucleotide-binding effects is essential, as these mechanisms may contribute to the im-
pact of many reported variants. Additionally, expanding the analysis to broader functional
properties-such as long-range effects and phosphorylation site modifications within the MAV-
ISp framework-could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the clinical significance of
these variants.

6
Conclusions and Future Prospects
6.1
Conclusions
In this study, we explored the dynamics of RAD51B and RAD51C, key components
of the BCDX2 complex. Our analysis revealed that while both proteins exhibit some in-
herent flexibility, their distinct positioning within the complex explains why only RAD51B
maintains high mobility. This distinction likely reflects their functional roles, with RAD51B
remaining dynamic while RAD51C contributes to structural stability.
We also systematically assessed the functional impact of disease-associated variants
across the BCDX2 complex, focusing on two key mechanisms: local interactions and stability.
This analysis identified critical regions for monomer folding, as well as key regions involved in
intersubunit interactions. The latter were evidenced by clustering of destabilizing mutations,
many of which have been associated with cancer.
These findings highlight functionally
relevant variants that should be prioritized for experimental validation.
To support our PPI investigation, we developed a computational workflow that en-
hances the scope and confidence of retrieved interaction data. This workflow automates
structure retrieval, assesses interface completeness, and, if no satisfactory complex structure
is available, suggests structure modeling. By streamlining the preparatory steps for binding
free energy calculations, this approach facilitates and accelerates PPI studies. We hope it
encourages further investigations into local interactions beyond this study.
6.2
Future Directions
While our analyses provided valuable insights, stability and local interaction assessments
alone were insufficient to fully explain the pathogenicity of ClinVar variants and, therefore,
to holistically determine the importance of uncharacterized variants. Expanding functional
analyses is necessary to improve classification confidence.
Future investigations should explore additional functional layers relevant to the tumor
suppressor role of BCDX2 in homologous recombination (HR). Since RAD51B, RAD51C,
RAD51D, and XRCC2 are involved in ATP and ssDNA binding, assessing how their variants
impact these functions could reveal alternative mechanisms of pathogenicity.
Moreover, the impact of BCDX2 components variants on protein-protein interactions
should be examined beyond the complex itself to include key external partners such as
RAD51, which relies on BCDX2 for RPA displacement, and XRCC3, a RAD51C-associated
protein identified in our interactome analysis. Since these interactions are crucial for HR
repair, their disruption may contribute significantly to pathogenicity.
A more extensive
investigation of how variants disrupt the wider interaction network of BCDX2 components
is essential.
The analysis could also be extended to the more general effects of genetic variations in
proteins, such as their influence on long-range regulation and post-translational modification
(PTM) sites, which are both supported by corresponding MAVISp modules. This would
allow for a more robust and comprehensive assessment.
Page 60 of 61
==PAGE:68==
6.2
Future Directions
Finally, from a computational perspective, future refinements to our PPI analysis could
involve revisiting the interaction scoring thresholds used for STRING database data. Our
approach currently filters out many interactions from Mentha, which often correspond to
low-confidence experimental evidence. Adjusting the STRING threshold could help iden-
tify a broader yet biologically relevant set of interaction partners, ensuring no meaningful
interactions are overlooked.
