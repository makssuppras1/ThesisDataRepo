ABSTRACT 
The typical design service life of the buildings is 50 years. This age is also commonly 
used in the Life Cycle Assessment studies as the actual service life of the analysed 
buildings [1]. There are no economic or environmental reasons for that choice. In fact, 
the longer the building exist and function the lower its environmental impacts [2].  
This research identifies possible features of the long-lasting buildings based on 
a literature review. The characteristics are then analysed using buildings that have 
already exceeded the lifespan of 50 years. The parametric analysis of the public 
buildings in Lyngby relies on the information from the FBB database. Further, 
the results of the analysis are applied to the buildings aged below 50 years. It was 
assessed if they have characteristics of long-lasting buildings and whether there are 
likely to remain for many years. Finally, the guidelines for design for longevity were 
created. 
It was found that potential aspects of long-lasting buildings are: aesthetic, uniqueness, 
durability, reparability, flexibility, public appreciation and localization.  
When comparing the architecture, size, external wall and roof material, placement and 
style the younger buildings performed worse than the older structure. The younger 
buildings fulfilled on average only 38%, of the requirements, while the same 
criteria applied to the advanced in years buildings resulted in 68%. It implies that 
nowadays constructions are significantly different than the older buildings, hence they 
have moderate chances to last for long.  
To create long-lasting structures, the architects should design unique buildings with 
a distinctive character and wisely-chosen materials. They should also consider ease of 
maintenance and renovation as well as future changes of the function. The buildings 
should be valuable piece of architecture for public and have strong relations to 
the environment in which it is placed. Moreover, the longevity also depends on 
the owners’ actions, who should preserve the original appearance of the building and 
maintain it.

uncertain if the results can apply to all buildings in Denmark or in the world. 
The requirements for the longevity are location dependent. Moreover, the research is

the building is influenced by many other factors like economics and political aspects.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 PROBLEM DEFINITION 
Nowadays sustainability is well recognized and addressed in many aspects of life, 
which is reflected for instance by the UN Sustainable Development Goals [3]. In 
the building sector, which is a significant contributor to the climate changes, where  
39% of energy-related carbon emissions come from constriction and buildings [4], there 
is much effort put to achieve an environmental sustainability. There is a big focus in 
the industry to prevent impacts in all stages of buildings’ life. 
The materials are chosen carefully. The construction phase is being planned in detail to 
avoid unnecessary harmful actions. The use phase was recently addressed extensively. 
Proper insulations and structure tightness significantly decreased the energy use [5]. 
With the use of renewable energy sources, the environmental impacts from building 
energy usage are being minimised. Nowadays, highly energy efficient buildings are 
constructed [4]. Building Regulation (BR18) requires energy consumption of 
maximum: (30+1000/heated area) kWh/m²/year for residential buildings and 
(41+1000/heated area) kWh/m²/year for offices, schools, institutions, etc.[6]. Energy 
Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD) comes into life by 2018, that means all 
members of The European Union are obliged to build nZEB (net-zero energy buildings) 
[7]. At the end of building’s life, there are many methods implemented e.g. materials 
recycling or re-use.  
Building’s impacts are evaluated by Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), which is 
a commonly used tool for building sustainability assessment in Denmark [8] and 
worldwide [9]. It usually assumes 50 years lifespan of the buildings [2]. It is a typical 
design service life for building, according to the Green Building Council Denmark 
(DGNB) [10] and the Danish Building Research Institute (SBi) [11]. Nevertheless, 
there are no clear reasons for using this time frame [2]. Moreover, the assumption is 
inaccurate, as it is proofed and further analysed in [12]. 
Quantification of the environmental impacts by LCA tool must be done in function of 
the provided service and time. Therefore, the functional unit is used, which is often: 
1 m2/1 year of provided function. Typically, the load is distributed over the service life 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
2 
 
of the building, which means that total impacts are divided by the assumed 50 years. 
Consequently, the service life has a direct influence on the final LCA results.  
With the significant reductions in the use phase, the environmental impacts from 
the construction materials grow in importance. Despite many actions to reduce 
the environmental footprint, there is little focus on the buildings’ life extension. As the 
impacts of the building are distributed over years of its exitance, it is reasonable to 
prolong their life. The influence is minimized in the way that the harm is distributed 
over more years. For example: except erecting two e.g. office buildings in 100 years 
period, there could be only built one. Further, what are the environmental impacts of 
a building that lasts for the centuries? Their burden from the construction is distributed 
over many years and impacts from demolition are avoided until their end of life. 
Extending the lifespan can significantly reduce impacts from buildings. Compared to 
the lifespan of 50 years, the environmental footprint can be 38% lower from building 
aged 100 years and even 44% lower for 120-year-old structure [2]. 
The best for the building is to last as long time as possible. It would bring numerous 
advantages. First of all, the demolition process has its impacts, it consumes massive 
amounts of the energy and produces waste [13]. Early demolition of buildings has its 
environmental and economic consequences [2]. The ideal would be to avoid it 
completely or at least delay it to the longest possible. 
Despite the environmental benefits of the long service life of buildings, there are also 
economic and social benefits. The resources, both financial and natural, can be saved. 
Developing long-lasting structures creates an identity and heritage, which impacts all 
members of the society.  
Last but not least, the long-lasting buildings would satisfy the architects’ desire to 
preserve their design. As it is a visible proof of their capabilities and artistic skills, no 
one would like to see the effort of many hours of work to be destroyed or demolished.

1.2 PROJECT GOALS 
The project’s aim is to increase the lifespan of newly designed buildings and by this to 
reduce their environmental impacts. The parameters of long-lasting buildings are 
analysed based on existing public buildings in Lyngby, that are aged 50 and more years. 
The study suggests possible features that buildings should have in order to live long. It 
investigates what physical characteristics allow buildings to stand longer than their 
design service life. The correlation between various buildings’ features and their age is 
found. The project also aims to create guidelines for “design for longevity” by 
recognizing characteristics of long-lasting buildings. This project identifies and 
formulates architectural and engineering methods to design and construct buildings that 
last longer than the average service life. 
For the purpose of this study, public buildings were chosen. This kind of buildings are 
owned by the public and serve the community. The private buildings are influenced by 
even more constraints like market/industry which are driven by income levels. 
The public owned buildings are administrated in a more predictable way than private. 
1.3 RESEARCH QUESTIONS 
This study aims to answer the following questions: 
▪ What are the characteristics of long-lasting buildings? 
▪ How can those characteristics be assessed? 
▪ Do recently constructed buildings (under 50 years) have a chance to survive for 
many years? 
▪ How to design buildings for longevity?

1.4 GLOSSARY 
The glossary was prepared to eliminate potential misunderstandings in the definitions 
used in this text. It clarifies the definitions and meanings used. 
Bedre byggeskik is translated as “Better Construction Practice”. It was a reaction to 
the inconsistency in architecture, which was a consequence of historicism. It is not 
a style, but more of an attitude to the buildings’ design and construction. It applied in 
years 1910-1940 to the newly constructed low-rise buildings, like the row or semi-
detached houses, but also to the renovation of the “bygmesterhuse”. It used traditional 
Danish architecture as an inspiration to create quality architecture. It promoted 
aesthetic, functionality and good craft [14]. 
Built-up area is a building’s footprint on earth. It is measured on the outside surface 
of the external walls in the ground level. Closed balconies, entrances, garages within 
the house (under same roof) are included in the built-up area. Opened elements like 
terraces, open balconies, external staircase are excluded [15]. 
Bygmesterhuse are type of the detached houses built in Denmark between 1900 and 
1940. They have brick walls, almost square floor plan and two levels. The roof can be 
jerkinhead or gabled tilted around 50 degrees and covered with tiles. This house became 
the first Danish type house (typehus) [16]. 
Design life is the “service life intended by the designer” [17]. 
Durability is “the fact of something continuing to be used without getting damaged” 
or “the fact of something continuing without failing” [18]. 
Lifespan is “the length of time for which a person, animal, or thing exists” [18].  In this 
study, the lifespan is understood as a time from when a building starts to provide 
a function until the end of time when building is still being useful. Its condition must 
be sufficient to provide a function. Although the functions may change, it is mandatory 
for the building to serve for any possible purpose. The building must be functioning 
and used by people. It is not only technically able to provide the function, it does 
provide a function and remains useful. 
Longevity, according to [18], is “living for a long time” or “remaining popular or useful 
for a long time”. 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
5 
 
Longevity versus durability – this research deals with buildings’ longevity, which is 
a broader term. The durability is resistance to damage, which is included within 
the longevity definition. The longevity is being useful, which in case of buildings is 
providing function. 
Maintenance is a “combination of all technical and associated administrative actions 
during the service life to retain a building, or its parts, in a state in which it can perform 
its required functions” [17]. 
SAVE is Survey of Architectural Values in the Environment, it is a tool used for 
mapping, recording and assessing value of buildings. They are graded in scale from 1 
to 9 in terms of architecture, cultural-historical value, environment, originality and 
condition. The final conservation value is a mean of those five criteria. 
The conservation value of a building is:  
• high for values 1-3  
• medium for values 4-6  
• low values 7-9  
The method is widely used in Denmark since 1991. According to the text written in 
2011:  “More than 360,000 buildings are already assessed by the SAVE method and 
added to the FBB, which makes the database central to maintenance of information 
about the conservation-evaluated buildings” [19]. 
Service life is “period of time after installation during which a facility or its component 
parts meet or exceed the performance requirements” [17].

-

-

thinking and knowledge.

2.9 PARAMETRIC ANALYSIS - RECOGNIZING CHARACTERISTICS OF LONG-LASTING BUILDINGS  
In order to recognize properties that newly-designed long-lasting building should have, 
the parametric analysis of already existing buildings over 50 years was held.

2.10 APPLICATION OF RESULTS 
2.10.1 Prediction background 
After concluding about properties of the long-lasting buildings, the results were implemented 
to the validation set. For the building to stay long on the ground, it is important to have some 
of the features highlighted by parametric analysis. It is impossible to set clear boundary of how 
many features of long lasting buildings should the buildings have. Therefore, it cannot be 
precisely assessed which building will or will not survive, although a prediction was made. 
The more characteristics a building have, the more probable that it will last for many years. 
Naturally the certainty will come with time. 
2.10.2 Evaluation of building from validation dataset 
The 101 buildings below 50 years were assessed in terms of potential longevity based on eight 
categories, as explained below. The buildings with the highest percentage of the longevity 
features are expected to be long-lasting. 
2.10.2.1 BBR data 
Firstly, the size of the buildings was checked. It was assessed if the building is relatively small. 
The criteria are: low-rise structure (one- or two-floor) and the total building area lower than 
the average area of buildings form validation set (below 780 m2).  
The materials used on the exterior of the buildings were analysed. Half-timber structures are 
the oldest on average and brick walls are considered durable [30]. Therefore, it is assumed that 
buildings with this type of external wall material have a potential to last.  
The oldest buildings on average are covered with thatch. Cement blocks and slate are also on 
roofs of the old buildings. The tile is the most common roof material in the calibration dataset. 
The buildings with those four roof materials are considered likely to live long. 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
 
14 
 
According to the results, the location of the building is related to its age. The buildings placed 
in the country zone are significantly older than those in the urban setting. It was assumed that 
the buildings in the countryside are favoured. 
2.10.2.2 SAVE 
Further, the SAVE evaluation was used to assess architecture, environment and originality of 
the buildings. The buildings rated 4 and higher are considered valuable (scale 1-highest to 9-
lowest). This number is a median of all SAVE criteria. 
SAVE architectural value annotates the type of the building and its visual aspects, while 
environmental value relates to the quality of its surroundings and how well the building suits to 
that context. The originality value measures how much the building changed since 
the construction. Later extension and components replacements are affecting the value. 
2.10.2.3 Building construction, FBB data 
The style in which the buildings were designed was considered. Although there is much more 
information on building construction provided by the FBB, it is mostly for buildings in age over 
50. There are only four buildings in the validation set with some description of the building 
construction. A simplistic comparison was done. If a building fulfilled the criteria that emerged 
from the parametric analysis, it was given one point, and if had undesirable parameters, one 
point was subtracted. The main plan, building elements and materials are (among many other 
features of architecture) assessed by the SAVE. Therefore, they were used to reflect on the 
results of previous estimations.  
2.11 GUIDELINES FOR DESIGN 
Based on the results of literature review and parametric analysis, the guideline for buildings’ 
longevity were created. The advice is directed to building designers and owners. Because, as it 
was recognised, the longevity depends not only on the design, but also on the actions that 
happen during service life.

3 RESULTS 
3.1 POTENTIAL CHARACTERISTICS OF THE LONG-LASTING BUILDINGS  
This section presents a summary of the literature review. To be able to design for a long life, 
one must be aware of the features of such buildings. The potential characteristics of the long-
lasting buildings are listed here. Many are related to each other and share some parameters. Not 
all of them can be measured or assessed and for others, there are several indicators of the 
qualities. 
3.1.1 Aesthetics and perception 
The aesthetics of buildings is a measure of its attractiveness. People like to look at beautiful 
things, but beauty has many definitions. Aesthetics of building is among others the quality of 
architecture itself. The beautiful building is appealing to the eye [31]. This describes all that 
one could see: the colours, textures, building components and decorations. “God is in the 
details” as said Mies van der Rohe. Details are very important in architecture as it is an 
expression of values and meaning of the construction. Details and decorations are the means to 
achieve “beauty”, it is like telling a story that is behind the building [2].  
Nevertheless, the architecture is not separate components but one whole. The harmony, scale 
and proportions of the buildings influence the perception. The whole composition must be 
pleasant. The style of the architecture also reflects how it is perceived. The architecture could 
be expressed in multiple ways and styles.  
There is so much more in architecture than the look. The architecture is not only judged by its 
appearance. The feelings that are evoked by it also influence the judgement. It can make an 
impression of hard or soft, heavy or light, of being welcoming or overwhelming. That includes 
the perception of the space, amount of daylight, artificial lights and fresh air, but also 
acoustics [31]. 
As Bijoy Jain writes in his text “Praxis”, the program or functionality is not important, 
the building may change its purpose [32]. The quality of the space, light, air and acoustics are 
sought by the architects [33]. Aesthetics and perception express the whole experience of the 
architecture.  
 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
16 
 
3.1.2 Uniqueness 
Contemporary society is highly demanding, everyone wants to express oneself and stand out 
from the crowd. It was not possible in building industry in the recent century when 
industrialization resulted in the production of mass and repetitive buildings. As mentioned in 
[33], we are living in the times of the second wave of industrialization. The technology allows 
changing mass-produced buildings into individual products.  
The identity of the architecture is as well significant. To have a long lifespan, the building must 
be recognizable. It must be a symbol or a distinctive feature of the context. Most of 
the buildings are one in a kind, yet only some are attracting attention. Even some buildings 
designed by such well-recognized architect as Frank Lloyd Wright did not survive. 
Approximately 15% (79 out of 511) of his designs, that were erected, were demolished or 
destroyed [34]. The building should be irreplaceable, one of a kind.  
3.1.3 Durability 
The durability of structure compromises the quality of the materials and their stability. 
The buildings that are solid are more likely to stay for many years. The materials must be of 
proper durability to remain, they also need to be attached with high quality. The buildings with 
a weak construction decay fast and do not stand over time. 
When a building is constructed from fragile materials, it is easier to demolish it than one made 
of solid components. The feature describes the materials’ quality and craftsmanship. People are 
often more willing to renovate a brick house than the wooden frame structure, which could be 
easily rebuilt. It would take too many resources to demolish the old brick wall and too much 
time to build a new one, therefore it would be most likely renovated.  
3.1.4 Reparability - ease of maintenance 
As with any other product, buildings must be maintained in order to be preserved. Nevertheless, 
the maintenance could be easier or harder to perform. The easier the maintenance is, the more 
likely it will be done. Same applies to the renovation which is often expensive and complicated 
to do [35].  
Buildings have different layers, as the concept of Stewart Brand’s 6 S’s describes, where each 
layer has a different service life [20]. Moreover, various materials need different maintenance 
actions with various frequencies. For instance, wall finished with timber cladding would require 
recoating every few years [36], while bricks are almost maintenance-free [37]. Therefore, it is 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
 
17 
 
important to design buildings in the way that makes it easy to maintain and replace the parts 
when it is necessary. If the structure is simple – it is easier to repair it and there are fewer 
elements to break down like in the old type of houses, where there were walls made of single 
material. In addition to that, high maintenance cost could be a reason for early demolition. 
3.1.5 Flexibility 
A flexibility of architecture is described in many ways, has many aspects and various terms are 
used to precise it. Adaptability, transformability, convertibility [38] and polyvalence [39] are 
among those terms, which all slightly differ. 
Despite the specific definitions, the ability of a building to transform with changing 
requirements is a feature preventing demolition. It is more practical to adjust the building for 
the needs (if such changes are easily made) than to demolish and raise the new building.  
The flexibility varies depending on the building function. In housing the flexibility is reflected 
by a possibility to arrange rooms in a suitable way, while in commercial and industrial buildings 
is the possibility to accommodate different activities. 
The adaptability is a possibility of accommodating various functions without the need for 
rebuilding. The building is adaptable when it can be used despite the changing demands of 
users. High adaptability means that the building can change function without major makeovers 
[39]. It is for instance ease of replacing the ventilation system. This can be achieved among 
others by generality and simplicity of the structure. 
Flexibility was much easier to achieve in the previous centuries. Back then it was just a kitchen 
that was quite rigid in placement because it needed a chimney. Nowadays chimneys are not 
required in each apartment, but each room has services attached. In the apartment, there is 
a kitchen, bathroom and sometimes separated toilet – which all need a water supply and sewage 
system. In each room, there is an electric connection. It is even more complex if mechanical 
ventilation is used. In the 21st century, it is not that easy to rearrange the floorplan as 
the services must be moved with them. Before, one fireplace per household was enough to 
satisfy the tenants’ needs. Nowadays people have high comfort requirements, there is a need to 
provide warmth in each room. It was easier to achieve flexibility without the required services.  
==PAGE:33==
 
 
 
18 
 
Danish traditional building - længe is an example of extremely adjustable design. It was used 
through the centuries and its simplistic layout allowed changes and extensions as needed. It is 
able to accommodate various functions [40].  
Modern people like to have personalized products and prefer to stand out from the crowd in one 
or another way. Therefore, people arrange their rooms, gardens, offices in a way different than 
neighbours. This is a way to express own personality and it seems important for people to be 
able to do that. The structure should allow variation in the layout, finishing elements and  
etc. [40]. Possibility to adapt places to needs and peoples’ personality make them wanted and 
preferable compared to those that do not allow any changes. 
3.1.6 Public appreciation 
An interesting approach to the public appreciation of buildings is a concept of “Lovability” by 
Steve Mouzon’s, he claims that the way people see the building is related to the lifespan of 
the building [41]. People tend to keep things that they like and care more for such buildings. 
Building that are appreciated by the public have also the positive opinion of architecture 
critiques. The way the buildings are valued change over time. Initially, the new project is often 
considered unsuitable for the place, with time it is more and more regarded until it is too old – 
weathered, damaged and, as a consequence, disregarded [39]. 
Some types of buildings have high public appreciation due to their function or symbolism. 
Buildings like churches, libraries, courts and alike are important for the society from day one, 
therefore they are treated differently and often function for many years. 
3.1.7 Location 
Each design is (or at least should be) location specific. It is important to analyse 
the characteristics of the location – both measurable and not, before starting the design. Climate 
data, dimensions and potentials of the building site, neighbourhood and features of the 
surroundings must be investigated. “The most impressive projects are often those characterized 
by an unmistakable relation to the location” [42]. Christian Norberg-Schulz’s “genius loci” – 
also argues that the place is created by the sum of material substance, shape, surface, colour, 
atmosphere and character, orientation and identification [42]. The sustainable building that is 
accurately working in one location, does not necessarily fit another. It cannot be used without 
recognition of local characteristics or needs [43]. 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
 
19 
 
It must be born in mind that the building is not alone, there are also its surroundings that are 
important. For instance, what would be the Eiffel Tower without the park around it? It would 
be just an iron tower in the middle of the city, not appreciated that much. It is important how 
people feel the space and what they can do there. As it is from the urbanism perspective, the city 
is created by solids and voids, buildings and spaces between them. This is what brings 
the quality to the building, the street/square/park that is nearby. 
The building shall reflect the identity of the place and be an integral part of the surroundings. 
The cultural heritage of the place is highly relevant to the longevity, the building shall 
contribute to the quality of the context of its location. 
An access to nature is another worth-mentioning feature. It seems that the well-being of people 
is also necessary for the building and space around it to be valued. People need to have free 
access to the open, green areas and spaces where it is easy to communicate with others. Those 
are places where people are encouraged to take a short walk or could come into interaction with 
others. 
Location could be also understood in another way. It might be a possibility to change place, 
like a transportable house that one would take with when moving to another location. This 
feature of the building could be named as movability. This brings opportunities for the building 
that is not wanted in the certain location anymore. It could be transported in the place where it 
will still deliver its function. The demolition and expenses associated with that are avoided in 
such case.

There is no straightforward way to measure the above-mentioned characteristics. Some of them 
are purely subjective, like aesthetics. There are many ways of defining beauty and for many 
people, certain building appearance would be desired, while for others it would be just ugly. 
Nevertheless, the attempt to assess those features is made in this paper. Table 7 presents the 
recognized characteristics and assigned to them parameters from the FBB database.

The study investigates the public buildings, that include the publicly owned, but also those 
serving public purposes. This category represents the type of the owner, there are five types, 
see Table 12.  
Amtskommune and primærkommune are remaining of a previous administrative system used 
in Denmark. Amtskommune is a country subdivision, translated as County Municipality. They 
were used from 1970 to 2006, afterwards replaced by 98 Municipalities [46]. It is uncertain 
why there are still buildings that are assigned to Amtskommune (four in the final dataset and 
seven in raw data). Two of the analysed cases were registered before a system change in 1999, 
two others after, in year the 2009. There is also one building that belongs to primærkommune, 
registered in 2007. Most probably the ownership of those buildings was transferred to 
the Municipality, along with responsibilities which were taken over by the new administration. 
Otherwise, these are remaining of the previous system. 
Denmark is divided into 5 regions, which were introduced in 2007. There is no region owned 
building. It is rational that most of the buildings are owned by the municipality that they are

3.3 PARAMETRIC ANALYSIS 
The results represent characteristics of the buildings aged 50 and more years. The oldest 
building in the dataset is 168 years old. Buildings constructed before 1850 were excluded from 
the investigation, it is based on the assumption that they are not relevant to the current days and 
if they still exist, they are preserved due to their age and historical value. The research aimed 
to find features which would allow buildings constructed nowadays to have a longer service 
life than typical (which is around 50 years). 
The final dataset was divided into a calibration (427 cases) and a validation (101 cases) set. In 
the calibration set, as shown in Table 13, there is in total almost 333 thousand m2 of the total 
building area. The average age in the dataset equals 92 years, while the set contains buildings 
aged between 50 and 168. The average age of the dataset is 91 years and 9 months, while 
the median is 91 years. The total building area is worse distributed. The average equals 780 m2, 
while the median is just 240 m2. The average is so high due to several large-scale buildings in 
the dataset, 79 buildings are over 1 000 m2 including two over 10 000 m2.

Renovation 
There are numerous reasons for the renovation. Sometimes it is carried because the building 
does not fulfil the requirements of users and there is a need for a new function. It could as well 
be caused by building decay, changing surroundings, or a new idea for the business. Moreover, 
there is an economic aspect of that: the renovation is very expensive. Therefore, it is often 
postponed as long as possible or even avoided.  
It must be remembered that this study is based on buildings that still exist. Obviously, they can 
get renovated at any time, the feature is dynamic. Therefore, a straightforward conclusion from 
this characteristic cannot be made. Nevertheless, the renovation often changes the use of 
the building, by the transition, the building is again attractive for owners and other people. 
The renovation does not seem a necessary requirement for building to be preserved for many 
years. There are numerous buildings that are over 100 years old and were never renovated. 
Materials 
The half-timber walls and thatch roof are traditional building materials. They were used many 
years ago, mostly in housing. Nowadays these materials are also available, but usually, more 
durable elements are chosen. The bricks for walls and tiles for roofs are extensively used today 
and seem to be a reasonable choice. They are not only solid structural, but also fire-proof, which 
cannot be said about thatch and timber. 
Location 
The buildings in the countryside are of higher age than those in the city. Although in the country 
zone older buildings are placed, the zone cannot be used as a recommendation for the design. 
In most cases, the architect does not have an influence on the choice of location of the project, 
but it is specified by the owner. Moreover, the zones are changing with the growth of cities. 
The criteria can be only used to assess the validation set, to predict if the buildings are likely to 
have the long lifespan.

buildings in the dataset, there are both high and low valued designs. This indicates 
the inconsistency of design trends in those times. The highest values are mostly assigned to 
the buildings advanced in years.  
It seems that the cultural-historical value is reached with time and it is hard to predict whether 
the building will or will not gain an appreciation of the society. The parameter seems highly 
related to the age of the building, but it is hard to say how to achieve such quality. The buildings 
with the highest cultural value have the lowest area range. 
The environment where the building is placed as well as the appropriateness of the building in 
relation to the context is an important characteristic. Although the location cannot always be 
chosen, the building can be designed in a manner so that is suitable for the surroundings. 
The originality value refers to what happens to the building after the construction. To last many 
years, building should be maintained in a good condition and its originality should be kept. 
Although the trend line indicated that the older buildings are in slightly worse condition than 
the younger, the average age is the highest for the buildings valued 2. Due to contradictory 
results, the criterium is not used in evaluation of validation set. Nevertheless, it is reasonable to 
maintain buildings in the best possible condition to avoid their decay. 
For the purpose of evaluating the validation set, it is assumed that buildings with SAVE rating 
4 and above are likely to survive many years. Table 28 shows how the categories can be used 
to evaluate the lifespan of the buildings and to guide designers.

3.3.3.13 Building construction results’ summary 
Style 
Half of the analysed buildings are designed in a specific style. The younger buildings 
often are in other style. That implies inconsistency in new structures, nowadays several 
styles are used. It is although possible that those styles are not defined or named yet. 
The style of architecture relates not only to its aesthetics but also uniqueness. Therefore, 
the preference for long lasting-buildings is to be designed in a distinctive and 
recognizable style. 
Main plan  
The building’s layout is an important aspect of its flexibility. The more complex 
the plans are, the better. In buildings with wings, there is a possibility to accommodate 
several functions, even at the same time.  
Building elements 
The general conclusion from the analysis is that: the more types of building parts, 
decorations, windows and doors the buildings have, the older they are. 
Not only the number, but also the elements’ complexity is related to the buildings’ age. 
Windows with complex shapes are preferable (any but rectangular). The roofs should 
have a distinctive shape (gable, hip, mansard, pyramid). Although it was impossible to 
compare the age of buildings with and without dormers, it can be assumed that 
the façade and roof dormers are adding to the architectural value of the building. 
A building is an integral part, but it consists of elements, which were evaluated here. 
The elements of the buildings create the overall aesthetics of the architecture, but also 
its uniqueness. It must be mentioned that analysing components of the architecture 
brings a risk of losing a holistic view. A building must be thought of as a whole. 
Materials 
The data about gable, cornice, plinth and external wall treatment consist of information 
about the material used in that building elements. The most common construction of 
the elements is masonry or brick, which are very durable components. The oldest 
buildings, though, are often made of timber. The most popular finishing material is 
plaster.

95 
 
Material choice is strongly related to the availability and price at the time of 
the construction. Despite that fact, the materials are playing a significant role in 
the appearance and durability of the building.

4 DISCUSSION 
4.1 ANSWERS TO RESEARCH QUESTIONS 
4.1.1 Characteristics of long lasting buildings 
Based on the literature review it was established which features have the long-lasting 
buildings. The characteristics were assessed by parametric analysis of information 
provided by the FBB database.  
4.1.1.1 Aesthetics and perception 
SAVE architectural, originality and condition values were used to assess the aesthetics 
of the buildings. The architectural quality is the key aspect of buildings’ appearance. 
Originality value brings a conclusion that, the building should not be altered from its 
original appearance. It is important to keep the building in its original form because 
new building components do not fit its appearance. The buildings also need also to be 
maintained, in order to stay in good condition and be visually good looking. That are 
recommendation for the owners rather than for building designers. 
Although [39] states that the key feature of vital buildings is oversize, the results of this 
study show that buildings of a small size are preserved for many years. The 1- or 2-
storey buildings with low building area are preferred. 
The buildings should have distinctive features like style, building elements (individual 
parts, decorations, appealing windows and doors). The character of the architecture 
relays on its components.  
The materials also play a role in the buildings’ aesthetics. They must be chosen 
considering their aesthetics now and in the future. For instance, bricks have decorative 
features within, and are available in various colours and structures. Moreover, the brick 
wall wears off in an eye-pleasant way without losing its physical properties. Bricks are 
very durable and maintenance free [33]. 
Nowadays, materials with lower durability are used and are susceptible to weathering 
in an unattractive way. Although initially, the (i.e. glass or synthetic) façade looks 
impressive, the need for replacement occurs relatively fast. It appears that the choice of 
materials is important not only in terms of their durability but also their appearance. 
==PAGE:118==
 
 
 
103 
 
The materials that are weathering in a pleasant way are appreciated, like brick and roof 
tiles. In the newer constructions, glass or plastic are being used, which usually age in 
an unattractive way, thus need replacement fast. 
The perception of the building is a compatible aspect of the whole structure, not 
separate elements and materials. The overall impression is influenced by shapes, 
quality, colours and many others, which were grasped in the architectural and condition 
value.  
4.1.1.2 Uniqueness 
The uniqueness of the buildings is measured by the means of conservation (final 
SAVE) value. The buildings with the high score (1-4) have architecture, cultural history 
and craftsmanship that are outstanding examples of their kind and, due to their relation 
to the environment cannot be replaced. 
The analysis of the style of long-lasting buildings indicated that the buildings should 
have consistent features. Most of the cases below 50 years are in undefined style. 
The building, to last long, should be designed in the way that it has its identity with 
recognizable style. 
The results show that there is a correlation between the number of distinctive building 
elements and the building’s age. The more elements the building has, the older it is. 
Although [48] argues that the buildings without decorations can achieve high 
refinement, it appears that decorations matter. Contemporary architecture is missing 
decoration, especially modernist – perhaps that is a reason for early demolition. 
4.1.1.3 Durability 
SAVE originality value shows how well the buildings were preserved. The category 
points out that the changes and newly looking elements are unwelcomed in long-lasting 
structures. 
The condition value is a reflection of the materials’ durability, but also buildings’ 
maintenance. The high quality of the buildings (materials and craftsmanship) seem 
obvious yet is still worth mentioning. During the design, the choices are often based on 
the financial basis. Therefore, the quality is often compromised. Low-quality materials 
age faster thus, the buildings’ appearance is worse. 
==PAGE:119==
 
 
 
104 
 
The durability of the materials and so building elements is the crucial feature of 
building for surviving. The structural elements are difficult to replace, and therefore, 
high-quality wall and roof materials should be chosen.  
In the analysed dataset, the oldest are half-timber buildings, though the material is not 
common nowadays. Brick can be load- bearing element, it ensures breathability of 
the structure, serves as thermal mass and assures flexibility. This material was used 
purposefully for many years. 
Taking into consideration roof material, the oldest are buildings with thatch. 
The research indicates that the most common material for the roof cover is tile. 
Although, [26] finds that roof material has no impact on the lifespan.  
4.1.1.4 Reparability 
Condition SAVE value indicated if the building was maintained well, which might 
suggest ease of maintenance. 
It is not easy to assess whether the fact that a building was renovated is an indicator of 
ease of maintenance and reparability. If the building did not undergo renovation, it 
might mean that it was easy to maintain and the renovation was not needed. On 
the other hand, the renovation might be conducted because it was easy to do that. 
The materials should be chosen carefully, taking into consideration the ease of their 
maintenance and possibilities of replacement. 
4.1.1.5 Flexibility 
The conclusion of the parametric analysis is that the size of a buildings is a factor 
related to their longevity. The low-rise buildings with a small total area are preferable. 
Small buildings are more flexible than the large. It is easy to use a single family or 
tenement houses for various purposes. The large buildings like educational or shopping 
malls are not likely to accommodate new functions. 
The buildings’ main plans that can accommodate divergent functions are preferable. 
The winged buildings, for instance, can be adapted to several functions at the same 
time. 
There is a need to be ready for the unexpected. No one can predict neither the future, 
not the requirements that it will bring. The technological development is so fast that it 
==PAGE:120==
 
 
 
105 
 
is highly unforeseeable what will happen and for instance, what appliances people will 
like to have and use in their houses. People born at the beginning of the 20th century 
arrived in the world without television or washing machines, not mentioning the 
computers, dishwashers and flat-screen TV’s. They could not even imagine or dream 
of it. Similarly, new inventions used in the apartments, offices and other buildings will 
come. Because these are unknowns, the buildings must be designed in the way that they 
will be able to adapt to yet unknown technologies. 
4.1.1.6 Public appreciation 
Public appreciation was uneasy to assess. The only data that relates to that parameter 
are the cultural-historical value of the buildings and the final SAVE value – 
conservation. The cultural-historical aspects of the buildings are created with the time 
of its existence. This could depend on the history of building, events and relation to 
know people. Although the public appreciation cannot be measured or assessed, it is 
certainly an important aspect of buildings’ longevity. The buildings loved by society 
are likely to be preserved [23].  
4.1.1.7 Location 
The study concludes that the buildings located in the country zone are older than those 
in the urban. Certainly, the surroundings are changing and often designers do not have 
an impact on the project placement. The building could be located today in the outskirts, 
and in a few years, it could be a vital centre of the city [49]. It is not important if 
the placement is in an urban or a country zone. More important is what relation with 
the surrounding does the building develop with time. It is the significance of the place 
and people’s feelings about it, that will ensure the preservation of the building. 
The building must be set on the site, not only accordingly to the environment but also 
in relation to the context – surroundings. Cultural and historical perspective must be 
taken into consideration.  
It can be concluded that the environmental quality of the building set-up is an important 
aspect to assure the longevity. Good environment ensures the attractiveness of 
the building. The higher the quality of the surroundings is, the higher the appreciation 
and care for a building are. It is important for the building to fit the context, such bound 
to local settlement guarantees the longevity. 
==PAGE:121==
 
 
 
106 
 
 “Above all, we must remember that nothing that exists or comes into being, lasts, or 
passes, can be thought of as entirely isolated, entirely unadulterated. One thing is 
always permeated, accompanied, covered or enveloped by another; it produces effects 
and endures them. And when so many things work through one another […]” [50]. 
4.1.2 Prediction on buildings below 50 years 
The size, external wall and roof material, location, style and originality were used to 
predict buildings’ potential to last for years. The buildings designed recently performed 
badly. Therefore, there is a need to learn from past. The younger buildings fulfilled on 
average only 38%, of the requirements. The same criteria were applied to the buildings 
over 50 years, they resulted in 68%. The single characteristic in which younger 
buildings performed better was the originality. This is reasonable as they have lived for 
shorter time, thus did not undergo so many changes as the older buildings. 
Furthermore, the building construction data was used to assess accuracy of 
the prediction. The style, main plan, building components and materials have impact 
on the SAVE evaluation. Therefore, the scores for building construction appeared 
proportional to the percentage of fulfilled requirements formed based on the BBR 
information and SAVE evaluation. Nonetheless the buildings’ construction data was 
available only for four buildings. Consequently, even though the prediction seems to 
be accurate, it cannot be concluded if it is reliable. The prediction of buildings’ potential 
to last, as any other prediction, will be proofed wrong or right with the passing time. 
4.1.3 Guidelines for buildings’ longevity 
4.1.3.1 Recommendations for designers 
To design buildings that last long, following guidelines should be followed: 
4.1.3.1.1 Design consistent architecture with distinctive character and identity  
Based on the characteristic: Aesthetics and perception 
The conclusion from the results is that the small buildings are favoured. The size of the 
buildings plays a role in their perception. New buildings are larger than the analysed 
long-lasting buildings. The smaller buildings feel cosy and welcoming. Nevertheless, 
with the population growth and development, the requirements are changing. Possibly, 
the large buildings will gain appreciation with time. 
==PAGE:122==
 
 
 
107 
 
The architectural design should have its character and be recognizable. The study shows 
that around half of the older buildings have a distinctive style, while almost all the 
younger have undefined style. The materials and building components and proportions 
must create a unified composition with harmony and good impression.  
The results indicate that there is a lot to learn from the historical architecture, which 
should be used as lessons and inspiration for contemporary designs. The buildings 
should be concise and have a strong identity. The design must be distinctive and the 
style recognizable. 
4.1.3.1.2 Avoid simplicity, use several buildings’ elements and decorations 
Based on the characteristic: Uniqueness 
The results question the minimalistic design of modern buildings. They show that the 
buildings with distinctive building elements are favourable in terms of the longevity. 
The results indicate that the use of building elements is a feature of the long-lasting 
buildings. It also shows that the number of different decorations, types of doors and 
windows is also important. Moreover, shapes should not be simplistic e.g. windows of 
more complex shape than the simple rectangular or the roof with a definite appearance. 
In architecture, it is possible to achieve a unique building thanks to the parametric 
design. It can be a tool to reach high personalization level within modular solutions [6]. 
Software such as Rhino with Grasshopper plug-in allows producing architectural 
shapes that are different than any other. 
4.1.3.1.3 Choose materials wisely (aesthetic, durable and weathering pleasantly) 
Based on the characteristic: Durability 
For the building to last long, the materials must have a long service life to enable 
building longevity. Not only the strength of the materials matters. It should also be 
considered how the appearance of the materials will change after years of use. 
4.1.3.1.4 Consider ease of maintenance and renovation 
Based on the characteristic: Reparability 
The building should be designed so that it will be easy to maintain it. The connections 
between the buildings’ layers must be done in a way that allows replacement of the 
parts that are no longer useful, or their lifespan ended. This would make it possible to 
==PAGE:123==
 
 
 
108 
 
sustain the life of building for longer. For that purpose, spatial design, as well as the 
engineering solutions, must enable easy maintenance and renovation.  
4.1.3.1.5 Enable function change with time 
Based on the characteristic: Flexibility 
Unfortunately, it is unpredictable what will happen with the building in the future. It 
may need to be rearranged to serve other functions. If it cannot do it, it will be most 
probably knocked down. It is reasonable to design buildings in the way that they will 
be able to change their purpose with time.  
Specific solutions could enable a fast transition from one to another function. The result 
shows that buildings with dividable floor plans are more likely to be long-lasting. The 
complexity (wings) of the buildings enable accommodation of various functions. 
When designing buildings to last longer, one must consider the needs of future tenants 
or users. The long service life of the buildings brings along uncertainty - unknown 
future needs. Therefore, it is essential to be prepared for unexpected. The design should 
be as flexible as possible so that it will be useful in the future. It could be only imagined 
what will happen in 100 years from now and how humankind will live. 
Moreover, the structures of the future should be able to carry an additional load. Most 
probably, additional floors will be raised to use the space more effectively. The 
structures should be designed with consideration of the future additions, so it will be 
easy to add up more elements [8]. 
4.1.3.1.6 Create value for public 
Based on the characteristic: Public appreciation 
The buildings that are valuable to society are often preserved. In order to create public 
appreciation, the design must allow people’s interaction with the building. It could be 
by a place for events or appealing appearance of the building. The aesthetics and 
perception of the architecture play here a significant role. An example of an intervention 
to gain the appreciation of even dreadful building is mural. (This is a piece of artwork 
painted on the surface of a building.) They increase the aesthetics, creates an identity 
and have much more social, economic and cultural values [51]. 
==PAGE:124==
 
 
 
109 
 
4.1.3.1.7 Bind the building to the context 
Based on the characteristic: Location 
The location of the building cannot be chosen by the designer. Although, the relation 
to the context can be carefully planned. The building should not only suit the 
surroundings but also develop a bond with the specific place. The relation to the 
placement and peoples’ feelings about the space created by the building are likely to 
extend its life. 
4.1.3.2 Recommendations for owners 
The longevity of the building does not exclusively rely on the design. It is also 
influenced by the actions that happen after the construction. The owners of the buildings 
are responsible for making decisions that influence their lifespan. Therefore, they must 
be informed about the consequences of later additions and replacements of both 
buildings’ parts and materials. The original form is most appreciated in terms of 
aesthetics. Moreover, the owners are responsible for maintenance and renovation which 
both influence the durability of the building and its appearance.  
4.2 OBSERVATIONS 
4.2.1 Society growth 
It is only since the 20th century that people live in highly densified cities. In previous 
ages, humankind lived and used only low-rise structures. Nowadays there are higher 
and bigger buildings which provide different life qualities to their users, than one- or 
two-story buildings. Because it is a new tendency, there was not enough time for the 
designers of the buildings to develop techniques that will provide high standards to 
tenants and users of such multi-storey buildings. Therefore, the lifespan of 
such buildings is likely to be short and it is probable that they will be demolished before 
the end of the designed service life. 
 
The technological development and fast population growth changed the way of 
the construction of the buildings. The high-rises and large-scale buildings are relatively 
new structures. The buildings are having an increasingly bigger built-up area, as well 
as the number of levels, thus the bigger total building area. The number of constructed 
==PAGE:125==
 
 
 
110 
 
buildings is also rapidly increasing. The fact that the younger buildings require 
renovation faster than ever before, means that the concept is still not fully understood. 
There is a lot to learn in terms of designing and maintaining new buildings.  
One approach could be to come back to the traditional proportions of the buildings and 
choosing materials that were used through the centuries. Known solutions are safe, but 
it might be opportunistic to think that the traditional shapes and sizes will satisfy current 
society needs. Instead, they shall be used as a lesson ang guidance for future projects. 
4.2.2 New technologies 
As discussed in 4.1.1.5, there are new technologies to come in the everyday life. The 
buildings shall be able to accommodate the new devices, but also the new technologies 
can change the architecture in great extent.  
With the invention of mechanical ventilation, the approach to the indoor climate in 
the buildings changed. The knowledge used for the centuries on natural ventilation and 
breathability of structures was replaced by the mechanical systems. Before that, 
location, orientation, materials and structures as well as sizes of rooms were carefully 
considered in order to achieve best comfort [42]. 
Mechanical ventilation also interrupted the flexibility of the spaces, as it has created 
a need for additional connection to the ventilation source and proper dimensioning of 
the system. With natural ventilation, there is only a need for a water supply to kitchen 
and bathroom/toilet and electricity to each room. 
4.2.3 Need to learn from past 
According to the SAVE evaluation, newer buildings (1-50 years) are less appreciated 
than those in the higher age ranges. Although there are some iconic buildings 
constructed recently, there is a significant inconsistency in style and material use. 
“A huge potential lies in the study of our traditional building methods, transferring 
the knowledge of these fields into modern ways of constructing” [33]. This could be 
a solution to some of the challenges in contemporary architecture. Now there is no 
choice but to build fast, the traditional methods cannot be directly applied. However, 
they can be adapted to new needs.  
It is important to learn from the past – this is when the long-lasting buildings were 
created. Through many years the art of designing buildings was developed and 
==PAGE:126==
 
 
 
111 
 
improved. It is also important to keep in mind the changing needs, so as to enable 
flexibility by using current (new) technological solutions. The classical development is 
no longer possible. As the society is growing rapidly, fast solutions are required. 
Although the qualities of spaces from previous times, when the buildings were 
developed slowly and naturally, must be kept [40]. 
“Architecture should […] return to dealing with basic questions of necessity and set 
new standards for sustainability and economy” as said by Andres Lepik in [42]. 
4.3 LIMITATIONS 
The work done in this project is only relevant for the buildings in Lyngby and similar 
municipalities in Denmark.  
The world is changing faster than ever before, the technology develops and 
the population grows. The research is based on an assumption that parameters of 
existing buildings (constructed from 1850 – until 50 years ago) should be investigated 
in order to reveal longevity characteristics. It is not certain if the features of those 
buildings will apply nowadays or in the near future. There are many unknowns to be 
faced.  
The most common reason to keep a building is the aesthetic or historical meaning. 
Nevertheless, some places are preserved for other reasons. Chernobyl is an example of 
a structure which is still there, it is because the demolition would be too risky. It will 
remain as it is, not because it has its value, but because simple it is dangerous for human 
health to enter the area and demolish it. 
Only several aspects of buildings were investigated. There are many other 
characteristics that should be studied to discover their relation to building age. Despite 
physical characteristic of the buildings, there are also other reasons (i.e. socio-economic 
or political) for the buildings to last longer than its design service life. One of 
the reasons could be the economic situation of the owner, who simply cannot afford to 
move to a new place and to demolish the old. Demolition is an expensive procedure. 
Policies can influence the decision to demolish or renovate the building to reclaim its 
value. Politics are unpredictable. Financial reasons (land price, national economy, 
owners’ finances, etc.) are also influencing the decisions, although it is not linear. It 
==PAGE:127==
 
 
 
112 
 
may cause the demolition but also preservation because the demolition would consume 
too many resources[25]. 
The reasons why long-lasting buildings are not erected in recent years might be lack of 
interest in such buildings. The investors are expecting the buildings to last only for the 
period that they (the owners) need them. Probably, only institutions, governments and 
emperors are likely to invest in structures that will last for the century and remain of 
the power of the authorities. 
4.4 FURTHER RESEARCH 
The study looks only at the public buildings in Lyngby. Buildings in other locations 
and different type of buildings – the private – should be investigated. Housing will most 
probably behave differently and require other features to last long. Moreover, buildings 
in other locations should be studied. It is highly possible that the longevity is related to 
the place. There is a requirement for the building to suit to the context. As the context 
varies, the requirements might be different as well. A further study investigating other 
municipalities should be carried out. The capital would be very interesting location to 
investigated because the average lifespan is shorter there than in any other place. It 
would be beneficial in aspects of preserving unnecessary demolitions and impacts form 
those.  
Demolished buildings were not included in the study. The primary reason is the fact 
that the study attempts to define characteristics of the buildings that are appreciated by 
society and has a probability to stand longer than the average lifespan within its own 
category. Unfortunately, there is no available data on reasons of demolition. What are 
the reasons for the early demolition of the buildings? (As the records are hard to find, 
it would be advisable to keep a register of those buildings and ask the owner for 
reasoning before demolition, that would help designers to avoid mistakes and learn 
from that buildings). 
Although it might be exaggerated, the ideal lifespan for any building, in terms of 
the environmental impacts, would be forever. By eliminating the stage of the life cycle: 
end of life, it would reduce impacts from the demolition process. This usually has 
a significant impact on the environment. It would be interesting to investigate whether 
==PAGE:128==
 
 
 
113 
 
it is possible to design and build a structure that would last and serve people forever. 
Possibly, all components would be changeable with ease. 
Lastly, the validation of the created guidelines would be interesting. A building 
according to the recommendations could be designed and constructed, and data on their 
lifespan shall be collected.

5 CONCLUSION 
5.1 Research question 
The study answered posed research questions. First of all, the characteristics of long-
lasting buildings were identified and measured by means of available building 
information. Secondly, an attempt to predict whether existing buildings will have a long 
lifespan was made. Finally, the design guidelines for longevity were created. 
5.2 Main findings 
This study found buildings’ characteristics that would allow them to stand and provide 
a function for longer than typical 50 years. The long-lasting buildings are aesthetic and 
unique, durable and easy to maintain, flexible, appreciated by people and have bonding 
relation to the context. 
The buildings constructed less than 50 years ago demonstrate little characteristics of 
long-lasting buildings. Only 38% of buildings younger than 50 years fulfil the created 
requirements. The new buildings are bigger and use different materials. The large scale-
buildings are new construction types. Therefore, there was not much time to learn how 
to achieve the best quality of such structures. With the growing needs of the society, it 
is impossible to build as in the previous century. Yet the learnings from the past must 
be applied to latest designs. The architecture should be more concise and quality-
oriented. 
To design for longevity, the designers should follow the guidelines: 
• Design consistent architecture with distinctive character and identity  
• Avoid simplicity, use several buildings’ elements and decorations 
• Choose materials wisely (aesthetic, durable and weathering pleasantly) 
• Consider ease of maintenance and renovation 
• Enable function change with time 
• Create value for public 
• Bind the building to the context 
 
==PAGE:130==
 
 
 
115 
 
Moreover, in order to keep a building many for years the owners should: 
• Preserve original form of the building 
• Maintain the building 
5.3 Conditions to the answers 
The research and so the conclusions are limited only to the public buildings located in 
Lyngby and municipalities alike it. Moreover, it only analyses physical properties of 
the buildings, while the impact on the longevity could also have economics and politics. 
Due to the fast-changing needs, it is uncertain whether characteristics of long-lasting 
buildings of future will be same as of those analysed in this study.
