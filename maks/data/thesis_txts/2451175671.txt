Abstract
Urea-SCR is an attractive technology to lower the NOx emissions from the marine diesel en-
gine exhausts. Urea decomposition in the SCR, however, leads to solid byproduct formation,
which can cause clogging and corrosion problems. The main objective of the present study
was to develop a mathematical model to describe the evaporation and decomposition process
of urea, as well as formation of byproducts, based on a literature study and the experimental
results.
The experiments were designed to investigate the evaporation and decomposition behaviour
of urea in the temperature range of 140-200°C. The eﬀect of various process conditions such
as sample surface area, initial particle size and initial mass of TGA samples on the decom-
position curve of urea and CYA was investigated. The sample surface area investigations
showed that the urea TGA curve reaches a steady state over an extended period of time for
the large surface area, whereas the TGA curves for the small surface area showed steady
mass loss over time.
A mathematical model was developed to predict the evaporation and decomposition kinet-
ics of urea. The evaporation rate is modeled using two diﬀerent approaches - the Stefan
problem approach and the Hertz-Knudsen equation. The Stefan problem approach showed
higher mass loss compared to the results from the Hertz-Knudsen equation for the sample
surface area of 0.899 cm2. The model indicated that for an extended isothermal time, the
TGA curves attain a steady state due to the formation of biuret.

Project Description
Background
Urea-based Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR) is a promising technology for the abatement
of NOx from the exhaust of maritime diesel engines. There is a strong incentive to reduce
the size of the engines’ mixing chambers, which are comparable in size to the engine itself
However, size reduction is expected to increase the problems associated with the decompo-
sition of urea and solid byproduct formation. Urea decomposition is a complex process and
the formation of decomposition byproducts is undesirable due to the consequent clogging
and corrosion problems. Experimental and numerical investigation of the chemistry of urea
decomposition can assist in designing an eﬀective evaporation and decomposition system
that addresses the aforementioned problem.
Objective
The objective of this project is to develop a mathematical model for the decomposition
process of urea and formation of the heavy molecular byproducts, constituting the undesired
deposits. The model should describe the evaporation and decomposition of urea, as well as
the formation of byproducts. The model will be based on laboratory experiments conducted
at DTU Chemical Engineering and data from the literature. Based on the modeling results,
the practical implications for controlling byproduct formation during urea decomposition
will be discussed.
Content
• A literature survey on the urea decomposition process with an emphasis on limiting
the byproduct formation
• Establishment of an initial model to describe urea evaporation and decomposition,
based on previous work at DTU and the literature
• TGA experiments at varying process conditions with urea and selected byproducts, as
well as their mixtures
• Development and validation of the model by comparison with the experimental results
and the data from the literature
• Parametric study and discussion of practical implications

Chapter 1
Introduction
Globally, the international shipping activities consume a considerable amount of fuel, the
combustion of which produces gases such as carbon dioxide, methane, carbon monoxide, ni-
trogen oxides (NOx), sulfur dioxide and non-methane volatile organic compounds [1]. Diesel
is the main fuel used by larger sea-going vessels due to its cheaper cost compared to gasoline,
and ease of handling. Marine diesel engines are predominantly used for propulsion and power
generations in these vessels.
The International Maritime Organization (IMO) is a United Nations specialized agency re-
sponsible for shipping safety and protection of the marine environment. IMO’s MARPOL
convention for the prevention of pollution from ships adopted an air-pollution annex, Annex
VI, in 1997 that came into force in 2005. Annex VI limits the emission of main air pollutants
(including SO2 and NOx) in the exhaust gases from ships [2]. The revised Annex VI, adopted
in 2008 to strengthen the emission limits through improved technology and implementation,
came into force in 2010, the details of which are shown in Table 1.1.

SCR is the technology that utilizes a reductant that is selectively reacts with the oxides
of nitrogen over a catalyst to form N2 and H2O [5]. Ammonia was the preferred selective
reductant for many years [5]. However, storage and transport risks related to anhydrous am-
monia and higher costs associated with aqueous ammonia has made urea-based SCR more
attractive [6]. Potential toxicity testing of urea and its byproducts has revealed that there
are no causes of concern [7]. Usage of urea as a reducing agent requires urea to decompose
to ammonia through thermolysis and hydrolysis. Although the selectivity of urea-SCR is
high, side reactions can lead to formation of byproducts such as biuret, cyanuric acid and
melamine, the deposition of which can clog the injection nozzles and form deposits on the
ﬂue gas duct [9].
The present work investigates the evaporation and decomposition of urea and the subsequent
formation of heavy molecular byproducts with the objective of developing a model that takes
into account the formation of these solid byproducts from urea decomposition.

2.1
Selective Catalytic Reduction of NOx from diesel
engines
Diesel engines have high thermal eﬃciencies and lower emissions of carbon dioxide due to
better fuel eﬃciency, compared to gasoline engines [10]. However, due to their lean burn
nature, diesel engines typically produce 30 % more NOx emissions than gasoline engines,
without after-treatment [11]. SCR is the most widely used secondary treatment technology
to curb NOx emissions from the diesel engine exhaust. The main reaction of NOx with NH3
is:
4 NH3 + 4 NO + O2 →4 N2 + 6 H2O
(2.1)
NH3 based SCR is a well-developed technology and commonly used for reduction of NOx from
stationary sources [12]. However, for mobile sources, NH3 can pose storage, transportation
and handling diﬃculties. Urea-based SCR is an attractive alternative for mobile sources, as
urea poses less of the aforementioned problems [13]. The reductant is supplied through an
aqueous solution of urea 32.5 wt % in mobile SCR applications. The urea-water-solution
(UWS) is preferred over solid urea pellets to simplify the urea dosage into the exhaust
pipe. The melting point of this solution is –11 oC. Also known as Diesel Exhaust Fluid
(DEF) and commercially as adBlue®, it is injected upstream of the catalyst system into the
exhaust gas stream, wherein urea dissociates to NH3, which is the active reducing agent in
the SCR process, and then undergoes a series of reactions to convert the NOx to N2. In large
ships, a 40 wt % urea solution is typically used as the urea solution is generally stored in
the engine room, where the temperatures are high enough to not pose diﬃculties with the
UWS’s relatively high melting point [14].

The urea sample was heated to 175 °C and kept isothermally at 175 °C for 15 minutes. The
evaporating species condensed on contact with the cooler glass plate. The sample was cooled
to room temperature, and the re-condensed vapors were collected and analysed. The results
indicated the presence of urea in the re-condensed phase. The re-condensed vapors obtained
at 225 °C and 275°C also indicated the presence of urea.
Schaber et al. [27] also observed a mass loss in urea between 140 and 152 °C and attributed
it to urea vaporisation. They reported of the presence of condensed urea on the surface walls
of the gas collection adapter from the melting point and HPLC determinations.
Bernhard et al. [87] studied the evaporation of urea at atmospheric pressure as a function
of temperature for 80-153 °C under ﬂow reactor conditions. They used cordierite mono-
lith impregnated with 5.8% UWS for the TPD experiments with a temperature ramp of 10
K/min. Urea was the main compound in the gas phase of these TPD experiments, before
the temperature was high enough for urea thermolysis [35, 87]. They attributed the presence
of urea vapor to the increased gas velocity and decreased amount of urea on the monolith
relative to the gas ﬂow through the channels, which allowed the urea evaporation to take
place at a faster rate than the thermolysis. Urea was found in a monomolecular form in their
gas phase FTIR spectra at 180 °C at atmospheric pressure. The saturation vapor pressure
of urea for the temperatures of 116-122 °C are thermodynamically suﬃcient to yield 100-150
ppm gaseous urea. As the distance between the urea injection point and the SCR-catalyst
entrance is usually short due to space constraints, the kinetics of urea evaporation at lower
temperatures can play a dominant role [35].

2.2.2
Byproducts from urea decomposition
HNCO, produced by the thermolysis of urea, can react with unreacted urea and form byprod-
ucts, such as biuret and triuret. Production of heterocyclic compounds such as cyanuric
acid, ammelide, ammeline and melamine has also been noted to have formed [30, 27, 29].
Melamine can undergo condensation to form oligomeric compounds such as melon and melem
[28]. These deposits can not only create problems by clogging the UWS injector and mixer,
but can also reduce the engine performance or (though uncommon) poison the catalyst [31].
Thus, byproduct formation is undesirable in urea-SCR systems.
These deposits occur when the rate of formation of thermally stable byproducts is higher
than the rate of evaporation and decomposition of urea [59]. Thus, if urea evaporates before
the temperature is high enough for its thermolysis, formation of byproducts can be lowered,
as was observed in the Temperature programmed desorption (TPD) experiments by Bern-
hard et al. [34]. The thermal behaviour of the wall and the kinetics of the reactions forming
these byproducts may aﬀect the rate of urea evaporation and decomposition. Various fac-
tors such as temperature, rate of heating, pressure, mass and heat transfer limitations can
inﬂuence the decomposition and the subsequent product formation [27, 39].

Second reaction region (between 190 °C and 250 °C)
Biuret starts decomposing at 193 °C into urea and HNCO. Above 210 °C, a white precip-
itate starts forming, which converts to a sticky solid matrix, likely composed of CYA and
ammelide, on further temperate increase to 225°C. The stickiness disappears at 250 °C.
Ammeline is also detected in the analysis of the residue at 250 °C.
Third reaction region (between 250 °C and 360 °C)
A signiﬁcant mass loss is observed between 250 and 275 °C, which is attributed to the
sublimation of CYA. At 320 °C, urea, biuret, and melamine are no longer detected. CYA
decomposition begins in earnest between 320 and 330 °C and sublimation of ammelide takes
place prior to 340 °C.

2.2.5
Spray/wall interaction
Impingement of a UWS spray on the exhaust pipe wall can result in cooling of the wall
temperature. Lower exhaust temperatures have been attributed to form more deposits [42,
41, 54]. Lower ambient temperatures increase the heat transfer between the exhaust pipe
and the surroundings as the temperature gradient between the two is large. This results in
the lowering of the exhaust temperature and can contribute to more deposit formation [56].
Thus, insulating the exhaust pipe can provide a way of assisting in lowering the amount of
deposits formed [40, 56], as is seen in Figure 2.6.
Impingement of the spray on walls at lower temperatures can cause incomplete thermolysis
leading to urea deposit formation. These deposits further lower the wall temperature, and
can accelerate the formation of deposits by providing favourable reaction conditions for

2.2.6
Eﬀect of sample surface
Eichelbaum et al. [28] carried out TGA experiments with two types of sample holders: a
cylinder-like crucible with a height of about 1.3 cm and inner diameter of 0.7 cm, and a plate-
like crucible having a diameter of about 1.7 cm. They observed a reduced solid byproduct
formation over the temperature range of 25 to 750 °C and attributed it to the increased sam-
ple surface in direct exposure to the exhaust gases in the case of using plate-like crucibles.
They explained that using plate-like crucibles leads to quick removal of the intermediate
product gases and avoids high local concentration of HNCO, consequently increasing the
mass transport of HNCO and decreasing the formation of HNCO-derived solid byproducts.
Although they observed decrease in the amount of the byproducts, there was no inﬂuence
of the crucible geometry on the type of byproducts formed. Also, the quantitative results of
the reduction in the byproduct formation were not provided.
Lundstrom et al. [29] also carried out DSC and FTIR experiments to study urea decompo-
sition in ﬂow reactor conditions and open vessel type conditions using a cordierite monolith
impregnated with UWS and silica cup respectively, using heating rates of 10 and 20 K/min
for the temperature range between 25 to 760 °C. They expected higher gas phase concentra-
tions of reactants in the cup experiments compared to the monolith ones, leading to higher
concentration of CYA in the cup experiments. They observed an additional peak on the DSC
corresponding to the HNCO at the temperature above 300 °C. This peak was not observed in
the monolith experiments. The presence of the peak indicated that the formed byproducts
decompose to HNCO in the gas phase, and that the overall distribution of byproducts was
aﬀected by the sample administration method [29].

2.2.8
Eﬀect of system layout and spray characteristics
Injection mounting geometry
Various aspects of the system layout can contribute to deposit formation, one of which is the
geometry of injector mounting. The angle of injection mounting inﬂuences the trajectory of
the droplets and can be designed to avoid impingement of the spray on the wall. Zheng et
al. [40] compared two diﬀerent types of injection mounting geometries: an injection nozzle
mounted onto a straight exhaust pipe at 45 degree, and an injection nozzle mounted coaxi-
ally on an S-shaped exhaust pipe, as is shown in Figure 2.10.
(a) A 45 degree injection mounted on
straight pipe
(b) A coaxial mounting on S-

Figure 2.10: Geometry of injection mounting.
In the experiments by Zheng et al. [40], the temperature was made an independent variable,
while the exhaust ﬂow rate and urea injection rate were 120 kg/hr and 0.115 kg/hr, respec-
tively. For low temperatures, it was found that S-pipe geometry produced less deposits as
compared to the straight pipe. In the absence of a mixer, S-pipe was deemed more suited
for low deposit formation [40].
Mixers
For the same experimental conditions, Zheng et al. [40] also tested the deposit formation
with and without a mixing unit and observed that for both types of injection mounting
geometries, the deposit formation was reduced by introducing a mixing unit. Two diﬀerent
type of mixing units were used: a butterﬂy mixer and a grid mixer. These mixers are shown
in Figure 2.11.
The setup with a grid mixer, which resembles a wiremesh mixer, was found to yield lower
amounts of deposits than with a butterﬂy mixer, hinting that large surface area may con-
tribute to lowering deposit formation. The placement of mixer can be equally important. If
the mixer is placed too far away from the injector, the injected spray can penetrate through
the exhaust and increase the chance of DEF pooling on the inside of the exhaust pipe, and
cause deposit formation [48]. A combination of suitable mixers with injectors can also aﬀect
the deposit formation [49].

Urea injection rate
In the experiments by Zheng et al. [40], the temperature and ﬂow rate were kept constant,
while the width of the injection pulse was increased until wall-wetting occurred. The results
showed that the evaporation of the droplets is barely sustained at low temperature and low
ﬂow rate. As the exhaust ﬂow rate is increased, the maximum urea injection rate increases
as well, but wall wetting is not observed. This indicates that an increase of the exhaust ﬂow
rate improves the heat transfer at the wall. An increase in the injection rate may cool the
wall of the exhaust pipe and contribute to deposit formation [42]. Zhu et al. [56] also state
that a decrease of 32 % in the deposit yield is observed upon decreasing the injection rate
by 20 %. The lower deposit formation is attributed to less ﬁlm formation at the wall due
to better atomization which is a result of decrease in the Sauter mean diameter(SMD) with
lower injection rate.
Flow ﬁeld
The position of the injection nozzle has been shown to have an inﬂuence on urea deposit
formation [56]. A nozzle placed at the center of the pipe can help in a proportionate dis-
tribution of the droplets, which inﬂuences their eﬀective evaporation and thus can decrease
the probability of wall ﬁlm formation and thus reducing the amount of deposits formed [56].

Eﬃcient and accurate modeling of urea evaporation and decomposition is necessary to better
understand the eﬀect of diﬀerent parameters on deposit formation.
Modeling UWS evaporation is complex, as the exact interaction between urea and water is
not clear. Urea, being a solute, aﬀects the evaporation rate of water [23]. As water evapo-
rates faster than urea, the droplet becomes concentrated in urea as water starts evaporating.
If the droplet size is small and the water evaporation rate is low, the urea concentration
increase is uniform throughout the droplet and a solid particle is formed. If the water evap-
oration rate is high, there is a urea concentration buildup at the surface of the UWS droplet
leading to the formation of a urea shell at the droplet surface [23].

3.1.2
UWS depletion model by Abu Ramadan et al. [15]
Abu Ramadan et al. [15] have used a multicomponent vaporization model to model the be-

that the gas phase surrounding the droplet is in a quasi-steady state, and that the gas is
not soluble in the droplet, i.e. diﬀusion is only one way (Stefan ﬂow). The model ignores

neglected. The evaporation model includes a gas phase analysis, a liquid phase analysis and
a gas-liquid interface analysis.
Urea depletion is modeled by two approaches: (1) vaporization and (2) direct thermal decom-
position. In the vaporization approach, the latent heat of vaporization of urea is obtained
from the ﬁrst law of thermodynamics. The direct thermolysis approach assumes that urea
decomposes much faster than it vaporizes [15]. Urea depletion rate is obtained by two dif-
ferent expressions: a surface expression and a volumetric expression, based on whether the
decomposition only takes place at the boundary layer region of the droplet (referred to as
Kinetics-S) or not (referred to as Kinetics-V).
Ramadan et al. [15] compared the results of the numerical model with the experimental
data on the temporal time evolution of a UWS droplet by Wang et al. [19]. As Wang et al.
[19] used radiative shells to minimize the radiation eﬀects, Abu Ramadan et al. [15] ignored
the eﬀects of radiation as well and used a DL approach to make the numerical predictions.

3.3
Summary of the literature study
In this literature survey, previous work on the urea decomposition process has been outlined
and discussed. Urea decomposition is a complex process and various authors have proposed
mechanisms to take into account the various byproducts that are formed as urea decomposes
and have studied the parameters that aﬀect the formations of these deposits.
Schabet et al. [27] investigated the thermal decomposition of solid urea in an open vessel, and
presented reaction schemes based on the distribution of byproducts at diﬀerent temperatures.
In addition to temperature, parameters such as the spray/wall interaction, heating rate and
system layout have been experimentally and numerically studied to determine their impact
on deposit formation.
Various authors have proposed numerical models based on certain assumptions to model
urea decomposition. Ebrahimian et al. [21] used an NRTL model to take into account the
presence of urea on UWS evaporation. Abu Ramadan et al. [15] have modeled the UWS
depletion both as a multicomponent vaporization process, and as a direct thermolysis one.
However, they neglected the formation of byproducts in their model. While Ebrahimian et
al. [21] have included the formation of byproducts in their urea decomposition model, their
kinetic data is obtained by data ﬁtting and is dependent on the initial mass of the sample.
Brack et al. [59] also considered the formation of the secondary byproducts in their kinetic
model and considered the eﬀect of crucible geometry in their model. Both Ebrahimian et
al. [21] and Brack et al. [59] have modeled the decomposition of a single droplet and hence
ignore the energy balance equation.
Allingham [73] modeled the urea decomposition process based on models by Brack et al. [59]
and Gan et al. [74] as both models take into account the urea byproduct formation, and
studied the eﬀect of varying heating rates on byproduct formation. Studies indicate that a
fraction of urea evaporates and thus does not decompose in the condensed phase [27, 46].
Therefore, it is of interest to further develop the model by Allingham [73], by including a
term for urea evaporation.

5.1
Modeling assumptions
Modeling urea decomposition involves various complications as the exact chemistry of the
process is highly disputed. The inclusion of evaporative mass losses further adds to the
complexity. Certain assumptions with regard to the urea evaporation and decomposition
process are made and described as follows.
• The sample is assumed to be at the temperature displayed by the TGA. i.e. the thermal
gradients between the heating element and the thermocouple are neglected. Also, The
sample is assumed to have the same temperature throughout at any point of time. The
assumption eliminates the need to include the heat balances in the model.
• The liquid and gas phases are assumed to be ideal. The ideal gas and liquid phase
assumptions allows for the calculation of the gas density.
• A thermodyamic equilibrium is assumed at the liquid-gas interface. This allows the
mole fractions to be determined from the saturation pressures by Raoult’s law.
• Air is assumed to be the ambient as its properties are readily available. The TGA
experiments have been performed with N2 as the ambient. However, as air mainly
comprises of nitrogen, the assumption should not introduce signiﬁcant errors.

6.1.2
Summary of the experimental results
Urea TGA experiments were performed to study the inﬂuence of various process parameters
on the decomposition of urea at lower temperatures. For the experiments with a holding
time of 3 h at 150 and 160 °C, it was observed that the mass loss curve eventually reaches
a steady state, hinting that the evaporation and decomposition kinetics eventually reach an
equilibrium. The mass loss curve for the isothermal experiments at 140 °C showed continual
decrease during the three hours. However, based on the mass loss curve behaviour observed
at 150 and 160 °C and the results by [88], it is expected that the mass loss curves stabilize
after a prolonged time.
The results from the particle size experiments indicate that the initial particle size of urea
samples does not have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the urea decomposition process at isothermal
temperatures. Similarly, the change in the N2 gas sweep ﬂow does not have much signiﬁcance
on the mass loss curves.
The initial mass of CYA samples was varied to investigate the inﬂuence on the TGA mass
loss curves. The results indicated that the mass loss rate of CYA is not highly inﬂuenced by
the initial sample mass.
The sample surface area investigations were performed with two crucibles types having sur-
face areas of 0.899 and 0.283 cm2 respectively at diﬀerent isothermal temperatures with a 3
h holding time. For the larger surface area, as mentioned previously, the mass loss curves at-
tained a steady state indicating an equilibrium between the evaporation and decomposition
kinetics. In the case of the smaller surface area, the mass loss curves showed a continu-
ous mass loss indicating that, as opposed to the large surface area, the evaporation and
decomposition kinetics do not reach equilibrium.

Conclusion
The project aimed at the establishment of a model that takes into account the evapora-
tion and decomposition of urea and its byproducts. The project also aimed at studying
the inﬂuence of various process conditions on the urea decomposition process through TGA
experiments.
The inﬂuence of various process conditions on the mass loss of urea and CYA was analysed.
The isothermal experiments with 4 h holding time indicate the evaporation and decompo-
sition processes eventually reach an equilibrium and stabilize the urea TGA curve. Initial
particle size, and ﬂow rate of N2 did not signiﬁcantly impact the mass loss curves for the
urea TGA experiments. Similarly, the eﬀect of initial sample mass on the CYA TGA curve
was negligible. The sample surface area experiments showed that crucible size has an evident
eﬀect on the TGA curves. For the urea samples, the TGA curves for the large sample surface
area eventually reached a steady state during the course of the 4 h isothermal time. The
TGA curves for the small surface area showed a steady mass loss over the isothermal period.
It indicates that the evaporation and decomposition kinetics may have reached equilibrium
in the large crucible, but fail to do so in the small crucible. In case of the CYA samples,
the CYA decomposition was faster in the large crucible compared to the small crucible. The
faster decomposition was attributed to larger surface area and lower mass transfer limita-
tions.
The data from the experiments with 4 h holding time was used to calculate experimental
mass loss rates, which were then compared to the theoretical calculations using the Stefan
problem approach and the Hertz-Knudsen equation respectively. These two approaches were
implemented in the mathematical model to investigate the eﬀect of evaporative mass loss on
the decomposition process. This model is based on the kinetic scheme by Brack et al. [59]
and accounts for the formation of urea decomposition byproducts. The model, primarily
adapted by Allingham [73], was modiﬁed to account for the isothermal holding-time, and
the evaporation of urea was modeled using two diﬀerent approaches - the Hertz Knudsen
equation, and the Stefan problem approach. The model was then compared with the results
from the TGA experiments. The modeling results indicate that TGA curves at 140, 150
and 160 °C reach a steady state after a ﬁnite time during the isothermal period due to the
formation of biuret. For the large crucible size, the model showed more mass loss when the

evaporation rate was modeled using the Stefan problem approach.
In conclusion, the model provides an overview of the urea evaporation and decomposition,
and byproduct formation process. Further improvements to the model in terms of optimisa-
tion of kinetic parameters based on the experimental results are needed to get more accurate
predictions.
7.1
Future work
The mathematical model shows quantitative deviations from the experimental results. It
also predicts that the rate of evaporation of urea does not have a signiﬁcant impact on the
decomposition process. These outcomes are based on limited experimental data and under-
standing of the chemistry of the urea decomposition process, and further work is needed to
produce more deﬁnitive results.
The 140 °C experiments are inadequate to accurately predict a purely evaporative mass loss,
as the byproduct formation initiates shortly after urea has melted. TGA experiments for
temperatures lower than 140 °C need to be performed. The experiments with isothermal
holding time of 4 h indicate that the TGA curves stabilize after a ﬁnite time. The isothermal
holding time should be prolonged to get the extent of mass loss after the mass loss curves
have stabilized for diﬀerent isothermal temperatures.
The sample surface area results indicate that the smaller surface area samples undergo con-
tinuous mass loss and do not reach a steady state, as opposed to the larger sample surface
areas. It is not clear if the mass loss curves for the small and the large surface areas achieve
the same mass loss after a ﬁnite time of more than 4 h. This can be evaluated with prolonged
time experiments with the two diﬀerent crucible sizes.
With regards to the kinetic model, the isothermal experiments with 4 h holding time in-
dicate that the model over-predicts the mass loss and byproduct formation. This is likely
because of the kinetic parameters for the model are ﬁtted for speciﬁc process conditions,
and need further optimization to better ﬁt the experimental data. The numerical results
for the isothermal period at 200 °C indicate that there is a steady mass loss due to the
decomposition of biuret. Experiments investigating the isothermal period of behaviour of
biuret should be performed to investigate if the model prediction is correct.
