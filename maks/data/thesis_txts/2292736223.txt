Abstract
ATCs and zoning in markets with high penetration of renewable energies
Richard Klafke
June 2015
DTU, Department of Electrical Engineering
The purpose of this thesis is the analysis of problems and effects of available trans-
mission capacity (ATC) settings in a zonal market environment with high penetration of
renewable energy production. The analysis is accomplished by using a computational opti-
mization algorithm which is implemented in the objective-oriented programming language
Python and solved with the optimization solver Gurobi.
First the mathematical formulation of the optimization algorithm is introduced and it is
explained how the unpredictable wind production is realized in the purposed model. The
model which consists of two separated optimization problems for the day ahead market
and the real time market is then applied to two network examples. For an basic analysis a
small 6 bus electrical system is used [1]. Afterwards the model is simulated with the IEEE
24 bus test system [2].
The results of the thesis identify that ATC settings have an inﬂuence on the total
system costs and the social welfare of a system and that an optimal setting for ATCs
in the day ahead market is existing. However this point is difﬁcult to determine due to
several inﬂuences from available capacities, balancing costs and occurring line congestions.
Additionally the effects of high amount of wind production with different wind scenarios
on the system costs were examined.
Keywords:
Energy markets, available transmission capacity (ATC), zoning, day
ahead market, real time market, optimization, Gurobi, Python, social welfare, system costs

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
1.1.1
Electricity markets
The ﬁrst competitive energy market system in the world was introduced in Chile in
1982 as a part of the Electricity Act. State owned generation, transmission and distribution
facilities were privatized and a competitive market was introduced. Since then power
producers are required to pay for transmitting their generated energy to the consumers,
to declare the availability of power plants and their marginal cost per produced unit.
The submitted bids from power generators to supply a certain amount of energy at a
certain price and made it possible to determine spot market prices for electricity [3]. The
monopolies of state owned electricity system came to an end. Although there was still only
one transmission grid existing, competition was introduced due to dividing the market
into producers, retailers, transmission and distribution system operators. The produced
energy is transmitted over physical power lines which are limited by their maximum
thermal liability and electrical capacity. As a result not all traded energy can be transferred
on a direct way from the producer to the end consumers which creates balancing and
transmission costs. Additional unpredictable outages of generation and transmission
equipment causes increased costs within the system.
In any economical market demand and supply determine the price for a good. Unfortu-
nately electricity cannot be stored in a large scale. It has to be produced and consumed in
the same amount and at the same time. Moreover electricity is often produced at a distance
to the point of consumption and has to be transmitted via transmission and distribution
grids with high efforts and expenses. Transmission system operators have to deal with dif-
ferent issues including frequency and voltage stability, line thermal liability, capacity and
unpredictable outages of system components. Additionally the high amount of ﬂuctuating

The electricity markets consists of a day ahead market which is a short-term transaction
market. The trading takes place one day in advance. At the next day in the real time market
the differences between the actual demand and the day ahead commitments is balanced
by considering the actual transmission grid. The transmission operator has to balance the
network to try to deliver the required energy to the end consumers. The electricity grid
is constraint by transmission capacities and generators by their minimum and maximum
up and down regulation and minimum and maximum capacity. The electricity price can
be determined with the found market equilibrium as shown in ﬁgure 1.1. To determine
the prices of electricity two different pricing models are used which are outlined in the
subsequent section [5].

1.2. REVIEW

1.1.2
Pricing models
In Europe a zonal market model is implemented. To determine the zones relative
strong connected areas of the electricity grid are combined into a zone consisting of several
producers and loads. Furthermore criteria including wind forecasts, historical load data,
line capacities and given constraints from the transmission grid operator are considered for
the zone determination. For each zone a market price is determined by ﬁnding the market
equilibrium with the supply and demand curves. In case of a constraint line due to power
overload the zone is split. Zones that are net exporters will pay the price of the importing
zone. Zones that are net importers pay the price from the zone they are importing from.
However this market pricing system does not reﬂect the actual physical conditions. For
example power plants that are poorly placed receive the same income as power plants that
are placed nearer to high load connection points [6]. Hence the additional costs lower the
overall social welfare.
In comparison in the USA a nodal market model is used which determines a price for
each node in the electrical grid. The beneﬁt from this pricing model is that line congestions
and inferior placed power plants are better represented in the pricing model. For relatively
weak interconnected electrical grids this model reﬂects line congestion and misplaced
power plants in a better way. High prices at certain points in the grid provide an incentive
to invest in new transmission lines or gives an indication of where to place potential new
power generators [5]. This pricing model needs a higher computational power than the
zonal pricing model. Furthermore in situations with no congestion nodal prices are equal
to the zonal prices.
A long term approach is to ﬁnd a model that represents the actual costs in a better way
than the zonal pricing model and reduces the computational effort of the nodal pricing
model. The goal is to ﬁnd a way to use given data like available transmission capability
(ATC), wind forecast, transmission line limits, load data and other given system data to
determine the zones dynamical one day in advance. In such a model the zones would
change with time depending on the actual system conditions and would represent the
real costs in a better way than in the present zonal pricing model. The uncertainty of
renewable energy production in the system could be managed in a more efﬁcient way
trough a dynamic zone environment.
1.2
Review
The existing pricing models have advantages and disadvantages. The nodal pricing
reﬂects the actual cost of a system with local prices at each connection point. From a
socio-economic perspective it represents the actual costs of the system in the best way.

However in case of no congestion the electricity prices in the zonal and nodal market are
equal. Therefore the prices are calculated with a higher effort in the nodal market [7].
Moreover in a very strong meshed grid the system cost increases not signiﬁcantly in a
zonal market environment [8]. The cost of congestion are better reﬂected by nodal pricing,
therefore for weak connected grids this is the better solution. Higher costs in zonal pricing
and an overall lower social welfare was stated already by Hogan in 1999 [9].
An alternative method could be to ﬁnd a zoning algorithm which determines the
zones dynamically in the day ahead by considering available transmission capacities
(ATCs), historical load data, renewable energy production forecasts, generator data and
transmission system conditions.
In central Europe, a zonal pricing system is applied which determines prices for each
country. The Scandinavian countries are divided in ﬁve zones which became necessary
due to weak transmission lines in the system [6]. For the division of networks different
factors are considered, namely line capacities, available generators, renewable sources
and existing system operation issues. There are different approaches to divide the power
network in zones for market administration, system monitoring and operational reasons.
Instead of using the boundaries of ownership in each country, the electrical distances in an
electricity grid can be used to determine zones which could make the planning for system
operation better and hence could safe costs during operation [10]. A dynamic zoning
method was discussed for the Guangdong power grid in China which uses a sensitivity
analysis to divide the network in zones depending on the actual system conditions. This is
done by calculating power transfer distribution factors (PTDF) which are used to create a
dividing matrix. The method was studied to make the monitoring and operation for the
system operator with lower amount of data easier [11]. Another method was proposed
by Shayesten [12] which instead of PTDFs uses the available transfer capability of the
network and different clustering methods to provide a faster system analysis method for
the operator. Another dynamic zoning algorithm for reserve zones was introduced. With
more unpredictable renewable energy production in the electricity grid, a dynamic zoning
determination can help to reﬂect the system status in a better way. The procedure is based
on statistical clustering methods and centrality measurement which are based on PTDFs
[13]. Although there are different research studies in dynamic zoning the idea of using
ATCs for determining the pricing zones of a system depending on the system conditions is
a new challenging approach. Further investigations and analysis have to be done before
a dynamic zoning pricing system could be possibly developed. This thesis focuses on
the impact of the ATC selection in the day ahead market, onto the total system costs and
the social welfare of a system. The effects of ATC selection for transmission capacities
between zones and the effect of high penetration of renewable energies with different
ATCs settings is investigated by a Python optimization problem which makes it possible to

1.3
Project objectives
The objectives of the project are the determination of problems and effects with varying
ATC settings in the day ahead market. Additional the impact of different wind scenarios
with different ATCs settings are analyzed. The simulation of a day ahead market model
and a real time market model is realized by building a Python optimization model. It can
be applied to networks with any desired number of nodes and components. Generator data,
load curves and wind scenarios can be inserted into the model to simulate different system
conditions. The model calculates system costs, power ﬂows, prices and production levels
of the generator. The main objectives are stated as follows:
1. Day ahead and real time market modeling with the objective-oriented programming
language Python and the optimization problem solver Gurobi [14; 15].
2. Building a Python/Gurobi model which can be applied to networks with any desired
number of nodes and components such as generators and loads.
3. Analysis of the inﬂuence of choosing ATCs in the day ahead market on social

Optimization model with Python and Gurobi
The mathematical optimization market clearing problem is implemented with the
object-oriented programming language Python using the optimization solver Gurobi which
is freely accessible for research purposes [14; 15]. The day ahead market clearing algo-
rithm in section 2.1.1 on page 7 is represented by a pyhton class, which includes a data
import function, a data export function and the actual market clearing algorithm with
sets, parameters, variables, constraints and the objective function. The real-time market
algorithm in section 2.1.1 on page 9)is realized with identical elements in a python class.
These classes are called in a main Python ﬁle. First the day ahead market is optimized
which hands over the generator bids to the real time market. Then the real time market
is simulated and the results are stored in a csv-ﬁle. Besides the mentioned functions the
market classes contain functions to update the limit of the transmission capacity in the
day ahead market, the relative asymmetry in the real-time market and the wind production
variable for the wind generator in the real-time market.
The different transmission limits between the zones in the day ahead market are
simulated by updating the upper bound of the transmission capacity variable with the
actual total transmission limit multiplied by a factor α. For each α several optimizations
of different line symmetries in the real-time market are realized by updating the upper
bound of the transmission lines with the total capacity multiplied by the factors β and
(1 −β). For both updating processes a python function is called which is included in
the respective class. A more detailed explanation for the transmission limit updates is
given with the network example in section 2.1.5 on page 20. The update function for the
wind production is used to perform a Monte Carlo simulation. The function simulates the

Deﬁnition of Available Transmission Capacity
”ATC is a measure of the megawatt capability of the system over and above already
committed use of transmission lines.” [22]
”The maximum power that can be transferred from one area to another area is called
transfer capability.” [23]
The available transmission capacity is calculated as follows:
ATC = TTC −TRM −ETC −CBM
(2.14)
Total Transfer Capability (TTC)
The amount of electric power that can be transferred reliably in an electric network
by meeting all speciﬁc system requirements such as voltage, current, frequency and
temperature [22; 23].
Transmission Reliability Margin (TRM)
Reserved amount of capability which ensures a secure transmission under a reasonable
amount of uncertainties of network equipment [22; 23].
Existing Transmission Commitments (ETC)
Already existing transmission commitments including retail customer service [22; 23].
Capacity Beneﬁt Margin (CBM)
Amount of capability which is reserved for generation reliability. It ensures the access
to other generation capacity in the interconnected transmission grid [22; 23].
ATCs can be calculated with different methods like DC power ﬂow, power transfer

the social welfare and the system costs. Different determination of ATCs are represented

3.1.5
Discussion of 6 bus system results
By examining the 6 bus system several important outcomes can be summarized. The
ﬁrst step of the 6 bus system analysis focused on the variation of different ATCs settings
in the day ahead market with a ﬁxed asymmetric line setting in the real time market. With
increasing available transmission capacity the day ahead and real time costs decrease,
whereby the real time costs at a certain point increase again due to congestion on one of the
transmission lines. Because of the congestion, the system balancing costs lead to higher
overall costs. This effect is interesting hence the ATCs could be used to determine zones
in a future pricing model. Furthermore the investigation with a ﬁxed line setting in the
day ahead market made clear that the line symmetry of intra zonal lines have a signiﬁcant
inﬂuence on the total system costs.
For this reason the second step of varying the real time model line settings for each
ATC factor α were achieved. The investigation verify the assumption that the asymmetry
line setting has a negative inﬂuence on the system costs. Hence asymmetric intra zonal
connections should be avoided to minimize the effect of congestion on lines with lower
capacity.
The third and last step of the 6 bus system analysis applied different wind scenarios
on the system by using a Monte Carlo simulation with three different beta distributions.
Due to low marginal prices of the wind farms with activated wind production the total
==PAGE:71==
3.2. IEEE 24 BUS SYSTEM SCENARIOS
51
system costs will decrease. Besides this effect two other important investigations were
made. Firstly the point of minimum system costs with a speciﬁc ATC setting shifts to
higher values compared to the scenario without wind production. Secondly the increase of
costs due to asymmetry of the transmission lines in the real time market can be reduced.
The scenario with low wind production values has the lowest system costs and maximizes
the social welfare to the highest value. The necessary regulations of the conventional
generators with a low wind proﬁle are lower compared to the higher wind scenarios. With
high differences of the wind production compared to the committed power bids in the day
ahead market the optimal settings for minimum cost of the system change. This problem
should be addressed with better wind forecasts and with considering the renewable energy
production when zones are determined. If possible, zones should be selected consisting
the same amount of low and high marginal cost generators.
The three main problems in the 6 bus system analysis which where investigated are
stated as follows:
1. The selection of intra zonal available transmission capacities inﬂuence the balancing
between the committed generation and the actual system conditions. A setting of
ATCs to reach the minimum system points is existing however the optimum can not
be generally determined yet.
2. Asymmetric intra zonal transmission capacity increases the total system costs due to
possible congestion on transmission lines and the resulting up and down regulation
of generators in the system.
3. High penetration of wind production lowers the total system costs and the effect of
increasing cost due to asymmetric intra zonal transmission lines. The minimization
of system costs is most efﬁcient with lower wind production values due to less
congestion and balancing costs. Because higher wind penetration can cause problems
with congestion in the grid, an optimal selection of the zones with a similar number
of renewable generators is recommended.

simulated with the IEEE 24 bus system [2].

CHAPTER 4
Conclusions
4.1
Summary
The purpose of this thesis was the analysis of problems and effects of available
transmission capacity (ATC) settings in a zonal market environment with high penetration
of renewable energy production. The analysis was accomplished by using a computational
optimization algorithm which was implemented in the objective-oriented programming
language Python and solved with the optimization solver Gurobi. The effects and inﬂuences
of ATC determination and asymmetric intra zonal transmission lines on the total system
costs have been identiﬁed. Additionally the inﬂuence of high amount of unpredictable wind
power production was found out. The increasing share of ﬂuctuating renewable energy
production in electricity networks requires new approaches for pricing methods, system
monitoring and operation. The existing zonal and nodal pricing models have advantages
and disadvantages. To develop a new dynamical zonal pricing model with ATCs the
inﬂuence of different ATCs settings on the total system costs has to be understood and
analyzed. The investigations, observations and analysis of this thesis help to understand
these effects and have not been studied before. In this thesis two main objectives have
been:
1. The analysis of the inﬂuence of choosing ATCs in the day ahead market on the total
system costs by using a computational optimization algorithm implemented in the
programming language Python.
2. The determination of the impact of high amount of wind generation on the total
system costs with different ATC settings.
Several different approaches to divide networks in zones for market administration
and system monitoring are purposed by different studies. Dynamic zoning methods

as introduced at the Guangdong power grid in China [11] and analysis method which
use PTDFs or ATCs to make system analysis faster [13; 12] are research studies in the
area of this thesis. With the explanation of electricity market models from Morales
[16] and Kirschen [4] the model was mathematically formulated and implemented in the
programming language Python [14]. The unpredictable wind production scenarios were
simulated with a Monte Carlo simulation [18; 25]. Two scenario networks were used to
simulate power ﬂows, production, demand, ATCs and line symmetry settings. The ﬁrst
investigations were made on the 6 bus system from Oggioni [1] and extended to a larger
24 bus network from IEEE [2]. Additionally a zone determination with a hierarchical
clustering method was realized and the resulting zone determination was applied to the
IEEE 24 bus system.
To summarize the results and investigations six main outcomes of the thesis can be
formulated:
1. An optimal setting for available transmission capacities in the day ahead market
is existing to reach the minimum system costs. However this point is difﬁcult
to determine due to several effects from available capacities, balancing costs and
occurring line congestions. The optimal available transmission capacity identiﬁed in
this thesis is rather an interval than a speciﬁc value.
2. Asymmetric line settings between the zones increase the system costs therefore
an extreme asymmetric line combination should be avoided because it leads to
congestion of the line with lower capacity. Similar to the ATC setting values only an
interval can be stated and not a speciﬁc value. The ranges of the symmetry scaling
factor β can be seen in table 4.1 and 4.2. Generally it can be recommended to choose
a symmetric line setting.
3. High amount of wind production decreases the total system costs due to low marginal
costs. The marginal costs can be even negative due to subsidies that wind farms
obtain. However with existing wind power and increasing wind production in
the system higher system costs due to higher balancing costs can be investigated.
Generally it can be stated that the effect of asymmetric line settings increases with
higher wind production. That means the interval of the symmetry scaling factor β
gets smaller.
4. Higher system costs appear if a low and high price zone exist. That means if
conventional and renewable generators with low marginal prices and generators with
high marginal prices are located in different zones a lot of power is transferred from
the low price zone to the high price zone. In the real time market additional costs

4.2
Perspectives
The results of the thesis provide several possibilities to study further in the ﬁeld of ATC
determination. Therefore the python model could be extended with additional constraints
and data for example ramping constraints, minimum capacities or transmission costs.
Although the model has to be developed further to get new results, it can already be applied
to larger networks. Further investigations should be done on the IEEE 24 bus system to
understand the optimal points for ATC settings in more details.
Furthermore clustering methods could be included in the model to ﬁnd patterns in the
investigated effects. Therefore the line symmetry, type of generators or transmission line
capacities could be included. Finally an algorithm could be developed that determines
the optimal ATCs if possible to implement a dynamical market pricing model. This is a
challenging approach with a lot of difﬁculties on the way. However this thesis had several
new outcomes which are interesting and can help in future analysis and studies.
