Abstract
The adverse effects of air pollution on humans are well known, and different methods of mitigation exist. One of these is
phytoremediation, in which plants are used to remove the pollution. Studies have mentioned stomata as a potential pathway
for particulate pollution to enter trough, but studies proving this pathway are lacking. Therefore the potential uptake of
aerosol particles through the stomata in plants was evaluated in this project. Due to their small size, ultra fine particles
(UFPs) were used as they had the best chances of entering through the stomata. Leaves of different species (Urtica dioica
L., Glycine max L, and Lactuca sativa var. capitata) were subjected to a stream of UFP­rich air in a gas­exchange chamber,
and their uptake was calculated using empty chamber uptakes as controls. The stomatal conductance of the leaves was also
measured, and correlations between the two was determined. In total, 6 experiments were made. Significant correlations
between particle uptake and stomatal conductance was found in 3 experiments, however one of these were found to be
the result of an increase in relative humidity. As such only 2 experiments showed significant correlations between particle
uptake and stomatal conductance. More research is needed to evaluate whether the particles are actually uptaken through
the stomata, but the results in this report show that there is at least some connection. Leaf area was found to be positively
correlated with particle uptake on one occasion, contrary to literature, but this was found to be most likely due to a flaw
in the experimental setup which did not allow fluttering, which seems to be the cause of the negative correlation found
in literature. It was attempted to determine whether leaf morphology would have an effect on particle uptake, but due to
unfortunate circumstances this could not be achieved. Lastly, correlations between particle uptake and wind speed were
investigated, but it was not possible to find any significant differences in particle uptake at 2.2 m/s and 8.4 m/s. More
research into the phenomena of particle uptake through the stomata is needed, but the preliminary results in this report
suggests that it is a possibility, and that further research is justified.

Introduction
When people wake up in the morning, open their windows and take a deep breath in, they might think that they are inhaling
clean air. They are most likely wrong. The WHO estimates that 9 out of 10 people breathe air that exceeds the guideline
limits on air pollution, with low­ and middle­income countries suffering the worst (WHO 2020). The WHO also estimates
that around seven million people die every year as a result of air pollution related illness such as stroke, heart disease, and
cancer (WHO 2020). Sources of air pollution are both natural and anthropogenic, however when talking about pollution
it is mainly the result of anthropogenic activity that is interesting. A major anthropogenic source of air pollution is the
combustion of fossil fuel for electricity production, transportation, industry, and heating (Perera 2018). The burning of
fossil fuels release aerosols known as particulate matter (PM) along with sulfur­ and nitrogen­oxides (IEA 2016). PM has
through cohort and time series studies been linked to an increase in cardiovascular and respiratory hospitalisation, and
mortality (Koistinen et al. 2008). It has similarly been linked to an increase in mortality regarding the COVID­19 virus
(Pozzer et al. 2020). There are different ways that air pollution can be mitigated. Better filters on cars and chimneys help
reduce the air pollution before it enters the atmosphere, however they are not much help when the air pollution has already
been released. This is where the idea of phytoremediation seems an interesting prospect.
Phytoremediation is a bioremediation process in which plants are used to treat waste in water, soil, and air. In terms of
air pollution, phytoremidiation utilises the enormous biologically active surface areas of plants. It has been estimated that
the surface area of above ground plants is at least 200 million km2 (Gawronski et al. 2017). It also utilises the plants
gas­exchange mechanism, during which gaseous contaminants can be adsorbed/absorbed (Gawronski et al. 2017). The
focus of research into PM pollution and phytoremidiation has mainly been on plants ability to accumulate the particles on
their foliage and the factors that affect the rate of deposition (Gawronski et al. 2017, Han et al. 2020). The studies mention
the plants stomata as a potential pathway in which particles can be uptaken and transported into the leaf, however research
into this phenomena is lacking.
1.1
Aim of the report
The aim of this report is to evaluate the potential uptake of aerosol particles through the stomata of leaves. As ultra
fine particles (UFPs) have the best chances of entering through the stomatal opening they will be the main focus in this
report. The uptake will be evaluated using an experimental approach by subjecting an assortment of leaves to a UFP­
rich air and measure the concentration of UFPs before and after subjection using devices known as P­Traks. To get a
better understanding of whether the particles are entering stomata or not, the stomatal conductance will be measured,
and correlations between stomata opening and particle uptake will be evaluated. Some hypotheses have been formulated
which will serve as questions to be answered and provide the insight needed to evaluate the potential uptake of UFPs. The
hypotheses were formulated after extensive research, so for a reader without prior knowledge of this subject, they might
make more sense after the theory section has been read.
1.2
Hypotheses
• Particle uptake and stomatal conductance will be positively correlated, as the resistance for a particle to enter the
leaf will be lower the when the stomatal conductance is high.
• Leaf area is negatively correlated to the particle uptake per area of leaf, as smaller leaves have smaller boundary
layers which will increase uptake. Larger leaves could have a higher uptake without correcting for leaf area.
• The leaf morphology will have an impact on the uptake of UFPs, regardless of stomatal conductance, with rough
leaves uptaking more particles than smooth leaves.

• Particle uptake and wind­speed will be positively correlated, as the boundary layer is reduced and the leaf is subjected
to more particles.
1.3
Assumptions
Due to the inherent heterogeneity in leaf biology and UFP behaviour, some assumptions have had to be made in order to
simplify the calculations.
• The behaviour of UFP is similar to that of gases due to their small size and lack of impact by gravity.
• Leaves from the same species have similar morphology, making the size of the leaves the only difference.
• The leaves used in this project are considered to be sufficiently flat, so that their measured leaf area is representative
of their actual leaf area.
1.4
Limitations
There are also some limitations to the way the experimental work was done, and how the results can be evaluated. Some
of the more obvious limitations are mentioned here, however more limitations are identified and discussed in the results
section.
• The experimental procedure is in such a way that only a couple of leaves are tested at the same time, and under
very controlled conditions. This is not representative of the natural state, as leaves in nature are plentiful and are
subjected to a variety of different conditions.
• The leaves in this report are assumed to have similar surfaces if they are of the same species. This is unlikely in
nature, where leaves of the same species can have different morphology depending on things such as position and
water availability.
• The results from this report are only usable in determining correlations. It is not possible to determine any causal
relationships without more in­depth testing using microscopy etc.

Theory
The theory will be presented in three parts; Aerosols, leaf morphology, and the resistance method.
2.1
Aerosols
Aerosols, by definition, are particles of liquid and solid manner which are suspended in the air (Merriam­Webster n.d.a).
Another term used to describe aerosols in scientific literature is particulate matter or PM (EPA 2020). In literature, PM is
generally categorised into three categories. PM10 (coarse), PM2.5 (fine), and PM0.1 (ultra­fine) are particles with aerody­
namic diameters less than 10 µm, 2.5 µm, and 0.1 µm, respectively (Kwon et al. 2020). Two different metrics are primarily
used to describe PM; number concentration and mass concentration (Kwon et al. 2020). PM10 and PM2.5 are typically de­
scribed in relation to mass concentration, whereas PM0.1 is described in relation to the number of particles. The reason for
this is apparent when looking at figure 1, which show how the mass of particles becomes negligible when the aerodynamic
diameter gets below 0.1, and the number of particles increases instead.
Figure 1: Particle­size distribution of atmospheric PM (Kwon et al. 2020)
2.1.1
Sources and formation
The sources of PM are plentiful, and both natural and anthropogenic processes play a part. Some particles are emitted, as
particles, into the air. These are called primary particles (Christensen et al. 2009). Other particles are emitted as gasses
that by chemical and physical processes are transformed into particles. These are called secondary particles (Christensen
et al. 2009). Some examples of primary and secondary particle sources are presented in table 1.
A well­known source of particles is smoke. Smoke is the result of an incomplete combustion of fuel, resulting from a lack
of sufficient oxygen. In the case of a complete combustion, only water and carbon dioxide is produced. In the case of
incomplete combustion, not everything is burned. Smoke is the visible result of the remnants of the combustion, which
is typically carbon (as soot), tar, oils, and ash (Science Learning Hub 2009). The particles used in this project are created
using a gastronomical smoke gun, which burns wood chips mixed with water to create smoke.

The formation of particles can be divided into three modes; Nucleation, accumulation, and coarse. Particles in nucleation
mode are generally <0.05 µm, and are created by gaseous molecules binding together to form a particle. This particle will
then grow via condensation of other gases and coagulation with other nucleated particles (Kwon et al. 2020). Accumulation
mode includes particles with sizes between 0.1 µm and 1 µm, which are typically formed as either direct emissions of fine
particles, or by the processes of condensation and coagulation on smaller particles, making them larger (Kwon et al. 2020).
The last mode, coarse mode, is primarily particles formed by mechanical processes (Kwon et al. 2020). As figure 1 shows,
ultra­fine particles (UFPs) are both nucleation and accumulation mode, with the majority of it being nucleation.
2.1.2
Transportation and deposition
As they are suspended in air, PM is generally transported by the wind. Depending on the size of the PM and the height
of the emission point, PM can be transported several thousand kilometers, and there is evidence that dust from the Sahara
desert has reached as far as northern Europe (Ansmann et al. 2003). Besides the transportation by wind, there are physical
processes that affect the particles and removes them from the atmosphere, ie. deposition processes. These processes
include sedimentation, diffusion, and impact by inertia. These processes are governed by particle sizes. Sedimentation is
the result of gravity pulling a particle down to the ground. This is the case for larger particles, as can be seen in figure 2.
Opposite to that is the diffusion process, where small particles are subject to Brownian motion instead of gravity. Brownian
motion is the random movement of particles in a surrounding medium in temperature equilibrium (Kramers 1940). As
such, the transportation method of small particles is primarily diffusion as a result of concentration gradients. The lifetime
of these small particles in the atmosphere is short, as diffusion makes them coagulate with other particles. This is also
apparent in figure 2. Impact by inertia is the case when larger particles are forced to exit a diverting stream of air due to
their inertia.

The deposition processes explained earlier are examples of dry deposition, which is deposition in the absence of water.
In the presence of water, both dry and wet deposition will take place. According to Fenger (2009), wet deposition may
take place through two different processes: in­cloud and below­cloud scavenging. In­cloud scavenging occurs when cloud
droplets are uptaking pollution that are later falling to the ground as precipitation (ie. as rain, snow, or hail). Below­cloud
scavenging happens when precipitation is already on its way down towards the ground, and pollutants are being ”caught”
in it while it falls. In general, more than 80% of wet deposition happens by in­cloud scavenging (Fenger 2009). Wet
deposition is, according to Fenger (2009), the most important removal method of fine particles from the atmosphere due
to its much faster process time. However, since it is not raining all the time, dry deposition might in the long term be of
the same order of magnitude.
2.1.3
Deposition on vegetation
The deposition of particles on vegetation as a method of phytoremediation has been researched extensively, with trees and
other plant structures (living walls etc.) having been found to successfully decrease the concentration of particles in urban
areas (Farmer 1993, Nowak et al. 2006, Song et al. 2015, Wu et al. 2018, Abhijith & Kumar 2019, Weerakkody et al.
2017, He et al. 2020, Han et al. 2020, Janhäll 2015). The deposition on vegetation can be described as a one­dimensional
deposition on a homogeneous layer of vegetation (Janhäll 2015). This also applies to a single leaf, in which case the
deposition velocity vd can be described as:

The unit for the deposition velocity is m s−1. As there is an inverse relationship between vd and Rtot, it follows that the
unit for the resistances is s m−1. From that it can be deduced that an increase in resistance will increase the time it takes
a particle to deposit onto a surface. If the deposition velocity and the ambient concentration of particles is known, the
deposition flux can be calculated as the product of the two. The analogy to electricity as a way to describe deposition was
developed by Wesely & Hicks (1977). It was called the resistance method due to the use of resistances in its methodology
(Fenger 2009). A more thorough explanation of the resistance method is presented in section 2.3. The next section will
describe the morphology of a leaf, which will help in understanding the causes of the resistances in equation 1.
2.2
Leaf morphology
According to the Encyclopaedia Britannica, a leaf is ”any usually flattened green outgrowth from the stem of a vascular
plant” (Petruzzello 2020). The primary function of a leaf is photosynthesis, the process of turning carbon dioxide and
water into carbohydrates and oxygen using the energy of the sun (Taiz & Zeiger 2002). Leaves also regulate water loss
to the environment, by transpiration through little pores called stomata (Defraeye et al. 2014). The upper and lower side
of a leaf is referred to as the adaxial and abaxial side, respectively. The deposition of UFPs on the surface of vegetation
(leaves in particular) is fairly well understood, as explained in section 2.1.3. However, the potential uptake of UFPs into
the leaf is not that well defined and research into this is lacking. As a result of that, the theory regarding the deposition
on the surface of a leaf will be based on literature, whereas the theory regarding the potential uptake into the leaf will be
more speculative. With the assumption that UFP behaviour is similar to that of gasses, as elaborated upon in section 2.1.2,

2.2.1
Air boundary layer
The first obstacle a particle must overcome when reaching a leaf is the air boundary layer (ABL). The ABL is a layer of
stagnant air just above the leaf surface, on both sides of the leaf, and is a result of wind speed being non­negligibly reduced
by surface friction (Schuepp 1993). This layer of stagnant air causes the transfer of heat and mass to be predominantly
by molecular diffusion, or Brownian motion in the case of particles. The ABL can have different thickness, depending on
the wind­speed the leaf is subjected to, the size of the leaf (Taiz & Zeiger 2002), and the roughness of the leaf (Kirkham
2014). The rougher a surface, the thicker the air boundary layer will be (Kirkham 2014). According to Treshow &
Anderson (1989) and Kirkham (2014), an increase in wind speed will make the ABL thinner, enabling a higher rate of
diffusion, which means that more pollutant will be uptaken. Kirkham (2014) also discusses the need for circulating air
in closed chamber experiments, as a way to reduce the ABL and provide conditions similar to those found in the natural
environment.

2.2.2
Cuticle and Trichomes
When a particle or gas has successfully diffused through the ABL it will reach the surface of the leaf. On the surface
it will encounter three important structures: Cuticula, trichomes and stomata. Cuticula is a waxy, multilayered structure
that coats the leaf. Its principal constituents are cutin and cutan, macromolecules consisting of many long­chain fatty
acids. Wax is also an important part of the cuticula. It consist of a complex mixture of long­chain acyl lipids, giving
the cuticula its hydrophobic properties. The wax often crystallises into a complex pattern of rods, tubes, or plates (Taiz
& Zeiger 2002). The main purpose of the cuticula is to limit water loss from passive diffusion through the leaf. It has
been estimated that about 5% of water loss from a leaf is through the cuticula, with the rest being primarily lost through
the stomata (Taiz & Zeiger 2002). The transpiration through the cuticle is lower in xerophytic plants than in mesophytic
(Yoshida & De Los Reyes 1976). Xerophytic plants are plants that have adapted to a dry climate, while mesophytic plants
are plants that have not really adapted to any climate. Trichomes are small outgrowths of various kinds on the leaf surface.
They are often referred to as leaf hairs, and the word ”trichome” comes from the greek word trichōma meaning ”growth of
hair” (Merriam­Webster n.d.b). Figure 5 shows the trichome structure on the leaf epidermis of the plant Nicotiana alata.
It has been theorised that trichomes affect the boundary layer positively in the sense that they increase the thickness of it
(Benz & Martin 2006, Amada et al. 2017), however the same researchers have found that the occurence of trichomes in
12 species of Tillandsia do not significantly reduce the transpirational water loss (Benz & Martin 2006), nor does it have
any considerable effects on the gas exchange for the plant Metrosideros polymorpha (Amada et al. 2017).
Figure 5: SEM image of the leaf epidermis of Nicotiana alata (Howard 2008).
Separately, the two structures have been found to be increasing the particle deposition on leaves (Leonard et al. 2016),
however, there seems to be evidence that a combination of the two can have the opposite effect and in fact be detrimental
to the particle uptake (Han et al. 2020). A study by Perini et al. (2017) tried to quantify the deposition of fine dust particles
in a vertical greening system in Genoa, Italy. They looked at different plant species such as H. helix, P. fruticosa, and T.
jasminoides. The leaves had different surfaces, with H. helix and P. fruticosa having hairier surfaces than T. jasminoides
that in turn had a more waxy surface. The study found that the waxy leaves collected the highest significant amount of
particles from the atmosphere, and that the hairy leaves were less effective and should not be considered a suitable option
for improving air quality according to the authors. A study by Sæbø et al. (2012) found similarly that a waxy surface is
positively correlated with particle uptake. Contrary to Perini et al. (2017) and Sæbø et al. (2012), a study by Gawrońska

2.2.3
Stomatal pore
Stomata, or stoma (singular), are small apertures in the epidermis that allow a transport of gasses and water in and out
of the leaf (Kirkham 2014). Each stoma consist of two guard cells and the stomatal pore. A guard cell is a specialised
cell that is capable of changing its shape in such a way that when the two adjacent guard cells changes shape they allow a
small opening to be formed between them; the stomatal pore. The opening of the stomatal pore reduces the resistance of
the diffusion of water and gasses, allowing CO2 to enter the leaf, and water along with O2 to leave it. This is an extremely
important function, as it allows the plant to regulate water and CO2 under different circumstances. It is also what poten­
tially would allow a particle of certain size to get into the leaf.
Stomata can occur in different places on a plant, like the stem, but they are most abundant on leaves (Kirkham 2014). The
two sides of a leaf might not have the same amount of stomata, and one side might not even have stomata at all (Kirkham
2014). According to Kirkham (2014), most plants have amphistomatous leaves, meaning that the leaf contain stomata on
both surfaces. Other types include hypostomatous and epistomatous, hypostomatous having stomata only on the abaxial
side and epistomatous having stomata only on the adaxial side. Contrary to Kirkham (2014), it is implied by Harrison
et al. (2020) that in fact the majority of plants species are hypostomatous. The arrangement of stomata on a leaf also
differ whether the leaf is a monocut or dicot, with monocots typically having parallel lines of stoma, and dicots having
a more random placement of them. This phenomena can be seen in figure 6. The density (number per area) of stomata
on a leaf differs greatly, with Evert (2006) claiming between 100 and 300 /mm2 for a number of species, and Mott &
Buckley (2000) claiming between 50 and 300 /mm2. The density is not constant throughout the lifetime of a leaf, and
might vary due to sun­exposure or humidity levels in the soil when the leaf reaches maturity (Kirkham 2014). In a review
by Mott & Buckley (2000), the authors discussed the heterogeneity of stomatal behaviour, a phenomena which they called
’patchy stomatal conductance’. In the review they imply that stomatal behaviour across a leaf is not uniform, and that
even adjacent stomata might have different responses to changes. They further argue that the measurements of stomatal
conductance are highly scale­dependant, as they are usually determined based on either single stomata or the gas exchange
of a whole leaf. This neglects the scale between the two, which is where the patchy stomatal conductance phenomena can
be measured.

2.2.4
Substomatal space
Should the particles be successful in their diffusion through the stomatal pore, they will reach the sub­stomatal space.
This is where research on UFPs has not reached yet, so the following will instead be based on what happens to CO2 as it
reaches the substomatal space. The substomatal space is also known as the substomatal cavity. Besides empty intercellular
space, it is occupied by two types of cells: Palisade mesophyll cells and spongy mesophyll cells (see figure 8). Palisade
mesophyll cells are packed with chloroplasts, the organelles in which photosynthesis takes place. The spongy mesophyll
cells have many air spaces and slightly thinner cell walls, which help in the assimilation of CO2 (Uemoto et al. 2018). The
spongy mesophyll cells also have water­filled pores in their cell wall, giving them a small water layer on their exterior
surface (Evans et al. 2009). When CO2 is travelling through the stomata and further through the intercellular air space,
its concentration will progressively decrease as a result of the dissolution into the wet mesophyll cell walls (Kozlowski &
Pallardy 1997, Evans et al. 2009).
Figure 8: Cross­section of a leaf, showing a simple arrangement of palisade and spongy mesophyll cells as they would commonly appear. Typically
leaves contain more layers of the mesophyll cells than are represented (Baillie & Fleming 2020).
As mentioned, research into the path of UFPs into the substomatal space is lacking. However, a study by Eichert et al.
(2008) on the foliar uptake of water­suspended hydrophilic nanoparticles in Vicia faba found traces of particles in mes­
ophyll cells, frequently adhering to the cell surfaces. They used fluorescent polystyrene particles with a carboxylate­
modified surface with a size of 43 ± 6 nm, and used confocal laser scanning microscopy to detect the particles in the
leaves. They also found that during their experiment, only 10% of stomata were penetrated by the particles.
2.3
The resistance method
In section 2.1.3, the analogy of deposition as an electric current that had to pass through different resistances was looked
upon briefly. In this section the resistance method will be described in more detail and will include the theory from both
the aerosol and leaf morphology sections.
There are three main resistances in the resistance method, which were presented in equation 1 in section 2.1.3: Aerody­
namic resistance, boundary resistance, and surface resistance. Their relationship to each other and the deposition velocity
is also apparent in equation 1. However, equation 1 is missing an important parameter; gravitational settling. Gravita­
tional settling is impacting the deposition velocity for particles depending on parameters such as size and mass (Fenger
2009). Since the focus in this report is on UFPs, which has negligible mass, as shown in figure 1, the gravitational settling

is irrelevant, and as such will not be looked further into. Diagrammatically, the resistance method looks like a series of
resistances going from the atmosphere onto a surface (figure 9). The surface in this case is the leaf surface.
Figure 9: Diagram showing the different resistances related to a leaf, from Kirkham (2014)
Figure 9 does not include aerodynamic resistance, and there is a reason for that. Aerodynamic resistance is the resistance
towards transport by turbulent movement down to the surface (Fenger 2009). It is dependant on parameters such as the
height of the surface and its mechanical roughness length. A larger roughness will result in more dry deposition, as the
aerodynamic resistance is decreased. As this project is only focusing on leaves in a controlled environment, the aerody­
namic resistance is not a concern. It should of course have been included had the experimental setup in this project been
of a different kind, where whole canopies had been tested, or if a comparison between a forest area and a residential area
had been the focus.
The next resistance in the resistance method is the boundary resistance. Fenger (2009) refers to this resistance as Quasi­
laminar sub­layer resistance, while figure 9 calls it air boundary layer resistance. Essentially, it is the resistance imposed by
the stagnant layer of air that sits just above the leaf, as explained in section 2.2.1. The thickness of the layer is determining
the resistance in a direct relationship, so a bigger thickness result in a larger resistance, as the path of diffusion/brownian
motion for a particle is increased. The appearance of trichomes have been theorised to increase the boundary layer thick­
ness, as mentioned in 2.2.2. Brownian diffusivity is dependent on particle size, with smaller particles (<0.1 µm) diffusing
faster than larger particles (Fenger 2009). The boundary resistance is under most conditions in nature much smaller than
the stomatal resistance (Taiz & Zeiger 2002).

After the boundary resistance comes the surface resistance. Figure 9
refers to this as the leaf resistance. The leaf resistance is comprised of
several other resistances; the stomatal resistance, cuticular resistance,
and the mesophyll resistance. As can be seen in figure 9, the stomatal
resistance and the cuticular resistance is working in parallel and not in
a series. This is due to there being two pathways into and out of a leaf;
through the stomata or through the cuticula. In section 2.2.2 regarding
the cuticula, it was noted that 5% of water loss happened through the
cuticula, with the rest being lost through stomata. This implies that the
cuticle resistance is large, which is why most of the gas exchange in a
leaf takes place through the stomata. The stomatal resistance is made
up of two smaller resistances: the pore resistance and the resistance of
diffusion through intercellular spaces, as can be seen in figure 10. The
pore resistance is the resistance imposed by the guard cells. The re­
sistance is high when the guard cells are flaccid and the stomatal pore
is closed, but decreases as the guard cells become turgid and the pore
opens. The resistance to diffusion in the intercellular spaces is the re­
sult of diffusion in a gaseous phase. The diffusivity of a substance in air is high, so the intercellular resistance is not a big
resistance (Taiz & Zeiger 2002). This makes the resistance related to the stomatal pore the governing of the two. After
a compound has either diffused through the stomatal pore or through the cuticula, and has travelled through the intercel­
lular air space, it will reach the mesophyll cells. These cells, as explained in section 2.2.4, are liquid. The diffusion of
substances in water is much slower than in air, so the nature of the mesophyll cells impose a resistance called mesophyll
resistance. In the case of CO2, the resistance has been calculated as being one­tenth the resistance imposed by the bound­
ary layer and stomatal pore when these are fully open (Taiz & Zeiger 2002). There are more than one reason for the low
resistance imposed by the mesophyll cells, but one in particular is the area of exposure that the mesophyll cells have to
the intercellular air spaces. It has been estimated that the exposed area of the mesophyll cells can be as much as 10 to 30
times the projected leaf area (Syvertsen et al. 1995).

UFP­source stability
The determination of the particle uptake of the leaves in this report is based on the difference in particle concentration

UFP­source, and evaluate whether it needed any modifications.
The testing was done by measuring the outlet concentration before the GEC in the main experimental setup continuously
over a long period of time using one of the P­Traks (ENV1). This was done a total of four times. A graph showing the
measured concentrations is presented in figure 31 in section 4.1.1, where it is subsequently discussed.

3.2.3
LI­6800/LI­600 comparison
Two different stomatal conductance instruments were available for use during this project: LI­6800 and LI­600. The
specifications for both are presented in section 3.1. To evaluate which one would be most beneficial to use in this project,
an experiment was made to establish the relationship between the measurements made by the two instruments.
The main benefits of the LI­600 are its manoeuvrability, short sampling time, and small aperture. This enables it to mea­
sure different sites on a leaf quickly, and makes it possible to evaluate how the stomatal conductance varies across it. As
this project focus on the relationship between stomatal conductance and particle uptake, it seems beneficial to know how
the conductance differs across a leaf. There are some caveats related to the LI­600 though. As explained in section 2.2.3,
stomatal behaviour is not uniform across a leaf, and might even be different for adjacent stomata (Mott & Buckley 2000).
When measuring the stomatal conductance with the LI­600 with the intention to establish relationships between a dose
of some sorts, and the response from the stomata, it is critical that the measurements are taken at the same spot, as the
aperture in the LI­600 is so small that a slight change in position might have large impacts on the measurements, as some
stomata are omitted and new ones are included. These newly included stomata might behave differently to the changes,
and render the measurements useless as it is no longer the same conditions.
The main benefits of the LI­6800 are its functionality and large aperture. The system is build to be used in a variety of
different applications, so it has vast user changeability. The use of its functionality in this project is limited though, as the
Page 21
==PAGE:29==

focus is on stomatal conductance at ambient conditions. Its large aperture solves the issue related to the LI­600, so a small
change in positioning should not affect the conditions of the measurements as much as if using the LI­600. However, as
with the LI­600, there are some caveats when using the LI­6800. It has a long sampling time due to its large aperture and
cuvette volume, which takes time to stabilise. In comparison to the almost instantaneous measurement from the LI­600,
the LI­6800 has to measure for at least 1.5 minutes to reach a steady state. This limits the number of measurements one can
do in a specific amount of time, however, in the context of this project, it is not an issue, since the experimental procedure
doesn’t require leaves to be measured consecutively with no time in between.
The experimental procedure for this experiment was as follows: First, 3 salad leaves were taken from a salad plant at
random. The first leaf was then tested on 6 points on its surface (points 1­6 in figure 24) using the LI­600. After that, it
was placed in the LI­6800 on location 7 (figure 24) for 5 minutes. After the 5 minutes, the second leaf was tested using
the LI­600 and so on. After the third leaf had been tested with the LI­6800, all leaves were left to rest for an hour, before
measurements with the LI­600 started on leaf 1 again . This was done a total of 3 times (t0, t1, and t2). The idea behind
the hour between the measurement cycles was that the leaves needed some time to react to their detachment. As such, the
resting could have been done either for a shorter or longer time.
Figure 24: Schematic drawing of the 7 measurement points used. Leaf drawing by Arnold (2020)
The 3 leaves used in the experiment are assumed to be replicas of each other. The measurements from each leaf are aver­
aged according to the point measured at each time. So the measurements from point 1 at t0 for all three leaves are averaged
into a single value with a standard deviation. This is the case for the measurements from the LI­600. As the LI­6800 is
taking a multitude of measurements, 30 in the span of the 5 min, only the last 15 measurements are used for each leaf.
These are averaged into a single value for each leaf, before they are averaged again with the values from the other leaves.
Treating the 3 leaves as 1 makes it possible to establish whether there are any inherent spatial differences related to the
stomatal conductance, which would make the LI­600 preferable, or whether the leaves are acting somewhat similar when
the amount of stomata looked at is larger, which would make the LI­6800 preferable instead.

Experimental procedure
The experimental procedure was the same for all experiments, as this allowed for a comparison between them, while also
making it easier to replicate by others. The main experimental setup from 3.2.1 was used. Before each experiment, the

set to logging mode, taking measurements every second. At the same time, the GEC was turned on. Its fan speed was set

Five different sets of data are produced during each experiment: Particle concentration before GEC (ENV1), particle

Particle concentration
During the experiments the two P­Traks were measuring the concentration of particles in the system before (ENV1) and
after (ARRIVA 4) the GEC. The difference in concentration before and after is considered to be what is retained inside the
GEC, either by the chamber itself or a leaf. Figure 30 shows how the measurements look when they are plotted together.

ARRIVA 4 measurements, as it was found graphically to be the time it takes a particle to move from before the GEC to
after the GEC with a flow of 1.8 L/min.

during the 2 mins was then considered to be representative for the full empty chamber or leaf measurement in that round.
Determining the amount of particles in the air stream is then a matter of multiplying the concentration, which is in particles
per cubic centimetre, with the amount of air going through the system, which in the case of a flow of 1.8 L/min is 1,800
cm3/min. Then the number of particles retained in the GEC can be calculated by subtracting the amount of particles after
the GEC with the amount of particles before it. However, the experiments did not have the same concentration before the
GEC at the beginning of measuring, so when calculating the amount of retained particles, the experiments with a higher
starting concentration are going to have the largest retention, making it difficult to compare the experiments. To circum­
vent this from happening, a normalisation of the particle concentration before the GEC is made.
The normalisation is done by picking a specific concentration and then relating all the averaged concentrations to that
concentration. The specific concentration chosen is 10,000 pt/cm3. The amount of air going through the system also need
to be defined. Due to the average concentrations being the result of a 2 min period, 2 min was chosen as the amount of
time that the GEC was subjected to the flow. By multiplying the amount of time with the flow, a total of 3,600 cm3 was
found to be the amount of air going through the system.
The normalised amount of particles, Nbefore, entering the GEC can then be calculated as the product of the specific
concentration and the amount of air going trough the system:
Nbefore = 10, 000 pt/cm3 × 3, 600 cm3 = 3.6 × 107pt
(2)
The normalised number of particles: 3.6 × 107pt, can then be used to determine the amount of particles retained in the
GEC, as the relationship is this:
Cafter
Cbefore
× Nbefore = Nafter
(3)
With Cbefore denoting the average concentration measured before the GEC, and Cafter the average concentration mea­
sured after the GEC. Nafter denotes the number of particles that manage to travel through the GEC.
Now the amount of particles retained inside the GEC can be calculated by subtracting the particles that have travelled
though the GEC from the amount of particles that entered the GEC:
Nretained = Nbefore −Nafter
(4)
The Nretained for all intervals is calculated this way.
Since the Nretained is the total number of particles retained inside the GEC, it is comprised of particles retained by the
GEC itself plus the potential particles retained by the leaves. This is why the empty chamber measurements were made,
as they can be used as controls, which can be subtracted from the measurements containing leaves, which will provide
the amount of particles actually retained by the leaves. Two ways this subtraction could be done was considered. Either
all empty chamber measurements had to be considered replicas of each other, and as such the average empty chamber
retention could be calculated, which could be subtracted from each measurement containing leaves. The other option was
to consider each empty chamber as a control only for the following leaf measurement, so that the particle retention by the
Page 28
==PAGE:36==
Investigation of aerosol uptake by vegetation at the leaf level
Technical University of Denmark
leaves could be determined by subtracting the retention in an empty chamber from the following retention with a leaf in
the chamber. It was decided to use the second approach, and consider each empty chamber separately. This was chosen
as it seemed unreasonable to assume that the conditions inside the chamber was the same throughout the testing. There
was also a concern that it would underestimate the retention by the leaves at low empty chamber retentions (prior to the
averaging), and overestimate the retention by the leaves in the opposite case.
The calculated retentions can be found in the excel­file ”Particle uptake measurements appendix” in the sheet ’Particle
calculations’ under ’Specific Empty’.
As the leaves used in the experiment are of different sizes, a final normalisation is needed in which the number of re­
tained particles is related to leaf size. Dividing the number of retained particles with the corresponding leaf size provides
a measure which can be compared across all experiments and leaves. The retention per leaf area is technically a flux, as
it is calculated over a 2 min period. To make this flux more intuitive, it is divided by 120, as this will give a flux per second.
During the data treatment of the particle data, a systematic error caused by the P­Traks was discovered. This meant that
some of the measurements were removed from the data before it was used in the particle retention calculations. The sys­
tematic error is explained in details in appendix A.
Stomatal conductance
The stomatal conductance was determined in the same way as in the LI­6800/LI­600 comparison experiment. The LI­
6800 is logging 30 measurements in the span of the 5 min a leaf is tested, but the first couple of measurements are not
representative and is influenced by the chamber trying to stabilise. Therefore only the last 15 measurements are used. For
each leaf at each time, the 15 measurements are averaged into a single value which is then used later as a variable in the
dose response curve.
Humidity
The humidity inside the GEC was measured continuously by the GEC every second throughout each experiment. The
clock on the GEC was synchronised with the clocks on the P­Traks so that the data could be easily put together at the right
time. The humidity data was averaged between the same intervals used in the particle concentration calculations. Humid­
ity can influence the coagulation of particles, which would impact the calculations regarding the retention of particles. To
make sure that the relationship between particle uptake and stomatal conductance was not a result of a higher humidity
due to a higher stomatal conductance, the correlation between humidity and the stomatal conductance was calculated.
Leaf area
The determination of leaf area was done using the program ImageJ (Schneider et al. 2012), an open source image process­
ing software used in the scientific community. The program is capable of measuring areas of specific subjects in a picture.
An example of how this is done is presented in appendix B. As the program is using a two­dimensional picture, and a leaf
is very rarely completely flat, the area it measures is not going to be exact. However, it is assumed that the leaves used
are considered to be sufficiently flat that the measured leaf area is representative of their actual leaf area.
Statistical analysis
The correlations between the variables were calculated using the regression tool in excel, which provided the R2, the
correlation coefficient, and the P­value. The P­value is evaluated based on a 5% significance level. A P­value below 0.05
would therefore mean that there is a significant linear relationship between the two variables. A paired t­Test is also used
in the estimation of whether particle uptake at different wind speeds is significantly different or not.

UFP­source stability
The graph produced by the measurements from the UFP­source stability experiment (section 3.2.2.1) is presented below
(figure 31). The graph shows promising results, as it looks like the concentration of UFPs inside the UFP­source is
decreasing in a very linear manner. All four series have an R2 above 0.85, suggesting that over 85% of the data can be
explained by the passing of time. There is also an interesting phenomena happening in the data, which can be seen on the
graph. The rate of change is larger, the higher the starting concentration. Considering that the volume of the UFP­source
is decreased in the same rate as the aquatic pump is taking air out of it, it might seem strange that the concentration is
decreasing as well. However, there seems to be an explanation to this, that encompass the nature of UFPs. Nucleation
mode particles, as explained in section 2.1.1 has the ability to grow by either condensation of other gases, or coagulation
with other nucleated particles. Disregarding condensation, as no chemical analysis have been made, an answer can be
found in the coagulation process. The higher starting concentration means that a larger number of particles are present.
This will increase the probability of one particle colliding with another, being bound together by the Van der Waal force,
forming a single particle of the two, thereby decrease the concentration.

more stomata, and as such is not as affected by the heterogeneity.

and not negative as the hypothesis stated. Besides the obvious limitations related to the experimental procedure such as
the assumption that the system is working in the same way always, and that different leaves from the same species have
the same morphological structures, there is an explanation for the positive correlation which has something to do with how
the experiment was set up, and which has less to do with the boundary layer, and more to do with how larger leaves behave
in windy conditions. In a study by Leonard et al. (2016), it was found that leaves with larger leaf areas accumulated less
particles on their leaves, than leaves with smaller leaf areas. This is in contrast with the results obtained in this experiment,
however Leonard et al. (2016) have an answer for that. In their study, they imply that the reason for the lower accumulation
of particles on larger leaves is due to increase in erratic fluttering that the larger leaves exhibit. This erratic fluttering, they
suspect, will increase the amount of particles dislodging from the leaves. So it is a possibility that the larger leaves in fact
accumulate a larger amount of particles as a result of their size, but at the same time their size is increasing the dislodging
of particles, making the accumulation smaller. It is this relationship that is suspected to be a possible cause of the positive
correlation found in this report. When the leaves are put in the GEC, they are placed between two nets. This was done to
ensure that the leaves were always flat and not being pushed to one side. However this also inhibit most of the otherwise
occurring fluttering. So it seems a possibility that the higher uptake in larger leaves shown in experiment 5 is the result
of the leaves not fluttering erratically. The results from Leonard et al. (2016) were found for particles in the size of 22µm
­ 100µm, so they cannot be considered UFPs, however the phenomena might still apply to UFPs as well. This could be
further analysed with an experiment in which the leaves were not placed between nets, but were instead attached in a way
that would allow the fluttering to happen. The resuspension of particles due to fluttering could prove to only be a concern
when looking at each leaf specifically. In nature, leaves are usually a part of a larger canopy with many leaves. These
leaves might alter the wind speed inside the canopy, leading to less fluttering. So large leaves in a dense canopy might be
more beneficial in terms of particle removal from the atmosphere, than smaller leaves in a similar dense canopy.

with experiment 1, in which the measurement interval with empty chamber was too short to reach steady state, along
with another unfortunate event where the lettuce grown by the other student at DTU Risø was thrown in the trash due
to a miscommunication, it has not been possible to make any relevant calculations which can be used to determine a
relationship. Research into this relationship has already been done, as mentioned in the theory section of this report, and
the general consensus seems to be that leaf morphological factors such as size (Leonard et al. 2016), trichome presence
(Perini et al. 2017, Leonard et al. 2016, Sæbø et al. 2012, Zhang et al. 2019), wax thickness (Perini et al. 2017), and furrows
(Pal et al. 2002), does have an effect on the uptake of particles on leaves. Whether these structures are also affecting the
uptake of particles through the stomata is a case for further research. An experiment could be made where a larger number
of leaves with different surface characteristics were looked on in the same way as in this report, and correlations between
the stomatal conductance and particle uptake could be made. Then it could be analysed whether some characteristics

4.3.4
Wind speed and particle uptake
The last hypothesis is that wind speed and particle uptake is positively correlated. This hypothesis was based on the theory
that an increase in wind speed would make the boundary layer surrounding the leaf smaller, lowering the resistance for
the uptake to happen. The increase in wind speed would also increase the turbulence inside the GEC, which in turn would
increase the amount of particles that the leaves were subjected to. Experiment 6 was made with a change in fan speed
after each round of testing, so looking at the data from that experiment might provide an answer. The fans speeds tested
were 1 and 3, which was measured to be 2.2 and 8.4 [8.1;8.7] m/s respectively. A paired t­Test was conducted on the
data, comparing the uptake flux of experiment 6 ­ FS1 and 6 ­ FS3 to determine whether a significant difference could
be found. The results from the t­Test showed a P­value of 0.06, making the difference between 6 ­ FS1 and 6 ­ FS3 not
significant. As such, the hypothesis cannot be accepted on the basis of the results from this experiment. Two other paired
t­Tests were made in order to further strengthen this statement. As stomatal conductance was found to be significantly
correlated with particle uptake for both 6 ­ FS1 and 6 ­ FS3, it could have had an effect on the comparison between the two.
Therefore it was tested with a t­Test whether the stomatal conductance was significantly different between the two. The
t­Test provided a P­value of 0.051, showing that the stomatal conductance between the two was not significantly different.
Similarly, relative humidity was found to be significantly correlated to particle uptake in experiment 6 ­ FS3, so this could
also have had an effect. A t­Test was made to compare the difference in relative humidity between 6 ­ FS1 and 6 ­ FS3,
which provided a P­value of 0.09, showing no significant difference in relative humidity between the two. A study by
Nowak et al. (2013) found that particle deposition on leaves increased when the wind speed increased. However this effect
was seen on canopy scale, so their findings might not be applicable on an individual leaf level. More research is needed
on a leaf level in order to determine how big an effect wind speed has on particle uptake in general, but also whether an
change in wind speed will influence the potential uptake of particles through the stomata.

Conclusion
This project aimed at evaluating the potential uptake of ultrafine particles through the stomata of leaves. To help limit the
scope of the project, some hypotheses were formulated which could be tested and either accepted or not. Through testing,
the following can be concluded for each hypothesis:
Particle uptake and stomatal conductance will be positively correlated.
A significant positive correlation was found between particle uptake and stomatal conductance in 3 (Experiment 3, 6 ­ FS1,
6 ­ FS3) out of 6 experiments. However when correcting for the fact that the correlation between stomatal conductance
and relative humidity is significant for all experiments, it was found that the significant correlation between particle uptake
and conductance in experiment 6 ­ FS3 was in fact due to the relative humidity. The other 2 did not show a significant
correlation with relative humidity, so it is a possibility that particles in these experiments were in fact uptaken through the
stomata, however more research into this is still required to accept this hypothesis.
Leaf area is negatively correlated to the particle uptake per area of leaf.
Contrary to the hypothesis, all experiments showed positive correlations, however only experiment 5 showed a significant
correlation. The reason for the positive correlation has been theorised to be due to a flaw in the experimental setup. In
literature, the negative correlation was due to erratic fluttering, which increased with a larger leaf area. The experimental
setup in this report could be actively inhibiting this behaviour by subjected the leaves between two nets. A different kind
of experimental setup is needed to avoid this inhibition of fluttering, so the hypothesis can be tested under more natural
conditions.
The leaf morphology will have an impact on the uptake of UFPs.
Due to unfortunate circumstances, no experimental results could be obtained in relation to this. However, when look­
ing in literature, the consensus seems to be that leaf morphology do play a part in at least the deposition of particles on
the leaf surfaces. The impact that morphology has on the particle uptake through the stomata needs to be researched further.
Particle uptake and wind speed will be positively correlated.
Through testing it was found that the uptake measured at different wind speeds (2.2 and 8.4 m/s) were not significantly
different from each other, suggesting that wind speed did not affect the particle uptake, and as such no correlation was
found. Literature suggest that wind speed do pose an effect, but it has yet to be tested on small scale.
In general, this report serves as a preliminary study into the potential uptake of particles through the stomata in plants.
There is a need for more elaborate testing and further research into this subject, but the conclusions drawn in this report
suggest that there might be something interesting to uncover.

Figure 43: Steps of the projected leaf area determination using ImageJ
Using ImageJ to determine projected leaf area is a quite straightforward process. First, an image is imported into the software (43a).
The program has a feature which allows you to set the scale of the picture using some sort of reference. By taking the picture with a
ruler, you can draw a line from one point to another with great accuracy. The program will then determine the amount of pixels that are
between the two points, and from that, it is able to determine how many pixels/cm2 there are in the picture (43b). The image is then
changed to 8­bit, as the threshold function used later only works with grayscale images (figs. 43c and 43d). When the picture is in 8­bit,
the threshold function can be used to divide the pixels on the picture into two separate ”classes”; a ’foreground’ and a ’background’.
By manipulating the cutoff­point for a pixels to get sorted into one or the other, the leaf surfaces can be covered with the foreground
type of pixels (figs. 43e and 43f). The built­in wand tool can then be used to select the leaves, and they are subsequently added to the
Region Of Interest (ROI) Manager (figs. 43g to 43i). The ROI manager has a measure function, which utilises the previously set scale,
and the threshold, to determine the area of the leaves (fig. 43j).
