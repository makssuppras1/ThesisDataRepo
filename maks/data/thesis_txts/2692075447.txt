Abstract
A modern tool for pavement condition assessment on a network level is the trafﬁc speed deﬂec-
tometer (TSD). The TSD is a heavy truck equipped with Doppler lasers that collects deﬂection
information while driving at highway speeds. When operated over asphalt pavements, deﬂection
measurements are inﬂuenced by the prevailing temperature proﬁle due to asphalt concrete’s ther-
mal sensitivity. The challenge for road owners is to present the deﬂection results with a standard
reference temperature – else, subsequent decisions may be biased. This master project aims to ad-
dress this challenge via a combination of thermo-mechanical pavement modeling. Field data are
provided by the Danish Road Authority and include TSD measurements performed over the same
pavements in different weather conditions, temperature measurements from in-pavement sensors,
nearby weather station data, FWD test results. Optimal pavement layers’ moduli are estimated
with backcalculation and materials’ thermal sensitivity is investigated. Modeled results are val-
idated through comparison with literature values. The thermal viscosity of the asphalt concrete
layer is ruled by the viscous nature of the bituminous material: moduli (and viscosity) are in-
creasing for decreasing temperatures. Unbound granular material constituting the aggregate base
and the aggregate subbase layers show an opposite trend: moduli are increasing for increasing
temperatures.

Chapter 1
Background and motivation
Many studies show that in several countries, road trafﬁc is rapidly and signiﬁcantly increasing.
Pavement structures are therefore exposed to trafﬁc loads which are often higher than the designed
ones, leading to faster damage development and premature failures of the structures (Hildebrand
& Rasmussen 2002).
Acquiring knowledge regarding the mechanical layers’ properties of road pavements under service
conditions is a fundamental part of the modern pavement management systems: the information
is used to plan more cost-effective maintenance and repair works.
The term ’bearing capacity’ is the value of loading intensity (usually expressed as stress) an ele-
ment can sustain without failure (Curtin et al. 2008). Primarily, the term is related to foundations
of buildings, which bearing capacity is dependent on the structure’s properties (e.g. material, ge-
ometry), soil’s properties (e.g. soil type, modulus), and soil-structure interaction (Keaton 2018).
Nevertheless, the term is widely used in other contexts of structural and geotechnical engineering
- e.g. bearing capacity of a beam refers to the maximum loading intensity it can withstand before
structural failure occurs.
The mechanical layers’ properties of the road pavement can be analyzed either with destructive
or non-destructive testing. Destructive testing methods evaluate properties and characteristics of
a structure (or material, system, etc.) by causing damage to the original conﬁguration. Testing
for bearing capacity usually requires the structure to reach its ultimate state. For instance, road
pavement cores can be collected and analyzed in laboratory. This means that the continuity of the
road surface is interrupted; moreover, the specimen can not be used for multiple bearing capacity
1
==PAGE:19==
tests, but only once. This approach has limitations, as it is not sustainable to obtain continue results
along a road path and it requires an intrusive perturbation. Moreover, once the core is extracted
from its environment, its stress state is altered, therefore laboratory test results will never be fully
comprehensive.
On the other hand, several non-destructive techniques were elaborated, based on the principle that
pavement deﬂections are a direct measure of the bearing capacity (Graczyk et al. 2014).
Traditionally, the two main devices used to evaluate pavement deﬂections were deﬂectographs and
the Falling Weight Deﬂectometer (FWD). The deﬂectograph is a slow-moving truck, the measure-
ments are carried out by an installed probe that registers the rebound movement of the road surface
after the passage of the device itself. Due to its very low speed (6 km/h), it represents a risk for the
approaching drivers, as it is difﬁcult to recognize. The FWD registers the deﬂection induced by a
falling weight, impacting on a plate positioned on the pavement surface. FWD is known to be the
best device for the assessment of the pavement bearing capacity (Hildebrand & Rasmussen 2002),
however, it turns out not to be suitable for network scanning: the test must be performed while
steady standing on the road (2-4 min.), meaning lane closures, thus disturbed trafﬁc. Moreover,
the results are referring to discrete points.
The need for a modern tool capable to efﬁciently evaluate the road pavement bearing capacity
and safely monitor the road condition development led to the design of a new device, originally
called High Speed Deﬂectometer and then Trafﬁc Speed Deﬂectometer (TSD), which design has
been proposed by the Danish Road Institute (DRI) and Greenwood Engineering in 2002 (Hilde-
brand & Rasmussen 2002). The TSD is a heavy truck equipped with Doppler lasers that collect
deﬂection-speed information while driving at highway speeds (up to 90km/h). It allows the mea-
surement of all the lanes at multi-lane roads with the minimum disturbance to the road users. From
deﬂection-speed data, it is possible to calculate the absolute deﬂection of the road surface, thus
structural indices and parameters of the pavement. TSD technology will be in-depth explained in
the following.
TSD measurements might be potentially affected by many factors, such as external environmental
and atmospheric conditions, pavement physical conditions, etc (Zofka et al. 2015). Since laser
sensors are used, it is not possible to perform measurements in rain or on wet roads ((Bˇrezina
et al. 2017)). The main challenge for the interested parties is to understand how these factors are
inﬂuencing the TSD measurements, in order to correctly interpret the results.
When the TSD operates over asphalt pavements, measurements are inﬂuenced by the prevailing

2.1.1
Overview
Figure 2.1 shows a sketch of the longitudinal section of the TSD and some of the elements con-
stituting it. The TSD is a semi-tractor-trailer truck, composed of a 2-axle tractor that can host the
driver and the operator in the passenger seat, and a single axle semi-trailer which hosts, among the
things, the measurement system and data acquisition and synchronization system (DAQ) (Zofka
et al. 2015, Pedersen et al. 2013).
The TSD version used to collect measurements for this project is the TSD 7 ”Greenwood En-
gineering”. The ﬁrst TSD was designed in 2002 and delivered to the Danish Road Directorate
(DRD) under the Danish Ministry of Transport in 2004. Currently, 15 Greenwood TSD’s versions
exist around the world (Trafﬁc Speed Deﬂectometer (TSD) 2021).
There are several advantages characterizing the TSD -in comparison with other systems-. For
instance, it is suitable for detailed and accurate network scanning at trafﬁc speeds, with the pos-
sibility to survey every lane of multiple-lane roads. Its weight is similar to a real truck. The risk
of queues and/or accidents is minimized and the comfort of road users is preserved. It collects
information in a non-destructive way. Results at the order of one test point per 20 mm can be
supplied (Hildebrand & Rasmussen 2002): the continuous data have high accuracy and resolution.
The results can be interpreted in different ways: for instance, the bearing capacity of the pavement
structure can be formulated in terms of estimation of the pavement fatigue, residual life, or other
indices and parameters. Considering that the TSD is moving at trafﬁc speeds (70 or 80 km/h, up
5
==PAGE:23==
to 95 km/h), the efﬁciency of the TSD is evident: the cost per measured km is low, thus it has a
high measuring capacity.
The central and back parts of the semi-trailer are occupied by the measuring beam system and its
accessories necessary to guarantee the best performance of the tool (e.g. fan and A/C unit guaran-
tee the optimal temperature in the semi-trailer). The front part of the semi-trailer is dedicated to
the data acquisition system and the server racks.
On the bottom of the semi-trailer, a main and a rear ballast are installed to provide stability to
the vehicle but also to simulate the likely weight of the heaviest vehicles passing on the pave-
ment.
An odometer and an inertial unit are installed to calibrate the sensors, thus obtaining reliable

This conﬁguration has been chosen to minimize disturbance on the measurement from the truck
(Zofka et al. 2015). Additional equipment can be installed too, depending on the needs of the

Line Scanner, etc. are some examples.

2.1.3
Principles of Laser Doppler vibrometry
Laser Doppler vibrometry is a well-acknowledged and commercially viable technique enabling
vibration measurements to be taken without the need for contact between the instrument and the
target (non-contact technique). The term laser is an acronym for ”light ampliﬁcation by stimulated
emission of radiation”. The difference between laser and other sources of light is that laser emits
monochromatic, coherent (same frequency and waveform, constant phase difference) and highly
collimated (accurately parallel) waves (Casaccia 2016). Laser Doppler vibrometry is based on the
Doppler effect and interferometry principle.
2.1.4
The Doppler effect
The Doppler effect is a physical phenomenon consisting of an apparent change of the frequency
(or wavelength) of a periodic wave, such as light, sound, or any other form of energy perceived
by an observer, depending on the fact that either the source or the receiver of the wave is in
motion.
The wavefront of a periodic emitted wave can be imagined as concentric circles that concentrically
propagate from the source, outwards. If both the wave source and receiver are steady, the receiver
will capture a wave having the same frequency as the emitted wave, meaning that the wavefronts
maintain the same mutual distance along the path source-receiver.
Consider now a moving source and a steady receiver. If the source is approaching the receiver,
the receiver will observe a lower distance among the wavefronts of the scattered wave, meaning
a lower wavelength and a higher frequency. Vice versa, when the source is receding from the
receiver, the latter will perceive a higher distance among the wavefronts, thus a greater wavelength
and a lower frequency. The same phenomenon occurs if the receiver is moving and the source is
steady.
In the case in consideration, the road pavement surface that is deﬂecting under the truckload is
the moving source, whereas the laser Doppler vibrometer is the steady receiver. Depending on the
position of the laser sensor towards the wheel, the pavement surface will either appear to recede
the receiver, if the sensor is installed before the wheel, or to approach the receiver if the sensor is
installed after the wheel.
In the case in consideration, when the laser beam emitted by the Doppler vibrometer impacts the
road, part of it is reﬂected back by the road pavement surface, which is moving, deﬂected under
8
==PAGE:26==
the truckload. The reﬂected laser beam is characterized by a different frequency, if compared to
the original wave.
The law (Hildebrand & Rasmussen 2002) ruling the phenomenon is:
fDoppler = −fsource · c
v
(2.1)
where fDoppler is the frequency shift at the receiver, fsource is the emitted frequency, v is the relative
velocity between source and receiver, c is the wave propagation speed, alias the speed of light.
The frequency of the wave emitted by the Doppler sensors is known, therefore the difference in
frequency between the original wave and the reﬂected one contains the frequency, thus the velocity
information of the road surface deﬂection (Pedersen et al. 2013).
2.1.5
Interferometry
The interferometry technique uses superimposing electromagnetic waves, which cause the phe-
nomenon of interference, to obtain information. Two waves either having the same or nearly the
same frequency or coming from the same source are deﬁned as correlated or coherent. The in-
teraction of two coherent waves produces a phenomenon called interference, according to which
two superimposing coherent waves give a resultant wave which amplitude is the vector sum of the
amplitudes of the individual waves. The resultant amplitude may be higher or lower than the one
of the original waves, depending on the relative phase shift of the individual waves. The resulting
images and/or graphs are deﬁned as interferograms.
2.1.6
Basic components of a laser Doppler vibrometer
This section aims to describe the basic components and measuring principles of a simple laser
Doppler vibrometer. The basic components are shown in Figure 2.3 and explained in the follow-
ing, according to the correspondent user manual (Polytec 2021) and Pickering & Halliwell (2021).
The instrument contains a light source emitting a helium-neon laser, giving a linear polarized
beam. The features of the emitted wave (e.g. amplitude, frequency) are known. The laser beam
hits the ﬁrst beam-splitter BS1, an optical device that splits the laser beam in two: the object
beam and the reference beam. The most common beam-splitter is a cube made by two triangular
glass prisms, glued together with a resin, which thickness is adjusted in such a way that half of the
incidence wave is reﬂected and the other half is transmitted due to the phenomenon of total internal
9
==PAGE:27==
Figure 2.3: Sketch of the conﬁguration of a simple laser Doppler vibrometer (Polytec 2021)
reﬂection. The beam-splitter is capable of both dividing and recombining the laser beams.
The reference beam then hits a prism, a Bragg cell, and the beam-splitter BS3. The prism gives
the reference beam a different direction. The Bragg cell is an acousto-optic modulator adding a
frequency shift to the reference beam, with the aim of determining the sign of the velocity of the
target movement.
The object beam is the part of the laser beam which will carry the information regarding the target,
alias the deﬂecting pavement surface. From BS1, the object beam passes through another beam
splitter BS2, a λ/4 plate, and a lens. The λ/4 plate is one of the common types of waveplates, an
optical device capable to alter the polarization state of a light wave. The lens helps to focus the
object beam on the object. Once the object beam hits the target, it is scattered back: here, the BS2
and the λ/4 plate act as optical directional and deﬂect the object beam to the beam-splitter BS3.
The net resulting path difference between the object and the reference beam is equal to twice the
distance between BS2 and the object, because the ”inner” paths of the interferometer are either in
common or symmetrical.
BS3 recombines the object- and the reference beam, directing the resultant wave to the detector.
If the target is steady-state, the detector would register no change in phase between the object-
and the reference beam. However, in the case in consideration, the target is the road pavement
surface, which is deﬂecting under the load of the truck. Therefore, the Doppler effect occurs and
a frequency shift, thus in phase shift, is registered between the object and the reference beam.
The frequency shift caused by the Doppler effect is the phenomenon on which laser Doppler
vibrometry relies on.

The detector converts the resulting interference signal into an electrical signal, which is ﬁnally
decoded in the controller. The binary ﬁles collected by the laser Doppler vibrometer are post-
processed to obtain readable data. Moreover, in the post-process the data are time aligned: in fact,
the sensors collect measurements independently, as they are not coupled together.
The laser Doppler vibrometer can provide both velocity and displacement signals, independently.
These are captured by correspondents, different detectors. For reasons related to background noise
and digital resolution of the fringe counter system (Polytec 2021), the velocity measurements are
characterized by a signiﬁcantly higher resolution, compared to the deﬂection measurements. In
TSD, velocity measurements are collected (Polytec 2018).
2.1.7
The role of reference sensor
Due to the particular conﬁguration of the laser beam and its movement in the space, while the
TSD is driven, the measured data has to be corrected through a procedure of calibration taking
into account several contributes affecting the measurements.
The Doppler sensors are not positioned perfectly vertically, therefore the laser ray would not strike
perpendicularly the horizontal road surface. Due to this angle of incidence, the measurements from
the Doppler sensors contain an unsolicited component of the driving speed. Moreover, even under
the assumption of constant driving speed, the truck is not moving perfectly horizontally, as the
road pavement surface is irregular. Therefore, even if limited, rotations and vertical translation of
the laser beam exist and have to be considered too.
The movement of the sensor itself is registered by a sophisticated arrangement of inertial sensors
which is installed on the same beam as the Doppler sensors. The inertial unit is composed of three
accelerometers and three gyroscopes (Simonin et al. 2005), to record data in all directions. The
accelerometer is an electro-mechanical device measuring the acceleration of a body, which is the
rate of change of its velocity, meaning that the device is built to register any vibration associated
with movement. The gyroscope measures rotational changes or changes in the orientation of a
body, alias angle speed.
The calibration procedure is carried out by a post-processing software (Hildebrand & Rasmussen
2002).

2.2
Asphalt thermal sensitivity
Asphalt pavements are the most frequent type of pavements used worldwide. Asphalt pavements
are layered structures generally constituted by well-compacted unbound granular layers covered
waterproof asphalt concrete layer(s) (Adlinge & Gupta 2013). Asphalt concrete is constituted by
5-10% bituminous materials and 90-95% aggregates (in weight) (Speight 2016). Properties of bi-
tuminous materials and aggregates constituting pavements are strongly dependent on temperature
and environmental conditions.
Asphalt concrete’s modulus, or asphalt modulus, increases for decreasing temperatures, and vice
versa (Taha et al. 2013). Generally, asphalt concrete’s modulus ranges between 300 MPa for very
high temperatures, and 30000 MPa for very low temperatures. The range of variability is con-
siderable, it covers three orders of magnitude. In very warm weather, asphalt concrete behaves
in a pliable/ductile way. In very cold weather, it performs similarly to Portland cement concrete,
and it is brittle (Kim 2009). The thermal sensitivity of asphalt concrete is mainly determined by
13
==PAGE:31==
the bituminous material properties. Bituminous materials are viscous media, their viscosity de-
creases with increasing temperatures (and vice versa), meaning a more ﬂuid behavior (The Editors
of Encyclopedia Britannica 2021).
Unbound granular layers do manifest thermal sensitivity, but in the opposite direction: the modu-
lus is increasing for increasing temperatures, and vice versa. Aggregates are thermally sensitive,
expanding in volume when temperature increases and vice versa Levenberg & Rocchi (2019). The
entity of the thermal expansion or contraction is depending on the geological source of the aggre-
gate, its petrophysical and mechanical characteristics. Nevertheless, when pavement materials are
well compacted and aggregates are not free to expand, an increase in stress occurs. Temperature
variations are the main climatic variable playing a role in pavement performance, as it clearly af-
fects asphalt moduli, inﬂuencing its strain-stress state. Moisture and wind are the second two main
environmental factors inﬂuencing pavement temperature, but not as signiﬁcantly as temperature
variations (Adwan et al. 2021).
Temperature variation might trigger some superﬁcial pavement distress, such as bleeding - accu-
mulation of a ﬁlm of asphalt binder on the surface due to high temperature, and transverse cracking
- cracks perpendicular to the driving direction, caused by shrinkage of the asphalt layer due to low
temperatures. Distresses not only are a symptom of bad pavement performance, but they also can
be a danger themselves for users’ safety. For instance, in the case of bleeding, the binder ﬁlm can
become sticky when dry and slippery when wet (Ragnoli et al. 2018).
Not only climate variation affects pavements, but the opposite is true as well, meaning that pave-
ment structures have an impact on climate variation too. Road networks are the apparatus of every
urban area. Because of its dark color, it can be compared to a black body that absorbs energy and
emits radiation. This heat exchange is responsible for the so-called Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect,
according to which city areas are warmer than rural areas, because of human activities and mod-
iﬁcation of land surfaces (Adwan et al. 2021). A new ﬁeld of research is testing the performance
of light color asphalt, eventually embedding waste material (Naus & Voskuilen 2016).
Prediction of temperature distribution within pavement’s structure has been one of the objectives
of pavement engineering since the 1990s (Adwan et al. 2021). The issue had been addressed
analytically, with subsequent development of numerical and probabilistic models, and methods
(e.g. backcalculation) capable to estimate layers’ properties from pavement surface deﬂection
measurements or similia, for instance, slopes, as in this project.

2.3
Layered elastic theory for pavements
2.3.1
Theory
Layered elastic theory (LET) is the simplest theory based on which mechanistic-empirical pave-
ment design and analysis are developed nowadays. This procedure calculates pavement responses
(stress, strains, and deﬂection) to trafﬁc levels and material characteristics (Coleri & Harvey 2014,
AASHTO 2008).
Figure 2.5: Cross sectional view of a standard layered elastic pavement model (Levenberg 2019)
According to the LET, a structural pavement model is a semi-inﬁnite medium, constituted by a
combination of horizontal layers, the bottom one being a half-space, thus having inﬁnite depth
(Levenberg 2019) (Figure 2.5). All the layers are extending inﬁnitely in the horizontal plane
(Coleri & Harvey 2014). They are identiﬁed numerically, the top layer being Layer 1, and the
half-space being Layer I. In this section, the i subscript (i=1, ..., I) identiﬁes the layer. Each layer
is characterized by Young’s modulus Ei, Poisson’s ratio νi and, except for the bottom half-space,
thickness hi. The system is loaded with vertical uniform stress, applied on the surface of Layer 1,
of magnitude q0, over a circular area with radius a, aiming to simulate the tire-pavement contact
interaction. The problem is axisymmetric, thus a cylindrical coordinate system is established:
the origin is located at the surface, in correspondence of the center of the circular vertical load,
the vertical z-axis, pointing down, measures the depth and the radial r-axis is parallel to the top
boundary.
The assumptions at the base of the LET include: (i) linear elastic, isotropic, weightless and homo-
geneous layers; (ii) no body forces acting on the layer.

LET models are the standard models used in pavement design nowadays (Coleri & Harvey 2014).
Nevertheless, it is important to remember that the assumptions at the base of the LET are ideal con-
ditions, which are imperfectly representative of asphalt concrete and natural deposits, as they are
by deﬁnition heterogeneous, multiphase, and porous materials (Burmister 1945). Moreover, the
surrounding environment is strongly inﬂuencing their characteristics and properties. For instance,
LET models appear reliable and accurate when the stiffness of the asphalt binder and the aggregate
are similar. This happens at low and medium temperatures. Instead, for high temperatures, the
18

stiffness may differ up to three orders of magnitude. High temperatures, together with the trafﬁc
action, lead to a mechanism of permanent deformation accumulation (e.g. rutting) that continuum
mechanics is not able to embrace and predict (Coleri & Harvey 2014). More comprehensive and
advanced models are based on multi-phase continuum mechanics, viscoelasticity, thus history and
temperature dependency (Coleri & Harvey 2014).
2.3.2
Computational implementation
Solution for stresses and displacements as deﬁned in Eq. 2.3.1 - 2.3.1 can not be ﬁnd analytically,
therefore a numerical integration over m must be performed (Levenberg 2019). Computational
cost and solution accuracy must be balanced, taking into consideration the following aspects: (i)
Each integration step ∆m implies inverting a matrix in order to ﬁnd the parameters Ai = Ai(m),
Bi = Bi(m), Ci = Ci(m) and Di = Di(m): the smaller ∆m, the higher the computational cost, but
also the higher the accuracy; (ii) Bessel’s functions behave similar to a damped sinusoidal func-
tion. For larger m, the integrands become highly oscillatory and present serious difﬁculties to
converge. The issue is usually efﬁciently controlled when the integrand is computed using the
Gauss–Laguerre quadrature rule (Chen 2012); (iii) When evaluating the responses at points close
to the surface, the convergence is slower. Acceleration techniques (e.g. the double exponential
formula or Richardson extrapolation (or both)) may be applied (Levenberg 2019).
The aforementioned formulation, considering full bond at layers’ interface, is coded in the Excel
worksheet ELLEA1 (Levenberg 2016), a structural analysis program based on isotropic LET de-
veloped by Levenberg & McDaniel (2009). The ELLEA1 workbook consists of a single worksheet
called ”Main”, containing the input parameters and tabulated calculation outputs. The program’s
user interface is displayed in Figure 2.6.
Yellow cells are for inputs, whereas green ones are displaying the outputs. Any change in the
value of one of the inputs results in an automatic update of the output. In the topmost table, ma-

2.4
Elastostatic backcalculation
Backcalculation is deﬁned as an iterative form of calculation of Young’s moduli of a multilay-
ered pavement system by optimizing the match among calculated and measured parameters Skar
et al. (2018). The optimization procedure involves the minimization of the average absolute error
between the two. In this project, the involved parameters are the pavement surface slope and de-
ﬂection. In particular, peak pavement surface deﬂection measurements are recorded by the FWD,
whereas pavement surface slope is estimated from the TSD records.
The method is called elastostatic because wave propagation effects, time dependency of materials’
properties are neglected (Levenberg 2019).

Chapter 3
Objective and methodology
The objective of this study is to interpret TSD measurements that were collected over an asphalt
road, under different temperature conditions, and subsequently investigate the thermal sensitivity
of the inferred pavement layer properties.
Data for this study will be based on TSD measurements performed by the Danish Road Directorate
over a 600 m long section of the Frederikssundmotorvejen highway, with known layer thicknesses.
This section has a nearby weather station and is instrumented with embedded temperature sensors
at different depths up to 90 cm below the surface. Separate TSD runs, performed in different
months within a period of one year, will be utilized for the analysis herein.
The interpretation will be based on layered elastic theory. Speciﬁcally, the entire TSD loading will
be applied to the model, i.e., ten loads corresponding to ten different truck tires. Then, model-
calculated deﬂection slopes, corresponding to TSD-measured slopes, will be best-matched by ma-
nipulating layer moduli. The manipulation will be done with a gradient descent minimization
algorithm guided by an error function that represents the mismatch between the two sets of slopes.
The optimal moduli set is obtained when the error function is minimized. This set is then assumed
to best represent reality.
Thermal sensitivity is investigated by plotting the inferred moduli against the prevailing tempera-
ture level at the time when TSD measurements were performed. Any observed correlation between
temperature and modulus will be reported and compared against other correlations from the tech-
nical literature.

The Solver tool, in Excel, allows ﬁnding the optimal value for an objective function by manipulat-
ing variable parameters, correlated to the objective function. In this project, the objective function
is the average absolute error that needs to be minimized by manipulating the layers’ moduli.
The chosen optimization algorithm is a Generalized Reduced Gradient (GRG) nonlinear method.
It is a gradient descent method, capable of ﬁnding a local minimum of a differentiable nonlinear
function. The objective function must be differentiable because the algorithm estimates the gra-
dient of the objective function at the point in consideration and proceeds in the steepest descent.
The method is iterative and it stops when a local minimum is found, i.e. when the gradient is zero
in all search directions.
The disadvantage of this algorithm is that is highly sensitive to the initial conditions: the solution
to which the solver converges is the closest local minimum, but it is not guaranteed it is the global

minimum. Therefore, the solution found by the solver might be not the optimal one. Actually,
there is no such algorithm that can ﬁnd a global optimum.

Results validation
In order to evaluate the correctness of the results, the thermal sensitivity of the backcalculated
aggregate base moduli is compared to standard values, found in literature (Levenberg & Rocchi
2019). The aggregate base and subbase are layers constituted by well-compacted unbound gran-
ular material, which constitutive stress-strain law is regulated by the resilient modulus MR, in
analogy with Young’s modulus for elastic materials. The resilient modulus MR is dependent on
several parameters, in particular on two unitless parameters k1, k2 which are found with regression
equations from soil physical properties, thus they depend on the material’s quality. In particular,
k1 and k2 are respectively the intercept and the slope of the regression line in a log-log plot of the
resilient modulus against the bulk modulus (Buchanan 2007).
The inﬂuence of parameters k1 and k2 on thermal sensitivity is displayed respectively in Figure
5.16 and Figure 5.17 (Levenberg & Rocchi 2019), with resilient modulus MR [MPa] on the y-axis
and temperature increment ∆T = T −Tref [°C] on the x-axis, where the reference temperature is
set to Tre f = 25◦C.
Figure 5.16: Thermal sensitivity plot for unbound granular material: resilient modulus MR [MPa] against temperature
increment ∆T [◦C]. Effect of varying resilient modulus parameter k1 (Levenberg & Rocchi 2019).
For unbound granular materials, k1 generally ranges between 700 - 1100 [−], whereas k2 between
0.5 - 0.9 [−]. For chosen k1 and k2, the resilient modulus increases for increasing temperature

modulus (Levenberg & Rocchi 2019). Two unbound granular layers at the same temperature T
present different resilient modulus, depending on the material quality. In particular, the higher
the temperature increment, the more dramatic the effect of the parameter. For instance, the effect
of k1, signiﬁcant both for low and high temperatures, is greater for high temperatures - e.g. at T
= 5◦C, ∆T = -20◦C, the resilient modulus MR ranges between ≈75 - 105 MPa, whereas at T =

Chapter 6
Conclusions
This Master’s Thesis project dealt with the interpretation of TSD measurements collected of as-
phalt pavement and the subsequent investigation of layers’ thermal sensitivity. A 600m portion of
the temperature loop has been selected and analyzed. Pre-processed TSD measurements were used
as input in a backcalculation model aiming to ﬁnd the optimal layers’ moduli, by minimizing the
average absolute error between calculated and measured slope values. The analysis was performed
twice: ﬁrst with a pavement model consisting of 5-layer and a second one consisting of 4-layer. In
the latter, the aggregate base and the aggregate subbase were merged into one layer.
For the asphalt layer, moduli values varied depending on the temperature level (see Table 5.7 and
Figure 5.8); values drop when temperature increase. The values and their temperature sensitivity
are within the accepted range commonly utilized for design. This ﬁnding indicates that the TSD
interpretation method utilized in this work is correct. Moreover, asphalt moduli values from the
4-layer and 5-layer analyses were practically identical. A separate comparison against asphalt
moduli based on FWD backcalculation also showed acceptable agreement. Speciﬁcally, and with
reference to Table 5.14, an average modulus of 4767 MPa was obtained, with a standard deviation
of 803 MPa, corresponding to a temperature of 21.2 °C. For this temperature level, and with
reference to the solid line in Figure 5.12 (i.e. Formula 2, Eq. 5.8.1), the TSD analysis gave a
modulus of about 3500 MPa. The two values are within 1.6 standard deviations from each other,
which corresponds to a 90% conﬁdence interval.
Backcalculated aggregate base and the aggregate subbase moduli, obtained with a 5-layer analysis
followed an inconsistent pattern in which one of the two was assigned a high value, while the other
was assigned a low value. This outcome indicates that the TSD interpretation method was unable
to reliably distinguish between the base and subbase properties. For this reason, 4-layer analysis
69
==PAGE:87==
was introduced, in which the two were modeled as one layer. Moduli values for base and subbase
are within the accepted range commonly utilized for design. This outcome further indicates that
the TSD method utilized in this work is correct. With respect to thermal sensitivity, the unbound
granular layers exhibited weak dependence on temperature level. The dependence is opposite to
asphalt concrete, i.e. modulus increases with temperature increase. This type of thermal sensitivity
is expected for granular layers. A separate comparison against asphalt moduli based on FWD
backcalculation also showed acceptable agreement. Speciﬁcally, and with reference to Table 5.14,
an average modulus of 125 MPa was obtained, with a standard deviation of 60 MPa, corresponding
to a temperature of 20.3 °C. For this temperature level, and with reference to the solid line in Figure
5.14 (i.e. Eq. 5.17), the TSD analysis gave a modulus of 187 MPa. The two values are within 1
standard deviation from each other, which corresponds to a 68% conﬁdence interval.
Subgrade moduli (both upper and lower) exhibited high variability and spanned a wide range from
23 MPa, up to unrealistically large values (see Table D)). This outcome indicates that the TSD
interpretation method utilized in this work cannot yield reliable moduli values for the subgrade.
Unrealistic values were not accompanied by a mismatch. This outcome deserves further investiga-
tion. For example, the inﬂuence of the TSD laser Doppler sensors’ accuracy on the results might
be further studied, in relation to their position.
