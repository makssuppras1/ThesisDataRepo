Learning Objectives
The master thesis should let students plan a project and complete it while achieving cer­
tain learnings. The author of this report has set up the learning objectives below.
A student who has met the objectives of the project will be able to:
1. Identify the different activities and phases in the « fuzzy » front­end of innovation,
including the technology development process.
2. Understand scenario planning methods and other technology foresight concepts.
3. Compare existing scenario planning methods and practices.
4. Identify uncertainty sources in the front end of innovation.
5. Understand the concepts, challenges, and drivers of sustainable innovation.
6. Execute scenario planning methods for sustainable innovation in manufacturing
companies.
7. Implement early­stage design practices to foster sustainability thinking.
8. Evaluate and measure possible sustainability­related impacts of technologies in fu­
ture scenarios.
9. Create support documents for early­stage design methods.
10. Evaluate the impacts of socio­technical context uncertainties in design activities in
the front end of innovation.

Abstract
In the transition to more sustainability in manufacturing companies, technology research
and development processes lack support to assess the environmental impact of new tech­
nologies.
Through literature review and empirical research within a manufacturing company, the
use of future scenarios as a foresight method to screen the sustainability of early­stage
technologies and support decision­making is investigated.
Scenario­building methods are reviewed and compared in their potential to support man­
aging uncertainty. As part of a sustainability screening toolkit (SST) development project,
scenario­building guidelines (SBGs) are designed based on requirements from the litera­
ture and the manufacturing company.
The SBGs are evaluated in a workshop involving experts from the company. The results
suggest a great interest in exploring and learning from the future, and the success of SBGs
in building different, consistent, and plausible scenarios. Room for improvement is left in
the connection of scenarios with technology development projects and the challenges to
work in the highly uncertain front end of innovation.
Keywords: Future Scenarios, Foresight, Decision­Making, Sustainability Assessment,
Sustainable Innovation, Technology Development, Manufacturing Company, Guidelines,
Front­end of Innovation, Uncertainty Management, Organizational Learning.

Introduction
1.1
Context
Nowadays innovation and technology are significant factors in the competitiveness of
manufacturing companies (Phaal, 2004). Indeed, in a new multi­polar, fast­evolving in­
ternational economic context, the rules are rapidly changing (Boons et al., 2013). More
specifically, sustainable innovation has been gaining momentum with the environmental
challenges the world is facing (Lopes et al., 2022). Other long­term challenges such as
population aging, desertification, or raw materials scarcity also impose sustainability con­
siderations (Boons et al., 2013).
Companies and other organizations are then increasingly taking into account sustain­
ability and circularity in their decision­making process, therefore increasing its complexity
(Parolin, McAloone, & Pigosso, 2023). All the more in manufacturing companies, sustain­
ability assessment in the early stages of innovation faces some challenges such as high
uncertainty, lack of data, and lack of formal guidance (Parolin, McAloone, Eriksen, et al.,
2023). Especially in technology development, this exploratory process in the front­end of
innovation already deals with a high level of abstraction regarding later applications and
markets adoption of the technologies in development (Cooper, 2006). This lack of global
context knowledge thus leads to a high degree of uncertainty in the strategic decision
process.
To support decision­making in such uncertain contexts, looking further into the future
through foresight methods reveals to be promising. Futures study or foresight can be
defined as “the ability, the skill, and art of describing, explaining, exploring, predicting
and/or interpreting future developments, as well as assessing their consequences for de­
cisions and other actions in the present” (Berkhout et al., 2007). Foresight is used as a
strategic planning method involving a systematic and participatory approach to analyze
the future. It includes several methods from forecasting to future scenarios (van der Duin,
2016). While forecasting relies on historical data and the extrapolation of trends, providing
a single prediction on the short­term future, scenarios support the exploration of a range
of possible futures in longer terms in which strategic pathways can be prepared. They are
described by Herman Kahn, one of the fathers of the discipline, as “a set of hypothetical
events set in the future constructed to clarify a possible chain of causal events as well as
their decision points” (Khan & Wiener, 1967). Scenario planning has then been argued
for as an effective method to support decision­making in a highly uncertain environment
(Chermack, 2004).
Scenarios first appeared in the 1960s with American military strategist Herman Kahn’s
work on geostrategy and potential escalation of military conflicts at RAND Corporation.
Since then, scenarios have become a major concept and methodology in future research,
introduced as a tool for strategic planning in public and private sectors (Van der Heijden,
1996). Its use has increasingly been spread in businesses in the US and Europe but it
gained even more popularity in the middle of the 1980s with the Harvard Business Re­
view’s publication of the Royal Dutch Shell success story in scenario planning by Pierre
Wack, claiming to be “thinking the unthinkable” (Varum & Melo, 2010).

1.2
Gaps
While scenarios seem to support the best sustainability screening in technology devel­
opment, there is no step­by­step approach to consider contextual or broader sources of
uncertainties and generate and use scenarios for this purpose. Therefore, some chal­
lenges are still to be overcome.
First of all, although the field of future scenarios has developed in the last decades, it is still
considered an emergent discipline. Numerous techniques have been developed by prac­
titioners but no consensus has been reached on definitions, typologies, or approaches. It
is therefore considered as a “methodological chaos” (Bradfield et al., 2005) in which best
practices and guidelines need to be carefully reviewed.
On the other hand, exploring the future requires to consider not only internal factors but
also external ones that can influence the global context in which a company or organi­
zation operates. Therefore a careful analysis of the macro­environment is essential to
support the development of future scenarios (Schwartz, 1991).
Finally, combining sustainability assessment and scenario planning happens to be chal­
lenging. While scenarios and sustainability assessments are frequently used in the litera­
ture, the different levels of abstraction and the lack of formal guidance reduce the quality
of these assessments (Bisinella et al., 2021).
1.3
Research Goals and Questions
The motivation of this research is therefore to provide support for decision­making when
assessing sustainability in technology development. To perform this task the following
main research question will be discussed:
“How can future scenarios support the evaluation of sustainability in technology
development?”
Considering the challenges previously introduced, three sub­research questions will guide
the development of this study:
• “What scenario­building methods are appropriate for evaluating sustainability in tech­
nology development?”
• “How can future scenarios support decision­making in uncertain technology devel­
opment context?”
• “How to systematically consider the macro­environment context while building future
scenarios?”
To answer these questions, a first literature review will provide insights into the develop­
ment of scenarios, the existing methods, and their outcomes. Then by working in collab­
oration with a case company (See Section 2.3), support based on best practices and the
company’s needs will be developed to provide guidance for mapping the environment,
building, and using scenarios for sustainability assessments. While the form might be
specific to this collaboration, the overall structure and content aim to be applicable to any
manufacturing company. Finally, an evaluation of this support will provide insights into
how scenarios can facilitate decision­making in technology development.

Methodology
2.1
Design Research Methodology
This thesis aims at providing support guidelines for design practitioners working in the
front­end of innovation based on existing research, methods, tools and a case study lead
in a manufacturing company. Thus the methodology followed for this thesis is based on
the Design Research Methodology (DRM). The DRM is a systematic research framework
developed by Blessing and Chakrabarti (2009), to provide more rigor in design research.
The DRM proposes a set of methods and guidelines to carry out and plan design research
in order to obtain more useful and valid results. It consists of four main stages: Research
Clarification, Descriptive Study I, Prescriptive Study, and Descriptive Study II. The DRM
is a flexible framework that can be adapted to different types of research, from an empir­
ical study to the development of support, by addressing these four stages with different
approaches.
2.1.1
Type of Research
The approach chosen for this thesis is a ’Development of Support’ (design research type 3,
see Blessing and Chakrabarti (2009)). Indeed, the literature review indicates that existing
supports to integrate sustainability in early stages such as technology development are
insufficient (Chapter 4). Therefore, review­based Research Clarification and Descriptive
Study I are followed by the development of a Comprehensive (i.e. empirical) Prescriptive
Study in collaboration with a case company (Section 2.3) as well as an Initial Descriptive
Study II for evaluation of the support.
2.1.2
Research Clarification
The first stage of the DRM is the Research Clarification (RC). The aim of this initial phase
of the design research is to get an overview of the available understanding of the discipline
of future scenarios and identify potential research goals and problems when used for
technology development.
To perform the review­based Research Clarification, several activities will be conducted:
1. Exploring the field of knowledge of future scenarios: Identifying and reviewing liter­
ature contributions on future scenario methods.
2. Clarifying main research goals and questions: Identifying potential research and
problems of integrating sustainability in technology development and an initial un­
derstanding of the usefulness of future scenarios.
3. Selecting type of research: Identifying the type of design research from DRM to be
undertaken in order to solve the research problem.
4. Formulating Overall Research Plan: Formulating and delivering research focus,
goals, and questions; relevant areas to be consulted; approach; activities and de­
liverables; time schedule (Gantt chart).
2.1.3
Descriptive Study I
The Descriptive Study I (DS­I) stage aims at increasing the understanding of the existing
challenges in the field of future scenarios by identifying and clarifying in more detail the
potential challenges that might be suitable to address in the Prescriptive Study to evaluate
sustainability in technology development.

The review­based DS­I is an in­depth literature review to help understand the different
methods of scenario planning/building, their outcomes, and links to sustainability and
technology development activities. Moreover, insights on activities, processes, and needs
from the case company will be gathered to determine requirements for support develop­
ment.
It will provide a sound basis for the Prescriptive Study for the effective development of
support that will address those requirements and for its evaluation during the Descriptive
Study II.
To perform the review­based Descriptive Study I, several activities will be conducted:
1. Reviewing the literature: Determining and extending literature level of understand­
ing in topics of interest.
• Exploring the different methods of building and planning with future scenarios.
• Determining the main outcomes of scenario planning.
• Determining how scenarios can support sustainability integration in early phases
of technology development and its challenges.
2. Conducting interviews at the case company (see 2.3) to collect thoughts, beliefs,
opinions, etc. about the technology development process and the integration of
sustainability evaluation, set the frame of the project, and identify requirements for
building future scenarios in technology development.
3. Drawing overall conclusions: Combining the results and findings of literature review
studies and interviews. Identifying the gaps to fill and determining future work in the
PS.
2.1.4
Prescriptive Study
Based on results from DS­I, the third stage of the DRM, the Prescriptive Study (PS) aims
to develop support to build future scenarios, addressing the requirements previously es­
tablished.
As a design task in itself, the support development will require a thorough analysis of the
findings from DS­I to extract the best and most suitable practices of existing methods and
conceptualize a new customized approach.
The support is developed, evaluated, and refined in an iterative process all along the PS
through reviews, interviews, and workshops with the case company.
To perform the comprehensive Prescriptive Study, several activities will be conducted:
1. Task Clarification: Gathering and reviewing results from earlier stages. Identify the
form and content of the support.
2. Conceptualisation: Filtering, Evaluating, Comparing, and Selecting scenario­building
methods that would fulfill the support requirements. Presenting and discussing the
methods with the case company to refine the requirements.
3. Elaboration: Review existing literature on suitable methods with which the Support
could be realized. Describing Initial Guidelines for the selected methods. Testing
internally the Initial Guidelines and refining the selection of methods. Refining the
Guidelines.
4. Realization: Connecting the scenario­building activity to the framing Sustainability
Screening Tool. Discussing implementation plan within the case company. Refining
the Guidelines.

2.1.5
Descriptive Study II
The fourth stage of DRM is the Descriptive Study II (DS­II). This stage focuses on evalu­
ating the application and impact of the Support that has been developed in the PS stage.
The main goal of DS­II is to understand the contribution of the developed support tool
toward the research goals. It consists of evaluating the usability and usefulness of the
support and its limitations.
To perform the Initial Descriptive Study II, several activities will be conducted:
1. Developing Evaluation Plan: Selecting factors to be addressed, the measurements,
setup, and type of support evaluation to undertake. Elaboration of a feedback ques­
tionnaire based on the usefulness and usability of the tool.
2. Undertaking Evaluation: Workshop at the case company to test the support and
collect feedback. Processing, analyzing, and interpreting the results.
3. Drawing Conclusions: Combining the results of the evaluation study, documenting
deviations, reflecting on the Evaluation Plan, drawing conclusions, and determining
future work.
2.2
Deliverables
The main deliverable of this research is support. This support will consist of guidelines
such as rules, principles, and heuristics of building future scenarios for manufacturing
companies working with early phases of technology development, to better integrate sus­
tainability considerations. The support will be developed, tested, and evaluated in collab­
oration with the CC but aims to be adaptable to any manufacturing company. The support
will be developed and evaluated through the following deliverables:
• Support Requirements: Description of the support in terms of needs or problems
addressed, the goals and objectives of the support, how it works, and how it is to be
realized.
• Guidelines: Guidelines for building future scenarios that support sustainability as­
sessment in technology development. A detailed step­by­step description of chal­
lenges and tasks to perform, supported with templates and examples.
• Support Evaluation: Analysis and discussion of the evaluation results. Determining
future work.
• Guidebook: The support developed will be gather in a guidebook for the CC.
2.3
Case Study with Manufacturing Company
This thesis is conducted in collaboration with a case company (CC) within the manufactur­
ing industry. The thesis is one of the projects that is encompassed in the wider context of
a Ph.D. in collaboration with the same company about sustainability screening in technol­
ogy development. It is conducted within the Section of Engineering Design and Product
Development, in the Department of Civil and Mechanical Engineering, at the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU). The CC is an international manufacturing firm based in
Denmark undergoing an organizational transition towards increased environmental sus­
tainability in its value chain.

Research Clarification
3.1
Introduction to Research Clarification
The Research Clarification (RC) aims at exploring and providing an initial understanding
of the relevant discipline of this project: future scenarios. The RC identifies the relevant
literature contribution from the existing body of literature and frames the theoretical foun­
dation for the research. It helps clarify and refine the focus of the study.
After reviewing what foresight studies entail and their relation with future scenarios, an
overview of the evolution of scenarios used through time will be given. A vocabulary clar­
ification will then define the concepts involved in future scenarios and the main schools
of thought of the discipline will be described. Finally, a conclusion will be drawn on this
Research Clarification.
3.2
Future Scenarios in Foresight Studies
Foresight or futures studies is the field of knowledge looking in the future. Berkhout et al.
(2007) defines it as follows: “Foresight is the ability, the skill and art of describing, explain­
ing, exploring, predicting and/or interpreting future developments, as well as assessing
their consequences for decisions and other actions in the present”. In his book Foresight
in Organizations, van der Duin (2016) reviews some of the main foresight methods. He
differentiates methods from predictive, explorative, and normative ones. Future scenarios
appear as one of the most popular foresight methods, qualified as an explorative one.
To understand the overall structure and type of activities used in foresight studies, a
generic approach to a comprehensive foresight project is proposed. This generic ap­
proach of six steps was introduced by Bishop et al. (2007) (see Figure 3.1) to classify
foresight practices. This generic approach provides a first overview of how scenario plan­
ning activities can be structured.

3.3
The Evolution of Scenarios
Scenario planning emerged after World War II as a method for military strategy planning
and expanded to the context of social forecasting and public policy with the work of Her­
man, often hailed in the literature as the ‘father’ of modern­day scenario planning (Van
der Heijden, 1996; Varum & Melo, 2010).
Scenarios have been introduced for application to businesses in the RAND Corporation
in the 1950s, developing scenarios for the Air Defense System Missile Command. (Brad­
field et al., 2005)
In the mid­1960s, Kahn founded the Hudson Institute, which specialized in writing stories
about the future to help people consider the “unthinkable” (Van der Heijden, 1996).
In 1967, he defines the term scenario in his book as “a set of hypothetical events set in
the future constructed to clarify a possible chain of causal events as well as their decision
points” (Khan & Wiener, 1967).
The use of scenarios is popularised in the USA as a strategic planning tool after the suc­
cess story of the Royal Dutch Shell company. The method was introduced in the early
1970s by Pierre Wack and allowed the group to anticipate and respond to the oil crisis
in 1973 (Varum & Melo, 2010). Shell’s success with the scenario planning process en­
couraged numerous other organizations to start thinking about the future (Chermack &
Lynham, 2002).
At the same time in Europe, more specifically in France, Michel Godet follows the work of
Gaston Berger and begins to develop scenarios for several institutions and companies,
contributing to the development of the La Prospective school (Bradfield et al., 2005).
Scenarios have then developed and Linneman and Klein (1979) reveal that almost 50%
of the US Fortune 1000 companies were actively using scenarios in the early 1980s.
Scenarios gained momentum in the early 2000s with numerous works and studies from
Bishop et al. (2007), Bradfield et al. (2005), Chermack (2004), Cooper (2006), Godet
(2000), and Hiltunen (2009) to name a few. The discipline developed for applications
in strategic decision­making, change management, finance, product or service develop­
ment, supply­chain management and logistics, economies, government and policies, and
environment (Varum & Melo, 2010).
Recently, scenarios have reached national and international levels, with users ranging
from government agencies, industries, non­governmental organizations, private compa­
nies, or decision makers and general audiences, such as the Intergovernmental Panel on
Climate Change and their emission scenarios (Walz et al., 2014). Nowadays, Wilder et al.
(2021) highlight that scenario planning is still an emergent discipline and its review of the
literature reveals that the most frequent thematic in scenarios literature is sustainability,
related to policy formulation, socio­economic factors, climate change, and technological
features.

the strategy development of a planning entity. Strategic scenarios focus on internal fac­
tors and are aimed to describe a range of possible consequences of strategic decisions.

3.5
Schools of Thoughts
Among the many different methods of scenario­building and planning, three different
schools of thought can be identified in the literature (Amer et al., 2013; Bradfield et al.,
2005): the ’Intuitive Logics’, the ’Probabilistic Modified Trends’ and ’La Prospective’. Each
school of thought will be reviewed and examples of methods will be presented.
3.5.1
Intuitive Logics
This approach of scenarios was first introduced by Herman Kahn at the RAND Corpora­
tion in the 1960s and was later used by Pierre Wack at Royal Dutch Shell in the 1970s
(Wack, 1985a, 1985b).
The name “intuitive logics” reflects the qualitative approach of this school of thought,
based on discussion and intuition. The purposes of intuitive logics methods can vary
from a “once­off activity making sense of situations and developing strategy, to an ongo­
ing activity associated with anticipation and adaptive organizational learning” (Bradfield
et al., 2005). The assumption behind intuitive logics is that decision­making is based on
the understanding of complex relationships of external factors to the company such as
12
Building Future Scenarios for Sustainable Innovation
==PAGE:23==
economic, political, resource, social and environmental (Amer et al., 2013; Huss & Hon­
ton, 1987).
Regarding the intuitive logics model, a large part of the “methodological chaos” comes
from the observation that there are almost as many ways of developing scenarios as
there are practitioners (Bishop et al., 2007). Indeed many variations of intuitive logics
methods were published in the literature, making it the school which has received the most
contribution in the scenario literature (Bishop et al., 2007). The technique developed by
Pierre Wack at the Royal Dutch Shell is described as the “gold standard of corporate sce­
nario generation” (Bishop et al., 2007) making it one of the most popular and frequently
used (Huss & Honton, 1987). This scenario planning method is adapted from the one
developed by Standford Research Institute (SRI) International, consisting of eight steps,
described by Huss and Honton (1987):
1. Analyzing the decisions and strategic concerns: The scope of the analysis, the
decisions, and the concerns are defined.
2. Identifying the key decision factors: Factors that most directly influence the out­
come of the decision are identified.
3. Identifying the key environmental forces: Environmental forces influence the key
decision factors and are typically social, economic, political, and technological.
4. Analyzing the environmental forces: The trends, uncertainties, and interrelations
of the environmental forces are analyzed.
5. Defining scenario logics: Scenario logics provide each scenario with a coherent,
consistent, and plausible logical plot, encompassing the conditions and uncertain­
ties identified in previous steps.
6. Elaborating the scenarios: Scenarios are written to provide more focus on key
factors of decisions.
7. Analyzing implications for key decision factors: Provide clear and informative
on what implications each scenario has for the key decision factors.
8. Analyzing implications for decisions and strategies: Implications are further an­
alyzed in regard to the specific decision considered but also on the larger strategies
of the organization.
3.5.2
Probabilistic Modified Trends (PMT)
This second school, the Probabilistic Modified Trends (PMT), also originates from the
RAND Corporation in the USA.
The scenario school of probabilistic modified trends encompasses two different quantita­
tive methodologies, trend­impact analysis (TIA) and cross­impact analysis (CIA). These
techniques involve the probabilistic modification of extrapolated trends. Starting as prob­
abilistic forecasting methods, but combined with judgments, qualitative analysis, and nar­
ratives about future events, they constitute scenarios (Amer et al., 2013; Bradfield et al.,
2005).
They are used as descriptive methods for “once­off activity to enhance extrapolative pre­
diction and policy evaluation” (Bradfield et al., 2005).
Trend-Impact Analysis (TIA)
TIA has been developed to overcome the fact that traditional forecasting methods were
failing at considering the effects of unprecedented future events. Even though TIA is
a frequently used method, references in the literature are relatively few in the literature
Building Future Scenarios for Sustainable Innovation
13
==PAGE:24==
(Bradfield et al., 2005). The concept of TIA is to modify simple extrapolations by consid­
ering unprecedented future events and their consequences, which could lead to deviation
from the extrapolated trend.
Huss and Honton (1987) provides a step­by­step description of the methodology:
1. Identify key scenario drivers: Two or three key scenario drivers are selected as
the major decision­making factors relevant to the topic being studied. Each of these
forces is then assessed into different alternative states. For example, world eco­
nomical growth is assessed as negative, neutral, or positive.
2. Create a scenario space: A scenario space is then created by selecting a subset
of combinations of the different alternatives defined earlier, that will form scenarios.
The following steps are performed for each scenario.
3. Identify important trends and collect time series data: Important trends to ana­
lyze within each scenario are identified.
4. Prepare a naive extrapolation: Trends are extrapolated into the future using stan­
dard time series techniques to generate ‘surprise­free’ future trends.
5. Establish a list of impacting events: Literature is reviewed and/or experts are
consulted to find events making a potential impact within the scenario from a social,
political, economic, or technological perspective.
6. Establish probabilities of events occurring over time including: Time­dependent
probabilities of the occurrence of each event are estimated. For each event: the
time from the occurrence of the impacting event until the trend begins to respond,
until the impact on the trend is the largest, and until it reaches a steady level are
assessed.
7. Modify extrapolation: The combination of impacting events and their probabilities
is used to produce an adjusted extrapolation of the trends.
8. Write narratives: Narratives are developed for each scenario within the scenario
space, based on the results of the modified extrapolations.
Cross-Impact Analysis (CIA)
The concepts behind TIA and CIA are similar as it assesses the probability of events that
might cause deviation from a naive forecast. However CIA brings an extra level of com­
plexity. Instead of thinking about events and their probabilities of occurring independently
from each other, it considers the interdependence among these future events. The goal
of CIA can be described as moving from “unprocessed initial probabilities” to a set of “cor­
rected probabilities” (Bradfield et al., 2005).
CIA model was developed by Gordon and Helmer in 1966 at the RAND Corporation. By
the late 1960s, different companies had incorporated the concept of CIA into their scenario
analysis (Kadaifci et al., 2020). A range of causal and correlation cross­impact variants
have since been developed by researchers and practitioners, resulting in a great num­
ber of different methods. These include IFS (Interactive Future Simulations, previously
known as BASICS) developed by the Battelle Memorial Institute, INTERAX (Interactive
Cross­Impact Simulation) developed by Enzer at the University of California, and SMIC
(French acronym for Cross Impact Systems and Matrices) developed by Duperrin and
Gabus (Amer et al., 2013).

Huss and Honton (1987) provides the following detailed step­by­step approach of the
INTERAX method:
1. Identify and Project the key indicators: Key indicators of the analysis are defined
as “characteristics of a system which can be measured, counted or estimated at
any point in time”. They are projected in the future with forecast methods based on
current and past data to create trends.
2. Identify impacting events: As in TIA, literature is reviewed, and/or experts are con­
sulted to find events making a potential impact in the future from a social, political,
economic, or technological perspective.
3. Develop event probability distributions: The forecast horizon is divided into smaller
time periods and cumulative probabilities that each event will occur prior to expira­
tion of the time period are estimated.
4. Estimate impacts of events on trends: Evaluate the impact of events on trends
for each time period of interest.
5. Complete cross­impact analysis: A cross­impact analysis assesses the impact
of events on events for each time period of interest.
6. Run the model: A mathematical model is run and estimates which event occurs or
not in each time period as well as their impact on the trends. Based on the Monte
Carlo method, each run can provide different results. therefore each run results in
one scenario.
3.5.3
La Prospective
In Europe and more specifically in France, around the same time the other schools have
been developed in the US, in the 1970s, the school of ’La Prospective’ has emerged.
French philosopher Gaston Berger introduced it as a scenario planning approach for long­
term planning and he named it ’La prospective’ or prospective thinking (Amer et al., 2013).
Later on, Michel Godet, head of the Department of Future Studies at SEMA (firm in the
defense sector), started developing scenarios for national institutions. He continued the
work of Gaston Berger and further developed the ’La Perspective’ methodology. His work
was largely focused on mathematical and computer­based probabilistic approaches to
scenario development (Bradfield et al., 2005). ’La Prospective’ combines elements of the
intuitive logics and the probabilistic modified trend methodologies but usually produces
normative scenarios (Bradfield et al., 2005) whose use was pioneered by French compa­
nies (Wiebe et al., 2018).
Godet’s methodology emphasizes participatory activities and involves a lot of different
tools that can be used at different stages of scenario analysis. This involves analytical
methods such as the MORPHOL to build the scenarios or the MICMAC, a structural anal­
ysis to identify key variables, the MACTOR to analyze stakeholders’ strategies, or the
SMIC­Prob­Expert to analyze the probabilities of the scenarios. (Wilder et al., 2021).
All of these methods are computer­based but the MORPHOL relies on the principle of the
morphological analysis used to build scenarios (Amer et al., 2013). Godet (2000) presents
a guide on how to lead the analysis (see Figure 3.2) resulting in scenarios. This template
can be adapted with the type and number of questions and alternatives.

Scenarios for Technology Development
As part of the front­end of innovation, technology development (TD) can be described as
creating “new knowledge, new technology, a technical capability, or a technological plat­
form” (Cooper, 2006).
TD occurs usually right before product development (PD) in the innovation process. Once
a technology has been developed, it can be integrated into the development of new prod­
ucts (Brilhuis­Meijer et al., 2016). TD is therefore a fundamental and crucial competitive
factor for manufacturing companies (Gaubinger & Rabl, 2013).
Future Oriented TD
TD is a very uncertain future­oriented process exploring a wide range of possibilities for
technologies and new product development. For Fahey and Randall (1998) it is cru­
cial to consider all potential influences at this early stage and foresight methods such as
road­mapping and future scenarios are used to integrate systematic future planning in TD
(Bühring & Liedtka, 2018; Wilder et al., 2021).

contexts in which technologies could be developed.

4.3
Scenarios for Decision-Making
When looking at the outcomes of future scenarios’ use, the literature presents it as a great
support for decision­making (Chermack, 2004; Varum & Melo, 2010) and a great learning
tool both at organizational and macro­environment levels.
This section will therefore address the two sub­research questions:
• “How can future scenarios support decision­making in uncertain technology devel­
opment context?”
• “How to systematically consider the macro­environment context while building future
scenarios?”
4.3.1
Failures in Decision-Making
One of the main outcomes of scenario planning is the support and improvement of the
decision­making process (Chermack, 2004; Varum & Melo, 2010). Scenarios can help
decision­makers by providing a platform for discussions and involving experts’ knowledge
(Wiebe et al., 2018), allowing them to embrace uncertainty and be to respond to future
challenges (Chermack, 2004).
Indeed Chermack (2004) explains how scenario planning can support decision­making
by considering four potential failures in decision­making:
• Bounded rationality: Bounded rationality refers to the limitations of the mental
abilities of human decision­makers to process all of the available information and
alternatives in making decisions. Future scenarios address this issue by providing
“relevant information in a format that is easily accessible from memory”.
• Endogenous and exogenous variables: Decision makers tend to only consider
one type of variable in their strategic planning. However, considering both endoge­
nous (internal) and exogenous (external) allows one to avoid overlooking critical
factors when exploring the future. Scenario planning addresses this challenge by
identifying the factors organizations are dealing with and also the ones they might
potentially deal with.
• Stickiness and Friction of information: When sharing information in an organiza­
tion, stickiness refers to the cost of information to be transferred among individuals,
whereas friction refers to double­checks preventing errors from being spread.
Scenario planning can decrease the stickiness of information by engaging individ­
uals from different knowledge backgrounds to discuss and develop shared under­
standings. On the other hand, friction serves as an essential way to share reliable
information, develop real expertise, and therefore avoid decision errors.
• Mental models and decision policies: Decision policies are defined as “the guid­
ing norms within the context that help individuals make decisions when they are
uncertain about the information at hand”. Considering decision­making in a highly
uncertain context, scenarios create a strategic conversation allowing individuals to
reconstruct their mental models and therefore the organization’s decision policies.
4.3.2
Organizational Learning
One of the first outcomes of scenario planning that seems to be consensual in the litera­
ture is organizational learning enhancement.
Indeed, scenario planning involves the collection of diverse information. This information
is discussed and processed during collective and participatory activities involved in sce­
nario planning. Fahey and Randall (1998) argue that the diversity of organizational func­
tions during these activities is crucial to the success of a project. Indeed, it allows one to
22

challenge and break current individual­held mental models and perceptions during collec­
tive strategic conversations by identifying new issues and problems which may arise in the
future (Varum & Melo, 2010). This results in increasing foresight attitude (Bootz, 2010)
and strategic “alertness and readiness” and therefore building common mental models
within the organization (Chermack, 2004).
In their paper Ramirez et al. (2020) also explore the reverse proposition, that organiza­

highlight that scenario planning practitioners’ experience of using related tools has a great
influence on the time and quality of the activities performed (Ramirez et al., 2020).
The influence on organizational learning can also be reflected in the different schools of
thought and the types of methods presented in the RC. Indeed, probabilistic methods
from the PMT school involve more quantitative than qualitative data. Participatory ac­
tivities are then limited and so is their contribution to foster organizational learning. On
the other hand, more qualitative methods from the intuitive logics school gather experts
for qualitative discussions and assessment, providing greater support for organizational
learning.
4.3.3
Learning from the Macro-Environment
Scenario Drivers
As mentioned by Chermack (2004), scanning the macro­environment or “context in which
an organization operates and will operate” is essential to support decision­making.
Indeed, identifying scenario drivers represents a common first step in several scenario­
building methods that have been reviewed, such as the SRI, GBN, INTERAX, Morpho­
logical Analysis, etc. These drivers are commonly identified by exploring external factors
that could influence an organization in the future, involving an analysis of the macro­
environment in which it operates.
Such a macro­environment analysis involves a set of factors such as economic, political,
technological, social, environmental etc. (Amer et al., 2013; Wiebe et al., 2018). While
each method proposes its own way to perform it, the PESTEL analysis provides a sys­
tematic way to analyze the macro­environment.
PESTEL Analysis
PESTEL (Political, Economical, Sociocultural, Technological, Environmental, and Legal)
is an analytical tool developed to analyze the macro­environment, based on six main
factors. For each of them, sub­factors can be declined. The factors or sub­factors can
then be individually assessed by experts. Usually used to provide a current analysis of
the environment, it can also be used by organizations to predict future situations when
building scenarios (Yüksel, 2012).
Generic examples of factors for each category are listed below:
• Political: Relations with European Union (EU), Regional relations, Political stability
• Economical: National income, Investment incentives, Monetary policy, Energy cost
• Socio­cultural: Education, Social trends, Lifestyle
• Technological: New patents, Research support, Rate of change
• Environmental: Oceans level, Deforestation, Water resources, Oil scarcity
• Legal: Competition Laws, Juridical system, Consumer rights

4.4.1
Frame of the project: the Sustainability Screening Toolkit
This project is part of an ongoing Ph.D. at DTU, in collaboration with the CC and MADE
FAST. Its main purpose is to develop a Sustainability Screening Toolkit (SST) (Parolin,
2023).
The SST follows a semi­quantitative participatory approach for assessing environmental
sustainability and circularity aspects of technologies in manufacturing companies. By al­
lowing a streamlined sustainability assessment to be performed during the development
process, the toolkit can influence design decisions and steer technologies towards more
sustainable outcomes, ultimately leading to more sustainable and circular products and
processes.
Aimed at non­sustainability experts, the goal of the SST is to offer a comparison between
the new under­development technology and the current one. Due to the highly uncertain
and future­oriented nature of technology development, including and quantifying uncer­
tainties is an important part of the assessment. By the end of the screening procedure,
the sustainability impacts of the technology and opportunities for value creation are visu­
alized.
The toolkit was designed following the DRM and is currently undergoing testing and vali­
dation via case studies in multiple companies.
24
Building Future Scenarios for Sustainable Innovation
==PAGE:35==
The SST was designed to tackle four challenges for decision­making in technology de­
velopment, specified by Parolin, McAloone, Eriksen, et al. (2023):
• Data availability: “Lack of data regarding low­maturity technologies does not favor
the use of traditional sustainability assessment methods like LCA or other lagging
indicators.”
• Breadth of scope: “Technology development projects can vary greatly in scope
and maturity.”
• Context uncertainty: “The sustainability of technology can be affected by future
socio­technical developments.”
• Lack of clarity: “Sustainability can be a fuzzy concept at the operational level, with
competing interpretations.”
The SST is composed of a modeling phase, where important environmental sustainabil­
ity factors are mapped and a custom scorecard with leading indicators is constructed,
and an evaluation phase where technologies are scored and results are visualized in a
two­dimensional plot. In the modeling phase, participants discuss possible environmental
impacts and value­capture opportunities from the technology and rank them according to
importance and strategic relevance. From the ranking, leading indicators are chosen to
represent these most important environmental factors or preferences. In the evaluation
phase, the indicators are scored for each technology and each future scenario being con­
sidered, giving rise to a final sustainability score.
Futures thinking is critical for the assessment, especially in the evaluation phase. By in­
cluding foresight and future scenarios, contextual uncertainties can be incorporated into
the assessment, leading to greater confidence in the results. The inclusion of a partici­
patory scenario­building approach aims at solving the challenges outlined above, namely
context uncertainty and lack of clarity, as group scenario­building has been shown to fos­
ter collective sense­making.
Building future scenarios, therefore, is a central activity of the SST and plays a major in
supporting the sustainability evaluation. Developing support in the PS will take into con­
sideration the SST as a frame. The guidelines developed will therefore be connected to
the other activities performed in the SST.

4.4.3
Findings
The CC is starting to shift from a cost­driven business approach to a sustainability­driven
one. Indeed, the CC has engaged in the long term to meet sustainability goals in the
future. In the early days of this transition, some challenges are arising.
First of all, the future is now considered a few years ahead with the use of a yearly road­
mapping method, compared to the long­term future environmental targets. Therefore
there is a balance between a short­term cost­driven approach in TD and PD and a long­
term sustainability­driven approach in decision­making. Indeed working towards more
sustainability can increase costs, leading to potential trade­offs in decision­making. The
CC thus needs to create a future vision, i.e. a strategic direction for their sustainabil­
ity transition. Indeed there are no targets defined in the mid­range future (5 to 10 years
ahead). Therefore, the CC is willing to explore the different alternatives of the future.
Moreover, it is mandatory in the TD process to evaluate the sustainability of technologies
even though there is no systematic method to do so. The assessment is qualitative and
done post­development, reflecting the still cost­driven approach. Sustainability can actu­
ally be assessed in PD, but uncertainty and lack of incentives prevent one from delivering
these assessments. In TD, sustainability assessment reaches a higher level of abstrac­
tion as how technologies will be used is not known yet. Therefore, a systematic method
of sustainability assessment in TD would support PD in their choices when considering
technology to use and also assessing the sustainability of new products. The SST is
developed to be used early in the TD process (Stage 1: Project Scoping and Stage 2:
Technical Assessment in Figure 4.2) to predict the potential sustainable value and impact
of technologies. As the early stages of the TD process are from the highest uncertainty
and the data available is therefore qualitative.
Finally, TD also involves foreseeing and exploring the future technological landscape, re­
quiring a careful analysis of the context and trends. Indeed, new technologies are evalu­
ated by their maturity level inside and outside the CC by the need to either accelerate their
development to meet future targets or oversee it when there is still too much uncertainty.
At the moment, exploring this technological context does not follow any specific method
and is made through individual knowledge and relations with academies, consortiums,
universities, etc. Gathering the knowledge and expertise of employees throughout the
company, not only from TD, seems then essential for the CC when exploring the future.

Description of filtered methods
In order to compare, discuss, and scope further down the number of suitable methods,
a more detailed description of the five filtered methods’ step­by­step activities is given,
based on the reviews of Amer et al. (2013) and Bishop et al. (2007):
• Incasting:
Incasting (or scenario incasting), developed at the Hawaii Research Center for Fu­
tures Studies, is a scenario­building method generating specific details of possi­
ble futures based on general scenarios’ descriptions (Bishop et al., 2007; Schultz,
2003).
This participatory method consists in two different steps performed in small groups.
It starts with participants reading a short paragraph that describes a rather extreme
version of an alternative future, based on emerging trends and issues in the society,
economy, technological innovation, environment, or political activity. Schultz (2003)
describes the classic Incasting scenario logics:
– Continued Growth: “A future in which the global economic system becomes
more and more efficient and effective at mass production, distribution, and
marketing.”
– Decline and Collapse: “A future of global breakdown: economic, political, and
environmental.”
– Sustainable Development (or Green Future): “Emerges from the growth of en­
vironmental values and ecological concerns worldwide.”
– Disciplined Society: “Emerges from worldwide trends in political extremism,
nationalism, ethnocentrism, and fundamentalism.”

31
==PAGE:42==
– High Technology: “Assumes that the innovations [...] have flowered into a va­
riety of applications, both commercial and personal, that completely overturn
our concepts of normality.”
Participants are then required to accept the fundamental assumptions of the sce­
nario logic they receive. Although extreme, the logic should not be questioned and
disbelief must be set aside.
The second step of the Incasting method is then to discuss and describe the im­
pact of this alternative future and a series of different domains such as law, politics,
family life, entertainment, education, work, etc. It ends with the presentation of the
different scenarios discussed with the other groups of participants.
• Stanford Research Institute:
The Standford Research Institute (SRI) method is one of the first explicit scenario­
building methods (Bishop et al., 2007). As a decision­focused method, the starting
point is the clarification of the decision the scenarios aim to address and of key
decision factors ensuring that the resulting scenarios focus sharply on the decision­
making process.
Then the macro­environment driving forces analysis is performed. The key decision
factors are analyzed by carefully mapping the forces that will influence the decision­
making process. The analysis ends with the assessment of each driving force’s
impact on the decision to make and their uncertainty level.
Then, scenario logics are developed. This is the most crucial as it provides the
structure for the scenarios. Participants imagine a series of alternative future plots
on how the world will be, based on the identified uncertainties and driving forces.
Logics developed are usually the expected future, the worst case, the best case,
and a highly different alternative regarding the specific decision.
Finally, a more detailed description of each scenario is developed using the logical
structure defined and by assessing the state of each driving forces and the future
implications on the decision­making process.
This method can usually be used with a matrix template where scenario logics are
defined in the columns and driving forces are assessed in the rows (see Figure 5.1).

1998)

• Global Business Network:
GBN is one of the most popular scenario­building techniques since Schwartz (1991)
published his best­seller, The Art of the Long View. The method supports decision­
making by generating scenarios based on uncertainty axes.
It starts with an analysis of the key factors of decision and influential driving forces
of a given project. Once the analysis has been conducted, usually the two most
important and uncertain driving forces to the decision are selected.
Then, these two driving forces become the two axes of uncertainty or polarities of a
two dimensions matrix. Each of the four cells generated represents a unique com­
bination of the two uncertainties’ poles. Each of the four combinations represents a
logic of an alternative future scenario.
Figure 5.2: Example of the GBN matrix from Ringland (1998)
The last step of the method is then to elaborate in more detail each scenario logic
into a complete story and discuss the implications for the focal issue or decision
(Bishop et al., 2007; Ringland, 1998; Schwartz, 1991).
• Morphological analysis:
Morphological Analysis is more generally a method for “modeling structural rela­
tionships between objects and phenomena” (Johansen, 2018). It is used in many
different scientific fields but also for foresight studies. MA is a qualitative modeling
method that explores all possible aspects and solutions to problem spaces.
When used to build scenarios, MA stands as a generalized version of the GBN. Not
only two but any number of driving forces are used to build the scenarios and each
of them is assessed in any number of alternatives (Bishop et al., 2007).
Considering a problem, challenge, or decision formulation, the first step of MA is to
define the uncertainties, as parameters of this formulation. The next step is therefore
to define the possible states or values of each of these parameters. A matrix can be
used where parameters of uncertainty set the columns and the different alternatives
are listed in the rows (see Figure 5.3).

The matrix contains then all the potential solutions and one scenario is generated by
picking one alternative from each parameter. The number of solutions is the product
of each parameter’s number of alternatives, potentially leading to a great number of
scenarios. In order to avoid reviewing each scenario, a consistency check helps to
reduce drastically this number by assessing the logical and empirical consistency
of alternative pairs. This step is usually performed by using a cross­consistency
matrix.
Finally, once the solution space has been reduced, the remaining scenarios can be
reviewed. If there are still too many, similar scenarios can be gathered in common
classes or only the bests can be selected.
• Fuzzy Cognitive Maps:
Fuzzy Cognitive Maps (FCM) based scenario­building method combines qualitative
storylines and quantitative models. The method is based on system thinking and
causal cognitive map concepts (Amer, 2011).
The first step is to generate the FCM. Considering the project, its boundaries, and
the future scope, relevant concepts or scenario drivers are identified, reviewed, and
merged by experts into a conceptual FCM model.
The next step is to define and assess the causal links among the concepts by giving
the polarities and weights of influence for each link.
Once the FCM is generated, is then translated into an FCM matrix (see Figure 5.4).

The methods and the comparison table (Table 5.3) have been presented and discussed

• Scenarios are relevant for the CC if they are generated as pre­defined scenarios

• The method should remain qualitative and its complexity as low as possible.

capture the subtleties of the TD projects. It has then been agreed that the scenarios logics
should be project­specific and generated.

Based on this first iteration, two methods remain as potential candidates to best support

5.3.1
Definition of Key Concepts
To clarify the meaning of the key concepts used in the Guidelines, some definitions are
given:
• Project and Decision: The Project refers to the specific TD project considered in the
SST and more specifically in the scenario­building activity. The Decision refers to
the goal of the Project, implying going through a decision­making process. In TD,
this decision typically consists in fostering the development of one technology over
others.

• (Environmental) Criteria: The Environmental Criteria are the crucial factors on which
the Decision will be based. These Environmental Criteria are most likely to be inter­
nal to the organization and linked with its micro­environment, i.e. its financial state,
manufacturing process, supply chain, etc. Environmental Criteria are internal, cru­

• Relevance: the contribution to gaining knowledge about the future to support decision­

• Novelty: the challenge of conventional wisdom about the future in the company,
• Differentiation: the structural difference of scenario’s themes.

6.3.1
Observations from Evaluation Workshop
As a first observation before the workshop started, participants seemed to know very little
about future scenarios but were still curious and willing to discover about them, which
proves the interest in this new discipline within the CC.
Scanning the Driving Forces of the macro­environment:
When performing this first step of the SBGs, some confusion about the PESTEL template
presented was observed. While some participants thought the goal was to place the En­
vironmental Criteria within the PESTEL categories, some thought each category had to
be filled up at the end of the step. Supposedly being a frame used for inspiration, the
template seemed more confusing than helpful.
In the same step, the identified Driving Forces did not reflect the own expertise of each of
the participants. Indeed, the Driving Forces remained very similar to the examples given,
and few thoughtful suggestions were given.
Selecting the Scenario dimensions:
Overall, the second step of ’Selecting the scenario dimensions’ worked well. All par­
ticipants were involved in the discussion for ranking Driving Forces by importance and
uncertainty. However, the selection of the scenario dimensions was more challenging
because of the uncertainty to make the right choice, but still ended up with the desired
output.
Building the Scenario Space and Defining Consistent Logics:
These two steps of the SBGs also challenged the participants to embrace uncertainty and
move forward in the process. By lack of time, the logic has been defined in a few words,
resulting from a common discussion with the help of the workshop facilitator. The consis­
tency check has been briefly mentioned without being completely performed.
Finally, the last step of fleshing out the scenarios could not be performed in the given time
schedule.
Evaluating indicators for each environmental criteria:
The next activity of the SST (Activity 4) was prioritized in order to test the connection of
the SBGs within the SST. However, participants did not succeed in completing the tasks.
Participants could not see any difference in the indicators to score among the developed
scenarios. This difficulty brought the workshop to an end as any further activities could
not be performed without the results of this activity.

Moreover, there is high room for improvement regarding the usability of the tool. First of

6.3.5
Improvement Suggestions
All these results leave room for great improvements of the SBGs.
First of all, a better connection of the SBGs within the SST needs to be established, from
the input, and the output.
Indeed the applicability of the SBGs in a specific TD project needs to be reflected before­
hand. Depending on the maturity of the project, the uncertainty level decreases, reducing
the need to explore the future and therefore using future scenarios. This can be reflected
in regard to the classification of TD innovation projects within the CC: incremental, evo­
lutionary, or radical. Future scenarios would tend to be more useful for radical innovation
TD projects involving a true organizational change within the CC, for which the uncertainty
is the highest level.
In order to connect the output of the SBGs to the next ’Evaluating indicators for each cri­
terion’ activity of the SST, i.e. make sure that the scenarios can make a difference when
scoring the indicators, two options can be considered:
• To switch the order of the ’Scenario Building’ activity and the ’Choosing indicators for
each criterion’. By defining the indicators after building the scenarios, the precision
and relevance of the indicators can be adjusted from the scenarios’ level of detail.
Therefore it would be made sure that the different scenarios would have a potential
influence on the indicators to score.
• A second option is to amplify the scenarios and adjust their level of detail to the de­
fined indicators. This could be achieved by proceeding to a more detailed ’fleshing
out’ of scenarios. Fahey and Randall (1998) review some tasks for “amplifying” the
scenarios that could be added in this step. This includes for instance imagining the
headlines of the newspapers in each scenario, identifying a main character and his
actions, and imagining specific events that would lead to scenarios.
Moreover, there is great room for improving the SBGs’s usability. Indeed the SBGs seems

before its use. One way to bring more clarity to each step of the guidelines is to mention

The preparation of participants to face uncertainty and embrace it can be addressed by

purpose of the SST and more specifically the SBGs, a description of their expected con­

6.4
Conclusion on the Descriptive Study II
The evaluation of the SBGs has been conducted through a workshop held at the CC.
Trying the SBGs with a concrete TD case and in connection with the SST has brought to
light successes, improvement opportunities, and useful learning.
First of all, the interest and potential of foresight by using scenarios have been confirmed
by the qualitative data and observations gathered. The SBGs succeeded in generating the
expected outcome, i.e. a set of plausible, consistent, and different scenarios. Moreover,
this evaluation allowed to better understand of to answer the sub­research question:
• “How can future scenarios support decision­making in uncertain technology devel­
opment context?”
Indeed the evaluation reflected that the need to look to the future for a technology project
relies on the level of innovation and that the SBGs might not be applicable to any TD
project.
Moreover, even though the scenario­building method, on which the SBGs were adapted
(GBN), has been selected for its low complexity, exploring the future remains counter­
intuitive as it requires embracing a high level of uncertainty. Therefore, preparation and
introduction to the concepts and tasks seem to be essential before using the SBGs.
As part of the SST, the SBGs need to connect and adapt their level of detail to support the
assessment of sustainability criteria evaluated within a specific TD. It requires then a de­
gree of adaptability, unique for each project, regarding its maturity and level of innovation.
Finally, improvement suggestions have been discussed. They include:
• Reflecting in advance the applicability and usefulness of the SBGs for a specific
project.
• Reviewing the connection of the SBGs within the SST by rearranging the SST pro­
cess or amplifying the scenarios level of details with extended guidelines in the
’fleshing out’ step.
• Preparing participants by informing them in advance of the project, the goals, and
the tasks of the SST and the SBGs, gathered in a guidebook.

Discussion
7.1
Frame of the Project
The development of this project was framed in a very specific context. This project is
indeed part of an ongoing Ph.D. in collaboration with the CC. The Ph.D. explores the sus­
tainability of technologies developed at the CC through a toolkit, the SST. The support
developed in this project is meant to be one of the main activities of this toolkit. Therefore,
this project is framed by the specific CC in collaboration and the specific Ph.D. project.
This context limits the contribution to the main research question: “How can future scenar­
ios support the integration of sustainability into technology development?”. In this project,
the type of sustainability assessment is given by the SST, and the TD process is defined
by the CC. Thus this main research question could be answered to a larger extent by ex­
ploring the use of different scenario­building methods with different types of sustainability
assessment and also within different TD processes.
Modular guidelines could therefore be defined depending on those three variables (sus­
tainability assessment method, scenario­building method, and TD process).
7.2
Scenario-Building Methods Literature
The DS­I relies on an in­depth literature review of scenario­building methods identified in
studies and on other reviews. However, the list of methods provided is not exhaustive.
Some methods identified were not presented due to a lack of available data. Indeed, as a
relatively recent discipline, future scenarios literature has been described as a “method­
ological chaos” (Bradfield et al., 2005). Used essentially for strategic purposes, variations
of scenario­building methods are developed to meet specific organizations’ needs. It re­
sults in a great number of variants for the same method, leading sometimes to confusion
or even contradiction in their description.
For this project, one scenario­building method was selected to develop the SBGs. Thus,
a more systematic literature review could potentially identify other scenario­building meth­
ods that would also fit the requirements for the support.
7.3
Future Scopes
One of the main gaps identified in the DS­I is the missing connection between the current
technology road­map planning in the short term and the environmental targets set in the
long­term future.
Although the SBGs are meant to explore the future on a range in­between those two
futures, they don’t address this connection in their output. Indeed, this is not the direct
purpose of the SBGs or the SST to make this connection but the strategic management’s.
However, SBGs could support this task if scenarios generated were documented and used
by strategic managers to learn from them and imagine the different pathways to make this
connection.
Moreover, there is no limitation on how far in the future the scenarios can be developed
and it is then important to define and mention the future scope when using the SGB. The
precision of the future scope is limited by the increasing level of uncertainty. Thus, even
if the scenarios generated are consistent and plausible, they might reflect different future
ranges and therefore bias the sustainability evaluation of the SST.

Conclusion
Manufacturing companies are facing great challenges in their transition towards more
sustainability. Especially in TD, as part of the front­end innovation, the assessment of
sustainability lacks methods and formal guidance, in comparison to later stages of inno­
vation such as product development. TD deals with high uncertainty because of the lack
of data and clarity and the breadth of technologies’ potential evolution, in markets and
applications.
As a future­oriented process, TD can be supported by foresight methods. Foresight stud­
ies are used to explore, describe, predict, and/or interpret future developments and there­
fore support decision­making. Among foresight methods, future scenarios explore and
develop alternative futures. They are of special interest because of their exploratory na­
ture, allowing them to embrace high uncertainty.
For this reason, the main research question of this project has been:
• “How can future scenarios support the evaluation of sustainability in technol­
ogy development?”
The design research contributes to this challenge through both literature and empirical
research.
After a research clarification on the field of future scenarios, a first descriptive study has
evaluated the potential of scenarios to support the integration of sustainability assess­
ment in TD. Through a systematic literature review, sixteen methods have been identified
and classified. While studies combining environmental assessments and future scenar­
ios methods from different types are numerous, they often fail at providing valuable re­
sults because of the high uncertainty. However, as exploratory methods, future scenarios
methods supposedly support these highly uncertain activities in the TD process.
Moreover, the descriptive study reviewed the outcomes of using future scenarios which
are the enhancement of organizational and global context learning, therefore supporting
decision­making processes.
Finally, insights from the CC have been gathered. The main challenge of the CC is to link
its cost­driven short­term planning to long­term sustainability engagements. This chal­
lenge is intended to be addressed by the ongoing development of a sustainability screen­
ing toolkit (SST) for TD projects, in which the use of scenarios will support the evalua­
tion of environmental criteria. The use of a qualitative and participatory scenario­building
method for a multidisciplinary audience, generating original scenarios was established as
a requirement to design scenario­building guidelines within the SST.
As a result scenario­building guidelines (SBGs) were developed to address these chal­
lenges. Based on the Global Business Network (GBN) scenario­building method, the
guidelines were adapted to pair with the SST. The environmental criteria defined in the
SST to evaluate the sustainability impact and value of technologies are used as a starting
point for the SBGs. PESTEL analysis is used to support the original guidelines with a sys­
tematic exploration of the macro­environment’s influences. Inspired by the morphological
analysis scenario­building method, a consistency check of the scenario logics has been
added to avoid any contradiction in the scenarios produced.

The SBGs developed consist of five steps:
1. Scanning Driving Forces from the macro­environment
2. Selecting the Scenario Dimensions
3. Building the Scenario Space
4. Defining Consistent Logics
5. Fleshing Out the Scenarios
In a second descriptive study, a workshop was conducted at the CC to evaluate the us­
ability and usefulness of the SBGs. Activities from the SST, including the SBGs were
performed on an ongoing TD project from the CC. A quantitative and qualitative analysis
of the results of this evaluation leads to the following findings:
• The participants were interested in using future scenarios.
• The PESTEL analysis allowed to identify driving forces influencing the environmen­
tal factors in the different categories.
• The participants were able to evaluate the importance and uncertainty of the driving
forces.
• The participants were able to choose a pair of driving forces making a difference in
the scenario logics.
• Consistent scenario logics were defined in the scenario space.
• Participants understood the implications of the different scenarios.
The evaluation also highlighted some limitations:
• The PESTEL template brought confusion among certain participants.
• The different scenarios could not make any difference on the indicators to evaluate
in the SST.
• Some participants were confused to embrace uncertainty.
While a potential in the usefulness of the SBGs was confirmed, the guidelines still have
room for improvement. First of all, the applicability of the SBGs in a TD project needs
to be reflected. Then the usability of the guidelines can be improved by allowing enough
time for the tasks, clarifying the goal of each step, and ensuring good preparation of the
participants. Finally, the connection of the SBGs within the SST needs to be improved by
adapting the level of detail of scenarios to the indicators assessed in the SST.
Through ongoing refinement and practice, the SBGs can become a systematic method
to explore the sustainability futures of new technologies. The author sees great potential
for SBGs to become a gold standard in the CC’s strategic process to reach long­term
environmental targets.
