Abstract
This thesis explores the DTU FUSOR, an inertial electrostatic confinement fusion device,
through both experimental investigations and simulation using a COMSOL model. The pri-
mary objective of this research is to assess the limits and applicability of the simulation model
in accurately representing the behaviour of the FUSOR plasma. This is done in order to explore
possible operational improvements.
To achieve this, a series of experiments were conducted to gather empirical data on deuterium
and, later, hydrogen, plasma characteristics under various reactor conditions. These experimen-
tal results, namely the plasma’s size and distribution, were then compared to simulation data
generated by the FUSOR COMSOL model.
This project sought to add charge exchange collisions, a relevant source of energy loss, to an
already existing COMSOL model. Additionally, new methods of data analysis were developed
in order to more comprehensively investigate the simulation data. The FUSOR equipped with
an ion gun was also simulated in order to find the optimal settings to increase ion density within
the FUSOR cathode grid.
The comparative analysis revealed key insights into the performance of the simulation model.
Areas were identified where the model accurately predicts experimental outcomes and discrepan-
cies were highlighted, suggesting avenues for model refinement. The Debye length was estimated
to be λD ≈1 −5mm and aspects of the FUSOR, like the potential well and spatial plasma dis-
tributions, were quantified and their mechanisms explored. The simulations suggested that the
potential well plays an important role in forming convergent ion trajectories and the variation
between different observed plasma distributions was found to be caused by ions. The ion core
was found to vary between 0.1cm and 0.25cm and was proportional to the FUSOR current and
inversely proportional to the FUSOR potential. Depending on the sampling technique used, the
ion’s velocities were found to be well approximated by Maxwell-Boltzmann distributions with
characteristic energies 10 times lower than what would be assumed from the FUSOR potential.
Based on the improved model, simulated FUSOR modifications to the quartz tube and grid
were implemented and tested, but did not result in significant performance improvements.
These findings not only enhance the understanding of the FUSOR plasma dynamics but also
provide a critical evaluation of the current simulation model’s strengths and limitations. The
insights gained in this thesis pave the way for future improvements in simulation accuracy, ul-
timately contributing to the advancement of inertial electrostatic confinement fusion research
and fusion energy as a whole.

1
Introduction
Energy is one of the most important resources for humanity. Whether it is directly like solar
energy, or through complex processes like the creation of fossil fuels, the vast majority of energy
forms on earth are powered by the sun. Fossil fuels, formed from ancient organic matter, have
long been a quick and easy energy source. This method proved highly effective for early humans
and remained dominant throughout much of history. However, humanity is now faced with a
number of climate related challenges. Through a combination of population increase and in-
creased living standards, the average energy consumption of humans has increased significantly,
illustrated in figure (1) below.
Figure 1: Historical global energy use by source [1].
If humanity is to slow, reverse, or even just stabilize the climate-related effects of our energy
consumption, a clean, economically viable, and scalable energy source is desperately needed.
Such an energy source could be fusion, which uses abundant fuels like hydrogen and produces
no greenhouse gas emissions.
1.1
Fusion
The Sun, and all other suns in the universe, is powered by a process known as nuclear fusion.
Fusion is the process of two atoms overcoming their mutual electric (coulomb) repulsion and
fusing together. Overcoming this mutual repulsion is associated with an energy barrier which
the reactants, the fusion species, must overcome in order to fuse. Such a fusion reaction can be
seen illustrated in figure (2).

Figure 2: An illustration of the mutual Coulomb repulsion of two atoms approaching each other which,
if overcome, results in the atoms fusing [2].
To overcome this potential barrier, the atoms must have exceedingly high velocities and, there-
fore, high temperatures. At these temperatures, neutral atoms separate into two species, posi-
tively charged ions and negatively charged electrons. This "soup" of ions and electrons is called
plasma, the fourth state of matter [3].
The energy released in fusion reactions comes from the discrepancy between the binding energy
per nucleon of the products and the reactants. This energy, of course, does not come out of
thin air, but from the conversion of mass into energy following E = mc2. A graph showing the
binding energy per nucleon as a function of mass number can be seen below in figure (3).
Figure 3: Binding energy per nucleon as a function of mass number [10]
As indicated in Figure (3), energy is released through fusion when fusing light atoms to form
heavier atoms up until iron. After this, energy cannot be liberated by fusion. Instead, splitting
heavy atoms into lighter atoms releases energy for elements heavier than iron. This process is
known as nuclear fission, which is the conventional form of nuclear energy.

Creating a man-made sun on earth, a machine capable of harnessing the energy of fusion,
has been a goal of physicists for over 60 years. Though not yet possible, the understanding of
the fundamental processes and requirements improve year after year [4]. These machines typi-
cally come in two different variants. The first kinds is magnetic confinement fusion (MCF)[5],
where magnetic fields are used to shape and confine the fusion plasma. The second kind is in-
ertial confinement fusion (ICF)[6], where, typically, ions are shot into a pellet containing fusion
fuel. The DTU FUSOR is a subvariant of ICF, namely inertial electrostatic confinement (IEC),
where an electric field is used to guide and contain a plasma.
The Sun currently performs hydrogen-hydrogen (H-H) fusion, but will move on to other re-
actions when its hydrogen reserve is depleted. Despite not having extremely high temperatures,
the Sun can still facilitate fusion due to its immense size and pressure. These advantages make
H-H fusion possible on the Sun. On Earth, however, we must create suitable fusion conditions
by achieving very high temperatures. Temperatures within fusion devices such as tokamaks, a
type of MCF device, typically exceed 100 million degrees [7], while even the core of the Sun is
only about 15 million degrees. Different fusion reactions have different reactivities at different
temperatures. A graph showing the Maxwell averaged reactivity ⟨σv⟩of a few earth-bound
fusion reaction candidates can be seen below in figure (4).
Figure 4: A graph showing the Maxwell averaged reactivity ⟨σv⟩of different fusion reactions as a function
of temperature[8].
The reaction with the highest reactivity, for the temperature range at which fusion devices
operate, is the deuterium and tritium (D+T) reaction. This reaction can be seen below in eq
(1).
D + T →He4 + n + 17.6MeV
(1)
This reaction releases 17.6 MeV, which is over a million times more than a typical chemical
process such as burning fossil fuels. Deuterium is an isotope of hydrogen and has a natural
abundance and can be found in Earth’s oceans. Deuterium accounts for 0.0312% by mass of all
hydrogen atoms in the oceans, representing a practically limitless resource. Tritium, another
isotope of hydrogen, is radioactive with a half-life of 12.3 years and thus only exists in trace
amounts in nature.
However, tritium is produced in heavy water-moderated nuclear fission

reactors and can even be produced within the fusion reactor itself through the use of "Breeder
blankets" [11].
1.2
Fusion challenges
Building fusion reactors capable of sustaining net energy positive fusion reactions has proven to
be more challenging than initially anticipated. As the competencies of fusion researchers and
the collective fusion knowledge has grown, the field has become increasingly complicated. What
was initially assumed to be a based on just particle interactions, plasma physics research has
now expanded into fluid dynamics and magneto-hydrodynamics (MHD).
The ITER (International thermonuclear experimental reactor) project, an experimental MCF
tokamak being built in southern France, is expected to have a Q=10, that is, it will produce 10
times more energy than it consumes. However, there is not yet agreement on whether such a
machine will be economically viable. The economic viability of tokamaks as power plants will
be not be determined until the DEMO (Demonstration power plant) project, ITER’s successor,
is completed. Then again, there is not even agreement that the tokamak is the optimal fusion
reactor design. Two other possible designs are stellarators, spherical tokamaks [12] and ICF
devices like the DTU FUSOR.
As will be explained in the following theory section, fusion reactors require high temperatures,
densities and confinement times. Somewhat surprisingly, creating plasmas with temperatures of
100 million degrees is the easiest of these three parameters to achieve. One method of increasing
the confinement time in tokamaks is to increase the plasma volume. Since volume scales with
r3 and surface scales with r2, increasing the volume increases the ratio of volume to surface.
This is important as it is in the volume that energy producing fusion reactions occur, and it
is through the surface that energy is lost. Practically, this volume/surface balancing requires
tokamaks to be exceedingly large and, therefore, expensive. The ITER project, including its
cryostat, will be approximately 30 meters tall with a diameter of 28 meters, with total budget
estimates ranging between €18 to €65 billion [13][14].
Universities like DTU and other fusion related research institutions are constantly working
together in collaboration to further the development of the field of fusion research within both
MCF and ICF devices.

1.3
SDG Sustainability
As stewards of planet earth, we humans have a societal and environmental responsibility to
analyse the effects and ramifications of our activities. This is particularly relevant with regards
to the technologies we use and develope, as they set the framework of how society functions
as a whole. Fusion energy represents a potential energy source that is sustainable and clean
aligning with multiple United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). This thesis,
focusing on the DTU FUSOR and its advancements, is relevant with regards to several SDGs,
in particular SDG 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy), SDG 13 (Climate Action) and SDG 9
(Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure).
Figure 5: United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 7, 13 & 9 [15].
SDG 7 & 13: Affordable, Clean Energy and Climate Action
The pursuit of fusion energy directly addresses SDG 7, which aims to ensure access to affordable,
reliable, sustainable, energy for all. Fusion energy represents a practically limitless source of
energy with minimal environmental impact compared to current fossil fuel-based energy sources
used today. By advancing the understanding of the DTU FUSOR, this research contributes to
the goal of making fusion energy a viable solution to global energy needs. The potential of
fusion energy to be a sustainable and large-scale source of energy also aligns with the SDG 13
which aims to reduce carbon emissions and mitigate the effects of climate change.
SDG 9: Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure
Not only can IEC research be used to further fusion energy technology, it is hypothesised that
devices like the DTU FUSOR can also be used as neutron sources. Current neutrons source
technologies are expensive and access to them is limited. This thesis is relevant with regards
to SDG 9 as the innovation of such a technology would benefit the research efforts that use
neutrons sources, such as the medical industry.

1.4
This project - The DTU FUSOR
Figure 6: The DTU FUSOR.
The DTU FUSOR, seen in figure (6), is an experimental fusion device capable of creating plasma
and facilitating fusion reactions. Though fully operational, many aspects of the FUSOR, the
plasma it creates and its operation, are poorly understood. Key fusion related phenomena such
as ion and electron density, temperature and distribution, are almost completely unknown. By
combining experimental and simulation-based methods, this project seeks to create a simulation
model capable of reproducing results seen in real life as well as investigating possible avenues of
improvement with regards to the FUSOR as a fusion device. The main research questions of this
project are: "Can a COMSOL model be developed such that key ion and electron parameters can
be predicted?" and as a result "Can the FUSOR’s reactor conditions be improved by modifying
the experimental setup?".
1.5
Outline of the thesis
The thesis begins by outlining the fundamental theory of fusion and COMSOL simulation in
section (2). After this, a number of experimental and simulation-based methods are described
and documented in section (3). Following this, the results section of this thesis starts off by
expanding upon an existing FUSOR COMSOL model [25] by integrating charge exchange in
section (4.3). Using these results, new predictions are made, quantifying the possible range
of key plasma parameters such as the Debye length in section (4.4). Additionally, the spatial
distributions of the plasma is investigated and quantified in section (4.8). After that, modifi-
cations are made to the FUSOR’s design in the COMSOL model in order to explore possible
avenues of improvements in section (4.11). A discussion of the assumptions and approximations
are discussed in section (5) and the conclusion of this thesis can be seen in section (6).

2
Theory
The following subsections will give a brief overview of some of the fundamental theoretical
concepts and context of fusion and simulation with regards to this project.
2.1
DTU FUSOR theory
The DTU FUSOR[29] is an experimental inertial electrostatic confinement (IEC) device. A
schematic of the FUSOR can be seen below in figure (7). Most importantly, the schematic
shows the cylindrical FUSOR vessel along with the FUSOR grid and quartz tube. The vessel
is 55.5 cm tall and has a radius 17.8 cm and a volume of 58 L. The cathode grid used in this
thesis is spherical football patterned grid with a radius of 4 cm.
The main operational tools the FUSOR uses is applied voltage, applied current and gas flow.
Applied voltage refers to the voltage difference between the cathode grid and the grounded
walls. The applied current refers to the current of electrons running from the grid to the wall,
however, it also represents the current of recirculating ions. In simplified terms, the voltage
refers to the maximum energy a plasma species can have and the current is best understood
as a gauge of the ion density within the grid. With regards to the technical operation of the
FUSOR, the realised voltage or current may not correspond to the applied voltage or current.
Such mismatches often arise depending on whether the FUSOR is in a voltage limited or current
limited regime. The gas flow is the main parameter that is used to control the pressure within
the FUSOR. Practically speaking, the FUSOR is evacuated to bring the pressure down and then
gas is added continuously in order to maintain the desired pressure by adjusting the voltage of
a flow controller.
Figure 7: (Left) Schematic image of FUSOR and (Right) and image of a 3D printed titanium grid[29].
An image of a 3D printed Titanium grid with a radius of 6cm can be seen in figure (7) (Right).
The grid used in the FUSOR throughout this project has a radius of 4cm, a wire thickness of
1mm, 32 openings and is 93% transparent.
Plasma conditions are reached within the FUSOR by applying a large potential difference
between the grounded anode walls and negatively charged cathode grid.
If the potential is
large enough for a given gas and pressure, plasma breakdown [16] will occur, separating neutral
atoms into positively charged ions and negatively charged electrons. These charged species, in
the presence of an electric field, will be subject to a Lorentz force, seen in eq. (2) (Right).
E = −∇U
F = q(E + v × B)
(2)

==PAGE:13==

where E is the electric field, ∇U is the gradient of the potential, F is the force applied on
charged species, q is the charge of the species and v its velocity. The magnetic field, B, is also
present in the Lorentz equation, but in the FUSOR B=0.
In the FUSOR ions will move towards the cathode grid and some will hit it while others
proceed to orbit in and out. The electrons will mostly move towards the walls, but some will
also be drawn to the ions creating Debye shielding, which will be elaborated in section (2.3).
Throughout this thesis, "standard reactor conditions" will refer to the electrical potential being
6kV and 2µbar pressure.
Ions with sufficient energy that collide have a chance of undergoing fusion, releasing energy
and in some cases neutrons. The desired fusion reaction is D+T, as mentioned previously, the
FUSOR, however, does not use tritium due to its radioactivity. Instead, this project focuses on
D+D fusion. This reaction has two possible outcomes with equal probability which can be seen
below in eq. (3) and (4).
D + D →He4 + n + 3.27MeV
(3)
D + D →H3 + H + 4.03MeV
(4)
When comparing MCF with IEC, MCF seems to be the design that is most capable of sustain-
ing energy positive fusion reactions. While IEC can create fusion conditions, due to electron
confinement issues IEC may not be optimal with regards to energy production. However, IEC,
being the cheaper and much simpler design capable of emitting neutrons with a well defined
energy, could be used as a neutron source instead. Energetically well defined neutrons are dif-
ficult to create, expensive and a sought-after resource used in various industries. With regards
to operating the FUSOR as a neutron source, it is the neutron released in the reaction seen in
eq. (3) that is of interest. In any case, whether the goal of the FUSOR operation is to create
an energy surplus or neutrons, the goal of FUSOR improvement, at this stage of its develop-
ment, is to improve reactor conditions with regards to a parameter called the Lawson criteria [4].
The Lawson criteria describes the necessary plasma conditions for a reactor to become self
sustaining. This is called reactor ignition. The majority of the fusion processes occur in the
center of the grid as this is where the ion density is highest. Ion density, ni, is one of the 3
parameters of the Lawson criteria, niTτE, the other two being temperature T and confinement
time, τE. The temperature, T, refers to the temperature of the ion as they are the species that
undergo fusion. The confinement time, τE, represents the average time that the energy remains
within the plasma before being lost to its surroundings. Increasing the ni means increasing
the number of fusion reactions possible, increasing T means increasing the fraction of ions at
temperatures capable of undergoing fusion and increasing τE translates to reducing the amount
of energy lost. Improving these 3 parameters in the FUSOR could be done in many ways.
For example, increasing the ion convergence would increase the ion density and modifying the
electric field’s shape could improve confinement time, thereby reducing energy loss.
So far the theoretical model that has been used on the FUSOR is the Thorson model [39],
which is a spherically convergent ion focus (SCIF) model. The model is a collisionless model,
the validity and applicability of which will be scrutinized throughout this thesis.
2.2
Collisions
A plasma’s interaction with a neutral background gas is an important aspect of its dynamics.
This phenomena is especially important for fusion devices like the FUSOR, as its ionization
degree is presumed to be quite low at nic/n0 = 3 · 10−5 [29]. In other words, even when a
Page 12 of 71
==PAGE:14==
DTU - FUSOR
June 22, 2024
plasma is created in the FUSOR, there is still a substantial amount of neutral background
gas.
Charged species of the plasma inevitably interact with the neutral species of the gas,
colliding and altering their properties. The three main collision phenomena of plasmas and
their reactivates can be seen in figure (8) below. It is worth noting that figure (8) is based on
hydrogen targets while the rest of the thesis is based on deuterium. This is a source of error,
but the collision cross section of deuterium is likely very similar to hydrogen, due to them being
isotopes.
Figure 8: A graph showing the reactivity σv of charge exchange, electron impact ionization and proton
impact ionization on hydrogen targets[9].
Electron impact ionization is when an electron of the plasma collides with a neutral species and
ionizes it. Proton impact ionization is the same process, only with an ion colliding with the
neutral species. Since this thesis describes deuterium plasmas and not hydrogen, proton impact
ionization is more aptly referred to as ion impact ionization.
Charge exchange (CX) is a process in which a high energy ion of the plasma collides with

2.4
COMSOL theory
Simulation is often used in physics to build computational models that aid in the understanding
of a given phenomena. Experiments can be expensive and time consuming, and so it is often
advantageous to develop a model of a given system before or in tandem with experimentation in
order to not waste resources. Usually, simulations start off simple with the most basic and easy
to implement physics. Gradually more and more complicated phenomena are added. Often, as
the complexity increases, approximations are added in order to simplify the model, while still
accurately representing reality.
While the governing equations that describe the continuum of fusion systems are known, the
analytical solutions are often highly cumbersome to solve or have solutions that are not yet
known. That being said, the simulation program COMSOL is aptly equipped to deal with these
difficulties.
COMSOL is a multiphysics simulation platform that approximates the "strong-form" governing
PDEs (partial differential equations) equations, with easier to solve "weak-form" PDEs. It does
this by discretizing space into a finite but large number of points and creating a mesh between
these points. For each point, the surrounding mesh is called a mesh-cell and within these mesh-
cells, test functions are calculated and, aided by boundary conditions, the weak-form equations
are solved. This is called FEM (Finite element method) [20].
Throughout this project, the DTU FUSOR is simulated with COMSOL, where the strong
form PDE that is used is the Laplace equation seen below in eq. (9).
∇2V = 0
(9)
Additionally, Dirichlet boundary conditions are used, fixing the value of the electrical potential
V to a certain value at the FUSOR walls and grid. In addition to this, particles are inserted
into the continuum that COMSOL solves, and their motion is solved using the Lorentz force as
seen in eq. (2).
The mesh upper and lower boundaries are determined using convergence tests and optimized
with regards to processing power and an acceptable level of error [25].
The time steps of the solver are also optimized in a similar fashion and are based on the velocity
of the particles and the size of the FUSOR. For standard simulations with FUSOR potentials
of 6kV, the time step is 1.175ns but this is adjusted for different potentials due to the difference
in particle velocity.
The FUSOR geometry was created within COMSOL and documented in previous work [25]
using real life measurements [29].

Methods
The following subsections will give an overview of the methods used in the FUSOR experiments
as well as COMSOL simulations. These methods can be viewed as tools that, throughout the
project, are used to understand experimental results and develop the COMSOL model.
3.1
COMSOL
COMSOL as a multiphysics program has many different tools that can be used to represent a
desired physical system. The following subsections serve as a documentation of the most im-
portant COMSOL features used. A more descriptive documentation of the COMSOL program
and its nodes can be found on the COMSOL website [18]. All of the COMSOL features listed
below, except for those related to collisions, are identical in their application to the tools used
in previous work [27].
3.1.1
Charged particle tracing
After solving the continuum fields of the FUSOR, the use of the charged particle tracing module
allows for the insertion of charged particles of specific mass and charge. In the case of this
project, the simulated ion’s mass and charge match that of deuterium.
3.1.2
Forces on particles
The force of the electric field on the ions is implemented using the electric force node. The
interaction between ions is implemented using the particle-particle interaction node. Although
this node has a predefined coulomb force, the equations used can be modified to suit the need
of the simulation.
3.1.3
Space charge effects
The particle-particle interaction equation modification is done in multiple ways. The first way
is to increase the particle interaction strength. This increase is done to compensate for the fact
that only a small number of particles are simulated in the COMSOL model, when in reality,
the ion density in the FUSOR core is nic ≈1015m−3 [29]. This is done in the following manner.
It is assumed that the ion density in the FUSOR core is nic ≈1015m−3. However, the whole
FUSOR likely has on average nif ≈1014m−3. The FUSOR is 60L and, therefore, it can be
assumed that there are n ≈1014m−3 · 0.06m3 = 6 · 1012 ions in the FUSOR.
500 is the chosen amount of ions required to be simulated in order to minimize simulation time
and statistical uncertainty. To replicate the space charge effect created by these 6 · 1012 ions in
a 500 particle model, the coulomb force must be multiplied by a factor of 6 · 1012 ·
1
500 ≈1010.
Henceforth, this factor 1010 will be known as the scaling coefficient. For 5000 particle simula-
tions, the scaling coefficient is reduced to 109.

Debye length λD
The second way that the particle-particle interaction equations are modified is with regards to
Debye screening. Implementing the Debye length into the simulations is done by adding the
multiplicative exponential term seen in eq. (7) to the particle-particle interaction node’s force
equation in COMSOL. Doing this requires a choice of Debye length that is constant throughout
the entire geometry.
3.1.5
Charge exchange and other collisions
The charged particle tracing module has a collision node which allows the modeling of the in-
teractions between particles of interest and a background gas. COMSOL uses a Monte Carlo
[19] model in which, at each step taken by the solver, particles have a probability to interact
with the background gas based on the collision cross section, the distance traveled between time
steps and the gas density.
Introducing charge exchange to the COMSOL model is done by first adding a collision node. In
this node, the number density of the background gas is defined as nbg = 5 · 1019 1
m3 , the molar
mass as 0.002 kg
mol and the temperature of the background gas, 293.15 K. After this a resonant
charge exchange node is added where the collision cross section and the scattering angle is de-
fined.
The cross section is calculated using figure (8)[9], where the energy of the deuterium ions
is a assumed to be 1.65keV, (This specific value is found in section (4.3)). This results in a
collision cross section of σ = 2 · 10−19m2. It is worth noting, that in reality, the cross section
is dependent on the velocity of the particle and is not constant. Defining a constant collision
cross section is, therefore, an approximation. However, as can be seen in figure (8), within the
relevant energy region, the collision cross section is relatively constant.
That being said, with regards to this specific COMSOL model, collisions have another in-
teresting feature. This COMSOL model, to avoid nonconvergent time steps, scales the time
step length with the particle velocity and the total time simulated scales with the size of the
time step. That is to say, as the particle velocity is increased, through modifying the FUSOR
potential, the time steps get smaller and so does the total time simulated. For example, a 6kV
simulation has a time step of 1.1757 · 10−9s and a total simulation time of 1.5µs and a 17kV
simulation has a time step of 6.9437 · 10−10s and a total simulation time of 0.89113µs. The
specifics of this calculation are somewhat arbitrary and are in general mostly just scaled ad hoc
to illustrate particle dynamics in the FUSOR.
With regards to collisions, however, these time scalings are exceedingly important. The time
scalings result in two simulations, with different FUSOR potentials, while keeping all other
reactor conditions the same, having the same number of collisions. To further elaborate, an
increased number of collisions due to an increased FUSOR potential is only realised if the total
simulation time is the same.
A caveat to this consideration is that in a steady state regime, where the total number of par-
ticles is roughly constant, this effect is inconsequential.

Measuring the ion core is done by first taking a picture of it using one of the FUSOR web-
cams. This image is then imported into an image processing program like ImageJ, converted to
gray-scale, distance calibrated, and a line profile is taken across the ion core as seen below in

Some of the light that is emitted by the plasma is blocked by the FUSOR grid. To remove this
measurement artefact, the line profile is run through a code that "cleans" the data, by repeat-
ing a data point if the next data point is too different from it within a definable sensitivity (5 %).
The light observed from the plasma is light coming from a 3D object. The act of taking a
picture of this light transforms the 3D data (Light) into a 2D projection of the data (Image).

==PAGE:20==

The effect of this is that the light that is measured on one side of the plasma, is in fact the sum
of the light coming from the front side and the back side. It is of interest to find the size of
the core, and thus, this "summation" gives a false impression of the ion core’s size. To tackle
this 3D to 2D projection effect, an inverse Abel transform[33] is performed on the data. This
transformation creates a new set of data where in effect, the 2D data is transformed back into
a slice of the 3D data. From this Abel data the ion core radius is measured as the half width
at half max (HWHM). In practical terms, the Abel inversion is only applied to one side of
the light line profile at a time. By performing this procedure on both sides (left and right) an
average and standard deviation can be calculated. When calculated in this way, the uncertainty
describes the asymmetry of the light profile.
Performing this measurement is not an exact science. By comparing figure (11) (Left) and
figure (10), it can be seen that not all ion cores have the same shape. This raises the question of
where to place the line from which the line profile is measured. When the ion core is spherical,
this is easy to answer, but when the shape is complex, containing jets and asymmetries, the
answer is not so obvious. In these instances, it is incumbent upon the researcher to delineate
an area that accurately represents the ion core. It is important to note, that all images taken
of FUSOR plasmas must use the same camera settings, such as exposure time, in order to be
directly comparable.
Also in figure (10) a "jet" can be seen, going from the core and out of the grid.
Though
this phenomena is not yet understood and is, therefore, very interesting, it will not be explored
in this thesis.

the ion core is defined as the average minimum radius of the ions to the center of the FUSOR
for particles that have passed through the grid. This will be referred as the Rmin method.

3.3
Ion gun
Another feature of the FUSOR not previously mentioned is the ion gun. The ion gun is a device,
separate in operation to the FUSOR, which creates its own plasma and then fires ions upwards
into the FUSOR chamber. It has been theorised that by incorporating the use of the ion gun,
the ion density and fusion rate can be improved [28].
Page 19 of 71
==PAGE:21==
DTU - FUSOR
June 22, 2024
Before stepping into experimentation, the ion gun was simulated in COMSOL, in order to
understand the dynamics of the ions. The ion gun has a range of different settings which pre-
vious work [28] has documented. The main points of interest are what the optimal acceleration
voltage and beam current settings are, such that the ions are guided towards the center of the
grid.
The acceleration voltage of the ion gun defines the initial velocity of the ions as they enter
the FUSOR vessel and the beam current defines the rate at which they are released. There
are many competing effects of the voltage and current when it comes to the trajectory of the
ions. A higher number of ions may be desirable in order to increase parameters such as the
ion density in the core. However, since the ions are positively charged, and there is little to no
Debye shielding at the FUSOR walls, the ions repel each other. A higher current means more
ions repelling each other, which, in turn, broadens the ion beam, reducing the number of ions
entering the grid. Increasing the voltage increases the average distance between the ions and
reduces their mutual repulsion, thus collimating the ion beam. This may sound good, but since
the ion gun does not point directly at the grid, increasing the voltage means running the risk
of shooting the ions past the grid and hitting the opposing wall.
Having a high rate of ions entering the grid is a balancing act between the voltage and the
current of the ion gun, a balancing act which can easily be simulated in COMSOL.
Implementing the ion gun into the simulations is done by adding a region at the bottom of
the FUSOR where ions can be released throughout the simulation. Along with the release area
matching the real size of the ion gun used in experiments, it is possible to tune the simulated
particle’s release velocity and rate. The rate of ion release in the simulation used a scaling
coefficient of 109 to increase the number of particles simulated and, thereby, reduce statistical
uncertainty.

The first rendition [25] of the COMSOL model was a collisionless model where particle-particle

In this third rendition, particle collisions will be introduced, and the effect of this introduction

which they originated. This effect can be seen in figure (17) (Left), where the ions do not fol-

again. Instead they approach the center, and then, on average, slowly continue to get closer.

a source of energy loss and one of the results of this is that the ions, on average, do no have
enough energy to reach this region of the FUSOR. In this way, it can be said, that the addition
of charge exchange improves ion confinement.

4.3.3
Velocity distribution
Looking at the velocity distribution of ions at a given time is another way of investigating the
effects of charge exchange. To get a distribution of velocities, that matches what might occur
in reality, a steady state model is used. Steady state models are, in the context of this thesis,
simulations, where particles are added at approximately the same rate that they are lost to wall
and grid collisions. The particle addition rate is assessed based on the particle loss rate in non
steady state simulations and then adjusted iteratively. Optimally, steady state simulations have
a constant number of particles throughout the simulation.

Figure (19) (Left) illustrates that the velocity distribution is well approximated by a Boltzmann-
Maxwell distribution with E = 600eV. The same goes for figure (19) (Right), however, the en-
ergy of the Boltzmann-Maxwell distribution is considerably lower at E = 150eV. Additionally,
the maximum velocities of the ions are very different. The maximum velocities of the ions in

This difference clearly illustrates the effect of charge exchange in that the highest velocity ions
have the highest chance of undergoing said collision and losing their energy. Particles, after
colliding, are left with close to zero energy. This effect is also illustrated by these graphs when
comparing the number of particles at very low energies, where the no CX model has very few
low energy particles and the CX model has a considerable amount.
Another important observation is the relatively low energy of the distribution of both mod-
els compared to the FUSOR potential. In these simulations the FUSOR potential was 6kV and,
as previously stated, the fitted Boltzmann distributions have characteristic energies of 600eV
and 150eV. This means the plasma has anywhere between a factor 10 to 40 less energy than
what the FUSOR potential might indicate.
A caveat to this consideration is the ion birth radius which relates directly to the maximum
energy that the ions can acquire through E-field acceleration.
In the simulations the ion birth radius is uniform in a sphere around the grid. This is, of course,
just an assumption and the real birth radius of the ions is not known.
The ions of a plasma are created through a Townsend avalanche [16] where electrons ionize neu-
tral atoms. The electrons, being accelerated outwards towards the grid, have a higher chance
of having the necessary ionization energy the further out they have traveled. Because of this, it
could be imagined that the ion’s birth radius would be better approximated by the surface of
a shell instead of the volume of a sphere. Were the simulated birth radius to be on the surface
of the sphere, the ions would have a higher energy on average. However, regardless of whether
this is a more or less realistic assumption, it must also be considered, that when ions are born
uniformly in a sphere, there are already more high energy ions than low energy ions (due to
spherical geometry).
It must also be restated that these histograms are sampled within a steady state regime. This
is an important aspect of the simulations to note, as the ions that are added continuously to
the simulation have had less time to accelerate and, thus, have a lower energy.

4.4
Debye length - Rmin method
The introduction of charge exchange significantly changes the dynamics of the ions in the FU-
SOR as the ions now have mechanism of energy loss. One of the significant findings of previous
work[27] was the Debye length of the plasma, which was found using interpolation, to be 0.23cm.
However, this calculation was based on a collisionless model and was by no means conclusive.
Now that charge exchange has been introduced, the Debye length will be revised.
To investigate this new collisional model and its effect on the Debye length, a number of simu-
lations were performed, 500 ion CX 6kV S-S, 500 ion CX 17kV S-S and 5000 ion CX S-S. For
each of these series, the simulations were run using Debye lengths of 0.5cm, 0.1cm and 0.01cm.
The goal of these simulations was to identify the Debye length required to achieve an Rcore
size comparable to what is seen in experimentation and to investigate the impact of FUSOR
potential on said radii. Additionally. the 5000 ion simulations were performed in order to check
that the COMSOL model was not sensitive to simulated particle number. These 5000 particle
simulations also provided high resolution data with regards to shot noise and uncertainty. A
plot of the ion core radius evaluated using the Rmin method described in section (3.2) as a
function of different λD can be seen in figure (24).

In section (4.4) the Debye length was estimated from simulations using the Rmin method. Since,
among other things, the resulting electron temperature didn’t seem reasonable, another method
was devised.
Experimental data comes from pictures taken of the plasma. Developing a data analysis method
in which "pictures" could be taken of the simulation data would allow the direct comparison of
the experimentally acquired images and the simulated data.
Since deuterium only has a single electron, the light produced by the "plasma" actually comes
from the collision excitation of neutral particles. In the cases of heavier elements with multiple
electrons, light can come from excitation of the plasma itself. Of course, the simulation does not
"emit light" like the experimental plasma does and so an assumption is made. Though it is not
the ions that emit the light, if it is assumed that the background gas is uniformly distributed
in the FUSOR, then the simulated ion trajectories can be used as a proxy for the neutral ion
excitations. In other words, even though the light doesn’t come from the ions themselves and
in fact the neutral background gas, it can be assumed that the light profile matches the ion’s
distribution profile. With this assumption, the resulting light profiles can now be mapped using
the simulated ion profiles.

the ions do not repel each other as much and thus their trajectories converge. This results in a
large amount of ions hitting the grid.

neutral atoms hit by electrons close to the FUSOR walls will ionize. These considerations were
also discussed in section (4.3.3).

4.8.2
Potential well
The previously described scenarios are extreme examples where fundamental aspects of plasma
physics are omitted. Extreme as they may be, they describe key elements of simulating the
FUSOR plasma. From these examples it can be seen that to achieve small, well defined ion core
radii, Debye shielding is required, as this allows the ions to get close to each other within the
grid. The omission of charge exchange and Debye shielding, respectively, both seemed capable
of creating the elevated tail structures. That being said, omitting Debye shielding seems more
capable of creating the broad and noisy contribution seen at large distances from the center.
Ignoring charge exchange for the moment, the previous consideration begs the question of how
a small ion core can be generated, which requires a prominent Debye shielding effect, while at
the same time generating elevated tails, which requires minimal Debye shielding effect. This
scenario seems to be contradictory as it requires both a prominent and a minimal Debye shield-
ing effect at the same time. How this, in fact, could be possible requires the consideration of
the FUSOR potenial geometry.
The COMSOL model has a constant Debye length. This is obviously an approximation as,
when there is a potential gradient, and a resulting electric field, electrons are accelerated and
thus have varying energies depending on location. This approximation may, however, be more
egregious than previously thought as the FUSOR’s potential is not just simple gradient from
the walls to the grid. Due to the geometry of the FUSOR grid, the FUSOR also creates a
potential well.
A potential well in physics often refers to a region of space where particles become trapped
due to the topography of their relevant fields. To illustrate this, the COMSOL simulated 6kV
electric field of the FUSOR as a line cut through the x-axis at y=z=0 and through the grid can
be seen below in figure (34).

From the perspective of ions, they are accelerated up hill as they approach the grid. The ions
then oscillate in and out of the grid until they hit the grid or another atom.
The electrons, however, follow drastically different trajectories. From their perspective, they
are accelerated downhill. Electrons outside the grid move freely and are accelerated outwards
until they hit the FUSOR wall. The electrons within the grid are, on the other hand, bound
by the steep topography of the electric field. Only electrons with sufficiently high energies can
escape the potential well created by the grid, leaving behind the low energy electrons.
Based on these simple considerations, the previously assumed constant Debye length can now
be nuanced. Outside the grid, electrons are free to accelerate, leading to high electron temper-
atures and a large Debye length. Within the grid, there is reason to believe that low energy
electrons can accumulate leading to a region with a small Debye length. Additionally, increasing
the FUSOR potential would results in a deepening of the potential well, making it capable of
trapping increasingly energetic electrons, increasing the electron density, further reducing the
Debye length. Such effects have also been considered in research concerning other ICF devices
[40].
If the previous interpretation of the FUSOR potential is true, then the potential well could
be one of the effects responsible for the, otherwise contradictory, small Rcore and elevated wings
combination.

the particle-particle interaction node that Debye shielding is introduced. In all previous simula-
tions, a single interaction node is defined across the entire FUSOR. If the effects of a potential
well on the Debye length are to be implemented, multiple interaction nodes must be defined,

4.8.3
Initial velocity
A possible explanation for the increased intensities further from the center (line profile tails)
could be that there is some kind of spreading phenomena in the FUSOR, that isn’t included in
the COMSOL model. Such a spreading phenomena could be the initial velocity of the ions.
An investigation into the the effects of the ion’s initial velocity was also conducted in section
(4.7). While varying the initial temperature between 0 and 2.5 eV yielded a change in the Rcore,
the histogram line profiles did not exhibit up-shifted tails or tops at any initial temperature, as
was previously hypothesised.
4.8.4
Electron investigation
One clear difference between the COMSOL model and real plasmas is the presence of electrons.
As has been previously stated, including electrons vastly increases the computational time of
simulations. This is due to electrons traveling orders of magnitude faster than ions and, there-
fore, requiring much smaller time steps. To address this, the COMSOL model omits electrons,
but includes a Debye shielding factor in the particle-particle interaction equations. Based on the
realistic Rcore values found using the histogram method, this approximation seems reasonable.
When considering which collision phenomena to include in the model, the mean free paths for
various interactions were calculated. Based on the average velocity of the particles in section
(4.3), it seemed clear that only charge exchange collisions were of relevance. This conclusion
was expanded upon in section (4.3.3) when it was found that the characteristic velocity of
Page 46 of 71
==PAGE:48==
DTU - FUSOR
June 22, 2024
the Maxwell energy distribution for steady state simulations was much lower than the average
maximum velocity used in the preceding calculation. This recalculation reduced, among other
collisions, the mean free path of electron-neutral ionization, making it more relevant. However,
the mean free path was still much larger than the mean free path for charge exchange, and thus
it was concluded that the omission of electrons was still a viable approximation.
It is of importance to note, that when calculating the mean free paths of the electrons, it
was assumed that they had the same energy as the ions, but as of yet, this is just an assump-
tion. The Debye length calculated based on the histogram method suggests that the electrons
have an energy of 1.80eV but this is just in the core, and their energy is likely to vary greatly
depending on the point of evaluation as was highlighted in section (4.8.2).
On the other hand, with regards to comparing simulated line profiles to experimental line
profiles, it may be misleading to omit electrons. The mean free paths of the electron-neutral
ionization are larger than the charge exchange’s but they do still exist, and thus do contribute to
experimentally observed plasma characteristics. Additionally, while the mean free paths based
on figure (8) are relevant for the simulated particle dynamics, the light coming from the FUSOR
plasma is due to excitation and not ionization. It is conceivable that the ions of the plasma have
a distribution similar to what is seen in the COMSOL model but as of yet, nothing is known
about the distribution of the electrons. Taking both ions and electrons into account, the light
coming from the FUSOR is in actuality a sum of the light coming from two, possibly different,
distributions, leading to a more complex profile that isn’t represented well by the COMSOL
simulation.
Based on these considerations, it might be concluded that there is knowledge to gain from
including electrons in the simulation.
To this end, a number of COMSOL simulations were performed where electrons were included.
The general strategy was to insert ions at the start of the simulation based on the positional
data from an S-S ion-only simulation. This was done to achieve the assumed correct distribution
of ions without having to run the simulation for the length of time needed to achieve an S-S
distribution. Then came the question of how to initiate the electrons. Since close to nothing
is known about the electron distributions, the electrons where initiated using the same S-S ion
distribution, though slightly radially minimized with regards to not creating particles on top
of each other and to try and recreate the high electron density within the grid discussed in
section (4.8.2). The ions were initiated with their own velocity, and the electrons, due to lack of
knowledge, were initiated with 0 velocity. It is also worth noting that, since both electrons and
ions were present in these simulations, the Debye shielding contribution to the particle-particle
interaction equation was omitted.
The time steps and total simulated time were then scaled to electron-appropriate lengths and
the simulation was performed. In such a simulation, the ions are practically stationary. Upon
analysing the outputted data, the electrons were found to have a somewhat Gaussian distribu-
tion that was wider than the ion’s distribution, supporting the idea of the ions and electrons
having different distributions. However, upon close inspection, the electrons were found to have
velocities exceeding the speed of light.
Exceeding the speed of light in simulation is a clear indication of a violation of the funda-
mental laws of physics, typically suggesting errors or limitations in the simulation parameters
or the theoretical framework being used. Results from such a simulation are unreliable and
should not be trusted.

4.8.5
Hydrogen and Deuterium comparison
The previous subsections laid out possible sources of the elevated peak and tail structures ob-
served when comparing experimental line profiles to fitted Gaussian profiles. In order to better
understand these peak and tail structures, they will now be quantified.
In order to increase the applicable range of data and avenues of investigation, additional hy-
drogen experiments were performed at similar reactor conditions as the preexisting deuterium
experiments. This additional data is useful because it enables new information and relations to
be deduced based on the comparison between the two isotope plasmas.
For example, in the previous subsection, it was hypothesised that electrons play a role in shap-
ing tails and tops seen in experimental data, but not in simulation. Hydrogen and deuterium,
being isotopes, have the same electron contribution. That is to say, both isotopes can be ionized
once, and thus, the two plasmas must have roughly the same amount of electrons and ionization
degree. Any differences observed between the two plasmas must, therefore, be caused by ion
trajectories.
Quantifying this is difficult as there are many underlying differences between the two plasmas,
and their measurements, that must be taken into consideration. Nevertheless, the goal of this
subsection is to identify differences between the two plasmas, hydrogen and deuterium, while
accounting for secondary effects, in order to investigate whether the differences are caused by
electron or ion trajectories.

have shown that the Rcore is influenced by it. This parameter is measured as the current of

4.10
FUSOR parameter summary
Throughout the results section of this thesis, many investigations were conducted using the
COMSOL model in order to try and identify the range within which important plasma param-
eters might vary. This short subsection will summarize these findings to give an overview of the
findings.
Using the Rmin method, the Debye length was estimated, but was found to be below room
temperature and thus not reliable.
Following this, the histogram method found the Debye
length to be 1mm. In section (4.8.2), the potential well was investigated, and it was found that
to recreate the shape of experimental line profiles, multiple different Debye lengths could be
used in different FUSOR regions respectively.
After this, the FUSOR current was investigated.
Using the number of particles simulated
while maintaining a constant scaling coefficient as a proxy of core ion density, a method of re-
producing the current dependency of the Rcore seen in experiments was found. The desired 1.5
Rcore-current dependency was not reached. However, the highest Rcore-current dependencies
Page 54 of 71
==PAGE:56==
DTU - FUSOR
June 22, 2024
were found using increasingly large λD, the largest of which was 1.257 found at λD = 1cm.
Finally, by varying the scaling coefficient in order to reproduce the ion currents seen in exper-
imentation, it was found that the scaling coefficient, which is based on the Thorson model, is
likely highly overestimated. Based on a reevaluated scaling coefficient, the Debye length was
found to be 0.5cm.
Depending on the electron density assumed, the electron temperature, based on the Debye
lengths found, was calculated to be Te = 0.45 −1.8eV.
The variation between these predictions are caused by the uncertainty of the assumptions upon
which they are based. These uncertainties can be summarized as the electron temperature, the
electron density and the ion density. The validity of the simulations depend on the accuracy of
the assumptions of these parameters. Without a measurement of any of these three parameters
on the FUSOR itself, their assumed values are capable of varying greatly within the COMSOL
model.
If even just one of these parameters is measured, ideally, the ion density, then measurable
features like the ion flux, the Rcore and its distribution could be used to find the remaining
parameters. It is, therefore, highly recommended that a Langmuir probe, a device capable of
measuring these parameters, is installed on the FUSOR as part of future research.
4.11
FUSOR modifications
Equipped with a seemingly viable method of measuring the Rcore, the histogram method, mod-
ifications can be made to the FUSOR in order to investigate possible avenues of operational
improvement. In this case, operational improvement is defined as anything that can increase
any of the 3 parameters of the Lawson criteria as described in section (2.1), ni, T and τE. A
discussion of these results can be seen in section (5.7).

Improved quartz tube

The "per-
fect grid" investigation showed that it is not the symmetry of the grid that hinders further Rcore
improvement. The varied size investigation showed that there is an optimal grid size. These
findings beg the question; "If it is not due to grid symmetry, then why does the grid size impact
the Rcore?".
Addressing this question can be done by assessing the too extremes of the grid sizes, too small
and too big. If the grid is too small, then, despite the grid converging the ions well initially, the
ions, as they approach the grid, have a smaller and smaller volume to converge in, causing the
ions to be repelled, leading to larger a Rcore. Understanding the large grid effect requires the
Page 57 of 71
==PAGE:59==
DTU - FUSOR
June 22, 2024
consideration of how the ions converge as they approach the grid, and after they have entered
the grid. As the ions approach the grid they converge towards the center. However, as soon as
they have entered the grid, the electric field starts to pull them towards the grid once again,
in a sense, defocusing the ions. This defocusing effect is likely to be more prominent the larger
the grid is.
Based on this, the optimal grid size is found to be one that is large enough to fully encompass
the ion core such that ions are not repelled outside the grid, but also small enough such that
the defocusing effect, on the ions that have entered the grid, is minimal.

Asymmetric emission line profiles
As described in Section (3.4), the emission lines measured from the light of the plasma during
spectral analysis of the FUSOR, which are supposed to be delta or at least Gaussian distribu-
tions, were found to be asymmetric. A possible explanation for this phenomenon is pressure
broadening. Therefore, the relevant equations will now be applied to FUSOR parameters to test
if this could be the cause. It is worth noting, that these equations are functions of background
gas number density, nbg. This parameter denotes the number of atoms per cubic meter, but
can be understood as a gauge of the pressure within the FUSOR.
The investigation started with the development of Python code that could plot P1, P2 and
P12. It was at this stage that it was found that, to achieve what quantitatively resembled the
observed asymmetric emission line, extreme background gas densities had to be used.

Discussion
The following section is a discussion of the various assumptions and approximations used
throughout this thesis. Additionally, caveats, limits and applicabilities will be highlighted at
scrutinized.
5.1
Plasma species
Throughout this thesis deuterium FUSOR experiments have been compared to COMSOL sim-
ulated deuterium plasmas. For the sake of simplicity, only deuterium (D), atoms consisting of
one proton and one neutron, in a background gas of D, (Not D2), were simulated. In reality the
background gas of the FUSOR is likely composed of D, D2 and D3 and their ionised versions.
Research [41] suggests that different plasma species have different distributions. An illustration
of this can be seen below in figure (46)
Page 60 of 71
==PAGE:62==
DTU - FUSOR
June 22, 2024
Figure 46: An illustration of calculated spatial distributions of various hydrogen species [41].
Only simulating D is a significant assumption. With regards to the investigations performed in
section (4.8), the different plasma species likely play a role in forming the plasma distribution
seen in experiments but not in simulation.
Since little is known about the composition of the background gas, let alone its ionization
degree, different plasma species were omitted from this thesis but could with great utility be
investigated in future research.
5.2
Charge exchange
Charge exchange is one of the key additions of this thesis to the FUSOR COMSOL model.
As charge exchange is one of the key mechanisms of energy loss, it is a necessary addition in
order to achieve reliable simulation results. However, there are a couple of sources of error with
regards to how it is applied to the model.
Firstly, the collision cross sections in figure (8) are based on hydrogen targets.
While hy-
drogen and deuterium are very similar and, therefore, are expected to have roughly the same
charge exchange cross section, this is still just an approximation. Another caveat to this ap-
proximation, and perhaps a more damning one, is that, when implemented into COMSOL, the
charge exchange cross section is constant and not velocity dependent.
When implemented, the charge exchange cross section was calculated based on the average
maximum velocity. It was later found that, with regards to how the energy of the particles is
distributed, that this was most likely an overestimation. Even so, the cross section was not
changed with regards to consistency between simulations. To add to this, the ions also change
in velocity throughout their trajectories, having low velocities at the peripheries of their orbits
and high velocities within the grid.
The omission of the velocity dependence of the charge exchange cross section likely results in
over- and underestimations of said cross sections depending on the location of investigation.
It is hard to judge what effect this approximation has on the model as a whole, but it would
definitely be more accurate to implement a velocity dependent charge exchange cross section.
This could be addressed in a future project.
5.3
Quartz tube drift
After introducing charge exchange to the COMSOL model it was found that the loss of energy
of the ions increases their confinement. This observation was based on the fact that when the
ions lose energy, they are no longer capable of drifting to undesirable areas of the FUSOR.
To nuance this conclusion it is worth saying, that the ions within the FUSOR are in reality
not born uniformly in a sphere as in the simulations. It is likely that ions are born all over
the FUSOR and as such, ions born in the upper regions of the FUSOR likely still drift up
and towards the voltage stalk and are subsequently lost. Additionally, this lack of drift due to
Page 61 of 71
==PAGE:63==
DTU - FUSOR
June 22, 2024
energy loss phenomena is likely to be one of the aspects of the simulation that is sensitive to the
approximation that the collision cross section is the same for all particles and not dependent
on velocity. The degree to which the ions drift up and towards the quartz tube probably lies
between the collisional and non collisional simulations seen in figure (18).
5.4
Simulated Debye length
Strictly speaking, the Debye length could be defined as different values at different locations or
regions in the FUSOR simulation, but this was only done in section (4.8). Having a constant
Debye length is an approximation.
In real life, the FUSOR grid is also shielded by the electrons. This situation is no longer a
particle-particle interaction and Debye screening, as implemented in this thesis, does not apply
to the grid. The real life effect of this shielding of the grid is that the ions feel a smaller potential
than what is being driven by the FUSOR, depending on the distance from the grid.
The investigation of the grid shielding effect and introducing multiple Debye regions could
be assessed in the future research.

5.6
Steady state particle input sensitivity
The iterative process of finding the correct amount of particles to add throughout simulations
in order to achieve a constant particle number is both very difficult and very time consuming.
In practice, the simulations were repeated and particle input rate adjusted until a total number
of particles at the end of the simulation were within 15% of the initial particle amount.
The particle loss rate, and consequently the required particle addition rate, varies with initial
particle number, FUSOR potential, and to a large degree the Debye length. For example, for
the 500 ion simulations, at λD = 0.5cm the necessary particle input rate was 1 particles every
15 time steps. Reducing the debye length to λD = 0.01cm, the necessary particle input rate was
1 particles every 3 time steps. This was later found in section (4.6) to be caused by the Debye
length impacting the trajectories of the ions in such that more ions are lost to grid collisions at
lower Debye lengths.
5.7
FUSOR improvement
While the FUSOR modification investigations did not predict any improvements through mod-
ifying the quartz tube or the grid, this is not a definitive conclusion.
To measure the "improved reactor conditions" the COMSOL model was modified and the re-
sulting Rcore was measured. Simulation and modeling inaccuracies aside, using the Rcore as a
measurement of improvement is a surrogate endpoint of the investigation. That is to say, the
link between the Rcore as a measurement and any of the three Lawson criteria is an assumption.
An actual improvement of the reactor conditions would result in an increased fusion rate mea-
sured through neutron production. Fusion rates and neutron production are not included in the
COMSOL model, and, therefore, can not be simulated directly. As a result of using the Rcore as
a surrogate parameter, there is a possibility that this method of measurement of improvement
is inappropriate, inaccurate or in need of refinement.
5.8
Ion gun FUSOR degradation
Using a high beam current yields the largest amount of ions passing through the grid. However,
this high current also means that many ions miss the grid and hit the FUSOR walls. While
the wastage of ions may or may not be significant, the degradation of the FUSOR itself due to
bombardment by these wasted ions is a potential concern. The magnitude of this effect may be
worth considering when performing experiments.
5.9
Asymmetric emission lines and the Stark effect
The Stark effect is the shifting, splitting and broadening of spectral lines due to the presence
of an electric field. Calculating the energy shift requires a quantum mechanical derivation. In
essence, the energy shift of the quantum levels, of which the electrons occupy, depends on the
electric field strength and the electric dipole moment of the transition.
The electric field in question in this case could be the electric field of the FUSOR, but could
also be the electric field between the individual species of the plasma. In a simplified case,
the splitting of the energy levels is symmetrical resulting in symmetrical broadening.
This
seems to go against what is observed in the FUSOR, but here it must be remembered that,
not only is the E-field of the FUSOR relatively complex, but so is the distribution of particles.
These considerations could be of relevance when attempting to explain the observed asymmetry.

==PAGE:65==

The analysis of this phenomena becomes even more complex if a model of the electric field
between plasma species is formulated. At this point in time, there isn’t an experimental method
that can be used to verify the results, if such a model was formulated. Due to these factors
the Stark effect was not be explored in this thesis as such an investigation could encompass an
entire thesis by itself.
It is interesting to note, that the asymmetric emission lines appear for all light sources. Lasers,
calibration lamps, FUSOR or NORTH; all emission sources exhibit the same effect. This, of
course, begs the question of whether the asymmetric emission lines are caused by the spectrom-
eter itself. This has been investigated [30] and while many interesting features of the CCS100
spectrometer were identified, none seemed to be capable of causing the asymmetric emission
line effect.
5.10
Orbital modes
Throughout this thesis experimentally acquired line profiles have been compared with simulated
line profiles following the procedures outlined in sections (3.2) and (4.5) respectively.
The
histogram method is applied indiscriminately to all data sets, but the same is not the case
for the experimental line profiles. The lines used to sample the FUSOR plasma are drawn by
hand. While this procedure is simple for spherically symmetrical plasmas, it becomes difficult
for more esoteric plasma shapes. In figure (48) below, some examples of different plasma shapes,
observed through the experiments in this thesis, can be seen.
Figure 48: Three examples of different ion orbitals observed during the FUSOR experiment of this thesis.
The outer most images are from hydrogen plasmas and the center image is from deuterium.
Looking at the various plasma shapes seen in figure (48), the left most plasma is very dispersed.
Drawing a light intensity sampling line across such a plasma can be done in most directions
without much variance. The middle plasma is a very well defined X shape and the right most
plasma is somewhat dispersed but has a clear horizontal structure. In these last two exam-
ples, the direction in which the line is drawn is quite important. Sampling across the "legs" of
the X yields a very different distribution to a line sampled across the horizontal line of symmetry.
These structures are presumably ion channels caused some ion orbits being more favourable
with regards to stability than others. Since little is known about these ion channel structures,
the general sampling technique used in this thesis was to avoid whatever channel structures
were present as much as possible as to only sample the core itself. Ideally this method would
ensure consistency and comparability across all data sets.
This sampling method has some merit as it produces reasonable data with few outliers with
regards to Rcore and intensity. However, when it comes to quantifying the residuals, as seen in
section (4.8.5), this may not be the correct method to use.

Conclusion
The quest for net energy positive fusion reactors necessitates continued research in the field
of fusion. This project aimed to develop a COMSOL model and investigate its capability in
predicting key reactor parameters for operational improvement in conjunction with FUSOR
experiments.
Through the implementation of particle collisions, it was found that charge exchange increases
the Rcore and increases the ion confinement due to energy loss. The histogram method was
developed and identified as a superior tool of data analysis when compared to previous meth-
ods, estimating the Debye length at approximately λD ≈1 −5mm. The velocity distributions
of the simulated ions where found to be, in some cases, Maxwell distributed with characteris-
tic energies 10 times lower than what the FUSOR potential would indicate. Additionally, the
shapes and distributions of various experimental and simulated line profiles were compared and
it was found that the observed variation of plasma distributions is caused by ion trajectories
and not electron trajectories. Other interesting differences between deuterium and hydrogen
plasmas were also identified, namely their shape and their intensities. Lastly, modifications to
the FUSOR quartz tube and grid were simulated in order to test whether the reactor conditions
could be improved. However, no significant improvements were observed.
The development of the COMSOL model is significant as it provides an investigative tool that
can be used in tandem with or in preparation for FUSOR experiments. Even though the model
does not include all possible plasma phenomona, by comparing with experimental data, phe-
nomena can be explored and quantified by process of elimination. Through the investigation
of the shape of the plasma distribution, a possible cause, the potential well, was identified, an
aspect of the DTU FUSOR which has until now not been studied. In agreement, both simula-
tion and experimentation identified which reactor parameters the Rcore depends on, namely the
assumed Debye length, the FUSOR potential and the FUSOR current. Quantifying these re-
lations is of importance if any of the three parameters of the Lawson criteria are to be improved.
Although the simulation was able to replicate experimental findings, the limitations of the
COMSOL model must also be acknowledged. From the scaling coefficient that dictates the
particle-particle interactions, to the comparison of simulated trajectories and emitted light pro-
files, nearly every aspect of the physics, that have been implemented in the model, are in some
way based on approximations and assumptions. In some cases this was done due to lack of
knowledge, like in the case of hydrogen target based collision cross sections. In other cases
this was done to simplify the simulation, like in the case of the omission of electrons. There
is also the case of parameters like the FUSOR current, which is not addressed in the current
COMSOL model but is known to impact the Rcore in experiments. These approximations and
assumptions introduce sources of error that must be considered when interpreting the results.
Looking forward, future projects could benefit from exploring the FUSOR current and consid-
ering the electron dynamics. Based on, or in tandem with, electron investigations, a Langmuir
probe could be installed into the FUSOR. The use of a Langmuir probe would be a a key tool
needed for critical measurement, comparison and verification of simulated and experimental
data. Unfortunately, this project was not able to test the predictions made regarding the opti-
mal settings of the ion gun, but this could aptly be achieved in future research.
The model and analysis method developments as well as the results obtained throughout this
project provide key insight into the FUSOR as a plasma device and enlightened areas of uncer-
tainty. Based on the findings of this project, the understanding of the FUSOR has increased
Page 66 of 71
==PAGE:68==
DTU - FUSOR
June 22, 2024
and avenues of future research have been revealed. Though the DTU FUSOR is a small device,
through collaboration with others and interest driven research, the field of fusion research can
be expanded step by step and some day net positive fusion reactors may be come a reality.
