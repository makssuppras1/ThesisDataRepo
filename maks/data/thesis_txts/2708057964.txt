Abstract
There is an increasing problem with spread of Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR). It is es­
timated that this will result in 10 million deaths attributable to AMR in 2050. Surveillance
is a cornerstone in combating this issue. As part of the surveillance efforts a global
dataset of clinically relevant bacterial isolates has been gathered in the Two Weeks In
The World (TWIW) project.
In this study the an overview of the global chloramphenicol resistance gene distribu­
tion has been created based of the TWIW dataset. Furthermore, has the species:gene
correlations been described. In the end a thorough analysis of the gene synteny of the
most prevalent CmR gene with Cm specificity, catB3 was examined, elucidate mecha­
nisms of spread.
CmR genes are widespread globally and the distributions roughly corresponds to
the usage. They are identified in a wide variety of species but mainly in gram­negative
bacteria. The gene synteny of catB3 is similar when only comparing a 100bp flank­
ing region, but is variable when the flanking region is increased to 8000bp. The long
flanking regions were accomplished by a combined use of Illumina Nextseq and Ox­
ford Nanopore Technologies Sequence data. This enabled an in depth analysis of the
gene synteny not possible if only the Illumina NextSeq data had been used. The con­
text catB3 was found in most often is in a gene cassette together with blaOXA­1 and
aac(6’)­Ib­cr, this could indicate that co­selection play a role in spread. The most often
observed version of catB3 is the same truncated variant also identified within the same
gene cassette.
Extensive description of CmR distribution and species:gene correlations also in con­
text of therapeutic use. Analysis of gene synteny and speculations on likely modes of
spread.

Introduction
In 2014 a global estimate of the future burden of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) was
made by a Review team summoned by the UK Prime Minister. This estimate was,
that 10 million deaths per year in 2050 would be attributable to AMR[1]. This is more
deaths than caused by the highest cause of deaths in 2019, which was ischaemic heart
disease[2].
The burden of AMR deaths is not equally distributed. The lowest estimated fre­
quency of deaths attributable to AMR in 2019 was in Australia and New Zealand. Here
the estimate was 6.5 deaths per 100.000 people.
The same year 27.3 deaths per
100.000 people was estimated in Western Sub­Saharan Africa, which was the highest
estimate of any region. The higher frequency in Western Sub­Saharan Africa is partly
due to the fact, that there is a higher overall number of infections[3]. This burden will
likely continue to be unevenly distributed. By the Review team summoned by the UK
Prime Minister the frequency of deaths attributable to AMR in each region in 2050 also
was estimated. It is expected that the frequency will increase to more than 100 deaths
per 100.000 people in Africa. While an increase to ∼50 deaths per 100.000 people in
Europe, Northern America and Oceania is expected[1]. Though the exact estimate of
has been questioned[4], an increase in the estimated yearly deaths attributable to AMR
has already been observed from 700.000 in 2014[1] to 1.270.000 in 2019[3].
It is clearly a growing concern and has been included as one of the top 10 human
health threats by the World Health Organisation (WHO)[5].
An important part of combating the spread of AMR is surveillance[6]. In 2015 WHO
initiated the Global Antimicrobial Resistance and Use Surveillance System (GLASS). At
the time of the last publicised GLASS report in 2021, 109 countries and territories were
participating[7]. This also shows a political interest in tackling this problem.
1.1
Surveillance of Antimicrobial Resistance
To inhibit spread and combat some of the difficulties caused by bacterial infections and
AMR, knowledge of the current state of AMR and observations of the developments
occurring over time are needed. In turn, proper surveillance systems are required for
the generation of these insights[6].
Currently many different surveillance systems are in place all over the world. How­
ever, the majority are in Europe and North America[8].
Furthermore, monitoring is
mostly of specific species and phenotypes[8], which causes the risk of emerging species
and phenotypes going undetected. Another issue is the comparability of these surveil­
lance systems. Since international standards of sampling, testing and reporting have
not been established, it is difficult to compare and compile[8].
One way of standardization is to use whole genome sequencing (WGS). Genome
sequences from all over the world can be compared and the developments within the
field makes this approach promising[9]. WGS alone is however currently not considered

sufficient as a surveillance method by WHO[10]. GLASS is also an attempt to standard­
ise AMR surveillance. Some of its current limitations are uneven distribution of sampling
sites and volumes and that there no requirements to quality of the surveillance data[11].
With an increasing number of participating countries and territories and developments
in the individual surveillance systems these limitations may be mitigated in the future.
In surveillance ”One Health” is an important concept to consider. The spread of mi­
croorganisms and AMR between humans, animals and the environment is constant. It
is therefore necessary to look into developments in all these areas to get the full picture.
1.2
Spread of Antimicrobial Resistance Genes
AMR is classified as either intrinsic or acquired. Intrinsic resistance is when a certain
species is always resistant to a specific antimicrobial. This can be due to the antimicro­
bial being unable to enter the cell, not having a target or that resistance genes are en­
coded within the core genome. Acquired resistance is when a microorganism becomes
resistant due to either uptake of resistance genes or by mutations in the genome. Only
acquired resistance genes are analysed in this project.
The acquired resistance genes are spread via horizontal gene transfer. Transfer be­
tween cells are accomplished by either transformation as uptake of naked DNA, trans­
duction as spread by bacteriophages or by conjugation of plasmids or integrative co­
jugative elements. Mobility within the cell is also a contributor of AMR spread. Insertion
sequences, transposons and integrons are important for mobility within or between DNA
molecules. These can also be combined in structures such as composite transposons
moving around gene cassettes. These are all known to play a crucial role in spread
of acquired AMR genes[12]. These mobile genetic element are also often difficult to
assemble. This is especially observed for short read assembly, where the repetitive
regions within these elements are longer than the insertion size of the sequencing[12].
1.3
Selection for Antimicrobial Resistance
The fundamental basis for spread of AMR is selection. When considering selection,
a few basic concepts should be mentioned. Selection of a certain phenotype usually
involves an advantage of having that phenotype ­ this is commonly known as natural
selection[13]. In the case of AMT, use of antimicrobials are a way of selecting for the
bacteria that are resistant to the antimicrobials, since these have a phenotypic advan­
tage. However, some of the AMR genes confer more than one phenotype, for instance
promiscuous efflux pumps, and will therefore be under selective pressure by use of all
these antimicrobials. Additionally, co­selection is the selection of multiple genes when
one is under selective pressure. This is often seen for genes on the same mobile ge­
netic element such as on the same plasmid.

1.4
Chloramphenicol
One antimicrobial which has been used extensively, in a historical perspective, in both
food­producing animals and in humans is Chloramphenicol (Cm). Cm was discovered
in 1947 as a product of a soil bacteria later identified as Streptomyces venezuelae[14].
In 1949 it became the first antibiotic to be synthetically produced[15].
Cm is a broad­spectrum antibiotic that inhibits protein synthesis by binding to the
50S ribosomal subunit and inhibiting the growth of the peptide chain. It easily spreads
throughout the body and is able to reach most tissues, importantly it is efficient at cross­
ing the blood­brain barrier[16, 17]. Due to its high efficiency and low cost it has been
widely used as treatment for a variety of bacterial infections in humans, as well as in ma­
jor food­producing animals[18]. Unfortunately, severe adverse events were observed
soon after the drug was discovered. Both acute and chronic toxic effects have been
associated with Cm use, a few examples are aplastic anaemia, and gray syndrome[16].
In most countries use of Cm is now under some sort of restriction. In 1994 Cm was
banned for use in food­producing animals in the EU, USA, Canada, Japan, China and
Australia[19]. This ban came as a consequence of the risk of toxicity and suspected
carcinogenicity in the consumer and not as an attempt to limit spread of Chlorampheni­
col Resistance (CmR)[19]. This is also evident by studies on Cm use focusing on the
presence of Cm in the food products rather than the presence of CmR in bacteria[20,
21, 22].
Cm is, however, still in use in some low income countries, where safer alternatives
are not available due to cost[16, 17]. It has been shown to be used illegally in Pakistan,
where it was identified in many different food products of animal origin[20]. Furthermore,
cross over use in poultry as been seen in Uganda[21]. And even though use in food
producing animals is not approved in Senegal it is identified in milk[22].
In both Uganda, Pakistan and Senegal the use in production animals are restricted,
but since it is cheap and available, it is used. These are just a few examples of continued
use in countries where restrictions are in place.
In Denmark Cm is only approved in drugs for ocular administration towards infec­
tions in the eyes such as conjunctivitis[23]. Additionally to ocular administrated drugs,
only one drug for injection remains in use in USA though others have been approved by
the FDA[24, 25, 26].
1.4.1
Infections Treated with Cm
Cm is mainly used to treat conjunctivitis, which is most commonly caused by strepto­
coccus pneumoniae, staphylococcus aureus or Chlamydia trachomatis, depending on
age. In Denmark the recommendation for bacterial conjunctivitis requiring treatment, is
to use eye drops containing Cm unless the causative agent is Chlamydia trachomatis
or there are known allergies[27]
In a draft report on use of antimicrobials from 18th November 2021 the WHO rec­
ommends use of Cm to treat bacterial meningitis if no other option are available. This is
the only indication for Cm use in this report[28], and the only reason that Cm remains on

the list of essential medicines in 2021[29]. Bacterial meningitis is most often caused by
Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae or Neisseria meningitidis[30, 31].
Cm is known to be used in countries where cost is a major issue and where self­

1.5
Chloramphenicol Resistance
Both acquired resistance genes and point mutations have been found to confer chlo­
ramphenicol resistance (CmR). As mentioned only acquired resistance genes are ex­
amined in this study and focus will therefore be on these. The two main mechanisms for
CmR genes are modification of Cm and efflux pumps[34]. Most often the modification
is caused by acetylation by a Chloramphenicol Acetyl Transferase (cat). The added
acetyl group inhibits the binding to the ribosome thereby inhibiting the action of Cm.
Both specific and multidrug efflux pumps affecting Cm have been identified[34].
Many CmR genes are usually found on mobile elements though some of the cat
genes, catB1,catB7 and catB9 are generally chromosomally integrated in P. aerugi­
nosa, Agrobacterium tumefaciens and Vibrio cholerae respectively[34].
1.5.1
Chloramphenicol Acetyl Transferase B3
The gene catB3 was first described in 1991 where it was discovered in Salmonella ty­
phimurium[35]. It belongs to the type B cat genes that are structurally different from
the type A cat genes, but have the same function. It was first identified on an integron
in a gene cassette with other AMR genes. In this study ResFinder version 4.1 is used
to identify resistance genes, which is described separately in section 2.2 page 8. Res­
Finder has two variants of the catB3 gene included in the database. The second variant
was identified in 1995 in Klebsiella aerogenes, previously known as Enterobactor aero­
genes in 1995[36].
Since its discovery in the 90’s catB3 has been identified in different contexts both
species wise and in varying genetic context. The catB3 is specific for gram­negative
bacteria, and has been seen in a variety of Enterobactriaceae[37, 38], but also in for
instance Pseudomonas aeruginosa[39]. Predominantly catB3 has been described in
some sort of mobile element in a gene cassette with other resistance genes[35, 36, 39,
37], often a class­1 integron[37, 39].
1.6
Second and Third Generation Sequencing
The type of data used in this project is WGS data. When working with genetic data the
type of sequencing is important to consider. All samples in the TWIW collection have
previous to this project been sequenced using Illumina NextSeq, further description in
section 2.1. This next generation sequencing (NGS) method was used to generate
paired end reads of 150 base pairs (bp) each. Albeit short, the accuracy of these reads
Context of Antimicrobial Resistance Genes
4
==PAGE:12==
is relatively good with an error rate at ∼0.59%[40]. The result of assembling the raw
reads is a number of contigs in fasta format. When studying the gene synteny, which
is a part of this thesis, long assembled regions are required. This is not always possi­
ble to ascertain from short­read sequencing, especially for genomes containing certain
types mobile genetic elements[12]. Oxford Nanopore Technology (ONT) sequencing
was therefore used to complement Illumina sequencing in order to generate long as­
semblies allowing for gene synteny studies of catB3. ONT sequencing is a third gen­
eration sequencing method. It results in long reads, however with a lower accuracy
than Illumnina sequencing. In october 2021 a study published by C. Delahaye et al.[41]
showed an error rate at ∼6% depending on species. Further developments in both flow
cells and basecallers since this study are likely to have decreased the error rate.
Each of the methods have their own advantages depending on the which type of
research is being done. While Illumina sequencing results in short contigs with high ac­
curacy, ONT sequencing results in long contigs with lower accuracy. Combining these
two methods in a hybrid assembly results in long pieces called scaffolds, sometimes
with completely covered chromosomes and plasmids, with an overall high accuracy.
More information on how this was done in section 2.3 page 9. Throughout the report all
pieces of DNA that have been assembled will be called contigs.
1.7
Two Weeks in the World (TWIW) Project
TWIW is a global research project undertaken by the group for Genomic Epidemiology
at the National Food Institute, Technical University of Denmark (DTU). One of the main
scopes of TWIW is to create a dataset of clinically relevant samples, that can shed light
on distribution and phylogeny of pathogens and AMR­conferring genes, and potentially
be used in surveillance efforts. This database is a collection of clinically obtained iso­
lates from partners around the world. At the current state partners in 35 countries have
contributed, see figure 1.1. Samples were collected over the course of a week, some­
where between March and December 2020[42].
This thesis is a part of TWIW, and the emphasis is on CmR. The purpose of this
project was to generate insights regarding the prevalence and distribution of CmR genes,
in a geographical and species­related context. This was done by 1. creating an overview
of the global distribution of identified CmR genes, 2. creating an overview of the species:gene
correlations for CmR genes. The 3. purpose was to generate insights of relevance to
the global spread of the geographically most abundant CmR gene, namely catB3. This
was done by examining catB3 itself and its surrounding genetic context, also known as
its gene synteny. Lastly the differences in results attained by use of different sequencing
technologies were evaluated.

2
Materials and Methods
The project workflow is illustrated in figure 2.1. The first steps were performed on the
Illumina­based WGS data of the TWIW dataset. The first task was to identify resistance
genes using ResFinder v. 4.1[43]. The distribution of CmR genes was subsequently put
into context of species and geography. This allowed for identification of catB3 as the
most prevalent CmR gene with a specificity toward Cm in the TWIW dataset, with a wide
geographic and species­distribution. The catB3 gene synteny was examined through
phylogenetic analysis of the flanking regions neighbouring catB3. The isolates contain­
ing catB3 were divided into synteny types to describe the variation of gene synteny.
A total of 29 isolates were selected for ONT sequencing, based on unique geog­
raphy, species and AMR profile. The sequence data from both Illumina and ONT was
combined in a hybrid assembly using Unicycler[44]. The resistance genes were again
identified using ResFinder. Phylogenetic analysis of the genetic context of catB3 based
on a larger flanking area was now performed utilizing the longer contigs in the hybrid
assembly. In the end some of the differences between the two types of assemblies was
described.
2.1
Previous work
Previous to this project samples had been collected and sequenced with Illumina NextSeq
and run through the FoodQCPipeline[45]. In this pipeline FastQC is first run on the data.
followed by trimming with bbduk2, then an additional run of FastQC on trimmed data.
Subsequently, the reads are mapped to a bacterial database and in the end assembled
using SPAdes.
The validated genomes used in this project consist of those with fewer than 500 con­
tigs in the SPAdes assembly, a minimum N50 of the SPAdes assembly of 15,000 bp,
and validated species identification from rMLST[46] and KmerFinder[47, 48, 49] includ­
ing a final assembly size corresponding to the identified species. If a species could not
be concluded it was assigned ”NULL”. The validations were carried out using a script
created by Judit Szarvas. For the analysis performed in this thesis, both the assembled
genomes, the trimmed reads, the concluded species and meta data on sampling loca­
tion were used.

2.2
ResFinder version 4.1
ResFinder[43] is a program used to identify AMR genes and to assign a predicted phe­
notypes based on these genes. The output from ResFinder was then summarized using

a script created by Frederik Duus Møller. ResFinder searches for specific genes known
to cause AMR from a database. The run were performed on assemblies. Since intrinsic
resistance was not a scope of this study the species was set as ”other”. This results
in all genes in the database being included even if they are intrinsic in some species
and that point mutations are not identified. The CmR genes included in the ResFinder
database are presented in table 2.1
2.3
Unicycler version 0.4.9
To create an assembly utilizing both the Illumina and the ONT data Unicycler[44] was
used. In all cases default settings were used.
The main steps of unicycler are presented in figure 2.2 First, a short read assembly is
performed using SPAdes, where the optimal k­mer size is estimated based on getting as
few contigs as possible, while having as few dead ends as possible. Eliminating dead
ends is usually not as high a priority when only Illumina reads are being assembled,
but dead ends decrease the ability to do long read bridging. Secondly, copy numbers
are assigned to each contig. Then short read bridging is performed between single
copy contigs. At this point the long reads are included and used for another round of
bridging, this will especially help in resolving repeat regions. These bridges are then as­
signed a quality score based on how much of the data supports them. The bridges with
the highest quality are assigned to the graph first continuously adding bridges of lower
and lower quality. In the end the contigs are merged and the sequence is polished with
Bow­Tie and Pilon to remove mismatches utilizing the high quality of the short reads.[44]
2.4
Flankenstein
Flankenstein (created by Patrick Munk[50]) was used to examine the flanking regions of
the catB3 gene. The input was the blast file output from ResFinder specifying which con­
tig and where the gene was found and a fasta file containing the contigs. Flankenstien
excludes all contigs where the flanking regions are shorter than the specified length and
they are there not part of the further analyses. The part of the output that was used,
was a distance matrix of the flanking regions of the specified gene in this case catB3.

Chloramphenicol Resistance Genes in Each Species
Using the bioinformatically concluded species and the ResFinder output, the number

3.4
Oxford Nanopore Technologies Sequencing
Only a selection of the samples were ONT sequenced. First it was decided only to
sequence samples with 100% identity catB3 and not catB3∆It was decided to focus
on the full length (and CmR­conferring) variant due to limited resources and due to a
seemingly more varied phylogeny amongst the catB3 subset. The second step was
to exclude the 23 samples where only the DNA and not the isolate was available. To
choose which of the remaining 34 samples to sequence, they were categorized based on
country, species and AMR profile. If more than one sample were identical on all these
factors, only one of them was randomly chosen to be sequenced. The AMR profiles
were based on ”barcodes” created from hits from ResFinder. For detail on these see
Appendix 5. The categorization is presented in table 3.4 together with sequencing status
and reasons for not sequencing specific samples.
Before assembly the raw reads from the sequencing were run through KmerFinder.
This was done to identify if contamination had occurred. The results are summarized
in Appendix 5, but the main take away is that the identified species are the same as for
the Illumina sequencing.

4
Discussion
The general findings of this thesis are that CmR genes are identified globally in the
TWIW dataset. The geographic distributions of the individual genes varies and could de­
pendent on different factors. One of these factors is the species dependencies observed
for certain genes. In the analyses of gene the synteny of catB3 it has been revealed that
the context is very similar when looking at a small area surrounding catB3 but relatively
large when expanding this area. The main point that will be further discussed are 1. how
resistance and usage seem to correspond and how the observed resistance may affect
treatment with Cm, 2. how the gene synteny catB3 is and how this effects selection and
spread and 3. how the results differ depending on which sequencing technology is used
and how these differences could be further analysed.
4.1
Chloramphenicol Resistance and Usage
The results summarised in 3.1, indicate that there is some correlation between high
Cm usage and high occurrence of CmR genes. However, data on the use in topical
infections, self­medicating and in animal production is in many cases lacking, which
may be a factor contributing to the discordance between estimated use and observed
CmR gene prevalence. In addition to this, spread seems for some of the genes to be
dependent on other factors than Cm usage.
When looking into the specific CmR genes it is clear, that especially some of the
genes are affected by something else as well. In some cases, such as mentioned for
catB7, there is a species bias. It is also unknown if the high concentration of cmlA1 and
catA2 in Mozambique is due too a high usage, since no estimated usage is available.
However, since the other CmR genes are not found at similar high degree in Mozam­
bique there is probably some other mechanism at play.
Some countries where the estimated usage is 0 (figure 3.6 page 19) do use Cm
to treat conjunctivitis. This is the case for Denmark and could be one of the effects
contributing to the presence of CmR genes in countries estimated to not use Cm.
Of the ten CmR genes described in section 3.2.2 three are also involved in other
types of resistance than CmR. These are the efflux pumps oqxA, oqxB and floR. The
use of other antimicrobials will have large impact on selection of these. Their distribution
is therefore effected by the use of all antimicrobials causing selective pressures for the
individual genes. The remaining seven genes are specific to Cm. Their distribution
would not initially be considered dependent on use of other antimicrobials. For catA1
figure 3.16 and catB3 figure 3.18 the distribution fit quite well with Cm usage with a high
concentration in Ecuador, and West Africa and low concentrations in Canada, Oceania
and most of Europe.
A country that really stands out is Algeria.
One explanation could be that self­
medicating with antimicrobials is practiced in Algeria[60]. Use in animals for food pro­
duction could also be an explanation. Data on chloramphenicol used in self­medicating
or in animals in Algeria have not been found. Studies on this may exist but are not

4.1.1
CmR Affecting Treatment with Cm
Even though Cm is no longer as widely used, it is still important in some circumstances
and remains on WHO’s list of essential medicines. Many of the infection types where Cm
is recommended as treatment, are caused by the the gram­positive bacteria S. aureus
and S. pneumoniae. And as described in section 3.1 there are not many observations
of CmR genes in gram­positives in the TWIW dataset. Furthermore, catB3 is specific
to gram­negatives and the observations concerning selection of catB3 may not reflect
CmR spread in gram­positive species. The two gram­negative species H. influenzae
and N. meningitidis are not well represented in the dataset. The final dataset contains
only 11 samples of H. influenzae and none of N. meningitidis. The fastidious and mi­
croaerophilic species represent less than 1% of all the samples in the dataset (data from
TWIW, not shown). Furthermore, of those received only isolates from Europe were vi­
able. Therefore, the fact that CmR genes are not observed in these does not indicate
anything.
4.2
Gene Synteny and Variation of catB3
The truncated variant of catB3 is quite noteworthy. The question is, why a gene that
no longer provides CmR is spread at such a high level? An initial thought was, that
Cm was used commonly in the past and that catB3 was spread all over the world while
the selective pressure was maintained. The truncations would then have occurred when
CmR were no longer selected for. This does however not explain why all the catB3∆are
identical. If the variation was due too CmR no longer being needed and the preservation
of the gene therefore disappeared, more different non­functioning variants should be
observed. Two main hypotheses explaining what is actually observed are:
1. catB3∆is co­selected with some of the surrounding genes.
2. catB3∆has some other unknown function.

These hypotheses are not mutually exclusive. As described in section 3.3.2 the syn­
teny of catB3 and catB3∆are in some ways similar and is very conserved for especially
catB3∆. In 84% of cases catB3∆is identified within the same gene cassette and for the
remaining it cannot be excluded that they are as well. This very strong association be­
tween catB3∆, blaOXA­1 and aac(6’Ib­cr) could indicate that catB3∆is co­selected and
this is why it is spread. Though it was not possible to confirm with the Illumina contigs in
this study, this gene cassette has been observed surrounded by IS26 sequences[57].
This could explain how catB3∆is moved between the various species all over the world.
What the first hypothesis does not explain is why there are no random point mu­
tations in the observed catB3∆. If catB3∆really does not have any function at all,
random replication errors would be expected. So not only is the truncated version very
widespread, but it is also completely conserved. Usually when a genetic area is con­
served it is because it has some important function. This could in this case both be a
function of the gene product itself, but also in regulation or mobility. Further analyses of
the function of catB3∆is needed to reveal what its role is, if it has one.
A bias to consider with this second hypothesis is that it is the full length catB3 that
is searched for with ResFinder. This means that the expected variants of catB3∆with
point mutations may actually be present, but they are too unlike the full length catB3
and are therefore not recognized. A first step would therefore be to look for these in the
TWIW database to confirm if they exist or not.
The gene synteny of catB3 is a bit more variable than catB3∆. However, 79% of the
catB3 cases are also within the same gene cassette. Unlike for catB3∆it is possible for
most of the remaining 21% to determine that they are not within this gene cassette.
4.3
How is catB3 Spread?
The gene cassette most often observed in this study, has been described previously[38,
57, 61, 37, 58, 59]. In two of the cases it was the catB3∆variant that were found[57,
61]. In both these cases the gene cassette was associated with IS26, which in one case
was mentioned to have made assembly difficult[57]. This could explain why most of the
contigs containing catB3∆are very short and that many are approximately the same
size.
The types of resistance the genes in this cassette causes are according to ResFinder:
1. Beta­lactam resistance: blaOXA­1
2. Aminoglycoside resistance: aac(6’)­Ib­cr
3. Chloramphenicol resistance: catB3
In some cases the Rifampicin resistance gene ARR­3 was also found within this
gene cassette[37, 58, 59].
Even though most of the studies where catB3 has been identified are not specifically
trying to describe the context of catB3, the gene cassettes most often identified in this
study, seem to be well described. As it was observed in this study, assembly of the

flanking regions of catB3 is difficult and often impossible when working only with Illumina
Nextseq reads. This is likely why the broader gene synteny of catB3 has not been as well
described. From utilizing the combined possibilities of Illumina and ONT sequencing it
has in this thesis been possible to show that the genetic variation of the context of catB3
is limited when only looking at small flanking regions, but increases when looking at
longer flanking regions. This clearly indicates that catB3 almost exclusively is present
within the described gene cassette and possibly even within the same integron, but

or geographical location. The majority of the contigs containing catB3 in the hybrid

Some of them may be the same plasmid, where the gene cassette has been moved to
a different location. This would most often result in very variable flanking regions. To
further examine how this gene cassette is spread, the plasmid types and mobile genetic
elements in the hybrid assemblies should be analysed.
The observations of varied context are important in explaining how catB3 spreads.
From the results in this thesis it does not seem to be spreading within in very similar
species or on a particular plasmid. It is rather a specific short mobile genetic element that
is moved around on different plasmids in different species all over the world. Similarities
between the genetic elements in this study and previously described mobile elements,
highly indicates that catB3 is mainly spread within a few very similar gene cassettes in
a class 1 integron surrounded by IS26 insertion sequences[59].
When trying to predict spread of AMR, spread of mobile genetic elements makes
predictions much more difficult. Even though a lot of research has revealed some of the
mechanisms there is still a lot to learn before these structures are fully understood[12].
Knowledge is however the basis for understanding, predicting and inhibiting the spread
of AMR. The results obtained in this thesis also show that co­selection has a large role
in spread of resistance toward rarely used antimicrobials. If a bacteria has catB3 it is
very likely to also have blaOXA­1 and aac(6’)­Ib­cr. To take these mobile elements into
account in surveillance it could be considered to monitor the elements in datasets such
as the TWIW, that not species specific, since they easily jump from one species to the
other.
4.4
ONT Sequencing and Unicycler
An explanation for the extra copy of catB3 in the hybrid assembly could be the lim­
itation of the Illumina reads to assemble across mobile genetic elements. This should
however, result in a higher depth of coverage of this contig compared to the other con­
tigs covering the same plasmid. A ”true” presence of multiple identical genetic elements
should result in an accordingly high read depth of this element if it was only assembled
once based on the Illumina reads. Illumina­based contigs corresponding to different
places on the hybrid contigs containing catB3, were identified by presence of the same
resistance genes.

The coverage of the Illumina­based contigs were compared for different positions
on the plasmid (data not shown). This did not result in any conclusive observations,
possibly due to some of the other identified resistance genes being part of other mobile
genetic elements which may also be occurring multiple times in an isolate.
Overall ONT sequencing has the advantage of creating longer contigs, which makes
it possible to look at a much broader genetic range, especially in mobile genetic ele­
ment with repetitive regions that cannot be assembled using only Illumina reads. [12].
The quality of the reads are getting better, but for the current state Illumina sequences
are of noticeably higher quality. Unicyler did perform well in utilizing the qualities of both
methods, however some problems were experienced as described in section 3.4.3. Un­
derstanding what is causing this is important for further analyses of gene synteny. If the
hybrid assemblies are not assembling correctly this will have a huge influence on the
results. It is furthermore important to be aware that certain genes may be overlooked
when using a hybrid assembly. In this case the gene synteny could ofcourse not be
examined in the assemblies where catB3 disappeared. Understanding mechanisms
behind could maybe enable prevention in the future.
A couple of hypotheses to why these differences occurred are:
1. The hybrid assembly is correct and two catB3 genes are present on different plas­
mids. In the Illumina­based assembly this is not identified due to the repetitive
IS26 surrounding the gene cassettes.
2. The disappearance of catB3 is due too the weight on limiting the number of dead
ends in the initial step of assembly.
Based on the observations of surrounding genes (table 3.5 page 39) and the knowl­
edge from the previous studies on the gene cassette[59], the first hypothesis seems
plausible. There is however not definitive proof in this theses. The first way to test this
hypothesis would be to look for IS26 in the hybrid assemblies.
The second analysis would be to examine if the placement of the gene cassette on
both plasmids in the hybrid assembly is supported by the Illumina data. The Illumina
reads are expected to cover the whole genome including the areas not covered in the
Illumina­based assembly. Mapping the Illumina reads to the hybrid assembly could
reveal if the Illumina data supports the area surrounding the Illumina­based contig in
the hybrid assembly. If the hypothesis is true the illumina reads will cover this area,
even though it was not possible to assemble it.
The reasoning behind the second hypothesis is that the Illumina data is used as
starting material for both assemblies. This data covers the area with catB3. Therefore
the differences in the output assemblies must be due too differences in how this data is
utilized. The first step of Unicycler is to assemble the short reads, if this was done in ex­
actly the same way as the Illumina assembly, we would see catB3 in the final assembly.
So what are the differences of these two short read assemblies? Both are performed
using SPAdes, but the weight on limiting dead ends is higher in the hybrid assembly than
in the Illumina assembly. Therefore this hypothesis seems reasonable. A consequence

5
Conclusions
Throughout this report CmR genes have extensively been examined. This was done on
a global dataset of clinically relevant bacterial isolated, collected in 2020. An overview of
the global distribution of CmR genes has been created, with both an overall distribution
of all CmR­conferring genes, the distribution of each gene in each region and the ge­
ographic distribution of selected CmR genes. Furthermore, species:gene correlations
have been described. Lastly the gene synteny of catB3 has been analysed in depth
utilizing a combination of Illumina and ONT sequence data. This has revealed insights
into a mechanism for global spread of catB3.
Based on the data presented in this report, CmR genes are widespread, and found
all over the world. Though the number of samples collected from a country has an
effect on the amount of observed CmR, the data indicates that Cm usage does as well.
Most CmR genes are present at low numbers and only few are found all over the world.
There are differences in the distributions of the different CmR genes that could indicate
different selective pressures and mechanisms of spread that are not only dependent on
Cm usage.
The species:gene correlations shows that a few of the CmR genes are species or
genus specific or have a preference for certain species, but mostly they are found in a
wide selection of different species. There is however a much higher occurrence in gram
negatives than positives and especially in Klebsiella spp..
The predominant Cm­specific CmR gene observed in the TWIW dataset, is catB3.
The mechanisms behind the spread of catB3 are not yet completely known. However
spread within a gene cassette together with blaOXA­1 and aac(6’)­Ib­cr seem to be the
main vehicle. This gene cassette is placed within variable contexts that are not species
or geography dependent. The wide distributed of catB3∆, could indicate spread due to
co­selection with one or both of the other genes in the gene cassette. This gene does
not confer CmR and therefore is not selected for by Cm usage. It is however found in
a very similar gene cassette to catB3 and is therefore expected to be under the same
co­selective pressures.
Furthermore, it has been shown that different sequencing and assembly methods
give rise to different data outputs and that limitations should be examined further in
order to be fully understood.
This thesis has also illustrated that the TWIW dataset can be used to examine both
broad questions such as general geographical species distributions of certain predicted
phenotypes, but also to analyse more narrow questions such as gene synteny, though
use of ONT may be necessary depending on the gene.

Lysis

2.	 Bind

d.	 Incubate the plate for 5 minutes at room temperature
