Abstract  
Capsule coating formulations using a spray coating system has been assessed for this project. 
Thorough assessment of current formulations with subsequent optimization has been performed, 
including assessing the weight gain to determine the impact on in vitro dissolution of model drugs 
and devices. EUDRAGIT® polymers have been used for the coating system. This coating allows 
enteric delivery of an oral device, which is a self-unfolding foil based on PDMS ensuring a 
unidirectional drug release in close proximity to the intestinal tract [2]. 
 
Several disintegration and drug release studies of the enteric coated capsules enclosing the self-
unfolding foil have been carried out and assessed. In vitro drug release profiles of acetaminophen 
loaded into enteric coated capsules and banded with HPMC-AS around the joining area of the shells 
demonstrated almost fully protection at gastric pH 2 for 2 h, and subsequently foil unfolding upon 
capsule disintegration at small intestinal pH 7.4 with a weight gain of 50%. Most results obtained 
using the µDISS Profiler™ were found to be within the acceptable 10% limit of drug release in HCl 
within 2 h [46]. Enteric coated capsules enclosing a self-unfolding foil showed better protection in 
gastric pH and subsequently small intestinal pH compared to capsules without the self-unfolding foil 
loaded. This is possibly due to a protection of the joining area of the capsule, as the foil works as a 
sealing and thus protection of media entering the capsule.

1 Introduction  
Drugs that are poorly stable in an acidic environment or that would be susceptible to degradation by 
pepsin, which is active at an acidic environment, must be protected when they pass through the low 
pH environment of the stomach [3]. Current strategies for effective oral drug delivery include coating 
and encapsulation. Capsule dissolution characteristics are crucial to investigate, as it affects the drug 
release profile, bioavailability, and therapeutic effectiveness [3]. The EUDRAGIT® portfolio of 
functional polymers for oral solid dosage forms helps protect the active pharmaceutical ingredient 
(API) of a drug. EUDRAGIT® polymers are pH sensitive and present sustained release, thereby 
allowing the drug product to transit the acidic environment of the stomach and release its content at 
gastrointestinal pH [4]. Commercially, oral versions of injectable peptides also allow new intellectual 
property (IP) to be generated on new formulations and provide competitive benefits [5]. 
 
For this project, a foil is folded and loaded into an enteric coated capsule. It is made of an elastomeric 
material and has the potential of increasing drug absorption. Coating the capsule in which the foil is 
loaded with a pH sensitive polymer prevents it from disintegrating until it reaches the small intestine 
[6]. The design behind the delivery concept of the self-unfolding foil (SUF) is shown on Figure 1 
below.

Foils used for this project are based on polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS). It is a silicone polymer widely 
used in several areas of the industry, from cosmetics (such as shampoo) to food and beverages where 
they are sometimes found as a food additive. PDMS is optically clear, chemically inert, thermally 
stable, and non-toxic [7]. As PDMS is biocompatible, its use is found in many areas of the industry 
including the pharmaceutical industry, where it is well-suited for engineering applications [8].  
 
However, coating on its own would not be sufficient for this project. The model drug for this project 
is acetaminophen, however, ultimately the drug for this project is a therapeutic peptide such as insulin, 
which has a much higher molecular weight (MW). The high MW makes it difficult for insulin to be 
absorbed in the small intestine. Oral delivery of peptides of this size is practically impossible without 
the use of permeation enhancers. So even if insulin reaches the small intestine intact, it might not be 
absorbed in sufficient concentrations to produce the required therapeutic effect. [9]. Permeation 
enhancers are widely used strategies for improving oral delivery of therapeutic peptides [10]. 
However, this aspect belongs to the later stages of this project and will not be investigated for now 
but should be when the concept of enteric coating SUFs loaded into enteric coated capsules has been 
validated. 
 
1.1 Oral drug delivery  
Several administration routes exist, including oral, intravenous, intranasal, intradermal among others. 
However, the oral route is generally preferred over other routes given that oral formulations result in 
easier and more convenient administration, thus greater patient compliance [3]. It is estimated that a 
considerable percentage of the population suffers from needle phobia [11]. Oral delivery does not 
require any specialized training or equipment (such as syringes or needles). Instead, patients can 
safely administer their medication at home, cutting down on trips to the doctor’s office. These benefits 
are advantages to both doctors and patients, increasing compliance, and ultimately, the likelihood of 
a successful drug therapy.  
 
Despite the apparent advantages, oral drug delivery remains challenging. Orally administered drugs 
face several physical and chemical barriers in the stomach, mucus layer and degradation by 
proteolytic enzymes in the intestine, resulting in low bioavailability [9]. As orally administered drugs 
pass through the gastrointestinal tract (GIT), the pH changes quite drastically from a highly acidic 
environment in the stomach (pH ~2) to a more alkaline environment with a pH of ~6 in the duodenum

approved in 2020, and Lupkynis® capsules (1215 Da) approved in 2021. However, there are currently 
very few FDA approved oral peptide formulations for systemic delivery as they come with low and

physiological processes that take place. Secretion is the transport of fluids, electrolytes, peptides, and 
protein (such as saliva, gastric acid, pancreatic secretions etc.) to the lumen. Digestion is the 
breakdown of food into smaller structures, as a preparation for absorption. Drug absorption can occur 
through different mechanisms, including trans- or paracellular passive diffusion, active transport, and

permeability and solubility of drugs and their dissolution rate. If both the solubility and permeability

is also responsible for low bioavailability of a drug. In contrast to proteases that present a chemical 
barrier, the mucus is responsible for a physical barrier[9]. 
 
1.2 Drug absorption in the small intestine  
The small intestine is ~6 meters long, and is divided into the duodenum, jejunum, and ilium. What is 
so special about the small intestine is its absorptive ability, due to its large surface area (more than 
200 m2), which is possible as the intestinal mucosa is folded into the lumen and covered in finger-
like structures of ~1 mm in length, called villi. Additionally, each villus is covered by microvilli of 
~1 µm known as the intestinal brush border, creating this large surface area as illustrated on Figure 3 
below.  
 
Figure 3. The three levels of complexity found in the small intestine. These include the circular folds lining the small 
intestine. These folds are rich in villi. Each villus contains absorptive cells, which has microvilli, creating a large surface 
area [14]. 
 
The first area of the small intestine, the duodenum, is where gastric content access first, and also 
where many prodrugs are enzymatically hydrolysed to their active form. The API may likewise also 
be target for enzymes, and therefore challenging to be administered by the oral route. As we go 
further, we have the jejunum and the ilium with a gradual increase in pH, and a decreasing diameter

1.3 Polymer coating  
Enteric coating material are polymers applied to solid oral dosage forms (such as tablets or capsules) 
to delay the release of drugs to the lower regions of the GIT. In the case of small intestinal drug 
targeting, delivery is highly dependent on the use of such polymer coatings to survive the pH 
conditions in the stomach. These formulations have in the past few years gained a growing attention 
and popularity due to their localized targeting of drugs to treat diseases. Currently, several different 
enteric coating systems exist, including the pH dependent EUDRAGIT® S coatings. EUDRAGIT® 
S coating formulations are designed to dissolve in media of pH >7.0 [15], [16]. The most popular 
enteric coatings use pH-dependent polymers that contain carboxylic groups. These are not ionized in 
the low pH of the stomach and become ionized in the higher pH conditions of the small intestine, 
allowing the dissolution of the coating and the drug to be released [17]. The polymer is resistant to 
the low pH of the stomach because the carboxylic groups ionize in aqueous solutions at pH 5.5 and 
higher [18]. 
 
The success criteria for the polymer are to be sufficiently chemically resistant to survive the acidic 
environment of the stomach and to release its active ingredient quickly after in the intestinal media. 
The aim was to investigate the minimum amount of coating needed for the capsules to act in this 
manner. Many experiments were conducted to find the required weight gain of enteric coating to 
apply to the capsules. Literature has been found to state that 20% weight gain with EUDRAGIT® L 
30 D-55 is enough to achieve protection in stomach pH and subsequent release in small intestinal pH. 
One example is a similar unfolding device concept paper from S. Berg et al. where they used a mini 
glatt to coat a capsule. They also use Eudragit L 30 D 55 as the polymer coating [19]. In another 
interesting paper from A. K. Dhalla et al. the caleva mini coater was used for enteric coating of the 
capsule comprising a device. The therapeutic drug was delivered upon inflation of a balloon folded 
within the capsule and injects a needle into the wall of the small intestine. They also use Eudragit L 
30 D 55, and the caleva mini coater [20]. In another interesting paper from Abramson A. et al an 
unfolding microneedle injector for oral delivery of macromolecules was developed. The capsule was 
coated with Eudragit L 100-55 and Eudragit S100. When the coating reaches the small intestine, the

1.4 Biopharmaceutics classification system   
According to the World Health Organization, the biopharmaceutics classification system (BCS) is a 
scientific framework for categorizing drugs based on dissolution, water solubility and intestinal 
permeability, which affect the absorption of active API from immediate-release solid dosage forms. 
The BCS considers the excipient composition, dissolution, solubility, and intestinal permeability as 
the main factors that control the rate and extent of API absorption [22]. 
 
BCS system classifies drugs into four classes. The system is based on solubility, dissolution rate and 
permeability, which controls absorption as illustrated on Figure 4 below. For this project, the model 
drug was acetaminophen, which belongs to class I, as it is well absorbed. It is commonly used as a 
painkiller to treat mild to moderate pain such as headaches, menstrual periods, and fever. With a 
molecular weight of 151 Da, Acetaminophen is a small molecule [23]. It is a commonly used model 
drug in the research group IDUN as it is easily detectable via UV, highly soluble, goes into solution 
quickly and is cheap to purchase.

2 Research Aim  
The aim of this project was to investigate and develop enteric coated capsules using caleva mini 
coater to be used for SUFs.  
 
3 Hypothesis 
SUFs loaded into the enteric coated capsules work as drug carriers and enable release only at the 
specific site due to a pH sensitive polymer coating, thus providing an increased drug absorption. 
 
The hypothesis is that using enteric formulations and the caleva mini coater we can create a functional 
enteric-coated capsule for SUF that can tolerate the acidic pH in the stomach for at least 2 h and 
release SUF at a higher pH in the small intestine.

5 Methods 
This chapter describes the methods and techniques that have been used for this project, including 
fabrication of foils, spray coating of capsules and dissolution tests.  
 
5.1 Preparation and fabrication of PDMS foils   
The PDMS is cast by mixing base resin and curing agent in the ratio 10:1. With a pipette, the curing 
agent was added to the middle of the base resin in a vial. The system was mixed with a lab spoon 
until bubbles were created. The mixture was placed in the desiccator for 45 min. The vials cannot be 
more than half full when placed in the dessicator.  7 mL PDMS was placed on the wafer and placed 
on the spin coater (Laurell Technology Corporation, Lansdale, PA, USA) with 300 revolutions per 
minute (rpm) for 15 s, followed by solidifying on a heat plate at 160°C for a few min.    
 
The next day, the polymer was ready for fabrication. The preliminary steps of the polymer fabrication 
include testing the thickness of the polymers. The thickness had to be in the range 700 mm ± 100 
mm, otherwise discarded. The edges had to be cut off as they were thicker than the rest of the polymer. 
The polymers were then ready to be cut in the correct sizes as the final step. One “circle” was used 
for fabrication of three foils. The polymers were cut in the sizes length 5 cm and width 1.5 cm. as 
illustrated on Figure 5 below.  
 
 
Figure 5. Polymer fabrication. The edges are cut off using a scalpel followed by cutting the polymer into three identical 
sizes and shapes.

5.2 Polymer preparation  
5% PlasACRYL® HTP20 relative weight in weight (w/w) dry Eudragit L 30 D-5 was initially used 
for this project. 0.85g of the PlasACRYL® HTP20 was weighed in a glass beaker, and 11.6 g 
EUDRAGIT® L 30 D-55 was weighed in a separate glass beaker. PlasACRYL® HTP20 was pipetted 
into the beaker of EUDRAGIT® L 30 D-55 and mixed for 10 min. with magnet stirrer to ensure 
proper partitioning of the plasticizers into the polymer. The mixture was diluted with 37.35 g milliQ 
water and stirred for another 10 min before use [26]. 
 
Later in the project, PlasACRYL® HTP20 was excluded from the polymer solution, leaving a 
solution containing of only EUDRAGIT® L 30 D-55 and milliQ water. Thus, 12.45 g 
EUDRAGIT® L 30 D-55 was weighed in a glass beaker and diluted with 37.35 g milliQ water. The 
mixture was stirred for 10 min. and ready to use.

5.4 Spray coating 
Coating the capsules is a necessary step to ensure that the drug delivery occurs at a specific location. 
To apply a layer of enteric coating on the capsules, a spray coater was used. The spray coater was the 
Tablet and Pellet Mini Coater Drier obtained from Caleva Process Solutions Ltd, and can be seen on 
Figure 8 . It is adjustable according to agitator speed, fan speed, temperature, and pump speed which 
allows a relatively great degree of freedom in applying coating to a solid dosage form as a liquid and 
can coat from a 1 to up to 150 capsules, and from few grams to 20 grams of pellets [28]. The caleva 
mini coater works in a similar way as a fluid bed coater. The aqueous solution evaporates and the 
solids it contains form a coating layer. The liquid serves to transport the solids to the surface of the 
capsules [29]. In the pharmaceutical industry, most coating is done using a pan coating which is like 
a tumble drier.

5.5 Scanning Electron Microscopy

5.6 In vitro dissolution and drug release studies 
Initially, dissolution tests were carried out with the use of a fluorescent dye, rhodamine B, loaded into 
the enteric coated capsules, to determine the release profile. When this was established, in vitro 
dissolution tests were carried out using the µDISS Profiler™ to determine the release profile of the 
model drug acetaminophen.  
 
5.6.1 Dissolution test  
After the capsules were enterically coated, release studies were conducted using rhodamine B as the 
tracer dye to determine if there was release in hydrochloric acid (HCl), and to determine the rate of 
release in PBS. Vials containing the medias simulating the pH conditions of the stomach and the 
small intestine were placed in a water bath for 10 min. before use to ensure that the temperature in 
the vials were 37°C during the whole experiment. The capsules were placed into the vials and placed 
in water baths of 37°C, 40 rpm and the dissolution tests were performed. The capsules were placed 
in 10 mL HCl pH 2 for 2 h, following incubation in 10 mL PBS pH 7.4 until it was observed that 
unfolding of foils were complete. The amount of release of rhodamine B was determined 
qualitatively, where the darker the pink color was, the more release there was into the surrounding 
media. Dissolution tests using a water bath were carried out until the desired amount of enteric coating 
of capsules was achieved.

5.6.2 In vitro drug release studies  
After the desired amount of enteric coating was achieved, the release profile was ready to be 
determined quantitatively using the µDISS Profiler™, which is a UV-monitoring system specifically 
designed to measure a small volume concentration in a sample as illustrated on Figure 11 [32]. For 
this project, the µDISS Profiler™ was used for measuring the concentration of release of 
acetaminophen from the capsules into the surrounding media simulating the environment of the 
stomach and small intestine, respectively.  
 
Calibration curves had to be made prior each dissolution test. The stock solution of 10 mg/mL 
acetaminophen in HCl pH 2 was prepared prior the experiment. The calibration curves were done by 
adding a specific volume of a stock solution to the vials until saturation of drug was observed.  
 
In vitro release studies were carried out in 20 mL HCl pH 2 and subsequently PBS 7.4, magnetic 
stirring was set at 100 rpm, and the temperature was maintained at 37°C. Each sample was placed 
with the lid pointing upwards in a vial on a rotational cylindric magnet with carbon tape. 20 mL HCl 
was carefully poured into each vial. After 2 h, the HCl media was carefully poured out of the vials 
and replaced by 20 mL PBS pH 7.4 for 12 h [33]. The experiments involved five channels (ch), 
namely ch1, ch2, ch3, ch4, and ch5. UV probes with 1mm mirrors were attached.

6 Results   
When developing a new pharmaceutical drug many experiments need to be carried out in the 
laboratory to test the bioavailability, stability, and effects of the drug. Thus, many experiments have 
been carried out for this project; however, a lot more needs to be done.  
In this chapter, the data obtained for spray coating capsules for enteric release, and subsequent 
dissolution testing and in vitro dissolution release studies will be presented. 
 
6.1 Spray coating  
For all experiments, the weight gain was measured at different time points. After many experiments, 
it was concluded that after 40 min. the weight gain was ~20%, and after 80 min. ~50% using the same 
parameters of the spray coater. The parameters on the spray coater were the same for all experiments

7 Discussion  
In the following section, the challenges faced when using the spray coater, and the data obtained 
from the dissolution studies throughout the project will be discussed. 
 
7.1 Challenges faced when using the spray coater  
The initial phase of the project was to get familiar with the Caleva Mini Coater. This was a huge 
challenge due to several reasons. The caleva mini coater is supposed to be able to coat from a single 
to up to 150 capsules, and from few grams to 20 grams of pellets [28]. However, it is much more 
challenging to coat capsules with a low weight than it is to coat capsules of higher weight. When 
coating capsules without foils, and thus low weight, many experiments had to be repeated, due to 
capsules sticking together, sticking to the filter on the fan, or sticking to the cone of the caleva mini 
coater (Appendix Figure 39).  
 
Sedimentation of solids in the spray tubes was also experienced a lot. The solid particles of the coating 
suspension settled in the tubes, which could lead to nozzle blockings. This was experienced with low 
spray rate of the spray solution. When the spray rate was low, the solids would sediment in the tubes.    
 
The positioning of the spraying nozzle was also a subject to be investigated for this project. The 
optimal distance between the nozzle and the fan filter of the coater. This was investigated as huge 
chunks of flakes were produced during coating of capsules (Appendix Figure 40). As the diameter of 
the fan filter was 4 cm., the diameter of different distances between the nozzle of the spray was 
measured until a diameter corresponding to the diameter of the fan filter of 4 cm was achieved. This 
was achieved with 12 cm between the nozzle and the tissue, thus the distance between the nozzle and 
the fan filter was set to 12 cm.   
 
7.2 Optimization of the parameters of enteric coated capsule formulation 
Furthermore, challenges with clogging of the spray coater were experienced throughout the project. 
One of the challenges of using the spray coater includes risks of clogging in the tubes of the 
instrument. Several incidents of clogging were experienced during the experiments which shows that 
the spray coater needs to be used carefully. The concentration of solid in the suspension going through 
the spray coater must be low enough, the viscosity of the suspension had to be very low, otherwise it 
would clog. In addition, the running experiment could not be paused at any time if suspension was 
==PAGE:44==
Master’s Thesis Project 
Leila Alex Botansen 
23-07-2023 
 
 
39 
running through it. This would also result in clogging. Another reason for clogging was sedimentation 
of solids in the bottom of the beaker which would give an inhomogeneous coating suspension. This 
challenge was overcome by leaving the coating suspension stirring at all times during the 
experiments. If the coating suspension was not stirred continuously the solid concentration would be 
higher towards the end of the coating process and would therefore stick in the tubes and nozzle and 
cause blockings [38].  
 
 
7.2.1 Orange peel effect  
As mentioned in the result section, challenges with coating structure, giving an orange peel look to 
the capsule, were experienced. This could also be due a high degree of humidity in the coater. One 
way to overcome this may be using a more organic solvents (like ethanol or isopropanol) mixed with 
milliQ water, instead of using pure milliQ water to dilute the polymer. Using an organic solvent to 
make the coating solution is something that is explored in literature. The organic solvents allow for 
less wetting when coating, however the tagginess/stickiness of the capsules increase which also gives 
challenges as we do not want our capsules to stick together and form a ball [35].  
 
7.2.2 The use of plasticizer  
Using plastizisers in polymer solutions (like we did in the beginning where we used 
PlasACRYL® HTP20) should allow for more flexibility of the coating. This might result in less 
cracking of the polymer and thus a fully polymer covering of the capsules. However, no difference 
was observed using PlasACRYL® HTP20 and therefore it was decided not including it. If the effects 
of using PlasACRYL® HTP20 in the polymer solution was investigated more thoroughly, a change 
might have been observed. 
 
 
7.3 Dissolution test 
Papers have been found to state that 20% weight gain with EUDRAGIT® L 30 D-55 is enough to 
achieve protection in stomach pH and subsequent release in small intestinal pH e.i. in the study by A. 
B. Nair et al. and in the study by S. Berg et al. where they state that 8% and 21% weight gain is 
enough for gastric resistance, respectively [39] [19]. After many attempts of trying, the effect of a 
gradual increase of polymer weight gain was investigate until a satisfied weight gain was achieved. 
Despite this has been demonstrated to work in literature, it has been extremely challenging to achieve 
similar results as other scientists state is possible. Additionally, it is not stated clearly if this 20% 
==PAGE:45==

increase in weight is based on the weight of the empty capsule or the capsule with the device loaded. 
The lack of clarity from existing literature could be avoided, if the weight percentage (wt%) was 
converted to units such as mg/cm2, as this would allow easier comparison. The amount of coating 
would be related to the surface area of the capsule. For that reason, it would be expressed in mg of 
dry polymer per cm2 of the surface area of the capsule [40].  
 
It was investigated how much remaining polymer there was on the foils. All dissolution tests show 
that upon foil unfolding an area of 2.5 cm on the foil is covered by the remaining polymer. The 
remaining polymer could potentially sit on the surface of the foil and therefore prevent the drug to be 
properly released and not allow the foil to get close proximity to the tissue. Thus, it is desired to 
minimize how much polymer to add as a weight gain of coating (see Figure 15 and Figure 17). 
 
After many dissolution tests, where the weight gain was gradually being increased from 20% until 
acidic protection was achieved, the acidic protection for 2 h was achieved at 50% weight gain and 
banding of joining area of capsules with HPMC-AS. Rhodamine B was used for this project to study 
its fluorescence in aqueous solutions [41]. Upon capsule opening, the dye would stain the solution 
immediate as the dye is very strong. The dye was used qualitatively to visualize the capsule 
dissolution. The dye was diluted in some cheap bulking powder as it is very bright and a bit wasteful 
to use pure rhodamine B for capsule filling. Talcum was used to dilute rhodamine B, however, other 
agents such as milk powder could also have been used for this purpose. Furthermore, as it is desired 
to have a rapid/immediate release of the enteric coated capsules when it reaches the small intestine, 
it was decided to use Plantcaps® capsules, and not Coni-snap® hard gelatin capsules, as Plantcaps® 
capsules dissolves faster when exposed to pH 7.4.  
 
The next thing was to use the µDISS Profiler™ for the in vitro drug release studies and 
acetaminophen was used as the model drug.

for the performance of the dosage form in the GIT. The most used dissolution media are those that 
contain phosphate buffers since they have previously shown reasonable in vivo-in vitro (IV-IVCs) 
correlations. However, other than pH, the use of a phosphate buffer media is not super representative 
of the in vivo environment in terms of characteristics like the fluid volume and viscosity, which varies 
significantly before and after a meal, and the fact that the GIT is subject to rapid and continuous

8 Conclusion 
Based on the findings and observations in this project, several conclusions can be drawn in terms of 
the use of enteric coatings for dosing oral devices. The caleva mini coater was used to develop enteric 
coated capsules to be used for SUFs. SUFs loaded into the enteric coated capsules work as drug 
carriers. The enteric coating only allows release at a specific site due to the pH sensitive polymer, 
thus providing an increased drug absorption [2]. 
 
In a series of dissolution experiments, the enteric-protective properties of the commercially available 
polymer EUDRAGIT® L 30 D-55 on Plantcaps® capsules and Coni-snap® hard gelatin capsules 
were evaluated. The results showed that Plantcaps® capsules dissolve more quickly than Coni-snap® 
hard gelatin capsules in intestinal pH. As it is desired to have a rapid release of the enteric coated 
capsules when it reaches the small intestine, it was decided to use Plantcaps® capsules. 
 
Another interesting observation seen was the remaining polymer on the foil after dissolution. The 
remaining polymer on the foils measured 2.5 cm in length. This is significant because the foil is meant 
to unfold when it reaches the small intestine allowing close proximity. Remaining polymer on the 
foil would possibly prevent this close proximity between the foil and the small intestine.  
 
Dissolution tests showed that 20% weight gain and 30% weight gain was not sufficient to protect the 
capsule from release in HCl within 2 h. Increasing the weight gain to 50% showed better results, 
however, fully acidic protection was still not observed. Sealing the joining area of the enteric coated 
capsules with absolute ethanol showed good results for capsules with foils loaded, however, capsules 
without foils were not prevented from release in HCl within 2 h. However, by sealing the joining area 
of the enteric coated capsules with HPMC-AS the enteric coated capsules were prevented from 
release in HCl for 2 h with and without foils. 
 
In vitro drug release profiles of acetaminophen loaded into enteric coated capsules and banding of 
joining area of capsules with HPMC-AS were performed. The in vitro studies were performed using 
the µDISS Profiler™. The studies included capsules with and without foils. The coated capsules 
could indeed selectively release acetaminophen in the pH conditions of the small intestine, instead of 
the pH conditions of the stomach [9]. Results show that 50% polymer weight gain was sufficient to 
protect the capsules from release before reaching the pH of the intestine with and without foils loaded.

9 Future perspectives  
Future work within the project should include repetition of in vitro dissolution tests using 
acetaminophen to be able to verify that 50% weight gain using Eudragit L 30 D 55 is sufficiently 
chemically resistant to survive the acidic conditions of the stomach and release quickly after in the 
intestinal media, and to verify that the maximal drug release in HCl pH 2 within 2 h is 10% [43].  
 
Future work should also include optimization of the coating system. The effects of using 
PlasACRYL® HTP20 could be one way to optimize the coating system, where the effects of different 
wt% of PlasACRYL® HTP20 could be investigated. Effects of other Eudragit® polymers such as 
Eudragit® S100 and Eudragit® FL30D-55 could also be interesting to investigate. 
 
Regarding the in vitro dissolution tests using the µDISS Profiler™, the effects of using dissolution 
cages/baskets to ensure capsules would not detach and end up floating in the vials should be 
investigated.  For this project the rotational cylindric magnet with carbon tape was not successful, as 
the capsules ended up detaching from the tape and floated in the media, thus the capsules were not 
affected by the stirring.  
 
Acetaminophen served as the model drug for this project, however, the actual drug would have a 
significantly higher MW. Loading the actual peptide into the foil should be investigated. Oral delivery 
of drug with a high molecular size is challenging due to their degradation in the acidic environment 
of the stomach and low permeability [45]. Thus, permeation enhancers should be investigated, as 
peptides such as insulin cannot effectively be delivered orally without the use of permeation 
enhancers.  
 
Another interesting aspect is development of a double-coating system, where not only the capsule is 
coated but also the foil. With this system there will be an outer coat and an inner coat. This could 
possibly accelerate drug release in the intestinal conditions, allowing a rapid release [17]. 
 
Finally, ex-vivo assessment of the coating performance and dissolution of model drug using ex-vivo 
tissue should be conducted.
