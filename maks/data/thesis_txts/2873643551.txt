Abstract
Designing flexible facilities for unknown future products is a challenge for today’s pilot scale
pharmaceutical production industry. Methods exist for facility design using discrete event sim-
ulation, in which the design is typically based on one or two products. However, no workflow
exists for the design of flexible facilities capable of producing a wide range of products, especially
when the products are unknown.
This thesis addresses this challenge by proposing a novel workflow that integrates flexibility
analysis into the design process using discrete event simulation. A fictional combined chemical
modification and purification facility in pilot scale serves as the case study to which the workflow
is applied.
The workflow begins with the generation of an artificial product portfolio consisting of 1000
products. One of these products is then selected as the ”model product”, which is used to make
a baseline design for the facility. Next, a flexibility analysis of this baseline design is performed,
in which all 1000 products are tested on the baseline design. Using these results, the redesign
phase is initiated. A heuristic consisting of four iterations is proposed for the redesign phase,
where for each iteration, changes are made to the design and all products are retested. The
workflow results in a flexible facility design, meaning that it has a satisfactory capacity for all
products.
The results demonstrate that the proposed workflow effectively generates a satisfactory and
flexible design. Additionally, a robustness check in which the workflow was repeated with a
different model product for the baseline design also produced a valid solution, but a different
one. Therefore, the use of the heuristic leads to a solution with relatively little computational
time, but does not necessarily give an optimal result. For future work, it is suggested to define a
cost function and implement this into the redesign phase, potentially with more iterations, such
that the workflow is more robust and less sensitive to the selected model product.
Overall, the proposed workflow is a significant improvement over the current state-of-the-art,
due to the inclusion of a much more comprehensive base of products in the design. The devel-
oped workflow accounts for many possible future products as opposed to the industry-standard
methods, which base the design on current and past products.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Chronic diseases such as diabetes and obesity are among the most pressing health challenges
globally. Obesity, in particular, has evolved into an epidemic, significantly increasing the risk
of severe health complications like diabetes, heart disease, and various other conditions [1].
Notably, 16% of adults worldwide are considered obese, with rates climbing to 40% in the
United States, making it one of the countries with the highest prevalence of obesity [2].
With the introduction of GLP-1 medicines for obesity management in 2021, the landscape
for obesity treatment significantly shifted [3]. These innovative medicines have proved highly
effective, with mean weight losses of 15%. With one-sixth of the world population suffering from
obesity, the demand for these medicines has skyrocketed. The global use of obesity medication
has increased by 70% per year (5-year compounded annual growth rate for 2019-2023) [4].
With the growing demand for medicine, especially in obesity, the landscape is competitive for
pharmaceutical companies [5]. Currently, the majority of the market share within obesity is
held by Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly. However, companies such as Pfizer, Roche, and Amgen all
have anti-obesity medications which are expected to reach the market within the coming years.
Collins et al. argue that being the ”first-in-class” is not suﬀicient to maintain market share, and
it is necessary to provide ”best-in-class”, medications [5]. To achieve this, companies must have
more drugs in the development pipeline.
Novo Nordisk is the company that will be in focus in this thesis. The growing demand for
diabetes and obesity medications is reflected in their 2024 annual report, showing a sales growth
of 25%, which is significant considering the size of the company [6]. They are therefore looking to
innovate within their established markets (obesity/diabetes) in order to maintain their market
status, but also diversify by moving into new markets. By diversifying, the company is less
vulnerable and also able to care for more patients. These factors lead to an expanding pipeline.
A development pipeline includes the path a drug takes from discovery to being approved for the
market. This is illustrated in Figure 1.1. There are initially a great number of drug candidates
in early development and preclinical research. This reduces to a smaller number when entering
the clinical trials, in which the drugs are tested on patients. There are three phases of clinical

Having the capacity to produce for clinical trials is vital for the success of pharmaceutical
companies such as Novo Nordisk. When planning for capacity improvements at the clinical trial

in both future demand and future products which will be produced. Thus, there is a need for
highly flexible facilities in order to handle these uncertainties. This thesis will focus on the
design methodology for multipurpose facilities with an unknown product portfolio.
When designing facilities and evaluating capacity improvements, discrete event simulation is a
widely utilized tool for capacity modelling. Through simulation, the capacity of various designs
can be assessed with a higher degree of accuracy. In this thesis, discrete event simulation will
be applied to a large portfolio of products, enabling the evaluation of design flexibility. This
flexibility will be integrated into the final design, ensuring that the facility is capable of producing
a wide range of products with the desired capacity.

Previous work
This thesis builds upon three sources of previous work: 1) previous theses done in collaboration
with Novo Nordisk, which outline the benefits of capacity modelling and how it is implemented,

A summary of each of these sources is given in the following sections.
1.2.1
Theses at Novo Nordisk
Andersen developed a systematic method for ramping up capacity in a purification facility,
which was in start-up phase [8]. Using a discrete event simulation based model, he was able
to find the current state capacity and identify the limiting step. By performing a sensitivity
3

analysis on various inputs, he was able to propose improvement ideas and develop a roadmap to
increasing capacity. This was sorted into short, medium, and long-term improvements, leading
to a combined capacity increase of 359%.
More recently, Jacobsen and Vogel utilized a capacity model within CMC API Pilots for a solid
phase peptide synthesis process [7]. They had the objective of determining capacity and capacity
utilization improvements in order to meet the future demand. Using bottleneck analysis, they
identified improvement ideas and tested them individually and in combinations. They highlight
that it would be important to test these ideas for different products and analyze how this impacts
the model results and thus the design of new pilot plants.
This thesis is a natural continuation of their work, applying their methodology for capacity
improvements to a larger number of products.
1.2.2
Novo Nordisk state-of-the-art
In Novo Nordisk, a well-established workflow for facility design utilizing capacity modelling is
in place. First, a model is developed based upon a ”model product”, selected to represent the
purpose of the facility. For example, a facility for phase 1 or phase 2 would be designed using
a phase 1 or phase 2 product, as this is the corresponding level of product maturity. Similarly,
phase 3 facilities are designed using either a phase 3 product or, in some cases, a market product,
as products at this stage are considered fairly mature. For this model product, a design is chosen
that provides suﬀicient capacity and is within design constraints.
Given that the design must be suitable for a variety of products, it is important to test this
chosen design with additional products to see how it performs. The typical method for doing so
in Novo Nordisk is by choosing a few additional model products and evaluating the design on
these products.
Although the products are chosen to be representative, this comes with no guarantee that these
new products are representative of the complete process design space. By relying on only a few
products, there is a risk that the design has blind spots. As a result, certain combinations of
process parameters could lead to scenarios where the facility design yields low capacity. Worst
case, some products could be operationally infeasible. Addressing the risk of this design method
is important for the design of future facilities, which must be flexible in order to produce for the
expanding pipeline.
1.2.3
Cases of integrated capacity modelling and flexibility analysis
Bertran and Babi evaluate flexibility within pharmaceutical process design, noting the impor-
tance of both portfolio flexibility and capacity flexibility [9]. Their methodology aligns with the
Novo Nordisk state-of-the-art described in Section 1.2.2 and utilizes discrete event simulation.
They evaluate a chosen design with two additional products and with a change in demand. For
each of these scenarios, the unit utilization at each step is evaluated to ensure all equipment is
capable of handling the process at the desired capacity. They note the desire to have a balance
in equipment utilization and that process variables may be adjusted to achieve this.
4
==PAGE:18==
Lindahl, Babi, and Sin investigate the impact of uncertainty in both demand and product on
capacity expansions for multi-stage manufacturing networks [10]. They use an optimization-
based approach with both deterministic and stochastic models. For each product, they sample
several demand scenarios and evaluate what capacity expansions are needed.
There are various other cases which examine design with uncertainty within pharmaceutical
manufacturing [11, 12]. However, these cases focus on the planning of a product launch rather
than a facility design. While these results are of some relevance, there is a gap in the literature
relating to the flexibility analysis and capacity modelling for pilot scale process design.
1.3
Problem definition
The purpose of this thesis is defined as follows:
”Develop a workflow for a rigorous and systematic design process for a flexible pilot scale
production facility with an unknown product portfolio.”
As described in Section 1.2.2, the current approach to facility design relies on evaluating flexi-
bility based on a small number of representative products, leading to potential blind spots and
risks. There is a need for a new method to systematically incorporate flexibility in design across
a broader range of possible products and operating conditions, especially for pilot scale facilities
used in phase 3 production.
While some cases are given in Section 1.2.3, these focus on manufacturing for market products.
Phase 3 production facilities differ fundamentally from manufacturing facilities for market prod-
ucts: they must handle a broader range of products and face uncertainty in future products.
Therefore, these facilities require a greater degree of flexibility to accommodate these variations
and uncertainties.
The problem, therefore, is the lack of a rigorous methodology to include flexibility in pilot scale
facility designs. Addressing this gap is critical to ensuring robust and adaptable designs for phase
3 production. To do so, a method for evaluating the flexibility of a design with an unknown
product portfolio must be developed, along with a workflow for facility design integrated with
this analysis. The workflow must be rigorous and systematic to lead to reliable results with
reproducibility.

2.1
The process design problem
Process design is a field within Process Systems Engineering (PSE) [13]. In chemical process
design, a systematic method for the design synthesis process consists of concept generation,
generation of alternatives, and analysis of alternatives.
Using the problem specification, concepts for the process are generated [13]. There are multiple
ways to produce the same product, and in this stage, all the possibilities are mapped out.
This could be producing a product using the traditional process or using a newer or more
unconventional process. For each concept, alternative design ideas can be generated using, for
example, a process superstructure of the design space. For each of these design alternatives, an
analysis and evaluation are performed for cost, performance, flexibility, etc., depending on the
objective. In the end, a final process flowsheet is selected.
For a production of a single product in a continuous plant, in this flowsheet, all equipment and
links will be well-defined and optimized for this product [14]. During the design stage, a specific
market demand is known, and the design is made to perform this task.
2.1.1
Process design for pharmaceutical production
Pharmaceutical production brings multiple challenges to this well-laid-out design process.
The first complication is that since the products must be approved by the authorities, there are
restrictions on making changes to the process in order to improve the design.
The second complication is that the production is done in batches. This means that the pro-
duction must be scheduled; this schedule must be considered within the design phase [13]. This
adds a layer of complexity to the design problem.
The third complication is that the many different products are produced on the same equipment,
but with varying demands and not necessarily in the same order. This classifies as a multipurpose
batch plant, in which different products can be produced by sharing equipment, resources, and
production time [15]. An example is visualized in Figure 2.1. Therefore, in a multipurpose batch
production, the links will change, and the units are not as rigidly defined. Having to consider
6
==PAGE:20==
multiple processes makes the scheduling problem much more complicated. Additionally, the
design problem becomes more complex since not only one product needs to be conceptualized
in the first stage, but multiple products [14].
Figure 2.1: Multipurpose structure, where products A and B are produced on the same equip-
ment 1, 2, 3, and 4, but with different sequencing. Inspired by [15].
In the end, the flowsheet is not as easily selected based on some simple design criteria, since a
design that can serve multiple products must be generated [13].
2.1.2
Pilot scale pharmaceutical production
Pilot scale pharmaceutical production brings additional challenges, with some key differences
compared to market scale pharmaceutical production.
The most obvious difference is the scale. In pilot scale, the batch size is much smaller since the
demand for each product is much smaller. This means that some equipment and process steps
will operate differently than in a larger scale production.
A second difference is that the processes that come to a pilot facility are less mature and have
a greater variation. When the processes first come to a pilot facility, they come from lab scale,
where the equipment can be different, and there is a large variation in the products. As the
same product is produced multiple times in the pilot facility to make API for three clinical trials,
process experience and data are gained at a larger scale, and the process is improved gradually
to a mature market process.
A third difference is that the portfolio of products to be produced is less known. When designing
a market scale facility, it is generally known which products will be produced in the facility
and the process is known from the production for clinical trials. Therefore, the facility can
be tailored to these products specifically.
In pilot scale, the upcoming products are largely
unknown, especially in the first phases. Therefore, the design is usually done based on one
selected product and with a lot of redundancy.
All in all, when considering process design for pilot scale pharmaceutical production, it is impor-
tant to distinguish this from market scale pharmaceutical production, since the design problem
and challenges are different.

Purification
In final purification, the goal is to remove impurities and to reach the desired concentration of
product [8]. The unit operations for performing these tasks include chromatography columns
and UF/DF (ultrafiltration and diafiltration). An overview of both of these operations is given
below.
Chromatography
In this work, the type of chromatography process which is relevant is high-performance liquid
chromatography (HPLC). In HPLC, there are two phases: the stationary phase (the resin) and
the mobile phase (the liquid). For chromatography of proteins, the stationary phase is solid and
consists of small (30-100µm in diameter) porous beads. The surface of the pores of the resin
beads is modified to facilitate the adsorption of certain components. The resin is packed in the

force (pressure) [16].

is dissolved causes a specific component to attach to the stationary phase. Then, the other
components can be washed out in a chosen solvent. Following this, using a different solvent, the
attached component can be eluted from the column.

Types of HPLC
There are various applications for chromatography: separating products from impurities, in-
creasing the concentration of the product, or exchanging the buffer. Depending on the product
and the purpose, different types of chromatography with different solvents are utilized. The
main types of relevance to this thesis are: Ion Exchange Chromatography (IEC), Reverse Phase
Chromatography (RPC), and Hydrophobic Interaction Chromatography (HIC).
Ion Exchange Chromatography
In IEC, the attachment is due to electrostatic interactions
between charged areas of the biomolecule and oppositely charged functional groups in the sta-
tionary phase [17]. Therefore, a low ionic strength buffer should be chosen to best facilitate the
binding. The desorption for elution of the protein is done by either adding a solvent of higher
ionic strength or changing the pH.
Reverse Phase Chromatography
For RPC, the product is dissolved in an aqueous solvent
which is more polar than the stationary phase [18, 19]. This way, the polarity of the solvent
causes the product to bind to the nonpolar ligands (the ”reversed phase”) on the surface of the
resin beads. The product can then be eluted by decreasing the polarity.
Hydrophobic Interaction Chromatography
HIC separates compounds based on their
hydrophobicity [20]. By using a high-salt buffer for loading the product, the solvation of the
product will decrease, and the hydrophobic regions of the product will be adsorbed by the sta-
tionary phase. By gradually decreasing the salt concentration, it is possible to elute compounds
of increasing hydrophobicity.

Ultrafiltration and diafiltration (UF/DF) are used for both concentration increase and buffer

2.3
Flexibility analysis for process design
With flexibility being a key focus of this thesis, it is important to define what flexibility means
in process design and how to assess it.
Within PSE, flexibility analysis is recently garnering significant attention as processes must
be designed for a fast-changing market [23, 24]. Flexibility of a design is generally defined as
the ability of a design to operate feasibly over a variation of uncertain conditions [13, 25, 24].
Evaluating flexibility is particularly relevant in pilot scale pharmaceutical manufacturing, where
variability and uncertainty in future products are a challenge for facility design.
12
==PAGE:26==
2.3.1
Methods for flexibility analysis
For flexibility analysis, the problem can be formulated as either a flexibility test problem or a
flexibility index problem.
Flexibility test problem
The flexibility test problem defines upper and lower bounds on the uncertain parameters, then
evaluates the feasibility of the design over the entire range [13, 24]. The result is either that the
design is flexible to operate in that defined range, or that it is not. For example, a range of flow
rates is defined, and this range is tested to see whether or not the facility can operate over the
entire range. If it can, then it has passed the ”flexibility test”.
Flexibility index problem
When evaluating the flexibility of a design, one may be interested in how large the range is for
the uncertain parameters in which the design is feasible [13, 24]. This can be determined by
solving the flexibility index problem. In this, a flexibility index can be found that represents the
largest possible range of uncertain parameters for which all values are feasible. Relating to the
example given before, for the flexibility index problem, no range of flow rates would be defined,
but a ”normal” flow rate would be defined. Then, it would be tested how far above and how far
below this normal value the facility can operate. This gives a flexibility index.
2.3.2
Application to pilot scale pharmaceutical manufacturing
In pilot scale pharmaceutical production, the uncertain condition is related to what the product
is and its recipe parameters. Feasible operation is usually evaluated by the constraints given
in the design problem [24, 26]. Some examples of such constraints would be the filling level of
tanks, equipment connections, holding times, or load on membranes.
For this thesis, focus will be on the flexibility test problem.
A predefined range of recipe
parameters will be established based on expected product variability, and the system design will
be tested for both feasibility and capacity across this range.

2.5
Capacity and bottlenecks
Capacity analysis involves evaluating the throughput and utilization of the facility and individual
units, while bottleneck identification determines which units are limiting the capacity. This
section provides definitions of key capacity metrics and explores the different types of bottlenecks.
2.5.1
Capacity definitions
Capacity can be defined in a variety of ways depending on the industry and context. In this
thesis, definitions from CMC API Pilots are used and defined as follows.
Throughput
Throughput (Equation (2.4)) is defined as the amount of API produced in a facility over time.
Throughput = Mass API
Time
(2.4)
where mass API refers to the corresponding amount of finished API, and time refers to a rep-
resentative time frame of the production. A representative time often corresponds to multiple
cycle times at steady state or the tact of the system. For example, in one week, a facility can
produce two batches of 10 kg intermediate. The yield of further downstream processing is 80%.
This gives a throughput of 10 kg × 2 × 80%/1 week = 16 kg/week.
This will vary over time depending on the operating state of the system. The throughput may
not even come close to the facility capacity if the demand does not require it.
Facility capacity
If the throughput were increased to the maximum possible, this would give the facility capacity.
However, if this level of throughput can not be sustained for a longer period of time, it is not
15
==PAGE:29==
representative. Therefore, the definition of facility capacity is the maximum average throughput
of a system that can be maintained for an extended period. This is a theoretical metric, not
including any disruptions, downtime, working hours, or other constraints.
Expected capacity
While facility capacity gives a good indication of what is possible, it is typically of interest to
make an estimation as to what the actual throughput is expected to be during operation. Thus,
the expected capacity can be defined as shown in Equation (2.5).
Expected Capacity = Facility Capacity × Success Rate × Overall Line Eﬀiciency
(2.5)
where success rate refers to the proportion of batches meeting the right quality, and the overall
line eﬀiciency reflects the capacity lost by suboptimal operation. With expected capacity, values
for the success rate and overall line eﬀiciency must be assumed.
Scheduled throughput
If a further degree of accuracy is desired, the scheduled throughput can be determined by replacing
the overall line eﬀiciency with the actual manning, tact, and batch size requirements.

Some of the important metrics in capacity analysis are unit utilization and equipment usage.

2.5.3
Bottleneck identification
When the capacity has been determined, the next step is to perform a bottleneck analysis. A
bottleneck can be easily understood to be the units in a process that limit the overall capacity
[29]. Therefore, this unit would not be capable of handling a throughput increase.
Within batch processes, there is typically a distinction made between a size bottleneck and a
time bottleneck, which are defined as follows [30].
Size bottleneck
The step that sets the maximum batch size is known as the size bottleneck
[30]. This is found by the unit that has the highest equipment usage, for example, a tank that
is filled to the maximum filling level. This tank would not be capable of handling a larger batch
size.
Time bottleneck
The time bottleneck is the step in which the unit has the highest unit
utilization [30]. This sets the minimum cycle time. For example, a unit that is busy all the time
can not process the same amount of material in a shorter time period, whereas a unit that is
only busy part of the time can process the same amount of material in a shorter time period.
To evaluate capacity metrics and identify bottlenecks, a detailed analysis of the process is re-
quired. Process simulation is a tool used to create representations of processes which can be
used for for this exact purpose.
2.6
Process simulation as a solution method
There are many solution methods available for capacity analysis of planning and scheduling
problems. Process simulation is known to be a useful tool for both planning and scheduling,
and analyzing capacity [29, 31]. In process simulation, a mathematical model is used with the
goal of obtaining a representation of the behavior of the process [32].
When investigating process simulation methods, the first differentiation to make is between
static and dynamic simulation [7]. A static model is not time-dependent and is generally used
for modelling continuous, steady-state processes. A dynamic model is time-dependent. This
is the choice for processes that change over time, including batch processes. As described in
Section 2.4, batch processes consist of different tasks which must be scheduled. In order to
represent this, the simulation must be dynamic.
17
==PAGE:31==
Within dynamic simulation, there exist both continuous and discrete simulation. In continuous
simulation, differential equations are solved in order to find a solution for every point in time.
Discrete simulation only calculates the solution at certain points in time.
In discrete time
simulation, these time points are at evenly spaced intervals. In discrete event simulation (DES),
the solution is only calculated for important points in time (events). Figure 2.10 illustrates the
difference between continuous simulation and DES.
Figure 2.10: Continuous simulation (left) versus discrete event simulation (right). Adapted from
training material from INOSIM.
Batch processing is usually described using operations, such as filling a tank, pH adjusting,
or allowing a reaction to occur.
Therefore, it is not possible to represent the system using
differential equations, since the changes are not continuous, but instead jump from one operation
to another. Consequently, discrete simulation is more appropriately applied to batch processes.
Within this context, discrete event simulation is better suited than discrete time simulation, as
DES calculates the solution at specific time points which are known to be important, avoiding
potential miss of important events if they are between evenly spaced times.
2.6.1
Discrete event simulation
DES is used within many industries as a simulation method [7]. For batch processes, this method
is advantageous due to its ability to capture the complexity of scheduling while representing
events in the system without solving differential equations.
In DES, the state of the system is only modeled at discrete points of time, defined as events
[7]. At each event, the state of the system is updated, and the list of future events is updated.
The state of each variable in the system is saved together with the simulation time. Then, the
simulation proceeds to the next event. This differs from discrete time simulation in which the
state of the system is modeled with set time steps [33].
2.6.2
INOSIM
The chosen software for this thesis is INOSIM, a discrete event simulation software for process
simulation [7, 34]. In INOSIM, three inputs are needed: facility (the equipment and connections),
recipe (the operations and sequence), and order (which executes the recipe). With these defined,
the process can be simulated and, if feasible, a solution to the scheduling problem can be found.
18
==PAGE:32==
The output from INOSIM can be displayed in a Gantt chart, in order to visualize the allocation
of units and understand the schedule [7, 34]. Additionally, all results can be exported, including
filling levels, transfers, mass balances, and unit utilizations. This provides all the data needed
to perform a bottleneck analysis and generate capacity improvement ideas.
A more in-depth description of the INOSIM software and how simulations are set up can be
read in the thesis of Jacobsen and Vogel [7].
2.7
Sampling methods
Since the focus of the thesis is a production facility with an unknown product portfolio, an
artificial portfolio of products must be generated. The last section covered in the literature
review therefore covers different sampling methods. Since the flexibility analysis must cover the
full range of recipe parameters, the sampling should ensure that the generated products are
representative of the entire space.
In order to generate a representative product portfolio, a method of sampling must be chosen.
This section gives an overview of Latin Hypercube Sampling, orthogonal sampling, and random
sampling with probability distributions.
2.7.1
Latin Hypercube Sampling
Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) is a method of pseudo-random sampling which ensures that
the samples are distributed over the space [35]. This is done by dividing the space into n equal
subspaces, where n is the number of samples. Then, one sample is randomly sampled for each
subspace. An example is given in Figure 2.11.
Figure 2.11: LHS for five samples of x between 0 and 1. The space is divided into five subspaces
from which one random sample is taken from each subspace.
This method is also used for the sampling of multiple variables. An example of LHS in two
dimensions is given in Figure 2.12.
19
==PAGE:33==
Figure 2.12: LHS in two dimensions for five samples of x and y. The space is divided into 25
subspaces from which five subspaces are chosen such that each has its own row and column
(left). Then, a random point is chosen in each of these five subspaces (right).
The first step is dividing the space into nd subspaces, where n is the number of samples and d is
the parameter dimension. As seen in Figure 2.12, where n = 5 and d = 2, the space is divided
into 25 subspaces. Next, n of these subspaces are selected such that every row and column is
represented exactly once. This can be seen in the left graph in Figure 2.12, where each selected
subspace has its own row and column. Finally, a point is randomly sampled from each subspace,
as seen in the right graph in Figure 2.12.
This method can be used for any number of dimensions or samples. The advantage of LHS over
random sampling is that a lower number of samples is needed in order to have a representative
sampling of the space. However, LHS does not guarantee that all corner points are sampled,
and can lead to unintended correlations between variables, for example by sampling all points
along the diagonal.
2.7.2
Orthogonal sampling
Orthogonal sampling is similar to LHS in that it also splits the space into equal subspaces [36].
For orthogonal sampling, the space is split into only n subspaces rather than nd. In addition,
the method requires that the number of samples must be n = sd where s is an integer.
An example is given in Figure 2.13, where n = 32 = 9 samples are used. Orthogonal sampling
has the advantage over LHS of better reaching the corner points, but lacks the guarantee that
all variables are spread evenly across their range.
2.7.3
Orthogonal LHS
Lastly, orthogonal LHS combines both methods. The number of samples required is the same
as for orthogonal sampling. An example comparing LHS, orthogonal sampling, and orthogonal
LHS is given in Figure 2.13.
20
==PAGE:34==
Figure 2.13: LHS (left), orthogonal sampling (middle), and orthogonal LHS (right) in two
dimensions for nine samples of x and y.
Figure 2.13 illustrates LHS, orthogonal sampling, and orthogonal LHS for nine samples in two
dimensions. This demonstrates the advantages of each type of sampling. With LHS, it is ensured
that each variable is distributed evenly across its range. With orthogonal sampling, it is ensured
that each combination of variables is represented, for example, high x and low y, medium x and
medium y, etc. These benefits are both present in orthogonal LHS.
As will be discussed in Chapter 3, the disadvantage of both orthogonal sampling and orthogonal
LHS is the number of samples required when the dimension is high. Therefore, LHS is the
selected pseudo-random sampling method, although there are concerns regarding the inability
of LHS to adequately represent corner points. Therefore, additional products are generated
in which the probability distribution is skewed to the edges, in order to ensure that there are
products representing the entire space. The probability distribution is described below.
2.7.4
Random sampling using probability distributions
As opposed to LHS and orthogonal sampling, random sampling chooses each point randomly,
without regard for other points.
With random sampling, it is possible to follow a defined
probability distribution such that points will be distributed in a certain way.
In order to ensure that all ”corners” of the space are adequately sampled, it may be relevant
to use a distribution that is skewed towards one of the edges. An example of this would be the
beta distribution [37]. The beta distribution is defined by two parameters, α and β, which can
be used to define various distributions, as seen in Figure 2.14.

Figure 2.14: Probability density function for different beta distributions.
The pink line in Figure 2.14, corresponding to α = 5 and β = 1, is an example of a distribution
that favors higher values. This will be used for generating products that have all values in the
higher range.

3
Methodology
To solve the problem described in Section 1.3, a workflow is defined and tested for the design of
flexible production facilities with an unknown product portfolio. As shown in Section 2.1.3, the
scope for the design is the design of a combined modification and purification pilot scale facility.
This design is for a fictional facility, meaning that it is possible to make assumptions in order to
move forward without a need for complete accuracy. By evaluating how the workflow performs
for a fictional facility, suggestions can be made for application to a real case. Therefore, the case
is designed to be as close to reality as possible.
The first step is proposing a workflow that will be followed for the design. Next, an INOSIM
model must be developed to be utilized throughout the design process. The methodology used
for each step of the proposed workflow is then covered. Lastly, in order to test the robustness
of the proposed workflow, the workflow is repeated using a different starting point to check if
the same results are achieved.
3.1
Proposed workflow
An overview of the proposed workflow is given in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Overview of the proposed workflow for integrated facility design and flexibility
analysis.
First, since the facility has an unknown product portfolio, artificial products are generated.
Next, one of these products is chosen as the model product, and a baseline design is made
based on this product. This is done to have a starting point for the flexibility analysis, in which
all of the products in the portfolio are tested on the baseline design. For each product, the
performance can be measured in terms of feasibility, capacity, equipment utilization, etc. With
these results, both design and operational changes can be made, and the product portfolio tested
again, until a satisfactory design is found.
After the workflow is completed, a robustness check repeats the process starting from the baseline
design with a different model product selected. This will show how sensitive the resulting design
is to the initial model product selection.

==PAGE:38==
3.2
INOSIM model development
For the baseline design, flexibility analysis, and redesign, an INOSIM model must be developed
in order solve the scheduling problem, calculate the capacity and visualize the utilization of
equipment. INOSIM Expert Edition 13 is the version used. The model is built from scratch, al-
though prebuilt modules developed internally within Novo Nordisk are used for chromatography
steps and UF/DF steps.
The model should be flexible to run a variety of products; thus, a dynamic recipe is developed
by assigning each possible step in the product recipes to a unit procedure. Then, the VBA code
reads the product recipe from the Excel sheet and dynamically activates each unit procedure
that corresponds to a step in the product recipe. The process parameters for each step are also
read from the Excel sheet, which automatically updates values based on the selected product.
3.3
Portfolio mapping
The use of a large portfolio of artificial products is central to the purpose of this thesis. The
inclusion of a flexibility analysis by means of testing a variety of products with different process
steps and setpoints provides the opportunity to generate a design that can adapt to a large
degree of product variation.
To generate this portfolio, some inputs are needed. The first step is mapping out a superstructure
of all the steps that would exist in a combined chemical modification and purification facility.
Then, the setpoint ranges and probabilities for each step must be mapped out. Lastly, a sampling
method must be defined.
3.3.1
Superstructure of steps
A superstructure of steps consists of all the steps that would possibly exist in a process. An
example is given in Figure 3.2.
Figure 3.2: Example of a superstructure for a process.
In this example, steps 1, 1a, and 2 can be bypassed while step 3 must be selected for all products.
Additionally, the constraint exists that step 1a can only be selected if step 1 is selected. The
superstructure describes all pathways a product can take. Visualizing the steps in a process in
this way is useful for understanding the different paths and which steps can be selected.
25
==PAGE:39==
3.3.2
Setpoint ranges
After the superstructure has been defined, the next step is mapping out the parameters for each
step, called setpoints. In this stage, the scope of the problem is important to consider. For
discrete event simulations, it is most important to capture the mass and time of operations.
Additionally, having fewer parameters to sample will mean that a representative sampling is
possible with a smaller number of products.
Therefore, each step in the superstructure is
generalized into operations that represent the step. Similar operations, such as adding reactants
for a reaction, can be grouped into one operation with a wider range for the ”amount” setpoint.
In this way, products with a variety of different reactants can be captured accurately in mass and
time, while the differences in the type of reactant can be disregarded, leading to more effective
sampling.
3.3.3
Types of parameters
With the superstructure and setpoint ranges identified, it is possible to categorize the different
parameters that must be sampled.
• Boolean: These parameters can either take the value of true or false. These are used to
represent whether or not a certain step in the superstructure is selected for the product,
or whether or not a sub-step is selected (for example, if a column step is included or if the
column has a dilution).
• Discrete: For these parameters, the value is chosen from a discrete selection. This can
be, for example, which type of resin is used in the column. These parameters will not be
included if their corresponding step or sub-step is not included.
• Range: These parameters are a value chosen from a continuous range. Some examples
of these parameters are reaction time, column load, or step yield. These parameters will
also not be included if their corresponding step or sub-step is not included.
3.3.4
Sampling of products
An overview of sampling methods was given in Section 2.7. Below, the selection of sampling
method is given, along with the methodology.
Selection of sampling method
While orthogonal LHS clearly provides the best distributed pseudo-random sampling, there is
a challenge when the dimension is high. The lowest possible s value is two. If the number of
variables to sample is, for example, 50, this would require n = 250 = 1125899906842624 samples.
This is clearly unreasonable for the scope of this thesis, as there is not enough time to run this
many products through INOSIM.
Therefore, LHS is the chosen method. Although it can miss some regions and accidentally create
a correlation between variables, it can be used for problems with high dimensions. Additionally,
with a high enough number of samples, it will be able to capture most combinations of variables.
In order to compensate for the inability of LHS to ensure that corner points are represented,
additional products are generated using random sampling with the beta distribution. These
26
==PAGE:40==
products are specifically designed to represent extreme cases: those likely to result in high
capacity and those expected to perform with low capacity. To do so, the ”range” parameters
are categorized based on their impact on capacity:
• Positive impact: parameters where higher values lead to higher capacity, such as yield.
• Negative impact: parameters where higher values lead to lower capacity, such as reaction
time.
For products designed to achieve high capacity (”good” products), the beta distribution with
α = 5 and β = 1 is used to favor higher values for positive impact parameters and lower values for
negative impact parameters. The opposite is done for products designed to result in low capacity
(”bad products”). Since this method can only be applied to ”range” parameters, boolean and
discrete parameters are sampled using LHS. Thus, this collection of ”good” and ”bad” products
has the same variation of process steps and discrete parameters as the main products.
By generating exceptionally good and exceptionally bad products, it is possible to test if the
LHS sampling was suﬀicient on its own, or if the generation of the additional products was
necessary in order to properly represent the product space.
Methodology for product sampling
As described previously, different types of parameters must be sampled for each product. The
sampling is done as follows:
1. Sampling of all boolean, discrete, and range parameters.
2. After these parameters are sampled, some values must be removed if they correspond to
a step or sub-step not included in that product.
The challenge here is that LHS ensures that all combinations of parameters are represented.
However, by removing the generated values, some important combinations of parameters may
be lost. A different method would be to only generate the setpoints for each step one at a
time, but then there is no assurance that combinations of setpoints across steps are represented.
Therefore, it is decided to generate all setpoints at once, afterwards deleting the ones that belong
to steps not included in the product.
Number of products
In setting the number of products to generate, two factors must be balanced: having enough
products to perform a robust flexibility analysis representing all possible products in the space,
and being able to simulate all the products in a reasonable time frame, considering the duration
of the thesis. It is only possible to evaluate if the number of products was suﬀicient after the
simulation is done. Thus, it is decided to define a reasonable time frame and evaluate as many
products as possible within this time.
3.4
Baseline design
After the portfolio generation, the next step is making a baseline design for the facility. This
is done using a model product and a target capacity, and making a design which is suitable for
27
==PAGE:41==
these inputs. In this workflow, the purpose of the baseline design is to have a starting point for
testing of other products and improvement. Additionally, this serves as an indicator for how
much the flexibility analysis changes the design.
3.4.1
Selection of model product
In choosing a model product, there are different approaches: 1) choose a ”worst-case” product,
2) choose an ”average” product, and 3) choose a random product.
The chosen approach is
selected based on the purpose of the baseline design.
Choosing a worst-case product would mean that the design will most likely be capable of produc-
ing all other products with suﬀicient capacity. However, the result will also be an over-design.
By catering to a worst-case product, equipment will need to be sized much larger than is needed
for most products. Additionally, it is not certain that the chosen worst-case product is even
realistic, so oversizing equipment just to ensure that all possible products can be produced with
suﬀicient capacity may not be the optimal choice.
Choosing an average product is another option for the starting point. For this approach, im-
provements can be made for products that perform poorly, but some products will also perform
well. The issue with this approach is that in a real-world scenario, it is hard to know if a prod-
uct is ”average” or not. Thus, choosing the average product adds a bias to the output of the
analysis.
Lastly, choosing a random product is more similar to how design is done in CMC API Pilots.
The product is, of course, not random, but selected by process experts to be representative.
By choosing a random product, it is possible to evaluate the risks of the current state-of-the-
art method. Additionally, the ability of the redesign method to produce a successful design
independently of the model product can be evaluated, which demonstrates the robustness of the
workflow.
The chosen approach is therefore to choose randomly, but within some constraints which ensure
that the product is closer to average by requiring at least the inclusion of one of each type of
process step.
3.4.2
Problem definition for baseline design
For a design problem, the first step should be to map out a problem definition, outlining the
inputs, outputs, and constraints for the design. For this case, the problem definition for the
baseline design is as follows.
Given:
• Product recipe
• Target facility capacity
Determine:
• Equipment number and size (from selection of available equipment sizes)
28
==PAGE:42==
Subject to:
• Feasible schedule
It was chosen to select the target capacity as a facility capacity, since tact and manning are not
considered for the initial design phase. Additionally, during the flexibility analysis, correctly in-
cluding tact and manning would be technically challenging to implement in INOSIM considering
the large numbers of products.
In order to solve this design problem, the decisions relating to the scheduling problem must be
made: batching, unit assignment, sequencing and timing. In this case, batch size and equipment
are decided first, and the simulation generates a schedule.
3.4.3
Equipment size and number
There are different strategies for allocating equipment. Equipment can be used only for one
process step or multiple process steps. When starting with a baseline design, one would typ-
ically start by allocating one of each equipment type needed per process step. For example,
for each column step, allocating an application tank, column, pool tank, and collection tank.
After implementing this design, more equipment can be added if the target is not reached, or
equipment can be removed if it is underutilized, and the step can be performed on another piece
of equipment. Additionally, equipment as a starting point can be set to the largest possible size,
and subsequently adjusted to avoid oversizing.
3.4.4
Batch size
In order to have a starting point, the batch size can be fixed by considering the lower and upper
limit of batch size.
With a given target capacity, there will always be a lower limit on the batch size that can reach
this capacity. If there is a modification step, the reaction time is independent of batch size,
meaning that parts of the cycle time for the reaction equipment are fixed, and thus reducing
the batch size does not proportionally reduce the cycle time, causing a lower capacity. Even if
there is no reaction step, due to fixed cleaning times of column and filtration equipment, there
will also be a lower limit of batch size for which the target can be reached.
The upper limit of batch size will be limited by the largest possible equipment size, since discrete
equipment sizes are given. Even if this were not the case, there are other factors limiting batch
size, such as holding times. There are limits on how long a protein can be processed due to
stability, thus limiting the column and filtration durations.
In this work, holding times are
assumed to be infinite, such that the maximum equipment size dictates the largest possible
batch size.
As a starting point, both the smallest and largest batch sizes are considered for the baseline
design. A small batch size will result in smaller tanks (reaction tanks, application tanks, etc.),
and a big batch size will result in smaller sizes for flow equipment (column diameter, filter area).

==PAGE:43==
3.5
Flexibility analysis
Following the product generation and baseline design, the next step in the proposed workflow
is testing the generated products on the baseline design.
3.5.1
Simulation setup and inputs
The scheduling problem is solved for each product using the INOSIM simulation, and the results
are output. The inputs for each product are the equipment assignment and batch size. Therefore,
if some equipment from the baseline design is interchangeable, a calculation is done to determine
how the equipment should be arranged for the current product to result in the highest capacity.
Additionally, the batch size must be calculated.
Since the equipment sizes are set, the size
bottleneck can be identified and the batch size assigned to the maximum possible according to
this unit. It is advantageous to choose the largest possible batch size since this will nearly always
result in a higher capacity. In order to quantify the simulation results, it is necessary to define
the relevant outputs.
3.5.2
Output and evaluation
For each product, the capacity is measured, along with the equipment usage and utilization.
With these results, first, an overview of how different products perform on a design that was
based on one product can be made.
Next, a bottleneck analysis can be performed with all
results in order to identify where design improvements can be made to improve the capacity for
infeasible products and products with low capacity.
To evaluate the capacity, a clear definition of the target must be made. The baseline design is
done according to a target facility capacity, but it must be defined how this target translates to
thousands of products. One method of defining this target would be to require all products to
have a capacity above this. However, this would likely result in most products greatly exceeding
this capacity, resulting in much larger equipment than what is necessary for most products.
Therefore, it was decided that this target should be the median of the capacity of all products.
Thus, with some products above and some products below, the median capacity of the facility
over time should be above the target facility capacity. Additionally, to avoid products having a
very low capacity, a minimum acceptable capacity is defined in order to prevent any products
from having an unacceptable capacity. Lastly, all products should be feasible.
3.6
Redesign
Based on the evaluation of products in the flexibility analysis, the next phase is the redesign.
In the redesign phase, the baseline design is modified in order to reach the defined target for all
the products present in the flexibility analysis.
3.6.1
Debottlenecking methods
In debottlenecking, there are a variety of methods available. Generally, the first step is identi-
fying improvement ideas. These ideas fall into different categories; these could be adding new
equipment, increasing equipment size, or operational changes. For each idea, there may be dif-
ferent variations, such as the different sizes to which the equipment could be increased. With all
30
==PAGE:44==
improvement ideas identified, the best method would be to test all combinations of these ideas.
In order to choose the best combination, it is necessary to have a cost function. This way, the
combination that reaches the target capacity with the lowest total cost is clearly the best one.
When the number of combinations to test is too large for the available computation time, the
next best approach is a simulation-based optimization method.
Instead of testing all com-
binations, only some are selected to begin with. Based on the performance of these, the best
combinations are modified and reevaluated. The endpoint of the algorithm could be either when
the improvement between iterations is very small, or after a certain amount of time or number
of iterations. This method also requires a cost function to evaluate the different combinations.
The computation time is reduced compared to the testing of all combinations, but there is no
guarantee of reaching a global optimum.
In this case, where a large number of products must be tested for each iteration, the computation
time for a simulation-based optimization is too large. Additionally, in this work, the focus is
on finding a design for which all products are feasible and reach the target capacity. Therefore,
optimizing the design in relation to a cost function is reserved for future work. Considering
these factors, the chosen method for the redesign is the following proposed heuristic.
3.6.2
Iterations of the redesign phase
By using a heuristic rather than a more computationally demanding method, a solution can be
reached quickly, although it may not be the optimal solution. The goal of this heuristic is to
simply reach a solution in which all products are feasible, have a capacity above the minimum
acceptable level, and have a median capacity matching the target as closely as possible without
being below.
The proposed heuristic consists of four iterations, as shown in Figure 3.3 and Figure 3.4.
Figure 3.3: Proposed iterations in the heuristic for the redesign phase.
The reason the redesign is split into these iterations is to be able to solve one problem at a
time, rather than trying to adjust the capacity of all the products at once. The first step is
handling the infeasible products, since there needs to be a starting capacity to identify necessary
improvements. Next, the products under the minimum capacity can be examined in order to
bring them above the minimum. This is split into iteration 1a and 1b (see Figure 3.3), to allow
for the main bottlenecks to be focused on in iteration 1a, and a more case-by-case analysis to
be done for any remaining products in iteration 1b. Lastly, iteration 2 either increases capacity
or reduces capacity in order to adjust the median to the target. By following this sequence, the
31
==PAGE:45==
approach avoids tackling too many issues at once and allows for more precise adjustments in
each stage.
Figure 3.4: Graphic displaying an example of the change in capacity of various products in each
iteration of the redesign phase.
An explanation of each iteration shown in Figure 3.4 is given below.
• Start: After the flexibility analysis, the products can be sorted by their capacity into four
categories: products which are infeasible, feasible products under the minimum acceptable
capacity, products above the minimum acceptable capacity but under the target capacity,
and products over the target capacity.
• Iteration 0: The first iteration handles any products that are infeasible for the baseline
design. These products should be fixed before any capacity improvements can be made,
since a starting point is necessary to determine what improvements to make.
• Iteration 1a: In this iteration, the products under the minimum capacity are separated
32
==PAGE:46==
into groups where the solution to the bottleneck is ”isolated”. This means that the bot-
tleneck is within a single process step, which has a clear solution that can be improved
independently with minimal impact on other steps. By making these improvements, most
products should move above the minimum acceptable capacity, but the capacity of other
products may also increase, causing some to move from under the target to over the target
(visualized by the dotted line in Figure 3.4).
• Iteration 1b: This iteration is still concerned with products under the minimum capacity,
in the case that some products remain due to not being grouped, or if improving the
isolated bottleneck revealed another bottleneck resulting in too low capacity. This allows
for the remaining products to be evaluated case-by-case to determine what improvements
are needed for all products to be above the minimum acceptable capacity.
• Iteration 2: In iteration 2, adjustments are made to the median capacity in order to match
the target by either increasing or decreasing the median. In the example in Figure 3.4, too
many products have a capacity over the target, such that the median is too high. Thus,
one product is reduced in capacity to be under the target. The changes made here must
not cause any products to be reduced below the minimum capacity.
• Final design: In the final design, no products should be infeasible or below the minimum
acceptable capacity. Additionally, the number of products with a capacity under and over
the target should be relatively equal, such that the median matches the target as closely
as possible.
The final design can be evaluated by the distribution of the capacity, utilization of units, and
usage of units.
3.7
Robustness of workflow
To further evaluate the workflow, the purpose of the last section of the work is to check the
robustness of the proposed workflow. With the starting point of the design relying on a randomly
selected model product, this serves to investigate what would occur if the design were based on
a different model product. Therefore, if the workflow is robust, the resulting design should be
the same after the redesign phase, despite having a different baseline design.
The robustness check begins by selecting a different model product, still randomly, but based
on new constraints to ensure the product is different from the previous one. Next, the baseline
design, flexibility analysis, and redesign are performed using the same methodology. Lastly, the
final design can be compared to the original one to determine if there are differences due to the
original model product.

Results
This chapter contains the results achieved following the methodology given in Chapter 3. First,
the product portfolio of artificially generated products is described, including the steps and
setpoints. Next, the development of the baseline design based on a model product is shown.

Product portfolio
The products are generated based on the identified superstructure of process steps and setpoint

4.1.1
Process steps
As mentioned in Section 2.1.3, the process in scope is both chemical modification and final

In Figure 4.1, it can be seen that the ”chemistry” portion of the process consists of three different
reaction steps (green) with UF/DF steps in between (red). The ”purification” consists of three
column steps (blue) followed by a final UF/DF. Since there exist some constraints regarding
which paths the process can follow, it is useful to visualize the superstructure of all process

Figure 4.2: Superstructure of steps in chemical modification and final purification. Hollow circles

As seen in Figure 4.2, it is possible to bypass all process steps except for the first column and
the last UF/DF. Additionally, it is not possible to include a UF/DF step in the chemistry
section without including the previous reaction step. The first reaction step must, however, be
followed by a UF/DF. The last column step can only be included if the second column step is
also included. The columns were chosen to be represented this way since the setpoint ranges for
all three columns are identical, so there is no difference between the second and third columns.
Thus, there is no need to sample both products with only the second and only the third column.
Using this superstructure, the total number of possible combinations can be calculated. For
reaction 1 and UF/DF 1, there are two possibilities (yes or no). For reaction 2 and UF/DF
2, there are 3 possibilities (yes yes, yes no, or no no); the same is the case for reaction 3 and
UF/DF 3. For the columns, there are 3 possibilities (1 column, 2 columns, or 3 columns). The
UF/DF 4 is always included.
Combinations = 2 × 3 × 3 × 3 × 1 = 54
(4.1)
One method of sampling products would be to generate all 54 combinations and duplicate these
products until the desired total number of products is reached.
This would ensure that all
combinations are accurately represented. However, this would result in an uneven number of
each step being represented in the portfolio. For example, reaction 2 would be represented in
2/3 of products, since of the three possibilities for reaction 2 and UF/DF 2, reaction 2 is present
in two of them. Meanwhile, reaction 1 would only be represented in 1/2 of products.
While it is desired to include all combinations of steps, it is also important to consider the
frequency of each step. If a step is disproportionately represented, this can skew the results of
the design analysis. While it is true that different steps will be more frequently present in real
product recipes, these frequencies are diﬀicult to estimate, especially when considering future,
unknown products. Thus, the best choice remains the LHS method, such that each step has
a 50% probability of being selected for a product. However, since the UF/DF steps following
reactions 2 and 3 may only be selected if the reaction step is selected, they end with a 25%
probability of being selected. Additionally, for the sampling of the columns, if the third column
is selected without the second column being selected, the result is changed such that the second
35
==PAGE:49==
column is selected rather than the third. This results in 75% of products containing a second

4.2.2
Equipment size and number
With the model product selected, the equipment needed can now be determined.
An overview is given of the equipment types needed for this particular pilot facility.
Reactors
All reaction steps occur in the same type of reactor: a stirred-tank reactor. This
differs from an ordinary tank since it contains stirring, heating/cooling, different sensors, etc.
The same reactor can be used for all the types of reactions in the process.
UF/DF filters
A UF/DF filter is necessary to perform UF/DF steps. The filter consists of
a number of individual filters placed in a filter skid. This means there is some flexibility in the
42
==PAGE:56==
total filter area, based on how many filters are used. Before each batch, the filters are replaced
and conditioned. It must be used in combination with a UF/DF application tank.
UF/DF application tanks
A UF/DF application tank is used in combination with a UF/DF
filter to perform a UF/DF step. The tank contains the product, which is sent through the filter,
where the retentate is returned to the tank and the permeate is sent to waste, as seen in
Figure 2.4.
Columns
For each of the chromatography steps, a chromatography column is needed. The
column is packed with resin specifically for each column step. The columns are identical and
can be interchanged for different steps, but the time to pack and prepare the column for the
specific product is large. Thus, the same column can not be used for different steps in the same
product, since the time to unpack and repack the resin is too much. Each column step contains
an application tank, a pool tank, and a collection tank. It is important to include both an
application tank and a collection tank for scheduling reasons to ensure a high utilization of the
column itself.
Column application tanks
The application tank contains all the product for the column
step, and loads the column for each run.
Column pool tanks
The pool tank collects each collection pool from the column before
sending it to the collection tank. The purpose of a pool tank is to allow for a quality check of
each pool before adding it to the larger collection tank. In the case of a quality problem, the
individual pool can be discarded, rather than needing to discard the entire batch.
Column collection tanks
The collection tank contains all the product processed in the
column step. From an equipment perspective, an application tank and a collection tank are
identical.

4.5
Robustness of workflow
With the workflow completed and a flexible design successfully generated, the next step is to
evaluate the robustness of the workflow. The purpose is to test how the selection of a different
model product impacts the final design result. Thus, the same product portfolio is used to allow
for an accurate assessment of the influence of the choice of model product.

UF/DF 1.2 could be reduced from 9 m2 to 5 m2, and UF/DF 2 could be reduced from 15 m2 to

5
Discussion and future work
With all results and findings presented, the next step is an assessment of how the different
decisions made throughout the work impacted the final result. Additionally, it is important to
put the work into perspective and describe how it can be applied practically. Lastly, suggestions
are made for future work.
5.1
Methodology and impact on results
In each phase of the workflow, different decisions and assumptions were made. An assessment
of how each these affected the final result is given below.
Product portfolio
For the product sampling, it was chosen to use LHS as well as a beta distribution for generating
exceptionally ”good” and ”bad” products. The purpose of doing so was to evaluate if the LHS
reached the edge points, which was found not to be the case. The results show the ”good”
products reaching much higher capacity, and many of the ”bad” products had much lower
capacity than the main products. This shows that the definition of the product portfolio can
have a significant impact on the results.
If the ”bad” products were to be included in the
products used for the design, equipment sizes would need to be larger to accommodate these
products.
It was also noted in iteration 1b of the redesign that to have all products above the minimum
acceptable capacity, it was necessary to increase some equipment sizes only due to a very small
number of products.
For example, the column had to be increased from 80 cm to 100 cm
because one product needed that size in order to reach the minimum capacity. If the sampling
were performed again, another set of products may not have such a product, or perhaps an even
worse product would appear, which would require an even larger piece of equipment. This shows
that the number of products was not enough to reach all the corner points of the space. However,
a larger number of products would not have been feasible considering the time available for the
thesis.
The only way to ensure that all worst-case products can reach the desired capacity would be to
generate a product consisting of all the worst setpoint values and design around this. However,
it is typically not needed to design for the absolute worst-case product, since it is unlikely that
this product would exist.
The setpoint ranges were made through discussions with process
experts, and depending on how conservative these estimates are, the likelihood of a product
with the worst-case combination of setpoints varies. Thus, there must be alignment between the
definition of setpoint ranges and the target of the design: whether it should cater to worst-case
products, if there is a minimum acceptable capacity, and if all possible products must meet this
minimum acceptable capacity.
86
==PAGE:100==
Baseline design
In making the baseline design, the first step was the choice of the model product. As shown
in the robustness check, this choice has a large impact on the redesign process and the final
result. The first model product was especially good and lent itself well to a baseline design
with a smaller batch size. This led to a flexibility analysis, which showed many products to
be infeasible or below the minimum acceptable capacity, leading to many improvements needed
in the redesign phase. For the model product chosen in the robustness check, the product was
especially bad and lent itself more to a baseline design with a larger batch size. Therefore, the
flexibility analysis showed all products meeting the capacity targets and a significant downsizing
being necessary in the redesign phase. In the end, both designs matched the capacity targets
closely, but varied significantly in equipment sizes.
Flexibility analysis
The flexibility analysis phase consists of testing all generated products on the baseline design
and evaluating their performance. To do so, the target that was used for generating the base-
line design was set as the target median capacity for all products. Additionally, a minimum
acceptable capacity was defined. These decisions significantly impact how the redesign phase is
performed, with the iterations defined based on this method of defining the target. Having a
minimum acceptable capacity made the bottleneck analysis more straightforward, since it was
clear which group of products to focus on and how much to improve. If the only target was
the median, the choice of design improvements may have been different, such as choosing the
changes with the largest impact. The minimum acceptable capacity is also a realistic target,
since having a design in which some products perform with an unacceptably low capacity cre-
ates a risk that the overall capacity of the facility over multiple campaigns could be very low if
some of these worst-case products came into production. However, this target also meant that
some equipment had to be oversized for nearly all products, simply so that very few products
could just reach the minimum acceptable capacity. Realistically, the approach should be more
pragmatic, such that having one or two products just under the minimum capacity would be
acceptable.
Redesign
For the redesign phase, a heuristic was designed in order to improve the design in as few iterations
as possible. This worked well, with the only issue being that the robustness check resulted in a
design which needed to be downsized significantly. Therefore, an additional iteration was needed
for iteration 2, although the first three iterations could be skipped so the redesign was still done
in very few total iterations.
With an approach with more iterations, it would be possible to test more ideas and combinations,
which could ultimately lead to a better, more robust result. This would require a well-defined
cost function, such that designs could be fairly compared. While selecting a model product has
a large impact on the final design, so does the method used for the redesign, especially when
the original design is far from the capacity target.

5.2
Use cases and benefits
With the facility designed in this case being fictional, it is important to consider how this work
can be applied practically, as well as how else the results are beneficial.
The most obvious and significant use case for the developed workflow is the design of a new
pilot scale production facility. As mentioned in the introduction, there is a need for increased
capacity in production for clinical trials, and such facilities must be flexible. The state-of-the-art
workflow in Novo Nordisk described in Section 1.2.2 has a significant risk by only relying on a
few products. As was shown in the results of this work, it is possible that the model product
is especially good, and that many other products will be operationally infeasible or have low
capacity. The implementation of the suggested workflow has the benefit of ensuring that the
facility design is flexible enough for all possible products in the scope to perform with suﬀicient
capacity. Even the flexibility analysis alone could be used to test proposed designs and evaluate
their flexibility, without the proposed redesign method, which would be beneficial.
This workflow also eliminates the need to know what products to design for, since no real
products are needed at all.
With the current state-of-the-art, the products selected as the
model products can be older, since many of the products in the development pipeline are too
immature to be a model product for a phase 3 facility design. By instead defining the product
variation, future products can be considered, and any new technology could be incorporated
into the design without the need for any real product recipes.
The results from this work could also be applied outside of pilot scale pharmaceutical production.
Other industries that need flexible production facilities for batch production which are designed
with capacity modelling could make use of this new workflow, such as food production or other
types of manufacturing.
Lastly, the results could be applied back to drug development. With some further analysis of the
results, it would possible to identify critical points of the product recipe which are most likely
to impact production capacity. This feedback could then be given in order to develop products
with higher expected capacity.
5.3
Future work
Considering how the different decisions and assumptions made impacted the final result, and
what would be needed for a practical implementation of the suggested workflow, some recom-
mendations are made for future work.
Product sampling
The first point for future work would be to further examine the product sampling method. An
investigation could examine if it is necessary to use so many products, or if it is possible to
capture the full space with fewer, such as by only using the edge points. Part of this analysis
could be performing a robustness check where a new portfolio of products is generated, but the
baseline design is the same. This would allow for a check of whether this method of product
88
==PAGE:102==
sampling covers the entire sample space.
Additionally, if the product portfolio should be more realistic, the use of probability distributions
for the various setpoints could be done. This way, a better assessment of how important it is
that the design caters to the worst-case products can be made.
Selection of model product
A critical part of this workflow is the reliance on a model product to generate the baseline design.
Further work could be done to investigate how to select the model product in a way that gives
the best design and the least need for iterations in the redesign phase.
Flexibility analysis
The focus of this work was the integration of flexibility analysis into the design workflow. How-
ever, if the flexibility analysis was to be used on its own, it could be interesting to investigate the
flexibility index problem rather than the flexibility test problem (both defined in Section 2.3.1).
This way, an assessment could be made of what setpoint ranges are feasible for the facility
and/or result in a satisfactory capacity, rather than defining ranges for setpoints and evaluating
how the design performs for those ranges.
Alternative workflows
As proposed in Section 4.6.2, another point for future work would be to test these alternative
workflows to achieve a more robust workflow. The purpose of these alternatives is to achieve
a more optimal design, for which a cost function is needed.
For the purpose of this work,
the workflow successfully generates designs for which all products meet the capacity targets.
However, in a real case with a cost function, it would be of interest to implement this into the
workflow to ensure that the result is more optimal in terms of this.
Increasing modelling detail
Another focus for future work could be increasing the level of detail of the model by incorporating
factors like manning, utility constraints, and cleaning limitations. This would not only allow
for a more precise assessment of capacity but also improve the accuracy of the cost function.
Currently, setting the target as facility capacity requires estimating eﬀiciency losses due to
manning, tact, and success rates to calculate the expected capacity.
By including manning
and tact in the model, scheduled throughput could be used as the capacity measure, providing
greater accuracy. Additionally, incorporating manning costs into the cost function would make
it even more accurate.

6
Conclusion
Returning to the problem definition given in Section 1.3, the purpose of this thesis is to:
”Develop a workflow for a rigorous and systematic process design for a flexible pilot scale
production facility with an unknown product portfolio”.
The first step was to propose a workflow that incorporated flexibility analysis into the design
through multiple phases, where the design was tested on a portfolio of artificially generated
products. This workflow was then tested, yielding a successful design for all products in the
generated portfolio. To check the robustness of the proposed workflow, the process was repeated
with a different model product as the starting point.
This also yielded a successful design,
although a different one.
This led to the suggestion of expanding the workflow to be more
robust, although this requires some sort of cost function to be formulated.
In the workflow, the first phase is the development of an artificial product portfolio. A sampling
method is selected and used to generate products according to the defined steps and setpoints.
Next, a random (with some constraints) product is selected as the model product and used to
generate a baseline design. This phase is common practice in the current state-of-the-art. The
workflow continues with a flexibility analysis where the baseline design is tested using the entire
portfolio of artificial products. Using these results, a redesign is performed in four iterations,
leading to a final design for which all products are feasible and reach the capacity target.
Thus, the workflow was developed successfully and could certainly be used in its current form to
design future pilot scale production facilities. The problem definition states that the workflow
should be both rigorous and systematic. The method is both of these things, with each phase
clearly defined along with each iteration in the redesign phase. However, there are some points
where a decision needs to be made without clear guidance, since there can be multiple ways to
remove a bottleneck. Therefore, it is expected that the use of one of the alternative workflows
would lead to an even more rigorous and systematic workflow.
This novel workflow provides significant benefits compared to the previous state-of-the-art for
pilot scale facility design. The vulnerability of the design to unknown future products is reduced
by quantifying the variation of future products and incorporating this into the design process.
Additionally, the reliance on very few model products to be representative is eliminated.
