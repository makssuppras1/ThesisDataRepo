Abstract
Overheating in buildings is constantly growing due to the general trend towards the rising of outdoor
temperatures and due to the air-tightening of the building envelope. This thesis refers to the potential of
reducing the overheating in Danish dwellings, with means of ventilation strategies and shading systems.
Since Denmark has set an ambitious goal for reducing the total greenhouse emissions in the next decades,
it is becoming a necessity to renovate the existing buildings for reducing their primary energy demand.
This causes overheating, which is harmful for vulnerable groups or people who are exposed to it for long
periods of time. Existing studies on the topic of energy renovation measures, which can potentially reduce
overheating and/or energy demand, are highlighted. All of the methods and inputs to IDA ICE, a simulation
tool for buildings, are described and justiﬁed. As internal loads inﬂuence the overheating signiﬁcantly, it
was decided to make an investigation on the internal loads, always keeping in mind, which one is the most
realistic one. Other investigations were performed, such as shading controller investigation. In the ﬁnal
model, the shading systems and ventilation systems were implemented to reduce the amount of overheating
hours, while also taking the energy consumption into account. Additional small studies were carried out, in
order to conclude on the eﬀectiveness of diﬀerent parameters, i.e. window opening percentage and glazing
unit, on the reduction of overheating and energy demand.
After the simulations were performed, the results were extracted and categorized to show the eﬀect of
shading systems, ventilation strategies and shading of the surrounding buildings. Consequently, it was
allowed to open the windows less, in order to investigate the eﬀect in the case when the people do not open
their windows adequately. The glazing was improved to examine if it would be possible to cope with the
overheating without implementing the mechanical ventilation systems. Thereafter, the results were justiﬁed
and discussed, and the particular outcomes were interpreted by employing IDA ICE graphs.
It was found out that the external shadings reduce the amount of overheating hours more eﬀectively than
the internal ones, whereas their inﬂuence on the energy demand is not as substantial for most of the
ventilation strategies. Mechanical ventilation strategies, such as relative humidity controlled Variable Air
Volume ventilation or Constant Air Volume ventilation set at a high airﬂow, are eﬀective at reducing
the overheating hours, compared to the renovated case with Natural Ventilation alone. It was also found
out that it primarily depends on the amount of the supplied air, whether the ventilation strategy has a
higher primary energy demand than the renovated case with natural ventilation. Reduced window opening
increases the amount of overheating hours in the apartment compared to initial window opening, while
it has almost no eﬀect on energy demand. Triple glazing, instead of double, has a large eﬀect on both
overheating and energy demand, since it causes a reduction on them both.

Resumé
En generel tendens til stigning af udetemperaturer, samt klimaskærmens tæthed, resulterer i overtempe-
ratur af bygninger. Dette opgave omhandler potentialet for at reducere overtemperaturen i danske boliger
ved hjælp af ventilationsstrategier og solafskærmningssystemer. Da Danmark har sat et ambitiøst mål for
at reducere de samlede drivhusgasemissioner i de kommende årtier, bliver det nødvendigt at renovere de
eksisterende bygninger, for at reducere deres primære energibehov. DDette forårsager overtemperaturer,
som kan være skadelige for ældre og syge mennesker eller mennesker, der er udsat for det i længere pe-
rioder eller for Eksisterende undersøgelser af energifornyelses foranstaltninger, der potentielt kan reducere
overtemperatur og/eller efterspørgsel efter energi, nævnes. Alle metoder og input til IDA ICE, et bygnings-
simuleringsværktøj, beskrives og begrundes. Da interne belastninger betydeligt påvirker overtemperaturen,
blev det besluttet at foretage en undersøgelse af de interne belastninger, idet man altid husker, hvilken der
er den mest realistiske. Undersøgelser af solafskærmnings controller blev ligeledes fuldført. I den endelige
model blev solafskærmningssystemerne og ventilationssystemerne implementeret for at reducere mængden
af overtemperaturstimer, samtidig med at energiforbruget blev taget i betragtning. Yderligere undersøgelser
blev udført med henblik på, at karakterisere eﬀektiviteten af forskellige parametre, fx vinduesåbningspro-
cent, vindue enhedens opbygning, reduktionen af overtemperatur og efterspørgsel af energi.
Efter simuleringerne blev resultaterne ekstraheret og kategoriseret, for at vise eﬀekten af solafskærmningssy-
stemer, ventilationsstrategier og skygge fra de omgivende bygninger. Det var derfor muligt at åbne vinduerne
mindre, for at simulere eﬀekten af overtemperatur, når folk ikke åbner deres vinduer tilstrækkeligt. Vinduer-
ne blev forbedret for at undersøge, om det ville være muligt at klare overtemperaturen uden at implementere
de mekaniske ventilationssystemer. Resultaterne blev herefter fortolket og diskuteret ved anvendelse af IDA
ICE grafer.
De eksterne solafskærmningssystemer reducerer mængden af overtemperaturstimer mere eﬀektivt end de
interne, hvorimod deres indﬂydelse på energibehovet ikke er så væsentlig for de ﬂeste ventilationsstrategier.
Mekaniske ventilationsstrategier, som fx relativ luftfugtighedsstyret Variable Air Volume ventilation eller
Constant Air Volume ventilation, indstillet til høj luftgennemstrømning, er eﬀektive til at reducere over-
temperaturstimerne sammenlignet med naturlig ventilation. Det bliver også vist, at det primært afhænger
af mængden af den tilførte luft, om ventilationsstrategien har et højere primært energibehov end naturlig
ventilation. Reduceret vinduesåbninger øger mængden af overtemperaturstimer i lejligheden i sammenlig-
ning med den indledende brugte vinduesåbninger, mens den næsten ikke har nogen eﬀekt på energibehovet.
Trippelruder, i stedet for dobbeltruder, har en stor eﬀekt på både overtemperatur og efterspørgsel efter
energi, da det medfører en reduktion af dem begge.

Preface
This thesis was prepared in fulﬁllment of the requirements for acquiring a M.Sc. in Engineering in Sustain-
able Energy, Energy Savings study line at the Department of Civil Engineering, DTU Byg.
The thesis deals with the overheating risk in renovated residential buildings and preventive strategies as
the primary objective. The project was supervised by Associate Professor Daria Zukowska-Tejsen and
Associate Professor Jakub Koloarik.
This project corresponds to 30 ECTS, developed in the summer and autumn of 2018. This thesis is a
part of a project, which has been privately funded by the Landowners Investment Foundation (Landowners
Investment Foundation (Grundejernes Investeringsfond (Danish)), 2018) and it is called "Reduction of
overheating risk in multistory dwellings in connection with facade renovation".

Chapter 1
Introduction
Reduction of energy consumption and improvement of the indoor climate have been on the agenda in the
building sector in recent years. The EU’s goal is to reduce its total greenhouse gas emissions by 30% by
2020 compared to the levels of 1990, and by 80% by 2050 (European Parliament, 2010). Denmark has set
an even more ambitious goal of being completely free of fossil fuel by 2050 (Danish Government, 2014).
Energy consumption in buildings, including primarily energy used for heating, ventilation, domestic hot
water and lighting, accounts for around 40% of the total energy consumption in Europe (Economidou et
al., 2011). Eﬃciency of the energy use in buildings plays therefore a crucial role in achieving the political
goals. At the same time, newly built buildings account for only about 1% of the building stock in many
developed countries. Thus, a reduction of energy use in buildings can be only achieved with a focus on
the existing buildings. The residential stock is the biggest segment with an EU ﬂoor space accounting for
75% of the building stock and should be therefore in focus. Commonly applied renovation approach aims
to limit heat transfer through the building envelope (thermal insulation, window replacement, tightening
of building envelope). These solutions lead to energy savings during the heating season, but at the same
time to high indoor temperatures during periods with higher solar heat gains. Therefore, even though
energy renovation in Danish buildings is needed, its potential disadvantages should not be disregarded, e.g.
overheating and poor indoor air quality due to air-tightening.
Increased energy eﬃciency and reduction of CO2 emissions are major cornerstones of the Denmark’s build-
ings regulations. For instance, new and renovated residential buildings are better insulated and air-tight in
order to prevent heat loss through transmission and uncontrolled air ﬂows. Furthermore, in Danish housing
market, ventilation aims to supply unconditioned fresh air in the occupied space, but not simultaneous cool-
ing of the air. Thus, there is a risk of overheating, especially in the spring and the summer. This eﬀect will
be even greater due to the general trend towards the rising of outdoor temperatures as a result of climate
change (Hacker, Holmes, Belcher, & Davies, 2005). According to Danish Building Regulations 2018 (BR18,
2018), the operative temperature in residential buildings should not exceed 27 °C for more than 100 hours a
year and 28 °C for more than 25 hours a year. In many existing homes in Denmark, the temperature during
the spring and the summer is higher. The problem arises in both renovated and non-renovated dwellings,
whereas people spending more time indoors the increasing number of them working from home, makes the
problem even bigger.
There are numerous studies, which have demonstrated that an excessive indoor temperature aﬀects health,
well-being and human productivity negatively (Hub, 2012). For the majority of the population, short-
term overtime periods of overheating are a matter of thermal discomfort and lower productivity, but if this
condition is chronic, it can lead to health problems and develop into serious illnesses. For certain vulnerable

groups, such as infants, children, elderly, obese and people suﬀering from chronic illnesses, overheating may
have signiﬁcant health consequences in even shorter periods (Brown & Walker, 2008). In addition, high
temperatures during the night results in deterioration of sleep quality (Raymann, Swaab, & Van Someren,
2008) and therefore reduction of concentration during the next day (Strøm-Tejsen, Mathiasen, Bach, &
Petersen, 2016). It has been reported that a change of even 1°C in skin temperature can aﬀect sleep quality
especially for the elderly (Raymann et al., 2008).
Although overheating is diﬃcult to be precisely deﬁned, there is a more abstract deﬁnition; overheating
is the result of internal (occupants, appliances, domestic hot water systems) and/or external (solar gains,
gains through the envelope, urban microenvironment) heat, which builds up indoors and creates conditions,
namely high temperatures perceived as discomfort by the occupants (Kolokotroni & Heiselberg, 2015).
Overheating in buildings is emerging as a challenge both at the design stage and during operation of the
buildings. This is due to a number of reasons. High performance standards aim to reduce heating demand
by utilizing high insulation levels (Kolokotroni & Heiselberg, 2015). At the same time, in cold climates
especially, low energy design makes the windows bigger and restricts the inﬁltration (Richards Partington
Architects, 2012). Meantime, occurrence of higher external temperatures during the cooling season due
to climate change and increase of internal heat gains during operation intensify the problem, since these
changes have not usually been considered at the design stage (Kolokotroni & Heiselberg, 2015).
There is a variety of ways to reduce overheating hours in a multistory building. Natural ventilation is
a ﬁrst option, but it is not always recommended to open the windows, due to reduced cooling potential
(when overheating, there are also high temperatures in the ambient), noise and safety. Another solution
could be mechanical ventilation, which is also examined in this report, but it could lead to higher cooling
demand. Solar heat gains have the highest contribution to overheating. One possible solution is to limit the
sun’s contribution through windows in the facade, which has been proven eﬀective in residential buildings
(Dolmans, Hall, Dutoo, & Seppänen, 2010). Thus, solar shading and special glazing units can be used
to reducing the eﬀect of the sun on the facade of the building. Hutchins (2015) proves that the dynamic
solar shading can lead to energy savings associated with ventilation of more than 70% for southwest-facing
facades for the buildings located in Stockholm.
Objectives
This study investigates the potential of reducing overheating in three types of old residential buildings
located in Copenhagen, which were constructed in the period of 1850- 1960, after the necessary renovation
for reducing the energy demand. The main focus is to examine the potential of the facade renovation. More
speciﬁcally, the main aim is answering the following questions:
• Is the overheating reduced by adding shading devices on the windows?
• Is the installation of mechanical ventilation necessary, in order to prevent overheating?
• Which is the impact of the shading devices on overheating and energy demand?
• Which is the impact of the ventilation strategies on overheating and energy demand?
• Which is the impact on overheating if the opening of the windows is reduced to half?
• Which is the impact on overheating and energy demand, when the initial glazing is changed for a
better one (lower U-value and lower g-value)?
• Is the inﬂuence of the aforementioned parameters same for all the Building types?

Chapter 2
Literature review
Energy renovation measures can be categorized as active and passive. The passive ones reduce the energy
demand of the building itself. Examples of passive measures could be the insulation and improve of air-
tightness of the building envelope to reduce the heating demand, shading of the windows to reduce the
cooling demand, or change of windows (fenestration) to improve the thermal bridges. On the other hand,
active energy renovation measures intend to produce the same or better comfort with less energy output,
making energy supply more eﬃcient.
These measures include heating, cooling and ventilation systems
(Gustafsson, 2017).
Therefore, there are four main types of renovation practices: insulation and air-tightness, fenestration, solar
shadings and ventilation strategies. This section conducts a literature review by comparing each type with
regards to overheating, primary energy consumption and daylight.
2.1
Insulation and air-tightness
This section presents the common insulation practices, which are implemented in order to renovate a
residential building along with practices for tightening the building envelope.
Thermal insulation is one of the most signiﬁcant building components in terms of reducing the buildings’
energy consumption. Building retroﬁts can improve heat retention by limiting thermal transmittance and
air inﬁltration (Psomas, 2017). According to Building Regulations 2018 (BR18, 2018), insulation must be
included to new buildings’ construction, whereas for the old buildings no such requirement has to be met.
During renovation, a layer of insulation is added to reduce the heat loss through the building envelope.
External walls can be insulated in two ways: external (outside on the facade) or internal (inside the
building). Both solutions have advantages and disadvantages.
External insulation is often the most beneﬁcial solution in terms of energy savings, since it almost eliminates
the thermal bridges, as shown in Figure 2.1. More speciﬁcally, from an economic and technical point of
view, external insulation is often the most advantageous solution, because it is easier to implement and
less labor-intensive than internal insulation, while it does not reduce the internal (usable) area (Knowledge
Center for Energy Savings in Buildings, 2018).
For buildings constructed in 1960’s and 1970’s it is recommended to have external insulation, which should

be more than 175 mm (Knowledge Center for Energy Savings in Buildings, 2018). Since in this study no
such buildings are examined, another solution is to keep the previous brick wall and cover it with insulation,
after which another layer of brick wall, similar to the original one, is constructed again. This solution is
recommended for buildings constructed from 1850 to 1970 and the minimum required insulation is 125 mm
(Knowledge Center for Energy Savings in Buildings, 2018).
Internal insulation can be a suitable solution, if external insulation is eliminated. In many older buildings,
such as Type 1, described in Section 3.1, external insulation will aﬀect the cultural and architectural value
of the building. This applies to the facade towards the street view especially, if the facade is protected
from Danish law (fredede) and worth preserving. The possibility of destroying facades, which have heritage
value and are protected by the law, is the main reason for using internal insulation. Insulation on the inside
has a lot of disadvantages and should therefore be the last option. In the case of internal insulation, it is
important to assure that water vapor coming from the indoor air cannot penetrate the insulation towards
the facade. This could cause condensation on the cold surface and moisture problems. This could cause
moisture damage and mould issues. In order to avoid this situation, it is necessary to implement an eﬃcient
vapor barrier, which is located close to the internal walls and ﬂoors. Thermal bridges probably will not
be avoided, as illustrated on Figure 2.1 (Kjerulf & Toft, 2017). Knowledge Center for Energy Savings in
Buildings (2018) recommends the internal insulation for buildings, which have been constructed between
1850 and 1920, due to their architectural value. Nevertheless, it is advised that the insulation is up to 100
mm due to possible moisture problems. Furthermore, it is crucial to move installations, which are placed
along the outer walls, mostly radiators (Munch-Andersen, 2008).

Another way applied during renovation of buildings is closing the leaks to reduce the inﬁltration rate of
unconditioned air. Leaks are usually noticed in places, where building envelope is penetrated, e.g. for
electrical installations, exhaust ducts and chimneys. In addition, leaks are observed around older windows
and doors. This practice could help with improving the energy performance of the older residential buildings

of envelope tightening in U.S. and found that this renovation strategy alone can reduce the energy demand
of a home up to 4500 kWh for climate conditions similar to Copenhagen. This is especially relevant, if the
renovation includes installation of a mechanical ventilation system, since the eﬃciency of the heat exchanger
in mechanical ventilation system depends on the air tightness of the envelope as mentioned by Mortensen
and Bergsøe (2017).

2.2
Fenestration
Windows are fundamental components of the buildings, because they provide view/ vision to the outdoor
environment, natural ventilation, passive solar gains and daylighting, which has been proven important for
the well-being of the people. However, since they have substantially higher U-values in comparison to the
other components of the building envelope, their contribution to the raise of energy consumption is crucial
(Cuce & Riﬀat, 2015).
(Jelle et al., 2012) mention that windows are responsible for up to 60% of the total energy consumption of
a building. Due to the signiﬁcance of the windows both in energy consumption and on the well-being of
the occupants, attention will be paid in this thesis to achieve the best combination.
Window consists of a few main components and they all have to be calculated in order to have the total
U-value of the fenestration system.
This includes the glazing itself, which can also be described as a
transparent insulating material, the coatings, which serve to control solar gain, the spacing between the
panes, the gas contained in it, and lastly the frame.
The use of solar gains can meet a substantial share of the heating demand, even in cold climates. In a
building heated by passive solar gains, windows are oriented and arranged so as to optimize the utilization
of solar gains. Due to minimizing heating demand in residential buildings and popularity of large window
areas, passive solar design is commonly used in low-energy architecture (Marsh, 2011).
In accordance
with this, one widely accepted way of constructing low-energy residential buildings has been to have large
windows facing south to gain as much solar heat as possible penetrating the south side and smaller windows
towards the north to minimize losses on the north side. This approach has also been supported by research
on the selection of appropriate window size and the thermal performance of various types of windows
(Gasparella, Pernigotto, Cappelletti, Romagnoni, & Baggio, 2011), which has shown that orienting the
largest window area to the south gives the lowest space-heating demand. Moreover, other research studies
also indicated that, depending on the glazing type, the overall energy needed for heating decreases with
an increase in window size to the south and that southern windows should be as large as possible if the
right glazing is used (Vanhoutteghem, 2013). Nevertheless, if the windows are large, without having low
U-value, the heating demand might increase due to the heat loss, whereas if large windows are installed in
the south without any shading device, there is a risk for overheating.
2.2.1
Ideal window
When the sun rays reach glazing material, it is either transmitted, reﬂected, or absorbed by the glazing
depending on its optical properties. Sun rays consist of visible and invisible radiation and over a broad
spectrum of wavelengths. Solar energy is distributed diﬀerently among these spectra. As it can be seen
in Figure 2.2, the solar energy distribution is diﬀerent for various wavelengths (Chen, 2004). Solar energy
from invisible solar spectrum comprises more than 50% of the solar energy. Controlling the invisible solar
spectrum using diﬀerent glazing materials, and coatings can play a signiﬁcant role in building energy savings.
(Rezaei, Shannigrahi, & Ramakrishna, 2017)

Five key parameters are used to evaluate the performance and characteristics of glazing and windows: Over-
all heat transfer coeﬃcient (U-value), visible transmittance Tvis (also VT, LT), solar heat gain coeﬃcient
g-value (SHGC), solar transmittance (ST), and emissivity (ϵ). The U-value indicates heat loss through
a window arisen from indoor and outdoor environment temperature diﬀerence (Yianoulis, Giannouli, &
Kalogirou, 2012). Tvis is a factor for visibility of glazing material (Jelle et al., 2012) and is the portion of
visible light that passes through the window (Cuce & Riﬀat, 2015). The g-value is the measure of solar
thermal energy transmitted directly or indirectly (absorbed and then transmitted inwards) and ranges from
0 to 1, for which higher value means higher solar heat transmitted. Solar transmittance is the fraction
of energy transmitted directly, and it should always be lower than g-value. Finally, emissivity is the heat
radiated, when compared to a black body’s emissivity. Its value ranges from 0 to 1 (Yianoulis et al., 2012),
where emissivity 1 is the black body’s characteristic.
Before describing the types of windows in the following chapter, the mechanism, that solar radiation aﬀects
overheating through the windows, needs to be described. For a better perception, it would be beneﬁcial
to keep in mind Figure 2.3. As aforementioned, there is the short and the long-wave radiation, where the
short one contains the visible light. If a clear glass was installed, both the short and the long-wave radiation
would penetrate the building. That means that the radiation would not only heat up the building, but also
the window and the facade, which would transfer the heat in the space also through conduction. Thus,
low U-value of a glazing unit is signiﬁcant for avoiding heat transfer through conduction. Low g-value, on
the other hand, means low solar energy transmittance through the window, which would be beneﬁcial for
overheating conditions, but it would aﬀect negatively the heating demand. At the same time, the long wave
radiation would be reﬂected and/or absorbed by the furniture in the apartment, depending on the color of
the furniture, and would remain in the space for shorter or longer period of time, respectively.

2.2.2
Types of glazing
There is a large variety in glazing types, while the most common used in residences is the clear glass. Its
visible transmittance can reach up to 0.91 for a clear glass of 4 mm, while it has also high g-value, namely
the solar energy transmitted in the building, and this can lead to overheating and glare during the summer
(Rezaei et al., 2017). Clear glass has almost perfect colour rendering index, which is a quantitative measure
of the ability of transmitted daylight through the glazing to portray a variety of colors compared to those
seen under the daylight without the glazing. Its scale varies from 1 to 100. This index should be taken into
account during the analysis, since if it is low, the colours in the room are distorted and the view to the
outside is less appealing.
Another type of glass is the tinted glass, which is clear glass with metal components, aﬀecting the colour
as well as the optical properties of the glass (Rezaei et al., 2017). Tinted glass has a substantially worse
rendering index, compared to clear glass. Tinted glass reduces solar transmittance, while decreasing visi-
bility. At the same time, it warms up and reradiates the heat inside the room (Leftheriotis & Yianoulis,
2012). Therefore, it is not considered as an optimal solution for implementation in residential buildings.
Multi-pane windows are used in environments, where heat losses have to be avoided. By increasing
the number of panes and with the use of ﬁled gas insulators in between, multi-pane windows can be
manufactured and employed to mitigate the heat transfer coeﬃcient. Air, argon, krypton and xenon gas
are used to ﬁll between the glass panes. There is an optimum distance between each glass pane to minimize
heat transfer through ﬁlled gas. This optimum space is less for xenon compared to air, argon and krypton.
A study in diﬀerent climates of Turkey illustrated that if the double-pane window is replaced with triple-
pane window or the triple pane with quadraple, the heat loss can be reduced up to 50% or 67% respectively.
(Arıcı, Karabay, & Kan, 2015). Cost wise, however, they do not reﬂect the optimal solution, because the
==PAGE:28==

gases used to ﬁll the gaps are quite expensive and the cost of the glazing itself raises (Thalfeldt, Pikas,
Kurnitski, & Voll, 2013). Thus, they are not preferred in the market.
Furthermore, multi-pane windows can also improve the sound insulation, which is one of the reasons why
they should be preferred.
The heat transfer coeﬃcient is aﬀected also by the frame; its heat transfer
coeﬃcient and its glazing area to frame ratio (Jelle et al., 2012). Figure 2.4 depicts glazing types that use
diﬀerent coatings and glasses (Cuce & Riﬀat, 2015).
Low emissivity coatings have become popular in the glazing industry during the last few years. When
a glass is radiated with the sun, solar energy is absorbed and its temperature increased, while the heat
will be reradiated inside and aﬀect the temperature of the building. This phenomenon can be avoided
with the use of low-E coatings, which will reﬂect a big part of the radiation. Furthermore, low-E coatings
have high reﬂectance in the infrared region (wave length 3-50 µm) and high light transmittance, despite
their low emissivity. In other words, low-E coatings are favorable in cold conditions, where the extra heat
in the building will reduce the heating demand, while they have a great potential to limit overheating in
residences.

2.3
Solar shadings
Potentially eﬀective renovation strategy for buildings is the installation and use of solar shading systems.
The most important reason for using a shading device is to prevent the penetration of direct sunlight and
solar radiation into the building during the warm periods. This will oﬀer a signiﬁcant reduction of the
==PAGE:29==
2.3 Solar shadings
13
cooling loads, as reported by (Thalfeldt et al., 2013) for a cold climate. Another reason for installing solar
shadings could be that the solar shadings are installed to maintain occupants’ privacy. There are several
factors to be taken into account, when designing the glazed facades with shading devices in any building,
such as the Building type, the natural light prospects and the latitude.
Especially, building form and
orientation play signiﬁcant role on the shading device types. For example, if the building is located on a
high latitude in northern hemisphere, the sun rays will be oﬀered in abundance ﬁrst on the south, east and
west facade of the building. The type of shading device used has an eﬀect on providing desirable daylight,
thermal comfort and visual comfort levels (Lim & Kim, 2010). While choosing, the cost-eﬀectiveness should
also be considered (Kuhn, Bühler, & Platzer, 2000).
However, during the heating season, it is strongly advised to let the sunlight in, for the heating demand
to be reduced (Kuhn et al., 2000). This can be achieved with a simple control strategy (Thalfeldt et al.,
2013).
Solar shadings though have also disadvantages, such as obstructing daylight. It has been reported that
good daylight conditions in residential spaces reduce the use of electrical lighting, which leads to energy
savings and reduction in CO2 emissions. Besides that, daylight controls the biological clock and is therefore
an important regulator of human physiology and performance (Duﬀy & Wright Jr, 2005). Therefore, it is
important that the eﬀect of the diﬀerent available sun-shading solutions at daylight level is monitored, as
well as the distribution and quality of daylight in living spaces. In this way, visual discomfort of residents
can be avoided. It has to be noted that visual contact with the exterior is a signiﬁcant aspect even for
the occupants’ visual comfort.
A study of Wienold and Christoﬀersen (2006), performed in Freiburg,
Germany, showed that many people will accept some disturbance by glare in order to be able to see the
outside.
Restriction or even blocking of the view to the outside, which has been proven to inﬂuence
occupant satisfaction and productivity (Thalfeldt et al., 2013). Insuﬃcient daylight levels can cause lower
concentration, impaired performance, decreased well-being, sleep disturbances and tendencies to winter
depression (Boubekri, Cheung, Reid, Wang, & Zee, 2014). Correct daylight increases the visual perception
and gives a better color perception than most electrical lamps.
Visual contact with the outside world
through windows aﬀects people’s state of mind, and it is proven that it increases productivity and makes
people feel happier and less stressed (O’Connor, Lee, Rubinstein, & Selkowitz, 1998).
When deep renovation is performed in old residential buildings, daylight is one of the factors chosen to
be maintained for psychological and performance reasons. Therefore, it is a real challenge to choose the
proper solar control systems in order to both prevent overheating and to ensure enough daylight. At the
same time, the daylight during the winter plays double role: it reduces the heating demand, due to the
increased solar gains, whilst if solar shading is employed, may result to raising of the heating demand. The
use of some types of solar devices can also increase the U-value of the window. In that way, a large amount
of solar radiation can be reﬂected back outside through the window, also in the case of internal shadings,
although the reﬂection will not be as eﬃcient (Tsangrassoulis, 2015).
Unfortunately, it is not easy to select solar control systems correctly or properly operated. There are plenty
of studies researching the occupants’ preferences and interactions with shadings, mostly in oﬃce spaces and
commercial buildings (Mahdavi, Mohammadi, Kabir, & Lambeva, 2008), (Hong & Lin, 2013), but almost
none, which concludes for residential buildings (R. V. Andersen, Toftum, Andersen, & Olesen, 2009). When
the luminance level at a working plane is higher than accepted by the occupants and the reﬂections are
disturbing the vision, occupants are likely to activate the shadings in order to decrease the amount of
daylight transmittance through the windows (Hoﬀmann & Lee, 2015). It has to be mentioned that not all
the occupants are aware of the proper operation of the solar shadings, in order to ensure satisfying daylight
levels (Hoﬀmann & Lee, 2015). Thus, a solution could be, occupants to be taught how to operate solar
shadings by the practitioners.
There are several types of shading devices, both ﬁxed and movable ones (Bellia, Marino, Minichiello, &
Pedace, 2014). In Figure 2.5, the external and intermediate shadings are shown, although both external
and internal will be used in this study.

If shading devices are mounted on the exterior, they can block solar radiation eﬃciently, before it penetrates
through the window glazing. However, when if are mounted to the interior, far-IR radiation reradiated from
the interior surfaces cannot pass through the glass. Therefore, the heat is trapped inside the building. This
results in the increase in cooling loads during overheating period (Kim, Lim, Lim, Schaefer, & Kim, 2012).
Venetian blinds
Venetian blinds are widely used in commercial buildings for adjusting the daylight penetrating through the
glass to the interior. They are eﬀective on providing privacy, visual and thermal comfort. They are made
up of separate, equally-spaced horizontal louvers (Kirimtat, Koyunbaba, Chatzikonstantinou, & Sariyildiz,
2016). The biggest advantage for the venetian blinds application is the movability of the system. The
slats or louvers can be positioned in a way that they reﬂect the direct solar radiation towards the ceiling,
providing pleasant daylight conditions by reﬂecting it and distributing it in the whole space (Slipek, 2017).
Roller blinds
Roller blinds are simple, relatively inexpensive and widely applied devices and thus they are are used
extensively for managing daylight and glare control with a range of manual and motorized applications in
both residential and commercial buildings (Tzempelikos & Shen, 2013). Both material and color of the
roller blinds have signiﬁcant impact on its transmittance, reﬂectance and absorbance and thus also on its
performance. Therefore the roller blinds should be carefully selected, depending on the location, orientation
and function of the building (Slipek, 2017). They have signiﬁcant impact on space lighting, heating and
cooling. According to recent studies, the automated versions show eﬀective performance on energy savings
(Tzempelikos & Shen, 2013).
Awning blinds
Awning blinds are included in the external solar shading systems category and they are non-coplanar shading
systems. Similarly like roller blinds, the solar-protecting material is fabric, which has to be selected carefully,
according to the parameters of each building. However, awning blinds, unlike roller blinds, are tilted. As
a result, they do not block the daylight to the extent that roller blinds do (Slipek, 2017), while they also
provide view to the outside to some extent. That is why they may have worse overheating eﬀectiveness,
compared for example to venetian blinds, if the latter have their slats in a vertical position (totally closed).

2.4
Ventilation strategies
Ventilation through natural or mechanical methods is an essential part of building operation for comfortable
and healthy environments (Kolokotroni & Heiselberg, 2015). The type of installed ventilation system and
ventilation control strategy is in high degree dependent on regulation requirements, climatic conditions,
installation and operational cost, building and site characteristics, thermal loads, and design preferences
(Dimitroulopoulou, 2012). Balancing between high air quality and low energy consumption in buildings is
crucial, whereas it can be proved challenging.
The dominating ventilation system in residences in Europe is natural “stack or wind driven” ventilation
(Litiu, 2012). Natural ventilation, though, causes higher heating energy consumption and it can cause
draft.
Furthermore, it is not certain that occupants will open the windows during the winter, which
can lead to poorer indoor air quality. For these reasons, when new low energy buildings are constructed,
mechanical ventilation is installed. Specially, in Nordic climates, in new or retroﬁtted buildings air tightness
is improved, which leads to poor indoor air quality unless accompanied by mechanical air supply (Villi,
Peretti, Graci, & De Carli, 2013). Nevertheless, in existing buildings it is not always possible to install
mechanical ventilation buildings during renovation due to lack of space available for ducts and Air Handling
Unit.
(Ridley, Fox, Oreszczyn, & Hong, 2003) analyzed the impact of window replacement on the inﬁltration
rate of dwellings and recommended controllable ventilation not only to avoid moisture problems but also
to comply with regulations. Thus, mechanical ventilation systems are necessary to improve air quality with
minimum air change rates (0.5 ACH) (Dimitroulopoulou, 2012). The most common systems could be total
air volume systems, e.g. Constant Air Ventilation (CAV), where they provide a constant airﬂow, regardless
of the occupancy or the temperature. Another solution, which is also included in the total air volume
category, is Variable Air Ventilation (VAV) systems, which provide variable, within some limits, according
to the CO2 level (occupancy) or temperature or relative humidity. VAV systems save energy, since they do
not provide their maximum airfow constantly. On the contrary, they are adjusting their airﬂow to provide
good Indoor Air Quality (IAQ).
In Denmark it is necessary to include heat recovery in the designed ventilation system. Mechanical ventila-
tion systems in Danish dwellings do not usually have a heating coil, since it is not cost-eﬀective. Without
heat recovery, unconditioned, sometimes below 0°C air, would enter the space during the winter months,
leading to increasing substantially the heating demand and oﬀsetting all the potential energy savings that
were gained by the renovation. Heat recovery is supplying heat without any additional operational cost.
(Smith, 2015) mentions speciﬁcally, that controllable mechanical ventilation should utilize heat recovery
to improve air quality, reduce heat losses in temperate climates and increase the eﬃciency of the system
simultaneously.
Another solution which totally diﬀerent from the aforementioned ones is single-room ventilation units. This
is a good solution for the cases of buildings, where there is no space for ducts. They occupy openings or
drilled holes in the façade, which can minimize the planning, required space, labor and frictional losses
associated with duct installation. Single-room units can also limit issues with biological growth in ducts,
spread of smoke and ﬁre (Smith, 2015). Nevertheless, this unit will not be used in this particular project,
since some of the buildings have architectural value.

3.3
Internal loads
In the present project, three diﬀerent variations of occupancy and equipment schedules were considered.
The ﬁrst internal load combination follows the Danish guideline for indoor climate calculations (Vorre et
al., 2017). The second type of internal loads is constant, which means that both the occupants are in the
apartment and the equipment is on all the time. The third case of internal loads is the variable one and the
way it has been built is believed to be the most realistic of the three. It is recognized that a realistic schedule
for the internal loads is something extremely diﬃcult to obtain, especially as a general rule, without having
examined the speciﬁc apartment and the people living in. Therefore, variable represents a schedule close
to reality for this project. The diﬀerent parameters for the internal loads are presented and described in
more detail in Sections 3.3.1, 3.3.2, and 3.3.3.

2018), is assumed in this case. This load has been then transformed to occupancy density according to the
heat production calculated by IDA ICE for one person at 1 MET. IDA ICE states that an average person
with metabolic rate 1 MET produces 105 W of heat.

3.5
Shading variations
The selection of the shading devices in this project was based on another master thesis (Paparizou, 2018),
which was performed a few months before the current one. Thus, these shadings will be taken to perform
this study.
The main selection criteria for choosing movable solar shadings are daylight conditions and indoor thermal
comfort. At the same time, the architectural appearance of the buildings as well as operational usability
and ﬂexibility are other selection criteria, especially for the residential buildings. Occupants also report that
it is important for them, that the shadings are convenient in terms of maintenance and cleaning (Slipek,
2017). There is a high level of uncertainty, in regards to how the occupants are operating the solar shadings
and even the literature has not concluded on that yet (R. V. Andersen et al., 2009). There has been a
study, though, which gives indications that the occupants operate the shadings with regards to the daylight
and/or solar radiation (R. V. Andersen et al., 2009).
The most signiﬁcant issue was to select the devices, which would have a minimum eﬀect at the architectural
view of the buildings. This was more important in the case of case study of Building type 1, that has a
heritage value and any changes to this building should be implemented with respect to the original design
(Slipek, 2017). That is why light colors of the devices were applied. In general, the most common options
that are already used and also are likely to integrate in the old, residential buildings in the center of a
Copenhagen, were taken into account. Table 3.29 shows the ﬁnal selection of the solar shading devices,
which were investigated. On the same table, the speciﬁcation of the chosen solar shadings is also included.
For each shading device, a daylight controlled PI controller was selected with setpoint at 500 lux, after
an investigation conducted in the topic. This investigation is presented in Section 4.3. The daylight was
selected as the controlled parameter ﬁrstly to replicate the behaviour of the occupants in regards to shading
devices as realistic as possible, and secondly because it was reported that most of these buildings do not
have enough daylight.
As illustrated, awning blinds are non-coplanar shading systems, leaving a big part of the window exposed
for the light to penetrate, whereas marquisolettes are coplanar, parallel to the building envelope until a

3.6
Ventilation strategies
Six ventilation cases for each type of building were chosen to be investigated. The buildings before renovation
were naturally ventilated (UR_NV). For the cases of renovated buildings natural ventilation (R_NV) and
four mechanical systems: Constant Air Ventilation with the minimum airﬂow required by Danish Building
Regulations 2018 (BR18, 2018) (CAVmin), Constant Air Ventilation at the minimum extraction required for
the wet rooms according to (BR18, 2018) (CAVmax), Relative Humidity controlled Variable Air Ventilation
(VAV_RH), Temperature controlled Variable Air Ventilation (VAVtemp), were simulated. The airﬂows for
each case are illustrated in both L/(s · m2) and L/s in Tables 3.30, 3.31 and 3.32 for each Building type
respectively.
CAV with minimum airﬂow is determined according to BR18 (BR18, 2018), which mentions that all resi-
dential spaces, the whole building as well as each occupied room, should have fresh air supply of minimum
0.3 L/(s2) of heated ﬂoor area. The hallway is is not required to be ventilated, since it is not occupied.
Thus, the airﬂow for the ventilated rooms, weighted against the heated rooms, is increasing. In the rooms
with exhaust, the airﬂows have been calculated for having a balanced system. CAVmax system represents
the case where design determines the supply airﬂow according to the extraction airﬂow from kitchen and
bathroom. This is determined as 20 L/s and 15 L/s respectively and the supply is designed, so that the
system is reaching balance. Therefore, many practitioners set the CAV system to operate constantly with
aforementioned airﬂows (del Carmen Bocanegra-Yanez et al., 2017).
The VAVtemp system is a solution for the apartments, located in noisy or unsafe areas, where occupants
are not willing to open the windows, except in extreme thermal conditions indoors. The VAVtemp system
was controlled with a proportional-integral (PI) controller; Tset−point = 24.5°C, K = 0.3. The control
==PAGE:54==
38
Methodology
algorithm included also a condition with respect to outdoor temperature: Tout ≤(Troom −2)°C. The
VAVRH is controlled by RH sensor, located in the exhaust duct. The VAVRH system was controlled by
proportional (P) controller; RHset−point = 50%, P −band = 20%. The minimum airﬂows for both systems
were the same as the airﬂows for the CAVmin system. The maximum airﬂows were determined to fulﬁll
the requirements to the amount of exhaust air from a bathroom and a kitchen and therefore assumed to be
300% of the minimum airﬂow.
A typical air handling unit used in Danish residences consisting of heat recovery, supply and exhaust fans
was simulated. The dry temperature eﬃciency of the heat recovery was set to 85% at the maximum airﬂow.
The set point for the supply temperature was 18 °C. The maximum speciﬁc fan Heat load (SFP) for each fan
was set to 0.5 kW/(m3 · s) fulﬁlling the requirement in the Danish building regulations (BR18, 2018) for a
maximum total SFP = 1 kW/(m3 ·s). The primary energy factor for ventilation was set to 2.5 according to
BR18 (BR18, 2018) and it is used to calculate the primary ventilation demand, whereas heating’s primary
energy factor is 0.8.
It has to be noted that for the cases of non-renovated and renovated buildings with natural ventilation,
ﬁxed inﬁltration was set up at 0.5 and 0.3 L/(s · m2) of ﬂoor area respectively. For the renovated case the
inﬁltration was set to 0.3 L/(s · m2), since this is the minimum requirement of airﬂows in the apartment
according to BR18 (2018). Non-renovated buildings are assumed to have more leaks that the renovated
ones. For all the other ventilation strategies, the inﬁltration was set as wind driven with air tightness at
1 L/(s · m2) at pressure diﬀerence 50 Pa, as BR18 (2018) mentions that when the mechanical ventilation
is installed the uncontrolled air has to be prevented for better eﬃciency of the system. Furthermore, the
minimum requirement is covered by the mechanical ventilation itself.

In the current chapter investigations, which were conducted during the course of the thesis, will be presented.

4.1
Internal loads’ investigation
As described in the Section of Methodology there are three diﬀerent variations for internal loads. ILBV is
designed according to the Danish guideline (Vorre et al., 2017), whereas ILC (constant internal loads) have
been designed for the case that the occupants are staying in the apartment all the time, e.g. elderly people
or people working from home. Finally, ILV (Variable internal loads) is considered to be the most realistic
of the three.

Another small investigation was performed by using IDA ICE, in order to develop the shading controller,
which was ﬁnally used.
The ﬁrst attempt was to combine the solar radiance on the window with the
daylight, which is provided in the space. The conditioning was that if the daylight on the reference plane
is less than 500 lux, then the shadings would not be drawn. On the other hand, if the dayligt level is more
==PAGE:69==
4.3 Shading controllers’ investigation

than 500 lux and the radiance is more than 120 W/m2 then the shadings would be drawn due to glare and
the overheating risk, respectively. The second developed controller was a daylight-determined PI controller,
with a setpoint at 500 lux, since there is a risk of insuﬃcient daylight in these buildings. Consequently, two
overheating- dominated controllers were built, one of which was using radiance-controlled PI controller with
a setpoint at 120 W/m2. The second one was using a thermostat at 120 W/m2. The diﬀerence between
the two is that PI controller is more smooth, whereas the thermostat has an on/oﬀmode.
Figure 4.13 illustrates, how the diﬀerent controllers draw the shading devices during one day in March. In
this graph, 1.0 means that the shading is fully drawn, whereas 0 means that it is not drawn at all. It is easy
to observe that the controller, which combines both radiance and daylight, opens and closes the shading
constantly, which is not realistic. Therefore this controller was rejected. As for radiance controllers, they
works as expected (drawing the shading in the morning and opening it in the afternoon, when the sun sets).
The PI shading controller for daylight was selected, ﬁrst because there are problems with the amount of
daylight in these types of buildings and second because it works smoother, which helps the simulations to
be faster and more accurate.

and interpretation

6.1.1
Inﬂuence on Overheating hours
Building type 1
As it can be seen in Figure 5.1 for Building type 1, the external shadings are the most eﬃcient in terms of
overhearing, whereas the internal shadings oﬀer a potential. This is due to the fact that the external shadings
do not allow the heat to penetrate the building envelope, and this along with the window opening and the
ventilation strategy neglects the overheating. More speciﬁcally, for Building Type 1 it has been illustrated
to be the combination of Marquisolette with Constant Air Volume at minimum has low overheating hours
and low energy demand, although many would disagree on whether CAVmin is legal.
On the other hand, the scenarios with no shading have more overheating hours overall and even for the
best ventilation strategy, the overheating hours do not go below 40. More speciﬁcally, according to Figure
5.1, the Marquisolette and external venetian blinds are the most eﬀective at reducing the overheating in the
apartment, whereas awning blinds follow with a small diﬀerence. The reason for this, is that Marquisolette
is parallel (coplanar) to the window for 0.7 meters and then it becomes non-coplanar for letting some
daylight to come in. On the other hand, awning blinds are non-coplanar throughout their length, which
results in more light penetrating the building envelope along with more heat.

reduce as much the heating demand as it would without the presence of the external shadings. On the other
hand, with the internal shadings, for all the presented cases of ventilation strategies, the eﬀect of the lack
of the surrounding buildings is larger, since the heat penetrates the building envelope before it is blocked.

6.3
Impact of window opening variations and interpretation
6.3.1
Inﬂuence on Overheating hours
The inﬂuence of the smaller window opening on the overheating hours is illustrated on Tables 5.7, 5.8, and
5.9 for Building types 1, 3, and 4. The impact of the smaller window opening is as it was expected; it
increased the amount of overheating hours, even with the use of external shadings for all Building types.
The eﬀect of higher overheating hours is normal since the solar radiation is so high that the VAVRH can
not compensate alone and it needs the windows opening for bigger cooling potential, but the windows
have lower airﬂow as shown in 5.8. Nevertheless, by employing VAVRH and external shadings, even if the
window opens at 30%, the amount of overheating hours will not exceed 100 hours for all the Building types.
6.3.2
Inﬂuence on Energy Demand
As shown in Figure 5.7, the diﬀerence between the W30 and W60 for the diﬀerent scenarios of the external
shadings and surrounding buildings is extremely small. This is due to the fact that the windows open during
the cooling period, thus there is no change in the heating demand. Moreover, the ventilation strategy used
for the analysis, is not aﬀected by the temperature itself, but by the relative humidity, which is slightly
aﬀected by the smaller intake of the ambient air.

6.3.3
Inﬂuence on Airﬂows
In the scenario, where the window opening is smaller, the airﬂow through the external is consequently
becoming lower. The trend of the mechanical inﬂow is, however remaining the same, since the VAVRH is
not aﬀected substantially by the temperature diﬀerence. The only aspect that may aﬀect the VAVRH is
that the smaller window opening reduces the amount of air with a small moisture content coming from the
ambient and thus, the VAVRH might have higher inﬂows.
6.4
Impact of glazing variation
The impact of the glazing change to a triple glazing with two solar coatings instead the double glazing is
great, both in terms of overheating and in terms of energy demand.
In terms of overheating the hours, they are decreased by at least 35% for all the Building types and shading
variations. More speciﬁcally, the decrease reaches 70% and in very few cases (Building type 1 without the
surrounding buildings and without shading devices) exceed the limit of 100 hours. This is due to the fact
that the g-value along with the Solar Transmittance value are lower and, more speciﬁcally, the g-value is
reducing by 18%, from 65% to 53% and the Solar Transmittance by 20%. That illustrates that the change
of the new glazing along with internal shadings is enough to cope with the overheating hours.
The energy demand is reduced by up to 15%, 17% and 20% for Building type 1, 3. 4 respectively.The
triple glazing has lower U-value compared to the initial (from 1.3 W/m2 ·K to 0.6 W/m2 ·K) which plays a
signiﬁcant role, especially in the Building types with the large window areas. Therefore, the energy demand
is reduced considerably for all the cases, regardless if a shading device is installed or not.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In this study the internal loads, the construction of the buildings and the control of the shading systems
were assumed in order to approach reality. In this chapter, the questions asked in the Introduction will be
answered, in order to conclude towards, which strategy is eﬀective in regards to overheating as well as not
that energy-intensive.
During this project it was proven that the overheating is reduced substantially by adding external shadings
devices. Installation of mechanical ventilation is not necessary, for reducing the overheating hours of the
apartment, at least not in the initial scenario, with surrounding buildings’ shading. Occupants open the
windows, according to perceived indoor quality among other parameters, according to R. Andersen, Fabi,
Toftum, Corgnati, and Olesen (2013). Nevertheless, it is important to assure that fresh outdoor air will be
introduced in the space in order to keep indoor air quality within acceptable levels.
As aforementioned, external shadings reduce the amount of hours over 27°and the eﬀect is greater, when the
window area is bigger. More speciﬁcally, Marquisolette and external Venetian blinds are the most eﬀective
among the external shadings, since they reduce the overheating hours from 60% up to 96%, whereas in few
cases they eliminate overheating. Internal shadings also help at reducing overheating hours, although they
have been proven not as eﬀective, since they reduce overheating to half. As for energy demand, the shading
systems do not aﬀect at all the energy demand for the small windows’ case (Building type 1). In the cases
of larger windows there is an inﬂuence, which comes from the increase of the heating demand due to the
reduction due to the external shadings of solar radiation, which penetrates the building.
The impact of some ventilation strategies is great on the overheating hours. For example VAVRH and
CAVmax is demostrated to reduce the hours over 27°C to less than half compared to the naturally ventilated
apartment (renovated). Diﬀerent ventilation strategies aﬀect in various ways the energy demand. In general,
the more air is introduced to the space the more heating demand is necessary to heat up the space, since
the air is entering the space at 18°C in the winter. Cooling demand is inﬂuenced also by the amount of air
supplied in the space, since the fans are consuming more energy.
Window opening aﬀects the overheating and in some cases the overheating hours increase fourfold, since the
cooling potential of the windows is reduces, but it has a minimal inﬂuence on the energy demand, because
the windows open only during the cooling period. A better glazing, with lower U-value and g-value, is
also reducing the occupied hours over 27°C, whereas it also reduces the total energy demand. The eﬀect is
greater, when the window area is larger, since for the initial glazing the U-value is four times higher than
the U-value of the wall, whereas with the second glazing only two times. The energy demand decrease by

The pattern inﬂuence of the aforementioned parameters is in most of the cases similar, but the amount
of inﬂuence gets bigger or smaller according to the special characteristics of the construction, i.e. window
area, wall construction, location of the buildings compared to the neighbouring ones.
Overall, external shadings have been proven extremely eﬀective for reducing overheating even by employing
a simple controller. A better glazing could be proven an asset at reducing the energy demand and the hours
over 27°C even more. As for ventilation strategies, VAVRH seems eﬀective in regards to overheating during
simulations, but there is a high degree of uncertainty in regards to occupants’ behaviour.

Chapter 8
Future work
Further work could include testing diﬀerent glazing units for other ventilation strategies and shading devices.
At the same time, windows opening percentage can be argued to be too much and smaller window opening
should be tested for other ventilation strategies than the most eﬀective one. It is also signiﬁcant to examine
the results, if the apartment is oriented diﬀerently, e.g. if the Kitchen is facing towards the south, or the
impact of opening the doors inside the apartment. Another parameter could be the ﬂoor of the apartment.
Finally, if the venetian blinds will be used, it is important to build a controller, which would regulate the
slat angle according to the position of the sun and the cut-oﬀangle, in order to provide both protection from
overheating and daylight in the space. The occupants could obtain this knowledge in the future through a
guideline.
The daylight should be also considered as a parameter to be investigated, since many of the external shading
strategies do not take into account the daylight in the space and/or the view out.

Potential of mechanical ventilation for reducing 
overheating risks in retrofitted Danish apartment 
buildings from the period 1850-1890 – A simulation-based 
study 
 
Daria Zukowska*, Jakub Kolarik, Myrto Ananida, Mandana Sarey Khanie and 
Toke Rammer Nielsen  
 
Technical University of Denmark, Department of Civil Engineering 
Brovej 118 
2800 Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark 
*Corresponding author: dz@byg.dtu.dk 
 
 
 
ABSTRACT 
 
Advancing energy efficient renovation solutions in buildings necessitate adopting high-insulation and airtightness 
to avoid heat loss through transmission and infiltration, which can result in overheating. Elevated indoor 
temperatures have a highly negative effect on building occupants’ health, wellbeing and productivity. With the 
possibility of remote working, people spend more time at home, and therefore addressing the elevated indoor 
temperatures and the overheating risks in residential buildings proves to be essential. Even more so, as these high 
temperatures during daytime are followed by consequent high temperatures during night-time, which distorts sleep 
quality. The current Danish building regulations suggest that the operative temperature in dwellings should not 
exceed 27 °C for more than 100 hours per year and 28 °C for more than 25 hours per year. However, in many new 
and renovated dwellings in Denmark the temperature during spring and summer exceeds these measures. The 
paper presents the first results from a larger study focused on typical Danish apartment buildings from the period 
1850-1950 many of which currently undergo extensive renovation. The main objective of the project is to study 
facade solutions that eliminate overheating. The present paper reports an effect of different ventilation strategies 
on overheating in renovated apartment buildings from the period 1850-1890. The investigation showed that energy 
renovation in this type of buildings, including adding insulation and exchanging windows, yielded energy saving 
of approx. 60%, but resulted in an increase of overheating hours when no mechanical ventilation system was 
added. All studied mechanical ventilation systems with heat recovery were able to decrease the overheating hours 
below limit specified by the Danish building code in the case of the building situated in the narrow street canyon. 
In the absence of shading from surrounding buildings, the CAV ventilation operating with minimum airflow 
required by the Danish building code reduced overheating hours insufficiently. 
 
 
KEYWORDS 
 
Overheating, energy renovation, residential ventilation, apartment building, energy consumption 
 
 
1 
INTRODUCTION 
 
Reduction of energy consumption and improvement of the indoor climate have been on the 
agenda in the building sector in recent years. The EU's goal is to reduce its total greenhouse gas 
emissions by 30% by 2020 compared to the levels from 1990, and by 80% by 2050 (European 
Parliament, 2010). Denmark has set an even more ambitious goal of being completely free of 
fossil fuel by 2050 (Danish Government, 2014). Energy consumption in buildings, including 
primarily energy used for heating, ventilation, domestic hot water and lighting, accounts for 
around 40% of the total energy consumption in Europe (BPIE, 2011). Efficiency of the energy 
==PAGE:133==
use in buildings therefore plays a crucial role in achieving the political goals. At the same time, 
newly built constructions account only about 1% of the building stock in many developed 
countries. Thus, a reduction of energy use in buildings can only be achieved with focus on 
existing buildings. Furthermore, the residential stock is the biggest segment with an EU floor 
space accounting for 75% of the building stock and should consequently be in focus. Commonly 
applied renovation approach aims to limit heat transfer through the building envelope (thermal 
insulation, window replacement, tightening of building envelope). These solutions lead to 
energy savings during the heating season, but at the same time to high indoor temperatures 
during periods with higher solar heat gains. 
 
Elevated indoor temperatures have a highly negative effect on occupants’ health, wellbeing and 
productivity (NHBC Foundation, 2012). With the possibility of remote working, people spend 
more time at home, and therefore addressing overheating risk in residential buildings becomes 
more important. Moreover, certain vulnerable groups as infants, children and the elderly, as 
well as the obese and people with chronic diseases mostly stay at home. Excess heat even in 
shorter periods can have significant health implications for them (Brown and Walker, 2008). 
Additionally, higher night-time temperatures are documented to increase the risk to health due 
to the inability to recover from daytime heat stress (Kovats and Hajat, 2008) and interrupt sleep 
(Raymann et al., 2008; Strøm-Tejsen et al., 2016). It has been reported that a change of as little 
as 1 K in skin temperature can affect the quality of sleep obtained, particularly in the elderly 
(Raymann et al., 2008). 
  
According to the Danish building regulations 2018 (BR18, 2018) the operative temperature in 
dwellings should not exceed 27 °C for more than 100 hours per year and 28 °C for more than 
25 hours per year. However, in many new and existing dwellings in Denmark the temperature 
during spring and summer often exceeds these measures (Psomas et al., 2016; Larsen, 2011). 
Ventilation required in new and renovated Danish dwellings is designed to supply fresh air and 
avoid moisture related problems. It is not designed to cool. Thus, there is a risk of overheating, 
especially in warm months. The effect will be multiplied by the overall trend of increased 
external temperatures due to the climate changes (CIBSE TM36, 2005). 
 
In Denmark 28% of the residential building area is associated with apartments (BPIE, 2011). A 
currently ongoing research project “Reduction of overheating in Danish dwellings by use of 
effective solar control without comprising visual comfort” focuses on buildings from the period 
1850-1950. Energy effective solutions to the problem with overheating include limiting the 
solar heat gains through the glazed parts of the facade (REHVA Guidebook no. 12, 2010) and 
effective ventilation (IEA EBC, 2018). The project’s challenge lays in identifying combinations 
of shading and glazing solutions that reduce overheating risk without disturbing appearance of 
the facade and aggravating daylight conditions. However, in the study reported in this paper the 
use of external solar shading is limited due to the architectural heritage value of the facades of 
buildings from the period 1850-1890. The paper reports results from the underlying study that 
had an objective to examine the impact of different ventilation strategies applicable in renovated 
buildings on overheating risk and energy consumption. The results will be further utilized for 
investigation of facade solutions. 
 
 
==PAGE:134==
2 
METHODS 
 
2.1 
Building and its surroundings 
 
Altogether three apartment buildings representing relevant building typologies according to 
Engelmark (2013) were identified. The present paper reports results for one of the buildings – 
a historical residential 5-storey building located in the northern area of Copenhagen – Figure 1 
(left), representing a typical apartment building from the period 1850-1890 common in Danish 
cities. Solid masonry and bricks are the main characteristics of the facades. The inner walls are 
mainly made of timber construction and bricks. Floors and staircases are made of wood. The 
roofing material is brick, slate or metal carried by a wooden structure. All windows consist of 
four openable parts where the two lower parts count for 2/3 of the window size. This type of 
buildings comprises parts of a rectangular arrangement of similar buildings with the backside 
facing to the courtyard in the middle and the front facade facing the street with other buildings 
of the same height – Figure 1 (right). 
 
    
 
Figure 1: Case study building (left) and outline of surrounding (right) 
 
The IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA ICE) software was used to model the building, 
Figure 2 (left), with and without the surrounding constructions and the greenery always omitted. 
The cases without shading from the surrounding buildings represent the apartments on higher 
floors as well as occasions where similar buildings are not located in a tight street canyon. In 
the model, the apartment was located on the first floor with a gross floor area of 56.6 m2 and 
consisted of a kitchen, living room, bedroom, small bathroom and hall – Figure 2 (right).  
 
The windows in the building before renovation were assumed to be clear double pane (4-12-4) 
with the heat transfer coefficient of glazing Ug = 2.8 W/(m2∙K), light transmittance LT = 82% 
and total solar heat transmittance g = 79%. The heat transfer coefficient of the window frame 
was set to Uf=2.0 W/(m2∙K). The fraction of the frame to the total window area was calculated 
to 39% for windows in the living room and the bedroom, and 28% in the kitchen.

Energy renovation for the simulated building included an addition of 95 mm of internal thermal 
insulation and exchange of all windows. Internal insulation brings a risk of moisture 
condensation in the construction and reduction of the internal space; however, it is often used 
in practice due to the architectural value of the facades in this type of buildings. The Knowledge 
Centre for Energy Savings in Buildings (2017) recommends the thickness of the internal 
insulation to be below 100 mm to reduce the risk of condensation. The windows were changed 
to clear double pane energy windows (4-12Ar-S(3)4) with Ug=1.3 W/(m2∙K), LT = 82% and g 
= 65%. The U-value of the window frame was changed to 1.3 W/(m2∙K) with the same frame 
sizes as before the renovation. 
 
In none of the simulated cases solar shading devices were used. 
 
2.2 
Location and weather data 
 
The building model was located in Copenhagen, Denmark. The simulation period was set from 
the 1st of January to the 31st of December 2010 using weather data by the Danish Reference 
Year, DRY 2013. 
  
2.3 
Heat loads and schedules for occupancy 
 
With respect to internal loads (heat from appliances, sensible and latent loads from occupants, 
moisture load from activities), a recent Danish guideline for indoor climate calculations (Vorre 
et al., 2017) was followed. Based on the apartment size and only one bedroom, the apartment 
was assumed to be occupied by two adults employed with standard work hours (weekdays 8:00-
16:00). The occupants were assumed constantly present in the apartment during weekends. The 
distribution of occupancy in the particular rooms followed the guideline and is shown in Table 
1. Metabolic rate of the occupants was assumed to be 1 met during the day and 0.8 met while 
sleeping. Occupant loads included both sensible and latent heat production. The schedules 
suggested by Vorre et al. (2017) include an overlap in the occupancy for common spaces and 
bedrooms to accommodate various users’ behaviour. As this overlapping leads to relatively 
high occupant heat loads during afternoon/evening hours it was decided to reduce the 
occupancy loads to 54% in order to obtain an average load of 1.5 W/m2 as assumed in the 
Danish energy frame calculations (Aggerholm, 2018).

2.5 
Moisture load from activities 
 
The moisture production from typical activities occurring in the apartment was set to 60 g/h per 
person based on Valbjørn et al. (2000). Since the guideline by Vorre et al. (2017) does not 
assume occupancy in the bathroom, the whole moisture production was added in the kitchen 
and was constant during 24 hours for the whole week. 
 
2.6

modelled using fixed infiltration of 0.5 l/(s∙m2) of the heated floor area (i.e. including external

fixed infiltration of 0.3 l/(s∙m2) of heated floor area corresponding to the minimum fresh air 
supply required by the Danish building code (BR18, 2018). Additionally, four mechanical

represented a minimum requirement of 0.3 l/(s∙m2) fresh air supply to the whole apartment as 
well as each habitable room (BR18, 2018). The CAVmax system simulated the case when

2.8 
Window, doors and infiltration 
 
A crude estimate of the occupants’ window opening behaviour was simulated using temperature 
control by a PI controller: Tset-point = 23 °C, K = 0.3. Additional conditions with respect to 
outdoor temperature: Tout  ≤  (Troom - 2) °C and the presence of at least one of the occupants in 
the apartment were also applied. The effective window opening area was assumed to be 60% 
(15% during night-time) according to Vorre et el. (2017). It was assumed that the upper parts 
of the window were never open (inconvenient due to a high position of these parts), while 60% 
of the window opening was implemented to the lower parts. Only one of the two windows in 
the living room was set to open. 
 
The VAVT system was assumed to be used in apartments located towards polluted and noisy 
street as a solution allowing minimising a need for window opening. In this case, the set point 
for window opening was 27 °C i.e. - the occupants open the windows only when the mechanical 
ventilation cannot reduce the overheating. 
 
The doors to all rooms were kept closed during the whole simulation period in order to avoid 
unrealistic large airflows between the rooms. A leak with an area of 0.01 m2 and discharge 
coefficient Cd = 0.65 was placed at each door.

Energy consumption 
 
In the present study, the energy consumption analysis included only energy demand for 
ventilation and heating. The energy consumption for lighting and domestic hot water was 
neglected. 
 
 
3 
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
 
The results regarding energy consumption and overheating are summarized in Figure 3. Energy 
renovation clearly intensified overheating regardless the presence of surrounding buildings. 
 
Figure 3: Energy consumption and a number of hours with indoor temperature over 27 °C in the zone with 
maximum overheating hours (kitchen) for the investigated ventilation strategies for cases with surrounding 
buildings (left) and without (right) 
 
The mechanical ventilation system running constantly on the minimum supply airflow 
(CAVmin) could bring the overheating hours below 100 only in the case of the building in the 
street canyon. The CAVmax and both cases with VAV systems showed ability to keep 
overheating within the required limits. 
 
==PAGE:139==
The lack of shading from surrounding buildings (e.g. apartments on higher floors or a building 
facing a square) resulted in increased overheating in summer. On the other hand, reduced solar 
gains to the building in the street canyon led to higher energy use for heating. This effect was 
the highest for the building before renovation (about 11 kWh/m2year more primary energy use 
for the shaded building), while the mean increase for the renovated building with mechanical 
ventilation was 7.7 kWh/m2year. Energy renovation reduced the energy use for heating by 57% 
and 62% for the cases with and without surrounding buildings, respectively. With respect to 
mechanical ventilation, the CAVmax system used 34-105% more electricity and approx. 70-80% 
more energy on heating compared to the other three mechanical ventilation systems. However, 
it still reduced the energy use for heating by 31-39% compared to the renovated building with 
natural ventilation. Compared to natural ventilation, the CAVmin or VAV systems reduced the 
total primary energy use by approx. 40% and 50% for the cases with and without surrounding 
buildings, respectively. Both VAV systems performed equally well in regards to the total 
energy consumption and overheating, except that the system with temperature control for the 
case with the exposed building has slightly higher energy consumption for ventilation due to 
frequent boost of the airflows during elevated temperature in the apartment. CAVmax can also 
effectively reduce the heat excess, but it results in higher electricity use for ventilation. 
 
In the present study, the overheating hours were evaluated for occupied hours for particular 
rooms of the apartment. This parameter indicated that the kitchen was the most problematic 
room. As the kitchen was oriented towards north, the overheating was rather caused by high 
internal loads from occupants and equipment per a relatively small floor area than by solar heat 
gains. Figure 4 (left) presents yearly duration curves for the operative temperature in all rooms 
for the VAVRH  system, which showed the best potential for reduction of overheating. It is clear 
from the figure that the living room had the highest amount of hours above 27 °C, when 
considering also unoccupied period. Moreover, it can be seen from the figure that the operative 
temperature in the kitchen was consistently higher than in the rest of the apartment. 
 
Figure 4: Duration curve for operative temperature for a whole year (left) and development of operative 
temperature during a week in August (right) in the case of VAVRH system without surrounding buildings 
 
Figure 4 (right) shows operative temperature development for one week in August. It illustrates 
that the operative temperature in the kitchen follows the occupancy schedule rather closely, 
while in the living room it is driven by solar gains, which besides weekend, occur when the 
apartment is unoccupied. This highlights the importance of occupancy schedules with respect 
to occupants’ exposure to elevated indoor temperatures. Several alternative occupancy 
schedules will therefore be investigated during the continuation of the present study. 
 
Window opening behaviour of the occupants is another crucial factor, when simulating 
residential ventilation. The present study applied rather crude estimate of window opening 
based on indoor temperature. The detailed studies focused on human behaviour, e.g. Andersen 
==PAGE:140==
et al. (2013), show that window opening behaviour is a complex phenomenon and is not 
necessarily driven by indoor temperature. However, the Danish energy frame calculation 
assumes venting or increased mechanical ventilation in periods when the room temperature 
exceeds 23 °C (Aggerholm, 2018). The same approach was used in the present study. 
Aggerholm (2018) recommends the mean airflow through windows during warm summer 
months equal to 0.9 l/(s∙m2). Table 4 shows an example of the monthly mean supply airflow 
through open windows (including infiltration) for the NV and VAVRH cases together with the 
monthly mean supply airflows provided by the mechanical ventilation in the VAVRH case. It 
can be seen that the airflows provided by venting in the case of the naturally ventilated building 
are larger than in the case of the building with mechanical ventilation, but the grand mean 
normalized by the total heated floor area did not exceed 0.9 l/(s∙m2).

4 
CONCLUSIONS 
 
 Energy renovation (adding insulation and exchanging windows) of a Danish apartment 
building from 1850-1890 yielded energy saving of approx. 60%. 
 At the same time energy renovation resulted in clear increase of overheating hours when no 
mechanical ventilation system was added. 
 All studied mechanical ventilation systems with heat recovery were able to decrease the 
hours with overheating below the limit specified by the Danish building code in the case of 
the building situated in a narrow street canyon. In absence of shading from surrounding 
buildings, the CAV ventilation operating with minimum airflow required by the Danish 
building code reduced overheating hours insufficiently. 
 The VAV system with a centrally located sensor for relative humidity was the most efficient 
system in the recent study. The temperature controlled VAV system gave worse 
performance than the humidity controlled one with respect to overheating while using 
similar amount of energy. This was probably caused by the increased set point for window 
opening as this system was intended to be utilized in buildings where window opening is 
not preferable due to outdoor noise or pollution.
