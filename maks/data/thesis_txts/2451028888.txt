Abstract
Spectral X-ray detectors have a wide spectrum of applications spanning from detect-
ing star explosions at the edge of the observable universe, to testing the authenticity
of paintings in museums, to creating 3D images of human organs in medical imaging.
One important performance parameter of a spectral X-ray detector is its energy reso-
lution. It is a measure of the detectors capability to distinguish between two X-rays
of similar energy. In semiconductor detectors, there are multiple sources of noise that
contribute to degrading the energy resolution. One of the main sources of noise is
the temperature. Electrons that are thermally excited contribute to what is referred
to as intrinsic leakage current or dark current in the detector. This current is added
to the signal induced by an X-ray interaction and degrades the measurement of the
energy. Several experiments were conducted to improve the energy resolution of the
detector while considering distinct factors. The work in this thesis was done using
an Amptek XR-100CdTe X-ray detector, an Advacam AdvaPIX TPX3 CdTe X-ray
detector (Timepix3), a peltier cooling system and a laboratory X-ray source. Ex-
periments were conducted in collaboration with researchers and engineers from both
DTU in Denmark and Advacam in Czech Republic. Lowering the operational tem-
perature of the detectors showed promising results. The quality of spectral images
of CdTe hybrid pixel detectors was enhanced, which reduced the intrinsic leakage
current, leading to a better energy resolution.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The importance of colour in our daily life is vital. Colour plays a lead role in distin-
guishing between different things and highlighting specific spots. It would be difficult
to try to separate between similar and different entities in a world that lacks the broad
diversity of colours. To highlight the significance of colour, imagine a world where
only black, white and shades of grey are present. Take Figure 1.1 as an example. Is
it possible to link between entities of same colour in this figure? Or is it possible to
distinguish what colours are present in this network?
Figure 1.1: A network of entities in greyscale.
When observing these entities in greyscale, certain colours tend to appear as one and
can be easily confused with each other. In delicate fields such as medicine, radiology
and security X-ray scans this can make a huge difference. The presence of colour can
provide a better classification and understatement of different things and can provide
==PAGE:22==

more information and details that can’t be obtained otherwise. When revealing the
colours of these entities as shown in Figure 1.2, a distinct perception of the figure can
be devised.
The ability to see and differentiate between colours is a result of the eye’s ability to
detect different wavelengths of visible light. The addition of colour to a greyscaled
image can increase the accuracy of the interpretation of the scene.
Similarly, X-
rays and gamma-rays have different wavelengths that would influence the way these
photons interact with matter. The impact of the presence of these ionising photons
can be better predicted by distinguishing between distinct wavelengths of X-rays.
This could be benefited from greatly in diverse fields such as in X-ray imaging facilities,
where it can assist in establishing improved protection against harmful effects that
exist in potentially radioactive environments.
Figure 1.2: A coloured network of entities [1].
Energy resolution is considered important as well for the generation of the image. It
represents a measure of the detectors capability to distinguish between two X-rays of
similar energy. In fact, energy resolution is best achieved in semiconductor detectors.
The presence of leakage current degrades the energy resolution, making it difficult
to distinguish between different energy resolutions. Therefore, reducing the thermal
excitation that occurs in the detector degrades the leakage current and improves the
energy resolution.

1.1 Background and problem motivation
3
1.1
Background and problem motivation
One of the essential parameters in X-ray and gamma-ray spectrometers is the energy
resolution. X-ray semiconductor detectors, that are classified as hybrid pixel detec-
tors, are technologies designed with direct conversion, which allows single quantum
processing of the incoming X-ray beam. These detectors work with multi-energy pro-
cessing operations that exhibit an amended capacity to separate materials with close
atomic composition.
Figure 1.3: Single energy.
Figure 1.4: Dual energy.
Figure 1.5: Multi energy.
There are three different energy detection methods, which are single, dual and multi
[2]. Single energy detection method is indirect and have one average energy data
which is related to the X-ray photon integration. The use of single energy results
in attenuation grey levels and contrast in image with poor discrimination (Figure
1.3). Another detection method which is dual energy. It is an indirect method that
has two averaged energy data which is related to the X-ray photon integration. The
use of dual energy results in very high or low energy filtering image with limited
discrimination (Figure 1.4). Whereas, multi energy detection method is direct and
has multi energy data that depends on the counting and energy measurements of
photons. The use of multi energy results in several energy bands which leads to an
image with high discrimination and better spacial resolution. Usually this method
has a high energy resolution that allows it to be implemented in many investigations
(Figure 1.5).
In spite of the fact that multi energy detection methods are considered of high energy
resolution, but still there are some factors that affect it’s energy resolution. In semi-
conductor detectors, there are multiple sources of noise that contribute to degrading
the energy resolution, but one of the main sources of noise is the temperature. Elec-
trons that are thermally excited contribute to what is referred to as intrinsic leakage
current or dark current in the detector. This is a process where lattice vibrations
provide enough energy to transfer electrons to a higher energy band (e.g. from the
valence band to the conduction band).
Electrons in the conduction band can be
swept by the applied bias voltage and their signal cannot be distinguished from the
signal induced by an interacting photon. Hence they are often referred to as leakage
current. The average number of electrons occupying the conduction band increases

In this thesis, the purpose was to enhance the energy resolution of spectral X-ray
detectors by lowering the operational temperature of the detector, thus reducing the
leakage current. It focuses on a CdTe hybrid pixel detector using a Timepix3 chip.
The CdTe material was a perfect choice as a sensor for the detector due to it’s large
energy gap of 1.44 eV at room temperature (20° C) that prevents thermally generated
charge carriers from being exited into the conduction band, which reduces the thermal
noise to acceptable levels. Besides, they have a high atomic number compared to Si,
which provides a more effective absorption of higher energetic radiation and higher
detection efficiency.
The Timepix3 chip is a readout chip ASIC designed at CERN for the Medipix col-
laboration.
It is a hybrid pixel detector that functions as a camera for radiation
having a sufficient high speed that would allow the recording of particles hitting the
detector, which in turn permits high-resolution, high-contrast, and almost noise-free
X-ray imaging. Timepix3 consists of a 256x256 pixel matrix with a 55 µm pitch and
a timing resolution of 1:56 ns. It permits the simultaneous measurement of energy
and time while utilising an event-driven data stream with a maximum data rate of
up to 5.12 Gb/s or up to 85 million hits per second.
The experimental work in this thesis was done using an Amptek XR-100CdTe X-ray
detector, an Advacam AdvaPIX TPX3 CdTe X-ray detector (Timepix3), a peltier
cooling system and a laboratory X-ray source. The XR-100CdTe is a one-pixel X-ray
detector that was used to observes the impact of distinct factors, mainly the tempera-
ture, on the energy resolution of the detector. This detector contains a thermoelectric
cooler inside that can cool the detector down to approximately -50° C.
The obtained results were used to study the effect of temperature on the energy
resolution using the AdvaPIX TPX3 detector. This detector is a multi-pixel detector
that is provided by Advacam company in Czech Republic which contains the newest
Timepix3 readout ASIC. This detector can be acquired with different types of sensors,
where CdTe material was the one used in this project. The detector is not equipped
with a cooling system, which is why a peltier cooling system was used to cool the
detector and to study the impact of temperature on the energy resolution.
The results were promising where the quality of spectral images of CdTe hybrid pixel
detectors was enhanced. The mean FWHM of 121 pixels of the AdvaPIX TPX3 CdTe
X-ray detector was decreased from 2.78 at 20° C to 2.58 at -18° C. This result shows
that lowering the operational temperature of the spectral X-ray detectors improves
it’s energy resolution.

2.1
Radiation detection & measurement
Radiation have always been present throughout man’s history in nature. It refers to
the process where energy is emitted into space in the form of waves out of it’s origin.
When this energy is deposited, partly or wholly, in a suitable medium, it can produce
an effect [5]. There are numerous sources of radiation, but for simplicity, it can be
divided into two groups, natural and man-made. Radiation includes electromagnetic,
acoustic, and particle radiation, as well as all forms of ionizing radiation [6].
Radiation is categorized into two forms, non-ionizing and ionizing. The non-ionizing
radiation doesn’t have enough energy to interact with matter and produce ions, while
the ionizing radiation, which has enough energy to interact with matter, can remove
tightly bound electrons from the orbit of an atom, causing the atom to become charged
or ionized [7]. Neutral atoms or molecules can acquire either a positive or negative
electrical charge as an effect to ionizing radiation. Moreover, ionizing radiation is
commonly divided into several types including alpha, beta, gamma, neutron and X
rays [5]. Some of these types such as gamma, neutron and X rays which are referred
to as neutral radiations, possess an indirect ionizing effect, whereas charged particles
such as alpha and beta have direct ionizing effect.
Although ionizing radiation has been present in nature, it remained unnoticed until
about 100 years ago when W.C. Röntgen opened the door for a completely new field

Human senses are not able to visualize or detect ionizing radiation, hence, for it
to be used properly, it is important to detect and measure this radiation [5]. This
is achieved through detecting and measuring the effect of radiation in a medium.
Radioactive material emits ionizing radiation without any external influences on it.
The characteristics of any radioactive substance is defined by the type and energy of
the radiation that is emitted from it. Many radiation detectors take into consideration
these characteristics to detect and measure the amount of energy distributed from
every radiation source.
Scientists have tried to develop ways to observe and measure the radiation transmitted
from the used materials, while taking into consideration the early days of radiation
testing that were done by Röntgen and Becquerel [8]. One of the earliest means for the
visualization and detection of any type of radiation was the photographic plate, which
was found after the fluorescent screen that was introduced by Röntgen. After that,
the electroscope which is another type of detector was invented. This type of detector
used a pair of gold leaves that repels each other when it is ionized by radiation, which
provided a way to measure more types of radiation like alpha or beta particles. Later
on, more types of detectors were introduced, which are nowadays classified into three
types that include Gas-Filled detectors, Scintillators, and Semiconductor detectors.
Each one of these detectors has different characteristics, that can hold some strengths
and weaknesses which in turn recommends them to their own specific roles.
2.2
X-ray detectors
X-ray detectors are used to measure a lot of X-ray properties, such as spectrum,
flux, spatial distribution, time of arrival, polarization and others [9]. X-rays are a
wavelike form of electromagnetic energy carried by particles called photons. In order
to detect and measure an X-ray, an interaction with matter must occur. To register
the presence of X-rays and measure their energy, the charge liberated after interaction
should be collected [10]. This can be done either directly with a proportional counter
or a solid-state semiconductor detector, or indirectly with a scintillation detector. At
the end, the energy deposited in the detecting medium will be transferred into an
electrical pulse, which will be digitized afterwards. Gas filled detectors, scintillator
detectors and semiconductor detectors are further discussed in the following sections,
focusing on the semiconductor detector which is used in the experimental part.

2.2.1
Gas filled detectors
Not all materials that can be ionized can take a part in the detection instrument [5].
It needs to be able to generate charges by radiation to move under the influence of
an electric field. The first generation of ionization detectors were gas filled. This is
due to the characteristics they hold, that were considered as the perfect match to be
used in the radiation detection and measurement.
Gas filled detectors consist of a sensitive volume of gas, such as Xenon or Argon,
that is located between two electrodes. When X-ray photons interact with these gas
atoms, they become ionized creating electron-ion pairs [9]. Positive ions are known
as a hole. Energy from 10 to 20 eV is enough to create an electron-hole pair. Besides,
hundreds or thousands of electron-hole pairs are produced from the interaction of one
X-ray photon.
Chambers are usually cylindrical in shape with a very thin wire stretched axially at the
center of the counter (Figure 2.1), which is done to achieve high field intensity for gas
multiplication without excessive applied voltage [11]. Due to the potential difference
between the tube housing and the center wire, strong electric field is introduced in the
gas volume. The influence of the electrical field helps the electrons, that are created
from the ionizations in the gas, to travel to the center wire, which in turn produces
a voltage pulse on the wire after each interaction [10]. This voltage pulse, which is
captured by the wire, is then shaped and counted by the electronics.
Figure 2.1: Principle of a gas-filled detector [11].
Some disadvantages for gas detectors prevent them from being the best choice for the
detection of x-rays in some cases [9]. They have low energy resolution compared to
semiconductor detectors, but higher resolution than scintillators. In addition to that,
they lose their linearity at high-count rates.

2.2.2
Scintillators
Scintillators are formed from sensitive materials as solid, liquid and gas [12]. One
of the first radiation counting techniques for the detection of ionizing radiation was
done by using a scintillation light induced in zinc sulphide [5]. Besides, it was also
the first solid material to be used as a particle detector [12].
Scintillators produce sparks or scintillations of light when ionizing radiation inter-
acts with them [12]. However, the amount of light produced after the interaction
is very small.
For it to be recorded as a pulse it must be amplified.
Combining
scintillators with electronic photomultiplier tubes transforms weak light flashes into
usable electric pulses [5]. As shown in Figure 2.2, the way where light is transformed
into electric pulses is by an amplifying process, where it is multiplied many times to
achieve a strong pulse as an output [12]. This made it possible to count these pulses
electronically.
Figure 2.2: Photomultiplier tube [11].
For a scintillator to be recognized as a good radiation detector, a large fraction of the
incident radiation energy as possible should be converted into prompt fluorescence [5].
Moreover, the light-yield needs to be equivalent to the deposited energy, the lumines-
cence should be measured in a short time, and the material has to be homogeneous
and of good optical quality . These criteria are hardly found in almost any material,
therefore the choice of a particular scintillator is always a compromise among these
and other factors.

Semiconductor detectors, which are also called “semiconductor detectors”, are detec-
tors that operate essentially like ionization chambers [11]. The structure of semicon-
ductor detector is crystalline solid, which holds atoms together by a covalent bond
[13]. Their electrical conduction properties lie between insulators and conductors, and
that’s why they are known as semiconductors. The most commonly used semiconduc-
tor materials are silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge). Materials as cadmium telluride
(CdTe) and cadmium zinc telluride (CZT) are also used, which have low band-gap
energy, and are referred to as semi-insulating materials [9]. The charge carriers in
semiconductors are electrons and holes, unlike the gas counters which are electrons
and ions [11]. As X-ray interacts with semiconducting junction, it creates electron-
hole pairs (Figure 2.3). The presence of an electric field drifts electrons and holes
away, inducing a signal on readout electrodes [9]. The energy deposited is propor-
tional to the signal induced. To determine the deposited energy, the signal should be
after that amplified, recorded and analyzed.
Figure 2.3: Principle of a semiconductor detector [11].
One of the most desirable characteristics for any spectrometer system is to have a
good energy resolution [14]. The reason behind this is because it separates X-rays
with very similar energies leading to a precise identification. In fact, energy resolution
is best achieved in semiconductor detectors.
Silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge) semiconductor detectors have a major advantage
which is their perfect energy resolution. It is determined by statistical variations in
the number of charges in pulse. When the number of charges produced in the semi-
conductor are large, the percentage of the statistical variations in signal amplitude
decreases, which in turns leads to a very good energy resolution.

Although silicon and germanium detectors have a superior performance in terms of
energy resolution, yet they are not the best choice to be used in a spectrometer sys-
tem. One of the major reasons is that they conduct a significant amount of thermally
induced electrical current at room temperature. This leads to a background noise
current, which interferes with the detection of currents induced after radiation in-
teraction. That’s why they are operated at a temperature below room temperature.
Another reason is that they have some impurities, that leads to the disturbance of the
arrangement of silicon and germanium atoms in the crystal matrix. This disturbance
results in releasing electron traps and capture electrons in ionization events. Not to
mention that they have low atomic numbers which restricts their detection efficiency,
and their size is limited in small dimensions due to their impurities. To solve this
problem lithium was used as a compensating crystal to fill the electron traps created
by other impurities, which allows to increase the size for a better practical use. But
still there were some problems. Lithium ions tend to condense within the crystal
matrix at room temperature. Therefore, not only must Si(Li) and Ge(Li) operate
at a low temperature, but they should also be stored at a low temperature. A liq-
uid nitrogen is used during operation and storing. Without the cooling process, the
detectors can be ruined by only an hour at room temperature.
Cadmium telluride (CdTe) and cadmium zinc telluride (CZT) were recently devel-
oped as room temperature semiconductors, operating without excessive electronic
noise. They have good stopping detection efficiency due to their high atomic number.
However, these types of detectors have also some disadvantages. The large number of
charge carriers (electron-hole pairs) is an equivalent source of line broadening. More-
over, leakage current is considered as the predominating cause of line broadening
through the detector itself, added to the incomplete collection of charge carriers af-
ter the interactions with the detector. The primary causes for these factors are the
operating voltage and the detector configuration. Besides electronic noise which are
still present in the detector itself, is considered also as an important contributor for
line broadening.

should be as low as possible during the operation process. This would reduce the

2.3.1
Band structure in solids
In pure material, the energy of any electron should be bounded to an energy band
[16]. These energy bands could be detached by gaps and ranges of forbidden energies
as it can be seen in Figure 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Classification of solids on the basis of electricity conduction [17].
The valence band, which is the lower band, refers to the outer shell electrons that are
components of the covalent bonding which occurs in the crystal. The following higher-
lying band is referred to as the conduction band. It corresponds to electrons with free
transition and movement through the crystal. The band gap refers to the distance
between the valence and conduction band. The size of this distance defines whether
the material is a semiconductor or an insulator.
All free sites within the valence
band are completely occupied by the amount of electrons within the crystal. When
the valence band is fully occupied, and the conduction band is entirely empty, this
indicates the absence of thermal excitation in both semiconductors and insulators.
2.3.2
Charge carriers
Charge carriers are electrically charged particles or holes that move freely within a
material depending on the excitation process [16]. An excitation process occurs when
the thermal energy shared by the electrons within the crystal at any temperature
different from zero becomes sufficient enough to excite an electron, which leads to its
elevation from the valence band across the band gap towards the conduction band.

This process creates what is called an electron-hole pair, which involves the presence
of an electron in the former empty conduction band, as well as the creation of a hole,
which refers to a vacancy, in the previously full valence band.
However, the occurrence of the thermal excitation process depends on the proportion
of the band gap energy in relation to the absolute temperature. In case of a large
band gap, the material will have a low probability of thermal excitation. While, if
the band gap is low, the material with suitable thermal excitation will generate high
conductivity.
The presence of an electric field prevents the thermally formulated
electron-hole pair from ultimately recombining after excitation. Both the electron
and the hole are involved in a random thermal movement after the electron-hole pair
is formed, which leads to their spread apart from their original position.

2.3.3
Migration of charge carriers in an electric field
In a semiconductor material, applying an electric field would result in a net migration
of both holes and electrons in a movement which combines both a random thermal
velocity and a net drift velocity, which is also parallel to the direction of the applied
field [16]. The transition of electrons from a valence site to occupy an existing hole
would result in the movement of a hole from one position to another. The motion of
the holes is similar to the direction of the electric field, while the movement of electrons
is opposite to the direction of the electric field. Moreover, holes and electrons move
in the same order in semiconductor materials.
On the other hand, occurrence of some spread in the arrival position is the result of
diffusion. Hence, they are not collected in one origin or one point and this will give
separate calculation of the charge carriers or photons. Similarly, a spread out in the
collection time of the charges also result from diffusion.

Impurities & doping
• Intrinsic semiconductors: represents pure semiconductors where thermal excita-
tion is the reason behind the presence of any holes in the valence band and any
electrons in the conduction band [16]. In this case, the number of electrons in the
conduction band must be similar to that of holes in the valence band, considering
that each electron leaves a hole behind. The current of the opposite side of holes
and electrons can be calculated. Since they have different or opposite charges, they
will not eliminate each other, instead they will be added.
• n-type semiconductors: have an impurity type that is referred to as donor impurity,
since they are ready to provide electrons to the conduction band. They have a
sufficiently high probability of thermal excitation to assure that a large portion of
all donor impurities are ionized. This is due to the small energy spacing between
the donor levels and the bottom of the conduction band which can be seen in
Figure 2.6. The donor impurities contribution controls the number of conduction
electrons completely. The concentration of electrons is at excess compared to the
number of holes, which leads to an increase in the rate of recombination between
the electrons and the holes. For this reason, electrons are referred to as majority
carriers, while holes are referred to as minority carriers.

some electrons will always be accessible to do so. On the other hand, due to the
presence of additional holes, there are higher chances of recombination between
conductors and holes. Also, considering that the number of holes is more than

Photon matter interaction processes
The interaction of both X- and gamma-rays with matter can occur with and without
deposition of energy [19]. The energy of the incident photon is not changed in the

be specified based on observation technique, detector material and energy regime of
observation. The basic interactions that occur in ionizing detectors are photoelectric
effect and Compton effect, as well as pair production [20].

2.5.1
Indirect conversion
As shown in Figure 2.14, detectors with indirect conversion have a scintillation layer
that converts the X-ray photons into optical photons (visible light), which are then
absorbed by a photo detector (e.g., photodiode), that converts them after that into
electrical charges [27].

directing the light to a specific pixel, but still it can’t prevent some lights from being
shared by neighboring pixels.
2.5.2
Direct conversion
In direct detection systems the X-ray photons interact in the (semiconductor) detector
material directly [29, 30]. The energy deposited after the interaction creates a number
of electron-hole pairs.
The presence of an intense applied electric field separates
the charge carriers induced, which is after that migrated toward the corresponding
electrodes as shown in Figure 2.14. This drift to the collection electrodes creates
an electric current, which is after that integrated by charge-sensitive amplifier and
converted into a voltage pulse.
Taking into consideration the energies used in medical applications, a charge from
4000 to 25000 electrons is released after each colliding photon in a CdTe semiconduc-
tor detector [30]. While in an indirect conversion system, the range for the detected
electrical signal is 100 to 1000 electrons depending on the characteristics of the scintil-
lator. Therefore, direct conversion technology is considered as the ideal spectroscopic
system, where the electrical charge is much higher than that in indirect conversion
system.

2.7.6
Charge sharing due to diffusion
The presence of an electric field, creates cloud charges from electron and hole pairs,
which are drifted subsequently to the corresponding electrodes [9]. A diffusion occurs
during this drift, resulting in the expansion of the volume of the charge cloud. The
interaction of photons near the border between neighboring pixels would result in

charge in those pixels or near by. As the ratio of pixel pitch to the sensor thickness
increase, the effect of charge sharing decreases. Moreover, the increase of the sensor
bias voltage is accompanied with the decrease in the impact of charge sharing, where
the charge tends to drift faster, and the diffusion time becomes lower.

2.12.1
Noise (leakage current)
A noise in the signal is introduced from both the readout ASIC and the sensor into
the system [57]. The presence of leakage current in the sensor causes a signal to be
generated even without any interaction. The thermal generation at the surface of
the device and in the depleted volume of the sensor is one of the most important
contribution to the leakage current.
Shot-noise to the signal is added by the leakage current of the sensor which is pre-
sented to the readout electronics. The readout ASIC can be compensated with a leak-
age current circuitry inside of it. Taking into account the signal polarity, a source or
sink, can be placed in the front-end between input and output of a low-noise amplifier.
Several sources of noise are found in the readout ASIC itself. One of the contributors
to the noise of the analog front-end is the cross-talk from digital logic to analog. This
can occur through either direct capacitive coupling of frequently switching digital
signals or indirect coupling via digital power supply or ground bounce through a
silicon substrate. The analog front-end is very sensitive to noise coming from the
digital logic, which is due to signals that can be detected by the analog front-end,
that may be only 400-500 electrons in magnitude or even smaller [58]. Consequently,
the minimum detectable charge is going to be increased by the extra noise injected
into the analog front-end. This noise is falsely recognized as an extra pixel hits, which
is originating from the readout ASIC itself and not from the sensor. Equivalent noise
charge (ENC) is a typical measurement unit of noise for HPD systems, which shows
a point where signal-to-noise ratio is equal to 1 [59].

2.13
Fluorescence emission spectroscopy
To measure the fluorescence emission, three main components are used, the source,
sample, and the detection system [62]. The sample element is irradiated by a source,
which in turns emits radiation, that are simultaneously measured by a detector. When
X-ray interacts with an element, a process whereby electrons are shifted from their
atomic orbital positions occurs (see section 2.4.3), which releases a blast of energy
that resembles the characteristic of the target element [63]. This is known as X-ray
fluorescence (XRF). Usually the X-ray source is responsible for the generation of the
primary X-rays that are aimed at the surface of a sample [64]. The fluorescence rays
are often measured at a 90° angle proportional to the emitted X-rays. The sample is
placed in between at a 45° angle. This is done to avoid the interference of the X-rays
emitted from the source. When measuring at a 90° angle, only the rays scattered by
the sample causes stray rays. This results in a better signal-to-noise ratio. To modify
the X-ray beam, the rays sometimes travel through a filter. Secondary X-rays are
produced by a reaction that occurs when the atoms in the sample are hit by the beam
(Figure 2.36).
Figure 2.36: Fluorescence detection method that consists of an X-ray source with a
primary filter, a sample element and a detector [64].

X-rays have to pass through the superficial layer of the element sample in order to
reach the interior atoms , which absorbs part of the incoming radiation before it hits
the atoms inside [62]. The characteristic radiation produced also passes through this
layer when it is leaving the sample, where part of the radiation is going to be absorbed.
The absorption increases as the energy of the radiation decreases, and as the path
length, density and atomic number of the elements in the layer increases.
Both the incoming radiation and the characteristic radiation emitted by the atoms

3.1.2.1
Theory of operation
An average of one electron/hole pair is created for every 4.43 eV of energy lost in the
CdTe when x-rays & gamma rays interact with CdTe atoms [71]. This energy loss
is either dominated by the photoelectric effect or Compton scattering based on the
energy of the incoming radiation. As the thickness of CdTe increases, the probability
or efficiency of the detector to “stop” the incoming radiation and create electron/hole
pairs becomes higher.
The application of a +500 volt potential is done in order to facilitate the electron/hole
collection process in the CdTe detector. For operation at room temperature, this
voltage is considered too high, where it will lead to excessive leakage, and will end
in a breakdown. The cooling of the detector in the XR-100T-CdTe will result in
a considerable reduction of the leakage current, which in turn allows the high bias
voltage.
Cooling of both the CdTe detector and the input FET transistor to the charge sensi-
tive preamplifier is achieved by the thermoelectric cooler. When the FET is cooled,
its leakage current is reduced and transconductance is increased, resulting in turn in
the reduction of the electronic noise of the system.
Both the feedback capacitor and part of the current feedback network to the pream-
plifier are also positioned on the same substrate as the detector and FET as a means
of further reducing electronic noise. This reduces parasitic capacitance at the input.
To supply a direct reading of the temperature of the internal components, a temper-
ature monitoring sensor is set on the cooled substrate, which will change with room
temperature.
3.1.2.2
Signal formation
Measuring the deposited energy, which is proportional to the total charge rather than
the current, is a desired task in most applications [71]. Considering that charge is
the integral of current, the detector is thus attached to a charge-sensitive preampli-
fier, which results in the production of an output pulse with a voltage step directly
proportional to the time integral of the current. The preamp output is afterwards
transmitted to a shaping amplifier, which forms the pulse to permit accurate mea-
surements under realistic conditions, amplifies them, and filters out noise to maximize
the signal-to-noise ratio. The formed and amplified pulse, which represents a voltage
pulse with peak amplitude proportional to the deposited energy, is then transmitted
==PAGE:86==
66
3 Methodology
to a multi-channel analyzer, where it computes the peak amplitude of many pulses,
generating a histogram that displays the number of pulses with amplitude computed
within the range of each channel. This is known as the output spectrum.

Second step was the equalization tab. The one performed at -18° C can be seen in

adjustment to the global threshold is included in each pixel. Moreover, every pixel
has 16 levels of adjustment (0 to 15). To specify the values of these DACs, the noise
floor in each pixel must be determined. This process is automatized, and the user is
only required to insert the valid initial parameters.

green distribution represents the results of the equalization, which is broad compared
to the other distributions. The threshold of each pixel was modified to attain the most
homogeneous distribution possible. In a perfect world, this would be represented by
a single line. However, in reality, this is represented by a Gaussian. The standard
deviation of the equalization must be lower than 7. A 256 x 256 matrix was obtained
from the DAC equalization, which contains an integer number between 0 and 15 for

of the spread of the position acquired from the obtained data with respect to the ideal
position was calculated. It’s mean and standard deviation values related to the Kα1
of the six variable fitting and the Kα (1 & 2) of the one Gaussian fitting of 121 pixels

value of the percentage of the position error decreased from 0.06 at 20° C to 0.04 at
-18° C, which resulted in a decrease in the mean value of the position error by 0.02%.
The same results were obtained from the one Gaussian fitting as well.

detector improves position of the energy peaks, which in turns gives a more accurate
result (this result can also depend on the calibration setup of the detector).

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis is to enhance the energy resolution of spectral X-ray detectors
by lowering the operational temperature of the detector, thus reducing the leakage
current. The target detectors are the CdTe hybrid pixel detectors using a Timepix3
chip.
First, a one-pixel Amptek XR-100CdTe detector was used to observe the influence
of temperature on the energy resolution. Besides this detector is easy to work with
and results can be conducted in a short period of time. Lowering the temperature
of the detector using an Silver element for fluorescence emission from 20°C (at room
temperature) to -50°C resulted in a change of FWHM from 1.82 to 0.66 and an energy
resolution from 0.08 to 0.03. This shows that reducing the temperature of the CdTe
detector reduces the FWHM leading to a better energy resolution.
Besides, the temperature was used in another experiment to show the spectrum for
each element sample as an influence of temperature, which can give a better view on
the similarity of results in each element. The elements used was Silver, Potassium
Iodide, Gadolinium, Tantalum and Lead. As a result, the temperature had the same
influence on the number of counts for each element sample used under the experiment,
which shows that the temperature have the same influence on the energy resolution
for every element used.
Since the effect of leakage current have the largest influence on the energy resolu-
tion of the detector, the data collected before was analyzed to show the variation of
FWHM as an influence of temperature and compare it with the leakage current of
the CdTe crystal by calculating it using the leakage current equation. In conclusion,
as the temperature increases, the thermal excitation that takes place in the detector
increases, which in turns leads to the increase of the leakage current as well. This
determines also that the temperature has a direct influence on the energy resolution
of the detector as it affects the FWHM of the collected peaks, which results in a
better energy resolution as the temperature of the detector decreases.
Taking the influence of high bias voltage on energy resolution into consideration.
Another experiments were conducted by the same detector used before to observe
the effect of high bias voltage on the energy resolution of the detector taking the
==PAGE:118==
98
6 Conclusion
influence of temperature also into consideration. As a result, the increase in the high
bias voltage results in a decrease in the FWHM, which in turns improves the energy
resolution. Moreover, the decrease in the temperature also results in an enhanced
energy resolution. Even though the two have an impact on the energy resolution,
however the temperature is considered as the main influencing factor.
After that a multi-pixel AdvaPIX TPX3 CdTe X-ray detector was used to observe
the influence of temperature on the energy resolution. Lowering the temperature of
the detector using an Silver element for fluorescence emission from 20°C (at room
temperature) to -18°C resulted in a change of FWHM from 2.78 to 2.58 and the H0
position error from 0.06% to 0.04% (the position result can also be related to the
calibration setup).
Taking everything into account, both the temperature and the high bias voltage
have an influence on the energy resolution of the detector, where the temperature
have the highest influence. Furthermore, lowering the operational temperature of the
spectral X-ray detectors improves it’s energy resolution, thus degrading the thermal
excitation’s occurring in the detector, which in turns lead to the decrease of the
leakage current (dark current).
