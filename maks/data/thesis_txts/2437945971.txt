Preface
The research presented in this thesis was conducted by Victor Giødesen Thystrup, student at
the Technical University of Denmark, for his ﬁnal project at the graduate programme of Civil
Engineering. The project was carried out from 22nd January 2018 to 22nd June 2018. The work
corresponds to 30 ECTS points. John Forbes Olesen and Peter Noe Poulsen, both professors at
the Department of Civil Engineering, have supervised this.
The paper focuses on the material optimization of four diﬀerent kinds of structures. The method
of material optimization is based on a modiﬁcation of numerical limit analysis and ﬁnite element
method.
This paper examines and validates the implementations of material optimization in
MATLAB.

Abstract
This paper examines the material optimization of a structure consisting of reinforced concrete.
How can the material used in a structure be distributed in a way that the load carrying capacity
of the structure can be utilised completely, when a load is applied? An optimal material distri-
bution means minimized material consumption which ensures an advantage for both economy and
environment. This examination is carried out on four diﬀerent structures; bar, disc, slab and shell.
The method of ﬁnite element limit analysis is applied to the optimization problem. The limit load
analysis is based on the lower bound theorem, where rigid plasticity is assumed. It describes the
collapse mechanism, which can be used for an analysis of the ultimate limit state. In order to
identify the collapse load, a convex optimization is required.
In the case of material optimization, the numerical limit load analysis is modiﬁed, so convex
optimization can be applied to minimize the material consumption. Here, the applied load is ﬁxed
and the material distribution is subjected to optimization and a restriction of yield conditions. A
vast amount of literature exists in the ﬁeld of numerical limit analysis, however, few articles are
dedicated to the context of material optimization. This paper focuses on the implementation of
material optimization in MATLAB in order to demonstrate the application of material optimization
for the design problem of the ultimate limit state analysis.
The ﬁrst structure in the study is the bar structure which can only take axial force. The axial force
in a bar element is restricted, so it does not violate the tensile limit stress and the compressive limit
stress by ensuring that the bar element has a suﬃcient cross sectional area to resist the internal
force contributed by the applied load. It is established that the material optimization can indeed
achieve an optimal material distribution, so that the carrying load capacity of the bar structure is
utilised completely and some bar elements may even be removed from the structure without risk
for collapse.
The disc structure is subjected to an in-plane force. Here, the internal forces are described by a
two-dimensional stress state. For reinforced concrete, a yield condition is applied to ensure that
the concrete and the reinforcement are able to resist compression and tension respectively. In this
paper, it is shown that both the thickness of concrete and the area per length of the orthogonal
reinforcement can be optimized, so that the structure can resist the applied load while the carrying
load capacity is utilised completely.
The case of the slab structure proves to be a challenge because of the out-of-plane force that is
present in the structure. Due to bending, the internal force takes the appearance of a moment
state. Here, the yield moment is calculated by the limit stress reinforcement, while the moment
state is restricted by a yield condition. The distance between the reinforcement and the midplane
of the slab ensures that the reinforcement can take the tension contribution from the bending, so
it is shown that the area per length of the reinforcement can be optimised to resist the tension.
An optimization for the thickness of the concrete is, however, not established, but it is noted that
the thickness can be derived from the force in the reinforcement.
The paper ends with the shell structure that can handle both in-plane and out-of-plane forces, so
both stress state and moment state are obtained. The layer-based approach is utilised to calculate
the stress contribution from the bending to the concrete and the reinforcement. Therefore, just the
stress state is restricted by a yield condition. It is shown that the reinforcement can be optimized,
when it is subjected to both membrane force and bending. The paper suggests a modiﬁcation of
basis vectors, so the local coordinate follows the desirable pattern of the reinforcement.
In conclusion, it is possible to apply the convex optimization in order to minimise the material
consumption of a structure, while the equilibrium between the external forces and the internal
forces is ensured and the internal forces are restricted by the yield condition. However, it proves
be an issue, when the moment state is considered, as it is not able to optimize the thickness of
concrete, so the paper suggests further examination in this ﬁeld.

Rapporten undersøger materiel optimering af en konstruktion best˚aende af armeret beton. Hvordan
kan materialet fordeles, s˚aledes at bæreevnen er udnyttet fuldstændigt, n˚ar konstruktionen er
belastet af en bestemt kraft.
Den optimerede fordeling af materialet, som medfører minimalt
forbrug af materiale, s˚aledes at der er fordele for b˚ade økonomi og miljø. Denne undersøgelse
udføres p˚a ﬁre forskellige typer af strukturer; stang, skive, plade og skal.
Problemet om optimeringen af materialet er baseret p˚a metoden om analysen af numeriske grænse-
laster og ﬁnite element metoden. Grænselasten kan ﬁndes ved brug af teoremet om den nedre
værdi, hvor stift plasticitet er antaget. Den beskriver kollapset af strukturen, som kan bruges til
analysen af brudgrænsetilstand. Den konvekse optimering benyttes til at ﬁnde størrelse af lasten
ved kollapset.
Analysen af den numeriske grænselast er modiﬁceret for at udføre materiale optimeringen, hvor
den konvekse optimering kan minimere materialeforbruget. Ved materiale optimeringen er lasten
en konstant størrelse og fordelingen af materiale skal optimeres i forhold til begrænsning af ﬂy-
debetingelsen. Der ﬁndes mange bøger om den numeriske grænselast, men f˚a af dem handler om
materiale optimeringen. Rapporten undersøger derfor om implementering af materiale optimerin-
gen i MATLAB for at vise anvendelsesmuligheder af materiale optimeringen til brug for design
med hensyn til brudgrænsetilstanden.
Rapporten undersøger først stang-strukturen, som kun kan optage aksial kraft. Den aksiale kraft i
et stang element er begrænset, s˚a den ikke overskrider grænsespændingen ved træk eller ved tryk.
Tværsnitsarealet skal derfor være stort nok til at kunne modst˚a kraften. Det er vist at materiale
optimeringen kan opn˚a den optimal fordeling af materialet, s˚a bæreevnen er udnyttet fuldstændigt
og nogle stanger kan eventuel droppes.
Skive-strukturen kan optage skivekraft, hvor de interne kræfter kan beskrives af spændingen i
planen. Flydebetingelsen for armeret betonen bruges til at sikre sig, at betonen og armeringen kan
modst˚a henholdsvis tryk og træk. Det er vist, at b˚ade tykkelse af betonen og størrelse af armeringen
kan optimeres, s˚a strukturen kan modst˚a kraften, mens bæreevnen kan udnyttes fuldstændigt.
Plade-konstruktionen viser sig at være en udfordring, da de interne kræfter beskrives af mo-
menterne. Flydemomentet regnes med udgangspunkt i armeringen og moment-tilstanden er be-
grænset af ﬂydebetingelsen. Armeringen placeres med en afstand til pladens tyngdepunkt, s˚a den
kan optage trækket, som er for˚arsaget af bøjningen S˚aledes kan størrelse af armeringen optimeres,
men det er ikke vist, at tykkelsen af betonen kan optimeres. Det foresl˚as at undersøge, om betonen
kan optimeres i et fremtidigt arbejde.
Den sidste struktur er skal, der kan optage b˚ade skive- og pladekræfter, s˚a b˚ade spændingstil-
standen og momentstilstanden opst˚ar. Ved at inddele et element i ﬂere lag, kan momentet om-
regnes til spændingen i b˚ade betonen og armeringen. S˚aledes kan ﬂydebetingelsen kun bruge til
at begrænse spændingstilstanden. Det er vist, at armeringen kan optimeres med hensyn til b˚ade
skive- og pladekræfter. Rapporten indeholder et forslag til at modiﬁcere basis vektorerne, s˚a de
lokale koordinatsystemer følger armeringens mønster.
Konklusion er, at det bestemt er muligt at bruge den konvekse optimering til at reducere mate-
rialeforbruget, mens ligevægten mellem de interne og de eksterne kræfter bevares og de interne
kræfter er begrænset af ﬂydebetingelsen. Det er dog vistt, at der er udfordringer med hensyn til
momentstilstanden, da der ikke er udført optimering af tykkelsen af betonen, hvilket det i stedet
foresl˚as at arbejde videre med.

1
Introduction
This paper concerns the material optimization of reinforced concrete structure. When a structure
is subjected to a load, it must be able to resist the force without collapsing. The distribution of
material in the structure is crucial for the load carrying capacity to resist the resulting internal
forces. This paper examines how the material distribution can be optimized, so the load capacity
of the structure can be utilised completely and the material consumption is minimised with beneﬁt
for both economy and environment. This paper aims to solve this material distribution problem.
The basis theory is the ﬁnite element limit analysis, FELA, and the lower bound method with
applied convex optimization to maximise the applied external forces. The main assumption is the
rigidity and the plastic behaviour of the structure in question, so the ultimate stress distribution
and the collapse mode can be obtained [13, p. 125], which is ideal for the analysis of the ultimate
limit state. The limit load analysis can be modiﬁed, so the optimization is carried out to minimize
the material variables subjected to a constant force in stead of maximizing the load variable. The
numerical analysis requires the application of the ﬁnite element method, in which the structure
model is divided into a number of elements that interact with each other.
This paper examines four diﬀerent types of structure;
1. Bar
2. Disc
3. Slab
4. Shell
The bar structure is the simplest structure, as it can only take the axial force and does not have
any bending stiﬀness. The disc structure is a plate that is subjected to in-plane force, where the
sectional forces are described by a stress state. The slab structure is a plate that is subjected to
out-of-plane force, which causes it to bend, so the moment state is crucial. The last structure
to be examined is the shell structure, which combines both the membrane behaviour of the disc
structure and the bending behaviour of the slab structure.
1.1
Numerical limit load analysis
The material model is assumed to be rigid plastic without signiﬁcant elastic deformation. This
means, that the material does not deform before collapsing plastically [16, p. 139], which ﬁts the
assumption of ideal plasticity. With this assumption, the extremum principles can be applied to
identify the limit load [9, p. 11] with the help of three theorems. When the plastic analysis is
considered, there are three criteria to fulﬁl. The equilibrium between the internal forces and the
external forces must be maintained, so the state of stress is statically admissible. The mechanism
condition states that the structure can deform as a mechanism at the collapse. When a suﬃcient
number of mechanisms appear, the system becomes unstable, which causes it to collapse. The
state of deformation is said to be kinematically admissible. Furthermore, the state of stress or
moment must be inside or on the yield surface, which fulﬁls the yield criterion [13, p. 130].
The lower bound theorem states that the force equilibrium is satisﬁed and the stress state does
not surpass the yield surface.
If the structure is statically indeterminate, then only a part of
the stress parameters are needed to ensure the force equilibrium, while the rest can redistribute
the load [4, p. 6012]. Among all the statically admissible solutions, the collapse load should be
maximized [16, p. 165].
The upper bound theorem aims at identifying the failure mechanism. The internal work should
be equal to external work, which satisﬁes the force equilibrium. Since several kinematically admis-
sible collapse modes exist, the collapse load should be minimised to ﬁnd the collapse mode that
requires the least work.
The unique theorem states that one unique and true solution is identiﬁed, when the maximal
collapse load from the solution of the lower bound method is equal to the minimal collapse load

Through a modiﬁcation, the limit load analysis becomes a design problem that can optimize the
material distribution when the ultimate limit state is considered. It must be pointed out that
the assumption of no elastic deformation is made, so the method cannot be applied, when the
serviceability limit state is considered.

1.3
Literature
A vast amount of literature on FELA is available. Central contributions to this literature will
be mentioned here. The signiﬁcant literature on the bar structure is found in a compendium [13]
written by John Forbes Olesen, Peter Noe Poulsen and Sebastian Andersen, prepared for the course
11346 Non-linear Finite Element Method at the Technical University of Denmark. It provides a
core introduction to the numerical limit load analysis for the bar structure, which forms the basis
understanding of the ﬁeld in subject. The compendium explains how the force equilibrium is set
up and how the yield condition can be applied to restrict the stress state. It is also a primary
source on the application of ﬁnite element method for the optimization analysis. Additionally, the
compendium has two pages on material optimization, which is based on the limit load analysis [13,
p. 142 - p. 143].
When the disc structure is considered, Lars Damkilde and Peter Noe Poulsen have written an
article on the limit state analysis of disc structures consisting of reinforced concrete [4].
The
article derives the force equilibrium of a disc element and outlines the yield condition of reinforced
concrete for the limit load analysis. However, the article also suggests the linearisation of the yield
condition, in which the authors believe that this method is suitable for material optimization.
Peter Noe Poulsen has also written an article [11] on slab structures together with Leif Otto
Nielsen.
It covers the force equilibrium of a slab element but does, however, lead to a rather
complex system matrix to assemble. A simpliﬁed version of force equilibrium is applied in this
paper, which is also provided by Peter Noe Poulsen and John Forbes Olesen for the course 11345
Computational Structural Modelling: Plates and Shells. In addition to the force equilibrium, the
article also outlines the yield condition based on the yield moment. In order to calculate the yield
moment based on the limit stress, the book [12] written by Mogens Peter Nielsen and Linh Cao
Hoang is consulted. The book provides a theory on the reinforced concrete with regards to the
limit analysis as well.
The shell structure is a rather complex subject. As a part of his PhD thesis [5], Morten Andersen
Herfelt has worked with the shell structure, which provides a basic knowledge to this paper. Herfelt
has used Kristian Krabbenhøfts note [7] on the shell element, which is also made available for this
paper. Regarding the yield condition, Herfelt has used Kasper Paaske Larsens PhD thesis [9] to
construct the layer-based criteria of reinforced concrete.
This rich literature on numerical limit analysis, however, does not examine the material opti-
mization as the primary subject. The author has not found any literature that has the material
optimization as primary subject, where it is based on the FELA of ideal plasticity. It is noted that
literature on topology optimization is available, for example the book by Martin P. Bendsøe and
Ole Sigmund [2]. However, it is based on the stiﬀness approach in order to achieve the minimum
compliance with maximum global stiﬀness. Here, the structure is linearly elastic [2, p. 2]. By
addition of penalization, the black-white design, where the density of an element is either solid or
void, is possible, but the optimization problem becomes non-convex with multitude of local optima
[6, p. 2]. In the context of material optimization, a material parameter should be able to vary to
any value, so the penalization is not needed. It is possible to make the topology optimization based
on strength rather than stiﬀness and the stress constraints are enforced [6, p. 17]. The topology
optimization is not part of the research and this paper makes use of the convex optimization for
the plastic structure.
This paper responds to the identiﬁed gap in the literature. The purpose is to ﬁnd a method
to modify the numerical limit load analysis in order to apply it to the material optimization,
when both are based on the same theory of force equilibrium and yield conditions. This paper is
focused on the material optimization and, in some cases, also gives a suggestion to the application
of this optimization problem. The main question is how to optimize a structure, so the material
consumption can be minimised, when it is subjected to a load and is restricted by the strength of the
materials. Here, reinforced concrete is chosen as the material in subject, as it allows for optimization
of concrete and reinforcement, which each has its own strength and material parameters.

1.4
The Structure of the Report
The main part of the report consists of four sections. Each section examines one type of structure.
In order to establish an optimization problem for one structure, the force equilibriums and the yield
conditions are derived and explained, as they form the basis of the optimization problem of material
distributions. In addition to the derivation of theory, some simple models are subjected to tests
in order to validate the implementation of the material optimization. More complex examples are
also included to demonstrate the application of the optimization problem with regard to material
consumption.
The ﬁrst section is on bar structures that can only take axial force. It is the simplest structure,
so it is examined to gain the understanding of the optimization problem.
Here, the material
parameter is just the cross section area of a bar element, as it is crucial for the capacity to resist
the axial force. Several applications are tested to investigate the options and the limitations of the
optimization problem. Firstly, a simple implementation is made. Thereafter the implementation
is expanded to include multiple load cases, so a structure must be able to handle several load
scenarios. Additionally, multiple materials are implemented as well in order to study, how the
algorithm can make a choice between several available materials. The further examination on the
manipulation of material parameters and the non-linear material can be found in Appendix A, as
they are not relevant for the examination of reinforced concrete structure.
The second section is on the disc structure that is subjected to in-plane force. Three diﬀerent
cases of material and yield conditions are examined. The ﬁrst case is the simplest, as it examines
pure concrete based on a linearised yield condition, so just one material parameter per element
and LP is required. Thereafter, the reinforcement is added to create reinforced concrete that has
three material parameters per element, but is still based on the linearised yield condition. In the
last case, the generalised yield condition is applied, so SCOP is required. In addition to the three
cases of materials and yield conditions, the implementation of multiple load cases and self-weight
is applied and examined.
The third section examines the plate subjected to out-of-plane force. It proves to be a challenge,
since the section force appears as a moment. If the material restriction should be based on the limit
stress, some further calculation is required, if the yield condition and the force equilibrium is to be
based on the moments. Additional work is required to make the implementation. In this case, the
material is still reinforced concrete. However, only the material parameters of the reinforcement
are variable, while the thickness of the concrete is constant. The implementation of multiple load
cases is made.
The fourth section is dedicated to the shell that combines both in-plane and out-of-plane actions.
It is a complex implementation, since the state of stress and the state of moment must be able to
interact with each other and they have their own yield conditions, while the material optimization
is restricted by the limit stress of concrete and reinforcement. As with the case of the slab, the
thickness of concrete is constant, while the material parameters of reinforcement are variable. This
section focuses on showing that it is possible to optimize the material of a shell structure - at least
with regards to the reinforcement.
When all four types of structure are examined, the report concludes on its ﬁndings, in which
the option on application of material optimization is concluded, but the possible limitations are
also outlined. In addition to these, further examinations or inquires are also suggested in order to
improve the implementation of material optimization.
The report has a digital appendix that contains four MATLAB packages that can optimize the
material distribution for all four types of structure; bar, disc, plate and shell. An overview of the
packages can be found in Appendix B.

3
Disc
In the previous section it is established how a truss structure can be optimized by minimizing
its total volume. The optimization problem of the truss structure is simple and straightforward,
because a bar element only has one section force, which is axial force.
This section examines rigid-plastic plates subjected to in-plane forces, a so-called disc. A ﬁnite
element formulation for material optimization is developed for the discs. The structure is based on
a triangular disc element. Unlike the simple bar element, a disc element has three section forces,
which are two stresses, σx and σy, and one shear stress, τ.
Firstly, a basis formulation of material optimization is established. Thereafter the optimization
problem can be expanded to handle multiple load cases and inclusion of self-weight. Two diﬀerent
optimization algorithms are applied and compared. The ﬁrst one is Linear Programming, LP,
which is also used with the truss structure. The second one is second order conic programming,
SOCP, which can handle the more complex yield conditions. Both pure concrete and reinforced
concrete are applied as the material of the structure.
Three MATLAB packages are developed for pure concrete restricted by linearised yield condition,
reinforced concrete restricted by linearised yield condition and reinforced concrete restricted by
generalised yield condition. The overview of the late one can be found in Appendix B.2, since
they use mostly the same functions and the package with generalised yield condition is the most
complex.

3.2
Force Equilibrium of Disc
A disc element is subjected to only an in-plane force. The external loads are transferred through the
structure as section forces in the form of two stresses; σx in the x-direction, σy in the y-direction
and and shear stress τ. The three stresses are interpolated to each node of the triangular disc

3.3
Linearised Yield Condition of Disc for Pure Concrete
A disc structure is restricted by a yield condition that limits the allowable stresses in the material.
However, the appearance of a yield surface depends on the choice of material. The von Mises yield
criterion applies to ductile materials like steel and the Mohr-Coulomb yield surface handles a brittle
material like concrete, [12, p. 17], [8, p. 380]. In this paper, the examination is carried out on
reinforced concrete because it oﬀers an option to include two materials in the form of concrete and
reinforcement in a structure. Therefore the modiﬁed Mohr-Coulomb yield surface that includes
reinforcement is applied as yield condition for the disc structure.

3.6
Generalised Yield Condition of Disc for Reinforced Concrete
Concrete is a brittle material that is the Mohr-Coulomb yield surface, which is a non-linear function.
The Mohr-Coulomb yield surface can be modiﬁced to include reinforcement. In order to implement
this yield condition in LP, Damkilde and Poulsen [4] suggest a linearisation of the yield condition,
as it is described in equation 3.18. This linearisation leads to 24 restrictions per element and
is demanding for the optimization procedure. It is shown in Table 9 that a model with a large
number of elements requires more time to optimize. This limits the modelling capacities, which
is problematic, if a big and complex model is subjected to the analysis.
It must be pointed
out that the implementation is improved by preallocating the variables in the matrix without
deﬁning the zeros in order to reduce the time during the executing of computational calculation.
But it is still not suﬃcient to reduce the execution time vastly, so further improvement must be
sought. Furthermore, the linearisation of yield surface is an approximation. The implementation of
generalised yield surface without any linearisation is examined in this section to see, if it improves
the execution time, as it does not require a number of plane facets to approximate the yield
condition. The calculation can therefore be done straight on the generalised yield surface.
Second Order Cone Programming, SOCP, can be applied to a yield surface with non-linear
restriction, since it does not require linearisation of the surface to a number of plane facets. The
yield surface is transformed into two cones that can be formulated in SOCP. This method requires
fewer restrictions compared to LP. It may be able to achieve a better result with less time spent.
The yield condition of reinforced concrete is derived for SOCP by Olesen and Poulsen [13, p.
150] for the limit load analysis with the load factor as subject to optimization. The following text
describes the system matrix that can be used for the limit load analysis. Thereafter it is modiﬁed
in order to apply the material optimization.

Slab
It is shown how a disc structure can be optimized with regard to material consumption, when it is
subjected to an in-plane force. In this section, a rigid-plastic plate is subjected to an out-of-plane
force, which causes it to bend. The term ”slab” is applied to this plate type [12, 74]. As the
plate bends, its plane stress state can be described by plate moments. the slabs are required to
be reinforced at the top and bottom. They are restricted by the plane stress yield condition. It is
assumed that the plate is symmetric with regard to geometry, supports and constitutive conditions
about the middle plane [11, p. 69].
This section begins with derivation of force equilibrium for a slab element, which requires a
set of stress variables, that are diﬀerent from the ones used for the disc elements. Thereafter,
a yield condition is deﬁned and modiﬁed to the SOCP problem in order to apply the material
optimization of reinforced concrete.

4.3
Opportunity for Including Self-weight
The option of implementation of self-weight is discussed. It is assumed that a slab is subjected
to an out-of-plane force and, more importantly, that a slab is horizontal. The last assumption
ensures that the downward (vertical) force always work perpendicularly on the element. With this
assumption, the self-weight can be added using the same procedure as with the distributed load.
The self-weight is converted to three corner forces.
The question, however, is how the self-weight is calculated.
Like with the case of the disc
structure, the self-weight can be chosen to be entirely dependent on the thickness of the concrete.
The total thickness of the concrete can be considered as as a function of area per unit length
of a reinforcement.
From equation 4.25 it is established that the yield moment is dependent
on two geometric parameters; area of reinforcement and moment arm. Equation 4.16 shows the
equilibrium between the force in a given strand and the force in the compressive zone within the
concrete. The thickness of the concrete can then be found by

5
Shell
In the two previous sections, it is established that a disc structure is subjected to an in-plane
force and a slab structure is subjected to an out-of-plane force. When both membrane force and
bending force are to be considered together, a model consisting of shell elements is required. In this
section, a mixed-formula shell element is modelled using a combination of in-plane behaviour and
out-of-plane behaviour, so it can describe both a stress state and a moment state. When dealing
with two section force states, it is necessary to deﬁne a yield condition that can handle both stress
and moment.
This section begins with a derivation of force equilibrium for a shell with contributions from
both in-plane and out-of-plane behaviours. It may be possible to combine the disc element from
Section 3 and with the slab element from Section 4. However, this raises some questions on how
a two-dimensional plane element can appear in a three-dimensional coordinate system and how
in-plane behaviour can interact with out-of-plane behaviour. Litterature on shell elements and
their behaviour is found in Morten Andersen Herfelts PhD thesis on numerical limit analysis [5].
His numerical limit analysis on shell structures is based on Kristian Krabbenhøfts shell element
[7], which is applied in this paper.
A yield condition is deﬁned based on a layer-based criterion, which is presented by Kasper Paaske
Larsen [9] and modiﬁed by Herfelt [5, p. 135]. This criterion is applied to the material optimization
with regards to reinforcement.
The optimization of concrete is not considered in this section,
so only optimization of reinforcement is performed in order to demonstrate the application and
challenges of shell elements. The application of self-weight and multiple load cases are established
in the previous sections, so they are not considered for the shell model, since the method of
implementation is the same.
Herfelt has been kind to hand his script out for the numerical limit analysis of shell structures,
so the author can modify it to ﬁt the material optimization. The scripts are added in Appendix
B.4.

5.3
Yield Condition of Shell
It is established that the disc element has a yield criteria that is dependant on the yield function
of stress, while the slab element uses another yield function, that is based on moment. In the case
of a shell element, the internal forces are described by both stress and moment, which may require
two yield functions.
In his PhD thesis, Herfelt demonstrates two diﬀerent approaches to estimate yield criteria for
the shell structure [5, p. 133]. The ﬁrst yield criterion is simple, as it requires two sets of yield
functions for in-plane action in the form of stress and slab moment deﬁned by
f σ (σ, Sσ) ≤0
f m (m, Sm) ≤0
(5.26)
Sσ and Sm denote the material parameters. However, the two yield functions are complete inde-
pendent. If it is desirable that they share the carrying capacity by interaction, an auxiliary variable
ζ can be added to deﬁne a linear combination by
fσ (σ, ζSσ) ≤0
fm (m, (1 −ζ) Sm) ≤0
ζ ∈[0, 1]
(5.27)
The yield condition of in-plane behaviour from Section 3.6 and the yield condition of out-of-plane
behaviour from Section 4.2 could be considered to be applied for shell elements. However, it may
prove to be a clumsy way to handle the two yield conditions. The yield condition of moment can not
handle optimization of the thickness of the concrete, as it can only optimize reinforcement. This
does not work with the yield condition of stress that can handle both thickness and reinforcement.
It must also be mentioned that two sets of yield conditions make the optimization process more
demanding. Furthermore, it may be practical to use a linear combination, but it is uncertain what
the auxiliary variable ζ means for the case of material optimization.
Herfelt also describes another yield criterion which is based on layer approach, as presented by
Larsen [9, p. 109]. In stead of calculating yield moment from material parameter and yield stress,
the application of a layer-based criterion allows converting of the moments to the stress state level,
so only one yield condition is required. It ensures a proper relationship between stress state and
moment state, when they are all subjected to one yield condition which is based on limit stress.
This approximates the actual yield envelope of the shell structure [5, p. 135]. The application
of a single yield condition also makes the optimization process less demanding, as compared to
including two yield conditions.

The base idea of the layer-based approach is that the internal stress states are deﬁned through a
number of layers. Each layer is considered as a concrete disc in a plane stress state, so it is subjected
to the yield condition of limit stress [9, p. 110]. Larsen’s version of the layer-based criterion involves
the eﬀects of the transverse shear stresses in the structure, so it can handle the shear reinforcement
in the concrete. However, this requires semideﬁnite programming for the convex optimization.
Herfelt suggests a simpliﬁcation of the layer-based criterion by not considering the transverse
shear stress, so the SOCP is suﬃcient [5, p. 135]. However, it means that the reinforcement is
solely orthogonal without any shear reinforcement. In addition to this, the reinforcement is treated
as separate layers with its own yield condition. In this paper, it is suﬃcient to demonstrate the
application of the layer-based criterion for the optimization of orthogonal reinforcement for a shell
structure consisting of reinforced concrete without any shear reinforcement.

Conclusion
The purpose of this paper is to examine the material optimization of four diﬀerent kinds of struc-
tures by using the FELA. The material optimization is achieved by modifying the numerical limit
load analysis.
The primary assumption made is that the structure is rigid-plastic.
The behaviour of ideal
plastic states that the structure does not deform before it collapses. The elastic contribution is
therefore neglected in this case. The stability of a structure ensures that it does not collapse. The
structure becomes unstable, if it is not able to resist the applied load and begins to yield. In order
to identify the collapse load, both lower and upper bound methods can be applied. However, the
analysis of collapse load has several feasible solutions by application of lower and upper bound
methods, which requires the approach of optimization to determine the true collapse load.
The numerical limit load analysis aims at maximising the external load, when the topology and
material distribution of a structure are ﬁxed and the allowed stress state is restricted by the yield
condition. By constructing the ﬁnite element method, the equilibrium between the internal forces
and the external forces is established and it is restricted by the yield condition. Because of this
restriction, the feasible solution is within the convex surface of yield condition and thus makes the
optimization problem convex.
In the case of material optimization, the applied load is ﬁxed, while the material distribution
is subjected to minimization of the total volume, so the bearing load capacity can be utilised
completely. This allows for a reduction in material consumption with beneﬁt for both environment
and economy. The material optimization can be applied to the design problem of ultimate limit
state. Since the analysis is based on the assumption of rigid-plastic behaviour, the serviceability
limit state is not feasible.
The bar structure has a simple stress state that is described by axial force. The yield condition
is simple, as the axial stress is not allowed to surpass the limit stress. The cross section area of
a bar element is a variable, that is subjected to optimization. It is to be minimized, still being
suﬃciently large to resist the applied load. The yield condition of a bar structure is linear, so
Linear Programming is suﬃcient to solve the optimization problem.
In order to execute the optimization problem, a system matrix consisting of force equilibrium and
restriction of yield condition and material strength is set up. The variables are stress parameter
and material parameter - the latter one being subjected to minimisation. The implementation of
material optimization for the bar structure has been validated, as the optimal solution fulﬁls the
simple hand calculation. In this paper, it is also shown that it is possible to apply several load
scenarios. Each load case has its own set of force equilibrium and stress parameters, but they all
use the same material parameter. This approach can be applied to any structure.
The addition of multiple material parameters per bar element is also established and examined.
It is shown that the optimization algorithm is able to make a design choice regarding what material
to apply to the structure. If a bar element is in tension, the algorithm prefers a material that is
strong at tension. The same apply to the case of compression that prefers the strong compressive
material. However, in the case of multiple load scenarios, where a bar can experience both tension
and compression, the algorithm is not able to make a choice between two materials, but prefers to
combine them. It is analogue to the case of reinforced concrete, where concrete takes compression
and reinforcement handles the tension. In this case, the composite material has the advantage that
it can handle both tension and compression in order to resist diﬀerent load scenarios.
The disc structure is a plate structure that is subjected to an in-plane force. The internal stress
state is described by stresses; σx, σy and shear stress τ. The yield condition becomes non-linear,
as it has to handle three diﬀerent stresses. In this paper, the reinforced concrete is applied, as it
allows for the option to use multiple material parameters per element, as thickness of concrete and
reinforcement area per unit length in x- and y-directions are subjected to optimization.
The force equilibrium is created with basis in the nodal force. The nodal forces at the three
corners of one element are used to ensure the equilibrium of stresses across the element boundaries.
In addition to this equilibrium, the internal equilibrium within the element is also set up by the
central nodal force.

The Mohr-Coulomb yield surface with the addition of restriction of reinforcement is applied
as the yield condition of reinforced concrete. Here, the reinforcement is located at the mid-plane.
However, it is not linear. In this case, the linearisation of yield condition is examined, as it requires
Linear Programming. Both pure concrete and reinforced concrete are examined and validated. It is
established that the optimization algorithm is able to ﬁnd an optimal solution for the disc structure,
where the concrete takes the compression and the reinforcement takes the tension. The composite
material proves to be a beneﬁt, as the bearing load capacity is utilised completely, wherever an
element experiences both compression and tension, when multiple load scenarios are applied.
The linearisation of the non-linear yield surface comes comes with the price of long execution
times in MATLAB. This becomes unacceptable, if a large number of elements is required to approx-
imate the complex model, for example a simply supported deep beam. This paper also contains
the implementation of generalised yield condition by applying Second Order Cones Programming.
The yield surface is transformed into a cone, so the material optimization can be carried out. By
doing this, the execution time is reduced.
The implementation of material optimization for the disc structures is valid for the case of both
pure concrete and reinforced concrete. This can also be applied to the pure steel structure, if the
von Mises yield condition is applied.
Furthermore, the implementation of self-weight is examined and validated. The self-weight is a
constant force that is distributed to the central nodal force of the elements and works downward.
In order to resist the accumulated self-weight of a wall, it hass to be thicker at the bottom than at
the top. This shows that self-weight is an important factor to consider, if the material consumption
is to be reduced. Heavy materials may lead to a demand for more material to resist the self-weight
which, for example, is undesirable for a bridge with a large span.
The slab structure is a plate structure, that is subjected to out-of-plate force that causes it to
bend. Because of the bending behaviour, the internal stress state is described by moments; mx, my
and mxy. Through downward bending, the moment creates both tension at bottom surface and
compression at the top surface. In order to resist the tension, the reinforcement must be placed
at a distance from the mid-plane. It is common that the reinforced concrete slab has two layers of
reinforcement with a distance to the mid-plane. The force equilibrium is set up with basis in the
Kirchhoﬀ-Love plate theory which describes the linear variation of moment between three corners
within the element and the equilibrium between the element boundaries.
The material optimization is based on the limit stress as restriction, so it proves to be a challenge
for the slab structure because of the moment state.
The failure in a given material is caused
by surpassing its limit stress. In this sense, the yield moment is derived from the limit stress
in collaboration with the dimension of the material. In this paper, it is suggested that the yield
moment should be calculated from the limit stress. The yield moment is equal to the multiplication
of reinforcement area per unit length, limit stress and the distance between the center of the
compression zone in the concrete and the reinforcement.
However, a new problem arises, because the distance between the center of the compression
zone and the reinforcement layer is an indication of the thickness of concrete. In consequence, the
material parameters of concrete and reinforcement are multiplied together, which is unsuitable for
the construction of a system matrix of force equilibrium and material restrictions. In this paper,
the implementation of material optimization for the slab structure aims at minimisation of total
volume of reinforcement, while the concrete thickness is ﬁxed.
It is suggested to improve the implementation by examining the separation of material variables,
so the material optimization can be carried out for both reinforcement and concrete. It is mentioned
that the distance between the reinforcement and the center of the compression zone in the concrete
is an indication of the thickness of the concrete. More reinforcement leads to to a larger compression
zone which, in return, leads to thicker concrete. In a sense, the distance is ﬁxed, but the concrete
thickness depends on the variables of the reinforcement, so this relationship is suggested to be
examined further in a future work.
The implementation of load cases is made, but the implementation of self-weight is not performed
for the slab structure. The self-weight depends on the total thickness of the concrete, however,
which may be unknown. It is suggested to examine in the further work.
Furthermore, it is noted that the implementation of force equilibrium has a numerical error

involving the moments that are not supposed to appear according to the equivalent beam model,
so it is suggested to improve the force equilibrium by applying another element composition. The
paper also shows that the optimal result depends on the number of elements, as a large number
leads to a more reﬁned distribution. Additionally, it is shown that the mesh of a structure has an
importance, as this is supposed to approximate the yield lines.
The shell structure can handle both in-plane force and out-of-plane force. In order to establish
the relationship between the stress state and the moment state, a layer approach is applied to
divide a shell element in a number of layers. The magnitude of moment can then be converted to
stress in each layer. In other word, the moment state is converted to the stress state, and therefore
the yield condition with basis in the limit stress can be applied.
A problem arises since a shell element is a plane element with its own local coordinate in a three-
dimensional space. In order to ensure the consistency of reinforcement between the elements, a
manipulation of local coordinates in relation to the desirable direction of the reinforcement is
suggested by the author.
It is established that the implementation of force equilibrium and the yield condition of stress
state can optimize the shell structure with regard to the reinforcement. As with the case of the slab
structure, the reinforcements are laid out in two layers in order to resist the tension contribution
from the bending. It is suggested that the implementation on optimization of thickness of concrete
in shell structures must be examined in a further work, it shares the same problem as the slab
structure.
The shell model is rather complex in that it involves the interaction between the membrane
behaviour and the bending behaviour. The implementation of both multiple load cases and self-
weight is not made and examined, so it is suggested that it be examined in further work.
As with the case of the slab structure, the numerical error also appears, which may lead to a
strange optimal result of reinforcement that is not supposed to appear. The computer believes
that the result fulﬁls the optimal solution of force equilibrium and inequality of restriction. It is
suggested to improve this in a further work.
In conclusion, in this paper, it is established that the ﬁnite element limit analysis can be used
for the material optimization that aims at minimization of the total volume of material, which
allows for lessening material consumption with beneﬁt for both environment and economy. The
implementation is rather straightforward, if the sectional forces are only described by the stress
state. However, the moment state proves to require more work to implement, but is, however,
possible. In the ﬁeld of design problem, the material optimization can be applied for the ultimate
limit state analysis, as it leads to an optimal material distribution for the rigid-plastic structure
to avoid a collapse in the plastic domain. By application of the ﬁnite element method and convex
optimization, the author’s contribution makes it possible to ﬁnd the optimal solution without
toilsome hand calculations which involve several iterations and guessworks to dimension a structural
member.

material. It is emphasised that the minimisation of weight can be carried out as a minimisation of
price tag or volume.

This section contains an overview of the MATLAB packages that execute the material optimiza-

B.1
Bar - Bar LP
The MATLAB package Bar LP consists of a main script that controls the properties and the
appearance of a truss structure and four functions.
The simple truss structure made by three bar elements is an example in the main script Bar3mo.m.
It is ready to be modiﬁed to apply multiple load case and multiple material parameter.
The function LP mo.m creates and assembles the equilibrium and inequality matrices. Thereafter
it executes the LP to ﬁnd the optimal material distribution with a minimization of total volume
of structure. The functions plot master.m, plotTop.m and plotLP.m generates some plots that
displays topology, material distribution, collapse mode and axial forces and utilisation ratio.

Disc - Disc SOCP RC
The MATLAB package Disc SOCP RC consists of a main script that controls the properties and
