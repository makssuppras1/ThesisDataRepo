Summary
The soil beneath a former dry cleaning facility, situated at Rødekro, southern Jutland is contami-
nated with concentrations of Tetrachloroethene (PCE) and its degradation products Trichloroethene
(TCE), cis-1,2-Dichloroethene (cDCE) and Vinyl Chloride (VC) above the thresholds of Danish EPA
for groundwater. In 2006 thermal remediation was implemented at the source area, which resulted
in release of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) in the subsurface that altered the redox conditions,
microbial activity and contaminant concentrations. The changes that resulted from the remediation
are well characterized by previous investigations, by using groundwater sampling and microbial
tools.
Direct current (DC) resistivity surveys are used for the characterization of the geology of contam-
inated sites. Induced Polarization (IP) method is used, in combination with DC method and is
sensitive to diffusion processes at the interface of the grain and pore water. In order to evaluate
how Direct current and Induced Polarization method (DCIP) can contribute to the characterization
of the contaminant plume at Rødekro, a literature review of geophysical signatures in contaminated
sites with organic pollutants was conducted.
Combined Direct Current and Induced Polarization (DCIP) surveys were conducted at 2 locations
(1000 m and 750 m downgradient of the plume) across the plume with multiple gradient array, using
the newly acquired by DTU instrument ABEM Terrameter LS. At the ﬁrst location, two acquisition
were made, a 50% duty cycle waveform and a 100% duty cycle waveform were applied along a
400 m and a 200 m proﬁle respectively. At the second location a 100% duty cycle waveform was
applied along a 400 m proﬁle. The objective of the surveys was to investigate if the DCIP method
is an effective tool for characterizing the geochemical processes and microbial activity triggered by
thermal treatment.
The data were inverted using Aarhusinv inversion code, incorporated in Aarhus Workbench soft-
ware. Resistivity and spectral IP models were interpreted by comparing with borehole logs, and
were compared with knowledge about redox conditions and microbial activity from previous inves-
tigations.
The results of the survey could only resolve saturated melt-water sand and gravel and unsaturated
melt-water sand and gravel. The contaminant plume, the difference between iron-reducing and
sulfate reducing conditions and pyrite were not detected by the method. Data quality was very
poor, especially for the 400 m Proﬁles and less for the 200 m Proﬁle, which can be attributed to
coupling effects and unknown sources of noise.

Introduction
Drinking water supply in Denmark is entirely based on groundwater. Chlorinated solvents, such
as Tetrachloroethene (PCE) and Trichloroethene (TCE) are widespread groundwater contaminants
that have affected groundwater supplies at multiple locations throughout Denmark. The contami-
nation often originates from releases from dry cleaning facilities. PCE is degraded through reductive
dechlorination to TCE, cis-1,2-Dichloroethylene (cis-1,2-DCE), Vinyl Chloride (VC) and ﬁnally ethy-
lene under anaerobic conditions. PCE and VC are known carcinogens and can cause adverse effects
on human health in small concentrations.
Delineation of contaminant plumes is traditionally conducted through borehole drilling and chemi-
cal sampling. However, borehole drillings can create preferential paths for vertical spreading of the
contamination, are expensive and provide information only at their speciﬁc location. In contrast,
near-surface investigations with geophysical methods are non invasive, cost less than boreholes and
provide continuous imaging of the subsurface. Moreover, current commercial data acquisition in-
struments enable multichannel measurements, rendering geoelectrical surveys faster. Direct current
(DC) resistivity methods are sensitive to the pore water chemistry and grain-ﬂuid (surface) interface.
Induced Polarization (IP) method is an extension of resistivity method that is sensitive to diffusion
mechanisms of ions at the grain-ﬂuid interface. However, the mechanisms that cause IP signals in
contaminated subsurface are not well known, often rendering interpretation of DCIP results am-
biguous.
1.1
Aim of the project
In this study, Direct Current and Induced Polarization (DCIP) measurements were conducted at
a former dry cleaning facility, located in Rødekro, in Southern Jutland, Denmark. A plume with
PCE and its degradation products has migrated more than 2 km from the facility. In 2006 thermal
remediation was implemented in the source area.
The main advantage of the study site is that the geology, contamination and redox conditions before
and after remediation are well characterized by previous investigations (Badin et al., 2016; Hunkeler

2.1.1.7
Flow of electric current in earth materials
Electric current can ﬂow through a rock in three different ways. These are electrolytic conduction,
electronic conduction and dielectric conduction. Electrolytic conduction is the process by which the
current ﬂows via ion movement in groundwater. This a relatively slow process comparing to elec-
tronic conduction, by which current ﬂows rapidly, for example in metals via free electrons. Dielectric
conduction occurs by external alternating current being applied in insulators and in this way caus-
ing shift of the atomic electrons with respect to the nuclie (Reynolds, 1997). Electrolytic conduction
is likely the most common mechanism in environmental applications, while electronic conduction
takes place in mineral surveys when minerals, such as sulﬁde and graphite, are present.
Loke (2001) has reviewed the typical resistivity values of rocks, minerals and soils (Figure 2.6).
Different classes of rocks and soils present overlapping values of resistivity, since the resistivity of a
speciﬁc kind of rock and soil is controlled by a variety of factors such as the porosity, the degree of
saturation, the concentration of total dissolved solids (TDS), the clay content and the presence of
immiscible and insulating phases in the pore space (Loke, 2001; Revil et al., 2012a). Clayey soil is
more conductive than sandy soil (Loke, 2001).
Archie’s law (Archie, 1942) is an empirical model that describes the relationship among the bulk
resistivity of the granular medium (ρ [Ohm·m]), its porosity (φ [-]), the ﬂuid resistivity within the
pores (ρw [Ohm·m]) and saturation (S [-]) for coarse-grained rocks and soils (Equation 2.8):

2.1.2
Principle of induced polarization
The phenomenon of Induced Polarization (IP) was introduced by Conrad Schlumburger in 1912, but
developments of the ﬁeld method were not published before late 1950s. It has been primarily used
for mineral exploration and only in recent decades in environmental applications. Induced polar-
ization expresses the reversible storativity of geological charges of geological materials. Speciﬁcally,
when a current is applied at the soil surface, some energy is stored in the subsurface during the
current ﬂow. According to (Telford et al., 1990), the energy is stored mainly in the form of chemical
energy. The two following mechanisms contribute to the aforementioned chemical energy storage
at low frequencies (<1 kHz): a) membrane or electrolytic polarization in non metallic materials
and b) electrode polarization or overvoltage when metallic materials exist in rocks. The ﬁrst mech-
anism is associated with the differences between the mobility of cations and anions in electrolyte in
a porous material. The second is caused by the difference between electronic conductivity (through
the metallic mineral) and electrolytic conductivity (through pore water).

2.1.2.1
Membrane polarization
Current can exclusively ﬂow via electrolytic conduction in rock structures when metallic minerals
are absent like in silica and the frequency is low (Telford et al., 1990). Therefore, the soil matrix
should be porous to allow ﬂow current. When in contact with water, the surfaces of minerals grains
like silica grains have a net negative charge due to chemical reactions (Revil and Florsch, 2010).
Cations (counterions) are as a result attracted towards the grain surface, while anions (co-ions) are
repelled from the grain/water interface. Therefore, a diffuse layer where mostly counterions are
present coats the silica grain. Some counterions are sorbed onto the charged surface and constitute
the ﬁxed layer, also called Stern layer (Revil and Florsch, 2010). The net surface charge of the Stern
layer is measured by the ion exchange capacity (IEC) (Sogade et al., 2006). The Stern layer and
the diffuse layer form the so called electrical double layer (EDL) presented in Figure 2.7. Since the
concentration of ions in the Stern layer is higher than the one in the diffuse layer, the conductivity
of the Stern layer that is also called surface conductivity is higher (excess conductivity of the Stern
layer).
Figure 2.7: Sketch of the electrical double layer (EDL) coating a silica grain (Revil and Florsch,
2010). The Stern layer causes the excess conductivity ΣS in S. The Stern layer lies
between the mineral surface (o-Plane) and the inner side of the diffuse layer (d-Plane).
When an electrical ﬁeld is imposed through this porous medium, cations in the electrolyte move in
the direction of the applied electrical ﬁeld, while anions move to the opposite direction (Figure 2.8b,
c). Membrane polarization arises when the diffuse layer is thick enough and pores are narrow so that
only ions of certain size and polarity can pass by. The mobility of charge carriers is thereby reduced
and charge storage is created. In this case the diffuse layer is called membrane zone (Sogade et al.,
2006). When the current is switched off a secondary or residual voltage is created as the ions move
back to their equilibrium position in a ﬁnite time.

2.1.2.2
Electrode (Grain) polarization
Electrode or grain polarization appears when metallic particles exist in the rock structure, and thus
conduction is both electronic and electrolytic. Figure 2.8d illustrates a metallic grain with opposite
sign net surface charges on each face, that attract counterions of the adjacent electrolyte. After
applying an exciting current, the phenomenon of overvoltage, in which an electron of the adjacent
electrolyte is exchanged with an electron of the metal takes place (Figure 2.9). Since the current
ﬂows faster through the metal than through the electrolyte, the external voltage sustains the charge
buildup at the metal/electrolyte interface (Telford et al., 1990). After the interruption of the primary
current, there is a secondary or residual voltage as ions diffuse back to their equilibrium positions in
the electrolyte within a ﬁnite time period. Electrode polarization takes place when the minerals are
good electronic conductors, such as most sulﬁdes (expect for pure sphalerite, and likely cinnabar
and stibnite) (Telford et al., 1990). According to Ntarlagiannis et al. (2005) electrode polarization
can arise for as low as 1% volume concentration of metal sulﬁdes. Some oxides such as magnetite
and graphite are also good electronic conductors that exhibit electrode polarization (Telford et al.,
1990).

2.2
Noise sources
Several superﬁcial and underground man-made structures can serve as sources of electromagnetic
(EM) noise. The proximity of 2D resistivity proﬁles to elongated good conductors should be avoided
because current can leak to the good conductor. Typical examples of elongated good conductors
are metallic fences with galvanic contact to the ground, underground power lines and telephone
lines, buried pipelines (Møller et al., 2006). In addition, location of 2D resistive proﬁles parallel
to elongated resistors such as streams, dykes, ditches and road beds can distort the data to a lesser
extent (Møller et al., 2006; Szarka, 1987). Møller et al. (2006) suggest to place the proﬁles at
a safety distance of 1.5 to 2 times the expected penetration depth from the aforementioned good
conductors and resistors. Man-made constructions crossing the proﬁles perpendicular do not distort
data quality (Møller et al., 2006).

Another possible source of noise is electrode charge-up, or electrode polarization. Electrode po-
larization is a phenomenon that originates from charge build up at the interface between metal
electrodes that are conductive and the less conductive surrounding subsurface (Dahlin, 2000). This
effect arises when an electrode is used for current injection and may affect data quality if the same
electrode is used for potential measurement in short time (Dahlin, 2000). The multiple-gradient
array protocol used in this study is designed so as to maximize the time gap between two electrodes
being used as a AB injection dipole and a MN potential measurement dipole (Gazoty et al., 2013).
Electrode polarization effect decreases by increasing Ton and Tof f (Fiandaca et al., 2012).
Capacitive coupling is the leak of electrical current to nearby conductors (Dahlin and Leroux, 2012).
This mechanism is more probable to arise as the cable length increases due to the fact that the
total capacitance is proportional to the cable length (Dahlin and Leroux, 2012). Capacity coupling
can cause unrealistically high chargeability values (Dahlin and Leroux, 2012). Dahlin and Leroux
(2012) demonstrated that capacitive coupling can be reduced by connecting current and potential
electrodes to separate cables.

2.3
Inversion theory
Inversion is an iterative process aiming to ﬁnd a model, i.e. an idealized mathematical representation
of a section of the earth that is the best approximation of the observed data (pseudosection). A
model of the distribution of the resistivity and chargeability of the subsurface is initially created,
from which a new distribution of resistivity and chargeability is produced (forward). Then, the
model is iteratively modiﬁed until the difference between the peudosection and forward response is
minimized (optimization) (Loke, 2001).
In 1D Laterally Constrained Inversion (LCI) each 1D sounding corresponds to a layered model (Fig-
ure 2.14). All models are inverted at the same time with constraints between neighboring models.
Through lateral constraints information is spread to adjacent models. Therefore, these constraints
can be regarded as a priori information on the heterogeneity of the geology and contamination in
the subsurface in the horizontal direction (Auken et al., 2005). The lower the heterogeneity is, the
higher is the penalty function that is added to the objective function. A-priori information that is
retrieved from existing borehole logs or previous investigations can be used for ρ, m0, τ, c and layer
thickness of depth to layer.
Figure 2.14: Sketch of 1D Laterally Constrained Inversion (LCI). The pseudosection is divided into
n 1D models with the same subset of parameters. Each dataset contains resistivity (ρ),
chargeability (m0), relaxation time (τ), frequency exponent (c) and thickness (thk),
as well as lateral (horizontal) constraints between neighboring models and constraints
from a priori information. The datasets are inverted simultaneously (Fiandaca et al.,
2012).

(10-30) with ﬁxed layer boundaries. Smooth inversion can be performed either with L2 smoothness

Laboratory studies and ﬁeld applications have revealed different effects of non-aqueous phase liquids
(NAPL) on resistivity and induced polarization (Johansson et al., 2015). Therefore, the mechanisms
that cause resistivity anomalies and measurable IP signal are not yet fully understood. Subsurface
microbial communities, which grow in the presence of organic contaminants, can alter geoelectrical
signatures. The study of this phenomenon belongs to the ﬁeld of biogeophysics. The interpretation of
geophysical signatures triggered by microbial processes is usually qualitative, pertaining to changes
from initial subsurface conditions (Slater and Atekwana, 2013). This chapter reviews geophysi-
cal signatures, speciﬁcally regarding electrical conductivity and induced polarization in petroleum
hydrocarbon and chlorinated solvents impacted sites.
3.1
Petroleum hydrocarbon-impacted areas
Hydrocarbon contamination emanating from spills in petrochemical industry sites poses concern
in the environment since hydrocarbons are known carginogens and neurotoxic. Petroleum hydro-
carbons are light non-aqueous phase liquids (LNAPLs). When released in the subsurface, LNAPL
partitions in the four following phases illustrated in Figure 3.1: a) vapor phase in the vadoze zone,
b) pools of free phase composed of pure LNAPL that ﬂoats on top of the water table, c) immobile
residual phase that corresponds to trapped LNAPL in pore spaces between soil particles in a smear
zone due to seasonal ﬂuctuations of the water table, d) dissolved phase that involves the hydro-
carbons that are dissolved in pore water (Allen et al., 2007). Biodegradation of hydrocarbons can
proceed in two different ways: a) complete mineralization into carbon dioxide (CO2), water (H2O)
and cell protein or b) transformation to simpler organic compounds by populations of microorgan-
isms (Das et al., 2011).

3.1.1
DC resistivity and IP signatures
Previous studies have found different effects of LNAPL on the bulk resistivity of the soil. According
to Werkema (2003), a study carried out by Lien and Enﬁeld (1998) at a hydrocarbon contaminated
site with direct push resistivity method showed increased bulk resistivity. The elevated resistivity
was interpreted as a result of displacement of pore water by LNAPL free phase, which is more
resistive than groundwater. Werkema (2003) conducted a ﬁeld survey with vertical resistivity probes
(VRPs) and obtained elevated conductivity in the smear zone of LNAPL that was inferred to be
associated with microbial activity. According to Werkema (2003), this result indicates that the zone
of most active biodegradation might be in partly saturated conditions and not in the saturated zone.
Atekwana and Atekwana (2010) performed a resistivity survey at a hydrocarbon contaminated site.
Two 2D resistivity proﬁles were acquired, one across the source zone, where both residual phase
and dissolved phase existed, and another downgradient where only dissolved phase was present.
From the ﬁrst proﬁle low resistivity anomaly was derived in the vadoze zone near LNAPL source,
extending a little in the saturated zone. On the other hand, the dissolved phase of hydrocarbons did
not cause any resistivity anomalies in the second proﬁle, although high TDS and redox conditions
indicate on going active biodegradation.
Allen et al. (2007) listed the three following reasons for signatures of higher electrical conductiv-
ity at LNAPL sites: 1) Metabolites produced by biodegradation of hydrocarbons, such as carbonic
acid and organic acids cause mineral dissolution that releases ions in groundwater, increasing pore
water conductivity σw and thus also electrolytic conductivity according to Archie’s law. Moreover,
mineral dissolution may increase the porosity of the medium, thereby also increasing electrolytic
conductivity (Abdel Aal et al., 2004). 2) Microorganisms attach on mineral surfaces and may form
bioﬁlms that accelerate mineral weathering and 3) some bacteria from the Geobacter and Shewanella
genera produce bacteria nanowires that are conductive pili and could therefore increase subsurface
conductivity.
Martinho et al. (2006) performed laboratory experiments on clayey soil samples with different ratios
of gasoline, isopentane, toluene and benzene to water, and derived decrease in chargeability m for

with some ﬁne sand, clay and silt lenses. The resistivity model did not correlate with contaminant
concentrations.

3.2
Chlorinared solvents-impacted areas
Chlorinated solvents belong to the category of dense non-aqueous phase liquids (DNAPLs). Figure
3.3 illustrates a sketch of residual DNAPL in saturated unconsolidated deposit. DNAPL separate
phase can form mobile (continuous) pools or immobile (residual) blobs and ganglia (Kueper et al.,
2003). Continuous phase can exceed groundwater capillary pressure and migrate in the subsurface,
while residual phase forms droplets (called ganglia) that are not connected to other droplets in
the pore space. Their capillary pressure is lower than the capillary pressure of groundwater and
therefore they cannot displace the pore water (ITRC, 2015). When chlorinated solvents are released
in the subsurface, mobile DNAPL penetrate through the saturated zone, because they are denser
that water and migrate vertically until stopped by an impermeable layer where they create ponds.
DNAPLs have low solubilities and thus dissolve slowly in the saturated zone forming large plumes
(aqueous phase). The contaminant mass exists mostly in concentrated pools (Ajo-Franklin et al.,
2006).
DNAPLs are non polar insulators (Ajo-Franklin et al., 2006). The electrical resistivity of pure TCE is
1.25 ·106 Ohm·m at 20 oC (Lucius et al., 1992), and decreases in soil mixtures where microbial ac-
tivity exists, while the electrical resistivity of water is 10-15 Ohm·m (Cardarelli and Filippo, 2009).
Most dissolved chlorinated hydrocarbons are hydrophobic and thus adsorb to the soil particles. Ad-
sorption is associated with IP signatures (Sogade et al., 2006).
Figure 3.3: Conceptual model of dense non-aqueous phase liquids (DNAPL) distribution in uncon-
solidated sediments (Kueper et al., 2003) after Pankow and Cherry (1996).
In anaerobic conditions the main path of biodegradation for chlorinated solvents is reductive dechlo-
rination, in which one chlorine atom (Cl– ) is sequentially replaced by one hydrogen atom (H+), to
eventually produce ethylene. Tetrachloroethene (PCE) is thereby biodegraded to Trichloroethene
(TCE), from TCE to cis-1,2-Dichloroethene (cDCE), from cDCE to Vinyl Chloride (VC) and from
VC to ethene (Figure 3.4). The lower is the oxidation state of chlorinated ethenes (i.e. from PCE

organic carbon to deplete alternative inorganic electron acceptors (i.e. dissolved oxygen, nitrate,

3.2.1
DC resistivity and IP signatures
Johansson et al. (2015) applied the DCIP method at a former dry cleaning facility in Sweden to
examine if the DNAPL PCE source can be delineated with this method. The geology of the site
consisted of sandy chalk till underlaid by a limestone bedrock. On top of the till, there was a varved
clay layer and ﬁll material. The DNAPL PCE source existed in the top clay layer or ﬁll and in the
till. Their study gave promising results, since a high resistive anomaly was obtained at the location
where the soil sampling indicated high concentrations of separate phase PCE (0.1-300 mg/kg TS) in
the till, which was believed to mostly form a continuous phase in the subsurface (Johansson et al.,
2015). However, these anomalies could also be related with coarser till matrix. No IP effect was
detected at the wells with highest DNAPL concentrations. However, IP anomalies in chargeability
m0 and exponent c models exist next to the boreholes adjacent to the continuous DNAPL phase.
In this area, Johansson et al. (2015) suggest that biodegradation might fractionate the continuous
phase into droplets. The IP effect is related to membrane polarization mechanism due to release
of a high amount of chloride ions that increases salinity, in combination with the DNAPL droplets
blocking the pore paths.
2D and 3D DCIP surveys were conducted at Grindsted landﬁll, in terms of GEOCON project that
”aims at advancing geological, geophysical and contaminant monitoring technologies for contam-
inated site investigations” (Maurya, 2016; Møller et al., 2016). The 3D resistivity measurements
at the leachate plume that contained high concentrations of cDCE and VC (14-3800 µg/L) were
capable to resolve an anomaly of low resistivity that is associated with reductive dechlorination of
PCE to its degradation products and concomitant release of chloride (Cl– ). Moreover, the fact that
this anomaly was found north of a stream downgradient of the landﬁll and not at the south part of
the stream, indicates that the contaminant plume has not entered the stream (Maurya, 2016). The
spectral IP parameters normalized chargeability MN, τ and c, where not affected by contamination,
but they may characterize clay content in the studied area and grain size.
Sparrenbom et al. (2017) conducted a DCIP survey at a contaminated site with chlorinated solvents,
with time lapse inversion to investigate if it is a tool that can be used to monitor stimulated reductive
dechlorination with organic carbon injection by direct push into a TCE source area. Organic stimulus

4.1
Study site description
The study area, previously described by Hunkeler et al. (2011), is situated in the town of Rødekro
in southern Jutland. A regional dry-cleaning facility that operated from 1967 to 1980 has caused
groundwater contamination with chlorinated ethenes. Release of PCE that likely contained impuri-
ties of TCE was inferred to have originated from an underground storage tank and/or dry-cleaning
machines, as well as the connection pipes from the machines to the tank. As a result, a plume
extending more than 2 km downgradient from the source has been created. The contaminants of
concern are PCE and chlorinated degradation products, i.e. TCE, cDCE and VC (Figure 4.1).
55 multilevel monitoring wells have been established at the site. The head data from the wells,
in combination with the contaminant distribution suggest that the groundwater ﬂow direction is
towards the south for about 1 km, and subsequently alters to southeast (Hunkeler et al., 2011).
The average groundwater velocity was estimated to be around 0.24 m/day (Hunkeler et al., 2011),
with possible local variations because of particle size distribution that causes different hydraulic
conductivities (Badin et al., 2016).

Method

investigations.

5.4
Measurement setup
The measurements were set up by following ABEM Terrameter LS user manual (ABEM Instrument
AB, 2016a). The instrument array, transmitter and receiver setting for the 3 data acquisitions are
summarized in Table B.1. For Proﬁle 1 the resistivity and induced polarization (RES, IP) measure
mode was used, with a 50% duty cycle waveform. During the survey, resistance was measured and
chargeability was calculated. The received voltages for each quadrupole were repeatedly measured
with 2 stackings. The delay time, i.e. delay from current turn-on until resistivity measurement
starts, was set to 3 s. The acquisition time, i.e. integration time for resistivity measurement, was
set to 1 s. Overall, the on-time was equal to 4 s. IP data were acquired using 17 gates in total
(Table B.2) from 25-3520 ms covering the full IP decay using log-gating (Gazoty et al., 2013). The
powerline frequency was set to 50 Hz to ﬁlter powerline noise (ABEM Instrument AB, 2016a). The
transmitter current was set in the range between 20 and 500 mA. Before the start of data acquisitions,
Terrameter conducted electrode contact tests using the focus one method. In this method contact
resistance measurement is acquired for each electrode against all other electrodes, using an electrode
test current of 20 mA in order to ensure good grounding of all electrodes before measurement starts.
The threshold for acceptable contact resistance (bad electrode) was set to 10 KOhm.
For Proﬁles 2 and 3 the resistivity (RES) mode was applied and thereby a 100 % duty cycle waveform
was used. During the survey resistance was measured. The received voltages for each quadrupole
were repeatedly measured with 2 stackings. The delay time and the acquisition time were set to 4
s and 0.1 s respectively, giving an overall on-time equal to 4.1 s. The transmitter current was set
in the range between 200 and 500 mA to improve signal-to-noise ratio. The sample rate, which
is especially useful for extracting IP information from full waveform, was set to 3750/4500 Hz. A
high value was used so that more samples could be used for data calculation (For proﬁle 1, the old
version of Terrameter used a default sample rate of 1000 Hz). The threshold for acceptable contact
resistance was set to 20 KOhm.
Full waveform was recorded for all three proﬁles, which enabled signal processing. In order to
prevent electrical breakdown in relay, cable and connector insulators the maximum output voltage
of ABEM Terrameter LS of 600 V was used. The maximum delivered power was also limited to 250
W due to the restriction in magnitude of output voltage (Gazoty et al., 2013). The survey along
Proﬁles 1 and 3 was performed along a line 400 m long with 5 m electrode spacing, while the length
of Proﬁle 2 was 200 m with 2.5 m electrode spacing.

5.7
Inversion procedure
The processed DC and IP full decay data were inverted with the inversion code AarhusInv of Aarhus
Workbench software, using Cole-Cole model (http://hgg.au.dk/software/aarhusinv/). AarhusInv
can perform 1D DC or combined DC/IP inversions using smooth, sharp or layered inversion methods,
as well as 2D DC or combined DC/IP inversions using smooth or sharp inversion methods. AarhusInv
can produce models of resistivity (ρ), chargeability (m0) and spectral IP parameters time constant
(τ) and exponent (c) (see paragraph 2.1.2.4).
Besides these models, AarhusInv can calculate the depth of investigation (DOI) and data residuals
for DC and IP. A conservative and a standard DOI are calculated, with the section between them con-
taining information that is poorly constrained by the data, while the region below the standard DOI
is not constrained by the data (unreliable). Residuals are indicative of the quality of the inversion.
If the data residual is close to 1, the misﬁt between the forward response and data is approximately
equal to assumed noise. Too high data residual might indicate that there is more noise in the data
than assumed by the noise model, or that the inversion type used (1D/2D or smooth, sharp, layered)
does not represent the examined piece of earth (SkyTEM Aps, 2010).
Initially, only the DC data were inverted with different 1D inversion methods to inspect residuals,
assuming 2% noise (Olsson et al., 2015). The residuals exceeded unity by far for all proﬁles, and

7.1
Interpretation
7.1.1
Resistivity
Based on the available knowledge of the geology from the geological conceptual model in Figure 4.2
along the plume, 3 layers were expected to be found in the resistivity models, a 2-3 m thick layer of
unsaturated melt-water sand, an around 10 m layer of saturated melt-water gravel and ﬁnally sat-
urated melt-water sand. Jørgensen et al. (2003) reported resistivity values in fresh-water saturated
melt-water sand and gravel in Denmark above 60 Ohm·m, based on comparison of transient elec-
tromagnetic (TEM) surveys and borehole logs. Across the plume, along Proﬁle 2 borehole logs B35,
B29 and B34 from Jupiter database provide geological information, while near Proﬁle 3 only one
borehole (B28) exists. Therefore, the geology across the plume is well known 1050 m downgradi-
ent of the source area and less known 750 m downgradient of the source area. Moreover, resistivity
surveys have the advantage to provide a continuous image of the subsurface, which can supplement
the knowledge from boreholes that provide geological information for their speciﬁc location.
The fact that the aquifers of Quartenary age in this area of Southern Jutland are mainly sandy would
make it easier to interpret potential anomalies of lower resistivity due to redox changes, while in
many studies the presence of clayey or clayey till that are more conductive due to their additional
surface conductivity render interpretation ambiguous.
According to Archie’s law, resistivity correlates with pore water resistivity. In this site, the redox
conditions 750 m and 1050 m downgradient of the source area, where the DCIP surveys were carried
out are different and they also vary with depth (Figure 4.3). Therefore, the degradation rate of
chlorinated solvents, and as a result, the concentration of released Cl– could be different in the
two locations and also vary with depth. However, the measured values of electrical conductivity in
monitoring wells B28 and B34 are of the same order of magnitude, for varying depths (Table 5.1),
which shows that redox conditions and microbial activity might not affect resistivity. It should also
be noted that the highest concentrations of chlorinated solvents of Grindsted leachate plume, where
a resistivity anomaly was found (Maurya, 2016), were 1 order of magnitude higher than the highest

concentrations in this site in 2014 (Table 5.1).
Inverted resistivity models show that the geology across the plume is quite homogeneous. Resistivity
results for Proﬁle 1 and 2 exhibit two different layers that can be interpreted as unsaturated melt-
water sand and gravel and saturated melt-water sand and gravel. The interface between the two
layers in the model is 2 m deeper than the measured water table in May, i.e. the thickness of the
unsaturated zone is overestimated by the model. As aforementioned, DC resistivity measurements
tend to overestimate the thickness of layers, as a result of anisotropy, since the current patterns
traverse through layers (Christiansen et al., 2007). From the models it can be inferred that sand,
gravel and stone are mixed in the subsurface and do not create separate layers.
Higher values of resistivity were measured in the unsaturated zone along Proﬁle 3 than 2, which
indicates either that there is higher amount of gravel and stone or the soil matrix is more coarse.
The thickness of the layer is around 5 m, while the water table was measured 4m bgs at B28, which
is west of the line, so the model matches with the measurement quite well. The only difference from
Proﬁle 2 is that between the unsaturated zone and the saturated melt-water sand and gravel there is
a 4 m zone in the coordinate interval x=120-385 m that exhibits resistivity 20 Ohm·m higher than
the rest unsaturated zone. This can also be attributed to coarser soil matrix or higher amount of
gravel and and stone.
Regarding redox changes and microbial activity triggered by DOC release after the thermal treat-
ment, it can be assumed that they are not resolved by DC measurements, since the inverted resistivity
models exhibit values above 100 Ohm·m.
7.1.2
Induced polarization
The literature review in Chapter 3 demonstrated that NAPL sources can be associated with IP sig-
nal arising from membrane polarization mechanism, though this mechanism is not well understood
and the research is on going. Pore constriction due to the presence of NAPL droplets, in combi-
nation with increased salinity due to the release of Cl– is a possible interpretation. The present
study focuses on the contaminant plume with chlorinated solvents, where DCIP is less studied. The
contaminant plume is not resolved in the models of m0, c and τ. Maurya (2016) and Møller et al.
(2016) conducted 2D and 3D DCIP surveys at a contaminant plume with chlorinated solvents orig-
inating from Grindsted landﬁll, as a part of GEOCON project. Their inverted models did not show
any IP anomalies due to the contaminant plume.
Previous studies have shown that pyrite may exist at the area around borehole B34 at 19-50 m
depth (Badin et al., 2016). Pyrite is a semi-conductor mineral that is eminently polarizable and can
increase IP signal (Veeken et al., 2012). In the ﬁeld data of this study, the presence of pyrite (marked
area with red dashed line in inverted model for Proﬁles 1 and 2 did not give rise to any chargeability
anomalies. Pyrite concentration is probably lower than IP method can resolve. Another reason could
be that IP signal cannot be resolved due to noise sources of unknown origin. The low chargeabilities
of 25-35 mV/V lie in the interval observed by Gazoty et al. (2012) for a sand layer [20-50] mV/V.
Sightly higher chargeability values by 10-20 mV/V were observed around the water table, which
do not have a consistent trend, to enable correlation with the plume. For proﬁle 1 and 3 this layer
extends along the whole model, while for Proﬁle 2 it extends mostly outside the boundaries of the
plume.
Time constant τ is below 10 s for all proﬁles, i.e. below the reported values of [10,100] s for

7.2
Discussion
The ﬁeld data in this study were characterized by poor data quality, which increased with increasing
depth. In the DCIP survey conducted in October, a 100% duty cycle waveform and a higher sample
rate were used. Saltwater was also applied to the electrodes, in contrast with DCIP acquisition
in May 2016. Moreover, for Proﬁle 2, a 200 m line was used with higher resolution, but lower
apparent penetration depth than the other two Proﬁles. Although, the electrode contact resistance
of the electrodes was decreased and the higher sample rate that was used enabled more advanced IP
signal processing, the IP data quality was poor and a high amount of IP data were removed during
processing.
Noisy DC data were acquired for all the proﬁles, but mostly for Proﬁle 1 and 3, where 20% noise
was assumed for the inversions. The corresponding percentage for Proﬁle 2 was 5%. Moreover,
the standard depth of investigation (DOI) of Proﬁle 2 was higher than that of Proﬁles 1 and 3,
though the apparent penetration depth of data acquisition along Proﬁle 2 was lower. The electrode
separation for Proﬁle 2 was half of the other two arrays, which means lower geometric factor, thus,
higher signal. Therefore, for Proﬁle 2 the signal may be less covered from noise because of capacity
coupling that can be responsible for isolated values of high chargeability, according to Dahlin and
Leroux (2012). To reduce capacitive coupling, separate cables for current and potential electrodes
can be used (Dahlin and Leroux, 2012).
The maximum number of DC data acquired for a multiple-gradient array is 1022 data. Less data
were acquired along Proﬁles 2 and 3, because some of the electrodes were excluded and also due
to errors during data acquisition. It is important to ensure that as many electrodes as possible are
used. Different inversion methods were tried. Due to the lack of distinct anomalies 1D and 2D L1
norm inversions gave similar results. Lower residuals were achieved with 2D DCIP inversion, since it
creates a 2D forward response by using linear interpolation between adjacent models. However, 2D
combined DC and IP inversion is more time consuming. The start model does not affect the results
of the inversion with minimum-structured multi-layered method signiﬁcantly.
In this study, the location of DCIP proﬁles was selected in order to cover both contaminated and
uncontaminated areas, and also locations with different redox condition so that a qualitative in-
terpretation could be done by comparison. In order to render DCIP surveys more effective, more
research is needed in the direction of quantitative interpretation. For example, it would be useful
to know how high concentrations of chlorinated solvents and dissolved organic matter should exist,
as well as how high should the microbial activity be in the subsurface, in order to acquire measur-
able DC and IP signatures. Therefore, a quantitative interpretation scheme for contaminant plumes
originating from landﬁlls with be made as a part of the GEOCON project (Maurya, 2016).

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion and Perspectives
Geoelectrical ﬁeld survey across a contaminated plume with chlorinated ethenes at Rødekro, Den-
mark was carried out. Three DCIP proﬁles were measured using multiple gradient array, with the
newly acquired instrument by DTU Environment, ABEM Terrameter LS. From the acquired data, 2D
models of resistivity and spectral IP parameters (chargeability, time constant and exponent) were
created with Aarhusinv code in Aarhus Workbench software.
The resistivity models were quite consistent with the known geology from borehole logs. An unsatu-
rated melt-water sand and gravel layer with resistivity above 200 Ohm·m and a saturated melt-water
sand and gravel layer with resistivity around 100 Ohm·m were resolved c. 1050 m downgradient of
the source area. However, the thickness of the unsaturated layer is overestimated, which is observed
in DC resistivity measurements because current patterns traverse through layers. The chargeability
of the unsaturated sand and gravel was around 25-35 mV/V, while near the groundwater level the
chargeability was 10-20 mV/V higher. The contamination plume was not resolved in the resistivity
and chargeability models or the IP spectral parameters tau and c based on a qualitative interpeta-
tion of the results. The area of interest was poorly resolved by the data, since high amount of data
were removed during processing.
In order to increase signal-to-noise ratio, it is important to inject 200-500 mA current, and for this
reason, saltwater should be applied to electrodes. It is important to examine possible sources of
noise before choosing the location of a DCIP survey. DCIP method is non-invasive, less expensive
and faster than other tools of contaminated site characterization. However, the mechanisms that
cause IP signals are still being researched.
The DCIP survey was conducted at the site 10 years after thermal treatment was implemented at the
source area, with focus 1050 m downgradient of the source area, where DOC release has created
more reduced conditions. Based on the literature review in the ﬁrst part of the thesis, it would be
interesting to perform DCIP survey at a site where the source zone is subjected to thermal remedia-
tion before the remediation and multiple times after remediation, in order to study the changes how
the biogeochemical processes resulting from the released organic matter can affect resistivity and
spectral IP parameters m0, τ and c. In order to develop a better understanding of the applicability
of DCIP in contaminant plumes, future research is required to enable quantitative interpretation of
geophysical signatures due to microbial processes in contaminant plumes.

APPENDIX F
Number of acquired DC data
During the acquisition that was performed in May 2016, 1022 DC data were acquired, which is the
exact number of DC data that can be acquired with ABEM Terrameter, using the multiple-gradient
array and a 4x21 cable setup. However, in October 2016, 825 data were acquired for Proﬁle 2 (200
m), and 903 data were acquired for Proﬁle 3 (400 m).
In order to investigate the reasons for missing data (19% for Proﬁle 2 and 12% for Proﬁle 3), the
acquisitions from Autumn 2016 semester were compared with those in Spring 2016 semester using
Matlab. For Proﬁle 1 (Figure F.1a) 146 different current dipoles A and B (corresponding to 146
measurement IDs) were used, and potential was measured in 7 different channels (M and N) for
each measurement ID. For Proﬁle 2 electrodes could not placed at x=87.5 m and x=90 m because of
the cycle path (Figure 5.2b). Moreover, an electrode could not be placed at x=187.5 m because the
soil was very hard. By excluding these three electrodes, some quadrupoles were not used and this
is why 19% less data were acquired for Proﬁle 2 than for Proﬁle 1. For Proﬁle 3 electrodes placed
at x=265 m and x=350 m were excluded during data, acquisition because they failed the electrode
contact test, even after checking the cable jumpers and adding more saltwater. A possible reason
could be that the soil around the electrodes in the crops ﬁeld was loose (Figure 5.2d).
Moreover, during the acquisition 8 messages for ”load resistance too high or no contact”, because
current above 184 mA could not be injected. In order to improve the contact of electrodes with
the ground more salt water was added and the same quadrupole was tested. If the same message
appeared, the speciﬁc measurement ID was skipped. However, in this case the minimum injected
current could be preferably decreased to 150 mA in order to acquire more resistivity data.

APPENDIX H
Cable tests
Cable tests were conducted at Dyrehaven on 21st of September 2016. In order to test the function of
the cables, four resistivity acquisitions were performed with one cable each time and same electrode
positions. Schlumberger protocol 2x21 (2 cables with 21 take outs) was used with 5 m electrode
spacing, and one cable was excluded. 119 DC data were acquired with each cable and full waveform
was recorded.
Afterwards, the data were imported in Terrameter Toolbox, where pseudosections of apparent re-
sistivity were ploted and looked the same for the four cables. Additionally, the full waveform was
extracted in .txt ﬁles and Input Voltage (V) was ploted vs Time (ms) for each quadrupole in Matlab.
Figure H.1 shows four examples of plots out of 103. In most of the plots the measured voltages
during on-time for all cables are parallel with each other, and therefore it seems that the cables
