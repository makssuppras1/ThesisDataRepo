Abstract
In marine engines, cold corrosion of cylinder liners depend mainly on the low engine load and
the sulphur content of the fuel. This study is made in collaboration with DTU-MEK, DTU-KT,
and MAN Diesel and Turbo, who is researching SULphuric acid CORrosion (SULCOR) for 2
stroke marine diesel engines. The main focus is on the controlled corrosion test of a modiп¬Ѓed
BUKH engine by utilizing the test rig set up by DTU-MEK. The engine is electrically powered,
and a mixture of sulphuric acid and water vapor is added to the intake gas.
This study aims to create a modiп¬Ѓed version of the ATILA model, which was created by the
supervisor of this study in 1992. The modiп¬Ѓed ATILA model, consists of two main models: A
model for condensation and mass diп¬Ђusion to the lubrication interface for sulphuric acid and
water. The second model describes, the migration of condensates free п¬‚uid droplets t, the lu-
brication interface as a diluted or emulsion. When the п¬‚uid have migrated to the lubrication
interface, it will diп¬Ђuse through the lubrication oil. If the п¬‚uid do not react with the lubrication
oil additives, it will diп¬Ђuse through the lubrication oil until it reaches the cylinder liner.
The base simulation for the BUKH engine show diluted sulphuric acid, which migrated through
the lubrication oil interface, where it was neutralized. This high neutralization rate is because
of the high reaction rate between diluted sulphuric acid and calcium carbonate additives in the
lubrication oil. After one cycle with a 70 TBN oil, 60% of the calcium carbonate will be depleted.
Water in this model is assumed to be inert. This means it will not react with sulphuric acid
or calcium carbonate in the lubrication oil, 25% of the water entering the lubrication interface
will therefore reach the cylinder liner, before the exhaust valve opens (EVO). The validity of
inert water diп¬Ђusion is discussed, because calcium carbonate as a salt means it can absorb water.
Furthermore, mixtures of water and diluted sulphuric acid are known to produce hydronium ions
acidizing the solution. Lastly, with oxygen at the iron cylinder liner, normal water corrrosion
can occur.
A parametric analysis of the inп¬‚uence of the lubrication thickness on the acid corrosion at
the liner, shows that the thicker the lubrication oil is, the more calcium carbonate additives will
be able to neutralize the diluted sulphuric acid. In a deeper analysis, diluted sulphuric acid was
seen near the cylinder liner at lubrication thicknesses below 0.1 micron for one cycle, and 0.3
microns for п¬Ѓve cycles. The lubrication thicknesses show, due to low calcium carbonate diп¬Ђusion
coeп¬ѓcients, an ineп¬Ђective neutratlization taking place, which gives an indication that oscillation
phenomena can take place in the lubrication oil.
Increasing the concentration of sulphuric acid in the gas mixture before injection show that
the mass fraction of the condensate sulphuric acid will converge toward 9.15 %. The mass frac-
tion of water increases linearly with increasing sulphuric acid concentration. The base model
condensed mass fraction is found to be 0.4%.

Chapter 1
Background for study
In the last couple of decades, there have been a growing focus on the environmental impact for
marine transportation of goods and passengers, which is depended on economicals and timely
operations. These operations are focused on limiting fuel consumption. One way of reducing
fuel consumption as well as CO2 emission is to reduce maritime vessel speeds. It is obvious
that reducing the vessel speed, reduces the power requirement substantially, according to MAN
Diesel and Turbo [1][Fig. 1]. The Figure shows, by reducing the vessels max speed by 4 knots
the power requirement reduces by 50%. This means the engine must be able to operate at low
loads for long periods, for better fuel economy. Typically, what happens is that a round trip
normally lasting 8 weeks is changed to a duration of 9 weeks, leaving more time and a reduced
vessel speed demand. Most shipowners still want to retain the ability to operate vessels at higher
speeds, at least occasionally. This means, the SMCR power of the main engines may still be
maintained, while the changed operation pattern for the vessel may result in operation, with a
relatively lower load on the main engine, with only few days of operation on high engine loads.
This change in operation results in the following vessel operation:
В€ Reduced SFOC.
В€ Compliant NOx.
Which results in changes for the cylinder environment:
В€ Higher partial pressures i.e. higher saturation temperatures, for water and sulphuric acid.
В€ Higher air excess ratio at part load i.e. more SO3 production.
В€ Colder liners in part load i.e. more condensation.
The International Maritime Organization (IMO) has voted for legislation that will lead to a
maximum of 0.5 ww % fuel sulphur in 2020, and 0.1 ww % from 2015 in some environmentally
controlled areas (ECA). Until the new legislations applies vessels will use sulphur content fuels
up to 3.5 ww% (outside ECA), which is the current limit [2]. The higher sulphur content is used,
because fuel prices for high sulfur content fuels is lower than prices for low sulfur content[7], and
is supported by the average sulfur content in used bunker oil[3], see Figure 1.1.

The high sulphur fuel content can form sulphuric acid during combustion, where some of the acid
is condensing on the lubrication п¬Ѓlm. Sulphuric acid (H2SO4) is formed in the cylinder gas, by
reaction between sulphur trioxide (SO3) and water (H2O). Sulphur dioxide (SO2) and sulphur
trioxide are formed during combustion, by oxidation of the fuel sulphur content. When the gas
temperature is higher than the saturation temperature of the given compounds, sulphuric acid
is formed according to following reaction scheme.
SO3(g) + H2O(g) в†’H2SO4(g)
(1.0.1)
When the gas temperature gets lower, than the gaseous sulphuric acid saturation temperature,
the acid converts to liquid sulphuric acid.
H2SO4(g) в†’H2SO4(l)
(1.0.2)
These changes gives complications for the cylinder liner. Mainly because cold corrosion is de-
pended on 5 things, which changes the severity of the corrosion:
В€ Temperature of the liner
В€ Partial pressure on the liner
В€ Sulphur in the fuel
В€ Water content in air
В€ Air surplus during combustion
To summarize: Sulphur dioxide is formed together, with sulphur trioxide which forms a reaction
with water, and creates sulphuric acid under the combustion process. The low load cause liner
temperatures below the condensation temperature for both water and sulphuric acid, which
condensates on the lubrication oil п¬Ѓlm, and diп¬Ђuse through the oil.

Chapter 2
ATILA model
This chapter explains a modiп¬Ѓed version of the ATILA model, the ATILA model was created by
this study supervisor. ATILA is a program, which explains the Acid Transportation In Liner
Area it should however not be used to quantify the acid transportation, but rather qualify,
this transportation, and give an idea of the sulphuric acid transportation trends. The same
applies to the modiп¬Ѓed ATILA version, which includes a more detailed condensation model.
This model, takes mass transportation of sulphuric acid and water to the lubrication oil into
account.
The modiп¬Ѓed ATILA model, uses parameters, which are stated in section 1.1 to
clarify inп¬‚uences diп¬Ђerent parameters have on cold corrosion. Due to, the polarity of the solutes
(condensed molecules) and solvent (lubrication oil) a model, which assumes the mass diп¬Ђerence
between condensed mass of free sulphur acid and water droplets on lubrication interface, and the
mass of said solutes in the lubrication oil interface will dictate the migrating of the condensing
sulphuric acid and water into the lubrication oil. An absorption limit for sulphuric acid and

Discussion
One should be careful using the modiп¬Ѓed ATILA models results. The results gives a view of
trends for a system, where condensation, mass transportation, and diп¬Ђusion is the main mech-
anisms. The results should not be used quantitatively. The model has the ability to predict
the inп¬‚uences of changes for parameters or assumptions. The modiп¬Ѓed model calculates the
condensated acid from a 0d condensation model. This transportation mechanism is one of the
reason for lower sulphuric acid concentration at the liner surface, compared to Schramm et al.
[22].
In reality the gas is highly heterogeneous, when evaluating the irregular turbulence intensi-

The combined condensation and diп¬Ђusion model for the BUKH engine test rig, which contains
sulphuric acid and water is studied at low power output, to match low load operation for marine
vessel operations. A phenomenological 0D engine model is used for this purpose to benchmark
physical parameters, which is used in combination, with H2O в€’H2SO4 vapour-liquid equi-
librium to determine the cylinder gas saturation temperature.
Condensation is modelled as
diп¬Ђusive mass transfer from bulk gas to lubrication interface, and is couplet with the convective
heat transfer coeп¬ѓcient for a constant liner temperature, which is held constant under controlled
test environment.
The gas condensates as free sulphuric acid and water droplets on the lubrication oil, if the
molecule diп¬Ђerence between the droplets and molecules in oil grant migration over the lubri-
cation interface, the migrating п¬‚uids will diп¬Ђuse through the lubrication oil, which is modelled
with FickвЂ™s second law.
The amount of condensed sulphuric acid compared to the added acid to the gas mix is found
to converge toward 9.15% when added sulphuric acid concentration in the gas mix increases.
The condensed water when compared to water in added gas mix is 0.4%, and increases linearly
with the added sulphuric acid concentration in the gas mix. Therefore, will the condensation
eп¬ѓciency for water be a fraction compared to sulphuric acid condensation. The study show at
homogeneous motoring simulation most sulphuric acid and water will condensate at upper half
of the cylinder liner.
The lubrication saturation limit was calculated for water to be 11.1 mol/m3, and for diluted
sulphuric acid, 3.75 mol/m3, which is a fraction of the calcium carbonate additive concentration
at initial condition. These concentration limits for water and diluted sulphuric acid, where not
reached by the modiп¬Ѓed ATILA model simulations. The model show most water and diluted
sulphuric acid will enter the lubrication interface at the upper area of the cylinder. This, liner
area will not see diluted sulphuric acid reaching the cylinder liner when using a 70 TBN lubri-
cation oil. Due to the couplet diп¬Ђusion equation for diluted sulphuric acid is governed by the
reaction rate, most of the diluted sulphuric acid will be neutralized at the lubrication interface.
Around 25% of the water, which enters the lubrication interface area, with most water presence,
which where mentioned earlier. Indicates water have an inп¬‚uence on the corrosion mechanism.
The water diп¬Ђusion gradient in the lubrication oil is heavily depended on the condensation rates
for water.
A simulation with the modiп¬Ѓed ATILA model for the lubrication area with most diluted sul-
phuric acid and water presence, shows calcium carbonate additives cannot supply the concen-
tration needed to neutralize the diluted sulphuric acid at the lubrication interface with diп¬Ђusion
alone, which indicates a oscillation phenomenon may occur in the lubrication oil, or low diп¬Ђusion
coeп¬ѓcient used in mathematical model was estimated.
A parametric analysis for the Risk Factor when the lubrication п¬Ѓlm thickness changes is con-
ducted for 5 cycles, the Risk Factor will increase when lubrication thickness gets below 0.1
microns, and 0.3 microns after 5 cycles, without new calcium carbonate additive injection.
Therefore, with suп¬ѓciently large lubrication thickness the acid additives will remove the diluted
sulphuric acid by chemical reaction.
The second parametric analysis changes the sulphuric acid concentration in the gas mix, when

Since it have been stated, that A = LU, the tridiagonal system, is equal to (LU)v = L(Uv) = d.
By substituting w = Uv, the system becomes Lw = d. Since L, is a lower triangle matrix, w
can be obtained by solving Lw = d. When w is known, v can be calculated, by solving Uv = w.
Thereby when L and U have been found, the v vector can be computed in a two step process.
Lw = d
Uv = w
Since L is a lower triangular, the п¬Ѓrst of the two above equations, is a forward substitution,
because U, is a upper triangular, the second equation, is a backward substitution. The the
Crank-Nicholson scheme to diп¬Ђusion equation, yields a coeп¬ѓcient matrix, which does not change
per time step. This means the LU decompositions only have to be obtained once, at the beginning
of the simulation. The triangular solver, is performed once for each time step.
% --- Forward substitution to solve L*w=d
w(1)=d(1)/e(1);
for i=2:n
w(i)=(d(i)-a(i)*w(i-1))/e(i);
end
% --- Backward substitution to solve U*v=w
v(n)=w(n)
for i=n-i:-1:1
v(i)=w(i)-f(i)*w(i+1);
end
The above MATLAB code show, two preceding loops, computes v only after w is created, and
v(i) is obtained only after v(i + 1) is calculated. This makes sure, the w vector can be entirely
eliminated and use v in its place. This exclude, the need of creating a temporary w vector.

function [D,rho] = fnc diffcon(VISC,TW,Vn,LUBEMOLW,Phi sv,Ru,dHv)
%fnc diffcon calculates the diffusion coefficient a modified
%Wilke-Chang method.
%
%
INPUT:
%
DCT is a integer telling which BC method to use
%
VISC is the absolute viscocity in cP
%
TW is the average liner/wall temperature in degC
%
Vn is the specific volume for element in cm3/mole
%
LUBEMOLW is the lubeoil molar weight in g/mole
%
Phi is the association parameter for the solvent
%
dHv is the evaporation enthalphy change
%
Ru is the universal gas constant
%
Phi sv is the association parameter for the solute
%
OUTPUT:
%
D is the diffusion coefficient in m2/s
%
% Author: Mikkel Brendstrup
if (dHv - Ru*(TW+273)) > 0
rho = sqrt((dHv - Ru*(TW+273))/Vn);%Solubility parameter (Hildebrand)
else
rho = 0;
end
if rho <= 23.53 && rho >= 9.16
Phi a = -5.11*10Л†(-3)*rhoЛ†2+2.8*10Л†(-1)*rho-1.16; %Estimation of
%molecular diffusivity
%in liquid phase
%system by Wilke-Chang eq.
elseif rho < 9.16
Phi a = 1;
else
Phi a = 2.6;
end
D = 7.4E-8*(Phi sv*LUBEMOLW)Л†0.5*(TW+273)/(VISC*(Phi a*Vn)Л†(0.6))*1E-4;%[m2/s]
end

function [A,B] = fnc coefficent(bound,D,dt,dx,nx,coeff)
%fnc coefficent creates a tridiagonal matrices to the system
%
%
INPUT:
%
bound is a string either 'dichneu' or 'hneuneu'
%
dt is the timestep
%
dx is the spacious step
%
nx is the number of spacious nodes
%
coeff is the reaction depended coefficient(if no reaction then it's 0)
%
OUTPUT:
%
[A,B] coefficient to the tridiagonal system
%
% Author: Mikkel Brendstrup
sigma = D*dt/(dxЛ†2);
a = -sigma*ones(nx,1);
bA = (2+2.*coeff.*dt+2.*sigma).*ones(nx,1);
bB = (2-2.*sigma).*ones(nx,1);
c = a;
if strcmp(bound,'dichneu') %dirichlet and homogen neumann BC
% --- Coefficients of boundary nodes
bA(1) = 1;
a(1:2) = 0;
c(end-1:end) = -2*sigma;
bB(1) = 1;
% --- MATRIX of coefficients
A = full(spdiags([a,bA,c],[1,0,-1],nx,nx)); %Creating Coefficient matrix
B = full(spdiags([-a,bB,-c],[1,0,-1],nx,nx));
elseif strcmp(bound,'hneuneu')
%homogen neumann and homogen neumann BC
% --- Coefficients of boundary nodes
a(1:2) = -2*sigma;
c(end-1:end) = -2*sigma;
% --- MATRIX of coefficients
A = full(spdiags([a,bA,c],[1,0,-1],nx,nx)); %Creating Coefficient matrix
B = full(spdiags([-a,bB,-c],[1,0,-1],nx,nx));
end
end
