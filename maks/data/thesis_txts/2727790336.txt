Abstract
This project is a study on the potential of surface layers for reducing transport processes in cementious
binders. The investigation begins with an analysis of field samples consisting of Portland cement and
calcined clay limestone cement exposed in Øresund, Denmark for up to 18 months. This allows for
an initial estimation of the conditions before the marine life forms an eventual protective layer. A
protective side dominated by barnacles and a non-protective side dominated by sand and seaweed
was analyzed.
Model samples were then developed composed of mortar of White® cement and an
incorporated protective surface consisting of glass strips. The model samples aims to isolate the effects
of marine organisms on concrete structures. The transport processes in focus in this study are chloride
ingress and carbonation.
In the analysis of the initial conditions based on the field samples, it was observed that the chloride
penetration depth was lower for the protective side compared to the non-protective side. This lead
to differences in chloride profiles where the service life was predicted using the error function solution
to Fick’s second law.
The service life was shown to be increased by 37 % for the protective side
compared to the non-protective side. Additionally, the analysis of the field samples also revealed that
the calcite content from the protective side was lower compared to the non-protective side. This further
indicates that marine colonization does not only have an influence on the chloride ingress but also on
the carbonation rate.
From the investigation of the model samples, it was revealed that the permeability was lower for
the protective side leading to a smaller chloride penetration depth. The chloride penetration area was
then investigated, indicating a slower transport process for a greater chloride penetration area. An
investigation of the chloride profile revealed differences in exposure conditions, where redistribution
of chlorides was shown for the sample exposed to a NaCl + NaHCO3 - solution for six weeks due to
carbonation of the sample. The carbonation front was visually assessed to be larger for the sample
exposed to the combination solution as well. However, the XRD and TGA analysis showed a higher
content of calcite in the sample exposed to a pure NaHCO3 - solution. Furthermore, the TGA revealed
a higher water content in samples exposed to a pure NaCl - solution, indicating either an effect of
carbonation on the C-S-H content or a higher degree of e.g. hydration thus leading to a higher capacity
of physical binding of chlorides in C-S-H.
All the analyses ultimately resulted in a clear indication of marine colonization contributing to
slowing transport processes in marine exposed concrete structures, and hereby increased durability.

Resumé
Dette projekt undersøger potentialet af overfladelag der reducere transportprocesser i cementbindemi-
dler. Analysen tager udgangspunkt i feltprøver der har været eksponeret i Øresund, Danmark i op til
18 måneder. Prøverne består af beton med enten ren Portland cement eller calcined clay limestone ce-
ment, hvor 40 % af Portlandcementen i prøver er erstattet med kalcineret ler og kalkfiller. Feltprøverne
gør det muligt at foretage en indledende vurdering af forholdende før marinelivet sætter sit præg på
betonenkonstruktionerne. Der tages udgangspunkt i to sider af prøverne. Den ene side betragtes som
værende beskyttende og er domineret af begroning med roer hvorimod den anden side betragtes som
værende ikke beskyttende og er primært dækket med tang og sand. For at kunne isolere effekten af
marine organismers beskyttende effekt er modelprøver, bestående af mørtel med White® cemet blevet
udviklet og støbt. For at imiterere den beskyttende marine begroning blev der indstøbt glasstrimler i
mørtelprøverne. Det primære fokus for projektet er kloridindtrængning og karbonatisering.
I de indledende undersøgelser af feltprøverne blev det observeret at kloridindtrængning var mindre
fra den beskyttende side sammenlignet med den ikke-beskyttende side. Dette førte også til forskelle i
kloridprofiler, hvor levetiden blev estimeret ud fra fejlfunktionsløsningen til Fick’s anden lov. Levetiden
viste sig at være op til 37 % højere for den beskyttende side sammenlignet med den ikke-beskyttende
side. Desuden viste analysen af feltprøver også, at indholdet af calcit på den beskyttende side var lavere
sammenlignet med den ikke-beskyttende side. Dette er en yderligere indikation for at det marine liv
der vokser på betonkonstruktioner ikke kun påvirker kloridindtrængning, men også karbonatiseringen.
Undersøgelserne af modelprøverne viste at permeabiliteten var lavere på den beskyttende side,
hvilket kan medføre en mindre kloridindtrængning.
Området, hvori kloridindtrængningen var ob-
serveret, blev også undersøgt, og det viste en langsommere transportproces af klorid i takt med at
området blev større med flere reaktionspartnere. Kloridprofilerne viste også en forskel ved forskellige
eksponeringsforhold. Her kunne det ses at kloridionerne var blevet omfordelt i prøven efter eksponering
af en NaCl + NaHCO3 - opløsning i seks uger grundet karbonatisering af prøven. Karbonatiseringsdyb-
den var visuelt større for prøver udsat for NaCl + NaHCO3 - opløsning, hvor XRD- og TGA-analysen
viste et højere indhold af calcit i prøven udsat for en ren NaHCO3 - opløsning. TGA resultaterne viste
derudover et højere vandindhold i prøver udsat for en NaCl - opløsning iforhold til prøver eksponeret for
en opløsning med NaHCO3. Dette kan indikere at karbonatisering har indflydelse på C-S-H-indholdet
i prøven og evt. indikere en større hydratiseringsgrad ved overfladen. Dette kan derved resultere i en
højere kapacitet for fysisk binding af klorider i C-S-H for prøver der ikke er påvirket af karbonatisering.
Samlet set resulterede analyserne i en klar indikation af, at tilstædeværelsen af marine organismer
bidrager til mindre transport af klorid i marine betonkonstruktioner der dermed øger også holdbarheden.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Concrete is a widely used construction material in the marine environment due to its several advantages,
such as low price, high durability, and the fact that it can be cast in various shapes.
The human
settlement near coastal zones introduces concrete construction elements, including bridges, tunnels,
harbor piers, as well as marine energy installations like windmills and oil platforms, in contact with
the marine environment [1]. However, the marine exposure conditions present challenges to the long-
term performance of concrete structures, as they are subject to damage mechanisms leading to reduced
durability. These mechanisms include low frost resistance, alkali-silica reactions (ASR), sulfate attack,
or deterioration of reinforcement. Notably, corrosion of the reinforcement due to for instance chloride
ingress or carbonation can result in cracking of the concrete [2].
Moreover, marine structures have the potential to provide habitats for marine organisms, although
the presence of the structures also alters the natural habitats and affects local natural physical char-
acteristics such as hydrodynamics, sedimentation patterns, and the light regime which are crucial for
the biological development [3]. Research has shown that barnacles and oyster crust have been found to
act as natural protective surface coatings on marine structures [4]. Nonetheless, this protective layer
does not develop immediately upon concrete exposure, and the extent to which marine biology impacts
transport processes in marine concrete structures is not fully understood. Therefore, there is a need
and interest to investigate whether marine organisms can positively influence the durability of concrete
structures through their interactions, ultimately contributing to extending the service life of marine
concrete structures, and preservation of natural marine environments.
The production of cement accounts for around 8 % of the global CO2 emission [5]. By extending
the service life of concrete in marine environments, the need for frequent repair, maintenance, and
replacement with new concrete can be reduced. Another possibility to lower the CO2 emission is to
replace some of the cement using supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs), such as silica fume, fly
ash, or calcined clay and limestone filler. Although the substitution of cement can alter certain concrete
properties, this aspect is however beyond the scope of this project.
This master’s thesis investigates the transport processes in concrete exposed in the marine envi-
ronment and aims to understand the influence of marine organisms on concrete in such environments.
The project begins by analyzing field samples composed of Portland cement, particularly Rapid® ce-
ment, and calcined clay limestone cement. The field samples have been exposed in Øresund for up to
18 months and have a water-to-binder ratio of w/b = 0.45. These field samples allow for an initial
estimation of conditions before marine life forms the eventual protective layer. Additionally, model
samples are developed, consisting of only mortar with White® cement and an incorporated protective
surface, which aids to describe transport mechanisms that have been diﬀicult to describe previously. By
analyzing these model samples, the study aims to isolate the effects of marine organisms on the concrete
structure. The transport mechanisms in focus in this thesis are chloride ingress and carbonation.

2.1.1
Portland cement
Portland cement is a hydraulic binder, which forms a binder and hardens in the presence of water. To
produce Portland cement, limestone and clay minerals are heated to a temperature of 1400-1500°C,
resulting in the formation of clinkers. The clinker is finely ground and mixed with gypsum to create
the Portland cement [6]. During the heating, calcination of limestone occurs, resulting in the release of
CO2 from the limestone, in the following process [7].

2.1.2
Chemical composition and hydration of cement
Chemical reactions of the clinker minerals result in the hydration of the cement. The different minerals
in Portland cement, react either simultaneously or individually when mixed with water. The hydration
process can be described by the reactions of each of the four main clinker minerals [7]. During hydration,
calcium hydroxide, monosulfate, ettringite, and calcium silicate hydrates (C-S-H gel) are formed. The
C-S-H gel binds the cement paste and is the most significant component contributing to the strength of
hardened cement paste. Calcium hydroxide (CH), also known as portlandite, causes a highly alkaline
environment in the cement paste (pH = 12-14), which protects the reinforcement against corrosion. To
simplify the chemical reactions, a cement chemical notation is used, as shown in Table 2.2.

2.1.3
Pozzolans
Pozzolans are a group of supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs) containing a varying content
of SiO2 and Al2O3, which do not react with water at ordinary temperatures. Pozzolans have no or
little cementing properties in themselves. However, the SiO2 and Al2O3 react with calcium hydroxide
(CH), formed during the hydration of cement clinker, and forms C-S-H as shown in Equation (2.9).
The pozzolanic reaction happens simultaneously with the hydration of cement clinker, and therefore
the products from the pozzolanic process can vary in the calcium/silicate ratio of the C-S-H, and some
of the products can also contain aluminum, often referred to as C-A-S-H [6]. The pozzolanic reaction
can generally be described as

Compared to the hydration of clinker minerals, the pozzolanic reaction is slow. Concrete with SCMs
will therefore have a lower early-age strength compared to concrete without SCMs.
Calcined clay is a material used as pozzolan in concrete as it has pozzolanic properties. The addition
of calcined clay to Portland cement alters the hydration products, where portlandite is consumed and
additional calcium aluminosilicate hydrate, C-A-S-H, is formed [11]. The addition of carbonates found
in limestone leads to a formation of monocarboaluminate and hemicarboaluminate as the AFm-phases
instead of monosulfate. This results in a higher ettringite formation. The result of a higher amount of
cementing hydration products leads to pore refinement and increases its strength, this is also known
as the synergetic effect [11]. Using calcined clay combined with limestone, interactions between the
aluminates in calcined clay and the carbonates from limestone have made it possible to substitute 30-50
% of the clinker mineral, without a reduction in the compressive strength [12].

2 Theoretical background
2.1.4
Concrete in the marine environment
Concrete is the preferred material for the construction of marine infrastructure [1]. However, concrete
in the marine environment has a high risk of deterioration as a result of the chemical action of seawater.
Deterioration mechanisms include alkali-aggregate expansion, frost action in cold climates, physical
erosion, and corrosion of reinforcing steel. These forms of attack can increase the permeability leading
to further deterioration by either the same mechanism or another type of mechanism [7].
Concrete structures submerged in marine environments can create artificial reefs. By providing a
solid substrate, concrete attracts and supports diverse marine life. This will be further explained in
Section 2.5 [1].
In marine environments, the two cement types CEM I and CEM II/A-V are allowed for the exposure
classes XS2 (Permanently under water) and XS3 (Intertidal, splash and spray zones) in accordance with
the Danish standard DS 206 NA 2023 [13]. This requirement limits the reduction of embodied CO2 of
concrete structures in the marine environments, as only CEM II/A-V allows cement replacement of up
to 20 mass % [14].
2.2
Corrosion of reinforcement
Corrosion of reinforcement is mainly caused by either chloride ingress, which will be described in
Section 2.3 or carbonation, which will be described in Section 2.4. Corrosion is dependent on the amount
of oxygen and water present in the concrete. As previously described, the pore solution in concrete
is highly alkaline (pH = 13-14) due to the portlandite formed during hydration. The reinforcement
is thereby protected by a passive layer composed of hydrated ion oxides in this environment, which
can be destroyed by carbonation or the presence of chloride ions [15]. Corrosion initiates when the
passive layer is degraded or not fully formed.
The corrosion is divided into two main processes, a
reduction of oxygen (cathodic process: 2H2O + 4e−+ O2 →4OH−) and oxidation of iron (anodic
process: Fe →Fe2+ + 2e−) that occurs at the same rate. The products from the two processes then
create, FeO · (H2O)x, also known as rust [7].
Corrosion of the reinforcement is a problem as the cross-section of the reinforcement bars is reduced
and thereby compromises the structural integrity. The products of corrosion are expansive, which can
cause cracks in the concrete or scaling of the cover layer, which can accelerate degradation [15].
2.3
Chloride transportation mechanisms
2.3.1
Chloride ingress and binding
The transport rate of chlorides in concrete is influenced by the concrete’s ability to bind chlorides and
the pore structure. The transport of chloride ions into concrete can be divided into four different mech-
anisms: (i) diffusion, (ii) migration, (iii) permeation, and (iv) convection. (i) Diffusion is a mechanism
that occurs due to different concentrations of chlorides and the chloride ions are therefore transported
from zones with a high concentration to a zone with a low concentration. (ii) Migration occurs due to
a difference in the electrical potential, where chlorides are transported into a zone with less electrical
potential. (iii) Permeation occurs due to a difference in hydraulic pressure and can potentially occur
in concrete with cracks and defects. Lastly, (iv) Convection occurs due to a difference in the moisture
content [16]. Diffusion is the focus of this project.
==PAGE:19==

of chlorides into the concrete, as only free chloride ions can destroy the passive layer on reinforcement
steel and initiate corrosion. A higher binding capacity of chlorides can therefore have an influence on the
durability against corrosion [17]. The chlorides can be either physically bound in C-S-H or chemically

When carbon dioxide permeates into concrete, CO2 reacts with the free lime, Ca(OH)2 and forms
calciumcarbonate, CaCO3. The process is also known as carbonation and generally follows the reaction
in Equation (2.15). Carbonation models are usually based on Fick’s First law [22].

As a result of the carbonation the pH in the pore fluid is reduced from 12-14 to 7-9 and therefore
the reinforcing steel is more vulnerable to corrosion. However, carbonation requires the dissolution of
atmospheric carbon dioxide, CO2, to form carbonic acid, H2CO3. Here, CO2−
3 -ions react with Ca2+-

concrete is saturated, due to a low solubility of CO2. The carbonation can also be stopped by suﬀicient
dry concrete that can prevent the ingress of CO2.
Studies have indicated that a relative humidity

by factors such as the w/c ratio, the cement type, pore size, and the temperature.
Carbonation

capacity.
2.5
Biological colonization
To determine whether a concrete structure can offer an ecologically enriched environment for marine
colonization, it is necessary to understand the process of marine settlement on hard substrate. This
process is typically characterized by a succession of developmental stages leading to the establishment
of a new ecological system. By considering the progression of succession, insights into how marine
organisms colonize and thrive on concrete substrates are gained and the interactions between submerged
concrete structures and marine life can be investigated.
The organisms have different requirements in order to settle. The mechanisms of marine colonization
can be divided into different processes over successional phases shown in Figure 2.1 [24].
==PAGE:21==
2.5 Biological colonization
9
Figure 2.1: Successional stages of marine colonization on hard substrate, figure from [25].
Succession can be divided into two categories: primary or secondary.
Primary succession refers to
structures that have not yet been colonized, whereas secondary succession refers to structures where
the colonization has been disturbed or partially removed. When a clean concrete surface is submerged in
the sea, the surface is quickly colonized by primary succession. Here, microorganisms (early successional
species) such as bacteria, algae, and cyanobacteria rapidly colonize the surface forming conditioning
film and biofilm. This biofilm then provides a foundation for further colonization by stabilizing the
substrate and creating a microhabitat [24].
Then secondary colonization initiates, where larger species such as corals, oysters, and barnacles
colonize the structure. These are also known as habitat-forming species, and they contribute to trans-
forming the structure into a habitat for living organisms. At some point, a steady state is reached,
where the maximum potential is reached [24].
It is essential to acknowledge that marine colonization is an ongoing process. Environmental changes,
such as human activities, can disturb the successional phases, leading to new opportunities for different
species to establish and develop marine communities. Figure 2.2 shows how marine life is dependent
on the time of year and time to settle. This represents the field setup of concrete samples analyzed in
this project.
Figure 2.2: Pictures of marine growth on concrete field samples in Øresund (2021 and 2022) [26].
Barnacles and oysters are categorized as sessile organisms, which means they permanently attach them-
selves to a solid substrate. They play a significant role in surface coating. Barnacles, for instance,

attach to a surface using bio-organic proteins composed of calcium carbonate, creating a natural sur-

2.6
Problem statement
The durability of concrete structures is a major concern in marine environments, among other things,
due to chloride ingress and carbonation. Some studies have shown that biological life such as barnacles
and bacterial films extend concrete life [4, 27]. However, the influence of marine life on chloride ingress
and carbonation is still uncertain. This thesis aims to address the following questions:
• How does marine growth impact transport mechanisms influencing durability aspects such as
chloride ingress and carbonation in concrete structures?
• How does marine growth and exposure conditions affect the mineralogical composition of concrete
and mortar?
• Is it possible to make a model setup that can explain the transport mechanisms excluding the
initial chloride ingress?
This project begins with an analysis of field samples based on ordinary Portland cement and calcined clay
limestone cement exposed in Øresund for up to 18 months. This investigation allows for an estimation
of the initial conditions occurring before the protective layer has formed and furthermore includes the
effect of marine growth. Therefore, model samples are developed, in order to investigate the influence
of the protective layer, without initial conditions of exposure. Thereby, it is possible to investigate how
surface protection in the form of marine growth influences the transport process.

A visual inspection of the chloride ingress and carbonation front was performed as the first step in the
analysis. Here a 0.1M AgNO3-solution and a Thymolphthalein-solution are used to indicate chloride
ingress and carbonation front (by pH), respectively. The test set-up is shown in Figure 3.7.
Figure 3.7: Test set-up for spray test with AgNO3 and Thymolphthalein.
The AgNO3 spray test and measurement of the chloride depth was carried out according to Baroghel-
Bouny et al (2007) [32] and NT Build 492 [33], which deals with measurements of the chloride depth
after migration tests. First, the 0.1M AgNO3 solution was sprayed over the freshly split surface, which
after drying, will show the chloride front. The chloride front was afterward measured at intervals of
10-20 mm, in order to obtain valid depth readings (an average). The spray test indicates the depth of
the free chlorides and was the basis of the further analysis of the chloride profile.
The Thymolphthalein spray test was performed similarly to the AgNO3 spray test. Thymolphthalein
is used as a pH indicator, where its transition range is pH 9.3–10.5. At a pH below this, the indicator
is colorless, whereas it is blue/purple above this threshold. For the field samples, it was diﬀicult to
determine a difference in the colors when tested, and the carbonation was therefore assumed to be close
to non-existing. For the model samples, a small colorless field appeared near the edges, which needed
to be further investigated in a Stereomicroscope, which is further explained in Section 3.5.

3.6
Grinding of samples
The samples were grinded on dry-grinding equipment, where eﬀicient ventilation was required. The
grinding was performed on one of the Ø100 mm cylinders, with a grinding interval of 1 mm to obtain
powder samples. The powder was collected in an aluminum tray, which was prepared for this purpose

(TGA) as discribed in Section 3.10.
Based on the depth of the chloride ingress determined by the visual assessment in Section 3.4, the
grinding depth was determined by adding approximately five mm to the vidual penetration depth. The
grinding intervals were determined accordingly where the intervals depended on the test samples, with
which penetration depths varied. Nine different intervals were chosen for the analysis where the sample
intervals varied between 1 and 12 mm. The intervals got narrower close to the surface. To increase
the accuracy of the largest intervals, the last one mm of the interval was collected in separate bags for
analysis. All samples were then stored in a desiccator until analysis. As the model samples only had
a thickness of 3 cm, it was diﬀicult to grind more than 10-12 mm for the testing, here nine of the one
mm intervals were analyzed.
3.7
Determination of chloride profile
The chloride analysis was performed according to NT Build 208 [34], but instead of using the Volhard
titration, the chloride content was determined by Potentiometric titration. The Potentiometric titration
was performed with the Metrohm 888 Titrando system using the Tiamo 2.5 software.
All samples (approximately 5 g) were initially dried for at least 2 hours at 105 °C, and afterward
cooled in a desiccator for 20 minutes. The sample was then dissolved in water and 65 % Nitic acid
(HNO3) and filtered as shown in Figure 3.8. To increase the accuracy of the potentiometric titration
at low chloride concentrations, 1 mL of 0.1M sodium chloride was added to the solution.
The first derivative method was used for the analysis.
Here small amounts of the titrant 0.1M
silver nitrate (AgNO3) were added to the dissolved sample while measuring the potential changes. The
chloride irons (Cl−) reacts with the silver irons (Ag+) and form AgCl. When all the chloride ions are
bound in AgCl the double derivative of the potential measured will change sign and the titration stops.
Hereby the volume of AgNO3 used, corresponds to the volume of chloride present in the solution.
The chloride content was then calculated using the theory described in Section 2.3. Here a non-linear
regression is performed on the measured data, to both determine the diffusion coeﬀicient, Dapp, and the
surface concentration, Cs. As a reference, a non-exposed model sample was analyzed after two weeks of
curing to determine the background concentrations. Unfortunately, no reference could be measured for
the field samples, as all remaining samples from the previous projects had been exposed in Øresund.

X-ray Diffraction, XRD
X-ray diffraction (XRD) is used to analyze crystalline fine-grained materials which produce a pattern
with varying intensities of peaks at characteristic diffraction angles [37]. The pattern can then be used
to identify the different crystal structures which are present in the sample.
The tested samples are prepared with the backloading method, using the powder obtained from
grinding, described in Section 3.6. The equipment used for preparing a sample for XRD analysis is
shown in Figure 3.10 (a). First, the powder samples were hand-ground in a small mortar grinder, to
avoid eventual large pieces, which can occour during grinding. The desired particle size is less than
five µm [37].
The sample was then pressed into the sample holder, where any excess powder was
removed with a knife blade and a brush. An empty (left) and a filled (right) sample holder is shown
in Figure 3.10 (b). The sample holder was loaded into the magazine in the XRD instrument, where
a sample gripper automatically moved the sample into the sample spinner before the XRD analysis
started. The equipment was thoroughly cleaned between measurements with new samples, to avoid
contamination. Due to the risk of carbonation, only four samples were prepared and analyzed at a
time.

(b) Empty (left) and filled (right) sample holder.

The used XRD instrument is the PANalytical X’Pert PRO, which has a radiation source Kα 2θ with a

3.10
Thermogravimetric Analysis, TGA
As a supplement to the XRD analysis, a thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed. The TGA
is used to determine the content of C-S-H, AFm, AFt, portlandite (Ca(OH)2), and calcite (CaCO3) in
the field and model samples. TGA uses the mass loss of the different phases during the thermal process
to determine the content of the different components, here the mass loss originates from the mass loss
of either H2O or CO2. A crucible made of aluminum oxide (Al2O3) is filled approximately halfway with
∼30 mg of the sample. In order to obtain comparable measurements, it is important to use each time a
similar sample weight as the difference in the weight of the analyzed sample can influence the content
of the gypsum in the sample. The present fraction of gypsum in the sample influences the shape and
peak position of the dehydration of gypsum in the temperature range of 50 to 200 °C [37]. Between
measurements, the crucible was cleaned with compressed air to avoid contamination of the samples.
The measurements were performed by raising the temperature around 10 °C per minute, from 35
°C to 900 °C, while registering the mass of the sample. The TGA analysis is performed in the machine
from NETZSCH, TG 209 F1 Libra. The machine preheats to 35 °C and waits five minutes before the
analysis begins, to ensure a stable temperature. The airflow during the measurements contains both
atmospheric air and nitrogen (N2). A TGA Curve are shown in Figure 3.11 (a).
The derivative thermogravimetric (DTG) data is deduced from the TGA measurements. This is done
by finding the first derivative, and afterwards smoothening the curve by taking the moving average of
the first derivative. Here each average value is calculated over a sliding window with a length of five
measurements, across neighbouring elements of the DTG. An example of the smoothening is shown in
Figure 3.11 (b).
(a) TGA curve.
(b) Smoothing of the curve using the moving average.
Figure 3.11: TGA and smoothing of DTG curve.
The DTG can be used to determine the content of the different components in a sample, by calculating
the area between the curve and the x-axis. The different phases are more visible on this curve than
on the TGA curve. An example of the content determined based on the area above the curve, in this
case, portlandite, is shown in Figure 3.12 in a defined temperature interval related to the component
analyzed. Here it is seen that the base of the DTG curve is below to the x-axis, indicating a loss
of mass. This mass loss could be part of the decomposing of other phases than the one investigated.
However, it is unknown in the case shown in Figure 3.12 how much is actually due to the decomposing

4 Investigation of field samples
4.6
Summary of analysis
The analysis of the field samples discovered that marine life such as barnacles, which were observed
on the side of the sample facing the bottom of the sea, has a protective effect on concrete structures
in the marine environment. The visual assessment of the chloride penetration depth showed a lower
penetration depth on the protective sides of the samples compared to the non-protective side, indepen-
dent of the cement type used. Furthermore, a difference in the chloride ingress was also observed when
comparing concrete samples with different cement types. Here it was seen that the chloride penetration
of concrete consisting of calcined clay limestone cement had a lower penetration depth compared to
concrete samples consisting of Portland cement. Additionally, it was seen that the visual assessment
agrees well with the measured chloride profiles, as the chloride ingress did not extend further into the
sample than shown by the indicators. This was even though the visual assessment is based on only free
chlorides in the pore solution.
In the analysis of the mineralogical composition of the samples, CalLi10 samples showed no or little
content of portlandite. This agrees well with the know theory, as calcined clay limestone cement is
known to form less portlandite during hydration, resulting in a higher carbonation rate. However, no
difference in the calcite content was revealed between the two cement types analyzed. Nevertheless, the
protective side of the samples indicated a lower calcite content, indicating that the protective layer of
barnacles also influenced the carbonation of the samples.
The service life of the samples was investigated, where it was shown to be increased by up to 37 %
due to the protective surface, even though the immediate results showed a higher diffusion coeﬀicient
for the protective surface compared to the non-protective surface. A higher diffusion coeﬀicient usually
tends to a lower service life. The analysis of the chloride profile also revealed a significantly lower
surface concentration of 0.5 % for the protective side compared to 0.7-0.9 % for the non-protective
side. Furthermore, it was also estimated, that the service life of concrete with calcined clay limestone
cement had an increased service life of 37 % compared to concrete with Portland cement (based on the
non-protective surface).
All the results take the initial chloride ingress, before marine colonization into consideration and
the results therefore represent an estimation of the initial conditions. In order to fully understand how
chloride transport and binding are influenced, model samples will be analyzed, to further deduce the
potential of biogenic coverage on chloride transport.

5 Investigation of model samples
It is seen from the table, that the content of calcite in the inner layers is the same as the calcite content
of the reference sample, corresponding to approximately 3.5 wt %. It is also seen that the calcite content
increases for longer exposure times, and carbonation might have reached the second layer, which was
analyzed, after exposure of six weeks. The full results can be found in Appendix C.9.
Additionally, it is seen that the water content near the surface of samples exposed to NaCl is signif-
icantly higher than the water content in the reference sample and samples exposed to other solutions.
For the pure NaCl-solution, the content is increased from 3.46 wt % to 6.23 wt %. The higher water
content can be related to a higher paste content, more free and bound water along with a possible higher
degree of hydration and degree of saturation due to external water. This is previously seen in other
studies as well [40]. The tendency is not found for the sample exposed to the pure NaHCO3-solution.
The sample exposed to the combination NaCl + NaHCO3-solution also shows a higher water content
of 4.77 wt %. This is not as significant as for the pure NaCl-solution. According to A Practical Guide
to Microstructural Analysis of Cementitious Materials, the water content in the TGA analysis can be
influenced by the sample mass and thereby the gypsum content. The measured reference sample had
a weight of 30.4 mg, where the measured outer layers of samples exposed to the NaCl, NaHCO3 and
NaCl + NaHCO3 - solution had a weight of 33.0 mg, 32.7 mg, and 32.4 mg respectively. Therefore, it
is assessed that the weight of the different samples was similar and cannot be the full reason for the
difference in the water content. Carbonation is known to affect the C-S-H content, and therefore a
lower water content can be observed in samples exposed to NaHCO3 compared to samples exposed to
NaCl [41]. It is known that chloride can be either chemically bound in Fiedel’s salt or physically bound
in C-S-H [18]. This can therefore also indicate that the increase in water content can result in a higher
binding capacity of chlorides.
The content of both water and calcite is lower for the combination solution compared to the pure
solutions with either NaCl or NaHCO3. This indicates that carbonation and chloride ingress influences
the other transport process, resulting in both less binding of chloride (release of chlorides) and lower
carbonation. However, the shown differences can also be a result of unknown factors.

5.6 Summary of analysis
51
5.6
Summary of analysis
The development of model samples has shown to provide new knowledge and understanding of the
transport processes in cementitious binders. The analysis of the model samples discovered that the
protective surface has a lower permeability along with a lower penetration depth of chlorides, based on
a visual assessment. By simplification, the permeability could be described as a second-order polynomial.
Furthermore, it was seen that the permeability was dependent on the exposure opening, leading to a
lower permeability for smaller exposure openings. However, when the data was converted to fit with
the actual exposure area, the exposure opening did not have much influence. In general, the exposure
opening was still shown to have an impact on the permeability, expecting an influence on the chloride
ingress.
During the test of permeability, free water under the incorporated protective glass surface was
observed. This, however, did not appear to affect the results in the visual assessment, where the glass
was proven to be acting as a non-permeable layer. The chloride penetration depth was observed to be
higher for the non-protective side compared to the protective side, independent of the exposure solution.
For the protective side, changes were diﬀicult to assess for samples exposed to the combination solution
NaCl + NaHCO3, indicating that carbonation has an influence on the chloride ingress resulting in
a redistribution of the chlorides.
The chloride penetration area was investigated, showing a slower
transport of chlorides for larger areas as the percentage increase between the visible areas decreases
with the increase of area. Lastly, the visual assessment revealed a higher carbonation depth for the
combination NaCl + NaHCO3 - solution.
The chloride profiles showed clear differences in the chloride ingress when comparing samples exposed
to different exposure solutions. However, the penetration depth from the chloride profile agreed well with
the observed 11 mm in the visual assessment. The surface concentration was shown to be significantly
lower for samples exposed to the combination NaCl + NaHCO3 - solution, compared to the pure NaCl -
solution. Additionally, a redistribution of chlorides was shown in the sample exposed to the combination
solution after six weeks of exposure. The chloride profile had a shift in the maximum measured chloride
content and a more flat curve. This indicates that carbonation had an influence on the chloride ingress.
XRD and TGA results revealed a decrease of the portlandite content along with an increase of the
calcite content for samples exposed to a solution containing NaHCO3. In addition, the water content
of samples exposed to a solution containing NaCl was shown to increase. As the difference between the
sample mass was small, the differences in water content are assessed to be associated with the chloride
ingress or the carbonation of the sample. As general carbonation was not found for the sample exposed
to the pure NaCl-solution, the increase in water content could be caused by a higher paste content or a
higher degree of hydration. The last mentioned could result in an increase of physically bound chloride
in C-S-H. However, the shown differences can also be due to unknown factors.
Nonetheless, the analysis did show that carbonation and chloride ingress affect each other, along
with the protective layer having an influence on the transport processes in cementitious binders. To fully
understand the transport processes, the knowledge gained through the analysis of the model samples
should be combined with the knowledge gained from the analysis of the field samples.

CHAPTER 6
Summary and final remark
In order to have a full understanding of the potential of surface layers for reducing transport processes,
the results from both the field and model samples needs to be combined.
Already in the field samples, it was seen that the (assumed) protective surface had an influence
on the chloride ingress, resulting in a higher service life for the protective side compared to the non-
protective side. The exposure of 18 months made it possible to gain realistic service life predictions,
along with an understanding of marine colonization. The service life was increased by up to 37 % for
the protective side dominated by barnacles compared to the non-protective side dominated by sand
and seaweed. This trend was further confirmed in the investigation of model samples. Even though
only a visual assessment of the protective side was possible for the analysis, it still clearly showed a
decrease in permeability ultimately leading to a lower chloride penetration depth of the protective side
independent of the exposure solution. Furthermore, the visual assessment revealed that with increasing
exposure times, the chloride penetration area increases which results consequently in a slower transport
of the chlorides, as the exposure conditions are assumed to be constant. This was further confirmed
by an investigation of the percentage increase in the chloride penetration area. Here it was clear that
a smaller increase of area was found for the longer exposure times, leading to greater areas. This was
especially shown in samples exposed to the NaCl-solution where the percentage increase of area from
two to four weeks was 44 % compared to an increase of 19 % when looking at the difference of areas
from four to six weeks (based on the 5 mm opening).
The analysis of the model samples made it possible to investigate the transport processes without
the initial chloride ingress. This included an investigation of the mineralogical composition of mortar
when exposed to different exposure solutions.
XRD and TGA analysis revealed that a decrease of
portlandite was observed as the calcite content was increased, thus corresponding to the process of
carbonation. This was shown in both model and field samples. Carbonation was shown to be lower for
samples exposed to chloride as well, indicating that chloride ingress could affect the carbonation process.
In the initial conditions from the field samples, it was also observed that the content of calcite from
the protective side was lower compared to the non-protective side. This further indicates that marine
colonization does not only have an influence on the chloride ingress but also on the carbonation rate.
The field samples made it possible to compare the effect of different cement types. Here it was clear
that calcined clay limestone cement was more resistant to chloride ingress. However, the carbonation
rate was increased in these samples due to the generally low content of portlandite content in calcined
clay limestone cement.
Even though Friedel’s salt was not found in the XRD analysis of the model samples, the chloride
profile did show further ingress of chlorides compared to the results of the visual assessment indicating
binding of chlorides. White® cement has a low content of aluminum, resulting in less potential for
forming Friedel’s salt. However, in general, the visual assessment agreed well with the chloride analysis
giving a preliminary indication of penetration depth.
The chloride profiles for the model samples,

further revealed a redistribution of chlorides due to carbonation when exposed to NaCl + NaHCO3
for six weeks. Friedel’s salt was however observed in field samples, formed by the free chloride and
monocarbonate, which were found in the inner layers of the samples.
An analysis of the tensile strength of the field samples was also performed, where it was clear that

Despite the limitations in the model samples, such as limited reaction partners and the fact that
only a visual assessment was possible for the protective side, the model samples correspond with field
samples, showing the same tendencies. The use of glass to represent a non-permeable layer was therefore
a success.
The understanding is, based on the results just explained, that marine colonization provides surface
protection of the exposed concrete structure, showing a slower transport of chloride ions and carbonation
as the permeability is lower and the penetration area is larger due to new chloride penetration path.
Even though the marine protective layers do not develop immediately upon concrete exposure, the
chloride ingress can be slowed after the initial exposure. The chloride ingress and damages due to the
exposure can therefore be prevented or slowed resulting in a longer service life for the exposed concrete
structures.
All in one resulting in higher durability.
The longer service life and higher durability
ultimately lead to increased reliability and lower maintenance thus reducing the environmental impacts
and less disturbance of the marine life.

CHAPTER 7
Outlook
Based on the studies and results obtained in this project, there are several new aspects that would
be interesting to investigate further. Time was a limitation for the model setup of this project, and
longer exposure times would have been preferred. However, the exposed model samples did already
show results after only two weeks of exposure, giving a possibility to duplicate the experiments without
using a significant amount of time. A duplication of the experiments would increase the accuracy of
the results and provide furhter knowledge.
An analysis of the exposure solution could also provide knowledge about the ion transport in the
samples, knowing how the exposure conditions changed with time, and maybe provide other knowledge
and answers to processes that could not be explained in the current setup. Additionally, model samples
could be exposed to seawater, with a constant supply of e.g. chlorides, or alternatively, ensure a constant
NaCl and NaHCO3 concentration by exchanging the exposure solution.
This study was performed on White® cement and it would be interesting to investigate if differences
occur when using other cement types. Especially further research on the potential of surface layers
combined with the use of calcined clay limestone cement, due to the lower environmental impacts.
Lastly, in the model investigations, it was not possible to make a quantitative analysis of the protec-
tive side, only a visual assessment. Here a Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) analysis would make
it possible to have a further and more thorough analysis of the protective effects in the model samples.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
From the analysis presented in this thesis, knowledge about the transport processes is gained along with
answers to the presented questions in Section 2.6. The transport processes and the durability of marine
exposed concrete structures have been investigated, and it can be concluded that marine colonization
can increase the service life by decreasing the transport processes.
Based on the investigations of the field samples, it can be concluded that the protective surface
dominated by barnacles has a lower chloride ingress compared to the non-protective side dominated by
sand and seaweed. This ultimately leads to an increase in the service life of up to 37 % for the protective
side. It was generally seen that the protective surface lead to a lower surface concentration of chlorides.
Additionally, it was shown from the TGA analysis that the calcite content was also affected by the
protective surface, showing lower contents for the protective side. Additionally, concrete consisting of
calcined clay limestone cement was found to be more resistant to chloride ingress compared to concrete
consisting of Portland cement. Here an increase in service life was found to 37% for the CalLi10 sample
compared to the PC10 sample (non-protective side). It could be concluded that even after 18 months
of exposure, the exposed samples had comparable tensile strength to the calculated tensile strength at
day 85. The initial conditions were thereby determined for the further analysis and development of
model samples.
In connection with the conducted investigations of the model samples, it is concluded that the
permeability was lower for the protective surface compared to the non-protective surface. The visual
investigation of the chloride ingress showed a clear effect of the protective surface. Here it was concluded
that the chloride penetration depth was decreased with a decrease in exposure openings. Different
exposure conditions were also investigated, where it was concluded that even when being exposed to
a NaCl + NaHCO3 - solution, the chloride penetration was lower for the protective surface. In an
analysis of the chloride penetration area, it was shown that an increasing area leads to an increased
amount of reaction partners, which leads to slower chloride ingress. Only a carbonation front for the
non-protective side was observed in the visual assessment of samples exposed to a solution containing
NaHCO3. This showed a higher penetration depth for samples exposed to a NaCl + NaHCO3 - solution.
However, from the XRD and TGA analysis, it was concluded that the calcite content was higher for
samples exposed to a pure NaHCO3 - solution, indicating that the carbonation can be affected by
the chloride ingress as well. Carbonation was also shown to influence the chloride ingress, where the
chloride profile showed a redistribution of chlorides for the sample exposed to the combination NaCl
+ NaHCO3 - solution for six weeks. Lastly, the TGA analysis also revealed a higher water content for
samples exposed to a solution containing NaCl.
It can be concluded that the development of model samples provided insightful knowledge, where
transport mechanisms could be investigated without the influence of the initial chloride ingress. However,
it was not possible to have quantitative investigations of the protective surface which could be necessary
to investigate further.
