Abstract
ANNs often produce overconfident predictions due to their reliance on single point­estimates
for weights, whereas BNNs provide robust uncertainty estimates at a high computational
cost. This thesis investigates posterior expectation distillation, specifically the GPED al­
gorithm, as a method to create computationally efficient ANNs that retain the uncertainty­
quantification benefits of BNNs. The work establishes the necessary theoretical founda­
tion by connecting conventional regularized training to MAP estimation, which creates a
theoretical bridge between ANNs and BNNs. Leading to a review of MCMC sampling
methods for a BNN’s posterior distribution, ending in the selection of the SGLD for its
scalability.
The viability of the GPED framework is demonstrated through a series of empirical stud­
ies. First, on a toy regression problem with a known analytical solution, the framework is
shown to successfully distill both the predictive mean and the epistemic uncertainty from
a BNN teacher. Second, a replication of a benchmark MNIST classification experiment
reveals challenges in reproducing published results and highlights the practical and the­
oretical importance of the MCMC sampler’s configuration. Finally, a novel application of
the framework is used on the Parkinsons Telemonitoring regression task to confirm the
method’s versatility. The findings consistently affirm that posterior distillation is a pow­
erful technique for creating fast, uncertainty­aware models, but also underscore that the
success of such methods is highly sensitive to the underlying sampling scheme and its
hyperparameters.

Introduction
In the last decade Artificial Neural Networks (ANN) have seen an increase in use cases
due to their performance compared to traditional algorithms.1 These ANNs use a single
point­estimate for each weight. The weights are found using an optimization algorithm
such as Maximum Likelihood Estimate (MLE), usually with a regularization term to pre­
vent overfitting. A drawback for ANNs is their incapacity to quantify their predictive un­
certainty2 potentially resulting in overconfident predictions. For some electronics such
as clinical diagnoses or self driving cars, overconfident estimates can be unacceptable.
Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) remedies this issue by learning a full posterior dis­
tribution over the weights allowing for the model to quantify its uncertainty however the
storage cost and inference time associated with BNNs made them ineligible for practical
use until recently.
The goal of this thesis is to explore the trade­offs between the accuracy of BNNs and
the inference speed of ANNs using the method of ”posterior expectation distillation” from
the paper ”Generalized Posterior Distillation Expectation”3 (GPED) a generalization of the
foundational work presented in the paper Bayesian Dark Knowledge4 (DK) in which the
predictive posterior expectation Ep(w|D)[p(y∗|x∗, w)] is used as the point­estimate for the
ANN, a process called distillation.
We first establish the theoretical foundation for supervised learning, Bayesian inference
and Markov Chain Monte Carlo5 (MCMC) sampling. Using the Bayesian framework we
show that the MLE with a regularizer is equivalent to a Maximum A Posteriori (MAP)
estimate and using the theory developed in GPED posit that a better point­estimate for
the weights in an ANN student is the expectation of the posterior predictive distribution
Ep(w|D)[p(y∗|x∗, w)] derived from the BNN teacher posterior p(w|D).
In practice the posterior is often intractable and one most resort to MCMC­methods for
approximations. One such method is the Metropolis­Hastings algorithm6(MH­algorithm).
Including the original MH­algorithm we present three samplers, all derivations of the MH­
algorithm each with their own drawbacks and advantages. Finally we decide upon using
the sampler called Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics7 (SGLD) algorithm for sam­
pling.
We validate our methodology by replicating a classification experiment from GPED on
the ”Modified National Institute of Standards and Technology database” (MNIST) dataset8
showing that the student almost matches the performance of the teacher. Finally we show
a novel application of the GPED algorithm by using it on the Parkinsons Telemonitoring
dataset9, a dataset in which its features are applied to predict the severeness of parkin­
sons in a patient, a regression problem.

3
Bayesian Theory
This chapter first introduces Bayes’ theorem16 to define a posterior distribution. Next,
the theoretical bridge between ANNs and the Bayesian framework is defined by showing
how training an ANN is equivalent to performing Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE).
Building on these two concepts, it is shown that regularization in ANNs is a special case
of the Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimate. From a Bayesian perspective, the limita­
tions of such a point­estimates is highlighted, focusing on the MAP’s inability to capture
uncertainty. Instead the predictive posterior expectation is proposed as a superior point­
estimate. The chapter concludes by introducing the ”Generalized Posterior Expectation
Distillation” (GPED) paper and its corresponding GPED algorithm, a practical method for
finding the aforementioned point estimate.

3.4
Generalized Posterior Expectation Distillation
Having established the connection between MAP­estimates and Regularization for ANNs
we elaborate on why point­estimates such as MAP are insufficient for capturing uncer­
tainty in the model, we then introduce the core proposal of thesis, ”Bayesian Posterior
Distillation”, and finally define the distillation framework presented in ”Generalized Poste­
rior Distillation Expectation”.
Both MLE and MAP are point estimates, which means they collapse the optimization
landscape down to a single ”best fit” of weights, this makes for efficient calculations but
discards vital information about the distributions’ confidence in its weight estimates. We
say that MLE finds the peak of the likelihood distribution and MAP finds the mode of
the posterior distribution, but neither manages to capture the ”landscape”. To describe
the uncertainty on the optimization landscape we can leverage the entire posterior, for
Bayesian inference27 we are particularly interested in the predictive posterior distribution
p(y∗|D, x∗) =
∫
p(y|w, x∗)p(w|D)dw
= Ep(w|D)[p(y∗|w, x∗)]
By integrating over all the weights we get the prediction for every possible model and
hence a distribution. The posterior tends to be analytically intractable due to the evidence,
though MCMC can be used as an approximation method, i.e. Ep(w|D)[(p(y∗|w, x∗)] ≈
1
S
∑S
s=1 p(y|ws, x∗) where ws ∼p(w|D). For real­time inference, leveraging the posterior
comes with clear disadvantages, as hundreds or thousands of forward passes are needed
for each prediction and for time­critical software this can be unacceptable.
The GPED paper proposes a generalized framework to distill knowledge from a BNN

4.2
Metropolis Hastings Algorithm
Having established the posterior’s tendency to be intractable, we introduce the founda­
tional Metropolis Hastings Algorithm, an MCMC method for drawing samples from a target
distribution. Namely the posterior. At the end a naive implementation of the MH­algorithm
is introduced called the Metropolis Hastings Random Walk (MHW).
A Markov Chain32(MC) is a sequence of probabilistic events, in which the current state
depends only on the previous state. We write P(Wt|Wt−1, . . . , Wt0) = P(Wt|Wt−1). An
important fact of Markov chains is that given a specific set of conditions and sufficient
number of steps the chain converges to its stationary distribution. Once a Markov chain
is in a stationary state, we can continue to draw samples from the stationary process’
stationary distribution. Denoting the Markov chains stationary distribution π(x) we let
π(w) = p(w|D).
A Monte Carlo33 methods(MC) are a class of algorithms that use repeated random sam­
pling to obtain numerical results. In the context of this thesis, this refers to the process of
drawing enough samples from the posterior to approximate its properties, such as mean
and variance.
MCMC5 methods starts a Markov Chain and run for an initial period, called burn in. We
discards these samples and afterwards the subsequent samples are collected to perform
Monte Carlo estimation. The MH algorithm is one such method.
We can use the MH algorithm for generating samples of a given posterior distribution
π(w). First an initial state, w0, is chosen. Then the algorithm iteratively performs three
steps:
1. A sample candidate is drawn w′ ∼g(w′|w(t)).
2. The acceptance probability A(w′, w) is calculated by considering how likely the can­
didate sample is under π(w) this probability is adjusted by the proposal densities
and lastly a random number between 0 and 1 decides whether to accept or reject
the candidate.
3. If the candidate is accepted, then the weight is updated w(t+1) = w
′. Otherwise the
old weight remains, i.e. w(t+1) = w(t).
Running over a sufficient number of iterations the sequence of samples forms a Markov
chain whose stationary distribution is p(w|D).

4.3
Langevin Diffusion
To provide a more efficient sampler, we’ll introduce an approach known as Langevin Diffu­
sion, originating from statistical physics, Langevin Diffusion is a continuous­time stochas­
tic process. We will approximate this process using the Euler­Maruyama method. These
concepts are explained to provide the theoretical justification for the gradient­proposal
distributions used in MALA, ULA and SGLD.
To create a more efficient choice for the metropolis acceptance probability A(w′, w(t)) one
can use the concept of Langevin Diffusion in which the gradient of the log­posterior guides
the sampling process towards higher probability regions. This process is described by the
stochastic differential equation35
dXt = 1
2∇log π(Xt)dt + dWt
Where Xt is the state of the Markov chain at some time i.e., the weight vector w(t) in the
MH­algorithm. And ∇log π(Xt) is the The gradient of the log­posterior which ”nudges” Xt
towards the mode of the distribution π. The second term dWt is called Brownian motion36
and its purpose is to inject noise into the process so that the distribution is explored as
t →∞.
Because Langevin Diffusion uses continuous­time we must resort to numerical approx­
imations of the stochastic differential equation. The arguably simplest method is called
the Euler­Maruyama discretizations37
Xt+1 = Xt + η
2∇log π(Xt) + N(0, hIdxd)
where η is a fixed step size of the gradient, a hyper parameter38. The Brownian motion
is defined as N(0, hIdxd) where its variance is now tied to the step­size of the gradient.
Intuitively we want our step towards the top of the distribution and the perturbations to
agree on magnitude, so that one does not dominate the other.
The numerical approximation in Euler­Maruyama comes from the gradient being con­
stant over some constant finite step η, according to Langevin Diffusion the change in the
gradient is infinitesimal. As Euler­Maruyama continues to sample discrete time steps, the
stationary distribution becomes an approximation of the stationary distribution in Langevin
Diffusion. This difference is commonly referred to as ”the discretizations error”. And re­
sults in the approximated posterior pη(w|D), whereas Langevin finds the actual posterior
p(w|D).
We introduced the Langevin Diffusion and a numerical method for approximating it. By
using a gradient­informed update rule combined with stochastic noise we can make in­
telligent suggestions for various choices of g, leading to more effective samplers than
MRW.

ULA
Using MALA we established a way for quick convergence towards a posterior, but the
storage space required quickly makes MALA infeasible on large datasets. In this section
we reassess the need for the metropolis acceptance probability calling the solution for the
Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA), an alternative sampler which significantly reduces
the storage requirements by doing away with the acceptance criteria.
The Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm 40 works by removing the computationally expensive
metropolis acceptance step. Reducing storage costs from two gradients to one gradient
per step. As a result, the discretization error from the Euler­Maruyama method implies the
sampler no longer converges to the true posterior p(w|D) but to a biased approximation
ph(w|D).

Student Distillation
The core distillation process is the student distillation. A simple artificial Neural network
trained to mimic the output of the teacher network. The goal is to effective learn the
teacher’s posterior predictive mean. The student’s learning progressed is tracked through
various timestep in its regression fit and quantified by the MSE relative to the teacher.
To evaluate the distillation process a student network is designed with a simple linear
layer. The student model is trained using the Adam optimizer,44 a PyTorch optimizer for
MAP estimates. The Adam optimizer has a learning rate of 10−3 and an L2­regularization
of 10−3. Lastly the architecture of the student is identical to that of the teacher.
Having established the posterior approximation, the student network is now trained to
mimic that of the teachers predictions, this is the fundamental idea of the GPED algorithm.
Choosing loss function for the student is chosen to be the MSE between its own outputs
and that of the teachers. To demonstrate the efficacy of this approach the figure below

it becomes possible to approximate the predictive variance thus creating a method for a
student model to quantify its own uncertainty.

Dataset Explanation
The GPED paper focuses on classification, by showing the GPED algorithm can be ex­

To demonstrate the GPEDs algorithm flexibility we use the ”Parkinsons Telemonitoring
dataset”46. Detailing the clinical significance of the target variables and describe the voice­

Medical professionals use the Unified Parkinson’s Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS) as a
metric for how progressed the Parkinsons9 disease is in a patient. It is split into two re­
sponse variables, ”motor UPDRS” and ”total UPDRS”, for the model it suffices to consider
only ”total UPDRS” as ”motor UPDRS” is a subset of this response variable. In general a
higher ”total UPDRS” implies a more advances parkinsons.
The input features for the model consist of demographic data and a variety of advanced
acoustic metrics derived from voice recordings, which are known to be affected by Parkin­
son’s disease. These can be grouped as follows:
• Jitter: A group of five related measures (Jitter(%), Jitter(Abs), Jitter:RAP,
Jitter:PPQ5, Jitter:DDP) that quantify the cycle­to­cycle variation in the funda­
mental frequency (vocal pitch).
• Shimmer: A group of six related measures (Shimmer, Shimmer(dB), Shimmer:APQ3,
Shimmer:APQ5, Shimmer:APQ11, Shimmer:DDA) that quantify the cycle­to­cycle vari­
ation in the amplitude (loudness) of the voice.
• Harmonic and Noise Ratio: The NHR (Noise­to­Harmonics Ratio) measures the
ratio of noise to tonal components in the voice signal.
• Nonlinear Complexity Measures: Three features that capture the complex dynam­
ics of the voice signal: RPDE (Recurrence Period Density Entropy), DFA (Detrended
Fluctuation Analysis), and PPE (Pitch Period Entropy).

Discussion
This chapter discusses findings and broader implications of the experimental work done
in chapter 6 and 7. A central theme across both the MNIST classification and Parkinsons
regression experiments was the challenge of achieving a fully converged posterior for the
BNN teacher. In the MNIST replication, the teacher model failed to learn effectively with
the constant learning rate specified in the source literature, suggesting the benchmark
results rely on hard to reproduce conditions. In the Parkinsons experiment, the teacher
showed a stable learning process but did not reach convergence within the allotted itera­
tions.
The distillation process proved itself throughout both experiments. Showing the GPED
algorithm is a robust and viable tool for transferring knowledge from a BNN, despite the
teacher’s posterior being non­stationary. The results validate the framework’s potential to
bridge the gap between the inference speed of ANNs and the uncertainty quantification
of BNNs.
8.1
Experimental Findings
The central goal presented in this thesis was to find a compromise between the inference
speed of ANNs and the uncertainty quantification of BNNs. This objective was pursued
through two distinct experiments: a replication of a benchmark classification task from
the GPED paper, and a novel application of the distillation framework for a real­world
regression problem. A unifying observation for both experiments showed the posterior
struggled to converge. Albeit for different reasons.
For the MNIST experiment the teacher failed to learn effectively. One reason suggested
is the benchmark NLL may rely on a fortunate combination of fortunate approximate pos­
terior pη(w|D) and lucky seeding from the computer. The argument is further supported
by the subsequent experiment with a theoretically sound decaying step­size, this sampler
produced a stable learning curve, yet had its own issues, it failed to match the benchmark
performance and, like the constant learning rate, had overfitting albeit much later than
its predecessor. The findings lead to the conclusion that practical success of this distilla­
tion framework is profoundly contingent on the non trivial task of configuring the MCMC
sampler, suggesting the benchmark results hinge on specific, hard­to­produce conditions.
Looking to the regression experiment, the teacher exhibited healthy signs of learning and
the student replicated it a like. Yet, the teacher still did find a converged posterior, this
time due to a lack of iterations.
In both cases, this underlines the importance careful consideration when utilizing sampling
methodology. Despite the teachers challenges to reach a converged posterior the student
was capable of imitating the teacher in both settings. Making a precedence for the GPED2
algorithm as a viable tool for bridging the gap in inference between ANNs and BNNs.
8.2
Broader Implications
The findings in this thesis carries the implication that practical applications of Bayesian
methods in deep learning is becoming viable. This work confirms that the posterior expec­
tation distillation can serve as an effective bridge between the computational efficiency of
Bayesian Posterior Distillation for Deep Neural Networks
41
==PAGE:49==
ANN point estimates and uncertainty quantification of BNNs. Practitioners can leverage
this method for critical components, in order to gain close the performance of a BNN with
the inference speed of an ANN all while quantifying the definitive factors of the model,
the weights. However, this is not a silver bullet49 as the upfront cost of reliably training
a BNN has not been solved. As shown in the experiments, the training process is highly
sensitivity to the configurations of the MCMC sampler and its hyperparameters. Therefore
the impact of the distillation framework is only justifiable in cases where the considerable
effort to produce a high­quality teacher model is justified. In these cases, it is possible to
produce trustworthy, uncertainty aware predictions with the inference speed of ANNs.
8.3
Limitations of the study
A limitation of the study was the distillation process occurred only on non stationary pos­
teriors, potentially overestimating the students ability to distill information on a converged
posterior. Furthermore the hyperparameter sweeps, though extensive, did not exhaust
the parameters space of the SGLD sampler. The sensitivity to the results to parameters
like the prior precision and the decay schedule suggests additional tuning can significantly
improve the teachers performance, and importantly, ensure convergence of the posterior.
Finally, the experimental work relied on the SGLD­sampler, though appropriate for large
datasets. As established in chapter 4 the MALA sampler had been a more appropriate
choice for the two smaller datasets presented in the classification and regression experi­
ments.
8.4
Future Work
Several promising avenues for future works is apparent. Primarily focusing on improving
the posterior convergence and deepening the analysis of the distillation process to allow
for evaluation of the GPED algorithm for converged posteriors. In order to build upon the
limitations section, utilizing the MALA sampler is recommended. As the MALA promises
faster convergence with fewer parameter tunings. Making MALA Ideal for small datasets
such as the MNIST and Parkinsons. Alternatively to remedy the number of iterations
needed for convergence, feature engineering could have been utilized, such as removing
features which were inverses of one another e.g. Jitter(%) and Jitter(Abs). With a con­
verged teacher it is possible to perform a full uncertainty analysis, using calibration plots,
credibility intervals, epistemic uncertainty and the like. The performance of the BNN’s
predictions should be critically examined, particularly in the Parkinsons dataset. Finally,
future work should systematically investigate the impact of the student model’s architec­
ture, given the GPED paper only describes student model’s architecture for classification
tasks, insight into the impact of student’s architecture for regression problems would be
a novel insight.
In this chapter, we discussed the experimental findings, their broader implications, and
the limitations of this study. The results confirmed that posterior expectation distillation is
a viable technique, with the student network consistently demonstrating its ability to learn
from a BNN teacher across different tasks. The work highlighted practical challenges
in training a reliable teacher model, as evidenced by the convergence issues in both
experiments. Building on the current work, we outlined several avenues for future work,
including the use of alternative samplers like MALA and a more systematic investigation
into the student model’s architecture.

Conclusion
This thesis addressed the fundamental trade­off between the computational efficiency of
Artificial Neural Networks and the uncertainty quantification capabilities of Bayesian Neu­
ral Networks. A theoretical foundation was established by demonstrating that common
regularization techniques in ANNs are equivalent to Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) esti­
mates within a Bayesian context . Building on this analytical fact, it was hypothesized that
a more robust point­estimate could be derived from the expectation of the BNN’s posterior
predictive distribution Ep(w|D)[p(y∗|w, x∗)].
To overcome the analytical intractability of the posterior distribution p(w|D) this work lever­
aged Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling methods. Following a comparative analysis,
the Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics algorithm was selected for its computational
efficiency on large datasets. The viability of the ”posterior expectation distillation” algo­
rithm was validated across the classification and regression domain. First replicating a
benchmark experiment on the MNIST dataset, and afterwards, a novel regression task
using the Parkinsons Telemonitoring dataset.
On the task of classification the MNIST dataset, the distilled student demonstrated strong
capacity to approximate the performance of the teacher. In an experiment utilizing a
polynomial­decay learning rate for the SGLD­sampler, the teacher model archived a 96.00%
accuracy and a 0.162 NLL while the student model achieved a comparable NLL of 0.178
and 95.91% accuracy. Confirming the students ability to mimic the teachers ability but
achieve inference speeds to equivalent to that of a traditional ANN.
The GPED algorithms efficacy was further exemplified through the application of the
Parkinsons Telemonitoring regression task. The teacher models final NLL was 0.461 while
the student achieved an NLL of 0.462, once again demonstrating the student’s ability to
distill the teacher’s predictive distribution while retaining the inference efficiency to that of
an ANN. In conclusion, this work validates the posterior expectation distillation algorithm
as a robust and flexible technique. While the quality of the distillation is contingent on the
task of training a reliable teacher model, the experiments shows the GPED algorithm is
successful on producing fast, uncertainty aware models for both classification and regres­
sion. Marking a significant step towards practical employment of Bayesian deep learning.

def bayes_predictions(network , weight_samples , loader , device):
115
"""
116
Performs Bayesian Model Averaging to get final predictions and uncertainty
.
117
Returns:
118
(np.array, np.array, np.array): y_pred_mean , y_pred_std , y_true
119
"""
120
network.eval()
121
all_means_across_samples = []
122
123
# Get predictions for each weight sample
124
for sample_weights in tqdm(weight_samples , desc="Getting BMA Predictions")
:
125
network.load_state_dict({k: v.to(device) for k, v in sample_weights.
items()})
126
batch_means = []
127
with torch.no_grad():
128
for data, _ in loader:
129
data = data.to(device)
130
mean, _ = network(data) # We only need the mean from each
model
131
batch_means.append(mean)
132
all_means_across_samples.append(torch.cat(batch_means))
133
134
# Collect ground truth labels once
135
y_true = torch.cat([target for _, target in loader]).cpu().numpy().flatten
()
136
137
# Calculate the final predictive mean and standard deviation
138
stacked_means = torch.stack(all_means_across_samples , dim=0)
139
y_pred_mean = torch.mean(stacked_means , dim=0).cpu().numpy().flatten()
140
y_pred_std = torch.std(stacked_means , dim=0).cpu().numpy().flatten()
141
142
return y_pred_mean , y_pred_std , y_true
