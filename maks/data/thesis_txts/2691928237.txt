Abstract
In the recent years, the problem of Question Answering has been targeted using the
solutions based on the usage of the pre-trained models, yielding exceptional results.
These approaches revolve around the retrieval of the documents with the components
utilizing the BERT model.
The goal of this thesis was to tackle the problem of the medical-open-domain
multiple-choice question-answering. It has been achieved by creating two solutions
based on the state-of-the-art architectures, namely ColBERT and REALM. Both
have been further fine-tuned on the medical corpus and their performance has been
evaluated on the set of questions from the professional medical examinations. The
main focus of the implementations revolved around the quality of the component
responsible for the evidence text retrieval. The obtained results have been thoroughly
analysed and the suggestions for further improvement have been presented.
Moreover, the thesis contains the general overview of machine learning and deep
learning theory, followed by the description of the approaches commonly used for the
Natural Language Processing tasks. It explains the concepts around the usage of
attention and describes both the architecture of the Transformer and BERT models.
That knowledge gives the context to the later presented overview of the solutions that
have achieved state-of-the-art results in the domain of Question Answering.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Problem description
Question Answering (QA) is a field of computer science utilizing the information
retrieval and natural language processing methods for creating answers to the
questions stated in the natural language. It is a very ubiquitous problem encountered
by millions of internet users daily. Multiple existing solutions are targeting it, Google
Search engine being the most common example.
When it comes to the question-
answering tools, there are two crucial aspects that they need to cover:
• the need of proper question comprehension to look for an answer which matches
the context and underlying message of the question
• high effectiveness of such system when it comes to accuracy and time perfor-
mance
In the field of computer science, solving the automated QA problem can be split
into two categories derived from the type and form of the used data:
• closed-domain question answering: for each question and answer pair, there
exists a mapping to a text passage, which either contains the evidence for in-
ferring the answer or explicitly contains it as a span. Hence answering a single
question requires a relatively small amount of knowledge and focuses more on
the reading comprehension sub-task.
• open-domain question answering (ODQA): there is no such mapping; the
developed system is responsible for creating the answer having the question and
the whole corpus data as the potential input. This category poses the challenges
met in the closed-domain version of the task and, on top of that, requires an
eﬀicient information selection from the vast corpus of knowledge. Moreover, the
ODQA solutions aim not to limit the model’s performance to the usage of the
single corpus, but to make it applicable to utilizing the world knowledge. That
poses a significantly greater challenge than the former category due to the size
of the processed amount of text.

1.2
Motivation
The recent development of BERT [Dev+19] module and the solutions built upon using
pre-trained models have contributed towards great results in the QA field. Several
novel architectures have been developed and yielded excellent results on multiple
benchmarks, beating the previous state-of-the-art approaches. However, most of these
projects are targeting solving the QA problem using the corpora not focusing on
the particular topic. The most common datasets are built upon the real questions
queried by the users of the search engines, and they rarely focus on a particular
domain - and each domain can require different levels of text comprehension to grasp
a proper meaning behind either the question or the candidate evidence texts used for
formulating an answer. The question answering problem targeting a complex domain
is a rarer phenomenon, worthy of trying to use the newest pre-trained models to solve
it.
Moreover, it is more common for the projects targeting the QA problem - in both
open and close domains - to utilize the data, in which the answer to the question
is either a span within a single passage of the corpus or that passage proves to be
enough evidence for the pre-trained models to create the correct answer. However,
some questions - especially those targeting complex domains - might need multiple
passages for grasping the context behind the question in order to formulate a correct
answer. A worthy example would be the questions in the medical domain, e.g. about
the diagnosis of the illness given the text description of the patient’s condition.
1.3
Thesis objectives
The main objectives of this thesis were to explore the approaches used for the ODQA
problems and to create a solution for the open-domain medical question answer-
ing task. The following subgoals were determined in order to achieve them:
1. Get familiarized with the architectures commonly used for solving the ODQA
tasks and create the general overview of the solutions achieving strong results.
2. Implement the solutions inspired by the existing state-of-the-art methodologies
built upon the usage of BERT model. The main focus of the implementations
should revolve around the quality of the component responsible for the evidence
text retrieval.
3. Evaluate the end-to-end performance of the created modules on the set of multi-
choice-questions from the real-life United States Medical Licensing Exams (de-
tails in section 5.2).
4. Based on the obtained results, present an insightful analysis that can become
useful as a potential future reference for similar projects.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
This chapter gives a general overview of the machine learning theory, focusing primar-
ily on the deep learning part. As the solutions discussed in this paper utilize models
built upon the neural networks, it presents a general idea behind them, explains the
learning mechanism, and describes the most commonly used network types.
2.1
Machine learning
Machine learning, as a subarea of artificial intelligence, is a field of computer science
aimed towards creating the algorithms that can learn to solve problems. It differs
from the conventional engineering approach for designing the algorithmic solutions,
which typical workflow is first to gather the complete domain knowledge, create a
mathematical model capturing the physics of the studied problem and prepare the
optimized algorithm to solve it [Sim18]. In contrast, the machine learning approach
focuses on building a model trained on a suﬀiciently large set of examples of the
desired behaviour to learn how to handle the given task and similarly use this learned
skill with the new data. The created solution is supposed to use the past experiences
to obtain the ability allowing it to tackle the future, similar versions of the task. That
ability to correctly categorize new examples that differ from those used for training
is known as generalization [Bis06].
2.1.1
Task types
There are three main categories that machine learning techniques can be split into,
based on the data it uses for the learning process:
• Supervised learning: the dataset consists of the inputs of the problems mapped
to the desired output values. Given the input x, the machine learning algorithm
is trained to create the predictions y, which are as close as possible to the
labelled correct answers. If y is supposed to be the discrete value, then it is a
classification task, while in case that the learned output is continuous, then it
is a regression problem.
• Unsupervised learning: the inputs are unlabelled, which mean that there is no
assignment of the expected outputs to the dataset’s examples. The machine

learning algorithm’s goal is to learn the underlying patterns of the dataset and
discover the new labelling on its own. Clustering, density estimation, anomaly
detection, association mining and dimensionality reduction are tasks belonging
to this category.
• Reinforcement learning: every time the reinforcement learning algorithm per-
forms an action during the training procedure, it receives feedback from its
environment indicating whether that action was good or bad for achieving its
goal. Partly, it is similar to supervised learning - as there is some ground truth
from the environment passed to the algorithm informing it about whether the
decisions it made were helpful or not. However, that passed information is not
determining the final expected outputs, and the algorithm still gets ”rewards”
even for the suboptimal action, in order to make it not only utilize the knowl-
edge it learned but also explore new aspects of the domain by taking different
approaches [KLM96].
2.1.2
Training
The goal of training the model is to make it create accurate predictions for the unseen
before input data. The way the model predicts is the consequence of the model’s
architecture, weights connecting the neurons and also the parameters used during
the training process, called hyperparameters. To measure how well the trained model
generalizes, the mentioned earlier set of examples used for training is usually divided
into three parts: training set, validation set and test set.
The training procedure can be split into two parts: model training and evaluation.
1. First, the model is trained on the training set of data, during which its parame-
ters are being adjusted. Usually, most of the available data is used for training
to increase the model’s generalization capabilities.
2. Next, the quality of the model’s generalization is measured by checking how it
performs on the validation dataset. During validation, the model’s parameters
are not being altered to see how it performs on unseen data without any ad-
justments throughout iterations. This cannot be performed with the previously
used data, as the model can memorize the data used during the training. That
would make it biased towards the already seen examples and lead to poorer
performance on the new samples.
The validation process is usually used for tuning the hyperparameters being
used during the training on the training set. To get the unbiased performance
of the solution, it is a common practice to check the predictions for yet another
sample of unseen data, the test set.
Observing how well the model generalizes to new data compared to the data used
for training can be described by the measurement referred to as model fitting. The

most common approach for evaluating fitting is creating plots of the produced by
the model loss on given datasets in respect to the number of epochs of the training,
where the value is calculated as average over the whole set. In general, there are three

• underfitting: model performs poorly on both training and validation data. It
indicates that either the model’s architecture might be unsuitable for the given
task or is a consequence of the implementation errors.
• overfitting: model’s performance on training data improves with every epoch,
but it does not happen for the validation set.
It indicates that the model
adjusts its approach to the characteristics of the training data, including random
fluctuations and noises. As these cannot be applied to the new data, it decreases
the model’s ability to generalize - the model is biased towards the details of the
training set.
• good fit: the results for both training and validation datasets are improving
with training. It means that the model was designed well, successfully trained
and managed to grasp the underlying concepts of the problem, allowing it to
generalize well.
2.1.3
Loss functions
Earlier mentioned loss (also referred to as cost) describes the difference between the
predictions created by the model and the labelled desired outputs. Depending on the
given problem, the goal is for the model to minimize or maximize it (in the latter
scenario, it is called a reward). An indication that the model is trained properly is
denoted with the cost function value gradually decreasing (or increasing) throughout
the training. Depending on the machine learning task and the number of created
predictions, the loss is calculated using one of several functions.
The loss function used for the solutions developed in this thesis is Cross entropy
loss. It is based on the information theory concept of entropy, which measures the
level of uncertainty of the variable’s possible outcomes - the greater the value, the

2.2.1
Backpropagation and gradient descent
It was earlier mentioned that the better performance of the model could be denoted
by minimizing the loss function value, achieved by the adjustment of the network’s
parameters. Intuitively, the partial derivative of a cost function with respect to the
weights and biases of the model can provide information on how alterations of the
weights could affect the loss. Following that logic, the standard way of adjusting the
model parameters is to use backpropagation algorithm together with the gradient
descent optimization.

The vector of all calculated partial derivatives of the cost function can be used by

result in not reaching the minimum throughout the whole training process.

2.2.2
Neural networks usage in NLP
Neural networks such as feed-forward neural networks and convolutional neural net-
works treat all inputs (and outputs) as independent of each other. That trait makes
them an unintuitive choice for NLP tasks which take the text sequence as an input,
in which each word can have an impact on the desired result. For instance, given
the translation task, the direct translation of the single word can be inaccurate with-
out considering other words surrounding it or keywords in the sentence influencing
the context (e.g. negations). Despite that, to some extent, the feed-forward neural
networks with the fixed-length context can still be applied to the language modelling
problems [Mik+10]. However, the Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) do not have
the fixed-length constraint and became a popular choice for NLP tasks.

This chapter focuses on summarising the mainly used deep learning approaches for the

3.1
Sequence-to-sequence model
Deep neural networks are the most common approaches for learning tasks like speech
or image recognition, yielding powerful results. For such problems, the inputs are
encoded into the vectors of the fixed dimensionality and fed to the network, which
learns the weights of the layers leading to desired results during the training. Per
each input, the output produced does not need any information about other inputs
- it is independent of them. These two traits: the fixed encoding size and the lack
of the influence of other inputs on the output of the currently processed input - can
prove to be an obstacle for the sequence modelling tasks, for which the input is the
sequence of words (each sequence can be of a different length). There are multiple
sequence modelling problems, such as:
• machine translation - converting a sequence of text written in one natural lan-
guage into another
• sequence classification - assigning the given text a label, e.g. the sentiment score
==PAGE:26==
14
3 Deep learning approaches for NLP
• text summarizing - creating a sequence of words shorter than the input sequence,
which preserves the important information and meaning of the input
• question answering - which can be perceived as creating a mapping between a
sequence of words formulating a question to the sequence of words representing
an answer
For these type of problems, to create the output, it is crucial to comprehend
the whole sequence not as the set of independent inputs (words), but as a chain of
elements with non-trivial relationships between each other. In other words, perceiving
the context of the whole input sequence is necessary to produce a valid output.
The authors of Sequence to Sequence Learning with Neural Networks came up
with an idea of the sequence-to-sequence model [SVL14]. It proved to yield strong
results for the machine translation task [SVL14] and was later on further extended
by the addition of the usage of the attention mechanism by [BCB14] (see section 3.2).
Most importantly, the sequence-to-sequence model became a foundation for multiple
NLP applications in the domain of question answering [Nie+17], text summarization,
machine translation, headline generation and more [Shi+20].

3.1.2
Main weakness
The way the RNNs and LSTMs work is that they produce the sequence of hidden
states, where each hidden state is based on both the currently processed input and
the previous state. That leads to the main problems when it comes to training such
model while using the longer input sequences:
• the deterioration of the performance of the RNNs proportional to the growing
length of input sequences. The fixed-length vector representation might be too
little to cover the encoding of the long, complicated sentence. The consequence
could be that the neural network loses some important information for the sake
of creating the encoding matching the fixed-size requirement [Cho+14b].
• parallelization of processing a single example is impossible because the next
hidden state depends on the previous one. Therefore, batching while processing
long input is not possible.
The former issue was overcome with the enhanced encoder-decoder architecture
with the usage of the attention mechanism, which was explored by [BCB14] (further
details in section 3.2.1).
Unfortunately, that was not enough for the latter problem.
The authors of
[Vas+17] explored an idea of relying purely on the attention mechanism, without
the RNN part preventing the usage of the parallelization for processing the long text
examples. Their efforts resulted in a Transformer architecture being created (section
3.3).

3.2
Attention
3.2.1
Origin
In the context of NLP, the general idea behind the attention mechanism is to make
it possible to assign to the words in sequence weights that mark their significance
[GLT20]. It resembles the way humans process the sentences - given the context,
some words have more impact on the sentence and are more important than the
others. Figure 3.2 illustrates an example of how the attention mechanism can be
applied in the machine reading task to mark the significance of the previous words
when it comes to the processing of the current word.
The concept was introduced in the [BCB14], where it was used in combination with
the bidirectional RNNs as an improvement over the earlier developed LSTM-based
sequence-to-sequence model (more details in the 3.2.1). The usage of the attention
was further explored in [Vas+17], where the new concepts: Scaled Dot-Product At-
tention and Multi-Head Attention, were introduced. Together with the self-attention
mechanism, they are described in subsection 3.3.2.

mentioned problem of deteriorating performance caused by the usage of the fixed-

3.3
Transformer model
The transformer model was introduced in the Attention Is All You Need paper by
[Vas+17]. As the name suggests, the authors decided to create an architecture based
purely on the attention mechanism, combined with the feed-forward neural networks
in place of using the RNNs (or CNNs), which was a common practice in the previously
used approaches. The proposed solution not only yielded superior results but also
allowed the tasks to require significantly less time to train due to the possibility of
parallelization, which was not possible with the usage of, e.g. LSTMs (see subsection
3.1.2).
3.3.1
Architecture
A transformer consists of two modules: an encoder and decoder, stacked on top of
each other. The way they are constructed and work differ from the ideas used in the
sequence-to-sequence model:
• they do not utilize the usage of the RNNs. Instead, FFNNs are used
• the encoder does not read the input sequentially, but whole at once.
The
created input embeddings are enhanced by the addition of the positional encod-
ings. They inform the model about the positions of the tokens in the sequence.
Thanks to that, the model can use the order of the sequence elements without
using convolutions or recurrence.
The encoder’s construction can be split into six identical layers, each consisting
of two sub-layers:
• First is a Multi-head self-attention layer
• Second is a fully connected feed-forward network
To each of these sublayers, a residual connection is applied [He+15] and the output
is normalized. The output of each sub-layer is processed as follows:
Sublayer(x) = Norm(x + Sublayerfun(x))
Similarly, the decoder is also built of 6 identical layers, with each having 3 sub-
layers - two of which are the same as in the encoder. The third one, in between
them, is the Multi-head attention layer over the encoder’s output. In order to ensure
that predicting the i’th position is based only on the known outputs at the previous
positions (not greater than i), the self-attention sublayer is slightly modified [Vas+17].

3.3.2.1
Self-attention
Self-attention, also known as intra-attention, aims to assign for each word in the input
sequence the set of weights indicating how relevant with respect to that word the other
words are. In simpler terms, it allows the input words to interact with each other
and underline to which other words they should pay more attention to. It is used
for obtaining the contextualized word embeddings of the input sequence’s elements.
Attention can be calculated using different score functions - to allow the reader to
grasp its concept better, the following example uses the dot-product-attention func-
tion (steps 1 and 2).
For the inputs represented by n-dimensional vectors, three weight matrices W K,
W Q and W V are constructed. Multiplication of inputs with these matrices results
in the creation of three new representations per each input: keys, queries and values.
The process of obtaining the contextualized vectors per each input is as follows:
1. Calculating the dot products between the processed input’s query and all inputs’
keys
2. Passing obtained outputs through the softmax functions to get the attention
scores.
3. Multiplying the scores by the values and summing the results, forming the final
output - the re-weighted vector representation of processed input

BERT
BERT stands for Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformer. It is a
multi-layer Transformer encoder, which, thanks to the created along it Masked
Language Modelling method allowing the joint condition on both left and right
context during the pre-training, is bidirectional.
That trait comes with utilizing
the self-attention mechanism in both directions and, according to the authors, is
important for making the distinction of their work from the previous architectures.

BERT usage for various NLP tasks, including QA, proved to yield strong results
on its own and became a core component of future state-of-the-art frameworks.
3.4.1
Input
BERT’s input consists of three same-length embeddings sequences:
• sequence of token embeddings created using the WordPiece tokenizer [Wu+16]
with a 30000 token vocabulary, prefixed with the classification token marked
as [CLS]. That input sequence can be created from a single sentence and the
sentence pair, e.g. question + answer pair. In that case, both parts embeddings
are concatenated and separated with the special [SEP] token.
• segmentation embeddings sequence, which contains information about the be-
longingness of each token to either first or second input sentence
• sequence of position embeddings, informing about the position of each token
within the token sequence

3.4.2
Output
BERT outputs the vector of the encoder’s final hidden state corresponding to each
input sequence’s token embeddings. The hidden vector corresponding to the previ-
ously mentioned [CLS] token can be used as the aggregate sequence representation for
classification-based downstream tasks. In the base version of the model, the hidden
size is equal to 512.
3.4.3
Pre-training
BERT consists of 110M parameters, which form an enormous space for storing the
knowledge about the understanding of the language.
To adjust them and make

BERT learn about natural language comprehension, the model is pre-trained on
two datasets:
• set of 2.5B words from the English Wikipedia articles
• 800 million words from English books
which is achieved by running the following two tasks:
• Masked Langauge Modelling (MLM): a task based on the Cloze Task [Tay53]
in which a percentage (15% [Dev+19]) of random tokens of the input is marked
for being masked, and the model aims to predict these absent parts. The marked
tokens are replaced 90% of the time:
– 80% of the time with the [MASK] token
– 10% of the time with the random token
The [MASK] token is not used 100% of the time because it is only present in
the pre-training task. That way, the mismatch between the pre-training and
fine-tuning is mitigated [Dev+19].
• Next Sentence Prediction (NSP): this task aims to make the model compre-
hend the relationship between sentences. Given a sentence pair A and B, NSP
is about the model evaluating whether B is the sentence appearing after A in
the original text. For BERT pre-training, 50% of the used pairs consisted of
consecutive sentences, while the remaining pairs were matched randomly.
This task proved to enhance the results yielded by BERT in the QA environment.
The authors compared the BERT model with and without the usage of NSP
on multiple QA sets, and for each one of them, the model pre-trained NSP
performed significantly better (higher accuracy ranging from 0.2% up to 3.5%)
[Dev+19].

Related work
This chapter focuses on presenting the state-of-the-art architectures connected with
the ODQA task. The ideas behind them were the main inspiration for the architecture
of the solutions presented in this paper.
4.1
DrQA
The DrQA solution proposed in the Reading Wikipedia to Answer Open-Domain Ques-
tions paper [Che+17] aimed to use Wikipedia as the unique knowledge source for
answering questions while tackling the challenges of documents retrieval and machine
comprehension of text.
It utilizes the two-module construction, consisting of the Document Retriever
and Document Reader.
Document Retriever
is a module responsible for selecting a subgroup of doc-
uments (or paragraphs) that most likely contain either the necessary evidence for
inferring the answer or the answer explicitly. That way, the search space for finding
the answer is significantly narrowed, especially for large-scale knowledge sources, such
as Wikipedia. The document retriever part of the DrQA solution is implemented us-
ing the TF-IDF weighted bag-of-vectors improved by the bigram hashing [Che+17].
It returns the top 5 documents, which are passed further on to the Document Reader
module.
Document Reader
has a purpose of inferring the answer based on the provided
evidence from the Document Retriever.
DrQA authors decided to follow up the
trend of using the machine learning models for the comprehension tasks and chose a
bidirectional LSTM with three layers and 128 hidden states to handle the question
answering part of the workflow. Question and the retrieved knowledge passages are
passed on to the LSTM, producing the answer.
This two-component design allows converting the ODQA into the machine reading
problem, where one component aims to infer from the texts provided by the other
one. This methodology has been reused in the multiple follow-up solutions obtaining
state-of-the-art results.
==PAGE:38==
26
4 Related work
Training using supervised data
The reading comprehension component is trained
using the question-passage-answer pairs as the input, where the passage contains the
answer to the question as a span of text. In the end-to-end question-answering setting,
the retriever component produces the replacement for this gold evidence. Therefore,
one cannot say that the project targets a pure open-domain question answering prob-
lem, as strongly supervised data is used to train the reader component.
Moreover, the information retriever selects the documents as an evidence for the
question based purely on the matching between the semantic and lexical structures of
the question and the documents. It does not try to grasp the connection between the
question’s and document’s words based on their meaning. That technique’s perfor-
mance deteriorates when both question and potential passage treat about the same
thing but described differently (synonyms bear the same meaning, while they are
represented with different words).

4.2.1
Retriever pre-training
ORQA uses latent-variable-based methods for both retrieval and inference. In order to
properly train the reader component in the end-to-end setup, it needs to be provided
with decent evidence passages to obtain a meaningful learning signal.
The authors point out that training can be diﬀicult due to two reasons:
• Firstly, ORQA uses the open evidence corpus, resulting in the enormous search
space for the retriever (over 13 million evidence blocks).
• Secondly, latent-variable-based retriever can be a victim of spurious ambiguities
while looking for the evidence. That risk can be illustrated with the following
example (from [LCT19]): while looking for the passages fitting the question
”How many districts are in the state of Alabama?” (answer: seven), the re-
trieved evidence can be supportive - ”...Alabama is currently divided into seven
congressional districts, each represented by ...” - as well as deceiving - ”...Al-
abama is one of seven states that levy a tax on food at the same rate as other
goods..”. Both pieces of evidence give a foundation for formulating the correct
answer to the question - they treat Alabama and have the word ”seven” in it.
However, the latter does not target the context of the question.
ORQA targets these diﬀiculties by pre-training the retriever component using the
Inverse-Cloze Task. All the evidence blocks need to be encoded for that; hence
they are pre-encoded before the end-to-end fine-tuning, which targets the first issue.
The second one is addressed by the ICT mechanism, which discourages the retriever
from marking the spurious ambiguities as supportive evidence.
4.2.2

The authors wanted to train the retriever component on a task resembling the pro-
cedure of looking for the evidence given the input sentence. Given such a task, it is
crucial to grasp the meaning behind that sentence and find the passage that matches

Given the passage with some words taken out, the goal of the Cloze Procedure is to
predict the words, which upon filling the gap, match the context and meaning of the
pre-processed text. In other words - to predict missing words given the context. The
Inverse Cloze Task (ICT), as the name suggests, has an inverse goal - the prediction of
the context given the input sentence. It treats the input sentence as a pseudo-question
and its context as pseudo-evidence. Given the batch of pseudo-evidence candidates,
the task is to select a single candidate which is most relevant for the pseudo-question.
The prediction score is computed using the following formula:

4.3.4
Training
The training for both pre-training and the fine-tuning aims to maximize the log-
likelihood logp(y|x) of choosing the correct output y. However, the summation over
all of the documents from corpus in formula 4.8 is approximated by the summation
over the top k documents with the highest p(z|x).
To choose these top k documents valid, the authors describe that for the pre-
training process, they create a search index over the pre-computed document embed-
dings and use the Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS) algorithms to get the top
k documents with the highest relevance score (equation 4.11).
This approach yields one risk, which was addressed in REALM - during the train-
ing, the parameters of the retriever change, hence the pre-computed indexed embed-
dings are not up-to-date with the model’s state. To make the MIPS algorithm return
valid results, the index is asynchronously regenerated with the recomputed embed-
dings multiple times throughout the training process. In their tests, the authors re-
freshed the index once per 500 training steps and achieved significantly better results
compared to the run without the index update [Guu+20]. However, the refreshing of
the index required the usage of 16 Google TPUs.

Prohibiting trivial retrievals
In the case of using the same corpus for pre-training
(X) and fine-tuning (Z), the masked sentences can also occur in Z, which might lead
the retriever to look for the exact string matching. To avoid it, it is crucial to prevent
such situations by either discarding such sentences or using different corpora for both
tasks.
Salient span masking
The prediction of the masked tokens should be based on
the given documents containing the world knowledge. However, the authors mention
that for dates and named entities (e.g. ”United States of America”), the context
might not be necessary for making the accurate masked token prediction. Hence such
silent spans are detected and masked whole.
Null document
On top of the retrieved documents formulating a knowledge source
for the mask prediction, a null document entry is added. If there are some examples
where the world knowledge is unnecessary despite silent span masking, that additional
document ”gets the credit” for making the mask prediction possible.
4.4
ColBERT
Multiple projects targeting QA or Information Retrieval tasks have the exact gen-
eral mechanism: each query and context pair is passed through a deep, pre-trained
Language Model, such as BERT. Due to its considerable size, the computational cost
increases by orders of magnitude over previously used approaches [KZ20]. The au-
thors of ColBERT: Eﬀicient and Effective Passage Search via Contextualized Late
Interaction over Bert aimed to create an architecture still utilizing the capabilities
of BERT model but reducing the computation weight of the workflow using it. They
created a ranking model based on contextualized late interaction over BERT, Col-
BERT.
4.4.1
General overview
ColBERT is a model allowing to rank the documents in terms of how well they match
the input query, which can be used for:
• re-ranking the set of documents retrieved by, e.g. another model
• end-to-end document retrieval
It uses a single BERT model for encoding the documents and queries into the bags
of fixed-length embeddings, where each one of them is contextualized based on the
other terms surrounding it.

4.4.3
Documents re-ranking
ColBERT’s document re-ranking is intended to be used for ranking a relatively small
set k << |N| of documents from corpus N given an input query q. Firstly, each
document is encoded into a two-dimensional embedding matrix, where one dimension
corresponds to the number of tokens in the document and another one to the length
of the longest embedding of the encoded document’s tokens. This can be done before
conducting a re-ranking procedure, so the matrix is simply loaded from the disk
during the procedure itself. All documents are then stacked into a three-dimensional
matrix D.
Given the input query encoded into the bag of embeddings Eq, ColBERT calcu-
lates the dot-product of Eq and D. Its output is further reduced
• across the document embeddings via the MaxSim computations (cosine similar-
ity)
• across the query embeddings via summation
The final result consists of two vectors of length k: one containing the document
IDs and another one the scores.
4.4.4
End-to-end document retrieval
In the context of QA, ColBERT’s end-to-end document retrieval capability makes
it a potential candidate for the previously mentioned retriever component in the
architecture around the retrieve-predict approach.
In the pre-processing stage, all the documents are encoded into the embeddings.
Next to it, a mapping between each embedding and the ID of the corresponding
document is created. All embeddings are then indexed into the Faiss index, allowing
the fast vector-similarity search between the input and all documents’ embeddings.
The end-to-end retrieval is split into two major steps:
1. Firstly, per each embedding from the input bag, a vector-similarity query is
issued to Faiss index to retrieve k′ matching document embeddings. Each of
these outputs is mapped using the earlier mentioned mapping, resulting in the
2-dimensional output matrix.
It describes the list of IDs of the documents
having some embeddings highly similar to the input query embeddings. These
lists are further filtered to remove the duplicates and forwarded to the second
stage mechanism.

2. The second stage is the previously mentioned re-ranking mechanism, having the
output of the first stage as an input.
This stage structure grasps the quintessence of the late interaction between the
queries and the document. Thanks to the first stage, the MaxSim operator does not
need to be applied to every embedding of every document from the corpus. This can
come particularly useful in a scenario of a considerable amount of documents’ data -
utilizing the effectiveness and speed of the fast vector-similarity structure has a huge
impact over the single query processing latency.

Popular ODQA datasets
When it comes to the Question Answering problem, the choice of used datasets needs
to fit the domain, whether it is a closed or open one. For closed-domain QA, the
data is required to include a mapping between the pair of question and answer and
the passage(s) containing the evidence supporting the answers. For the open-domain
QA, this requirement is removed. It should be the responsibility of the developed
model to choose from the vast dataset the subgroup of documents or passages which
are most probably helpful in inferring the answer instead. That way, the developed
system can be applied to massive data sources, such as Wikipedia, and resembles
real-life question answering scenarios. However, it is relatively easy to convert the
closed-domain QA dataset into the open-domain QA one by simply removing the
mapping between the questions answers pairs and the passages containing the answer
(as they did it in ORQA, for example).
The datasets generally used for the question-answering problem include:
• SQuAD [Raj+16], a dataset commonly used as a benchmark for the reading
comprehension task. Questions were created by human annotators, who, given
the passages - paragraphs extracted from Wikipedia articles, created the match-
ing questions. Each question has a corresponding passage, and each answer is
the span present in the passage.
• Natural Questions [Kwi+19] containing the questions from real-life Google
searches, mapped to the Wikipedia spans containing the answers.
• WebQuestions [Ber+13], a dataset created for answering the questions using
the Freebase database as the knowledge source, with the questions being selected
by using Google Suggest API.
• CuratedTREC [BŠ15] consisting of question-answer from the TREC question-
answering set. The set contains real queries from multiple sources and have been
curated by Baudis and Sedivý.
• TriviaQA [Jos+17], another reading comprehension dataset, which consists
of question-answer pairs authored by the trivia enthusiasts, matched with, on
average, six corresponding evidence paragraphs. However, the authors claim the

7.3.1
Baseline 1: IR-ES
The first baseline solution for this thesis is an own implementation of the MedQA’s
IR-ES method.
Firstly, an Elasticsearch index is constructed using the textbooks split into chunks
(referred further as documents). Next, per each question q with four answer options
ai, queries in format ”ai q” are generated as passed to the search engine, which
returns the top 10 documents with corresponding scores (Okapi BM25 ranking) with
respect to which they are sorted. Next, each query gets assigned a sum of all retrieved
documents’ scores and the one with the highest value is chosen as the final answer.
Because Elasticsearch is not a tunable module and the way it returns the outputs
is deterministic, to speed up the future computations utilizing it, assigning the ten
most relevant documents to each question has been conducted beforehand and saved
in JSON files used for future usage.

7.4.1
Solution 1: REALM-like retriever with BERT reader
7.4.1.1
Idea
The idea follows the Baseline Solution 2, replacing the sparse retriever with one
based on the dense representations generated by the pre-trained BERT model. This
approach has been explored in [Kar+20], [Guu+20], [LCT19] and more. It revolves
around using the BERT model to firstly encode the whole document corpus as the
pre-processing step. Next, during the QA task performance, the retriever encodes the
question and compares it with the previously created document encodings. Retrieving
top k documents is picking documents having the embedding vector representations
most similar to the one of the question query. Training such model focuses on altering
the way BERT encodes the questions to make their generated embedding vectors more
similar to those corresponding to relevant context documents.
7.4.1.2
Approach iterations
The first iteration of this implementation has used just a pre-trained BaseBERT model
as the embedder. In mentioned papers, the authors usually trained such component
on its own before applying it to the downstream task, but the goal was to check
whether, without these extra processes, it could be still a viable option. Unfortunately,
after several experiments with the architecture, manual verification of the retrieved
documents, and analysis of the training outputs, the conclusion was that it could not
return the documents that seemed relevant to the question query.
Henceforth, the next idea was to follow the architecture and use the checkpoints for
the embedder of one of the reviewed projects. The initial choice was DPR [Kar+20] as
it yielded solid results and did not require any additional computationally heavy pre-
training procedures. However, the authors mention that to obtain better results. the
embedder can be trained on its own using a mapping between input question query
and corresponding a) golden passage containing the context suﬀicient for inferring
an answer b) set of negative passages which do not contain any valid context. The
used MedQA dataset did not offer such mapping, and the only idea that emerged
throughout the development phase was to use some off-the-shelf solution to identify
these passages (e.g. Elasticsearch). While this seemed to be a decent approach for
selecting negative ones (e.g. documents with the lowest BM25 score), it did not seem
viable for choosing the gold contexts. Though DPR authors used BM25 ranking to
choose the gold passage (document from top 100 retrieval results which contains the
answer) for dataset without the mapping, this approach seemed to not be applicable
for this thesis’ problem, as it revolves around training using just one gold passage -
and it is not expected that it would be suﬀicient enough for backing up any answer
option in the given domain. Hence, the usage of DPR was discarded.
REALM workflow fit the picture of this project more, as the authors conducted the
training of the dual-component solution end to end without needing any additional
mappings.

7.4.1.3
Pre-processing
As mentioned before, the first step is to create the index of the encoded documents
used by the retriever. To achieve it, the documents are tokenized and passed through
the REALM-like-embedder (BERT-based module constructed in a fashion resembling
the original REALM’s one) to create the whole document encodings. These encod-
ings are then indexed using Faiss, allowing to conduct the eﬀicient Maximum Inner
Product Search to retrieve the document with the representations most similar to the
one of the input query. The used Faiss index has been implemented as the Flat index,
which copies the added data without further encoding or organization. Upon receiv-
ing a search request with the query encoding, it compares it with the documents’
encodings using the inner product operation.
It is important to note that throughout the whole training process, the index
remains unchanged. It means that the adjusted trained embedder does not re-generate
the document representations, and as time passes, they become stale compared to
the encodings of the questions. This is the critical difference between this approach
and the one presented in REALM, where the authors re-encode the whole corpus
multiple time throughout the training. Unfortunately, it requires a great amount of
computational resources and time [Guu+20], posing too great of a challenge for this
thesis.
7.4.1.4
Retriever
The available online 4 REALM’s tensorflow model has been converted to a pytorch
version implemented according to the original architecture (referred to as REALM
embedder).
Given a question q with four answer options ai, the query of format
”ai [special token] q” (where [special token] is one of the BERT’s unused tokens)
is tokenized using the same tokenizer as in the pre-processing and passed to the
embedder. Then, using Faiss, a MIPS is conducted to retrieve IDs and scores of the
top 5 documents from the index, used for fetching the contents of the corresponding
documents. Next, the concatenated documents are passed together with the input

7.4.2
Solution 2: ColBERT end-to-end
The second solution focused purely on using the retriever model implemented to
resemble the way the ColBERT functions.
7.4.2.1
Document index creation
Similarly, as in Solution 1, the first step is to create the index of vector representations
of the documents. However, ColBERT focuses not on encoding the whole documents
into single vectors. Instead, each document is represented as a set of embeddings
corresponding to all its token. While for x documents of length y the index would
be populated by x embedding vectors in REALM approach, in ColBERT this number
would be equal to x ∗y.
The documents’ embeddings’ vector representations are furtherly stacked on top of
each other, forming a two-dimensional embedding knwoledge_matrix. That matrix
is indexed using Faiss and saved in the CPU memory, together with a mapping from
each embedding to its origin document. Both of these components are later used
during the retrieval.
The used Faiss index has been implemented as the IVFPQ index, which partitions
the embedding space into 1000 cells based on k-means clustering and then assigns each
document embedding to its nearest cell based on the cosine similarity. There was an
attempt of using the Flat version of the index instead, potentially improving the
quality of the obtained results, but unfortunately, it required too much GPU memory
considering the assets occupied by the BERT embedder and scores calculations during
the training.
==PAGE:67==
7.4 Main solutions

7.4.2.2
Context retrieval
Per each question, four option [unused token] question” queries qi are tokenized and
passed through the ColBERT embedder in the same fashion as described in [KZ20].
Next, the retriever follows the workflow presented in the paper: first, it chooses the
subset of documents using Faiss and then re-ranks them to get the five highest scored
documents.
Per each qi, the matrix of size (number of tokens, embedding size) consisting of
the embeddings corresponding to the input’s tokens (query matrix) is flattened and
issued to Faiss index. For every input token embedding, it returns top k (k = 1000
in final implementation) IDs of the documents which representations contain one or
more embeddings highly similar to it, as the outputs are ordered based on the cosine
similarity score between the query and documents’ token embeddings.
A list li of (number of query tokens∗k) document IDs is obtained per each query
qi. The lists are filtered from duplicates and, for optimization purposes, have their
length narrowed to either the length of the shortest one (mini<4li) or to a 1000. These
IDs are next used with the mentioned earlier mapping to gather the corresponding
documents’ representations from the knowledge matrix and stacked, forming the 3-
dimensional matrices (documents matrices).
7.4.2.3
Score calculation
Per each query, the documents’ scores are calculated by:
• computing the dot products of query matrix and corresponding documents ma-
trix
• followed by the computation of the score of each document using the maximum
cosine similarity equation (equation 4.20)
The mean over the highest five scores per query represents its final score. All
four scores are gathered together, passed through the softmax function, and used to
calculate the cross-entropy loss. The main idea behind this approach is as follows: per
each question-option, the retriever returns top k documents with scores measuring
the resemblance between the query and the documents. That means that the higher
the score, the more similarities between the pair and the higher the chance of the
corresponding option to be the correct answer.
7.4.2.4
Training information and results

• tuning the number of epochs, batch size and learning rate
• running own ColBERT module based on either BaseBERT or BioClinicalBERT

Poor first iteration results
Both developed solutions have been utilising the BERT models checkpoints from the
referenced papers. Underwhelming and slightly unexpected was the outcome of using

1. For both solutions, the initially created document representations are not strongly
contextualised.
2. The fact that the ColBERT-based solution performed slightly better off-the-
shelf can mean that for the given multiple-choice question-answering problem,
its score-based answer-choosing approach is better than the used BertForMulti-
pleChoice reader component without extra initialisation techniques.

The former could be further investigated by the manual inspection of the retrieved
documents, while the latter by creating a solution based on the ColBERT retriever

The authors of REALM address the scenario of poor initial embeddings. As a
countermeasure, they warm-start the retriever by conducting an ICT task over the

with the Inverse Cloze Task over the MedQA corpus would yield better off-the-shelf
results than the ColBERT solution.
Stale document indices
Both developed solutions rely on the usage of the Faiss index with the pre-computed
document encodings. Throughout the training, the parameters of the embedders used
on input questions are being adjusted, resulting in the altered embedding results of the
same queries in the later stages of the training. In the meantime, the indices remain
the same. An alternative approach would be to follow the REALM’s asynchronous
MIPS refreshes, but the hardware requirements for that task surpass the potential
of the available resources. However, it is worth noting that the authors conducted
experiments showing that the solution’s performance with the index refreshes yields
considerably better results than without it.
Consequence of corpus filtering
Subsection 7.1.1.1 presented the filtering of the corpus, due to which a considerable
number of sentences has been removed. Unfortunately, throughout the experiments,
some retrieved documents were still contaminated by the poor parsing consequences.
Hence the dataset cannot be denoted as clean. Though most of the removed sequences
were short and did not contain valuable text, the removal of the type B sentences - and
their existence in the first place - definitely subtracts the amount of knowledge that
the text representations have compared to the original books’ contents. Moreover,
it could make the sentences around the deleted sequence lose some contextual value.
Chunks created over the concatenation of initially not adjacent sentences could have
the meaning behind them polluted, making them poor as the retrieval candidates
compared to the pre-edited version.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The main focus of this thesis was to create the medical-domain ODQA system based
on the usage of BERT pre-trained models. In order to achieve it, it was crucial to
understand its underlying mechanism - namely, the reliance purely on the attention
mechanism.
Moreover, several state-of-the-art solutions have been discussed and
analyzed to give both a proper understanding of the topic and the inspiration for the
final model’s architecture. Next to the two baseline solutions relying on the sparse
index representation for document retrieval, two solutions have been implemented:
a retriever-based end to end framework utilizing the ColBERT workflow and the
retriever-reader architecture based on the REALM neural knowledge retriever.
Their performance has been evaluated on answering the questions of the USMLE
multiple-choice exam - one taken by the candidates aspiring to become professional
medical physicists.
It proved to be a great challenge due to the diversity of the
questions, the complexity of the medical descriptions they consist of, and the need
for multiple different text evidence that only combined can become suﬀicient enough
context for supporting the correct answer. The final models are the results of thorough
experiments and several attempts of applying different strategies which relied on the
power of the pre-trained BERT models.
The obtained results proved to be an interesting output for the analysis of the
usage of such complex architectures in the domain-specific variant of the ODQA.
They underline the importance of additional pre-training that has been applied to
the referenced models in order to be capable of eﬀicient evidence retrieval for the
more complex and highly detailed topics such as the one tackled in this thesis.

Future work
9.1.1
ColBERT-QA and creation of own training data
Some of the actions that could be taken to obtain better results with the developed
solutions have been already mentioned in the previous chapter. However, in the late
stage of the thesis development, an interesting article about the usage of ColBERT for
ODQA has been discovered, namely Relevance-guided Supervision for OpenQA with
ColBERT [KPZ20]. The authors follow the architecture consisting of the ColBERT
retriever with the BERT reader on top. An exciting addition that they present is
how they train both their components, putting a strong emphasis on the retriever.
==PAGE:74==
62
9 Conclusion
That approach revolves around the weak supervision strategy that iteratively uses
ColBERT to create its own training data. More specifically, the system’s bootstrap is
the pre-trained ColBERT, which is later used for creating a training set for the next
training session. It is achieved following the retrieve-and-filter heuristic: to obtain
the gold and negative passages, a query is issued to ColBERT to retrieve top k highest
scored documents. Then, they are used to mark top t ones that explicitly contain the
answer as gold passages, while the remaining ones that do not contain it are marked
as the negative passages. That way, the authors created the training set, which can be
used for enhancing the already pre-trained ColBERT model. Moreover, they proved
that this model improvement could be performed multiple times, as they report that
the second-iteration model has achieved even better results than the first-iteration
one.
This approach addresses the issue mentioned in the previous chapter, namely the
problem of not having the mapping between the questions and the passages useful for
inferring the answer. It would be interesting to check whether applying this type of
ColBERT-supervision on itself would yield more potent results. Moreover, having the
possibility of creating the mapping between the questions and the relevant passages
would make the usage of the DPR solution viable for this thesis. Unfortunately, the
paper has been discovered in a late stage of the project, and the time constraints did
not allow to pursue its strategy.
9.1.2
Usage of generative model for creating the answer
Even though the main focus of the thesis was on the dense index representations
for document retrieval, the ODQA solutions also benefit from the quality implemen-
tations of the answer-inferring systems.
This project has covered only the reader
component, but another popular approach that has not been explored is the usage
of the generator models. Instead of finding the answer in the obtained context pas-
sage, the generator - as the name suggests - generates the answer from scratch. The
authors of Leveraging Passage Retrieval with Generative Models for Open Domain
Question Answering present the architecture utilizing the DPR retriever together with
the generative model based on the sequence-to-sequence network. They mention that
it performs well when combining the evidence from multiple passages, which sounds
like an exciting asset for this thesis’s scope. Moreover, they achieve superb results on
the Natural Questions and TriviaQA datasets, beating both REALM and DPR by a
considerable margin.
