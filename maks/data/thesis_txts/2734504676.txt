Abstract
This thesis presents the development and validation of a Computational Fluid Dynamics
(CFD) model designed to simulate the atomization process in a pressure-swirl atomizer,
focusing on sheet formation, droplet breakup, and the final droplet size distribution. Uti-
lizing the StarCCM+ software, the study aimed to enhance existing models’ accuracy and
computational eﬀiciency through several strategic innovations. The primary contributions
of this thesis include the adoption of Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR) to reduce initial
cell count and computational demands, allowing for focused computational resources on
regions of interest such as the vicinity of the atomizer orifice and the evolving free surface.
A significant methodological shift was implementing the Lagrangian multiphase model,
facilitating detailed observation and analysis of droplet size distributions and spray char-
acteristics. The developed CFD models underwent rigorous validation against experimen-
tal data, demonstrating high degrees of accuracy in predicting various parameters of the
atomization process. Models 1 and 2 showed minimal error margins, indicating reliable
predictive capabilities, while Model 3 highlighted areas for further refinement, particu-
larly regarding the sheet angle and Sauter Mean Diameter. The thesis concludes with
suggestions for future research, emphasizing the exploration of alternative atomizer con-
figurations, investigating surface tension effects, incorporating environmental conditions
to more accurately replicate spray drying processes, and optimising the computational
domain. This work not only contributes to the existing body of knowledge on atomization
within pressure-swirl atomizers but also lays the groundwork for future studies to enhance
the eﬀiciency and applicability of CFD modeling in this domain.

Introduction
Pressure-swirl atomizers, widely utilized in food, pharmaceutical, automotive, and aerospace
industries, are crucial in atomizing liquids into fine sprays, essential for applications like
eﬀicient combustion, spray drying, fire suppression, and agricultural spraying. These at-
omizers are particularly valued for their ability to produce a uniform and controlled droplet
size, which is critical for ensuring consistent application in these fields.
The core mechanism of a pressure swirl atomizer involves imparting a high-speed rotational
motion to the fluid, typically through a specially designed swirl chamber. Combined with
the inherent fluid dynamics, this rotational motion results in the fluid exiting the nozzle in
an annular shape. The intricate interplay of centrifugal, aerodynamic, and surface tension
forces at this stage is responsible for forming the characteristic hollow cone spray pattern.
According to Laurila et al.[1] ,this pattern is highly eﬀicient in achieving fine atomization
of liquids, a feature that makes these atomizers particularly suitable for the applications
above.
During the atomization process, the liquid undergoes a complex transformation. Initially,
it forms into a thin sheet, elongating into ligaments due to instabilities and aerodynamic
forces.
These ligaments eventually break down into smaller droplets.
This process is
influenced by various factors, including fluid properties, nozzle design, and operating con-
ditions, making it a rich study area for optimizing atomizer performance.
In spray drying, particularly in the food industry, the uniformity and size of the droplets
are paramount. The droplets rapidly dry, forming a crust on their surface while the interior
remains moist. This differential drying leads to the formation of particles with specific
morphologies, which, as studied by Okada et al.[2], are crucial in determining the quality
and characteristics of the final powdered product. The control over droplet formation and
size distribution is a key factor in ensuring the quality of products ranging from powdered
milk to pharmaceuticals.
However, the complexity and variability of the atomization process pose significant chal-
lenges in predicting and controlling atomizer performance. As mentioned by Sopha, [3]
traditional theoretical approaches often fall short of accurately predicting outcomes, es-
pecially in droplet size distribution. This limitation necessitates extensive experimental
investigations, which, while insightful, are often costly and time-consuming. Advanced
experimental techniques, like laser diffraction and high-speed imaging, capture detailed
spray characteristics. However, these methods require sophisticated setups and are not
always feasible for routine application.
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has emerged as a powerful tool in studying and
optimising pressure-swirl atomizers. By simulating the fluid dynamics within the atomizer,
CFD allows for a detailed understanding of the flow patterns and atomization mechanisms.
This understanding is crucial for designing more eﬀicient atomisers and better suited for
specific applications.
However, CFD simulations also face their own set of challenges.
Accurately capturing the interface between the liquid and air and modelling the different
flow regimes (like laminar or turbulent flows) that occur within the atomizer requires
sophisticated modelling techniques and considerable computational resources.
Significant advancements in CFD techniques for pressure-swirl atomizers have been seen
in recent years. These improvements primarily centre around enhancing computational

eﬀiciency while maintaining, or even increasing, the accuracy of simulations.
Key ad-
vancements include Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR), flow initialization techniques, and
the transition from Eulerian to Lagrangian frameworks for particle modelling.
AMR is a crucial development in CFD simulations of atomization processes. This tech-
nique dynamically adjusts the resolution of the computational mesh based on the evolving
flow characteristics, focusing computational resources on areas with high gradients or com-
plex flow features. According to Yu et al.[4] in the context of pressure-swirl atomizers,
AMR is particularly beneficial for accurately capturing the interface between liquid and air
without necessitating a uniformly fine mesh throughout the entire computational domain.
By increasing the number of cells only in regions of significant interaction between liquid
and air, AMR considerably reduces the total number of computational cells required. This
targeted approach significantly reduces computational load and time, making simulations
more eﬀicient while preserving accuracy in critical areas of the flow.
Flow Initialization Methods have also advanced, contributing significantly to the eﬀiciency
of CFD simulations. In the study of pressure-swirl atomizers, initializing the flow field to
approximate the expected swirl and air core formation at the onset of the simulation can
significantly expedite the process of reaching a steady state. This initialization mimics the
physical conditions within the atomizer more closely from the start, reducing the compu-
tational time required for the flow to develop naturally from arbitrary initial conditions.
This approach not only speeds up the simulation process but can also provide more stable
and realistic initial conditions for analysing atomization dynamics.
Lastly, the Transition from Eulerian to Lagrangian Frameworks for particle tracking marks
a significant advancement in modelling the atomization process, as proven by Zhou[5]. In
traditional Eulerian approaches, the fluid is treated as a continuous phase, which can
be computationally intensive, especially in regions with fine droplets or ligaments. The
computational burden is substantially reduced by transitioning to a Lagrangian framework,
where droplets or blobs are treated as discrete particles. In this approach, AMR does not
need to refine the mesh in regions dominated by Lagrangian particles, further enhancing
computational eﬀiciency. This method captures the behaviour of individual droplets more
accurately, providing deeper insights into the atomization process, including droplet size
distribution and spray characteristics.
1.1
Objectives
The overarching goal of this thesis is to enhance and extend a CFD model previously de-
veloped[3]. This enhancement will incorporate AMR, flow initialization methods, and the
transition from Eulerian to Lagrangian frameworks for particle tracking. These improve-
ments aim to refine the accuracy and eﬀiciency of simulations concerning the atomization
process in pressure-swirl atomizers. Upon developing the model, the thesis will analyse
the atomization characteristics produced by the Delavan SDX atomizer with SB-30 swirl
chamber under varying operational conditions. Specifically, the study will examine the
cone angle, pressure drops, droplet distributions, and Sauter mean diameter of the spray
for three sets of fluid properties: densities, viscosities, and flow rates. This comprehensive
analysis will provide deeper insights into the behavior and performance of pressure-swirl
atomizers under various operational scenarios. To ensure the validity and reliability of the
simulation results, this study will compare the computational findings with experimental
data sourced from the paper ”Drop size spectra in sprays from pressure-swirl atomizers” by
Tratning [6]. This comparison aims to validate the enhanced CFD model against empirical
observations, offering a robust test of its predictive capabilities.

Theoretical Background and
Industrial Context
This chapter highlights the fundamental aspects and eﬀiciencies of spray drying processes,
focusing on the atomization of droplets and their impact on spray drying performance.
2.1
Technical Aspects of Spray Dryers
Within the expansive landscape of drying technologies, spray drying emerges as a standout
method, particularly favored for its adeptness in converting aqueous and organic-aqueous
solutions or suspensions into powders. Central to this process is the atomization of the
liquid feed into a chamber flushed with hot, circulating gas. This pivotal step ensures the
immediate drying of material, as the atomized droplets present a vast surface area to the
drying medium, facilitating unparalleled heat and mass transfer rates.
The intrinsic value of spray drying extends beyond its immediate eﬀiciency. For industries
such as pharmaceuticals and food, where the thermal sensitivity of products can dictate the
viability of a processing method, the almost instantaneous drying process of spray drying
safeguards the integrity and quality of the final product. This is a critical consideration
when handling bioactive materials or flavor compounds prone to degradation or loss of
potency when exposed to prolonged heat exposure.
Further underscoring its industrial appeal, the continuous operation mode of spray dry-
ing aligns with the demands for high throughput and consistent product quality. When
juxtaposed with batch processing methods, the continuous nature of spray drying not
only enhances production eﬀiciency but also significantly reduces operational costs. This
economic advantage, coupled with the speed of production, positions spray drying as an
optimal choice for drying operations across many sectors.
The adaptability of spray drying technology allows for meticulous control over the process
parameters. Variables such as inlet air temperature, feed flow rate, and atomizer speed
can be fine-tuned, offering precise manipulation over the physical characteristics of the
resulting powder. This control is instrumental in achieving desired particle size, moisture
content, and morphological properties, which are paramount for the functionality and
stability of the final product.
The evolution of spray drying technology has ushered in new applications beyond con-
ventional drying. Microencapsulation, a notable advancement, utilizes spray drying to
encapsulate particles or droplets within a protective coating. This process is invaluable
for enhancing active ingredients’ stability, controlled release, and bioavailability, particu-
larly in the pharmaceutical industry. The ability to microencapsulate allows for innovative
drug delivery systems and the stabilization of volatile compounds, marking a significant
leap in product formulation and development.
Moreover, the progression of spray drying techniques has facilitated the production of pow-
ders with bespoke technological attributes. Through strategic formulation and processing
adjustments, it is possible to engineer powders for specific applications, such as soluble

2.1.1
Spray Drying Process
The process of spray driying begins with preparing the starting material, which may
involve dissolving, emulsifying or dispersing the main substance in a solvent or solution of
support material. Once prepared, this mixture is ready to be atomised, a critical step for
forming fine droplets. A picture showing the operating principle of a conventional spray
dryer is shown below.
Figure 2.1: Stages of the spray drying process [10]
Droplet formation occurs through a dual fluid nozzle (indicated by the numbers 1 and 2
in the picture), where the starting material is forced through the nozzle under pressure to
create an atomisation. Dual fluidisation refers to the combination of the liquid material
with a gas, usually air, which helps to produce smaller, more uniform droplets.
The formed droplets then enter the drying chamber (3), where they meet with a hot gas
stream, as indicated by the heater (4).
The air, typically pre-heated to temperatures
of up to 250°C, rapidly removes the solvent through evaporation. This conductive heat
exchange between the hot gas and the sample droplets leads to the almost instantaneous
drying of the particles, reducing them to a dry solid form.

As the particles dry, they lose mass and, consequently, undergo a reduction in their rate of
descent. This allows them to be transported by the gas flow towards the collection system.
A fraction of the particles are collected at two possible points (5), while the majority are
guided towards the cyclone (6). The cyclone uses centrifugal force to separate the solid
particles from the gas. Due to their inertia, the particles are pushed towards the cyclone

After the cyclone, the gas still loaded with fine particles passes through a filter (7), where
the finest particles are collected. This ensures that the product is collected more completely
and protects the operator and the environment from any emissions. Finally, the desiccant
gas, now free of particles, is expelled from the system through the exhaust fan (8), thus
completing the drying cycle.
The design and optimisation of the spray dryer must consider numerous factors to ensure
effective separation of particles and minimise material loss. Operating parameters, such
as nozzle size, atomisation pressure, gas temperature and feed flow, must be carefully
calibrated to maximise process eﬀiciency and finished product quality.
2.1.2
Pressure-Swirl Atomizer
The pressure-swirl atomizer, commonly referred to as a simplex atomizer, plays a pivotal
role in spray drying applications. This device operates by imparting a swirling motion to
the liquid as it passes through specially designed nozzles, termed swirl ports. The process
involves several key stages:
1. Swirl Induction: The liquid accelerates upon entering the swirl ports, leading to
a central swirl chamber. Here, centrifugal forces act upon the fluid, causing it to
spread into a conical sheet while simultaneously forming a hollow air core.
2. Film Formation: The liquid emerges from the atomizer orifice as a thin, unstable
sheet. The initial film thickness at the nozzle is denoted by δo.
3. Sheet Breakup: The thinning liquid sheet undergoes breakup, transitioning into
ligaments and subsequently into droplets. The initial thickness of the film as it exits
the nozzle is represented by 2h0, and the thickness at the breakup point is denoted
by 2hb.
4. Atomization: This final stage results in the formation of fine droplets characteristic
of the spray drying process.

2.2.1
Surface Tension and Energy in Liquid Breakup
The disintegration of liquid structures, such as drops, jets, or films, is primarily facilitated
by amplifying wave oscillations at the liquid-gas interface. Mandato et al.[12] found that
these oscillations, under certain conditions, can grow suﬀiciently to cause the breakup of
the liquid structure or promote the detachment of liquid portions. The oscillations are
driven by kinetic energy from the liquid jet, a gaseous flow interacting with it, or by
mechanical energy supplied through vibrating or moving mechanisms, which fosters an
atomization regime.
This process is governed by a balance between capillary forces, attributed to surface ten-
sion, and inertial forces, resulting from the relative velocity between the liquid and gas
phases. Viscosity acts as a moderating force, dampening the instabilities of the liquid
structure, thus delaying its disintegration and affecting the size of the resultant droplets.
The key parameters in this process include surface tension (σ), the density (ρ) of the
medium, and its viscosity. Given its critical role in the disintegration of liquid structures,
we delve into the concept of surface tension, offering an operational definition supple-
mented by an illustrative diagram.

Conceptualizing Surface Tension
Surface tension arises from the mutual attraction forces among molecules, known as cap-
illary forces. A molecule located away from the interface experiences isotropic forces from
surrounding molecules, resulting in a net force of zero. In contrast, molecules within the
liquid predominantly attract a molecule at the interface, leading to a net force directed
into the liquid. This phenomenon encourages molecules to ”sink” back into the liquid,
effectively reducing the interface area, likening the surface to an elastic membrane under
tension. This tension, however, is not proportional to the surface area, elucidating the
unique characteristics of surface tension.
Formally, surface tension is defined as the force per unit length exerted in the plane tangent
to the liquid-gas interface, acting on an arbitrary segment of this surface. An experimental
setup involving a rectangular, threadlike structure with a sliding side [2.4] can demonstrate
the effect of surface tension and facilitate its measurement.

teracting this retraction with a stabilizing force, it is observed that the force required is
linearly proportional to the length of the sliding side, with the proportionality constant
equated to twice the surface tension, considering the forces acting on both faces of the
film. At equilibrium, the force F is represented by:

Secondary Breakup
The interaction between a droplet and the surrounding air leads to uneven pressure dis-

interaction and its effect on droplet stability and breakage can be quantitatively described
by the Weber number (We), the ratio of aerodynamic forces to the surface tension force.

mary mechanisms of liquid droplet rupture in a gaseous environment have been identified,
which are summarized as follows:

1. Vibrational Breakup (Weg < 12): Characterized by the droplet oscillating due
to the external flow, leading to its division into a small number of large fragments.
This mechanism is noted for its significantly longer break-up time.

5. Wave Fracture (Catastrophic Breakup) (Weg ≥350): High Weber numbers
induce small surface waves, leading to catastrophic break-up through a multi-stage
fragmentation process.

3.1
Governing Equations
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is fundamentally based on solving the Navier-
Stokes equations derived from three classical conservation laws of physics: conservation of
mass, conservation of momentum, and conservation of energy. These equations provide a
comprehensive mathematical framework for predicting fluid flow behaviour under various
conditions and interactions. CFD plays a crucial role in understanding and optimising
the underlying mechanisms in the context of atomization processes, which involve the
disintegration of a liquid into fine droplets within a gaseous medium. The complexity of
atomization, characterized by phase changes, surface tension effects, and intricate fluid
dynamics, necessitates using advanced numerical modeling techniques and solvers. The
following sections present the main models and solvers utilized in this project, laying the
groundwork for CFD simulations that specifically address the challenges and phenomena
associated with atomization. This introduction underscores the importance of a robust
theoretical foundation in physics and mathematics for conducting meaningful and accurate
CFD simulations to study atomization processes.

Methodology
This chapter aims to delineate the array of techniques deployed within the simulation
framework to enhance computational eﬀiciency. The focus is on adopting strategies that
not only preserve but potentially augment the accuracy of the simulations. A systematic
exploration aims to illuminate the methodologies that have significantly contributed to
optimizing computational resources without compromising the precision of the outcomes.
4.1
Adaptive Mesh Refinement
The atomization process within pressure swirl atomizers epitomizes a complex multi-scale
phenomenon, ranging from macroscopic swirling flow patterns to the formation of diminu-
tive ligaments and subsequent microscopic droplets. Capturing the entirety of this range
poses a substantial challenge in computational modeling. Utilizing a traditional static
mesh approach to resolve the intricate dynamics of droplet formation would necessitate a
mesh of such fineness that it would render even the most rudimentary simulations com-
putationally infeasible. By its nature, the static mesh lacks the flexibility to adapt to
the localized requirements of mesh density, leading to excessive computational demands
without corresponding gains in accuracy where it is most needed.
Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR) technology presents a solution to this limitation. By
employing a dynamic adjustment of mesh granularity in response to the evolving require-
ments of the atomization process, AMR allows for an increase in resolution specifically in
areas experiencing complex breakups. This increase in mesh density is critical in capturing
the nuances of ligament and droplet formation. Conversely, the mesh can remain coarser in
regions characterized by smoother flow dynamics or where the liquid sheet remains largely
intact. This methodological refinement ensures that computational resources are allocated
eﬀiciently, enhancing the fidelity of the simulation in critical areas while conserving com-
putational power in less dynamic regions. The application of AMR thus facilitates a more
precise exploration of the atomization phenomena, yielding insights into the physics of
fluid breakup without the prohibitive computational cost associated with a uniformly fine
mesh.
Various mesh refinement techniques have been devised in computational fluid dynamics
to address localized phenomena emergent at arbitrary time points during the simulation.
Each technique has merits and is particularly adept at resolving certain computational
challenges. The choice of an appropriate mesh refinement strategy is contingent upon the
nature of the problem under consideration.
p-Refinement Method
In the p-refinement approach, the mesh size and the number of nodes are maintained
constant. Enhancement in solution precision is achieved by elevating the polynomial order
of the finite element basis functions in regions where higher error is detected. Initially
proposed by Basu[15] for continuous solutions in single-fluid flows, Baker[16] found that
its utility is somewhat circumscribed for discontinuous phenomena due to the propensity
for oscillations in high-order schemes. Despite these challenges, the p-method remains
extensively utilized within the finite element method framework.
r-Refinement Method
The r-refinement technique preserves the order of the polynomial and the node count
within the mesh, instead opting to reposition existing nodes towards regions where refine-
ment is necessitated to attain desired accuracy. This technique has demonstrated eﬀicacy
24

==PAGE:37==
in simulations of non-viscous compressible flows over unstructured grids. However, the
resulting non-orthogonality of the mesh can lead to inaccuracies in the treatment of vis-
cous terms. Dynamic mesh deformation exemplifies the r-method’s capacity to adapt to
evolving temperature and velocity fields.
h-Refinement Method
The h-refinement method stands as the preeminent approach within the scientific commu-
nity. It is characterized by a fixed polynomial order while permitting temporal variation
in mesh granularity and nodal density. Nodes are inserted in regions of significant error
to enhance solution accuracy and are correspondingly removed from zones deemed exces-
sively refined. The h-method commences with an initial structured grid and identifies the
domain requiring refinement. The refinement process involves subdividing each selected
cell into four smaller cells, thereby introducing new nodes at the midpoint of the original
cell edges, an operation indicative of mesh enrichment. This technique invariably transi-
tions a structured mesh into an unstructured one, facilitating enhanced solution fidelity
in localized regions of the computational domain. The reader is referred to Berger[17] to
better understand the method.

4.2.1
Velocity Initialization
Velocity initialization is crucial due to the substantial rotational component inherent in
the flow within a pressure swirl atomizer, vital for the ensuing atomization process. This
initial swirl in the velocity field is pivotal for several reasons. First, it mirrors the actual
physical conditions within the atomizer, where liquid is tangentially introduced to create
swirling motion. Implementing a pre-defined swirl profile as the initial condition ensures
the simulation starts in a state that closely mimics the atomizer’s real-life fluid behavior.
Furthermore, commencing with a velocity field that embodies the swirling flow’s primary
characteristics significantly reduces the time to reach a computational steady state. Ab-
sence of an initial swirl would necessitate additional computational effort for the solver to
naturally develop this flow pattern, thereby prolonging the simulation time. This initial
swirl stabilises the numerical solution from the outset, preventing unrealistic flow patterns
and numerical inaccuracies that could emerge from non-physical starting conditions.
4.2.2
Phase distribution
Phase distribution at the onset of the simulation is equally critical. Typically, simulating
the atomizer with air at the center and liquid at the edges aligns with the atomizer’s
physical design, which often features an air core surrounded by a liquid film. This setup
facilitates capturing the correct flow dynamics right from the beginning and expedites the
interaction between the liquid and air phases, essential for atomization. This immediate
proximity of the two phases allows the simulation to quickly engage with the interfacial
dynamics critical for droplet formation, ensuring a smoother transition to the atomization
process.
From the start, a clearly defined phase distribution also maintains numerical consistency,
particularly at the interface between the liquid and air. This approach minimizes potential
convergence problems tied to the intricate interactions between the phases, safeguarding
the simulation’s integrity.
The synergy between accurately initialized velocity and phase distributions is indispens-
able for guiding the simulation towards a steady state that conserves computational re-
sources and enhances the results’ reliability. Such precision in initial conditions enables
the model to more faithfully replicate the physical atomization processes, providing a solid
foundation for subsequent design and optimization endeavors of pressure swirl atomizers.
This meticulous approach to flow initialization underscores its importance in accurately
and eﬀiciently simulating spray atomization phenomena, ensuring that the computational
model is a robust tool for exploring and enhancing atomizer performance.

During the primary and secondary breakup stages of atomization, the fluid disintegrates

necessitates a highly refined mesh in their vicinity. However, as the number of droplets
escalates, so does the computational demand due to the increased number of refined cells.
This escalation can counteract the inherent advantage of AMR, which is to save com-
putational time while maintaining accuracy by refining the mesh only where it’s needed

Process of Transition
The transition from an Eulerian to a Lagrangian framework is employed to address this
challenge. In the Eulerian approach, the fluid’s behavior is resolved on a fixed grid, which
is ideal for capturing the fluid dynamics up to droplet formation. However, once droplets
form and interact more individually than part of the continuous phase, tracking them as
discrete entities in a Lagrangian framework becomes more eﬀicient. Once the transition

capture the detailed physics of the droplets. Consequently, AMR can coarsen the grid in
these regions, drastically reducing the number of cells and, therefore, the computational
demand.
This dynamic adjustment ensures that computational resources are focused
eﬀiciently on areas of the flow where the Eulerian framework is necessary for capturing
the fluid dynamics leading up to the droplet formation.

Simulation Setup
This chapter provides an overview of the development and parameters of a Computational
Fluid Dynamics (CFD) model for the Delavan SDX atomizer, highlighting the creation of
its geometry and computational domain tailored for simulating atomization processes. It
also covers the optimization of transition criteria from Eulerian to Lagrangian frameworks
to enhance simulation accuracy and facilitate detailed analysis of droplet distribution.
5.1
Geometry
The geometry creation for the Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) model was con-
ducted by Sopha as part of his thesis [3]. Due to the absence of technical drawings for
the Delavan nozzle, sourced from an external supplier, Sopha resorted to manual mea-
surements of each component utilizing a digital caliper. A 3D laser scanning technique
was employed to complement these measurements, enabling the generation of Computer-
Aided Design (CAD) files for each part. These CAD files were instrumental in accurately
replicating the intricate internal structures of the nozzle within the StarCCM+ software.
The Delavan SDX atomizer, characterized by its modular pressure-swirl design, allows for
various swirl chambers and orifice disks combinations. This adaptability facilitates the
achievement of specific atomization and mass flow rates, with the remainder of the nozzle
components remaining unchanged. Sopha’s thesis provides a comprehensive overview of
this process, including a depiction of the key elements involved in atomization: the end
plate, swirl chamber, and orifice disk.
Figure 5.1: Sketch of the Delavan SDX atomizer.[6]
Continuing the work of Sopha, the unique aspect of the Delavan pressure-swirl atomizer
involves its swirl chamber, which is distinguished by a single inlet slit allowing for the feed
to enter. This design strategically utilizes an end plate to block upward flow, directing the
feed exclusively through the orifice disk. Sopha’s numerical analysis evaluated two distinct
orifice disks: one with an opening diameter of 1 mm and another with a reduced diameter
of 0.762 mm. This examination was underpinned by a comprehensive set of measurements
for the swirl chamber, as documented by Tratnig, with Sopha’s consistent findings.
The swirl chamber design is notably asymmetric around the central axis, contributing to
the complexity of the simulation. Each orifice disk is characterized by a uniform thickness
of 3.16 mm and an inlet curvature radius of 1.85 mm. The choice of orifice disk was pivotal
in the simulations, with the first disk being employed for single-phase simulations and the
second, smaller-opening disk being utilized for multiphase simulations, a topic elaborated
upon in a subsequent chapter.

tegration following atomization, deliberately omitting any analysis of processes occurring
upstream or downstream. The simulations utilize geometrical dimensions based on the
comprehensive specifications of the Delavan SDX atomizer, as detailed in the preceding
section.

• Inlet: Liquid is introduced into the domain at its uppermost point through a cylin-

• Swirl Chamber: The liquid descends into a chamber exhibiting a helical design,
intended to induce swirling motion. This swirling is presumed to facilitate the at-

5.3
Physics
5.3.1
Models
The models employed and their roles within the simulation framework are shown below:
• Three-Dimensional: The asymmetric design of the swirl chamber necessitates a
three-dimensional simulation. Initial trials with a 2D-axisymmetric model proved
inadequate, highlighting the importance of a 3D approach to accurately model the
fluid dynamics.
• Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS): Serving as the foundation for tur-
bulence modeling, the RANS equations provide a time-averaged solution to the
Navier-Stokes equations, offering a computationally viable method to simulate tur-
bulent flows.
• SST (Menter) K-Omega Turbulence Model: This model is utilized for turbu-
lence closure, effectively blending the k-ω model in the near-wall region with the k-ϵ
model in the far field. It is particularly suited for atomization simulations, where
accurate turbulence representation is critical.
• All y+ Wall Treatment: Targeting a low y+ value to optimize near-wall flow
dynamics, this approach ensures the viscous sublayer is properly resolved, critical
for accurate turbulence modeling near walls.
• Implicit Unsteady Simulation: Acknowledging the transient nature of atomiza-
tion, this model captures time-dependent flow behaviors, essential for simulating the
dynamic atomization process.
• Adaptive Time-Stepping: Implemented to maintain numerical stability and ef-
ficiency, adaptive time-stepping dynamically adjusts time step size, targeting a
Courant number of 0.8 without defining an explicit time step size.
• Lagrangian Multiphase Model: Tracks individual particles or droplets in a La-
grangian framework, enabling detailed analysis of droplet dynamics and distribution
within the atomization process.
• Adaptive Mesh and Solution Interpolation: Enhances simulation accuracy
through dynamic mesh adjustment for improved resolution and facilitates consistent
solution transfer between meshes of varying resolutions.
• Segregated Fluid Isothermal Model: Simplifies thermal analysis by assuming
isothermal flow, focusing on fluid dynamics aspects of atomization.
• Cell Quality Remediation and Wall Distance: These functionalities improve
mesh quality and calculate the proximity of each cell to the nearest wall, vital for

• Turbulent Gradients, Multiphase Flows, and Volume of Fluid (VOF): Ad-

5.4
Initial and boundary conditions
5.4.1
Initial Conditions
In pressure-swirl atomizers, the initial volume fraction condition plays a crucial role in
predicting the behavior of different phases within the computational domain. This con-
dition sets the stage for developing flow patterns and is critical in reaching a realistic
steady-state solution. The computational domain is initialized with two distinct phases
represented by red and blue colours. The red region indicates the liquid phase, the pri-
mary medium filling the domain, while the blue region represents the air phase. Initially,
the swirl chamber and orifice area is filled with the liquid phase (red), which indicates the
liquid’s expected dominant presence at steady state. An exception to this homogenous
condition is the center line, deliberately initialized with the air phase (blue). This specific
initialization anticipates the formation of an air core along the orifice and the center of
the swirl chamber.

5.5
Mesh Setup
The mesh configuration is a pivotal element in accurately capturing the physical phe-
nomena of atomization simulations. A coarse mesh is employed in regions removed from
the swirl chamber due to the less complex flow characteristics and the reduced need for
computational detail in these areas. This mesh grading strategy enhances computational
eﬀiciency by avoiding unnecessary refinement in regions where detailed physics is not
crucial.

==PAGE:53==
Figure 5.5: Mesh structure of the CFD model
It is standard practice to utilise a Trimmed cell mesh, recognized for its capability to
manage complex geometries, and augmenting it with prism layers and a surface remesher.
Prism layers are instrumental in resolving boundary layer flows precisely, a critical factor
in atomization processes. The surface remesher ensures mesh quality on curved surfaces,
thus enhancing simulation accuracy.
Simulations typically commence with a base mesh size of 15.0 mm, larger than standard
practices, to take advantage of Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR). AMR permits targeted
refinement in areas where free surface dynamics are critical, such as below the orifice. This
strategy reduces the initial cell count, significantly accelerating the simulation’s early
stages compared to methods that do not utilize AMR.
A volumetric refinement of 10% of the base size is executed at the center of the orifice and
the swirl chamber. This refinement is necessary to capture the evolution of the air core,
which is affected by the fluid’s swirling movement.

Conclusions
The objective of this thesis was the development of a functional Computational Fluid Dy-
namics (CFD) model within the StarCCM+ software environment. This model aimed to
predict the atomization process in a pressure swirl atomizer, focusing specifically on sheet
formation. An essential goal was to enhance the accuracy and computational eﬀiciency
compared to a predecessor model.
An initial strategy to mitigate computational demands involved exploring an axisymmet-
ric model. However, this approach was promptly discontinued due to the swirl cham-
ber’s asymmetry in the Delavan SDX atomizer, underscoring the necessity for a three-
dimensional modeling approach.
The foundation of the new model retained much of the physics from the previous iteration.
A significant innovation was the introduction of Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR), which
permitted starting the simulation with a significantly reduced cell count. The implemen-
tation of AMR, alongside the strategic assignment of initial swirl conditions, facilitated
the model’s progression to a steady state within a reasonable timeframe. This approach
was pivotal in enabling the detailed observation of the atomization phenomenon.
A key advancement in the model was the incorporation of the Lagrangian multiphase
model and the transition from Eulerian to Lagrangian phases. This allowed for the ob-
servation of sheet breakup into ligaments and droplets, as well as the capture of the final
droplet size distribution. The Lagrangian approach, coupled with AMR, ensured that the
model remained computationally manageable, even in droplet regions, thereby avoiding
overloading due to the active AMR algorithm.
Validation of the developed CFD models yielded commendable results, although there
remains scope for refinement. Models 1 and 2 exhibited high accuracy, with minimal error
margins across all evaluated parameters, indicating strong alignment with experimental
data. These models prove to be reliable tools for predicting the behavior of pressure-
swirl atomizers under analogous conditions. Conversely, Model 3 displayed considerable
error rates, especially concerning the Sheet Angle α and Sauter Mean Diameter. This
variance from experimental observations suggests areas for improvement, potentially in
the underlying assumptions, or the simulation methodologies for these specific metrics.
In conclusion, this thesis represents a substantive stride towards optimizing CFD modeling
of atomization processes in pressure swirl atomizers. Through strategic enhancements in
computational techniques and the integration of advanced modeling approaches, it lays a
foundation for further investigation and refinement in the field.

Suggestions for further research
Building upon the current thesis focused on developing a Computational Fluid Dynamics
model for atomization in pressure-swirl atomizers, this chapter outlines key areas for future
research. These suggestions aim to expand the study’s scope, exploring various atomizer
configurations, the role of surface tension, the effect of environmental conditions, and
computational domain optimizations.
Future research to improve the model can be directed towards several promising areas, as
outlined below:
• Exploration of Alternative Atomizer Configurations:
Investigating other
configurations of Delavan atomizers, such as the SD, SC, and SF models, to un-
derstand the influence of design variations on atomization dynamics and spray char-
acteristics.
• Investigation of Surface Tension Effects: Given the discrepancies observed in
Model 3, particularly with sheet angle and Sauter Mean Diameter, a detailed study
on the impact of surface tension on sheet formation could lead to significant insights
and model improvements.
• Incorporation of Environmental Conditions: Simulating the atomization pro-
cess in a hot air stream to more accurately replicate the conditions of the spray
drying process, focusing on the effects of temperature on droplet evaporation and
final product characteristics.
• Optimization of the Computational Domain: Streamlining the computational
domain by excluding regions that minimally impact the atomization process, such
as the area above the swirl chamber, to enhance computational eﬀiciency without
sacrificing the accuracy of the simulation.
