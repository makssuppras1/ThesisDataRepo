Abstract
Cytometry often uses ﬂuorophores to accurately extract quantitative information about
biological cell samples. Due to overlapping emission spectra, only a limited number of
diﬀerent ﬂourophores may be used in analyses. ChemoMetec investigates whether deep
neural networks may extract information from optical imaging that is otherwise obtained
from ﬂuorescence imaging. This would allow researchers to search for additional biomark-
ers simultaneously. The optical imaging may be performed in an unlimited number of
conﬁgurations leading to diﬀerent visualizations of the cell.
The goal of the thesis is to investigate methods for selecting the most useful visualizations,
i.e. image channels, for classiﬁcation tasks. The classiﬁcation task considered in the
project consists of determining whether cells are healthy, apoptotic, or dead using a real
dataset containing ten optical imaging channels. Standard feature selection methods,
such as forward selection, are not feasible as they require training way too many deep
neural networks.
The thesis proposes two channel occlusion methods that manipulates channels in the
input data for a trained network to measure the performance impact that is used to
determine the importance of channels.
A teacher-student [14] method and two deep
subspace clustering (DSC) [8, 23] methods have been modiﬁed to be used for channel
selection in our dataset. The methods are compared with two baseline methods based
on information entropy and correlation measure, respectively. The methods are ﬁnally
evaluated by measuring the test accuracy on residual networks (ResNet) trained on the
resulting channel subsets from the methods.
It was shown that almost all methods performed better than randomly selected subsets.
The loss-based DSC method has excellent performance and its best subset containing four
channels match the best possible subset found by testing all subset combinations with
four or less channels. However, both DSC methods have a weakness when determining
their subsets with one channel, as these subsets perform below the random reference.
The best channel subset is found to contain four channels and achieves a test accuracy
of 97.36%.

Chapter 1
Introduction
ChemoMetec is a company that specializes in the development and production of au-
tomated cell counters and advanced image cytometers. The purpose of these devices is
to extract statistically meaningful and quantitative information regarding biological cells
while being easy to use. By pressing a single button, image acquisition, image analy-
sis, feature extraction, and data presentation may be performed. Cytometry often uses
ﬂuorophores for quantifying DNA and RNA using nucleic acid probes, intracellular ions
using indicator dyes, and antigen molecules using antibodies.
However, a ﬂuorophore is associated with an emission spectrum which sets a limit on
the number of diﬀerent ﬂuorophores used in imaging of biological samples. Methods for
replacing a ﬂuorophore without losing the corresponding information are of great interest,
as it provides researchers with the possibility to search for additional biomarkers in the
sample simultaneously. Three or four ﬂuorophores are commonly used at the same time
without having a too large overlap of their respective emission spectra.
Central to this thesis, ﬂuorescence imaging may be used to determining whether cells
within the biological sample are healthy, undergoing apoptosis, or dead.
To free up
ﬂuorophores, ChemoMetec has lately turned to the use of deep neural networks, that
may be able to determine the healthiness of cells solely from optical imaging.
1.1
Problem Statement
By varying the physical parameters for the optical imaging, a number of diﬀerent image
visualizations of the same cell sample may be generated. These visualizations will be
referred to as channels. Determining which channels that provide useful unique infor-

mation is a nontrivial problem in combination with deep neural networks due to their
long training time. Common feature selection techniques, such as forward selection, are
unsuited for this application as they require training a large number of networks, that
is way too time consuming. Therefore, we will consider methods for selecting channel
subsets in this thesis. More speciﬁcally, ChemoMetec has provided a dataset with more
than 182,000 single-cell images with 10 channels, where the underlying task is to classify
whether the cells are healthy, undergoing apoptosis, or dead. The ground truth1 for the
cell classes is available from ﬂuorescence imaging, but the included 10 channels are all
from non-ﬂuorescence optical imaging. As a baseline, ChemoMetec has trained a neural
network with a test accuracy of 95.69% using a subset of ﬁve channels chosen by visual
inspection.
One should note the following important simpliﬁcations of the problem. The acquisi-
tion time of the channels are most certainly not equal for all channels as some channels
may have very long acquisition times while some may be free for all practical purposes.
This inﬂuence which channel subsets that are considered as the best. However, we con-
sider this to be out of scope for this thesis, and we determine superior channel subsets
solely based on the resulting test accuracies.
There may be diﬀerences between the
individual accuracies for the classes, but we will only consider the overall accuracy.
1.2
Project Aims
From the problem statement described above, the following project aims may be deﬁned:
1. Implement deep learning algorithms to solve the classiﬁcation problem
consisting of whether cells are healthy, undergoing apoptosis, or dead in
the provided dataset.
2. Investigate channel subset selection methods for the provided dataset
and classiﬁcation problem combination.
3. Find suitable channel subsets for the provided dataset and compare with
original reference subset from ChemoMetec.

Chapter 2
Background: Dataset
In this chapter, background information is presented w.r.t.
the dataset provided by
ChemoMetec. It explains brieﬂy which biological cells that are present in the dataset
and how they have been treated in order to obtain the dataset consisting of healthy,
apoptotic, and dead cells. The imaging acquisition modes, that generates the diﬀerent
channels will also be described without going too much into depth as optical physics is
considered to be out of scope for this thesis. Next, the image preprocessing performed
prior to obtaining the dataset is described followed by the training, validation, and test
dataset splits.
2.1
Cell Samples
The cell samples consist of cells belonging to the Jurkat A3 cell line. It is a line of human
T lymphocytes, that has been biological immortalized meaning that the cells no longer
die from biological aging. They can still die from other means such as injury, disease, or
lack of resources.
The cells require nutrition in order to survive and not otherwise undergo apoptosis,
which is a form of programmed cell death. In the dataset, some of the samples have
been deliberately starved while others have been treated with camptothecin (CPT) that
damages the DNA. Both treatments lead the cells to apoptosis. Therefore, the dataset
consists of a mixture of healthy, apoptotic, and dead Jurkat cells, which constitute the
classes for the classiﬁcation task.

2.2
Channels
The simplest of all optical microscopy illumination techniques is brightﬁeld microscopy.
The samples are observed from above while being illuminated with white light from below.
Diﬀerence in attenuation of the light in the samples is responsible for contrast in the
imaging. Many of the acquisition modes present in our dataset are based on the brightﬁeld
acquisition mode. In the following, the ten imaging modes constructing the channels in
the dataset are listed and brieﬂy explained. Note that the index preceding the channel
names represents the associated ID number, which later are used when referencing the
channels without name.
0. Aperture is brightﬁeld imaging with a limiting circular aperture of ﬁxed diameter
in the imaging path.
1. ApodizedAP is brightﬁeld imaging with a limiting aperture in the imaging path.
In contrast to the Aperture channel, the aperture has an apodized transmission
proﬁle meaning that the transmission decreases continuously with increased radial
distance from the optical axis following a gaussian function.
2. BrightField is brightﬁeld imaging with the full numerical aperture of the imaging
system.
3. DarkField is imaging where direct light from the background is blocked. Only
refracted and scattered light at small angles are allowed through to the optical
axis. This includes the light refracted through the cells.
4. DFIOpen takes two images with small focus positions oﬀsets on each side of the
ideal focus position. A combined diﬀerential image is constructed by subtracting
the two images from each other. The cell contrast is usually higher on either side
of the ideal focus position, so the diﬀerential image enhances the cell contrast while
removing out of focus objects.
5. DFIPhase is identical to DFIOpen, but with a pupil phase plate ﬁlter in the
imaging path.
6. DPI takes two images with a pupil phase plate ﬁlter and oﬀsets on each side of the
optical axis. A diﬀerential image is constructed by subtracting the two images.
7. iSSC forms the image from scattered light from the sample illuminated at a large
angle.
8. Phase is brightﬁeld imaging with a pupil phase plate ﬁlter in the imaging path.
9. UVPhase is brightﬁeld imaging with a combination of an apodized aperture ﬁlter
and a pupil phase plate ﬁlter in the imaging path.

2.3 Preprocessing
7
All the imaging modes use UV light with a wavelength of 365 nm, except iSSC that uses
blue light with a wavelength of either 405 nm or 488 nm. Figure 2.1 shows how these
image acquisition modes visualize the same spatial section of a biological cell sample. We
refer to the outcome of the modes as channels.
2.3
Preprocessing
Once image acquisition has been performed for all modes on all cell samples, ﬁeld of view
(FOV) images of resolution 1920×1440 are obtained. Before running a cell segmentation
algorithm, ChemoMetec normalizes each channel to have mean equal to zero and standard
deviation equal to one. A cropped image with size 64 × 64 is then extracted for each
segmented cell in the FOV images, which results in a total of around 182,000 single-cell
images. To address the problem that cropped images may contain cells other than the
intentional cell, all pixel values that do not belong to the crop-speciﬁc cell are set to zero.
This is illustrated in Figure 2.2.
2.4
Classes
The ’healthiness’ of a cell is discretized to one of the three following classes:
0. Healthy
1. Apoptosis
2. Dead
The ground truth of the cell classes is determined by biologists outlining appropriate gates
(regions) for indicative protein concentrations quantiﬁed via ﬂuorophores.
This gives
three somewhat distinguishable populations in the multidimensional space of indicative
protein concentrations, allowing each cell to be assigned to its proper class. The gating
is performed such that cells, whose ground truth classes are uncertain, are excluded from
the dataset. Examples of the diﬀerent classes are shown in Figure 2.3, where 28 random
cells from each class are shown in the Phase channel.
The class distributions in the training, validation, and test dataset may be seen in Table
2.1. It is veriﬁed that the class distributions across the dataset splits are similar which
is necessary in order for the classiﬁcation accuracies to be properly compared.

2.5.1
Re-splitting Training and Validation Datasets
The reshuﬄe of the training and validation datasets is performed in a way that ensures
that the two sets have the same class distribution. For the new training dataset, 90% of
the samples from each class in the original validation and training datasets are extracted.
The remaining 10% in the original datasets constitute the new validation dataset. This
method ensures a fair size ratio between the splits, but more importantly, that the class
distributions across the splits are equal. The percentage, 90%, is chosen such that the
training set constitute around 70% of the total data, see Table 2.1.

3.1
Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
Artiﬁcial neural networks (ANNs) are computing systems utilized by a family of machine
learning methods referred to as deep learning. ANNs are inspired by the mammalian
brain neural structure and consists of interconnected units also called neurons or nodes.
The ANNs consist of a computation graph propagating the input signal through the layers
of units to obtain the output signal. In deep learning, the adjective ’deep’ refers to the
use of many layers in the ANN. The output of the ANN is designed to ﬁt the application.
For instance, the output may be class probability predictions in a classiﬁcation problem,
or a continuous value prediction in a regression problem.
The simplest type of ANN is a fully connected neural network (FCNN). The FCNN
consists of fully connected layers in where each unit in one layer is connected with each

The learning rate η determines the magnitude of the weight update in each iteration.
It is a hyperparameter that often needs careful tuning to achieve fast convergence. If

3.1.3.2
Stochastic Gradient Descent
In Equation (3.3), it was implicitly assumed that the partial derivative was calculated
using the entire data set. This is often not feasible due to insuﬃcient computer memory.
An extensively used way to deal with this issue is to estimate the gradient of the loss
function using only a random subset of training examples, referred to as a mini batch or
just a batch. Because the loss function changes if we include or exclude some training
examples, the resulting gradient estimate is not equal to the true gradient using the entire
data set, which is why the name contains stochastic. However, the obtained gradient is
still an unbiased estimate of the true gradient. This method is called mini batch stochastic
gradient descent which is often just referred to as stochastic gradient descent (SGD) as in
this thesis. SGD has some beneﬁts compared to the standard gradient descent including
a better ability to escape local minima. Neural networks have a lot of local minima in
its loss function which potentially do not exists in the approximated loss function using
only a batch of training examples. How well the approximated loss function and gradient
estimate match the true loss function and gradient depends on the size of the batch. A
batch of size 128 statistically provides a better estimate than a batch of size 16.

3.2
Convolutional Neural Networks
A convolutional neural network (CNN) is a group of deep neural networks based on
architectures with shared weights. They were originally described in LeCun et al. [13] in
1998 but was not popular until the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge
was won in 2012 with a CNN referred to as AlexNet.
One of the major ideas in CNNs is local receptive ﬁelds. Instead of connecting every
unit in one layer to every unit in the next layer as in the fully connected neural network
(FCNN) architecture, only ’nearby’ units are connected. This means that the activation
at a speciﬁc unit does only inﬂuence a small number of units in the next layer. The
convolution operation which is performed in convolutional layers is the center of CNNs
and implements the idea of receptive ﬁelds and weight sharing that is another key aspect
of CNNs.
The convolutional layer is explained in the next subsection followed by explanations
of pooling and batch normalization layers, that are commonly used in combination with
convolutional layers in CNNs.
3.2.1
Convolutional Layers
Depending on the data structure, convolutional layers may perform one, two, or three-
dimensional kernel convolution. The dimensions are determined by the number of in-
dependent spatial directions.
In an image, we can go up/down and left/right corre-
sponding to a two-dimensional input. The data in this thesis consists of images where
two-dimensional convolutional layers are used. Therefore, convolution will in our case
refer to two-dimensional convolution.
The convolutional layer moves a kernel across its input image in all spatial directions
to produce the output image.
This is illustrated in Figure 3.3 where the kernel is a
matrix of size 3 × 3. For each element in the output image, an image patch of similar
size to the kernel is extracted from the input image. The output value is obtained by
summing up the element-wise multiplications between the image patch and the kernel.
The next output element one step to the right is calculated the same way, but with the
image patch extracted from a position moved one step to the right. This also applies for
upwards/downwards direction such that the kernel can be thought of as a window sliding
over the image. For kernel sizes larger than the trivial 1×1 kernel, the output image will
be smaller than the input image as the entire kernel must ﬁt within the input image. A
padding method may be used to manipulate the output image size. For instance, zeros
may be appended to the borders of the output image to yield and output image with the
same size as the input image.
The stride of the convolution operation determines the distance that the window
slides for each element in the output map. In Figure 3.3, a stride equal to one is used
with no padding. If a stride equal to two is used, the resulting output is half the size of
the input image, thus reducing the spatial resolution.
In the paragraphs above, it has been assumed that the input image only has a single
channel, and that the convolutional layer outputs an image with only a single channel.

images. In CNNs, convolutional layers are stacked such that one convolutional layer’s
output is used as another convolutional layer’s input. As in FCNN, an activation function
is usually applied after each convolutional layer. A strength of CNNs is that they detect
patterns independently on the location of the pattern in the input image as the same
kernels are used everywhere on the input feature maps. This is known as translational
invariance.

3.2.2
Pooling Layers
A pooling layer can be considered as an information simpliﬁer to the output of a convo-
lutional layer which serves two purposes. First, it shrinks the feature map size such that
the number of computations in the subsequent layers is reduced. Second, it provides a
regularizing eﬀect by adding additional translational invariance to the architecture. As
opposed to a normal convolutional layer, it does not have weights and performs another
operation instead of the dot product operation. The operation is often the average or max
operation referred to as an average pooling and max pooling layer respectively, where
the latter is the most commonly used. A pooling layer works on each input channel
independently, so the number of output feature maps is always equal to the number of
input feature maps. Otherwise, the pooling layer functions like a normal convolutional
layer as it has a pooling size (equivalent to kernel size), a stride, and a padding option.
The stride is very often above one for pooling layers such that the output has reduced
spatial size.
A special type of pooling layer is a global pooling layer, which takes the average or
maximum across the entire feature map and thus reduces a feature map to a single value.
This may be thought of as a pooling layer with a pool size equal to the input image
dimensions.
3.2.3

When stacking many layers in neural networks, changes made to the weights in layers

to as internal covariate shift and increases training time because it requires using a lower
learning rate and careful parameter initialization [7]. The problem may be addressed
by making normalization a part of the architecture in the form of batch normalization
layers.

tracting the mini-batch mean µB and dividing with the mini-batch standard deviation
σB. The optimal distribution for the subsequent layer may not necessarily be a cen-
tered around zero with unit variance, which is why the batch normalization layer has
the two learnable parameters β and γ that are the desired standard deviation and mean,
respectively, of the batch normalization output.

3.3
Residual Neural Networks
Adding many layers to a CNN, which is the meaning of ’deep’ in deep learning, facilitates
the learning of complex features. Theoretically, the performance of the network increases
as the depth of the network increases. Practically, it holds up to a certain point where
the performance saturates. The performance may even decrease as the depth increases,
as the networks become increasingly diﬃcult to train as the depth increases.
Residual neural networks (ResNets) addresses this problem by skipping one or multi-
ple convolutional layers using an identity shortcut connection. This allows the gradient to
ﬂow to early layers unobstructed and makes it possible to train networks with hundreds
of layers, while we in this thesis at maximum consider 50 layers.
The core building blocks in ResNets may be referred to as Residual Units [6]. There
are multiple variants of Residual Units where the most recent one is illustrated in Figure
3.4, which is also the unit used in this thesis. A ’unit’ in this context generally corresponds
to a collection of layers with one of more paths. The illustrated residual unit consist of two
paths where the ﬁrst passes the input directly through to the output only participating
in an element-wise addition with the result from the other path. This is the referred
to as an identify mapping, which makes training easier and improves generalization [6].
These units are stacked after each other such that the data ﬂow in the network splits
and merges many times. The term ’residual’ is explained by the fact that each unit adds
something to the existing information.
ResNets are usually constructed by a stem, which is a convolutional layer followed
by a max pooling layer, followed by the sequence of residual units that ends in a global
average pooling layer. This pooling layer is connected to a single fully connected layer.
For classiﬁcation as in this thesis, the last layer will have softmax activation, that en-
sures that the outputs sum to unity, which allows the outputs to be interpreted as class
probabilities.

Chapter 4
Related Work & Overall
Method
This chapter covers the related work that constitutes the basis for the methods used in
this thesis. It will simultaneously cover which methods that are considered throughout
this thesis and the motivation of choosing these speciﬁcally. Additionally, the chain of
thoughts for methods that we propose ourselves in this thesis are described.
In the context of common image classiﬁcation problems with datasets containing one
(grayscale) or three (color) channels and deep convolutional neural network classiﬁers,
channel selection is rarely relevant due to low number of channels.
However, in the
context of hyperspectral imaging, band selection methods are frequently used and may
relate closely to the problem of this thesis [1, 18]. Although they usually work with a
much larger number of bands, the concepts may still apply to our application with only
ten channels. Therefore, we mainly consider related work in the domain of hyperspectral
imaging. To avoid confusion, we will state again that the band and channel terms are
used interchangeably throughout this thesis.
To address the problem in this thesis, we will investigate multiple methods for select-
ing channel subsets for our classiﬁcation problem. It is desired to include some simple
methods, that are based on easily calculated measures which may function as a baseline
to our other methods. These are described in the next section below.
Because ChemoMetec is going to use a deep neural networks for solving classiﬁcation
problems like the one considered in this thesis, there is a large interest in methods that
may be able to ﬁnd the same complex signals as deep neural networks. Which methods
should be able to do this better than methods that indeed are based on deep neural
networks? It is reasonable to assume that whatever information the DNNs use, the DNN
based channel selection methods will be able to use that as well. Therefore, the remaining

methods are entirely DNN based.
4.1
Simple Baseline Methods
Bajcsy et al. [1] provides in 2004 methodology for selecting hyperspectral bands by com-
bining unsupervised band selection methods based on the information entropy, ﬁrst and
second spectral derivative, contrast, spectral ratio, correlation, and principal component
analysis ranking that are then evaluated using the linear regression, k-nearest neighbors,
and regression tree classiﬁers. Based on their evaluations, they recommend selecting the
top 4 to 8 bands using the information entropy measure and perform the classiﬁcation
using the supervised regression tree method. We will take note of their results and use
the entropy measure as one of our simple baseline methods. However, for the actual
classiﬁcation task we will use a CNN instead of the regression tree classiﬁer. This neural
network is the same network that we will use to evaluate all channel selection methods.
The correlation is another simple measure used by Bajcsy et al. [1]. Because the
bands in their case are ordered by increasing bandwidth, they calculate the correlation of
all adjacent bands. By ﬁrst choosing the band with the lowest adjacent band correlation,
the subsequent selected bands are chosen as the ones that have the lowest correlation
with already selected bands. This is an interesting simple metric that we will include as
the other baseline method. However, the method will be adjusted to be applicable to
channels without an associated bandwidth and therefore no ordering.
The entropy and correlation measures have the lowest error rate for a small number
of selected bands in Bajcsy et al. [1] and are easy to implement due to their simplicity.
When we later introduce more advance methods, these simple baseline methods prove to
be valuable in the evaluation of the more complex methods.
4.2
Subspace Clustering
Sun et al. [18] provide a comprehensive review of hyperspectral band selection methods.
They tested the following six methods: maximum variance principal component anal-
ysis (MVPCA), minimum misclassiﬁcation canonical analysis (MMCA), WaluDI, linear
prediction (LP), fast density peak-based clustering (FDPC), and the improved sparse
subspace clustering (ISSC) method. The last method, ISSC, outperforms the rest and
provides a higher average classiﬁcation accuracy on the selected bands in the two datasets
used than using all available bands. Therefore, the ISSC method by Sun et al. [19] and
generally the family of subspace clustering methods are of interest. The other methods
from this review are not considered in the rest of this thesis. The review mentions several
deep learning methods that they did not quantitatively evaluate, that we will describe in
the following paragraphs.
Zhan et al. [24] train a 1-D CNN on the full pixel spectrum, i.e. they isolate each
pixel and discard all spatial information. Then, band subsets based on a distance den-
sity metric are evaluated using the CNN without retraining to obtain the best subset.
The classiﬁcation accuracies of their proposed methods are only higher than the typical
==PAGE:37==
4.3 Input Sparsity Constraints
27
algorithms they compared against when selecting more than 30 bands. This is properly
not that useful for us, because we want to investigate eﬀective methods for selecting as
low as 2-5 bands. Additionally, it is expected that there is a lot of important information
in the spatial positioning of pixels in our classiﬁcation problem. Therefore, the method
does not seem beneﬁcial for our application when discarding this valuable information.
Tschannerl et al. [21] propose using autoencoders (AE) for unsupervised hyperspectral
band selection. As in [24] they represent each pixel as a 1-D vector consisting of its band
intensities. The purpose of AEs in this context is to reconstruct an input but forcing the
information ﬂow through a bottleneck of low dimension compared to the input dimension.
In combination with a sparsity constraint on the input weights, it forces the network to
learn a low dimensional latent representation of the input and use as few input bands as
possible to construct that representation. By using stacked AEs, their method matches
the performance of other state-of-the-art methods, but unfortunately not if a low number
of bands is chosen.
Not mentioned by the review by Sun et al. [18], Zeng et al. [23] propose an unsu-
pervised hyperspectral band selection method based on deep subspace clustering (DSC)
networks, that are originally proposed by Ji et al. [8]. A DSC network learns to recon-
struct an input like an autoencoder normally does, but it is forced to have the information
ﬂow through a sort of bottleneck referred to as a self-expression layer. In this layer, the
network must reconstruct the latent codes only from latent codes originating from other
samples in the dataset. This incorporates the so-called self-expressiveness property into
the network and leverages a similarity matrix that can be used to perform a clustering
analysis. Originally in Ji et al. [8], the inputs were facial images of a low number of
subjects where the DSC network successfully clustered images from the same subjects
together. In Zeng et al. [23], the inputs are instead the individual bands in a single hy-
perspectral image. In their experiments, the method outperforms other state of the art
methods including the ISSC method used by Sun et al. [18] mentioned earlier. This is of
great interest to us because it is a state-of-the-art method, that uses the same subspace
clustering assumption as the ISSC method but seems to be superior. Additionally, it is
deep learning based which makes it very likely that it is complex enough to be able to
potentially ﬁnd the relevant information, which may be hidden in the channels behind
a highly non-linear relation. In contrast to [21], the method is very well armed to take
potential spatial structures and patterns into account. Therefore, we choose to use the
DSC networks to represent subspace clustering methods.
4.3
Input Sparsity Constraints
Mirzaei et al. [14] present the teacher-student feature selection framework. A deep neural
network referred to as the teacher learns a sparse low-dimensional representation of the
data. It is learned by training the network for classiﬁcation, where the representations
are extracted from the last activations prior to the actual classiﬁcation layer.
By extracting the representations for the entire training dataset, they may be con-
sidered the target for a new, but much simpler, student network. The student network
==PAGE:38==
28
Related Work & Overall Method
has a sparsity constraint on the weight matrix for the ﬁrst layer and is thereby trained
to reconstruct the representations from the teacher network from a few important input
features. This has not been done for bands/channels, but we may transfer the concept
to create a student that selects channels in contrast to say pixels that were used in [14].
This method has the advantage, that regardless of which network architecture we want as
our ﬁnal classiﬁcation network to perform the actual classiﬁcation, we can use that exact
same architecture to construct the low-dimensional representation. This is a promising
method that will be investigated further in this thesis.
In the domain of hyperspectral face recognition, Taherkhani et al. [20] do, in contrast
to Mirzaei et al. [14], embed the feature selection into the classifying network itself.
All input bands are passed to the ﬁrst layer of a convolutional neural network (CNN)
which is regularized by a group Lasso algorithm to remove the use of redundant bands.
This is related to Farokhmanesh et al.
[2] that proposed an enhanced sparse group
lasso algorithm for deep feature selection. Mirzaei et al. [14] state that it is necessary
for feature selection to have a simple network in order for the error to be easily back-
propagated and input features selected eﬃciently. At the same time, a simple network
is likely not suﬃcient to construct proper representations. Therefore, only the teacher-
student method will be used to represent sparsity constraint methods, that addresses this
problem by making it a two-stage approach.
4.4
Explainable Artiﬁcial Intelligence
Explainable artiﬁcial intelligence (XAI) contains techniques that attempt to explain why
an algorithm made a decision and which features were important in making that decision
[12]. This is a quite diﬀerent topic than the above, but it may contain methods that can
leverage which channels that were important for a certain classiﬁcation decision.
Montavon et al. [15] provide an overview of layer-wise relevance propagation (LRP)
that allows a relevance measure to be propagated backwards through a neural network
to yield relative relevance measures of the inputs.
LRP has a ﬁne spatial node-wise
resolution of relevance on a trained network architecture. By training a network and ag-
gregating the relevance measure for each input nodes channel-wise, selecting the channels
with highest relevance may be possible. Even though this is an interesting approach, it
requires implementation for the speciﬁc architectures used and the pixel-wise resolution
seems wasted when it is nevertheless aggregated channel-wise. Therefore, simpler XAI
methods are considered in the following.
Smilkov et al. [17] propose a method for smoothing the gradient of the predicted
class probability w.r.t. to the inputs for identifying pixels that have a strong inﬂuence
on that speciﬁc class probability. The raw gradient is rather noisy, but they reduce this
noise by aggregating the gradients of 50 samples of the input image with intentionally
added noise. These gradients might be considered as a measure of relevance and thereby
constitute a simpler and lower quality XAI candidate than LRP. This method was initially
considered, but after some introductory experiments, a major problem was observed. If
the classiﬁcation network is very certain of a prediction due to some pattern, a pixel
==PAGE:39==
4.5 Performance Evaluation
29
value within that pattern can be altered a lot before the class probability changes. This
yields a relevance measure close to zero even though the pixel that is part of the pattern
is important for the prediction. It invalidates the current concept of using the raw pixel
gradients as relevance measures. Instead, it gave the idea that we need to alter many
pixels together before inspecting how it aﬀects the network.
Fong et al. [3] occlude the input images with random gray squares as a data aug-
mentation approach for a classiﬁcation network. This makes the network more robust
to changes. Zeiler et al. [22] systematically occlude parts of an image to reveal which
parts of the scene are important for classiﬁcation. While [3] is not directly related to
XAI, they were both inspiring sources for the channel occlusion method proposed in this
project, where all pixels in a channel are replaced while inspecting the change in network
performance. This is the only XAI method we consider.
4.5
Performance Evaluation
To assess which methods that perform well and which methods that perform bad, a
common method for evaluating the methods are needed.
Each method generates its
own channel subsets for N = {1, 2, . . . , 9} selected channels and we choose to evaluate
the methods based on the test classiﬁcation accuracy of a neural networks trained only
on these channel subsets. For instance, if a certain method chooses the Aperture and
ApodizedAP channels for N = 2, a network is trained to classify whether the cells belong
to the healthy, apoptosis, or dead class using only those two channels instead of all ten
channels. Then, after training the network on the training dataset, the samples in the
test dataset are classiﬁed to obtain the overall test accuracy. This is a measure that will
determine the performance of a given method for a given N.
The network architecture used for the classiﬁcation task is a residual network (ResNet),
which is a type of convolutional neural network (CNN). For the ChemoMetec’s baseline
solution to the classiﬁcation problem, they used the famous ResNet-50 architecture, which
is described in He et al. [5] in the year 2016. However, later the same year, He et al.
[6] proposed the so-called identity mappings, which improves to the ResNet architec-
ture. The new architecture is often referred to as ResNet v2 and is the architecture used
throughout this thesis. The speciﬁc network architecture, hyperparameters, and opti-
mizers used for training these performance networks are described in detail in Chapter
6.
To be able to know whether a certain test accuracy is good, reference values are
needed. Therefore, a network is trained for each subset combination using N = {1, 2, 3, 4}
channels. It provides estimates of the best possible subsets for N < 5. By calculating
the mean of the test accuracies across all combinations for a given N, a random subset
reference accuracy may be obtained. The best subset and the random subset reference
are referred to as the best reference (Ref-Best) and random reference (Ref-Random),
respectively. It is incredibly time and resource consuming to try all combinations which
is why we have limited ourselves to only consider N < 5, which still requires training a
total of 385 networks, see Appendix A.

6.1.2
Feature Map Resolution
Commonly in convolutional neural networks (CNN), the feature map resolution, which
we will refer to as the spatial resolution, decreases gradually as the input ﬂow through the
network. The feature maps in the later layers generally represents higher level features,
where spatial resolution is less important. Reducing the feature map resolution reduces
the number of computations and memory footprint required, while it additionally can
lead to better generalization performance. As seen in Table 6.1, we reduce the spatial
resolution from the size of the input 64 × 64 to a size of 16 × 16.
The ResNet-50 architecture used by ChemoMetec reduces the spatial resolution all
the way down to 2 × 2. This is rarely seen in designs of convolution neural networks
because the power of convolution is not utilized with 2 × 2 maps. Generally, 8 × 8 is
a good resolution for the last layers in the architecture, such that there still is some
localization of the higher level features. The ResNet-50 is designed for image with a size
of 224 × 224 so the 32x spatial reduction originally ends up at a spatial resolution of
7 × 7. The low spatial resolution is therefore due the dataset consisting of images with
an already low image size equal to 64 × 64.
In the design of our network architecture, we have been greatly inspired by the cre-
ators of the ResNet, He et al. [5], that has designed ResNet architectures for a dataset
==PAGE:50==
40

CIFAR-10, which is comparable in complexity to the dataset considered in this thesis.
For the CIFAR-10 dataset, they have a stem of the network (i.e. before the sequence of
residual units) that consists of a single convolutional layer with a kernel size of 3 × 3 and
a stride equal to 1. Combined with the absence of a max pooling layer this performs no
spatial reduction in the stem, which is a setting we have implemented in our architecture
as well. The CIFAR-10 dataset consists of images of size 32×32 and the feature map size
in the last convolutional layer is 8 × 8, so why do we choose to have 16 × 16 as our ﬁnal
resolution?
The objects analyzed in the CIFAR-10 dataset usually occupy the entire image. This
means that it is likely that every pixel in the spatially reduced feature map in the end
of the network is used giving a total of 64 (8 × 8) possible positions for a given high-
level feature. A few CIFAR-10 examples are shown in Figure 6.2. In our dataset, the
cells occupy a small section of the image centered around the center of the image, which
means that a lot of the pixels along the border of our feature maps are wasted. For
instance, if only half the pixels in each dimension of the ﬁnal feature maps are used, we
only have 16 (4 × 4) possible positions for a given high-level feature. This motivates that
we use a larger ﬁnal feature map size than 8×8 as our objects occupy as small part of the
input image. Therefore, we use a ﬁnal feature map size of 16 × 16 thereby increasing the
spatial resolution considerably compared to the baseline from ChemoMetec. It is seen in
an experiment later, that reducing the size to 8 × 8 does in fact hurt performance.
6.1.3
Number of Feature Maps
As opposed to the feature map size, the number of feature maps have been signiﬁcantly
reduced compared to the baseline. The baseline used a number of 2048 ﬁnal feature
maps, whereas we use 128 ﬁnal feature maps. This change is motivated by looking at the
ResNet architectures designed by He et al. [5]. The ResNet-50 originally uses 2048 ﬁnal
feature maps to distinguish a total of 1000 classes. Because we only have 3 classes in our
dataset, we should be able to perform well even with a large reduction in the number
of ﬁnal feature maps.
Theoretically if we do not consider the concept of overﬁtting,
the performance of the network should always increase if the number of feature maps is
increased. As a reference, He et al. [5] use 64 ﬁnal feature maps when distinguishing
the 10 classes in the CIFAR-10 dataset. Therefore, we consider it reasonable that we can
distinguish our 3 classes with only 128 ﬁnal feature maps. Note that achieving the best
possible accuracy probably requires using more feature maps. It is seen in one of the
experiments later, that we have no accuracy gain from increasing the feature map size.

Optimizer
The baseline is trained using the Adam optimizer. Because He et al. [5] and [6] uses
stochastic gradient descent (SGD), we will use SGD as well. Additionally, the SGD is
claimed to generalize better than the Adam optimizer if properly tuned according to
Keskar et al. [9]. Tuning refers to the learning rate schedule used during the training of
the network. We have found the following learning rate schedule to perform well for this
application:
epochs = {0, 50, 70, 90},
lrs = {10−1, 10−2, 10−3, 10−4}
which should be understood such that when the epochs listed in epochs are reached,
the learning rate is set to the index-wise corresponding element in lrs. Learning rate
schedules are often not necessary when using the Adam optimizer, so the tuning is a
drawback when using SGD compared to Adam. The cross-entropy is used as loss function.
6.2.2
Batch Assembling & Augmentation
In order to ensure that the network weighs all classes equally, it needs to be trained on an
equal number of samples from each class during an epoch or use some kind of uneven loss
function. ChemoMetec addressed it by physically saving additional augmented samples
from the underrepresented classes such that the training dataset roughly consisted of an
equal number of samples from each class. This corresponds to using oversampling (with
augmentation). The augmentation consists of added noise and random horizontal ﬂips.
In this thesis, the augmentation is performed during training in order to allow more
variation in the augmentation. To ensure that the network weighs all classes equally, we
construct each batch such that it contains close to an equal number of samples from each
class by undersampling the training dataset in each epoch. Thus, an epoch consists of
considerably less samples than then number of samples in the training dataset in practice.
We use a batch size of 64 and added noise, random horizontal ﬂip, and random horizontal
and vertical translations as augmentation steps.

The ﬁrst channel selection methods that will be considered in this thesis are the baseline
methods. The ﬁrst baseline method is the Entropy method, while the second method is
the Correlation method. As described in Chapter 4, these have been chosen from Bajecsy
et al. [1] due to their superior performance over the other unsupervised channel selection
methods.
7.1
Method
This section describes the methodology for the Entropy and Correlation baseline methods.
They both utilize raw pixel values within the images to calculate a measure without taking
spatial positioning into account. However, it should be noted that because the images in
our dataset are preprocessed such that all pixels not containing the cell subject are set
to zero, only pixels strictly diﬀerent from zero are used to calculate the measures.
7.1.1
Entropy
The entropy, in the domain of information theory, measures the average level of informa-
tion in a variable’s outcome. For the application of this thesis, the pixel values in a given
channel is considered multiple outcomes of a single variable associated with the channel.
For each channel, we associate such a variable, which means that a total of 10 entropy
variables are present in the dataset. For one of the entropy variables X with n possible

Chapter 8
Channel Selection: Channel
Occlusion
In this chapter, we will propose a method for channel subset selection. It referred to
as the channel occlusion method as it is based measuring the impact of ’occluding’ the
individual channels during inference of an already trained network. It is inspired by Fong
et al. [3] that occlude parts of training images during training as a data augmentation
step and Zeiler et al. [22] that systematically occlude a part of an image to reveal which
parts of the scene are important for classiﬁcation.
8.1
Method
8.1.1
Network Architecture
This method assumes that you have a trained neural network with two requirements
w.r.t. the architecture. It needs to take the full 10-channel images as input and it needs
to output class probabilities, i.e. from a softmax activation layer.
As the architecture is used to select channels that another classiﬁcation architecture
is going to use, it is beneﬁcial to constrain these two architectures to be the same. This
way, we ensure that there is no diﬀerence in architecture complexity. Otherwise, it could
occur that the one architecture is able to extract some information from the input that
the other does not have enough parameters to do so. This could potentially result in two
diﬀerent optimal channel subset selections. To avoid this, we simply use the exact same
ResNet-24 architecture and training parameters as described in Chapter 6.
==PAGE:62==
52
Channel Selection: Channel Occlusion
8.1.2
Input Image Manipulation
Recall that the output of the network for our classiﬁcation problem consists of three
values from the softmax layer. These values may arguably be considered as the networks
guess at the probabilities for the input image belonging to the diﬀerent classes.
By
changing the input image in some way that we control, the output class probabilities will
change as well. By comparing the class probabilities from the changed image with the
original image, we can measure the eﬀect of the change in the input image, which is the
core idea of this channel occlusion method. Assuming that the network performs with a
high accuracy, only the class probability for the true class is interesting. The following
example explains why.
Say we have an original image of a healthy cell with class probabilities equal to
[75%, 15%, 10%] for the healthy, apoptotic, or dead class respectively. A change to the
image now yields the new probabilities [45%, 50%, 5%]. The healthy probability dropped
by 30 percentage points; thus the change has a negative impact on correct prediction. On
the contrary, if the change yields the probabilities [75%, 0%, 25%], the healthy probability
is still 75% so the change had no impact w.r.t.
correct prediction even though the
probabilities for the other classes changed.
Therefore, we will only measure how the
probability for the true class changes in response to the change in input image. In the
following, two methods for manipulating the input image are presented.
8.1.2.1
Zero Occlusion
This manipulation method, that we will refer to as zero, is the simplest way of changing
the pixel values in a channel. It sets all values in a given channel to zero and measures
how this occlusion changes the probability for the true class. This is repeated for each
channel such that the drop in true class probability may be compared for each channel.
Channel-wise averaging the drop values across a subset (N = 1500) of the validation
dataset provides robust measures of channel importance.
8.1.2.2
Average Occlusion
The zero occlusion method may be considered unrealistic as the network has never ex-
perienced an input image with an entire channel ﬁlled with zeroes. In an attempt to
improve it, the average method aims to manipulate the channel with something that
network is familiar with. It calculates the class-wise and pixel-wise average across the
entire validation dataset. This results in an image of dimension 64 × 64 × 10 for each
class that we refer to as the class average image. The idea is that such an image rep-
resents the typical image of a cell belonging to the speciﬁc class. Instead of replacing a
channel with zeros, we replace it with the channel in a class average image from a class
diﬀerent from the true class. There are two classes beyond the true one and thereby two
options. Instead of choosing, we do it for both and aggregate the results by calculating
the average. As with the zero occlusion method, we do this for a subset (N = 1500) of
the validation dataset.
==PAGE:63==
8.2 Results
53
8.1.3
Channel Selection
After iterating through the subset of the validation dataset, we should have a single value
for each channel. This value is the averaged delta in true class probability. These are
generally negative values as a change in the input image often decrease the network’s
performance. The ﬁnal channel importance scores are obtained by multiplying the ten
values with minus one followed by a normalization such that they sum to one. Finally,
sorting the normalized importance scores from high to low provides the channel selection
order for the channel occlusion method.
To investigate the variation between diﬀerent runs, this is repeated for three trained
networks with the ResNet-24 architecture.

9.1.1
Teacher Network
Mirzaei et al. [14] train a teacher network to obtain an encoder which nonlinearly maps
the inputs to low dimensional representation codes. The teacher network may be trained
for classiﬁcation problem if a supervised approach is desired. The activations in the layer
prior to the dense classiﬁcation layer are considered the representation codes. Training
the teacher for classiﬁcation ensures that the teacher learns codes that are relevant for
the classes in the dataset. For an unsupervised approach, the representation codes may
instead be the bottleneck in an autoencoder (AE). The teacher network may be as com-
plex as the problem requires as long as it is forced to compress the input information
into a latent space with much lower dimension than the input, i.e. l ≪d.
In this thesis, we use almost the same ResNet-24 architecture for the teacher network
architecture as we use to solve the classiﬁcation problem, which is described in Chapter
6. This ensures that the teacher has a fair amount of representational power. We use the
output from the global average pooling layer positioned just before the dense classiﬁcation
layer in our ResNet architecture as our representation codes. The only diﬀerence from
the other ResNet-24 architecture is that the number of feature maps in the end of the
network is 16 as opposed to 128. Thus, it yields a 16 dimensional representation code
yi ∈R16 for each image passed through the network. This large reduction in feature
maps is expected to hurt performance of the teacher network to some degree and it was
shown with a drop in average test accuracy from 96.98% to 96.74% (standard deviation
σ = 0.08 percentage points). However, it is considered beneﬁcial to achieve the lower
dimension of the representation codes.

Chapter 10
Channel Selection: Deep
Subspace Clustering Network
In this chapter, we ﬁnd similarities between channels and leverage that to select channels
in an unsupervised way. By clustering the channels into groups of similar channels, we
can deliberately select channels from diﬀerent groups increasing the chance that each
channel provides unique information about the cell’s healthiness.
Inspired by Ji et al. [8] and Zeng et al. [23], a deep subspace clustering network (DSC-
net) method is proposed that obtains a similarity matrix. Next, a clustering analysis is
done on the channels. The outline of the chapter is as follows. First, the methodology
is explained in the Method section including an analysis for consistency. Second, the
resulting selected channels are presented in the Results section.
10.1
Method
The method considered in this chapter uses a customized neural network to ﬁnd a simi-
larity matrix containing similarities between all pairs of channels constituting a similarity
matrix. Clustering methods may use such a matrix, or the related distance matrix, to
group channels together. We choose to use agglomerative clustering due to its ability
to construct hierarchical clusters. The clusters together with the similarity matrix may
then be used to select an appropriate channel subset. The proposed method consists of
the components listed here:
1. Network architecture in the DSC-net trained to learn a weight matrix C used
to reconstruct each latent code of a channel from the latent codes from all other
==PAGE:82==
72
Channel Selection: Deep Subspace Clustering Network
channels. The weight matrix is considered as a raw measure for similarity between
channels.
2. Similarity processing steps are needed to convert the raw weight matrix into
a trustworthy similarity or distance matrix. This involves removing the sign of
negative matrix elements, normalizing the matrix by its row sums, forcing it to
be symmetrical by summing it with its transpose, and ﬁnally converting it to a
distance matrix.
3. Clustering method that performs the agglomerative clustering analysis based on
the obtained distance matrix. Diﬀerent linkage methods may be used, but single-
linkage is used to achieve the highest consistency between consecutive runs. The
dendrogram (a hierarchical clustering tree) is the product of the clustering method.
4. Channel selection method that utilizes the cluster information. Two selection
methods are proposed.
The ﬁrst method uses the similarity matrix to select a
channel subset. For selecting N channels, the dendrogram is cut to a number of
clusters, K = min(N, Kmin), where we put the restriction that multiple channels
from the same cluster may be not chosen for the channel subset. After this re-
striction, the permutation that minimizes the sum of similarities between included
channels while maximizing the sum of similarities between included channels and
excluded channels. The second method also uses the cut dendrogram, but selects
the channels based on the reconstruction error of the original input channel.
Each component is explained in depth in the following subsections starting with the
architecture of the DSC-net.

expressiveness property was explained. In that section, it was explained w.r.t. diﬀerent
data points - i.e. how data points may be reconstructed from one another. However,
in this project, the self-expressiveness property refers to the reconstruction of channels
in a single image or data point.
Note that when using a self-expressive layer in our
architecture, there is no guarantee that the reconstruction task is possible. A channel may
be impossible to reconstruct if it contains unique information that cannot be retrieved
from any of the other channels. A channel must be similar to another channel in our data
but is allowed to be so in a highly non-linear way due to the transformation performed
by the encoder and decoder.

Usually for autoencoder architectures, it is desired to learn a compact latent code,
so the dimension of the encoder layer outputs decreases towards the end of the encoder
yielding a ﬁnal latent code with dimension much lower than the original input. This
involves using convolutional layers with strides bigger than 1 and/or using pooling layers

a compact latent representation, because we just want the encoder to facilitate the self-
expression property of channels by transforming the input channels in a (potential) non-
linear way. Therefore, the encoder should be able to learn such a transformation without
forcing it to compress the information at the same time. Note however that forcing the
encoder to reduce the dimension may have some beneﬁcial regularizing eﬀects, so the
optimal architecture probably includes some dimensional reduction. Quick experiments
did not show any beneﬁts from spatial reduction, so it is kept ﬁxed during all the encoder
and decoder layers.
10.1.1.4
Decoder
The design of the decoder architecture is based on a mirror image of the encoder, but
with deconvolution layers. Brief experiments early in the implementation showed that
having an extra layer in the decoder compared to the encoder was beneﬁcial. This is most
likely because the last decoder layer initially had no activation because it needed to be
able to reconstruct values that are negative or have a magnitude greater than one. This
would not be possible with ReLU, Sigmoid, or Tanh activation. However instead of having

from earlier development that was not considered worth changing.

The network is trained using a multi-objective loss function consisting of a reconstruction
term for the original image, a reconstruction term for the latent representation, and the
regularization term responsible for minimizing the magnitude of the C weight matrix in
the self-expression layer. Omitting the trivial L2 regularization loss term for the encoder
and decoder layers, the following total loss may be formulated:

layer respectively. We use the hyperparameter values λ1 = 1 and λ2 = 1e - 5.
In Ji et al. [8], they use one single batch containing the entire training set because
the architecture of their network depends on how many images they use - i.e. there is a
node in their self-expression layer for each image. In Zeng et al. [23], they use a single
image with 200 channels and train the network from only this image. In this project,
we have +100,000 images with 10 channels each in our training set and we want the
transformations performed in the network to be valid for all the images and not just a
single image. Therefore, unlike [8] and [23], we train the network using batches with 32
images. This number was chosen from a small experiment documented in appendix F.2.
The Adam optimizer was used as it was used in [8] and [23]. As opposed to SGD, it
requires a lot less tuning w.r.t. hyperparameters. A learning rate of η = 1e - 4 is used
based on a small experiment documented in appendix F.2. A L2 weight decay of λreg =
1e - 5 is used as a default value for the encoder and decoder parameters without further
consideration. No data augmentation is used because the overall network architecture
has relatively few parameters compared to the high number of images used for training.
The training uses early stopping meaning that the training is terminated if the validation
loss does not improve for n = 10 epochs. This results in a training duration between 90
and 170 for the DSC-net.
10.1.2
Similarity Processing Steps
After extracting the weight matrix C from the self-expression layer, the signs of the
matrix elements are discarded because it is diﬃcult, if not impossible, to distinguish
between positive and negatives contributions w.r.t. similarity. Figure 10.2 illustrates
how the matrix changes for each processing step. After removing the signs, we denote
the matrix Cabs. Next, we may optionally choose to normalize the rows in Cabs by their
corresponding sums, which gives us Cnorm where each row is ensured to sum to unity.
Recall that the latent code Ui is reconstructed using the weights in row i, so if the row
sum to one, each element is then a contribution percentage value. It makes it easier to

10.1.3
Clustering Method
The clustering method used is hierarchical agglomerative clustering which is a bottom-up
approach, i.e. it starts by having each data points in its own unique cluster and then
merges the two clusters that have the smallest distance between them. This proceeds
iteratively until all points have been clustered together and a dendrogram (hierarchical
clustering tree) is obtained. Multiple options for calculating the distance between two
clusters exist, referred to as the linkage method. The three linkage methods are considered
in this project:
• Single linkage uses the smallest pairwise distance between any point from the
ﬁrst cluster and any point from the second cluster.
• Average linkage uses the average pairwise distance between any point from the
ﬁrst cluster and any point from the second cluster.
• Complete linkage uses the maximum pairwise distance between any point from
the ﬁrst cluster and any point from the second cluster.
The single linkage was chosen based on the consistency analysis described in the following
section.
10.1.4
Consistency Analysis
Initially, only a few networks were trained to give an indication of the consistency of
the clustering analysis. The average linkage was used at that time because it intuitively
seemed to provide the most consistent results. The dendrograms from these runs are
seen in Figure F.1 in Appendix F.1. The fact that the dendrograms turned out to be
quite diﬀerent motivated a consistency analysis that allow us to i) select the parameters
that maximizes consistency and ii) choose a representative network/run among all the
trained networks that is used for our channel subset selection.
10.1.4.1

normalization in C or not, and another is the linkage method in the clustering method
that may either be single, average, or complete. Instead of choosing the hyperparameters
that provide the best accuracy for the channel subset, which is a way to overﬁt, the
hyperparameters are chosen to provide the most consistent clustering results. To quantify

10.1.5.2
Loss Based Selection
The loss-based method is the second selection method and it will be abbreviated DSC-
loss. It uses the same initial step as the similarity-based method (DSC-sim). All steps
are listed below.
1. The dendrogram is cut to K = max(Kmin, N) clusters, where Kmin = 5. This is
the clustering level that we ’trust’.
2. To obtain a measure for the reconstruction ability of the individual channels, we
may calculate the channel-wise mean squared error between an input image and
the corresponding reconstructed prediction image. By aggregating these loss values
across all images in the validation dataset, we have a loss value for each of the 10
channels.
3. The N channels are selected in the order according to their loss value, i.e. high-
est ﬁrst, with the requirement that all selected channels must represent separate
clusters.
This method assumes that channels that are diﬃcult to reconstruct by only using infor-
mation from other channels contain valuable unique information. A corner case, where
the loss for a given channel is zero, would indicate that the channel is perfectly re-
constructed from the other channels and thus provides no additional information. The
opposite corner case, where the loss is very high, indicates that the channel contains a lot
of information that cannot by inferred from the other channels. This is the motivation
behind using the channel-wise loss values as a channel selection order.

Outperform Random Selection
In Figure 11.1, it is observed that all proposed subsets lie above the random reference
for N > 1 except for the average channel occlusion (CO-Average) method. It means that
any of our methods for selecting channels is indeed better than blindly selecting channels.
Although the random reference for N = 5 has not been created, the initial subset
by ChemoMetec (Ref-Chem) seems to perform slightly below a random subset if the

similar to the subsets chosen by the zero channel occlusion and teacher-student methods.

Baselines
The next observation in Figure 11.1 is that the baseline method entropy achieves a
quite good performance for our classiﬁcation problem even though the complexity of the
method is low. This particular dataset may be especially well suited for the entropy-based
channel selection for the following reasons. First, the dataset contains no noticeable noise,
which means that we will not have any images with high entropy that does not translate
to a high level of signal. Second, the images only contain a single object which means
that the entropy will not be high due to disturbing background objects. For instance,
this would be diﬀerent in a classiﬁcation task of pedestrians in a cityscape dataset where
cars and building would contribute a huge deal to the ﬁnal entropy value.
As the object to be classiﬁed is the only object present and all pixels not belonging
to this object are set to zero, it is guaranteed the signal measured with the entropy
measure relate to the biological cell. Because the images visualize the underlying three-
dimensional cell structure, it is likely that any pixel contrast is due to actual diﬀerences in
the three dimensional curvature/structure of the cell. It is reasonable to think that these
diﬀerences are related to the ’healthiness’ of the cell which is the classiﬁcation problem.
Therefore, it makes sense that the simple entropy measure is relevant.
As the method does not have any mechanisms to avoid selecting highly correlated
channels, its performance may be considered a rather lucky coincidence as the channels
with the highest entropy could just as well have been highly correlated. As it does not
account for redundancy, it would likely provide poor subsets for datasets with more chan-
nels as the need to account for redundancy likely increases as the number of channels in
the dataset increase. The entropy measure is considered valuable, especially if combined
with other measures, but we should not blindly trust the subsets from this method if we
were given a diﬀerent dataset. The same arguments apply to the correlation method.

Channel Occlusion
The proposed zero channel occlusion method performs well except for the subset with
N = 1 channels where it could barely perform worse. It chooses the Aperture channel
as its ﬁrst channel. From Figure 5.1 in Chapter 5, the channel seems to contain little
detail and mainly illustrates the silhouette of the cell. The method shows that a network
trained on all channels does use this channel a lot. This is interesting as it looks like the
channel has little detail and therefore information compared to the other channels. In the
exploratory analysis in Chapter 5, it was seen that the size information was a signiﬁcant
trend w.r.t.
the class of the cell.
A hypothesis might be that it is easier to extract
the size information from the Aperture channel. A network will initially learn from the
inputs where it is easy to learn from. Even though the size information is available from
the other channels with more intra-cell detail, the trend is likely easier discovered in the
blurred Aperture channel. As mentioned earlier, once the network begins using a channel,
it is not encouraged to stop using that channel even if the information is available in other
channels. This statement does not apply if the network uses other regularization than
L2 as the student network in the teacher-student method.
These speculations match the fact the Aperture channel is chosen ﬁrst in the similarity
based deep subspace clustering (DSC-Sim) method. This indicates that the Aperture has
the highest average similarity with all other channels, which makes sense as it provides
little information but the size and silhouette of the cell and therefore is suitable as a
general building block in the reconstruction of the other channels.
Additionally, the
speculations match the evolution of importance scores from the teacher-student network
in Figure 9.4 and 9.5, where the Aperture is seen to be the most or second most important
channel in the beginning of the student training.
Then, it drops and other channels
are considered more important.
This hypothesis should ideally be investigated with
additional experiments, which is a subject for future work.
The proposed average channel occlusion method is the worst performing method. The
intention is that channels are occluded with patterns that the trained network is familiar
with, but at the same time has nothing to do with the original image. Calculating the
channel-wise average across all the images may be a poor design choice as it inevitably
produces a blurry image, i.e. there is no ﬁne detail at all. The averaged class-wise images
basically only capture rough low frequency trends in the images such as size diﬀerences
or mean intensities. It is indeed seen in Figure C.1, C.2, and C.3 in Appendix C that the
average class images captures the above mentioned trends.
By replacing a high detail channels such as the BrightField or DFIOpen channel
with a blurry image, the network is indeed presented with patterns it has never seen
before as high frequency patterns are always present in those channels. Instead, one
could consider replacing the channel with one or multiple representative samples from
the validation dataset. But then again, the network would not be used to a cell size
inconsistency across the occluded channel and other channels. Overall, the zero channel
occlusion method should be used over the average channel occlusion method.

Teacher-Student
The teacher-student method performs signiﬁcantly better than the random reference
but is not in the very top. As opposed to the other methods (except average channel
occlusion), the teacher-student method still selects a good channel for its N = 1 subset.
The teacher-student method should provide ideal subsets if the following assumptions are
true. First, the latent codes from the teacher should only represent features relevant to the
classiﬁcation task. As we train the teacher-student network for the actual classiﬁcation
task, i.e. supervised, this assumption should hold. Second, the student network should
perfectly predict the latent codes from the input images. This assumption is not valid
as there is an error associated with the predictions. As seen in Figure E.1 in Appendix
E, where the true code values are plotted versus the predicted values, the errors are
quite large. Recall that this is not unexpected as the architecture of the student is much
simpler than the teacher network.
Thus, it should of course not be able to perform
the latent code extraction from the input images as well as the teacher network. This
introduces a potential issue with the teacher-student method.
The student network
currently prioritizes the prediction of all latent codes equally. However, it is unlikely
that the features represented by the latent codes contribute to the classiﬁcation task
equally. The fact that we restricted the teacher network to have 16 latent codes instead
of 128, should make them all important, but there is no guarantee that they are equally
important. To address this potential problem, one could weigh the latent codes unequally
in the student loss, such that the student prioritizes minimizing the error of the most
important latent codes. How to determine the weight values is a topic for future work.
The above problem may be a reason why the teacher-student method does not ﬁnd
the best subsets. Otherwise, we consider the teacher-student method as one of the best
methods because it performs well, requires little hyperparameter optimization, and is
very ﬂexible. By ﬂexible, we mean that the network architectures may be tailored to be
application for many domains.
Deep Subspace Clustering
The similarity based deep subspace clustering (DSC-Sim) method performs slightly above
random while the loss based (DSC-Loss) method performs superior compared to all other
methods except for its N = 1 subset. The clustering result, i.e. the dendrogram both
methods share, seems trustworthy as it agrees both with the correlation method and
the image acquisition background.
The ﬁrst ﬁve selected channels in the correlation
method each belong to diﬀerent clusters in the dendrogram.
More importantly, the
clustering result match with the image acquisition background described in Section 2.2.
The Aperture, ApodizedAP, Phase, and UVPhase channels, that constitute the largest
cluster, are all variants of brightﬁeld imaging. The BrightField channel, which is the last
brightﬁeld imaging variant, is in its own unique cluster, which seems fair as it diﬀers
from the other brightﬁeld modes by not being aﬀected by anything such as a ﬁlter in
the imaging path. Later in the dendrogram, the BrightField channel is merged with the
other brightﬁeld based channels before merging with any non-brightﬁeld channels. The

DarkField and iSSC channels constitute a cluster in the dendrogram and they are both
based on imaging from refracted and scattered light while blocking direct light. Next,
the DFIOpen and DFIPhase channels also constitute a cluster and they are both based
on diﬀerential focus imaging. Finally, the DPI channel has its own cluster, which makes
sense as it is quite unique by merging two images with an oﬀset on each side of the optical

There is an issue with the channel selection methodology within the DSC-Sim method.
The DSC-Sim selection method aims to select channels such that the entire dataset is
represented. This means that when it is forced to select a single channel, it selects the
channel that has the highest average similarity with other channels in the dataset such
that all channels are ’represented’.
Without including any metric that measures the
amount of signal present in the channels, the selection of the very ﬁrst channel seems
random. It is unreasonable to think that the channel that has the most in common with
the other channels is the best channel for the classiﬁcation task. Indeed, we observe that
it chooses the Aperture channel, that performs poorly on its own. This may be diﬃcult
to avoid as the DSC methodology is unsupervised and therefore does not use information
regarding the classes.
The proposed channel selection scheme within the DSC-Loss method uses such a
metric by measuring the channel-wise reconstruction errors. It selects channels that are
most diﬃcult to reconstruct in the order high to low. As opposed to DSC-Sim, this is
related to unique information present in the channels. The larger reconstruction error a
channel has, the higher is the amount of unique information present in the channel. This
performs very well, but again, except for N = 1 where the iSSC channel is selected. This
channel is the worst channel besides the DarkField channel to have in a single-channel
subset. In Figure 5.1, the channel seems to provide poor contrast between the cell and the
background. This motivates a hypothesis explained in the following. Although the iSSC
channel may contain a huge amount of unique information, the low contrast between
the cell and background may likely limit a neural networks ability to determine the size
and shape of the cell as precisely as using the other channels. As we know that cell
size is relevant for the classiﬁcation, it seems reasonable that the iSSC channel performs
poorly on its own. The same applies to the DarkField channel. However, when chosen
together with additional channels, this is no longer the case as the size and shape may
be determined from the other channel. This may explain why the N = 2 subset from
DSC-Loss performs superior compared to the N = 2 subsets from the other methods,
while its N = 1 subset performs poorly. Its N = 2 subset includes the BrightField and
iSSC channel, where BrightField is best performing single channel subset, see Best-1 in
Table G.1 in Appendix G. The accuracy increases from 95.89% to 96.68% by adding
the iSSC channel. Adding more channels increases the accuracy until the maximum is
reached at N = 4 with an accuracy of 97.36%.
The reconstruction error, that is considered as a measure of channel-wise unique
information, does not account for the total amount of information present in the channel.
This is highly relevant if few channels are selected. For instance, the N = 1 subset should
ideally contain as much total information as possible regardless of its uniqueness as no
additional channels are included anyway. Therefore, it makes sense that the ﬁrst N = 1

selection is performing poorly, as it should be a coincidence if the hardest channel to
reconstruct also is the best to use for classiﬁcation. This is a drawback of the selection
scheme that may be improved by in including a total information metric into the channel

The DSC-Loss method performs well for N > 1 channels and should be taken into

11.4
Future Work
The work of this thesis is well suited to be continued and improved. This section describes
the diﬀerent areas where the work could be continued:
• Combine entropy and correlation measure.
The simple baseline methods may be combined into a single channel selection
scheme that ensures that the chosen channels have high entropy while being uncor-
related. The channels from the baselines already satisfy this, but to generalize to
other datasets, it should be ensured.
• Mutual information selection method.
Like the above, mutual information takes the correlation into account and while
being a measure of information. This could provide another simple baseline, that
may perform well.
• Combine loss based deep subspace clustering (DSC-Loss) with informa-
tion measure.
As mentioned earlier, the DSC-Loss accounts for redundancy by selecting channels
from diﬀerent clusters and uses the reconstruction error as a measure of unique
information. It could be combined with an information measure, such as entropy,
to also select channels with an overall high level of information. This may solve the
issue where the N = 1 subset for DSC-Loss is bad.
• Improve channel occlusion method with occlusion augmentation.
It was mentioned that it should be beneﬁcial that the channel is occluded with
patterns that the network is familiar with. This is not satisﬁed for zero channel oc-
clusion as the network does not see a channel only containing zeros during training.
Instead of replacing channels with patterns from some advanced method, we could
instead ensure that the network is familiar and still functions with a channel con-
taining only zeros. Similar to dropout, we could randomly turn oﬀentire channels
==PAGE:109==
11.4 Future Work
99
during training. This way occluding a channel during inference would be provide
a more accuracy estimate of the drop in class probability.
Another idea regarding the channel occlusion method is proposed in the following.
Instead of testing how well the network performs without a speciﬁc channel, we
could test how well it performs by only using some speciﬁc channels. This is more
intuitive method than the original channel occlusion method, but it requires the
network to be specially trained to function well even though a large number of
channels are replaced with zeros. This could again be done with an augmentation
step that systematically turns oﬀmultiple channels during training. By randomly
determining whether each training sample should train using only one, two, or three
channels, the network learns to predict the cell class for each of the 175 subsets
corresponding to 10 single channel subsets, 45 dual channel subsets, and 120 triple
channel subsets. Using these subsets for inference afterwards, we should be able to
obtain a much more accuracy estimate of a subset’s performance than the original
channel occlusion, though with signiﬁcantly longer training times.
• Improve hyperparameters in teacher-student method.
Performing experiments to obtain proper hyperparameters could likely reduce the
error for predicting the latent codes, and thus provide more accuracy channel sub-
sets.
• Implement latent code weighting in teacher-student method.
As mentioned earlier, the teacher-student method could potentially be improved by
introducing a latent code weight concept. Inspired by the channel occlusion method,
we could determine a weight for a latent code by training a new dense classiﬁcation
layer on all latent codes except the given latent code. The weight could then be
determined by the negative drop in accuracy or correct class probability.
This
would make student network prioritize prediction of the latent codes that led to
the largest drop in accuracy or correct class probability.
• Perform analyses on multiple datasets.
This thesis has applied methods to a single real dataset and dummy datasets con-
structed from this dataset. To properly evaluate the channel selection methods, we
should have multiple datasets with multiple types of cells and underlying classiﬁ-
cation tasks. This would allow us to make more general conclusions that do not
only apply to our speciﬁc dataset.
Although the channel selection methods are applied to a speciﬁc dataset regarding cell
classiﬁcation, the methods considered in this thesis may be applied to any problem where
input channel selection is beneﬁcial.

Chapter 12
Conclusion
This thesis investigated channel selection methods on the provided dataset consisting of
diﬀerent kinds of images of cells categorized as healthy, apoptotic, or dead. Ten imaging
modes constructed the channels in the dataset; all from optical microscopy illumination
techniques.
For channel selection, simple baseline methods (entropy and correlation)
were included together with diﬀerent deep learning based methods: channel occlusion,
teacher-student, and deep subspace clustering.
The entropy, zero channel occlusion (CO-Zero), teacher-student, and loss-based deep
subspace clustering (DSC-Loss) methods performed signiﬁcantly better than average ran-
dom selections, which emphasize the usefulness of the methods. Furthermore, the DSC-
Loss method provided channel subsets that performed close to the best possible subsets,
except for the subset containing a single channel. Subsets of that size from the methods
generally showed poor performance.
For the speciﬁc classiﬁcation problem, testing all combinations showed that the best
subsets with less than ﬁve channels contained the BrightField, DFIOpen, iSSC, and Phase
channels that achieved a maximum test accuracy of 97.36%, which may be compared with
the original and new reference subset from ChemoMetec that achieved a test accuracy of
96.59% and 97.20%, respectively, with our implementation.

Appendix D
Teacher-Student Dummy
Dataset Results
D.1
Dummy Dataset
To validate the teacher-student method, we have constructed a dummy dataset from
the real dataset. A single channel, DPI, was used to create nine extra channels. Three
channels are created by discretizing the pixel intensity values, three by blurring, and
three by adding noise. These channels are referred to as Discr<NUM>, Blurr<NUM>, and
Noisy<NUM>. The idea is that these channels are redundant as all the information is
already present in the original DPI channel. Furthermore, two artiﬁcial channels are
added to the dummy dataset where class information has been injected into these channels
such that both channels in combination may explain 100% while a single of those channels
is useless. These channels are referred to as X1 and X2. Forward selection would not select
these channels as they do not contribute with anything on their own. Backwards selection
however would select them as removing just one of them would yield a huge performance
hit.
D.2
Results
In Figure D.1 and D.2, the channel importance scores for multiple runs of the teacher-
student method on the dummy dataset are visualized for the original and new regular-
ization, respectively. The method correctly selects feature X1 and X2 as the two most
important features, where the original DPI channel follows next. The stronger sparsity
inducing regularization is indeed seen to result in a more sparse solution as the impor-
tance scores for all the degraded channels are very close to zero except the Discr1 channel
which is chosen by the student network in three out of nine runs instead of the original
channel. This intuitively makes sense because those channels are very similar and the
slightly discretized version may be able to provide the same features that the teacher
network uses for its latent space.

G.3
Performance of Sub-Combinations
Figure G.5 shows the test accuracies of any combination that can be made of a method’s
ﬁrst three selected channels. It is relevant if the speciﬁc order that the channels are
selected is not especially trustworthy, but it is desirable to evaluate the performance of the
three most important channels. For instance, the entropy method’s ﬁrst selected channel,
DPI, performed just above the random reference. As the ﬁrst 4-5 selected channels are
seen to have very similar entropy scores in Figure 7.1 in Chapter 7, the uncertainty in the
speciﬁc order of these channels is high. To account for this uncertainty, Figure G.5 may
be used. It is seen that the entropy method could have chosen the best possible N = 1
subset by choosing BrightField ﬁrst. The same applies to the correlation, zero channel
occlusion (CO-Zero), and loss based deep subspace clustering (DSC-Loss) methods that
all include BrightField in their N = 3 subset, without selecting it for their N = 1 subset.
It may be considered unlucky that none of the methods choose their best N = 1 subset,
but instead 5 out of 7 methods choose the worst possible N = 1 subset among their
N = 1 sub-combinations. This may partly explain why the general performance for our
N = 1 subsets are poor.
Another point seen in Figure G.5 is that all possible N = 2 combinations are above
the random reference except for a single combination from the correlation method.
The subsets that the methods actually choose are marked with black dots in the
ﬁgure. Recall that the DSC-Sim method is not based on a ranking so the chosen N = 1
and N = 2 subset may not be included in the possible sub-combinations from its N = 3
subset.
This is why the DSC-Sim has its black dot for N = 2 in between two sub-
combinations corresponding to the test accuracy of its chosen N = 2 subset.
