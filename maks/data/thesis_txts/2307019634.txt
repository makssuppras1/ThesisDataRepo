Summary (English)
Crystal plasticity is a topic which has been investigated since the 1900s. However, the amount
of experimental data is very limited in the literature. Thus, the goal of this thesis is to obtain
qualied experimental data to calibrate so-called crystal plasticity models. The experimental
data is obtained by performing channel die compression tests on polycrystalline and single
crystalline aluminium samples. The polycrystalline samples provide as basic reference data for
a numerical conventional plasticity model and are used for the initial experiments. The single
crystal experimental results are used for the calibration of a single crystal plasticity model.
The tested single crystals have a face-centred cubic crystal structure with the crystallographic
orientation following slip systems within {111}⟨110⟩where the x1-direction coincide with
[¯110], the x2-direction coincide with [001], and the x3-direction coincide with [110].
The
yield stress of the poly and single crystalline experiments are found to be σp
y = 15 MPa
and σs
y = 10 MPa, respectively.
EBSD and XRD are carried out on a single crystal to
verify the crystallographic orientation before the channel die experiments. Nanoindentation
measurements with a Berkovich tip are conducted on some of the single crystalline samples
after deformation to investigate the hardness.
It is found that the yield stress estimated
from the hardness results diers with more than a factor of 10 compared to the experimental
results.
A 2D plane strain numerical plasticity model, representing the sample in the channel die
compression, is modelled in the commercial software program Abaqus. Conventional plasticity
based on isotropic hardening is implemented for calibrating the model due to the results of the
polycrystalline experiments. Afterwards, the numerical model is reformulated to represent
a single crystal, though without friction, by implementing single crystal plasticity utiliszing
a UMAT made by Huang (1991) with the given orientation of the crystal as stated in the
above. The hardening parameters in the single crystal plasticity numerical model are tted
to the single crystal experimental results. It is found that the hardening parameters i.e. the
initial hardening modulus, h0, and the stage I stress, τs, vary depending on the amount of
latent hardening. Assuming that there must be some latent hardening e.g. q = 1 and that
the problem is considered rate-independent with a rate sensitivity exponent of n = 60, the
hardening parameters are found to be h0 = 20 MPa and τs = 200 MPa with the initial shear
stress of the slip systems τ0 = 10 MPa.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Plasticity is often observed on a macroscopic scale but when studying plasticity on a mi-
croscopic level it is found that the plastic distortion is conned to narrow slip bands which
extend through the crystal lattice. This phenomenon is known as crystal plasticity which
has been investigated for years to obtain a better understanding of the plastic behaviour on
macro scale.
In the 1900's plastic deformation in polycrystalline metals was observed by Ewing and
Rosenhain (1899) which lead to the discovery of slip bands. Later on the scientic work of
nite distortion of cubic metals was experimentally investigated by Taylor and Elam (1923),
leading to a further understanding of crystal plasticity.
Constitutive relations of the elastic-plastic behaviour, which are based on the modern con-
tinuum mechanics, have been formulated by Hill (1965). This was subsequently extended to
nite strains formulated by Hill and Rice (1972) and Asaro (1983). Following, the theory
has been formulated into strain gradient crystal plasticity by Fleck and Willis (2009) and
presented numerically by Niordson and Kysar (2014) where the numerical models deal with
polycrystalline solids on the micron scale assuming small strain deformation. The numerical
models are both studied as rate-dependent (visco-plastic ow) or rate-independent (plastic
ow) cases. However, there does not exist much experimental data in the literature to cal-
ibrate these plasticity models. Thus, the purpose of this project is to obtain qualied data
from experiments which can be implemented in numerical crystal plasticity models. Well-
formulated crystal plasticity models can provide the necessary information about the slip
system activities, the slip resistance and the hardening of the material. The results from the
crystal plasticity model can then be compared to existing numerical models like the strain
gradient crystal plasticity models mentioned in the above.
This thesis is a result of the ever-existing human curiosity to material behaviour and the
pursuit to fully understand plasticity of metallic materials.

1.1
Thesis Objectives
This thesis has two main objectives. The rst main objective is to extract qualied data from
experiments by use of a channel die compression test. The test samples are single and poly-
crystalline aluminium, respectively, thus solids with a face-centred cubic crystal structure.
The samples are to be compressed under controlled conditions and hence the slip systems can
be activated one by one in the crystal lattice. The channel die yields an approximation of
plane strain assumption which improve the ease of replicating single crystal plasticity theory.
The experiment makes it possible to predict the slip resistance and hardening of the material
by use of numerical models which are calibrated to the experimental data. A single crystal is
used for the purpose that the slip behaviour here is easier to treat than of a polycrystalline
solid. Afterwards the proper adaptation to polycrystalline materials can be made.
When conducting the experiment it is of most importance that the single crystalline sam-
ples are orientated correctly so that [001] is parallel to the loading direction and [¯110] is
parallel to the ow direction. Thus, another aim is to clarify the orientation of the single
crystalline samples. Electron Backscatter Diraction (EBSD) measurements are carried out
on a well-prepared sample surface and X-Ray Diraction (XRD) is performed to verify the
results from the EBSD measurements.
The second main objective is to calibrate the obtained experimental data with a numer-
ical single crystal plasticity model. The model is made in the commercial software program
Abaqus utilizing a prescribed User-Material (UMAT) subroutine, which deals with single
crystal plasticity.
The mentioned main objectives can be subdivided into several hypotheses which are in-
vestigated and used as milestones during the project period. In the end of this thesis it is
concluded whether or not the hypotheses are true. Following hypotheses can be listed for this
thesis:
1. The crystallographic orientation of a single crystal aluminium sample can be claried
by use of EBSD.
2. Channel die compression experiments are reproducible.
3. A numerical conventional plasticity model can be tted to the response from the poly-
crystalline aluminium experiments.
4. The single crystal plasticity UMAT can be implemented in a numerical model in Abaqus.
5. The single crystal plasticity model describes the slip system activity in the single crystal.
6. The single crystal plasticity model can be calibrated to t the response from the single
crystal aluminium experiments.
It should be noted that the main focus is on carrying out the experiments and to obtain a
numerical model which is calibrated due to the obtained experimental results. The theory

Chapter 2

This chapter shortly introduces the fundamentals of the material used in the present thesis and
describes in simple terms what happens in metallic materials during nite strain i.e. crystal
plasticity. Crystal plasticity typically results from plastic deformations of a solid occurring
due to crystallographic slip. It should be noted that this thesis only considers slip due to
dislocations.
2.1
Aluminium
The material used for the experimental model (channel die compression test which is described
in Chapter 7) is two dierent types of aluminium with two dierent crystal structures.
1. Aluminium alloy 1050 (A1050) - polycrystalline structure.
2. Pure aluminium (Al) - single crystal structure.
The aluminium samples have a geometry of approximately 3 × 3 × 3 mm3.
A1050 is a
commercially pure wrought aluminium alloy with relatively low mechanical strength and
high ductility. A1050 can be strengthened by work hardening but not by heat treatment.
The A1050 samples are heat treated in 24 hours at 500 −520◦C followed by quenching and
left at room temperature for 24 hours. This treatment results in a softer solid which can be
fairly comparable to the single crystal pure aluminium samples.

A single crystal, also called a monocrystalline solid, is a continuous and unbroken crystal
where no grain boundaries exist in contrast to a polycrystalline solid. It is a single crystal
when the periodic and repeated arrangement of atoms extends perfectly throughout the entire
specimen. Single crystals are grown from one grain which is specically orientated, however
the process for growing a single crystal and obtain a specic orientation is not a part of this
thesis. The single crystal samples are not heat treated.

Dislocations
A dislocation is described as a one dimensional linear defect consisting of misalignment be-
tween some of the atoms. A metal contains defects of such type even though it is crystallized
into the very organized crystal structure described in the previously.

screw dislocations. Illustrations of edge and screw dislocations are shown in Figure 2.2. For
an edge dislocation the dislocation line distorts the lattice in the plane. For a screw dislo-
cation the lattice is distorted out of the plane. The magnitude and the orientation of the
distortion is related to Burger's vector which magnitude is given by:

Dislocations can act as a permanent net movement of a large number of atoms due to
an applied stress. The dislocation movement gives rise to sliding (or gliding) eects which
is inuenced by the number of defects in the lattice.
The more lattice defects, in form
of inclusions and imperfections e.g. substitutional or interstitial solute atoms, vacancies, or
grain boundaries, the more increases the resistance against dislocation motion. Single crystals
have no grain boundaries and for an almost pure solid there will be very limited imperfections
in the crystal lattice, and thus reducing the resistance to deform.
2.4
Slip Systems
There are some crystallographic planes that are more densely packed than others in the crystal
lattice which can be seen by considering Figure 2.1. In these planes along certain directions,
having the greatest atomic packing, slip can occur. Slip is the net movement of dislocations
which moves easier in some planes than others which are called slip planes. The preferred
directions of slip are similarly called slip directions. Combining the preferred slip planes and
directions yields a slip system. The notication of slip directions, planes, and systems can be
seen in Table 2.1.
Activating a large amount of dislocations on each slip plane in the crystal is what denes
crystal plasticity. Slip displacements are microscopically small (Ashby et al. (2010) states
that one dislocation results in a displacement of approximately 10−10 m). Though, a large
number of dislocations travelling through the atomic lattice will yield a deformation at the
macroscopic length scale.
For an FCC crystal structure there are 24 slip systems, however, half of them are parallel
which is why a total of 12 slip systems can be considered (since there are four slip planes each
having three slip directions see Figure 2.3). For other crystallographic structures e.g. Body-
Centred Cubic (BCC) or Hexagonal Closed Packed (HCP) there are more or less slip systems.

Finite Element Method
This chapter deals with the basics behind the nite element method from the simple elastic
formulations to the nonlinear formulations due to large deformations and plasticity. It should
be noticed that the focus of this thesis not is to develop a new nite element code but utilize
the already existing FE codes and tools, thus only the fundamental theory is described in the
present chapter.
The chapter is primarily based on Cook et al. (2002) and uses the same notation i.e. { }
denotes a vector and [ ] denotes a matrix.
5.1
Basics of FEM
The Finite Element Method (FEM) is basically about dividing a large problem into smaller
and more simple parts called elements. These elements are assembled into a global system
where an approximate solution is estimated due to initial values of the original problem.
The smaller elements the more details, though the computational time will increase with
increasing number of elements. Consequently, a solution must be a compromise including

5.2
Nonlinearity
A nonlinear problem, e.g. the channel die compression test where large deformations, plastic-
ity, and friction occurs, cannot be solved by simple linear FE analysis. The global system in
Eq. (5.12) cannot immediately be solved for {D} since the stiness matrix, [K], and the load
vector, {F}, now may be functions of {D} in contrast to a simple linear problem. Instead,
the solution is found by utilizing an iterative process for obtaining an equilibrium between
the product [K]{D} and {F}.
A typically used method is the Newton-Raphson method which is also used by Abaqus.
After each load step (increment) the Newton-Raphson method makes iterations to enforce
equilibrium. This method does not guarantee convergence but the probability for convergence
to a correct solution is enhanced by taking smaller load steps.
Figure 5.1 illustrates the principle of the Newton-Raphson method. In the rst step the
displacement, u, is zero and then a load, P1 is applied where the corresponding displacement,
u1, is the wanted solution. As mentioned in Chapter 3, the stiness for nonlinear material
is often found by the tangent stiness which is denoted kt in the following with the initial
tangent stiness, kt0. In this case the initial load increment is the same as P1 i.e. ∆P1 = P1
thus the load increment and the displacement increment, ∆u, are used to update the initial
tangent stiness. The current load is found by the previously load plus the load increment i.e
ui = ui−1 +∆u which for the rst iteration yields uA = 0+∆u. This estimate is not accurate
and an error, ePA, can be found from the stiness evaluated using the current displacement,
uA, which is subtracted from the applied load, P1, (ePA = P1 −k(u)uA). Now the next
load step corresponds to ePA from where the new tangent stiness can be estimated from
kta∆u = ePA which also leads to a new current displacement, ub. The iterations will at last
lead to an approximate solution where the error is less than a specied minimum error which
means equilibrium is reached. Then a new increment can start (load level P2 in Figure 5.1).

constructive interference occurs, i.e. when two waves mutually reinforce each other, is when

Bragg's law only denes the diraction condition for primitive unit cells i.e. unit cells
where there is only atoms at the corners of the cell. Non-primitive structures as an FCC
crystal structure have additional atoms which means that there are some reections that are

forbidden reection or not depends on the crystal structure. For an FCC solid the Miller's
indices i.e. h, k, l should be all even or all odd to obtain an allowed reection. If h, k, l are
mixed odd and even the reection is forbidden. This is especially important for the X-Ray
diraction method described in Section 6.5.

Basics of XRD
XRD is also a non destructive method which is used to characterize crystalline materials.
Figure 6.13 sketches the principle of XRD where a xed X-Ray soruce sends out X-Rays
with wavelengths on the order of the atomic spacings in the sample. When the beam collide
with the sample a portion of it scatters in all directions due to the atoms that lie in its
path. The diracted beams are detected by the detector which moves along the curved axis.
The incident angle, θ, is dened by the X-Ray source and the sample, and is not to be
compared with Bragg's angle which is only when Bragg's law in Eq. (6.1) is satised. Since
the system is mechanically coupled the angle θ will be accompanied with the diraction angle,
2θ, which ensures that the incident and diracted beams are maintained equal (Callister and
Rethwisch (2011)). The detector (or counter) moves with a constant angular velocity and
plots the diracted beam intensity as a function of 2θ. When a specic orientation is studied
a so-called rocking curve method may be preferred.

This chapter describes the experimental model which is used to achieve data for calibration

7.1
Basics of Channel Die Compression Test
The channel die compression test (CDCT) is a compression test performed in an open die
which provides a controlled deformation of the specimen. The sketch in Figure 7.1 illustrates
the principle of the experiment. A sample is placed in the channel die which constrains the
sample in the z-direction which prevents the specimen to deform in this direction yielding a
plane strain condition with a good aproximation.
This test method is chosen since there is a very limited amount of single crystalline ma-
terial, thus not enough material to make specimens for tensile tests. A tensile test does not
have to consider friction compared to a compression test, but for a compression test friction
will occur which can result in the phenomena called barrelling.
7.1.1

Barrelling happens due to the frictional force between the specimen and the die/punch since
it opposes the outward ow of the material.

Chapter 8
Simple Plasticity Model
This chapter deals with a numerical model representing the channel die compression test in
2D. The problem is modelled in Abaqus where conventional plasticity is implemented using
the power law in Eq. (3.14). The hardening exponent is estimated by tting the plasticity
model with the experimental results from the polycrystalline experiments.
8.1
Problem Statement
The problem is based on the experimental model where an aluminium sample is exposed
to large deformations. The problem can be considered two dimensional as can be seen in
Figure 8.1(a) and the sample has a geometry of 3× 3× 3 mm3. Following assumptions apply:
1. Isotropic material: The specimen has the same material properties in all direction e.g.
density, modulus of elasticity etc.
2. Plane strain conditions: When having plane strain it is assumed that one way of the
structure is much larger than the others.
This is not completely true for this case
but since the specimen is supposed to be obstructed by a "wall" in the out of plane
direction, it can be assumed that this direction will not deform in contrast to the other
two directions. This makes 2D modelling benecial.
3. Large deformations: Large deformation theory (or nite strain theory) is very common
for plastic deformation of materials. It applies when the deformations are caused by
==PAGE:69==
56
Simple Plasticity Model
arbitrarily large strains or rotations and thus invalidates the assumptions of small de-
formation theory (innitesimal strain theory). Here the strains are large compared to
the specimen.
4. Incompressible: No volume changes during deformation.
A simple 2D plane strain plasticity model is created in Abaqus using implicit analysis. This
method uses the Newton-Raphson iterative method which is described in Section 5.2. The
chosen element type is a two dimensional, 4-noded quadrilateral (Q4) plane strain element
called CPE4 which is a fully integrated solid element. The option NLGEOM=ON accounts
for geometric non-linearity and is used when modelling e.g. plasticity problems and/or large
deformation analysis. Since large deformations are expected for this problem Abaqus needs
to distinguish between reference (undeformed) and current (deformed) congurations.
A sketch of the model can be seen in Figure 8.1(b). Here, the deformable body represents
the sample which is 3 mm high and 3 mm wide. The upper and lower surface of the deformable
body are in contact with two analytical surfaces. The upper analytical surface is pressing the
deformable body by a prescribed displacement in a reference point and the lower analytical
surface is constrained in the x1 and x2-directions in another reference point. These contact
surfaces allow for friction to be applied. It should be noted that the contact modelling is
investigated using a symmetric version of this model and is shortly described in Appendix D.1.
The initial material properties used for the analysis are listed in Table 8.1. The yield stress
is found from the channel die experiments, whereas the other properties are assumed to be
approximately those given values. The values of plastic strain and the corresponding yield
stress, which are used as input parameters in Abaqus, are found using the power law given
in Eq. (3.16) for N = 0.25.

8.2
Mesh Convergence Study
It is important to use a suciently rened mesh to ensure that the results from the simulations
are adequate, though the computational time is also an important factor. Coarse meshes can
yield inaccurate results in the analyses since the numerical solution will tends toward a unique
value as the mesh density increases. However, a too ne mesh uses too much computational
time and will eventually not give a better solution than a coarser mesh. The mesh is converged
when further renement results in a negligible change in the solution. Following steps are
carried out for the mesh study:
1. Creating a simple mesh i.e. using fewest, reasonable number of elements →analyse the
model.
2. Rening the mesh with a denser element distribution →re-analyse the model →com-
pare the results with the results from the previously mesh.
3. Continuing to rene the mesh and re-analysing the model until the results are converged
satisfactorily.
The interesting topic of this study is to see when the force-displacement values converge
due to the element density since it is these values that must match the experimental force-
displacement results. Six meshes are used for the convergence study. The dierent mesh sizes
are listed in Table 8.2 and the force-displacement output is plotted in Figure 8.2. It is found

mesh. However, when dealing with friction the element size is more important for depicting
an approximate barrelling eect which is discussed later.

Single Crystal Plasticity Model
This chapter deals with a single crystal plasticity model of the channel die compression prob-
lem as described in Section 8.1. Single crystal plasticity is formulated in a User Material
(UMAT) subroutine developed by Huang (1991) which is implemented in an Abaqus. The
model is calibrated to t the results from the single crystal channel die experiments. The slip
system activity in the single crystal is investigated due to the shear strain in the dierent slip
systems and compared to the slip formulations by (Kysar et al., 2010). The entire process in
modelling the single crystal plasticity model has been trial and error to obtain an understand-
ing of the dierent hardening parameters. The numerical model described in this chapter is
based on the studies in Chapter 8 however, the friction is not considered in the following due
to unsolved problems with implementing both the UMAT and the contact formulations at
the same time.

9.6
Comments and Discussion
Unfortunately it was not possible to implement friction as for the conventional plasticity
mode since it gave some instability problems when implementing the UMAT. The instability
could be reduced by lowering n (see Figure 9.1). However, these problems were eliminated
by modelling a more simple model without contact surfaces. It is not known what exactly
caused the instability for the contact model since the problem varied depending on the value
of q, n, and sometimes the mesh density including how the combination of h0 and τs was.
It was found that the hardening parameters can be tted for both q = 0, q = 1, and q = 1.4
yielding a very accurate t due to the experimental results. This means that additional tests
must be carried out to investigate how much latent hardening that actually occurs in the
material during deformation since this cannot be claried only from the response of the
conducted experiments. It may be possible to investigate the latent hardening by performing
path changing tests where a crystal is exposed to deformation in one direction, which triggers
a certain set of slip systems and then in another direction leading to the triggering of another
or the same set of slip systems.
The slip system activity was studied and found to be in accordance to what was expected
based on the work of Kysar et al. (2010). The maximum absolute shear strain of the active
systems was found to γmax = 0.67 which corresponds to a 2/3 deformation of the specimen
in 1 second (one time step in Abaqus corresponds to 1 second in this case). This is higher
than the actual shear strain for the experiments where 2/3 of the sample was deformed in 400
seconds. However, for a rate-independent case this has no signicant impact on the response.

10.1
Basics of Nanoindentation
Nanoindentation is in principle the same as macro or micro indentation tests where a tip made
of a very hard material with known mechanical properties (often a diamond) is pressed into
another material with unknown properties. From the applied load and the residual plastic
impression i.e. the projected area of the indent in the material, the hardness can be measured.
However, nanoindentation measures the depth of the penetration as the load is applied to the

indenter which yields load-displacement curves from where the mechanical properties as the
elastic modulus and hardness can be extracted.
There exists dierent types of indenter tips but the most commonly used for small scale
indentation studies is the Berkovich tip. The Berkovich tip is a three sided pyramid tip which
makes it easier to obtain a sharp point that is readily for nanoindentation tests.
10.2
Test Set-Up
The nanoindentation tests are conducted with a Berkovich indenter using a nanoindetation
tester from CSM instrument. The material to be tested is three single crystal samples in
three dierent stages of deformation: 1) Undeformed, 2) deformed 1 mm, and 3) deformed 2
mm, as can be seen in Figure 10.1. The three dierent samples are chosen for the purpose of
investigating the eect of the plastic deformation of the aluminium single crystals.
The surface perpendicular to the [001] direction is prepared very carefully for the three
samples at the same time to obtain the same surface nish. The nanoindenter is very sensitive
and if the tip is placed on scratches, inclusions, or voids this will yield inadequate results. The
nal preparation method is almost the same as for the EBSD measurements i.e. mechanical
and chemical polishing of the surface. Often electropolishing is used for the surface prepa-
ration, but as mentioned for the EBSD the samples are too small for the electropolishing
equipment. Thus, the present preparation is developed on a trial and error basis since no
standard procedure existed. The procedure can be seen in Appendix F.1.
Both standard and continuous multi cycle (CMC) tests are made for measuring the mean
hardness and elastic modulus, and the hardness and elastic modulus as a functions of the
penetration depth, respectively. For the standard test a load and a set of coordinates for the
measurement points are given and then the nanoindenter presses the indenter into the mate-
rial and gives the force versus penetration depth as output. The mean and standard deviation
is estimated from the output of the number of measurements for one set. The CMC test is
a cyclic version of the standard test where the load continuously increases over a number of
cycles.

Concluding Remarks and Future
Work
The aim of this thesis was to produce qualied experimental data for calibration of plasticity
models for metallic single crystals. The material used for the experiments was an aluminium
specimen with the dimensions 3 × 3 × 3 mm3. Since aluminium has an FCC crystal structure
the results of this thesis only apply for this type of crystal structure. It can be considered to
do the same for BCC and HCP crystal structures.
Both aluminium single crystalline and polycrystalline samples were tested in a so-called
channel die compression experiment. The single crystal was tested with the [001] direction in
the loading direction and [¯110] in the ow direction following the work of Kysar et al. (2010).
The crystallographic orientation was determined by EBSD and XRD where it was found that
the orientation did not agree with the expected result. The measured crystallographic orien-
tation was found to have the [110] direction in the expected [001] direction.
During the initial channel die compression tests with the polycrystalline samples, it was
found that the reproducibility of the experiment was acceptable after increasing the displace-
ment rate from 0.03 mm/min to 0.3 mm/min. The experimental results of the polycrystalline
samples were used to t a numerical model applying conventional plasticity assuming isotropic
hardening. The power hardening exponent was found to N = [0.24; 0.26]. The model was
able to introduce friction between the sample and the channel die equipment (which was
modelled as two analytical surfaces in Abaqus). However, the level of friction was limited due
to convergence problems. From LOM pictures of the deformed polycrystalline samples the
friction coecient in Abaqus could be measured to µ = 0.06. A lot of practising in Abaqus
was made to enhance friction in this model where also adaptive remeshing was tried, though
it increased the computational time without providing particular advantages in regards to the
actual friction problem.
==PAGE:93==
80
Concluding Remarks and Future Work
It was possible to implement the single crystal plasticity UMAT subroutine by Huang
(1991) in Abaqus, however it was not succeeded to implement friction in this model due
to numerical instabilities. The model was made to investigate the material behaviour and
the slip system activity during large deformations of a single crystals. Here, the results of
the single crystalline experiments were used to calibrate the model by tting the hardening
parameters. It was found that the numerical model led to the expected slip system activity
as formulated in Kysar et al. (2010) i.e. that the eective slip system (1) and (3) were active
and (2) was not since the load was directly perpendicular to the eective slip system. It was
found that the model could be calibrated using dierent levels of latent hardening e.g. q = 0,
q = 1, and q = 1.4. The hardening parameters for the calibrated model are listed in Table 9.3.
To determine the level of latent hardening, further studies must be conducted.
With only very limited time left, nanoindentation was carried out on single crystalline
samples which have been exposed to dierent amounts of deformation. The purpose was to
investigate the hardness due to the stage of deformation and to check how well the yield stress
found by the channel die experiments matched the yield stress estimated from the hardening
measurements. It was found that the results of the yield stress were not consistent and diered
with more than a factor of 10. However, this has been discovered before for single crystals,
but not yet understood. A continuous multi cycle test (CMC) was performed which showed
that the hardness decreased as a function of the penetration depth, but it was uncertain if
this was due to an oxide layer on the surface or if it has to do with size-eects. It was also
discovered that slip bands became visible when using an indentation load of 36 mN.
Interesting topics for future work (some of them are mentioned in the above) could be:
• Make the numerical model for other crystallographic structures as BCC or HCP an try
to calibrate the parameters due to the results of a channel die compression test.
• Investigate how consistent other numerical methods predict the experimental result.
• Make a compression test of a single crystal in one crystallographic direction and then in
another direction to see how the slip systems are aected. Maybe the latent hardening
can be described from the results.
• Make indentation tests on the single crystals with a wedge for investigating size-eects.
• Study the slip bands in a SEM for both the undeformed and deformed single crystals.

Normally during small deformations the change in the area whereupon the force is applied
is considered constant. However, when large displacements are considered a change in the
area will aect the stress-strain relation. It is called the engineering stress and engineering
strain when only the initial values are considered i.e. the original dimensions of the specimen
after force is applied. The following is used to estimate the engineering and true stresses and
strains from the experimental force-displacement results.

The engineering stress and strain in (B.1) can be applied for both tensile and compressive

true strain. During compression the specimen shortens and tends to spread in the lateral
