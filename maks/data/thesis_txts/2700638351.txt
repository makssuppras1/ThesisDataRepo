Abstract
With a constantly increasing use of energy in today’s world but on the other hand with
the danger of climate change, we need energy solutions that will be greenhouse gas
emissions­free and will still deliver required energy. One of them is geothermal energy
which is clean but not widely utilized. Traditional geothermal systems are created from
heat, fluid and permeability. However, such conditions do not occur everywhere or are not
always economically sufficient. Lack of required permeability in the reservoir is the main
factor why its big potential is not yet fully used. Enhanced Geothermal System tries to
tackle that problem by using existing hot rocks where permeability is artificially enhanced
with hydraulic fracturing and water is injected to be warmed up and produced. Hot dry
rocks can be found in many places in the world depending on the geothermal gradient but
often it means that they are at significant depths of around 4­6 km. Creating permeability
in homogenous rock is not trivial and requires a sophisticated approach, especially at
significant depths.
The project investigated the technical feasibility of producing power from the Enhanced
Geothermal System. The sensitivity analysis of nine parameters controlling reservoir in­
cluding fracture transmissibility, flow rate and fracture height, demonstrated which param­
eters have the most impactful influence on thermal power generation. This project models
flow and heat transport to estimate the thermal power that can be produced from a single
fracture stage.
The technical analysis of the investigated system illustrates that for the desired power
of 40 MW, a system with several fracture stages should be considered. It also implies
that an artificially created Enhanced Geothermal System with no occurrence of naturally
fractured rocks would be commercially challenging to operate.
Throughout the process of creating the conceptual model and designing the reservoir
model in numerical simulation software, numerous constraints caused the simplification
of a real­life scenario. Modelling of large scale Discrete Fracture Networks with low per­
meability is still a challenging task for commonly available software.

Introduction
With a constantly increasing use of energy in today’s world and need for the lower CO2
emissions due to climate change, non­emitting energy sources are highly demanded.
Wind and solar power are popular solutions, but they also come with a few restrictions.
They are not always available for use for example, during the night, on rainy days or
when there is no wind. Current technology is not yet mature enough to store significant
amounts of energy produced by these energy sources for a long time. Hydro­power is
another energy source that is renewable and non­emitting but is heavily dependent on
the land form.
Here comes the fourth solution that uses the heat from inside the Earth’s crust. It is called
geothermal energy and its source is linked with the internal structure and processes that
occur inside our planet. The heat moves from the Earth’s inside towards its surface. It
can be measured with a geothermal gradient which on average increases with depth by
30°C/km. However, it is not constant all around the world. There are areas, where due to
the particular geological conditions, the geothermal gradient is anomalously high. It can be
due to the magmatic activity or other geological structures. The heat can be transferred
to the subsurface with two phases, first by conduction and then by convection where
geothermal fluid is the transporting medium. Such occurrence, with hot rock warming the
fluid, is called geothermal aquifer and provided that it is within accessible depth under
Earth’s surface, it can be regarded as a recoverable geothermal reservoir. Barbier 2002

Hot water can be produced, the heat extracted and used for various purposes, for ex­
ample, to generate electricity (high temperature) or for space heating and industrial pro­
cesses (low­temperature). The cooled water is then re­injected into the reservoir helping
produce more hot water and prevent subsiding. After some decades the reservoir loses
its initial heat and needs time to recover.
As described above geothermal reservoir is currently most popular in geothermal power
plants, has many advantages and will play an important role in energy transition, such
conditions do not occur everywhere. In some areas in the world for example Iceland or
Italy, it is easy to access geothermal reservoirs, when in other places there are no geother­
mal reservoirs near­surface that could be used to extract energy.
Traditional geothermal systems are created from heat, fluid and permeability. When one
of these elements is missing the system will not work and needs artificial enhancement.
Enhanced Geothermal System tries to tackle that problem by using only naturally existing
hot rocks and artificially creating permeability and injecting water to be warmed up and
produced. Known as Hot Dry Rock, this concept was introduced for the first time in a
patent filed in 1974 and then implemented in the early 1970s at Fenton Hill, the US by
Los Alamos National Laboratories. 1 As patent describes it: ”Hydraulic fracturing is used
to interconnect two or more holes which penetrate a previously dry geothermal reservoir,

The main objectives of this project are:
1. Perform a literature review and extract main parameters controlling Enhanced Geother­
mal System.
2. Create a conceptual model that reflects the real life design of EGS system.
3. Create a 3D reservoir model in numerical simulation software based on the concep­
tual model.
4. Prepare a Base Case scenario and variables for Low and High cases based on
parameters from literature.
5. Run simulations with varying parameters and measure how they will affect flow rate,
produced water temperature and pressures under given constraints.
6. Calculate usable thermal power that can be produced from varying each parameter
in each case.
7. Compare results to find parameters that have the most impact on usable power
generation from Enhanced Geothermal System.
This project aims to perform a parametric study of reservoir parameters and asses their
influence on the usable thermal power generation from an Enhanced Geothermal Sys­
tem with objectives as described above. The project is done in collaboration with ROSS
Offshore company (ROSS DK A/S) and commercial software Rubis by Kappa Engineer­
ing, which is used for reservoir simulation. The project consists of two main sections:
the parametric study and the sensitivity analysis. In the parametric study, a conceptual
model of the considered problem has been created and key parameters identified. After
the technical analysis of the system with various parameters, it has been decided to focus
on sensitivity analysis of possible power generation. The detailed structure can be found
below.

2
Literature Review
2.1
Utilization of Geothermal Energy
For thousands of years, geothermal energy has been understood and exploited. Its name
is derived from the Greek words ”geo” (earth) and ”thermos” (heat). The term derives from
the fact that it is a natural heat flow caused by temperature differential from the core of the
earth (7000°C) to the surface. The original source of heat was hot springs, which were
used for bathing and ancient space heating. Geothermal energy is now used in a variety
of ways. The temperature is the most important element in determining it. The greatest
temperatures (over 175°C) enable for the generation of power, while lower temperatures
can be utilized for district heating and other purposes. The lowest temperatures from 5°C
to 30 °C can be used for the geothermal heat pumps and space heating and cooling,
which makes them the most available geothermal resource in the world that can be used
anywhere. (Lund 2013)
2.1.1
Electricity Generation
Improvement to the technology of the organic Rankine cycle or binary power plants made it
possible to lower the minimum temperature required for electricity generation from geother­
mal resources. The first commercial power plant that used geothermal energy was built
in 1913 at Larderello, Italy and it had the power of 250 kWe. After that, in the early 1960s,
the first power plants built in Mexico, United States and Japan used steam directly from
the earth (dry­steam fields). The first true binary power plant, 680 kWe using 81°C water
was created in the former USSR at Paratunka on the Kamchatka peninsula. At that time,
it was the lowest temperature used for power generation from geothermal energy. In the
1970s and 1980s, many countries followed making use of geothermal energy resources.
As of 2019, there were 15406 MWe installed capacity for power generation in the world.
Almost 24% and 3.68 GW are installed in the United States, 2.13 GW in Indonesia, 1.92
GW in the Philippines and 1.53 in Turkey. (ThinkGeoEnergy 2019)
There are three main power station types used for electric power generation (Lund 2013):
• Steam plant using a vapor­ or dry­steam­dominated geothermal resource.
• Flash steam plant using a water­dominated geothermal resource with a separator
to produce steam.

2.1.2
Direct Utilization
Geothermal energy direct utilization is one of the oldest and most common forms making
use of it. In many cases, it doesn’t require building complex power plants and can be
regarded for more personal use. According to the Direct Utilization of Geothermal Energy
2020 Worldwide Review (John W. Lund 2021), an estimation of the installed thermal
power for direct utilization at the end of 2019 was 107,727 MWt, which was a 52% increase
since 2015. The countries being on top of the list with the most installed capacity for direct
use are China, the USA, Sweden, Germany and Turkey, accounting for 71.1% of the world
capacity. The direct use of geothermal energy can be split into several categories:
• Geothermal heat pumps
By far the most popular type of direct energy use. These ground­source heat pumps
account for 71.6% of the installed capacity and 59.2% of the annual energy use. The
installed capacity of 77 547 MWt and the energy use is 599 981 TJ/yr.
• Space heating
Space heating includes individual space heating and district heating. The installed
capacity totals 12 768 MWt and the annual energy use is 162 979 TJ/yr, in most
countries over 90% is used for district heating.
• Greenhouses and covered ground heating
The installed capacity is 2 459 MWt and 35 826 TJ/yr in energy use.
• Aquaculture and agriculture
Combined installed capacity for Aquaculture and agriculture is 1 207 MWt and 17
102 TJ/yr in energy use.
• Industrial process heat
These operations tend to be large and have high energy consumption, often oper­
ating year­round. The installed capacity is 852 MWt and the energy use is 16,390
TJ/yr.

2.3
Concept of Enhanced Geothermal System and first
project
Enhanced (or engineered) Geothermal System has evolved from the Hot Dry Rock con­
cept, which was first implemented in the early 1970s at Fenton Hill, the US by Los Alamos
National Laboratories. (1) It resulted in the patent filed in 1974 “Method of extracting heat
from dry geothermal reservoirs”(5), which described the formation of an entirely natural
block of hot rock located at between 4 and 5 km below the earth’s surface that contains
sufficient geothermal energy for it to be possible to extract quantities capable of producing
electricity on the surface. The first completion of the EGS reservoir at Fenton Hill in 1977
at a depth of about 2.6 km, with rock temperatures of 185°C, was a significant milestone
achieved after a few years of conceptual engineering work. The next step was enlarg­
ing the reservoir in 1979 with additional hydraulic stimulation and operating for about 1
year. Such an exercise demonstrated that heat could be extracted at reasonable rates
from a hydraulically stimulated region of low­permeability hot crystalline rock. After the
initial success, in 1986 the project leapt with the second reservoir that was hydraulically
circulated and heat extraction was tested. The 30­day flow test was run, the constantly
reinjected fluid had a temperature of 20°C and the production temperature steadily in­
creased to about 190°C, which resulted in obtaining about 10MW thermal power level
and it confirmed the great potential of HDR (EGS).(Geothermics 2018)
In general, throughout the project duration, the feasibility of mining heat from the deep
Earth was proven in two separate HDR reservoir flow demonstrations ­ each involving
about one year of circulation, conducted by the Los Alamos National Laboratory between
1978 and 1995. The HDR test site that these tests took place in was Jemez Mountains
of north­central New Mexico at the Laboratory’s Fenton Hill location, at depths of over 2.5
km and rock temperatures exceeding 180 °C.(4) The results of these tests demonstrated
the potential power and feasibility in exploring the energy of the revolutionary new HDR
geothermal energy concept. Due to budget cuts, further study at Fenton Hill was discon­
tinued. Due to this day the only project that achieved such success in terms of recovered
energy using the HDR concept.

Heat transfer in porous media
Heat transfer in porous media is mainly driven by two basic processes, thermal conduc­
tion and convection. Thermal conduction is the transfer of heat between particles without
the movement of the transferring medium. In thermal convection, heat is transferred by

both.

3.5
Numerical simulation stability problems
The approach chosen for this thesis uses discrete fracture modelling. Such a method al­
lows for a better representation of heat exchange and fracture flow simulations but is very
challenging to describe with mathematical models. In addition to that, for comprehensive
Enhanced Geothermal System representation, a model should include: full­length wells
with pressure and temperature losses along the way, the fracture should be connected
to these wells and pressure and geothermal gradients should be applied. A problem that
seems to be common to solve with industry­established numerical model simulation soft­
ware packages, turned out to be too complex at its initial state.
While working on a model set­up that could help better understand the complexity of
the Enhanced Geothermal system, the transmissibility of the fracture was one of the key
parameters that should be measured but also a bottleneck of simulation software. In Ru­
bis from Kappa Engineering, when using the DFN module, fracture transmissibility is not
controlled by fracture aperture, which is a separate parameter. According to the Interna­
tional Partnership for Geothermal Technology (IPGT) (Fairley, Ingebritsen, and Podgor­
ney 2010) there are several key challenges with the numerical modelling of EGS. Among
others:
1. Realistic, fully coupled thermal­hydro­mechanical (THM) models.
2. Realistic, fully coupled thermal­hydro­chemical (THC) models.
3. Thermal­hydro­mechanical­chemical (THMC) models.

4
Parametric study of Enhanced
Geothermal System
A parametric study, also known as sensitivity analysis, is a study on how different values of
an independent variable impact a dependent variable in a model under specified boundary
conditions and how a change of an independent variable influences the whole solution.
The parametric study determines how target variables are affected based on changes in
input variables. It also shows which variables have the biggest impact on the result.
Recalculating and analysing a given numerical model with alternative assumptions leads
to determining the impact of a variable and increases understanding of relationships be­
tween independent and dependent variables. It can also reduce uncertainty by identifying
which input parameters have the key to outcome uncertainty. By recognizing input param­
eters with the low impact the model can be simplified with reduction of these parameters,
which will lead to the reduction of computational expense.
The method of sensitivity analysis used in this study is one­at­a­time (OAT) which is the
simplest and the most common. It is based on changing one parameter at a time to
determine how to impact it has on the result. The OAT method is based on changing one
variable while the other is set to the base case, then after recording results, changing it to
the base case and repeating the step with the remaining variables the same way. Such
an approach helps to compare the results because all results are compared to the same
base case scenario with unchanged boundary conditions. The disadvantage of the OAT
method is it cannot simulate the variation of several parameters at the same time and
possible interactions between them.

4.2
Methodology
Each of the selected parameters has been studied in the literature as presented in the
Table 4.7. From the range of values specified for the given parameter the Base Case
scenario has been recognized. Then the variables defined by High and Low values were
used for simulations to determine the sensitivity of the model to the parameter which was
changed. In eight cases impact on flow rate and produced water temperatures was mea­
sured, in one case on constraints defining Enhanced geothermal system ­ injection and
production pressures. A reference scenario was built from the Base Case parameters
and is used as a reference value for the sensitivity analysis.
These results were later used to calculate usable thermal power produced by the system
using equation 4.1:
P = ρwcwQ(Tprod −Tinj)
(4.1)
Where ρw is water density [kg/m3], cw water specific heat [J/(kg∙K)], Q volume flow rate
[m3/s], Tprod production temperature [°C] and Tinj injection temperature [°C].

4.4
Simulation setup
A reservoir model has been built to reflect the real­life setup within the numerical simu­
lation software constraints. The reservoir is placed at the depth of 5000 m. In the Base
Case scenario, there are three layers, each 10m thick (Figure 4.1). Such a solution helped
significantly reduce simulation time and is presented in Figure 4.3. Inside the middle layer,
the two horizontal wells are placed, one injector (I01) Figure 4.4a and one producer (P01)
Figure 4.4b. Parameters of the wells for the base case scenario are presented in Figure
4.4a. Those wells are perforated and connected to the fracture build using the Discrete
Fracture Network model as shown in Figure 4.5. In the Base Case scenario, the distance
between spots where each of the wells penetrates Fracture is 200m. The wells are con­
strained by the injection well pressure and production well pressure. Later in the text, are
also described as Buildup (a rise in well pressure measured at the bottom of the hole) and
Drawdown (the differential pressure that drives fluids from the reservoir into the wellbore
or just a decline in pressure). In six parameters simulations, those pressures are fixed to
be accordingly 75 bar higher than the reservoir pressure and 75 lower than the reservoir
pressure. When injection and production pressures parameters were varied, the values

were ranging from 50 bar to 100 bar in all configurations. In one case, when the flow rate
was set as a constraint, pressure values were measured. The interest in such setup was
to see how the flow rates and production temperatures respond to each parameter that
was changed.
The fracture parameters for the base case are shown in Figure 4.6. Reservoir rock is con­
sidered to be Granite with no natural fractures. Rock parameters are presented in Figure

temperature was fixed at 45°C except injection temperature parameter where this value
varied. Simulation time reflects heating season which is on average 5500 hours per year
in Denmark.
Figure 4.2 shows pressure, temperature and flow rate equilibrium times for a run with
Base Case parameters. It can be observed that the equilibrium state happens over less
than one hour. Compared to the whole simulation time, this is a very short period. The
system quickly reaches equilibrium due to the very low system storage affected by the
Granite’s low rock compressibility. Granite is also distinguished by its low porosity and,
as a result, low storativity. Another reason for the rapid reaching equilibrium is the low
volume of water in the fractures (low fracture storage). Finally, the model does not ac­
count for fracture aperture (hence fracture porosity and storage) varying as a function of
pressure, which is a limitation of the simulation software. Figure 4.8 shows a 2D vertical
cross­section of the reservoir after 5500­hour simulation in a Base Case scenario.
The fluid used for operating the system is water. Its viscosity determinate how easy it is to
move fluid through the fracture. Water viscosity depends on the temperature presented

single fracture stage reservoir? What pressures in the production and injection wells are
needed to achieve those flow rates? What usable thermal power can be achieved from
the one fracture stage in EGS system?

5
Conclusion
The goal of this research was to look into the parametric study of characteristics influenc­
ing usable thermal power generation from an Enhanced Geothermal System. It was done
by creating a conceptual model, a reservoir model and simulating heat and flow transfer
through an artificially created fracture in impermeable Granite. The influence of param­
eters controlling the Enhanced Geothermal System was assessed by developing Base
Case scenario, Low and High value cases and doing sensitivity analysis. Each parame­
ter variation’s effect on usable power generation has been calculated, then compared.
Due to the limits of numerical simulation software, the model has been simplified and
compromised while still attempting to reflect real­life situations as accurately as possible.
Because of these compromises, certain findings may differ from the values published in
the literature.
Results obtained from the sensitivity analysis show that for sufficient usable thermal power
generation from a single fracture system, the flow rate needs to be higher than recorded
in the literature which implies very high pressure difference across the fracture. It is the
bottomhole pressure in the injection well (injection pressure) and the bottomhole pressure
in the production well (drawdown) that controls the hydraulic gradient across the fracture.
The two parameters that have the most impact on usable thermal power generation were
the flow rate and the fracture transmissibility. By adjusting constraints controlling those
values, better performance of the Enhanced Geothermal System could be achieved. As
the simulation findings show, a higher flow rate results in a lower produced water tem­
perature. The explanation for this relationship is that a faster­moving fluid has less time
to extract heat from the matrix. Because of this, the flow rate must be optimal, as a high
flow rate would result in insufficient heat extraction from the reservoir.
Higher fracture transmissibility allows more fluid to flow through the fracture. With higher
fracture transmissibility, the same injection pressure results in a higher flow rate. It can
also be used to reduce the injection pressure keeping the flow rate at the same level.
Fracture height can also influence power production from the Enhanced Geothermal Sys­
tem. Taller fractures extend upwards and are in contact with rock with a lower temperature,
due to the thermal gradient. Therefore, the average temperature in a tall fracture will be
less than that of a short fracture. Thus, the produced water temperature is lower for a tall
fracture. Even the higher flow rate in a taller fracture will not offset temperature loss in

As the study showed, the Enhanced Geothermal system with one fracture stage and a

40 MW of generated power. More fracture stages are required to build such a system,
four to six fracture stages depending on each fracture parameters and available surface

5.1
Future work
1. This study was run for 5500 hours, which is the length of a heating season. In
the future, the simulation could be run for a number of heating seasons. Such an
extension would allow the matrix to be measured in terms of the impact of running
an Enhanced Geothermal System.
2. The current study looked at a system with a single fracture. In future research, an
Enhanced Geothermal System with multiple fracture stages could be considered,
as well as the behavior of the matrix in such a configuration.
3. The current study considered vertical fractures that penetrated the entire reservoir.
Future research could look into different fracture placement and dimensions.
4. Future studies could benefit from less hampered and simplified simulation with com­
plete control over measured parameters.
