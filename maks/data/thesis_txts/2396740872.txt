Abstract
Hydrogen peroxide is an important chemical used for several industrial processes. It can be
used as a disinfectant for water puriﬁcation due to its ability to oxidise a large variety of
molecules in aqueous solutions. Due to a growing demand for more eﬃcient and localised
processes, electrochemical production of hydrogen peroxide by the Oxygen Reduction Reac-
tion (ORR) has gained increasing attention over the recent years. Abundant carbon based
materials are promising candidates as catalysts for electrochemical production of H2O2 as
an alternative to noble metal-based catalysts.
This thesis investigates whether sulfur doping of a carbon material has an eﬀect on the
faradaic eﬃciency for electochemical production of H2O2. Three samples of sulfur-doped,
mesoporous Black Pearls 2000 carbon is made by annealing the carbon samples at 500-
900 ◦C in the presence of a H2S(5%)/Ar(95%) gas ﬂow. Untreated Black Pearls 2000 and
sulfur-doped samples of Black Pearls carbon 2000 are characterised by XPS and nitrogen
adsorption-desorption using BET theory.
XPS experiments show that the carbon samples have oxygen contents of 1-2 at.% and sul-
fur contents of up to 2 at.% and that the sulfur content increases with annealing tempera-
ture. Adsorption-desorption experiments show that the surface area of the untreated carbon
BP2000 is 1575 m2/g while the surface area of the doped samples are generally 5-15% lower,
1356-1495 m2/g. Electrochemical measurements are performed using a three-electrode set-
up for studying the cathodic half cell reaction and a rotating ring disc electrode (RRDE)
for studying the activity of the catalyst materials. Studies of the three-electrode ORR half
cell using a catalyst sprayed gas diﬀusion layer (GDL) as a working electrode submerged in
an O2 saturated electrolyte shows that all samples have faradaic eﬃciencies of ≈70-75% and
current densities of 6-7 mA/cm2 at -0.2 V vs. RHE. There was no signiﬁcant improvement
when using sulfur-doped Black Pearls carbon 2000 as catalyst material. Untreated Black
Pearls carbon 2000 has a sulfur content of 0.4 at.%. This intrinsic sulfur content may by
itself be the required sulfur content for saturating the improvement of the electrochemical
properties.
Boron doped diamond nanopowder (BDD) was tested as a catalyst in a three-electrode
set-up under conditions identical to sulfur-doped Black Pearls carbon 2000 with a lower
faradaic eﬃciency of 51%. Due to the lower faradaic eﬃciency, diamond nanopowder was
not subject to further studies in this MSc. project.

Resum´e
Hydrogen peroxid er et vigtigt kemikalie, som bruges i adskillige industrielle processer. Det
er en stærk oxidant og hydrogen peroxid kan oxidere en lang række molekyler i vandige
opløsninger ved oxygen reduktion reaktionen (ORR), hvilket muliggør at bruge hydrogen
peroxid som disinﬁcerende middel til vandrensning.
Grundet den stigende efterspørgsel
p˚a en mere eﬀektiv og lokaliseret produktion, har elektrokemisk produktion af hydrogen
peroxid f˚aet en tiltagende interesse igennem de seneste ˚ar. Lettilgængelige carbonbaserede
materialer er lovende kandidater som katalysatorer til elektrokemisk produktion af hydrogen
peroxid som et alternativ til katalysatorer baseret p˚a ædelmetaller.
Denne kandidatafhandling søger at afdække hvordan svovldotering af et carbonmateriale
vil p˚avirke den faradiske eﬀektivitet for elektrokemisk produktion af hydrogen peroxid.
Tre prøver af svovldoteret, mesoporøst Black Pearls carbon 2000 er fremstillet ved at
opvarme prøverne til 500-900 ◦C under tilstedeværelse af en H2S(0.05)/Ar(0.95) gasstrøm.
Overfaldekarakterisering af udoteret carbon Black Pearls 2000 og svovldoterede prøver blev
udført ved brug af XPS og BET teori ved brug af nitrogen adsorption-desorption.
XPS-m˚alingerne viser, at alle carbonprøver har et oxygenindhold p˚a 1-2 at.%, et svovlind-
hold p˚a op til 2 at.% of at svovlindholdet stiger ved for voksende opvarmningstempera-
turer. Adsorption-desorption m˚alingerne viser, at overﬂadearealet af en ubehandlet car-
bon Black Pearls 2000 har et overﬂadeareal p˚a 1575 m2/g, mens svovldoterede prøver har
overﬂadearealer p˚a 1356-1495 m2/g, hvilket er 5-15% lavere end for en ubehandlet prøve.
Det beviser, at svovldotering ved høje temperaturer har en mindre betydnnig for over-
ﬂadearealet. Elektrokemiske m˚alinger udføres under anvendelse af en tre-eletrodeopsætning
med et gasdiﬀusionslag (GDL) med p˚adeponeret carbon katalystor som arbejdselektrode
til undersøgelse af den katodiske halvcellereaktion.
Ved brug af denne opstilling blev
den faradiske eﬀektivitet bestemt. En roterende ring-skive eletrode (RRDE) benyttes til
undersøgelse af katalysatormaterialernes aktivitet.
Undersøgelser af den katodiske halv-
cellereaktion foretaget ved -0.2 V vs. RHE viser, at alle katalysatormaterialer har faradiske
eﬀektiviteter omkring 70-75% og udviser strømtætheder p˚a 6-7 mA/cm2. Der var ingen sig-
niﬁkant forbedring da svovldoteret Black Pearls carbon 2000 blev benyttet som katalysator-
materiale. Ubehandlet Black Pearls carbon 2000 har et svovlindhold p˚a 0.4 at.%. Det er
muligt, at dette intrinsiske svovlindhold er det svovlinhold, der kræves for at n˚a en satureret
forbedring af carbonmaterialets elektrokemiske egenskaber.
Bor doteret diamond nanopulver (BDD) blev undersøgt som katalysatormateriale i et tre-
elektrode set-up under samme forhold som Black Pearls carbon 2000. En lavere faradisk
eﬀektivitet p˚a 51% blev bestemt. Grundet den lavere faradiske eﬀektivitet, blev BDD ikke
yderligere undersøgt.

Chapter 1
Introduction
At present, there is an increasing demand for more eﬃcient and decentralised processes
for chemical synthesis and energy conversion conducted closer to the place of consumption.
Some of the most produced inorganic chemicals such as hydrogen peroxide and ammonia are
currently produced at large scale, centralised production plants. Hydrogen peroxide is an
important chemical involved in several industrial processes, mainly for chemical synthesis
and paper bleaching, as seen in Fig. 1.1. Furthermore, hydrogen peroxide is used as an
desinfectant for water puriﬁcation. The global annual production of hydrogen peroxide is
5.5 million metric tons (2015) [44] .
1.1
The importance of H2O2 and its production challenge
Today, hydrogen peroxide is mainly produced by the anthraquinone oxidation (AO) process
at highly centralised, large-scale facilities. In the conversion towards decentralised hydrogen
peroxide production facilities, electrochemical reduction of oxygen to hydrogen peroxide is
an emerging method with great potential. Electrochemical production can be carried out at
ambient conditions, tunable rates in a continuous ﬂow and with a small production plant.
There is no need for a hydrogen gas due to H+ generated by the anode in an electrochemical
set-up. If the electrical power used as feedstock comes from renewables sources such as
wind or solar power, the synthesis of hydrogen peroxide can be fossil-free.
Developing
small-scale, decentralised plants for hydrogen peroxide production that can be coupled to
renewable power sources would be of great beneﬁt, especially for water puriﬁcation in the
developing world. [7] Water is the only degradation product of hydrogen peroxide, which
makes hydrogen peroxide an important chemical for an environmentally friendly chemical
industry.

1.2
Current production methods of H2O2 and its problems
Hydrogen peroxide is a strong oxidant and has the ability to oxidise a large variety of
organic and inorganic molecules in aqueous solutions. It may undergo ﬁve kinds of reactions
depending on the chemical environment: decomposition, oxidation, reduction, addition and
substitution: [5]
Decomposition: 2H2O2 →2H2O + O2
(1.1)
Oxidation: H2O2 + M →MO + H2O
(1.2)
Reduction: H2O2 + R →RH2 + O2
(1.3)
Addition: H2O2 + A →AH2O2

Hydrogen peroxide is mainly produced by the AO process which is a mature technology
introduced in Germany in the 1940s. A reaction schematics of the AO process is seen in

tion and treatment of the working solution. A 2-alkylanthraquinone (AQ) in a solvent is
hydrogenated catalytically to a corresponding anthraquinol or anthrahydroquinone (AQH).
The reaction is carried out at temperatures of 40 −50◦C under a hydrogen partial pressure
up to 4 bar. Ni and supported Pd are used as catalysts. The anthrahydroquinone is oxidised
by air to produce hydrogen peroxide and regenerate the quinone. The oxidation is executed
non-catalytically at temperatures of 30-60 ◦C. The produced hydrogen peroxide is extracted
and distilled allowing for hydrogen peroxide concentrations up to 95 wt.%. [5]

the AO process.
The biggest advantage of the AO process is the high yield of hydrogen peroxide per cycle.
By separating the hydrogenation cycle and the oxidation cycle explosions are avoided since
hydrogen and oxygen are not mixed directly. As a consequence of these cycles, the AO
process steps are performed in batches which is highly cumbersome. Other rawbacks in-
clude the generation of by-products requiring regeneration of the working solution and the
hydrogenation catalyst and the separation steps involved in the overall cycle. The cumber-
some process needs centralised large-scale production facilities. Lastly, long transportation
to the point of consumption is required for centralised production and transport of highly
concentrated H2O2 is associated with safety concerns. Other production methods include
oxidation of alcohols which produces an aldehyde or ketone as by-products making it diﬃ-
cult to obtain a high H2O2 purity. Two of the most frequently used alcohols are 2-propanol
and methylbenzylalcohol. [5]

2.1
Catalysis
The purpose of a catalyst is to increase the rate of a chemical reaction without being
consumed during the chemical reaction. Heterogeneous catalysis involves solids that catalyse
reactions of molecules in gas or in a solution phase. The heterogeneous catalytic reaction
cycle can generally be divided into three steps: adsorption, reaction and desorption [9].
The cycle may start with bonding of one or two molecules, A and B. Secondly, A and B
reacts with the surface to form a product P bounded to the surface of the catalyst. Finally,
the product P desorbes from the catalyst. By investigating potential energy diagrams of a
non-catalytic and a catalytic reaction it can be explained why the catalyst accelerates the
reaction. The diﬀerence in Gibbs free energy from reactant A + B to product P is ∆G.
In Fig. 2.1 the bonding of A and B to the catalyst is exothermic and the free energy is
thus lowered. A and B reacts to product P on the surface where the role of the catalyst is
to decrease the energy of the transient state between reactants and product by providing
additional adsorption sites..
Figure 2.1: Illustration and energy diagrams of a catalytic reaction. Figure modiﬁed from [12].

As seen in Fig. 2.1, the energy path for the catalytic reaction is more complex but energeti-
cally favourable due to the smaller activation energy compared to the non-catalytic process.
The overall Gibbs free energy for the reaction is unchanged.
The optimum strength of the bond between the catalyst and the reactants and products is
qualitatively described by the Sabatier principle. The bond between the catalyst and the
reactant cannot be too weak, otherwise a reaction will hardly occur. On the other hand,
if the bond is too strong between the catalyst and one of the reactants, intermediates or
product, the catalyst will mainly be occupied by this very species, poisoning of the surface,
and only small amounts of product will be formed. The Sabatier principle is graphically
shown by plotting the activity (reaction rate) versus the adsorption energy.
Such plots
have a global maximum activity with a corresponding optimum adsorption energy, which
is neither too strong nor too weak, as seen in Fig. 2.2. These plots are known as volcano
plots due to their shape.

3.3
Surface characterisation techniques
When a surface is studied at atomic level it is essential that the cleanliness of the surface
can be controlled to a very high degree. In XPS, the signal from a sample comes from
electrons with element characteristic energies, and it is important that these electrons do
not collide with gaseous species and change energy. Therefore, surface sensitive experiments
must ideally be carried out under Ultra High Vacuum (UHV) conditions corresponding
to pressures below 10−10 mbar while the standard atmospheric pressure is 13 orders of
magnitude larger, 1.013 bar.
UHV is obtained by using vacuum pumps which can be
divided into two kinds. Open pumps that remove gas from the chamber and closed pumps
that captures gas by implantation, reaction or by freezing out. For the experimental methods
used in the framework of this project, vacuum is obtained by a combination of a roughing
pump with base pressure of 1 × 10−6 mbar and a turbo pump.
3.3.1
Surface composition using XPS
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) relies on the photoelectric eﬀect explained by Ein-
stein in 1905. The photoelectric eﬀect is observed when photons with a suﬃciently high
energy hit a surface and an electron adsorbs all the energy and escapes the surface. The
escaping electron will have an energy related to the energy of the incoming photon and the
binding energy of the electron. The kinetic energy of the electron with respect to vacuum
level is given by: [9]
Ekin = hv −EB −Φ
(3.1)
where hv is the energy of the incoming photon, EB is the binding energy of the electron
and Φ is the work function of the analyser and sample studied.
If the incoming X-ray beam is monochromatic, it is possible to map the electron density
as a function of kinetic energy of the emitted electrons. The electron density as a function
of binding energy is unique for each element.
This makes XPS an eﬃcient method for
both qualitative and quantitative element analysis of the surface of a sample. The chemical
surroundings of an element will cause a small shift in binding energy and therefore XPS is
also used for chemical analysis.

XPS is a surface sensitive technique. When electrons escape the sample they will have a

Set-up
The XPS instrument used in this project is a Theta probe from ThermoFisher Scientiﬁc.
The major components are seen in Fig. 3.4. The X-ray source generates the X-rays by
bombarding an anode of aluminium with electrons. There is a chance that a bombarded
Al atom will ionise and shortly after relax by either emitting a Kα photon (1486.6 eV) or
an Auger electron. Before the X-rays hit the sample, they pass a monochromator crystal
(monocrystalline Si) in order to avoid satellite peaks. The photoelectrons ejected from the
sample are analysed in a hemispherical analyser. When a sample spot is exposed to X-rays,
positive charging eﬀects can occur which results in shifts of the characteristic XPS peaks.
This can be compensated for by using a ﬂood gun.
Experimental approach
The purpose of the XPS experiments is to quantify the composition of a given sample
and to investigate the chemical states of the atoms by identifying shifted peaks. For each
experiment, four samples is examined: One raw BP2000 sample and three BP2000 samples
doped at 500 ◦C, 700 ◦C and 900 ◦C. Initially, an XPS sample was prepared by sticking the
carbon powder to a double adhesive carbon tape. The carbon tape contains small amounts
of silicon, which allows for distinguishing between XPS signals from the carbon tape and
the carbon powder.
In later measurements, the XPS sample substrate is changed to a silicon chip, where a
carbon ink is mixed and drop-casted on top of the silicon chip. The Si chips are ordered

Nitrogen adsorption-desorption experimental procedure
First the sample is degassed at high temperature to ensure a high cleanliness of the porous
system and the external surface. Degassing is done by decreasing the pressure from atmo-
spheric pressure to 400 µbar at a rate of 13.3 mbar/s at 20 ◦C. When the vacuum level of
400 µbar is reached, the sample is heated up to 200 ◦C at a rate of 10◦C/min. The temper-
ature of 200 ◦C is kept constant for three hours before cooling down to room temperature.
The sample mass is measured after degassing with a 0.05 mg accuracy. The sample mass
depends on the expected surface area and lies in the range of 70-200 mg. After degassing,
the nitrogen adsorption-desorption measurement is performed with a nitrogen gas at -195.8
◦C. This is done by dosing known amounts of N2 to the system for adsorption and then
decreasing the pressure for desorption. During a measurement, the absolute pressure goes
down to approximately 1 µbar. Depending on the surface area the mass of the sample this
takes approximately 12 hours for high surface area carbons.

Electrochemical measurements
For the electrochemical measurements two diﬀerent set-ups are used: a three-electrode
set-up allowing studies of half-cell reactions for electrocatalytic selectivity and a Rotating
Ring Disk Electrode (RRDE) for investigating the activity of the catalyst materials. All
electrochemical measurements have been carried out using the data collection program EC-

3.4.1
Half-cell ORR studied by a three-electrode set-up
The three-electrode set-up, allows the study of the half-cell reactions. It is used for testing
the faradaic eﬃciency for hydrogen peroxide production and the current densities of the
diﬀerent WE. The three electrodes used are known as the working electrode (WE), counter
electrode (CE) and reference electrode (RE). The current is measured between the WE
and the CE and the potential is measured between the WE and the RE. The WEs are
fabricated by spay deposition of a carbon ink on carbon paper, further described in the
following section. The three-electrode set-up is seen in Fig. 3.11.
Figure 3.8: Illustration of the three-electrode set-up used for electrocatalytic production of hydrogen
peroxide.
For electrocatalytic production of hydrogen peroxide by ORR, the WE serves as the cathode
and the CE as the anode. The WE and CE half-cells are separated by a naﬁon membrane
which is a polymer material serving as a proton conducting membrane. The naﬁon mem-
brane allows H+-ions to travel from the CE half-cell chamber to the WE half-cell chamber
but preventing the transfer H2O2 to the CE half-cell chamber. Naﬁon membranes are used
for PEM-fuel cells (proton exchange membrane) and have proven to have excellent thermal
and mechanical stability. The test of an electrode on the three-electrode set-up includes
three diﬀerent measurements:
1. Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) to compensate for the ohmic losses in
the electrolyte.
2. Cyclic Voltammetry (CV), for investigating the potential needed for a reduction cur-
rent.
3. Electrocatalytic hydrogen peroxide production at a constant overpotential for deter-
mining the faradaic eﬃciency.

Limitations for high-surface area materials
For high-surface area (> 500 m2/g) powders, the results should generally be questioned
especially if the measured C-value (from BET eq. 3.3) is larger than 300 or has a negative
value, which is a sign of micropores. BET theory is not perfectly applicable to micropores
without modiﬁcations, since micropores fall out of the general BET assumptions. When
adsorption takes place in a micropore rather than free space, only a limited number of
adsorbates can be adsorbed before the micropore is ﬁlled. For data analysis of the BET
results it was ensured that the C-value was positive and below 300. However, from Fig.
4.5 it is concluded that all carbon have mesoporous structures. From the BET results it is
concluded that the S-doped carbons have a slightly lower internal and external surface area.
From the XPS experiments it was concluded that the sulfur content does not vary signiﬁ-
cantly between the samples. Therefore the small decrease in surface area may be explained
by minor structural changes in the nanopowder when the particles were heated up in a H2S
environment. This proves the high thermal stability of carbon BP2000 nanoparticles.

6.2. Discussion of the ﬁts
6.2
Discussion of the ﬁts
The ﬁtted envelopes for the carbon and oxygen peaks follow the measured peaks with a high
precision, while the ﬁtted envelope for sulfur is of lower accuracy which is due to a low sulfur
content in the samples and thus a low S/N-ratio. This problem cannot be solved only by
increasing the number of scans or the X-ray dwell time since sulfur can be degraded when
exposed to X-rays. [2] For a homogeneous sample with sulfur contents, the X-ray exposure
of a spot can be minimalised by sampling an array of well-separated points (eg. a linescan)
with short dwell time. [2]
In order to ensure reproducibility of the results from the same sample for the same experi-
ment it would have been desirable to have XPS spectra from several data acquisition points
for all experiments, which is only the case for experiment 3. For every experiment, survey
scans from 3 points per sample were made but often small amounts of silicon from the
carbon tape were observed, as seen on Fig. 6.1 and thus the data point had to be discarded.
For data from the experiment 3, only one single peak was used to ﬁt the oxygen peaks, while
two peaks have been used to ﬁt the oxygen peaks for experiment 1 and experiment 2. For the
carbon spectra, chemical peaks at ≈284.4 eV, ≈286 eV and ≈289 eV can be observed which
are likely linked to C-C, C-O-C and O-C=O chemical shifts, respectively (table 3.1). In Fig.
6.3 (a) and (b) it is seen that the carbon peak shape changes signiﬁcantly for all samples
from experiment 1 to experiment 2. Fig. 6.4 (a) and (b) show carbon peaks for experiment
3 at two diﬀerent sampling points. It is clear that the carbon peak shape does not only
vary from one experiment to another. This variance is also observed for the same sample
in the same experiment at two diﬀerent acquisition points. Based on the large variances in
carbon peak shapes it can be concluded that chemical states of carbon varies not only from
one experiment to another but also for diﬀerent acquisition points for the same experiment.
Experiment 3 was performed with 2 acquisition points per sample, and the survey scans for
the two points for 1%-S are seen in Fig. 6.5.

6.4. Reproducibility and sample uniformity
6.4
Reproducibility and sample uniformity
XPS reproducibility can be checked by either observing the reproducibility from separate
experiments on the same sample or from the same experiment but from diﬀerent acquisition
points on the same sample. The easiest method for conﬁrming reproducibility should be
to show the reproducibility for diﬀerent points at the same sample, since sources of error
such as the possibility for minor variations in sample preparations, instrument settings and
small variations in X-ray focus can be excluded. Based on the peak visualisations shown in
Fig. 6.3 and 6.4 and the varying atomic contents, it is concluded that these experiments
are non-reproducible.
The varying O/C ratios indicate that the signal from the experiments does not only come
the carbon powder. Previous studies (by V. ˇColi´c, seen in Fig. A.1) of carbon tape spectra
show two non-negligible Si-peaks with a higher signal-to-noise ratio than any sulfur peaks
for experiments 1-3. Furthermore, XPS measurements of the carbon tape with the glue
removed were performed. The glue was mechanically removed by cleaving a piece of carbon
tape to a sheet of alu-foil. Survey spectra of carbon tape without glue are found in Fig. A.2
and A.3. Removing the glue prior to XPS measurements did not lead to a conclusion.
To gain further knowledge into the XPS sample homogeneity, a 0.4%-S (untreated carbon
BP2000) sample and a piece of carbon tape was investigated in an optical microscope, the
10x and 50x magniﬁcation images are seen in Fig. 6.6. The yellow colours seen on the
carbon tape are due to light reﬂected from ﬂuorescent roof lamps.

It is also seen that there are small bubble-like structures in the tape which could be explained

the samples which was seen in the optical microscope and conﬁrmed by reproducible XPS

Chapter 7
Conclusion and outlook
The main idea of the presented work is to investigate whether sulfur doping of a carbon ma-
terial aﬀects the faradaic eﬃciency and catalyst activity for electrochemical production of
H2O2. By annealing carbon BP2000 in a tube furnace in the presence of a H2S(5%)/Ar(95%)
gas ﬂow at temperatures 500 ◦C, 700 ◦C and 900 ◦C it was possible to increase the sulfur
content from 0.4 at.% up to 2 at.% .
Surface characterisation was performed using XPS and nitrogen adsorption-desorption. The
XPS results were drastically improved when the sample preparation method was changed
from sticking carbon powder on a carbon tape to drop-casting a carbon ink on a Si chip.
XPS allowed for the determination of the atomic contents of the carbon samples.
Car-
bon, oxygen and sulfur were present in all samples. The XPS experiments indicate that
the oxygen content decreases from 2% to 1% when the BP2000 carbon is heated up to
temperatures of 500 ◦C or more, but more experiments have to be done before this can be
clearly concluded. For untreated carbon BP2000 and S-doped carbon samples, type IV BET
isotherms were obtained with nitrogen adsorption-desorption hysteresis indicating that the
powder had mesoporous structures. The surface area for annealed carbon samples were
in the range of 1356-1495 m2/g and decreases by 5-15% compared to unannealed carbon
BP2000 with a surface area of 1575 m2/g. This proves that carbon BP2000 has a high
thermal stability. Identical pore size distributions were seen for all samples.
The sulfur doped carbon BP2000 powders were tested as catalysts in a three-electrode
set-up for studying half-cell reactions and in a RRDE set-up with 0.1M HClO4 as elec-
trolyte. In the three electrode set-up faradaic eﬃciencies were all at approximately 70-75%
but the diﬀerence in faradaic eﬃciency from one sample to another lied within the statistical
uncertainties. Reproducibility tests show that the statistical uncertainty is slightly lower
for untreated carbon BP2000 than for the sulfur doped carbons. Results from the three-
electrode set-up for studying half-cell reactions showed that increasing the sulfur content up
to 2% did not have an impact on neither the H2O2 selectivity nor the current density. No
clear conclusions can be drawn from the RRDE measurements, partly because 10 µL KOH
was added to two carbon inks to improve the carbon disperson in the ink for two of the
carbon catalysts. The addition of KOH to the ink seems to have an impact on the faradaic
eﬃciency.

Chapter 7. Conclusion and outlook
7.1
Outlook and further studies
Prior to this project it was known that untreated carbon BP2000 had a sulfur content of
0.4%. In order to clearly conclude that sulfur doping has no impact on the faradaic eﬃciency
and current density, a background experiment has to be carried out: A carbon material
with similar properties as Carbon BP2000 without sulfur content should be sulfur-doped
and tested electrochemically in its untreated and sulfur-doped form.
New potential dopant elements should be investigated, potentially at higher doping concen-
trations than 2 at.%. Literature studies showed that the oxygen content of a carbon material
aﬀects the selectivity. In these studies oxygen contents of up to 10 at.% were investigated.
[24] The role of the oxygen content should be better understood before introducing other
dopant elements.
Initially, the XPS samples were prepared by sticking carbon powder to a carbon tape. This
method seemed reliable due to the presence of silicon in the carbon tape, which should
make the tape and powder signal distinguishable. However, it was proven that this sample
preparation method is unsuitable and for future studies this sample preparation approach
should be avoided.
For RRDE measurements it was seen that adding 10 µL of KOH improved the ink dispersion
but it seems to have the unfortunate side eﬀect of changing the electrochemical properties
of the drop-casted carbon ink. A more suitable method should be investigated for better
ink dispersion. A general investigation of the eﬀect of ink pH on the faradaic eﬃciency and
current density is also of interest, especially in the context of electrochemical production of
hydrogen peroxide.
