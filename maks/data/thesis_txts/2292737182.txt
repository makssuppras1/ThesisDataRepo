Simulation and Analysis of Floating Vertical Axis Wind Turbines using
Object Oriented Programming
by Dimitrios Bizopoulos
Wind turbines are classiﬁed into two general categories depending on the axis of ro-
tation: horizontal axis (HAWT) and vertical axis (VAWT). Both have about the same
ideal eﬃciency, but VAWTs have not yet been beneﬁted from the years of development
undergone by HAWTs. Due to the large interest in application of wind energy deep
water, VAWT are experiencing a renewed interest in the wind industry.
The aim of this thesis is to provide the implementation of a design model for two types
of ﬂoating VAWTs based on the Double-Multiple Streamtube model (DMS) originally
developed by Ion Paraschivoiu[1]. DMS model calculations are based on the principle of
the two actuator disks in tandem at each level of the rotor and upwind and downwind
components are considered separately.
Later, the model is extended to an oﬀshore
platform and the dynamic analysis is conducted using the principle of virtual work
as a method to set up the correct mass matrix, stiﬀness matrix and damping matrix.
Diﬀerent wave and wind conditions are selected to calculate the dynamic responses, such
as the global motion. The whole process is implemented in MATLAB® using object
oriented programming (OOP) in order to have the advantage of extension for future
research and utilization of a graphical interface for a non-programming use (GUI). To
sum up, the main objective of this thesis is to implement a preliminary tool from scratch
and investigate the motion of the platform for diﬀerent designs of ﬂoating VAWT.
The results indicated fair agreement between the implemented DMS model and the
experimental results. Reasonable results also in the time and frequency domain for the
ﬂoating designs have been observed. It is also concluded that OOP has a signiﬁcant
advantage over procedural programming for complex problems. Moreover, a software
with GUI was developed which could be a useful tool in further research of vertical axis
wind turbines. This software can easily be updated to future double multiple streamtube
extensions.

1.1
History of Wind Energy
Since early recorded history people have harnessed the energy of the wind, wind powered
machines have ground grain and pumped water; while in sailing ships the wind has been
an essential source of power. One of the ﬁrst known instances of wind use in history
is the wind wheel of the Greek mathematician Heron; his wind wheel operated a mu-
sical instrument (Fig.1.1a). Moreover, windmills were used in Persia at early 200 B.C.
These early windmills consist of a simple tower supporting paddles made from bundled
reeds which spin the paddles around a vertical axis to grind grain (Fig.1.1b). Other
well known patterns of wind usage include the ‘panemone windmils’ in Iran at the early
middle ages, together with the ‘sunk post mills’ in Europe and the Dutch windmills at
late middle ages.
(a) Heron’s wind-powered organ
(b) The Persian, horizontal windmill
Figure 1.1: First known devices of wind use [2]
In fact the use of wind turbines that generate electricity can be found for the ﬁrst time
in the end of the 19th century. More speciﬁcally, in 1887 when James Blyth installed
a battery charging wind machine to light his home. It was a simple drag vertical-axis
wind turbine. A year later Charles Brush invented and installed a 12 kW direct current

was in the mid 1970s when the government of the United States funded NASA to enable
development and deployment of large commercial wind turbines which paved the way for
many multi-megawatt technologies used today. In the 1980s, low oil prices threatened
to make electricity from wind not aﬀordable but due to federal tax incentives in the US,

an invention which is still used today. Furthermore, in 1991 the ﬁrst oﬀshore wind farm

Speaking numbers, by the end of June 2014, the worldwide wind capacity reached 336

classiﬁcation for wind turbines is determinated by the axis that they rotate, vertical

Both HAWTs and VAWTs have about the same ideal eﬃciency, slightly higher for
HAWTs, but the ﬁrst one dominates the majority of the wind industry. The HAWTs
oﬀer the ability to adjust the pitch of the blades to catch the wind at the right angle and
collect the maximum amount of wind energy depending on the environment conditions.
Additionally, the blades are bounded at the top of the tower giving access to higher
winds. A structural advantage is that the aerodynamic torque ripple, provides cyclic
loading on drive train. On the other hand, VAWTs accepts wind from any direction,
and its heavy machinery is placed at the ground level. This gives an advantage to this
type of wind turbines as it is more convenient for operating in potential icing conditions
and is more suitable due to maintenance issues, especially for large scale units[1]. Due
to the design of VAWT, the center of gravity is also lower compared with HAWTs pro-
viding a beneﬁt considering the ﬂoating wind turbines designs. Furthermore, VAWTs
have simpler design-no yaw gear, drop cables and lower installation cost due to reduced
over turning moment. Also VAWTs may be quieter due to lack of blade tips. VAWT’s
drawback is that it requires more area due to the guy wires connected to the turbine to
the ground (Darrieus wind turbines), which increases the stiﬀness of the tower at the
upper bearing, for the onshore installations. Regarding the safety system VAWT needs
a mechanical brake system for extreme conditions where HAWT can solve this issue
with pitch control. Furthermore, both types have the same general components:
(i) a tower to support the rotor,
(ii) a rotor to convert wind energy to mechanical power,
(iii) a gearbox to connect the low-speed shaft to the high-speed shaft,
(iv) a foundation which could be diﬀerent types depending on whether it is oﬀshore
or onshore,
(v) a control system to monitor operation.

Taking a more thorough look at the VAWTs, they are classiﬁed also according to aero-
dynamic and mechanical characteristics and the lifting surfaces. Types of vertical axis
design include:
(i) the Darrieus wind turbine, a turbine which consists of a number of curved
aerofoil blades mounted on a vertical rotating shaft or framework which have
good eﬃciency, but produce large torque ripple and cyclical stress on the tower,
providing poor reliability.
(ii) the giromill which is a subtype of Darrieus turbine with straight, as opposed
to curved blades.
It has high starting torque, especially when combined with
cambered airfoils and pitch passive control, high coeﬃcient of performance and it
is more eﬃcient in turbulent winds
(iii) the Savonius wind turbine which is a drag type device consisting of two or three
scoops. They are self-starting but they have low eﬃciency.
(iv) the V-shaped type where the blades to each other form a ‘V’. They are well
known for the easy self-starting.
(v) the Troposkien wind turbine, which is designed as a ﬂexible cable of uniform
density and cross-section. By this, the stresses created by the centrifugal loads are
transferred as tensional stresses causing no ﬂapwise moments. This increases the
fatigue-life of these types of wind turbines.
It is imperative to be mentioned that ‘H’ and ‘V’ turbine conﬁgurations for a given level
of power production have shorter blades, compared to the rest models, adding an ad-
vantage to these designs. Furthermore, concerning the power coeﬃcient, ‘H-type’ have
constant tip speed ratio along the blade giving higher maximum Cp but lower accept-
able Cp in the whole range of the operational tip speed ratio. Subclassifying HAWTs
they are of upwind design, meaning the rotor is in front of the nacelle when the wind is
blowing. However, some turbines are of a downwind design, meaning the rotor is behind
the nacelle when the wind is blowing.

1.2.1
Myths and Facts
In 1997, an expert in R&D after several years of studies in both types of wind turbines,
VAWT and HAWT, concluded the following comparisson facts[1, 12]:
• MYTH
– HAWTs are commercially successful due to their inherent technical superior-
ity.
• FACT
– HAWTs are commercially successful due to enormous investment. As a mat-
ter of fact, one of the highest ﬁnancial support until 2000 for VAWT was
provided from the Department of Energy (DOE) in the U.S. in 1977, which
approved approximately $15 million of which only $1.5 million supported the
VAWT program[13].
• MYTH
– VAWTs had little commercial success and therefore have little commercial
potential.
• FACT
– The surface has not yet been explored on the technical potential of VAWTs.
• MYTH
– VAWTs have many inherent deﬁciencies to be technically viable.
• FACT
– HAWTs have at least as many deﬁciencies as VAWTs. HAWTs have merely
beneﬁted from the resources aiming to overcome the deﬁciencies.

2.1
Survey of Momentum Models
Aerodynamic streamtube models are based on the conservation of the momentum prin-
ciple in a quasi steady ﬂow where momentum based models are based on the actuator
disk theory. Below, a list of analytical momentum based models that can be found in
the literature are introduced:
(i) the single streamtube model which was ﬁrst developed by Templin and assumes
that the rotor is enclosed in one streamtube, thus the induced velocity is assumed
to be the same through the upwind and downwind area of the rotor. The model
is based on the propeller actuator disk theory which assume constant induced
velocity through the disk and relates to the turbine drag. The model seems to
overestimate power output and drag coeﬃcients for high solidity rotors at low
rotational speed[1, 30, 31].
(ii) the multiple streamtube model which divides the swept volume into a series
of bordering streamtubes. The momentum equation with Glauert’s blade element
theory is used after to ﬁnd streamwise forces on the blades in this streamtube
model. It was originally developed in 1975, by Strickland and it is considered
more accurate compared to the single streamtube model[32].
(iii) the upwind and downwind rotor halves model which replaces the turbine
with two rotor halves in independent series from an aerodynamic point of view[33].
(iv) the momentum-type wake model which assumes that the ﬂuctuation in the
induced velocity can be approached by a periodic-cosine type interference factor
in both half’s of the wind turbine[34].

2.2
Other models
Momentum models predict fairly the performance of VAWT when the blades are mod-
erately loaded and for small and medium tip speed ratio. On the other hand, for high
tip speed ratio and high solidity (S > 0.2) of the rotor, the prediction is non-accurate,
as the momentum equations are invalid in these regions. This is due to the fact that
momentum models assume steady-state ﬂow where they moderate the ﬂow momentum
losses through the VAWT rotor over one revolution[37]. Beyond momentum based mod-
els, other models to simulate vertical axis wind turbines can be found in the literature.
The two most well known models are the vortex (Fig.2.1a) and the CFD models with

2.2.1
Vortex models
Vortex models are based on the vorticity equations. They replace the blade element with
a lift-line representing the ﬂow ﬁeld at distances higher than one chord from the airfoil.
The advantage is that the pressure ﬁeld is not needed to obtain the induced velocities.
Therefore, the model can be used for high solidity and large tip speed ratio. Moreover,
vortex models can provide information for the wake structure near the turbine. There
are two main models for VAWT turbines based on vorticity equation:
(i) the free-wake vortex model (Fig.2.1a) which was established by Strickland et
al.[39] who was based on Helmholtz’ theorems for vorticity. It considers the rotor
blades as a series of segments along their ﬂight path.
The blade elements are
replaced by a straight vortex ﬁlament which represents the ﬂow ﬁeld.
A well-
known computer program based on this method is the VDART (Vortex method
for the Darrieus turbine). A modiﬁcation for the free vortex model was done by
Beyer et al.[40] by using curved vortex ﬁlaments instead of straight ones.
(ii) the ﬁxed-wake momentum based model which was developed by Wilson and
Walker[41]. It is a combination of the vortex theory and the momentum method
for single streamtube.
As long as the performance of the wind turbine is being concerned, vortex models
provide results of equal accuracy with those of momentum. Scheurich and Brown [42]
by using a vorticity transport model succeeded satisfactory results for power curves,
compared to the experimental results. Moreover, Sebastian, in his Ph.D, thesis presents
the advantages of applying vortex methods to ﬂoating wind turbines where he concluded
that satisfactory results are obtained[40]. The main disadvantage of this approach is
the high implementation and computational time needed to output results.
2.2.2
CFD models
The widely known computational ﬂuid dynamics models (CFD) oﬀer satisfactory results.
The computational model uses a grid around a three dimension model of the wind
turbine, in order to calculate the ﬂow around it. The higher the number of the grid
nodes incorporated in the model, the higher the accuracy of the results will be.
A
description of a CFD model, applied to VAWT designs, is described by Hansen and
Sorensen, where the main idea is that the inner part of the grid is rotated relative to the
stationary outer grid[43]. However, the time needed to implement the model in addition

with the computer power needed makes CFD scarcely usable in the wind industry where
lower order models are preferable.
2.2.3
Cascade models
Cascade models are based on the cascade theory, which is used in turbo-machinery
design.
The blades are assumed to be placed on a plane surface with the spacing
between adjacent blades equal to the rotor circumference divided by the number of
blades. Various adjustments have been made to this model such as the improvement
of Hirsch and Mandal who incorporated a dynamic stall[44]. The model requires more
computational time than typical momentum models but provides more accurate results
for low and high solidity rotors. Furthermore, these models do not result in convergent
problems.
2.3
Double Multiple Streamtube Model
Momentum models are based on the actuator disk theory, as mentioned before. The
basic momentum model, is the single streamtube model which was described in section
2.1. This model undertook two development lines. The one was splitting the single
streamtube in more streamtubes parallel to each other and the other direction was the
double actuator disc analysis. These two models were not able to calculate the inﬂuence
of the upwind part on the downwind part of the rotor. The combination of these models
produced the Double Multiple Streamtube Model (DMS).
DMS is an analytical model that takes into account a multiple streamtube system divided
in two parts. The model was originally developed by Paraschivoiu and it is described
extensiely in his book ‘Wind turbine design with emphasis on Darrieus Concept’[1].
Furthermore, the ﬁrst published model used two constant interference factors in the
induced velocities,one for the upwind part and one for the downwind part, which are
calculated by a double iteration. Later, Paraschivoiu with Delclaux F.[45] extended the
DMS model by considering diﬀerent induced velocities for every azimuthal angle for each
streamtube (see section 2.3.2.3). This model is referred in the literature as the DMSV
model. Additionally takes into account the inﬂuence of secondary eﬀects such as the
presence of the rotating center tower and the presence of struts and spoilers. Finally,
various dynamic stall models have been coupled in the DMS/DMSV models including
Gormont’s model[46], the adaptation of Strickland[39], the modiﬁcation of Berg[47],
the adaption of Paraschivoiu and the MIT Dynamic Stall model modiﬁed by Noll and
Ham, each and every one of them with diﬀerent accuracy and complexity (see section

The original model of DMS considers the interference factor constant in function of the
azimuthal angle. Therefore, there is one interference factor value for the upwind part

those circumstances, in a realistic environment, the width of the upstream area should
be smaller and the downstream area greater than half of the swept area.

2.3.3
Dynamic Stall
The dynamic stall is a non linear unsteady aerodynamic eﬀect that appears at high
rate of change of angle of attack, ˙a, in time domain. The rapid change can result in
strong vortex to be shed in the airfoil at the leading edge.
The vortex, containing
high velocity airﬂows, shortly increases the lift. However, the separation of the ﬂow
streamlines continues further in the airfoil and the lift decreases dramatically while drag
is increasing. The phenomenon appears at low tip speed ratios at ranges of zero to four.
Therefore, the aim of a dynamic stall model is to take into account the eﬀect of this
==PAGE:55==
Chapter 2. Simulation Methods
27
phenomenon and modify the lift and drag coeﬃcients in that range. Various models
have been implemented to couple DMS. In the case of vertical axis wind turbines, where
the airfoils are rotating, the angle of attack α is varying periodically during a whole
revolution. As a result of the lower tip speed ratio the angle of attack is high causing
loss of eﬃciency. Commonly, the eﬀect of dynamic stall occurs at very high wind speeds
or at really low rotational speed, as the value of tip speed ratio comes from the ratio
of ωR/V0. In this section, various adaptations of Gormont’s dynamic stall model are
presented, and have been coded in the preliminary tool.

2.4.4
Wave loads
The hydrodynamics loads on a platform, in oscillatory ﬂow have three components:
(i) the hydrostatic forces consisting of the buoyancy and the restoring terms, (ii) the
diﬀraction loads which consist of scattering wave loads and the Froud-Krylov force, (iii)
the radiations loads, due to the motion of the body and composed by added masses and
damping loads. For the simulation of the wave loads the Morison equation (MOJS) is
used. This is a semi-empirical equation for the inline force of a body in oscillatory ﬂow.
One of the assumptions behind the Morison equation is that it is only valid for slender,
submerged bodies. This breaks down when the ratio of diameter to wave length is above
0.2 (D/L > 0.2). When this ratio is exceeded diﬀraction plays a signiﬁcant role, with
wave propagation from the object dominating. In this case simulation of diﬀraction
loads should be included. For a body with velocity υ(t) the force is given by[56]:

(iv) all variables are calculated in the global reference system.

3.3.2
Second Design: Buoy Design
The characteristics of the Buoy design are adopted from the report ‘Deﬁnition of the
Floating System for Phase IV of OC3’ of the national renewable energy laboratory
(NREL) [29]. In OC3 report the designs concern a ﬂoating horizontal axis wind turbine.
In this report, the same values are considered for the ﬂoating part but the characteristics
of the wind turbine are changed by those of the VAWT . The design is simulated by
four degrees of freedom as described in a section 2.4.5. To be mentioned that in the
plots presenting the frequency domain results the ﬁrst vertical dashed line correspont to
the surge and sway natural frequency, and the second one to the pitch and roll natural
frequency. The assumptions made for this design are the following:
(i) four degrees of freedom are considered: platform surge and sway, pitch and roll.
(ii) the ﬂoater is considered as a cylinder and slender (D/L < 0.2)
(iii) the waves are considered small to allow application of linear wave theory
(iv) the wind and waves act in one and similar direction
(v) the correlation of the wind is not into consideration
==PAGE:93==
Chapter 3. Results
65
(vi) The body ﬁxed locations, where the mooring lines attach to the platform, are
located at a depth of 70 meters below the SWL.
It should be mentioned that the linear model is only valid for small displacements.
Additional information concerning this issue can be found in the report of OC3 [29].

To conclude, the results in the Buoy design show a worth mentioning coupling between
the pitch and the roll motions of the ﬂoating platform. This is due to the fact that
the wind turbine acts like a gyro during its motion. More speciﬁcally, this means that
when the tower is turning, a moment applied on the x axis causes a reaction moment
in the y axis and vise versa. This eﬀect is due to inserting the gyroscopic eﬀect to the
calculations. Furthermore, the hydrodynamic and aerodynamic loads seem to dominate
the dynamics of the system. It is crucial to mention that as in both designs the yaw
degree of freedom is not simulated, the substantial yaw torque moment generated by
VAWT does not appear. This yaw, if simulated, is counteracted by the mooring system.

4.1
Description
One of the goals of this thesis was the implementation of a preliminary tool for the
analysis of ﬂoating vertical axis wind turbines. After composing the main code of the
tool it was observed that the amount of inputs and outputs, were signiﬁcantly relevant
and diﬃcult to be organized.
Under these circumstances, a graphical user interface (GUI) in order to achieve better
control over the tool was developed. This was also encouraged by the fact that object
oriented programming (OOP) was used to implement the main code, which made the
procedure of creating a GUI straightforward. Furthermore, in OOP, programming prob-
lems are analysed in terms of objects and the nature of communication between them.
When a simulation is executed, objects interact with each other by sending messages.
Some of the advantages of OOP include:
(i) code re-usability: the objects created can be used for other research programs as
well. As an illustration, the airfoil object, developed in this tool, can be used for
various applications, by employing the object methods to ﬁnd the lift and drag
coeﬃcients as well as the critical and zero lift angles of attack.
(ii) design beneﬁts: large programs are diﬃcult to be scripted. OOP forces designers
to go through an extensive planning phase which helps in the later phases of the
design.
(iii) software maintenance: programs are not disposable and with OOP it is easier to
modify and keep the software updated.
(iv) encapsulation: once an object such as a blade is created, the knowledge of its
implementation is not necessary for its use.

5
Conclusion and
Recommendations
5.1
Conclusion
This study intended to investigate the history, feasibility and potential of the oﬀshore
ﬂoating vertical axes wind turbines. Driven by this goal, a simple preliminary tool was
employed with a graphical user interface.
The double multiple streamtube model by Paraschivoiu was implemented in order to
ﬁnd the aerodynamic loads of the VAWTs. The comparison of the results of the DMS
model with experimental data from the Sandia laboratories concluded satisfactory re-
sults providing a sound ground for the later workload. Various results are presented
which raise geometric considerations. Thereafter, two diﬀerent ﬂoating designs were
implemented, one TLP design with two degrees of freedom followed by a Buoy design
of four degrees of freedom. The deployment process as well as the characteristics of the
TLP design were adapted from Marinet D2.4 report and the characteristics for Buoy
from the OC3-HYWIND project intended for HAWTs design[28]. After the coupling of
the model, the analyses for the two designs were conducted. More speciﬁcally, the ad-
dress of the global analysis of these designs in time and frequency domain. The designs
were checked through statistical analysis of the time series and power spectra of the
global motions for the degrees of freedom that have been implemented. Furthermore,
the loads at the rotor of the VAWT were analysed.
Through the simulations for both designs the eﬀect of the 2P frequency is clear. The 2P
eﬀect, resulting from the rotation of the shaft of the FVAWT, leads to a large ﬂuctua-
tion of the aerodynamic loads on the rotor and of the internal loads in the rotor even in
steady wind conditions. The 2P eﬀect, could also be observed in the platform motion
in all the degrees of freedom.
Based on the comparisons between the mean values, standard deviation and maximum
values of selected response parameters under the steady wind conditions and turbulent
81
==PAGE:110==
Chapter 5. Conclusion and Recommendation
82
wind conditions, the eﬀects of the turbulent wind on the global motions have been eval-
uated. For both designs the mean values remain almost the same with the standard
deviation being excited by the turbulent characteristics. Furthermore, it is showed that
the low-frequency responses are signiﬁcantly excited by the turbulent wind. A criti-
cal aspect is that FVAWT suﬀers large platform motions in cases of above rated wind
speed. The higher the wind velocity, the higher the loads applied to the rotor. Finally,
the importance of the gyroscopic eﬀect is addressed, which greatly ampliﬁes the peak
amplitude in roll.
The most well ﬁtted conﬁguration for oﬀshore wind turbines remains ambiguous. HAWT
has an advantage due to the fact that its design research has continued to advance
throughout the years. On the other hand, VAWT technology almost stops in mid-1990.
However, wind industry seems to turn to VAWT conﬁgurations for high scale oﬀshore
wind turbines, even if the risks are higher due to their lack of development.
Concerning the programmatic environment, it is generally concluded that the use of
OOP is a strong advantage for implementing aerodynamic models for wind turbines as
well as simulations for ﬂoating wind turbines. The characteristics of the wind turbine
are represented relatively adequately and the ability of storing the properties as well
as using the methods provides further comprehensiveness to an external programmer.
Furthermore, the capability of using the objects in diﬀerent projects and software lessens
the programming time in the long term. Under a broader perspective, starting utiliz-
ing OOP in wind energy could create a code library beneﬁting for the later research of
future projects. A minor disadvantage is that OOP is not appropriate at representing
non-objects, such as the case of the simulation() object in which physically, a simu-
lation cannot be considered as an object. Overally, having gained experience in both
procedural programming and object oriented programming it is concluded in this study
that the later is more eﬀective in implementing complex simulations and for creating a
GUI and it is highly recommended, compared with procedural programming for wind
energy research.
5.2
Recommendations
Based on this preliminary study, the characteristics of the FVAWT have been captured.
The results show that the aerodynamic forces have large variations for the FVAWT.
This disadvantage could be overcome by the deployment of a novel rotor type, such as
helical blades. At the same time, this modiﬁcation vanishes one of the primary advan-
tages of the VAWT which is the simplicity of the design of the rotor.By the same token,
an optimized structure design and the adoption of three blades could reduce these large
variations of the loads. Furthermore, the global motion of the platform of the FVAWT
==PAGE:111==
Chapter 5. Conclusion and Recommendation
83
at high speeds could be alleviated by using a diﬀerentiated control strategy, in which the
rotational speed of the rotor could be reduced and sustain the generated power at the
same level when the rated speed is overpassed. Another aspect of this disadvantage is
the initiaton of the emergency shutdown. K.Wang et al has proposed an application of
both a hydrodynamic brake and a mechanical brake and has concluded that the appli-
cation of the hydrodynamic brake is expected to be eﬃcient for rotor shutdown and also
for reducing the platform’s global motions as well as the applied structural loads[67].
Due to the diﬀerent characteristics of VAWT and HAWT research should also focus
on a ﬂoater exclusively designed for FVAWT, as all the simulations done for VAWTs
are based in ﬂoaters intended for HAWTs. In ‘Floating axis wind turbines for oﬀshore
power generation- a conceptual study’ by H.Akimoto et al.[68] a diﬀerent approach is
proposed named FAWT, which has an inclined rotor support. It allows considerable
inclination of the turbine axis and its major mechanisms are installed above the water
surface. Therefore, the FVAWT is considered to be promising in the oﬀshore industry.
Concerning the preliminary tool implemented to fulﬁl the requirements of this study,
apart from optimizing the code in the simulation time perspective, additional function-
ality could be added. As in this study the structure is considered rigid, in later research
the elastic behaviour is recommended to be simulated, utilizing the standard Euler-
Berboulli beam theory, to compare the results with the rigid body design. Furthermore,
heave and yaw degrees of freedom can be added, for more aquarate results and to in-
vestigate the coupling between the motion in diﬀerent degrees, such as the coupling
between rotational speed and the yaw motion, which is considered decisive in VAWT
research and application. Additionally, the speciﬁc geometry of the ﬂoater design could
be implimented as in this study and in the preliminary tool the ﬂoating designs are
considered as cylinders.
Taking under consideration the above implications of this study further research should
be conducted on time and frequency domain of the VAWTs systems which could result
in insightful results for the improvement of these models.
