Abstract
In this study, The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro was evaluated gait analysis in
mind. Twelve subjects were recruited to participate in the experiment. The
subjects had to solve a few motoric tasks while wearing the Rokoko Suit
Pro, two types of recordings were made; single take recordings where the
Rokoko Suit Pro was calibrated before every take; drift recordings where
subjects did the tasks in a sequence that was repeated ﬁve times with-
out interruptions. The data was processed with custom made scripts in
MATLAB. Locomotion data was analysed where focus was on gait, and
joint angles of the knee and ankle, respectively. Five subjects returned and
were recorded using Vicon Nexus for comparison. The Rokoko Smartsuit
Pro showed higher standard deviation for knee joint angles in 2 out of 3
cases compared to Vicon Nexus. For ankle joint angles the Rokoko Smart-
suit pro showed lower standard deviation in all four cases, compared to
Vicon. Drift was also investigated where average absolute regression co-
efﬁcients,b, were found for the knee joint angle, where they ranged from
0.32 to 0.75 for all subjects. Standard deviation was lower for the drift
recordings than the single take recordings.

2.1
Gait and movement - Biomechanics
The science of Biomechanics deals with the mechanics, function and struc-
ture of biological systems. In humans, biomechanics has a strong focus
on ﬂowdynamics and the mechanics of the musculo-skeletal system. The
following sections focus on locomotion. The musculo-skeletal system pro-
vides beings with form, stability, support and movement of the body. The
skeleton offers form and support while the muscles create stability and
movement. Locomotion, or human gait is one of the most common motor
tasks there is.[9] [19]

2.1.1
Knee Joint
The knee joint connects the thigh and the shank, the femur and the tibia.
It is usually classiﬁed as a modiﬁed hinge joint. While hinges only move
around one axis, but the knee is capable of ﬂexion, extension and a ro-
tation of the leg and is therefore often refferred to as a complex ellipsoid

joint. Two large ellipsiod surfaces are separated by a large fossa at the dis-
tal end of the femur. The proximal end of the tibia is ﬂattened and smooth
laterally bearing two articular facets that are separated by a small peak
called the intercondylar eminence. Menisci, thick articular discs of ﬁbro-
cartilage make up the edge of the tibia that deepens the articular surface.
The knee joint is secured by multiple ligaments. Between the intercondy-
lar eminence of the tibia and the fossa of the femur are two cruciate lig-
aments. The anterior cruciate ligament prevents anterior displacement of
the tibia relative to the femur and the posterior cruciate ligament prevents
posterior dispacement of the tibia. The collateral and popliteal ligaments
reinforce the joint around the knee. Tendons of the thigh muscles aid in
stabilising the knee.[14] [19] When the knee joint is investigated during
gait analysis, it is usually deﬁned as the meeting point between femur and
tibia. All three axes are of interest with the sagittal plane being most often
in focus. Figure 2 displays a classic gait cycle. A few key points can be
easily identiﬁed in Figure 2, the stance peak ﬂexion is the lower peak at
the end of the ﬁrst green zone, stance peak extension is at the end of the
second green zone and the swing peak ﬂexion is at the end of the fourth
green zone.

2.1.3
Variability
Variability is found most biological processes. In short it can be described
as the inability to repeat a process without any differences. This is seen
in all systems of the human body. The central nervous system has a great
ability to memorise motions and motor tasks, thus making them easier to
repeat. This is especially appearent in gait analysis. A gait cycle is raraly
or never repeated in the same exact manner. Due to intra-subject variabil-
ity, recieving exact same measurements for the same subject is not feasible.
Studies have reported statistical difference between different age groups,
and also between various unhealthy and healthy groups. It is well docu-
mented that gait variability increases with age and with certain conditions

2.2
Concept and purpose of gait analysis
The objective of movement and gait analysis in humans is to gain knowl-
edge and understanding of the musculo-skeletal system whilst perform-
ing motor tasks by collecting quantitative data. Ultimately the aim is to
use the information to enhance medicine, provide pations with higher-
wuality medical care and consequently improve their quality of life[4].
Gait analysis is an essential tool for the diagnostic process and follow up
for various conditions that affect the musculo-skeletal system in particular,
e.g. cerebral palsy [A:cp]. Studies have also found statistical signiﬁcance
between a healthy group and groups with disorders that affect the central
nervous system.[20][A:]
Quantitative information on various elements can be gathered. Concept of
interests are e.g. center of mass, that can be of one or more segments or the
entire body, joint kinematics which investigates the relative movement be-
tween adjacent bones, the forces exchanged with the environment, loads
that result from being transmitted across sections of body segments or be-
tween body segments, body segment energy variation, muscular work.
The focus of the thesis is solely on gait and joint kinematics. Gait anal-

ysis has been carried out for the past decades and knowledge has only
increased with the constantly improving and developting technology. In
the past, such analysis was usually recorded with video cameras, which

2.3
Stereophotogrammetry
Stereophotogrammetry is the most common technology used to gather
movement data in a clinical setting. The beneﬁts of using stereophotogram-
metry is that it is considered as highly reliable. One such system is Vicon
Necus which is considered as the "gold standard" in the industry.[a:viconrev]
However, implementing that technology demands a large capital as state
of the art systems are expensive. The magnitude of the set up, both the size
of the equipment needed along with a studio that binds the motion cap-
ture to one particular place, present another disadvantage[4] The system
is based on multiple infrared cameras that are lined up around the space
where motion capture is intended to take place. Markers capable of reﬂect-
ing both visible and infra-red light are placed on anatomical landmarks of
the body. The cameras emit infrared light, which hits the markers lead-
ing to light reﬂection which is being recorded by the same cameras. For
a marker to be registered in the recorded space, it has to be visible to at
least three cameras simultaneously. Multiple markers on anatomical land-
marks, which then build up a 3D skeletal model . [a:vicon] Vicon Nexus
and other marker based systems demand knowledge and experience in
marker placing at the anatomical landmarks, which is one of two main
contributors to error. The other one is the soft tissue artifact, caused by
effect of the soft tissue between the markers and the bone [1][2].
2.4
Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs)
Inertial measurement units (IMUs), are build up of three small sensors; a
gyroscope, an accelerometer and an electro-magnetic sensor, respectively.
The gyroscope measures angular velocity and is used for orientation, the
accelerometer measures acceleration and the magnetometer acts as a com-
7
==PAGE:14==
pas. Combining the three provides rotational and position data of the IMU
[17].
Usage of IMUs for capturing movement data can range from using a
single IMU, to collect data on acceleration, angluar velocity or angular
acceleration, to multiple IMUs connected together where more complex
analysis are done e.g. the relation between segments is investigated as is
done in this study. IMUs have decreased dramatically in size since they
were invented and are commercially widely available. IMUs have already
been utilized for studies within sports[5], ergonomics[21] and gait[15].
Another beneﬁt of IMUs is that small devices are easily portable. [18]
The biggest advantage of using IMUs for motion capture is that it can be
done everywhere and is not bound to a location or a studio. The biggest
disadvantages of IMUs are the IMUs tendency to drift and intolerance of
proximity to metals. [17]
Now that prices and proportions of IMUs have fallen, some parties
have seen opportunities in connecting multiple IMUs together to build
frames or suits that can gather motion data of different segments of the
body to create a digital model of these motions.
One such suit is the
Rokoko Smartsuit Pro.
2.5
The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro
The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro consists of 19 IMUs, connected together within
the fabric of the suit, with the suit comes the program Smartsuit Studio.
The 19 IMUs are located on different segments of the body: one each for
right and left, foot, shank, thigh, hand, underarm , overarm, head band,
six are located on the back and the shoulders of the suit. Those IMUs
connect all together to the suits hub, a center connected to a computer
via wiﬁ. The signal is received in the Smartsuit Studio where a model is
reconstructed live. To operate the suit, a few measurements have to be
registered in a body proﬁle in SmartSuit Studio. Those are total height,
arm span, shoulder height, shoulder width, hip hight, foot length, ankle
hight, heel offset. The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro was designed with anima-
tion in mind as a low cost alternative to the expensive IMU suits that al-
ready exists on the market. However, the data extracted from the Rokoko
Smartsuit Pro could provide aid to gait and movement analysis. Other
IMU suits on the market are already in use to investigate gait and move-
ment.[13] Thus the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro is expected to provide similar or
same information. The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro generates data on each of the
body’s segments. The data provided is a timestamp, position (X,Y,Z), and

Materials and Methods
3.1
Subjects
Twelve healthy young adult subjects were recruited to participate in the
experiment,11 men and one woman ranging from 24 to 32 in age (SD).
3.2
Instrumentation
For the experimental process the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro was used, along
with its program, Smartsuit Studio. The bit rate of the Smartsuit Pro is
915.2 kbps at 100 frames per second. The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro was oper-
ated with a 6000 mAh Sandstrøm powerbank dual USB S660PPW14.
3.3
Experimental setup
An ambitious experimental protocol was created to keep options for a big-
ger analysis open. The experiment was executed in two parts. In the ﬁrst
part the focus was on recording data with the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro. All
subjects had to wear the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro while performing a se-
quence of motor tasks. Those tasks were as follows: 7 meter walk; sit-
down and stand up from a chair; step ups on an aerobics platform; hop-
scotch; and countermovement jump. The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro was cali-
brated between every single take. The ﬁrst three tasks were recorded ten
times, and the last two were recorded ﬁve times. At last the subjects did
all tasks in a sequence, ﬁve sequences in a row without interruptions. This
was done in order to investigate drift. For the second part of the study,
ﬁve subjects returned and repeated the ﬁrst three tasks of the experiment
but now using Vicon Nexus. A photograph of the experimental setup is
shown in Figure 5.
3.3.1

clear deﬁnitions of all joints as in the middle of the space between a me-
dial and lateral marker on the knee joint and ankle joint, respectively. The
Rokoko Smartsuit Pro does currently not provide joint angles, so the knee
joint was deﬁned as the the intersection between the thight segment and

3.3.2
Raw data to joint angles
Once data was recorded both using the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro, and Vicon
Nexus. The data processing was similar. Data was exported from Smart-
Suit Studio on a comma separated values (.csv) format and then analysed
in MATLAB. Custom analysis scripts were created to do the analysis in
MATLAB. The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro provides an ocean of values to anal-
yse, for the purpose of this study the lower extremities were in focus. The
segments for thigh, shank and foot were isolated and the the joints be-
tween them analysed. The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro provides position and
rotational values as mentioned earlier in section 2.5. The rotational values,
provided in quaternions were then derived to knee and ankle joints, re-
spectively. Once the joints had been isolated in quaternions they were con-
verted to Euler angles which were then presented in degrees. The process
for Vicon Nexus was simplerdue to its system software which automati-
cally calculates values for both ankle joints and knee joints, respectively,
which can be exported and analysed further in MATLAB.
3.4
Quaternions
Quaternions were ﬁrst described by an irish mathematician in the 19th
century but quaternions remained a mathematical fantasy until the 20th
and 21st centuries. Quaternions describe rotation in space. Nowadays
quaternions are commonly used in animation and video games produc-
tion, but also in operating systems of airplanes, jets, submarines and drones.[11]
Quaternions are preferred in those ﬁelds as they provide help with orien-
tation of the body, and because of other issues that rise with using euler
angles for this kind of rotation, that can result in gimbal lock, where it can
be impossible to detect between orientation. In clinical work at hospitals
and in the scientiﬁc community joint angles are most commonly repre-
sented in degrees. Therefore, the quaternions will have to be converted to
degrees. [A:Q] The focus of this chapter will from now be on how to ob-
tain the joint angles represented in degrees from the quaternion obtained.

4
Statistical approach
The ﬁrst task of this thesis is to see if the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro is cabable
of measuring joint angles. To do so measurements of joint angles were
obtained with the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro and Vicon Nexus, respectively.
Firstly, a Bland Altman plot comparing joint angles betwen 10 measure-
ments for each subject from the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro and Vicon Nexus,
respectively. Secondly, a comparison between standard deviations of ﬁve
subjects of Vicon Nexus compared with the same subjects with the Rokoko
Smartsuit Pro. A larger collection of standard deviation of the Rokoko
Smartsuit Pro will suppliment. This is to attempt to quantify the variation
can be expected from each subject using the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro and

with Vicon Nexus, respectively. The variation can also shed a light on the
Rokoko precision.
To address drift, a well known issue with IMUs, 2 minute recordings
from all 12 subjects where they perform multiple tasks in a sequence have
been analysed. Five rounds of 7-meter gait were performed and 2 gait cy-
cles for each subject have been isolated and analysed. First, once gait cycle
land marks had been isolated they were plotted with standard deviation
to investigate the variation. Therefore linear regression for each subject is
investigated. Thus, linear regression will be investigated from both cali-
brated recordings and from the longer 2 minute takes, respectively, to in-
vestigate trends. And at last the standard deviation between the calibrated
measurements of Rokoko Smartsuit Pro and the 2 minute recordings will
be compared, to see if the range of the measurements increase.

6.2
Addressing drift
Drift was investigated by ﬁtting least squares regression model for every
subject. Three respective points within each gait cycle were tested. Those
were the maximum ﬂexion during swing phase, ﬂexion during shock ab-
sorbtion right after heel strike and extension at toe off. As can be seen in
ﬁgure 19, not all regression lines were above zero. This applies to all three
points of the knee. The drift recordings showed lower standard deviation
than the calibrated single take recordings, but higher average absolute re-
gression coefﬁcients. The standard deviation indicates how spread the
values for each mean are. This variation is partly due to biological vari-
ability, but can also indicate how accurate the motion capture system is.
When comparing the standard deviation between the drift recordings and
the calibrated single take recordings, it can be assumed that the calibra-
tion process of the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro is a source of error. When the
regression coefﬁcients were considered, it was decided to investigate the
absolute values because the regression lines were not all above zero. This
gives the opportunity to focus on quantifying drift if it is found without fo-
cusing on directions. The average absolute regression coefﬁcient can also
be used to estimate the drift. since only absolute values of the regression
coefﬁcient were considered. The average absolute regression coefﬁcients
for the entire group ranged from 0.32 to 0.75, which is higher in all three
cases than the calibrated single takes, which were from 0.15 to 0.43. This
indicates that there is drif present. Since the recordings ranged from 2 to 2
and a half minute, it is difﬁcult to draw conclusion on exactly how much
drift there is.
However, when the drift recordings are compared to Vicon Nexus record-
ings, the standard deviations were lower than those of Vicon in 2 out of 3
cases, but the regression coefﬁcients were lower for all cases with Vicon
Nexus. This shows that the regression lines for the calibrated single takes
is both lower and within a smaller range than those of off drift. How-
24
==PAGE:31==
ever, the variability within the calibrated single takes is higher than those
of off the longer drift recordings. But since this was only investigated in
the sagittal plane, no conclusions can be drawn on the overall drift in 3
dimensions.
On errors and accuracy; Vicon Nexus and Rokoko do not deﬁne the
joint angles exactly the same way and are recorded differently which might
increase inconsistency. Another factor which limits the comparison is that
it is difﬁcult to reach and maintain a constant velocity over just seven me-
ters. For future references it might be smart to increase the distance. The
gait cycles that were isolated were the ones in the middle closest to a con-
stant velocity, usually 3rd to 5th stride.. That applied both for the an-
kle joint angles and knee joint angles, respectively. The calibration of the
Rokoko Smartsuit Pro is a source of error since a slight change in feet po-
sitioning will effect the model. As this was a pilot study it is now easy
to see what could have gone better throughout the process. For example
which tasks to have subjects perform and in what kind of circumstance.
We tried to optimise the choice of location as much as possible to resolve
issues stemming from magnetic interference. It was decided to record data
inside and barefoot to be able to compare the gait cycles between the two
systems. However it might have been better to record data outside.
Vicon Nexus users are demanded to have anatomical knowledge to be able
to place the reﬂective markers correctly on the joint, this is a source of error
that has been reported to decrease with experience [10]. This experience is
not needed with the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro, since it is simple to put on.
6.3
Limitations
Unfortunately there can be inherent ﬂaws in the application of surface
markers when attempting to quantify movement of the underlying bones.Skin
artefacts [4] and experience level of the investigator applying the marker
[5] both contribute to errors in data collection accuracy.
6.4
Feasibility
Today, the most common technology used to capture movement data for
analysis is Stereophotogrammetry, and Vicon Nexus in Particular, often
referred to as the "gold standard" because of accuracy. However such sys-
tems are very expensive as mentioned earlier. The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro is
a much cheaper alternative provided that it can measure accurately. When
gait or movement is analysed for clinical purposes, that usually requires
25
==PAGE:32==
patients going to the gait laboratory. The recording process can be quite
rigid since the motion capture can only occur within a deﬁned space. Gait
laboratories are often located at hospitals, where the setting is often very
controlled which might have an effect on people’s natural movement pat-
terns. Rokoko presents a viable alternative as data can be recorded vir-
tually everywhere as long as the suit is within range of a computer. This
could decrease visits to traditional gait laboratories and help capture more
natural movement patterns. In theory, patients could borrow a suit for a
certain period of time, wear the suit while doing everyday tasks in their
natural habitant, then upload the data collected that would then be anal-
ysed by professionals when they arrive.
It proved to be a challenge to get the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro to func-
tion at ﬁrst due to its sensitivity towards metals. On Rokoko’s website,
www.Rokoko.com, it is instructed that the suit should be at least one me-
ter away from any metals. The sensitivity towards metals is the Rokoko
Smartsuit Pro biggest challenge if it is to be useable inside. Most mod-
ern buildings today include metals where they are used in structure, pip-
ing, furniture, decorations and applications, this list is not exhaustive. The
biggest challenge of the experimental process became ﬁnding a suitable lo-
cation, which became very time consuming. Eight different locations were
tried with various results, sites tested include a gait laboratory, a parking
basement, dormitories party rooms, class rooms, a gaming studio, hall-
ways and outside in a ﬁeld. We also attempted to shield the IMUs from
magnetic interference by wrapping them in aluminum foil and also in foil
shiled bag, those attempts were not successful. Buildings from different
eras were tested and the ﬁnal location chosen to record the data proved to
be one of the older ones, i.e. a building that included the least amount of
metal.
A battery has to be plugged into the suit, the data gathered by the IMUs
is then broadcasted from the suits center hub via wiﬁ, so a router or any
device capable of broadcasting wiﬁis applicable, e.g. a smartphone. The
data is then received by the suits computer program, SmartSuit Studio
as was mentioned in 2.5. Before the suit is operated eight measurements
have to be registered in Smartsuit Studio. Then once the powersource has
been connected, the suit is ready to connect to SmartSuit Studio, where
it has to be calibrated. The calibration process can be done instantly, or
over three seconds. The user has to stand in a straight pose during that
process. The suit is calibrated with the straight pose shown in Figure ??,
which takes 3 seconds, but it can also be calibrated instantly. After doing
the whole process a few times, it was very easy to operate. The recording
process with Vicon Nexus is a little bit different where the marker place-
26
==PAGE:33==
ment is key and can be time consuming. The measurements needed to
operate Vicon Nexus is weight, total height, left and right pelvis height,
pelvis width, left and right knee width respectively, left and right ankle
width, respectively. The cameras are calibrated before recording and can
take a little over a minute, then a static recording is made to calibrate the
subject. Considering the recording process, the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro is
much easier to operate than Vicon Nexus. This opens the door to patients
borrowing a suit and then record themselves at home and send the data
recorded for analysis. The anthropometric parameters needed for the two
systems differ in a way that left and right are not assumed to be symmet-
ric in Vicon Nexus since measurements for both left and right sides are
needed. But the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro assumes that the users anthropo-
metrics are symmetric. The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro also leaves out an input
measurement for the width of the pelvis,
The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro is not the only IMU based suit on the mar-
ket today. The best suit iof that kind today has to be Xsens. Xsens have
developed their suit for over a decade so they have the edge on their com-
petitors. Xsens have faced the same problems that any producer of an IMU
suit has faced, their suit has been used for multiple studies ranging from
sports such as skiing, running, throwing to ergonomics. They have also
managed to sheild their suit well from magnetic interference caused by
proximity to metals.
To summarize: The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro is a much cheaper alterna-
tive to Vixon Nexus and can be used everywhere. But the Rokoko Smart-
suit Pro is very sensitive to metals and it drifts. The Rokoko Smartsuit Pro
is easier to use, but Vicon Nexus is more precise and is able to take as-
symmetrical body segments into consideration, the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro
does not require pelvis width, . The Rokoko Smartsuit Pros calibration
process could be optimised by producing an object with a certain length
which would be used to determine leg spacing during calibration in order
to decrease the error associated with the calibration process. The Rokoko
Smartsuit Pro should not replace systems like Vicon Nexus at this point,
however it can be a supplimentary tool to gait analysis
6.5
Future work
The future work with the Rokoko suit can be seen in two parts; developing
and improving the suit, in particular focus on magnetic interference. The
suit needs further analysis on drift in particular, the experimental setup
for this study could have been better since the long drift recordings lasted

merely 2 and a half minutes, instead it should have been a focus to investi-
gate longer periods. It could also be good to have direct comparison of the
same movements by having subjects both wear the Rokoko Smartsuit Pro
and reﬂective markers, and record data simultaneously with both systems
as was done with the Xsens MVN BIOMECH [15]. Further testing should
be done with different movements and expand into sports.

Conclusion
The Rokoko Smartsuit was estimated with gait and movement analysis
in mind. It was possible to record movement data and visualise the joint
angles without the restrictions of a conﬁnded space such as a gait lab. The
Rokoko suit displayed higher standard deviations for knee joint ankles
than Vicon Nexus, but the Rokoko suit showed lower standard deviation
for ankle joints than Vicon Nexus. For complete validation and as initially
planned, data should ideally to be recorded with Vicon Nexus and the
Rokoko suit simultaneously. This was the ﬁrst idea for this project which
stranded on the Rokoko suits sensitivity to metals.
