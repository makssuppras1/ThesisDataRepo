Abstract
An electrical car battery pack consists of a large number of individual battery cells. These battery cells
have to be interconnected by a joining technology to deliver the demanding driving range. Resistance
spot welding is a promising joining technology due to its good weld quality and low cost. The applied
process was a two-sided resistance spot welding due to machine availability. A tab-to-tab joint was man-
ufactured by resistance welding. This project aims to weld thin high conductivity sheets by a resistance
welding method and to evaluate the quality of the joint by mechanical and electrical tests. The material
coupons used to join was pure aluminium (Al) and pure copper (Cu). Before performing any welds, the
new welding setup was validated and calibrated to ensure good repeatability. The welding experiments
showed it was possible to weld different high conductivity sheets. However, it showed also frequent splash
and electrode sticking when welding aluminium or copper coupons. Aluminium and copper coupons
showed similar mechanical strength. The Cu-Al combination showed a higher load than the Al-Al com-
binations due to brittle behavior. It was possible to determine the electrical connection resistance by the
Brand method. The electrical connection resistance study showed the resistance decreased as the cur-
rent level increases for the aluminum coupons. The connection resistance did not decrease for copper
coupons due to similar nugget sizes at both levels of current. The lowest electrical connection resistance
was obtained on the Cu-Cu coupons and the highest resistance was obtained on Al-Al coupons due to
higher electrical resistivity for Al. Two methods were applied to calculate the connection resistance. The
Brand method and the single connection method were compared to the electrical resistance at the weld
joint. Single connection estimated the resistance at the weld joint best. The experiments involved a large
number of single welded samples containing different welding process characteristics.

Chapter 1
Introduction
As the transportation sector generates one of the largest shares of greenhouse gas emissions, new green
technology solutions are needed in this sector. In 2017, the transport sector contributes to 27% of the
total greenhouse gas emissions (based from EU-28) [1]. Just cars are responsible for around 12% of to-
tal emissions of CO2 in EU (CO2 is the main greenhouse gas generated by automotive vehicles ) [2] [3] .
To reduce the emissions from cars, is by making the vehicles more efﬁcient or changing the fuel used in
cars. Despite the cars are being more efﬁcient than previous decades (Society of Motor Manufacturers
and Traders claims the average new car emits 29% less emission than the average car from 2000), it has
been seen the new car ﬂeet average CO2 has increased over the last years [4]. One reason that the average
emission for new cars sold in Europe has increased is, due to the rise of SUV cars which are taller, heavier,
and less aerodynamic than conventional cars. Besides more SUVs on the roads, the European Parliament
also made a new regulation (applying on 1 Jan 2020) that new car ﬂeet average emission must be at 95 g
CO2/km [3]. Therefore, automotive manufacturers are trying to avoid multi-million euro ﬁned by replac-
ing the petrol and diesel cars with electriﬁed cars which are reducing the emission [5]. To deliver enough
power and driving range for an electrical car, a large number of battery cells has to be connected which
then forms into the entire battery pack. Each of these individual battery cells needs to be connected by a
joining process [6]. Resistance spot welding is a fast and good quality joining process due to high autom-
atization capability and maintainable process stability [7]. This joining process enables a connection of
each cell by an interconnection. This interconnection provides an electrical and mechanical connection
between each individual battery cell. However, these cells are interconnected by thin high conductivity
metal sheets which makes it difﬁcult to join by resistance welding since these materials have low electri-
cal resistivity and high thermal conductivity. Early papers have shown resistance spot welding can join
thin high conductivity metal sheets [8] [9]. These papers have only evaluated the joint mechanically.
Since these interconnections also work as an electrical connection, it is also important to evaluate the
joint from an electrical point of view. The joint is designated as a connection resistance and will act as an
electrical resistance. To ensure good weld quality, the connection resistance and mechanical resistance
have to be evaluated [10]. One paper has evaluated the connection resistance and mechanical properties
of a joint but the materials used were not high conductivity metals [10]. This paper aims at resistance
welded high conductivity metal coupons and will evaluate the joints both mechanical and electrical.

1.1
Problem Description
Experimental studies from earlier papers have shown resistance spot welding is capable of joining high
conductivity metal sheets. But these experimental studies have a focus on only evaluating one property
(e.g mechanical, electrical, thermal). To better judge, the joint and the joining method, several properties
of each weld joint have to be evaluated. Two different high conductivity metals coupons were investi-
gated in this project; Aluminium (Al) and Copper (Cu). The goal was to determine if the high conduc-
tivity metals could be welded by resistance spot welding and to determine the weld joint properties like
mechanical and electrical.
The experimental path followed three phases. Firstly, the power supply was validated and the load cell
was calibrated. Secondly, the thin high conductivity metals were welded by resistance spot welding in
three different coupons combination; Cu-Cu, Al-Al, and Cu-Al. Thirdly, the quality of the joint coupons
was examined by mechanical and electrical analysis.
The mechanical test on the weld joint specimens was the lap-shear test to evaluate the strength of the
joint. An electrical test on the joint was the four-point probe test for measuring the electrical resistance
and the Brand method to evaluate the electrical contact resistance of each joint.

Chapter 2
Battery and Joining technologies
2.1
Batteries technology
Electromobility becomes increasingly important as the automotive market in Europe is responsible for
27% of the total emission in Europe. Besides Europe, the world’s largest automotive market, China, aims
to have 75% of all gas-powered cars be electriﬁed by 2030 and 100% by 2035 [11]. There is a general trend
of shifting away from fossil fuel cars to electric vehicles [11] [12]. For example, in 2010 there were only
17000 electric cars driven on the world´s roads, while in 2019 the number had increased to 7.2 million
[12]. This results in high demand for electric vehicles as these cars is reducing the emission [5]. Typi-
cally, an electric vehicle battery pack consists of interconnected individual battery cells [8]. Lithium-ion
rechargeable batteries are the most common battery cells to employ in vehicles due to their high power-
to-weight ratio and high-efﬁciency [13]. The automotive industry uses three different battery cell types
which are solid cylindrical cells, prismatic cells, and soft pouch or polymer cells [14]. Figure 2.1 shows
the three common battery cell types and their features. The three battery types feature mainly differ in
size, geometry, and individual cell parameters such as capacity and supplied power [7].
Figure 2.1: Overview of the three battery cell types used in automotive battery applications. (left) cylindri-
cal cell, (middle) prismatic cell, (right) pouch cell [7].
3
==PAGE:11==
The battery pack in an electrical vehicle is commonly designed and manufactured in a pack-module-
cell structure, see Figure 2.2. The ﬁgure shows the battery pack position within an electrical car and the
stacking pattern of individual battery cells in a module together with other necessary components (here
it is pouch cells stacked together). To have enough power and driving range for an electrical car, a large
number of battery cells have to be electrically interconnected. To fulﬁll power and capacity requirements
for electrical cars, these single battery cells are connected in parallel and subsequently connected to
modulus consequently in series. The parallel connection ensures the highest capacity and amperage
requirements, while series connections enhance the supplied power [15]. This connection of cell-to-
cell in parallel or series can be achieved by appropriate joining techniques. The following subsections
addressed typical challenges found in battery interconnection joining and joining techniques.
Figure 2.2: Illustration of the battery pack and schematic of stacking pattern composed of cells and neces-
sary components [8].
2.2
Challenge in Batteries
When connecting a large number of battery cells by a joining technique there are several challenges the
battery pack is facing. These challenges associated with joining could be categories into mechanical,
electro, and, metallurgical challenges. For more information on other challenges, see Zwicker [7].
• Mechanical Challenges
The mechanical aspect plays an important role when it comes to battery module operation and
safety requirements. As an electrical car is exposed to a harsh driving environment, the battery is
experiencing dynamic loading and vibration. This harsh environment can cause short circuits and
ﬁre [6]. The battery interconnection joints will to some extent be pre-stressed due to the intercon-
necting by joining which may affect the dynamic response of the entire battery pack signiﬁcantly
[16]. If thin prismatic or soft polymer cells are used in the battery pack, these stresses may be en-
hanced due to dimensional changes of the cells during operation. It has been shown polymer bat-
teries cell behaves dynamically during charging and discharging where the cell increases in thick-
ness in the charge state and decrease in thickness at the discharged state [17]. The volume change
during charging and discharging is due to lattice expansion of the host material [17]. Therefore,
the joint should have a satisfactory joint strength with low strength variability to withstand these
4
==PAGE:12==
dynamic loading. Besides the joint strength, the joint method should not cause excessive deforma-
tion to the cell which may damage the internal connection [6].
• Electro Challenges
As the battery cells are interconnected regardless of the joining technology and the electric circuit
type i.e. parallel or series connection, one will obtain a joint that will act as electrical resistance.
This resistance will be situated at the connecting point between the battery cell and interconnector.
This resistance is also designated as connection resistance. The connection resistance has two
direct effects on the cell, module, or battery pack during charging and discharging of the battery
assembly. These two effects are loss of electrical energy across the interface and heat generation at
the contact region [7]. Figure 2.3 shows two battery cells connected in parallel. If the resistance is
larger on battery cell 1 more heat will be generated at cell 1 during charging and discharging [10].
This heat energy generated at cell 1 will be dissipated and thus less electrical energy can be used to
drive the car i.e the driving range is reduced [7]. The higher resistance at cell 1 will also cause the
total current to be divided unequally [10]. The heating at the contact region can also cause faster
cell degradation, and thereby shorting the vehicles driving range [18].
Figure 2.3:
Two battery cells are connected in parallel with uneven thermal and electrical load due to
different electrical contact resistance [10].
• Metallurgical Challenges
When connecting dissimilar materials formation of the intermetallic layer may occur. These inter-
metallic layers are not preferred to have at the connecting point because of their higher electrical
resistance and brittle nature. Intermetallic compounds found in the Cu-Al binary phase diagram
are causing a loss in mechanical strength and increase of electrical connection resistance. The
formation of intermetallic compounds is possible during joining and often controllable [19]. In-

2.4.1
Basic principle of Resistance welding
Resistance welding consists of the joining of two or more workpieces by heat and sometimes force. The
required heat generated by resistance welding is a combination of current ﬂow and the total electrical
resistance between the joining pieces. The heating generated can be described with Joule´s law, see the
following Eq. (2.1) [22]. Q(t) is the generated heat in Joule, t is the actual time, and T the total welding

the circuit. This electrical resistance consists of two types of electrical resistance. There is the volume
resistance of the material and the interfacial resistance of the contacting surfaces [23]. The current and
the total resistance may change over time. The law describes how electrical energy is transformed into
thermal energy [24].

To describe the basic principle of resistance welding, a simple two-sided two-sheet resistance spot weld-

a weld nugget. In the last step, the welded sheets are further cooled down and the upper electrode are

2.4.4
Contact resistance
The contact resistance depends on the contact area, pressure, surface condition, and metal composition.
Since all solid surfaces are rough on the microscale, it is necessary to consider the contact surface on
a microscopic level. Fig 2.12 illustrates mechanical contact of asperities on two surfaces. There is only
direct contact through the asperities which makes the area of true contact a small fraction of the nominal
contact area. As the electrical current ﬂows from surface A to surface B (see the electrical ﬁeld lines),
the electrical current lines bundle together at the separate contact spots. It will increase the electrical
resistance since the volume of material used for electrical conduction is reduced to a small fraction of
contact spots. The contact resistance can be reduced by increasing number of contact spots and their
dimensions. This can be achieved by introducing a higher mechanical load which will result in a larger
deformation of the asperities. The contact resistance can also be reduced by modifying the sheet sur-
face condition. For example, removing aluminium oxide layer on the Al sheet surface (The oxide layer
introduces a high electrical resistance) results in lower contact resistance [29] [24].

2.4.5
Dynamic of contact resistance
The contact resistance described above did not consider the inﬂuence of temperature caused by the re-
sistance spot welding process. Dickinson et al [30] proposed a six stage model for the dynamic contact
resistance of steel shown in Figure 2.13. The ﬁgure shows how the total resistance develops through a
welding sequence. In stage I) the sheets or workpieces are brought into contact due to the pressure pro-
vided by the electrode force. Under normal conditions, the sheets contain either surface ﬁlms, oxide lay-
ers, or other contaminants. This causes an initial high contact resistance. As the heating is concentrated
at the interface, the surface contaminant breaks down and it results in a sharp drop in resistance. Dur-
ing stage II), the heating continues which increases the temperature. This soften the asperities and the
contact area increases. This results in the lowest resistance (at α) . However, the bulk resistivity increases
as the temperature increases which results in the total resistance increasing. In stage III) the resistance
increases due to increase of the electrical resistivity. Between stage III) and IV), local melting would start
at the asperity contacts. At stage IV), the resistivity continues to increase until a peak (β) is reached. The
resistance starts to decrease as the weld nugget growths. In stage V) the melted region growth further and
mechanical collapse occurs at the weld region. This results in the contact area increasing and therefore a
further decrease of resistance. In step VI expulsion may occur if the nugget grows too large. The material
surrounding the weld nugget would also collapse under the electrode force. The electrode force would
squeeze the molten metal out of the welding zone and cause an increase in contact area. Therefore the
resistance drops sharply.

2.4.6
Peltier effect
The Peltier effect is a thermo-electrical phenomenon existing in the resistance welding process. The ef-
fect appears due to different Fermi levels of the metals to be joined. When a current passes through the
three material interfaces (two-electrode/workpiece interface and workpiece/workpiece interface) which
have different Fermi levels, the electrons will change orbits. The different Fermi levels imply that elec-
trons have different energy level which means electrons absorbs or release energy for the different current
direction. This results in generations of Peltier heating or cooling which can inﬂuences the weld quality.
The Peltier heating or cooling can be reversed by changing the current direction. The Peltier effect is
most severe when weld current ﬂows in one direction like in the DC power supply. The Peltier effect is
also referred to as the polarity effect in literature [31]. The Peltier effect has some inﬂuences only when
the difference in the Fermi levels of the two materials is large. However, Joule heating still dominates the
heat developed in the resistance spot welding process [32]. This phenomenon has been conﬁrmed in
other micro-welding cases where the DC power supply was applied. It resulted in higher heat generated
at the positive electrode which caused more electrode sticking [9].
2.4.7
Misalignment of electrode
The welding tasks performed in this project are considered in the category of small-scale resistance spot
welding. Therefore, geometry features and dimensions for the electrode are relatively small. This makes
it difﬁcult to do a good alignment of the electrodes. There are two types of electrode misalignments.
Figure 2.14 shows the two conditions for electrode alignment. One condition is wheb the contact surfaces
between the upper and lower electrodes are not parallel (a). The other condition is when the center of
the contact of the upper and lower electrodes are not coincident (b). These electrode misalignments
can lead to expulsion, excessive wear of electrodes, voids, and undersized weld nuggets [33]. Bobin et
al. [34] study the misaligned electrode degradation on galvanized steel that is 1mm thick. The upper
electrode was manually rotated to 5◦to create angular electrode misalignment. At 200 welds, the weld
strength dropped to 80% of the initial value. This was considered to be the end of life for the electrode
tip. Void was found at the edge of the nugget. The void reduces the effective diameter of the fusion zone
and thus reduced the joint strength. The upper electrode had developed small voids at the center of the
electrode at weld number 50. These voids further enlarged and a formation of cavities was observed at
weld number 200. The electrode misalignment caused faster electrode degradation and lower electrode
life [34].

2.6
RSW of large scale aluminium
As a response to ever-increasing requirements for fuel-efﬁcient and environmentally friendly vehicles,
the demand for using aluminum products in automobiles is increasing day-to-day. The reason is, that
aluminum is a lighter metal compared to mild steel which weighs two to three times as much. Aluminum
also offers high strength and good formability and can absorb twice the crash energy of mild steel [40]
[41]. Resistance spot welding is the most popular method to join car body sheets. However, resistance
spot welding of aluminium and its alloys has found to be more difﬁcult to join. One reason is that the
aluminium material is excellent at conducting heat due to its good thermal properties. That means a
high amperage current has to be passed through the electrodes for a very short welding time. Otherwise
considerable heat dissipation would take place during welding and a proper nugget will be difﬁcult to
form at the sheets interface [42]. Another problem is that aluminium has a tough, non conducting, ad-
herent oxide ﬁlm (Al2O3). The oxide layer has a high melting temperature at 2050◦C while aluminium has
a melting temperature around 660◦C. The oxide layer provides corrosion protection, but it also induces a
high electrical contact resistance at the interface between the electrodes and workpiece. When the elec-
trode contacts the sheet, the oxide ﬁlm fractures, creating small points for welding current conduction.
As the high amount of current is applied, these small points experience excessive heat, resulting in local
melting [43]. In the following subsection some important aspects regarding RSW of aluminium and its
alloys are listed.
2.6.1

electrode degradation, undersized nugget formation was observed and reduction of joint strength. The
rapid electrode degradation also leads to increase cost of redressing or replacing the electrodes and the

Dissimilar materials
To improve performance and to exploit the advantages of different materials, hybrid structures, such as
Al/steel, Al/Mg, Al/Al are widely used in many applications. However, it is important to understand the
joining mechanism and performance of dissimilar joints. For example, steel is currently used as the pri-

as more aluminium is introduced in the vehicle [43]. Joining Al to steel is difﬁcult since there nearly no
intersolubility between them. This results in the formation of brittle Al-rich intermetallic compounds at
the interface mainly consisting of Fe2Al5 and FeAl3 [51]. Figure 2.21 shows an image of the interfacial

specimens and only IF mode occurred. The cracks propagated mainly through the IMC layer and par-
tially through the aluminium fusion zone. By decreasing the thickness of the IMC layer has shown to
increase load-capability [51].

2.7
RSW of copper
Copper is widely used in ﬁelds of the electronic industry due to copper’s good electrical conductivity
property. In electronic devices such as electronic packaging and interconnections, it is desirable to have
joints that can handle high-temperature environments. With the RSW method, it is possible to form joints
that can withstand higher temperatures than bonding temperature [57]. But when copper is being used
as a workpiece the conventional RSW process becomes impossible. The conventional RSW uses copper
or copper alloy as electrodes which makes it difﬁcult to weld copper sheets together because the electrical
resistance of pure copper is lower than the electrodes or similar magnitude. This will cause the electrodes
to locally melt and causing severe electrode sticking. Using the joining method brazing it is possible to
achieve satisfactory joint at the copper sheets. Brazing joints use a ﬁller material that is placed between
the copper sheets (base metals) and the ﬁller material bonds to the copper sheets to achieve a joint. The
joining method still uses resistance welding technology to heat the ﬁller material and workpieces. Alariﬁ
and Suzuki [57] [58] both investigated silver oxide nanoparticle paste to bond copper sheets and achieved
bonding. Suzuki [58] applied 50 µm thick silver oxide paste between the copper plates and performed
the resistance welding with a current of 2200A and weld time of 99ms. By using silver oxide paste and
a welding current of 2200A it was possible to join the copper plates. A low-current level could be used
to bond copper plates. Duraivelan [59] study the strength of resistance spot welding joint of copper and
brass using ﬁller materials. Introducing silver phosphate as ﬁller material the weld strength was increased
compared to joint without ﬁller material. The weld nugget had obtained better penetration between base
metals. Hayashi [60] investigated spot brazing of aluminium to copper with a cover plate. A 20 µm thick
brazing ﬁller metal was placed between 0.8 mm thick pure aluminium and pure copper sheets. The ﬁller
material had high speciﬁc resistance and ensured to supply heat to the base metals. A current ranging
from 700 to 1100 A was applied and with a weld time of 2s. When a brazing ﬁller metal was used, the
tensile shear strength was increased by approximately 40 percent. Zhang [61] study dissimilar joining of
aluminium alloy 5052 to pure copper via RSW. 1 mm thick aluminium and copper sheet could be welded
21
==PAGE:29==
together by introducing a process tape between the copper sheet and the electrode. The process tape
was a 0.2 mm thick stainless steel type and it works as a thermo-compensating band. Bonding hardly
occurred between tape and copper sheets because the tape had the highest melting point and was cooled
down by the electrode.
2.8
RSW of small scale aluminium and copper
The application of resistance welding in the fabrication of electronic components and devices, such as
batteries for pacemakers is generally termed as small-scale resistance welding. The metal sheets to be
welded are relatively thin or small in diameter compare to large-scale resistance welding. Small-scale
resistance spot welding or SSRSW is different compare to large-scale RSW. For example, a lower electrode
force is applied to workpieces. More precise electrical and mechanical equipment is used. Materials
to be welded in SSRSW are mostly non-ferrous metal [39]. Zhou et al. [39] [9] study weldability of the
thin sheet during SSRSW using the different power supply. Molybdenum electrodes were deployed. Pure
aluminium, brass, and pure copper with a thickness of 0.2 mm were resistance welded. It was found
Capacitor-discharge (CD) power supplies could only weld Al and brass and not Cu sheets due to high
thermal conductivity and low electrical resistivity of copper. More severe electrode sticking at the positive
electrode was observed of Al welds when using high frequency (HF) and CD power supplied due to the
polarity effect. It was found to be easier to determine the weldability of Al and brass sheets. While SSRSW
of Cu causes severe electrode sticking [9]. Das et al. [8] study weldability and shear strength for differ-
ent battery tabs interconnect combinations. Tungsten electrodes were deployed to perform the welds.
Three different material combinations were resistance spot welded Aluminium coupons with thickness
ranging from 0.2 to 0.3 mm was welded with a single pulse. For copper coupons with the same thickness
required two pulses due to copper’s low electrical resistance. A single pulse was used to weld dissimilar
combinations. The shear strength results showed the loads for Cu-to-Cu with a cross thickness of 0.3
mm were lower than Cu-to-Cu with a cross thickness of 0.2 mm stack-up. That is because the heat gen-
erated in thicker coupons during welding was conducted away from the welding point. Al-to-Al joints
exhibit relatively low strength (Maximum mean load at 40N) compare to other combinations. While 0.3
mm Cu to 0.3 mm Al joints showed a maximum mean load of 173.1N for lap shear tests. This showed
resistance spot welding exhibits comparatively good joint strength for dissimilar material combinations.
However, RSW of Al and Cu tabs is generally found to be difﬁcult because the workpiece materials have
low electrical resistivity and high thermal conductivity. Cu and Al are also difﬁcult to resistance weld due
to different melting temperatures (Al 660 ◦C and copper 1080 ◦C). Projection welding which is a different
way of spot welding could overcome these challenges. In projection welding one of the tabs contain geo-
metrical features which focus the heat. Figure 2.22 shows how the current ﬁeld lines are concentrated at
the interface between the tabs interfaces. Projection welding increases current density and focuses the
heat. This increases the electrode lifetime because a ﬂat tip electrode can be used instead of a domed
tip[6]. Projection welding also enables to increase the thickness of the tabs and larger size weld can be
produced which results in lower electrical resistance of the joint [62].

3.1.2
Power supply MFDC
The power supply used in this project was a Medium Frequency inverter DC (MFDC). The machine was
the model Akzent T-5000 welding controller from Resistronic. This medium inverter works by, an incom-
ing three-phase AC passes through a rectiﬁer bridge, step (1) illustrated in Figure 3.4. The bridge changes
the current to a DC current which is fed into a capacitor across the DC bus. Here the noise is ﬁltered out
and the ripple effect due to the rectiﬁcation process is minimized, shown in step (2). The DC current is
inverted at step 3 to provide a bipolar square wave with middle frequency. Then the transformer converts
the high voltage down and the low current up before it is going through the rectiﬁcation process again
at step (4) [63]. This machine was found to have a middle frequency of 700Hz. The current is rectiﬁed
from the bi-polar waveform to a current ﬂowing in one direction which ﬂows into the weld head (also
called a secondary circuit). Using MFDC current welding machine has some advantages over AC ma-
chine. Compared to AC machines, the MFDC does not have any off time and no unbalanced line loading
which means MFDC has a higher efﬁciency heat input to the workpiece. This means the welding speed is
higher for MFDC compared to AC. As the MFDC can complete weld faster than an AC machine, it causes
less damage to the workpiece as less pressure being exerted during the weld. MFDC has a fast response
time, which can construct a constant-current control [64] [65]. The welding controller can deliver up
to 4800A according to the datasheet [66]. The controller consisted of 32 different pages with different
functions on each page. However, the only page no. 60, 49, and 1 were enough to perform this welding
task. The complexity of the welding process times is determined on page no.60 (welding schedules). The
welding process times were set to 4 times due to the simplicity of constructing and performing a single
pulse welding which is illustrated in Figure 3.5. If it was needed to have two pulse welding, the welding
process times could be increased which are illustrated in appendix A.6. Page no.1 on the current con-
troller contained relevant welding parameters which are listed in Table 3.1. Other welding parameters
such as squeeze time and holding time were not relevant to set on the controller since the mechanical
movement of the electrode was controlled on the programming unit. Page no. 49 (operating type) is just
used if welding type has to be changed from single to series spot welding.

3.2.1
Rogowski coil
A Rogowski coil is an electrical device that is used to measure alternating current (AC). It can also be used
to measure pulsed current. Figure 3.6 shows the Rogowski coil mounted around the current-carrying
conductor which need to be measured. The Rogowski coil works as alternating current develops a mag-
netic ﬁeld, an interaction between the coil and the magnetic ﬁeld will give rise to an induced voltage
within the coil. This voltage induced in the coil is proportional to the rate of change of the current being
measured [67]. Therefore it is necessary to connect the Rogowski coil to an integrator circuit in order to
produce a voltage proportional to the input current signal. This is also shown in the ﬁgure where the
square wave current proﬁle is integrated or converted to a sinusoidal current proﬁle.
Figure 3.6: Sketch of rogowski coil wrapped around current conductor [68]
.
In the Rogowski coil, an air core is used. This provides a good linearity since the coil is wound over a non-
magnetic material (air core). The air core cannot be saturated by the magnetic ﬁeld like the iron core can
on a conventional CT. A good linearity or low linearity error is the difference between the true current
value ﬂowing in the conductor, I, and the measured value (Vout/RSH) (where RSH is the sensitivity in
V/A). The other advantage of using the Rogowki coil are ﬂexibility, easy installability and a fast response.
However, Rogowki coils cannot measure DC current [68][67].

A current transducer can be used to monitor DC and AC current measurements during a welding opera-
tion. The current transducer was a LEM LT2005-S device. The device uses a Hall effect sensor in a closed

After calibrating the load cell, it was used to adjust the pneumatic system. The load cell was placed
between the two electrodes and a known pressure (bars) was apply to the load cell for a few seconds
(until a least 5-sec constant voltage signal was measured on the computer). This was done three times
for each known bar. The pneumatic system is used to transmit the compressed air into a given electrode
force. The system consisted of four different air service units shown in Figure 3.22. Figure 3.22a shows the
airﬂow diagram where the air ﬂows from the left to the right out to the solenoid valve. The ﬁrst air unit
(1) is used to relieve air pressure. Then the air is sent through a ﬁlter (2) where it is being separate from
water. The ﬁltered air ﬂows to the two pressure regulator valves (3) and (4) which control the pressure of
the air to the desired value. Both valves are connected to a solenoid valve that controls the direction of
the compressed air to the electrode piston.

Another problem with using one valve for the welding operation was the too high velocity of the upper
electrode. Too high velocity may damage the electrodes on the workpiece and shortens the electrode life.
A very high velocity might also cause a large deformation on the workpiece which creates a too large con-
tact area on the electrodes. While too low velocity will waste time and may require a longer squeeze time
[76]. By using one pressure valve it was not possible to control the speed of the upper electrode. Therefore
it was decided to use both pressure valves at the same time even though it increases the complexity of
the welding operation. It was also decided to mark the two valves shown in Fig 3.22b (by yellow stickers)
to control the pressure more accurately. Using both valves at the same time made it possible to control
the velocity of the upper electrode and therefore avoiding too high speed. This pneumatic system works
like the other pneumatic system on the TECNA welding machine which was in the same lab. The Fig 3.23

lower and upper chamber, a low force electrode head descent can be obtained. After the squeezing stage,

due to pressure at the lower chamber. The pneumatic on the PECO machine works by the valve 1 supply

4.1
Tungsten electrodes
It was decided to choose pure tungsten for material for the electrodes after discussing with electrodes
manufacture and supplier (Plansee and sunstone welders), and looking in the standard (RWMA class
material). To weld copper into copper coupons, it is recommended using tungsten material RWMA class
13. Tungsten is a suitable material since it has excellent stability at high temperatures (but the elec-
trical conductivity is relatively low for tungsten 1.79x107
Ωm
compare to copper 5.95x107
Ωm
). That is due to its
high melting point of 3420 ◦C and therefore high recrystallization temperature [77]. Besides, tungsten
is extremely hard and provide excellent wear resistance and is able to transmit proper electrode force to
the workpiece. ISO 5821 is a standard for electrode shapes for two-sided resistance spot welding cases.
However, it is for large scale resistance spot welding. Therefore, if a design is chosen according to the
standard all dimension has to be scaled down to work for micro welding cases. The Figure 4.1 shows the
chosen design for the tungsten electrodes. The design was decided based on a study from Masomtob et
al [78]. In the study, three different electrode tip shapes were compared. The Figure 4.2 shows the three
electrode tips where the best shape was found to be the round tip. This project focus on two-sided spot
welding and therefore the electrode tip was placed in the center, which made the design axisymmetric.
The axisymmetric design makes the electrodes alignment easier. Six electrodes was manufactured for
this project.

4.4
Electrical Connection Resistance
To evaluate the electrical connection resistance of the weld joint, it was decided to conduct the electrical
resistance measurement similarly as Brand et al. [10]. The resistances were measured by the four-point
probes method. It is brieﬂy introduced in the following.
The four-point probe method is commonly used in the ﬁeld of semiconductors to measure resistance
[79]. The four-point probe is used when the resistance is small (few Ohms or less). The advantage of
the four-point probe technique it eliminates or at least minimizes the contact and wire resistances from
the measurement. Figure 4.5 shows the four-point probe principle where the two outer probes 1 and
4 are used to supply the current. The two inner probes 2 and 3 are used to measure the voltage drop
across the sheet. The applied current enters and leaves the test sample by the outer probes. The current
has only one path (e.g going from probe 1 to probe 4). The current will not ﬂow through the inner two
probes because the voltmeter has typically high electrical impedance. The only voltage being measured
is between the inner probes which means the wire resistances (RW 2 and RW 2) and the contact resistances
(RC2 and RC2 does not contribute to the measurement (or has very small contribution). By knowing the
applied current and the voltage drop across the inner probes, the sheet resistance RS2 can be determined
[80].

wire resistances RW and contact resistance RC. The green arrows represent current ﬂow [80].

6.1.1
Initial resistance welding experiments
The depicted process was two-sided resistance spot welding of interconnector sheets to sheets repre-
senting the battery terminal. For the initial experiments, two different sheet combinations have been
employed. The input parameters applied in this initial study were based on literature and available ma-
chine settings. Zhou et al [9] conducted a study on two-sided resistance spot welding of batteries in a
similar welding setup. They used electrode force between 23N to 68N to weld copper to copper. A weld
time between 30 to 60ms was applied to weld copper to copper. However, when the welding time was
above 50 to 60ms the electrodes became overheated and surface voids were observed. Similar level of
electrode force was used to weld aluminium to aluminium [9]. 20ms welding time were used to weld the
aluminium combination. It was decided to begin the experiments with the same amount of weld time
for both sheet combination and around the same magnitude of electrode force. A single pulse with the
current time of 30ms (30ms current time set on Akzent) was chosen to see if it could weld these coupons
combination together.
A couple of aluminium and copper coupons were cut to smaller pieces to save materials for the ﬁnal weld-
ing. Each 100mm long coupon was cut down to around 33mm long sample. Each sample was cleaned
with acetone to remove oil or grease and afterwards the samples were cleaned from dust with an air pres-
sure hose. The squeeze and hold time were not measured due to complexity of measuring time both

Because the electrode tip was not etched for aluminium content before this expulsion occurred. Splash
could occur because of the current being too high which causes the nugget to grow too large. As the
nugget grows too large, the electrode force would squeeze out some of the molten metal of the welding
zone. The material around the weld nugget would also collapse under the electrode force. When the
molten metal is squeezed out of the welding zone and the surrounding material collapses, the electrodes
loses contact with the workpiece for a few ms. As the electrodes are not in contact with the workpiece,
no current can be supplied which results in zero voltage measurements on the transducer. It results in a
lower amount of heat input applied to the workpieces. Through all the welding combinations, two types
of expulsion was observed. One type of expulsion was severe splash which causes interruption of the
voltage measurement and relative thick aluminium or copper content on the electrode tip. For example,
severe splash could cause the whole weld nugget to stick on the upper electrode which destroyed the
connection between the weld nugget and coupons. Second type of expulsion was minor splash which
cause no interruption of the voltage measurements at the transducer and minor aluminium or copper
content on the electrode tip could be observed. The minor splash could reduce the joint strength as
material is lost.

6.1.3
Removing aluminium from Electrodes
When splash was encountered during welding of Al-Al, solidiﬁed aluminium was found on the tip of one
of the electrodes. The upper electrode experienced most aluminium content on the tip of the electrode
which could be due to the Peltier effect [31]. The upper electrode (+) would experience more heat than
the lower electrode (-) which causes more aluminium content to melt on the tip of the electrode. As
aluminium content sits on the electrode it creates a lower contact area (high roughness) on the tip which
can cause a high heat input and therefore splash. Figure 6.7a shows the tip of the upper electrode after
splash. Most of the splash observed during welding was observed on the upper electrode. To remove
aluminium from the electrode, a sodium hydroxide solution was used. 50ml of water was poured down
in a plastic bottle and after 4-5 teaspoon (3-5g) of sodium hydroxide pellets was blended in the water. Fig
6.7b shows the setup of for etching aluminium from the electrodes. The tip of the electrodes was held in
the sodium hydroxide solution for 3sec and cleaned in the water can and dried with a paper towel. Then
the electrode was inspected for aluminium content to see if more exposure of sodium hydroxide solution

6.5
Methods for calculating the connection resistance
As mention before in chapter 2, the electrical properties of cell connections are important to evaluate
for ensuring the quality of the battery assembly. If the electrical contact resistance varies a lot between
each weld it will lead to the unequally current distribution of the battery assembly. To determine the
connection resistance two methods were used, the Brand method and the single connection method.
These two methods were compared to the resistance of the weld joint to see which method performs
better. The following subsection describes the procedure of using the two methods.
6.5.1
Brand method for determining the connection resistance
To determined the connection measurement according to Brand et al. [10] a similarly set up of the test
sample shown in Figure 6.39 was established. To measure the resistance across the test sample, Brand
uses the four-point probe method. Each welded sample consists of two coupons, A and B, with a weld
area between the A0 and B0. Three voltage measurement points were marked on each coupon (e.g B1,
B2, B3). The electric contact resistance Rc is situated at the interface between the overlap. The parallel
resistance shown in Figure 6.39b, represents the electrical contact resistance. To determine the electrical
contact resistance, the actual electrical resistance of the overlap RA0,B0,meas has to be determined ﬁrst.
The contact resistance Rc and the ideal overlap resistance both contribute to the actual resistance of the
overlap. The ideal overlap resistance is between A0 and B0 where all contact resistances are equal to
zero (e.g at RA,CN = 0). All resistances shown in the resistance network can be calculated if the material
electrical resistivity ρA, ρB and dimensions of the coupons are known. The electrical contact resistance
Rc was calculated according to the following procedure:

6.6.2
Different layout
The two samples were tested with two different layouts, "Brand" layout, and "uneven distance" layout.
This was to test the robustness of the calculation method (Brand) and also to test the setup. According
to the Brand method, it should result in the same electrical contact resistance value since the theoretical
resistance can be determined at any point from the overlap (knowing bulk resistivity and dimensions).
The two layouts are illustrated in Figure 6.45. Each welded sample consisted of specimen A (to right)
and specimen B (to left). A pen and a ruler were used to mark these measurement points on the welded
samples. However, using a ruler and a pen has a limitation of accuracy. If the ruler´s scale was read
wrong it would result in the wrong placement of the measurement points. If the ruler was not parallel
to the welded samples it could also result from inaccurate marked distances. These measurement points
marked on the welded samples were around 1 mm in diameter to place the voltage probes at the dis-
tances. Smaller marks could be done to ensure more accurate distances but it was very difﬁcult to place
the voltage probes at the marks since the probes were handled by the user. It was decided to use a pen and
ruler for marking since they did not damage the weld joint. These weld joints were considered fragile to
handle since they required small forces to break the joint (below 100N). After marking the measurement
points, the distances between each point were measured again with a ruler. If the distances were wrong,
new measurement points were marked. On both ends of the welded samples, an area was marked for
the current supply (crocodile clips had a width of 5 mm which were marked on the ends of the coupons).

6.6.5
Measured electrical resistance over time
Doing the measurement of resistance at the nugget, it was found the resistance increased overtime at
the beginning of the experiment. Therefore, it was decided to test how much the resistance increased
over time. Two methods were done to evaluate the resistance over time. In the ﬁrst method, the voltage
probe was applied at one certain distance from the overlap (same distance for each voltage probe). The
resistance was measured ﬁve times at the distance by lifting the voltage probe up and down on the test
sample. After measuring the resistance ﬁve times on the test sample, the voltage probe was raised to a
certain height position (z-direction) in order not to contact the test sample. This method was similar
to how the measured resistance was obtained in earlier results. A certain period after the last measured
resistance, the resistance was measured again ﬁve times. This was done until the measured resistance
did not change over time. In the second method, the voltage probe was applied at one certain distance
from the overlap. The voltage probes were never removed from the test sample. After an amount of time,
the measured resistance was obtained. The measured resistance was conducted until the resistance did
not change signiﬁcantly over time or if the period was too long. The measurement of the resistance was
128
==PAGE:136==
done immediately after turning the Nano-volt meter and the current source on in both methods. Figure
6.65 shows the resistance over time obtained by the method 1. Each voltage probe was placed 57 mm
from the overlap. From 0 to 90 min, the measured resistance increased signiﬁcantly. From 90 to 150 min,
the measured resistance did not further increase. The lower measured resistance at 120 min could be
due to bending of the voltage probe or wrong placement of the voltage probes. Figure 6.65b display how
much the electrical contact resistance Rc changes over time due to an increase of resistance. The contact
resistance increase by 0.026mΩ. After the experiment, the current source was turned off and the sample
was replaced with the same sample (to simulate when conducting resistance on multiple samples on
the same day). The current source was turned on again and the voltage probe was placed at the same
distance. The sample showed same magnitude of resistance in the 40min it was exposed. To perform the
second method, the instruments were turned off for 2 hours. If the instrument (Nano-volt meter) was
turn off for 5-10 min it will still show a constant resistance.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The present work concerned the application of resistance spot welding in the ﬁeld of battery welding
tasks. The following can be concluded. The machine implementation study showed the new welding
setup had good stability and low force level which is required to have repeatable welds with low scat-
tering and to weld thin coupons together. The power supply results showed also good stability which is
important to ensure good quality of the welds. However, it was difﬁcult to monitor the actual weld force
level due to the current setup.
Joining thin high conductivity coupons by using the RSW method were successfully manufactured on
the new welding setup with MFDC current. Welding aluminium coupons with a thickness of 0.3 mm
were welded in a current range of around 418 to 550A. However, welding aluminium coupons resulted in
frequent splash due to solidiﬁed aluminium content at the electrode tip. Welding copper coupons with
a thickness of 0.3 mm were welded in a current range of around 294 to 380A. This current range could
be enlarged but would result in more severe electrode sticking. It was also possible to join copper and
aluminium coupons with a thickness of 0.3 mm by the RSW method. However, if a too high current was
applied it cause everything in the weld area to melt.
It was possible to evaluate the connection joint mechanically on all three welded coupon combinations
by lap-shear test. The highest mechanical load was obtained at the highest applied current on the alu-
minium coupons which also result in a different type of failure. The mechanical load obtained on the
copper coupon was at a similar magnitude as aluminium coupons. The Cu-Al coupons showed a higher
load than the Al-Al joint due to brittle behavior.
Conducting electrical resistance measurement on the joint by using a four-point probe technique were
successfully done on the new setup. The high current applied to the workpieces resulted in enlarging of
the nugget diameter which results in lower electrical resistance at the joint. The larger nugget diameter
resulted in the lowest electrical connection measurement and lowest scattering for the Al-Al combina-
tion. Two methods were applied to calculate the connection resistance, single connection and Brand
method. By using the Brand method, it was found the connection resistance decreased as the applied
current increased for Al-Al samples. The joint of Cu-Cu samples showed the lowest connection resis-
tance while the joint of Al-Al samples showed the highest connection resistance due to aluminium higher
electrical resistivity. Comparing the two methods to the resistance at the nugget showed the single con-
141
==PAGE:149==
nection is estimating the resistance at the nugget better. While the Brand method is overestimating the
resistance at the joint.
7.1
Future work
Based on the welding results it is recommended to use the resistance welding machine for further battery
welding cases. However, the welding result showed also problems like electrode sticking and difﬁculties
with estimating the weld force. For further investigation on battery welding cases such as tab-to-tab joint,
the following subsection describes the recommendation.
7.1.1
Weld head
The process force showed good stability of the force. To better related the mechanical and electrical
properties of the weld joint to the resistance welding process the load cell has to be implemented in the
welding head. Implementing the load cell would mean the actual weld force and welding current could be
monitor simultaneously. By knowing the actual weld force, will enable veriﬁcation of the actual stability
of the electrode force. It will make it easier to compare each individual weld joint by having both the
mechanical and electrical properties from the welding process. That will require a different design of the
welding head and new parts have to be designed and manufactured. With the existing weld head, it was
also difﬁcult to judge if the alignment of electrodes were good. Therefore, equipment that could align the
electrodes at the welding head could be developed.
7.1.2
Electrode sticking
Before using these electrodes again in a welding task, the tips need to be dressed which resets the elec-
trode tips shape and geometry. At the workshop, the electrode tip could not be dressed since the electrode
was made of tungsten. Based on the electrode wear observed from the Cu-Cu welds it is recommended
to ﬁnd a solution to minimizing the wear. When electrode sticking is prevented or minimized, higher
heat input can be introduced in the copper coupons and hence larger nugget formation. To prevent or
minimizing electrode sticking when welding high conductivity coupons could be done by introducing a
ﬁller material at the interface of the workpieces. Then lower current level has to be applied to the work-
pieces. Process tape could also be used for preventing electrode sticking. The electrode tip should also be
monitor frequently and by an instrument. For example, a surface roughness proﬁle could be obtained on
the electrode tip after certain welds to see how the pitting evolves. The electrode tip lifetime could also
be investigated to get an estimation of how many welds could be performed during welding of high con-
ductivity coupons. That could be done by using a new electrode tip and perform a certain weld several
times and inspect how the mechanical performs of the weld joint changes.
7.1.3
Coupon and surface condition
The copper coupons condition were not optimal and it is recommended to deﬁne the coupons in more
detail before producing them. Due to the bad handling of the strips, an excessive deformation was ob-
142
==PAGE:150==
served on the copper coupons. This cause a large variation in the welding results and welded samples
were more fragile in the electrical and mechanical tests. Frequently splash was observed on the Al-Al
welds, especially at the upper electrode. This could be minimized by modifying the surface condition
on the Al coupons. For example, mechanical grinding could be introduced on the surface to reduce or
removed the oxide layer which will cause lower electrical contact resistance. However, it will require
the mechanical grinding process close to the welding process to perform welding immediately after the
grinding process.
7.1.4
Four-point probe setup
Before performing more connection resistance measurements on other weld joints, the Four-point probe
setup should be systematically tested to see other errors which can be encountered. This error could be
minimized by improving the setup for measuring the electrical resistance. The following lists show some
example which could be tested on the Four-point probe setup:
• The voltage drop was read on the display on the Nano-Volt meter. Reading error could easily occur
as several digits have to be typed down between each measurement. One could take several volt-
age drop measurements over a period for one electrical resistance measurement to see how much
resistance varies.
• The digital ﬁltering could also be misinterpreted and cause the wrong reading of the voltage drop.
One could investigate how digital ﬁltering was programmed and how it works.
• Different applied pressure could also be investigated from low to high, to see how much the elec-
trical resistance measurements changes.
• Investigate how much the placement of the voltage probes affects the electrical resistance measure-
ments. The placement of the voltage probe could be done by a mechanical device that lifts voltage
probes up and down. This method could be compared to the placement of the voltage probe by the
user.
• Investigate how much the marking affects the electrical resistance measurements. The marks for
measurement points could be applied with a different method like a pen and a ruler or engraving
by laser.
• Investigate copper clamps with and without tightening the bolt to see if it inﬂuences the current
distribution.
• The electrical current distribution should also be investigated at several y and x-positions on the
test sample.
• The electrical current distribution could also be tested on different types of welded samples.
• Measure electrical resistance when a gap is introduced at the overlap.
143
==PAGE:151==
• The electrical contact resistance should also be determined as a function of distance for the whole
sample to verify if the contact resistances increase as voltage probe distance becomes smaller when
the instrument had been stabilized.
• Measuring the resistance at the nugget could also be tested. Measuring the resistance at the nugget
when the voltage probes are aligned and not aligned.
• One could also test the setup in a controlled environment where the temperature is steady.
7.1.5
Metallurgical analysis
The Cu-Al joint should be investigate to see if an Intermetallic layer has formed during welding. This
layer should be kept at a minimum thickness as it has a brittle nature. This would help to evaluate the
welding process parameter and further optimizing the process parameters.
