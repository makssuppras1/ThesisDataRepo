Abstract
Agriculture plays a significant role in global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, notably
contributing nitrous oxide (N2O), carbon dioxide (CO2), and methane (CH4) due to live­
stock management, manure handling, crop production, and soil cultivation practices [1].
In Denmark, agricultural activities account for approximately 26.1% of the nation’s total
GHG emissions, underscoring the need for precise, localized emissions data to inform
sustainable practices and policy development [2].
This study investigates GHG emissions within a 6­kilometer radius of an eddy covariance
(EC) tower near Hove, Denmark, representing a diverse agricultural landscape.
The
research aims to adapt and evaluate the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change
(IPCC) Tier 1 and Tier 2 methodologies to accurately capture localized emissions and to
identify discrepancies between local assessments and national inventory data [3].
Geospatial analysis techniques were employed to collect detailed data on livestock pop­
ulations, manure management, crop types, fertilizer application, and soil characteristics
using Danish agricultural databases [4]. An R­based program was developed to model
emissions precisely, incorporating Monte Carlo simulations to address uncertainties in­
herent in GHG estimation [5].
The results provide a comprehensive breakdown of emissions sources in the study area,
including enteric fermentation from livestock, emissions from manure management, fer­
tilizer use, and soil management practices [6]. Comparisons with national data highlight
significant discrepancies, emphasizing the importance of regional adaptations in emis­
sions accounting [7].
This research offers actionable insights into sustainable agricultural practices and regional
climate policies. By advancing methods for localized emissions assessments, the study
contributes to refining GHG accounting in small­scale agricultural contexts and supports
the development of more effective mitigation strategies [8]. Future research directions
include further refinement of regional methodologies and exploration of their implications
for climate­smart agriculture and policy development [9].

Introduction
Agriculture plays a significant role in contributing to greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions,
mainly from livestock management, crop production, and soil cultivation. Accurately un­
derstanding these emissions is vital for effective climate action strategies; however, ob­
taining precise, localized measurements presents significant challenges [10]. This thesis
examines GHG emissions within a 6­kilometer radius of an eddy covariance (EC) tower in
rural Hove, Denmark, offering a representative view of Danish agriculture, which includes
both livestock and crop operations.
Agriculture contributes approximately 10–12% of global GHG emissions and holds con­
siderable potential for mitigation through emission reductions and carbon sequestration
initiatives. In Denmark, agriculture accounted for 26.1% of national emissions in 2021,
with livestock as the leading contributor. Since 1990, Danish agricultural emissions have
decreased by 13.1%, largely due to reduced nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions. Methane
emissions from enteric fermentation have declined by 7.7%, although a 21.5% rise in
emissions from manure management resulted in an overall 2.8% increase in methane [2].
Advancements in nitrogen management have contributed to a 26.8% reduction in nitrous
oxide emissions.
The land­use, land­use change, and forestry (LULUCF) sector also impacts emissions,
averaging 4.3% of Denmark’s total emissions over the past decade. Forests serve as
carbon sinks, mitigating atmospheric carbon, while cropland and drained organic soils
remain net sources of emissions, contributing 9.6% of emissions in 2021 [2].
Climate Change and Agriculture
Climate change, driven by human activities such as fossil fuel combustion, deforestation,
intensive agriculture and livestock, produces GHGs—mainly carbon dioxide, methane,
and nitrous oxide—that trap heat and drive global warming [3].
This warming alters
ecosystems, threatens food and water supplies, and increases the frequency and inten­
sity of extreme weather events, directly impacting agriculture [11]. The interplay between
agriculture and climate is profound, with each significantly affecting the other. Address­
ing agriculture’s climate impact requires implementing sustainable practices, such as im­
proved livestock management, optimized fertilizer use, and increased carbon seques­
tration. Precise GHG measurement, aligned with Intergovernmental Panel on Climate
Change (IPCC) standards, is critical for developing policies, setting realistic reduction
targets, and fostering resilience to climate change [3].
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), established by the United Na­
tions in 1988, is a leading authority on climate science, synthesizing global research on
climate change, its impacts, and possible mitigation strategies [12]. Comprising experts
from around the globe, the IPCC’s reports influence climate policies worldwide. Among
the IPCC’s key contributions are its guidelines for calculating and reporting GHG emis­
sions [3]. These guidelines provide a tiered approach that balances the need for data
accuracy with practical feasibility. This ensures emissions reporting is consistent, com­
parable, accurate, and transparent. For agriculture, national­level GHG calculations often
rely on generalized IPCC emission factors, which can overlook region­specific variations
[3]. Since its founding, the IPCC has periodically updated its guidelines to enhance accu­
racy in GHG reporting, with key milestones including:
• 1996 – Basic GHG inventory methods introduced [13].
2
==PAGE:15==
• 2000 – Good Practice Guidance (GPG) introduced best practices [14].
• 2003 – Expanded guidance for Land Use, Land­Use Change, and Forestry (LU­
LUCF) [15].
• 2006 – Major updates adding flexibility and tiered methods [16].
• 2013 – Supplement for emissions from wetlands [17].
• 2019 – Further refinements to incorporate scientific advancements [3].
Study Focus
This thesis examines greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and carbon uptake within a 6­
kilometer agricultural zone in rural Denmark, focusing specifically on emissions of N2O,
CO2, and CH4.
By gathering and analyzing localized emissions data collected from
September 2023 to 2024, this study evaluates the feasibility of adapting IPCC method­
ologies, traditionally applied at national scale to a smaller, localized context [1].
The key objectives of this research include establishing a baseline for GHG emissions
within this agricultural landscape, assessing the adaptability of IPCC guidelines to cap­
ture local emission variations, and identifying discrepancies between localized data and
broader national inventories. To achieve these goals, an R­based program was devel­
oped to facilitate precise GHG calculations from agricultural activities in the target area.
This program supports data processing, model adaptation, and uncertainty analysis, tai­
lored specifically for the Hove region [18]. The findings aim to provide actionable insights
that promote sustainable agricultural practices and inform climate mitigation strategies.
The eddy covariance (EC) tower, standing 100 meters tall, is located in Hove (55°42’59’’N,
12°14’14’’E) within a diverse agricultural landscape near Roskilde, Denmark. Since Novem­
ber 2023, the tower has been measuring greenhouse gas (GHG) fluxes, including CO2,
CH4, N2O, and CO, between the landscape and the atmosphere. It provides direct, con­
tinuous data on GHG exchange, offering insights into the dynamics of biogenic and an­
thropogenic emissions in this complex environment. These measurements help identify
both known and previously unaccounted fluxes while examining uncertainties associated
with the data [19].
This study will calculate the relvant GHG emissions based on IPCC guidelines. These
measurements provide a valuable baseline that can be use to asses how well existing
methodologies align with the tower’s observations. Although this project does not directly
compare the calculated results with the tower data, it establishes a foundation for future
work to map GHG fluxes and refine the accuracy of inventory methods.
An analysis of land use data reveals shifts in the distribution of land categories between
2023 and 2024. In 2023, cropland dominated the area, covering 69.2% (4,631.246 hectares)
of the total 6,690.903 hectares, followed by grassland at 29.1% (1,950.091 hectares), for­
est land at 1.1% (74.690 hectares).
In 2024, these proportions shifted slightly.
Cropland decreased to 68.1% (4,555.377
hectares), while grassland expanded to 30.9% (2,070.766 hectares). Forest land ex­
perienced a modest reduction to 1.0% (64.760 hectares). These changes, while minor,
highlight a trend toward increased grassland coverage at the expense of cropland and
forest land, reflecting evolving land management practices in the region.

The grasslands in this landscape consist of various types, including fallow lands, perma­

Forested areas serve multiple purposes, from afforestation and natural woodlands to com­

Their integration within the agricultural landscape demonstrates a commitment to balanc­
ing ecological preservation with productive land use [4].

Problem Statement
Accurately assessing agricultural emissions at localized scales remains a significant chal­
lenge, even with advancements in technology and methodology [1]. Traditional approaches
often lack the precision required for smaller, heterogeneous agricultural areas, complicat­
ing efforts to accurately represent the complexities of such regions. Factors such as soil
type, agricultural practices, management strategies, and livestock production contribute
to this variability. These challenges are particularly evident within the 6­kilometer study
zone, which encompasses diverse soil types, crops, and management practices [22].
The Danish National Inventory Report adheres to IPCC guidelines for GHG emission cal­
culations. However, these inventories often rely on generalized data and assumptions,
making it difficult to accurately capture region­specific conditions. The limited availability
of detailed local data and resources further complicates the application of IPCC method­
ologies at finer spatial scales. While the Danish inventory benefits from access to exten­
sive data and specialized tools, this study relies exclusively on open­source data that is
publicly available to ensure the approach can be replicated by anyone. The Hove study
zone highlights this gap, emphasizing the need for methodological adaptations to better
account for the unique characteristics and emission dynamics of local environments. This
study aims to apply these methodologies to the specific target area, bridging the gap by
refining existing approaches to suit the Hove region [2].
Research Goal and Objectives
The primary aim of this thesis is to apply IPCC methodologies to evaluate greenhouse gas
(GHG) emissions within the Hove study area, focusing on localized agricultural practices.
By tailoring Tier 1 and Tier 2 approaches to the specific characteristics of the region, the
study seeks to provide more precise emissions data for CO2, CH4, and N2O.
This research will develop a localized emissions profile that reflects specific land uses and
livestock characteristics (e.g., animal types, manure management, and grazing periods)
within the study area. It will assess the effectiveness and adaptability of Tier 1 and Tier 2
IPCC methodologies when applied to smaller scales, identifying modifications necessary
to improve their accuracy for local assessments. The research will provide data­driven in­
sights to support climate­smart agricultural practices and inform regional climate policies,
ensuring better alignment between national strategies and local realities.
By addressing these objectives, this study aims to establish a replicable model for cal­
culating GHG emissions assessments in agricultural regions. This will contribute to Den­
mark’s environmental goals and support sustainable land management strategies, creat­
ing a foundation for more precise and localized emissions monitoring [23].

Hypothesis and Methodological Approaches
The central hypothesis is that IPCC emission calculation methods, typically designed for
national­level assessments, can be effectively adapted to provide accurate and meaning­
ful estimates at a localized scale. National­scale guidelines may not fully capture the vari­
ability in agricultural practices, land use, and environmental conditions found in specific
regions. By customizing these methodologies to incorporate local data and conditions, the
resulting emissions estimates are expected to be more representative and precise, offer­
ing relevant insights for regional climate policies and agricultural management. Success
in this adaptation could serve as a model for emissions accounting in other agricultural
regions, such as towns or municipalities, thereby enhancing the ability of policymakers
and stakeholders to implement targeted, climate­smart strategies [1].
To test this hypothesis and meet the research objectives, a comprehensive methodolog­
ical approach was developed to assess GHG emissions from key agricultural sources.
The study focuses on emissions of CO2, CH4, and N2O, employing strategies designed
to capture localized data and conditions for greater accuracy. The methodology consid­
ers various GHG sources from both crop cultivation and livestock production. Initially, all
collected data for the study area were processed and adapted using Geographic Informa­
tion System (GIS) software to accurately reflect local characteristics. For the emissions
calculations, a program was developed in R to ensure precise computations and facilitate
result visualization [18]. The calculations involved adapting and applying Tier 1 and Tier
2 methodologies from the IPCC guidelines. In this context, Tier 1 calculations use IPCC
default input parameter values, while Tier 2 calculations incorporate country­specific val­
ues whenever available to improve accuracy and relevance. To estimate uncertainty and
enhance the reliability of the findings, a Monte Carlo simulation was applied to the results.
The analysis primarily focused on parameter uncertainty and systematic errors inherent
in the IPCC methodology, while uncertainties in input variables, such as animal numbers,
management practices, and field areas, were not included in this simulation.
This thesis contributes to a more precise understanding of agricultural GHG emissions by
adapting IPCC methods to a specific Danish region. By employing both Tier 1 and Tier
2 approaches, it provides a comprehensive emissions baseline for the Hove area and
examines whether localized emissions data align with national inventories [22]. Through
detailed, region­specific GHG measurements, the research supports local climate actions
and policy decisions [24]. The findings aim to inform Denmark’s environmental strategies,
support sustainable agricultural practices, and offer a model for similar assessments in
other regions, ultimately reinforcing the agricultural sector’s capacity to mitigate climate
impacts [1].

Literature Review
This literature review examines greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and mitigation strate­
gies in Denmark’s agricultural sector, with a focus on adapting Intergovernmental Panel
on Climate Change (IPCC) methodologies in conjunction with national data to assess lo­
calized agricultural and livestock operations. The primary research question guiding this
review is: How can IPCC methodologies and Denmark’s national GHG inventory calcu­
lations be adapted to accurately assess and mitigate greenhouse gas emissions from
localized agricultural and livestock operations?
The literature search draws from reputable academic databases offering diverse and rel­
evant coverage. Web of Science, Scopus, and ScienceDirect provide peer­reviewed ar­
ticles on environmental science, agriculture, and climate studies, while Google Scholar
includes scholarly and grey literature such as theses and reports. Specialist databases
like AGRICOLA and Environmental Science & Pollution Management focus on agricultural
and environmental topics, while ProQuest Dissertations & Theses Global offers in­depth
research studies. Other resources, including SpringerLink, Taylor & Francis Online, and
JSTOR, contribute to cross­disciplinary insights. Additionally, IEEE Xplore supports re­
search on technological solutions for precision agriculture and emissions monitoring.
To ensure comprehensive coverage, keywords such as Greenhouse Gas Emissions (GHG),
Methane (CH4) Emissions, Nitrous Oxide (N2O) Emissions, Agricultural Emissions, Ma­
nure Management, and IPCC Guidelines were used. Terms emphasizing regional rele­
vance, such as Denmark Agriculture and Localized Emissions, further refined the search.
References to specific IPCC guidelines, emission factors, and mitigation strategies en­
sured alignment with the review’s goals.
The IPCC Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories(2006) and their 2019
Refinements provide standardized methods and recommendations for estimating GHG
emissions for national inventories, using tiered approaches ranging from default emission
factors (Tier 1) to detailed, country­specific parameters (Tier 2). These refinements in­
corporate the latest scientific findings, making them particularly relevant for Denmark’s
agricultural practices. Tier 2 methods, in conjunction with national datasets, offer more
precise assessments of emissions from sources such as enteric fermentation and manure
management [16, 3]. The choice of this methodology is based on its widespread adoption
and standardization, ensuring consistency and comparability of GHG emission estimates
across countries. Furthermore, it is the methodology used in Denmark’s national inven­
tory reports, making it directly applicable to the localized context of this study. By aligning
with the IPCC guidelines, this thesis ensures methodological rigor and compatibility with
international and national reporting frameworks
In Denmark, the Danish National Inventory Report 2023 and the Annual Danish Informa­
tive Inventory Report 2024, published by the Danish Centre for Environment and En­
ergy (DCE), provide essential national data on greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions [2,
8]. These reports cover Denmark’s emission inventories from 1990 to 2021 and adhere
to the IPCC guidelines for GHG estimation, with emissions categorized under five key
sectors. Particularly relevant to this thesis are the sections on ‘Agriculture,’ ‘Land Use,
Land Use Change, and Forestry (LULUCF),’ and ‘Energy.’ These reports not only detail
trends in GHG emissions and provide sectoral descriptions but also include uncertainty
assessments, methodological explanations, and progress tracking toward reduction tar­

gets, thereby supporting Denmark’s compliance with international climate agreements.
The data and methodologies outlined in these reports provide essential parameters, such
as emission factors, activity data, and methodological refinements, that align with Den­
mark’s specific agricultural practices and land­use changes. Furthermore, these reports
are the basis for comparing the results of this study with national trends, allowing for an
evaluation of how localized estimates contribute to or differ from the broader national con­

Several articles were used to understand methane (CH4) and nitrous oxide (N2O) emis­

drivers, mitigation strategies, and methodological approaches. Research highlights that
feed composition, dietary optimization, and improved productivity are critical strategies for
reducing enteric fermentation emissions from livestock[25]. Manure management prac­
tices, such as appropriate storage capacities, anaerobic digestion, and strategic timing of
manure application during crop­growing seasons, are equally important for reducing both
CH4 and N2O emissions [2, 26].
Of particular relevance, the article ”Livestock and Greenhouse Gas Emissions: The Im­
portance of Getting the Numbers Right”[6] explores the variability in global estimates of
livestock­related emissions, which range from 8% to 51%. It attributes these discrep­
ancies to differences in methodologies, particularly deviations from internationally stan­
dardized protocols like those of the IPCC and FAO. This underscores the importance of
adhering to consistent, well­recognized methodologies for emission calculations, which is
a central approach in this thesis.
Additionally, the article ”Impact of Danish Livestock and Manure Management Regula­
tions on Nitrogen Pollution, Crop Production, and Economy”[27] highlights the critical role

such as farm­specific nitrogen quotas, mandatory manure storage capacities, and timing
restrictions for manure application have reduced nitrogen losses while improving nitro­
gen use efficiency. These regulations are directly relevant to this study, as they align
with the IPCC methodologies and provide a practical framework for analyzing emissions
reductions in Denmark’s agricultural sector.
Nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions, primarily from fertilizer use and manure application, are
addressed in studies on nitrogen cycling and volatilization [28]. National guidelines on nu­
trient management, such as the Vejledning om gødskning og harmoniregler, complement
IPCC methodologies, focusing on emissions reduction strategies tailored to Denmark’s
climatic and soil conditions [29].
Land­use changes and soil management practices significantly influence GHG emissions.
Emissions from drained peat soils, for example, are critical in Denmark due to their trans­
formation from carbon sinks to sources of CO2 and N2O. Reports on soil organic carbon
stocks and rewetted soils emphasize mitigation strategies such as rewetting peatlands
and adopting less intensive agricultural practices [30, 31].
Crop rotations and residue management impact N2O emissions through nitrogen cycling.
Improved practices can reduce emissions while enhancing soil fertility, aligning with IPCC
guidelines for sustainable land management [26].
Finally, the uncertainty in greenhouse gas emissions calculations was assessed in align­
ment with the guidelines outlined in Chapter 3.5: Uncertainties associated with the Ap­
proaches of the IPCC 2006 Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories[16]. This
chapter encourages the use of Monte Carlo simulations for propagating uncertainty in

leveraging the strengths of Monte Carlo simulation for uncertainty estimation.

3
Theory
This section outlines the theoretical foundations necessary for understanding greenhouse
gas (GHG) emissions from the agricultural and livestock sectors, as defined in this thesis.
Here, all emissions categorized under livestock are referred to as animal husbandry, en­
compassing methane (CH4) emissions from enteric fermentation and both nitrous oxide
(N2O) and CH4 emissions from manure management. Key factors influencing these emis­
sions include feed intake, dietary composition, and manure handling practices [10, 32]. In
contrast, all emissions categorized under agriculture pertain to emissions from cropland,
forest land, and grassland. These emissions are primarily driven by activities such as
soil disturbance, fertilizer application, and land­use changes, contributing significantly to
nitrous oxide (N2O) and carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions [33, 34, 31].
To ensure accurate accounting of GHG emissions, this thesis draws on established method­
ologies from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and other relevant
guidelines. These include the IPCC’s tiered approaches for emission estimation and rec­
ommendations for incorporating uncertainty into calculations, providing a robust frame­
work for emissions analysis [16, 3].
3.1
Livestock
Livestock plays an essential role in global food production, providing essential nutrition,
income, and employment. However, the environmental impact of livestock production has
become a growing concern due to its significant contribution to greenhouse gas (GHG)
emissions [32].
The main sources of GHG emissions from livestock include methane (CH4) from enteric
fermentation in ruminants and manure management, as well as direct and indirect nitrous
oxide (N2O) emissions resulting from manure storage and processes such as volatilization
and leaching. It is important to note that carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions from livestock
respiration are excluded from GHG accounting according to the IPCC (2006) guidelines
and the Kyoto Protocol. Consequently, these emissions are also excluded from this as­
sessment, although they do influence the overall CO2 balance. This exclusion is based
on the fact that livestock respiration is part of a rapid biological cycle, where the CO2
absorbed by plants during photosynthesis offsets the CO2 released by animals [32].
In Denmark, the National Inventory Report [2] underscores the significant contribution of
livestock to the overall agricultural GHG emissions profile. Estimating GHG emissions
accurately in this sector depends on comprehensively understanding different factors,
including feed intake, manure excretion rates, housing systems, manure management
practices, and the number and types of livestock. The type and magnitude of emissions
generated are influenced by these elements collectively, which provides insight into ef­
fective mitigation and policy development strategies.
The upcoming sections will cover the GHG emissions from livestock and provide a com­
prehensive description of their characteristics and factors that impact their emissions.
3.1.1
Enteric Fermentation
Enteric fermentation is a natural digestive process that occurs in the stomachs of ruminant
animals such as cattle, sheep, and goats. During this process, microbes in the digestive
system break down feed, particularly fibrous plant materials, to aid in nutrient absorption.
10
==PAGE:23==
This microbial digestion produces methane (CH4) as a byproduct, which is then released
by the animal primarily through belching. Methane is a potent greenhouse gas, and emis­
sions from enteric fermentation contribute significantly to the overall GHG emissions from
the livestock sector. The magnitude of CH4 emissions from enteric fermentation depends
on diverse factors, including the type of digestive tract, feed consumption, age, and weight
of the animal [3].
The digestive system significantly influences the rate of methane (CH4) emissions from
livestock. Ruminant animals, such as cattle, buffalo, goats, and sheep, possess a spe­
cialized digestive chamber known as the rumen. This chamber allows them to efficiently
break down plant­based fibers, including cellulose, through microbial fermentation. This
process is a major contributor to methane emissions due to the fermentation activity within
the rumen. In contrast, non­ruminant livestock, such as horses, produce relatively lower
levels of methane because their digestive systems do not support the same extent of
microbial fermentation. Nevertheless, emissions from non­ruminant animals are still con­
sidered in enteric fermentation accounting, as they contribute to overall greenhouse gas
emissions [32].
Feed intake is another critical factor that directly impacts methane emissions. The greater
the feed intake, the more methane is produced, as the volume of fermentable substrates in
the digestive system increases. This relationship is particularly relevant for larger animals
or those in high­production stages (e.g., dairy cattle), as their higher energy and nutrient
requirements result in increased feed consumption and subsequent methane output [32].
Age and weight of the livestock also influence methane emissions. Younger animals may
initially produce lower levels of methane due to their developing rumen, but as they mature
and their digestive systems become fully functional, emissions increase with their capacity
to process more fibrous feed. Additionally, the weight of an animal is linked to its feed
consumption—heavier animals typically have higher energy needs, leading to greater
feed intake and subsequently higher methane emissions. Accounting for these factors
ensures more accurate estimations of CH4 emissions from enteric fermentation across
different livestock groups and life stages [32].
3.1.2
Manure Management
Manure management refers to the methods used for storing and treating livestock ma­
nure, which includes both urine and dung. The choice of manure management system
varies and is influenced by the type of housing system and livestock category. Different
systems impact greenhouse gas emissions differently due to variations in storage condi­
tions, treatment processes, and storage duration. Consequently, manure management is
associated with methane (CH4) emissions and both direct and indirect nitrous oxide (N2O)
emissions [3].
Methane (CH4) emissions from manure management arise from the anaerobic decom­
position of organic material. When manure is stored or treated under conditions limiting
oxygen exposure, such as in lagoons, pits, or storage tanks, methane­producing microbes
(methanogens) break down the organic matter, producing methane as a byproduct. The
amount of CH4 emitted depends on factors such as livestock type, manure management
system, temperature, and storage duration. Liquid systems, such as slurry and anaero­
bic lagoons, generally produce more methane due to more anaerobic conditions, whereas
solid storage and pasture­based systems have lower emissions due to aerobic exposure.
Longer storage periods allow for more microbial activity, increasing methane production,
while shorter storage durations may limit methane output. Temperature also plays a sig­
nificant role, with warmer conditions enhancing microbial activity and boosting emissions
11
==PAGE:24==
[3].
Direct N2O emissions result from the processes of nitrification and denitrification in stored
manure. Nitrification, an aerobic process, converts ammonia nitrogen to nitrates, which
can then undergo denitrification—a natural anaerobic process—producing N2O and N2.
The ratio of N2O to N2 is influenced by factors such as acidity, nitrate concentration, and
moisture levels in the storage environment.
Indirect N2O emissions result from the volatilization of ammonia and nitrogen oxides
(NOx). During manure management and storage, a portion of the organic nitrogen ex­
creted by animals is mineralized to ammonium nitrogen. Under alkaline conditions, newly
mineralized ammonium from urea (in mammals) and uric acid (in poultry) is converted to
ammonia, a highly volatile gas. This volatilized ammonia can later be redeposited on soil
surfaces, where microbial processes can convert a fraction of the reactive nitrogen to N2O
[3].
Additionally, nitrogen losses can occur through leaching into the soil when manure is
stored outdoors, in feedlots, or during grazing. These leached nitrogen compounds con­
tribute to indirect N2O emissions as they are transported and processed in the environ­
ment.
Environmental factors, including temperature, moisture, and pH, play critical roles in N2O
emissions. Higher temperatures and moisture levels enhance microbial activity, speed­
ing up nitrification and denitrification. Nitrifying microorganisms adapt to pH variations to
maintain homeostasis, obtain energy and carbon, neutralize reactive nitrogen species,
and generate membrane potential, all of which affect N2O production [35].
Similar to methane emissions, N2O emissions are influenced by livestock diet and manure
management systems. High­protein diets increase nitrogen content in excreta, raising
the potential for N2O emissions. The choice of manure management system impacts the
balance of aerobic and anaerobic conditions, influencing nitrogen losses such as leaching,
especially in liquid­based systems. Bedding materials mixed with manure also contribute
to N2O emissions as they serve as additional nitrogen sources.
Lastly, grazing further contributes to N2O emissions due to direct deposition of urine and
dung on pastures. Urine patches, with their high nitrogen concentration, promote rapid
nitrification and denitrification, especially under variable soil conditions. Soil moisture,
temperature, and oxygen availability can amplify emissions, making grazing management
a crucial factor in N2O mitigation. Dung decomposes more gradually, contributing to sus­
tained, lower levels of emissions compared to the immediate, higher emissions from urine
[36].
Recognizing how these diverse factors influence GHG emissions from livestock produc­
tion and understanding the underlying processes is essential for refining calculations. By
doing so, the emissions estimates for this project can be adjusted to more accurately
reflect the specific conditions and characteristics of the livestock involved.
3.2
Agriculture
Agricultural emissions stem from a range of sources. Carbon dioxide (CO2) is primarily
emitted through land­use changes, such as deforestation, land clearing, and soil distur­
bance from tillage, as well as from energy use in machinery, irrigation, transportation
fueled by fossil fuels, liming and urea [10, 1, 37]. Direct emissions of nitrous oxide (N2O)
are generated by the application of synthetic and organic fertilizers, manure management,
and the breakdown of crop residues, with soil microbial processes converting nitrogen into
12
==PAGE:25==
N2O under moist conditions that promote denitrification [36, 38]. Indirect emissions of N2O
occur when nitrogen from fertilizers and manure leaches into water systems or volatilizes
as ammonia, later being converted to N2O through various environmental pathways [16,
35]. Energy­related emissions from agriculture result from reliance on fossil fuels for ma­
chinery, irrigation, and transportation, contributing significantly to the sector’s total GHG
output [39, 2]. By addressing these sources through improved land and nutrient manage­
ment, the adoption of renewable energy, and increased efficiency, the agricultural sector
can achieve substantial reductions in its emissions and enhance sustainability [12, 22].
The IPCC classifies land use into categories such as Forest Land, Cropland, and Grass­
land to better assess and manage greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and removals [9].
This classification is essential for reflecting the unique carbon dynamics of each land­use
type, enabling targeted mitigation strategies and consistent GHG inventories that support
global climate targets.
• Forest Land: Areas with woody vegetation that meet national thresholds for forest
classification, as well as areas with the potential to meet these thresholds in the
future [9]. Forest Land serves as a significant carbon sink, absorbing and storing
CO2 through vegetation growth and soils.
• Cropland: Land used primarily for crop production, including rice fields and agro­
forestry systems where vegetation does not meet Forest Land criteria [9]. Croplands
often contribute to GHG emissions through activities such as tilling and fertilizer ap­
plication, which release CO2 and N2O [17, 40].
• Grassland: Rangelands and pasturelands not classified as Cropland, including ar­
eas with woody and non­grass vegetation like herbs and bushes that do not meet
Forest Land thresholds. This category also includes wild lands, recreational areas,
agricultural lands, and silvi­pastoral systems [9]. Grasslands can act as carbon
sinks or sources, depending on management practices and soil conditions [31, 33].
In subsequent sections, detailed explanations on the diverse sources of greenhouse gas
(GHG) emissions from agriculture will be provided [12, 2].
3.2.1
N2O Direct Emissions
The emissions from N2O direct result from applied fertilizers, crop residues, managed
organic soils, and the mineralization of nitrogen from soil organic carbon losses in mineral
soils [16, 34, 33].
3.2.1.1
Synthetic Fertilizer
Applying synthetic fertilizers to crops provides essential nutrients like nitrogen, phospho­
rus, and potassium, which are crucial for plant growth and high yields. By offering nu­
trients in a concentrated, readily available form, synthetic fertilizers allow for rapid and
targeted delivery, ensuring crops can meet their nutritional demands, particularly during
critical growth stages. This leads to increased crop yields, making it possible to produce
more food on limited land, which is essential for feeding a growing population [10]. The
consistent nutrient composition of synthetic fertilizers allows for precise application, en­
abling farmers to maintain soil fertility, address nutrient deficiencies, and adapt effectively
to varying soil types [22]. Furthermore, synthetic fertilizers provide farmers with greater
control over crop nutrient management. Proper usage of these fertilizers is essential to
mitigate environmental impacts, such as nutrient runoff and greenhouse gas emissions
[12, 1].
Synthetic fertilizers produce nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions due to their impact on the ni­
trogen cycle in the soil. When these fertilizers, which often contain high levels of nitrogen,
13
==PAGE:26==
are applied to crops, they release nitrogen in forms such as ammonium and nitrate. Soil
microbes process this nitrogen through nitrification (converting ammonium to nitrate) and
denitrification (converting nitrate to nitrogen gases, including N2O and N2) . During these
microbial processes, N2O is released as a byproduct [36, 38]. The risk of N2O emissions
increases when excess fertilizer is applied, leading to an oversupply of nitrogen that can’t
be fully absorbed by plants. This surplus nitrogen, combined with certain soil conditions
like waterlogging or low oxygen, accelerates microbial activity and results in higher N2O
emissions [16]. This greenhouse gas has significant environmental impacts, contributing
to global warming and climate change [12].
In Denmark, fertilizers containing over 16% nitrogen from ammonium nitrate are tightly
regulated, with purchases restricted to registered businesses through the Danish Agricul­
tural Agency. Private use is prohibited, and a nitrogen tax applies to unregistered buyers.
Suppliers must report deliveries electronically, detailing the fertilizer’s composition and
recipients [2, 41]. Globally and in the EU, synthetic fertilizers are governed by regula­
tions covering safety, handling, and environmental standards, with Denmark adhering
strictly to the EU’s Common Agricultural Policy (CAP). This often results in higher na­
tional standards, reflecting Denmark’s strong commitment to environmental sustainability
[3]. Prohibited chemicals must be disposed of safely through official channels [33].
3.2.1.2
Organic Fertilizer
Organic fertilizers, such as compost and plant­based organic materials, produce nitrous
oxide (N2O) emissions as they decompose and release nitrogen into the soil. Soil mi­
crobes metabolize these nitrogen compounds through processes like nitrification and
denitrification, which can emit N2O, particularly in moist or low­oxygen conditions [16,
35]. Despite this, farmers use organic fertilizers because they improve soil health by en­
hancing structure, increasing water retention, and boosting aeration. Organic fertilizers
also offer a slow, consistent release of nutrients, reducing leaching risks and supporting
sustainable practices [34]. They contribute beneficial microorganisms and organic matter
to the soil, fostering long­term fertility and nutrient cycling. Additionally, their use aligns
with environmentally friendly practices, meeting market demand for organic produce and
often adhering to regulatory guidelines for sustainable farming [33, 42].
Organic fertilizers, derived from natural sources and excluding synthetic chemicals, align
with practices like composting and crop rotation to enhance soil health and biodiversity
[10]. Danish regulations require annual reporting of organic fertilizer deliveries to ensure
compliance with usage limits, such as a maximum of 170 kg N/ha [41]. International stan­
dards, like those from IFOAM, influence organic practices globally. Denmark’s strong
focus on organic production, with around 12% of its farmland cultivated organically and
high consumer demand for organic goods, underscores its influence on EU policies pro­
moting organic and sustainable fertilizers [2].
In the Hove area, fertilizer data from the Danish Agricultural Agency’s database confirmed
that no sewage sludge or industrial waste is applied to soils. Organic fertilizers here
include all compost and other annual organic amendments used as fertilizer [33].
3.2.1.3
Manure
Manure applied to crops produces nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions primarily due to its high
nitrogen content and decomposition processes in the soil. When manure is applied, its
organic nitrogen and ammonium forms are broken down by soil microbes through micro­
bial processes such as nitrification (conversion of ammonium to nitrate) and denitrification
(conversion of nitrate to nitrogen gases, including N2O) [16, 38]. The presence of excess
nitrogen, combined with soil conditions like high moisture, low oxygen, and warm tem­
peratures, can accelerate microbial activity and increase N2O emissions [35]. The slower
14
==PAGE:27==
decomposition of manure also means that nitrogen may persist in the soil for longer peri­
ods, contributing to ongoing emissions [36].
Manure enriches the soil with essential nutrients like nitrogen, phosphorus, and potas­
sium, contributing to plant growth and crop productivity. It also enhances soil structure,
increasing water retention, aeration, and microbial activity, which improves overall soil
health [34, 33]. Manure provides a slow and steady release of nutrients, reducing the risk
of nutrient leaching compared to synthetic fertilizers [10]. By applying manure, farmers
can recycle animal waste, reducing disposal costs and turning a byproduct into a valu­
able resource. Using manure aligns with sustainable agricultural practices by promoting
organic matter content and long­term soil fertility, and it often meets market demands for
organic and sustainably grown produce [31].
Denmark regulates manure application to reduce environmental impacts, setting a general
limit of 170 kg of nitrogen per hectare for organic fertilizers, including manure [41]. Cattle
farms may qualify for an exemption allowing up to 230 kg N/ha under specific conditions.
Regulations aim to minimize nutrient runoff and emissions, promoting sustainable farming
practices, with detailed guidance available from the Danish Agricultural Agency [29].
Manure production depends on animal type, numbers, housing systems, and corrections.
Farmers must document these details to calculate nitrogen and phosphorus production,
with data transferred to fertilizer accounts. Calculations distinguish between yearly and
produced animals, and correction factors can be applied based on farm operations [3].
3.2.1.4
Crop Residue
Crop residues consist of plant materials left in the field after harvesting, such as stalks,
leaves, roots, and husks. These residues play a critical role in agriculture by enhancing
soil health, reducing erosion, and contributing organic matter that boosts soil fertility [1,
34]. However, as crop residues decompose, their nitrogen content is released and trans­
formed by soil microbes through processes like nitrification and denitrification, resulting in
emissions of nitrous oxide (N2O) [16, 38]. The level of N2O emissions depends on sev­
eral factors, such as the nitrogen content of the residues, soil moisture, temperature, and
residue management practices [31]. Methods for managing residues—including incorpo­
rating them into the soil, burning, or allowing them to decompose naturally—significantly
affect the rate and quantity of N2O emissions [36].
Effective residue management is vital for minimizing N2O emissions, preserving soil fertil­
ity, and mitigating environmental impacts [10]. Plant residues left on fields provide numer­
ous benefits, such as reducing soil erosion, conserving moisture, enhancing soil structure,
and minimizing nutrient and pesticide runoff [40]. As they decompose, plant residues in­
crease organic matter in the soil, contribute to carbon sequestration, and improve soil
health by fostering better soil structure and reducing compaction [12]. However, to fully
capitalize on these benefits while addressing challenges like pest buildup, careful man­
agement is necessary [33].
The prevalence and management of plant residues, commonly known as mulching, vary
across regions. For example, in Denmark, only 4% of arable land is covered by plant
residues, highlighting differences in soil management practices and the adoption of residue­
based strategies [37]. This variability underscores the importance of region­specific ap­
proaches to residue management to optimize environmental and agricultural outcomes
[3].
3.2.1.5
Managed Organic Soils
In Denmark, organic soils are defined as having more than 6% organic carbon (OC) in
the topsoil, whereas the IPCC classifies organic soils as having more than 12% OC [16].
15
==PAGE:28==
Denmark also recognizes a transitional category of soils with 6­12% OC, referred to as
”half­emission” soils [33]. Organic soils typically develop under poorly drained conditions
and are rich in decomposed organic matter, such as peat, and generally contain more
than 12% organic carbon by mass, with examples including peat and muck [31]. These
soils, often classified as Histosols, must meet specific criteria related to their thickness
and proximity to the soil surface, as well as the composition of moss fibers and layers
of organic material, distinguishing them from mineral soils [17]. Due to their high organic
matter content, organic soils have a greater capacity for carbon storage but are also more
susceptible to greenhouse gas emissions if improperly managed or drained [34].
Nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions from managed organic soils are primarily driven by micro­
bial processes that become more active once these soils are drained [38]. The drainage
of organic soils for agricultural purposes increases oxygen availability, which stimulates
microbial activity, particularly nitrification and denitrification processes [10]. These pro­
cesses convert nitrogen from soil organic matter and applied fertilizers into N2O. The
combination of moist and aerated conditions in drained organic soils further accelerates
these reactions, often resulting in substantial releases of N2O [35]. Additionally, soil distur­
bance caused by tillage and other management practices can further enhance microbial
activity and N2O emissions. Mitigation strategies, such as minimizing soil disturbance,
can help reduce these emissions [22].
3.2.1.6
Mineralized Nitrogen from Loss of Soil Organic Carbon in Mineral Soils
Mineral soils, characterized by less than 6% organic carbon (OC) in their topsoil (0–30
cm), are predominantly composed of inorganic materials such as sand, silt, and clay.
These soils form through the weathering of rocks and minerals and generally exhibit lower
organic matter content, particularly under well­drained conditions [16, 1]. Unlike organic
soils, mineral soils are critical for estimating soil carbon stocks and nitrous oxide (N2O)
emissions. Common classification systems include the USDA taxonomy and the World
Reference Base for Soil Resources [34]. The carbon dynamics within mineral soils are
heavily influenced by land use and management practices [40].
Mineralized nitrogen (N) is produced through the microbial breakdown of soil organic car­
bon (SOC) in mineral soils. During the decomposition of soil organic matter, nitrogen
is released in inorganic forms such as ammonium (NH4+), which can further transform
into nitrate (NO3­) through nitrification. These processes can result in nitrous oxide (N2O)
emissions via denitrification under specific environmental conditions [31]. As a potent
greenhouse gas, N2O emissions are significantly influenced by soil management prac­
tices and environmental factors such as moisture, temperature, and nitrogen availability,
making these emissions highly variable and context­dependent [10].
The accumulation and decomposition of SOC stocks and mineralized nitrogen are influ­
enced by various factors that can be classified into natural or environmental factors and
management­related factors, both of which play a significant role in regulating SOC levels
in the soil.
Among the natural factors, temperature significantly affects these processes, with higher
temperatures accelerating microbial activity and SOC decomposition, while cooler cli­
mates slow these processes, favoring SOC accumulation. Soil clay content also plays
a pivotal role, as clay­rich soils stabilize SOC by forming aggregates and chemical com­
plexes that protect organic matter from microbial degradation, thereby reducing miner­
alization rates. Additionally, plant biomass production also impacts SOC levels, as in­
creased biomass leads to higher organic matter inputs, as long as decomposition rates
are not excessive.
16
==PAGE:29==
On the other hand, management­related factors include decisions about land use, agri­
cultural practices, and other human interventions. These may involve tillage methods,
crop rotations, residue management, or the application of organic and synthetic inputs.
The influence of such practices on soil organic carbon (SOC) dynamics and nitrogen min­
eralization is well­established.
Crop rotation and the use of cover crops further support SOC levels by ensuring contin­
uous organic matter inputs, reducing erosion, and enhancing soil structure [3]. Residue
management is equally important; retaining crop residues increases SOC, whereas residue
removal depletes SOC stocks [33].
Tillage Practices (Fmg) refers to the tillage operations carried out between the harvest
and following sowing/cultivation operation and play a significant role in determining SOC
stability and nitrogen emissions. Tillage practices can be categorized into three meth­
ods: conventional tillage, conservation tillage and zero tillage. Conventional tillage or
full tillage, involves extensive soil turning through ploughing and harrowing, typically per­
formed with tractors, resulting in complete soil inversion. This practice, exposes buried
organic matter to microbial decomposition, accelerating nitrogen mineralization and in­
creasing nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions [1, 16]. In contrast, conservation tillage mini­
mizes soil disturbance, leaving a significant portion of crop residues on the soil surface.
This has a direct effect by slowing SOC decomposition and releasing nutrients more grad­
ually, thereby lowering the risk of N2O emissions [10, 40]. Lastly, zero tillage completely
eliminates soil disturbance by utilizing machinery designed to plant seeds directly into the
soil with precision and minimal impact on its structure [43]. This practice prevents SOC
disruption, reduces mineralization, and directly influences greenhouse gas emissions by
mitigating their release [31].
Land Use Change (Flu) refers to the alteration of land from one use to another, such
as converting forests or grasslands into croplands, or transitioning agricultural systems to
less intensive practices. These changes have significant effects on soil organic carbon
(SOC) and nitrogen dynamics. The conversion of natural ecosystems to cultivated land
often results in SOC depletion and increased nitrogen mineralization due to soil distur­
bance and the removal of vegetation cover [10, 33]. In contrast, transitioning to perennial
systems helps stabilize SOC and reduce nitrogen mineralization over time. Perennial
systems are characterized by continuous root activity and permanent soil cover, as these
plants do not require annual replanting, which minimizes soil disturbance [9]. Similarly,
set­aside land, where agricultural land is temporarily removed from active crop produc­
tion to allow the soil to recover, and grassland systems, which remain undisturbed, tend to
accumulate SOC. These practices contribute to stabilizing nitrogen cycling and reducing
mineralization rates, further enhancing soil health and carbon sequestration [34].
Input Regimes (Fi) describe the nutrient inputs applied to agricultural systems, which
significantly impact nitrogen cycling and N2O emissions. Low­input systems, character­
ized by minimal fertilizer application, result in slower nitrogen cycling and typically lower
N2O emissions due to reduced nitrogen availability [22]. Medium­input systems provide a
balanced approach, maintaining adequate soil fertility and nitrogen availability while pro­
ducing moderate levels of N2O emissions [3]. High­input systems that rely exclusively on
synthetic fertilizers are associated with elevated N2O emissions, driven by an oversup­
ply of nitrogen that exceeds plant uptake capacity [16]. By contrast, high­input systems
incorporating manure as an organic amendment can enhance SOC stocks and improve
soil structure. However, these systems may also lead to increased N2O emissions if ni­
trogen inputs surpass crop requirements, highlighting the need for careful management
17
==PAGE:30==
of manure applications [36, 38].
Finally, the Carbon­to­Nitrogen (C:N) Ratio of Organic Matter (R) plays a critical role in
SOC decomposition and nitrogen availability. Organic materials with high C:N ratios (>20–
30), such as straw, decompose slowly, contributing to SOC accumulation while slowing
nitrogen mineralization [1]. In contrast, low C:N materials (<20), such as green residues,
decompose rapidly, releasing nitrogen quickly and potentially increasing N2O emissions
[10]. A balanced C:N ratio achieved through diverse organic inputs optimizes microbial
activity, stabilizes SOC, and ensures sustainable nitrogen availability [34].
These factors underscore the complex interplay between environmental and management
practices in regulating SOC stocks and nitrogen mineralization, while also influencing
associated greenhouse gas emissions.
3.2.2
Indirect N2O Emissions
Indirect N2O emissions arise from the atmospheric deposition of volatilized nitrogen (N)
from managed soils, as well as nitrogen losses through leaching and runoff [16]. These
emissions, while originating locally, can have far­reaching impacts, affecting regions far
removed from their source.
Accurately quantifying indirect N2O emissions is vital for understanding the agricultural
sector’s overall contribution to climate change and for developing effective mitigation
strategies. However, these emissions are challenging to measure directly due to their
diffuse nature. Tools like the eddy covariance tower, while invaluable for monitoring direct
emissions, cannot capture indirect emissions. As such, estimation methods are critical
for providing a comprehensive picture of agricultural N2O emissions, even though these
estimates are inherently complex and uncertain [22].
3.2.2.1
Atmospheric deposition of N volatilized from managed soils
Nitrogen volatilization occurs when nitrogen inputs, including synthetic fertilizers, manure,
and organic amendments, release ammonia (NH3) or nitrogen oxides (NOx) into the atmo­
sphere [36, 38]. These gases can travel over long distances before being redeposited on
land or in water systems through dry or wet deposition. Following deposition, microbial
processes convert the nitrogen compounds into N2O, particularly under conditions that
favor nitrification and denitrification [16, 35]. When nitrogen sources such as synthetic
fertilizers, manure, or organic amendments are applied to agricultural soils, a portion may
volatilize as ammonia (NH3) or nitrogen oxides (NOx). These compounds disperse into
the atmosphere and are deposited on terrestrial or aquatic surfaces via dry or wet de­
position. Once deposited, microbial processes, such as nitrification and denitrification,
transform these nitrogen compounds into N2O, a potent greenhouse gas [31, 10].
The extent of volatilization and subsequent N2O formation depends on factors such as
nitrogen source type, soil properties, climatic conditions, and management practices. A
robust estimation of these emissions is crucial to provide a holistic perspective on agri­
culture’s role in greenhouse gas emissions and guide targeted mitigation efforts [34].
3.2.2.2
Nitrogen Leaching and Runoff in Managed Soils
Leaching and runoff of nitrogen occur when nitrogen moves beyond the plant root zone
into groundwater or surface water systems.
These processes are driven by nitrogen
sources such as synthetic fertilizers, manure, crop residues, and nitrogen released during
the mineralization of soil organic matter [10, 33]. Under conditions like heavy rainfall or
poor drainage, excess nitrogen can leach into water bodies, where microbial activity in
anaerobic environments converts it into N2O [31].
Quantifying N2O emissions from leaching and runoff is essential for understanding nitro­

3.2.3
CO2 Direct Emissions
CO2 emissions originate from a range of sources, including changes in soil carbon stocks,
alterations in biomass, liming, and the cultivation of organic soils [16, 1].
3.2.3.1
Biomass
CO2 emissions from biomass arise from various natural and human­induced processes
that release stored carbon, significantly influencing the global carbon cycle [34, 10]. While
biomass acts as a carbon sink through photosynthesis, it also becomes a CO2 source
through respiration, decomposition, land­use change, and harvesting.
Plants release
CO2 through respiration as they break down sugars for energy, while dead organic mat­
ter decomposes, emitting CO2 through microbial activity [31]. Land­use changes, such
as deforestation and the conversion of forests to croplands, involve removing biomass,
contributing further to CO2 emissions [9]. Harvesting trees for timber or fuelwood reduces
biomass stocks, with carbon released either through combustion or gradual decomposi­
tion. Natural disturbances, like plagues and storms, also lead to biomass mortality and
increased CO2 emissions [2]. The interplay of photosynthesis, decomposition, carbon
exchanges between different biomass types, and the effects of land­use and manage­
ment practices ultimately governs the dynamics of CO2 emissions and their impact on the
carbon cycle.
3.2.3.2
Liming and Dolomite Application
Acidic soils limit plant nutrient uptake, affecting plant growth and health. The application of
lime (calcium carbonate) and dolomite (calcium magnesium carbonate) neutralizes acid­
ity, addressing soil nutrient deficiencies and improving productivity. Lime corrects calcium
deficiencies, while dolomite supplies calcium and magnesium, enhancing soil pH and nu­
trient availability, such as phosphorus and potassium, while reducing toxic levels of alu­
minum and manganese [44]. Additionally, a balanced pH promotes microbial activity and
nutrient cycling.
Lime is suited for soils low in calcium, whereas dolomite is ideal for deficiencies in both
calcium and magnesium. Application rates, guided by soil tests, typically range from 1–2
tons per acre for sandy soils to 2–4 tons per acre for clay soils. Application is best done in
fall or early spring, with materials evenly spread and tilled into the soil. Over­application
should be avoided to prevent excessive alkalinity, and finely ground products enhance
reactivity.
The application of lime and dolomite contributes to CO2 emissions during soil reactions
and production, particularly through calcination. For example, the reaction of calcium car­
bonate releases CO2 as a byproduct. Dolomite, with its magnesium carbonate, releases
additional CO2. Emissions can be mitigated by optimizing application rates, using alter­
natives like gypsum, adopting carbon capture in production, and enhancing soil carbon
sequestration.
In Denmark, lime and dolomite are widely used to manage soil acidity, particularly in west­
ern Jutland. Regular soil testing determines lime needs based on the reaction number
(Rt), with maintenance applications of 2 tons every 4–6 years [45]. These practices im­
prove soil fertility, promote sustainability, and support robust crop productivity.
While lime and dolomite benefit soil health and agriculture, they contribute to CO2 emis­
sions. However, judicious application, guided by soil testing, and innovative practices can
minimize environmental impacts, aligning with sustainable agricultural goals.

3.2.3.3
Managed Organic Soils
As described in 3.2.1.5, organic soils are defined and characterized in detail. Apart from
N2O emissions, they can also contribute to CO2 emissions. Carbon dioxide (CO2) emis­
sions from organic soils primarily arise due to the decomposition of soil organic matter
when these soils are managed for agricultural use. When organic soils, such as peat­
lands, are drained, the exposure of previously submerged organic matter to oxygen leads
to rapid decomposition, resulting in the release of significant amounts of CO2 into the at­
mosphere [31, 33]. Additionally, agricultural practices such as tillage and land preparation
further disturb the soil, accelerating the breakdown of organic matter and leading to even
greater CO2 emissions [17]. Effective management of organic soils is therefore crucial to
mitigate these emissions [3].
3.2.3.4
Loss of Soil Organic Carbon in mineral soils
Mineral soils also contribute to CO2 emissions. A detailed definition of mineral soils can
be found in 3.2.1.6.
CO2 emissions from mineral soils primarily result from changes in soil organic carbon
(SOC) caused by human activities that disrupt the natural balance of carbon within the soil.
When soils are disturbed through practices such as plowing, tilling, or land­use changes,
the soil structure is altered, exposing organic matter to oxygen and accelerating micro­
bial decomposition, which releases CO2 [1, 16]. The conversion of natural ecosystems,
such as forests or grasslands, into agricultural land further exacerbates this process by
reducing the carbon input from vegetation and increasing the risk of erosion and leaching,
which deplete SOC [40, 3].
The application of nitrogen fertilizers can also stimulate microbial activity, accelerating
the breakdown of organic matter and leading to additional CO2 emissions [34, 33]. Fur­
thermore, agricultural practices often subject mineral soils to varying temperatures and
moisture levels, which amplify microbial activity and enhance carbon loss. Deforestation
is another significant contributor, as it removes trees and vegetation that play a crucial
role in replenishing soil carbon. This results in a net loss of SOC and increased CO2
emissions [8, 1].
These processes highlight the importance of conservation practices, such as reduced
tillage, cover cropping, and preserving natural vegetation, to protect soil carbon and mit­
igate climate change [10, 39]. For a more detailed discussion of the factors influencing
CO2 emissions, refer to 3.2.1.6.
3.3
Energy Emissions
According to both the IPCC guidelines and the Danish inventory, energy emissions from
agriculture are reported under the energy sector [16, 2]. These emissions are significant
sources of greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrous
oxide (N2O) [10].
Energy emissions in agriculture occur mainly due to the use of fossil fuels in various
farming operations, including powering machinery and equipment, heating and cooling
facilities, transportation of goods, and on­site processing activities [3]. The combustion
of these fuels releases substantial amounts of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere,
contributing to climate change by enhancing the greenhouse effect [34]. Therefore, en­
ergy emissions from agriculture are a critical concern because they significantly affect the
environment and global climate systems [22].
20
==PAGE:33==
Energy sources can be broadly categorized into two main types: stationary equipment and
off­road vehicles and machinery. Stationary equipment refers to machinery that remains
fixed during operation and does not move between locations. This category encompasses
equipment that relies on a variety of fuel types, including liquid fuels, solid fuels, gaseous
fuels, and biomass [17]. In contrast, off­road vehicles and machinery comprise mobile
equipment specifically designed for use off paved roads, often operating in rugged terrains
such as farms, construction sites, or forests. These vehicles and machines primarily utilize
fuels like gasoline, diesel oil, and biomass [33]. Together, these categories illustrate the
diverse energy needs and fuel consumption patterns in different operational contexts [12].

Data Collection
This chapter outlines the methodologies and data sources used to analyze greenhouse
gas (GHG) emissions and carbon flux within a 6­kilometer radius around the eddy covari­
ance (EC) tower in Hove, Denmark. The study integrates geospatial data, activity data,
and emissions factors to establish a baseline for N2O, CO2, and CH4 emissions, focusing
on local agricultural practices.
4.1
Geospatial analysis
The study area was defined as a circular boundary with a 6 km radius centered on the
eddy covariance (EC) tower located in Hove (55°42’59’’N, 12°14’14’’E). This radius was
selected to encompass a representative mix of land uses while aligning with the spatial
footprint of the flux measurements captured by the EC tower, ensuring coherence be­
tween observed data and land­use characteristics. The delineation of this area and the
processing of geospatial data were conducted using QGIS, an open­source GIS software
[46]. The process began by importing base maps, including administrative boundary lay­
ers and high­resolution satellite imagery, to provide a detailed geospatial context. A point
layer was created to mark the coordinates of the EC tower, and the Buffer tool (Vector
> Geoprocessing Tools > Buffer) was used to generate a circular boundary with the
specified 6 km radius. This boundary served as the spatial extent for further data pro­
cessing.
Relevant datasets such as land use maps, soil characteristics, crop distributions, and
livestock locations were imported into QGIS and clipped to the boundary using the Clip
tool (Vector > Geoprocessing Tools > Clip). These geospatial datasets were critical
for delineating the study area, analyzing land­use trends, and mapping livestock distribu­
tions, forming the foundation for integrating activity data and evaluating greenhouse gas
(GHG) emissions associated with agricultural practices in the region. The data processing
ensured consistency across all datasets by maintaining proper alignment and using the
EPSG:25832 projection system, which is commonly applied in Danish geospatial analy­
ses [47]. This standardization facilitated the seamless integration of diverse spatial data
into the analysis, ensuring accuracy and consistency in the study’s outputs.
The primary geospatial data source for this study was the Landbrugsgeodata portal, an
online platform managed by the Danish Ministry of Food, Agriculture, and Fisheries [4].
This portal provides comprehensive geospatial datasets, including detailed information on
land use, field boundaries, crop distributions, and agricultural characteristics, which are
essential for data­driven environmental assessments and planning. The integration of
these datasets in QGIS facilitated a thorough understanding of the relationships between
land use, soil conditions, and livestock distributions within the 6 km radius, allowing for a
robust evaluation of GHG emissions associated with diverse agricultural practices in the
defined study area.

5
Methodology
This section outlines the methodology used to calculate emissions from both livestock and
the agricultural sector. All calculations were performed following the 2019 Refinement to
the 2006 IPCC Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories (IPCC, 2019) [3] and,
when necessary, the 2006 IPCC Guidelines[16]. The approach involved applying IPCC­
provided equations and developing adapted versions of Tier 1 and Tier 2 methodologies
to suit local conditions. In this context, Tier 1 refers to calculations that utilize default input
parameters provided by the IPCC tables, ensuring a standardized approach to emissions
estimation. In contrast, Tier 2 uses country­specific parameter values developed and
calculated from Denmark’s National Inventory Report, when available [2]. This approach
provides a more accurate representation of local practices, conditions, and circumstances
in Denmark, capturing the unique characteristics of its agricultural sector.These equations
were applied and scaled to the study area to accurately represent its unique characteris­
tics and emissions profile, using the previously mentioned R program developed specifi­
cally for this purposes. The relevant code and supporting files required for execution are
provided in the appendix A.2 and appendix A.3 .
In the following subsections, detailed explanations will be provided for the calculation of
each type of GHG emission for both sectors, including the equations used, the assump­
tions made during the calculations, and any additional considerations taken into account.
5.1
Livestock
As previously described, emissions from livestock primarily originate from enteric fer­
mentation and manure management, contributing to greenhouse gases such as methane
(CH4) and nitrous oxide (N2O). The calculations in this study followed the methodology
outlined in the 2019 IPCC Refinement, specifically Chapter 10: Emissions from Livestock
and Manure Management[3].
In line with the 2019 IPCC guidelines [3], the livestock population within the study area
was divided into subgroups. This classification was based on data collected from the
Central Husbandry Register (CHR) [48] for each livestock farm. The subgroups include
dairy cattle, non­dairy cattle, swine, horses, sheep, goats, poultry, and pheasants. Ad­
ditionally, farms were categorized into low and high productivity systems as defined by
the IPCC guidelines [3]. High productivity systems refer to confinement­based operations
focused on commercial milk or meat production for national or export markets, whereas
low productivity systems represent low­yield, local market­oriented livestock production.
The classification into productivity categories was determined by the number of animals
per farm and was supplemented by visual assessments of farm facilities using geospatial
tools such as QGIS. Due to limitations in available data, it was assumed that farms with
more than 50 animals were classified as high productivity systems. It is worth pointing
out that the study area is predominantly characterized by small livestock farms, with the
exception of a swine farm production with 3560 animals.
Before performing calculations, farms in the study area were assigned manure manage­
ment systems. Each farm was assigned a primary manure management system (MMS
1) based on the most common system used for each livestock category, guided by data
from the Danish National Inventory (DNI) [2] and IPCC guidelines [3]. The assignment
31
==PAGE:44==
considered not only statistics on typical manure management systems by animal cate­
gory but also the number of animals per farm, with geospatial data analysis employed for
verification to ensure accurate classification. Additionally, some farms were assigned a
secondary manure management system (MMS 2), determined by the number of grazing
days specific to each livestock category, as outlined in the Danish National Inventory [2].
The manure management systems assigned to farms included:
• Liquid/Slurry: Manure is stored in tanks or ponds with minimal addition of water or
bedding material, ensuring most volatile solids are retained.
• Solid Storage: Manure is kept in unconfined piles or stacks for several months,
potentially covered or compacted to reduce moisture loss.
• Manure with Litter: Used primarily for poultry operations, where litter and manure
are mixed with added bedding and cleaned between cycles.
• Pasture/Range/Paddock (PRP): Manure from grazing animals is left as deposited
on grass without active management.
One additional consideration in the livestock emission calculations was the occasional
lack of specific data for pheasants. In cases where such data was unavailable, it was
assumed that pheasants have the same parameter values as poultry, due to the similarity
in their physiology. This approach was only used when specific parameters for pheasants
were not available, ensuring the continuity of emission calculations while acknowledging
the data limitations for this subgroup.
These assignments and classifications were essential as the first step in accurately ac­
counting for and implementing the equations needed to estimate emissions from different
sources. This foundational work ensured that the subsequent calculations for enteric
fermentation and manure management reflected the specific characteristics of the study
area.

tors by the corresponding livestock subgroup populations. The total emissions were then
estimated by summing the emissions across all farms.

Tier 1
In this approach, emission factors were selected based on the assigned productivity sys­

Tier 2
The Tier 2 approach also employed Equation 5.1.1, but used emission factors specifi­
cally calculated for Denmark, as provided by the National Inventory Report (DNI) 2023
[2]. These implied emission factors were developed following the IPCC’s Tier 2 method­
ology, which incorporates a comprehensive analysis of local conditions. The calculation
of these factors included a detailed consideration of feeding practices during winter and
summer, actual feeding plans based on Danish normative data, and animal weights spe­
cific to Denmark, among other relevant parameters. This method ensures that the implied
emission factors more accurately represent local feeding practices and livestock charac­
teristics compared to the default IPCC values. Thus, leading to a more precise reflection
of Denmark’s livestock production systems. Similar to the Tier 1 approach, total emissions
were obtained by summing the emissions from all farms in the study area.
5.1.2

Building on the previously outlined classification of manure management systems and
farm productivity categories outline in Section 5.1, here the methodology used to estimate
CH4 emissions from the storage and treatment of manure is described. The calculations

emissions reflect the unique practices and conditions within the study area. The calcu­

Refinement to the 2006 IPCC Guidelines [3].

Tier 2
The Tier 2 approach utilizes country­specific values to estimate emission factors that more
accurately represent national conditions. These emission factors are estimated by con­
sidering three key components: the daily amount of volatile solids (VS) excreted, the
maximum methane­producing capacity of the manure (B0), and the methane conversion
factor (MCF) for manure management in each specific system.
The daily volatile solids excreted per animal category are derived from country­specific
values, as detailed in the Danish Inventory Report. These values are calculated by con­
sidering data on the amount of manure, dry matter content, share of volatile solids in the
dry matter, amount of bedding material, and grazing days [2].
For B0, the IPCC default parameter values were used [3], following the approach outlined
in the Danish Inventory Report (DNI). For the methane conversion factor (MCF), the DNI
primarily used IPCC default values for most manure management systems, and this ap­
proach was adopted in the Tier 2 calculations as well. However, for liquid slurry systems
for cattle and swine, country­specific MCF values developed by the DNI were used to
better represent national conditions[2].

5.2
Agriculture
Before initiating the emission calculations for agriculture, several preparatory steps were
required. First, the fields in the study area were categorized into specific land use types
based on the IPCC guidelines [3]. These land use types were organized into three main
categories: Grassland, Cropland, and Forest Land, as described in Section 3.2.
Grassland includes two primary types: Permanent Grassland and Rotational Grassland.
• Permanent Grassland refers to areas with long­term grass cover that remains out­
side crop rotations for more than five years. This type of grassland plays a vital role
in maintaining biodiversity and promoting soil health [3].
• Rotational Grassland involves short­term grass cover included within crop rota­
tions to restore soil nutrients and enhance fertility. It includes fallow land and clover,
which are used as rotational crops for soil rest and sustainable land management.
For calculation purposes, Rotational Grassland is grouped under Grassland [1].
Cropland is divided into two subcategories: Annual Crops and Perennial Crops.
• Annual Crops complete their life cycle within a single growing season, from germi­
nation to seed production. Examples include wheat, corn (maize), beans, spinach,
and radishes. These crops are favored for their quick yields and compatibility with
intensive farming systems but require replanting each year [42].
• Perennial Crops live and yield for more than two years after initial planting. Ex­
amples include fruit trees (e.g., apples, peaches), nut trees (e.g., almonds, wal­
nuts), berries (e.g., blueberries, raspberries), and certain grasses. Perennial crops
contribute to soil health, reduce erosion, and often require fewer inputs over time
compared to annual crops [16].
Finally, Forest Land primarily encompasses areas dedicated to forests but can also in­
clude specific uses such as Christmas tree cultivation, balancing ecological and economic
objectives [17].

5.2.1
Organic Carbon Stock Change in Mineral Soils Calculations
The methodology for the calculation of Soil Organic Carbon (SOC) and the change in soil
carbon due to land use (∆Cmineral), provided by IPCC, is based on the concept of equi­
librium SOC. Equilibrium values are calculated as specific deviations from a reference
SOC, depending on crop and management­specific factors. Transitions between equilib­
rium states are modeled as a linear process over a 20­year period, with annual changes
estimated by dividing the difference between two equilibrium states by 20[7]. While this
approach is a bold simplification of the inherently non­linear processes involved in organic
matter turnover, it provides a practical framework for global reporting.
However, for this thesis, the most complete dataset available covered only a 10­year pe­
riod (2014 to 2024), as explained in Section 4.1.2. Consequently, 2014 was selected as
the reference year for these calculations. In Denmark, 1990 is typically used as the ref­
erence year, which may result in some discrepancies between the findings of this project
and the national figures [2]. This approach assumes that the land use for each field re­
mained unchanged for the 20 years preceding 2014 [16]. Therefore, fields lacking data
for 2014 were excluded from the analysis and omitted from the calculations.
With this assumption in place, the calculation of SOC for each year was performed using
Equation 2.25 from the 2006 IPCC Guidelines [16]. This method provides a framework for
estimating soil carbon dynamics over time based on available land use and management
data.
SOC = SOCref · FLU · FI · FMG · Area
(5.15)
Where:
• SOCref: Reference SOC stock, in tC/ha.
• FLU: Land­use factor, dimensionless.
• FI: Input factor, dimensionless.
• FMG: Tillage management factor, dimensionless..
39
==PAGE:52==
• Area: Land area, in hectares of targeted area.
The equilibrium SOC reference condition (SOCref) in the equation serves as a baseline
for evaluating top soil SOC levels in specific soil types, land uses, and climatic contexts.
It provides a benchmark for assessing changes in SOC stocks and is integral to models
evaluating the impacts of land management and climate change [9]. SOCref values are
typically derived from field measurements, historical data, or standardized datasets rep­
resenting undisturbed conditions such as forests or grasslands in a region. Comparing
current SOC levels to SOCref facilitates the evaluation of practices aimed at increasing or
sustaining SOC stocks [22].
To calculate the change in Soil Organic Carbon (∆Cmineral) due to land use, this study
adapted the methodology presented in Box 2.2, Chapter 2 of the IPCC 2006 Guide­
lines [16]. The example in Box 2.2 illustrates a step­by­step approach to estimating SOC
changes over a defined inventory period, which was carefully analyzed to understand the
underlying principles and calculation steps.
Based on this analysis, the approach was tailored to fit the specific characteristics of the
study area and, most importantly, to address the limitations in available data. While the
IPCC methodology assumes a 20­year inventory period, the data available for this study
covered only a 10­year period as mentioned. Therefore, the equations and procedures
provided in Box 2.2 were modified to reflect the shorter inventory period and align with the
data constraints. This adaptation ensured that the calculation of ∆Cmineral accounted for
the available land­use history and provided results relevant to the study area’s conditions.
The customized equations derived from this process are outlined below and were applied
to estimate SOC changes for the defined inventory period.
To calculate the annual change in Soil Organic Carbon (SOC) and the associated ∆Cmineral
due to land use, the following steps and equations were implemented:
The difference in SOC between consecutive years was calculated to determine the net
change over each time step. This difference is obtained by subtracting the SOC of the
current analyzed year (T) from the SOC of the previous year (T −1):
∆SOC = SOC(T −1) −SOC(T)
(5.16)
• SOC: Soil organic carbon stock, tonnes of Carbon.
• ∆SOC: Difference in SOC stock between consecutive years, in units of carbon mass
(e.g., tC/ha).
• SOC(T): SOC stock in the current year T.
• SOC(T −1): SOC stock in the previous year T −1.
Since the IPCC methodology assumes factors are averaged over a 20­year period, the
calculated SOC difference (∆SOC) was divided by 20 to represent the annualized change:
∆SOCannual = ∆SOC

• ∆SOCannual: Annualized change in SOC stock, in tC/ha/year.
• 20: Time frame in years, as defined by IPCC for land­use­related SOC changes.

the previous year (T −1) and the annual SOC change (∆SOCannual):
SOC′(T) = SOC(T −1) −∆SOCannual

By applying these steps iteratively for each year within the 10­year inventory period, the
study aimed to adapt the methodology with the highest level of accuracy achievable given
the data constraints. While advanced carbon modeling tools [2], such as Denmark’s C­
TOOL, are often employed for such calculations—integrating additional parameters like
average annual temperature—this study represents a practical adaptation of the IPCC
guidelines tailored to the specific conditions and data availability of the study area. This
approach balances methodological rigor with the limitations of the data, ensuring results
that are both reliable and contextually relevant.
Finally, the results of these calculations can be either positive, indicating an increase in
soil carbon storage due to land­use practices, or negative, representing a net release of
carbon as emissions. The calculated emissions, along with the methods and emission
factors applied, are discussed in detail in the relevant subsections. As the scope of the
project is limited to 2023 and 2024, emissions were calculated exclusively for these two
years.

5.2.4
CO2 Direct Emissions
This section outlines the calculations required to estimate CO2 emissions from biomass,
liming, dolomite application, managed organic soils, and soil organic carbon (CSOC).
Notably, emissions from crop burning are excluded, as Denmark has prohibited the field
burning of agricultural residues since 1990 [33]. Emissions from dead organic matter
(CDOM) and litter are also considered negligible. For non­forest land­use categories, the
carbon stock in CDOM is assumed to be zero at the baseline according to Tier 1 (T1)
methodologies in the IPCC 2006 guidelines [16]. Furthermore, for unchanged land­use
categories, inflows and outflows of CDOM are assumed to be balanced, resulting in no net
change in carbon stock [10].
Due to data limitations, adopting a more detailed Tier 2 (T2) approach for certain cate­
gories, such as biomass, liming and dolomite application, management of organic soils,
and dead organic matter, is currently infeasible. This limitation underscores the critical
need for enhanced data collection to support more accurate carbon accounting practices
in the future [22].
Additionally, data from the Danish Fertilizer Database [41] confirm that no urea or sewage
sludge is applied in the Hove area. Consequently, CO2 emissions from urea are not
calculated, as they are not relevant to this region.

5.4
Uncertainty Analysis
The uncertainty analysis in this thesis focuses on the methodological framework outlined
by the IPCC, specifically addressing uncertainties inherent to the parameter estimates
and model selection. Parameter uncertainty arises from random errors in the estimation
process, while systematic uncertainty is introduced through the choice of models used to
simulate greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from agriculture. A third source of uncertainty,
related to input variables such as livestock numbers, management practices, and choice
of tillage practices, is not included due to the unavailability of corresponding uncertainty
estimates. Nonetheless, the high quality and transparency of the Danish reporting system
and geodetic data provide a robust foundation, potentially mitigating the impact of this
omission on the overall uncertainty analysis.
The uncertainty assessment was conducted exclusively for direct emissions from both
livestock and agriculture. Although indirect emissions were calculated, they are outside
the scope of this analysis. Similarly, uncertainty related to energy emissions was not
included, as this thesis focuses solely on emissions from livestock and cultivated areas.
The rationale for excluding energy emissions was addressed earlier and aligns with the
methodological choices made in this study.
The uncertainty analysis for liming emissions was excluded from this study as well. As
discussed in Section 5.2.4.2, the emission factor (EF) likely encompasses both direct
and indirect emissions. The complexity of carbonate transformations, coupled with the
51
==PAGE:64==
variability in transport processes through soil and water systems, makes it challenging to
accurately distinguish between emissions and other forms of carbon loss, such as runoff.
Given these challenges and the inherent uncertainty already embedded in the default EF,
conducting an additional uncertainty analysis for liming emissions was deemed unneces­
sary for this research.
Estimating greenhouse gas emissions from agricultural systems inherently involves di­
verse parameters and complex interactions, introducing significant uncertainty. To quan­
tify and propagate this uncertainty in emissions estimates, Monte Carlo simulation was
adopted, following the 2006 and 2019 IPCC Guidelines, which recommend this approach
for uncertainty analysis and model validation [16, 3]. Monte Carlo simulation is particularly
effective in this context due to the complexity and number of equations and parameters
involved, each with unique uncertainties that may not be symmetric [5].
In many cases, the uncertainty in parameters is asymmetrical, resulting in confidence lim­
its that are often skewed rather than evenly distributed around the mean. For example,
instead of a straightforward ±% range, the actual distribution may exhibit greater variabil­
ity in one direction, reflecting the inherent skewness of the data [50].
Given the number of equations and varying parameters (depending on whether the emis­
sions are related to livestock or cropland), a systematic approach to uncertainty analy­
sis was necessary. For the analysis, two main options were considered: analytic error
propagation and error propagation. The first option, while precise, would be extremely
labor­intensive given the many equations and parameters involved, each with its own un­
certainty range. Therefore, error propagation using Monte Carlo simulation was selected
as the more efficient and practical approach.
As outlined in the IPCC Guidelines (Chapter 3, Section 3.5, IPCC 2019) [3], uncertainty in
default parameter values is represented using confidence intervals, while the parameter
values were given as average value of the distribution. These intervals establish the plau­
sible range for parameter values, bounded by upper and lower limits, which indicate the
maximum and minimum plausible values within a specified confidence level.In the context
of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, a 95% confidence interval is employed. Addition­
ally, the IPCC specifies that GHG­related parameters must always be non­negative, which
makes the log­normal distribution an ideal choice for modeling these uncertainties, as it
ensures that all values remain positive [3].
These values are then used to generate random samples from the probability distributions
of the parameters, producing a range of possible outcomes and creating a log­normal
distribution for each parameter. This approach effectively captures how parameter un­
certainty impacts the results [5]. It is important to note that the confidence intervals and
averages are based on the IPCC and DNI guidelines, depending on the tier being ana­
lyzed, while adhering to the same definition of uncertainty limits.
For both livestock and agricultural calculations, the same approach was employed, while
adapting it to the specific equations and parameters relevant to each case. First, the
parameters in the equations that need to account for uncertainty are identified. Each
parameter is defined by its mean, lower, and upper values, and combined into a data
frame that represents the uncertainty ranges, effectively capturing a skewed distribution.
For each parameter used in the equation, a matrix is created where the number of rows
corresponds to the length of the parameter, and the number of columns represents the
number of simulations (in this case, 10,000). This matrix stores the generated samples,
which follow a log­normal distribution.

The parameters µ and σ of the log­normal distribution were numerically estimated based
on the parameters provided by the IPCC, which include the lower and upper confidence
limits as well as the non­logarithmic mean. The estimation was performed using the R
function optimise [51].

For a given mean, µ was expressed as a function of the known non­logarithmic mean
and the yet unknown logarithmic σ, as shown in Equation 5.38. The optimal value for
σ was determined as the value that minimized the absolute difference between 0.95 and
the range of cumulative probabilities calculated for the known upper and lower confidence
limits using the R function plnorm [51].
Once µ and σ are calculated, the rlnorm() function [51] generates N random samples
from a log­normal distribution with the specified µ and σ[52]. This step is repeated for as
many parameters as needed in the equation [5].
The uncertainties are aggregated for each unique combination of parameters. Once all
parameter matrices are generated, an empty matrix (‘res‘) is created to store the final
results for each row, with N columns representing the number of simulations. These
results are obtained by combining the parameter matrices with other constants used in the
equations, ultimately aiming to propagate uncertainty from the parameters to the results.
From the simulated distribution of the results, the parameters µ and σ of the log­normal
distribution were fitted using the R function fitdistr from the R package MASS[53]. Based
on these fitted parameters, the non­logarithmic mean and the lower and upper confidence
limits were calculated, providing a robust characterization of the uncertainty in the results.
The subsequent step involves fitting the simulated values to a log­normal distribution us­
ing a function that takes the mean, lower, and upper limits of each parameter. The lower
and upper confidence limits are then calculated using the QLNorm function, along with es­
timating the mode and mean of the distribution [50]. The QLNorm function is the keystone
of the Monte Carlo simulation because it calculates the quantiles of the fitted log­normal
distribution. Specifically, it is used to determine the lower and upper confidence limits at
the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles, respectively—key measures for defining the uncertainty
range of the distribution. By accurately calculating the quantiles, QLNorm ensures that the
95% confidence interval is represented for each parameter, allowing for a robust assess­
ment of the variability and reliability of the emissions estimates [5]. Essentially, QLNorm
helps define the boundaries of uncertainty for each parameter, enabling the propagation
of uncertainty throughout the entire calculation process.
As a final step, a visual representation of the uncertainty in the results is created through
a combination of histograms, density curves, and key statistical markers. The histogram
represents the frequency distribution of the simulated values, showing how often different
outcomes occur across all iterations. On top of this histogram, a smooth density curve
is added, illustrating the fitted log­normal distribution, providing insight into the general
shape and spread of the data [50]. To further enhance the understanding of the results,
vertical lines are plotted for key statistics: the lower and upper confidence limits mark
53
==PAGE:66==
the boundaries of the 95% confidence interval, indicating the plausible range of values;
the mode shows the most frequent outcome, representing the peak of the distribution;
and the mean highlights the average of the simulated values. Additionally, the original
calculated value is plotted to provide a reference to the initial deterministic estimate before
the uncertainty analysis. These elements, combined with a legend for clarity, make it
possible to easily visualize the variability and central tendencies of the emission estimates,
effectively communicating the influence of uncertainty on the results [5].
Lastly, it is worthy to note that in the uncertainty analysis specifically for the agricultural
calculations, it was critical to distinguish between variability and uncertainty. Variability
refers to differences inherent in field size—such as small versus large fields—which af­
fects the distribution of emissions due to scaling factors but is not inherently uncertain [2].
On the other hand, uncertainty pertains to the unknowns or ranges in parameters used
for emissions calculations.
To effectively propagate uncertainty without inflating it due to known variables, the ap­
proach taken was to categorize fields into unique combinations according to the equation
considered in each case, rather than calculating uncertainty for each of the 1673 individ­
ual fields [5]. Each category represented fields with similar properties, and the emissions
uncertainty for each category was then scaled by the total area. This allowed for efficient
computation and ensured that the resulting uncertainty accurately reflected parameter
uncertainties rather than field size variability.
By aggregating the categories and multiplying by the total area—which is accurately
known—the uncertainty captured was primarily due to the variability in emission fac­
tors and other parameters, and not from scaling due to area differences. This approach
provided a broader but more representative distribution of uncertainty across the entire
dataset, isolating the effects of parameter uncertainty from field size differences or animal
numbers per farm.

Results
In 2021, Denmark’s greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions were primarily driven by the energy
sector, which accounted for 61.8% of the total emissions. Agriculture was the second­
largest contributor at 26.2%, followed by Land Use, Land­Use Change, and Forestry
(LULUCF) at 5.3%. Emissions from industrial processes and product use represented
4.0%, while waste contributed 2.7%. Including LULUCF, Denmark achieved a significant
41.0% reduction in total emissions from 1990 to 2021, underscoring the success of its
climate policies and the ongoing transition toward sustainable practices [2].
Agriculture and LULUCF are significant contributors to Denmark’s greenhouse gas (GHG)
emissions, primarily through the release of carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and
nitrous oxide (N2O).
In Denmark, CO2 emissions are predominantly associated with the LULUCF sector, ac­
counting for 88.5% of total CO2 emissions. Agricultural activities also contribute, with
liming responsible for 11.3%, urea application for 0.05%, and the use of other carbon­
containing fertilizers adding 0.14%. These figures highlight the critical role of land use
and management practices, as well as fertilizer application, in addressing CO2 emissions
in Denmark [2].
In contrast, Hove’s CO2 emissions represent a very small fraction of Denmark’s emissions
across all categories. For instance, CO2 emission from liming in Hove account for only
0.17% of Denmark’s total, while its emissions from LULUCF contribute a mere 0.03%. No­
tably, no emissions were reported in Hove for urea application or other carbon­containing
fertilizers. Within Hove itself, LULUCF is the largest contributor, responsible for 60.61%
of CO2 emissions, followed by liming, which accounts for 39.39%.
In Denmark, CH4 emissions are primarily linked to agricultural activities, with enteric fer­
mentation being the largest source, accounting for 55.4% of total CH4 emissions. Manure
management is the second­largest contributor at 40.7%. Emissions from LULUCF are
minor, contributing 3.9%, while the field burning of agricultural residues accounts for less
than 0.1% [2].
Hove’s CH4 emissions constitute a very small proportion of Denmark’s total CH4 emis­
sions. For example, Hove’s emissions from enteric fermentation account for just 0.043%
of Denmark’s total in this category, and its emissions from manure management contribute
only 0.031%. No CH4 emissions were reported in Hove for field burning of agricultural
residues or LULUCF. Within Hove itself, enteric fermentation dominates CH4 emissions,
contributing 64.97%, followed by manure management at 35.03%.
In Denmark, N2O emissions are distributed across various agricultural activities. The
largest contributors are inorganic fertilizers (19.5%), crop residues (18.9%), and the ap­
plication of animal manure to soils (17.1%). Other significant sources include manure
management (12.2%), cultivation of organic soils (10.9%), and nitrogen leaching and
runoff (10.7%). Minor contributors include atmospheric deposition (3.2%), sewage sludge
applied to soils (0.4%), other organic fertilizers (0.5%), and field burning of agricultural
residues (0.01%) [2].
In comparison, Hove’s N2O emissions are negligible compared to Denmark’s. For in­
stance, emissions from inorganic fertilizers in Hove account for only 0.17% of Denmark’s

In this section, the results of the uncertainty analysis are presented. As explained in
Section 5.4, the uncertainty was assessed through a Monte Carlo simulation to capture
the propagation of parameter uncertainties into the emission results. Here, we focus on
a few key emission sources, while the complete set of results is provided in the Annex.
The results of this analysis are presented by comparing the original calculated value, also
referred to as the deterministic estimate, to its respective position in relation to the mode
and mean values derived from the simulations. This allows to assess how representative
the calculated value is compared to the most probable outcome (mode) and the average
outcome (mean) of the log­normal distribution. Additionally, confidence limits are provided
to illustrate the range and spread of potential outcomes, offering insights into the level of
uncertainty associated with specific emission sources. These limits not only allow for the
analysis of individual emissions but also facilitate the comparison of aggregated emissions
across different tiers, providing a broader understanding of uncertainty at both granular
and systemic levels.

narrower confidence intervals and more reliable estimates compared to Tier 1, under­
scoring the value of region­specific parameters over generalized assumptions. Similarly,
for agriculture, the analysis of N2O and CO2 emissions revealed that Tier 2 approaches
reduced uncertainty, particularly for manure applied to soil and crop residues. However,
specific emission sources, such as organic soils and synthetic fertilizers, exhibited persis­
tent high variability, emphasizing the complexity of modeling these processes accurately.
The comparison of aggregated emissions further reinforces the advantages of Tier 2
methodologies in reducing uncertainty. Total emissions from agriculture, converted to
CO2 equivalents, showed narrower confidence intervals under Tier 2, suggesting a more
consistent representation of emissions. Nevertheless, the persistent variability in certain
emission sources, evident in the wide error bars for Tier 1 and in specific years, highlights
the challenges of accurately modeling complex agricultural systems with generalized pa­
rameters.
However, it is important to consider the limitations inherent in the uncertainty analysis.
In this work, only those parameters with explicitly defined confidence limits were incor­
porated into the Monte Carlo simulations, while parameters without such bounds were
treated as constants. This exclusion introduces a limitation by ignoring potential variability
in these parameters, which could affect the overall uncertainty of the emission estimates.
Consequently, this may result in underestimating the true uncertainty for specific emission
categories.
In real­world scenarios, parameters treated as constants may still exhibit inherent vari­
ability due to environmental conditions, management practices, or measurement errors.
By not accounting for this variability, our analysis may present a narrower view of the ac­
tual uncertainty. This could give a misleading impression of higher confidence in certain
emission estimates than is realistically justifiable.
This limitation is particularly important for emission sources where the unaccounted pa­
rameters contribute significantly. The reported confidence intervals, as well as the differ­
ences between the mean, mode, and original calculated values, may underrepresent the
94
==PAGE:107==
true uncertainty in these cases. Therefore, acknowledging these constraints is essential
for interpreting the results accurately.
In conclusion, the uncertainty analysis underscores the importance of using Tier 2 method­
ologies for both livestock and agriculture to improve the precision and reliability of emis­
sion estimates. However, the findings also highlight areas for further refinement, including
incorporating a more comprehensive representation of parameter variability.

Discussion
The results (6) demonstrate that Tier 2 (T2) provides more accurate estimates for CH4,
N2O, and CO2 emissions compared to Tier 1 (T1), particularly in key sources such as live­
stock, fertilizers, and energy systems. These improvements are attributed to enhanced
methodologies and the integration of localized data. The overall percentage reduction
from T1 to T2 is approximately 21.36%.
The comparison of T1 and T2 emissions reveals significant reductions across all cat­
egories. In livestock systems, enteric fermentation (CH4) emissions were reduced by
13.3%, while manure management (CH4) experienced the largest decrease of 69.8%.
N2O emissions from manure management showed an overall reduction of 47.4%, with
specific reductions of 43.8% for volatilization and 7.6% for leaching.
Total emissions
across all livestock­related categories decreased by 35.3%, highlighting the efficacy of
improved methodologies.
In agriculture, emissions from fertilizers decreased by 15.49%, while crop residue emis­
sions dropped by 21.93%.
Organic soils experienced a modest reduction of 5.27%,
whereas mineral soils showed a minor increase of 0.33%. Indirect emissions demon­
strated the most substantial reduction at 37.25%. Overall, total emissions across all agri­
cultural categories were reduced by 20.64%.
Energy systems exhibited mixed trends. In stationary sources, emissions from liquid fuels
increased by 6.11%, and solid fuels rose by 4.37%, while gaseous fuels saw a slight
reduction of 1.13%. Biomass emissions from stationary sources showed a significant
decline of 18.25%. In off­road vehicles, emissions from gasoline increased by 5.33%,
while diesel oil emissions decreased marginally by 0.002%. Biomass emissions in this
category dropped notably by 35.73%. Collectively, these variations led to an 8.47% overall
reduction in emissions across energy systems.
By focusing on major emission sources such as enteric fermentation, manure manage­
ment, fertilizers, and energy systems, T2 establishes a foundation for targeted mitigation
strategies. Although the select study region represents less than 1% of the total agricul­
tural and livestock emissions from Denmark, these insights can help to further improve
regional and local calculations.
A notable application of this work lies in its potential to build scenarios for improving land
use in Hove. Ideally, one could now use this project and software to explore scenarios
of how the land in Hove can be optimized, balancing production needs with other crit­
ical ecosystem services such as CO2 sequestration and biodiversity conservation [60].
These scenarios would inherently require addressing complex considerations, such as
integrating the Hove area into Denmark’s Green Transition roadmap [61] and aligning
with the goals of local municipalities and private landowners [62]. While these scenarios
are beyond the scope of this thesis, the methodology developed herein provides a robust
framework to analyze the effects of land­use transitions on the greenhouse gas (GHG)
budget [63], offering a valuable tool for future research and decision­making.
Moreover, the results from this thesis can serve as a basis for comparison with actual GHG
measurements taken at the Hove tower [64]. While this comparison is not attempted in
the current work, it is essential to note that not all emission estimates from this study are
directly comparable with tower measurements. The Hove tower records all emissions,
96
==PAGE:109==
including those from transportation, residential activities, and CO2 sinks and sources not
captured in the UNFCCC inventory [65]. However, for non­CO2 GHG emissions, the direct
emissions from agriculture estimated in this thesis are more closely aligned with the tower
data, providing a useful point of comparison for refining future emission models [66].
Greenhouse gas emission estimations across the evaluated sectors are subject to sig­
nificant limitations stemming from methodological constraints, data availability issues,
and gaps in supporting policies [2]. Specific challenges for this thesis include insuffi­
cient region­specific data, inconsistencies in datasets, and reliance on generic IPCC Tier
1 methodologies [16].
For instance, the LandbrugsGIS data [48, 4] regarding animal husbandry data is not up
to date and does not align with the Central Husbandry Register (CHR) data. As a result,
some livestock farms within the study area were excluded from the analysis, although
these farms may still exist but are not accurately represented in this study. Another chal­
lenge involves the lack of detailed information regarding the housing conditions, manure
management practices and, productivity system at each farm. As previously discussed,
these factors can significantly influence emissions and therefore have a considerable im­
pact on the accuracy of emission estimates. Similar limitations are present in the agri­
cultural calculations where the lack of specific data required assumptions to be made.
As a consequence, relevant agricultural aspects such as tillage practices or the fertil­
izer application rates might not accurately represent the actual practices or trends in the
area. Additionally, some datasets required for calculations, particularly those spanning 20
years, were unavailable, and thus calculations were performed using a shorter, 10­year
dataset instead.
In terms of the methodology applied, the use of different tiers and equations provided a
valuable framework for estimating greenhouse gas emissions. However, the final results
indicated that Tier 1 approaches, although convenient, often oversimplify the complexity
of the underlying processes and interactions between different systems. This issue was
particularly evident in the agricultural calculations. It is well known that for more accurate
estimations, as demonstrated in the Danish Inventory Report, more advanced methods
like C­TOOL model are used [2]. C­TOOL incorporates numerous parameters and inputs
to more comprehensively capture the intricacies of soil and agricultural processes, thus
offering a more reliable representation of actual emissions. The simplified Tier 1 equations
applied in this study highlighted both the benefits and limitations—while these simpler
methods made it feasible to calculate emissions with limited data, they also failed to fully
account for the variability and complexity of agricultural systems.
These considerations are also evident in the results of the uncertainty analysis. A com­
parison of Tier 1 and Tier 2 methodologies shows clear differences in uncertainty levels,
assessed by comparing how closely calculated values aligned with the mode and mean
of the distributions. The lower uncertainty in Tier 2 can be attributed not only to the use
of country­specific parameters that better reflect local conditions but also to the fact that
these parameters tend to have more precise and well­defined uncertainty bounds. In
contrast, Tier 1 relies on generalized IPCC default values, which often lack the level of
precision needed to account for complex interactions, leading to larger discrepancies and
broader uncertainty ranges.
Uncertainty levels also differ significantly between livestock and cropping systems. Live­
stock emissions, particularly methane emissions from enteric fermentation and manure
management, exhibit smaller uncertainties. This may be because the processes in live­
stock systems can be better represented through equations, and the available data cap­
97
==PAGE:110==
tures the system’s characteristics effectively. Conversely, the cropping sector presents
a higher degree of uncertainty, largely due to the complex interactions between soil, air,
and plant systems that are more difficult to capture using simplified equations. Such in­
teractions mean that Tier 1 and Tier 2 methodologies, which rely on general assumptions,
may miss important nuances, leading to either overestimation or underestimation of emis­
sions. This highlights the importance of implementing more refined and comprehensive
models that can accurately represent greenhouse gas emissions in agricultural systems.
Aggregated results suggest that livestock emission estimates generally exhibit a close
alignment between the calculated values, the mode, and the mean, indicating relatively
narrow uncertainty ranges in the analysis. This pattern suggests that the determinis­
tic estimates for livestock emissions are likely representative of both the most probable
outcome (mode) and the average outcome (mean). In the case of agricultural emissions,
similar trends were observed for some emission sources, where calculated values closely
matched both the mode and mean. However, certain sources displayed greater variabil­
ity, with larger differences between the calculated value and the mode or mean. This
indicates that agricultural emission estimates, may have more significant uncertainties,
reflecting the inherent variability of agricultural systems and the limitations of generalized
assumptions in the calculation process.
It is important to recognize the broader implications of the limitations present in this uncer­
tainty analysis. By treating parameters without explicitly defined confidence limits as con­
stants, the potential variability in these factors remains unaccounted for. This approach
likely results in underestimating the overall uncertainty, thereby providing a potentially
misleading representation of these emission estimates.
To address all these limitations, it is evidently that enhance the data collection framework
is essential. Conducting region­specific surveys on manure management, lime applica­
tion practices, fertilizer use, and crop residue handling would enable the development of
tailored emission factors [8]. Utilizing advanced tools for soil carbon modeling can refine
calculations and provide more precise estimates [3]. Expanding temporal models to in­
clude annual changes in biomass and transitioning to Tier 2 or Tier 3 methodologies for
livestock and soil emissions would capture local variations and improve accuracy [16].
Implementing targeted emission reduction strategies alongside methodological and policy
improvements is crucial. Optimizing livestock feed with dietary supplements and additives
such as tannins or seaweed can significantly reduce methane production [25]. Transition­
ing to anaerobic digestion systems can capture methane and produce renewable biogas
[27]. Composting and separating liquid from solid manure can reduce both methane and
nitrous oxide emissions [3]. Practices like using straw bedding, rotational grazing, and
increasing pasture time minimize emissions while enhancing nutrient recycling [36].
Adopting conservation tillage, no­till farming, and cover cropping can reduce emissions by
enhancing soil carbon storage and minimizing soil disturbance [43]. Precision agriculture
techniques optimize fertilizer application, reducing nitrogen losses while maintaining pro­
ductivity [34]. Shifting from synthetic to organic fertilizers and incorporating crop residues
into the soil instead of burning them are effective strategies for emission reduction [29].
Re­wetting degraded peatlands and limiting the drainage of organic soils can prevent
substantial carbon loss [31]. Adding biochar to mineral soils can sequester carbon and
improve soil health [39]. Implementing improved soil conservation practices and updating
stock factor modeling are essential for maintaining soil carbon stocks [3].
Developing models that account for emission transfers across regions and promoting local
98
==PAGE:111==
production and consumption patterns can reduce transport­related emissions [10]. Adopt­
ing circular economy principles minimizes waste and enhances resource efficiency [24].
Afforestation and reforestation efforts can offset emissions, while sustainable harvesting
practices prevent overexploitation [3]. Updating land classifications and refining models
to reflect annual biomass changes will enhance emission reduction efforts [9].
Optimizing liming application timing and quantities based on soil testing can prevent overuse
[45]. Alternatives such as gypsum or biochar offer additional benefits [39]. Educating
farmers on best practices and providing subsidies for soil testing can refine emission fac­
tors and improve sustainability [41]. Transitioning to renewable energy sources such as
solar or wind power can significantly reduce emissions [58]. Improving the efficiency of
farm machinery and transport systems, and adopting electric or biofuel­powered equip­
ment, are practical strategies for reducing fossil fuel dependency [59].
Policy reforms are essential to advance sustainable practices and improve emission method­
ologies. Governments should fund research to develop region­specific emission factors
and incentivize the adoption of advanced tools through subsidies [8]. Mandating detailed
agricultural and land­use reporting will enhance data quality [14]. Promoting collabora­
tion among researchers, farmers, and policymakers ensures robust and adaptable ap­
proaches, while international alignment with climate agreements supports standardized
yet region­specific solutions [12].
Policies encouraging renewable energy adoption through tax breaks, grants, and low­
interest loans can accelerate the transition to clean technologies [58]. Support for forest
protection, reforestation, and regulated biomass harvesting is crucial [24], as is fostering
local supply chains to reduce dependence on imported agricultural inputs [37]. Incentives
for land restoration, biochar use, and biogas infrastructure, paired with farmer training
programs, can drive the widespread adoption of sustainable practices [34]. Promoting
innovation in feed optimization and the development of low­methane­emitting livestock
will ensure long­term impact [25].
By addressing the limitations in current methodologies and implementing targeted emis­
sion reduction strategies along with robust policy frameworks, a comprehensive approach
to mitigating greenhouse gas emissions can be achieved. Enhancing data collection,
adopting advanced tools, refining practices, and fostering international collaboration will
enable agriculture and land management systems to significantly contribute to global cli­
mate goals [12]. Policies that align incentives with sustainability objectives will ensure
that these efforts are both practical and impactful, driving long­term environmental and
economic benefits [2].

8
Conclusion
This thesis set out to evaluate greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions within a 6­kilometer
radius of the eddy covariance (EC) tower in Hove, Denmark, by adapting the Intergov­
ernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) methodologies to a localized context. By
integrating detailed geospatial analysis, comprehensive data collection, and both Tier 1
(T1) and Tier 2 (T2) methodological approaches, the study aimed to provide a more ac­
curate and region­specific assessment of emissions from agricultural activities, livestock,
and energy systems.
The results demonstrated that the T2 methodology, which incorporates country­specific
data and refined emission factors, offers a more precise estimation of GHG emissions
compared to the T1 approach. Across all categories—livestock, agriculture, and energy
systems—T2 calculations consistently yielded lower emission estimates, highlighting the
limitations of generalized T1 methodologies when applied to localized settings. The over­
all reduction in emissions from T1 to T2 was approximately 21.36%, underscoring the
significant impact that refined data and methodologies can have on emissions invento­
ries.
The findings of this study have significant implications for national climate policies and
GHG inventory practices. By demonstrating the effectiveness of adapting IPCC method­
ologies with localized data, policymakers can improve the accuracy of national GHG in­
ventories, leading to more informed decision­making and better alignment with interna­
tional climate agreements such as the Paris Agreement. The adoption of T2 methodolo­
gies at regional and national levels could facilitate the setting of more accurate emission
reduction targets and enhance compliance with reporting requirements.
For agricultural stakeholders and farmers in the Hove area and beyond, the study high­
lights practical strategies for reducing emissions. Implementing the recommended sus­
tainable practices, such as optimizing livestock feed, adopting precision agriculture tech­
niques, and improving manure management systems, can lead to both environmental
benefits and potential economic gains. Overcoming barriers to adoption, such as the
need for education, financial incentives, or technological support, will be crucial. Gov­
ernment programs that provide subsidies, training, and access to technology can play a
pivotal role in facilitating this transition.
This research advances the current methodologies for localized GHG emissions assess­
ment by integrating high­resolution geospatial data with refined emission factors. The use
of detailed land­use data, soil characteristics, and localized activity data provides a more
nuanced understanding of emissions at a fine spatial scale. By addressing specific gaps
in the literature related to the accuracy of localized emissions data and the applicability
of IPCC methodologies at smaller scales, the study contributes valuable insights that can
inform future research and methodology development.
Despite the advancements made, the study faced limitations, particularly regarding data
availability and quality. Challenges included outdated livestock inventory data, misalign­
ment between datasets, and the reliance on generalized emission factors in certain areas
due to the lack of region­specific information. Additionally, temporal constraints limited the
ability to capture dynamic changes over multiple years. Future research should focus on
enhancing data collection frameworks, perhaps by conducting region­specific surveys and
integrating real­time data from monitoring stations like the EC tower. Incorporating Tier
100
==PAGE:113==
3 methodologies, which involve more sophisticated modeling and higher­resolution data,
could further improve the accuracy of emissions assessments. Expanding the scope of
the study to include other environmental impacts, such as biodiversity, water quality, and
socio­economic factors, would provide a more holistic understanding of the sustainability
of agricultural practices.
The methodologies developed in this thesis are adaptable and can be applied to other
regions with diverse agricultural landscapes and environmental conditions. Adjustments
may be necessary to account for regional differences in climate, soil types, agricultural
practices, and livestock management. By tailoring the approach to local contexts, other
regions can enhance the accuracy of their GHG emissions inventories and develop tar­
geted mitigation strategies. Furthermore, the approach can be scaled up to national lev­
els, contributing to improved national inventories, or down to more localized farm­level as­
sessments, enabling individual farmers to understand and manage their emissions more
effectively.
Adopting the recommended emission reduction strategies offers potential economic ben­
efits, such as cost savings from improved efficiency, enhanced productivity, and access
to new revenue streams like carbon credits. Engaging local communities, farmers, and
industry stakeholders is essential in the development and implementation of these strate­
gies. Collaborative efforts can ensure that mitigation measures are practical, economi­
cally viable, and culturally acceptable.
The adoption of sustainable agricultural practices not only reduces GHG emissions but
also yields co­benefits such as enhanced soil health, reduced erosion, improved water
retention, and increased biodiversity. These practices can enhance the resilience of agri­
cultural systems to the impacts of climate change, including extreme weather events and
shifting growing seasons. By promoting ecosystem services, sustainable practices con­
tribute to long­term environmental sustainability and food security.
In conclusion, this thesis demonstrates the critical importance of adapting global method­
ologies to local contexts to accurately assess GHG emissions. The urgency of addressing
GHG emissions in agriculture is clear, and the opportunity presented by this research is
significant. By integrating localized data and refining emission factors, it is possible to
achieve a more precise understanding of a region’s emissions profile, which is essential
for developing effective mitigation strategies. The findings from the Hove area provide
valuable insights that can inform national climate policies and support Denmark’s com­
mitment to reducing GHG emissions. Policymakers, stakeholders, and the scientific com­
munity are encouraged to take these findings forward, implement the recommendations,
and continue advancing the field through collaboration and innovation. By doing so, we
can make meaningful progress toward mitigating climate change and promoting sustain­
able agricultural practices, contributing to global efforts to safeguard our environment for
future generations.

A.3
R codes
DK_CH4_N2O_animalHusbandry.R
This script calculates methane and nitrous oxide emissions from enteric fermentation and
manure management. It relies on parameters and functions provided in DK_IPCC_livestock
parmFunctions.R, which are loaded as an RData file. The script uses the openxlsx pack­
age for handling Excel inputs and requires the user to adjust file paths for data loading
and saving.
108
==PAGE:121==
DK_IPCC_livestock_parmFunctions.R
This script provides functions and parameters for calculating CH4 and N2O emissions from
livestock. It outputs data as an RData file (DK_DCE_2023_CH4_animal_husbandry_parms.RData),
which can be used in related scripts such as DK_CH4_N2O_animalHusbandry.R. Excel in­
put files A.2 and A.2 are required to define parameters and configurations. Additionally,
the script includes a simple_parameter_merge.f function to streamline parameter merg­
ing.
DK_IPCC_crops_Emissions.R
This script performs all calculations of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from crop pro­
duction activities. It depends on functions and parameters defined in DK_IPCC_crops_parmsFunctions.R
File paths for reading and writing data need to be adjusted by the user. The script outputs
comprehensive emissions results for crop­related activities and integrates seamlessly with
Excel­based inputs and storage.
DK_IPCC_crops_parmsFunctions.R
This script defines functions and parameters necessary for calculating carbon dioxide
(CO2) and nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions from crops. The parameters and functions are
stored in an RData image file (DK_IPCC_crops_parms.RData) for easy reuse. Excel inputs
A.2 and A.2 are required to initialize parameter values. The script also includes a helper
function, simple_parameter_merge.f, to efficiently manage parameters and variables.
Uncertainty_animalHusbandry_emissions.R
This script evaluates uncertainties in CH4 and N2O emissions from animal husbandry. It
leverages outputs from DK_IPCC_livestock_parmFunctions.R, particularly parameters
stored in the RData file DK_DCE_2023_CH4_animal_husbandry_parms.RData. The script
includes a custom function, evaluate.f, which calculates and visualizes distributions,
confidence limits, and uncertainty intervals for emissions estimates. Optional graphing
functionality allows the user to explore results and gain deeper insights into the nature
and extent of uncertainties. It integrates with existing emissions calculations and relies
on Excel files A.2 and A.2 for parameter data.
Uncertainty_livestock_parmFunctions.R
This script builds on DK_IPCC_livestock_parmFunctions.R, enhancing it with functional­
ity for assessing uncertainties in livestock emissions. It incorporates statistical modeling
capabilities through the MASS package, enabling detailed analysis of variability in CH4
and N2O emission parameters. The script retains the ability to define and store param­
eters in an RData file (DK_DCE_2023_CH4_animal_husbandry_parms.RData). Excel input
files A.2 and A.2 provide the parameter data needed for analysis. The output integrates
seamlessly with related scripts, offering a robust framework for uncertainty evaluation in
livestock­related greenhouse gas emissions.
Uncertainty_crops_Emissions.R
This script quantifies uncertainties in greenhouse gas emissions from crop­related activi­
ties. It uses functions and parameters defined in DK_IPCC_crops_parmsFunctions.R and
integrates outputs stored in DK_IPCC_crops_parms.RData. A key feature is the mode.f
function, which computes distribution modes from statistical inputs such as mean values,
lower bounds, and upper bounds. The script employs the MASS package for probabilistic
modeling and offers tools for visualizing the variability in emissions estimates. Excel input
files A.2 and A.2 provide the necessary parameter and configuration data.
Uncertainty_crops_parmsFunctions.R
This script extends the functionality of DK_IPCC_crops_parmsFunctions.R by incorporat­
ing features for uncertainty analysis. It provides functions to manage variability in param­
109
==PAGE:122==
eter values and supports statistical modeling using the MASS package. The script also
utilizes openxlsx to handle Excel­based input files A.2 and A.2. Parameter uncertainties
are calculated and stored alongside emissions estimates in an updated RData file. This
functionality supports comprehensive uncertainty evaluation in crop­related greenhouse
gas emissions.
