Abstract
Vitamin D deficiency is prevalent among populations with limited sun exposure,
particularly in northern latitudes during winter. Most vitamin D3 supplements are
derived from lanolin, raising considerations due to its animal origin. Research
exploring alternative sources of vitamin D3 has highlighted the potential of mi­
croalgae, given their advantageous biochemical composition. Nannochloropsis
oceanica is notable for its ability to synthesize 7­dehydrocholesterol, a precursor
of vitamin D3, making it a potential non­animal source. Cultivation parameters
were systematically varied: temperature (19°C and 27°C) versus salinity (23 PPT
and 37 PPT), and light intensity (150 µmol photons m­2 s­1 and 350 µmol photons
m­2 s­1) versus pH (7 and 8). Vitamin D3 content was analyzed using UHPLC­
MS/MS. Lowering the temperature from 27°C to 19°C significantly increased vita­
min D3 content: 232.178 ± 1.533 (19°C, 37 PPT) compared to 75.174 ± 10.989
(27°C, 23 PPT) and 87.251 ± 15.812 (27°C, 37 PPT), despite challenges in culti­
vation at 19°C. Light intensity and pH did not show statistically significant effects
on vitamin D3 content, although cultivation challenges may have influenced these
results. Growth rates were primarily influenced by temperature and light intensity
rather than salinity and pH. Cultivating Nannochloropsis oceanica under stress
presents challenges, hence the need for a balance between biomass yield and
optimizing pathways for 7­dehydrocholesterol synthesis. This study contributes to
understanding the impact of cultivation conditions on vitamin D3 production dur­
ing UVB exposure. Future research could explore harvesting at different growth
stages, investigating less pigmented mutants, or implementing a two­step cultiva­
tion approach.

1
Introduction
This study forms part of a PhD project focused on researching sustainable pro­
duction of microalgae proteins and other bioactive compounds.
This study aims to evaluate how different cultivation conditions, such as temper­
ature, salinity, pH, and light intensity, affect the subsequent production of vitamin
D3 in Nannochloropsis oceanica during UVB exposure. Additionally, this study
seeks to optimize the combination of these cultivation conditions to maximize vi­
tamin D3 production in Nannochloropsis oceanica during UVB exposure.
The hypothesis proposed is that the production of vitamin D3 in Nannochloropsis
oceanica during UVB exposure is influenced by cultivation conditions, including
temperature, salinity, pH, and light intensity.
1.1
What is Vitamin D?
Vitamins are essential for maintaining a healthy life and promoting human growth.
They can be classified into two major categories: water­soluble vitamins and fat­
soluble vitamins. Water­soluble vitamins include vitamins C (ascorbic acid), B1
(thiamine), B2 (riboflavin), B3 (niacin), B6 (pyridoxine), B12 (cobalamin), pan­
tothenic acid, biotin, and folate. They are generally excreted through the urine
when the plasma concentration reaches a certain renal threshold, hence not stored
in large amounts in body tissues [1]. In contrast, are fat­soluble vitamins, includ­
ing vitamins A, D, K, and E, stored in the body’s fatty tissues and liver, rather than
being excreted [2].
Vitamin D is particularly interesting because, unlike most other vitamins that are
primarily obtained through the diet, it can be synthesized in our skin when exposed
to ultraviolet B (UVB) sunlight in the range of about 280–315 nm [3, 4], where
the narrow­band within 295­300 nm is particular effective [5]. Vitamin D can be
categorized into two major forms: D3 (cholecalciferol) and D2 (ergocalciferol).
Vitamin D3 is the form that is synthesized in our skin [2, 3]. Sources of vitamin D3

1.2
Biosynthesis of Vitamin D3
Sterols contribute to the fluidity and permeability of the cell membrane, making
them important for maintaining the structural stability of the cell. They are com­
prised of four rings, A, B, C, and D, which make up their backbone, from where a
variety of sterols can be generated by substituting different side chains and chem­
ical groups to the backbone [3]. 7­dehydrocholesterol is the precursor of provita­
min D3 which is converted to previtamin D3 by exposure to UVB light. Previtamin
D3 is biologically inactive and thermodynamically unstable, hence it is converted
to vitamin D3 by thermal isomerization [3]. Both the liver and the kidney play a
key role in the activation of vitamin D3. Following the thermal isomerization in
the skin, vitamin D3 will bind to vitamin D binding protein (DBP), which will guide
its way in the body through the blood. The first stop is in the liver where vitamin
D3 is hydroxylated at C­25 by a 25­hydroxylase, resulting in 25­hydroxyvitamin
D3 (25(OH)D3). The vitamin D3 that is absorbed when digesting food is trans­
ported to the liver with the help of chylomicrons and DBP and hydroxylated as well.
The second stop is in the kidneys, where 1α­hydroxylase acts on the α­position
of C­1, which results in the biologically active form 1α,25­dihydroxyvitamin D3
(1,25(OH)2D3). The synthesis is illustrated in Figure 1.1. This active form helps
regulate and maintain our bodies’ calcium and phosphorus levels [3].
Both 25­hydroxyvitamin D and 1,25­dihydroxyvitamin D serve as biomarkers for
measuring the vitamin D concentration in the body. These biomarkers account for
vitamin D3 produced in the skin as well as vitamin D3 and vitamin D2 obtained
from dietary intake. The concentration is typically the sum of these two forms [8].

1.3
Vitamin D Deficiency
Optimal vitamin D concentrations are crucial to maintaining bone health [2, 3].
Populations at higher risk include individuals with limited sun exposure, such as
elderly people, Muslim women who wear traditional attire that covers most of their
skin, and individuals who do not engage in outdoor activities. Individuals with
very dark skin are prone to vitamin D deficiency because the high pigmentation
protects the skin against both UVA and UVB light [4]. Health conditions that affect
vitamin D metabolism or a diminished diet can also increase the risk of vitamin D
deficiency.
Countries positioned at northern latitudes, 40° or above, do not receive sufficient
UVB light during the cold and dark months from October until April [9], which in­
creases the risk of seasonal variations in vitamin D deficiency. The Danish Health
Authority recommends taking a daily 5­10 µg vitamin D supplement from October
to April, with exceptions for specific groups who are advised to take vitamin D
supplements throughout the entire year [10].

1.4
Vitamin D Supplements
The vitamin D market in Danish pharmacies is dominated by the D3 version. This
preference arises because vitamin D3 is the form synthesized in our skin and
because some studies have demonstrated that it is more potent in the human
body compared to vitamin D2 [11, 12, 13]. However, controversies exist regarding
which of the two forms, vitamin D3 or vitamin D2, is most potent. Some studies
have shown that vitamin D2 is equally effective [14, 15].
Conventional supplements containing vitamin D3 are produced by deriving 7­
dehydrocholesterol from lanolin, which is then exposed to UVB light to produce
vitamin D3. Lanolin is derived from sheep’s wool [16]. Since sheep are also
slaughtered for meat, lanolin from the wool can be considered a byproduct of
the meat industry. This connection makes the production of vitamin D3 derived
from sheep’s wool controversial and raises ethical concerns. Figure 1.2 illustrates
the selection of vitamin D from both non­animal and animal sources in a Danish
pharmacy. The vegan alternative costs nearly twice as much as the conventional
option, as shown in Figure 1.2A. The red box in Figure 1.2B highlights that the ori­
gin of the vitamin D3 is sheep’s wool, which is more transparent than many other
vitamin suppliers who do not disclose the source of their vitamin D. The pursuit
of more sustainable and ethical sources of vitamin D3 has become increasingly
important, which is why research into alternative sources is highly relevant.

1.5
Potential of Microalgae
Microalgae are photosynthetic unicellular microorganisms capable of converting
nitrogen, phosphorus, and CO2 from their environment into biomass. They prefer
to grow in aquatic environments, such as freshwater or saltwater depending on the
species, which is a great advantage since cultivation can be performed in areas
not suitable for traditional agriculture. They can also grow quickly and adapt to
various conditions making them very versatile [17].
Chlorella pyrenoidosa and Chlorella vulgaris have demonstrated the capability
and potentials of microalgae to grow on industrial process water, which is advan­
tageous because it recycles nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus. This reduces
the need for additional fertilizers and improves water quality, and potentially lessen
the environmental impacts of wastewater discharge and promote a more efficient
use of resources. Additionally, this process supports the production of lipids and

proteins, adding further value to microalgae cultivation [18].
A Chlorella pyrenoidosa strain has also demonstrated to be very efficient in utiliz­
ing CO2 as their carbon source, making them potential bio­fixers of atmospheric
CO2 [19].
Their rapid growth rates make them more effective than terrestrial
plants. This is largely due to their simple cellular structure and large surface­to­
volume ratio, which allow them to absorb substantial amounts of nutrients from
their environment [20, 19].
Optimizing technologies that utilize microalgae to
biosequester atmospheric CO2 could play a crucial role in addressing the global

Arthrospira platensis, more commonly known as Spirulina, has gained significant
popularity due to its rich biochemical composition, safety profile, and ease of di­
gestibility. It offers a high content of proteins, essential vitamins and minerals
[21].

Optimization of Vitamin D3 Production in

Manipulating cultivation conditions affects the biochemical composition of organ­
isms because they must adapt to their environment to survive and grow. By ma­
nipulating the cultivation conditions of Nannochloropsis oceanica such as tem­

pathways responsible for sterol biosynthesis, hence increase the content of 7­
dehydrocholesterol, the precursor of vitamin D3. Optimizing cultivation conditions
has the potential to tailor the biochemical profile of Nannochloropsis oceanica ef­
fectively, meeting specific industrial needs for non­animal sources of vitamin D3.

2.1
Microalgae Strain and General Cultivation
Conditions
Standard cultivation medium can be considered ASW consisting of 30 PPT Instant
Ocean Salt (Aquarium Systems, France) and 20 mM HEPES buffer, pH adjusted
(827 pH lab, Metrohm, Switzerland) to 7.5 with 2 M NaOH. Nutribloom (Necton,
Phytobloom, Portugal) was filter sterilized using a syringe filter with a pore size
of 0.20 µm (Labsolute, Th. Geyer, Germany), and 0.2­0.4% v/v was added to
the medium after autoclaving for 15 minutes at 121°C (CertoClav MultiControl 2,
Austria). 1% agar was additionally added for cultivation on solid medium.
The original wild­type strain of Nannochloropsis oceanica, NIVA­2/03, was ob­
tained from NORCCA (Norway), and was initially cultivated on solid standard
medium at room temperature under continuous light at ∼100 μmol photons m­2
s­1. Microalgae were inoculated into sterile cell culture flasks (Nunc EasYFlask,
Thermo Scientific, Waltham, Massachusetts, US) with 10­20 mL standard culti­
vation medium. The densities of the cultures were monitored by measuring the
OD750 using a spectrophotometer (Jenway 7205 UV/VIS, Buck & Holm, Denmark).
They were cultivated until reaching an OD750 of no more than 3­4 under continu­
ous light at 150 μmol photons m­2 s­1, room temperature, and with gentle shaking.

4
Discussion
The objective of this study was to examine key parameters that might affect the
synthesis of 7­dehydrocholesterol in Nannochloropsis oceanica during cultivation
and to analyze the vitamin D3 content of the cultures after exposure to UVB. The
parameters tested were temperature, salinity, pH, and light intensity. The cultures
were cultivated at both low and high levels for each tested parameter, as summa­
rized in Tables 2.2 and 2.3. The findings indicate that temperature during culti­
vation had a very significant impact on the growth rates, as summarized in Table
3.4 and visualized in Figure 3.1. Temperature during cultivation also significantly
affected the subsequent production of vitamin D3 during exposure to UVB, as
presented in Table 3.3, whereas salinity appeared to play a less crucial role. Fur­
thermore, the results indicated that light intensity has a more significant impact on
growth rates than pH, as seen in Figure 3.4. However, despite these differences
in growth rates, the production of vitamin D3 did not show statistically significant
variations with changes in pH and light intensity during cultivation, as indicated in
Table 3.7. This suggests that while these parameters influence growth, they may
not directly affect the synthesis of 7­dehydrocholesterol during cultivation and the
production of vitamin D3 during UVB exposure. The findings also illustrate how
stress during cultivation might increase the vitamin D3 content, though cultivation
under stressed conditions is more challenging. Several challenges arose during
the experiments, which will be discussed in this section as well.
4.1
Temperature and Salinity During Cultivation
Temperature is widely recognized as a crucial factor affecting the growth and de­
velopment of living organisms, influencing how quickly and efficiently biological
processes occur. Temperature is known to affect the growth rate, cell size, bio­
chemical composition, and nutrient absorption in microalgae. Decreasing or in­
creasing the cultivation temperature affects the sterol content of the cell depending
on the microalgal species [27]. In this study, it was demonstrated that 27°C rather
Developing Cultivation Conditions for Production of Vitamin D3 in Nannochloropsis oceanica 31
==PAGE:42==
than 19°C accelerated the growth rate of Nannochloropsis oceanica (Figure 3.1).
However, despite this accelerated growth, the vitamin D3 content in the freeze­
dried powder samples were approximately 3 times lower at 27°C compared to
19°C ([ng/g DM], Table 3.3). This demonstrates how temperature affects both
the growth rate and the biochemical composition. The dry matter content in the
UVB exposed cultures (Table 3.3) showed consistent biomass values [g DM/L]

The productivity per day was highest at 27°C than at 19°C (Figure 3.2). The slight
difference observed in productivity between 23 PPT ASW and 37 PPT ASW at
27°C could suggest that while salinity might have an effect, temperature is the
dominant factor affecting productivity, as also visualized in Figure 3.1.
In the study by B. Véron et al. (1996) on Phaeodactylum tricornutum cultivated
at 13°C and 23°C, it was observed that the sterol content drastically decreased
when the temperature was raised to 23°C, with notable changes in the sterol dis­
tribution as well. Specifically, steryl glycosides represented 64% of sterols at 13°C
compared to 31% at 23°C. The proportion of free sterols increased from 33% to
45% with rising temperature [35].
Steryl glycosides, which can be found in plants, algae, and fungi, play important
roles in cellular hydrophobicity and mobility under stress conditions. In the re­
view article ”Structure, metabolism and biological functions of steryl glycosides
in mammals” by M. Shimamura (2020) [36], it is explained how glycosylation of
sterols can enhance stress resistance through membrane modifications. Simi­
larly, stress­inducing conditions like lower temperatures may stimulate sterol pro­
duction in cell membranes and biosynthesis of 7­dehydrocholesterol, the precur­
sor to vitamin D3, which could explain the higher vitamin D3 content observed at
19°C compared to 27°C in this study (Table 3.3).
In another study by G. S. Kalacheva et al. (2002) on Botryococcus braunii, the
effect of temperature on the lipid composition at 18°C, 25°C and 32°C was investi­
gated. They discovered that higher cultivation temperatures did not hinder growth
rates but resulted in a lower sterol content, particularly at the highest temperature

Salinity is another important factor capable of affecting the biochemical compo­

during cultivation.

pH and Light Intensity During Cultivation
pH and light intensity were each tested at low and high levels as the second set
of cultivation parameters. The initial experiment with these parameters was con­
ducted at 19°C with a salinity of 37 PPT. Neither of the cultures in the initial exper­
iment were able to grow, as seen in Figure C.1 (Appendix). These observations
highlight potential challenges associated with cultivating Nannochloropsis ocean­
ica when subjected to excessive stress during cultivation. To address these condi­
tions, the temperature was increased to 23°C, with minor adjustments made to the
low and high settings of light intensity. Under these conditions, all cultures at 350
µmol photons m­2 s­1 grew effortlessly, reaching an OD750 value of 6, compared to
the cultures at 150 µmol photons m­2 s­1. Two cultures, one from each biological

positioned near the wall of the cabinet, while the successful cultures were placed
in the middle, as depicted in Figure 3.5. This difference in positioning may have
affected their growth due to potentially insufficient light exposure, as the cultures
near the wall received light from fewer angles compared to those in the middle,

Experimental Design and Potential Sources of
Error
Increasing the number of replicates could enhance statistical power and reliability.
The ANOVA model did not meet the model assumptions, indicating potential is­
sues with the validity of the statistical analysis. Non­parametric tests or data trans­

samples be treated randomly to avoid introducing bias into the results.
Quantifying and standardizing light intensity during cultivation in the cabinets is
challenging, which could be a potential source of error, especially since the light
intensity is measured with a handheld device. Variations in how and where the
light intensity is measured can introduce inconsistencies.
Inadequate shaking of the cultures before OD measurements could impact the
results, as it might lead to incorrect biomass density readings for the subsequent
UVB treatment.

Conclusion
This study aimed to investigate the important parameters affecting the synthesis
of 7­dehydrocholesterol in Nannochloropsis oceanica during cultivation and an­
alyze the vitamin D3 content after UVB exposure. The tested parameters were
temperature, salinity, pH, and light intensity, each at defined low and high levels.
The results revealed that temperature during cultivation significantly impacts growth
rates and the amount of vitamin D3, with higher growth rates at 27°C but increased
vitamin D3 content at 19°C; 232.178 ± 1.533 (19°C, 37 PPT) compared to 75.174
± 10.989 (27°C, 23 PPT) and 87.251 ± 15.812 (27°C, 37 PPT). Salinity appeared
less influential than temperature, but showed a possible advantage at 37 PPT
compared to at 23 PPT. While light intensity had a more noticeable effect on the
growth rates compared to pH, neither the change in pH nor light intensity during
cultivation significantly impacted the vitamin D3 content.
The findings illustrate that stress conditions during the cultivation of Nannochlorop­
sis oceanica may introduce challenges, which is why it is essential to find a bal­
ance between achieving sufficient biomass and optimizing the metabolic path­
ways for 7­dehydrocholesterol synthesis.
This study contributes to the understanding of the effects of temperature, salinity,
pH, and light intensity on the cultivation of Nannochloropsis oceanica and the
subsequent production of vitamin D3 during UVB exposure, providing valuable
insights into optimizing cultivation conditions, which is important for improving the
yield of vitamin D3.
5.1
Future Perspectives
Future studies on the cultivation of Nannochloropsis oceanica could investigate
the impact of UVB exposure at different growth stages, comparing cultures ex­
posed during log phase versus stationary phase to determine potential vitamin
D3 content variations.

Exploring less pigmented mutants of Nannochloropsis oceanica could be valu­
able, hypothesizing that reduced pigmentation might allow more UVB penetration
and subsequently increase vitamin D3 production.
Implementing a two­step cultivation process, beginning with optimal conditions
followed by stress­inducing cultivation, could possibly balance high biomass pro­
duction with optimal 7­dehydrocholesterol synthesis. Increasing the density of the
initial inoculum might result in a higher tolerance to stress factors during cultiva­
tion.
Analyzing additional bioactive compounds of Nannochloropsis oceanica under op­
timal cultivation conditions to fully uncover its biochemical profile could further re­
veal its commercial potential. Scaling these conditions for industrial applications
would be crucial to uncover the commercial potential of Nannochloropsis ocean­
