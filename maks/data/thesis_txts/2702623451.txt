Abstract 
 
Many ideas have already arisen for the challenge of the next generations supersized 
onshore wind turbines. From new forms of transport to innovative ideas such as flexible-
length blades, all of them strive to maintain the rate of growth of the blades, and 
therefore, the technology. Blade-segmentation seems to be the most likely to succeed 
among the rest of the ideas. 
By implementing the concept of segmentation from the first stage of blade design, the 
Nabrajoint® technology is introduced in the future sized blades. In order to set a product 
development strategy, four different shapes are studied. 
The Preliminary Blade Design is conducted in order to set the Baseline Blades that will 
continuously be optimized for the segmentation concept. The blade structure is 
determined aiming minimum impact in the joint implementation, maximizing its efficiency. 
Once the Baseline Blades are determined, an iterative process is performed. At this 
stage, the focus is on reducing the size of the joint, minimizing its impact mass, cost and 
static moment. Aerodynamic and structural optimizations are achieved in the blades, 
finding the correct balance between AEP and loads reduction. 
Additionally, by varying the operating conditions of the wind turbine, a wide range of load 
cases have been generated for each of the blades, easing the joint implementation along 
the blade at the expense of AEP. 
The results showed that an optimal modular blade geometry should consider the 
implementation of the root extension, as it makes possible to increase the energy 
production by maintaining loads at the tip. Besides, the tip of the blade should be 
slenderized, reducing loads and allowing the joint to be closer to the root. 
Two main different product development strategies have emerged from the results, 
depending on the perspective of the client. If efficiency and AEP is sought, a standard 
blade with root extension is proposed, where different sets of moulds would produce the 
joint in different sections, depending on transport issues. 
If cost reduction is aimed, a hybrid geometry between standard and slender with a single 
joint location is suggested. The joint implementation lets the blade be built in two different 
sets of moulds of 60% and 40% of the length, reducing manufacturing and transport 
costs with a fairly competitive blade.

State-of-the-art 
State-of-the-art 
 
For the past few years, the reduction in the cost of wind energy has come from increasing 
the size of turbine rotors. Due to the increase in swept area and the ability to capture 
higher wind speeds with the increase in height, the energy produced increases 
significantly while the cost of energy is reduced.  
Currently, this trend of increasing size is being affected in the case of onshore blades for 
a number of reasons. While the size of offshore blades already exceeds 100m, onshore 
blades are substantially smaller.  
One of the biggest challenges facing onshore versus offshore blades is the transport of 
the latter. While offshore blades are meant to be produced on the coast and transported 
by ship to the point of installation, the transport of onshore blades is much more 
complicated. 
The construction of roads for the specific purpose of transporting these huge blades, 
together with the need for specialised vehicles and limitations in the dimensions of the 
blade due to the possibility of having to cross bridges or tunnels, means that large rotors 
lose their attractiveness or even their viability.  
One of the solutions that has been most widely considered in recent years has been the 
segmentation of blades, although this is not currently a widespread practice. The 
increase in the mass and complexity of the blade increases its cost, as a result of which 
only a few manufacturers are currently offering this solution. 
One of the solutions that has been most widely considered in recent years has been the 
segmentation of blades, although this is not currently a widespread practice. The 
increase in the mass and complexity of the blade increases its cost, which means that it 
has not been a practical solution to date. 
One of the solutions have been the use of T-bolt joint. It has been used in different 
prototype segmented blades. The following picture shows three of them:

1.2 
Transportation cost 
 
The modular blades in question are intended for onshore use, so only the onshore cost 
of the blades will be estimated. 
The cost of transport becomes more important in the design of the blade as the size of 
the blades increases. In fact, it directly affects different geometrical aspects such as 
maximum chord or blade length and structural aspects such as mass.  
The viability of onshore projects is also affected, due to factors such as the construction 
of roads to the yard or the need for specialised vehicles to transport such long goods. 
It is estimated that above 75m, the cost of transporting the blades increases to such an 
extent that the viability of the project is put into question (7). This is why modular blades 
are so important in the nearby future. Transporting a blade in different sections minimizes 
the complexity of transport, reducing its effect on the total cost of energy. 
 
1.2.1 Transportation Constraints 
 
Transportation of the blades is mainly affected by four factors: the overall length of the 
vehicle, the width, the total height and the combined weight (truck and freight). 
Additionally, special permits and escort vehicles will increase the cost of transportation.  
 
1.3 
Impact of segmentation on preliminary design 
 
A wind turbine blade can be designed with different purposes. From lowering costs to 
achieving maximum production in the minimum swept area, the blade will differ in 
material composition, geometry, weight or length, among other aspects.  
Introducing the concept of segmentation in the first steps of blade design directly affects 
the definition of the blade. The design of an ordinary blade consists of an iterative 
process between aerodynamic (geometry, loads) and structural aspects (selection of 
materials, mass distribution, cost) until the desired results are achieved. When a modular 
blade is designed, a third aspect must be considered: the joint definition. Loads, 
geometry and material selection directly affect to the efficiency of the section.

Objective of the study 
 
This study aims to develop a preliminary design for a modular blade by employing the 
NabraJoint® technology and defining a product development strategy for a standard 
modular blade development project. 
The NabraJoint® technology is so called MBJ (Modular Blade Joint) and represented in 
the following illustrations: 
 
 
 
Figure 4. Representation of MBJ system 
 
 A standard blade built in two sections will be designed and optimized in function of 
energy production and structural calculations. This blade will be called NBT95. Two 
different variations will be studied. 
Firstly, a root extension is proposed in order to have a flexible product that could fit easily 
with the requirements of the buyer. This aspect of the project will not affect to the split 
location, as loads from the tip do not differ, but only AEP and loads at the root increase. 
This idea lays on the aspect of building blades with different lengths with the same set 
of moulds, reducing production costs.

Blade Technical Specifications 
 
After determining the blade length, max chord and BCD, the next step is to create the 
chord, thickness and twist angle distributions. In order to focus on the implementation of

During the iteration period, they may vary aiming more efficiency.

2.2.1 Airfoil Selection 
 
There are two main factors that determine the airfoil selection. The first one, efficiency: 
they should be efficient in order to maximize the nominal power of the blade. The second 
one, modularity: the section where the split will be located should have a shape that 
facilitates the implementation of the MBJ. Shapes with smooth designs and high radius 
of curvature will be selected, as joints work best the closer they are from the centroid of 
the airfoil.

2.2.3 Design Loads 
 
The structural dimensioning will be determined according to the strain limits of the 
materials. By calculating the extreme loads, the plybook of the blade is generated and 
the maximum strains are calculated.

In order to determine the extreme loads, a BEM code is generated. This code has been 
developed along the aeroelasticity related courses accomplished throughout the last two 
years. The code allows to calculate loads and AEP by introducing a Kaimal spectrum for 
the wind. 
 
As explained, a simplified BEM model was used to calculate the flap loads. In this model, 
an optimum TSR for each blade and a constant rotational speed were established. This 
way, the blade pitches in order to maintain that speed, until it reaches Rated Power. By 
performing the BEM calculation for a range of speeds between Vcut-in and Vcut-out, the 
maximum load to which the blade is exposed is found. 
By multiplying this value by 1.35, the extreme flap loads are obtained (9). 
Extreme edge loads are sized according to emergency stops. In order to determine them, 
a simulation was carried out where the blade suddenly pitches under a fast increment of 
the wind speed- 
Loads for the slender blade are determined exactly with the same procedure. Even the 
wind vector randomly generated for the standard blade is stored and re-used for the 
slender loads.

3  Phase II. Optimization Process 
 
Once the preliminary design of both blades has been established, Phase I of the project 
is concluded. Thus, the Baseline blades are established, which will subsequently be 
optimised in an iterative process in which the following aspects will be considered: 
• 
Joint definition 
• 
Aerodynamic optimisation 
• 
Structural optimisation 
• 
AEP & Blade Cost 
 
The joint definition will be a key aspect to consider in the iterative process. Both 
aerodynamic and structural optimisations will depend on its results. 
The aim of the optimisation will be to reduce as much as possible the number of elements 
needed and to have as few transitions as possible. This will result in a lower impact on 
mass, cost and static moment on the resulting blade. 
Since the number of elements in the joint depends on the geometry and the cap load, 
the reduction of the number of elements will be attempted through aerodynamic 
optimisation. On the other hand, the length of the transition is dependent on the geometry 
of the cap, so structural optimisation will be focused on obtaining a cap where the 
transition exerts a minimum impact.

4.1 
Conclusions 
 
After studying a range of different load cases for each blade, the following graph shows 
the maximum possible AEP as a function of inboard length, considering all the data: 
 
 
Figure 38.Graph of maximum possible AEP per inboard length 
 
As can be seen, for long inboards, the NBT100 will be the most productive blade. In that 
range, it is necessary to limit the wind turbine operating conditions and thus reduce the 
loads in order to be able to introduce the MBJ with a maximum number of 24 elements. 
It is for this reason that the AEP is reduced as the joint approaches the root. 
The NBT95S, on the other hand, is only the most productive for a 50m inboard, as it is 
the only blade with a valid joint at that section. 
Both results were as expected, as the Standard blade was set to be the most productive, 
while the Slender was designed for a short inboard. Instead, the most interesting results 
are found in the 60-64m range. For 64m, the most productive modular blade is provided 
by the NBT95, but if the inboard were to be reduced by 4m, the NBT100S offers a valid 
binding with a higher AEP. This is a great advantage, as the second solution offers 
slightly greater versatility in transport and higher output. 
==PAGE:54==
42 
 
From the determined results, different conclusions can be drawn. 
 
4.1.1 Optimum Geometry 
 
This transition of blades gives valuable information on which geometry could be the best 
fit for segmentation. The NBT100S solution compared to the NBT95 only offers solutions: 
higher production, lower root loads and easier transport.  
 
 
Figure 39. Difference of geometries between NBT100S and NBT95 
 
The reason that the NBT100S blade performs better than the NBT95 is because of the 
balance found by reducing loads at the tip and increasing AEP from the root. The load 
reduction due to a thinner tip allows the MBJ to be brought closer to the root. The cost 
of AEP is compensated by the root extension. Since the relationship between AEP and 
loads is not linear, the end result is to have a higher AEP at lower loads than NBT95. 
 
4.1.2 Product Development Strategy 
 
Case I  
 
From a conservative point of view, even if nowadays seems obvious that next generation 
onshore blades will need some sort of optimization that overcomes transportation 
challenges, this same idea had happened before for much shorter blades and they are 
still growing without any main changes. 
A possible product development strategy of the future blades could be producing the 
same blade segmented or conventionally (one single piece), depending on transport 
feasibility. An effective method would be to have a single set of moulds for the blade and 
different MBJ options, much cheaper to produce than the blade itself.  
==PAGE:55==
43 
 
4  Conclusions and future work 
This option is seen as an opportunity in Figure 38 , where the NBT100 has a wide range 
of segmentation (66-80m) in which the AEP is not overly affected. 
This range of inboards could make the concept of segmentation very interesting, where 
it is only applied as much as necessary, offering a good deal at a relatively low price. 
This scenario may put the modular blades in a very competitive spot due to their high 
performance and slightly lower costs. 
 
Case II 
 
Another product development strategy could be only manufacturing modular blades. 
Even this option is less versatile than the first case, there are some advantages that have 
to be considered.: the geometry of the blade would be optimized to its maximum in terms 
of segmentation, being focused on cutting transportation costs. The blade could also be 
built in two different sections, reducing its manufacturing costs considerably, as 
requirements such as factory dimensions or curation process of the materials would be 
considerably lower than manufacturing in a single piece. 
This option may be seen as a less interesting case, but the reduced manufacturing and 
transport costs of the blade could fit in a market where the cost of a wind farm overcomes 
the performance of it. 
 
4.2 
Future work 
 
A possible development of this thesis could be finding the exact optimized geometry from 
a standard blade to a modular one. 
The path has already been established: reducing loads by implementing a slender tip 
and increasing the AEP by extending the root. Even though, it has not been set the exact 
chord and thickness distribution that could maximize the AEP having a very competitive 
modular blade. By optimizing the root extension length and the transition zone, as well 
as the tip dimensions could establish a modular blade with considerably low 
transportation costs and high AEP performance.

Additionally, studying deeply the market tendencies and the manufacturing process of 
both of the product development strategy proposals would find which of the scenarios is 
more likely to succeed.
