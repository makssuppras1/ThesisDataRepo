Abstract
Energy independence is one of the main goal of every country. This is even more apparent
now than ever. Demand for clean renewable energy is higher than ever as it solves problem of
independence and climate change. Wind energy is one of the sources that has been heavily
invested in, especially at the coastal countries with great energy resources. . Lot of these
regions experience cold climate environment which comes with the challenge of icing on the
wind turbines that accounts for over 20% in power production losses.
In order to better understand this issue, different methods of studying ice accretion are being
developed. This report is focused on developing fast and cheap process of studying ice accre­
tion on vertical axis wind turbines. This process includes design and construction of modular
VAWT for climatic wind tunnel testing. It also explores scanning of ice formed on blades using
photogrammetry. Furthermore, programming script was developed, that post­processes data
from the scans and outputs comparison between different experiments at correct scale.
It was found, that by merging 3D printing and traditional production methods, VAWT for testing
can be built fast with great variety of possible setups. High construction speed benefited from re­
purposing used VAWT blades. During icing experiments in the climatic wind tunnel, the effects
of changes tip speed ratio, solidity, angle of attack and chord length was observed. Results
showed that higher tip speed ratios lead to larger ice accumulation on the blades. Experiments
also showed that wile using same conditions, difference in chord length can have significant
impact on the amount and type of ice formed.
Ultimate goal of these experiments would be to match results with the computer generated
simulations with the same environmental conditions and same wind turbine setup. Even though,
wind tunnel testing can be done and compared for some cases, it is still expensive and time
consuming as well as impossible for large scale cases to be tested. Computer simulations
will tackle all of these problems at once, but first the software needs to be heavily tested and
validated by real world experiments.

Introduction
In December 2015, countries from all around the world agreed that in order to combat climate
change, carbon emissions need to be lowered, reaching net zero emissions by 20501. One of
the largest CO2 emitters is the electricity production sector. Currently, carbon free electricity
production comes mostly from nuclear power plants, hydroelectric power plants, solar panels
and wind turbines. Depending on location, different energy source or combination is more
viable.
For wind energy production, location is very crucial parameter when deciding on setting up the
wind farm. Wind resource on the location is decisive factor on weather the wind farm will be
economically viable or not. Many of the wind resource high areas can experience temperatures
below freezing, leading to ice accretion on the wind turbines and measuring equipment. Ice
formation can have large implications on wind turbine functioning. As ice builds up on blades, it
effectively changes its shape and mass. Blades on the wind turbine are very carefully developed
using complex simulations in order to maximise power production while minimising the material
used for the blades which lowers the price of the wind turbine. Additional mass and differed
aerodynamic performance can lead to mechanical failures of the wind turbines. Currently, the
best solution is to turn of the wind turbines during these times as the financial losses are lower
compared to maintenance costs caused by mechanical issues caused by icing. Furthermore,
ice formed on the blade could be a danger to the surrounding environment as the ice parts could
break loose and be thrown to far distance.
Studying ice accretion is therefore very important. Being able to predict the ice amount and
shape formed on the blades allows to adjust the behavior of the wind turbine and therefore
minimise financial losses and mechanical failures. Complex simulation models are being de­
veloped in order to estimate the ice accretion as it is cheapest and fastest method to predict
ice formation. In order to validate these models precision, experiments in the wind tunnels are
necessary as they can simulate almost any environmental conditions experienced in nature.
This paper will attempt to describes the process of developing wind turbine setup for scanning of
the ice formations in the climatic wind tunnel as well as post­processing of the data. Design and
construction of small scale modular vertical axis wind turbine will be described. Furthermore,
scanning processes including camera setup, lighting, background and picture post­processing
will be touched upon while data scaling and alignment will be discussed in more detail. Lastly,
results from 14 different setup cases(changes in tip speed ratio, angle of attack, solidity and
chord length) will be presented and relevant findings will be discussed.

2.3
Atmospheric icing
In the ”Cold Climate”(regions where atmospheric icing can occur, usually less than −10◦C),
different ice types can be formed depending on the conditions. Those are for example wind
speed, air temperature, atmospheric pressure, humidity and also more specific values as liq­
uid water content(LWC ­ the mass of water in a cloud given in [g/m3]) and median volumetric
diameter(MVD ­ defines the median size of water droplets in the cloud, given in [µm]). Depend­
ing on all of these conditions, in­cloud icing(glaze, rime and mixed ice type) and precipitation
icing(caused by freezing rain or wet snow) can occur[7].
Figure 2.4: In­cloud icing ice types depending on wind speed and temperature.
Rime ice generally forms when supercooled water droplet hits and fully freezes to the blade
on impact. The ice has white color and density can vary depending on LWC and MVD values
between 200 −600[kg/m3][8]. It is usually easy to remove from the object and therefore can be
dangerous on spinning object(WT blades) as it can be thrown and can damage either turbine
or its surroundings. As seen in the Figure 2.4, higher wind speeds and temperatures favours
formation of the glaze ice. It is transparent and density can reach 900[kg/m3][8]. Glaze ice
sticks much better to the surfaces and is very hard to remove. Mixed ice type is formed when
the conditions during ice formation change or are the conditions are in between the ones favoring

2.4
Theoretical modeling of icing
The ice accretion process starts when the cooled or super­cooled particles in the air collide
with the structure. These particles can be solid, liquid or mixture of water and ice[7]. Equation
2.6 introduced by Makkonen[7] estimates the rate of icing on objects depending on flux density
given as (w × v × A) and correction factors(α1, α2, α3) that range between 0 and 1.
dm
dt = α1 · α2 · α3 · w · A · v
(2.6)
where:
w is the mass concentration of the particles,
v is the velocity of the particles with respect to the object(blade),
A is the cross­sectional area of the object (with respect to the direction of the particle velocity
vector v ),
α1 is the collision efficiency,
α2 is the sticking efficiency,
α3 is the accretion efficiency.
The collision efficiency(α1), is the ratio of the flux density of particles hitting the object to
the maximum flux density. The value of α1 is smaller than 1 as smaller particles with smaller
momentum tend to follow the air stream and avoid the collision with the object while larger
and heavier particles tend to collide with the object. Therefore MVD is highly influential to this
parameter.
The sticking efficiency(α2) is the efficiency of particles hitting and sticking the the surface
instead of bouncing away. More particles bouncing of the surface lead to smaller value of α2.
The accretion efficiency(α3) describes the ratio of how many particles stick to the surface and
how many will run down the object. When there is no liquid particles running­off the surface
then α3 = 1 and the process is called dry growth leading to formation of rime ice. Opposite to
this leads to wet growth and formation of glaze ice [7]. This correction factor will play important
role later discussed in the results.

Design of the VAWT
3.1
Design parameters/limitations
Wind tunnel dimensions
The scale of the vertical axis wind turbine was dictated by the dimensions of the climatic wind
tunnel(Figure 3.1) used for the ice accretion. The cross section of the test section is 2x2m with
the length of 5m. Furthermore, water cloud generated by the nozzles, placed approximately at
the central vertical position of the test section, is roughly 50[cm] high and 2[m] wide for a given
testing conditions discussed later in this thesis. These two dimensions were the most important
when deciding on the length of the blades and the radius of the VAWT used for experiments.
Figure 3.1: CWT was developed and built as a collaboration between the Technical University
of Denmark (DTU) and Force Technology[10].
Due to limited vertical space it was decided to mount the the central rotating shaft to the floor
and the ceiling of the wind tunnel, providing stable setup with minimum space requirement.
Having VAWT mounted only at the bottom would require more robust mounting solution that
could offset minimum height placement of the blades, possibly missing the substantial area of
water cloud.
Wind turbine blades
For this experiments, two sets of blades were given to be used for the construction. NACA
64015 airfoil blades with the cord lengths of 10[cm] and 8[cm] were repurposed for the new
wind turbine. Two sets of blades had a length of approximately 1.2[m] so they fit well within
the range of the blade size based on wind tunel and water cloud specifications. Tip speed ratio

Furthermore, 6 blades used for horizontal connection of the vertical blades to the central shaft

RPM and wind speed
In order to achieve consistent RPM during the experiments, PID controlled servomotor JVL
MAC­800 was used. This servo motor can reach up to 3000 RPM. In order to ensure higher
control over the RPM, 3:1 gear ratio is used consequently limiting the RPM of the wind turbine to
approximately 1000 RPM. This servomotor was already used for HAWT and therefore all cable
connections, power supply and software for control were provided(more details are explained
in [11]).
Lastly, the wind tunnel wind speed has operating limit range of 2 −25[m/s], turbulence intensity
of 1 −20%, temperature range of −9◦C to +40◦C and in­cloud air humidity of 0.4[g/m3].
3.2
Requirements
It was required that the VAWT can run the experiments with the angles of attack of −2, 0, 2
degrees. Secondly, the wind turbine and the servo motor maintaining desired TSR needed to
be mounted on the top of the force gauge in order to capture all forces within the system. The
force gauges would be mounted at the top and bottom of the VAWT, where the central shaft is
mounted. Finally, it was desired to run the VAWT in the 2 and 3 blade setups.

bolt was being worn out when screwed in and out repeatedly. This then created an issue that
the bolt was no longer holding in the 3D printed part as tight as at the start and therefore it
needed to be replaced. There was no risk of it slipping out but ideally this should be changed
to make sure the most secure joint possible.
Secondly, driving belt was having little bit of slack to it which was being visible when accelerating
or decelerating the wind turbine rapidly. Therefore some kind of adapter providing constant
tension to the belt would be recommended but wind turbine is fully functional without it as well.
Furthermore, during the experiments it was found that calibrating force gauges proved to be
almost impossible and date acquired from it seemed to not make sense with what was expected.
For this reason it was decided not to do any further investigations based on the date from it.
More information on calibration and functioning of the force gauge can be found in [12] and [11].
Lastly, shaft clamps were milled just big enough to fit onto carbon fibre shaft which made it
difficult to move up and down. Clamped had to be stretched open by hammering in some wedge
before being moved up and down. This had to be done only once when the blades needed to
be lowered down but in case of testing blades with different lengths it would be suggested to
increase the size of inner diameter by a little.

5
Scanning setup and post processing
This chapter will attempt to explain how to get from physical results to virtual 3D reconstruction
of the blade. This will include the scanning setup, image post­processing and Matlab code for
generating the final output used in the Results chapter.
This process heavily relied on using Agisoft Metashape software https://www.agisoft.com/ which
uses many pictures of the physical object from many angles to generate 3D reconstruction.
5.1
Scanning setup
In order to achieve high quality of a reconstruction, high quality of pictures dependent on proper
setup is very important. For this project, two diffused light sources were used, high quality
camera and green screen. This project was run in a close collaboration with ’Optimizing 3D
techniques for capturing the build up of ice on wind turbine blades’[13] bachelor project. It was
focused on scanning setup. All details about lights, camera setup and settings, camera lens,
green screen and Agisoft Metashape settings for reconstruction can be found there.
Figure 5.1: Scanning setup for VAWT.
In the previous projects, picture taking process started by taking pictures from many angles
manually. The background was always changing which required extensive ’masking’ process
explained later. This process was later upgraded to stationary setup where each picture would
26
Thesis title
==PAGE:39==
be taken manually but camera would not move, instead, the blade would be rotated by hand by
certain angle and background(green screen) remained constant.
For this project, it was initially desired to generate approximately 20 or more scans(final count
was 20 plus more than 6 discarded scans that are not presented) each requiring estimated 100
or more pictures. This was taken into account at the beginning of the VAWT construction and
therefore following improvements were made.
First, as already mentioned in the Design and the Construction chapter, blade did not need to
be removed from the wind turbine but only joining mechanism was swapped for the blade being
scanned allowing it to freely rotate.
Second, small and portable device was built, which can turn the blade 360 degrees by speci­
fied angle steps and specified time delay between each step. This device is shown in Figure
5.2. Micro­controller device was attached with small stepper motor, simple screen and numer­
ical keyboard. Small interface was programmed allowing to control the stepper motor with the
keyboard while seeing the selection on the screen. The program lets user define the direction
of rotation(clockwise or counter­clockwise), the full rotation angle(for example 1000 degrees),
step size and time delay in seconds between steps.
Stepper motor was equipped with 3D printed gear which was interfaced to the gear connected
to the bolt of the wind turbine blade. Spinning stepper motor resulted in turning the blade.
The gear ratio was 3:1 due to construction reasons meaning 30 degrees on the stepper motor
resulted in 10 degree rotation of the blade. Furthermore, 31 degrees rotation on stepper motor
took almost precisely 1 second.
Figure 5.2: Turning mechanism for VAWT.
Finally, camera was then set to take picture every 6 seconds(1 second of stepper motor rotation
plus 5 seconds delay set on the turning mechanism) with auto focus of the camera turned
on. The camera was also set to take 37 pictures(1 picture each 10 degrees resulting in full
360 degrees scan). One full pas(360 degrees scan) took approximately 4 minutes without any
external input.
Distance of the camera to the blade determined how much of the blade will be captured in each
picture as well as the picture quality. It was found that the optimal results(quality and speed)
are achieved by 3 passes(full 360 degrees scans each at different height with small overlay to
Thesis title
27
==PAGE:40==
the previous height). Therefore after each pass, it was necessary to re­enter the wind tunnel
and adjust the height of the camera. After 3 passes were made, pictures were ready for 3D
reconstruction.
Figure 5.3: Set of pictures in sequence generated by the setup.
Same procedure was tested with 5 different heights resulting in 66% increase in the amount of
pictures but with very little picture quality improvement. More pictures also meant much longer
post­processing time in every step discussed in next section.
Automatic scanning did not only make the process more consistent and faster, it also removed
the need for re­cooling the wind tunnel after each scan pass as having people outside of the
tunnel slowed down heating up of tunnel. It made it also much more comfortable for the scanning
crew to operate as only minimum time was required to setup scanning in −8◦C and then they
could leave the wind tunnel.

5.2.2
Scaling technique
The biggest challenge of the whole post­processing was to make sure that dense cloud data
output was accurate representation of the real blade. The dense cloud data is unitless and and
two scans are also not necessarily comparable as the camera position(and therefore size of
the object on individual pictures) will determines unitless size. Therefore each scan had to be
correctly scaled in all of its directions in order to make sure that results are comparable.
In the Figures 4.9,4.11 and 4.12 it is shown that using airfoil data as a true size is not possible as
the manufactured blade is different to the theoretical airfoil shape. For this reason, one highest
quality blade was marked and scanned without any ice on it. The blade was measured in its
length, width and thickness in the areas shown in the Figure 5.7.

formed on the blade, it was safe to remove the ice from that area and use it for scaling.
This way, there was always one true reference between all scans.

Importing data
When Agisoft Metashape generates the dense cloud of the blade, it can be export as a .txt

then put back into XYZ matrix containing three centered vectors.
Initially, dry blade data was imported into MatLab script, aligned to the origin, properly scaled and

Results are significantly different between for cl = 8[cm] and cl = 10[cm] cases when RPM
is increased(TSR = 3). This is believed to be due to difference in Reynolds number for the
two cases. For the cl=10cm, TSR = 3 cases, Reynolds number ranges approximately between
values Re = 0.7 · 105 −1.3 · 105, average being Re = 1.0 · 105. For the cl = 8[cm], TSR = 3
cases, Reynolds number ranges approximately between values Re = 0.56 · 105 −1.07 · 105,
average being Re = 0.8 · 105.
In the paper ”In­Flight Icing of UAVs ­ The Influence of Reynolds Number on the Ice Accretion
Process”[14], authors found that when Reynolds number Re = 0.5 · 105, rime ice formation was
observed while for the Re = 1.0 · 105, mixed ice formation was observed.
It was earlier discussed how type of ice affects the rate of icing in the Equation 2.6, particularly
coefficient α3.
To sum it up, it is assumed that the difference in the amount of ice formed on the cl = 8[cm] blade
compared to the cl = 10[cm] blade is caused by the fact, that the Reynolds numbers for the two
blades are on the border of the conditions favoring creation of rime ice and glaze ice. Smaller
blade is favoring rime ice formation, resulting in the dry growth of the ice and consequently larger
accumulation of the ice(larger coefficient of α3) compared to the larger blade, where mixed ice
formation is favored instead, leading towards smaller ice accumulation with smaller coefficient
of α3.

6.2.6
Changes in ice type in different y positions
(a) Top of the blade(left), bottom(right).
(b) Top of the blade(left), bottom(right).
Figure 6.25: Influence of MVD on ice type formation on the blade, depending on y position, on
two different blades at TSR = 3.
It was found that towards the bottom of the blade(y = −25[cm] to y = −15[cm]), glaze ice was
formed while the rest of the blade had mixed or rime ice formed on its leading edge. This is most
likely due to MVD of the water cloud. Larger and heavier droplets would be more concentrated
at the bottom of the incoming cloud. These larger droplets would take more time to freeze and
therefore another droplet can hit the blade before the previous completely freezes, resulting in
wet growth(glaze ice)[8].
This could be potentially more significant on larger VAWTs as there is much larger height dif­
ference between the top and the bottom of the blade. This observation is mostly visible on the
VAWTs as the blade is at the same height during the rotation, while HAWTs blades are moving
up and down within certain range during rotation.

7
Conclusion
Building a wind turbine from reused components proved to have its benefits as well as down­
sides. Having functional blades right from the start of the project saved a lot of time in design and
manufacturing(probably 2 months or more) that could have been focused elsewhere. Down­
side being quality of the blades as well as some minor adjustments to the blades that needed
to be done. Recycling otherwise unused blades must be also acknowledged as a benefit to the
project and resources used.
Finished VAWT proved to be working consistently without any mechanical issues or damages
during the project even when it was assembled and disassembled multiple times throughout the
projects duration. Modularity also proved its use when it was found that ice was forming off the
center of the blades and the height was adjusted within few minutes. Angle of attack adjustment
as well as 3 and 2 blade setups change worked as expected.
Using 3D printed components worked very well in connection with other materials and compo­
nents. It acted as a bridge to the two components where it was difficult to interface them. Flex­
ibility of 3D printed components also opened many possibilities of solving design challenges.
Scanning of the ice on the blades was heavily automated and compared to the initial projects
where most of the steps were done manually, huge amount of time and worked was saved.
Time saving was not only due to automation but also because of picture quality which reduced
post­processing time significantly.
Post­processing in Matlab had also its pros and cons. On one hand, Matlab provided great
control over data as well as production of the plots for the price of time spent on developing the
script and some manual corrections needed. Processing pictures for a single experiment from
the camera all the way from Agisoft Metashape processes to the aligned scans in Matlab takes
approximately 1­2h, where work done in Matlab takes only about 5­10 minutes per scan and
rest of the work is more or less automated by the software and requires minimum user input.
The quality of resulting data was found to be good enough to see the difference in the ice shape
when angle of attack is changed only by 2 degrees. It was also found that there is change in
the size of ice formed when two different chord lengths of the same airfoil blades are used due
to changes in Reynolds number. Furthermore, no difference was found in the ice shape for 2
and 3 blade setups.
From the design of the VAWT to the final results, this method of observing the ice accretion on
the wind turbine blade was found to be reasonably quick and very cheap while providing high
variety of quickly swap­able setups that generate high precision data.
