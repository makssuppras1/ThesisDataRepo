Abstract
This study aimed to address the limited understanding of indoor climate perception and comfort among neurodi-
vergent individuals, specifically focusing on autistic people. The existing knowledge in this field predominantly
relied on data from healthy and younger individuals, with little research conducted on the specific needs and sen-
sitivities of neurodivergent individuals. The objective of this study was to enhance our understanding of autistic
individual’s perception of indoor environments and contribute to improving their well-being.
The study was divided into two parts, involving data collection from residential facilities housing autistic indi-
viduals with special needs and data collection from the private homes of autonomous autistic individuals. The
methodology included recording subjective and objective parameters of the indoor environment in both settings.
Specific instrumentation was used for long-term measurements left at the residences and short-term on-the-spot
measurements. The collected data included measurements of various indoor environmental factors such as ther-
mal conditions, lighting, air quality, and noise.
The researched care facility in this project was located in Lyngby and mainly focused on five non-autonomous
residents with level three autism. The residents themselves were not directly involved in the project; instead,
their caregivers provided overall and anonymous information about their well-being and preferences through a
semi-structured interview. Data collection in the facility was limited to common areas and did not include the
residents private apartments.
For the research conducted in private homes, five voluntary participants were involved. Data was collected in two
rooms of each participant’s home, including a preferred room and another selected room. Participants were pro-
vided with a comfort questionnaire, which they filled out at least twice a week. The questionnaire captured their
personal perception of the indoor environment, and their responses were compared with time-matched measured
indoor environmental parameters. The evaluation of the results primarily focused on participants satisfaction
within the four domains of thermal conditions, visual aspects, acoustics, and indoor air quality.
Regarding the care facility in Lyngby, the main concerns identified included slow heating regulation, visual
distractions, inflexible lighting, and noise from nearby construction work. Improvements in sleep and well-being
were observed in relation to daylight exposure, suggesting the potential benefit of installing artificial lighting
that mimics natural daylight.
In the study conducted within private homes, the collected data was insufficient to draw definitive conclusions,
but some results indicated an increased sensitivity in autistic people within the thermal domain, when compared
to the neurotypical population. Future study suggestions included ensuring all short-term light measurements
are made at desk height, adding a new short-term measurement point adjacent to the HOBO illuminance sensor,
placing RoomAlyzers in both rooms of participant’s homes (if available), reminding participants to alternate
between rooms when completing the questionnaire, and prioritizing the placement of HOBO sensors with voltage
probes in participants preferred rooms when limited in quantity.

1
Introduction
This project is a part of the SENSEwellbeing study that researches indoor climate comfort for people
with high sensitivity, specifically autistic people. The SENSEwellbeing project includes field studies
in private homes and care facilities, as well as studies of participants under controlled conditions in
Living Labs located at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). This report will focus only on the
pilot field study.
The current knowledge about indoor climate perception and comfort is predominantly based upon
healthy and younger individuals, as most research has been collected from the average standard pop-
ulation. While neurodivergent people tend to have a different perception of indoor climate, as they
can be both less or more sensitive to stimuli, there is little to no research on the topic. The existing
guidelines for indoor climate comfort may not apply to this group of people, and the research aims to
create new guidelines and thereby help improve their well-being. Autistic people are a growing part
of the population and accommodating their needs can potentially help them function better both at
home and in society as a whole.
1.1
Autism Spectrum Condition
Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC) is a neurodevelopmental disability. As the name implies it is a
spectrum disorder, meaning that it affects each individual differently. The DSM-V (Diagnostic and sta-
tistical manual of mental disorders V) distinguishes between three levels, which helps each individual
get the correct support. The levels are distinguished as followed:
• Level 1 - Requires some support and is the mildest form. Difficulties are found in social interac-
tions, planning and organizing, and switching between activities.
• Level 2 - Requires substantial support.
Stronger difficulties are found in social interactions.
Repetitive behaviours and special interests can be present.
• Level 3 - Requires very substantial support and is the most severe form. Previously mentioned
difficulties may be even stronger.
In this study, we mainly differ between autonomous and non-autonomous autistic people.
The disorder comes with both strengths and weaknesses, where the key challenges are found in social
communication and interactions, as well as restricted or repetitive behaviours and interests. Other
challenges that autistic people might face include a greater sensitivity to environmental stimuli, which
is the main focus of this study. [1] This particular challenge means that autistic people become more
easily overstimulated than people who are not on the spectrum. Thereby autistic people may have dif-
ferent requirements for indoor environmental comfort than the average person. If these requirements
are not met, they may be more subject to suffer from discomfort and health issues, and have a harder
time adapting to the environment.
1.1.1
Autistic meltdowns
When an autistic person becomes overstimulated it could result in an autistic meltdown. Although
meltdowns happen more frequently in children and non-autonomous autistic people, they also appear
in autonomous adults.
A meltdown is a response to the person being overstimulated by outer sensory stimuli, also referred
to as sensory overload. From an outside perspective a meltdown may appear to be similar to a temper
tantrum, but they are not the same. Although they may both appear as an angry, frustrated outburst,
often accompanied by crying, they are entirely different.
A temper tantrum occurs on the following grounds:
• Is goal orientated
• Is a way to get attention

• Can be controlled by the person
While a meltdown has the following characteristics:
• Stems from overload
• Happens even without an audience
• Is out of the person’s control
A temper tantrum often takes place when someone does not get what they want. It is a protest be-
haviour with the goal of getting their way. Therefore, is it controllable by the person having it. There
seems to be no point throwing a tantrum without an audience, as this will not further the goal the
person is trying to achieve.
A meltdown, on the other hand, is caused by an overload of stimuli and will occur even if the person
is alone. It cannot be controlled and may continue even after the stimuli is removed. Thereby the best
way of dealing with meltdowns is to prevent them from occurring altogether. [2]
1.2
Autism and indoor climate
Abnormalities in sensory perception are often found as symptoms of ASC and are even featured in
the original descriptions of childhood autism [3] and Asperger syndrome [4]. These abnormalities
could be hypersensitivity and/or hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli, or unusual interest in a sensory
stimulus. This includes actual or anticipated sounds, light, textures (especially with clothing and food),
odours and tastes, heat, cold, and pain [5]. The prevalence of sensory processing difficulties in autistic
people varies across studies, and depends on the methods used to assess sensory processing, the age
range of the participants, and other factors. However, it is generally accepted that sensory processing
difficulties are common with autistic people.
A study comparing sensory processing in children with ASC (n = 235) to age-matched, typically devel-
oping controls (n = 221) reported that 95% of the sample with ASC versus 16.8% of the controls demon-
strated some degree of sensory processing difficulty. The study used a tool called the Short Sensory
Profile, which is a caregiver questionnaire that assesses sensory processing across different domains
such as visual, auditory, touch, taste, and smell [6]. A literature review, [7], compares various studies
evaluating parents/caregivers questionnaires and first hand descriptions of sensory symptoms. These
tell a similar story and are summarized as follows:
• Particular sensory symptoms appear to be more common in ASC than in other developmental disorders.
• These symptoms lessen with age.
• These symptoms are correlated with the severity of social symptoms of ASC, at least in children.
However, the majority of research in this field is concentrated on children and/or has relied on care-
giver questionnaires. This raises the question of whether the observed dissipation in symptoms is
genuine or merely reflect caregivers’ misinterpretation of the children’s behaviour, resulting in an un-
derestimation of the extent of sensory processing abnormalities.
A study assessing sensory processing in 36 adults (18 with ASC, 18 comparison participants) aged
between 18 −65 years, found similar altered sensory perception in adults, as earlier research found
in children. The study used a 60-item self-report questionnaire called the Adult/Adolescent Sensory
Profile (AASP). This sensory profile had four quadrants, which can be linked to hypo- and hyper-
sensitivity, respectively:
1. Low registration
2. Sensation seeking
3. Sensory sensitivity
4. Sensation avoiding

The findings showed that sensory processing abnormalities were common also in adults with ASC, as
94.4 % of those with ASC reported experiencing extreme levels of sensory processing in at least one of
the four quadrants. [8]
A scoping review, [9], provides an overview of the present knowledge within the area of indoor en-
vironment design and censorial perception of people with ASC. A thorough screening of existing lit-
erature was conducted, and a total of 310 studies were included in the review. The identified studies
belong to two different subject groups:
• Group 1: Autism and Indoor Environment (74 studies identified)
• Group 2: Autism and Sensory Perception (236 studies identified)
Less than 25% of the identified studies concerns autism and indoor environment. Even though sensory
perception and perception of indoor environment are closely connected, most research has focused on
sensory perception alone. The majority of the identified studies in group 1 originates from other fields
of research such as medicine, psychology, social sciences etc. Only a few studies address the topic
from an indoor environment engineering perspective. Furthermore, most of the studies are based on
existing literature or questionnaire surveys, while only 19 studies investigated the actual effects of
environmental exposure (see fiugre 3). Out of the 74 studies, 17 addressed both children and adults, 40
focused only on children, and just 17 focused on adults alone. It is clear that research with children has
been prioritised. Figure 1 also shows that the majority of the research focuses on school environments.
A reason for this kind of environment being prioritised, could be that the negative effects of a poor
indoor environment on an autistic child, is more easily detected in a school environment than at home.
However, the indoor environment affecting the well-being of the individual with ASC is important not
only in schools, but in all environments. Figure 2 shows how the studies are distributed between the
different comfort domains. The acoustic and visual are the most explored domains, while the thermal
domain and the indoor air quality are the least explored. Some studies are not linked to a certain
comfort domain, but instead the indoor environment is considered from and architectural point of
view.

The scoping review summarizes the the main findings of the 310 existing studies in categories: Audi-
tory, visual-, thermal-, olfactory-, and multi-domain. The auditory domain is the most explored, and
some actual thresholds on acoustical parameters are defined. However, most of the recommended
thresholds are based on literature, and needs experimental verification. The following typical symp-
toms on the auditory sense are pointed out:

• Adverse reaction to specific sounds
• Pain from certain sounds
• Tinnitus
• Irrational fear of certain sounds
• Difficulties in auditory filtering
• Difficulties in speech recognition with background noise
• Hearing impairments
For visual domains autistic people can be more sensitive to both dark and bright environments, caused
by atypical pupilliary light reflex [9]. This also makes them more subject to light flickering being
bothersome and they are prone to avoiding specific visuals, such as particular shadow patterns from
shading systems e.g., venetian blinds. Furthermore, the impact of daylight exposure, may be even
more significant for people with ASC, as circadian and melatonin secretion dysfunction is common in
autistic people. This can affect their sleep patterns, causing difficulties falling asleep or staying asleep
at night, leading to daytime sleepiness.
Thermal domains are the least studied sense. The development of the hypothalamus is considered a
potential cause of ASC, and as the hypothalamus controls various physiological functions, including
body temperature, it may influence their perception and awareness of the indoor environment [9].
Melatonin and metabolism dysfunction also makes it harder to regulate body temperature and it can
impact clothing preferences and the perception of air drafts.
Olfaction is also one of the lesser studied domains, however sensitivity to olfactory stimuli can lead to
serious concerns. Sensitivity to odours can potentially cause issues with food, anorexia nervosa, and
other health problems such as headaches, neurological issues, and asthma from scented products [9].
The multi-domain aspects of comfort may have a stronger impact on autistic individuals compared to
the general population. The use of multi-censorial therapy has shown to be effective e.g., by creating
quiet or stimulating spaces, such as sensory rooms. These have shown benefits in terms of attention
and reducing repetitive behaviours.
Much research show that autistic people are more sensitive to sensory inputs and they sometimes
suffer as a result of this. As the current knowledge on autism and indoor climate is so limited, it is
important to further dive into this subject in order to help improve their well-being.

2
Case studies
In this report two case studies will be presented. The first case is a study of a care facility residing
non-autonomous autistic people. The second case is a study of various autonomous autistic people’s
homes.
2.1
Care facility
The care facility in this study is located in Lyngby city and is a permanent residence for many autistic
people. As part of the facility there is an activity centre where the residents from different housing units
get together for e.g., movement and dance, film nights, music, and festive events. During weekdays the
activity centre is used for daily activities by five specific people, who are the subjects for the study in
this care facility. They are all men between the age of 32 −46 years old, with autism level 3 and mental
retardation. Among the subjects the co-morbidities epilepsy and trigeminus neuralgy are found. In
this group the general communication level is that of a 2-year-old. One person is verbal, another uses a
combination of verbal communication and pictograms. Two people use sign language and pictograms,
and one uses only pictograms.
The activity centre consists of a large common area and a smaller area with various sensory rooms. The
common area measures 116m2 and consists of a kitchen area, a stage area with instruments, a couch
area, and an isolated area equipped with a desk. The common area is used as a multiroom and at
times it is accessible by outside people not residing at the facility, e.g., the stage area is used only by
outsiders and not by the residents. The common area is equipped with mechanical ventilation. In the
smaller area, which measures 68m2, you find the sensory rooms, consisting of a dark room, a ball room,
a crackle bed room and a waterbed room, which range from 5 −10m2. Each room is designed to be
sensory seeking or sensory avoiding. The layout of the activity centre is seen in figure 5. The building
has high ceilings of 4 metres, and windows with a sill height of 2.5 metres. In the figure the inner walls
marked in blue are partition walls that do not meet the ceiling, nor reach the sill height (see section 1
in the figure.) This allows the sunlight to enter each of the rooms, except for the dark room, which is
equipped with its own individual ceiling to block out the light.

3.3

3.3.1
Ethical approval
Since the field study collects data about people with disabilities, the National Research Ethics Commit-
tee had to approve the experimental design before any measurements or questionnaire surveys could
take place. Originally, the field study was supposed to involve both autonomous and non-autonomous
people. However, The National Research Ethics Committee concluded that it should not directly in-
volve non-autonomous people. The reason for this limitation was to ensure that participation was
kept voluntary. As the non-autonomous individuals are not necessarily able reach out and enrol in
the project themselves, it was doubted whether it could be considered voluntary if these individuals
were involved and therefore, they were excluded from the experiment. Based on this decision the ex-
perimental design had to be changed. An agreement was made that data could still be collected in
common and unoccupied areas at residential facilities, but there should be no direct contact with, nor
collection of individual information from non-autonomous people.
3.3.2
Semi structured interview
The method for collection of subjective parameters from people living in the residential care facility,
was originally planned to include proxy respondents. The caregiver would observe the participant’s
behaviour and fill out a special comfort questionnaire on behalf of the participant. This method entails
some uncertainty, but is required when involving people with communicative difficulties. However,
the method was not used since non-autonomous people could not be directly involved. Instead the
subjective parameters were collected through a semi-structured interview with the caregivers working
at the facilities. The interview was based on a script developed for this very purpose (see Appendix
C). The questions were designed to be global and general, while still providing us as much detailed
knowledge as possible, without violating the residents’ anonymity. This change of method decreases
the level of detail for the collected information, but still provides background knowledge of how the
indoor climate effects the residents, according to the caregivers. In this experimental design it is not
possible to compare time-paired subjective and objective parameters recorded. Instead the interview
will be used to map which rooms might cause discomfort, and subsequently the objective parameters
in these rooms will be tracked.
3.3.3
Measurements
Long- and short-term measurements of objective parameters were conducted to collect information of
the indoor environment at the care facility.
The long-term measurements included two campaigns. The first campaign ran from May 3rd 2023 un-
til May 31st 2023. The collection of data included measurements of air temperature, operative temper-
ature, relative humidity, CO2-concentration, and illuminance. The type and placement of each sensor
is shown in figure 13. Details on the various sensor types are found in figure 7. The second campaign
ran from May 31st through June 19th 2023. The parameters being collected were similar to the first
campaign, but the placement of sensors was changed (see figure 13).
The short-term measurements were conducted at the end of each campaign. They included measure-
ments of the sound level, surface temperatures, the flicker percentage of individual lamps, as well as
point measurements of the illuminance and colour temperature. The placement of the point measure-

3.4.5
PMV and PPD
As a part of the assessment of the participant’s thermal comfort, the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) for the
given thermal environments at the time of questionnaire completion, was estimated using the online
CBE Thermal Comfort Tool [17]. The tool calculates the PMV and PPD (Predicted percentage dissat-
isfied) according to EN ISO 7730 [18], based on five inputs: Operative temperature, air speed, relative
humidity, metabolic rate and dynamic clothing insulation. Since operative temperature was only mea-
sured in the participants preferred room, there were cases where only values for air temperature were
available. If short-term measurements did not indicate temperature asymmetry in the room, air tem-
perature was used instead of operative temperature as input for the PMV estimation. Temperature
asymmetry was evaluated from calculations of radiant asymmetry and percent dissatisfied (PD) ac-
cording to EN ISO 7730 equation (10) for cool wall [18]. The air speed in the participants homes was
not measured, and was assumed to be 0.1m/s. It is difficult to determine the metabolic rate precisely.
Although it can be assumed that the participants were sedentary while filling in the questionnaire, the
preceding activity will have a large impact on the metabolic rate. Hence, the PMV is estimated for
two different scenarios: One for metabolic rate 1.1MET (corresponding to sedentary work) and one
for metabolic rate 1.7MET (corresponding to walking about) [17]. In the questionnaire the participants
were asked which garments they were wearing. Their total dynamic clothing insulation was calculated
according to EN ISO 9920 [19], based on the values shown in table 7.

Results and Discussion
4.1
Care facility in Lyngby
The results and discussion for the study of the care facility is based upon the semi-structured interview
as well as the collected indoor environment data from the two campaigns.
4.1.1
Semi-structured interview with caregivers
From the interview it is know that the residents are not able to express their perception of the indoor
environment. The caregivers will look for physical cues of discomfort, like signs of distress or removal
of clothing and then help the residents regulate by e.g., closing the curtains during daytime and re-
moving disturbing factors. Mainly regulations are based upon the caregivers own perceptions of the
indoor environment, with the assumption that the residents share the same preferences.
Out of the five subjects three of them have problems with sleep, where one of them sleeps more than
usual. The caregivers have found that physical activity and spending time outdoors generally im-
proves the sleep quality for the residents. Furthermore, one of the residents have shown great im-
provement in both their overall well-being as well their sleep quality, by the help of daylight and
music therapy.
At times, the caregivers will throw parties for the participants, with music and a disco ball in one of the
rooms. At these parties, the participants mainly chose to stay in this room. Many of the residents are
sensory seeking and make use of the different sensory rooms. One of the rooms has a heated waterbed,
which the residents enjoy. However, depending on their current mood and needs, both this room, the
visually stimulating rooms, as well as the dark room, are used by the residents.
In the semi structured interview the caregivers mainly expressed concern about the following:
• Slow regulating floor heating in apartments
• Visually distracting items in the common area
• Lighting not being dimmable
• Construction work disturbing the residents
The installed thermostats in the residents apartment cause issues with the thermal environment, mak-
ing the rooms either very warm or very cold at times. This causes discomfort for the residents who can
not regulate themselves, and who will sometimes be found fully covered by a blanket or duvet, even
at high temperatures.
For the common area, it is overall not very autistic friendly, as it is not only used by the facility’s
residents, but also as a multiroom. It is equipped with many visually distracting items, which are not
used by the residents e.g., musical instruments and kitchenware. Screens / room dividers have been
set up to accommodate for this issue, but the caregivers do not find this sufficient.
One of the caregivers expressed that they would like better control of the lighting and wish to try
out different or dimmable lighting. As one of the residents benefited greatly from daylight exposure
therapy, the caregiver wants to test if better lighting options could be beneficial as well.
For the past couple of years there have been construction work in the surrounding areas of the care
facility. This is a temporary concern, however; it is disturbing the residents both visually and audible
and causes them stress. The residents show irregularity in their response to noise, e.g., by having no
reaction to the fire alarm going off, but being distressed by background noise from multiple people
talking simultaneously.

fluorescent lamps, the flicker percentage on these light sources is very low. Figure 16 shows parame-

blue light. However, the flicker percentage was higher in these lamps, which could be disturbing to
light sensitive people.
From the semi-structured interview we learned that the resident’s sleep and well-being improves with
exposure to daylight. This observation from the caregivers aligns with current knowledge on day-
light’s impact on human health and well-being. The natural variations in daylight is important for
maintaining the body’s circadian rhythms, which regulate essential functions such as sleep patterns,
eating habits, brain activity, and hormone release. [20]. The current lights in the care center lower the
colour temperature in the rooms when turned on, so installing lights with different colour and intensity
options, to mimic daylight cycles, may be beneficial.

light measurement with short-term measurements in the same measurement point and the associated
measurement values in the rest of the room.

5
Conclusion
This study aimed to increase our understanding of how autistic people perceive their indoor environ-
ment. This report presented the initial field study and its preliminary results, as well as gave proposals
for the further perspectives of the field study. Hopefully the future results of the SENSEwellbeing
project can contribute to standards and guidelines being expanded to also accommodate the special
needs autistic people might have regarding the indoor climate.
5.1
Care facility
The study of the care facility in Lyngby included five non-autonomous autistic residents. As the res-
idents could not be directly involved, all conclusions are based upon the information gathered in
the semi-structured interview with their caregivers. The main concerns and causes for distress that
were found were slow heating regulation, visual distraction, lack of lighting flexibility and noise from
nearby construction work.
The resident’s sleep and well-being have shown improvement in connection with daylight exposure.
This observation aligns with current knowledge on the topic, stating that daylight exposure play an
important role in regulating sleep patterns, eating habits, brain activity and hormone release. It could
then be beneficial for the residents to install artificial lighting that mimics daylight.
5.2
Private homes
The study of indoor climate and well-being in private homes, included five participants with ASC
and various comorbidities. A combination of long- and short-term measurements of physical indoor
climate conditions was used for collection of objective parameters. The subjective parameters were
collected using a online comfort questionnaire, and the replies were matched with the correlating mea-
surements at the time of questionnaire completion. The main focus of the questionnaires was the sat-
isfaction level within the four domains: 1. Thermal, 2. Indoor air quality, 3. Visual and 4. Acoustical.
In total 32 questionnaires were filled in.
1. Within the thermal domain, four out of five participants found themselves particularly sensitive.
No direct correlation between the participants evaluation of their own sensitivity to the thermal
domain and their satisfaction level was found. In 50% of the completed questionnaires, the par-
ticipants were either dissatisfied or just dissatisfied with the thermal conditions. A general trend
of the participants feeling warmer than expected is seen, when comparing their TSV with the
calculated PMV. However, to clarify this pattern further, it is suggested that a question regarding
activity level is added to the comfort questionnaire, in order to refine the calculated PMV.
2. In the domain of air quality, four out of five participants found themselves particularly sensitive
as well. In only 7 out of 32 completed questionnaires were the participants satisfied or just satisfied
with the air quality, even though CO2-concentrations below 1000 ppm were measured along with
29 out of 32 completed questionnaires. No correlation between air quality and satisfaction level
was found, but might appear when more data is collected.
3. All five participants found themselves sensitive to the visual domain. Assessing the satisfaction
level based on the measured illuminance was not feasible because long-term measurements of
light were only conducted at a specific point in the room, which may not accurately represent
the overall lighting conditions. To enhance understanding of the room’s lighting conditions, it is
necessary to analyze the long-term measurements in conjunction with more precise short-term
measurements. Additionally, it is recommended to include an additional short-term measure-
ment point adjacent to the HOBO with illuminance probe in the subsequent stages of the field
study. This approach would allow for a better approximation of the room’s lighting conditions
by correlating the HOBO light measurement with short-term measurements taken at the same
point and the associated measurement values in other areas of the room.
4. All five participants found themselves particularly sensitive to the acoustic domain and it is pos-
sible that they will be disturbed by lower sound levels than the 40 dB(A) that the RoomAlyzer
Page 37 of 86
==PAGE:43==
Autism and indoor climate
Perceptions and effects on neurodivergent individuals
can measure. With the current data, it was not possible to comment on the connection between
the measured sound levels and the participant’s satisfaction level.
For the future of the study the following corrections are suggested:
• All short-term light measurements should be made at desk height and a new short-term mea-
surement point adjacent to the HOBO illuminance sensor should be added.
• If the number RoomAlyzers is sufficient, these should be placed in both rooms in the participants
homes.
• The participants should be reminded to alternate between the rooms they occupy when filling
out the questionnaire.
• When a limited amount of HOBO with voltage probe is available these should be placed in the
participants preferred rooms.
The findings of this study represent preliminary results. To draw conclusions about the variations in
how autistic individuals perceive the indoor climate compared to the neurotypical population, it is
essential to gather more data, preferably from a larger sample of participants. With the current data it
is difficult to find specific overall correlations between the measured values and the satisfaction level.
