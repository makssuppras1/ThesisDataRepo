Abstract 
The aim of this project is the investigation of two biosynthetic pathways, and an additional study of a 
transcription factor connected to a novel compound, x, produced by Aspergillus aculeatus. The main 
pathways studied are associated with the production of the secondary metabolites acucalbistrin A and 
acurin A + B, respectively.  
 
The biosynthesis of the decalin-like structure of acucalbistrin A was suggested recently (Andersen 2016). 
Single-gene deletion mutant strains, based on a proposed gene cluster, were constructed. Analysis 
showed involvement in the pathway for two of the thirteen gene products deleted. Although, these 
data were not sufficient for full pathway elucidation, an updated gene cluster is proposed. In addition, 
a second gene cluster is suggested for a putative aculeacin A analog NRPS, with a possible connection 
to eight of the constructed mutant strains. 
  
The novel compound x producing synthase gene was discovered in the pursuit of a synthase gene 
connected to acurin (Andersen 2016). I showed the overexpression of an adjacent putative 
transcription factor lead to higher production of compound x. The transcription factor reveals 
properties as a global repressor when the strain is grown on solid yeast-extract-sucrose medium (YES). 
The overexpression strain is thus well suited for future purification of the compound and structure 
elucidation. 
  
For acurin biosynthesis, participation of four out of five putative tailoring enzyme was demonstrated by 
deleting the corresponding genes. Two genes encoding a putative methyltransferase and a CYP450 
monooxygenase, respectively, can with strong indication be linked to an O-methylation in the final step 
of the biosynthesis and an oxidation step, respectively. Deletion of a putative aldehyde dehydrogenase 
and an oxidoreductase both showed terminated production of acurin. None of these two deletion 
strains produced the expected intermediates, and their suggested function is therefore based on 
bioinformatics. Overexpression of an adjacent transcription factor reveals properties both as a local 
activator of acurin and a global repressor for several other undefined compounds. The overexpression

Chapter 1 – Introduction 
 
 
Introduction 
 
Aspergillus aculeatus is a common and well-known filamentous fungi in the industrial world 
and used for the production of enzymes like pectinases [Favela-torres et al. 2006] and 
secondary metabolites like aculeacin A (i.e. an antibiotic active against fungi and yeast) [Mizuno 
et al. 1976]. Even though the A. aculeatus fungi is an industrial workhorse, the knowledge of 
the genome and the annotation of the genes for specific metabolites is relatively limited. The 
genome sequence for this particular fungi only recently became available in March 2010 
through the genome portal of the U.S. Department of Energy, the joint genome institute 
database (http://genome.jgi.doe.gov/). With this in hand and with the proper bioinformatics 
tools it is now possible to search for genes with similarities from other species within the A. 
aculeatus genome. Thus, genes that already have a known function and annotation in one 
species can now be aligned and compared with genes in A. aculeatus.  
 
==PAGE:18==
Introduction 
 
2 
In work prior to this project, Petersen et al. and Lasse Norup Andersen did a substantial amount 
of research in finding secondary metabolites in A. aculeatus and annotate these to their 
respective genes (Petersen et al. 2014; Andersen 2016). Many secondary metabolites in A. 
aculeatus consist of polyketides (PKs), non-ribosomal peptides (NRPs) or a combination of 
them both. Synthases are responsible for the assembly of all these compounds, and Lasse 
Norup Andersen successfully deleted three polyketide synthase (PKS) encoding genes involved 
in the production of acucalbistrin A, acurin, and a novel compound referred to as compound x 
(Andersen 2016).  
 
The aim of the present project is the investigation of the biosynthetic pathways in acucalbistrin 
A and acurin. The PKS encoding genes are part of individual gene clusters including genes 
encoding putative tailoring enzymes responsible for the post modifications of the respective 
secondary metabolites. It is the aim of the present project to annotate the genes of these 
putative tailoring enzymes and investigate the role in the arrangement and modification of 
acucalbistrin A and acurin. The approach includes gene deletion strains, overexpression of 
transcription factors, and analysis of gene expression levels. In addition to the elucidation of 
the two pathways, a minor investigation of the transcription factor adjacent to the compound 
x synthase gene is also investigated for the purpose of a future purification and structure 
clearing. 
 
Secondary metabolites produced by microbial organisms like A. aculeatus represent a diverse 
lineup of chemistry, many of which remains to be discovered and investigated. These novel 
microbial compounds can have a favorable effect in human applications and the need for more 
research is ongoing and expanding. This project serves only a small part of the extensive 
research already concluded and to be concluded by DTU Bioengineering - Department of 
Biotechnology and Biomedicine.

Chapter 2 – Theory  
 
 
Theory 
The theory chapter is divided into two sections. The first section contains the background on 
the fundamental nature and metabolism of Aspergillus. Also, the enzymatic activities needed 
for the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites is explained. The second section is more toolbox 
orientated with a presentation of the background on gene mining and bioengineering 
technologies. 
Genetically Modified Organisms 
This project used genetically modified Aspergillus aculeatus to generate data. The gene 
modifications involved deletion of specific genes by use of homologous recombination and 
insertion of biomarkers. The classification of the work encloses in the category GMO 1, 
specifications for this class is low risk or no risk for human health and the environment.

The Aspergillus genus 
The kingdom of fungi belongs in the domain of eukarya entirely separated from animals and 
plants. The fungi kingdom is vastly diverse in appearance and growth, but all are heterotrophs 
and need energy and building blocks from the breakdown of organic carbon. As any kingdom 
in the tree of life, the fungi’s can be divided into subclasses, like phylum, class, order, etc. In 
the family of trichocomaceae, the genus Aspergillus appears as a branch for sorting about 340 
known species. The Aspergillus genus is a group of versatile organisms with both beneficial and 
pathogenic properties. The microorganisms can produce several biosynthetic products which 
are valuable in human application, but, some species can also invade humans and cause fatal 
aspergillosis. The species A. sojae and A. oryzae are used  in the production of soy sauce and 
sake, while the A. flavus causes an annual loss of more than a billion dollars worth of crop 
damage (Olarte et al. 2012). The Aspergillus species are highly divergent regarding proteome, 
and even closely related species such as A. fumigatus and A. fischerianus are as diverse as 
human and mice (Gibbons & Rokas 2013). Some species do have immense similarities in regard 
to the genome, e.g. A.orysae and A. flavus share 99.5% of their genes. As mentioned above, 
one is a domesticated microorganism producing enzymes for food production and the other is 
a mycotoxin-producing agriculture pest. All known Aspergillus have eight chromosomes with a 
length of 28 Mb to 40 Mb, and the number of genes can be very diverse. One A. niger (i.e., the 
CBS 513.88) has shown to have more than 14.000 ORF compared to A. aculeatus, the organism 
used in this project, with only 10.828 OFR (Olarte et al. 2012).  
The life cycle of Aspergillus  
Aspergillus aculeatus is a multicellular microorganism capable of surviving harsh conditions and 
low nutrient levels. The fungi belongs to the Aspergillus section nigri and was first isolated from 
soil and rotten fruit (Kunitake et al. 2014). A. aculeatus is a known industrial organism and is 
==PAGE:21==
Theory 
 
5 
used for the production of enzymes in both the food and feed industries, e.g. cellulose, 
protease and pectinases are enzymes produced by A. aculeatus. 
 
The vegetative growth of the fungus starts from a germling with the formation of mycelia, a 
network of hyphal cells. The fungi spread outwards over a short distance in search of nutrients 
(Lee et al. 2010). The hyphal cells grow from the mycelia center by dividing at the apical cell, 
and in that, constantly leaving non-dividing cells behind. Branching helps to create the complex 
mycelia network, which is often seen as a wool-like layer. The hyphal cell is equipped with a 
cell wall that allows secretion of catabolic enzymes and absorption of broken down compounds 
(Carris 2012). The secretion is mostly seen at the apical cell, or edge of the mycelium, where 
nutrients are more available. When the hyphae cell grows from a spore to a germling, it starts 
the development of septate hyphae, cells containing one or more nuclei divided by cross walls, 
so-called septa. The separation by the septa can be supported by a septa pore, allowing for 
transport of nutrients, cytoplasm, and organelles from compartment to compartment. Thus, 
nutrients and other components can be carried from the edge of the colony to the center of 
the mycelium.  
 
Aspergillus aculeatus resembles the A. niger and also forms black-pigmented asexual spores 
(Kunitake et al. 2011). Asexual sporulation can be an integral part of the life cycle or a result of 
unfavorable nutrient conditions (Adams et al. 1998).   
 
In the initiation of the asexual sporulation, the hyphae cell differentiates to a foot cell and 
develops further into a mitospore producing the conidia. Mitospores are spores formed 
through mitosis, in contrast to meiospores that are formed through meiosis, in Aspergillus 
known as ascospores. When the hyphal cell goes from normal growth to asexual development, 
the process creates a pouch-like structure and ends with the formation of a conidiophore, a 
==PAGE:22==
Theory 
 
6 
stalk that carries and matures the conidium. The conidium can survive extreme condition and 
be dormant for long periods of time.  
 
The mitosis reproduction is the essential part of the asexual cycle and leads to genetically 
identical spores, in contrast,  some Aspergillus are capable of producing ascospores, which is 
part of a sexual cycle. However, not all Aspergillus has proved to undergo sexual reproduction, 
see figure 1 for a schematic overview of all the different reproduction cycles. The sexual 
reproduction cycle has given Aspergillus its name and the result of the cycle is genetically 
different spores.  
 
In addition to the sexual and the asexual cycles, many Aspergillus species are also capable of a 
parasexual cycle. In this cycle haploid hyphae cell undergoes anastomosis (Becker & Alves De 
Castro-Prado 2006), a process where two streams reconnect in a fusion manner. The fusion 
leads to a formation of a heterokaryon cell that through nuclear fusion becomes a diploid 
hyphae cell. The heterokaryon status can be maintained over time without nuclear fusion, but, 
low stability will eventually lead to fusion. By arbitrary chromosome exclusion, the cell restores 
its haploid state, and the result can be a genetic recombination within mitosis (Lee et al. 2010). 
The ability to function as heterokaryon is another interesting fact for the Aspergillus fungi, one 
that has consequences for this particular project. In the process of introducing new DNA for 
homologous recombination DNA repair or transformation, the cell can end up with one nucleus 
as a mutant and one nucleus as a wild type, a situation that, when making gene deletions, leads 
to the wild type phenotype. The heterokaryon status of the Aspergillus makes the construction 
process of mutant strains more challenging and requires restreaking until it is possible to select 
for the mutant strain.

The appearance of Aspergillus 
On a larger scale and with a more visible perspective, Aspergillus is capable of a variety of 
morphological appearances within the same species. In A.nidulans, it is shown that pH plays a 
vital role in the morphology of the fungi. By increasing the concentration of bicarbonate, and 
additional pH, the fungi's ability in branching and specific growth rates changes significantly 
(figure 2) (Rodríguez-Urra et al. 2009). Other characteristics like color and sporulation can 
change in accordance with available sources of nutrients and compounds in the surroundings.  
 
Figure 2. An example of the morphological impact of a change in the pH environment,  
Here shown with Aspergillus nidulans.  [The figure is provided by (Rodríguez-Urra et al. 2009)]  
 
In some cases, in the section of Aspergillus Nigri and especially A. aculeatus, when stimulated 
under the right circumstances, the fungi can produce sclerotia, a compact aggregation of 
hyphae cell containing food reserves (Frisvad 2014; Traquair 1985). The sclerotia are visible as 
small white spots among the black spores. The morphology of the Aspergillus allows for the 
possibility to observe changes from a wild-type strain to a mutant. Also, changes in nutrient 
levels can lead to a different morphological appearance – a significant change can indicate 
impaired growth conditions or loss of essential properties.

The metabolism of Aspergillus 
Like most other heterotrophic eukaryotes Aspergillus depend on D-glucose for its primary 
metabolism and pathway of glycolysis, where D-glucose gets converted into pyruvate and 
further to acetyl-CoA before entering the citric acid cycle (Flipphi et al. 2009). The fact that D-
glucose hardly ever is available for the fungi in its free form forces the Aspergillus to produce 
enzymes for breaking down other possible sources of nutrients (e.g. cellulose). The fungi can 
produce and secrete enzymes through its cell wall, predominantly from the hyphal tip, into the 
surroundings. The enzymes can efficiently alter the complex nutrients into smaller sugars that 
can be absorbed and used in the primary metabolism. Primary metabolism involves processes 
related to development, growth, reproduction, and is a process that generates energy and long 
fatty acids, which is essential for cell membrane construction and storage bodies (Brown et al. 
1996). The production of fatty acids (FAs) use similar enzyme complexes as seen in the 
secondary metabolisms production of polyketides, and they can even be further linked through 
universal intermediates like acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA. Other intermediates as amino acids 
and mevalonate can also be found in both primary and secondary metabolism, but with 
different metabolic purposes. Aspergillus generates FAs through the use of enzyme complexes 
with a full reduction of the β-keto group after each condensation reaction cycle. In contrast, 
the reduction of the β-keto in the production of polyketides is optional, and the result is a much 
more different group of compounds.  
 
In comparison, secondary metabolism produces metabolites not essential to growth or other 
processes related to survival of the organism. Instead, the purpose is to heighten the living 
conditions for the fungi by creating chemical signals (e.g. quorum sensing), defending their 
habitat by inhibiting the growth of other organisms or produce pigments to avoid UV 
radiation(Brakhage 2013) - all related to making the fungi stand out and be competitive against 
other microorganisms. Secondary metabolites are low mass and structurally diverse molecules

Regulatory factors, transcription factors and signal transduction 
The biosynthesis and transcription of genes related to many secondary metabolites are often 
from silent gene clusters and requires activation under more sophisticated conditions than 
standard cultivation (Brakhage 2013). Silent genes or downregulated clusters provide a 
method to preserve energy and only produce the necessary SMs when the fungi require them, 
a tendency seen throughout all of life. Other reasons for silencing are related to epigenetics, 
where some clusters are silent due to densely packed nucleosomes. In these situations, DNA 
or some specific histone residues in the nucleosome can be methylated – the result is a 
repressed transcription of the nearby genes and clusters. Inspection of several fungal genomes 
has shown to contain a vast number of gene clusters related to putative secondary 
metabolites, many of these products are undetectable under standard cultivations and 
therefore must be silent or downregulated (Palmer & Keller 2010).  
 
Transcription factor genes, often embedded in a gene cluster, encode regulatory proteins that 
can act in response to a signal transduction cascade. The signal molecules can have different 
==PAGE:27==
Theory 
 
11 
roles and magnitudes on the transcription of the relevant genes. Most transcription factors 
found in SM gene clusters have a local effect, meaning that they either activate or repress a 
synthase encoding gene and additional tailoring enzymes in proximity to the transcription 
factor (Brakhage 2013). In fungal PKS gene clusters up to 90 % of the transcription factors 
belong to the Zn-family, a DNA motif binding protein. In the NRPS gene clusters, these 
regulators can be more diverse. In contrast, transcription factors acting globally, meaning 
regulation of several genes simultaneously, belong to a more general DNA motif binding 
protein like the CCAAT-binding complex. An example of a global transcription factor is the PacC 
pathway; a schematic overview is illustrated in figure 3. In alkaline ambient pH, PacC can 
function as an activator promoting alkaline-expressed genes, such as genes related to secreted 
enzymes, permeases, and genes encoding enzymes involved in the synthesis of secreted 
metabolites (Penalva & Arst 2002). PacC also works as a repressor on acid-expressed genes, 
for instance, pacA and gapA, genes coding for an acid phosphatase and a Ras GTPase-activating 
protein, respectively (Harispe et al. 2008). Crosstalk between gene clusters adds to the level of 
complexity in attempts to clarify new and unknown biosynthesis in an organism like Aspergillus, 
especially when working with a combinatorial pathway. Deletion or overexpression of a 
transcription factor can have a widespread consequence on the SM profile, where more than 
one product may disappear or appear. In a novel gene cluster with a newly discovered 
transcription factor, it is uncertain if the transcription factor acts globally or locally, or if it 
activates or represses. The answer has to come through examination.

expression of its SMs in respond to diversified environmental stimuli. These stimuli can, among 
others, come in the form of changes in the carbon or nitrogen source, temperature, pH, light, 
amino acids, reactive oxygen species, or the presence of other organisms. In respond to the 
environmental stimuli, surface receptors can initiate an activation cascade of regulatory

The Enzymes behind the biosynthesis of natural products 
For the production of secondary metabolites and natural products in general, the Aspergillus 
aculeatus requires enzymes that can speed up reactions, recognize the right precursors, and 
join them together to finalize a usable end product. All natural products are derived from a 
small pool of secondary metabolite precursors with four major pathways dominating the 
assembly. The first pathway, the polyketide pathway, use a variety of Co-enzyme-A bound 
precursors to construct the compounds. In Aspergillus, these precursors are mainly acetyl-CoA 
and malonyl-CoA. The second pathway is the non-ribosomal peptide pathway with the use of 
amino acids as precursors. An example is alkaloids which are produced in some Aspergillus. 
The two other pathways are the isoprene pathway and the shikimate pathway (Bentley 1999). 
A mixture of different backbones can also occur. In the present project, acurin represents an 
NRP-PK compound. 
 
The enzymes related to a compound are, in most cases, encoded from a close restricted area 
in the genome called a gene cluster. A gene cluster associated with a SM can consist of a 
primary synthase for the construction of a carbon backbone and adjacent genes encoding for 
tailor enzymes responsible for post modifications. This section will describe the many enzymes 
responsible for the biosynthesis of the secondary metabolites and how the chemical diversities 
are accomplished.  
Polyketide synthases (PKSs) 
Polyketides is a diverse group of natural products with complex structures and many 
applications. They have one of the most represented backbone structures within secondary 
metabolites, and postmodification of the polyketides can have a tremendous impact on the 
chemical properties and thereby the application of the compound (Du et al. 2001). The 
construction of the polyketide backbone is carried out by a polyketide synthase (PKS) in Claisen 
==PAGE:30==
Theory 
 
14 
condensations between a starter unit and extender units. The synthases are divided into 
different classes, and because of their close similarity to fatty acid synthases (FASs), the 
nomenclature between them are very close (Hertweck 2009). FASs are divided into two groups, 
type I and II, related to large multifunctional proteins seen in mammals and fungi, and 
monofunctional proteins observed in bacteria and plants, respectively (Dewick 2002). The PKSs 
have three classes instead of just two and are additionally divided into subgroups related to 
the iterative or noniterative behavior in the build up of the carbon chain. The PKS type 1 has a 
non-iterative and an iterative subdivision; the division is based on the KS-domains ability to 
catalyze more than one elongation per cycle (Hertweck 2009). The Type 1 is, similar to the FAS 
type 1, a large multifunctional enzyme with many modules responsible for the elongation and 
voluntary reductions. The non-iterative is found in bacteria, and the iterative is mainly found 
in fungi. The PKS type II, only iterative, can exclusively be found in bacteria. For further details 
on PKS classes see Table 1.

The fungal iterative type 1 PKSs are the must relevant regarding Aspergillus aculeatus or 
filamentous fungi in general. The major part of polyketides are synthesized in an iterative 
manner by a type 1 synthase in these microorganisms. Three subclasses regarding the β-keto-
reduction additionally divide the iterative type 1 PKSs into non-reducing (nrPKS), partly 
reducing (prPKS), and high reducing (hrPKS) (Boettger et al. 2012). The enzyme complex can 
resemble a round table with different workstations, which is responsible for an individual task 
in the elongation of the growing polyketide and optional modifications. In the middle of the 
table is the phosphopentethienylated acyl carrier protein (ACP) functioning as a substrate 
carrier between three distinct domains, i.e. the elongation domain, the reduction domain, and 
the release domain. At the first domain, an acyltransferase(AT) catalyze the starter unit into 
the ACP by establishing a thioester bond. In fungi, the starter unit is predominantly acetyl-CoA, 
and the extender unit is malonyl-CoA. The extender unit is also introduced into the ACP in a 
similar manner, and a translocation of the two units to the active site of a β-
ketoacylsynthase(KS) in domain 1 is then performed by a prosthetic group of the ACP. The 
starter unit is attached to the KS-active site and then subject to nucleophilic attack by the 
extender unit. In the elongating step, decarboxylation of the β-keto group from the extender 
unit releases carbon dioxide in a discharge of energy, and this gives rise to the driving force 
needed in the process.  The domains can now be reused in the second round of elongation 
directly, or the growing polyketide can continue to the reduction domain. The optional 
reduction domains are a β-ketoacyl reductase (KR), a dehydrogenase (DH), and an enoyl 
reductase (ER). These domains deliver a series of reductions in the newly added β-carbonyl 
group. The result can be either a β-hydroxy group, a α-β unsaturated carbon-carbon double 
bond, or a full reduction to a single carbon-carbon bond. The AT domain can now load a new 
extender unit into the ACP, and the process starts over. At the end of the elongation, the 
polyketide chain is transferred to a thioesterase (TE) domain, and a hydrolysis releases the 
product from the enzyme - other release mechanisms are also possible. The optional domains 
in each round of elongation contribute to the chemical diversities in polyketides. The length of

Non-Ribosomal Peptide Synthases (NRPSs) 
Enzymatic synthesis of SMs with amino acids as precursors characterizes the non-ribosomal 
peptide compound class. As the name indicates, these peptides do not need the genetic coding 
mRNA as a scaffold in the elongation process. Moreover, the NRPS is not limited in its use of 
building blocks, and can in that respect benefit from non-DNA coding amino acids and their 
diverse properties (Dewick 2002). NRPs have been known in filamentous fungi for a long time; 
an example is Penicillin produced by Penicillium notatum and discovered in 1928 by Alexander 
Fleming(Eisfeld 2009). Also, many new putative NRPSs are being found as a consequence of an 
extensive effort in genome sequencing, but the products of the NRPSs still need to be linked. 
The ability to detect the new synthases in the genome is based on the structures of the 
enzymes. NRPSs have different characteristics in their way of synthesizing end-products. A 
proposed three-group classification was suggested in 2002 by Mootz et al., divided by the 
biosynthetic logic. The groups are linear NRPSs (type A), iterative NRPSs (type B), and nonlinear 
NRPSs (type C) (figure 6) (Mootz et al. 2002).

The Type A linear NRPSs use a new module for incorporation of each new amino acid, and the 
basic formula for the domain organization has the template A-T-(C-A-T)n-1-TE/C, where n equals 
the sum of amino acids in the final peptide. The A-domains (i.e. adenylation domain) are 
responsible for substrate recognition, and thereby the combination and order of the amino 
acids in the peptide. The T-domain (i.e. thiolation domain) binds and transports the activated 
amino acid, the C-domain (i.e. condensation domain) performs the peptide bond formation. 
The final step, TE-domain, releases the peptide from the enzyme (Eisfeld 2009). Optional 
domains for N-methylation, epimerization, and heterocyclic ring formation have insertions in 
the specific modules related to the final peptide. Since the linear NRPSs require a module for 
each amino acid, the size of the enzymes becomes additionally bigger and the coding gene 
increases in proportion. The iterative type B NRPSs have a similar structure to the type A, but 
with an iterative activity that allows for reuse of modules. The type C non-linear NRPSs are 
more complex, and prediction of the end-products are not possible by interpretation of the 
enzymes structures (Eisfeld 2009). Many NRPs have high bioactivity and thereby great 
pharmaceutical potential, and the finding of new NRPSs can prove to be of immense 
importance. 
Terpene synthases 
Terpenoids are a class of natural products synthesized from two common C5 precursors, 
namely isopentenyl diphosphate (IPP) and dimethylallyl diphosphate (DMAPP) (Schmidt-
Dannert 2015). The synthase initiates a head-to-tail 1’-4 condensations of the two units and 
the result is a C10 molecule, further condensation yields C15 or C20 carbons. Longer terpenoid 
chains are catalyzed by other enzymes with the above molecules as precursors. Cyclization and 
rearrangement add to the structural diversity of the terpenoid compounds. Fungi are known 
to produce a vast amount of bioactive sesquiterpenoid SMs (Agger et al. 2009).

SMs derived from more than one group of backbone structures are commonly synthesized by 
hybrid synthases. An example of such a hybrid synthases is the fungal compounds consisting 
of PKs and NRPs, a group of complex molecules, some with high potent bioactive properties 
(Boettger & Hertweck 2013), e.g. fusarin C (figure 7), a PK-NRP compound, which are able to 
stimulate growth in breast cancer cells (Fisch 2013; Sondergaard et al. 2011).

Tailor enzymes 
In fungi, adjacent genes to the synthases generally encode for a variety of different tailoring 
enzymes responsible for post modifications of the backbones (Andersen et al. 2013). The 
organized genes make up the discrete gene clusters capable of adding functional groups to the 
core structure. In this part of the chapter, the mechanism and how some common enzymes 
work will be described. Only the enzymes related to the investigated gene clusters will be 
emphasized.  
Reductases 
In general, a reduction is the transfer of electrons and subsequently hydrogens to a recipient, 
which means that the reductase donates hydrogens in a reaction. The result is a reduction of 
the recipient and an oxidation of the reductase or co-enzyme. In the mevalonate pathway, the 
HMG-CoA reductase is responsible for the conversion of HMG-CoA to mevalonic acid, a 
precursor for both IPP and DMAPP (Schmidt-Dannert 2015). In the process, the carboxylic 
group, which is in an ester linkage to the thiol of the enzyme, is reduced to alcohol with an 
intermediate aldehyde step. The reductase uses NADPH as a reductant in the process, and the 
result is both reduction and release from the enzyme to yield mevalonate. See figure 9 for 
further description.

encoded gene will in theory yield a more oxidated intermediate compound.  
Oxidases 
Oxidation is the loss of electrons, meaning in an oxidative oxidation the oxidase removes 
hydrogens from the recipient and becomes concurrently reduced. In the process of oxidizing a 
hydroxy group to a carboxylic acid or ketone, hydrogen is removed from the substrate and 
passed to a molecular oxygen or peroxide. The reaction yield in both cases water as a product 
(Dewick 2002). The oxidase family has many subdivisions all depending on the behavior, 
oxidation degree, and substrates. The monooxygenase only introduces the first oxygen from 
molecular O2 to the substrate, the second oxygen forms water in a reductive step; the process 
includes a hydrogen donor, e.g. NADH or NADPH. The Dioxygenase incorporates both oxygens 
from molecular O2 into the substrate and can in the process cleave a carbon-carbon bond 
(figure10).

responsible is a Baeyer-Villiger monooxygenases (Dewick 2002). There are many oxidases with 
many applications, and in general, deletion of an oxidase encoding gene leads to a more 
reduced intermediate.

in the catalytic process as a co-factor, and the enzymes are associated with an endless amount 
of biological reactions (Kelly et al. 2009). Some of the reactions encountered by the CYP450’s 
include carbon hydroxylation, dealkylation, epoxidation (figure 11), aromatic hydroxylation, 
reduction, and dehalogenation (Bernhardt 2006).

Dehydrogenases 
The dehydrogenases belong to the family of oxidoreductases, an enzymatic activity that 
catalyzes the transfer of electrons between two molecules. In the reaction, the electron 
transfer can be recognized as the removal of two hydrogens from the substrate and relocation 
to a coenzyme acceptor, e.g. NAD+ or NADP+ (Dewick 2002). The result of a dehydrogenation 
can be oxidation of alcohol to a ketone, establishment of a carbon-carbon double bond, or a 
variety of other reactions where hydrogen is removed from the substrate (figure 12).

Methyltransferases 
Among all the methyltransferases, the donor of -CH3 namely S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) is 
the most common regarding post modifications of SMs (Gerke et al. 2012). This section will 
only concern these specific enzymes and provide an overview of the mechanism and yield.  
 
SAM functions as an enzyme substrate donor by adding a methyl group in the catalytic process. 
The methyl group can be added to S, O, N or C, and the accepting atom classifies the enzyme, 
O-methyltransferases represent the largest class. The mechanism resembles an SN2 reaction, 
a commonly known reaction type in organic chemistry. The methyltransferase can deprotonate 
in a similar way as a base and, at the same time, create a nucleophilic atom on the SM sidechain 
for methylation. The methyl carbon on the SAM is electrophilic, and that results in a non-steric 
hindrance for a nucleophilic attack from the SM atom(Liscombe et al. 2012), see figure 13 for 
more details.

α/β hydrolase 
Hydrolases catalyze a reaction where water is an integral part of the reactants, also known as 
a hydrolysis. The α/β part of the name relates to the structural composition of the enzyme. The 
core of the protein consists of eight beta-sheets joined by alpha helices (LOLLIS 1992). The 
carboxylic ester hydrolase catalyzes the hydrolysis of an ester bond with an alcohol and a 
parent carboxylic acid as a product. The mechanism includes a nucleophilic attack from the 
water on the C=O part of the ester (Levisson et al. 2009). Other specific hydrolases catalyze the 
breakage of carbon-nitrogen bonds, carbon-carbon bonds, or phosphorus-nitrogen bonds (Pait 
et al. 2014). The hydrolases belong to a class of more than 200 enzymes, and the substrates 
are equally diverse. Deletion of a hydrolase encoding gene can have different consequences, 
but knowing the nature of the enzyme activity can narrow down the search. 
Diels Alderases 
The Diels-Alder reaction involves a diene intermediate with an adjacent dienophile structure 
(figure 14). The Diels-Alderase can support a fusion of the two reactants by redrawing electrons 
from the dienophile making it electron poor. The four π-electrons in the diene makes it 
electron rich, and the two reactants can interact in a pericyclic transition. The result is a ring 
formation with one double bond (Klas et al. 2015). The composition and mechanism of the 
Diels-Alderase are not limited to the reaction type seen in figure 14. If the constellation is right, 
the reaction can occur intramolecularly, as seen in the acucalbistrin A pathway (figure 26).

Transporters 
Transporters are not enzymes in the same sense as the tailor enzymes and the synthases 
describe above, but nevertheless, the encoded genes are part of many gene clusters. They play 
a significant role in the active efflux and secretion of some toxic SM and thereby contributing 
to a competitive advantage against other microorganisms (Del Sorbo et al. 2000). The 
transporter can also participate in the external efflux of toxins like antibiotics and other natural 
derived toxic compounds. Thus, the transporter can contribute to drug resistance in the 
organism (Andrade et al. 2000). The two primary efflux transporters are the ATP-binding 
cassette (ABC) and the major facilitator superfamily (MFS), both plasma membrane proteins. 
The ABC transporter uses ATP hydrolysis as the driving force in the efflux of toxins or SMs. The 
ABC class can recognize a broad range of mostly hydrophilic substrates, like sugars, peptides, 
and drugs (Hayashi & Waard 2002). The MFS transporters are driven by a proton-motive force 
over the membrane and therefore needs a gradient to perform a coupled movement of the 
molecules. A number of MFS transporters in filamentous fungi have proved to mediate the 
secretion of toxins like aflatoxin and cercosporin (Hayashi & Waard 2002).  
Deletion of a putative transporter encoded gene can lead to accumulation of a potentially toxic 
compound for the organism. The consequence can be impaired growth or death of the fungi.

Gene mining for natural products 
Surveys of a broad range of Aspergillus genomes show possibilities for a largely untapped 
reservoir of gene clusters encoding SMs. Many of these clusters are potentially cryptic (i.e. 
silent) or downregulated, and therefore have not been annotated (Guo & Wang 2014). The 
possibility for the discovery of novel bioactive compounds through one or more of these cryptic 
gene clusters, makes Aspergillus, with all its species, a clear candidate for research and 
investigation for natural biosynthetic products. An advanced set of tools for exploration of 
genomes called gene mining allows for search of gene homologs in a new and uninvestigated 
organism, as long as the genome sequence is available. Gene mining uses DNA sequences of a 
known genotype for isolation of useful genes in related genotypes. The exploration can lead to 
new products with applications within areas of antimicrobial, anticancer, or agrochemical (Wu 
et al. 2015). The focus, in this chapter, is the theory behind the bioinformatics used in gene 
mining, and a description of the approach used in the elucidation of the biosynthetic pathways. 
Bioinformatics 
In the present project, a number of tools were used for finding genes in A. aculeatus, search 
conserved domains, and predict putative enzyme functions. The results were used  to assist in 
the interpretation of the generated data. The world of bioinformatics covers a methodology 
based on advanced computer databases, analytical software tools, and algorithms for 
understanding the vast amount of biological data accessible for today's researchers.  
 
BLAST, or Basic Local Alignment Search Tool, uses a sequence, either nucleotide or protein, as 
input to align against a target sequence of interest, or multiple sequences (e.g. a database). 
The search tool compares the sequences for the best alignment by allowing small gaps, 
removing complexity (e.g. repeats), and creating a list of the short sequences that makes up 
==PAGE:46==
Theory 
 
30 
the query input. It uses the short sequences or “words” in a scoring matrix to determine the 
best match. BLAST scores each alignment by taking gaps and, in the case of protein sequences, 
amino acid similarities into an account. The BLAST tool measures the similarities and identities 
between the sequences, not homology, but, it is possible to find similar genes between 
organism and from there determine the homology (Pertsemlidis & Fondon 2001). 
 
Conserved domain databases (CDD) provide means for comparing protein sequences with 
elucidated 3D protein structures with known catalytic activity. Just like BLAST, a sequence is 
compared to an immense amount of structures within a range of domain superfamilies. A 
superfamily is roughly a set of protein sequences related to a common trait (Marchler-Bauer 
et al. 2013). The superfamilies are, in that sense, more or less represented compared to the 
number of sequences supporting the models. The philosophy behind the CDD is to give the 
researcher an idea of the functionality of an unknown protein sequence of interest. In the 
present project, the knowledge of a given enzyme-encoding gene functionality can help in the 
elucidation of a putative biosynthetic pathway. 
Biosynthesis and pathway investigation 
Most known fungal PK compounds are synthesized by iterative PKSs, whose structural 
composition makes it difficult to predict the end product. Tailoring enzymes, encoded by a 
gene cluster together with a main synthase, add to the complexity and further complicates the 
product prediction (Holm et al. 2014). In the investigation of an orphan gene cluster (i.e. genes 
that miss detectable homology), it is, therefore, necessary to create mutants with genetical 
modification in correspondence to the presumed product. Deletion of a synthase gives insight 
into the structure and locus of the product. Deletion of a tailoring enzyme encoded gene can 
lead to termination of the compound, detectable by LC-MS analysis. Likewise, new masses may 
appear from the accumulation further back in the pathway representing a compound 
intermediate. In the identification of genes related to known compounds encoded by an 
==PAGE:47==
Theory 
 
31 
unknown gene cluster, a structural comparison with genetical elucidated compounds from 
other organisms can help the investigation. Example, in the pursuit of the synthase responsible 
for the biosynthesis of acucalbistrin A, the protein sequence from synthases associated with 
Lovastatin and Fumagillin were used as queries (Andersen 2016). The BLAST search resulted in 
four putative PKSs; one showed involvement in acucalbistrin A production, and by chance, 
another (PKsC) confirmed participation in Acurin production.

Genetic engineering  
The targeting and replacement of specific genes in loci of interest is accomplished by utilizing 
the evolutionarily conserved DNA repair pathway homologous recombination for gene 
targeting. This chapter will focus on the theory behind the biological mechanisms that permit 
the exploration and editing of the Aspergillus genome. This project is based on knockout data 
by deletion of specific genes encoding enzymes with assumed affiliation to nearby synthase 
genes. The particular focus will be the mechanisms of DNA repair and gene expression.  
DNA damage and repair 
The homologous recombination (HR) pathway is essential for the transformation in Aspergillus 
aculeatus. In the present project, the strain template Acu11 was a non-homologous end-
joining deficient strain, which makes the  HR pathway the dominant pathway. 
 
DNA Damage is a constant threat to all living cells at all times. The consequences for unrepaired 
DNA are, among other, mutations, loss of function and in higher eukaryotes the development 
of cancer. DNA damage can be induced by several factors, both exogenous and endogenous. 
A well-known cause of DNA damage is UV-radiation, an exogenous factor, capable of distorting 
the DNA double helix and create pyrimidine photodimers (Scrima et al. 2008), a situation where 
the helix partly dissociates and allows for single-stranded self-dimers. Internal factors, like high 
concentrations of reactive oxidative substances (ROS), can lead to oxidized bases, and single- 
and double-strand breaks (De Bont & van Larebeke 2004). ROS is continuously produced as 
part of the organism's metabolism, but oxidative stress occurs when the production of ROS 
exceeds the cellular levels of the antioxidant defense mechanism.     
 
It is essential to any organism to be able to overcome this challenge by repairing all kinds of 
DNA damage. For that reason, the Aspergillus has various systems for DNA repair. Regarding 
==PAGE:54==
Theory 
 
38 
UV-damage, a pathway called Nucleotide-excision repair (NER) is of particular importance. NER 
has two subpathways; the global genome NER (GG-NER) recognizes bubble structures on the 
DNA created by pyrimidine dimers, and the transcribed coupled repair (TCR) recognizes stalled 
RNA polymerases on a transcribed strand (Sugasawa et al. 2001). In the repair process of UV-
damaged DNA, an enzyme complex detects the area of the distorted DNA and cuts out a part 
of the strand, both on the 5’ side and the 3’ side of the dimer. Polymerase I can now carry out 
repair synthesis and DNA ligase can close the nicked strand at the 3’ end (Stryer 2012).  
 
DNA damage can have many different appearances, such as offending bases, and single- and 
double-stranded breaks. The NER pathway is an example of a single-strand repair system, and, 
like others e.g. mismatch- and base-excision repair, NER also uses sequence information from 
the uncompromised complementary strand. Double-stranded break (DSB), on the other hand, 
can not use the complementary strand as a template. Instead, the fungi can use one of two 
mechanisms, namely non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) or homologous recombination(HR). 
In multicellular eukaryotes (e.g. mammals and Aspergillus), NHEJ is the dominant pathway of 
DSB repair (Zhang et al. 2011). Two unique genes engage in the NHEJ pathway in filamentous 
fungi, namely ku70 and ku80. The genes encode for two DNA binding proteins that can bind 
the ends of double-stranded DNA breaks in a non-specific manner (Krappmann 2007). When 
bound, the ku70 and ku80 proteins recruit a complex of DNA repair proteins, including an MRX 
complex (Rad50-Mre11-Xrs2) and a ligase complex. The MRX complex binds adjacent to the 
ku70 and ku80 proteins on both sides of the DSB and connects the ends in a bridge-like domain. 
The ligase is responsible for ligating the two ends together, and the final step is the release of 
the repair complex. The deletion of the Ku proteins encoded genes has proved to increase the 
HR pathway significantly in filamentous fungi. Compared with both a wild-type and a Ku 
deletion mutant, the integration of exogenous DNA fragments increases rapidly in the mutant 
Ku strain. The technique is used for comprehensive gene functional studies that rely on the HR 
==PAGE:55==
Theory 
 
39 
pathway for integration of selectable gene markers in a gene locus of interest (Krappmann 
2007). 
 
The HR pathway requires a DNA template with homology on both sides of the DSB for a correct 
repair. The MRX complex recognizes the DSB and the Mre11 perform a 5’ to 3’ resection of the 
double-stranded DNA (Goldman & Kafer 2004). The recombination step occurs with strand 
invasion in which the single-stranded DNA end invades and displaces the intact DNA repair 
template, invading one strand and displacing the other. The consequence is the formation of 
a D-loop. The 3’ end, which now is paired to a contiguous strand of DNA, can function as a 
primer for a DNA synthase. The synthesis from the 3’ end and the strand invasion creates a 
Holliday junction between the template strand and the broken strand. Cleavage of the junction 
results in an intact DNA relative to the template DNA (Stryer 2012). The final step is the ligation 
of the nicked ends and release of the protein complex (figure 18).

The major part of the present project was based on gene deletion data generated by

Cloning, in general, is the creation of identical genetic individuals within a population. In 
molecular biology, cloning is the creation of recombinant DNA molecules resulting in new 
genetic constellations (Tirabassi Rebecca 1978).  
 
Uracil-specific excision reagent (USER) is a method for creating PCR fragments with single-
stranded overhangs for easy insertion in a vector. The USER-technique is based on a 
polymerase that has reduced stalling in DNA with uracil present in the sequence. A polymerase 
will generally stop or stall at sites with uracil, but a DNA polymerase from the archaea 
Thermococcus gorgonarius have the ability to oversee the foreign nucleotide and continue the 
DNA synthesis. The design of a recombinant Tgo- and Pfu-polymerase with reduced stalling at 
uracil-containing DNA improved the USER cloning technique and made it a highly useful 
method (Nørholm 2010). The primers needed for the method consists of two distinct parts, a 
USER overhang design to complement insertion in a given vector, and a regular primer 
sequence for the amplification of the fragment. Each primer contains an extension with a 
deoxyuridine that leaves a 3’ single stranded overhang when the resulting PCR product is 
treated with the USER enzyme mix. The enzyme solution contains a Uracil DNA glycosylase and 
a DNA glycosylase-lyase Endo VIII. The Uracil glycosylase anneals to the uracil nucleobase in 
the primer part of the amplified PCR fragment and creates an abasic position. The DNA 
glycosylase-lyase Endo VIII recognize the abasic site and initiates a single-stranded break by 
disrupting the phosphodiester backbone, both at the 5’ and the 3’ of the abasic position and 
leaves an overhang (Frandsen et al. 2008).  
 
The goal is to construct a vector for gene deletion by assembling a vector with two homologous 
sequences corresponding to the bordering regions of a gene of interest, flanking a selectable 
marker (Frandsen et al. 2008). A PacI and Nt.BbvCl endonuclease digestion step of a suitable 
vector before mixture with the PCR overhang fragments arrange for the correct annealing

transformation in E.coli without the utilization of a ligase.

CRISPR-Cas9 system  
The newest tool for genetic engineering has recently become available in filamentous fungi, 
namely the CRISPR-Cas9 system. The strain ACU11 was genetically altered to be NHEJ deficient 
by deletion of the akuA gene using the CRISPR-cas9 (Andersen 2016). The strain is also pyrG 
deficient, and it is, therefore, the gene deletion template in this project. 
 
Originally the CRISPR system was found as an immune mechanism in bacteria and archaea for 
fighting off invading viruses. The system has later been developed into a powerful genetic 
editing tool for a variety of organisms. The CRISPR-Cas9 system induces, in its simplest form, a 
DSB to a DNA sequence of interest by applying two components, i.e. the single chimeric guide 
==PAGE:62==
Theory 
 
46 
RNA (sgRNA) and the Cas9 nuclease. The accurate targeting of the gene of interest is conducted 
by the protospacer sequence of the sgRNA. The protospacer is the editable part of the system 
and consists of 20 nucleotides complementary to the genomic target (figure 22). The CRISPR-
Cas9 system has one limitation regarding the position of the protospacer, an adjacent motif in 
the DNA sequence, i.e. the PAM (Protospacer Adjacent Motif). For the Streptococcus pyogenes 
cas9, commonly used, the motif NGG allows for almost every gene position to be targeted. 
After the protospacer has located the correct sequence, the sgRNA-guided Cas9 nuclease 
induces a DSB at the exact cut site (Nødvig et al. 2015).

The metabolic profile of the reference strain and mutant strains were chemically analyzed 
using liquid chromatography-diode array detection mass spectrometry (LC-DAD-MS). This 
section will give an overview of the analytical technology of the equipment used to obtain the 
results for the interpretation of the data in the investigation of the biosynthetic pathways. A 
basic setup of the whole system can be seen in figure 23, each part of the tripartite system will 
be described in individual sections.

The first part of the system is illustrated with a binary solvent LC system. The liquid chromatograph (LC), consists of an injector

Liquid Chromatography (LC) 
In an LC-DAD-MS analysis, separation of the individual compounds according to their chemical 
properties is essential prior to the detection by the DAD and the MS. Polarity, weight, and 
==PAGE:64==
Theory 
 
48 
functional  groups of the compounds influence the retention time in the separation column, 
thus, physically retaining some compounds while others pass through more quickly. The 
separation column is the systems stationary phase; here small-scale chemically coated silica 
beads interacts with the compounds dissolved in the mobile phase. A high compound affinity 
towards the stationary phase slows down the elution. In a binary system, the mobile phase is 
a mixture of two solvents, which is controlled by a system of pumps allowing for a constant 
concentration, or a gradient where the mobile phase composition is changed linearly over 
time.  
 
The LC system can be divided into three system types; The reverse phase LC, the normal phase 
LC and hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC). The reverse is the most common and 
will only be described here. Reverse-phase (RP-LC) is the favored technique for separation and 
analysis of fungal secondary metabolites (Meyer 2013). The stationary phase in this system is 
commonly comprised of C18 chain coated beads, which generates an apolar environment in 
the column (Shalliker & Kayillo 2005). The mobile phase is polar and comprised of solvents such 
as water, acetonitrile, methanol, or a mixture of two. The polar solvent will dissolve the polar 
compounds keeping them from interacting with the stationary phase, thereby allowing them 
to elute faster. The most apolar compounds will be attracted to the apolar stationary phase 
with a high affinity and be eluted later. Thus, a high retention time will equal a large apolar 
compound and vice visa for short retention times. 
Diode Array Detection (DAD) 
The Diode Array Detection (DAD) system provides information about compounds conjugated 
double bonds, also known as chromophores. By emitting light through a sample in the UV 
spectrum (180-350 nm) and visual spectrum (up to 800 nm), the π-electrons of the 
chromophores absorb light relative to the compounds conjugation degree and structure. The 
light that passes through the sample is detected on a photodiode array panel and recorded for 
==PAGE:65==
Theory 
 
49 
inspection. The DAD procedure is non-destructure towards the compound. Thus, can be placed 
after the separation column and before the MS. All compounds have a unique absorption 
pattern, and the DAD system can identify known compounds by comparing the results with a 
database search (Nielsen & Larsen 2015; David et al. 2000).  
Mass Spectrometry (MS) 
The technique can analyze the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of compounds and in that also the 
essential element composition. The accurate measurement can give a highly qualified 
assumption of the empirical formula. The main application is the formation of gas-phase ions 
that can be isolated electrically based on mass and charge. An equal amount of energy is 
applied to each ionized molecule and depending on the velocity of the particle the weight can 
be calculated (i.e. E = ½mV2) (El-Aneed et al. 2009). 
 
The equipment consists of three main sections: the ionization source, the mass analyzer, and 
the mass detector. In a traditional method, the ion source bombards the vaporized compounds 
with a beam of electrons leaving the analysts with a charge. The vaporization of the compounds 
is usually done through an electrospray. Here an electric current is applied to the solvent liquid 
in which the compounds are dissolved. Aerosols are created when the mixture is passed 
through a thin needle into a warm neutral gas (e.g. N2). The result is a fine spray of electrically 
charged droplets.  
When all compounds are electrically charged, separated from the solvent, and in the gas-
phase, the sample can enter the mass analyzer. From here, the ions are separated based on 
their m/z values before they reach the detector. There are numerous systems to detect and 
isolate ions relative to their m/z values. Two main systems are the quadrupole analyzer and 
the time-of-flight (ToF) analyzer, which can be combined in a hybrid instrument to a Q-ToF. 
The quadrupole part of the system is composed of four parallel electrical rods. Two of the rods 
have a direct current (DC) potential applied, while the other two are creating an alternating 
==PAGE:66==
Theory 
 
50 
radio-frequency (RF). The vaporized ions are pulsed forward in the quadrupole, and the 
alternating frequency will apply an oscillating trajectory to the charged particles. Only ions 
within a given range of m/z values will pass the quadrupole and be able to continue to a 
secondary quadrupole for fragmentation, or directly to the ToF. In the ToF-part of the 
instrument, the ions enter free flight in a tube of 1-2 meters in length. The technique relies on 
the fact that all the ions are formed at the same time and with the same charge applied. When 
the ions enter the free flight, only the mass can variate the velocity before they collide with 
the detector. A computer can then calculate the mass based on the time for the ions to reach 
the detector. Figure 24 illustrates a schematic of both a quadrupole and a time-of-flight mass 
analyzer.
 
Figure 24. A Q-ToF mass spectrometer.  
The sample is ionized and enters the gas-phase at the ion source. From here it goes through the quadrupole before entering 
the TOF.    
 
The ions generated by the ESI can be [M+H]+, [M+Na]+, [M+K]+ and [M+NH4]+. When they 
collide with the analyzer, a distinct pattern emerges called an adduct pattern. Fragments due 
to bond breakage in a molecule do not create a similar pattern. Thus an adduct pattern can 
prove the presence of the original compound.

 Transformation of Aspergillus aculeatus 
The project had various problems in both the protoplastation and transformation of the 
Aspergillus aculeatus pyrG1 akuA∆ Acu11 mutant strain. Low transformation frequency in the 
akuA∆ strain and a high content of cell debris in the protoplast solution were some of the 
problems encountered. The methods were tweaked throughout the project, and the following 
protocols are the most sufficient of the many protoplastations and transformations conducted 
in the project.  
Protoplastation protocol 
The Aspergillus aculeatus pyrG1 akuA∆ Acu11 or the pyrG1 Acu9 mutant strains were grown 
on solid minimal medium with supplemented uridine as three-point stabs at 30˚C for 4-5 days 
before harvest. The conidium was removed from the plate with a Drigalsky spatula and 2-3 ml 
YPD medium. The mixture was transferred to a shake flask containing 100 ml YPD medium and 
incubated at 30˚C for 18-24 hours, at a 150 rpm rotation. 
On day 2, the mycelium was harvested from the shake flask by filtration through a sterile 
Miracloth and subsequently washed with sterile Milli-Q water. The remaining biomass retained 
in the Miracloth was transferred to a sterile 50 ml Falcon™ and by filter sterilization (45 µm) 
20 ml Glucanex dissolved in protoplastation buffer (40 mg/ml) was added. To digest the fungal 
cell walls, and subsequently expose the protoplasts, the mixture was incubated at 30˚C for 3 
hours, at a 150 rpm rotation. After the digestion step, the mycelia mixture was filtrated through 
a sterile Miracloth and the filtrate was centrifuged at 1200 g for 10 minutes. The supernatant 
was discarded, and the pellet was resuspended in 25 ml ST buffer. The procedure was repeated 
two times and in the final step, the ST buffer was discarded. The protoplast pellet was 
resuspended in approximately 2 ml STC buffer for a final concentration of 1.2x107 cells/ml. 
Additionally, PCT buffer was added to the protoplast solution for an approximately 20 % PEG 
concentration before the protoplasts were freshly used in transformation. No protoplasts were 
stored.

The acucalbistrin A pathway in Aspergillus aculeatus 
Calbistrin A is a polyketide derived compound. The composition can be divided into a linear 
twelve carbon PK doic acid backbone and a fourteen ring-structured carbon PK backbone. Both 
PKs have additional -CH3 groups added, as can be seen in figure 26. The PKs are linked together 
by an ester bond.

Domain search of the transcription factor regulatory protein 
A domain search of the transcription factor (JGI ID: 119191) gene nucleotide sequence 
revealed similarities to a Gal4-like Zn2Cys6 transcription factor, which is unique to fungi. The 
transcription factor of this kind has been reported to be involved in different regulatory 
functions (Zhao et al. 2011). The regulatory proteins can bind as dimers to DNA motif 5’-CGG-
3’ sequences and the protein recognition differ only by the distinct numbers of base pairs 
between the sequences (Rgt et al. 2003). The fact that the transcription factor (JGI ID: 119191) 
belongs to a more general DNA binding motif class could indicate that it has the ability to bind 
several places in the genome. Thereby being able to affect more than just the local synthase 
encoding gene (JGI ID: 43247). 
Conclusion for compound x  
The primary aim of the investigation was to establish a strain and prove the overexpression of 
the compound x, which later on will be used for a compound purification. It was possible to 
incorporate a constitutive promoter in control of the transcription factor and thereby produce 
significantly higher amounts of compound x, but, only when the strain was grown on solid MM. 
All production of compound x ceased when the strains were cultured on solid YES medium, 
despite overexpression of the transcription factor. Additional activation of other repressors 
with the ability to repress the transcription of the synthase gene (JGI ID:43247), when grown 
on solid YES medium, could be the reason for the terminated production of compound x. 
 
The transcription factor also showed tendencies to downregulate at least a handful of other 
compounds, meaning that it could have both a local activating effect on the synthase gene (JGI 
ID: 43247) and a global effect as a repressor of several other synthase encoding genes.

The acurin pathway in Aspergillus aculeatus 
Acurin is a PK-NRP derived compound consisting of a trimethylated heptaketide fused to a 
serine residue. The compound has two stereocenters and can be seen as two isomers (figure 
42) indicated by a doublet peak (1) both containing the same mass eluted with a little 
discrepancy in the retention time. The serine residue fused with the PK part is highly likely to 
be L – serine, thus in practice, only leaving acurin with one stereocenter. The two isomers, 
acurin A + B, were first described by Dorte K. Holm and Lene M. Petersen in 2013. The discovery 
came as a result of an overexpression of a transcription factor which was initially believed to 
be related to the 6-MSA gene cluster (Holm 2013). Acurin A was purified and investigated for 
bioactivity against cancer cells, but the compound showed no effect, and no toxicity test has 
yet been conducted so far. In 2016, Lasse Norup Andersen found the gene encoding the PKS 
involved in the biosynthesis of the acurin core structure through a series of gene deletions in 
the pursuit of identifying the PKS connected to calbistrin A (Andersen 2016). The gene 
encoding the acurin PKS is embedded in a gene cluster together with a NRPS-like gene. Deletion 
of the acurin PKS- and the nearby NRPS-like encoded genes both resulted in the termination 
of the acurin production. Additional genes in proximity to the two synthase encoding genes 
were analyzed for putative function in a conserved domain search. From here a suggested 
pathway for the compound was proposed (Andersen 2016). The gene cluster was suggested to 
contain twelve genes (figure 41).

The genes JGI ID: 48 and 122295 were both deleted and showed termination of the acurin production. The remaining genes 
were analyzed for putative functions using bioinformatics. [The figure is was provided (Andersen 2016)].

A methyltransferase (JGI ID: 122283) is responsible for the final step in acurin biosynthesis 
Deletion of the putative methyltransferase (JGI ID: 122283) resulted in the termination of 
acurin production. In correspondence to the suggested pathway, the -COOH intermediate with 
the mass = 360.1805 Da appeared in high intensities as a result of the missing 
methyltransferase in the 122283∆ strain (Acu35) (figure 46). In contrast, the same -COOH 
intermediate was not detectable in the reference strain. The gene deletion created a blockage 
in the biosynthetic pathway and stopped at the -COOH intermediate as the new end-product.  
 
Figure 46 shows the terminated production of acurin (2) compared to the reference strain. The 
-COOH intermediate was only detectable in the 122283∆ strain (1). Further investigation of the 
UV-chromatogram revealed that the 122283∆ strain had the expected UV-profile similar to the 
pattern of acurin with a maximum absorption at 362 nm. In comparison, the maximum 
absorption for acurin is 366 nm, see figure 42. The removal of a methyl group does not change 
the number of chromophores in the structure, and the UV-chromatogram is expected to be 
similar to the original pattern of acurin since the two compounds have the same 
chromophores. The adduct pattern of the -COOH intermediate indicates the presence of the 
expected compound and not a fragment from a larger compound. The retention time of the -
COOH intermediate further supports the data, the product was expected to be more polar and 
therefore have a lower retention time. This can be observed in figure 46.   
 
In addition to the gene expression levels from gene JGI ID: 122283 in response to 
overexpression of the transcription factor, the 122283∆ strain (Acu35) confirmed a direct 
involvement in the acurin pathway by the termination of acurin production. Furthermore, the

Data indicated the involvement of three genes in the oxidation of a methyl group 
The oxidation of the -CH3 (figure 49) to the -COOH intermediate was suggested by Lasse Norup 
Andersen (Andersen 2016) to be supported by the CYP450 monooxygenase (JGI ID: 45003). In 
the present project, I propose the oxidation is due to the enzymatic activity related to three 
genes. Two of the genes were deleted and showed definite involvement in the acurin pathway. 
The additional new suggested gene is based on gene expression data and bioinformatics.   
 
The two genes deleted from the acurin gene cluster related to the particular oxidation was a 
putative CYP450 monooxygenase (strain 45003∆ (Acu36)) and a putative aldehyde 
dehydrogenase (strain 1903547∆ (Acu38)). Both strains showed a terminated production of 
acurin, which strongly indicated the participation in the biosynthetic pathway for these 
enzymes. The LC data from both strains showed no detectable masses of any theoretical acurin 
precursors (Figure 47).

Overexpression of the embedded transcription factor yields a highly morphological strain  
The overexpression of gene JGI ID: 122290 changed the morphology of Aspergillus aculeatus 
(figure 53). Gene expression data and the chemical profile (figure 43) suggests that the gene 
encoding the transcription factor could have a global effect, with both activating and 
repressing behavior. A conserved-domain search through NCBI with the gene (JGI ID: 122290) 
sequence as input pointed towards similarities with a GAL4-like Zn(II)2cys6 binuclear cluster 
DNA-binding domain protein. A regulatory protein is known to activate transcription of genes 
required for the breakdown of galactose (Marmorstein et al. 1992). The GAL4-like Zn(II)2cys6 
transcription factors has exclusively been found in fungi, and it has shown to bind trinucleotide 
sequences in both single and in repeat form. This allows for multiple binding sites in the fungal 
genome (MacPherson et al. 2006). The similarities between the GAL4-like Zn(II)2cys6 
transcription factor and JGI ID: 122290, accommodated with the gene expression data, the 
chemical profile, and the visual morphology of the strain strongly indicates a global effect of 
gene JGI ID: 122290.

Conclusion for the acurin biosynthetic pathway 
Gene expression level data revealed activation of more genes than needed for the biosynthesis 
of acurin. The chemical profile and the morphological changes in the overexpression strain 
(Acu41) pointed towards a global acting transcription factor. Further, bioinformatics revealed 
similarities with a Gal4-like regulatory protein known to participate in different regulatory 
functions. The findings strongly indicated a global acting transcription factor with properties 
both as an activator and a repressor. 
 
Five genes were deleted from the gene cluster. Gene JGI ID: 122294 showed no involvement 
in the pathway of acurin. Aspergillus aculeatus was unaffected in the production of acurin, and 
the gene expression data supported the findings with a weak response to the overexpression 
of the proximity transcription factor. Gene JGI ID: 122283 strongly indicated to be responsible 
for an O-methylation in the final step of the biosynthesis of acurin. The expected -COOH 
intermediate was detectable in the 122283∆ strain. Three genes were suggested to participate 
in the oxidation of a -CH3 group to the -COOH intermediate needed for the final step of the 
biosynthesis of acurin. Two of the genes were deleted in the present project, i.e. JGI ID: 45003 
and 1903547; both showed a terminated production of acurin. This strongly indicated the 
participation of enzymes in the biosynthetic pathway of acurin. Gene deletion strain 45003∆ 
had a detectable -OH intermediate as expected from the suggested pathway. Strain 1903547∆ 
had no detectable intermediates corresponding to the pathway. The enzymes suggested 
activity, the oxidation of an -COH group is based on bioinformatics.  In addition to the oxidation 
of the -CH3 group, the gene JGI ID: 62004 was suggested to be responsible for the first step to 
yield a -OH group. Deletion of the putative oxidoreductase encoding gene JGI ID: 122285 also 
terminated the production of acurin. No intermediates corresponding to the pathway were 
detectable in the ∆-strain, and the specific participation in the reduction of a double bond was 
based on bioinformatics.

Further perspective for the acurin biosynthetic pathway 
For future experiments, deletion of the genes encoding JGI ID: 1903457 and 122285 could be 
performed in the O.E. strain (Acu41). The overexpression of gene JGI ID: 12290 led to a higher 
production of acurin, and deletion of the two genes in the O.E strain could likewise yield a 
higher concentration of the intermediates, thus making them detectable in the LC-MS data 
analysis. 
 
Deletion of the gene JGI ID: 62004 is needed to prove the hypothesis of the oxidation of the -
CH3 group to yield the -OH intermediate. Also, deletion of gene JGI ID: 61993 could serve as 
proof for the cyclization mechanism and participation in the biosynthetic pathway of acurin in 
general.  
 
The transcription factor JGI ID: 122290 showed global properties and a search for compounds 
affected by the overexpression could reveal additional novel secondary metabolites.
