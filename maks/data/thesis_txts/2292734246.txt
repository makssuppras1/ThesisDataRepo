Summary
Throughout the thesis, a numeric procedure is developed for determining the
pressure distribution on any locally-reacting absorber of ﬁnite size mounted in
an inﬁnite baﬄe. The thesis takes its starting point in Thomasson’s theories
from the 1980’s that are solved analytically for simple cases, after which an
extensive numeric foundation is built. The Jacobi family of polynomials are
extensively explained and their properties for integration and diﬀerentiation is
outlined as well.
Afterwards, diﬀerent methods for approximating solutions
to partial diﬀerential equations are described, including Collocation, Tau and
Galerkin. In order to fully understand the methods for discretizing Thomasson’s
problem, an extensive explanation of the spectral element method is given and
exempliﬁed by modelling an acoustic impedance tube. These methods are then
tailored and applied in the eﬀort of solving Thomasson’s problem numerically.
Finally, a program is written which determines the pressure distribution on any
plain locally-reacting absorbing surface. The program is based on the scheme of
a spectral element method. The results are compared to reference cases found
in Thomasson’s paper from 1982.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at DTU Electrical Engineering in the Acoustic Tech-
nology group in fulﬁlment of the requirements for acquiring an M.Sc. in Engi-
neering.
The thesis deals with numerical computations of absorbers with ﬁnite geometry
using variational formulations. It is important to know how size inﬂuences the
pressure distribution since this aﬀects the impedance and thus the absorption
coeﬃcient of a given surface. It is not possible to fully optimize a room to its
purpose unless absorption coeﬃcients can be determined exactly. Anyone who
has ever been occupied with room acoustics knows that it is hard to determine
coeﬃcients of the diﬀerent materials in a room. This thesis might aid people
who are interested in analysing the pressure distribution on absorbers with ﬁ-
nite geometry. The developed numerical method is based on an arbitrary-order
spectral element method, that can be thought of as a high-order extension of the
classical ﬁnite element method although the numerical implementation diﬀers
greatly.
The spectral theories presented in Chapter 2 are based on the slides from Allan
P. Engsig-Karup’s course "02689 Advanced Numerical Solutions of Diﬀerential
Equations" and on the books written by Canuto et al., 2006, [1] and Kopriva’s
book [2] from 2009.
The quadrilateral mesh in Section 2.7.2 is included to
prepare for implementations of the possible change of elements. The Chebyshev
polynomials described in Section 2.1.2 are included, as the developed program
is also able to approximate solutions on this basis. Originally, it was intended
to solve Thomasson’s problem with diﬀerent approaches. However, time only
allowed for the implementation of a method similar to Galerkin’s method. This
is also why the Tau and the Collocation methods are described and explored in

Section 2.5.
The high-order approach was chosen due to its potential for great accuracy and
low time consumption, which is highly relevant in acoustic domains, due to the
critical relation between wavelength and frequency.
Throughout the thesis, "Green’s function" refers to the free ﬁeld Green’s func-
tion.

Chapter 1
Introduction
An absorber is an element that absorbs energy in the form of a pressure change
or a phase change. It can be anything from open-celled foam to a box with
a hole.
Absorbers also introduce diﬀuseness to a room and is thus of great
importance.
Designers and architects are embracing the innovative diﬀusive
shapes into their projects, increasing the demand for theory about absorbers
with arbitrary geometry for design and room acoustic optimization purposes.
This thesis will cover how to determine theoretical absorption coeﬃcients for
locally-reacting porous absorbers of ﬁnite arbitrary geometry using a spectral
element-like procedure based on the variational principle.
1.1
Porous Absorption
Sound absorbers are thought of as elements that lower or absorb sound levels and
reverberation times in rooms. They are simple objects used everywhere, but the
way they work is a rather complicated matter. They consist of material where
sound propagation occurs in a network of interconnected pores in such a way that
viscous and thermal eﬀects cause acoustic energy to be dissipated, primarily due
to viscous boundary layer eﬀects. For the absorption to be eﬀective there must
==PAGE:19==
Introduction
2
be interconnected air paths through the material. Typically, one distinguishes
between open or closed cell structure like illustrated on Figure 1.1 [9].
(a) Closed cell structure.
(b) Open cell structure.
Figure 1.1: The diﬀerence between materials with open and closed pores.
Nowadays, absorption coeﬃcients are typically determined in an impedance
tube or in a reverberation chamber. The diﬀerent methods have contrasting
characteristics.
In the former of the two mentioned methods, one examines the attenuation
of plane waves reﬂected by the examined material at two observation points,
depending on the preferences of the investigation.
This method only treats
normal incidence. With a little help from Paris’ law [10], however, the absorption
coeﬃcient of normal incidence can be converted to the absorption coeﬃcient of
random incidence. The procedure is described in ISO 10543-2.
The second method is a time-dependent measurement and requires a big diﬀuse
room with a long reverberation time. In this case, one observes how fast the
sound pressure decreases in empty conditions relative to the case where the
absorber is present. This method is described in ISO 354.
Both methods have advantages and disadvantages, none of which will be dis-
cussed. More interesting are the ways to predict the absorption coeﬃcients of
porous material.
Nowadays, most absorption coeﬃcients are determined based on a material ﬂow
resistivity and the thickness of the material. However, all models assume the
absorber to be of inﬁnite size, which is rarely the case. This project will cover
a numerical procedure based on Thomassons theories [11][8], determining ab-
sorption coeﬃcients of absorbers of ﬁnite and arbitrary geometry by using the
variational formulation described by Morese and Ingard [6] in a spectral element-
like procedure described by Engsig-Karup [4].

• Galerkin: Select or construct a set of basis functions which all satisfy

and introduce constraints to enforce boundary conditions.
• Collocation: Require that the expansion coeﬃcients are selected to en-
force boundary conditions and that the residual is zero at as many spatial
points as possible. Enforce both diﬀerential equations and constraints at
every node. Choose basis functions conveniently.

2.7
Introducing the Spectral Element Method
On a general note: Henceforth, N deﬁnes the number of elements, while MP
deﬁnes the total number of nodes in one element and P denotes the order of the
basis functions. Also, this section is based on [4].
The general idea of ﬁnite element is to approximate solutions to boundary value
problems for partial diﬀerential equations.
It divides a whole problem into
elements, hence the name, and uses a number of methods from calculus to solve
the problems. Diﬀerent approaches can be used. This part covers the Galerkin
method. Basically, the equations are solved on each element after which they are
combined to obtain the solution of the whole system. The procedure is outlined
as
==PAGE:53==
Building a Numeric Foundation
36
1. Write the variational or weak formulation of the equations governing the
problem.
2. Use Gmesh [12] to discretize the geometry.
3. Make nodal approximation functions of the exact function for each sub-
domain.
4. Calculate elementary matrices by evaluating the approximation of each
sub-domain.
5. Assemble the elementary matrices to form the global matrices which de-
scribe the whole domain and enforce the continuity condition between
elements.
6. Impose appropriate boundary conditions in correspondence with the model.
7. Compute the solution by solving the system of algebraic equations.
8. Post-process computed results.

3.1
Spectral Element Procedure
The goal of this section is to arrive at a ﬁnal expression which can be imple-
mented in a numeric procedure that solves Thomasson’s problem for any geom-
etry. The problem can to some extend be thought of like the spectral element
problem in Section 2.7. The strategy is thus outlined as
1. Write the variational formulation of the equations governing the problem.
2. Use Gmesh [12] to discretize the geometry.
3. Import and rewrite results for further Matlab procedures.
4. Make nodal approximation functions pe of the exact function p for each
sub-domain Ωe.
5. Calculate elementary matrices by evaluating the approximation of the in-
tegral form on each sub-domain Ωe.
6. Assemble the elementary matrices to form the global matrix which de-
scribes the whole domain Ω.
7. Compute the solution by solving the system of algebraic equations.
8. Post-process computed results.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
Throughout the thesis, a numeric spectral procedure was developed for deter-
mining the pressure distribution on any locally-reacting absorber of ﬁnite size
mounted in an inﬁnite baﬄe.
Initially, the wave-equation was derived, followed by the Helmholtz equation,
after which Thomasson’s problem was ﬁnally derived and analytically solved by
use of the variational principle.
Afterwards, the numeric chapter described how Jacobi polynomials can be used
to do spectral approximations of solutions to various problems. The Legendre-,
Chebyshev- and Lagrange polynomials were thoroughly explained. Next, map-
ping was discussed in order to understand how to bend and solve general prob-
lems for various arbitrary domains. Afterwards, the collocation method was
described and used to solve the Helmholtz equation with Direchlet boundary
conditions for a simple non-square domain consisting of one element.
The
method was tested and veriﬁed by using the Method of Manufactured Solu-
tions. Then, the Tau method was derived for the same problem, though only
in a one-dimensional domain. The method was not tested, as it would be fairly
time consuming to implement.
Afterwards, a section was dedicated to getting familiar with the spectral ele-
ment method. It was described how to create a mesh and how to derive the
==PAGE:106==
Conclusion
89
weak formulation in order to implement an approximated solution based on the
Galerkin method, which was very similar to the variational principle. A spectral
element program was created for solving the Helmholtz equation on the same
domain used in the collocation method, though split up into triangular sub-
domains, making it a spectral element method. The program was, again, tested
and veriﬁed according to the manufactured solution. It was also clear that the
spectral element method was faster and more accurate than the regular ﬁnite
element approach, which is based on linear basis functions.
Then, the program was tested by modelling an impedance tube. The impedance
tube was also created in Comsol and the results compared. The spectral ele-
ment program was better at estimating the absorption coeﬃcient for normal
incidence than Comsol (around three times more accurate), although Comsol’s
absorption coeﬃcient for random incidence was more accurate (around dou-
ble the accuracy). The point of the model was to test whether the program
could compete with a professional piece of ﬁnite element machinery and to test
the validity of the implemented theories. The conclusion is that the program
is competitive and that the implemented theories are performing as expected.
Unfortunately, there was not enough time to cost-optimize and test the program
against Comsol, but in theory it should be both faster and more accurate.
Finally, Thomasson’s problem was discretized by use of the variational princi-
ple and deﬁning a basis much like the one used in the Galerkin method. The
program is not a traditional ﬁnite element model, neither is it a traditional
boundary element model. While it employs the bookkeeping of the spectral ele-
ment method, the program is based on general numeric procedures not directly
related to the classic spectral element model. This is mainly due to the presence
of the Green’s function, which is a kernel. These types of problems are more
often found in boundary element models, thus making this procedure diﬀerent
from other classic and standardized numeric methods.
Eventually, the pressure was computed on a locally-reacting absorbing area. The
program was tested by comparing the results with previously determined analyt-
ical results created by another student at DTU. The results of the comparison
was inconclusive. The pressure determined by the numeric procedure varied
somewhat diﬀerently than the pressure determined by the analytic approach,
although there were some similarities. The pressure was of the same average
magnitude, and the pressure distributions were somewhat similar. When the
directionality was examined, it was discovered that the basis was not deﬁned
correctly. It was changed, and the results were reproduced. This had a positive
impact on the computed pressure distributions, though it did not ﬁx all of the
issues.
At last, the absorption coeﬃcient was calculated based on the pressure distri-

Thus, it can be concluded that the program is not complete.

function [Ghat] = funGnm(x,y,x0,y0,k,Mp)

function [Pabs] = integrateP(C,Mstore,p,Ntot,Mp,A_z)
% Written by Jesper Bjerregaard
Pabs = 0;
for n = 1:Ntot
ptemp = real((p(C(n,:))).*conj(A_z*p(C(n,:))));
Mtemp = Mstore((n-1)*Mp+1:Mp*n,:);
Pabs = Pabs + sum(sum(Mtemp*ptemp));
