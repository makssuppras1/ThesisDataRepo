Abstract
In the last years, the European countries have paid increasing attention to re-
newable sources and greenhouse emissions. The Council of the European Union
and the European Parliament have established ambitious targets for the next
years. In this scenario, biomass plays a prominent role since its life cycle pro-
duces a zero net carbon dioxide emission.
Additionally, biomass can ensure
plant operation continuity thanks to its availability and storage ability. Several
conventional systems running on biomass are available at the moment. Most of
them are performant either in the large-scale or in the small power range. The
absence of a high ecient system on the small-middle scale inspired this thesis
project.
The object is an innovative plant based on a wet indirectly red gas turbine
(WIFGT) integrated with an organic Rankine cycle (ORC) unit for combined
heat and power production. The WIFGT is a performant system in the small-
middle power range; the ORC cycle is capable of giving value to low-temperature
heat sources. Their integration is investigated in this thesis with the aim of car-
rying out a preliminary design of the components. The targeted plant output
is around 200 kW in order not to need a wide cultivation area and to avoid
biomass shipping.
Existing in-house simulation tools are used: they are adapted to this purpose.
Firstly the WIFGT + ORC model is built; zero-dimensional models of heat
exchangers, compressor, turbines, furnace, dryer and pump are used. Dierent
uids are selected but toluene and benzene turn out to be the most suitable. In
the indirectly red gas turbine a pressure ratio around 4 leads to the highest
eciency. From the thermodynamic analysis the system shows an electric e-
ciency of 38%, outdoing other conventional plants in the same power range. The
combined plant is designed to recover thermal energy: water is used as coolant

in the condenser. It is heated from 60 ◦C up to 90 ◦C, ensuring the possibility
of space heating.
Mono-dimensional models are used to design the heat exchange equipment. Dif-
ferent types of heat exchangers are chosen depending on the working temper-
ature. A nned-plate heat exchanger is selected for the WIFGT heat transfer
equipment due to the high temperature, oxidizing and corrosive environment. A
once-through boiler with nned tubes is chosen to vaporize the organic uid in
the ORC. A plate heat exchanger is chosen for the condenser and recuperator.
A quasi-monodimensional model for single-stage axial turbine is implemented
to design both the WIFGT and the ORC turbine.
The system simulation after the components design shows an electric eciency
around 34% with a decrease by 10% compared to the zero-dimensional analysis.
The work exhibits the system potentiality compared to the existing plants from
both technical and economic point of view.

Sommario
Gli Stati membri dell'Unione Europea negli ultimi anni hanno posto grande at-
tenzione alle risorse rinnovabili ed alle emissioni serra. Il Consiglio dell'Unione
Europea ed il Parlamento Europeo hanno ssato importanti obiettivi per gli
anni a venire. In questo scenario la biomassa occupa una posizione di primo
piano tra le risorse rinnovabili. La quantità netta di biossido di carbonio emessa
durante il suo ciclo di vita è pressoché nulla; inoltre il suo facile approvvigiona-
mento ed il successivo stoccaggio rendono la biomassa una fonte programmabile,
che garantisce piena continuità operativa.
Oggigiorno molteplici sistemi che utilizzano biomassa sono disponibili nel mer-
cato, tuttavia la maggior parte di questi risulta performante solo se di piccola
o di grande taglia. L'assenza di un sistema eciente di potenza medio-bassa ha
ideato questo progetto di tesi.
L'oggetto principale del lavoro è un impianto innovativo costituito da una turbina
a gas con combustione esterna (WIFGT), integrata con un ciclo Rankine organ-
ico (ORC) in assetto cogenerativo. Il sistema WIFGT risulta di per sè piuttosto
eciente a potenze medio-basse. L'unità ORC è pensata per valorizzare sor-
genti a bassa temperatura. La loro integrazione è oggetto di studio di questa
tesi, nella quale sarà sviluppato il progetto preliminare dei componenti del sis-
tema integrato. La taglia dell'impianto è stata ssata a circa 200 kW in modo
da utilizzare biomasse locali ed evitarne il trasporto.
Software pre-esistenti sono stati adattati ed utilizzati per le simulazioni. Per
prima cosa il modello dell'intero sistema WIFGT + ORC è stato costruito
utilizzando componenti zero-dimensionali: scambiatori di calore, compressore,
turbina, caldaia, dryer e pompa. Sono stati scelti diversi uidi organici ma,
tra questi, toluene e benzene sono risultati quelli che garantiscono una migliore
performance del sistema. Nella turbina a gas con combustione esterna è stato

scelto un rapporto di compressione di circa 4 per massimizzare il rendimento
elettrico dell'impianto. Da un punto di vista termodinamico, il sistema rag-
giunge un'ecienza di circa 38%, superando quella di altri sistemi della stessa
taglia. L'impianto combinato oggetto di questa tesi è pensato per recuperare
calore che altrimenti verrebbe scaricato all'ambiente attraverso il condensatore.
La presenza di acqua come refrigerante garantisce la possibilità di cogenerazione,
sottoforma di riscaldamento ed acqua calda sanitaria.
Modelli mono-dimensionali sono stati utilizzati per il progetto degli scambia-
tori di calore. A seconda della temperatura di lavoro diverse tipologie di scam-
biatori sono state scelte: scambiatore "nned-plate" per il recuperatore della
turbina con combustione esterna, "once-through boiler" con tubi alettati per
il vaporizzatore dell'ORC ed, inne, scambiatori a piastre per condensatore e
recuperatore.
Un modello quasi-monodimensionale è stato implementato per il progetto delle
turbine assiali a singolo stadio presenti nel ciclo integrato.
La simulazione dell'intero ciclo, tenendo conto del progetto dei componenti,
ha mostrato un calo di ecienza di circa 10%, passando da 38% nell'analisi
termodinamica a 34%. Questo lavoro di tesi mostra le potenzialità del sistema
innovativo in oggetto sia da un punto di vista tecnico che da quello economico.

2.1
Indirectly Fired Gas Turbine
Externally Fired Gas Turbine (EFGT) or Indirectly Fired Gas Turbine (IFGT)
consists of a gas turbine whose combustion chamber is replaced with a heat
exchanger, namely a High Temperature Heat Exchanger (HTHE). This means
that in an IFGT the combustion process takes place in a separate furnace instead
of a common combustion chamber. Figure 2.1 shows the process ow diagram
of both Indirectly and Directly Fired Gas Turbine. For what concerns IFGT,
atmospheric air enters the compressor and, once compressed, it is heated in the
HTHE up to the Turbine Inlet Temperature (TIT). It is then expanded in the
expansor; an electric generator is driven by the expansor converting mechanical
power into electrical power. Typically, air exiting the turbine is still at a quite
high temperature. Thus, this stream is used as combustion air in the furnace.
The main advantages of the external ring are:
==PAGE:36==
6
State of the art
• The possibility of burning solid and "dirty" fuels like coal and biomass [3].
• Combustion process takes place at atmospheric pressure.
This avoids
problems with fuel feeding to the furnace [4].
• Since ue gas does not pass through the IFGT turbine, any turbine blades
damage occurs [3].

2.1.1
High Temperature Heat Exchanger
The presence of a HTHE, on the other hand, is the main limitation of the
plant, regarding both design and operation. This is due to a very challenging
environment. Temperatures up to 1300 ◦C limit the choice of suitable materials.
==PAGE:37==
2.1 Indirectly Fired Gas Turbine
7
Moreover, the high temperature setting leads to an enhancement of oxidization
reaction kinetics. Nowadays, heat exchangers operating at temperatures up to
650 ◦C are made of stainless steel, commonly Type 347 (18Cr-10Ni-1Nb). This
provide excellent oxidation resistance [5]. The presence of high percentage of
chromium in the steel leads to a stable formation of chromium oxide layer that
prevent further oxidation.
A quite important problem might occur because
of thermal cycling: the formed lm detachment. Thus, the metal will not have
enough chromium to produce a new layer, losing its high temperature resistance
[6]. Since above 700 ◦C cracking of Cr2O3 may occur, special alloys have to be
used. Aquaro and Pieve in [5] classied them into two species:
• Alloys with a creep resistance comparable with 347 SS: modied Alloy
803 (25Cr-35Ni), Alloy 602 CA (25Cr-63Ni), Haynes HR120 (25Cr-32.3Ni-
2.5Mo), Haynes HR230 (22Cr-52.7Ni-2Mo).
• Alloys with a higher creep strain than 347 SS: Alloy 625 (22.2Cr-61.2Ni-
9.1Mo), Haynes HR214 (16Cr-76.5Ni), ODS Alloys.
High nickel content improves metal high temperature stability. The creep re-
sistance depends on the grain size: the coarser the size, the higher the creep
resistance is. In order to improve creep resistance, Oxide Dispersion Strength-
ened (ODS) alloys are used. Fine particles of Y2O3 and ThO2 are dispersed
in the metal matrix. Their function is to hook grains each other, improving
creep resistance in terms of creep-rupture time. Generally speaking, ODS alloys
contain 0÷5% of an oxide dispersed in the matrix [5]. Since their creep rupture
process is pretty slow, their service life is limited by the oxidation behavior. The
maximum temperature they can cope with is around 1050÷1150 ◦C [5]. Ceramic
heat exchangers could be an alternative solution to metal HTHE. Ceramic ma-
terials have a very high corrosion resistance at temperatures up to 1300 ◦C [7].
On the other side they suer many drawbacks, the most important ones have
been outlined in few points by Sunden [8]:
• Tubes of extended length are dicult to produce with uniform material
properties.
• Due to dierential thermal expansion, tube-to-tube sheet joints are di-
cult to be made.
• Thermal expansion coecient may change during the operation because
of the reaction with species present in the corrosive gas.
• The service life of ceramic tubes with corrosive gases has not been tested
yet.
==PAGE:38==
8
State of the art
• High cost of the ceramic tubes.
As a result, the manufacture diculty prevent the ceramic heat exchanger to
be spread out.
Several HTHE designs have been taken into account in past literature.
For
micro-EFGT metallic Plate Heat Exchanger (PHE) are a viable solution. They
are highly ecient and very compact but small ash particles could obstruct the
narrow channels between plates. High temperature environment can even cause
gaskets and plates damages [7].
Stainless steel shell-and-tube heat exchangers have also been used in some pilot
plants [6]. The main advantage of Shell-and-tube heat exchanger is they can
easily handle the presence of solid particles in the ue gas [9].
Furthermore, a Bayonet conguration HTHE is an interesting alternative to the
previous one. It consists of a bundle of concentric tubes in the outer shell. The
outer tubes are xed only from one side, the inner tubes are free to expand at
both sides. Air passes forth through the inner tube and back through the outer
tube; ue gas passes through the shell side [8]. This conguration results very
eective when the heat exchanger experiences a high temperature dierence
between the two uids, minimizing thermal stresses [8].
Finned-Plate geometry has also been used during the years as heat transfer
equipment in several Micro-Gas Turbine (MGT) applications [10]. A compact
Finned-Plate recuperator seems to be an interesting solution for future high
eciency HTHE [10].

With reference to Figure 2.1, the thermodynamic cycles (Temperature-Entropy
diagram) of a Directly Fired Gas Turbine (DFGT) and an IFGT are shown in

2.2
Organic Rankine Cycle
An ORC is a system that uses an external energy source converting it into me-
chanical, electrical and thermal energy. It consists basically in a Rankine cycle
where an organic uid is used as process uid instead of water. The main reason
of this choice is the possibility of low temperature source exploitation. This is
due to organic uids thermodynamic properties: they have a lower vaporization
temperature than water. For an organic uid high pressure in the evaporator
can even correspond to low temperature, and vice versa [13]. As a result they
can use any kind of external energy source. As outlined by Colonna et al. in
[13] they can especially exploit renewable low-temperature sources:
• Geothermal reservoires (steam or liquid-dominated);
• Biomass combustion;
• Solar radiation;
• Industrial waste heat recovery;
• Municipal Solid Waste (MSW) and landll gas combustion;
• Salt-gradient solar ponds [14];
• Ocean and ground temperature gradient;
• Heat recovery from any kind of topping cycle.
Furthermore, Colonna et al. in [13] underscore how dicult designing a small-
size (from few kW up to few MW), axial or radial, steam turbine would be. It is
due to small steam mass ow rate, high expansion ratio and high specic work.
On the other hand, organic uids experience small enthalpy drop during the
expansion. This ensures the volume ow rate to be larger and thus passages with
high cross ow area are needed. As a consequence the expansor design results
easier. The small enthalpy drop allows also to have low number of turbine stages
[13]. Very low speed of sound values for organic compounds bring about high
Mach number values. As a result, a supersonic ow, at least in the rst stage,
occurs.
2.2.1
ORC: thermodynamic cycle
The ORC cycle basically involves the same components as a steam Rankine
cycle. The organic uid is vaporized in a high pressure evaporator. The result-
ing organic vapor is expanded in the turbine, producing mechanical work. A
==PAGE:43==
2.2 Organic Rankine Cycle
13
condensation process closes the cycle [14]. For the majority of the organic uids
the expansion is completely dry. It means that the uid at turbine outlet state
is still superheated. Two points can be addressed:
• Erosion process by condensate droplets does not take place, resulting in
avoiding any kind of blade damage [14].
• Heat can be recovered before condensing. A recuperator is usually intro-
duced in the process at this purpose. The presence of a recuperator can
boost the cycle eciency by several percentage points. If the advantage
is quite limited, the recuperator is anyway adopted because it reduces the
condenser size [13].

expanded in an impulse axial turbine, generating mechanical work. The gained
experience with that work gave him the possibility to build a new prototype at
University of Naples and a pilot plant set in the island of Ischia (Italy). The
former produced 2.6 kW from low temperature geothermal energy and the latter
was a 11 kW geothermal ORC plant. During the 1960s several ORC plants were
installed to distribute electric power to remote areas and as engine for pumping
systems (commonly used for irrigation). These plants focused more on reliability
and service life rather than on conversion eciency, that was around 5%. Their
power ranged between 0.2 and 6 kW. In the 1970s, the oil crisis made them
increase ORC capacity range up to few MWs. Renewable energy sources like
geothermal and solar energy were mostly exploited. During the 1960s and 1970s,
Professors G. Angelino, M. Gaia and E. Macchi posed the path for the scientic
and technical basis of modern ORC plants. Their research work was used in
several later applications. In Finland, Professor Jaakko Larjola developed kW-
size turbogenerators that were commercialized from 2000 by a company in the

2.2.3
Organic uids
The choice of the organic uid is a key point for a good ORC performance.
As above explained, since ORC units are thought to exploit low temperature
sources, working uids with low boiling temperature, compared with water, have
to be used. Organic uids like refrigerants, hydrocarbons or siloxanes satisfy
this request. Organic uids can be classied about the slope of saturation vapor
curve into [15]:
• Isentropic uids if the slope is innite (saturation vapor curve is vertical);
• Dry uids if the slope is positive;
• Wet uids if the slope is negative.
The dierence about the saturation curve slope is shown in Figure 2.6. Wet
uids are generally not used for ORC systems since the negative slope of the
saturation vapor curve causes the formation of liquid droplets during the ex-
pansion. As a result, turbine blade damage can occur. Furthermore, a good

For what concerns environmental sustainability, two important indices has to
be taken into account: Ozone Depletion Potential (ODP) and Global Warming
Potential (GWP).
The ODP measures the ability of a certain chemical of catalysing the O3 de-
pletion reactions in comparison with the ability of the refrigerant R11. The
reference chemical R11 has therefore a unitary ODP. Some chemicals, the ones
with a high ODP value, contribute to accelerate the kinetics of ozone depletion
reactions with comparison to the natural reaction velocity. The equilibrium of
production/depletion is then modied and, as a result, the concentration of O3
in the ozone layer decreases. Compounds with chlorine and bromine have the
highest ODP.
The GWP measures the greenhouse ability of a certain chemical in comparison
with the ability of CO2, that has a unitary GWP. Generally speaking, it is
due to the ability of absorbing some solar radiations (at specied wave lengths)
and re-radiating with a dierent wave length, tipically in the red and infrared
spectrum. These emissions contribute to the planet global warming. Methane,
HFC (hydro uoro carbons), sulfur hexauoride (SF6) are compounds with the
highest value of GWP [17].

called Hazardous Materials Identication System (HMIS) [18]. It is based on
labels with dierent colours:
• Blue label. It represents the health hazard;
• Red label. It represents the ammability;
• Orange label. It represents the physical hazard;
• White label. It represents the personal protection.

In the last decades, a big variety of biomass energy systems has been available
in the market but only few can be considered suitable for a specic CHP case
of study. In this scenario, a comparison among dierent systems is carried out.
As aforementioned, in order to achieve European climate future targets ([1], [2])
biomass is expected to play an important role in the energy market and, above
all, in the small-medium size. Plants in the power range from few kWs up to few
MWs have the advantage to be fed by local biomass. This is considered a rst
class benet since further carbon dioxide emissions for shipping are avoided.
Much attention is paid in small-scale plants since they are actually suitable for
decentralized energy generation. In addition, small communities, remote areas
are viable examples of stand-alone small-size CHP plant installations. As out-

advantages. It can reduce both transmission losses on the grid and the possibil-

to be built near thermal and/or electrical loads.
The main dierence among the plants running on solid biomass is the combus-
tion process. Two solutions are viable: Internal and external ring. Bram et

in order to produce biogas, processed via fermentation to produce bio-ethanol
or via transesterication to produce biodiesel. Refer to Appendix A for a more
detailed description of the processes involving biomass. An alternative solution
is the biomass gasication.
The gasication product can be either liquid or
gas. The gas is preferred since it causes less combustion problems. In small-

or Micro-Gas Turbines (MGT). For plants up to few MW a xed bed gasier
with a ICE seems to be the most common solution.

a positive number is added to the cost function as a correction.
In the turbine design optimization the cost function is the total-to-total e-
ciency:
costfunctionturbine = −ηT T + sigma.
(3.30)
The total-to-total eciency has a minus sign since the cost function will be min-
imized. For what concerns constraint violations it is sucient to recall sigma,
since it is already implemented in the axial turbine model. It is the sum of
the penalty functions of some geometrical parameters: Flare angle, number of
blades, blade opening, height to mean radius ratio, chord to mean radius ratio
and tangential velocity at blade tip.
Some independent variables have also to be selected: they can be continous,
discrete or both [38]. The user is also requested to set upper and lower bounds
of each variable: GenOpt will try dierent values of the variables within their
==PAGE:70==
40
Tools and methodology of the project
bounds in order to nd the minimum cost function.
GenOpt has a wide library of algorithms for one-dimensional or multi-dimensional
optimization. In the present work the "Hybrid Generalized Pattern Search Al-
gorithm with Particle Swarm Optimization Algorithm" is used. It consists of
two successive algorithms. The rst one is the Particle Swarm Optimization
(PSO) algorithm: a population-based probabilistic optimization algorithm. It
creates a set of potential solutions. Each solution is called particle and each
set of solutions is called population. The rst population is tipically initialized
randomly [38]. Particles are dierent solutions calculated with dierent values
of the variables (inside assumed ranges). This algorithm provides a result that
is used as input for the second algorithm, namely the Hooke-Jeeves Generalized
Pattern Search (GPS) algorithm. If the model has both independent continous
and discrete variables the discrete ones are xed at the value of the particle with
the lowest cost function value [38]. The output of this second algorithm shows
the minimum cost function, namely the optimized result.

3.3.2
Second step: Design of the plant components
Once the most suitable uids and the cycle parameters are selected, the work
focuses on the design of plant components. This step of the work is carried out
using one-dimensional models of the same library. The dierence between 0-

namely mass and energy balances for the whole component, while in the latter
the components are discretized into cells. For each cell equations are imple-

continuity and momentum conservation equations, integrated over the cross-
sectional area and with time derivatives equal to zero.
This project performs a preliminary design of the heat exchangers and the tur-

second step. An optimization analysis is carried out with the software GenOpt.
A cost function is chosen depending on the element to be optimized. The min-

As regards heat exchangers, the optimized design of High Temperature Heat Ex-

• an Organic Rankine Cycle as bottoming cycle.

This system could result suitable as a grid-connected device for decentralized

The process ow diagram, shown in Figure 4.1, is hereinafter explained. At-
mospheric air enters the compressor where it reaches the WIFGT maximum
pressure (pW IF GT,max). It is then heated up in the HTHE through the ue gas
stream which exits the furnace at combustion temperature. Air then reaches
the Turbine Inlet Temperature (TITW IF GT ). Compressed and high temper-
ature air drives the WIFGT turbine, where mechanical power is obtained by
means of momentum exchange between the uid and the turbine blades.
A
generator then converts the mechanical power into electrical power. After ex-
pansion, air enters the furnace as combustion air and oxidizes dried biomass.
The furnace technology depends on the type of biomass. In this project wood
chips are chosen as fuel due to their availability in the market. Since the size
of the plant is quite small a common boiler with underfeed stoker is chosen as
a suitable combustor. It is quite cheap and can easily burn wood chips. The
combustion process takes place at atmospheric pressure; fuel feeding problems
are then avoided. Besides ashes, the main combustion product is a very high
temperature gas. It is rstly cooled down in the HTHE and then, still at a quite
high temperature, it turns out to be the hot source for the bottoming cycle,
namely the ORC cycle. It is a closed cycle where the organic uid is vaporized
in the EVA, reaching the ORC cycle maximum temperature. The evaporation
process takes place at the maximum pressure (pEV A), if the pressure drops in
the heat exchangers are neglected. The organic vapor drives the ORC turbine,
it is expanded down to the turbine outlet pressure obtaining shaft power. With
an electric generator, mechanical power is converted into electric power. For the
majority of organic uids, the expansion is completely dry, thus the uid, at the
turbine exit, still results to be at superheated condition. For this reason a REC
is involved in the cycle. Generally speaking, the recuperator is very important
in the ORC system because part of the sensible heat is used to pre-heat the
organic uid before the evaporator. Without the recuperator, a higher amount
of sensible heat would be wasted in the condenser. The result is usually a gain
in terms of eciency. Nevertheless, in some cases there is no eciency gain but
the recuperator is still important as it allows to limit the size of the condenser
[13].
With the aim of maximizing the plant electric eciency, a recuperated ORC
cycle is considered in this work. Water is used as coolant in the condenser: hot
water is thus obtained. It can be used for distric heating, both as sanitary hot
water and space heating. The project assumes 60/90 ◦C as water temperature
at condenser inlet/outlet. They are commonly used values for district heating
but, as suggested by the Danish Energy Agency et al. in [40], the future trend
is to use lower temperature water for space heating. For example, a condenser
inlet/outlet temperature of 50/70 ◦C, or even lower, could be used. Generally
speaking, with the same pinch point in the condenser, the lower the water tem-
perature at condenser inlet, the higher the electric eciency results. In this
project a thermodynamic analysis will show the dierence in electric eciency
between the two above-mentioned couples of temperature. Nevertheless, the
==PAGE:78==
48

components design will be carried out with the couple 60/90 ◦C.
The exhaust gas, after the evaporator, can further be cooled down before reach-
ing the lower temperature possibile, namely the stack temperature. A dryer
is then considered in the plant to remove part of the water content from inlet
wet wood chips. The drying process exploits the exhaust gas stream to dry wet
wood chips. As a result, the dried fuel Lower Heating Value (LHV) exceeds the
wet fuel LHV.

Analysis with polytropic eciency
The above presented analysis was carried out with constant isentropic eciency,
as shown in Table 4.4. It is well known that the isentropic eciency depends
on pressure ratio. On the contrary the polytropic eciency does not depend
on the transformation. Comparing two machines working under dierent pres-
sure ratios with isentropic eciency can lead to mistakes. Therefore it is not
straightforward to vary the maximum pressure of both topping and bottom-
ing cycle keeping the turbines isentropic eciency constant. At this purpose,
the thermodynamic analysis is hereinafter carried on keeping the polytropic
eciency constant. Figure 4.16 shows the model of the compressor with the
possibility of specifying polytropic eciency.
Figure 4.16: Compressor model with polytropic eciency.

compressor and ORC turbine respectively. The criterion for the choice of these
values is the following: selecting the polytropic eciencies that correspond to
the isentropic eciencies chosen in the previous thermodynamic analysis at the
optimal pressure ratio (Table 4.4).
This criterion is chosen in order to have the same WIFGT + ORC electric e-
ciency. A small stages compression is implemented in Dymola model. 30 stages

Turbines design:
Simulations and results
This chapter will present the preliminary design of the WIFGT + ORC turbines.
It will focus on the simulations and on the obtained results. As fully explained
in Chapter 4, two turbines are present in the whole system: the ORC turbine
and the WIFGT turbine. A preliminary design of turbines in both toluene and
benzene congurations will be given.
A single-stage, axial turbine has been
chosen in the present work since the Dymola quasi-monodimensional model was
ready to be implemented. Further work may deal with the implementation of a
radial turbine model in order to compare their performances. As suggested by
Macchi in [51], the aim of a preliminary design with one-dimensional approach
is to select:
• Number of stages;
• Velocity triangles at mean radius;
• Rotational speed;
• Main blade geometry parameters;
• Loss coecient evaluation.

For what concerns turbine losses, the model implements Craig & Cox method
(1971) that takes into account several kinds of losses [52]. They classify losses in
two groups: The losses of the rst group are dened by a loss factor expressed in
[J/kg], while the second group losses are estimated by a dimensionless decrease
in the stage eciency. In the rst group, prole losses (referred to the boundary
layer development) and secondary losses (referred to the vorticity and devia-
tions from the main ow) are considered. In the second group, tip clearance
losses (leakage of uid in the blade-hub clearance) and windage losses (friction
due to relative motion between uid and blades) are considered. Other kinds of
losses are estimated by the Craig & Cox model but they are not reported in the
results.

8.1
Conclusions
This work performed a preliminary design and a feasibility analysis of a wet
indirectly red gas turbine integrated with an organic Rankine cycle unit for
combined heat and power production.
The targeted electric power for this plant was assumed around 200 kW due to
the absence of a performant system running on solid biomass in the small-middle
scale. In this project, the idea of CHP was born in order to recover heat from
the condenser. Therefore, the plant ensures also a production of hot water at
90 ◦C, usable for distric heating. Pre-existing models in Dymola environment
were improved and adapted for this purpose.
Firstly, optimal thermodynamic parameters were chosen by means of a sen-
sitivity analysis. Toluene and benzene turned out to be the most suitable work-
ing uids in the ORC cycle. A pressure ratio around 4 was selected for the
WIFGT as it showed the highest electric eciency. For what concerns the or-
ganic Rankine cycle, a maximum pressure of 12.6 and 25.1 bar (toluene and
benzene congurations respectively) turned out to be the most suitable for this
plant. The combined system showed an electric eciency around 38% in both
congurations. Thus, the rst aim of this thesis was reached: from a thermo-

dynamic point of view, the WIFGT + ORC system outdoes the conventional
plants running on solid biomass.
A preliminary design of the components was carried out in order to have a
better accuracy on the system performance.
For this purpose, Dymola one-
dimensional models of heat exchangers and a quasi-monodimensional model of
axial turbine were implemented. Finned-plate heat exchangers of 34 and 28 n
layers were selected for the HTHE (toluene and benzene congurations respec-

vaporize the organic uid in the ORC plant. The design of the recuperator and

reached with a single-stage axial model. A total-to-total eciency around 77%
and 74% was achieved for the ORC turbine driven by toluene and benzene re-

The outcome of the components design was used as input data in the system
model and the performance was investigated. An electric eciency of about
34% is obtained. The result shows the potentiality of the studied system: In
the small-middle power range, the WIFGT + ORC unit conrms to exceed other
biomass systems in terms of electric eciency (refer to Figure 2.7). The heat

For what concerns installation options, the investigated plant would be suitable
both as grid-connected unit and as stand-alone system. After a discussion with

be the most suitable. It might be a promising system in those remote areas

In order to avoid fuel shipping and related emissions, the best option for the

A.2
Biomass transformation processes
Raw biomass can either be burnt directly or transformed in order to obtain a
better fuel. Several chemical and biological processes can be performed. It is not
the goal of this work to present a detailed description of the existing processes
from raw biomass to a usable fuel. Therefore only a brief overview is given

A.2.1
Anaerobic digestion
Anaerobic digestion is biological process in which microorganisms transform
biomass into biogas [62]. The process is anaerobic: It is carried on without
oxygen. It can be divided into four successive stages, depending on the active
microorganisms:
1. Hydrolisis: It is the process during which bacteria break down complex
carbohydrates, cellulose, proteins and fats of which biomass consists. The
products of this process are simple sugars, amino acids and fatty acids.
2. Acidogenesis: By means of acidogenic bacteria simple sugars are further
broken down. Fatty acides, ammonia, carbon dioxide are the resulting
product.
3. Acetogenesis: Compounds from previous stage are transformed into acetic
acid, hydrogen and carbon dioxide.
4. Methanogenesis: In this nal stage bacteria convert intermediate products
into methane, carbon dioxide and water.
The gaseous product of the overall process is called biogas. It mostly consists of
methane and carbon dioxide. The process waste, rich in nitrogen, can be used
as a fertiliser, burned or gasied [61]. The overall process can be summarized
by the chemical reaction:
C6H12O6 7→3CO2 + 2CH4

and methane. Two dierent processes can be performed depending on the tem-

A.2.2
Fermentation
Fermentation is the process used to produce wine and beer. By the addition
of yeast or bacteria, glucose is converted into ethanol (C2H5OH). The same
process occurs if biomass with a high sugar content is used. Sugar beet and cane,
corn, sugar sorghum, sweet potato are some of the most common examples.
Starch crops like wheat, barley, oat and rice can be used as well. Lignocellulosic
biomass (for example straw and leaves residues from agriculture) can also be
used in Fermentation although it contains lignin that it is non-fermentable [63].
In order to obtain a higher purity, the product is then distilled and dehydrated
[63]. Bio-ethanol is the result of this procedure. It can be mixed with petrol
in low concentration (up to 5% [61]) and used in conventional spark-ignition
engines without important modication. In addition pure bio-ethanol can be
used as fuel in automotive but with consistent engine modications. The solid
waste from fermentation process can be used to feed cattle [63].
A.2.3

Transesterication is a chemical reaction that involves a triglyceride and an
alcohol, generally methanol, as reagent chemicals. The products are esters (R−
COOCH3) and glycerol. The triglyceride can be obtained from oil crops like
rapeseed, sunower, soy. Vegetal or animal waste oils can also be used. The
resulting biodiesel is a mix of dierent methyl-esters. Figure A.2 shows a general
transesterication reaction where R, R', R, R' are alkyl groups. R-group is
often methyl group (−CH3); in this case the alcohol is methanol. Biodiesel
has similar chemical properties to fossil diesel. The presence of oxygen inside
esters ensures a high quality combustion reaction, although lower heating value
of biodiesel is lower than diesel lower heating value.

A.3

In this section, referring to Bianchi et al. in [65], a brief overview of the existing

biomass is the grate combustor. Several types are available in the market: xed,
travelling, vibrating grate. It results very versatile since it can burn biomass
with non-homogeneous size. Logs, brashes, chips, bark can easily burnt in the

more dicult since ashes have low melting temperature and can cause problems
to the grate.

Small-size biomass (bark, chips, cereals) is often fed through an Archimede's
screw (cochlea) that allows to convey the fuel from the storage to the top part
of the grate. Fuel moves forward due to gravity or mechanical movement of
the grate. Figure A.3 shows the four combustion steps that biomass undergoes

fuel undergoes thermal cracking (pyrolysis), when volatile matter is released
and char remains on the grate (300-500◦C). The third stage is char burning
(700-1100◦C) in which the carbon solid matter (char) is oxidized by means
of primary air coming from beneath the grate. The non-combustible matter
(mostly heavy metals and sylicate minerals) remaining on the grate constitutes
bottom ashes, that are continously removed from the system by a hopper. Air is
conveyed through two dierent streams: Primary Air and Secondary Air or Over
Fire Air (OFA). The former, usually in stoichiometric proportion, is conveyed
from beneath. The high air excess over the primary combustion zone allows to
complete combustion, avoiding non-burnt fuel emission.

A more sophisticated and more expensive technology is the uidized bed com-
bustor. Burning dierent types of biomass (woody and non-woody biomass) is
possible, as long as they are uniform in size. The size homogeneity is a very
important point for uidized bed combustor in order to have a complete com-
bustion. Dierent kinds of bed are available in the market: xed, bubbling,
turbulent and recirculating bed. The dierence among these types of bed is the
inlet air velocity. For xed bed air enters with a maximum velocity of 1 m/s.
With an air speed range of 1-4 m/s the air entrainment in the bed leads to a
bubbling bed. Each particle reduces progressively its diameter during combus-
tion and, when the drag force exceeds the gravity, the particle leaves the bed.
For a air velocity of 5 m/s or more the bed is turbulent. In order to prevent
non-burnt particle emission a recirculating uidized bed combustor is designed.
By means of a cyclone separator it ensures a complete combustion. In Figure
A.4 all the dierent bed types are shown.

cannot be burned since the ash removing system is not ecient. Pellets, saw-
dust and chips are commonly used. They are fed from underneath the grate by
a fuel screw, as shown in Figure A.5.
Biomass dust burners are also available. They can use dust with a maximum
size of 10-20mm. Fuel is mixed with combustion air and pneumatically fed to
the burner.
