Abstract
This project analyzes how improving air quality in classrooms affects the spread of respi-
ratory infections and enhances the well-being of students in the classrooms.
26 classes across 3 schools participated in the project. The study was conducted as an
intervention study, where the classes were divided into 5 different experimental groups,
equipped with: decentralized ventilation, air cleaners, nudging sensors, and a combination
of nudging and air cleaners. The last group was a control group. All classrooms were also
equipped with an indoor climate sensor that recorded the indoor climate.
Based on the collected indoor climate and questionnaire data, the indoor environment
in the participating classes was analyzed, along with how the interventions affected the
indoor environment and the students’ well-being. Furthermore, based on the collected
indoor climate data, the infection risk in all classrooms was calculated for each day of the
project period using a modified version of the Wells-Riley equation.
The results showed that most classrooms, both before and after the interventions were in-
stalled, had problems with high CO2 concentrations. High concentrations of fine particles,
low temperatures, and high noise levels were also measured. Generally, the results showed
improvements in the indoor environment, students’ well-being, and infection risks after
the interventions were implemented. However, these improvements were likely due to the
seasonal change from winter to spring between the measurement periods rather than the
interventions. For the infection risk in the classrooms, it was found that classrooms with
high CO2 concentrations also had high infection risks. Additionally, it was found that a
sick teacher, under the assumptions made, had a higher risk of infecting other occupants
in the classroom compared to a sick child.
Based on the study, it can be concluded that the indoor environment should be improved
in certain aspects, and the most effective intervention was the decentralized ventilation
systems, as these significantly improved the CO2 concentrations in the classrooms and
had a positive effect on the infection risk.

Resumé
Dette projekt analyserer hvordan forbedring af luftkvalitet i klasselokaler påvirker spred-
ningen af luftvejsinfektioner samt forbedrer elevernes velvære i klasselokalerne.
26 klasser fordelt på 3 skoler har deltaget i projektet. Studiet er udført som et interven-
tionsstudie, hvor klasserne er blevet inddelt i 5 forskellige eksperimentelle grupper, der er
blevet udstyret med henholdsvis: decentral ventilation, luftrensere, nudging sensorer og
en kombination af nudging og luftrensere. Den sidste gruppe var en kontrolgruppe. Alle
klasselokaler var også blevet udstyret med en indeklima sensor, der registrerede indekli-
maet.
På baggrund af indsamlede indeklima- og spørgeskemadata er det analyseret dels hvor-
dan indeklimaet var i de deltagende klasser, dels hvordan interventionerne har påvirket
indeklimaet og elevernes velvære. Yderligere er på baggrund af de indsamlede indekli-
madata beregnet infektionsrisiko i alle klasselokaler for hver dag i projektperioden ud fra
en modificeret version af Wells-Riley ligningen.
Resultaterne viste, at de fleste klasselokaler, både før og efter interventionerne var blevet
installeret, havde problemer med høje CO2-koncentrationer. Der blev tillige også målt
høje koncentrationer af fine partikler, lave temperaturer og høje støjniveauer. Generelt
viste resultaterne forbedringer i indeklimaet, elevernes velvære og infektionsrisici efter
interventionerne var blevet implementeret. Disse forbedringer skyldes dog nok nærmere,
sæsonskiftet fra vinter til forår mellem måleperioderne end interventionerne. For infek-
tionsrisikoen i klasselokalerne blev fundet, at lokaler med høje CO2-koncentrationer også
havde høje infektionsrisici. Yderligere blev fundet, at en syg lærer under de foretagne
antagelser havde en højere risiko for at smitte andre i lokalet end et sygt barn.
På baggrund af studiet kan det konkluderes, at indeklimaet på nogle punkter bør forbedres,
samt den mest virkningsfulde intervention var de decentrale ventilations anlæg, da disse
kraftigt forbedrede CO2-koncentrationerne i lokalerne og havde en positiv effekt på infek-
tionsrisikoen.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.1
Background
When the COVID-19 pandemic hit in the spring of 2020, the world was in many ways
turned upside down. Many people faced a new everyday life, as several countries went
into lockdown. Schools and workplaces were shut down, and work-from-home and online
school became the new normal for both children and adults, along with masks, social
distancing, and sanitizing. Changes were not only seen in the everyday world. They also
happened in the research world where the focus now became issues like what is this virus?
How is it spreading? How can we control it? and so on.
Now, four years later, the world has in many ways settled back to what it looked like be-
fore the spring of 2020. The children are back in school, most adults are back at their
workplaces and the world has opened up again. Research is now focusing more on pre-
venting new pandemics from breaking out. In continuation hereof Statens Serum Institut
and Teknologisk Institut have started a pilot study to investigate whether a better indoor
environment in schools can slow the spread of respiratory infections, and improve the
student’s overall health and well-being (Iversen, 2023).
Previous studies of the indoor environment in Danish schools have found that many class-
rooms have insufficient indoor environment (Andersen et al., 2009; Clausen et al., 2014;
Toftum et al., 2021). This was also the case for the latest large-scale study called ”Masseks-
perimentet 2021”, a large citizen science project involving 709 school classes(Toftum et
al., 2021). The study tested the effect of sending the students outside and airing out during
recess. The study found that when the classes acted as they normally would, the measured
CO2-concentration in 53% of the classrooms was above the recommended level of 1000
ppm. However, this number was reduced to 36% on days where the students went outside
and windows were opened during recess (Toftum et al., 2021). Furthermore, it was found
that on the ”Airing out” day the students made fewer mistakes in a concentration test, were
more pleased with the indoor environment, and reported improved well-being(Toftum et
al., 2021). This study demonstrated that improving the indoor environment enhances the
students’ overall welfare and learning.
The pilot study by Statens Serum Institut and Teknologisk Institut includes 26 elementary
school classes from three selected schools; Egelundskolen in Albertslund, Måløvhøj Skole
campus Østerhøj in Ballerup, and Tranbjergskolen campus Kirketorvet in Aarhus. In the
participating classes, different technical solutions and their effects on the indoor environ-
ment and infection risk were tested. The solutions include decentralized ventilation, air
cleaners, nudging, and a combination of nudging and air cleaners.
The pilot study, which started in the fall of 2023 and is planned to run for two years,
is economically supported by the philanthropic organization Realdania and aims to test
solutions, methods, and organization in preparation for a larger-scale project. The pilot
study is conducted in collaboration with the Technical University of Denmark, Aarhus

1.2
Objectives
This thesis has the following objectives:
• Make detailed technical descriptions of the participating schools and their technical
installations.
• Evaluate the indoor environment in the participating classrooms before and after
implementing interventions.
• Evaluate how the interventions affect the measured indoor environment across class-
rooms.
• Evaluate how the interventions affect the students’ well-being.
• Evaluate the infection risk in the classrooms based on the measured indoor environ-
ment
• Evaluate how the interventions affected the infection risk.
• Link the calculated infection risks to data on sickness absence in the participating
classrooms.
The final objective has, however, not been possible to fulfill, as the data on sickness ab-
sence was not collected directly from the schools, possibly due to GDPR rules. Instead,
SSI applied to gain access to the data through Statistics Denmark’s database. This unfortu-
nately meant that SSI, could not share the data for use in this master’s thesis. After careful
consideration, it was decided not to apply for a permit to access the data and instead focus
on other aspects of the project.

Chapter 2
Indoor Environment Theory
This chapter describes general theory about the indoor environment to intro-
duce the topic to readers who are not indoor environment specialists.
Generally, indoor environment can be divided into four subcategories:
Atmospheric-, thermal-, acoustic-, and visual indoor environment. This
project was, however, only focused on the atmospheric-, thermal-, and acous-
tic indoor environments.

Chapter 2. Indoor Environment Theory
2.1
Atmospheric Indoor Environment
Atmospheric indoor environment refers to the part of the indoor environment related to
the air quality. The atmospheric indoor environment is affected by different factors such
as the CO2-concentration in the air, the humidity, odors, and pollutants from for example
building materials, furniture, and occupants (Vorre et al., 2021).
Evaluation of the indoor air quality (IAQ) is often based on CO2-concentration in the air,
as CO2 functions as a good indicator for bioeffluents from occupants in buildings where
pollution mainly stems from the occupants (Vorre et al., 2021).
The IAQ is also affected by volatile organic compounds (VOCs), which are gasses emit-
ted from sources such as building materials, furniture, and mold, as well as from human
activities such as smoking, cleaning, and cooking (Jørgensen, 2023). Examples of VOCs
include benzene, ethylene glycol, and formaldehyde (Minnesota Department of Health,
2022). The amount a source emits often increases when indoor temperature and humidity
rise (Jørgensen, 2023).
Particle levels are another parameter that can affect IAQ. Particles can be separated into
coarse, fine, and ultra-fine particles. Coarse particles (PM10) are particles with a size >
2.5 µm, fine particles (PM2.5) have a size between 0.1-2.5µm, and ultra-fine particles are
particles with a size <0.1 µm (Bekö, 2023). Particles in indoor air come from sources such
as the occupants, in the form of skin flakes and fibers from clothing, as well as activities
like cooking and smoking. Furthermore are dust and pollen also examples of particles
(Bekö, 2023).
2.2
Thermal Indoor Environment
The thermal indoor environment refers to the conditions inside a room or building that
affect the occupants’ thermal comfort i.e. whether the occupants feel hot, or cold (Vorre
et al., 2021).
The thermal comfort of a person depends on the 6 following parameters: Air temperature,
Mean radiant temperature (often determined by the surface temperatures of the room),
Airspeed, Humidity of the air, The occupants’ activity level, and The insulation of the
occupant’s clothing (Toftum, 2020).
The collective impact of the air temperature and mean radiant temperature in a room can
be expressed by the term operative temperature. This is the temperature that is most often
listed in norms and standards in relation to thermal indoor environment (Vorre et al., 2021).
When working with thermal comfort, a distinction is made between general thermal com-
fort and four types of local thermal discomfort: Draught, caused by local cooling of the
skin due to air movements; Radiation asymmetry, from either a cold floor, wall, or ceiling;
Vertical temperature difference; and Hot or cold floors (Toftum, 2020).
==PAGE:21==

Furthermore, relative humidity affects both the thermal and atmospheric indoor environ-
ments. High relative humidity influences the thermal indoor environment by creating a
sensation of dense air, which can make a hot room feel even warmer than it is. On the other
hand, low relative humidity can irritate and dry out the skin, eyes, and mucous membranes,
but it can also create a sensation of fresh air. (Vorre et al., 2021).
2.3
Acoustic Indoor Environment
The acoustic indoor environment refers to the auditory conditions inside a room or building
that affect how sound is perceived. This comprises aspects such as noise levels, dampening
of noises from both inside and outside as well as ensuring speech comprehension (Vorre
et al., 2021).
In acoustics, the terms sound pressure level and reverberation time are often used. The
sound pressure level corresponds to what is commonly referred to as noise levels, mea-
sured in dB(A). The reverberation time is the time it takes, in seconds, for a sound to die
out after being generated.
When it comes to the acoustic indoor environment, it is about finding the right balance
between dampening unwanted noises, such as chairs being dragged across the floor, noise
from technical installations, or traffic outside, without dampening the sounds so much that
speech intelligibility is affected due to the reverberation time being too short (Vorre et al.,
2021).

Chapter 3. Indoor Environment in Schools
3.1
Effect of Indoor Environment in Schools
The following sections describe the effect of the atmospheric, thermal, and acoustic indoor
environments in schools.
3.1.1
Effect of Atmospheric Indoor Environment
Studies of the effect of the atmospheric indoor environment in classrooms have mainly fo-
cused on CO2-concentrations and ventilation rates. There have been found strong connec-
tions between the students’ performance when doing school work and the CO2-concentration
(Vorre et al., 2021).
It has for example been found that children solved schoolwork 12% faster and 2% more
correctly when the CO2-concentration was kept at 900 ppm compared to 2100 ppm (War-
gocki & Wyon, 2021). Moreover, it has also been found that adequate ventilation in the
classrooms can decrease the absence of students (Wargocki & Wyon, 2021).
3.1.2
Effect of Thermal Indoor environment
Studies of the thermal indoor environment have shown that high temperatures in class-
rooms negatively affect the performance of the students (Vorre et al., 2021). One study
has shown that when the temperature in a classroom was increased by 10 °C, the students’
performance was reduced by 20% (Wargocki & Wyon, 2021). Additionally, it has been
found that increased temperatures have twice the negative effect on children’s performance
in school compared to adults’ performance in an office (Wargocki & Wyon, 2021).
Children also prefer lower temperatures compared to adults (Vorre et al., 2021; Wargocki
& Wyon, 2021). Often children are most comfortable at 20-21 °C, which most adults will
find cold (Vorre et al., 2021).
3.1.3
Effect of Acoustic Indoor Environment
Studies of the acoustic indoor environment have proven that noise in classrooms affects
speech intelligibility, general comprehension, and memory negatively (Wargocki & Wyon,
2021). This means that noise in classrooms makes it harder for the students to hear and
understand what the teacher tries to communicate to them, and harder for the teacher to
communicate with the children.
The negative effects of noise in classrooms are mainly seen in younger children, bilingual
children, and children who are hard of hearing or have other special needs. This is due
to these groups of children often having either lower linguistic skills or needing to con-
centrate more to understand the spoken language (Vorre et al., 2021; Wargocki & Wyon,
2021). Negative effects of noise in classrooms are not seen to the same extent in older
children and adults since their linguistic skills are better which gives them greater abilities
==PAGE:25==

11
to guess what an incomprehensible word means based on the context (Wargocki & Wyon,
2021).
Noise in the classroom does not only affect the students. High noise levels in the class-
rooms does also affect the teacher’s voice, as the teacher will need to raise their voice to
overcome the noise (Vorre et al., 2021). Furthermore, has studies shown long reverbera-
tion times make the negative effects of noise worse as it contributes to a higher noise level
(Wargocki & Wyon, 2021).

lists the requirements for the indoor environment in Danish schools. The requirements
are divided into 4 categories: Standard+, Standard, requirements from the current Danish
Building Regulations from 2018, and Minimum.

creased focus on the indoor environment but still in balance with the performance of the
students, energy consumption, and expenses. Standard+ gives recommendations for in-

indoor climate. The Minimum requirements are used regarding renovations where it is
inexpedient to apply the standard requirements (Vorre et al., 2021).

3.3
Ventilation Types in Danish Schools
In Danish schools, different types of ventilation are used. The used type often depends on
when the school was built or renovated. In newer schools, mechanical ventilation is most
often utilized to ensure adequate classroom ventilation, while older and non-renovated
schools tend to rely on natural ventilation (Toftum et al., 2021).
Mechanical ventilation can either be provided by central systems where the air is dis-
tributed in the building by ducts or through decentralized systems where each classroom
has its own system. Mechanical systems are beneficial to use in schools as they can pro-
vide large amounts of air to ensure adequate air quality.
Natural ventilation relies on the opening of windows or doors as well as cracks or other
leaks in the building envelope. This type of ventilation is often unreliable in order to keep
a satisfactory indoor environment as natural ventilation is easily affected by wind and
temperature differences from indoors to outdoors.

4.1
Virus Transmission Routes
Virus transmission can happen in different ways. The two main routes in Danish schools
are assumed to be (i) through droplets and particles exhaled from an infected person that
afterwards are inhaled by another person or land on the other person’s mouth, nose, or
eyes (MedlinePlus, 2023), (ii) or by touching contaminated surfaces or objects and then
touching the eyes, nose, or mouth (MedlinePlus, 2023).
In this thesis, the main focus is on airborne transmission of respiratory infections, which
are infections impacting the respiratory system that can travel through the air.
4.2
Relationship between Airborne Transmission and Indoor
Environment
Several studies have shown that ventilation rates and airflow patterns indoors have a large
impact on the indoor spread of viruses (Correia et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Xu et al.,
2022). Low ventilation rates in a room have been found to increase the risk of infection,
while higher ventilation rates can lower the infection risk by faster diluting the concentra-
tions of virus-laden aerosols (Wang et al., 2021). Correia et al. (2020) do however suggest
that HVAC systems not necessarily are the answer to mitigating airborne transmission in-
doors as this study points out that improper use of HVAC systems can help the spread of
viruses due to ineffective filters and re-circulation of air.
Furthermore, the temperature and relative humidity indoors also affect viruses’ ability
to survive. Studies have shown that viruses such as influenza and SARS-CoV-2 are more
stable at lower temperatures, possibly due to a slower decay rate (Wang et al., 2021). From
epidemiological evidence and animal studies, it has been suggested that the transmission
of respiratory viruses that infect the upper airways is favored at lower temperatures (Wang
et al., 2021). For the relative humidity, it has been found that some viruses survive better
at lower relative humidities while others survive better at higher relative humidities (Wang
et al., 2021).
4.3
Model for Infection Risk
Infection risks for airborne transmission of viruses can be modeled in different ways.
Overall there are three different approaches to this: Mathematical models, Numerical
models, and Full-scale experiments (Ferrari et al., 2022).
Mathematical models include the Wells-Riley model, the dose-response model, and the
Gammaitoni-Nucci model, which is an adapted version of the Wells-Riley model that can
be applied to transient conditions (Ferrari et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022). An example
of a numerical model is modeling how droplets or exhaled air from an infected person
spread in a room using computational fluid dynamics (CFD), as CFD models numerically
==PAGE:31==

17
solve Navier-Stokes equations to calculate fluid motion and heat transfer (Ferrari et al.,
2022). Full-scale experiments most often apply the tracer gas technique to simulate how
pathogens can spread in a room (Ferrari et al., 2022).

Decentralized Ventilation
Classrooms in the first experimental group were equipped with an Airmaster AM 1000 de-
centralized ventilation system, which is a decentralized system developed for classrooms
==PAGE:46==

and other rooms with a high occupational load (Airmaster, n.d.). The system had a maxi-
mum capacity in the range of 903-1015 m3/h depending on the class of the installed filters
and the operating noise level (Airmaster, 2023). According to the datasheet, the noise
level should be within the range of 30-35 dB(A) (Airmaster, 2023).
To keep the noise level down, the system had built-in active sound attenuation that espe-
cially dampened the low-frequency tones. This was possible due to several sets of speakers
and microphones that helped remove unwanted sounds by emitting ”counter-noise”. To
otherwise achieve the same dampening, a large absorption area would be required (Air-
master, n.d.).
The AM 1000 had a heat recovery temperature efficiency of >80% (Airmaster, n.d.) and
a low energy use corresponding to an SFP of 700-1000 J/m3 depending on the installed
filters and the allowed noise level (Airmaster, 2023). To keep the energy use low, it is
often desired to have demand-controlled airflow. This was also the case in the classrooms
where the demand was adjusted based on the CO2-level. For this reason, combined with
the draft risk due to the amount of air the AM 1000 can provide, the ventilation system
was equipped with automatically adjustable air distribution, so-called Adaptive AirflowTM
(Airmaster, n.d.).
Adaptive AirflowTM is a patent-pending air distribution diffuser that adjusts the air distri-
bution pattern according to the needed airflow and the geometry of the room (Airmaster,
n.d.). The idea behind the diffuser is that it is divided into two, making the air leave the
unit in two separate streams. The Adaptive Airflow principle varies the air distribution
pattern between larger and smaller airflow rates, by utilizing the ceiling and fluid dynam-
ics principles. In this way, a constant throw length can be maintained over a wide range
of air flows, and draft problems can be avoided (Airmaster, n.d.).

7.6
Application of Model for Infection Risk
The following assumptions were made to apply the adapted Wells-Riley equation pre-
sented in section 4.3.
• All students were present at all times.
• One teacher was present for the whole day.
• 1 person in the classroom was infected, this could either be a child or a teacher. The
rest of the occupants were susceptibles.
• The outside CO2-concentration were 400 ppm.
The infection risk was calculated over the time range 8:00-14:00 for all school days and
measured CO2-concentrations below 400 ppm were removed from the dataset as these
measurements resulted in a negative infection risk, due to the measured level being lower
than the assumed outside concentration.
7.6.1
Calculation of Re-breathed Fraction, f
In order to calculate the re-breathed fraction, it was necessary to calculate the volume
fraction of CO2 added to exhaled breath during breathing (Ca) first. This was done by first
figuring out how much air the occupants exhale when breathing which is the pulmonary
ventilation rate.
According to Eldrige (2024) does children, between the ages 6-12, breathe 18 to 30 times
per minute while adults breathe 12 to 16 times per minute. For the calculation of the pul-
monary ventilation rate, the median values were used for children and adults respectively,
as seen in Table 7.4.
The tidal volume in healthy children averages around 8 mL/kg (Kollisch-Singule et al.,
2022) while it for a healthy adult is 7 mL/kg (Hallett et al., 2023). Since the tidal volume is
given in mL/kg, the average weight for children and adults was looked up. Danish adults
were found, to on average, weigh 79.9 kg (SDU.dk, 2022). While the weights for the
children were given per age for both boys (sygebørn.dk, n.d.-a) and girls (sygebørn.dk,
n.d.-b). Based on this, the mean weight was calculated for the expected age for the grade,
as seen in Table 7.4.
Based on the respiratory rate and the tidal volumes, the pulmonary ventilation rate for each
grade and adults was calculated. The results are shown in Table 7.4.

Chapter 9. Discussion
9.1
Objectives
In the following sections, the results of the study are discussed along with the setup and
applied methods in relation to the objectives listed in section 1.2.
9.1.1
Indoor Environment in Classrooms
In the participating classrooms, high CO2-concentrations were found both before and after
the interventions were installed. At Tranbjergskolen concentrations were found to be even
above 5000 ppm in one classroom, which is concerning. In the rest of the classrooms at
this school, more than 70% of the measurements were found to be above the recommended
1000 ppm before the interventions were implemented. After the implementation, this was
reduced to 50% of the measurements for classrooms without decentralized ventilation
installed and 0% for the classrooms with decentralized ventilation. This indicates that
the indoor environment at this school needs improvement and that mechanical ventilation
would be a very effective solution for this school. At Egelundskolen and Måløvhøj Skole,
high levels of CO2 were also found, but not as high as on Tranbjergskolen.
Regarding particulate matter, higher concentrations of fine particles were found compared
to coarse particles. The concentrations of coarse particles were well within the recommen-
dations from WHO, while the fine particles exceeded the recommendations in some class-
rooms. It is concerning that fine particles exceeds the recommendations, considering that
fine particles deposit further down in the lungs compared to the coarse particles, which
could potentially affect the health of the occupants over time. The TVOC-concentrations
in the classrooms were primarily a minor issue at Egelundskolen and Tranbjergskolen. It
is also possible that the measured VOC’s come from the area where the sensors are placed
and not just from the students.
The temperatures in all classrooms were found to be rather low, which can be attributed to
several reasons. First, it is possible that the low temperatures were caused by the Airthings
sensor being mounted on a wall that was slightly colder compared to the air in the occupied
zone, as the sensor measures the temperature around it. Secondly, the low temperatures
in the classrooms could be due to regulations from the municipalities limiting how much
the schools were allowed to heat the classrooms, as there the previous winter was a maxi-
mum allowed temperature in public buildings of 19 °C, issued by the government, due to
the energy crisis in Europe (Ritzau, 2023). Unfortunately, it was not possible to confirm
whether this regulation was in effect at the schools before the finalization of this thesis.
The noise levels in the classrooms were generally found to be high, without much improve-
ment from before to after the interventions were installed, and appeared to be primarily
student-generated. It should however be noted, that the measured noise levels should be
taken with a grain of salt, as the noise sensor in the Airthings Space Pro monitor is not
fully optimized, according to the manufacturer (Airthings, n.d.).
==PAGE:103==
Chapter 9. Discussion
89
Finally, it should also be noted that the applied filtering criteria possibly can have removed
data from periods when occupants were present in the classrooms.
9.1.2
Effect of Interventions on the Indoor Environment
When analyzing the effect of the applied interventions on the measured indoor environ-
ment across classrooms, it was found that the interventions had an impact on the CO2-
concentrations, the particle concentrations, and the noise levels.
For the CO2-concentration, only the decentralized ventilation system made a noticeable
difference. This was expected, as mechanical ventilation is one of the most effective ways
to control the CO2-concentrations due to a higher air change rate. Regarding the CO2-
concentration it was also expected that the nudging sensors in the nudging and combination
classrooms would have made a difference, this was however not the case probably due to
the signaling of the sensor not changing early enough, or the sensors being overlooked
in the classrooms. It might have made a difference if the sensor, for example, changed
color to yellow at 800 ppm and to red at 1000 ppm instead of changing at 1000 and 1400
ppm. This would provide a buffer period for the occupants to open the windows before
the CO2-concentration reached the recommended maximum.
For the particle concentrations, a small improvement was seen in the classrooms with only
air cleaners and in the combination classrooms. This small improvement is expected to
be due to the air cleaners. However, during class, the air cleaners were running on lower
settings to maintain an acceptable noise level, and therefore could only filter around 90
m3/h or a little more, depending on the specific setting. Additionally, the air cleaners might
have been more efficient if they were placed in a free-standing position in the classrooms,
allowing air to flow around all four sides, instead of the 1-2 sides seen in some classrooms.
Regarding the noise levels, it was observed that the inter-quartile range of the measure-
ments in classrooms with air cleaners installed was centered around higher values com-
pared to the other of the interventions. This might indicate that the air cleaners negatively
affect the noise levels in the classrooms. However, there is still so much noise gener-
ated by the students that for the upper quartile, the air cleaners did not make a significant
difference.
9.1.3
Effect of Interventions on the Students' Well-Being
Based on the questionnaire, it has been quite difficult to analyze whether the interventions
had a positive or negative effect on the students’ well-being, due to many classes not an-
swering both rounds of the questionnaire. As a result, in several cases, the answers before
the implementation of the interventions came from some classes, whereas the answers
after came from different classes. This introduced some uncertainty in the results due to
local differences in the schools’ indoor environment and the preferences of the individuals.
==PAGE:104==
90
Chapter 9. Discussion
The questionnaire however provided a general picture of the students’ satisfaction with
the classrooms. Overall it was found that the students were satisfied with the indoor en-
vironment in the classrooms. Most of the students, however, were dissatisfied with the
noise level and reported it to be too high, which was not surprising given the measured
noise levels in the classrooms, which also was found to be quite high.
Regarding the symptoms, the students reported an improvement in both illness-related
symptoms and building-related symptoms. Considering that the improvements were also
seen for the control groups, it is assumed that the improvement is more likely to be caused
by the change of season rather than the interventions.
9.1.4
Infection Risk in the Classrooms
The applied approach to calculating infection risk found that classrooms that had problems
with CO2-concentrations also had high infection risks. This was also expected, consid-
ering that CO2-concentration is the only indoor environment parameter considered in the
adapted Wells-Riley equation.
To apply the Wells-Riley equation, many assumptions were made regarding how much
CO2 the occupants exhale, their activity level, and the quantum generation rate for each
virus. These assumptions consequently mean that the infection risks should be taken with
a grain of salt. The infection risk with a sick child might be underestimated, as the applied
quantum generation rate was for resting and oral breathing. Considering that the par-
ticipating classes are in elementary school, it must be assumed that these children move
around a little more during the day compared to older students. For the cases where it is
the teacher that is infected, it was assumed that the teacher’s activity corresponds to stand-
ing and speaking, which the teacher might not be doing the entire time. Additionally, it
was assumed that the same teacher was in the classroom all day. These assumptions might
have led to a slightly overestimated infection risk. A more realistic picture of the infection
risk for each school day could possibly be obtained by utilizing a quantum generation rate
and occupancy schedule that varies over time, as the calculated results also assume that
none of the occupants leave the room.
Furthermore, the adapted Wells-Riley equation assumes full-mixing of the air in the room,
which may not reflect the reality in the classrooms. This could lead to overestimating
infection risks in some parts of the classrooms and underestimating them in other parts.
9.1.5
Effect of Interventions on the Infection Risk
When assessing the effects of the interventions, it was found that only the decentralized
ventilation made a noticeable difference to the infection risk in the classrooms.This out-
come aligns with expectations, as the adapted Wells-Riley equation used in this study relies
solely on CO2-concentrations among the measured parameters. If other models were used

to estimate the infection risk in the classrooms this could very well look different as other
models depend on other parameters.
9.2
Future Work
With the data collected during this pilot study, it could be interesting to look into whether
there is a relationship between the measured indoor environment and how the students
answered the questionnaire. This aspect was not examined during this thesis as the primary
focus was on understanding how the interventions affected the students’ well-being.
Additionally, it would be interesting to investigate if there is a correlation between the
calculated infection risks and the actual sickness absences in the participating classes and
maybe also look into whether these things could be linked to what the virtual virus risk
sensor in the Airthings monitor measures.
9.2.1
Application to a Large-Scale Project
If the findings of the pilot study are to be applied to a large-scale project, it is recommended
to involve a diverse selection of schools, as the three schools chosen for the pilot study
were quite similar in layout and age. Therefore, for a large-scale project, it would be
recommended to include both older and newer schools, as well as schools of varying sizes
and different building typologies.
Suppose the pilot study ends up being applied to a large-scale project, consideration should
be given to whether questionnaires should be included in the study. Many classes did not
respond to the questionnaire, and teachers reported that it was difficult for students to
understand. Therefore, if a questionnaire is to be part of a future large-scale project, it
should either be simplified or the focus should be shifted to an age group better suited to
answering a questionnaire independently.
Furthermore, the timing of before and after measurements should be selected thoughtfully
to minimize the impact of seasonal variations and ensure that differences attributable to
interventions can be more easily detected. It should also be considered whether the applied
interventions are suitable for a large-scale study, considering that it from this pilot study
indicated that several of the interventions did not have the desired effect.
9.2.2
Recommendations to the Schools
Based on the observed indoor environment at the different schools, it is recommended
that Tranbjergskolen should implement mechanical ventilation in their classrooms, as the
current ventilation in classrooms is insufficient. Egelundskolen should have their diffuse
ventilation system checked, as the CO2 concentrations suggest the system may not be
functioning properly or delivering adequate air volumes to the classrooms.

Regarding the low temperatures measured in all three schools, it is advisable to increase the
temperature a few degrees if the majority of children find the classrooms cold. Responses
from the questionnaire showed a divided opinion among students regarding whether they
felt hot or cold.
The questionnaire clearly indicated that the students were dissatisfied with the noise lev-
els in the classrooms, which was supported by the measured sound pressure levels. To
combat the high noise levels in the classrooms, several things can be considered. The first
recommendation would be to install acoustic ceilings, which are already installed in many
of the classrooms. Secondly, it can be tried to use nudging to combat student-generated
noise. Thirdly, it would be recommended to add more soft materials to the classrooms,
such as soft furniture or bulletin boards with acoustic properties, and attach tennis balls or
felt pads to the bottom of chairs without wheels.

Chapter 10. Conclusion
In this thesis, the indoor environment and infection risk were evaluated in 26 classrooms
participating in the pilot study ”Healthy Indoor Air in Schools” conducted by Statens
Serum Institut and Teknologisk Institut. The study analyzed the impact of decentralized
ventilation, air cleaners, nudging, and combinations of air cleaners and nudging on the
indoor environment, the students’ well-being, and the infection risk in the classrooms.
The collected data revealed that the participating classrooms mainly had problems with
high levels of CO2 and fine particles, low temperatures, and high noise levels. For in-
stance, in 4.c’s classroom on Tranbjergskolen CO2-concentrations above 4000 ppm were
measured, highlighting Tranbjergskolen as the school facing the greatest difficulties in
maintaining a satisfactory indoor environment.
The installed interventions were found not to affect the parameters: Temperature, rela-
tive humidity, and TVOC concentrations. It was however observed that the decentralized
ventilation systems had a positive impact on the measured CO2-concentrations in the class-
rooms. Additionally, classrooms with air cleaners installed showed a slight improvement
in the particle levels for both fine and coarse particles. In classrooms where interventions
included air cleaners, fewer instances of low noise levels were observed. However, since
most noise in the classrooms appeared to be generated by occupants, higher noise levels
were not seen compared to other interventions.
The students were also surveyed about their well-being. Based on the responses, it was
not possible to determine whether specific interventions improved their well-being more
than others due to the lack of classes that responded to both rounds of questions. How-
ever, findings from the questionnaire indicated that the students overall were satisfied
with the classrooms and the classrooms’ indoor environment both before and after the
interventions were implemented. The students were however dissatisfied with the high
noise levels in the classrooms during both questionnaire rounds. Between the before and
after measurement periods, an improvement in the number of students who reported issues
like coughing, runny noses, headaches, and tiredness was reported.
From the calculation of infection risk, it was found that a sick teacher had a higher risk of
infecting others compared to a sick child, based on the assumed activity levels. Further-
more, classrooms with high concentrations of CO2 also attributed with higher infection
risks. This was due to the adapted Wells-Riley function, which only considers the CO2-
concentration of the measured parameters. Consequently, it was observed that only the
decentralized ventilation systems had a positive impact on the infection risk.
Finally, it can be concluded that while most indoor environment parameters, overall well-
being of the children, and infection risk in the classrooms improved after the interven-
tions were installed, these improvements were mostly attributed to the seasonal change
from winter to spring rather than the interventions themselves. Among the interventions,
decentralized ventilation was found to be the most effective due to its impact on CO2
concentrations and, consequently, on the calculated infection risks.
