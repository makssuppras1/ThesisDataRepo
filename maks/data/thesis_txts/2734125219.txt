Abstract
This work addresses the challenges in urban planning related to the acquisition of reliable
population data for the design of sustainable infrastructures, especially in the context of
complex simulation frameworks such as SimMobility. Using Generative Adversarial Net­
works (GANs), the study performs a comparative analysis on the generation of synthetic
populations to meet the requirements of SimMobility.
Three research questions are investigated: 1) How can data on population dynamics
be considered to generate a diverse and representative Danish population synthetically
to capture demographic complexities and variations? 2) What methods can be used to
create a synthetic population that fits various marginal distributions for certain attributes?
3) What methods can be used to create a synthetic population that fits various marginal
distributions for certain attributes?
For this purpose, the GAN models ­ Wasserstein GAN, Directed Acyclic Tabular GAN and
Conditional Input DATGAN ­ are selected on the basis of promising results from litera­
ture and comparable case studies. Individual parameter options of the respective GAN
architectures as well as two methods for fitting synthetic populations to specific marginal
distributions are tested and compared. Typical statistical parameters from the machine
learning community are applied to analyse the results.
The multiple analyses conclude that all three models have advantages and disadvantages
with respect to various criteria. While the WGAN model shows strong results in process­
ing and modelling numerical numbers, it fails to closely resemble attributes with a large
number of unique values. The DATGAN and ciDATGAN models, on the other hand, have
considerable difficulties in interpreting poorly distributed numerical values, but can gener­
ate more diverse and representative population through the influence of expert knowledge
added during the training process. The comparison of methods for adapting the generated
populations to real­life scenarios also depends on the situation. It is possible to generate
representative Danish populations on the basis of extracted marginal distributions of the
Danish population. The choice of model and method, however, affects training time and
adaptability. Considering the requirements of the SimMobility framework, the ciDATGAN
model proves to be the most promising approach. The thesis concludes with a discussion
of the results, limitations and future work.

Introduction
Modern urban planning is faced with the complex challenges of providing efficient infras­
tructure while promoting a sustainable urban environment [1]. A critical component of
this pursuit is a comprehensive understanding of population dynamics. Acquiring reliable
population data is critical for laying the groundwork for designing transportation systems,
modelling traffic patterns, optimising routes, and anticipating future mobility demands.
The heavy dependence on data poses several challenges. These are especially evident
in the collection and transfer of socio­economic data, a process often impeded by com­
plexities. One such challenge arises from data protection, as exemplified by regulations
such as the General Data Protection Regulation. In addition, these difficulties are com­
pounded by the significant financial costs associated with handling and managing such
data. As a consequence, it is likely that regions with limited data will be excluded from
modelling efforts.
To address these challenges and promote a more inclusive approach to urban develop­
ment, the exploration of synthetic data generation emerges as a promising avenue. With
population synthesis the goal is to generate agents that have similar statistical properties
as agents of the real population. [2]. The creation of synthetic data that closely resembles
real­world population structures has therefore the potential to overcome hurdles related
to data scarcity and privacy. For this reason, using synthetic population data for agent­
based simulation has become common practice in many transport applications, such as
the SimMobility framework [2].
SimMobility is a multi­scale simulation platform being developed as part of the Singapore­
MIT Alliance for Research and Technology (SMART) [3]. It integrates different mobility­
dependent behaviour models. This platform is capable of simulating millions of people
and vehicles to explore how people will react in future mobility scenarios. Using this plat­
form, the Danish Centre for Transport Analytics at DTU’s Institute of Management aims
to develop an understanding of local and national similarities and differences in travel be­
haviour and demographic trends [4]. To assure that the simulation results are relevant to
both scientific and policy interests in the Danish transport context, not only a large number
of simulated agents is required, but also a particularly realistic and representative Dan­
ish population imposing high dimensional information for each agent. These challenges
underscore the necessity for innovative population synthesis approaches, specifically tai­
lored to the unique requirements of frameworks like SimMobility.
Traditional population synthesis methods, which rely on deterministic algorithms and sta­
tistical models [5], are challenged by the complexity inherent in SimMobility. Recognis­
ing these limitations has spurred interest in advanced techniques capable of capturing
intricate data relationships, with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [6] emerging
as a promising path. Based on adversarial training principles, GANs excel at capturing
complex data dependencies and relationships between variables, thereby improving the
accuracy and reliability of synthetic populations. Effectively tackling the high­dimensional
challenges associated with urban and transport data, GANs have a unique advantage in
generating diverse synthetic populations [7, 2, 8].
The large number of structures available for generating synthetic populations has different
applications and dependencies on the datasets provided. However, there is no clear pref­

Given the importance of real­world scenarios in SimMobility, this thesis undertakes a

techniques for improving the quality of the generated synthetic population. This investiga­

• How can data on population dynamics be considered to generate a diverse and

• What methods can be used to create a synthetic population that fits various marginal

• What approaches can be used to compare and validate the generated synthetic

This study will utilise established methods, in particular deep generative adversarial mod­

generate a synthetic population that meets the requirements of the SimMobility framework

Population Synthesises. The paper concludes in with a discussion of the results, limita­
tions and suggestions for future work.

2
Literature Review
The generation of synthetic population data is one of the most important areas of research
in the generation of synthetic tabular data. As mentioned in the introduction, population
synthesis is the idea of creating a realistic but artificial population from samples of real
agents. This has been approached mainly through methodological perspectives such as
reweighting, matrix fitting and simulation­based approaches (Barthelemy and Toint [9],
Rich [10], Borysov, Rich, and Pereira [7]).
In this chapter, the literature review, we will first focus on the different methods of pop­
ulation synthesis. Older methods such as Iterative Proportional Fitting (IPF) and Gibbs
sampling (matrix fitting, reweighting) are considered, and finally the modern (simulation­
based) machine learning approaches for generating synthetic data are examined. The
section 2.1.3 then presents the modern orientation of the development of Tabluar Gen­
erative Adversarial Networks and explains which models are particularly suitable for the
generation of synthetic population data. It then discusses how conditioning can contribute
to the generation of realistic populations and how their results can be compared. Finally,
section 2.3 covers the evaluation of model comparisons and shows how the transportation
and Machine Learning (ML) communities evaluate the generated synthetic datasets.
2.1
Existing Approaches for Generating Synthetic
Populations
Agent­based models often rely on microsimulation techniques introduced by Orcutt [11].
These techniques, reviewed by Birkin and Wu [12] and Müller and Axhausen [13], follow a
multi­stage principle involving population adaptation to relevant attributes and constraints
before individual units are sampled. Historically, population synthesis has favoured syn­
thetic reconstruction and combinatorial optimisation [14]. In recent years, however, there
has been a shift towards statistical and ML approaches, driven by advances in computing
power. These approaches can be categorised into statistical methods, such as resam­
pling and simulation­based approaches, and ML methods. A comprehensive review of
these methods, along with their advantages and disadvantages, is presented by Leder­
rey, Hillel, and Bierlaire [8] in ”DATGAN: Integrating Expert Knowledge into Deep Learn­
ing for Synthetic Tabular Data”. The following sections provide a summary of the leading
methods in each category, as detailed in the table 2.1 by Lederrey, Hillel, and Bierlaire
[8].
2.1.1
Statistical Techniques
Lederrey, Hillel, and Bierlaire [8] categorize the statistical methods for generating a syn­
thetic population into based principles of Iterative Proportional Fitting (IPF) [15] and the
combination of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and Gibbs Sampling method pro­
posed by Farooq et al. [16] in 2013.
IPF, which is based on the proportional adjustment of matrices to obtain certain ranges,
lacks the interaction between variables in its basic form. Although easy to implement, it
struggles with detailed realism due to its limitations: difficulties with variable interactions,
problems distinguishing between structural and sampling zeros, and handling different
data types. In contrast, the MCMC and Gibbs sampling approach uses multidimensional
random variables to characterise population distributions. This approach outperforms IPF
methods based on statistical metrics such as R2 and Standardized Root Mean Squared

Error (SRMSE) [13]. However, MCMC methods, which are based on assumptions about
probability distributions, are limited by incorrect assumptions that can lead to incorrect dis­
tributions. Although both methods have made progress, MCMC methods are superior to
IPF methods in terms of statistical metrics. Nevertheless, they face the challenge that they
rely primarily on conditional information, which leads to limitations when the assumptions
about the distributions are flawed.
Lederrey, Hillel, and Bierlaire [8] conclude that despite their widespread use in the trans­
port industry, these statistical methods are considered outdated compared to ML meth­
ods. For example Borysov, Rich, and Pereira [7] (in 2019) and Garrido et al. [2] (in 2020)
demonstrate that their ML­based approaches are superior to MCMC­based methods and
also outperform random sampling and marginal sampling on several criteria respectively.
Accordingly, the research orientates towards modern ML methods.
2.1.2
Machine Learning Techniques
Those recent advances in ML techniques have made a significant leap forward in the
generation of synthetic population data.
Despite Jiang et al. [14]’s 2022 paper high­
lights the drawbacks of complex computational approaches in the domain of ML, such
as the Bayesian networks proposed by Sun and Erath [5], other researchers, such as
Borysov, Rich, and Pereira [7], highlight numerous advantages and opportunities in gen­
erating complex synthetic datasets in their developments. Notable contributions by au­
thors such as [6], [2] and [8] have demonstrated the potential and benefits of generating
high­dimensional synthetic datasets.
However, to date, none of the published work has demonstrated an improved solution
without mentioning limitations in methodology, configuration, or complexity. Therefore,
all three primary different ML approaches suitable for generating a synthetic population
dataset are explored: Bayesian networks, Variational AutoEncoder(VAE), and Generative
Adversarial Networks (GANs).
Bayesian networks, used to generate populations, function as graphical models that en­
code probability distributions for variables. They use a directed acyclic graph (DAG) to
represent variable dependencies and local probability distributions. Zhang et al. (2019)
use this concept and extend it to generate populations and social networks a three­step
process. Their results show that this approach can be superior to IPF and Gibbs sampling.
However, Bayesian networks face challenges in terms of scalability and complexity, es­
pecially when dealing with high­dimensional and complex data structures.
VAEs [17] focus on reducing the dimensionality of the data to a latent space, thereby
simplifying subsequent data generation. VAEs are based on Bayesian mathematics for
probabilistic inference and independence, and use a re­parameterisation trick to encode
data in a latent space. They focus on considering the influence of different parts of the
data, so that error propagation can take unrelated factors into account. This approach
helps to navigate large neural network graphs, especially in deeper layers. However,
understanding the inference and independence components may require a strong under­
standing of complex mathematical concepts. Borysov, Rich, and Pereira [7] demonstrate
the use of VAEs to generate synthetic populations. They found that VAEs outperform
Gibbs samplers and Bayesian networks for larger problems, thus offering advantages in
scalability. In addition, VAEs are unique in that they allow the generation of out­of­sample
agents (sampling zeros ­ [2]), which the Gibbs sampler cannot do.
Both Bayesian Networks ([5], [18]) and VAEs ([7], [2]) have been succesfully applied to
generate synthetic data. However, despite their strengths in one or the other area, they im­

GANs [6] are currently considered to be the state of the art in the generation of realis­

distinguishes between real and synthetic data. The success of GANs lies in the ability

random sampling, marginal sampling and VAEs. In addition to other innovations, which
include the integration of gradient penalties during training [21] and the inclusion of con­
ditionality [22], the development of the architecture in GAN research has also evolved.

2.2
Conditionality
While GANs have excelled in the generation of synthetic images, their application to tab­
ular data is still relatively unexplored, particularly in the integration of latent information
to guide the generation process. Moreover, there is the challenge of how to tailor a syn­
thetically generated dataset to fit specific conditional distributions. Lederrey, Hillel, and
Bierlaire [30] lists these methods ­ concatenation, auxiliary classifiers, projection mecha­
nisms, and conditional batch normalisation (CBN) ­ that have proven successful in image
generation, focusing on descriptive image details and reconstruction of hidden segments.
These encoding methods enable controlled data generation by incorporating specific de­
tails during training to shape the output. However, the need for specialised strategies
explicitly tailored to tabular datasets becomes apparent due to the unique challenges of
exploiting latent information that differ from image­centric approaches. In particular, socio­
economic tabular datasets often contain explicit ’latent’ data such as age or gender, which
makes the use of this information in GANs particularly challenging.
The transition of GANs to include any type of conditioning in the generation of tabular data
requires specialised architectures, such as those based on LSTM cells, FCNNs or CNNs.
Recent work such as the aforementioned CTGAN and CTAB­GAN approaches have at­
tempted to introduce this, each with its advantages and disadvantages 2.1. However,
their implementations rely on latent variables such as those used in image generation
approaches, leading to limitations in the use of the models or compromises in the accu­
racy of the synthetic data generated. For example, CTGAN uses concatenation­based

provide specific results on the conditionality itself [8]. Conversely, CTAB­GAN incorpo­

high­dimensional datasets, with more than just eight attributes.
In conclusion, it is advisable to investigate the possibility of combining the techniques

has shown good results in different areas. A mixed approach could potentially handle
scenarios with high­dimensional datasets and provide a more comprehensive, versatile
solution to the challenges of data synthesis.
2.3
Synthetic Population Evaluation
Evaluation methods for validating the results of generative models, especially those ap­
plied to high­dimensional data, include sophisticated approaches, as discussed by Gar­
rido et al. [2]. A common validation method is to estimate the empirical joint distribution
of the variables and compare it with the distribution of the test set [2]. However, in high­
dimensional datasets, this approach becomes unreliable due to the sheer volume of data.
Instead, the quality of the approximated distribution can be assessed by comparing partial
versions of the original distributions. This involves evaluating specific variable subsets us­
ing metrics such as Standardised Mean Squared Error (SRMSE), Pearson’s correlation
coefficient and coefficient of determination R2. These assessments provide a quantita­
tive understanding that is complemented by ’45 degree’ plots showing different variable
subgroups and their scores [2, 8, 21].
Furthermore, the authors Lederrey, Hillel, and Bierlaire [8] build on common methods
from the transport literature (Borysov, Rich, and Pereira [7], Xu and Veeramachaneni
[28], and Casati et al. [24]) and propose statistical tests to evaluate synthetic datasets.
They introduce joint frequency lists for multiple columns simultaneously, evaluate joint
distributions of different orders, and systematically test all possible column combinations
at each level of aggregation. This approach goes beyond simply examining frequency lists
for individual columns and allows for a more comprehensive analysis of the correlations
between columns in both the original data and the synthetic data.

3.1
Generative Adversarial Networks
GANs are categorised as structured probabilistic models and belong to the class of im­
plicit generative models [6]. The main goal of this class of models is to estimate the
joint probability distribution ˆP(X) that approximates the true joint distribution P(X) of the
population. Under ideal circumstances, learning the approximate population distribution
can result in the synthetic generation of agents n = 1, .., N (Where N is the size of the
population.), with the random variable (Xn) being representative of the entire population.
This makes them almost ideal for solving the problem of population synthesis, where the
explicit probability of a particular individual in the population is not known.
Best for abstracting the implicit probability densities to generate complex set of samples
[2] are Neural Networks (NNs), that can theoretically approximate any function [31]. This
also applies to the architecture of adversarial modelling. The basic concept of GANs
consists of two opposing models ­ the generator and the discriminator ­ engaged in an
optimisation problem. These are easiest to describe when both the generator and the
discriminator are defined as multilayer perceptrons (deep neural networks) [6].

applications, such as in tasks involving the generation of a synthetic population. These
are based on the fact that minimising the value function amounts to minimising the Jensen­

a partially optimised generative adversarial network that is unable to accurately generate
synthetic agents that could potentially originate from the data generation distribution.
3.1.1
Wasserstein­GAN
To mitigate the challenges posed by gradient vanishing, it is commonly recommended to
use loss functions that act as non­saturation heuristics [6]. In doing so, the Wasserstein
GAN (WGAN) proposed by Arjovsky, Chintala, and Bottou [20], has been proven to out­
perform various previous methods to generate accurate synthetic agents [2]. Instead of a
discriminator, the WGAN introduces a modified network known as the critic. The critic op­
erates on the Wasserstein­1 distance function (also referred to as Earth­Mover distance
­ EM), a metric that measures the dissimilarity between probability distributions. In the
training process of the WGAN, the following expression is maximised [20]:

Consequently, a decrease of the computed loss, which is equal to a decrease of the

continuous if it satisfies K­Lipschitz­continuity [20]. To ensure this constraint, a WGAN
uses a simple method: the parameter space W is stabilised within the limits of Lipschitz

modification, interested readers are referred to the original paper [20].
Unlike convential GANs a modified WGAN offers several advantageous properties in re­
gards to the training and results [20]:
• The Wasserstein distance exhibits continuity and near differentiability over its entire
domain, facilitating effective model training towards optimality.
• As a meaningful metric, the Wasserstein Distance shows convergence to 0 as dis­
tributions approach each other, and divergence as they move further apart.
• The critic in WGAN avoids saturation better by converging to a linear function and
addresses the reduction of the problem of gradient vanishing.
• WGANs eliminate a common problem in GANs ­ mode collapse [6, 20].
(The phenomenon of mode collapse occurs when the discriminator excels at dis­
criminating between real and generated samples. This causes the generator to
prefer generating a limited set that consistently fools the discriminator, rather than
exploring the full distribution of training data [35].)
These introduced modifications of a WGAN show in practice (in areas like i.e image gen­
eration, and speech imitation) that the quality of synthetic data generation improves sig­
nificantly. However, while this is very promising, they also have their limitations. One of
these is the challenge of precisely controlling the generation process. In fact, generative
deep learning models are inherently data driven and depend on learning patterns and
representations directly from the underlying structures of the data during training. Work­
ing in high­dimensional spaces is particularly challenging, which is evident in population
datasets. Not only do these set include a large number of individuals but also exhibit a
high cardinality of data attributes and types. Due to the complexity of the non­linear trans­
formations of the multi­layer perceptrons, the high dimensions of the representations and
the lack of explicit algorithms for generating the data, the results are often autonomous
and sometimes unpredictable. It is therefore very important to find a balance in the imple­
mentation of these constraints in order to take advantage of stability without compromising
training efficiency.

when using high­dimensional tabular datasets. DATGANs are characterised by the inte­

the training of the models. By leveraging the inherent structure of variables, DATGANs aim
to generate synthetic datasets more efficient and also more realistic and representative
[8]. As illustrated in Figure 3.4, this approach necessitates modification to the standard
GAN model, in particular to the structure of the generator.

implementations, and in particular the mathematical calculations, in the published paper
by Lederrey, Hillel, and Bierlaire [8]. Aspects of data processing, including encoding, label

Generator
To enable the generator of a DATGAN to effectively comprehend the concept of these
sets, the multi­level perceptron is augmented with LSTM cells. LSTMs belong to the cate­
gory of recurrent neural networks (RNN), which are designed to process sequential data
[36]. Unlike traditional feed forward neural networks, RNNs establish cyclic connections
of their layers, allowing them to retain a memory of previous inputs. The LSTM extension
represents a specific class of RNNs. They show remarkable effectiveness in modelling
sequences with long­term dependencies because they can selectively update and retain
information over time [37]. This feature is particularly useful for processing sequences
derived from DAGs.
The core components of an LSTM cell are gates, each of which has a specific purpose
in regulating the flow of information. The forget gate (ft) determines which information
should be discarded from the cell state, the input gate (it) controls the input to the cell
state, and the output gate (ot) sets the output based on the current input and cell state.
Together, all these operations update the cell state (Ct), which plays the crucial role of

size of Nx×Nb, while the tensors ht and Ct have dimensions given by the product Nh×Nb.

Structural Implementation
In addition to the structural changes to the generator, improvements are also being made
in handling the inputs of categorical and numerical values. Neural networks usually only

3.2
Population fitting
The previous sections have explained WGANs and DATGANs. While these models can
generate a sample of synthetic agents, the objective of this thesis is to generate a popu­
lation that fits certain marginal distributions for particular attributes.
3.2.1
IPF
As explained in Chapter 2, Iterative Proportional Fitting (IPF) is itself a method of popula­
tion synthesis. It allows the fitting of probability distributions for combinations of attributes
in a dataset by representing them in a matrix and iteratively adjusting them to specified
marginal sums. The algorithm uses information about known row and column sums (e.g.
from census data) as boundary conditions, and an initial solution (e.g. from survey data)
that represents the correlations between the data. The iterative process of the algorithm
improves the fitting of the matrix. The result of the algorithm is a matrix representing prob­
ability distributions for combinations of features where the sum of the rows and columns
matches the specified sums. The formal definition of the algorithm is as follows:

To subsequently generate a population dataset from the matrix, a further formal sampling
step is required. In this case, a quota­based sampling strategy is used. From the gener­
ated synthetic populations of WGAN and DATGAN, the selected combinations of features
are first grouped and then sampled according to the probabilities modelled by IPF. This
produces a synthetic dataset that reflects the specific distribution of characteristics in the
population as a whole.
Although this simple technique is particularly useful for correcting data tables where there
are problems such as incomplete, inaccurate, outdated or random information, it has lim­
itations. The main problem is the complexity of multiple combinations of attributes. The
challenge is not only to obtain information about the joint distributions of these categories,
but also to keep the diversity of the subsequent dataset high. For example, information
may be available about the combination of several attributes of an agent, but the synthet­
ically generated dataset of agents may contain few or no agents with that combination
of attributes. Shifts in the distributions will inevitably lead to a situation where sampling
from these small groups of agents will result in a significant repetition of the same indi­
viduals. For this reason, specific characteristics, such as age groups, gender ratios and
income levels, should be used in analyses where extensive information are available and
the synthetic population has a high degree of variance.
3.2.2

A more promising approach is a modified version of the DATGAN that incorporates a mix­
ture of latent conditionality and data completion as ”conditional inputs” ­ hence ciDATGAN
[30]. In theory, this conditionality serves two main purposes:
• To correct for biases in a dataset, such as unrepresentative samples.
• To merge information from two datasets to create representative synthetic popula­
tions. In practice, the model is specifically trained to generate, for example, detailed
mobility variables based on socio­economic census variables (cf. IPF).
By integrating the additional information from the census data, a detailed synthetic dataset
is created that covers the entire population. In the domain of population synthesis, datasets
cover a spectrum of detail, ranging from highly detailed small samples to less detailed
ones. ciDATGAN classifies the former, characterised by many variables but few rows,
as a feeder dataset (TF), while the latter, comprising more extensive samples with few

3.3
Statistical Validation and Comparison
Once the models have been trained and samples generated, it is important to evaluate the
models and sampling methods in terms of their training performance and generation ac­
curacy to compare them with each other. Finally, the degree of realism of the conditioned
and newly sampled synthetic populations must be determined on the basis of the real
Danish population. All the following methods are based on the premise that the original
dataset is initially divided into a separate training set and a test set.
The quality of the training performance of a model can be described in terms of the evolu­
tion and convergence of the loss functions or the min­max game between the adversarial
multilayer perceptrons. This can provide meaningful information about the possible out­
comes of the generated population, which is important in the context of optimising indi­
vidual models [34, 8]. However, the optimised parameterisation of the hyperparameters
of all models is only discussed in a limited way in this study. Instead, the validation of
the generation accuracy and the representation of a real population are crucial for the
evaluation of the models. The information content of the generated populations can be
evaluated in several ways. In the literature, some particularly useful methods have been
explicitly introduced for high­dimensional datasets. This is because for high­dimensional
datasets, it is unreliable to compare the joint distribution of the sample data with the joint
distribution of the test set due to data sparsity [2, 8, 7].
The central approach is to validate the models by assessing how accurately the sampled
data represents the marginal distributions of the test set [7]. A comprehensive evaluation
is achieved when not only individual characteristics yield good results in terms of the match
of the marginal distributions, but also aggregations of specific, meaningful combinations
of characteristics are tested [8]. By doing so, it is possible to determine not only whether
the distributions of individual attributes are interpreted correctly, but also whether the re­
lationships between these attributes resemble the distributions of the original dataset.
For this purpose, frequency tables are computed for each individual attribute and for cer­
tain combinations of attributes. (Note: Numerical variables must be binned). In order
to compare the results, typical static standard metrics are then determined. The Stan­
dardized Root Mean Square Error (SRMSE) [13], Coefficient of determination (R2) and
Pearson’s correlation (σ) are used. When denoting a frequency list with πsynth for the

4.1
Use case: SimMobility
The Danish Centre for Transport Analytics has been working for several years with var­
ious authorities and organisations to collect population data in combination with travel
behaviour. The overall aim of these groups is to develop an understanding of local and
national similarities and differences in travel behaviour and demographic trends. At the
centre of this interaction between urban planning and transport studies stands the Sim­
Mobility framework. SimMobility is a multi­scale simulation platform developed at the
Singapore­MIT Alliance for Research and Technology (SMART) that integrates various
mobility­sensitive behaviour models. This platform is capable of simulating millions of peo­
ple and units with their respective road users (pedestrians, drivers, etc.), time­dependent
events (traffic lights, GPS probes, etc.) and modes of transport (bicycle, car, public trans­
port, etc.) to study how people will react in future mobility scenarios. However, in order
to obtain diverse and realistic agent­based simulation results for real­life scenarios, it is
necessary to feed the entire framework with a large amount of data. For this reason, the
authors emphasise the necessary core dataset of a representative, diverse and realistic
population with individual agents. More specifically, SimMobility imposes certain require­
ments on the synthetic population. Each individual agent in the simulation must have a
fixed number of attributes that provide specific information; these are listed in the Table
4.1.
However, further information is required to assess and validate the realism of an individ­
ual agent of the generated synthetic population for this application. Due to the natural
complexity of real­world scenarios, which are characterised by a variety of demographic,
social and behavioural factors, the preparation of a comprehensive dataset is necessary.
It is therefore desirable to provide additional data to the specific attributes, that increases
insights for the model.

Careful pre­processing of the dataset, taking into account NaN values, categorical and

robust training and the effective generation of synthetic populations, which are analysed
and evaluated in the following chapter.

Integration of multiple DataSources
The decision to integrate the external data sources
is supported by a clear motivation based on the extension of the content with relevant
and required information. Information from the three TU datasets, the different Denmark
Statistics datasets and OTM Zones will be integrated.
Tables (A), (F) and (G) are used to create 4 additional attributes. The attributes KidsBe­

Handling of NaN values
Choosing a particular imputation strategy for handling NaN val­
ues can affect the results of GAN training. Choosing an inappropriate strategy may cause
the GAN to learn incorrect patterns or tend to generate unrealistic data 3.2.2. Therefore,
different approaches are chosen for numerical and categorical attributes. The intent is
to change the underlying distributions of the individual variables of these attributes only
when necessary. Hence, the first step is to develop a function that allows a stochastic
and data­driven imputation of missing values, taking into account the distribution in the
different groups of the dataset. The exact function and explanation can be found in the
appendix A.2.

Data Modification
After adding additional information/attributes and imputing NaN val­
ues in the data, it is important to perform the modification of the categorical and numerical
attributes to better prepare the data for GAN training. When performing this step, it is cru­
cial to consider different embedding techniques for categorical and numerical variables.
When using one­hot encoding for categorical values, the consideration of dimensionality
plays a central role. The aim is to eliminate unwanted categories. This step helps to re­
duce the dimensionality of the data and focus the model on the relevant information. For
example, individual categories of the attribute Education were aggregated (11 →8). In
contrast, normalising or computing Gaussian mixture models for numerical data requires
a specific adjustment of the range of values to eliminate outliers. Without this adjustment,
outliers could distort the mean and standard deviation during normalisation, leading to
incorrect normalisations. They could also interfere with the estimation of distribution pa­
rameters when using VLTMs (3.2.2). This can lead to problems with convergence in the
training phase and inferior generated data, as outliers affect the modelling of the data
distribution. For these reasons, the ranges of the income attributes are drastically com­
pressed. The values of IncRespondent2000 are capped at 8 million kr and the values of
IncFamily2000 are capped at 10 million DKK. The exclusion of 0.0008% ( 75 rows) of the
data leads to a significant improvement in the standard deviation (IncRespondent2000:
459 →195, IncFamily2000: 430 →342) and thus improves the accuracy during training.

5
Population Synthesises
The previous chapters, Methodology (Chapter 3) and Data (Chapter 4), discussed in de­
tail the methodology used to develop the WGAN, DATGAN and ciDATGAN models, along
with insights into the data used to train these models. In this chapter, we explore the
details of the specific implementation process for generating a synthetic population and
provide a comprehensive analysis of the results. The primary goal of this research is to
create a synthetic population of agents that accurately reflects the demographic profile of
Denmark, adheres to specified marginal distributions, and fulfils the criteria for seamless
integration into the SimMobility framework. The approach involves training the aforemen­
tioned models with a pre­processed dataset, followed by a careful validation process. The
initial focus is on identifying the most suitable model capable of generating a representa­
tive population of synthetic agents. The results obtained from each model are subjected to
detailed statistical analysis to enable meaningful conclusions to be drawn. These results
are then used to compare different methods for fitting the synthetic population to specific
marginal distributions.
5.1
Implementation
Before any models can be trained, the data needs to be split into a training set (70%)
and a test set (30%). As the HomeAdrZone attribute has almost 7000 different values,
it is very important to use a stratified sampling technique. This ensures the presence of
at least one data point from each zone code in the training set, emphasizing its crucial
importance. The resulting training dataset contains 97,895 observations, while the test
dataset contains 41,724 observations. This split is applied once to ensure that all models
are trained on the same dataset. Next, the parameters of the models are discussed.
Wasserstein GAN
The WGAN model is implemented using Keras and TensorFlow in
Python and is based on models developed by Garrido et al. [2]. As explained in the chap­
ter 3, the WGAN model consists of two neural networks: the generator and the critic. The
model has hyperparameters that control the architecture of these two neural networks
and the training of the WGAN model. While there is no explicit optimisation of the hyper­
parameters, they are tuned and different combinations are explored. Exemplary changes
are tested exploratively on learning rate and batch size. A crucial aspect of the WGAN
model is the coding and scaling of the training dataset. To use it, categorical attributes are
one­hot encoded and numerical values are scaled. When examining numerical attributes
such as IncRespondent2000 and IncFamily200 in Chapter 4, it can be observed that they
are inherently strictly positive and right­skewed. Therefore, the Yeo­Johnson power trans­
form [46] is chosen to scale the numerical attribute. This transformation is known to be
suitable for reducing skewness and approaching normality [46].
Directed Acyclic Tabular GAN
The DATGAN model is based on the datgan 2.1.10
project developed by Lederrey [47] and published on PyPI [47]. It is also implemented in
Python using Keras and TensorFlow. As explained previously in section 3.1.2, this modi­
fied GAN consists not only of ”simple” multi­layer perceptions, but also includes an LSTM
model, an algorithm for interpreting the DAG, and dense layers for processing and adapt­
ing data types. Due to the complexity of optimising this model, which includes not only the
learning rate and dropout, but also the balancing of loss functions, label smoothing and
sampling techniques, the model is implemented with the default parameters. These are
Synthetic Population Generation using Diffusion Models: A case study in Denmark
43
==PAGE:54==
based on successful tests with the Wasserstein loss function by the authors to generate
a synthetic population [8]. Furthermore, the aspect of expert knowledge is given more
attention. Based on the definition of a DAG (3.1.2), the construction of the graph is com­
pletely left to the user, as long as it respects the concept of directness and the exclusion of
cycles. Lederrey, Hillel, and Bierlaire [8] mention that a sensible approach to determining
a good DAG is to test multiple versions. The choice of data structure for all attributes goes
hand in hand with the characteristics of the DAG. Due to the choice of standard param­
eters and the Wasserstein loss function, the results also show that the training dataset
should be used almost exclusively with categorical attributes [8]. This option is kept open.
On closer inspection, however, it seems to be appropriate with the exception of four at­
tributes IncFamily2000, IncRespondent2000, RespAgeCorrect, WorkHoursPw (referred
to in section 4.3), all other attributes are one­hot encoded categorical values. The numer­
ical values are scaled by the implemented pre­processing function of the datgan­project
using VGGMs (3.1.2).
conditional input DATGAN
The ciDATGAN, as described previously, is a modified ver­
sion of the DATGAN model. Due to the favourable implementation of the changes in the
same datgan­project, the programming environment and the implementation of the pa­
rameters of the model, including the DAG and the encoding of the attributes, are exactly
the same. In contrast to DATGAN, however, ciDATGAN requires information about the at­
tribute to be conditioned during training. Following the sampling and requirements for the
dataset, the HomeAdrZone turns out to be a solid choice of an initial conditional attribute.
When setting parameters of the models, it quickly becomes clear that each of them offers
numerous possibilities to adapt the training and possibly improve the result. As no optimi­
sation approaches are pursued in this work, the following investigations are carried out to
determine the best version of each model:
• Two models of WGAN and DATGAN are trained with different numbers of numerical
and categorical attributes to observe the impact on the results.
• Two different DAGs are tested to assess the effect of changing sequential depen­
dencies among the attributes in DATGANs.
• Two different conditioned ciDATGANs are trained to study the potential effects of
combining multiple conditions.
5.2
Validation Concept
The validation of the models and methods for generating a representative Danish popu­
lation is carried out in two steps: (1) first the models are compared with each other, and
then (2) the methods for conditioning on a representative Danish population are analysed.
In the first step, a suitable parameterisation is determined for each GAN model and com­
pared with the corresponding models. For this purpose, a sample of the same size as the
test set is generated after training. The marginal distributions of all individual attributes of
this sample are then compared with the corresponding marginal distributions of the test
set using frequency lists. The statistical parameters and methods introduced in Chapter
3 are used to evaluate the model, including SRMSE, R2 and Pearson correlation. The av­
erage of the individual metrics across the attributes is then calculated as the comparable
value of the models. The values for HomeAdrZone, PrimOccZone, IncRespondent2000,
IncFamily2000 and RespAgeCorrect are removed from the average and analysed sepa­
rately. The same metrics are then measured for specifically selected joint distributions.
44
Synthetic Population Generation using Diffusion Models: A case study in Denmark
==PAGE:55==
Based on the results of this statistical validation, the best version is selected for further
population adjustment. The comparison between models is then based on the objectively
best tested version.
The second step builds on the results of the first step. In this case, the realism of the
different conditioning methods is tested. It is good practice to obtain information on distri­
butions from other sources in order to carry out the conditioning and validation using these
parameters [8]. For this purpose, the original TU dataset and the marginal distribution ex­
tracted from Denmarks Statistic are used. Two variants are tested: the conditioning of the
attribute HomeAdrZones (from 2020) and the conditioning of several attributes, namely
MunicipalityOrigin, Gender, PopSocio and Age (from 2020). For each of these, about 6
million samples are drawn for conditioning (the population of Denmark is about 5.9 mil­
lion). In the case of the WGAN and DATGAN models, the distributions are then fitted to
the population by IPF and resampling. In the case of ciDATGAN, sampling is done directly
on the basis of the conditioned marginal distributions. The representative Danish popula­
tions generated in this way are compared using the same statistical metrics as described
above.

5.4
Post­Processing for SimMobilty
As described in Chapter 4, the SimMobility framework requires specific input attributes.
While the pre­processing of TU data for generative model training ensures that all required
attributes are present, post­processing is also required. In order to make the population
a suitable input for the SimMobility model, additional adjustments need to be made. One
example is adding binary attributes which are derived from categorical attributes. One
problem that has not yet come into play is the fact that, due to probabilities, the gen­
erated synthetic population contains impossible combinations. For example, attributes
such as HousehNumPers and FamNumPers are both generated by the model. While
their definitions differ, TU specifies the family as a subset of the household, which means
that FamNumPers should always be less than or equal to HousehNumPers. Although
a robust model should capture this trend and generate only a few samples in which this
relationship is violated, this is not guaranteed. To ensure that the population is a suitable
input, these attributes are therefore post­processed and corrected. If HousehNumPers
is smaller than FamNumPers in this case, it is assigned the value of FamNumPers. An­
other example is the SimMobility requirement for IncRespondent2000 and IncFamily2000,
where the former should be less than or equal to the latter. Similar to the previous case,
this relationship is not guaranteed for all generated samples, so a correction is required
before integration into the SimMobility framework. Needless to say, that this rather simple
reworking has also an impact on how well the modelled population actually corresponds to
the original representative Danish population. Among other things, this will be discussed
in more detail in the next chapter 6.

6
Discussion
The previous chapter covers a comprehensive comparison of several GAN models. This
chapter discusses the results of these studies in order to answer the research questions:
• How can data on population dynamics be considered to generate a diverse and
representative Danish population synthetically to capture demographic complexities
and variations?
• What methods can be used to create a synthetic population that fits various marginal
distributions for certain attributes?
• What approaches can be used to compare and validate the generated synthetic
Danish population using demographic statistical indicators?
Additionally, the chapter covers the limitations of the study and future work.
6.1
Findings and Limitations
The main results of this study show that generative models such as WGAN and DATGAN,
when trained on the TU dataset, are able to generate a synthetic population of agents
that adequately represents the Danish population. In particular, they are characterised
by good performance compared to the test dataset, especially when the marginal distri­
butions are evaluated separately. However, looking at these marginal distributions alone
does not provide insight into the correlations between the attributes. Therefore, partial
joint distributions are also evaluated, with DATGAN achieving promising results for al­
most all joint distributions analysed. The WGAN model has difficulties, especially with
attributes that have a high number of unique values, such as ”HomeAdrZone”. However,
the DATGAN model successfully overcomes this obstacle, partly due to the influence of
expert knowledge in the training process.
Interestingly, the numerical income attributes show a different trend. The models are not
able to generate samples of sufficient quality. In particular, the DATGAN model performs
worse than the WGAN model. This is definitely a limiting factor, as an insufficient repre­
sentation of the income distribution leads to a less realistic population. As it is known that
generative models have difficulties in generating numerical features, one solution could
be to group the income attributes and make them categorical. However, this reduces the
variety of possible samples and does not meet the requirements of the SimMobility frame­
work. Therefore, approaches to improve the quality of the representation of income in the
population need to be investigated.
In addition, two ciDATGAN models are trained: one conditioned on HomeAdrZone and
the other on MunicipalityOrigin, Gender, RespAgeCorrect and PopSocio. Like WGAN
and DATGAN, they are tested against the test set and evaluated using individual marginal
and joint distributions. Both models show improvements over the DATGAN model. As a
ciDATGAN model is trained using conditions, it requires conditional input for the sample,
i.e. it is not able to generate synthetic agents without pre­specified input. However, this is
useful if the population is to reflect certain marginal distributions for specific attributes, or
if a particular set of agents is to be generated. Under these conditions they are especially
interesting for the SimMobility framework.
To generate a population corresponding to certain marginal distributions, the best versions
of the GAN models (WGAN­1, DATGAN­5) are selected. Populations representative of

67
==PAGE:78==
the year 2020 are generated in two different ways: One corresponds to the distribution of
the number of people in OTM zones and the other to the distributions of MunicipalityOrigin,
Gender, RespAgeCorrect and PopSocio. The results of both methods successfully show
that these approaches can be used to create a synthetic population that represents the
Danish population by adjusting the marginal distributions.
In fact, the methods provide further insights due to their different approaches. When
using IPF and resampling on the generated synthetic agents, it is possible to fit each
attribute to each marginal distribution without having to train a new model. However, this
method leads to less diversity as the quota­based sampling method is only a resampling
and does not generate new samples (DATGAN:613,854 vs ciDATGAN:3,540,206). When
generating a population with ciDATGAN, the model is trained on certain conditions, so
that the population can only be fitted to the conditioned attributes. A direct comparison
therefore shows a tendency in favour of ciDATGAN, even though a costly re­training has
to be accepted if the conditions change. However, both methods tend to be useful.
Finally, the extent to which the generated populations represent the Danish population
is analysed. For this purpose, the information from the data sources of the TU dataset
and Denmarks Statistic is compared, as they represent characteristics of the Danish pop­
ulation. The comparison with the TU dataset shows that the generated populations have
similar distributions to the TU data, especially when the marginal distributions are con­
sidered individually. This is to be expected, as the models were trained on this set of
data. Overall, however, all populations show some deviation from the data from Den­
marks Statistic. However, to be able to claim that they represent the Danish population
well, they would need to be closer to each other.
As all methods create synthetic populations based on Danish travel survey data, certain
marginal distributions may be closer to the Danish population. Nevertheless, the con­
clusion is that when comparing these methods, it cannot be said that one is better than
the other when it comes to creating a synthetic population that represents the Danish
population.
Lastly, it should be noted that some post­processing of the generated synthetic popu­
lations is necessary to meet the requirements of the SimMobility framework. A limiting
factor is that it is not possible to ensure that the population does not contain agents with
impossible combinations of characteristics. For example, this could be a child under the
age of 18 who has a driving licence, or a very young child who is employed. To adapt
the population to SimMobility, some of these combinations are corrected, as mentioned
in the 5.4 section.
6.2
Future Work
The work done so far demonstrates the effectiveness of different models in generating syn­
thetic populations within the required framework of SimMobility. Nevertheless, there are
a number of limitations and therefore opportunities for future work. This section explores
potential avenues for further improving the idea of generating a representative population.
These can be divided into three areas. Improvements range from the choice of architec­
ture to training strategies and new approaches to further improve the conditioning aspect.
An important area of optimisation is the fine­tuning of the architecture of the generator
and discriminator networks. Experimenting with the number of layers, nodes and over­
all structure can have a significant impact on the model’s ability to capture complicated
population characteristics. Techniques such as adjusting learning rates, using advanced
68
Synthetic Population Generation using Diffusion Models: A case study in Denmark
==PAGE:79==
optimisation algorithms and implementing regularisation methods help to stabilise training
and allow better divergence between generator and discriminator loss functions.
Exploring different loss functions is another route to optimisation. Tailoring loss functions
to the specific requirements of population synthesis can improve model performance. An
obvious option here is the concept of the Wasserstein Gradient Penalty extension [21]. A
loss function that has proven particularly successful when dealing with numerical values
[8]. This can contribute to more stable training dynamics and improved output quality.
Data pre­processing and augmentation techniques are also used. Optimising the prepa­
ration of input data for training and augmenting datasets to introduce diversity can have a
far­reaching impact on the model’s ability to generalise and generate a representative pop­
ulation. In particular, the ability to implement expert knowledge can achieve improvements
in the dependencies between variables in the generated population through customised
DAGs.
Another approach to improving synthetic populations is the concept of using multiple
GANs in image processing. This could involve constraining the models with specific rules
to ensure that the populations generated are more representative of real­world scenarios.
This also picks up on the idea of running models on smaller subsets of attributes. One
approach could be to use the existing dataset based on highly dependent attributes, such
as the fact that a student has no information on working hours, wages, workplace location,
commuting days and other work­related attributes. In contrast, the working population has
explicit information on all attributes. It would therefore be possible to separate the dataset
on the basis of population groups, which would later be reunited according to the true dis­
tribution. It is also possible to eliminate the numerical inconsistencies in the generation of
synthetic populations by treating the inputs for GANs differently. One suggested method
is to use regression or other predictive models to estimate important variables, such as
income, in the post­processing step. This would improve the accuracy and realism of the
synthetic populations generated.
To further improve the conditioning aspect, an interesting possibility is the integration of
conditioning from other diffusion models, such as Large Language Models (LLM). The inte­
gration of advanced language models could contribute to a more nuanced understanding
of the conditional dependencies within the synthetic population and allow the generation
of a wide variety of arbitrary scenarios. The exploration of the application of techniques
published in the paper ”TabuLa: Harnessing Language Models for Tabular Data Synthesis”
in October 2023 explicitly points to this [48]. It highlights the potential of large language
models (LLMs) to generate realistic tabular data, but also acknowledges their limitations,
such as long training times and limited reusability for new tasks. The paper presents
a new approach called Tabula, a tabular data synthesiser based on the structure of lan­
guage models. Tabula addresses the shortcomings of using pre­trained language models
to synthesise tabular data by proposing a dedicated underlying model that is specifically
tailored for this purpose.
In summary, the optimization of GAN models and methods for creating realistic popula­
tions is a multifaceted task. Researchers and practitioners can explore various dimen­
sions, including architectural choices, training strategies, loss functions and data pre­
processing as well as data post­processing to refine and enhance the capabilities of GANs
in generating diverse and representative populations.

7
Conclusion and Outlook
This thesis compares the generation of synthetic population data and methods to fit the
population to certain attributes. Based on three promising Generative Adversarial Net­
works (GANs) from literature, the aim is to generate a representative synthetic population
suitable for the SimMobility framework.
The models Wasserstein GAN (WGAN), Directed Acyclic Tabular GAN (DATGAN) and
Conditional Input DATGAN (ciDATGAN) are selected based on comparable case studies
and promising results with respect to the research questions. Due to their different struc­
tures, all GAN models are prepared differently in order to obtain good comparable results.
The data types of the individual attributes and the choice of the loss function are crucial
factors. Additionally, the integration of expert knowledge through directed acyclic graphs
and dedicated conditions also plays a decisive role.
The evaluation using typical standard metrics from the statistics and machine learning
community, such as the Standardised Root Mean Squared Error (SRMSE), the Pearson
correlation coefficient and the coefficient of determination, shows that all models gener­
ally perform better when less numerical and more categorical attributes are used. This is
particularly evident as a clear trend in comparisons with many and reduced numbers of
numerical values. Furthermore, it is observed that the WGAN model with specially scaled
numerical values ­ illustrated by the example of the income attribute ­ outperforms the re­
sults obtained with data transformed using the Variational Gaussian Mixture Model in both
the DATGAN and ciDATGAN models. This is reversed when the distributions of individual
attributes and combinations of attributes are compared in detail. DATGAN and ciDATGAN
show that significantly better results can be obtained by interpreting the relationships and
dependencies of individual attributes and the resulting highly modified GAN architectures.
However, this is highly dependent on the interpretation and design of the Directed Acyclic
Graph (DAG). An exemplary comparison, where instructions for modifying such a DAG
are followed, shows significant differences in the measured statistical metrics. This high­
lights the general sensitivity of these generative models to the training dataset and the
parameters of the structures themselves.
Conditioning methods are applied in two variants to compare the generated populations.
Iterative Proportional Fitting (IPF), resampling strategies and the modified ciDATAGAN ar­
chitecture are utilised. These approaches are used to generate two populations in which
the marginal distribution of HomeAdrZone and the marginal distributions of Municipality­
Origin, Gender, RespAgeCorrect and PopSocio are adjusted. Demographic trends from
Denmarks Statistic are extracted to assess the representation of the Danish population.
The results of this thesis demonstrate that these methods can be successfully used to
create a population that corresponds to a specific marginal distribution. However, it out­
lines that the method based on resampling with IPF leads to reduced diversity. In contrast
to ciDATGAN, where most of the individuals are unique, the previous variant of IPF and
resampling can in many cases only be used to resample individuals that have already
been created. Even if the results as a whole do not indicate a preferred method, the last
points can be interpreted as a confirmation that the ciDATGAN model is better suitable to
create diverse synthetic population.
The model comparisons and the tested methods for conditioning a population clearly show
that the ciDATGAN represents a solid development of the conventional GAN. The versa­

tile possibilities of this architecture can be well used to generate a population in respect
to the requirements of the SimMobility concept. Further research should be carried out
to determine the best model, especially with regard to typical exploratory optimisation
approaches.
In order to improve and enhance the described models, the following aspects should be
investigated:
• A detailed optimisation of the hyperparameters of individual GAN models that may
contribute to further performance improvements.
• Investigate effective strategies for integrating and processing both numerical and
categorical data in GAN models that improve the sampling of values.
• Investigate methods for the collaborative use of multiple GANs to generate a com­
prehensive synthetic population dataset in separate parts.
• Investigate the possibilities of using large language models to generate synthetic
tabular data, in particular for joint distributions of representative population charac­
teristics.
These research aspects hold potential for advancing and optimizing GAN­based condi­
tioning approaches for synthesizing population data.

Listing A.1: Replace NaNs in Categories
The ‘replace_nans_categories‘ function is designed to replace NaN values in a specific
categorical column of a DataFrame. First, a copy of the DataFrame is created, retaining
only the relevant columns. Group sizes are then calculated to analyze the distribution of
values within groups. Subsequently, a DataFrame for NaN values is created and merged
with the group information DataFrame.
For each group, a sampling function is defined, which randomly selects a value based on
the distribution of values within the group. This function is applied to the merged data to
replace NaN values with random values from the corresponding groups.
The results are merged back into the original DataFrame, with NaN values in the cate­
gorical column replaced by the newly selected values. Finally, the updated DataFrame is
returned.
