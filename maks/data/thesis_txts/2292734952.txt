Abstract 
 
Prostate cancer (PCa) is the most common non-cutaneous malignancy and a major cause of cancer-
related deaths among men, with approximately 280.000 new cases in the US each year. Typical 
therapy for localized PCa is radical prostatectomy, and for advanced disease – androgen depravation. 
However, the effectiveness of this treatment for the metastatic disease is limited and most patients 
relapse and develop castration resistant prostate cancer (CRPC) for which none effective treatment 
currently exist. 
The research group lead by Professor Flores-Morales at the University of Copenhagen has recently 
performed a global quantitative proteomic profiling of tumors confined to the prostate gland and 
metastatic CRPC tumors and compared the protein expression between the two. Among the proteins 
overexpressed in metastatic CRPC they have found several chromatin modifiers. One of such proteins 
was the lysine-specific demethylase PHF2.  
This projects aims to address PHF2 actions in prostate cancer cells and its potential role in PCa 
progression. The role of lysine-specific demethylase PHF2 in prostate cancer has not been addressed 
before and the function of this protein is not fully understood.  
We found that modifying PHF2 levels influences cell proliferation and invasive capacity. We also 
showed that PHF2 is a positive regulator of osteogenic genes; BMP2 and osteopontin, by which it 
could contribute to the development of bone metastasis. Finally, our data suggests that PHF2 
demethylates H3K9me2, implying its role as a positive regulator of transcription.

Introduction 
 
The prostate is a small, walnut shaped gland surrounding the urethra that produces seminal fluid and 
facilitates sperm motility (1). It is structurally composed by a pseudostratified epithelium supported 
by fibromuscular stroma and it can be subdivided into three zones; central, peripheral and transition 
zone. The secretory epithelium forms prostatic ducts composed of finally differentiated luminal cells, 
which produce prostate specific antigen (PSA) and prostatic acid phosphatase, with a layer of 
progenitor basal cells beneath them and scattered neuroendocrine cells (2).  Luminal cells express 
androgens and their growth is androgen dependent (as opposed to basal cells that do not depend on 
androgens)(1).  
The most common lesions associated with the prostate are: prostatitis, benign prostatic hyperplasia 
(BPH), prostatic intraepithelial neoplasia (PIN) and prostate cancer (PCa). Most cancer lesions arise in 
the peripheral zone (which composes around 70% of glandular tissue), while most BPH lesions 
develop in the transition zone. Central zone is the least prone to all the prostate diseases (3). 
 
Prostate cancer. 
Prostate cancer is the most common non-cutaneous malignancy diagnosed in men in the Western 
societies (figure 1). In 2014 in the United States it accounted for 27% of all incident cases in men, and 
it was the second cause of cancer-related deaths (10% of all cases) (4). As the population ages, the 
number of prostate cancer incidents worldwide grows, but age is not the only risk factor.  Several 
studies have indicated that inflammation, as well as race and dietary habits are involved in disease 
development (5). In most cases (over 95%) prostate cancers are adenocarcinomas of epithelial origin, 
but other histologic variants may arise (for example, the very aggressive neuroendocrine prostate 
tumors known as small cell carcinoma of the prostate).

In the US, the 5-year survival rate for localized PCa is almost 100%, while the survival rate drops to 
only 28% if the cancer is already metastatic at the diagnosis time (4). This indicates that early 
detection is crucial. Wide implementation of PSA screenings has dramatically increased early 
diagnosis of PCa. 
PSA is an androgen regulated gene belonging to the family of kallikrein-like serine proteases, 
secreted exclusively by prostate epithelial cells (6). Due to the inability of PSA measurements to 
predict tumor aggressiveness, increased diagnoses also resulted in excessive overtreatment of 
patients bearing indolent tumors if left untreated. Therefore discovering new molecular markers of 
the disease aggressiveness is required to narrow down patients with potentially lethal tumors who 
will benefit from an early active treatment. 
The development of PCa is a complex process with a cumulative sequence of genetic and epigenetic 
alterations. Prostatic intraepithelial neoplasia (PIN) is generally considered the precursor of PCa. PIN 
is a common name for various morphologic lesions, like basal cell hyperplasia or inflamed acini,that 
can precede the development of cancer by at least 10 years (7). A schematic progression of PCa is 
shown in figure 2.

There are several common genetic aberrations involved in PCa development. Loss of NKX3.1 protein 
expression is a frequent early event in PCa. This homeobox gene localizes to a chromosomal hotspot 
region and NKX3.1 mutant mice are shown to develop PIN-like lesions (9). Other commonly occurring 
processes are the loss of the tumor suppressor gene, PTEN/MMAC1 and the increased levels of the 
oncogene MYC.  PTEN maps to the 10q23.3 chromosomal region, and it is frequently mutated or 
deleted in other types of cancers (10), while MYC is a transcription factor that regulates cell 
proliferation and metabolism and it is frequently found up-regulated in prostate tumors as well as in 
PIN (11).  
As mentioned above, early detection of PCa is crucial for an optimal management of the disease. This 
is because effective treatment exists for early PCa in the form of radical prostatectomy and radiation. 
In case of a more advanced disease it is followed or replaced by hormonal therapy known as 
androgen deprivation therapy (ADT), which aims to reduce the circulating levels of androgens. 
Luminal epithelial prostate cells and PCa cells are dependent on androgens for survival, thus ADT 
initially reduces PSA levels and tumor size (8). Unfortunately, most patients eventually progress into 
castration resistant prostate cancer (CRPC) as their tumors adapts to androgen-deprived 
environment, for which no effective treatment is currently available. 
 
There are several ways in which the cells overcome the lack of androgens (12):  
- 
Androgen receptor (AR) overexpression by which they become hypersensitive 
- 
Gain-of –function AR mutation 
- 
Ligand independent activation of AR 
- 
Endogenous androgen synthesis by tumor cells  
 
Like most solid tumors prostate cancer can metastasize to distant places, like liver or lungs, yet the 
most common is the bone metastasis (seen in 80% of men with castration-resistant metastatic 
prostate cancer) (13). This is also a major cause of morbidity among PCa patients. Development of 
metastasis is a multistep process, starting with decreased cell adhesion and partial degradation of 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
ECM, intravasation, circulation and then extravasation into the metastatic site. The bone 
microenvironment, composed mainly by type I collagen (95%) and other non-collagen proteins and 
proteoglycans, such as osteopontin and osteonectin (14), is considered important for attracting 
prostate tumor cells to this site. 
Distinct to other cancer types that commonly cause bone lysis, PCa metastases predominantly result 
in osteoblastic, bone forming lesions (as seen on radiographies). However, the bone generated is 
abnormal (‘woven bone’) and easily fractured. Osteoclast number and activity are also increased in 
the metastasis, and patients have high levels of serum and urinary markers of bone resorption (such 
as bone-specific alkaline phosphatase), indicating high bone turnover (1). Most therapies are 
osteoclast-targeted and they have mostly palliative effects (13). Therefore, there is a need to better 
understand the mechanism of PCa progression to the bones, in order to develop new treatments and 
improve patients’ survival. 
 
Epigenetics and its role in cancer.  
Chromatin is a highly structured entity that allows compacted packaging of the eukaryotic DNA. Its 
fundamental unit, the nucleosome, consists of a histone octamer core (two copies of each histone 
protein: H3, H4, H2A, and H2B) and 147 bp of DNA wrapped around it (15).  
Chromatin is a dynamic structure that is constantly undergoing modifications. Its spatial and 
temporal changes regulate gene expression by swinging from compact, repressive structure to 
transcriptionally active and structurally available state (16). Histone tails at the N-terminal are 
unstructured domains that are especially prone to harboring modifications. There are many types of 
histone post-translational modifications (PTMs) and several are listed in table 1.

There are two mechanisms of action of histone modifications. The first is directly influencing the 
overall chromatin structure and the second regulates the binding of nonhistone, effector molecules. 
Acetylation for example is neutralizing the basic charge of lysine, and it can turn the chromatin into 
more accessible form (17).  
The histone code hypothesis describes how various histone modifications, on one or more tails, act 
together or sequentially and lead to various downstream events (19). Modifications at specific amino 
acid position, such as methylation or acetylation are established by a group of enzymes referred to as 
writers. Those patterns of modifications are recognized by enzymes with specificity towards the 
modified histones, called readers. Readers often form part of a larger protein complex possessing 
chromatin-modifying activities that can influence its structure. Finally, enzymes that remove histone 
modifications are called erasers (20). The model of how chromatin modifiers interpret and modify 
the information encoded by PTMs is presented in figure 3.  
  
Figure 3. Interpreting the histone code. The model showing how epigenetic information is read and 
modified by chromatin modifying proteins (20).  
 
Histone methylation plays an important role in regulating gene expression. The process is catalyzed 
by enzymes called methyltransferases. Histone lysine methyltransferases (HKMTs) function by 
transferring a methyl group from S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) to a lysine's ε-amino group and 
arginine methyltransferases (PRMT) into the ω-guanidino group of arginine. Addition of a methyl 
group does not change the charge of the histone protein (21). The opposite action is conducted by 
histone lysine demethylases, and there are two families of these proteins: the lysine-specific 
demethylase family (LSD), composed by two members: LSD1 and LSD2, and the JmjC domain-
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
containing demethylases (22). Histone Lysine modifying enzymes normally possess a high degree of 
substrate specificity with respect to their target amino acid and its methylation state.  
The use of ChIP-seq (chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by sequencing) methodologies in 
genome-scale location studies has shown that enrichment of specific histone modifications 
correlated to different chromatin states (17). Thus, H3K4me2, H3K4me3 are generally located at 
transcriptional start sites (23). H3K36me3 and H3K79me3 are found at transcriptionally active 
regions, while silenced sites are abundant in H3K9me3, H3K9me2, H3K27me3 and H4K20me3. 
H3K4me3 was found in both types (17). However, how these modifications influence each other and 
the chromatin is still a subject of intensive research. For instance, although H3K9 methylation is 
generally considered repressive, the levels of trimethyl H3K9 has been found elevated in actively 
transcribed genes, specially within the coding regions (24). The reason for this could be that the 
character of a histone mark depends on the context; H3K9me3 would be repressive at promoters, 
but activating in the coding region.  
 
Epigenetic changes have been linked to genome instability, defective chromosome segregation and 
cancer. Mice deficient for Suv39h methyltransferase activity (which regulates H3K9 methylation) 
show impaired viability, increased chromosomal instability and an increased risk of tumorigenesis 
(25). DNA hypermethylation of promoter regions and gene silencing associated with it can cause loss 
of tumor suppressor genes (26). Deregulation of histone demethylases has also been involved in 
tumorigenesis. Genomic locus containing the JMJD2C gene was amplified in aggressive, basal-like 
breast cancers and it showed transforming properties in vitro, such as growth-factor-independent 
proliferation and changed morphology (27). In prostate cancer cell line LNCaP, knockdown of JMJD2C 
lysine demethylase blocked demethylation of trimethyl H3K9. It inhibited the expression of 
androgen-receptor target genes, and androgen-induced proliferation of prostate tumor cells (28). 
 
Histone modifying proteins are considered putative drug targets because of their role in gene 
expression and development of diseases (20), and histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitors are an 
emerging class of therapeutics; three of them being approved by the U.S. FDA. Vorinostat and 
Depsipeptide serve for the treatment of cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (29), and Farydak is used for 
patients 
with 
multiple 
myeloma

HDAC inhibitors promote the acetylation of histones, but they also have many nonhistone targets, 
and they can induce a variety of responses; tumor cell apoptosis, growth arrest, senescence, 
differentiation, and inhibit angiogenesis (30).

Lysine specific demethylase PHF2. 
PHF2 is a lysine specific demethylase containing a plant homeodomain (PHD) and JumonjiC domain 
(JmjC), and it belongs to the KDM7 histone demethylase family of proteins together with PHF8 and 
KIAA1718 (31).  
Human PHF2 gene was first identified and described in 1998 (32). It was mapped to human 
chromosome 9q22as and therefore it was originally considered as a candidate gene for hereditary 
sensory neuropathy type I, HSN1, and multiple self-healing squamous epithelioma (33). This 
chromosomal region was also linked to a variety of tumors (34).  
PHF2 contains a zinc finger-like PHD-finger motif in the N-terminal (amino acids 5–56) that recognizes 
methylated residues and the JmjC domain (residues 197 – 353) catalyzing the removal of methyl 
groups. JmjC domain was found in 31 human proteins, and 17 of them function as histone 
demethylases (22). It is a 120 Daltons protein coded by 1,096 amino acids (UniProt). A schematic 
structure of PHF2 is shown in figure 4.  
 
 
Figure 4. Schematic structure of PHF2 (adapted from 32). 
 
PHF2 is highly conserved between human and mouse, with 98% identity at the amino acid level and 
95% nucleic acid homology across the coding region (32). The protein is ubiquitously expressed in all 
human tissues (high or medium expression levels in 78 out of 79 analyzed normal tissue cell types at 
Human Protein Atlas, 36), as well as in various cell lines.  
PHF2 is known to be a transcriptional regulator. It has been identified as a co-regulator of the 
adipogenic transcription factors C/EBPαand C/EBPδ and the knock-down of PHF2 greatly repressed 
adipogenesis (37).  It also interacts with several other transcription regulators such as ARID5B (38), 
NK-κB (39) or Runx2 (40), usually as a co-activator of transcription. However, PHF2 was also reported 
as a negative regulator of expression, as knocking down of PHF2 lead to increased levels of pre-rRNA 
(41). Two mechanisms of action were proposed: (i) PHF2 would be involved in recruiting the 
corepressor methyltransferase SUV39H1 and (ii) PHF2 would compete for rDNA promoter sites with 
the activator of transcription PHF8.  
 
The main reported function of PHF2 is histone demethylase activity. It is not, however fully 
understood yet. In liver cells, PHF2 is phosphorylated by PKA and this step is necessary to form a 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
complex with ARID5B (38), in which PHF2 plays a signal-sensing role directing the complex to its 
target promoter regions, where PHF2 removes the H3K9me2 mark leading to the activation of 
expression of the target genes (here phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase Pepck and glucose 6 
phosphatase, G6Pase). In the same study significant changes in H3K9me1 levels were not observed, 
suggesting that other enzymes are responsible for the subsequent demethylation. 
On the other hand, Wen and collaborators reported that PHF2 specifically recognizes and binds 
H3K4me2/3 and it demethylates H3K9me1 in vivo in U2OS and 293T cells (42).  
 
Figure 5. Peptide pull-down assay showing residues bound by PHF2.  
 
Additionally, PHF2 has also been reported to demethylate H4K20me3, a repressive mark, primarily 
associated with heterochromatin, present on a subset of proinflammatory genes after TLR4 
activation in X mice pre-adipocyte cell line 3T3-L1 (39).  
In the study of PHF2 role in adipogenesis (37) an increased dimethyl modification on H3K9 was seen 
in PHF2 knock-down cells at the PPARG, CEBPA and FABP4 promoter regions.   
 
There are some reports linking PHF2 with oncogenic processes. It has been found overexpressed in 
esophageal squamous cell carcinoma as compared to normal esophageal epithelium and it was 
associated with decreased overall survival of patients, although without the statistical significance 
(43).  
A completely different role of PHF2 was reported in colon cancer, where it interacts with p53 and 
acts as a tumor suppressor in HCT116 colon cancer cells. In the study of Lee et al (44) PHF2 was 
found downregulated in colon and stomach cancers. It also promoted p53 induced apoptosis after 
treating cells with anticancer drugs (oxaliplatin and doxorubicin). The proposed mode of action is 
that PHF2 gets recruited by p53 to the p53 target sites, where it removes dimethylation from H3K9 
and induces gene expression. 
 
Previous study conducted in the laboratory of Professor Flores-Morales indicated that PHF2 is 
overexpressed in metastatic PCa. In the light of this finding two reports linking PHF2 and bone 
formation and remodeling were of specific interest. Hansen et al. (45) observed that PHF2 gene 
mutation caused dwarfism in mice. Encouraged by these findings Kim et al (40) studied PHF2 role in 
chondrocytes and described PHF2 as a positive regulator of bone formation. PHF2 transgenic mice 
had larger and denser bones than the wild-type animals and the expression of osteogenic genes in 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
transgenic mice was higher. In isolated primary osteoblasts PHF2 interacted with Runx2 (runt-related 
transcription factor 2), and by demethylating it, enhanced osteoblast differentiation, and the cells 
had elevated expression levels of osteogenic genes ALP, OCN and BSP (osteoblast differentiation 
markers). PHF2 was also reported to demethylate chondrogenic promoter sites of transcription 
factor Sox9 target genes, to which it was recruited by ARID5B (46).  A model was developed where 
Arid5b would first form a complex with Phf2 and bring it to the target promoter sites. There PHF2 is 
demethylating H3K9 and increasing the availability of the chondrogenic genes to Sox9.  
The reported roles of PHF2 as lysine demethylase and modulator of chondrogenesis suggest it can 
play a role in the progression of prostate cancer, and they have rendered this protein an interesting 
subject to study.

Background 
 
A genome wide quantitative proteomic analysis of prostate cancer progression from benign prostate 
tissue to castration resistant bone metastasis was performed in Professor Flores-Morales´ laboratory 
at the University of Copenhagen. This state-of-the-art methodology allows identification and 
quantification of proteins from formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded tissue specimens. Analysis of the 
proteins differentially expressed between PCa and benign prostate tissues revealed that prostate 
tumors, opposite to other cancer types, preferentially utilize oxidative phosphorylation for energy 
production. Moreover, this data set allowed the identification of novel biomarkers of disease 
aggressiveness (Iglesias-Gato et al 2015, in revision European Urology).   
Furthermore gene ontology (GO) classification of proteins differentially expressed in PCa-derived 
bone metastasis versus localized prostate tumors showed metastatic tumors were enriched in 
proteins involved in cell cycle, DNA damage repair, RNA processing and lipid transport, while reduces 
carbohydrate catabolism and cell adhesion among others (figure 6). Interestingly one of the 
overrepresented GO categories in the metastatic tumors was that relative to chromatin organization 
and chromatin modifiers. In this group we found proteins such as members of the polycomb group 
(PcG) SUZ12, EED, BMI1; the DNA methyltransferases DNMT1 and DNMT3; histone 
methyltransferases EHMT1, EHMT2 and SETD1; the ubiquitin ligase RNF168, etc.  
The expression levels of the lysine demethylase PHF2 was also found elevated in most metastatic PCa 
tumors. Its proposed role in chondrogenesis together with the limited knowledge about its function 
and targets in PCa prompted us to investigate role of PHF2 in PCa.

Aim of study 
 
The aim of this project was to characterize the role of PHF2 in PCa cells proliferation and invasive 
capacity; to investigate whether PHF2 is a demethylase for H3K9 in PCa cells; and to identify 
potential targets of PHF2 actions in PCa cells.

Results 
 
The present study aimed to expand our understanding of lysine demethylase PHF2 role in prostate 
cancer and especially how this protein could contribute to the establishment of metastatic disease. 
We first focused in the model system which could recapitulate in vitro some of the features of the 
metastatic tumors. Most of the prostate cancer-derived metastatic lesions result from castration 
resistant prostate cancer tumors. These are cells that can grow in the absence of androgens and are 
able to establish bone tumors when injected in immunocompromised mice. Two prostate cancer cell 
lines: 22Rv1 and C4-2B, where selected in the light of these conditions. 
The CWR22R cell line was established from a primary prostatic carcinoma from a patient with bone 
metastases. CWR22R cells were grafted in mice and the tumors were allowed to recover after 
castrating the mice. The 22Rv1 cell line was established after several passages in castrated mice. 
22Rv1 cells induce tumors in nude mice at the injection site and exhibit a genomic instability with 
numbers of passages. It is expressing a constitutively active variant of the Androgen receptor (AR), 
which lacks the ligand-binding domain. These cells also carry a mutated p53 allele (Q331R) (47, 48).  
Similar to CWR22R, androgen sensitive LNCaP cells isolated form PCa lymph node metastasis were 
grafted in mice. Final C4-2B clone was isolated after sequential passages in castrated mice from 
metastatic PCa cell lesions in the lumbar spine of the athymic murine host (49). C4-2B cells show 
osteoblast like phenotype, expressing proteins like alkaline phosphatase, osteocalcin or 
osteoprotegerin and they are therefore a good model to study the mechanisms of disease 
progression and the way the osteoblastic lesions are formed (50).  
Taken together the characteristics and origin of both cell lines we found them to be a relevant model 
for this study.

Histone lysine demethylation activity of PHF2 
In order to evaluated and optimize our working conditions, we checked PHF2 protein levels after 
transfecting 22Rv1 and C4-2B cells with two specific siRNAs (siPHF2_2 and siPHF2_6) or vectors 
expressing PHF2 cDNA (pBABE and pcDNA3.1 vectors). As shown in figure 7 we can efficiently deplete 
PHF2 from cells using both siRNAs. These results were consistent throughout the study, with 
siPHF2_6 siRNA usually downregulating PHF2 more efficiently (also at the mRNA level). When 
comparing two expression vectors expressing PHF2 cDNA (pBABE and pcDNA3.1, for reference see 
table XX in the appendix) we decided to further work mostly with pcDNA3.1 due to higher expression 
levels of this plasmid.

PHF2 has been previously described as a lysine demethylase specifically acting on H3K9me2/me1 (38, 
42) and also on non-histone proteins, such as ARID5B (46). Since the exact demethylase activity of 
PHF2 remains unclear we decided to investigate the potential specificity of PHF2 on H3K9 
methylation in PCa cells. Cells were transfected with empty vector or plasmids containing PHF2 
cDNA, as well as control siRNA or the two siRNAs targeting PHF2 mRNA. The results are presented in 
figure 8. Consistent to both 22Rv1s and C4-2B cell lines is the lack of changes in the levels of 
H3K9me3 after modulating PHF2 expression. Also in both cell lines we observe an accumulation of 
H3K9me1 after PHF2 overexpression, with more profound effect in 22Rv1 cells. This effect would be 
a consequence of H3k9me2 demethylation, which seems likely in the case of C4-2B cells, or due to a 
compensatory mechanism of H3K9 methylation. The lack of consistency in H3K9me2 levels can be 
partially explained by low quality of the antibody used for most of the project duration.  
On the other hand knockdown of PHF2 in 22RV1 cells resulted in no major changes of H3K9 
methylation status, while the levels in C4-2B cells showed higher variability.  
Overall these results suggest that although likely acting as H3K9me2 demethylase, PHF2 is not a 
major H3K9 demethylase in PCa cells under normal growing conditions or its activity might be 
compensated by other demethylases in the cell. Further investigations directed to elucidate the role 
of PHF2 in H3K9 demethylation are needed.

Effects of PHF2 on PCa cells invasive capacity. 
One characteristic of aggressive tumor cells is their capacity to pass through the basement 
membrane and extracellular matrix and enter the circulation, what allows them to metastasize to 
distant locations. At the new location circulating cells will invade the vascular basement membrane 
and penetrate the surrounding tissues, forming the secondary tumor. Invading the basement 
membranes is thought to be one of the crucial steps in successful formation of a metastasis.  
To investigate the potential role of PHF2 in this process we performed the Matrigel Invasion Assay 
(figure 13). Matrigel is a basement membrane preparation composed of extracellular matrix proteins 
(such as laminin, collagen IV, entactin, and nidogen) extracted from the Engelbreth–Holm–Swarm 
mouse sarcoma (52). The cells are seeded on top of Matrigel matrix and their ability to pass through 
it into the bottom chamber (here filled with high-serum containing medium) is measured. The assay 
allows studying the invasive ability of tumor cells in vitro and can be translated into their ability to 
invade the basement membranes in tissues.  
 
 
Figure 13. Schematic overview of Matrigel invasion assay. Direction of cell migration is indicated with 
the arrows. Adapted from (53).  
 
 
As shown in figure 14, knocking down PHF2 led to decreased invasive potential, with up to 80% 
reduction in the number of cells passing through the Matrigel into the bottom chamber. Similar 
results were obtained in both C4-2B and 22Rv1 cells and would indicate that PHF2 enhances prostate 
cancer cell aggressiveness in their ability to invade extracellular matrix.

PHF2 role in regulating chondrogenic genes.  
 
Previous reports showed PHF2 as modulator of bone development and remodeling (40, 46) by 
regulating the activity of the transcription factor Runx2 and the expression of bone morphogenetic 
protein 2 (BMP2) and osteopontin (OSTP). Runx2 is a key transcription factor in osteoblast 
differentiation that was shown to be demethylated by PHF2 in primary osteoblasts and C2C12 
precursor cells (40). BMP2 induces the formation of bone and cartilages and it belongs to the 
transforming growth factor-beta (TGFB) superfamily, while OSTP is a part the bone matrix and it is 
involved in osteoclasts attachment to the mineralized matrix. Since we found PHF2 expression 
elevated in PCa bone metastasis, we wondered whether PHF2 might regulate the expression of 
theses osteogenic genes in PCa cells and therefore contribute to the establishment of the 
osteoblastic lesion characteristics of PCa metastasis. 
In order to investigate the effects of PHF2 in osteogenic genes expression C4-2B cells were 
transfected with an empty vector or a plasmid bearing PHF2 cDNA and the relative expression of 
OSTP, Runx2, BMP2 and PHF2 was analyzed by qPCR (figure 15). Results show that both OSTP and 
BMP2 expression is induced upon PHF2 overexpression, while Runx2 levels remain unchanged.  
These data suggest that increased expression of PHF2 in metastatic PCa cells modulates bone 
microenvironment through the production of BMP2 and OSTP resulting in the osteoblastic lesions 
typical of PCa metastasis. Whether this process is mediated through changes in methylation in the 
Runx2 transcription factor or changes in chromatin structure around the BMP2 and OSTP loci 
requires further investigation.

Discussion 
 
During the time of this project we investigated several functions of the PHF2 lysine demethylase in 
prostate cancer cells. We found that changes in PHF2 levels influence PCa cells proliferative and 
invasive capacity. PHF2 could also contribute to the establishment of bone metastasis by inducing 
the expression BPM2 and OSTP. Moreover, our data suggest that PHF2 would play a role modifying 
chromatin structure by preferentially demethylating H3K9me2. 
PHF2 has been reported mostly as an H3K9me2 demethylase (37, 38). Although we were not able to 
undoubtedly demonstrate it, our results obtained after overexpressing PHF2 suggest that this is 
indeed one of the functions of this protein in PCa cells. Dramatic changes in global methylation status 
can be difficult to induce specially in knock down experiments due to the many layers of control of 
this process and the redundant activities existing in the cell. Therefore, the accumulation of 
H3K9me1 in both C4-2B and 22Rv1 cells together with the reduction of H3K9me2 in C4-2B cell, 
support our hypothesis. PHF2 would therefore act as an activator of transcription in PCa cells. We 
can also exclude any direct effect of PHF2 in H3K9me3 levels since no changes were observed in any 
of the experiments.  
When we studied the effects of PHF2 on cell proliferation we observed a distinct response after PHF2 
knock down between the two cell lines. This different behavior cannot be explained with changes in 
protein expression levels since both cell lines express similar levels of PHF2 protein (see figure 18, 
appendix 5). However, our results indicate that C4-2B cells are more sensitive to changes in the 
intracellular concentration of PHF2. Interestingly PHF2 overexpression in both cells lines inhibits 
proliferation. These apparently contradictory results suggest that PHF2 levels must be tightly 
regulated: either because excessive demethylation activity has deleterious effects or because 
overexpression of PHF2 results in unbalance stoichiometry of functional cellular complex also fatal 
for the cells.  
Interestingly, in both cell lines decreased PHF2 levels resulted in reduced invasiveness. This 
phenotype may be related to the observed reduction in expression of PLCD3 after PHF2 knock down 
since PLCD3 has been reported as RhoA regulator, a protein with a critical role in cell motility (55). 
This would indicate that PHF2 promotes cell aggressiveness and facilitates passage through 
basement membrane and ECM and these are the characteristics necessary for tumor cells to invade 
distant tissues and metastasize. Prostate cancer is generally considered as slow growing, with tumor 
volume doubling time (TVDT) reaching months or years (56). The growth rates therefore do not 
necessarily reflect the tumors aggressive potential.  
Finally, in good agreement with the identification of PHF2 as protein with elevated expression in PCa 
bone metastasis, we demonstrate that PHF2 is able to induce the expression of chondrogenic genes 
(BMP2 and OSTP), which would potentially play a role in the development of metastasis. 
Overall our data indicates that PHF2 may play an important role in establishing PCa metastases in the 
bone through the induction of the expression of chondrogenic genes like BMP2 and OSTP as well as 
its role in inducing cell invasive capacity. 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
Future work 
 
In our study we started to investigate the role of the lysine demethylase PHF2 in PCa cells. Some of 
the results discussed above suggest that PHF2 may play an important role in PCa metastasis. Several 
lines of investigation may be developed at different levels: 
(i) Functional studies: downregulating PHF2 reduced cell invasiveness and in case of C4-2B cells also 
proliferation. To further examine changes in cells behavior evaluation of motility and ability to form 
colonies under PHF2 depletion conditions could be tested. Soft agar colony formation assay allows 
seeing if cells can growth in anchorage-independent way in vitro and by this assess their tumorigenic 
potential.  
To evaluate the importance of PHF2 in the interaction with bone microenvironment, chondrocyte 
could be grown in conditioned medium from PCa cells expressing or not PHF2 to study behavioral 
changes. 
(ii) Molecular studies: PHF2 was described as interacting with several transcription factors, and it was 
directed into the target promoters of proinflammatory genes to remove the methylation mark (39). 
Chances are, these are not the only targets of PHF2, and performing chromatin immunoprecipitation 
followed by sequencing (ChIP-seq) could identify what genomic regions are occupied by PHF2 in PCa 
cells and what processes it takes part in. Similarly, we could investigate chromatin regions containing 
mono-, di- or trimethylation of H3K9, and potentially other H3 lysine residues, in cells depleted from 
PHF2 and study methylation changes at these sites.  
Additionally, proteins other than histones may be targets of PHF2, such as ARID5B and Runx2 (40, 
46). By performing immunoprecipitation followed by mass spectrometry we could identify what 
other proteins are interacting with PHF2, and potentially discover new processes in which this 
protein is involved.  
 
Finally, in order to further study how PHF2 may influence bone development, we could investigate 
the influence of PHF2 in PCa cells on the production of growth factors that stimulate growth of 
osteoblasts at the metastatic site, such as urokinase-type plasminogen activator (57) and others.

Materials and methods 
This section will briefly introduce the protocols for the experimental procedures. Materials, 
chemicals and solutions used can be found in the appendixes. 
 
Cell lines and cell culture 
Two prostate cancer cell lines of different origin (C4-2B, 22Rv1, see table X) were used in this study, 
allowing more extensive assessment of the stated hypothesis. 
All cell lines were cultured in RPMI medium containing 10% complete fetal bovine serum (FBS), L-
glutamine and antibiotics (penicillin and streptomycin). Cells were maintained at 37°C in a humidified 
incubator with 5% CO2.  
Cells were passaged when reaching approximately 80% confluence. All cell lines were passaged 
according to the same procedure: medium was aspirated and the cells were washed once with PBS. 
For a 15 cm plate 3 mL of trypsin was added, followed by 3-5 minute incubation at 37°C. Next trypsin 
was inactivated by adding 7 mL of medium, cells were transferred into a 15 mL tube and centrifuged. 
They were next resuspended in 1 mL medium, and seeded with appropriate dilutions.   
Passage numbers used throughout the study: 
- 
C4-2B cells: 28 - 45  
- 
22RV1: 19 – 35 
 
Whole cell extracts. 
Cells were placed on ice, washed twice with PBS and treated with lysis buffer supplemented with 
protease inhibitors cocktail (Roche Diagnostics). Cells were scraped, collected into Eppendorf tubes 
and incubated on ice for 30 minutes. Next, the samples were centrifuged for 20 minutes (4°C, 13.2 
rpm) and the supernatant was collected.  
Protein concentration was determined with Bio-Rad Protein Assay.  
 
Nuclear extracts 
The cells were washed twice with cold PBS and collected in Hypotonic Buffer by scraping and 
pipetting up and down. They were then incubated on ice. After adding the detergent (10% NP40) 
they were mixed and centrifuged. Supernatant was collected as cytoplasmic fraction. Pellet was 
resuspended in Cell Extraction Buffer, incubated on ice and centrifuged. Supernatant containing 
nuclear fraction was transferred to a fresh tube.

Due to a high content of detergent the process was not compatible with Bio-Rad Protein Assay and 
protein concentration was not determined.  
 
Western Blot analysis 
Equal amounts of cell protein extracts (normally between 10-30 µg of protein) with 1x Sample 
Reducing Agent and 1x LDS Sample Buffer (Life Technologies) were loaded on 10% or 15% SDS-PAGE 
and normally run at 125 V for 1 hour 30 min. They were then electrophoretically transferred onto a 
PVDF membrane. After blocking in 5% non-fat milk in wash buffer for 1 hour at room temperature 
the membranes were incubated overnight at 4°C with primary antibodies. Following incubation, the 
membrane was washed three times and incubated for 1 hour at room temperature with the 
appropriate horseradish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibody. Proteins were visualised using 
the Amersham ECL Western Blotting Detection Reagents. Further on the membranes were washed 
three times and incubated for 1 hour at room temperature with β-actin antibody serving as loading 
control.  
 
Transfection 
Transfection of cells was performed using the Neon® Transfection System (Invitrogen) according to 
manufacturer’s instructions. Cells were first washed with PBS, trypsinized, diluted 10x in RPMI 
medium and mixed with Trypan Blue stain in 1:1 ratio. They were then counted using Countess II 
device (Life Technologies). Appropriate amount of cells (usually between 1 and 4 million) was 
pipetted into an Eppendorf tube, centrifuged, medium removed, and cells were resuspended in the 
Resuspension Buffer R. They were next transiently transfected with expression plasmids or small 
interfering RNA. 160 pmol of siRNA was used per reaction, and 10 μg of DNA per million transfected 
cells. The cells were then mixed with siRNA or expression plasmids in a Neon pipette and 
electroporated at 1250 mV with 2 pulses, after which they were plated and incubated overnight, and 
the medium was changed the following day.  
 
Matrigel Invasion Assay 
Matrigel is placed at 4°C to thaw O/N. Matrigel was diluted 1:4 in serum free medium and placed on 
top of cell culture inserts inside a 24 well plate. It was left to polymerize for 3 hours at 37°C. Next, 
RPMI with 10% FBS was added into the bottom chamber. Cells were trypsinized, counted and 
resuspended in RPMI with 1% FBS, and then placed on top of matrigel. Such prepared plates were 
incubated for 2 days at 37°C and 5% CO2. After incubation, the top matrix was removed cells were 
placed in formaldehyde. They were next permeabilized with 0.2% Triton, stained with DAPI and 
washed with PBS. Membranes were cut out and placed on microscope slides and the cells counted 
using fluorescent microscope. Five independent experiments were performed and in each set every 
group was measured in triplicate.

Pull down 
Whole cell extracts were prepared from 15 cm plates of cells (~90% confluent) as described above 
(see WCE section). 800 μg of protein was used for each pull down, and the volume adjusted to 800 μL 
with lysis buffer. Next, primary antibodies (α-PHF2 in 1:50 dilution) were added to the appropriate 
samples and they were incubated O/N in 4°Cwith rotation (control samples were incubated without 
antibodies). After incubation 30 μL of Protein A beads were added to all the samples and they were 
incubated for 1 hour at 4 °C with rotation. The samples were placed on a magnet and supernatant 
was removed and saved as Flow Through, and the beads with pulled proteins were washed 3 times 
with lysis buffer and resuspended in 45 μL SDS loading buffer and 5 μL of reducing agent, and then 
loaded on a 10% SDS-PAGE gel for further analysis. The gel was next stained, or subjected to western 
blotting with appropriate antibodies.  
 
Proliferation Assay 
Cell proliferation was measured using the xCELLigence real time cell analyzer system (RTCA, ACEA 
Biosciences). The system relies on a biosensor built into a 96-well E-plate, which measures the 
electrical impedance of the cells in the well. Cells in contact with the sensor change the electrical 
impedance (58).  
The wells of a 96-well plate were filled with 100 μL RPMI medium and the background impedance 
was measured. The cells were trypsinized, collected and counted as described above. 7.000 cells in 
100 μL were then added to each well and the measurements were initiated. Recordings were taken 
once every hour for 120 hours total. All samples were run at least as quadruplicates. The results were 
later analyzed using RTCA 1.2.1 software. The statistical analysis was performed using Microsoft 
Excel, Student's t-tests were calculated using Cell Indexes (CI) collected at the latest time point, and 
data is presented as Delta Cell Index, delta time being: 15h 30 minutes. At least three independent 
experiments were performed. 
Delta Cell Index (DCIti) is calculated as the Cell Index CIti at a given time point plus a Delta value, 
which is a constant value for each well and is the difference between a reference DCI value and the 
Cell Index at the Delta Time point (58):

synthesis were performed as described above. Microarray gene expression analysis was performed

To verify the microarray results the expression levels of 6 selected genes was examined by RT-PCR 
using the same RNA samples that were used for performing the microarray analysis (figure 17).

μg/mL). Plates were incubated for 16-18 hours at 37°C. The following day several colonies were 
picked and incubated in liquid LB medium with antibiotics for 16-18 hours. Plasmids were purified 
using NucleoSpin® Plasmid EasyPure kits according to manufacturer’s instructions. Plasmid 
concentrations were determined using NanoDrop® ND-1000 machine.

Appendix 8. Generation of PHF2 mutants 
 
The mutant PHF2 variants were generated using QuikChange Site-Directed mutagenesis Kit from 
Agilent Technologies following the manufacturer’s instructions. Two different plasmids were 
generated harboring mutations in JmjC domain (H249A), as well as a double mutation (H249A and 
W29A, the latter located in PHD finger domain) The expression vector used was pcDNA3.1. 
Plasmids and primers used in this procedure are listed in table 5 (appendix 3). The insertion of the 
mutations was confirmed by DNA sequencing and analyzed using CLC Sequence Viewer 7.6.  
 
 
Figure 19. Schematic overview of mutagenesis sites.   
 
 
Sequencing 
Mutant generation was validated with the DNA sequencing service value-read (Eurofins Genomics, 
Ebersberg, Germany). Premixed DNA samples with sequencing primers were prepared as follows; 15 
μL of DNA (100 ng/μL) was mixed with 2 μL of sequencing primers (10μM). The samples were labeled 
with pre-paid barcodes and shipped to Eurofins Genomics. Primarily, eight plasmids were sequenced 
at the mutation site, and afterwards two of them harboring the mutations were shipped to sequence 
the whole PHF2 protein. Primers designed for sequencing are listed in table 5. 
After successful confirmation of mutated PHF2 sequences three bacterial colonies transformed with 
single or double mutant pcDNA3.1-PHF2 plasmids; PHF2(H249A) and PHF2(W29A_H249A) were 
preserved at -80°C and can be used in the following studies.
