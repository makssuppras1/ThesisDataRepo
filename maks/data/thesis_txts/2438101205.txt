Abstract
The objective of this report is to optimize the photocatalytic degradation of lignocellulosic material for high-
value products and biogas production. The photocatalytic method in question is a novel method involving
the use of TiO2 nano-particles to be excited by Ultra Violet irradiation in the presence of Formic Acid.
The substrate to be used for this method is Wheat Straw. The optimization was done so as to maximize
expected high-value products (phenolic compounds). The software (JMP 13) predicted optimized values is
0.35M FA and 0 g/L TiO2. The Total Phenolic Compounds (TPC) predicted value is 66.8 mg/gWS. The
experimentally obtained value from testing the optimized solution was 67.8 mg/gWS, thereby supporting
the software prediction. Elemental analysis, lignin content change, XRD, FTIR and SEM analysis are also
done. The tests support each other in conﬁrming that this photocatalytic process acts mainly on lignin.
The change in lignin content is observed to be 1.6%, CrI is found to increase by 3%, FTIR spectra shows
change in lignin peaks, but no change in hemicellulose and cellulose peaks are observed. SEM analysis shows
change in surface structure of treated samples. An additional cycle test was done to test for repeated uses of
reaction solution with fresh substrate. A BMP assay was performed for treated samples from the Cycle Test.
Treated wheat straw samples of Cycle1 show a 10.72% increase in methane production over untreated wheat
straw, while the treated wheat straw samples of Cycle2 show a 15.86% increase in methane production over
untreated wheat straw. The novel photocatalytic method was found to be successful in lingin degradation
while leftover substrate can be used for Biomethanation process.
Key words: Photocatalysis, TiO2, Formic Acid, lignin degradation, JMP 13, optimization, XRD, FTIR,
SEM, CHNS, BMP

Chapter 1
Introduction
The European Commission and its 28 member states have upcoming 2020 Energy Strategy targets which aim
to (i) reduce greenhouse emissions by 20%, (ii) increase share of renewable energy by 20% and (iii) achieve
energy saving of 20% or more [1]. The Commission has outlined 5 action plans to meet these targets. Of
particular note is Action Plan Four which indicates the need for a technological shift to take place to meet
the 2050 targets of decarbonization of the electricity and transport sectors [2].
The technological shift can be met by a shift toward renewable resources of energy eg.
wind, solar,
biomass etc. The technologies require a shift away from heat engine based networks to electrical network.
Additionally, energy saving measures along with a reduction in waste will be essentially to meet the proposed
target. Of particular interest to us is the role bioenergy has to play in these sectors and the research required
for a technological shift in it.
In 2015, 10% of the total energy consumption of EU-28 was from bioenergy, 74% consumption went to
heat, 14% to electricity and 13% to transport [3]. Bioenergy can be split into solid fuels (70% of consumption),
biogas (11.5%), liquid biofuels (11.4%) and municipal waste (7.1%). These ﬁgures show that a signiﬁcant
portion of the energy consumption comes from bioenergy. There has been a lot of interest in the biofuels
sector in recent years, mainly on fermentation technologies, however, biogas and its usage in the grid has
been increasing with commericialization of upgrading technologies.
The biogas sector in Europe has been growing consistently over the last few years. Biogas plays the
biggest role in electricity generation accounting for 34% (5.238 ktoe) of the bioenergy used (2015) [3]. These
numbers indicate there is considerable interest in increasing production of biogas and eﬃciency of conversion
of energy from biomass to bioenergy.
The biogas market in Denmark shows considerable promise with energy produced forecasted to triple by
2020 as compared to 2012 [4]. It is interesting to note that the Danish Energy Agency limits the energy
crops used for biogas production currently to 25% of feedstock used so as to receive subsidies. This is set to
reduce to 12% of feedstock used by the end of the 2018 [4]. This necessitates an increase in energy recovery
from manure, sewage sludge, agricultural, industrial and household wastes to maintain the energy output at
similar levels. Such a technological shift is currently taking place with the BioReﬁnery concept.
The BioReﬁnery concept comprises of a facility or network of various facilities that process biomass to
produce biofuels, power and chemicals by various conversion processes. It is analogous to a petroleum re-
6
==PAGE:10==
ﬁnery which produces multiple products from a single input [5]. Such an approach enables maximum use
of the energy content of biomass. All biomass is made from lignocellulosic material that degrade to form
the products of interest. Lignocellulosic material consist of cellulose (40-50%), hemi cellulose (24-35%) and
lignin (18-35%) [6]. While technologies to process cellulose and hemicellulose to valuable products are well
established and economically viable [5], processes for degradation of lignin have been limited and the existing
ones are energy consuming. This is mainly due to lignin’s complex structure and its resistance to degradation.
[6].
Lignin degradation has a two-fold beneﬁt. They make the bioreﬁnery process more appealing economi-
cally due to production of high-value compounds [6] as well as breaking down the outer layer of the biomass
making the cellulose and hemi cellulose more accessible to microbes during Anearobic Digestion (AD) process
for biofuels [5]. This can help increase the biofuel production process yield while decreasing the time required
for the process. It also helps address the fuel vs food debate as agricultural wastes (Second generation biofuels
substrate) eg. Wheat straw, rice straw, corn straw etc., that have high lignin content [7] but low economic
value, and can be used instead of energy crops (First generation biofuels substrate) like sugarcane and beets
that are used as food.
Photocatalysis of biomass follows the bioreﬁnery concept as it aims to produce high-value chemicals, like
vanillin and phenolic compounds usable in industries, from the biomass. The waste products from the process
can then be used for biofuels production [8].
Most thermo-mechanical or chemical processes used for lignin degradation involve high temperature /pres-
sures or strong acids/bases. Photocatalytic degradation however is done at room temperature and pressures
and does not have any adverse eﬀects on the treated biomass [7]. Additionally, degradation by photocatalysis
is interesting as it can be done in sunlight (depending on catalyst used) with further research which will
reduce the energy consumed during the process making it more sustainable.
Objective of Report
The objective of this report is to optimize the photocatalytic degradation of lignocellulosic material for high-
value products and biogas production. The photocatalytic method in question is a novel method involving
the use of TiO2 nano-particles to be excited by Ultra Violet (UV) irradiation in the presence of Formic Acid
(FA). The substrate to be used for this method is Wheat Straw (WS), which is agricultural by-product and
available abundantly. It has been used in various photocatalytic degradation studies as substrate.
The report aims to optimize the concentration of FA and TiO2 nano particles loading in the reaction
solution. The substrate loading will be kept ﬁxed. The optimization is to be done for maximum production
of high-value products and qualiﬁcation of said products is also to be done. Evaluation of treated substrate
is to be done to ensure lignin degradation has taken place and biomethanation is done using substrate to
ensure its usability in bioreﬁnery process.

2.1
Lignin - Composition and Structure
Lignin is a complex heterogeneous organic polymer present in plant structures and acts like a glue giving
the plants structural rigidity. It has various interlinking monomers and three that have been identiﬁed are
p-coumaryl, coniferyl and sinapyl alcohols and shown in Figure.2.1 [6].
Figure 2.1: Diﬀerent Monomers that make up Lignin from Lekelefac et al.
These structures are connected by various linkages and are classiﬁed according to connections made. The
β-O-4 aryl glycerol ether bond is the dominant linkage type in native lignin, it generally constitutes of over
50% of the linkage types present in lignin [8]. Other types of linkages are 5-5, β-5, α-O-4, β-β, β-1, 4-O-5,
and dibenzodioxocin which be seen in Figure.2.2 [9].
Each linkage requires a certain amount of energy to be broken, called Bond dissociation energy (BDE)
and this depends on the type of bond to be broken. Diﬀerent processes act on and break diﬀerent bonds
in the lignin molecule yielding diﬀerent reaction products [6]. Two major bond types to be found are ether
bonds (found in β-O-4, α-O-4 and 4-O-5 linkages) and C-C bonds (found in 5-5, β-1 and β-5 linkages) [10].
In a theoretical study done by Parthasarathi et al.[10], C-C bonds were overall found to be stronger than
ether bonds. The weakest bonds are found to be α-O-4 and β-O-4 linkages.

2.2.1
Thermochemical Methods
Pyrolysis
Pyrolysis is the thermal degradation of an organic material at high temperatures in the absence of oxida-
tion/reduction compounds (oxygen) [11]. Fast pyrolysis is used to convert biomass directly into a liquid
product known as bio-crude, which can then be upgraded in processes similar to conventional petroleum
reﬁneries. At 450-550◦C the reported lignin pyrolysis products are 55% char, 35% liquids and 12% gases [12].
Patwardhan et al [12] report the compounds they identiﬁed from pyrolysis and conclude by observations that
9
==PAGE:13==
maximum methylated phenol yields are at 600◦C.
Hyrdogenolysis
Lignin depolymerization takes place at high temperatures and pressures and as seen in pyrolysis this results
in production of char and other volatile products. The presence of hydrogen during this process can terminate
the formed radicals and promote the depolymerization into oligomers and monomers. This is the process of
hyrogenolysis [11]. A variety of catalysts have been tested and reported, along with their respective reaction
products. Hydrogenolysis has been found to have higher net conversion, higher yields and less char formation
as compared to pyrolysis, and is the most promising method of production of phenols from lignin [7].
Hydrolysis
The lignin reacts with water, generally in the presence of a catalyst, to form a variety of high value products.
This is generally dependent on whether the hydrolysis is acid or base catalyzed. [13]. Hydrolysis under both
acidic [14] and basic/alkaline [15] conditions (using organic solvents) has been extensively studied. Alkaline
catalysts were seen to hinder the formation of char/coke formation [15] and a similar eﬀect was observed with
strong bases where char formation was reduced and as well as increase conversion eﬃciency [16]. Hydrolysis
is typically carried out at lower temperatures than pyrolysis and therefore less extreme conditions are seen
[17].
Oxidation
This thermal treatment (also called oxidative cracking) takes place in the presence of oxygen. Due to the
presence of hydroxyl groups in lignin molecule a variety of reactions can occur. Oxidation products of lignin
can range from aromatic aldehydes to carboxylic acids [7]. The products are determined by the severity of
the oxidation taking place.
Oxidation by hydrogen peroxide, a weak acid, is shown to solubilize and degrade lignin [7]. While successful
in degrading lignin, hydrogen peroxide causes degradation to organic acids products (eg. acetic acid) and
trace amounts of aldehydes or vanillin. Nitrobenzene, metal oxides and molecular oxygen are more successful
in producing high value products like aldehydes from lignin [7].
Photocatalysis is also an oxidation process and will be discussed further.
2.2.2
Biological Methods
Enzymatic hydrolysis is the way nature does lignin degradation [18]. Camarero et al. [18] investigated white-
rot fungi in breaking down ligno-cellulose and found species that act speciﬁcally on removing lignin from
wheat straw. Enzymes from the fungi, which are the sole organisms able to mineralize lignin, act indirectly
in a cascade manner that eventually results in the oxidation of lignin [11]. Bio-degradation of lignin can
substitute thermochemical methods of lignin as it does not involve acids/bases that are expensive to recycle
and do not generate inhibitor compounds that have a negative eﬀect on subsequent processes [18].

2.3
Photocatalysis
Photocatalysis is an Advanced Oxidation Process (AOP) that can be used to depolymerize lignin in mild
conditions. The oxidation process occurs in the presence of a light-sensitive catalyst. The light sensitive cata-
lyst absorbs photons to reach highly reactive higher energy states. These states create electron-hole pairs are
able to act on certain linkages in the lignin molecule, helping break it down into smaller components. Various
factors aﬀect photocatalysis such as the rate of electron transfer, the rate of charge recombination, the crys-
tal structure, surface area of catalysts and sample, and porosity of sample [6]. Illustration of photocatalysis
principle can be seen in Figure.2.3 [6].
TiO2, and n-type semiconductor is one of the most used photocatalysts with a band gap of 3.2 eV, and
hence is excited by light from the UV region of the light spectrum [Eqn. 2.1] [19]. Once, excited the e-
and h+ pair can react with H2O and O2 [Eqn. 2.2 and 2.6] to produce ·OH (hydroxyl radical) and O·−
2

2.7]. [6]. Machado et al. [20] report that the main oxidizing agent is the hydroxyl radical due to the unpaired
electrons. Therefore, it can react with organic compounds S to form an organic radical species [Eqn. 2.3, 2.4
and 2.5].

Lu et al.[21] in their analysis using TiO2 to depolymerize rice husks with H2O2 in aqueous solution which

diﬀerent solvents, namely water, hexane and acetone. Their results are promising as they are able to obtain

acetone≥water≥hexane. They also note that they observed a signiﬁcant reduction in molecular weight during
photocatalysis of wet milled water mixture.
The above results strengthen photocatalysis’s position as a technology able to produce value added compounds
at atmospheric temperatures and pressures.
2.4
Photocatalysis Reaction Parameters
Lekelec et al.[6] in their article "Photocatalytic Based Degradation Processes of Lignin Derivative" cover
extensively the diﬀerent reaction parameters and their eﬀects on the degradation process.
They list the
various studies researched, with a variety of starting points and diﬀerent process parameters of each before
12
==PAGE:16==
discussing their inﬂuences. They identiﬁed 5 major parameters to discuss, and this report will follow their
discussion on the same 5.
2.4.1
Inﬂuence of catalyst
There are a variety of photocatalysts that are available to study photocatalytic degradation with TiO2 and
ZnO being the two more commonly used ones. ZnO was used under visible light sources while TiO2 is used
predominantly under UV light. Their report discusses why each might be used but concludes that TiO2 is
more favored to be used due to its nontoxic property, cost eﬃciency, for having the more eﬃcient photo-
activity and highest stability and for its chemical and biological inertness. Catalyst loading/concentration
is factor that has been seen to aﬀect degradation rate, and hence is an important parameter to get correct.
Rideh et al. [23] study the eﬀect of catalyst concentration in their work with TiO2 and conclude that catalyst
concentration increases photo degradation rate linearly till a certain limit, thereafter a decrease is observed.
The limit is found to depend on the initial solution volume, which eﬀects the catalyst surface available. The
decrease in photo degradation after the limit is said to be due to increased turbidity of the solution decreased
the light transmission through it.
2.4.2
Inﬂuence of metal ion addition (Doping)
Doping of photocatalysts is done for a variety of reasons such as changing the band gap energy, reducing
recombination eﬀects, shifting the absorbance spectrum, increasing surface area of catalyst etc. Some common
metal dopants used include Fe2+ or noble metals like Au, Ag and Pt. Lekelec et al. [6] point toward various
studies with a variety of diﬀerent dopants that inﬂuence diﬀerent aspects of photo degradation. They conclude
that whether or not a signiﬁcant eﬀect on photo degradation is to be found, consideration should be given to
dopant concentration. Once the concentration surpasses the threshold value it has a negative eﬀect on photo
degradation as electron-hole recombination is favored.
2.4.3
Inﬂuence of Lignin Concentration
Analyzing the articles surveyed by Lekelec et al. [6] their conclusion that time for degradation depends on
initial degradation and that rate of degradation is higher at low lignin concentration can be agreed with.
They point towards a threshold limit for lignin concentration after which an inhibitory eﬀect on degradation
is observed. This threshold concentration is dependent on reaction system design and reaction parameters
such as optical density, catalyst concentration and reaction volume.
2.4.4
Inﬂuence of pH
The inﬂuence of pH is seen to be dependent on type of catalyst used. Lekelec et al. [6] in their analysis
refer to ZnO and TiO2, where TiO2 is seen to be showing higher degradation rates in acidic medium ( pH
= 3) whereas ZnO is showing to have increased degradation rates in basic medium ( pH = 11). They also
discuss the solubility of Kraft lignin which decreases at lower pH and the stability of the β-O-4 linkage at
13
==PAGE:17==
lower pH, both which point towards degradation increasing with increasing pH. However, they conclude that
the optimal pH is most likely reaction dependent and have to be evaluated experimentally.
2.4.5
Inﬂuence of Illumination
Diﬀerent catalysts requires lights of diﬀerent wavelengths to get activated. TiO2 with its band gap of 3.2
eV is usually activated by light from 280-420nm [6], while ZnO is activated by light in the visible spectrum.
TiO2 doped with metals has been found to get activated by visible light, but this is an area currently under
study.
Rideh et al. [23] in their study found that the electron-hole pair formation was directly proportional to the
incident ﬂux of eﬃcient photons. Lekelec et al. [6] in their analysis of authors found similarly ﬁndings where
higher illumination intensity correlated with higher initial degradation rates. They also further observe that
best reaction rates occur at low lignin concentration and high light intensities as this allows for maximum
light penetration into the solution medium.
Alvarado-Morales et al. [24] in their article test for diﬀerent times of irradation as well as catalyst loading.
Their results show that irradiance time in all cases signiﬁcantly improved degradation into products useful
for Biomethantion process.
Thus, it can be concluded that high light intensities, low lignin concentration and longer irradiation times
beneﬁt and improve lignin degradation.
2.5
Lignin Degradation with Formic Acid (FA)
FA is an organic acid that is used in a variety of industrial processes for eﬃcient in-situ hydrogen generation.
It is safer than using molecular hydrogen and is cheaper than most other hydrogen donation compounds as
it can be derived from renewable resources [25].
The use of FA along with a catalyst in photo catalyst is a novel pre treatment method that this study aims
to optimize. Literature to be found using similar treatments is sparse, but an understanding of the overall
process can be gained from a variety of sources put together.
Zhang et al. [26] in their article "Isolation and Characterization of wheat straw lignin with a Formic
Acid Process" successfully isolate the lignin from the wheat straw with their process involving H2SO4 as
catalyst. While the mechanism of the reaction is unspeciﬁed, the results are promising with a 83% of total
lignin recovery from the wheat straw, showing that FA is able to successfully dissolve lignin in the solution
by degradation to lower molecular weight oligomers.
Xu et al. [27] in their article "Depolymerization and Hydrodeoxygenation of Switchgrass Lignin with Formic
Acid" determined that FA along with a Pt/C catalyst at elevated temperatures leading to highest fraction
of lower molecular weight products, indicating maximum lignin depolymerization.
Oregui-Bengoechea et al. [28] in their article "Unraveling the Role of Formic Acid and the Type of Solvent
in the Catalytic Conversion of Lignin: A Holistic Approach" showcase that FA is active participant in the
depolymerization of lignin and propose a reaction mechanism for said conversion. The reaction mecahnism is

through formylation, elimination and hydrogenolysis. They also determine that solvent added to the reaction
act mainly as a stabilizer. The substitution of FA with other hydrogen donating molecules yields products
with diﬀerent properties and in diﬀering amounts as well.

2.7
Anaerobic Digestion
Anaerobic Digestion (AD) is a process which converts biomass into biogas with the help of micro-organisms
under anaerobic conditions i.e in the absence of oxygen. It is a multi-step process and requires a variety of
microbes that come together in biochemical conversions to produce methane and carbon dioxide. There are
4 major steps; Hydrolysis, Acidogenesis, Acetogenesis and Methanogensis.
2.7.1
Hydrolysis
The primary step of AD is the hydrolysis of carbohydrates, lipids and proteins into monosaccharides and
oligomers, long chain fatty acids and amino acids. This is done by exoenzymes secreted by fermentative
microorganisms. While some products of this process are small and maybe used by directly, eg. hydrogen
used by methanogens; the majority of the products have molecular weights relatively large enough that the
subsequent steps are required to produce methane. [30]. It is a relatively slow step and can limit the rate of
the AD process [31].
2.7.2
Acidogensis
Acidogensis takes the products of the previous step, namely monosaccharides, long chain fatty acids and amino
acids, and converts them into higher organic acids like propionic acid, butyric acid, hydrogen and carbon
dioxide. These higher organic acids are then transferred to acetic acid and hydrogen by acetogenic bacteria.
[30]. Fermentation of monosaccharides and/or amino acids also takes places during this step, depending on
the bacteria present at the site [31]. It is not always possible to draw a line between acidogensis and the
subsequent step of acetogensis as both produce acetate and hydrogen, substrates of the methanogensis step.
2.7.3
Acetogensis
As mentioned in the previous step, acetogenesis is the process of synthesis of acetate, done by the reduction
of CO2 and formation of acetate from organic acids [31]. This step also produces hydrogen, and can be
regarded as waste, as it inhibits the metabolism of acetogenic bacteria. However, as mentioned in hydrolysis,
the hydrogen is used by the methanogic micro-organisms [30].
2.7.4
Methanogensis
Methanogenic micro-organisms belong to the Archea domain and are classiﬁed as: Aceticlastic, Hydrogenotrophic
and Methylotrophic. They are strictly anaerobic and are responsible for the last step of methane formation
16
==PAGE:20==
from acetate, acetic acid, formate, alcohols, hydrogen and carbon dioxide. 70% of the methane formation
comes from acetate (Aceticlastic methanogens) while the rest of the 30% from hydrogen, carbon dioxide
(Hydrogenotrophic methanogens) and methyl compounds (Methylotrophic methanogens). [31]. They are
slow growing and extremely sensitive to changes in their environmentand grow optimally around a pH of 7.
The reaction speeds here are the slowest in comparison to other steps. A limiting factor to this process is
the supply of hydrogen [30].

Initial study is done to determine optimum loading of WS in 100ml solution. Tests were done with loading of

loading, as higher catalyst loading will limit light penetration into solution (as discussed in Section 2.3.5).
As solution volume used is 100 mL, 0.1 g/L is suﬃcient to turn solution milky white, and further increases
did not result in increase in the result parameters.

3.6.4
Determination of Soluble Chemical Oxygen Demand (sCOD)
COD is a method used for determination of the amount of oxygen required for both the organic and inorganic
compounds present in a sample. The procedure followed is in accordance with ASTM standards (D1252-06)
[38]
Destruction Procedure:
1. Heating block should be switched on to reach 148◦C.
2. Samples should be prepared - They might have to be diluted with DI water (to keep COD within range
of 50-600 mgCOD/L for high range detection and 15-150 mgCOD/L for low range detection).
3. PYREX Tubes should be marked - 5 Blanks + 3 control + samples (in triplicate)
4. Add 3 small glassbeads to each tube to stir up and avoid aggressive boiling under destruction.
5. 3.5 mL of blank, control and sample to be added to their respective tubes.
24
==PAGE:28==
6. Add 2.0 mL potassiumdichromate to each tube. (For high range - 0.025M, and for low range - 0.005M)
7. Add 4.5 mL Sulfuric acid with silversulfate to one tube at a time and screw the lid on immediately.
8. Place tubes in preheated blocks and let digest for 110 mins.
9. Once required time has elapsed, let cool down to <60 ◦C.
Titration Procedure:
1. Add 5.0 mL DI water to each tube.
2. Add 3-4 drop of Ferroin indicator to each tube and titrate with ferrousammoniumsulphate (FAS) ( For
high range - 0.0035M FAS, for low range - 0.007M FAS).
3. Record amount of FAS added each time solution turns into a rusty red color. Color change should be
kept consistent with all samples.
4. Exact FAS concentration is calculated by adding 1.00 mL of 0.017M potassiumdichromate for high
range, or 1.00 mL of 0.0033M potassiumdichromate for low range. Titrate again with FAS till color
change.
FAS conc. is calculated by:
M(FAS) = (1.00 mLK2Cr2O7 × 6 × Conc. K2Cr2O7 in M)/ml FAS used for titration
(3.9)
5. COD results are calculated as:
mg COD/L = ((mL FAS for Blank −mL FAS for Sample)×M(FAS)×8000)/mL sample (3.10)

Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) Analysis for Optimized
condition
Untreated WS show long ﬁbers with a smooth surface arranged in bundles. Pre treatment done at optimized
conditions shows breaking and cracking of surface into small parts, while still showing long unbroken chains
of cellulose underneath. Based on this it can be said that pre treatment causes breaking down of lignin
structure into lower molecular weight products, but not complete removal of lignin from the structure of WS.
This makes the pre treated WS suitable for biogas production as cellulose is more readily accessible by the
micro-organisms during Anaerobic Digestion (AD).
Alvarado-Morales et al.[24] show photocatalytic degradation of WS using TiO2.
In comparison to SEM
results obtained by them, it is seen that the treated WS from FA shows more segregation of the top layer,
indicating harsher treatment conditions.
Zheng et al.[42] show pre treatment of WS through a strong bases, hot water hydrolysis enzyme-hydrolysis.
In comparision, except for hot water pre treatment, all other pre treatments eﬀect the whole WS structure,
indicating cellulose, hemi-cellulose and lignin degradation. Hence, photocatalytic WS pre treatment in the
presence of FA is not as harsh as others, and therefore, the results are not expected to be as high as other
pre treatments indicate.

Another indicator of FA conc. decreasing with cycles was foaming of the solution during the start of the test.

gases from initial oxidation reactions, with ions and radicals eventually getting used in reaction. This foaming
was drastically reduced after Cycle1, indicating reduction of ions and radicals available in the solution.

Chapter 5
Conclusion and Future Work
The objective of the report was the optimization of photocatalytic degradation of WS using TiO2 activated
by UV irradiation in the presence of FA. Using ﬁxed WS loading of 1 g/L, the conc. of FA and TiO2 loading
were varied during a number of diﬀerent tests. The outputs measure are TPC% change and sCOD% change.
The optimization was done so as to maximize TPC% change while minimizing sCOD% change. The software
predicted optimized values after input of test data was 0.35M FA and 0 g/L TiO2. This corresponds to
TPC obtained at end of the test period of 66.8 mg/gWS. The experimentally obtained value from testing
the optimized solution was 67.8 mg/gWS, thereby supporting the software prediction.
To check for lignin degradation various tests were performed on treated WS. Lignin content of treated
and untreated WS was measured and the diﬀerence after the photocatalytic pre treatment was found to be
1.6%. XRD analysis done on the samples corroborates the results found from lignin content change with
an increase in CrI of 3%. Increase in crystallinity is theorized to be due to removal of amorphous (lignin)
content. Diﬀerence in FTIR spectra of samples indicates that peak reductions occur at 1648 and 1030 cm−1.
These peaks correspond to destruction of C-H and C-O linkages of lignin. The banks of hemicellulose and
cellulose remain the same and little to no change is observed in them. TCI can be calculated from FTIR
spectra wavelengths of 2900 and 1372 cm−1, both which correspond to bands related to cellulose. The change
in TCI value between treated and untreated WS was an increase of 5%. This also corresponds with CrI value
increase, substantiating the removal of amorphous material (lignin).
Images obtained from SEM analysis show considerable change in surface structure of the WS samples.
The treated samples show breaking and cracking of surface with long unbroken chains visible underneath.
These long unbroken chains can be identiﬁed as cellulose ﬁbers, the broken and cracked structures are said
to be lignin. The overall damage to the WS structure compared to acid/alkaline pre treatment is low. From
a visible perspective, change in color of WS can be observed post pre treatment, with loss of brown color.
Literature sources attribute loss of color due to reduction in ligin content of sample.
A secondary aim of the report was to attempt qualiﬁcation of high-value products that are obtained in
the reaction solution. This was done using a GC/MS and referred from NIST library. The compounds with
highest probability and closest matching ions and molecular weights were Acetic acid, Formic Acid, Isophta-
laldehyde, Phenol, Methylindole, Vanillin, Napthelenol, Phthalimide and Phenylfuran. An elemental analysis
of the treated and untreated WS samples was also done and observed was an increase of 1.9% carbon, an
increase of 4.8% hydrogen, a reduction of 23% nitrogen and a reduction of 2.3% oxygen. The reduction of ni-
trogen corresponds to detection of -amide compounds in treated WS samples from the GC/MS qualiﬁcation.
42
==PAGE:46==
It is also interesting to note that the C/N ratio increased from 77.3 in untreated WS to 96.9 in treated WS.
An additional test was done to check if formic acid in the solution could be used beyond one test procedure.
This process, called the Cycle test, used the same reaction solution but ﬁltered to remove the treated WS.
Each cycle had fresh WS added to it. The results from the test showed increase in the TPC production. An
analysis of the formate ion in the solution by HPLC indicated decreasing formate in every cycle, indicating
formate is not refreshed but degraded and lost through the test.
Increase in C/N ratio, from literature, shown and increase of methane production during the Biometha-
nation process. The BMP Assay done on treated and untreated WS samples show a similar trend, with
treated WS samples of Cycle1 showing a 10.72% increase in methane production over untreated WS, while
the treated WS samples of Cycle2 show a 15.86% increase in methane production over untreated WS. The
treated WS samples were also seen to degrade and reach the peak methane production faster than untreated
WS sample.
As a overall conclusion, the advanced oxidation of wheat straw by photocatalytic degradation in the
presence of formic acid is successful in removal and degrading lignin from the wheat straw into high-value
products in the reaction solution, while the leftover product can be used for the Biomethanation process with
no downsides.
Future Work
The current work investigated a novel method of photocatalytic degradation of lignin. Further work has
to be done as the mechanism of the process is yet unknown. An EPR study can be done in this respect.
Knowledge of the mechanism process will help increase the eﬃciency of the process.
The collimated beam experiment setup allows only a certain amount of UV rays to reach the reaction site.
A change in setup design could enhance the yield of high-value products while also increasing the eﬃciency
of the reaction.
The current study uses TiO2 which restricts the excitation energy to the UV spectrum. Change in catalyst
to one that may be excited by visible light in the presence of FA would be interesting to research.
