Abstract
DNA is said to be the code of life, but to understand it, learn from it, and utilize it
we need to decipher the information.
Conventional methods for calling variants rely on position-based statistical models. Re-
cently, Google employed a convolutional neural network to call variants using an context-
based approach. The prospect of encoding windows of read alignments as ”images” pro-
vides the tool with, so far, unused information.
This project aims to design a convolutional neural network speciﬁcally directed towards
calling biallelic genotypes and thereby identify single nucleotide variants.
Diﬀerent designs of convolutional ﬁlters were tested and concluded that valuable in-
formation resides within the sequence of a single read. The technical implementation is
an initial convolutional ﬁlter locally connecting the full image-width while only spanning
a single read.
The ﬁnal model, GeCCo, was benchmarked against state-of-the-art variant callers.
Despite the prima facie disadvantage of a relatively simplistic model, GeCCo yielded a
high performance (F1=98.9%) emphasizing the potential of directed designs.

Background in variant calling
1
Background in variant calling
1.1
General concepts of variant calling
This project revolves around calling Single Nucleotide Variants (SNV) through the identi-
ﬁcation of genotypes at independent loci. By calling, the community refers to ﬁnding and
annotating variations in the DNA. A genotype is, in this context, a representation of the nu-
cleotides at a locus in a diploid genome. Four diﬀerent nucleotides compose the building blocks
of DNA: Adenine (A), Guanine (G), Thymine (T), and Cytosine (C). The genotype is said to
be either homozygous or heterozygous based on the combination of parental nucleotides.
Single nucleotide variants are brought on by mutations.
Two types of DNA substitution
mutations occur: transitions and transversions. Transitions are mutations between A and G
or between C and T. The two groupings are based on the chemical structure of the nucleotides
and are referred to as purines and pyrimidines respectively. Transversions are interchanges
between the two chemical structures, purines and pyrimidines. Although there are twice as
many possible transversions, transition mutations occur more often since they are generated
at a higher frequency [15, 28].
At the same locus diﬀerent nucleotides may be present across a population. The diﬀerent
versions of variants are called alleles. The most frequently occurring allele in a population
is denominated the major allele. When a SNV constitutes more than 1% of the population
alleles it is annotated as a Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP).
When calling variants the query genome is aligned against a reference genome which simply
provides a standard of comparison by convention [24]. The reference represents a haploid
genome while the query genome, for humans, is diploid. At any locus the nucleotide in the
reference is termed the reference allele while each query nucleotide is coined the alternative
allele. Variants are categorized by the zygosity of the genotype. If a locus consists of identical
alternative alleles that match the reference allele the position is homozygous with the reference
and not a variant. However, if the alternative alleles are diﬀerent from the reference allele the
position is a homozygous variant. Heterozygous genotypes are also considered variants as the
diploid locus may contain both a reference allele and an alternative allele.
Variants may also consist of structural variants that occur over a larger DNA sequence.
Structural variants cover insertions/deletions (INDELs), duplications, copy-number variants
(CNVs), inversions and translocations [24]. These types of variants are, however, not consid-
ered in the project.
1.2
The principle and application of a variant calling tool
Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) has enabled entire human genomes to be sequenced
rapidly and aﬀordably.
However, the sequencing platforms do not yet produce complete,
ﬂawless genomes. Therefore, variant calling tools are being developed to identify true varia-
tions within the DNA and to ﬁlter out artifacts. Since diﬀerent platforms produce diﬀerent
artifacts and have dissimilar limitations, various approaches are being utilized, resulting in
a range of variant callers. Variant calling is not yet considered a solved problem and tool
development is ongoing, although the general performance is quite high [6, 55, 25].
The purpose of calling variants is to map the genome of individuals such that the genotype at
every locus is known. In the genome, researchers search for answers to e.g. evolution, heredity,
population genetics, migrations, and genetic diseases [16, 38, 1]. The biotech industry exploits
genomes of microorganisms to e.g. identify similar or novel genes in diﬀerent species.

Background in variant calling
1.2.1
The Genome Analysis Toolkit (GATK) haplotype caller
The GATK-HaplotypeCaller, GATK-HC, has been considered the golden standard of vari-
ant calling tools. The approach is assembly-based, contrary to conventional tools which are
position-based and require a read pileup. Further, the approach is novel in that it determines
genotype likelihoods to call variants.
GATK-HC speciﬁcally makes use of a phenomenon
known as haplotypes. Alleles located in the vicinity of each other tend to coexist more often
than by chance. These blocks of alleles are coined haplotypes [14].
The concept is based on constructing theoretical haplotypes via de Bruijn-like graphs from
a consensus of the reads covering a genomic region. The regions are selected based on high
concentrations of putative variants and are referred to as active regions. Thus, the active
regions are essentially reassembled and then realigned against the reference. The individual
reads are evaluated against the generated, theoretical haplotypes to determine the likelihood
for each combination. The putative single nucleotide variants contained in the active region
are then genotyped based on the nucleotide of the read with the maximum likelihood [50].
The workﬂow is illustrated in ﬁgure 1.
Figure 1: The four main processes constituting GATK-HC [50]. First, identiﬁcation of active regions deﬁned as
regions with high frequency of putative variants. Second, assembly of theoretical haplotypes using de Bruijn-
like graphs and realignment. Third, determine the per-read likelihoods given the haplotypes by pair-HMM.
Fourth and ﬁnally, assigning genotypes according to maximum likelihood of reads.

1.3.1
Reference genome
The purpose of a reference genome is to enable quick and aﬀordable assemblies of novel genome
sequences.
A reference genome is a representative example of a species’ sequence of nucleic acids. Refer-
ence genomes do not usually represent one speciﬁc individual, since they are often assembled
from DNA sequences originating from multiple donors. Such a mosaic of DNA from diﬀerent
donors is ﬁt for use because the major part of the genome is identical between individuals
and therefore provides a good approximation of the DNA sequence of any single individual.
Speciﬁc regions of the genome consist of high allelic diversity, such as the major histocompat-
ibility complex, where the query may diﬀer signiﬁcantly. These regions may be represented
with sets of alternative loci alongside the reference locus.
Thus, a reference genome is a
convention useful for nomenclature, but with no sense of correctness in terms of biological
functionality [24].
The limitations of mapping novel, individual genomes to a reference are that reads containing
DNA sequences not represented in the reference may cause mapping errors. Furthermore,
the genome consists of many identical or roughly identical sequences and repeats which allow
reads to map at multiple loci [13, 24].
Multiple diﬀerent assemblies or builds exist due to improvements implemented by the Genome
Reference Consortium. Diﬀerent assemblies might shift loci’s coordinates and are released
infrequently. It is therefore of high importance that the use of reference genome is clearly
cited.

As artiﬁcial neural networks are the most elementary models within the umbrella term, I will

2.1.3
Gradient descent and optimizations
Gradient descent is a method of minimizing an objective function, which, in the case of neural
networks, is the cost function C(θ), mentioned in 2.1.2 and further described in section 2.1.4.
The cost function is minimized by updating the parameters in the opposite direction of the
gradient of the cost function (∇θC(θ)). The learning rate η determines the size of the steps
taken to reach a minimum, be it local or global. The update is given by:
θ = θ −η · ∇θC(θ)
(6)
There are three variants of gradient descent: vanilla batch, stochastic, and mini-batch. The
methods diﬀer in how much data is used to compute the gradient of the cost function, resulting
in a trade-oﬀbetween accuracy of the parameter update and the time it takes to perform the
update [53].
Batch gradient descent updates the parameters based on the cost for the entire data set.
This is repeated for every epoch which results in redundant computations, as the gradients
are derived from similar examples. Thus, batch gradient descent is quite time consuming.
However, the method is guaranteed to converge to the global minimum for convex error
surfaces and to a local minimum for non-convex surfaces [53].
Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) updates the parameters based on the cost for every single
training example, and thus eliminates the redundancy making the updates much faster. The
limitation is that each example may not represent the true distribution of data and therefore
the method results in large ﬂuctuations in the parameters [53].
Mini-batch gradient descent updates the parameters based on the cost for a subset of the
entire training set. The method reduces the variance of the parameter updates, which lead to
more stable convergence. Common mini-batch sizes range between 32 and 256, but can vary
for diﬀerent applications [53].

2.1.3.1
Optimization algorithms
Despite the above mentioned methods gradient descent does not guarantee good convergence.
First oﬀ, selecting a proper learning rate can be diﬃcult. A learning rate too small may lead
to slow convergence, while a learning rate too large may hinder convergence by causing the
cost function to ﬂuctuate around the minimum or even to diverge. Additionally, the high
dimensional cost functions may contain diﬃcult regions similar to saddle points that are sur-
rounded by a plateau of the same error, which makes it notoriously hard for gradient descent
to escape, as the gradient is close to zero in all dimensions [8]. Therefore, diﬀerent optimiza-
tion algorithms have been developed.
Adagrad is an optimization algorithm that adapts the learning rate to the parameters [12].
Parameters associated with frequently occurring features are updated in smaller steps, i.e.
with a low learning rate.
Parameters associated with infrequent features are updated in
larger steps, i.e. with a high learning rate. Hence, it is well-suited for dealing with sparse
data. Unfortunately, during training the learning rates risk becoming so small that updates
practically cease to exist [61].
RMSprop is an extension of Adagrad that seeks to reduce its decreasing learning rate. Instead
of accumulating all past squared gradients, RMSprop restricts the window of past accumulated
gradients to some ﬁxed size and utilizes the average to update the learning rate. The method
is highly similar to the published optimizer Adadelta [61].
Further, the descent was optimized by additionally storing the exponentially decaying average
of past gradients. This second parameter provides aﬃnity for ﬂat minima in the Adaptive
Moment Estimation (Adam) algorithm [31]. The momentum-like parameter can of course
be modiﬁed using the Nestov approach, resulting in Nesterov-accelerated Adaptive Moment
Estimation (Nadam) [11].
2.1.3.2
Additional methods for assisting gradient descent
The optimization methods continue to unfold, however, other strategies can be implemented
to aid the gradient descent. By shuﬄing the training data after each epoch new paths through
the parameter space during the gradient descent are explored. Batch normalization ensures
stability of weights during back-propagation. Batch normalization normalizes activations at
each layer by subtracting mean and dividing by standard deviation. Consequently, batch nor-
malization adds the mean and standard deviation as two trainable parameters to each layer.
The normalization reduces the extent to which activations shift around due to changing inputs
and provides a more stable distribution. Thus, the gradient becomes less dependent on the
scale of the parameters. Finally, early stopping is a strategy to avoid descending into a local
minimum.
As mentioned in section 2.1.2, a general issue when working with deep neural networks is
vanishing and exploding gradients due to the chain rule. Extreme gradients can be handled
by clipping the gradient when it exceeds a set threshold or by normalizing the gradients.

2.2
Convolutional neural networks
A convolutional neural network (CNN) is an extension to artiﬁcial neural networks as de-
scribed in section 2.1. A CNN is based on the same mechanisms for propagating both forward
and backwards, however, the ﬂow of the information from the input is directed through some
additional operations. The main operations of a CNN are: convolutions, non-linearity trans-
formations, and pooling. These operations result in the network having additional types of
Page 10 of 59
==PAGE:19==
Background in neural networks
layers, compared to an ANN. The layers constituting a CNN are denoted: input layer, convo-
lutional layer, pooling layer, fully connected layer, and output layer. The following sections
will elaborate on the impact of these layers and their operations.
CNNs have proven very successful in image classiﬁcation and therefore I have adapted the ter-
minology and may therefore at times refer to an observation with all its attributes represented
as an image.
2.2.1
Input layer
The input, I, to a CNN is a three dimensional tensor, [W (I), H(I), C(I)]. In terms of an image
the dimensions are composed of the number of pixels in the ﬁrst and second dimension. The
third dimension is referred to as channels and store the red, green, and blue color intensities
at each pixel position of the image [17, 58, 18].
2.2.2
Convolutional layer
The convolutional layers are the main building blocks of a CNN, hence the name. Convolutions
in two dimensions can be considered image ﬁlters. The ﬁltering captures local dependencies
within the image which provides information that allows the network to classify the images.
The information is referred to as features and the result of a convolution is a feature map.
The feature map is produced by ”sliding” the ﬁlter over the image while dot products are
computed within the receptive ﬁeld. Thus, for each ﬁlter there exists one feature map. With
multiple ﬁlters the feature maps are said to be stacked, forming a three dimensional tensor in
which the third dimension is referred to as the depth (D) [62].
The ﬁlter may convolve past one or multiple pixels at a time, which is determined by the stride
(S). The ﬁlter size (F) determines the extent of local connectivity. The size and number of
the ﬁlters, as well as the stride, are tunable hyper-parameters [17, 58, 18].
The number of trainable parameters in the convolutional layer is proportional to the number
of ﬁlters and their size. Every neuron of the W · H · D neurons in the stacked feature maps
is connected to the input by W (F) · H(F) · C weights and a bias. The number of parameters
therefore amount to (W (F) · H(F) · C(I) + 1) · D. This means that all weights and biases
connected to the same depth slice are the same, and thus make use of parameter sharing.
The underlying assumption is that if a feature is useful to compute at some spatial position
[x1, y1], the feature should be equally useful to compute at position [x2, y2] [17].
The stride aﬀects the two ﬁrst dimensions of the feature map. With larger strides the di-
mensions decrease, which will later reduce the number of trainable parameters in the fully
connected layer. An approach to preserve the two ﬁrst dimensions is by padding the image
with zeroes, called zero-padding [17, 58, 18].
A CNN architecture may consist of one or more subsequent convolutional layers.
2.2.3
Pooling layer
Convolutional layers are usually followed up by a pooling layer. Pooling introduces down-
sampling of the feature map and thus reduces the number of parameters, while still retaining
the most important information. The technical implementation is to perform a selected oper-
ation within a spatial neighborhood, or window, of a feature map. The window slides across
the feature map, and for each stride the operation is repeated. The most applied operations
are maximum or averaging computations and the choice denotes the layer as max pooling or
average pooling. Thus, the hyper-parameters related to pooling layers are window size and
stride, while no trainable parameters are added [17, 58].

2.2.4
Fully connected layer
It is common practise that the second to last layer of a convolutional neural network consists
of a fully connected layer. In this type of layer every neuron is fully connected to every neuron
of the previous layer in contrast to the local connectivity. Therefore, this type of connectivity
is also called a dense layer. The three dimensional shape sustained in the previous layers is
ﬂattened to a single dimension.
The number of trainable parameters in this layer depends on the size of the ﬂattened tensor
and the number of hidden neurons. The hyper-parameters could be the number of neurons
in a layer and perhaps also the number of subsequent dense layers.
2.2.5
Output layer
The ﬁnal layer is the output, which presents the prediction or classiﬁcation of the network.
The output is always fully connected to the previous layer, whether it is a dense layer or a
ﬂattened convolutional layer. The number of output neurons correspond to the problem at
hand. In this project the network solves a classiﬁcation problem of 10 classes and therefore
the output layer consist of 10 neurons.
In a well functioning network the input image will be decomposed into features, or edges,
where speciﬁc neurons represent diﬀerent kinds of edges.
Combining signals from various
edges will form patterns, again represented by speciﬁc neurons. The simultaneous activation
of a set of patterns is what allows the network to classify the input. Thus, the input sets
in motion a cascade of neuron ﬁrings based on its contents of certain features. The training
process then becomes to piece together which patterns are correlated to which classes.
2.2.6
Miscellaneous layers
Other types of layers exist and may be included depending on the composition of the data and
how the model responds to their inclusion. If the input contains categorical attributes they
must be encoded to numerical values, however, still represent nominal values. This can either
be done with one-hot-encoding or with embedding. One-hot-encoding is carried out before the
input is fed to the network, while embedding is a layer within the network. One-hot-encoding
yields more channels of which the majority of the values will take the value zero. Embedding
circumvents this by projecting the categorical values in an n-dimensional space and ascribes
the coordinates to the channels representing that categorical attribute [43].
After the input and the embedding layer (if applied) the concatenating layer stacks the encoded
channels to form the correct tensor shape.
Later on in the network, batch normalizing layers may be implemented to standardize the
activations. This limits the range and zero-centers the activations. This may improve the
speed of training by reducing the covariate shift and make the weight update more stable.
Instead of updating every weight, only the parameters for normalization are updated [26].
Amending the network with the here mentioned layers can be tested and evaluated by regard-
ing them as hyper-parameters. Many more options exist, however, they have not been part
of the considerations for this project.
2.3
Neural networks - A new approach to variant calling
Despite dedicated endeavours, the currently available variant calling tools still falsely classify
variants and counterfeit variants. The expert-driven parameterized statistical models are lim-
ited by their required assumptions to make the computations possible. One such assumption
Page 12 of 59
==PAGE:21==
Background in neural networks
is that read errors are independent [9]. Although, the assumption is recognized as invalid, it
has been necessary to avoid the unknown likelihood function that models errors dependent
among multiple reads [41, 37, 36]. The advantage of neural networks is that such underlying
dependencies may be modelled within the complex interactions of neurons by learning from
data and without expert knowledge.
Further, conventional variant calling tools are built on statistical models speciﬁc for a par-
ticular NGS technology and data processing. With the continuous innovation of sequencing
platforms the current tools will shortly be insuﬃcient and outdated [49]. ANNs are amend-
able and hold the potential of being a universal tool, irrespective of preparation protocols,
sequencing technologies, genome build, and even species [48].
2.3.1
DeepVariant by Google Brain
DeepVariant is a variant calling tool which utilizes a deep convolutional neural network to
classify SNVs and small indels.
The tool was developed by the Google Brain Genomics
team in collaboration with Verily. In 2016 DeepVariant was submitted to the PrecisionFDA
Truth Challenge and won the SNP Performance category. The source code was made pub-
licly available in December 2017, and in September 2018 a paper was published in Nature
Biotechnology.
The tool consists of three main binaries, each respectively encode pileup positions, classify
the positions, and process the classiﬁcation into a standard variant call format (VCF). The
pipeline is illustrated in ﬁgure 3. The following sections elaborate on the diﬀerent steps of
the DeepVariant pipeline.
The very ﬁrst step in the DeepVariant pipeline is a haplotype-aware realignment of reads.
The method relies on building local de-Bruijn graphs that reveal the most likely haplotype,
similar to GATK-HC, as described in section 1.2.1.
Each locus in the genome is independently considered by collecting reads aligning to the
site. The diﬀerent alleles presented by the reads are classiﬁed into one of four categories: a
reference-matching base, a reference-mismatching base, an insertion with a speciﬁc sequence,
or a deletion with a speciﬁc length. The occurrences of the alleles are counted across all reads.
If the count exceeds a given threshold it is considered a true allele. If the proportion of reads
containing an allele exceeds yet another threshold, the allele is considered a candidate variant.
The biallelic candidate variants are recorded using a VCF-like format. During the allele count
reads are ﬁltered away using standard settings as in Samtools pileup [35, 54].
According to the published paper presenting DeepVariant [48], the candidate variants are en-
coded into RGB images. The images consist of a read per row while the columns correspond
to nucleotide sites up- and down stream from the candidate variant locus. The dimensions
are (100, 221). The RGB channel setup limits the number of attributes to three: base call,
base quality and strand orientation. Additionally, the pixel values are conﬁned within the
range of zero to 255. Thus, the ﬁrst channel containing bases encodes each nucleotide with
a discrete value at equal intervals between the distinct nucleotide values. Similar concept
is applied for read orientation with is also a categorical attribute. All three channel pixel
values are multiplied by a factor, alpha. Alpha represents whether the read supports any of
the alternative alleles or the reference. The reference sequence is included in the model by
allocating the ﬁrst ﬁve rows of each image. The reference is assigned a base quality score of
high value and assigned to the positive strand.
The model trained for variant calling is the Inception-v3 convolutional neural network which
was originally developed for image classiﬁcation problems [56, 57]. The architecture is very
advanced architecture consisting of 22 layers of complex, parallel convolutions and the number
of trainable parameters amount to approximately 23 million [57]. The parallel convolutions

3.1.2.2
Cleaning
Cleaning of the data was carried out in multiple steps. First, during pileup construction, then
during pileup parsing, and ﬁnally, during image encoding.
Filtering away reads may exclude the non-conforming bases from the pileup column, rendering
the position a non-variant, and therefore not included in the data set.
The ﬁrst step was to identify candidate variants from pileup columns for each available position
in the reference genome. The pileups were built using samtools mpileup, which has default
settings that ﬁlters the reads.
Reads are ﬁltered away if they are ﬂagged as unmapped, secondary alignments, failing quality
control, or ﬂagged as a duplicate. For a base to be considered in a pileup column it must be
recorded with a quality above 13, thus cleaning the pileup of low conﬁdence base calls. This
may also result in exclusion of non-conforming bases. Bases annotated as soft-clipped are not
included in the pileup, while the rest of the read may still form part of a pileup at some other
location.
When parsing the pileups, additional cleaning was implemented, if the instances did not carry
adequate information. Positions where the reference base is unknown were excluded, since a
genotype imputation is not applicable. If a pileup column only consisted of one or two reads
it was excluded, due to lack of information. Further, positions were also excluded where the
fraction of non-conforming bases was above 0.95. This last step was to reduce the number of
examples, assuming the classiﬁcation would be self-evident.
When encoding the images, reads containing indels were excluded. Including insertions would
potentially cause a lot of havoc in the ”image”. Insertions would result in padded areas in
the remaining reads, and if multiple reads contained insertions a relatively large proportion of
the image would consist of padding. Thus, relevant sequence could potentially be pushed out
Page 17 of 59

of the frame. The impact of a falsely annotated insertion was considered too great compared
to the gain of including insertions. Deletions would not cause the same issues, however, they
were not included either. It was assumed that the number of ﬁltered reads due to indels are
negligible.

The evaluation set was formatted as the training set to feed into the network.

subjected to recalibration.

using the haplotype comparison tool [45, 46]. Replicating the evaluation procedure of Deep-

restricted to variants within the conﬁdent regions.

consortium together with the validated VCF ﬁle.

3.1.4
Mini-batch generation
The ﬁnal step of data preparation for both training and evaluation was pre-formulation of
mini-batches. Mini-batches were prepared in advance due to slow encoding relative to a for-
ward pass through a network. The trade-oﬀis that a speciﬁc batch always consists of the
same examples in the same order. Each mini-batch was stored independently as an HDF5 ﬁle.
Summing up, the images, and thus the mini-batches, were generated by the following steps.
During the cleaning step, candidate variants were detected and their indexes in the reference
genome were logged. An index consists of the chromosome number and the position within the
chromosome. The genotypes for each candidate were inferred from the validated variant calls
if applicable, or otherwise from the reference genome. Based on the genotypes the 600,000
balanced training examples were sampled. The evaluation set was sampled from the GIAB
conﬁdence regions. For each instance in the sampled sets, up to 100 reads overlapping the
position in the genome were used to construct the pileup image of the instance. The selected
attributes were encoded or scaled, respectively, and written into an array centered around the
candidate variant. The dimensions of the array for each attribute are [100, 21]. The indices
of the array that were not covered by pileup reads were padded with zeros.
The mini-batch size determined the number of arrays produced for each attribute. While the
dimensions of a channel were kept invariant, diﬀerent mini-batch sizes were produced to test
the best conﬁguration for the problem. The mini-batch sizes produced for testing were 128,
1024, and 2048. It was assumed that the speciﬁc classiﬁcation problem would beneﬁt from
larger mini-batches than what is customarily used for other applications.

3.2.1
Model architecture
The learning method of this project is a convolutional neural network (CNN). The Python
deep learning library, Keras, was utilized as application programming interface for Tensorﬂow
to build CNNs.
To limit the scope of the project a general architecture, with a ﬁxed composition of layers,
was decided upon, see ﬁgure 8. The composition consists of: an input layer, an embedding
layer, three consecutive convolutional layers, a pooling, a dense layer, and an output layer.
Figure 8: Simple representation of the model architecture. The 3D input consists of four attributes: bases,
base quality, mapping quality, and read orientation, each with the shape (None, 100, 21). The dimensions are

layer consists of 10 neurons, one for each class.
3.2.2
Model training
A grid search was employed to search the hyper-parameter space for optimal combinations.
Parameters and values are detailed in section 3.2.3. Each combination of sampled hyper-
parameters was tested at least three times using a threefold stratiﬁed cross-validation strategy
and evaluated as the average performance (accuracy) of the three validation sets corresponding
to each model. The three models will in the following be collectively referred to as the threefold
model. The models were trained for a maximum of 200 epochs. However, genomic data is
exceptionally large for training so the term epoch was redeﬁned to 60 steps (mini-batch back-
propagation) which resulted in the epoch coverage of only a fraction of all data points. This
was done to increase the rate of validation set tests and facilitate early stopping.
Early
stopping monitored the validation accuracy for improvements above δ = 0.001 within 20
epochs.
3.2.3
Model selection
16 hyper-parameters were chosen for optimization. The optimization was divided into three
rounds of testing: i) rough test of parameter limits, ii) reﬁned test and iii) learning rate op-
timization. This was done to limit the total number of combinations and as such the total
number of model trainings needed for powerful statistical inference. Of course, such division
of the hyper-parameter selection masks potential inter-round parameter combinations, e.g.
ﬁxing parameter A in round 1 may inﬂuence the selection of parameter B.
The tested hyper-parameters and their respective values are presented in table 7 and 8. The
grid of the ﬁrst round was 7-dimensional and consisted of 1152 combinations of values. The
second grid was 4-dimensional and consisted of 288 combinations.
The ﬁnal grid was 1-
dimensional and thus did not contain any combinations, only a list of four values.

Method
The hyper-parameter values were sampled randomly from the grid.
However, parameters
favoring smaller models were sampled at higher frequency (probability). Other parameters
were favored as well, e.g. the gradient descent optimization algorithm Adam, since it is a high
performing common choice.
Model selection was carried out using Bayesian statistics to infer the diﬀerence between two
model parameter distributions: i) high-performance models and ii) low-performance models.
The hyper-parameter sampling frequency formed the basis for the prior probability distribu-
tion. The models were ranked according to the average validation accuracy across the three
folds and partitioned into top, medium, and bottom performing models. The frequency of
each hyper-parameter value was then collected in the top and bottom partitions. Assuming
even counts in both partitions, the likelihood of the actual count was computed for each
partition. Finally, the posterior probability distribution for each partition was approximated
using Marcov chain Monte Carlo sampling. The posterior distributions for the top and for
the bottom performing models were then compared by subtraction and the hyper-parameter
values were selected based on the 90% highest posterior density credibility interval of the
δ-distribution.
3.2.3.1
The ﬁnal model
After completion of three rounds of hyper-parameter tuning and selection, the ﬁnal model
was derived. The ﬁnal model is an ensemble of the three models comprised of the generally
best set of hyper-parameter values generated in the three fold cross-validation. Thus, the
ﬁnal model consists of three models with the same hyper-parameter values, but trained on
diﬀerent cross-validation sets. The ﬁnal threefold model was named GeCCo.
3.2.4
Model evaluation
The ﬁnal model evaluation can be split into two parts: model diagnostics and benchmarking.
The following two sections will each elaborate on the procedures.
3.2.4.1
Benchmark
In order to benchmark against DeepVariant and GATK-HC they too were run on the evalu-
ation set. Both tools were run as recommended by their developers. The resulting VCF ﬁles
were measured against the validated VCF using hap.py, a haplotype comparison tool. The
tool is not applicable for comparing the output of a CNN to a VCF. Therefore, the hap.py
metrics, precision, recall, and F1 score, were computed directly from the model output against
the encoded validated variant calls. Thus, the genotype classiﬁcation problem was collapsed
into a binary classiﬁcation of variant and invariant. Only the instances classiﬁed as variant
were included in the benchmark.
3.2.4.2
Model diagnostics
The convolutional neural network performance was evaluated on common machine learning
metrics such as accuracy, the Matthew’s Correlation Coeﬃcient, and the confusion matrix.
The metrics are accounted for in section 2.1.5. Additional custom-metrics were designed to
evaluate performance in relation to problem-speciﬁc characteristics. The diagnostics include
several approaches to elucidate the false classiﬁcations. False classiﬁcations are deﬁned as
classiﬁcations that do not match the genotype inferred from either the validated VCF or the
reference genome. In other words, the diagnostics do not consider whether a position is a
variant or not. That type of evaluation was restricted to benchmarking.
The problem-speciﬁc diagnostics consider the probability distributions of the predictions, the
classiﬁcation bias, and the read depths.

4.2.2
Round 2
Two parameters yielded credible, positive diﬀerences between top and bottom performing
models. The key-value pairs of the two hyper-parameters are as follows, i) pooling : maximum
pooling, ii) ﬁlter design : no. 22. Figures 12c and 12d present the values that were more
prevalent in top performing models.
Based on ﬁlter design no. 22, the optimal setting for convolutional and pooling ﬁlters was
determined to the following; the ﬁrst convolution consists of a (1, 21)-ﬁlter with a stride of
1, thus eﬀectively performing a 1D-convolution over the read dimension of the pileup. The
second and third convolution consists of a (5, 1)-ﬁlter, also with a stride of 1. At the end of
the three convolutions a single pooling step is carried out by a (2, 1)-ﬁlter with a stride of
(2, 1).
The 16th ﬁlter combination also yielded credible, positive diﬀerences (see appendix D.1.2),
however ﬁlter design no. 22 simply produced marginal better results. The two designs share
the common characteristic that the ﬁrst convolutional ﬁlter shape is ﬂat and thus facilitates
a horizontal local connectivity.
The number of convolutional ﬁlters as well as the number of hidden neurons were tested for a
second time. The selection of 30 ﬁlters per convolution still seemed to induce the best perfor-
mance with the credibility interval mostly to the positive side of δ = 0. Whether the number
of hidden neurons was 16 or 32 appears inconsequential to the performance. With a slight
skew to the left in the density distribution for 32 hidden neurons, this value was selected for
the ﬁnal model.
The selected values thus far comprise the detailed architecture conﬁgurations for the ﬁnal
model with a total number of 58,682 trainable parameters.

5
Discussion
5.1
Final model architecture
The overall aim of the project was to design a model speciﬁcally targeted towards identifying
genotypes and hence, call single nucleotide variants. The problem has been approached by
diﬀerent types of models of which most perform nicely, however, the problem is not yet said
to be solved. In this project the choice of learning model was a Convolutional Neural Network
(CNN). The hyper-parameters of the CNN were selected to optimize the predictive perfor-
mance. Of the limited hyper-parameter value ranges, a few values had a credible impact on
model accuracy.
The mini-batch was selected to contain 1024 instances, which is at least ten times more than
customary sizes [3, 40]. Often the claim is that models trained on large mini-batches do not
generalize well [30]. However, the counter-argument sounds that the optimal size must depend
on the data. A study found that mini-batch sizes up to 8k did not negatively aﬀect accuracy
[10], setting the boundaries for testing.
In this project it was found that despite mini-batch size, the mean number of epochs were
in all three cases close to 100. Thus, the training on the smaller mini-batch sizes included
fewer training examples to reach an accuracy maximum. However, the maximum validation
accuracies obtained with mini-batch size 128 were proven to be generally lower than the
accuracies obtained using a mini-batch size of 1024. Thus, mini-batch size of 128 led to sub-
optimal local maximum. If the NGS data is more noisy than the typical training data used
in most publications, the training requires larger mini-batch sizes. Increasing the mini-batch
size reduces the variances of the gradient descent updates and therefore the learning process
converges more steadily. Another motivation to test mini-batch sizes is often that larger sizes
speed up training time [3, 40], unfortunately this was not investigated.
Another surprising detail about the design of the model was how the local connectivity im-
pacted the performance. A regular convolutional ﬁlter is typically squared. The size is often
determined by the size of the salient parts. Due to unconventional location and shape of the
Page 34 of 59
==PAGE:43==
Discussion
candidate variant, the right ﬁlter shape for the convolution operation was of high interest in
the project. For the purpose of deriving the optimal ﬁlter shape, various designs of receptive
ﬁelds were tested.
The results showed a credible performance improvement when utilizing a 1D convoluting
ﬁlter spanning all 21 positions of the pileup. Additionally, similar ﬁlters spanning 10 positions
showed the same trend, however not as substantial. Favoring local connectivity within a single
read indicates that low level features are stored within the read sequence. The key attributes
of the feature extraction must, consequently, be the sequence of bases and the sequence of
base qualities, since mapping quality and read orientation is invariant within a read. Possibly,
surrounding base qualities give an indication of the validity of the base call of the candidate
variant and how a read should be evaluated by the model.
Filter design no. 22 inherently performs a 1D convolution over the reads, while the ﬁlters only
spanning 10 positions must convolve in 2D, such as ﬁlter design no. 16. The convolutions
in the subsequent layers are, of course, aﬀected by the previous convolutions through the
produced feature map dimensions, and alterations were therefore made to the ﬁlter designs
in some cases. Filter design no. 22 required a subsequent ﬁlter shape that corresponds to
the collapsed position-dimension. As a result, the ensuing ﬁlter convolved the feature map
in 1D across the read-dimension. Hence, the second feature map may store how the reads
aligned and whether they were concordant. Additionally, the impact of each read may be
substantiated by the mapping quality and the read orientation attributes.
With ﬁlter design no.22 the two tasks of extracting quality environment and read concordance
were clearly separated between layers, while the 2D ﬁlters by nature mixed the signals.
Another key observation to comment on is that uniform ﬁlter designs inﬂict highly diﬀerent
performance depending on the choice of padding. No padding, as in ﬁlter design no. 13,
15 and 17, appears highly detrimental to the performance, especially when compared to full
padding as in no. 12, 14, and 16. This indicates that necessary information is also located
at the edges. Unfortunately design no. 22 was not tested with padding, but it could have
been interesting to investigate how diﬀerent levels of padding aﬀected the performance in that
speciﬁc case also.
The notion that important information is located at the edges also begs the question whether
increased width of pileup image could have had a positive impact on performance. The input
dimensions were, however, not tested as hyper-parameters.
As mentioned above, ﬁlter design no. 16, and less so no. 12, also appeared frequently among
the top performing models. These two designs were both only tested in combination with
very aggressive pooling. The large receptive ﬁeld may be lossy and subversive to the learning
process, however the alternative was never realized.
In summary, the initial feature extraction occurs across the full width of the pileup image.
Thus, the low level features lie within the sequential information provided within a read.
Even the image edges appear inﬂuential, and perhaps the model would have beneﬁted from
a wider pileup image including more bases around the candidate variant.
The following
feature extraction occurs across reads and perhaps detects concordance to identify whether
the candidate variant is a heterozygous or homozygous genotype. Then ﬁnally, the model
may put the pieces together to ascribe the speciﬁc genotype.
5.2
Model performance
The benchmark proved GeCCo to be a high performing model, although, not top performing.
However, clearly DeepVariant and GATK-HC utilized post-ﬁltering to leverage their perfor-
Page 35 of 59
==PAGE:44==
Discussion
mance. Thus, to ensure the best possible model, thresholding the genotype probabilities to
improve variant call certainty would be required with GeCCo as well.
The benchmark also revealed that the ratio between heterozygous and homozygous genotypes
was larger in all query sets than in the validated variants. Thus, the callers generally as-
sign heterozygous genotypes too readily. This also aligned with the model diagnostics which
demonstrated in more cases that GeCCo is biased towards heterozygous genotypes. The full
confusion matrix (ﬁgure 14a) showed a stronger accuracy within the heterozygous genotypes.
Moreover, the counts of genotypes clearly showed the trend of underestimating homozygous
genotypes while overestimating heterozygous genotypes. Further, the counts revealed how the
evaluation data consisted of large class imbalances.
As the heterozygous genotypes inherently are interpreted as variants, the bias towards het-
erozygotes may cause the large number of false positives. As it is unlikely that sequencing
errors are identical at the same locus, the resulting artifacts come in the guise of heterozygous
genotypes. Consequentially, GeCCo is not speciﬁc enough to discern sequencing artifacts from
true variants.
The skew towards favoring heterozygous genotypes may have arisen from the artiﬁcially bal-
anced training set where heterozygotes were presented to the network more frequently than
in the evaluation set. The network has therefore not acquired the necessary knowledge of how
to handle the many diﬀerent and diﬃcult scenarios of homozygous genotypes.
Perhaps the model would have beneﬁted from a more natural representation of classes. How-
ever, a completely natural distributed training set would risk turning the predictions in favor
of homozygous genotypes. It has been proposed that while the minority class is still plentiful
class imbalance should not cause a bias [2], which ought not to be a problem with NGS data.
Alternatively, the reference genome could have been included as an attribute. The reference
could provide prior information of what might be expected at a certain position. However,
the reference genome does not provide a more truthful sequence of bases and is more or less
an arbitrary standard [24].
Instead, databases of known Single Nucleotide Polymorphism
(SNP)s could in some manner be included. Either a base-quality recalibration step could be
introduced as a mandatory part of the GeCCo pipeline as advised by GATK or the known
SNPs could be introduced as an attribute.
5.3
Comparison of deep learning methods
As expected, DeepVariant maintained the leading position as the best performing tool. The
advanced architecture of 22 layers involving inception modules amount to 23 million param-
eters [57]. In comparison GeCCo only consists of approximately 60.000 trainable parameters
and three sequential layers of convolutions. Further, the obstacle of adopting problem-speciﬁc
ﬁlter shapes was bypassed in the CNN architecture by the incorporation of inception modules
where several convolutions are carried out in parallel with various ﬁlter shapes [48, 57, 56].
Above placing notable consideration on memory and power usage the DeepVariant archi-
tecture does not reveal much of the learning process, which legitimises the development of
GeCCo.
Despite the prima facie disadvantage of a relatively simplistic model, the ﬁne performance of
GeCCo supply evidence in the potential of targeted designs.
A major diﬀerence between DeepVariant and GeCCo pipelines lies in the image encoding of
candidate variants. The encoding of NGS data into an RGB image results in a conversion
of nominal values into ordinal values which is not recommended for neural networks. The
implementation seems like a quick ﬁx to comply with the three channel structure of RGB
images. Preferably, one-hot encoding or embedding is applied for categorical attributes, as in
GeCCo. However, the apparent weakness does not keep DeepVariant from excelling.

The pileup image input of DeepVariant had the shape (100, 221) [57, 48], thus 110 bases
around the candidate variant position were included.
Based on the produced results, the
wider shape may have a positive impact on the predictive performance, as discussed in section
5.1. The input shape was, however, not considered a hyper-parameter in the development of
DeepVariant. Similarly, the mini-batch size of 32 was not considered a hyper-parameter by

DeepVariant was trained on the full HG005 genome as well as the full HG001 genome [48]
both provided by GIAB [64]. The vast amounts of data allowed the model to prepare for
many diﬀerent atypical and diﬃcult instances. Certainly, the number of instances selected for
the training of GeCCo were depleted, since the set was circulated by the epochs. Possibly the
model would have beneﬁted from a larger training set. Further, one might speculate whether
there is an advantage in training on genomes from multiple individuals.
5.4
Classiﬁcation bias’
The model diagnostics revealed a bias towards heterozygous genotypes, which was perhaps
explained by the class imbalance issue. Within the homozygous genotypes classiﬁcation of
’CC’ and ’GG’ was proportionally more erroneous. Again, the issue of class imbalance comes
into play, since the content of C and G in general is underrepresented in genomic data [4]. The
misrepresentation originates from technical issues in the sequencing due to stronger bonds of
G-C nucleotide pairs. Therefore regions containing a lot of G’s and C’s are often covered at a
lower read depth and with lower conﬁdence calls [4]. This trend was also displayed in both the
training and the evaluation sets, see appendix B. Furthermore, the model diagnostics showed
that there was a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in coverage between correct and incorrect classiﬁcations.
Thus, instances being classiﬁed in GC-rich regions are more likely to be misclassiﬁed, and the
chance that an instance is either a G or a C would be equally elevated.
Regarding the heterozygous genotypes the correct classiﬁcation conﬁdence interval was both
high and narrow. The unequal representation of heterozygous genotypes is expected due to
the natural substitution bias for transitions over transversions of nucleotides [15].
5.5
Limitations
The limitations of this project range from the training and evaluation data to the scope of
the analysis. The most high-level sources of insuﬃciency will be explored in the following.
5.5.1
The validated variants
Supervised learning is based on the foundation of labeled data, where the labels represent the
ground truth. However, when the data is an abstraction of a phenomenon that we can only
indirectly observe, the validity of the label becomes questionable. In the case of NGS data
we rely on the technology to mediate the DNA as it is. However, artifacts do occur and even
though they can, to some extent, be statistically modelled, we cannot rely completely on the
output. Diﬀerent technological platforms exist, each with their own shortcomings [64, 34].
Frequently occurring variations are self-conﬁrming and are annotated in databases as SNPs.
Unfortunately it is more diﬃcult to call rare and novel SNVs, since they might as well be
undetected artifacts [33].
In order to approach the ground truth, the GIAB consortium set out to deﬁne sets of high
conﬁdence variants [65, 64]. Even though an extensive amount of work has been put into
truthfully representing the sequenced genome the validated variant call set has some limita-
tions. The GIAB data set features variants only within regions of the genome that are easily
Page 37 of 59
==PAGE:46==
Discussion
sequenced by the existing tools.
Thus, diﬃcult regions such as homopolymer sequences,
moderately diverged repeats, and segmental duplications are depreciated. Because diﬀerent
variant calling tools fail to reach consensus in those regions the validated variants become bi-
ased [64]. When novel methods attempt to call variants within diﬃcult regions the absence of
the variant will result in false positive categorization and lead to an ostensible lower accuracy
[63, 34]. Thus, when evaluating new tools one might have to carefully inspect the variants
outside the conﬁdent regions.
Further, since GeCCo is based on supervised learning, the scope of the model may be limited
to a performance that is sub-optimal if it does not succeed to generalize beyond the amplitude
of current tools. Further, the means of validating previously unrecognized SNVs is not yet
established. These challenges may hamper future method development. In this project, the
benchmark was restricted to conﬁdent regions, and thus the potential for novel variant calls
was not explored.
Finally, even though a consensus of variant callers deemed a certain position a variant this
might not be the truth anyway. However, it is beyond the topic of this project to discuss the
accuracy of the technologies.
5.5.2
Evaluation set-speciﬁc pre-processing
In order to benchmark against GATK-HC additional pre-processing steps were taken to com-
ply with the best practises of the tool. The evaluation of GeCCo was then carried out on
two sets, one pre-processed just as the training set and one further pre-processed according
to GATK instructions. An initial apprehensiveness about the GATK pre-processing of the
evaluation set in contrast to the basic pre-processing was that the information structure would
change so much that the tuned parameters of the model would no longer recognize the same
signals. The resulting performances of GeCCo on either evaluation set, however, were indis-
tinguishable. The consistency of the results indicate that the pre-processing steps had little
eﬀect on how the information was picked up by the model. The base quality recalibration does
not change the scale of base qualities, but modiﬁes the single instance values according to how
much prior knowledge is available about variance at that locus [59]. The other pre-processing
step of local indel realignment realigns reads containing indels. Since these reads were not
included in the GeCCo input pileup images, the realignment would not aﬀect the information.
5.5.3
General pre-processing
The general pre-processing steps of down-sampling from 300x read coverage to 30x read cov-
erage has of course removed reads from the training set. Thus, reads containing variants may
have been removed resulting in elided candidate variant loci. Setting the limit to 100 reads
per pileup image did not exclude variants, since the maximum coverage of the instances was
60 reads. The coverage was shown to have an eﬀect on the accuracy of classifying genotypes.
Thus, lower than 30x coverage would require a diﬀerent approach in the modeling. A higher
than 30x coverage would presumably enhance performance.
Finally, exclusion of reads containing indels may have distorted the impression from the pileup
at the candidate variant loci.
5.5.4
Data and learning
It was assumed that the method for calling variants was independent of sample origin, as well
as chromosome number and location. Further, it was assumed that the genome build used
to map the sequence alignments was without signiﬁcant importance. The assumptions were
primarily based on the results from Poplin et al. describing DeepVariant [48]. Therefore, the
training data became limited to origin from a single individual aligned against a commonly
Page 38 of 59
==PAGE:47==
Discussion
used reference genome. Further the data was randomly down-sampled from the perception
that data was in excess. Accordingly, the epoch deﬁnition was customized so that the model
parameters could be saturated without having to pass the full training set. However, the
model parameters were not saturated before an entire cycle, but continued on average for 15
cycles. Therefore, more instances could have been included. Finally, from the progression of
validation accuracy and loss during training, the model performance seemed to reach a plateau,
while the training never progressed to a clearly, visible decline in performance. Perhaps the
patience could have been longer to actually register the overﬁtting.

Conclusion
6
Conclusion
With this project I have shown that it is possible to construct a simple convolutional neural
network which is speciﬁcally designed to classify genotypes and thereby call variants. The key
insights gained into the architecture were that feature extraction appears to occur in a single
dimension at a time. The single read context of bases and base qualities provided the ﬁrst, low
level features. The subsequent features learned whether the reads were congruent, weighted
by mapping quality. The amassed features call the genotype of an instance and do it to a
satisfactory accuracy of 97.2%. The model, denominated GeCCo, was benchmarked against
the golden standard variant caller, GATK-HC, and the precisionFDA awarded variant caller,
DeepVariant.
To perform the benchmark, the model predictions were post-processed into
the binary problem of calling single nucleotide variants. The existing tools were superior in
performance, however, the displayed performance supported the thesis that a targeted design
was comparable, nevertheless, with an F1 score of 98.9%.
6.1
Future perspectives
The scope of the project was to construct a model that was speciﬁcally designed to classify
genotypes for loci in the genome represented with inconsistent sequence reads. Therefore, the
decision was made to focus on single nucleotide variants and disregard other types of variation,
such as indels, in the classiﬁcation problem. To expand the model one might include indels
both in the input data and as labels. This would make the model generally more applicable
and probably also more robust, when including all available data.
The immediate future perspectives of GeCCo include training the model on reads from other
sequencing platforms than Illumina as well as training on data from multiple individuals.
Since diﬀerent sequencing technologies result in diﬀerent patterns of errors it has been diﬃcult
constructing a single variant caller that accounts for the various patterns. A CNN may be
constructed such that the sequencing technology appears as an input attribute. Similarly,
when including data from more individuals their ethnicities may become an attribute as well.
Additionally, a never ceasing challenge is to further improve the performance of GeCCo. More
data is deﬁnitely available to train on. This could allow for longer training until the model
has seen all instances or until a saturation. The early stopping criterion could be loosened in
favor of implementing regularization, such as drop-out.
The vast space of network topologies was, of course, not merely explored within this project
frame. An obvious step toward performance improvement is a deeper network topology. The
presented DeepVariant is an extraordinary example of a deep network that yields a high
performance. With plentiful computing power, more layers, more feature maps, and more
advanced topologies could be tested and adopted. The impact of pooling was not closely
investigated, and perhaps a targeted design could enhance the extraction of relevant features.
Finally, to facilitate applicability of GeCCo the run-time bottleneck of image encoding must
be addressed. The ideal scenario would be to replace pysam with a dedicated BAM-to-pileup-
image converter that can generate 1000s of pileup images on a sub-second level.
A natural future perspective of models like GeCCo is the utility within somatic variant calling.
Several tools also exist within this discipline, however, the consensus of the tools is often very
limited. Thus, the trade-oﬀbetween precision and recall becomes very large, and in clinical
settings this may be very costly. The main challenge lies within the heterogeneity of genetic
material from tumors, where only a small fraction of cells contain a mutation [60]. In order to
overcome the individual limitations exhibited by each of the current somatic variant calling
tools, GeCCo could potentially be extrapolated to navigate in the more intricate space of
Page 40 of 59
==PAGE:49==

somatic mutations.
Aside from variant calling speciﬁc for somatic cell lines, CNNs could be targeted towards
genomic data from ancient samples or metagenomic samples. These are also areas within
genomic research that are challenged by deeply underlying correlations, albeit very diﬀerent
problems. Ancient DNA is often scarce and of low quality, making imputations about the
original genome diﬃcult. Metagenomic samples consists of a pool of genomic material from
multiple microorganisms of diﬀerent species and, likely, also diﬀerent strains.
The major
challenge lies within identifying conspeciﬁc strains for strain-level analysis by disentangling
the pool of SNVs [7].
Further, the microbial genomes are generally haploid, making the

Each record represents a position in the genome. The position may have been called as a SNV,
an INDEL, a CNV, or the position may simply refer to an invariant. The ﬁrst two features
allow for localization of the variant. The type of variant may be deduced from the alternative
allele feature. The quality feature is only of interest when multiple samples are present in
the VCF, since it is a measure of how conﬁdently the call was made based on the presence
of variation in the available samples. The calls have been submitted to diﬀerent types of
ﬁltering according to base quality or if VCF represent multiple samples, a ﬁlter may annotate
positions covered in less than half of the samples. The information passed in INFO is very
diverse, from database annotations to simple statistics. Relevant databases are dbSNP and
HapMap. The statistics, however, are mostly interesting when multiple samples are contained
in the VCF ﬁle. If the record contains a structural variant the type is annotated along with
the end position of the variant.
Finally, the tag-value pairs of FORMAT and SAMPLE originally consisted of genotype, geno-
type quality, read depth, and haplotype quality. However, newer tools such as GATK-HC have
introduced additional measures such as allele depth and normalized Phred-scaled likelihoods
of the possible genotypes.
The genotype of a sample consists of two values for diploid organisms and may appear as
follows: 0/0, 0/1, 1/1. A 0 refers to the base present in the reference, while a 1 refers to the
base presented in the alternative allele ﬁeld. Thus, the genotype outcomes are: homozygous
with the reference (a non-variant), heterozygous, and homozygous with the alternative allele.
When consecutive variants originate from the same haplotype, the variants are said to be
phased, and are therefore separated with a ”—” instead of a ”/”. Thus, the genotype of the
ﬁrst record from a haplotype is always annotated with a ”/”.
The genotype quality is the Phred-scaled probability that the genotype call is incorrect. The
probability is calculated diﬀerently depending on the tool. The point of reference for , for
Page 44 of 59
==PAGE:53==
Raw ﬁle formats
example, is the probability of observing k reference alleles among M chromosomes given the
data and the sample allele frequency spectrum, which is a distribution of the allele frequencies
of a given set of loci, typically SNPs, in a sample. GATK-HC takes its point of reference in the
likelihoods of the possible genotypes. Each likelihood is transformed by the Phred-scale and
normalized so that the most likely genotype takes the value zero while the remaining genotypes
are annotated with the diﬀerence of their likelihood to the most likely genotype.
GATK
interprets the genotype quality as the Phred-scaled conﬁdence that the assigned genotype is
correct, and this value corresponds to the normalized Phred-scaled likelihood of the second
most likely genotype.
However, despite this standardized format, variants in a VCF ﬁle may be presented diﬀer-
ently because the same event can be expressed in multiple ways. The diﬀerences in variant
presentation may be due to inclusion of diﬀerent numbers of reference bases or combination of
two adjacent SNPs into one haplotype. To compare variant calls between diﬀerent pipelines
several tool exists.
