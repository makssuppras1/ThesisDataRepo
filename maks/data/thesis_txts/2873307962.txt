Abstract
Human norovirus (HuNoV) is a leading cause of foodborne illness globally, known for
its high infectivity, environmental persistence, and ability to remain viable under a
range of environmental conditions. This study investigated the effectiveness of UV-C
irradiation in inactivating murine norovirus (MNV-1), a surrogate for HuNoV, and
bacterial biofilms on stainless steel surfaces under different conditions.
A dose-dependent reduction in viral load was observed, with mean log10 reductions
increasing from 2.17 log10 PFU/cm2 at 12.5 mJ/cm2 to 3.5 log10 PFU/cm2 at 25
mJ/cm2, after which a plateau was reached. Comparative analysis with aqueous UV-
C inactivation data suggests that physical shielding on surfaces may limit treatment
efficacy. Additionally, the choice of drying method during the experimental setup
influenced viral viability prior to UV-treatment
For bacterial biofilms, UV-C exposure at 50 mJ/cm2 led to moderate reductions, with
no significant difference between 5- and 24-hour biofilm maturations, though older
biofilms appeared to be more resistant, likely due to increased concentration. Cross-
streak assays revealed microbial interactions that may influence species persistence,
with some strains showing reduced growth in the presence of others. However, no
direct inhibition was observed, indicating that UV-C effects were not confounded by
interspecies antagonism.
These findings highlight the limitations of UV-C disinfection on stainless steel surfaces
and biofilms, underscoring the importance of surface properties, microbial context,
and exposure conditions in determining treatment outcomes.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In recent years, food safety has become an increasingly important public health pri-
ority, aiming to protect consumers from foodborne illnesses through proper handling,
preparation, and storage of food. Among the various causes of foodborne illnesses, vi-
ral pathogens play a significant role. A leading example is human norovirus (HuNoV),
the primary cause of foodborne viral gastroenteritis, and one of the top five agents
responsible for food-related outbreaks globally (Pang 2017).
Norovirus (NoV) infections are transmitted primarily through person-to-person con-
tact, consumption of contaminated food or water, or touching of surfaces contam-
inated with viral particles.
Person-to-person transmission often occurs via direct
contact such as handshakes or indirect exposure to contaminated environments. Con-
sumption of contaminated food or water is another key pathway, particularly when
food is handled by infected individuals or water sources are polluted. Even being in
the same room where someone has vomited may result in infection due to aerosolized
viral particles (Verhoef et al. 2015; Marks et al. 2000; Kirsten Mattison 2011).
Common contamination sources include surfaces and equipment in food production
environments, such as door handles, faucets, knives, or point-of-sale terminals, par-
ticularly when handled by infected kitchen staff. Contamination can occur at any
stage of the food supply chain, from primary production to processing, distribution,
and serving. In agriculture, the use of contaminated irrigation water (e.g., for berries)
or shellfish harvested from polluted waters are known risk factors. In wholesale and

retail settings, outbreaks are often linked to the use of contaminated raw ingredients
or ready-to-eat foods as well as to employees working while still infectious.
Typical symptoms of NoV-related gastroenteritis include sudden-onset nausea, vom-
iting, and sometimes diarrhea. For otherwise healthy individuals, the illness is self-
limiting and resolves within a few days. The incubation period ranges from 24 to 48
hours (typically 33–36 hours), and symptoms usually last 24 to 60 hours. However,
individuals can remain infectious even after symptoms have subsided (Danish Veteri-
nary and Food Administration 2025; Thougaard et al. 2021).
NoV is a highly resilient, nonenveloped single-stranded ribonucleic acid (RNA) virus
and does not cause any visible, taste, or smell alterations in contaminated food. This
lack of sensory change is typical for viruses, which do not metabolize or produce
substances outside of a host. It can persist in the environment for extended periods
due to its exceptional stability. Lacking a lipid envelope, NoV can withstand freez-
ing temperatures, acidic and alkaline conditions, desiccation, and even exposure to
many commonly used disinfectants (Capece, Tobin, and Gignac 2025). NoV can be
effectively inactivated by exposure to high-concentration chlorine solutions or by heat
treatment, such as boiling (Danish Veterinary and Food Administration 2025).
The infectious dose of NoV is extremely low — fewer than 20 viral particles may be
sufficient to cause illness. Infected individuals excrete large quantities of the virus in
feces and vomit, ranging from 1 million to 1 billion particles per gram. While NoV
does not replicate on its own, it multiplies efficiently in human host cells, particularly
within intestinal epithelial cells. To mitigate the spread of NoV in food environments,
the Danish Food Administration recommends disinfecting surfaces with a 1:10 dilution
of household bleach containing 5.2% sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl), corresponding to
approximately 5000 ppm (parts per million) free chlorine, with a minimum contact
time of 5 minutes (Danish Veterinary and Food Administration 2025; Pang 2017;
Graaf, Villabruna, and Koopmans 2017).

1.1
NoV Outbreaks and Public Health Impact
HuNoV is recognised as one of the most common causes of foodborne illness and gas-
troenteritis globally, with a substantial impact on public health systems. The virus is
responsible for approximately 20% of all foodborne illnesses, which can be attributed
to its dominant presence in outbreaks linked to contaminated food and surfaces. In
2015, the World Health Organization (WHO) identified NoV as one of the top five
causes of deaths associated with foodborne illnesses. This classification placed HuNoV
alongside major bacterial pathogens such as Campylobacter and Salmonella in terms
of global burden. According to WHO estimates, NoV is responsible for an annual
burden of 685 million cases of diarrhea worldwide, with a 95% Confidence Interval
(CI) ranging from 491 million to 1.1 billion cases per year (Pires, Fischer-Walker, et
al. 2015). WHO estimates that NoV contributes to an estimated 212,489 deaths per
year (95% CI: 160,595–278,420), making it a significant public health concern, par-
ticularly in low-income regions. Approximately 85% of all NoV illnesses and nearly
99% of deaths occur in developing countries, where access to clean water, sanitation,
and medical treatment remains limited (Pires, Fischer-Walker, et al. 2015).
The geographical distribution of NoV-related deaths is highly disproportionate, with
Southeast Asia and Africa alone accounting for 85% of all global NoV fatalities. This
disparity is largely due to insufficient healthcare infrastructure, poor hygiene prac-
tices, and a lack of effective food safety regulations in many low-income countries.
Children under the age of five are particularly vulnerable to NoV infections in these
regions, making up 30% of illness cases and 25% of deaths. The high mortality rate
among young children is attributed to severe dehydration, malnutrition, and limited
access to medical interventions. In high-income countries, NoV infections tend to
result in lower mortality rates, as advanced healthcare systems and improved sanita-
tion help mitigate the severity of infections. However, NoV remains a leading cause
of gastroenteritis outbreaks in hospitals, nursing homes, and long-term care facili-
ties, disproportionately affecting elderly populations. Unlike in developing nations,
fatalities in high-income regions are more frequent among the elderly and immuno-
compromised individuals. National data from Denmark offer further insight into the

1.2
Economic burden of Norovirus Infections
A global economic modeling study by Bartsch et al. (2016) estimated the financial im-
pact of NoV, identifying substantial losses associated with both healthcare costs and
productivity reductions. The direct costs related to NoV cases, including hospitaliza-
tion, medical treatment, and healthcare system expenditures, were estimated to have
a median value of $4.2 billion annually (95% Uncertainty Interval (UI): $3.2–$5.7 bil-
lion). The most substantial financial burden however, comes from indirect costs, par-
ticularly in terms of lost productivity, which accounted for an estimated $56.2 billion
per year (95% UI: $40.9–$78.3 billion). When combined, the total global economic
burden of NoV is estimated to be $60.3 billion annually (95% UI: $44.4–$83.4 billion).
According to the authors, premature mortality accounted for approximately half of all
productivity-related losses. Productivity-related losses accounted for an overwhelm-

1.3
Surrogate Viruses for HuNoV
Surrogate viruses play a crucial role in HuNoV research, allowing scientists to study
its persistence, transmission, and response to disinfection despite recent advances
in laboratory cultivation, which remains complex and costly (Hayashi et al. 2024).
Until recently, it was not possible to cultivate HoNoV in the laboratory, which is
why surrogates have been extensively studied (Hayashi et al. 2025). The choice of
surrogate is important since different models provide varying degrees of similarity to
HuNoV, influencing how results can be interpreted. Murine Norovirus (MNV) and
Tulane Virus (TV) are among the most commonly used surrogates, though they differ
in key characteristics, each offering advantages and limitations depending on the study
focus (Kniel 2014; Hirneisen and Kniel 2013).
MNV is widely used due to its genetic similarity to HuNoV, belonging to the NoV
genus and sharing structural traits. It can be easily propagated in cell culture and
remains highly stable across a range of environmental conditions, including resistance
to pH fluctuations, chlorine exposure, and prolonged survival in refrigerated water.
These features make it useful for studying viral persistence and inactivation methods.
MNV lacks the ability to bind to histo-blood group antigens (HBGAs), a main mech-

==PAGE:20==

anism in HuNoV infection, making it less suitable for studying viral attachment and
host interactions (Hirneisen and Kniel 2013).
TV, on the other hand, does bind to HBGAs, making it structurally more comparable
to HuNoV with respect to host interaction studies. However, it is more sensitive to en-
vironmental stressors than MNV, and thus, exhibits greater susceptibility to chlorine
and extreme pH. For instance, TV infectivity decreases after five days at 4°C, whereas
MNV remains stable for over 30 days. These differences make TV more relevant for
receptor-binding studies but less effective for assessing environmental persistence and
resistance to disinfection (Hirneisen and Kniel 2013).
Other surrogates have been used in HuNoV research, including Feline Calicivirus
(FCV) and bacteriophage MS2. FCV was widely used prior to the implementation of
MNV as a surrogate virus, but has limitations; as a respiratory virus rather than an
enteric one, it exhibits distinct stability and inactivation profiles. Bacteriophage MS2,
though non-enveloped like HuNoV, is structurally different. As a bacterial virus, it
cannot replicate in human cells, limiting its use to environmental studies, particularly
in water systems (Richards 2012).
Given that no single surrogate fully replicates all HuNoV’s characteristics, different
surrogates are used depending on the specific research question. In summary, MNV
is ideal for studies on viral persistence and inactivation methods, while TV provides
insight into viral attachment to human receptors. FCV has historical relevance in
disinfection studies, though it has been largely replaced by more suitable surrogates
in recent years, and bacteriophage MS2 offers insights into virus behavior in certain
environmental conditions (Kniel 2014; Hirneisen and Kniel 2013).
1.4
UV-C and Inactivation of Microorganisms
Ultraviolet (UV) radiation can be classified into three types based on wavelength:
UV-A (320–400 nm), UV-B (280–320 nm), and UV-C (100–280 nm) (ScienceDirect
2025).
Among these, UV-C has attracted considerable attention due to its high-
6 of 63
==PAGE:21==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
energy photons, which can cause direct damage to the nucleic acids of microorganisms,
making it an effective tool for inactivation processes and a promising candidate for
applications for the disinfection of surfaces, water, air, and various materials (Gray
2014).
In a study investigating the inactivation of FCV, used as a surrogate for
HuNoV, UV-C exposure led to a significant reduction in viral infectivity. When FCV
was subjected to UV-C radiation at doses of 20–30 mJ /cm2 with UV255 and UV281,
a decrease of approximately 48–56% in measurable RNA copy numbers was observed,
corresponding to a 4- to 5-log10 reduction in infectivity (Tanaka et al. 2018a). These
findings suggest that RNA genome degradation is the primary mechanism responsible
for the loss of viral activity following UV-C treatment.
The study also revealed no significant changes in the structural appearance of the
FCV capsid after UV-C irradiation, indicating that the viral capsid remained largely
intact despite the internal genomic damage (Tanaka et al. 2018a).
The inactivation of bacteria by ultraviolet radiation primarily results from the ab-
sorption of UV light by bacterial deoxyribo-nucleic acid (DNA), which leads to the
dimerization of thymine bases. These thymine dimers disrupt the normal conforma-
tion of the DNA double helix and interfere with DNA replication, eventually damag-
ing vital cellular functions. In RNA viruses, UV-C primarily causes the formation of
uracil dimers instead of thymine dimers, as uracil (U) replaces thymine (T) in RNA.
These uracil dimers disrupt the replication of viral RNA, leading to inactivation. The
germicidal effect of UV radiation is most pronounced within the wavelength range
of 190–300 nm. Following DNA damage, bacterial cells may be capable of repair-
ing this damage if they are subsequently exposed to light, primarily in the visible
spectrum. This repair mechanism, known as photoreactivation, involves the reversal
of pyrimidine dimers and typically occurs at wavelengths between 300 and 500 nm.
The efficiency of this process depends on the specific microorganism involved. Unlike
bacteria, viruses generally lack the necessary repair enzymes required for photoreac-
tivation, making this phenomenon far more common in bacterial cells than in viral
particles (Harris et al. 1987).

Chapter 2
Aim and Objectives
The purpose of this study is to evaluate the effectiveness of UV-C irradiation in inac-
tivating HuNoV, using MNV as a surrogate model. Specifically, the study investigates
whether UV-C doses can achieve a 4-log reduction of viral particles located on stain-
less steel surfaces.
In addition to assessing viral inactivation, the study also investigates whether the
selected UV-C dose can reduce bacterial biofilm on stainless steel surfaces. This is
done to evaluate the standalone effect of UV-C treatment on established biofilms,
independent of any viral presence.
Furthermore, this study considers the role of microbial interactions.
Cross-streak
assays were conducted to examine whether any bacterial strain exerts antagonistic
effects that could influence the observed results. This was done to ensure that any
microbial reduction attributed to UV treatment is not confounded by interspecies
inhibition.
The central hypothesis of this study is that the selected dose of UV-C irradiation is
sufficient to achieve a ≥4-log reduction of MNV on stainless steel surfaces, and that
it also contributes to a measurable reduction in bacterial biofilm. By testing this
hypothesis, the study aims to contribute to the broader understanding of UV-C as a
disinfection strategy in environments where viruses or bacterial biofilms are present.

Chapter 3
Methods and Materials
This chapter describes the experimental procedures and analytical methods employed
to assess the effectiveness of UV-C irradiation in inactivating MNV-1 and reducing
bacterial biofilms on stainless steel surfaces. The stainless steel used was of food grade
(AISI 316, type 4 finish), with a thickness of 1 mm and a surface area of 0.25 cm2. The
methods include virus propagation and quantification, preparation and treatment of
viral and bacterial samples, and post-treatment recovery and enumeration of viable
organisms. Detailed protocols are provided for cell culture maintenance, plaque as-
says, UV-C calibration and exposure, biofilm formation, and cross-streak assays used
to evaluate microbial interactions. Where applicable, adjustments to experimental
parameters were made to ensure comparability with previous studies. All materials
and reagents were handled in accordance with relevant biosafety guidelines.
3.1
Host Cell Line and Maintenance
The cell line RAW 264.7 murine macrophage cells (ATCC TIB-71) used in the ex-
periments above is classified as Biosafety Level 2 (BSL-2) with a doubling time of
approximately 15 hours. Cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium
(DMEM) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), penicillin-streptomycin
(Pen-Strep), HEPES buffer, and non-essential amino acids
(NEAA), hereafter ref-
erenced as DMEM-10 (see Table B.1 for composition). DMEM-10 were also supple-
mented with 10% fetal calf serum (FCS) and maintained at 37°C and 5% carbon
dioxide (CO2) in a humidified incubator. Cells were routinely subcultured every 2–
3 days and used up to passage 20 to ensure optimal viability.

CHAPTER 3. METHODS AND MATERIALS
3.1.1
Cell Thawing
Cryopreserved RAW 264.7 cells stored at −130°C were rapidly thawed at 37°C in a
water bath for approximately 2 minutes. The vial exterior was sterilized using 70%
ethanol, and the thawed cell suspension was transferred to a 15 mL centrifuge tube
containing 4 mL of pre-warmed DMEM-10. The vial was rinsed with an additional
1 mL of DMEM-10, bringing the final volume to 6 mL. The suspension was centrifuged
at 125 × g for 5 minutes, and the supernatant was discarded. The resulting cell pellet
was resuspended in 6 mL of fresh DMEM-10 and transferred to a 25 cm2 flask. The
culture was incubated at 37°C and 5% CO2, and cell morphology and confluency were
monitored daily for 3 days.
3.1.2
Cell Subculturing
The DMEM-10 was aspirated, and the monolayer was washed once with Dulbecco’s
Phosphate Buffered Saline (DPBS) to remove residual medium and dead cells (2 mL
for a 25 cm2 flask, 5 mL for a 75 cm2 flask, and 5 mL for a 175 cm2 flask). Following
DPBS aspiration, fresh DMEM-10 was added to the flask (5 mL for a 25 cm2 flask,
10 mL for a 75 cm2 flask, and 20 mL for a 175 cm2 flask).
Cells were detached from the flask surface using a cell scraper and resuspended in the
medium using a 10 mL pipette by repeatedly aspirating and dispensing the suspension
against the bottom of the flask, ensuring an even cell distribution. The cell suspension
was split at a 1:10 ratio, equivalent to 1–2 mL of cells being transferred to 20 mL
of fresh DMEM-10 in a new 75 cm2 flask.
For larger flasks, 2–4 mL of the cell
suspension was added to 40 mL of fresh DMEM-10 in a 175 cm2 flask. The new flasks
were incubated at 37°C and 5% CO2 until cells were nearly confluent. The cells were
split every 2–3 days until a maximum of 20 passages.
3.1.3
Cell Freezing and Storage
For long-term preservation, cells were harvested and cryopreserved. Cells at 75–90%
confluence were harvested, resuspended in fresh medium, and transferred to a 15 mL
centrifuge tube for further processing.
Cells were centrifuged at 125 × g for 5 minutes, and the supernatant was discarded.

3.2
MNV-1 Propagation
3.2.1
Cell Culture and Maintenance for virus propagation
MNV-1 was propagated in RAW 264.7 macrophage cells, a murine monocyte/macrophage
cell line. Cells were cultured in DMEM-10. Cells were maintained in 175 cm2 tis-
sue culture flasks at 37°C and 5% CO2, and routinely passaged every 2–3 days when
reaching confluency. Cells were used up to passage 20 to ensure optimal viability.
The specific details for cell line maintenance are described in section 3.1.
3.2.2
Virus Infection and Adsorption
For virus propagation, 2×107 to 4×107 RAW 264.7 cells were seeded into two 175 cm2
flasks in 30 mL of DMEM-10 and incubated for 18–24 hours at 37°C and 5% CO2,
allowing the cells to reach approximately 90% confluency.
A frozen MNV-1 stock was thawed rapidly from −80°C in a water bath at 37°C, and a
4.1 × 105 to 8.3 × 105 plaque forming units (PFU)/mL viral suspension was prepared
in serum-free DMEM supplemented with HEPES, NEAA, and Pen-Strep.
The DMEM-10 was aspirated from the cell monolayer, and the cells were washed once
with 10 mL DPBS (without Ca2+ and Mg2+). Next, 7 mL of the virus suspension
(multiplicity of infection (MOI) = 0.05–0.1) was added to the flask and evenly dis-
tributed by gentle swirling. The cells were incubated for 1 hour at 37°C and 5% CO2
to allow viral adsorption, with periodic swirling every 10–15 minutes to prevent drying
of the cells. After the adsorption period, the medium containing unbound virus was
removed, and the cells were washed with 10 mL of DPBS. Then, 25 mL of DMEM-2
(see Table B.2 for composition) was added to the flask, and the cells were incubated
at 37°C and 5% CO2 for 48–72 hours.

3.2.3
Virus Harvest and Purification
The cytopathic effect (CPE) was monitored using a light microscope, and virus recov-
ery was initiated once >95% of the monolayer exhibited cell rounding, detachment,
or loss of contact inhibition. The detached cells were collected using a cell scraper,
and the suspension was transferred into 50 mL centrifuge tubes.
To enhance virus release, cells were subjected to two freeze-thaw cycles by freezing at

2,000 × g for 20 minutes to remove cell debris, and the supernatant passed through
a 0.22 µm filter. The final MNV-1 virus stock was aliquoted into cryovials and stored
at −80°C for long-term preservation (Hwang et al. 2014; ATCC 2024a).

Results
The purpose of this chapter is to present the results of a study investigating the
efficacy of UV-C disinfection on MNV-1 and bacterial biofilms applied to stainless
steel surfaces. MNV-1 was selected as a surrogate for human norovirus. Both viral
and bacterial samples were dried onto stainless steel coupons under controlled labo-
ratory conditions and subsequently exposed to UV-C light at doses of 12.5, 25, and
50 mJ/cm2 for virus samples, and a single dose of 50 mJ/cm2 for biofilm samples.
Viral inactivation was assessed by quantifying PFU/cm2, while bacterial survival was
determined using CFU/cm2. See Figure 4.1 for visual outcomes.
The chapter begins with data from the viral inactivation experiments, including log10
reductions of MNV-1 across increasing UV-C doses, followed by results comparing
recovery methods (vortexing vs. sonication) and drying conditions (laminar airflow vs.
silica desiccation). A subsequent figure includes a comparison with water-based data
from a previous student to provide contextual insight. The second part of the chapter
presents bacterial results, focusing on UV-C efficacy against biofilms matured for 5
and 24 hours, and concludes with cross-streak assays assessing potential interactions
between the constituent bacterial strains.

Chapter 5
Discussion
This chapter discusses the effectiveness of UV-C treatment against viral and bacterial
contaminants on stainless steel surfaces, with a particular focus on how variables such
as dose, biofilm maturity, and recovery conditions influence disinfection outcomes. By
interpreting key findings in the context of existing literature, the discussion highlights
critical factors that affect UV-C efficacy in real-world applications.
5.1
UV-C Inactivation of Murine Norovirus on Stain-
less Steel
A dose-dependent relationship was observed between UV-C fluence and MNV-1 in-
activation on stainless steel.
The minimal difference between 25 and 50 mJ /cm2
suggests a potential tailing effect in inactivation at higher doses.
These findings
confirm a trend toward dose-dependent inactivation of MNV-1 with UV-C exposure,
though the absence of a significant difference between 25 and 50 mJ /cm2 suggests a
tailing effect. This could indicate that beyond a certain threshold, additional UV-C
fluence yields diminishing returns, possibly due to surface-related shielding effects or
saturation of viral inactivation.
A comparison of MNV-1 inactivation in aqueous suspension from a previous DTU
study (Hansen and Kragh 2024), with the stainless steel surface results from this
thesis is shown in Figure 4.5. Most stainless steel data points fall close to or slightly
above the regression line derived from the liquid data. For example, at an adjusted
dose of approximately 18.5 mJ /cm2, reductions of about 2.7 log10 PFU/cm2 observed
33 of 63
==PAGE:48==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
on stainless steel, comparable to reductions in water under similar conditions. This
suggests that, under optimal surface conditions, UV-C treatment can achieve similar
levels of viral inactivation as in aqueous systems.
However, this contrasts with the surface data trend, where increasing the UV-C dose
beyond 25 mJ /cm2 did not yield a proportional increase in viral reduction. In liquid
environments, viral particles remain uniformly suspended and evenly exposed to UV-
C, supporting a more consistent log-linear inactivation trend (G. Park, Linden, and
Sobsey 2011).
On surfaces, by contrast, viruses may cluster, adhere unevenly, or become embedded
in microscopic surface irregularities. These factors can physically shield a partition of
viral particles from UV-C exposure, leading to reduced efficacy. This is supported by
Tanaka et al. (2018b), who found that while UV-C degraded viral RNA effectively, the
capsid remained structurally intact, suggesting that inactivation depends primarily on
genome damage, which may be limited if viruses are physically protected. Therefore,
even when UV-C fluence is increased, if viral particles are physically shielded, RNA
degradation may not occur effectively.
The limit of detection for the experiment was 3.98 log10 PFU/cm2, representing the
highest observable reduction under the given conditions. Although the starting titer
of MNV-1 was approximately 107 PFU/10 µL, reductions may have been limited by
viral inactivation occurring before UV exposure. Previous studies have shown that
MNV-1 can be sensitive to drying, potentially lowering recoverable titers (Lamhoujeb
et al. 2009; Fallahi and K. Mattison 2011; Cannon et al. 2006). To address this,
two drying methods were tested alongside the UV-C experiment: rapid drying in a
LAF cabinet and slower desiccation using silica gel. The results showed a significant
difference between the methods, with silica drying yielding higher viral recovery. This
indicates that slower drying better preserves viral infectivity, providing a more reliable
baseline for evaluating UV-C disinfection efficiency.
The impact of recovery methods, sonication and vortexing, was also evaluated to de-
termine whether the technique influenced viral titers following UV-C exposure. The
comparison revealed no significant difference between the two methods, suggesting
that both were equally effective in recovering virus particles from stainless steel sur-

faces. However, it was considered that the physical condition of the stainless steel, in-
cluding scratches, micro-crevices, or surface roughness, may influence recovery. These
surface imperfections can trap viral particles, potentially leading to reduced recovery
even in non-UV-exposed controls and underestimating total viral load. A study re-
ported that crevices in AISI 316 stainless steel can range from 0.05 to 0.1 mm in width
(Huang et al. 2022). If similar features were present on the stainless steel coupons
used in this study, murine norovirus particles measuring only 28–35 nm in diameter
could feasibly become embedded in such crevices, potentially shielding them from
recovery or UV-C exposure (Wobus, Thackray, and Virgin 2006).
This is notable given the initial viral titer of approximately 107 PFU/10 µL, as sub-
stantial losses were observed regardless of UV treatment. Although slower drying
using silica gel improved recovery compared to rapid airflow drying, a significant
portion of the virus still remained unrecoverable. These findings suggest that some
reductions may be attributed not to UV-C inactivation, but to limitations in viral
recovery from complex surface structures.
5.2
UV-C Inactivation of Bacterial Biofilms on Stain-
less Steel
The results from the UV-C exposure experiment on biofilms matured for 5 and
24 hours at a dose of 50 mJ /cm2 revealed moderate reductions in bacterial viability,
with mean values of 3.00±0.69 log10 CFU/cm2 and 3.40±1.01 log10 CFU/cm2, respec-
tively. Although a slight increase in reduction was observed for the 24-hour biofilms,
the difference of 0.4 log units was not statistically significant (p = 0.3761). It is as-
sumed that biofilms grown for longer periods become denser and structurally more
complex. However, the higher CFU counts observed in the untreated controls at the
24-hour condition suggest that as biofilms mature and microbial load increases, UV-C
disinfection becomes less efficient. This supports the notion that UV-C light primarily
affects the outer layers of the biofilm, while deeper, more compact regions may remain
protected. The limited penetration of UV-C through dense biofilm structures likely
reduces its ability to inactivate cells embedded within the inner matrix.
When comparing these findings with a previous study by M. Kim, S. Y. Park, and
35 of 63
==PAGE:50==

Ha (2016), it becomes evident that UV-C efficacy is highly dose-dependent. In their
experiments, L. monocytogenes biofilms on stainless steel were treated with increas-
ing doses of 300 mJ/cm2 to 1800 mJ/cm2, resulting in log10 reductions from 0.26
to 1.47. Notably, even at the highest dose of 1800 mJ/cm2, the reduction remained
under 1.5 log10 units. However, it was not possible to determine exactly how the au-
thors calculated the UV-C dose, so it remains unclear whether their dosing is directly
comparable to that used in this study. In contrast, treatment with a UV-C lamp in
aqueous suspension resulted in significantly greater reductions at much lower doses.
At just 60 mJ/cm2, E. coli O157:H7, S. Typhimurium, and L. monocytogenes showed
reductions of 4.68 log10, 4.97 log10, and 5.58 log10, respectively (Y.-J. Kim, Lee, and
Kang 2023).
These levels of inactivation were achieved with substantially lower energy input com-
pared to the dose applied in the stainless-steel biofilm study. The enhanced efficacy
in liquid suggests that UV-C disinfection is more effective in homogeneous aqueous
environments, where microorganisms remain planktonic and unprotected by a biofilm
matrix, likely due to improved light penetration and uniform exposure. In this study,
reductions exceeding 3-log10 were achieved at just 50 mJ/cm2, suggesting that the
UV-C treatment applied may be more effective, or that differences in experimental
design and dose calculation methods play a substantial role. Variations in how UV
dose is measured or reported, such as accounting for reflection, absorption, or actual
irradiance at the sample surface, can greatly affect perceived efficacy and complicate
direct comparisons across studies. Furthermore, literature comparing UV-C inacti-
vation across biofilms of different maturation stages and under standardized surface
conditions particularly on stainless steel is limited. This makes it difficult to contex-
tualize the findings within a consistent scientific framework.
5.3
Implications of Cross-Streak Assays for Bacte-
rial Competition
To evaluate whether bacterial interactions could influence UV-C treatment outcomes,
cross-streak assays were performed. These tests assessed whether certain strains in-
hibited or outcompeted others prior to UV exposure, which could otherwise lead to
36 of 63
==PAGE:51==
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
overestimation of UV efficacy. No inhibition zones or signs of autolysis were observed,
suggesting that the reductions seen in UV-treated samples were likely not confounded
by interspecies antagonism.
Cross-streak assays were performed and summarized using a mean interaction grad-
ing system, ranging from −10 to +10. These results were compared to a previous
study (Kragh et al. 2025) analyzing species persistence in biofilms regrown under var-
ious disinfectant conditions (benzalkonium chloride, BC; sodium hypochlorite, SH;
peracetic acid, PAA). Myroides odoratus strain T56B, which achieved the highest in-
teraction grade (+10), was the most dominant strain in the cross-streak assays. This
is mirrored in the biofilm study, where Myroides odoratus maintained consistently
high relative abundance across all treatment conditions, including 10.3% under PAA
and the highest percentage (18.1%) in water controls, suggesting strong resistance to
both microbial competition and chemical disinfection.
In contrast, Listeria monocytogenes (C16), with the lowest interaction score (−9),
frequently grew less compared to the other strains used in the cross-streak assays and
was barely present in biofilms before and after treatment (0.1–0.7%) in the previous
study, confirming its sensitivity to both microbial and environmental pressures. Al-
though it was not possible to determine the individual contribution of each bacterial
strain after UV exposure, it may be possible to draw a parallel from the previous
experiment. This experiment was also conducted to assess whether any of the bac-
terial strains produce inhibitory by-products that could affect the survival of others.
Such internal interactions could influence the observed reduction in the UV-C exper-
iment, potentially leading to a false-positive result if bacteria were killing each other
independently of the UV exposure.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This study evaluated the effectiveness of UV-C light in inactivating MNV-1 and bac-
terial biofilms on stainless steel surfaces, highlighting the complex interplay between
dose, surface conditions, and microbial structure.
A dose-dependent reduction in MNV-1 was observed, though the limited increase at
higher doses suggests a potential tailing effect. This phenomenon may arise from
physical shielding of viral particles in micro-crevices or uneven surface topography,
but could also be related to approaching the detection limit of the assay, where the
presence or absence of just a few plaques significantly impacts results. Comparisons
with inactivation in aqueous suspension demonstrated that while surface treatments
can achieve similar reductions under optimal conditions, uniform exposure in liquids
results in more consistent outcomes. For bacterial biofilms, UV-C treatment led to
moderate reductions, with limited improvement in more mature biofilms, reinforcing
the challenge of penetrating dense biofilm structures.
These findings underscore the importance of considering both microbial context and
surface properties when designing UV-C disinfection protocols. The variability in-
troduced by drying and microbial interactions should not be overlooked. Together,
these insights contribute to a deeper understanding of UV-C disinfection dynamics
and provide a valuable foundation for improving hygiene strategies in healthcare, food
processing, and other high-risk environments.
These findings highlight the need for optimized, evidence-based disinfection strategies
to mitigate pathogen transmission via contaminated surfaces.

Chapter 7
Future Work
Building on the findings of this study, future research should investigate the combined
effects of biofilms and viral contamination on UV-C disinfection efficacy. In real-world
environments, surfaces are rarely contaminated with viruses alone, rather, they are
often co-contaminated with microbial biofilms, which can act as protective barriers.
One proposed direction is to deliberately compound both viruses (e.g., MNV-1) and
bacterial biofilms on stainless steel coupons and evaluate UV-C inactivation dynamics
under these more complex conditions. This approach would help determine whether
the biofilm matrix provides physical or biochemical protection to the virus, potentially
reducing UV penetration or scattering light energy. Additionally, comparing sequen-
tial versus simultaneous contamination, where the virus is applied either before, after,
or at the same time as biofilm formation, could reveal how timing affects viral integra-
tion into the matrix and UV resistance. If biofilms do shield viruses from UV-C, this
could have significant implications for the disinfection of high-touch surfaces in clin-
ical, food processing, or public environments. Moreover, future work should explore
whether a true tailing effect occurs at higher UV-C doses. While the current findings
suggest diminishing returns beyond a certain fluence, further experimentation with a
wider range of doses and higher replication may clarify whether this trend reflects an
actual saturation point or experimental limitations.
