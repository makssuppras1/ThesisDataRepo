Abstract 
This thesis, conducted in collaboration with Team Danmark, aims to achieve a comprehensive 
understanding of the aerodynamic characteristics of a speedway motorcycle, with a specific 
focus on reducing drag to enhance performance. The study seeks to identify and analyze the 
primary causes of drag and eliminate these by redesigning the affected components. 
The analysis was conducted using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) with the software 
STAR-CCM+. Multiple flow studies were performed to investigate high and low-pressure 
zones, stagnation points, vortex shedding, and overall flow behavior. The results indicated that 
the primary drag-producing component was the motorcycle's front shield. With this knowledge, 
several concept designs were developed and simulated under the same parameters to determine 
the optimal design for low drag. Revision 15 of the concept design demonstrated a nearly 5.56% 
reduction in drag. 
Few CFD studies have been conducted on speedway motorcycles, positioning this thesis as a 
significant contribution to the field. The findings not only benefit Team Danmark but also have 
broader implications for motorsport engineering, potentially aiding other speedway teams. This 
thesis provides a detailed methodology for using CFD to optimize the aerodynamics of 
speedway motorcycles, highlighting the practical application of aerodynamic principles in 
enhancing performance in competitive motorsports. 
The study involved an extensive examination of various turbulence models, boundary 
conditions, and mesh configurations to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the simulation 
results. The research culminated in the development of an optimized front fairing design, which 
significantly reduces drag, thereby improving the overall aerodynamic efficiency of the 
motorcycle. 
The findings of this research contribute to the understanding of aerodynamic optimization in 
speedway motorcycles and offer a framework that can be utilized by other teams and 
researchers in the field. The study's implications extend beyond speedway racing, offering 
potential benefits to other forms of motorsports where aerodynamic efficiency is crucial.

1. Introduction 
The engineering of a Speedway motorcycle today is mainly focused on maximum power 
delivery throughout a race, while ensuring a proper handling and steering on the track. Because 
of this a critical aspect of performance has largely been overlooked: aerodynamics. Much 
literature mentions the importance of aerodynamic optimization for various vehicles such as 
cars, motorcycles, airplanes and many of them remarks the potential for performance gains 
through aerodynamic optimization. 
Literature studies such as [1] about the impact of turbulence models for sport motorcycles, [2] 
delving into the wake effects on Formula 1 cars, and [3] on drag effects imposed on vehicle 
wheels to reduce fuel consumption, demonstrates the importance of aerodynamic analysis.  
These studies indicates that optimization of the aerodynamics of vehicles, can result in 
significant improvements which is typically essential for competitive scenarios. Furthermore, 
the work done by [4] looking at the effects of windshield configuration and pilot position and 
[5] investigating handlebars and front-fairing configurations, underscores the impact of specific 
design elements on overall aerodynamic efficiency. 
Given these insights, the goal of this thesis is to explore the potential for aerodynamic gains on 
a speedway bike through Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). Given the limited research on 
this specific subject, this study will investigate various aspects identified as crucial in the 
previously mentioned literature. 
The first part of this study will be focusing on the fundamental mathematics underpinning CFD, 
such as the Navier-Stokes equations consisting of the continuity equation and the momentum 
equation. These equations form the foundation of CFD and are essential for understanding and 
interpreting simulation results, as highlighted by [4] and [5]. 
The Methodology section will provide a detailed walkthrough of the various simulations 
conducted, the simulation parameters, including the creation of the speedway model, its 
dimensions, Wind Tunnel (WT) testing with a scaled 3D-printed model, computational domain 
dimensions, boundary conditions, mesh parameters, and solver settings. Furthermore it will 
address the methods used for determining these parameters, hereby mesh, physical runtime and 
timestep convergence studies. This approach is inspired by the methods used in the studies [3] 
and [6], where the importance of detailed simulation setups is addressed.

the Speedway is in operation. We will compare the simulation data with experimental data

The Discussion and Conclusion section will summarize, discuss, and analyse the results to

By addressing these aspects, this thesis aims to contribute to the understanding of aerodynamic 
optimization in speedway motorcycles, leveraging insights from related studies to explore new 
possibilities for performance enhancement.

3.  Methodology 
3.1. Geometry 
Research for this project, was carried out using real size model of a speedway motorcycle, 
provided by Team Danmark, where a 3D scan was performed using the high-end 3D scanner 
MetraSCAN BLACK+ | Elite made by Creaform [7]. This scanner has a volumetric accuracy 
of up to 0.064 mm ensuring that geometry with significant effect on the aerodynamics could be 
accurately captured, however due to the compact build of the Speedway, some overlapping 
features, open holes, and staggering vertices appeared on the scanned file. This was afterwards 
fixed in the attached software, where the holes were closed, and flying vertices and overlapping 
features were corrected. Furthermore because of human error, during the scanning setup of the 
speedway, the front wheel is a bit skewed from the rest of the body. Effects of this will be 
discussed later. The geometry was constructed without spokes to prevent meshing problems 
and limit the cell count, however the impacts of drag due to wheel rotation remain relevant, as 
concluded in [8]. The speedway body was imported as a single mesh file, while the two wheels 
and the rear wheel plate were imported separately. No patch splitting was conducted on the 
speedway, which means that it acts as one big surface. 
The total length of the Speedway (𝐿) is 2.07 m, while the wheelbase (𝐿𝑤) is 1.37 m, the front 
wheel diameter (𝐷𝑓𝑤) is 0.71 m and the rear wheel diameter (𝐷𝑟𝑤) is 0.70 m. The handlebar 
height (𝐻) is 1 m relative to the base of the front wheel, while the width (𝑊) measured at the 
widest point on the body is 0.75 m and the width of the handlebar (𝑊ℎ𝑏) is 0.83 m. The 
geometry is seen on Figure 1.

3.2. Simulation scenarios 
For this study, several individual simulations were conducted to achieve the required analysis 
results which were mentioned in the introduction. Each simulation scenario was designed to 
address specific aspects of the aerodynamic performance of the speedway motorcycle and its 
components. Below, these different simulations are detailed, explaining how they differ from 
each other and the unique objectives they aim to fulfil. 
- 
Rotating vs. Stationary Wheels 
This study was conducted to evaluate the impact of rotating wheels on the aerodynamics of the 
Speedway Motorcycle. Previous research, such as studies [9] and [8], has compared the drag 
effects between stationary and rotating wheels in different contexts, including a car and a time 
trial bicycle. The conclusions from these studies showed varying results, making it essential to 
investigate this aspect for the Speedway Motorcycle. 
Two simulations were performed using identical mesh parameters and domain boundary 
conditions. For the simulation with rotating wheels, a sliding interface boundary was employed 
to accurately simulate the rotational effect of the wheels. Further details on this methodology 
can be found in section 3.3. 
 
- 
Wind tunnel test comparison 
This study was conducted to compare experimental data, carried out by [10], with simulation 
data to validate the accuracy of the simulation results. A scaled-down model of the Speedway 
was 3D-printed and tested in a Wind Tunnel (WT). Size comparisons are detailed in section 
3.3.1. The simulation maintained a consistent mesh, although some boundary conditions were 
adjusted to match the WT model. 
- 
Impact of turbulence modelling 
As concluded in [1], the use of different turbulence models can significantly impact flow 
interactions and vortex generation, but not necessarily drag and pressure. This claim was 
investigated in this study to further validate the reliability of the results. The simulation 
utilized the same mesh parameters and boundary conditions and was performed with rotating 
wheels. 
- 
New Nose Design 
The results of the rotational and stationary wheel simulations, showed that the front fairing 
significantly contributes to drag, aligning with the findings of several studies including [1], 
[5] and [11]. Consequently, redesigning of this component was included in this study. The 
simulation maintained consistent mesh and boundary conditions and was performed with 
rotating wheels.

Accelerated Velocity Inlet 
The most important part of a speedway race is in the start of the race. This study was

The experimental data provided a benchmark for assessing the accuracy and reliability of the

A second computational domain was designed based on established good modelling practices

3.4. Mesh 
In this study, a polyhedral mesh was utilized with refined regions around critical areas such as 
the speedway geometry and the wake, to accurately capture complex flow features. The final 
mesh comprised of approximately 7 million cells. This mesh configuration was selected based 
on the results of a mesh convergence study, which determined the optimal mesh density and 
number of prism layers, ensuring results independent of grid size while optimizing 
computational costs. 
The following mesh parameters were selected for the meshing process:  
• Surface Remesher: The Surface Remesher enhances the quality of surface meshes by 
refining, coarsening, and adjusting mesh elements on the surface of the geometry. This 
process achieves a more uniform mesh distribution. 
• Polyhedral Mesher: This function converts the mesh from tetrahedral cells to polyhedral 
cells. The Polyhedral Mesher offers improved numerical accuracy, better convergence 
rates, and higher solution quality. By providing more neighbouring cells, it enhances 
the approximation of gradients, leading to more accurate results [14].  
• Automatic Surface Repair: This feature automatically detects and fixes common issues 
in surface geometries that could lead to mesh generation errors. It ensures that the 
simulation can run even if the original model mesh contains gaps and overlaps. 
However, it may not always be able to resolve all such problems. 
• Prism Layer Mesher: The Prism Layer Mesher provides the option of adding prismatic 
cell layers along the surface geometry of the model. These cells are essential for 
accurately capturing the near-wall flow. These layers are typically very thin, allowing 
them to capture the steep pressure and velocity gradients that form close to the surface 
of any geometry.

3.6. Solver 
3.6.1. 
Solver type and settings 
The simulations in this study were executed using Simcenter STAR-CCM+ [16] by Siemens, a 
powerful multiphysics CFD software package designed for complex simulations involving 
fluid dynamics, heat transfer, and other physical phenomena. It is widely utilized in engineering 
fields for its ability to handle diverse and intricate simulation challenges. 
STAR-CCM+ operates by solving the Navier-Stokes equations, which are fundamental to fluid 
dynamics. These equations describe the motion of fluid substances and are based on principles 
of mass, momentum, and energy conservation. The software employs advanced numerical 
methods, such as the finite volume method (FVM), to discretize these equations over a 
computational grid. See chapter 2 for more information. 
The FVM discretizes the fluid domain in time and space, allowing the Navier-Stokes equations, 
including gradient, divergence, and Laplacian terms, to be linearly interpolated using Gaussian 
schemes. For coupling pressure-velocity gradients, the SIMPLE (Semi-Implicit Method for 
Pressure Linked Equations) algorithm was employed. The SIMPLE method uses implicit 
schemes, such as the implicit Euler method or the quadratic backward scheme (also known as 
the three-time-level scheme), which are stable for larger time steps. This is crucial for transient 
flows where time accuracy is needed without compromising stability. The implicit solver is 
typically favoured over explicit schemes when the simulation involves slow-speed flows and 
gradually evolving turbulent behaviour [15]. 
A segregated flow approach was chosen for the solving scheme. While the coupled approach 
offers faster convergence for strongly coupled variables and complex transient phenomena, the 
segregated approach provides a balance between computational efficiency and robustness. This 
makes it particularly suitable for transient simulations with moderate complexity and resource

3.6.2. 
Turbulence modelling  
In this study, the importance of turbulence modelling was analysed by comparing several 
different turbulence models. These models include some of the most commonly used Reynolds-
Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) models: 𝑘−𝜖, 𝑘−𝜔, and Spalart-Allmaras. 
For the 𝑘−𝜔 model, the Shear Stress Transport (SST) variant was used, while the Realizable  
𝑘−𝜖 Two-Layer model was employed for the 𝑘−𝜖 simulations. Each of these models offers 
specific benefits and has been selected based on their strengths in accurately capturing 
turbulence in various flow conditions. The models were studied under same mesh and boundary 
conditions. 
RANS was chosen for this subject, as it is solid stable method that is able to accurately predict 
the flow behaviour of many complex geometries, and is also capable of capturing transient 
phenomena, however as the RANS models averages multiple ensembles of flow at every 
calculation, random fluctuations are averaged out, eliminating parts of the turbulence. One of 
the main reasons for using the RANS method Is also the computational costs. Methods like 
DDES and LES require a more refined mesh, which with respect to the CFL condition, means 
a shorter time-step, all attributing to simulation size and increasing computational cost.  
•  𝒌−𝝐 Realizable Two-Layer Model 
The 𝑘−𝜖 Realizable Two-Layer Model, is typically effective for high Reynolds number flows, 
which makes it perform well for external aerodynamics. The model is known for its 
computational stability, as it is less sensitive to initial conditions and turbulence levels in the 
freestream [18] [15].  
•  𝒌−𝝎 SST Model 
The 𝑘−𝜔 SST Model can provide high accurate results in the near-wall region, making it a 
good choice for studies that wants to capture detailed boundary layer behavior. The model is 
better at predicting flows with large pressure gradients and separation compared to the 𝑘−𝜖 
model. Because of the accurate predictions in the near-wall area, the 𝑘−𝜔 SST Model is in 
theory better at making predicting drag [18] [15].  
 
 
==PAGE:27==
27

•  Spalart-Allmaras Model 
The Spalart-Allmaras Model is a computational efficient and simple model, generally good at 
capturing boundary layer behavior and is reasonable at drag predictions and flow separation 
prediction, however it lacks behind 𝑘−𝜖 and 𝑘−𝜔 in accuracy because of its simplicity [19].

4.1. Wind Tunnel test comparison 
The 3D printed model was tested in a WT described in section 3.3.1. The test was conducted 
with an inlet speed of 30.06 m/s. The resulting measured drag force had a value of 3.68 N, 
which resulted in a drag area (CdA) of 0.241 [10]. As discussed earlier, this result is not 
completely reliable as the conducted test, included a hold pin to keep speedway model in 
place and off the bottom boundary, which would result in a higher CdA. Furthermore, the 
rough surface of a 3D print, contributes to increased viscous friction [22], and thereby 
increased drag.  
In the study [10], eight different velocities were analyzed and the CdA was plotted as a 
function of wind speed. To make sure the Reynolds number did not have a significant effect 
on the results, the max variance of values of the CdA was found. The max variance was 18% 
between the inlet velocity of 10.05 m/s and 15 m/s, which indicates that the Reynolds-number 
has a big effect on the low velocity flow, however between the inlet velocity of 35.6 m/s and 
40.3 m/s, the max variance was 3.24%.  
The Reynolds number for the full-size speedway was found in section 3.6.1 to be 3.95 ⋅106. 
The required inlet velocity of the WT to achieve the same Reynolds number was calculated to 
be 180 m/s by solving the velocity in equation ( 3 ). Assuming that the variance of CdA 
would not increase/decrease more than the 3.24% by increasing the inlet velocity beyond 40.3 
m/s, it is concluded that Reynolds number does not have a significant effect on the CdA in the 
areas of interest. The WT results for the inlet velocity of 30.06 m/s were therefore used for 
comparison.

4.2. Drag analysis  
Drag occurs for any moving obstacle wherever any fluid exists and can be categorized into 
different kinds of drag; pressure drag, skin friction drags form drag, interference drag, lift drag, 
profile drag and wave drag. In this analysis, the main focus will be on pressure-drag as this 
would due to the controlled setting of the simulation, be the biggest contributor. Typically, skin 
friction drag would be of significance, but as the roughness of the Speedway was not defined, 
it is hard to model this with preciseness, however it will still be considered. 
When inspecting main contributors to drag caused by pressure, it is typically useful to look at 
high and low pressure zones on the model. These pressure zones often tell where the most drag 
occurs, however as pressure is both a function of force and area, a small area with high pressure 
would not make a significant contribution to drag. 
One other very important aspect is locating the main vortices that form on and around the 
Speedway. Vortices will show where flow separation happends, and thereby help locate areas 
that needs optimization. Elimination of these low-pressure regions will help reduce the pressure 
difference between the front and the rear of the Speedway and thereby reduce pressure-drag. 
Furthermore, vortices close to the surface of the Speedway can increase the turbulence induced 
skin-friction in the boundary layer and hereby cause more drag. 
It is very important do identify these causes when modelling CFD, as they can assist in the 
optimization of the geometry, and in the end, help reduce drag and increase performance. 
The following study will be based on the simulation incorporating rotation for the wheels, and 
with the turbulence model 𝑘−𝜔.

5. Discussion and conclusion 
The primary objective of this thesis was to explore the aerodynamic properties of a speedway 
motorcycle and propose design modifications to reduce drag. The findings from the reviewed 
articles provide valuable insights and comparative data that reinforce and expand upon the 
results obtained in this research. 
The results of the drag cause analysis aligned well with the findings of many studies, identifying 
the main drag contributors as the front fairing, front wheel, and front shocks, consistent with 
the findings of [1]. The effects of stationary versus rotating wheels showed a conclusive 
aerodynamic gain, resulting in a 2.38% drag decrease, which aligns with the results of various 
studies cited in this research. 
The new fairing design also yielded conclusive results, reducing drag by 5.5% and thereby 
optimizing the aerodynamics of the speedway motorcycle. While the final design was 
conceptual, it is expected that further revisions would yield even greater improvements. 
The turbulence model study confirmed that the k-ω model used throughout this study is the 
most accurate for the simulations conducted, aligning well with the initial hypothesis. 
Additionally, the results of the accelerated velocity inlet study indicated a 2.93% difference in 
the drag force's end value, which may be attributed to sensitivity to changes in the inlet 
condition. 
Further studies could be conducted to delve deeper into the results and analyse the reasons 
behind the different outcomes. This will be the focus of future work.
