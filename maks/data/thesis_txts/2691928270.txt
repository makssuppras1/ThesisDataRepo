Abstract
Measurements are important in the ﬁeld of quantum information and several types of detec-
tors have been demonstrated experimentally. This thesis concerns experimental tomography of
a quantum detector, the Dolinar receiver, is a receiver that consist of a displacement, photon
measurement and feedback. The Dolinar receiver is a feedback receiver meaning that the receiver
in real time adapts displacement of the incoming state based on measurements of part of the state
to obtain a better result.
This Dolinar receiver is used in particular, for state discrimination that is the ability of a receiver
to discriminate between a known pair of states. The receiver is used to discriminate two orthogonal
super positions of vacuum and single photon states, and in addition the optimal measurement
also optimally discriminates non-orthogonal states, it can be shown that the Dolinar receiver can
discriminate states with an error that approaches the Helstrom bound, which is the minimum
error for discriminating a pair of states, for higher number of feedback steps.
To judge the eﬀectiveness of the receiver, tomography is performed for the Dolinar receiver for a
few diﬀerent states on the Bloch sphere. Monte Carlo simulations are included to judge eﬀective-
ness of the states used for tomography before the experiments. A theoretical model have been
used to ﬁnd the settings for implementing the optimal measurement.
The receiver has been operated with up to 4 feedback steps and will be shown to have a eﬀective
discrimination rate of 96.4% compared to a non-feedback receiver of only around 93.7%.

Introduction
Discrimination of quantum states is a tool that is useful in several ﬁelds of quantum information
processing; sensing, communication and computation. The backbone of many processes in each
of these ﬁelds is storing information in discrete or variable system and to later retrieve this
information or communicate the information, in any case measurements of states are necessary.
However, for the system required to reliably extract the information from a stored state or to
read a message sent for communication, there may not exist a direct measurement. A simple and
relevant example of this is coherent state, which are very commonly used in optical communication,
due to the ﬁnite overlap of two coherent states a simple heterodyne measurement cannot perfectly
distinguish the theses states. In general, non-orthogonal states cannot be perfectly discriminated
and even for orthogonal states which in theory can be perfectly discriminated a receiver that can
discriminate these states are not trivial to design.
Although non-orthogonal states are not the topic of the experimental part of this thesis the fact
that non-orthogonal states are indiscriminate is a fundamental property of quantum states that are
used in cryptography. The simple case of this is the BB84 protocol in which secure communication
can be established by utilising the no-cloning theorem as well as two sets of pre-agreed states in
diﬀerent non-orthogonal basis, will allow two parties to communicate while detecting the presence
of any eavesdropper. However, quantum state discrimination (QSD) is also important in a more
general sense for communication as a low error rate in discriminating. For instance two qubits,
yields a higher eﬀective communication rate as the need for redundancy in the qubits storing the
information sent is restored. For protocols such as these eﬀective state discrimination is vitally
important.
Several diﬀerent types of receivers to discriminate diﬀerent types of states such as the Kennedy for
coherent state (in 1973 [Kennedy, 1973]), Dolinar suggested an improved receiver that can reach
the Helstrom bound using real time feedback (in 1973 [Dolinar, 1973]), another feedback case
speciﬁcally for quadrature phase-shift keyed (QPSK) coherent state channel (in 1993 [Bondurant,
1993]) and Sasaki-Hirota later devised a scheme that does not require feedback to reach the
Helstrom bound, although implementation is not trivial (in 1996 [Sasaki and Hirota, 1996]).
It has been shown experimentally that the modiﬁed scheme of feedback can be used to discrimi-
nate the QPSK-states beyond the standard quantum limit (SQL) meaning that the receiver can
discriminate better than the shot noise limit of classical communications [Izumi et al., 2020b].
1.1
Qubit
For quantum computation and communication a qubit is a system that consist of two orthogonal
states (referred to as |0⟩and |1⟩). Physically these qubit hold one bit of information similar to
classical counterpart, but the qubit diﬀers in that a qubit can be any superposition of these two
physical bits: a |0⟩+b |1⟩where a, b ∈C. Physically this superposition qubit is often represented by
a single rail qubit where the logical 0 state is represented by the vacuum state (|0⟩) and the logical 1
state is a single photon (|1⟩). Natural atoms and artiﬁcial atoms (such as superconducting circuits
or semiconductor quantum dots) are used for quantum processing as they have long coherence
time and can be stored in large arrays and in the case of artiﬁcial atoms can be engineer to have
certain desirable features [Buluta et al., 2011].
The Bloch sphere is a way to represent the single qubit in a visual way. Any linear combination
of the two bits |0⟩, |1⟩with complex coeﬃcients can be visualised in 3D space, and due to the
requirement of the state being normalized the states all lie in the surface of the sphere [Barnett,
2009, Chapter 2.4]. A representation of the Bloch sphere can be seen in ﬁgure 1.

the weighted average of these are is maximized PAV G = P

Minimum error measurement has the additional constraint from maximum conﬁdence that the
operators must form a complete set which makes ﬁnding the optimal measurement a lot harder

Theoretical model
The following section will describe calculations for the ﬁnding the minimum error probability. The
calculations are used to ﬁnd the optimal parameters of the Dolinar receiver for the experimental
setup. First of the model of the receiver will be presented then the calculations for ﬁnding the
error taking into account with dark counts and imperfect visibility and quantum eﬃciency.
3.1
Model of Dolinar receiver
Figure 5: Spatial representation of the Dolinar receiver.
The Dolinar receiver with the temporal feedback ﬁgure 4 can be modelled as a spatial system of
consecutive systems each containing a beamsplitter (BS), displacement and then a measurement,
repeated M times.
To ﬁnd a theoretical calculation we look at each BS with measurement separately: each stage in
the process is some state incoming to a BS with vacuum in the second mode, then a displacement
and a measurement which will trace out the modes reﬂected from the BS, leaving a new state
in a single mode going to the next BS. This new state is then undergoing the same process.
We thus only need to calculate one such system for an arbitrary state and can then quite easily
set up a stage for generalising to and arbitrary number of feedback steps. For each stage we
have potentially 5 parameters reﬂection in BS: amplitude of displacement for both detection and
non-detection in the previous stage, phase of detection/non-detection and reﬂection of the BS.
However, for the ﬁrst step the amplitude and phase is independent of previous measurement (as
there is none) and for the last stage the reﬂection is set to 1 to reﬂect that no state is going to be
measured after this detection.
The reﬂection of the BS is given by the temporal width of the state such that (T is the total
time): r1 =

Monte Carlo simulations
Before performing experiments, Monte Carlo simulations of the simple Kennedy have been used
as a tool to estimate the usefulness of sets of states used for the tomography, but also to give an
idea of how many measurements is required to gain good results. The feedback case have also
been simulated for M = 2, 3, 4.
The procedure of the simulations is to generate a set of states, then using Monte Carlo method
to simulate the measurements of the set and lastly using MLE (see section 2.6) to reconstruct the
POVM and calculate the error for the state.
Several diﬀerent choices for states can be made, in the experimental case, due to the easy gen-
eration, a set of states with radial symmetry such the ones seen to the left in ﬁgure 13 is used.
However, to investigate eﬀectiveness of other sets of states a ”grid array” (right in ﬁgure 13) have
been investigated with simulations.

Experimental setup
The setup is almost identical to the one used in [Izumi et al., 2020a]. With only a few changes
the intensity and phase modulator are slightly better versions of the ones used (the intensity
modulator having been installed during this project). In addition the way the power is stabilised
has been changed as will be explained in section 5.2. One FPGA is also of a diﬀerent type with a
higher bandwidth of the internal digital to analog converter. All characterization of components
have been remade, while the code for the FPGA have been prepared by Izumi, S.
The Dolinar receiver is a temporal feedback model ﬁgure 4, this relatively simple receiver is a bit
more involved to realise experimentally. The schematic the setup ﬁgure 20 shows the components
used to both prepare the state and the actual measurements itself, which consists of IM, PM, a
99/1 BS and FPGA to control, the rest of the components are for state preparation and power
stabilization. The setup and characteristics of components are described in this section.
Figure 20: Schematic setup, the setup and objective of each component will be explained below.
A larger scale ﬁgure along with pictures of the setup are available in appendix B.
The ﬁrst part of the setup is preparing both strong laser light (for power control and phase
stabilization respectively). The second and primary part of the setup is a MZ detector which is
used for state generation and the displacement of the state, then a SSPD (superconducting single
photon detector) is used for the actual measurement. The system is controlled by three diﬀerent
FPGA’s which are primarily used due to their fast operating times for the feedback part of the
setup these are described in section 5.1.1.
From the left of sketch above the laser is incoming into the system (through a inline polarizer to
clean up the polarization from the laser) here a 99/1 BS is splitting the laser light into a weak
path and a strong path (99% going to strong and 1% to weak).
In both paths of the interferrometer the power can be controlled via a variable attenuator (VA)
through the FPGA2. In the weak laser path there is an additional manual attenuator which is
used to control the power in this path. As only 1% of the light goes through this path (and
then attenuated by two attenuator) the strong light is not aﬀected much on the second path.
The strong light path also has a VA, which is used to control the strong power, and two sets of
switches. These are used to make sure that when the weak path is used as little light as possible
goes through the switches, each switch has two internal switches so the light is lead through a
switch 4 times in total, meaning that the strong light that goes through the switch when ”blocked”
after 4 times very limited should be left. The switches is controlled by FPGA1.
Both strong a weak light is connected by a 50/50 BS. And the polarization is cleaned up before
entering the PM ﬁbers by a polarization controller(PC) and an inline polarizer. After the polar-

The FPGA is a Field Programmable Gate Array and are typically used due to the large ﬂexibility
and high bandwidth of the FPGA which is useful for high speed operations such as the feedback

5.1.2
PZT
The PZT is primarily used to control the phase for the states used for tomography. From the strong
power in both arms and the visibility the expected power associated with any phase between the
two arms can be calculated from eq. (20). By applying a voltage to the PZT the piezo stretches
the ﬁber thus changing the phase. The voltage applied to the PZT is controlled from the FPGA
using PID control (Proportional–Integral–Derivative controller).
To measure the drift of the phase a set of measurements is made in which the phase lock is kept
on and then released. The power is measured which can be used to convert to the phase by
measuring and ﬁtting a cosine function to the interference fringes when applying a sine function
to the PZT (thus sweeping the phase).
By measuring the phase before and after releasing the phase lock we can estimate the drift of the
experiment over the timescale of our experiment we have 60 µs experiments + 20 µs in between
(not all of the experiments are run at this timescale, but this gives a upper bound of the phase
drift), taking 300 data points before and discarding due to allowing tie for the switch to block
the strong light and then measuring 300 data points we are in total measuring for 48 ms between
phase locking. Using the measurements below we can estimate the expected drift in the phase in
this timescale. Before letting go of the phase lock the phase is 1.571 ± 0.003 and after letting go
it is 1.600 ± 0.043. The phase drift is around 2%.
For larger times of measurement (100 µs) where used for the larger number of feedback steps,
which gives 72 ms of total measurement time, for this timescale the phase drifts from 1.571 ±
0.003 to 1.619 ± 0.059 this gives a drift of 3%.
It appears that the phase has a tendency to drift in one direction (increasing phase, associated
with a decreasing power). This could be due to power drifting or temperature gradient. The
measurement takes a couple of minutes so power drift and temperature changes is not uncommon.

To present the results ﬁrst of a way to visually represent the actual measurements for the tomog-
raphy states will be presented.
A way to intuitively think about the tomography would be to look at only two states with same
amplitude and phases of 0, π. The states are treated by a M = 2 Dolinar receiver as seen in
ﬁgure 31, the ﬁrst displacement serves to displace the state with 0 phase to zero which would
displace the π far away from vacuum. Not detecting a measurement would then be the hypothesis
that the state is in fact the 0-phase state and the second part of the temporal state is displaced
similarly. On the other hand, if a click is detected it is assumed that the state is the π-phase
state and in the next step the displacement is change with a π-phase to displace this state close
to zero. The 0-phase state will then most likely not click in the two steps (being displaced close
to zero) so we expect that the predominant outcome is two ”non-clicks”. However, if two click
is measured the hypothesis for the state will also be the 0-phase state. This can be thought of
as the ﬁrst click being a dark count and then the second click judging that the π-phase was also
wrong, reverting back to the hypothesis that the state is the 0-phase state.
On the other hand, ﬁrst a click and then not a click would mean that the π-phase since this state
would be displaced far away from zero (and click) then displaced by closer to zero and not click.
A non click followed by a click should be less common and judged to be the π-phase which does
not give a click in the ﬁrst bin due to the overlap with |0⟩, but then give a click in the second bin.
The full scheme is a bit more complex as the amplitude of the displacement is optimized so the
displacement is not the same for the ﬁrst and second clicks and states of amplitudes not exactly
the same as the displacements are used, meaning that the state is not displaced perfectly to zero
in the ﬁrst or second measurement.

7
Discussion
To summarise, the Dolinar receiver has been demonstrated to discriminate to orthogonal states
on the Bloch sphere both with real and complex phase.
As can be seen in the plot of the
characterization of the PM in ﬁgure 28 the PM can chose any phase so extending the setup to any
other state on the Bloch sphere would only be a matter of choosing the correct parameters. The
analysis for each angle (especially if including several diﬀerent number of feedback steps) would
take quite a lot of time. However, it would have been interesting to perform the measurement
for a few more angles, most easily would be the ”trivial” poles |0⟩, |1⟩but these state are not
as interesting from a state discrimination viewpoint since they can be discriminated by a simple
click detector. They require no displacement for optimal measurement and the whole framework
of the Dolinar (and even Kennedy) receiver is not relevant and therefore these states have not
been investigated, other states on the Bloch sphere would have been interesting to investigate if
time had allowed.
For M = 3 which is the highest feedback that is perform for both sets of state the error of the
state on the real plane of the Bloch sphere is 4.01 ± 0.20 %, while the error of the complex is 4.34
± 0.15 %. As can be seen, this is general also for lower number of feedback steps the error of the
complex state is higher than the real state, which makes it unlikely that the discrepancy is purely
statistical. This error can not be explained just by diﬀerent visibility or incorrect amplitudes as
the predicted value is lower than the spread of the experimental value for the complex state as
seen in ﬁgure 35. One explanation for this is that the theoretical model does not take into account
that the phase might come with some error. For the real state only the voltage of the signal to
the PM that gives the π-phase shift has to be found, but for the complex state there is a signal
for both the on and oﬀsignal (with a π-phase diﬀerence). In principle this means that there is
an additional parameter which if not calibrated entirely correct could give a slightly higher error
than the predicted.
The change to the setup of stabilizing the strong laser power used for stabilization in both arms
of the interferrometer appears to have made a small improvement to the setup as the power
demonstrably drifts a little less with the new method of power stabilization.
7.1
Possible improvements
As has been shown the performances of the receiver is quite close to the predicted values but
this value is a bit higher than the optimal. The main cause of this is the visibility, but stable
visibility of more than 99.7% has been diﬃcult to achieve with the setup. However, for a few of
the experiments above the visibility is only 99.4%, being a bit more careful with the setup might
decrease the error albeit at the cost of the process becoming more tedious. As the visibility is
one of the most important parameters if all experiments had been performed with a visibility of
around 99.6% as was often realisable it would possibly have generated slightly better results.
We have also seen how the Dolinar receiver in general performs better for higher number of
feedback steps. It principle the performance should be improved for each added step. However
at some point there will likely be a limit to how well performance can be achieved. As the steps
increases due to the increasing steps at some point the timescale of the experiment will likely
have to be large to allow for control of the optical power in each step. The larger time means
more dark counts which limits the eﬀectiveness of the total Dolinar receiver. Larger timescale,
on the other hand, decreases the relative loss associated with the linear loss due to delay in the
setup. However, in this case with the quick response of the PM and IM as well as the Red Pitaya
the delay time this loss is already quite low. In principle the dead time of the FPGA will be the
limiting factor for higher number of feedback steps. An improvement from an error rate of around
7.3% of the Kennedy receiver to 3.6% for the M = 4 Dolinar receiver is quite good, but going
to higher feedback steps should be possible to improve signiﬁcantly before reaching this limit.
In addition the SSPD itself also has some dead time which might start to limit the performance
Page 52 of 72

at some point, the dead time and the eﬀect of this have not been investigated, but is somewhat

In general, the amplitude and phase of the displacement is controlled to a quite good precision.
However, it would always be possible to achieve better results with values closer to the optimal,
from the discrepancy in the experimental and predicted error it seems that the phase for the

Conclusion
To summarise, this thesis have shown how the Dolinar receiver can be used to discriminate a set
of orthogonal quantum states experimentally. The Dolinar receiver has been shown to eﬀectively
discriminate two diﬀerent sets of states, based on the characteristics of the rest of the components
the setup will in principle be able to discriminate any two states on the Bloch sphere the states
used in this works are:
|±⟩=
1
√
2(|0⟩± |1⟩)
and
|φ±⟩=
1
√

The two states are chosen to be on the real and complex plane of the Bloch sphere. These two
sets of states have been discriminated using respectively 4 and 3 feedback steps. The setup is a
modiﬁed version of the one used in [Izumi et al., 2020a], but some components are new and the
whole setup has been characterized.
A theoretical model taking into account visibility and loss in the setup has been developed and
used to ﬁnd the optimal parameters for the receiver, but also to show that the achieved error is
approximately the same as predicted from this model. And Monte Carlo simulations have been
made to investigate how many and what type of probe states would be good for tomography of
the Dolinar receiver.
The discrimination error has been found for a variety of feedback steps and it has been shown
that the higher number of feedback clearly gives and advantage and that the Dolinar is a much
better state discriminator than the Kennedy receiver (no feedback). In addition the POVM used
for discriminating the orthogonal states have also been used to discriminate non-orthogonal states
as they are optimal for both cases, and the result of the Dolinar receiver is shown to approach
the optimal possible discrimination (the Helstrom bound) for larger number of feedback.
The minimum error has been found to be around 3.6% and 4.3 % (for the M = 4 and M = 3
feedback respectively) which is a large improvement from the Kennedy receiver which gives (7.3%
and 7.5% respectively).

be treated as a pair of single rail qubits: |H⟩= |0V , 1H⟩and |V ⟩= |1V , 0H⟩. Therefor a way
to convert states would be ideal as in that case single rail qubits can be used for the physical
settings (or measurements in a Dolinar receiver) and dual rail qubits can be used for information
processing and communication.
Recently a technique for interfacing single- and dual-rail qubits by generating a entangled re-
source between the two systems and then teleporting a dual-rail qubit to a single-rail qubit [Drahi
et al., 2021]. This means that the receiver for the single-rail qubit could be useful even in systems
that otherwise works in the dual-rail frame work by transforming form one framework to the
other, albeit such a transformation would introduce another source of loss. Another scheme for
entanglement and teleportation of dual-rail and continuous variable have been proved experimen-
tally meaning that any of the three most popular representations of quantum information can be
interfaced [Sychev et al., 2018].
Possible uses for detectors of single-rail qubits could be demonstrating non-locality across large
distances or preparation of single photon superposition states between two remote actors.

dim = 5
#dimmension of |a><a| matrix
beta = -0.707
#beta displacement amplitude
betaPhi = 0 #phase of displacement
M = 2 #number of states to discriminate
#states we want to discriminate
rp = np.array([[1,1],[1,1]])*1/2
rm = np.array([[1,-1],[-1,1]])*1/2
alpha = np.array([0.4,0.6,0.8,1]) # array of powers for simulation
phi = np.array([0,1,1/2,-1/2,1/4,-1/4,3/4,-3/4])*np.pi
size = 30*300 #number of elements from poissonian dist.
shape = len(alpha)*len(phi) #number of states
N = 5 #number of points to average
Errors = np.array([])
for p in range(N):
Table = np.array([0,0,0,0])
# table for values
AlphaTable = np.zeros([0,dim,dim]) #table for |a><a| matrix
#llop over all states
for j in range(len(alpha)):
for i in range(len(phi)):
#mean photon number from coherent state
mean = np.abs((alpha[j]*np.exp(1j*phi[i])+beta*np.exp(1j*betaPhi))
*np.conj(alpha[j]*np.exp(1j*phi[i])+beta*np.exp(1j*betaPhi)))
#poissonian distribution
A= np.random.poisson(mean,size)
#table for results values are: alpha, phi, Poff = #number =0/size, Pon
Q = np.count_nonzero(A==0)/size
Table= np.vstack((Table,np.array([alpha[j],1j*phi[i],
np.count_nonzero(A==0)/size,
(1-np.count_nonzero(A==0)/size)])))
#make density matrix for coherent state
Alpha = np.zeros([dim,dim],dtype = ’complex_’)
for n in range(dim):
for m in range(dim):
Q =
np.exp(1j*phi[i]*(n-m))/(np.sqrt(math.factorial(n)*math.factorial(m)))
Alpha[n,m] = np.exp(-alpha[j]**2)*alpha[j]**(n+m)*Q
#make last element so trace is 1
Alpha[-1,-1] = Alpha[-1,-1] + 1 - np.trace(Alpha)
#savve all alphas
AlphaTable = np.concatenate((AlphaTable, Alpha[None]),axis=0)
#remove placeholder in table
Table = Table[1:]
#Poff and Pon
clickOff = Table[:,2]
clickOn = Table[:,3]

#rho(tomography states) is alpha table
rho = AlphaTable.T
#trial POM stacked to match number of states
PI1 = np.dstack([np.identity(dim)]*shape)/dim
PI0 = np.dstack([np.identity(dim)]*shape)/dim
diff = 1 #difference in errors in two loop
error2 = 1 #previous error
#if difference in two consequitive error is small the code terminates
while diff >= 10e-10: #find R_l
R0 = np.sum(clickOff/np.trace(np.matmul(rho.T,PI0.T).T)*rho,axis=2)
R1 = np.sum(clickOn/np.trace(np.matmul(rho.T,PI1.T).T)*rho,axis=2)
#find lagrange operator
lamb = np.matmul(np.matmul(R0.T,PI0.T),R0.T) +
np.matmul(np.matmul(R1.T,PI1.T),R1.T)
lam = np.zeros([0,dim,dim])
for k in range(shape):
lam = np.concatenate((lam, scipy.linalg.sqrtm(lamb[k])[None]),axis=0)
#Find inverse of lambda
inlam = np.linalg.inv(lam)
#Find new POVM for next loop
PI0 = np.matmul(np.matmul(inlam,np.matmul(np.matmul(R0.T,PI0.T),R0.T)),inlam).T
PI1 = np.matmul(np.matmul(inlam,np.matmul(np.matmul(R1.T,PI1.T),R1.T)),inlam).T
#PI1 is the state displaced to zero (seen in clickOff in R1) so this is the rp
state in my scheme.
error = np.real(1/2*(np.trace(np.matmul(rm.T,PI0[:2,:2,0].T))
+np.trace(np.matmul(rp.T,PI1[:2,:2,0].T))))
diff = np.abs(error-error2) #calculate difference in error
error2 = error #set new error to old error
Errors = np.append(Errors,error)
MeanError = np.mean(Errors)
