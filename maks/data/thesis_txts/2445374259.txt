CHAPTER 1
Introduction
With the advancements of technology and the internet of things (IoT), an increasing
amount of data is being collected and used to devise new knowledge in fields of research.
This could for example be on the environment in buildings which involves lighting, air
quality, noise and motion data.
Another example could be detecting user patterns
through their location, heart rate and step count.
In these kind of projects, researchers face many challenges in handling data. It starts
with data collection, where multiple files — data files from sensors and previous work
— are currently manually consolidated and organised. This task becomes tedious as
the number of files grows into the thousands, but what is of a bigger concern is that
it may lead to organisational inconsistency and in the worst case loss of data. Due
to this manual labour, the researcher wastes valuable time that could have been spent
on something more productive.
In an institution like a university, where there are
many research projects running in parallel, more inefficiencies can be found in the data
processing. The overall data processing can be thought of as a pipeline, made up of
processes that each perform specific operations to data. An example of a process could
be pre-processing of data, where multiple files are parsed and formatted into one. This
process can be associated with file types or sensors to make it useful for anyone who
works with the same kind of data. Currently, researchers are implementing the same
processes that others may have implemented before, because there is no good way to
share the pipelines and processes with others. The above mentioned rework and manual
labour motivate for a platform that can assist in data management and sharing of data
processing implementations.
Nowadays, data privacy is also of a big concern. Considering that the General Data
Protection Regulation (GDPR) by the European Union (EU) recently took effect, the
way that data is managed has taken a drastic shift. The data collector, in this case
the researcher, must now add additional consent options for the subjects, the people
who have their data collected, to opt-in and out of how their data may be managed.
Basically, users are now given more control over how their data may be used and even
have the choice to be forgotten in the system. The data collector must manage export
and sharing of data more cautiously, as data taken out of the system cannot contain any
data that can be used to identify an individual. Researchers could greatly benefit from
having a system that assists in managing this as it could do away with some manual
labour — paper work — and rework.
There exists several solutions that attempt to aid in data collection. Some of them
support a more concrete scenario while others provide a more general solution. A con-

CHAPTER 2
Background and Problem
Statement
This chapter aims to provide the necessary background knowledge to understand the
motivation for building the platform. First, an introduction to data management will
be given, where the life cycle of projects and the important considerations for handling
data are described. It will be followed by a section about related work — including the
previous work that is extended upon. A case study is then presented to give a concrete
understanding of what EmpiRes will help support. Finally, a problem statement and
research questions are introduced to abstractly define the main issues to investigate and
solve.
2.1
Data Management
In empirical science, data management is different for each project as the scientific field
is very wide — ranging from mechanics and computer science to biology and psychology.
But in general, data need to be properly collected and stored, such that they can easily
be retrieved and used for analysis. The data must also be properly archived for future
researchers to access, utilise and reproduce the results.
To ensure that the aforementioned tasks are well executed, EmpiRes tries to follow
the FAIR principles [Wil+16]. The idea is manage data, such that they are: Findable,
Accessible, Interoperable and Reusable.
Findable:
The data must be findable through, for instance, a search on characteristic
keywords or a unique identifier. For this to work, meaningful metadata should be asso-
ciated with data, including a global, unique and persistent identifier. These should be
stored in a searchable resource, so researchers have a central place to look for metadata.
Accessible:
The procedure to access data must be available to machines and humans.
A procedure could involve some form of authentication and authorisation before data
is provided. Also, for the retrieval of data, a well-defined protocol — that is open, free
and universally implementable — must be used.
==PAGE:16==
6

Interoperable:
The data must be stored in open formats, such as the comma-separated
values format (CSV), that can easily be comprehended by humans and machines. If data
is generated from other data, a qualified reference — explaining the relation between
the data — must be included. This is done by referencing the metadata of the involved
data.
Reusable:
The data must be associated with detailed metadata that describe who
generated the data and how the data was generated, while adhering to minimal informa-
tion standards where relevant. The metadata should also include a clear and accessible
data usage license. The aforementioned is to ensure that others can comprehend the
data and know how it may be used for their own research.
While having FAIR data is important, there are other considerations that these principles
do not assists well in planning for. That includes: What data should be collected, how
should data be stored, what documentations are necessary etc. This is where a data
management plan [Man16] comes into play as it “covers all aspects of the Data Life
Cycle” [Man16] while helping to make data FAIR. On a higher level, the data life cycle
consists of research planning, active state of research and sharing results as seen in
Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Data Life Cycle (from [Man16]).
In research planning, early considerations should already be made towards the manage-
ment of data — including their collection, storage and archiving. For the last mentioned,
there needs to be a plan — especially for funding long-term storage — to ensure acces-
sibility to the data in the future. As for the active state of research, the storage, access
control, metadata and documentation must be planned for. Lastly, how data should
be published, archived and discovered, must be considered for the sharing of results.
For the creation of the data management plan, there are five main parts that require
thorough considerations:
• Data Collection

• Data Storage
• Documentation
• Sharing
• Archiving
Initially, the researcher must consider how the data collection should take place, and
the kind of data that will be yielded from it. The data formats must be defined, as well
as the expected volume of the data, to better facilitate the design of the data storage.
Also, to avoid legal actions, the data collection process must be ensured to comply with
the current rules and legislation.
With the different aspects in data collection considered, the researcher can form an
idea for data storage. Storing location, backups, access control, security and how data is
shared are the things that should be planned for here. It is important that the data are
stored, such that it can easily be found and used for e.g. processing. A good structure
for storing data not only eases their management for the researcher but also for future
users. Besides storing, conveying information about the data is also essential.
This is where a good documentation strategy is essential, where things like standard-
isation of metadata and versioning of files are looked at. The intention is to make data
understandable, but also ensure that data is not lost when changes are made. More
importantly, the results should be reproducible, so future users can validate them. The
above mentioned is useful for data comprehension, but how the data may be passed for
processing and viewing is a separate issue.
When sharing data, one must think of how and what data can be passed for viewing,
processing and/or analysis. The ’how’ would cover the tools that would be used to access
the data. While the ’what’ can be related to the rights that the researcher must grant
before certain data can be accessed. When granting access to data, there is a level of
security and privacy to be planned for, such as the time frame it should be permitted
but also the sensitivity of the data. Sometimes, the data could perhaps be made public
for others to use, but in scenarios involving sensitive data, special caution needs to be
exercised.
In relation to sharing, the findability of data must also be contemplated.
Sharing knowledge can help make the world a better place, but that knowledge would
be quite wasted if the wrong data was preserved over long time, cannot be found or
perhaps even lost.
That is why, one must lastly consider the long-term preservation. Here, a set of
criteria must be defined for the selection of data to archive. In connection with data
privacy and sharing of data, the criteria could also include anonymisation of the data
and the duration that data should be stored. As for the storage of the data, the plan
should also cover where to store the data — preferably somewhere accessible and stable.
Lastly, to keep the data accessible in the future, financing of the long-term preservation
must be taken into account.
Clearly, there are many considerations that go into a data management plan. By
creating a system that covers the aspects of a data management plan, researchers are

2.2
Previous Work
Earlier attempts have been made to build systems for data collection and management.
Some of these systems support a more concrete case while others are more general, but
each of them provide interesting ideas that could potentially be utilised in EmpiRes.
“MIDAS: Open-source framework for distributed online analysis of data
streams” [HT18]
Henelius and Torniainen present a framework, capable of collecting
time-series signals from devices that output 1 channel at 1Hz to 16 channels at 500Hz
of samples. The system is intended to assist researchers in online collection, processing
and viewing of data from various signal sources. It works in a distributed manner and
features load balancing for the three aforementioned tasks. The result of their research is
a significant improvement in online stream processing. The system has been used in ap-
plications to process EEG signals, but it is also designed to work with ECG data. Their
system demonstrates that efficient online data collection is realisable. This inspires for
EmpiRes to include this capability to provide more ways of data collection - online and
offline.
“The development of data collection tool on spreadsheet format” [NAW17]
Spreadsheet is a standard that is commonly used for data collection since it is easy to
view and work with. The advantage of this standard is that many people are familiar
with it. Nurdiantoro, Asnar, and Widagdo state: “However, using spreadsheet as data
collector, has several problems, such lack of support for: collaboration, input validation,
and data retention.” [NAW17]. While Microsoft Excel1 and Google Spreadsheet2 each
provide online collaboration and functionality to process data, both do not provide
mapping of data to a relational database.
Due to the aforementioned, Nurdiantoro,
Asnar, and Widagdo built a solution that can import spreadsheet data to relational
databases, perform data validation and data collaboration — i.e.
spreadsheet data
can be combined and mapped to a database table without duplicates. Overall, they
present an interesting technique of managing spreadsheet data, which could be useful
for EmpiRes, as spreadsheet data is likely to be involved in data collection.
“Design and Implementation of Distributed Data Collection Management
Platform” [MP15]
Besides time-series and spreadsheet data, there are other sources
that data can originate from. In an enterprise setting, it would most likely be from exist-
ing databases, but it could also involve other sources. Mei and Ping have implemented
a system “to further improve and provide reference and support to improve the data

“An Automated Big Data Processing Engine” [Dat+19]
Datta et al. present a
system that is capable of performing parallel data processing. The system is composed
of two parts: A web portal and the data processing. The former is used by an end user to
upload a data set and select the query to perform through the web portal. The queries
that can be performed on the data set are: Cleaning, aggregation, filtering, organisation
and visualisation. When an end user submits a request, the data processor is invoked
to perform the query, using a parallel processing technique. “In parallel processing tech-
nique, the large number of data processes to be performed on the whole dataset are
divided into a number of small parts and processed in parallel. Finally, all the outputs
of the processes are merged into a single output which can be treated as the final output
of the processing.” [Dat+19]. The system is built on Hadoop that is a framework which
“permits distributed processing of data across large clusters of computers using various
platforms for data analysis, data storage, etc. The main benefit of using Hadoop is that
it uses commodity servers for data storage and processing which are relatively cheaper
than dedicated servers, and hence Hadoop reduces the cost required for server mainte-
nance.” [Dat+19] The system has some interesting techniques to handle processing of
large amounts of data. Especially the distributed processing can come in handy for our
data processing system as it scales up to manage more and more data.
“Bio-Docklets: Virtualization Containers for Single-Step Execution of NGS
Pipelines.” [Kim+17]
Kim et al. present a system that utilises containers to host
the next-generation sequencing pipelines. They attempt to abstract “the complex data
operations of multistep, bioinformatics pipelines for NGS data analysis” [Kim+17] by
automating many of the manual steps with a meta-script that is used to start a container
and control the pipeline execution. The idea for the pipeline execution, is that it should
take place in the container, where input can be passed into to the container and output
can be received from the outside once data processing is done. Their solution combines
Docker, Galaxy — a scientific work flow platform — and meta scripts which “enables
users to run complex, multistep data analysis pipelines as simply as running a single

bioinformatics script” [Kim+17]. Their solution can be used as an inspiration for our

“Design and Implementation of an Extensible Framework for Securely Col-

In
a previous project by Lykkegaard [Lyk18], a system for data collection and sharing was

in mind. But the scope of that was limited to data from wearables — Fitbit3 and Em-

Processing of Data
Once data has been collected through experiments, the researcher would like to process

Processes
Data processing can be performed in many ways. Sometimes there are existing tools
that can manage all of the processing work, but there are also times when those tools
can only perform part of the work or none at all. There are many reasons for the latter
case, but one of them could be due to the specificity of the processing that the researcher

2.3.5.2
General Flow
Because data processing may involve several processes that are combined to form a
pipeline, the researcher has designed the individual process to work as follows: Data is
first read from input file(s), some operations are performed on the read data and then
the results are outputted to file(s). The researcher has designed these processes such
that the output of one process can directly be used as the input for another. One thing
to note about the data processing is that the number of files inputted may not be the
same number of files outputted. But for the above mentioned to work, a process must
at least read a file as input and output at least one file.

3.1
Software Development
To ensure a structured approach in managing and advancing EmpiRes, a software devel-
opment methodology is applied. For this, there is a wide range of methodologies that

Below, an elaboration is given on the considered models and methods that Som-

3.1.1
Waterfall Model
This model [Som11, pages 30-32] is the traditional way of structuring and handling a
software development process. It is composed of five phases as depicted in Figure 3.1.
The idea of this model is to thoroughly plan each phase before work is commenced
for them. In principle, the process runs sequentially, meaning that a phase should be
completed before the following one can commence. But in practice, these phases overlap
as problems can arise in for instance code implementation, which may in the worst
scenario require a revisit to the system and software design phase to find an alternate
solution.
Since this model is plan-driven, significant documentation — listing out the details
for each phase — is required. The advantage of this is that the product is well defined
from the beginning, likely resulting in the end product satisfying most of the initial
requirements set by the customer. But this in turn requires the customer to have a clear

idea of the end product at the beginning. And due to the rigidity of this model, changes
to any previous phase can become expensive to accommodate.
3.1.2
Agile Methods
A more modern approach to software development is Agile development [Som11, pages 57-
74], which is based on the idea of incremental development [Som11, pages 32-35], where
the software is developed in iterations — known as sprints — with activities running
concurrently as depicted in Figure 3.2.
Figure 3.2: Incremental Development (from [Som11, page 35]).
==PAGE:39==
3.1 Software Development
29
In agile development, each iteration begins with sprint planning. For the initial plan-
ning, the customer and developer discuss the requirements — formulated as user stories
— to work on. In subsequent plannings, the discussion also includes a validation of the
current product and a discussion of possible amendments. Based on the discussion, a
list of user stories are short listed and prioritised into sprints. The duration of the up-
coming sprint — typically lasting two to four weeks — is then determined and the sprint
is commenced. During a sprint, the features of the product are designed, implemented
and tested. The idea is to focus on the product development, so that it can be shown to
the customer in the next sprint planning. This repeats till the final version is reached.
Unlike waterfall, agile development gives the customer more space to suggest modi-
fications and continuously see the product progress. Because of this, documentation is
kept to a minimum to put a greater emphasis on the product development. A disadvan-
tage of having changing requirements is that it can become expensive to accommodate
them, but also that customers need to be closely involved during the entire process.
This method is mainly useful when there is a limited budget, or the customer does
not have a clear vision of the end product in the beginning. The result of using an agile
method is an end product that would likely support prioritised features but may exclude
others for future development.
3.1.3
Discussion and Application
To create a sense of direction for this project, the case study described in the previous
chapter will be the scenario that EmpiRes must at least cover. The intention here is
just as the previous work for wearables [Lyk18], to take something concrete that may
be generalised in the future — making EmpiRes useful for more users.
The case study, however, does not list the requirements that EmpiRes should support.
Although the software engineer, the author in this case, can devise many ideas that seem
good, most likely only a handful of them will be found useful. To find the right features
to implement, the actual users of EmpiRes must be approached. In this case, it is the
researcher of our case study, who will act as the customer.
For the selection of a methodology, the initial situation is considered. First, there’s a
time constraint and the project is an individual effort. Due to this, it would be very risky
to attempt a waterfall model methodology, as the workload on the required features may
be underestimated and the implementation only takes place late into the process, which
could ultimately lead to an incomplete and insufficient product. Second, there is no
exhaustive list of feature sets for this project. The outlook of the project is not entirely
clear.
Changes are definitely expected, hence, an agile methodology would be more
suitable for this scenario, as the requirements can progressively be devised as EmpiRes
is being built.
One of the most popular agile methodologies is extreme programming [Som11, pages 64-
68]. It suggests an approach, where the good practices of iterative development are taken
to the ’extreme’. Some of the principles that this methodology encourages are: Incremen-
tal planning, simple design and pair programming, to mention a few. There are some

3.2.1
Research Approach
While a software development methodology is useful for the management of the project,
another kind of methodology is included to enhance the quality of the end product, but
more importantly make this project scientific. Design science [HC10] is therefore intro-
duced to incorporate research knowledge that could inspire for other potential features

science research and knowledge base.
The environment is the application domain that we are working in. For our case,
it is the data collection, processing and analysis of data in research projects. For de-
sign science research, the development of an artefact — EmpiRes— is performed and
evaluated. Lastly, the knowledge base serves as the repository of previous work done in
relation to research.

There are three research cycles [HC10, pages 16-19] involved in design science: Rel-

The idea of each cycle is to perform some activity and evaluation on whether another

Relevance Cycle
The idea of this cycle is to identify the requirements — problems
and opportunities — that could be worked on within our environment. Field tests —
acceptance criteria — are also identified for evaluating the result of the design science
research, in our case for evaluating how well a requirement has been fulfilled by EmpiRes.
The requirements are formulated as research questions that are introduced below. For

methodologies. During the development of EmpiRes though, new ideas may occur from
gathering knowledge through the rigour cycle. Some research may conflict each other,
but both could have some relation to this project. In such a scenario, the relevance
cycle is used to evaluate, which parts of the research that are more relevant and may be
included for our project. The importance is based on our customer’s needs and whether
an idea would bring value to EmpiRes.
Rigour Cycle
In this cycle, existing work is investigated to provide the research
project with past knowledge — serving as grounding. This is done through literature
research to find design ideas, models or evaluation theories, which may include state-
of-the-art techniques. The idea is to present and use existing relevant knowledge and
possibly combine them to demonstrate that this project provides new value to the knowl-
edge base.

Design Cycle
With the requirements from the relevance cycle and previous knowledge

3.2.2
Research Objective and Research Questions
To clearly define the goal of the research, a research objective is given and broken down
into subproblems in the form of research questions.
3.2.2.1
Research Objective
To aid the problem, stated in the Problem Statement Section, the research objective
of this paper is to implement a platform that has reusable data infrastructures and is
capable of automating part of the researcher’s manual labour in research that involves
collecting, processing and analysing data.
3.2.2.2
Research Question
To achieve the research objective, three research questions are posed:
• RQ1:
How can data collection across different projects be unified?
• RQ2:
How can the collection and storage of experimental data be made easier
for the researcher?
• RQ3:
What can be done to reduce implementation work for similar data pro-
cesses?
RQ1
The problem in data collection lies in the fact that researchers collect, label and
organise their experiment data differently. This poses a problem as new users to a project
would need to be familiarised with different ways that data is managed for each project,
which is rework that can be avoided if there is a single standard across all projects.
==PAGE:43==
3.3 Development and Research

RQ2
Another issue lies in the collection of data. Researchers must currently supervise
their experiments and manually manage the storage of the collected experiment data.
These tasks could possibly be automated with some introduction of automated data
collection and organisation of collected experimental data. Researchers would be able
spend time working on the actual research.
RQ3
Data processes can be very complicated and tailored for very specific purposes.
Each of these data processes can include formatting, data correlation and other oper-
ations. If a slight variation of a data process was to be created from an existing one,
the worst case scenario would require significant changes in the implementation if the
implementation is not well structured for change. In such a scenario, it may be easier
to create a new data process implementation from scratch, but this may require signifi-
cant time to perform as well. On top of implementation, the new resulting data process
must also be tested to ensure that it actually works as intended. This data processing
implementation and testing is clearly time consuming, which motivates for the question
on whether the implementation work could be reduced.

Domain
This chapter presents a structured approach to define the domain by first introducing
a concept to store data for reuse. A glossary is then presented, where the keywords for
the case study are listed and described. With the preceding, the domain model is shown
and elaborated in smaller manageable parts with excerpts of the model.
4.1
Concept
One of the issues in research lies in reproducing the results of previous work and using
them in new research. Researchers at institutions are lacking an easy way to share their
work with their colleagues. To address the preceding issue, an efficient and consistent
structural data concept for the domain is introduced to organise the way that metadata
and data are stored. The structural data concept is made up of three levels: Definitions,
instances and running instances, each of them explained below.
Definition
A structure for the reusable parts of the researcher’s work, which could
for instance be a process definition, pipeline definition, experiment definition etc. The
idea is to define the reusable work once and reuse it in different projects that need it.
A basic definition contains a name and description as its default metadata, so that the
definition can be easily identified and be included in projects. Some definitions may hold
additional metadata and possibly data that is important for its use. Take for example a
process definition, which is used to process data. A process definition would in addition
to the basic definition metadata also have to hold data, the processing code. Otherwise,
a project that would like to use a process definition would be short of the necessary
data to perform the processing. The idea applies similarly to other definitions that are
implemented, which are introduced after the other two structures.
Instance
A structure that instantiates a definition. When thinking in terms of object
oriented programming, an instance can be thought of as an object while a definition as
a class. An instance can hold additional metadata or data that are different for every
instance of a definition. Take for example a project that utilises an experiment definition.
An instance of an experiment definition, which is also called an experiment, would be
created for that project. The created instance can be given a separate name that is more
meaningful for the project that it is utilised in. Because of this separation of definitions
==PAGE:46==
36
4 Domain
and instances, definitions do not need to compensate for however they may alternatively
be named or used in projects as that is what the instance is for.
Running Instance
A structure for tracking the state of tasks that are executed on
the developed platform. This could for example be used to track the progress of running
data processes. A running instance can be though of as an instance of the instance
structure. When a running instance is created, an instance would be used to set the
initial state and configurations for the task that is executed. For data processing, this
could for example be the files and arguments to pass to the process.
The intention of this concept is to reduce re-work among researchers.
For the case
study, it can be applied to experiments, data definitions, parse formats processing and
pipelining which are explained below. Not every aspect of the case study will utilise this
concept. It will mainly be applied to where repetitive patterns are recognised.
4.2
Key Terms
Based on our case study and our structural data concept, the key terms are identified
and listed below. For a full glossary, the reader is referred to Appendix A.
Data Type
A category that is used to label the type of data, for example time-series
and file.
Experiment Data
A piece of data that is yielded from performing an experiment.
Data Series
A set of experiment data in the form of time-series. In our case study,
this would include the time-series data collected from the skin response sensor and eye
tracker.
Experiment File
A piece of experiment data contained in a file. Related to our case
study, this could be questionnaire data, audio recordings, screen captures and even the
data series file in for instance CSV or TSV format.
Data Definition
A definition for the kind of data that experiments may output e.g.
GSR data, eye tracking data, screen capture video etc. A data definition is associated
with a data type and contains the sensitivity level of the data.
Parse Format Definition
A specification of the parse format for a spreadsheet file
to help facilitate in parsing and mapping e.g. time-series data to a time-series database.
Experiment Definition
A specification of an experimental setup that may be used
in several projects.

Process Definition
A definition for a data process that performs operations on ex-

researcher, which could for example perform cleaning, correlation of data, formatting
and so on.
Pipeline Definition
A definition composed of a sequence of process definitions. An

An investigation of a specific topic, which related to our case study is the

A stage in a project, which could for example be: Planning, data collection,
analysis or publication.
Experiment
An instance of an experiment definition which is performed in a phase
of a project.
Pipeline
An instance of a pipeline definition which is associated with a set of files to
input to the first process definition of the instantiated pipeline definition.
Running Pipeline
A running instance of a pipeline that is created when a researcher
runs a pipeline. There can be multiple running instances of the same pipeline in parallel.
Hence, a running instance is created for each run of a pipeline.
Running Pipeline Process
A running instance of the process definition that is exe-
cuted for a running pipeline.

To track the requirements and work that need to be done for a product, a backlog

There are two kinds of stories that can be put into a backlog. The first of the two kinds
is known as a user story which is “a brief statement of intent that describes something

a central place to store experiment files. The other kind of story is known as other work
items that basically state development related work such as refactoring, bugs, support
and maintenance.

address this, a user story is associated with tasks that represent the activities to perform.
Examples of tasks for the above mentioned researcher user story could be: Code front
end, code back end, write tests, define acceptance test etc. The tasks are not meant to
elaborate the exact behaviour of a requirement, like a detailed use case, as the point
of an Agile method is to keep documentation to a minimum and implement based on
continuous customer feedback.

there still needs to be a way for developers and customers to have when a story has
been fulfilled. Hence, tests are introduced to set some fulfilment criterias and provide
the developer and customer with an understanding of when the full intent of a story has
been met. Leffingwell makes a distinction between two kinds of tests: Story Acceptance
Tests and Unit Tests. The former would consists of tests to check whether a user of the
system can complete a certain action. For our researcher story, it could for instance be:
Uploading a 1 GB video file is possible, multiple files can be selected at once and so
on. As for unit tests, they are meant to ensure that functions and methods behave as
expected. More details on our unit tests will be provided in the Test Chapter.

6.2.2
File Storage
Besides time series and relational data, our case study also yields other kinds of data
such as audio and video. Unlike the primitive types (text, numbers and binary values),
these are typically saved as binary objects that are also known as files. For the storage of
these, a file system would usually be utilised as it is designed to efficiently manage files.
In such a case, the file storage locations and other metadata related to the files are kept
in a relational data table, so that the framework knows where to find the files for later
use. An alternative solution to file systems are relational databases, where relational
data and files are kept in one component, which ensures data consistency as opposed
to having separate components manage these data, where data may become unavailable
without other components knowing. While file systems are designed for file management,
databases are designed for the purpose of storing primitive data. Databases may there-
fore not be the most efficient way of storing files that can be of different sizes. Sears,
Van Ingen, and Gray state: “Folklore tells us that databases efficiently handle large
numbers of small objects, while filesystems are more efficient for large objects.” [SVG06,
page 2]. The preceding may be true, but one may ask where is the break-even point and
are there any trade offs between the two?
To answer the above mentioned, Sears, Van Ingen, and Gray present in their technical
report [SVG06] a performance comparison between storing large files, also known as bi-
nary large objects (BLOBs), in a database versus a file system. In terms of performance,
there are many factors that play part in determining that. Two of the factors that Sears,
Van Ingen, and Gray considered are the fragmentation of data and the available free
storage. They found that as a large file was stored over longer periods, their data tended
to become fragmented as insertions, updates and deletions of files were performed. The
preceding applies to both a database and a file system, but with the latter dealing with
it significantly better. Storage wise, it was found that larger volumes did not have their
performance affected by how much free space was available. But the same did not hold
for smaller volumes — like 40 gigabytes — which saw significant performance degrada-
tion once the free space reached below 10%. In conclusion of their research, they state
that files above 1 MB are better managed by the file system while those below 256 KB

on the frequency of writes and how long data may be retained for.

6.2.3
Versioning
In research, project files are typically subject to many changes as progress is made in
research. But changes are not only resulted from progress, it also occurs due to mistakes.
Seen in relation to our case study, there can be situations when experiment files were
incorrectly exported, e.g. eye tracking data from the tracking tool, such that some data
was left out. In this case, the researcher would attempt to re-export it and ensure that
everything is there. But what if the previous export turned out to be correct after all?
The problem here lies in the general perception that the most recent export should be
the best. While in most cases that holds to be true, there are also many times that
is very wrong. In research, experimentation and analysis of data are typically involved.
Many of these analytic and experimental attempts can end up differently than expected.
In these situations, it would be useful if the researcher could backtrack to an earlier
state of the project to attempt a different approach. This is a good example of why
data versioning is essential to prevent loss of data. A file versioning system is therefore
introduced to EmpiRes to improve its reliability.
One such versioning system was built by Mao and Zhou who present their work in
their paper [lvfc] titled “LvFS: A Lightweight File Versioning Tool for General Binary
Files”. They identify that the current file versioning systems do not efficiently version
binary files. Git1, a popular versioning tool for code projects, is for instance designed
to track version changes in supported text files. How it works is that it compares the
current state of text files with the previous version to find and only store the differences
— inserted and deleted text — between the two for space efficiency. Code projects may
also involve some binary files as well. For those, Git will make full copies of every new
version, which is clearly not space efficient. Mao and Zhou therefore created a binary
1A version control system: https://git-scm.com/
==PAGE:76==
66

file versioning system, named LvFS, that is capable of versioning binary files in a file
system efficiently.
Their system is designed to automatically track and commit any
changes made to files and directories by a user. A resemblance of their system would
be Dropbox2.
LvFS performs versioning by initially creating a snapshot of a file or
directory, also known as a checkpoint, to be tracked. Any new versions from that point
on have their applied changes stored in a database. The principle is the same as Git,
where only the modifications to text files — added and deleted texts — are tracked
beyond the initial version, but for binary files instead. To retrieve a previous version of
a file or directory, the initial snapshot is taken and applied with all of the changes up
to the wanted version. By only saving changes for subsequent versions instead of full
copied, their system can save significant space, especially for large files.
To develop a file versioning system for EmpiRes, a few inspirations are drawn from
the work [MZ15] of Mao and Zhou. Due to the time constraint on our project, a simple
file versioning system for EmpiRes is designed such that every version of a file is kept
in full in a file system. This functionality is implemented as a part of the file manager
component. But in order for our system to keep track of these files and versions, their
path, version number and other metadata are kept in our relational database component
— inspired by LvFS.

EmpiRes
There are two parts to running a process: Build and execute. The folders and files that
each of the two tasks require are different. To ensure file security, so that a process
only accesses the necessary files to perform its processing, the build and execution of a
process is hosted in an isolated environment. The reader is reminded that an isolated
environment has its own file system that works separately from the actual file system
on the hosting machine.
The actual file system of the hosting machine has the file hierarchy shown in Figure
6.4 implemented and is used to store all experiment files, processing code archives and
the processed files in the platform. As for the file system of isolated environments, the
implemented file hierarchy is the one of a process as shown in Figure 6.7. For a process
to be built or executed in an isolated environment, the files and directories kept in the
actual file system must somehow be accessible to the isolated environment. Access to the
actual file system is provided to an isolated environment by mapping files and folders in
the actual file system to files and folders in the isolated environment. For mapped files
and directories, any changes performed to them when a process is built or executed in
the isolated environment are reflected in the actual file system. Because of this mapping
functionality of isolated environments, there is no need to copy files from the actual file
system into the file systems of the isolated environments, saving copy operations and
space.
The mapping for building a code project of a process definition with ID 1 is shown in
Figure 6.9, which is an exact mapping of the code project folder in the actual file system
to the isolated environment. The in and out folders are not mapped to the isolated
environment as they are not necessary for building a code project.

7.2.3
Time Series Data
In the Design Chapter, it was mentioned that the tmp subdirectory in the actual file
system contained temporary files to be mapped to the time series database. These tempo-
rary files are spreadsheet files that are received by the framework from our user interface
through a REST call. For inserting time series data into the time series database, the
spreadsheet file is first received from the user interface and stored as a temporary file
in the tmp subdirectory. With the spreadsheet file saved as a temporary file, the data
series in the temporary file is parsed by the framework and inserted to the time series
database as a measurement. A measurement is the equivalent of a table in a relative
database, which is the way time series data is organised in InfluxDB. For the parsing of
the temporary file, a parse format definition — created by the researcher before upload-
ing the file — is retrieved from the relational database and used to configure the format
that the parser should use. A parse format definition basically states the format of the
file e.g. column separator symbol, line breaks, header row etc. After time series data
has been parsed and inserted to the time series database, the temporary file is deleted.

7.3
User Interface
In the previous work [Lyk18], Lykkegaard, utilised the React JavaScript library to build
a web user interface. Such an interface is made up of components that are basically the
building blocks of the interface. One of the key benefits of this library is that it utilises a
virtual DOM (Document Object Model). A DOM is a tree over the components in a web
user interface. Whenever some parts of the interface require an update, the DOM must
be mutated with the new components. In traditional websites, this mutation is done
inefficiently such that more components are re-rendered than necessary. With a virtual
DOM, only the components that require an update are re-rendered. This significantly
speeds up the web interface loading speed as an end user browses through the interface.
To supplement the creation of the web user interface, Redux was also incorporated to
keep track of the interface state. The benefit of including Redux is easier management
of the interface, as its components can be updated according to its state. React works
with Redux by informing interactions on the interface through actions that are captured
by Redux. This results in the current state being updated by a reducer that basically
mutates the current state to a new state.
Based on the new state, the interface is
updated accordingly.
Because this project extends upon the web interface of the previous work, the two
above mentioned libraries are retained.

Evaluation
To understand the scientific contribution of this thesis, the research questions posed in

9.1
Research Questions Revisited
Each of the research questions are revisited and answered below.
RQ1: How can data collection across different projects be unified?
To answer the first research question, fixed metadata parameters were defined in the
Domain Chapter to standardise the required metadata for different parts of projects.
Because of this standardisation, it is easier for new users to familiarise with the infras-
tructure, as projects can now be managed with the same infrastructure. To further
enhance data collection unification, a structural data concept was introduced to define
the reusable parts of a projects once, though creating definitions, and encourage reuse
in different projects as needed.
RQ2: How can the collection and storage of experimental data be
made easier for the researcher?
To answer the second research question, the design of the previous work [Lyk18] has
been enhanced with a file management service as introduced in the Design Chapter, and
the web interface from previous work [Lyk18] has been stabilised, extended upon and
refactored to make offline data collection seamless. Behind the scenes of the web inter-
face, the framework component of EmpiRes is responsible for organising the collected
data. For the storage of data, the time series database has been retained from previous
work for storing time series data. EmpiRes has introduced a file management service
that is responsible for organising experiment files on the hosting machine. Because of
the implemented web interface, researchers can now simply drag and drop their files for
storage in the platform. And since the framework is now delegated the task of organis-
==PAGE:112==
102
9 Evaluation
ing experimental data, researchers are relieved of manually managing their experimental
data.
Collection of data could be made even easier by supporting importation of data
from multiple sources [MP15], and include some collaborative tools for spreadsheet data
[NAW17], which many people are familiar with. Online collection [HT18] would also be
a posibility to ease data collection, as it would save the researcher an extra hassle of
having to manually import data to the platform.
RQ3: What can be done to reduce implementation work for similar
data processes?
As for the third research question, implementation work has now been reduced by Em-
piRes, as processes with basic algorithms can now be used to construct a pipeline that
performs equivalent to a complex process. Because processes are designed to work in
sequence to each other, through file input and output, researchers do not have to invest
time in implementing and testing variations of a complex process. Since processes and
pipelines are created as definitions in EmpiRes, they can easily be reused in several
projects to save the researcher time.
By comparing our data processing system to Bio-Docklets [Kim+17], our data pro-
cessing system improves upon the ideas laid by Bio-Docklet as our solution is capable of
handling more customised behaviour. While Bio-Docklet is still quite specific to bioinfor-
matics, it takes advantage of work flow platforms like Galaxy to reduce implementation
work for pipelines.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
This thesis has contributed with a platform that can be used to create reusable data
infrastructures and automate part of the researchers’ manual labour in collecting and
processing experimental data. With the introduction of a structural data concept in the
Domain Chapter, a standardised way of defining data infrastructures has been achieved
thanks to the distinction between definitions, instances and running instances.
For
experimental data collection, EmpiRes helps researchers organise the experimental data
into a file system, where every version of a file is kept to ensure no data is lost. EmpiRes
also provides the functionality to parse and map data series to a time-series database,
making it easier to query the data for analysis. The biggest contribution of EmpiRes is
the data processing system that can be used to construct different pipelines with defined
processes that contain data processing code. The execution of a pipeline runs securely,
as each process of a pipeline is only given read and write access to the necessary files,
thanks to Docker containers.
Technical improvements from the previous work [Lyk18] include fixing the very un-
stable user interface, documenting the implementation and introducing unit tests. These
improvements have resulted in an end user now having a better experience in interact
with the platform through a stable user interface, extensions to the platform can be done
with less hassle, and the tested features are better retained in future implementations.
Future Work
While some of the key features — collecting and processing of experimental data —
have been implemented by this thesis, there is still space for further improvements on
the platform. At the time of writing, there are already ongoing projects improving and
extending upon EmpiRes. For future work, the following could be worked upon:
• Complete test implementation
• Better integration of the data visualisation, such that analytic information can
automatically be configured and displayed in the user interface.
• Work on the user management system to enable sharing of work with other users
of the system.
• Data Privacy improvements related to the GDPR.

with a data type and contains the sensitivity level of the data.
Parse Format Definition
A specification of the parse format for a spreadsheet file
to help facilitate in parsing and mapping e.g. time-series data to a time-series database.
Experiment Data Definition
A label for experiment data that indicates the experi-
ment definition and the data definition that experiment data relates to. An experiment
data definition may optionally be associated with a parse format definition, to indicate
how the machine should parse the data if it is expected to be in spreadsheet format.
Process Definition
A definition for a data process that performs operations on ex-
periment file(s). A process definition has some processing code that is provided by a
researcher, which could for example perform cleaning, correlation of data, formatting
and so on.
Pipeline Definition
A definition composed of a sequence of process definitions. An
example of a pipeline definition could for instance consist on cleaning, data correlation
and formatting at the end.
Pipeline Process Definition
An indicator for a process definition use in a pipeline
definition. A pipeline process definition can be thought of as the mediator between a
process definition and a pipeline definition, which indicates the process definition to be
used in a pipeline definition and the order in the pipeline it is executed.
Pipeline File Set In
A set of files that are inputted to the first process definition of
a pipeline definition.
==PAGE:120==
110
A Glossary
Pipeline File In
An experiment file that is renamed and included in a pipeline file
set in. The renaming is necessary, as the source code of a process definition might only
process files with specific names.
Pipeline
An instance of a pipeline definition which is associated with a set of files to
input to the first process definition of the instantiated pipeline definition.
Running Pipeline
A running instance of a pipeline that is created when a researcher
runs a pipeline. There can be multiple running instances of the same pipeline in parallel.
Hence, a running instance is created for each run of a pipeline.
Running Pipeline Process
A running instance of the process definition that is exe-
cuted for a running pipeline.
Running Pipeline Process File Set Out
A set of files that a running pipeline
process outputs from running the process definition code on inputted files, introduced
below.
Running Pipeline Process File Out
A file of a running pipeline process file set out
that is outputted by a running pipeline process.
Running Pipeline File Set Out
A set of files that are outputted by the last running
pipeline process of a running pipeline.
Running Pipeline Process File Set In
A set of files that a running pipeline process
takes as input. For this, there are two kinds of file sets that can be inputted to a running
pipeline process, which are explained below.
Process In Pipeline File Set In
A file set — consisting of the files specified in a
pipeline file set in — that is inputted to a running pipeline process. This is mainly used
for the first running pipeline process of a running pipeline.
Process In Process File Set Out
A file set — consisting of the files in a running
pipeline process file set out — that is inputted to subsequent running pipeline processes.
This is mainly used to point to the set of outputted files from a previous running process
and use it as input to the next running process.

The user stories of EmpiRes are shown in tables that consist of the following columns:
Story number, role, activity, business values and assigned MoSCoW priority group. An
entry in the table can be translated to the written user stories as follows: As a <Role>
I would like to <Activity> so that <Business Values>, where the text encapsulated in
<> represents the column value of a table entry. The stories are grouped and presented
in tables based on domain relevance.
Table C.1 lists the user stories that are related to definitions that once created cannot
be changed due to projects depending on them. In the future, there could potentially
be different versions of a definition, but the user stories for those have been excluded in
the table to keep things concise. It can be seen from the table that the majority of them
have been assigned the Must Have priority, as excluding them would result in a product
that cannot be used for managing a project. Only the stories related to Parse Format
Definitions have been assigned a Should Have priority as those features are not crucial
for a usable product.
For project management, there are three kinds of artefacts created under a project:
Pipelines, phases and subjects. The user stories that relate to the preceding artefacts
are listed in Table C.2. While phases have been covered in the previous work [Lyk18],
the user stories for those are included in the table as Phases are now used to contain
Experiments and fixes have been applied to the front end. Only user stories related
to pipelines have been assigned a Must Have priority since they are required to utilise
process definitions for data processing. Stories related to phases and subjects have been
assigned a Should Have priority as they are a convenience features that would make data
management easier.
As for experiment management, an experiment itself simply acts as a container for
experiment data, but it is important for the data management. Therefore, the stories
related to those are given a Must Have priority. These can be seen in Table C.3.
Experiments contain experiment data that can be of the following kinds: Experiment
file, data series and possibly questionnaire in the future. Table C.4 lists the stories about
experiment data, which are mainly about uploading and labelling data. More stories
are created for each kind of experiment data, as they each are treated differently in the
system.
User stories about experiment files management are made up of some versioning and
file renaming that are intended to make files easier to manage and find. These stories
can be found in Table C.5.
