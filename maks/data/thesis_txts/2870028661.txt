Abstract 
This thesis examines the critical funding challenges faced by European Cleantech energy startups 
during the commercialization "Valley of Death" (VoD), focusing on the Series A and B funding 
stages. Three core research questions guided this study: (1) What are the unique risks, challenges, 
and drivers in Series A and B funding for energy startups and investors? (2) How can energy 
startups align their strategies with venture capital (VC) and corporate venture capital (CVC) 
expectations? (3) What entrepreneurial strategies can effectively bridge the funding gap and 
overcome the VoD? These questions address the urgent need to scale Cleantech solutions for global 
decarbonization efforts while navigating resource-intensive and high-risk commercialization 
phases. 
To answer these questions, a mixed-methods approach was employed, integrating cross-sectional 
surveys of startups and investors with semi-structured interviews. The analysis identified distinct 
risks and challenges, such as market uncertainties, regulatory complexities, and investor concerns 
over market readiness. It also explored drivers, including sustainability impact and financial 
returns. The study further mapped misalignments in how entrepreneurs and investors prioritize 
metrics such as Environmental Performance Indicators (EPIs) and financial benchmarks, offering 
insights into bridging these gaps. 
Findings reveal evidence-based entrepreneurial strategies that startups must adopt to align with 
investor priorities, including integrating EPIs with financial narratives, preparing robust risk 
assessments, and adhering to evolving regulatory standards. The research also emphasizes the 
importance of transparent communication and strategic partnerships in securing Series A and B 
funding. 
Finally, this thesis derived three conceptual models for Cleantech entrepreneurs to navigate funding 
challenges, offering practical recommendations to align with investor priorities. By addressing 
these barriers, startups can scale their innovations and contribute significantly to a sustainable 
energy future.

Introduction 
Investment in sustainable innovation is essential for addressing the global climate crisis and 
achieving critical decarbonization goals (D. E. de Lange, 2019; IMF, 2024). The United Nations 
(2024) has emphasized the urgent need for international collaboration to combat climate change, 
requiring transformative changes such as increasing renewable energy generation from 25% to 
100% by 2050 and doubling energy efficiency. These ambitious targets emphasize the importance 
of scaling and deploying innovative solutions to reduce emissions effectively. According to the 
International Energy Agency (IEA), nearly half of the required emissions reductions by 2050 
depend on the widespread adoption of these technologies, positioning Cleantech startups as pivotal 
actors in this global effort (IEA, 2023b). 
Cleantech, short for “clean technologies,” encompasses innovations that aim to improve 
environmental sustainability. Within the energy domain, startups focusing on renewable energy 
generation, energy storage, and energy efficiency play a critical role in enabling the transition to 
"green electrons", clean, renewable electricity that decarbonises power systems (Cleantech Group, 
2024). These startups are instrumental in addressing the largest contributor to global emissions, the 
energy sector, which is responsible for over 75% of greenhouse gas emissions in Europe alone 
(European Commission, 2024a). Despite their potential, Cleantech startups face significant barriers 
during the commercialisation phase, commonly referred to as the "Valley of Death" (VoD). During 
this stage, startups must transition from developing prototypes and achieving proof of concept to 
scaling their operations and entering the market.  
However, the high capital intensity, extended development timelines, and significant technological 
risks associated with Cleantech ventures make this phase particularly challenging. These 
difficulties are further amplified by market volatility, stringent regulatory compliance 
requirements, and the inherent risks of attracting private investment in a high-risk sector. This leads 
to a lack of funding resources to support entrepreneurs in bridging the gap between early stages, 
where they rely on a mix of grants, angel investors, and seed funding, and later growth stages, 
where demonstrated market traction and reduced risk attract larger amounts of venture capital or 
private equity funding. 
The challenges of the Valley of Death are particularly pronounced in Europe. While the region 
leads in Cleantech innovation, securing approximately €11 billion in venture capital (VC) funding 
in 2023 (Cleantech Group, 2024), European energy startups encounter unique structural and 
financial barriers compared to startups in other regions, such as North America which often benefit 
from more favourable funding landscape (Dealroom, 2024). These include a reliance on debt over 
equity financing, a less mature VC market, and regulatory complexities, all of which exacerbate 
the difficulty of bridging the commercialization gap (Dealroom, 2024a; Hammond et al., 2024). 
Furthermore, the region’s ambitious climate targets, such as achieving net-zero emissions by 2050, 
demand substantial private investment to complement public funding. The European Investment 
Bank (EIB) estimates that an additional €360 billion in annual investments is needed to meet these 
goals, highlighting the urgency of addressing the funding shortfall (European Investment Bank, 
2024a). Moreover, geopolitical challenges, such as energy supply disruptions and economic 
instability, further complicate the investment landscape for Cleantech startups. Despite these 
obstacles, Europe’s Cleantech sector remains a critical player in the global transition to sustainable 
energy, necessitating innovative solutions to overcome the Valley of Death.

This thesis seeks to address this gap by adopting a micro-level perspective, focusing on the 
relationship between entrepreneurs and investors. Although the existence of the commercialization 
Valley of Death is widely recognized, research on mechanisms to overcome it remains limited. 
Specifically, it investigates how Cleantech energy startups can better align with the expectations of 
venture capitalists, the primary funding source for Series A and B stages, and corporate venture 
capitalists (CVCs), which offer complementary funding and strategic support. By examining the 
dynamics of these investor-entrepreneur relationships, the research aims to identify actionable 
strategies for bridging the funding gap. The thesis also explores the role of Environmental 
Performance Indicators (EPIs) and regulatory compliance in enhancing the investment appeal of 
Cleantech startups, offering a nuanced understanding of how these factors influence investor 
decision-making. Additionally, it highlights the importance of aligning financial and environmental 
metrics to demonstrate both profitability and sustainability to potential investors. 
The thesis aims to identify actionable strategies for Cleantech startups to bridge the funding gap 
and secure investment during the critical commercialization phase. By employing a mixed-methods 
approach, combining cross-sectional surveys and semi-structured interviews with both investors 
and entrepreneurs. This methodology enables the synthesis of quantitative and qualitative insights, 
providing a comprehensive framework for understanding the challenges and opportunities within 
the European Cleantech funding ecosystem. The research findings are structured to contribute to 
three key areas. First, this thesis deepens the understanding of the Valley of Death by focusing on 
the specific context of energy startups within the Cleantech sector. It examines the unique risks, 
drivers, and decision-making processes of investors during the critical Series A and B funding 
stages, offering a nuanced perspective on the commercialization challenges these startups face. 
Second, it identifies sources of misalignment between investor expectations and entrepreneurial 
strategies. The research explores how energy startups can better align with VC and CVC 
requirements, particularly through the integration of financial and EPI metrics and compliance with 
regulatory frameworks. These elements are identified as critical components of investor decision-
making and essential for securing funding. Third, the thesis provides actionable recommendations 
by developing three conceptual models that offer practical steps for entrepreneurs to enhance 
alignment with investor priorities. By addressing gaps in strategy, communication, and 
performance metrics, the recommendations aim to improve funding success and support the scaling 
of Cleantech startups. 
This thesis contributes to the broader discourse on sustainable innovation by identifying the critical 
gap in the commercialization of Cleantech energy startups. By focusing on the intersection of 
entrepreneurial actions and investor expectations, it provides evidence-based recommendations for 
bridging the Valley of Death. The findings seek to empower Cleantech entrepreneurs with the tools 
and strategies needed to secure critical funding, scale their ventures, and drive meaningful impact 
in the transition to a sustainable energy future. In doing so, the thesis advances academic literature 
by integrating perspectives from venture capital, corporate venture capital, and sustainability 
metrics, creating a holistic framework for understanding and addressing the challenges faced by 
Cleantech startups. The research ultimately aims to contribute to a resilient and innovative 
Cleantech ecosystem that can drive the global energy transition forward. By addressing systemic 
barriers and emphasizing the need for tailored investment strategies, the research highlights 
pathways to enhance the scalability and impact of Cleantech startups, ensuring their role as key 
drivers in the transition to a sustainable energy future.

1. Key Definitions and Context 
 
This chapter explores the history and definition of Cleantech, focusing on its evolution and 
differentiation from related terms like ClimateTech and GreenTech. It establishes a clear 
conceptual framework for the research. 
 
1.1 What is Cleantech?  
History of Cleantech  
The concept of Cleantech, which is the focal high-growth sector of this thesis, has evolved over the 
last two decades. Cleantech, or "clean technology," began to take shape in the 1990s, although it 
wasn't widely recognised or clearly defined at the time (Fernando, 2022a). It gained significant 
momentum in the early 2000s, as environmental innovation and sustainability emerged as pressing 
global concerns (Killande, 2024). 
The first major growth phase, known as Cleantech 1.0, occurred from 2006 to 2011, with a primary 
focus on energy innovations, particularly solar and biofuels (Wan et al., 2022). However, despite 
the early optimism surrounding Clean Tech 1.0, the sector faced considerable setbacks. Of the $25 
billion in VC funding poured into this sector, more than 50% was lost by 2015 (Wan et al., 2022). 
The 2008 financial crisis compounded these challenges, leading to a steep decline in venture capital 
investment from 2008 to 2014. Investment round sizes decreased by approximately 80%, while 
pre-money valuations fell by nearly 50% (Looney et al., 2024). 
Despite these challenges, the sector rebounded following the 2015 Paris Agreement, marking the 
rise of what is now referred to as Cleantech 2.0. This new wave extended beyond clean energy 
innovations and began to address a broader array of sectors, including consumer goods, agriculture, 
manufacturing, maritime shipping, transportation, and construction (Wan et al., 2022).  By 2023, 
global funding for clean technologies reached unprecedented levels, reflecting a renewed interest 
in sustainable solutions driven by growing climate awareness and favourable government policies. 
Unlike the overly optimistic expectations of Cleantech 1.0, investors today approach the market 
with greater caution, informed by the lessons of earlier corrections (Sauvage, 2021). 
Several key factors contributed to the success of Cleantech 2.0, including international frameworks 
like the Paris Climate Accords and the increasing role of institutional investors. Companies listed 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
in the Global 1000 are actively integrating Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) 
initiatives, creating fertile ground for Cleantech startups to thrive (Sauvage, 2021). 
Defining Cleantech 
In more recent literature, various terminologies have emerged to describe technological innovations 
aimed at addressing environmental and climate challenges. These include ClimateTech, 
GreenTech, and Cleantech, with Cleantech often recognised as the broader umbrella term 
encompassing these other concepts. According to the Cambridge Dictionary, Cleantech refers to 
technologies that reduce or avoid environmental harm, such as renewable energy systems or 
pollution-free transportation (Cambridge, 2025). The Cleantech Group, which has held the 
trademarked definition since 2003, describes Cleantech as innovative technologies that offer 
superior performance, lower costs, reduce environmental impacts, enhance climate resilience, or 
promote the use of regenerative resources (Cleantech Group, 2025). Despite these subtle 
distinctions in terminology, all definitions of Cleantech converge on a common objective: 
leveraging technology to mitigate climate change and protect the environment (Lane, 2011). 
Other definitions highlight Cleantech's overarching goal of reducing humanity’s environmental 
footprint while promoting cleaner alternatives to traditional industrial processes (Paliychuk, 2024). 
ClimateTech, a subset of Cleantech, specifically focuses on technologies aimed at mitigating 
climate change by reducing greenhouse gas emissions (BBVA, 2024). 
While Cleantech covers a broad range of environmental challenges across various sectors, it is 
important to understand its differentiation to other terminologies used in the industry which cover 
other aspects of clean technologies. ClimateTech targets solutions such as carbon capture and 
storage and climate modelling tools that directly address the climate crisis (Paliychuk, 2024). On 
the other hand, GreenTech is often viewed as a broader, less precise term that encompasses any 
technology that is environmentally friendly or sustainable (Albrecht, 2023). A notable challenge in 
defining Cleantech lies in its ambiguity regarding which technologies should be included or 
excluded. Cleantech spans multiple industries rather than fitting neatly into a single category, 
leading scholars and practitioners to define their own industry classifications (Hoff, 2012).  
For the purposes of this thesis, the industry categories defined by the Cleantech Group and 
Cleantech for Europe (2024) are adopted. These categories include Agriculture & Food, Energy & 
Power, Materials & Chemicals, Resources & Environmental Management, Transportation & 
Logistics, and Waste & Recycling. 
1.2 A Focus on the Energy & Power Industry, the most emitting sector 
 
This section narrows the research scope to the energy and power sector, the most significant 
contributor to global emissions. It defines the research focus on "green electron" enablers which 
play a critical role in achieving decarbonization goals. 
 
An emphasises on green electron enablers 
This thesis focuses on startups within the "Energy and Power" sector of Cleantech’s six categories. 
This focus is critical as the electricity sector is the most emitting, responsible for 15.18 billion 
tonnes of CO2 out of the 47 billion tonnes emitted globally in 2020 (Ritchie et al., 2020). This 
makes it the most polluting sector, followed by transportation (7.29 billion tonnes), manufacturing 
and construction (6.22 billion tonnes), and agriculture (5.87 billion tonnes) (Ritchie et al., 2020). 
To decarbonize this sector and move toward near-zero emissions, further focus is needed on 
==PAGE:15==
 
15 
deploying low-emission fuel sources, such as renewable electricity for heating, advancing CO2 
utilization and storage technologies, and improving carbon capture strategies  (IEA, 2024). 
Despite being the largest contributor to global CO₂ emissions, the energy sector ranked second in 
global climate tech VC funding in 2023, securing 12.5 billion compared to 17.9 billion for the 
mobility sector (PwC, 2023). This disparity underscores the critical need for increased investment 
in the energy sector to accelerate the transition toward sustainable and near-zero-emission 
solutions. 
Thus, to ensure this research focuses on solutions which directly tackle these challenges, within the 
Energy & Power industry, this thesis further narrows its focus to the enablers of "green electron" 
build-out, technologies, systems, and practices that facilitate the generation, distribution, and 
utilization of green electrons (Ritchie et al., 2020). Green electrons are simply electrons produced 
from renewable energy resources, generating no greenhouse gas emissions or harmful 
environmental impact, and supporting the decarbonization of the energy sector (Fortescue, 2025). 
This thesis excludes solutions that produce further non-clean energy, including coal, natural gas or 
petroleum innovations. In this research, nuclear power is considered a clean energy source that 
produces low-carbon electricity and contributes to the production of green electrons (National Grid,

By adopting the Grimes et al. (2012) framework, this study focuses specifically on the subsections

electrons, and energy storage ensures their availability when needed (National Grid, 2023).

sectors like Wind, Solar, Renewable Fuels, Marine, Biomass, Geothermal, Waste-to-Energy,

the same output or producing more with the same energy input, thus reducing waste (EEA, 2024).

reductions needed by 2040. This includes sectors like Smart Grids, Green Buildings, Cogeneration, 
Electronics, Appliances, and Semiconductors, all aimed at optimising energy use and minimising 
inefficiencies in energy systems (Grimes et al., 2012). 
Thus, by narrowing its research to a specific subset of Cleantech, namely “energy startups”, this 
thesis covers solutions tackling renewable energy generation, energy storage and energy efficiency. 
1.3 Energy startup business models and funding stages  
 
This section categorizes energy startups by their business models, hardware, software, and hybrid, 
and stages of development. It focuses on Series A and B funding, emphasizing their critical role in 
overcoming commercialization challenges. 
 
What are energy startups  
A “startup”, in essence, refers to a newly established small business, typically characterised by its 
novelty and high growth potential (Cambridge, 2024). Skawińska & Zalewski (2020) define 
startups as young, small, independent companies driven by creativity and innovation, focused on 
research and development to address real-world challenges. These companies propose forward-
looking solutions supported by a unique business model and high growth potential. What 
differentiates startups from established companies is their novel approach, as they test and evolve 
their market fit iteratively (BPI France, 2024). Specifically, energy startups are innovative 
enterprises focused on developing new technologies and business models within the energy sector.  
==PAGE:17==
 
17 
Energy startup business models  
Energy startups can be categorized based on their business models, broadly divided into hardware, 
software, and hybrid models, following classifications by Bhuiyan et al. (2022). Each business 
model entails unique characteristics and challenges, particularly within the energy sector (see 
Figure 3). 
Hardware-based startups primarily focus on developing tangible products, ranging from consumer 
electronics to complex machinery, materials, and manufacturing processes (Stock & Seliger, 2016). 
These startups face distinct challenges, including high capital requirements, prolonged 
development cycles, and difficulties in scaling production. Particularly within the energy sector, 
hardware startups are characterised by high capital expenditure (CAPEX) demands and extended 
commercialisation timelines (Cleantech for Europe, 2024). This can make them less appealing to 
investors due to longer time-to-exit horizons, 10 to 20 years compared to 5 to 10 years for software 
startups, further increasing uncertainty and limiting their ability to attract funding (Looney et al., 
2024). The concept of time-to-exit horizons refers to the strategic timeframe within which startups 
aim to achieve a successful exit, such as acquisition or initial public offering (IPO) (In & Monk, 
2020). These factors are compounded by slow R&D cycles and supply chain complexities, which 
lengthen the path to market. 
 
Software startups develop innovative solutions focused on software products or services, including 
applications, data analytics tools, and control systems for energy management. According to Berg 
et al. (2018), software startups are vital sources of technological innovation, typically targeting 
rapidly growing markets despite limited resources and high uncertainty. Unlike hardware startups, 
software ventures are often more attractive to investors due to lower capital requirements and faster 
commercialisation cycles. This results in quicker returns on investment, making software models 
a preferred choice for many stakeholders (Full Scale, 2020; Gaddy et al., 2017). However, these 
startups still face challenges, such as operational scaling and navigating competitive markets with 
constrained resources. 
 
Hybrid startups integrate hardware and software to deliver value-added solutions, such as smart 
solar systems, grid analytics platforms, or energy efficiency technologies (Looney et al., 2024; 
Trautwein, 2023). These ventures combine the advantages of both models but also inherit their 
challenges. For example, they require the robust technological infrastructure of hardware startups 
while facing the fast-paced innovation demands typical of software startups. Kurowski (2024) 
identifies hybrid Cleantech startups as particularly constrained by resource mobilization 
challenges, sitting between non-digital (hardware) and fully digital (software) Cleantech startups 
in terms of complexity. 
Across all business models, Cleantech startups face additional barriers compared to conventional 
energy ventures due to their dual objective of environmental impact and profitability. Grid 
integration and interoperability remain significant challenges, as solutions must comply with 
existing energy systems and regulatory frameworks (Brown, 2024). Long lead times for project 
approvals and permitting further compound uncertainties, particularly for hardware and hybrid 
startups, which often experience slow commercialization cycles (Paternoster et al., 2014). Securing 
growth-stage financing is another critical hurdle, as many Cleantech startups struggle to attract the 
funding needed to scale operations and achieve commercialization (Brandenburg & Stedry, 1966). 
These difficulties often align with the Valley of Death, a critical funding gap that startups must 
navigate during the transition from development to market entry (Tim, 2024).

Startups’ stages of development 
 
This section defines different stages in an energy startups’ lifecycle. The development of startups 
typically progresses through several distinct phases through its growth. Understanding these is 
crucial for a common understanding of which stage of development this thesis entails by series 
A&B funding stages.  
 
Some scholars as Brandenburg & Stedry (1966) define stages according to the stage of development 
such as research and development (R&D), prototype development, pilot testing or proof of concept, 
market entry (initial customers acquired), scaling operations (expanding customer base or 
capacity), commercialisation and growth and Exit (Brandenburg & Stedry, 1966; Kazanjian & 
Drazin, 1989; Kumbhat & Sushil, 2018). 
 
However, the division is more generically based on funding rounds startups go through. Although 
these stages provide a structured framework for understanding startup development, it is important 
to recognize that not all startups will follow this linear progression. Variability in market 
conditions, funding availability, and entrepreneurial experience can lead to different pathways and 
outcomes. The key stages of startup development identified in the literature vary between scholars 
but genuinely include the following five stages.  
• 
The initial phase, pre-seed, involves idea generation and market research where 
entrepreneurs focus on validating their business concept and identifying potential 
customers (Sova & Ganotska, 2021).  
• 
Next, the seed stage, where startups begin to develop their product or service. Founders 
often seek initial funding to support product development and market entry strategies 
(Mueller, 2023; Sova & Ganotska, 2021).  
• 
Following lays the startup stage, often seen as Series A and early Series B, where startups 
launch their product to the market, aiming to achieve product-market fit. This phase is 
critical for gathering user feedback and making necessary adjustments (Kumbhat & Sushil, 
2018; Sova & Ganotska, 2021).  
• 
Fourth, is the growth stage where startups experience rapid growth, expanding their 
customer base and scaling operations. This stage often requires additional funding and 
strategic partnerships and can include Serie B, C and D (Kumbhat & Sushil, 2018; Sova & 
Ganotska, 2021).  
• 
Finally, the expansion stage is where startups may explore new markets or product lines, 
focusing on long-term sustainability and profitability (Sova & Ganotska, 2021).  
• 
The final phase is the exit stage involves either selling the startup or going public, allowing 
founders and investors to realize returns on their investments (Sova & Ganotska, 2021).

Europe Investment gap and need for Energy Solutions  
 
Decarbonizing Europe’s energy system is critical for achieving its climate goals and supporting the 
EU’s aim of reaching carbon neutrality by 2050 (European Commission, 2025a). In 2022, Europe 
emitted 3.7 gigatons of CO2. As the world’s third-largest emitter, after the Asia-Pacific and North 
America, Europe plays a key role in global climate efforts (IEA, 2023a).  With more than 75% of 
the EU’s greenhouse gas emissions tied to energy production and consumption (European 
Commission, 2024a) securing financial and regulatory support for Cleantech energy solutions is 
essential to driving decarbonization. The European Investment Bank (2024a) estimates that for the 
EU to meet its 2030 climate targets, climate-related investments, including Cleantech, need to 
increase by approximately €360 billion annually and it needs to reduce emissions by around 440 
million tons annually (IEA, 2023c). 
 
To bridge this investment gap, the EU has introduced various funding mechanisms, driving the 
demand for renewable energy in the EU over the past years, with the European Green Deal (EGD) 
being the central policy initiative. Launched in 2019, the EGD is one of the world’s most ambitious 
decarbonization agenda (IEA, 2023). It aims to achieve climate neutrality by 2050, reduce

Europe Unique Initiatives 
The EU’s Cleantech ecosystem benefits from a diverse set of policies and funds that collectively 
create a supportive environment for startups. This section explores the range of targeted funding

comprehensive strategy to address regional and global challenges while fostering a competitive

As part of the EGD and in response to the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), the EU developed 
the European Green Deal Industrial Plan (GDIP) in 2023. This plan seeks to boost the EU's 
manufacturing capacity for net-zero technologies and products, making it easier for clean 
technologies to enter the market (E3G, 2023). Cleantech is at the core of the GDIP, particularly in 
the Clean Energy sector, which focuses on energy efficiency, the development of renewable 
energy-based power systems, ensuring affordable energy, and creating an integrated and digitized

2. Funding Gaps for Cleantech Energy Startups  
 
2.1 Europe Energy Startup Valley of Death 
 
The Cleantech sector in Europe has notably secured more funding than other industries, with the 
recent boom highlighting the energy sector as a leader in attracting VC investment. In 2023, the 
energy sector raised an impressive $15.4 billion, outpacing health and fintech, which garnered $9.5 
billion and $8 billion, respectively (Dealroom, 2024a). Regarding investment rounds, it ranked 
third with 1,100 rounds, following health (1,300) and enterprise software (1,200) (Dealroom, 
2024a). This robust funding landscape indicates that Cleantech entrepreneurs operate in a 
favourable environment supporting industry growth. 
 
When examining median funding received at various stages, Cleantech companies consistently 
attract higher median amounts, particularly in later growth stages, compared to non-Cleantech 
innovators (European Investment Bank, 2024a). Notably, at least 43 climate tech startups have 
achieved “unicorn” status, with valuations exceeding $1 billion (Kauffman, 2022), making 
Cleantech increasingly appealing to entrepreneurs.  
 
This positive trend persists even amid a slowdown in the broader venture capital market since 2021, 
driven by rising interest rates and tighter liquidity. While overall VC funding across all sectors 
declined by 53% year-over-year, and mobility experienced a sharp 62% drop to $31 billion, clean 
energy startups bucked this trend with a 10% increase in funding in 2022 compared to 2021 (Fritz 
et al., 2024). 
 
Despite the significant expansion of the European Cleantech market, many startups confront the 
formidable challenge of crossing the VoD, the difficult transition from early-stage development to 
successful scaling (Mount, 2021). This phase is particularly perilous for Cleantech startups, which 
often face longer time-to-market, higher capital intensity, and greater technological risks than their 
counterparts in traditional sectors (In & Monk, 2020). Although these new ventures may initially 
attract interest and funding, securing sustained investment to scale remains a critical hurdle. 
Consequently, many Cleantech startups falter not due to a lack of early funding but because they 
struggle to maintain momentum through this crucial phase. 
 
Research indicates that Cleantech startups are more susceptible to the valley of death than those in 
other industries, with nearly half of potential innovations facing obstacles at the demonstration or 
prototype phase, hindering their advancement and scalability (Hammond et al., 2024). Reports from 
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
UK-based organizations like Harwell and Tech Nation suggest that energy tech startups are 200% 
less likely to succeed compared to tech startups (Harwell, 2022). Furthermore, the unique 
challenges posed by new energy technologies often impede promising startups from achieving 
commercial viability (Hartley & Medlock, 2017). 
 
Recently, the Cleantech sector has been grappling with an intensified valley of death as funding 
challenges worsen. Rising interest rates, delays in federal support, and increasing competition from 
booming sectors such as AI, life sciences, and defence tech have made it increasingly difficult for 
Cleantech startups to secure the capital necessary for scaling (Hammond et al., 2024). 
Concurrently, the climate tech sector is witnessing a contraction in available capital. Global 
economic pressures, including the Russia-Ukraine conflict and the collapse of Silicon Valley Bank 
in 2023, have rendered investors more risk-averse, leading to a reallocation of portfolios toward 
safer assets. This shift has particularly impacted climate tech startups in the critical Series A and B 
stages, where obtaining funds for the transition from research and development to 
commercialization has become a formidable challenge (Holy Grail Capital, 2024).

2.3 The Valley of Death in Energy Cleantech: Defining Critical Stages of 
Innovation Development 
This thesis focuses on the critical Series A and B funding stages, building upon an extensive review 
of scholarly literature on the Valley of Death to analyze the challenges faced by energy Cleantech 
startups during commercialization. By synthesizing perspectives on the innovation development 
lifecycle, it identifies the financing gaps most pronounced at these stages. Specifically, the study 
examines scholarly conceptualizations of startup development stages within the Cleantech context 
to define the boundaries of the VoD and pinpoint the phase of startup development where these 
challenges are most acute. 
Scholars’ perspective on the critical phases in the lifecycle of energy Cleantech startups 
Supporting Cleantech startups through the Valley of Death is crucial, as these ventures are essential 
in combating climate change (HEC, 2024). While scholars offer varied perspectives on the VoD, 
most scholars converge on defining it as funding issues that hinder the growth of startups after the 
early stage, particularly toward commercialization (see Table 3). Prior conceptualizations of the 
VoD highlight funding as the central challenge that startups face in this transition (O’Reilly et al., 
2024). 
To understand which specific stage of startup development lies within the VoD for Cleantech, this 
thesis analyses the scholarly conceptualizations of the VoD, particularly within the Cleantech 
context, and draws from this research to define the boundaries of this study. 
Based on scholars’ definitions, interpretations of the Valley of Death vary widely, particularly 
regarding the number of valleys that exist. Scholars such as Osawa & Miyazaki, (2006)

Deployment Valley focuses on large-scale commercialisation, supported by private equity.  
Wang & Yee (2024) articulate this complexity through their framework of four Valleys of Death 
that challenge Cleantech startups during their growth journey. The first Valley arises in the initial 
phase of spinning out technology and forming a startup, where founders grapple with building a 
team and securing seed funding. The second Valley centers on the critical search for product-
market fit, a stage where launching a minimum viable product can prove difficult amidst a complex 
market, often requiring early-stage venture capital (Series A) to navigate successfully. A third 
Valley materializes when startups transition from pilot projects to full-scale operations, a process 
that demands later-stage venture capital (Series B/C) to ensure product stability and profitability. 
The last valley entails achieving widespread market adoption, where startups must compete against 
established industries, necessitating growth equity or project finance to secure their position.

2.4 Europe’s Increased Challenge of Crossing the VoD  
This section explores how Europe’s unique structural and financial challenges intensify the 
difficulties Cleantech startups face in navigating the Valley of Death. It examines regional 
disparities in funding availability and highlights how these challenges create significant barriers to 
commercialization and scaling. 
European Cleantech startups encounter considerable funding challenges, particularly during the 
critical development stages, of the VoD, where financing gaps are most pronounced. Despite 
Europe’s substantial role as a global leader in sustainable innovation, holding approximately 27% 
of the world’s clean technology patents (European Investment Bank, 2024a), Cleantech 
entrepreneurs often struggle to secure the capital necessary for growth and commercialization. 
While Cleantech startups in the United States can typically draw on a blend of equity and debt 
financing, EU-based Cleantech firms, tend to be more debt-reliant, which can constrain their ability 
to scale effectively and limits their financial flexibility (Dealroom, 2024b; Halder, 2024). Europe’s 
Cleantech venture capital funding share was only 21.4% of the global total in 2023, compared to 
North America’s 37.13% and Asia Pacific’s 30.93% (Cleantech Group, 2024). These specific 
regional comparisons underscore the global disparity more starkly and indicate how both North 
America and Asia Pacific outpace Europe in funding (Cleantech Group, 2024). 
This funding bottleneck is particularly stark in Europe’s seed and Series A and B stages, where 
investment amounts are significantly smaller and grow more slowly than in the U.S., exacerbating 
the Valley of Death (European Investment Bank, 2024a). U.S. Cleantech sector seeing quicker 
increases in funding at different stages, which further clarifies the structural disadvantage for 
European startups. As a result, European Cleantech startups struggle to transition from concept to 
commercialization. For instance, in 2023, North America attracted $19.2 billion in Cleantech VC 
funding, while Europe lagged with approximately €11 billion, a substantial gap that underscores 
regional funding disparities at crucial growth stages (Cleantech Group, 2024). Furthermore, over 
30% of EU micro and small Cleantech companies report financial barriers as a critical obstacle to 
scaling their innovations, in contrast to just 12% of large companies, highlighting how smaller 
ventures are disproportionately affected by the financing shortfall (European Investment Bank 
Investment Survey, 2023). 
Furthermore, the EU Cleantech sector's funding insufficiency is compounded by the considerable 
investment gap needed to meet Europe’s ambitious climate and energy goals. While Cleantech 
group (2024) estimates at €50 billion the funding gap, Projections from the European Commission 
indicate that an additional €92 billion will be necessary between 2023 and 2030 to scale essential 
clean technologies, of which only €16-18 billion is expected to come from public funds. This leaves 
a substantial shortfall to be bridged by private investments, which are currently falling behind 
targets (Cleantech for Europe, 2023; European Investment Bank, 2023). With private investment 
struggling to meet this shortfall, Europe’s Cleantech ecosystem faces significant structural 
challenges, including limited VC market maturity, regulatory inconsistencies, and fewer high-
growth funding opportunities (European Investment Bank, 2024). These conditions collectively 
place European Cleantech startups at a disadvantage compared to their global peers, underscoring 
a uniquely challenging Valley of Death for EU innovators. 
2.5 A Focus on Micro-enablers Compared to Macro-levers Change 
This section highlights the thesis focus on micro-level enablers, particularly the relationship 
between investors and entrepreneurs, as key drivers for overcoming the Valley of Death. It 
emphasizes the focus on actionable strategies within the scope of startups and investors, rather than 
broader systemic factors, aligning with the thesis’s practical objectives. 
==PAGE:32==
 
32 
The decision to focus on micro-enablers stems from several key considerations. First, it 
complements existing literature, which often addresses a mix of internal and external levers from 
various actors, such as public authorities and service providers, by narrowing the lens to the specific 
relationship between investors and entrepreneurs (Michelfelder et al., 2022). Second, the practical 
constraints of a master’s thesis necessitate actionable recommendations that can yield immediate 
impact. By targeting venture-level dynamics rather than broader systemic actors like governments, 
this research offers insights directly applicable to startups. Third, empirical evidence indicates that 
external factors, while significant, have a limited and often indirect impact on startup growth 
(Horne & Fichter, 2022). Their research emphasizes the role of internal factors as primary drivers 
of sustainable growth. 
This approach draws on Horne & Fichter (2022) framework, which distinguishes two perspectives 
for understanding enablers of sustainable startup growth: the resource-based view (RBV), focusing 
on internal resources and capabilities, and context theories, which examines external factors such 
as regulatory environments and market forces. While external enablers, such as government 
policies or economic trends, are undeniably relevant, their influence is often diffuse and less 
actionable at the scale of individual ventures. Moreover, these externalities often fall outside the 
control of startups and require systemic change, which is beyond the scope of this research 
(Davidsson, 2015). 
By adopting the RBV, this study emphasizes the pivotal role of startups’ internal resources and 
dynamic capabilities in overcoming the VoD. The RBV posits that sustainable competitive 
advantage arises from firm-level resources (Bjornali & Ellingsen, 2014). This perspective is 
particularly pertinent for Cleantech startups, where bridging the commercialization gap associated 
with the VoD requires leveraging internal strengths, such as strategic partnerships and resources 
internal enablers as investors (Horne & Fichter, 2022). 
In this context, the thesis prioritizes one key internal actor: investors. Venture capital and corporate 
venture capital investors are instrumental not only in providing funding but also in shaping startups’ 
growth trajectories through strategic guidance and access to critical networks. While other internal 
enablers, such as team dynamics, organizational vision, and market positioning, are undoubtedly 
important, this research focuses on the investor-startup relationship as the central mechanism for 
addressing funding gaps. 
Through this micro-level lens, the study highlights how entrepreneurs can actively enhance their 
internal resources and capabilities to navigate the VoD effectively. By aligning with investors' 
expectations and leveraging their guidance, Cleantech startups can better position themselves for 
growth and scalability during critical Series A and B funding stages.

3.1 Venture Capital Scope and Funding Stages Aligned to Cleantech VoD 
  
This section outlines the structure and lifecycle of venture capital investments, focusing on their 
alignment with Cleantech startups’ funding needs. It provides a detailed overview of how VCs 
operate and support startups through early-stage commercialization (see Table 4). 
 
Venture capital is a vital form of private equity investment that finances high-growth startups in 
exchange for equity (Ganti, 2024). Typically, VC funds are structured as 10-year limited 
partnerships where outside investors, limited partners (LPs) contribute capital managed by general 
partners (GPs) (Cumming et al., 2022; Sahlman & Gorman, 1989). These funds target high-risk, 
high-reward opportunities, investing in around ten to twenty firms within the first five years of the 
fund’s existence (Marcus, Malen & Ellis, 2013). Beyond funding, VC firms provide strategic 
guidance, access to networks, and operational support, helping startups shape critical areas like 
growth strategy, management, and financial structuring (Macmillan et al., 1989). The VC 
investment process often follows a staged approach, allowing investors to periodically evaluate 
progress and adjust funding allocations as necessary. 
VC funds often target companies at specific growth stages, with some specialising in early, mid, or 
late-stage investments (Gompers et al., 2020). Scholars vary in their classification of these funding 
stages: Gompers (1995), for instance, identifies four key stages, with Dahiya & Ray (2012) further 
designating “Seed Stage” as “First Stage” financing, the initial external capital provided to 
entrepreneurs launching their ventures. “Early Stage” financing typically follows, representing 
“Second Stage” rounds for more developed firms. Some frameworks extend to include 
“Expansion” and “Late Stage” financing for firms with established market strategies and products. 
The IMF, for instance, defines five key lifecycle funding stages, each with distinct risk and growth 
potentials (Arnold et al., 2024). Pre-seed funding, characterized by the highest risk, often finances 
market research, business planning, and early team formation. In Europe, pre-seed rounds range 
from €10,000 to several million, with an average investment between €100,000 and €300,000 
(PitchBook (a), 2024). Seed-stage financing, crucial for building a scalable model and initial market 
presence, ranges from under €100,000 to over €5 million, with VC firms often securing 15–40 
percent equity (Pitchbook (b), 2024). Following this, early-stage (Series A and B) investments, 
targeting companies within their first five years support product development, market expansion, 
and team growth, with financing ranging from a few million to tens of millions of euros (Arnold et 
al., 2024). In later stages, for more mature startups (over five years old) aiming to scale, Series C 
and D funding rounds provide capital for expansion, often with contributions from institutional 
investors and banks, with investments ranging from millions to hundreds of millions of euros (see 
Figure 10).

3.2 VCs challenges posed by Cleantech Energy funding 
This section analyses the specific challenges venture capitalists face when investing in Cleantech 
startups, including long development timelines, high capital intensity, and uncertain exit strategies. 
Understanding these challenges is critical for aligning startup strategies with investor expectations 
(see Table 5). 
Early-stage Cleantech startups face significant barriers to attracting venture capital investment. 
Firstly, some general constraints of VC financing arise from the narrow set of innovations that meet 
institutional VC requirements; with only a limited pool of investors capable of directing substantial 
capital toward radical technological shifts; and, more recently, a relaxation in VC focus on 
corporate governance, which affects longer-term guidance (Lerner & Nanda, 2020). 
Secondly, attracting VC funding in Cleantech is challenging due to sector-specific hurdles. Demand 
sensitivity plays a critical role which means that changes in demand for an energy product or service 
vary significantly when its price is changed by a small amount (The Economic Times, 2024). 
Additionally, Cleantech startups, particularly those in energy supply like solar and wind, tend to 
struggle in producing the high returns typical of tech-focused ventures, as they face high 
competition and lower product differentiation. Although some energy efficiency technologies may 
yield substantial returns, they are generally lower than returns seen in information and 
communications technology (ICT) (van den Heuvel & Popp, 2023). Furthermore, VCs typically 
favour sectors with shorter cycle times and low capital intensity; Cleantech startups often require 
high capital and lengthy development timelines, making it difficult to align with VC preferences 
for faster and more flexible exit strategies (Bose et al., 2019; Upadhyayula et al., 2018). Even 
within non-capital intensive digital Cleantech, profitability and funding have shown volatility, 
highlighting the broad challenges Cleantech faces in securing VC support. 
==PAGE:36==
 
36 
Cleantech’s commodity nature further limits consumer-driven demand premiums; many consumers 
are unwilling to pay more for renewable energy, restricting the potential for high returns and thus 
reducing VC appeal (Van den Heuvel & Popp, 2023). Additionally, VCs share concerns with 
entrepreneurs about overcoming regulatory, market, and technological uncertainties (Susteras &  
Brito, 2023). Institutional challenges, such as government-imposed regulations, low customer 
clarity on the value of sustainable products, and the complex business landscape, present barriers 
specific to Cleantech startups (Lenox & York, 2013; Sousa-Zomer & Cauchick Miguel, 2018; 
Vernay et al., 2020). This leads to VC adjusting their investment behaviours based on anticipated 
regulatory and policy support, making clear policy paths essential for VC confidence (van den 
Heuvel & Popp, 2023).  
Furthermore, the studies by Cumming et al. (2016) and Hegeman & Sørheim (2021) highlight 
specific risks and challenges. Financially, the long investment horizons, scalability uncertainties, 
and high capital intensity increase the risk profile, while market challenges stem from the inherent 
difficulties of operating on the production side of the economy and navigating inconsistent 
governmental regulations. Additionally, while Cleantech provides significant societal benefits, 
these are difficult to capture privately, leading to challenges in attracting investors.  
Finally, Cleantech suffers from a lack of clear exit strategies for VCs. Unlike the biotech sector, 
where incumbents often acquire start-ups as complementary assets, energy companies are less

traditional

Financial, Regulatory and Policy Drivers for VC investment in energy startups 
Despite these challenges, several incentives drive VCs toward Cleantech investment. The push for 
sustainable funding in venture capital has grown significantly, largely influenced by regulatory

frameworks like the European Union’s Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR), which 
enforces transparency in environmental, social, and governance (ESG) practices within investment 
firms. This section highlights and explains these key drivers, which are regulatory and financial 
pushes for investing in energy startups.    
Within the Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR) a major driver is compliance with 
the EU’s SFDR Articles 8 and 9, which guide financial products toward sustainable and responsible 
investment standards. Introduced in March 2021, the SFDR categorizes investment products into 
Article 6, which includes funds without a specific ESG focus, Article 8 for “light green” funds that 
incorporate some sustainable characteristics, and Article 9 for “dark green” funds primarily focused 
on sustainability (Scale X, 2024). This regulation is part of the EU’s Sustainable Finance Action 
Plan, supporting the EU Green Deal objectives (EY, 2024; Scale X, 2024). Compliance with Article 
8 and Article 9 requirements, particularly for Cleantech investments, incentivizes VCs to integrate 
detailed ESG criteria into their processes.  
More specifically, Article 8 requires transparent reporting on principal adverse impacts, such as 
greenhouse gas emissions and energy consumption. VC firms are required to demonstrate that their 
investment products favour environmental and/or social characteristics. Article 8 funds promote 
sustainable characteristics by taking ESG criteria into account as part of the investment process, 
but without pursuing a sustainable investment objective (Scale X, 2024).  For Cleantech sectors, 
this aligns with specific metrics including GHG intensity, fossil fuel exposure, and renewable 
energy production, all of which are significant in assessing the sustainability impact of investments 
(Quaero Capital, 2023). 
Under Article 9, funds must adopt sustainability as their main objective, focusing on direct 
environmental and social impacts such as carbon footprint reduction. As of 2024, nearly a thousand 
funds were compliant showing that sustainability was an “objective” through evaluating the fund 
itself instead of the process (Scale X, 2024). To meet these standards, VCs are required to report 
under the Regulatory Technical Standards (RTS), which mandate comprehensive documentation 
of their sustainable contributions (Roure, 2024). 
Moreover, VC firms are motivated by alignment with global goals, such as the United Nations 
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly SDG 7 on affordable and clean energy. 
Investments in renewable energy technologies, including wind, solar, and hydro projects, appeal to 
investors aiming to support sustainability-focused objectives (Quaero Capital, 2023). Alignment 
with international standards, such as the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises and UN 
Principles on Business and Human Rights, further strengthens credibility in sustainable investing. 
Through pre-investment ESG analysis grids and rigorous reviews of governance and compliance, 
VCs ensure their portfolios meet these criteria, fulfilling both regulatory demands and growing 
investor expectations in sustainable finance. 
This drive translates in growing interest for positive impact investing. VCs and LPs are motivated 
by the desire to make a positive environmental impact in their investment decisions. A report from 
European Institute of Innovation and Technology (EIT) (2024) shows that 88% of VCs use an 
impact investing framework or tool to guide their investment decisions. Moreover a third 
considered reducing carbon footprint a top priority when choosing investments and more than half 
identified advancing climate change initiatives as a top priority for the next 12 months (EIT Urban 
Mobility, 2024).  
A key initiatives is the EU Taxonomy, a classification system establishing a list of environmentally 
sustainable economic activities to facilitate sustainable investment (European Commission, 2024c). 
The EU Taxonomy is a regulatory framework established to guide investments towards sustainable 
economic activities, aligning with the goals of the European Green Deal. It serves as a classification 
==PAGE:38==
 
38 
system that defines criteria for what constitutes environmentally sustainable activities, aiming to 
facilitate a transition to a low-carbon economy by 2050. This framework is crucial for investors, 
particularly those involved in impact investing, as it provides a common language and standards 
for assessing the sustainability of investments (European Commission, 2024b). The taxonomy 
identifies economic activities that contribute to six environmental objectives: Climate change 
mitigation, Climate change adaptation, sustainable use and protection of water and marine 
resources, transition to a circular economy, pollution prevention and control and protection and 
restoration of biodiversity and ecosystems (Doyle, 2024). 
Furthermore, a study from the European Investment fund of 2020 investigated the application of 
ESG considerations in the areas of VCs. The study confirmed the integration of ESG criteria in 
decision making and VCs apply ESG screening particularly on an exclusionary basis at due 
diligence. Through their study, the European investment fund investigated ESG investment drivers 
for VC, which include positive reputational signal and growing pressure from Limited Partners 
(Botsari & Lang, 2020; EIF, 2020)). The study reveals that 75% of respondents consider ethical or 
social responsibility factors in their investment decisions. Around 45% aim to drive change toward 
responsible business practices as part of their investment policies. Additionally, 39% view it as a 
positive reputational signal, while 39% cite growing demand from LPs and stakeholders. Lastly, 
36% prioritize risk management, 32% believe ESG criteria are crucial for investment performance, 
and regulations or specific initiatives influence decision-making. (e.g., UN PRI).  
Finally, VC investments in Cleantech are further driven by the level of environmental taxes and 
market pull mechanisms as feed-in tariffs and R&D subsidies (Bianchini & Croce, 2022). Criscuolo 
& Menon, (2015) highlight that market-pull mechanisms are effective tools for encouraging VC 
investments due to their long-term outlook and capacity to stabilize revenue streams. In contrast, 
short-term fiscal policies are less effective in achieving this goal. In addition, the effectiveness of 
VC investments in Cleantech is also influenced by the stringency of environmental policies. 
Research suggests that moderate levels of environmental tax stringency encourage private VC 
investments by mitigating risks and stabilizing returns, while excessively stringent policies may 
deter investment due to heightened costs and uncertainties (Cappellari & Gucciardi, 2024). In their 
paper, Bianchini & Croce (2022) further highlight that different types of policy instruments 
promote different types of environmental innovations. Incremental innovations are primarily 
encouraged by general economic instruments. Improvements based on modular innovation are 
enforced by general regulatory instruments, and technology-specific instruments are needed to 
support the development and deployment of radically new technologies. Thus, each policy 
instruments face unique challenges and implications for VCs.

Understanding the VC-LP Dynamic, a Push for VC Investment Decisions 
Moreover, it's important to understand the underlying motivations behind VC investments in 
Cleantech. VCs are profit-driven investors acting as intermediaries between Limited Partners 
(LPs), such as pension funds, endowments, and family offices, and entrepreneurs. In exchange for 
their capital, LPs receive a share of the profits generated by the VC’s investments (Paradis, 2023).

In the Cleantech energy sector, LPs are increasingly drawn to the high market growth potential. As

market is expected to reach $600 billion by 2030, presenting a significant opportunity for LPs and 
VCs to tap into growing sectors (Sable, 2024). Beyond profitability, LPs are also influenced by 
European regulations. For instance, fund managers in Sweden, representing 65% of invested 
capital, have committed to setting climate targets aligned with the Paris Agreement (AP6, 2022). 
Many LPs seek guidance on compliance with the EU climate benchmark regulations, the Corporate 
Sustainability Due Diligence Directive, expected by 2025 to 2026, and green investment 
frameworks like the EU Sustainable Finance Taxonomy. 
Additionally, LPs are increasingly implementing decarbonization strategies. Recently LPs are 
favouring climate VC funds in energy transition (Bradbury, 2024). According to Pitchbook (2024), 
institutional investors are increasingly focusing on specialist VCs for exposure to next-generation 
climate tech. In the first half of 2024, these investment funds attracted 5% of LPs capital and 
constituted over 3% of all VC funds (Bradbury, 2024). They engage with General Partners (GPs) 
annually to track progress, ensuring a certain percentage of assets under management (AUM) is 
invested in funds aligned with net-zero targets by 2050 (Ilpa, 2023). This includes tracking Scope

mindful of these requirements from LPs and align their investments accordingly. 
Moreover, certifications such as Article 9 compliance under the Sustainable Finance Disclosure

This chapter integrates corporate venture capital (CVC) research inputs as a complementary 
funding source for Cleantech startups. It discusses the unique advantages CVCs offer, such as 
industry expertise and legitimacy, in supporting startups through commercialization. 
 
4.1 General drivers for CVCs investment in energy startups 
 
This section outlines the motivations behind CVC investments in Cleantech, including strategic 
alignment with corporate goals and regulatory incentives. It provides insights into how CVCs can

==PAGE:41==

bridge funding gaps in the energy sector. It further extends the discussion of VCs by integrating 
the perspective of Corporate Venture Capitalists, the second focal investment unit in this thesis. 
 
CVCs can be simply defined as the minority equity investments by established corporations in 
privately held entrepreneurial ventures (Souitaris & Zerbinati, 2014). As highlighted earlier, CVCs 
play a critical role in conferring legitimacy to energy startups, signalling their disruptive potential 
and ability to attract further investment from other stakeholders ( de Lange & Valliere, 2020). The 
backing of a CVC often indicates the startup's potential for a lucrative exit, particularly for the 
corporate investor itself, thereby driving confidence and investment from additional parties ( de 
Lange & Valliere, 2020). 
 
The characteristics of Cleantech startups can present a disadvantageous financial return profile for 
traditional investors due to factors as  high capital intensity, unpredictable cash flows or regulatory 
risks (Harroch, 2018). Hence, motivations for investing in energy startups frequently extend 
beyond pure financial returns. CVCs exhibit more diverse incentives compared to conventional 
venture capital. While financial gain remains a factor, other motivations include gaining insights 
into new technologies, pursuing green opportunities, enhancing legitimacy, and leveraging the 
startup as a supplier for corporate operations (Hegeman & Sørheim, 2021). 
 
 
Firstly, some CVCs are driven by the potential for financial gain. De Lange & Valliere (2020) states 
that approximately 40 to 50 percent of the time, CVCs invest passively or purely for financial gain 
in the start-up's success (Anokhin et al., 2016; D. de Lange & Valliere, 2020). However, research 
indicates that firms achieve greater value from CVC investments when strategic objectives 
accompany financial motives, rather than focusing solely on returns (Dushnitsky & Lenox, 2006).  
 
Secondly, CVCs often invest to explore emerging technologies and industries. This strategic 
investment provides corporates with valuable information about cutting-edge innovation and 
potential market disruptions (Rossi et al., 2020). Next, Investments in Cleantech are sometimes 
part of a corporation's green responsiveness. Such actions reflect the company’s commitment to 
sustainability and alignment with environmental goals (Bocken, 2015). Fourthly, CVC-backed 
investments in Cleantech ventures confer legitimacy to both the startup and the corporate investor. 
CVC activity tends to be more prevalent in industries where sustainability and innovation are 
emphasized, helping corporates establish themselves as active players in such domains ( de Lange 
& Valliere, 2020). Finally, in some cases, CVC investments aim to make startups a suppliers, 
linking the financial returns on their investment to improved corporate operations  (Hegeman & 
Sørheim, 2021). 
 
While CVC investments offer numerous advantages, including access to complementary assets and 
privileged information about emerging technologies, they also come with potential drawbacks. 
These include misaligned incentives, internal conflicts, and incompatible objectives between the 
corporate investor and the startup (Dushnitsky & Lenox, 2006). Nevertheless, the flexibility and 
lower risk associated with CVC investments can make them uniquely suited to address the 
challenges of Cleantech startups ( de Lange & Valliere, 2020). Hegeman & Sørheim (2021) further 
highlight the heterogeneity of corporate investors in the Cleantech industry, showing that 
motivations differ significantly between government-owned and privately owned CVCs. Despite 
these nuances, CVCs continue to be pivotal actors in funding Cleantech energy startups, as 
demonstrated by their robust $10 billion investment in climate tech startups in 2022 alone 
(Maxwell, 2023).

4.2 Understanding CVC’s Regulatory Push for Cleantech Investment 
 
This section focuses on the regulatory drivers influencing CVC investments in Cleantech, such as 
compliance with sustainability directives. It highlights how these regulations shape investment 
strategies and create opportunities for startups. 
 
Stringent environmental policies have emerged as a significant driver of CVC investments in 
Cleantech, shaping the strategic objectives of corporations and their venture capital branches. 
Institutional and governmental venture capital actors respond differently to regulatory frameworks, 
with CVCs demonstrating a strong alignment to regulatory and policy-driven initiatives (Bianchini 
& Croce, 2022). This alignment underscores the importance of a supportive regulatory environment 
in fostering Cleantech innovation and highlights regulatory as an incentive for CVC investments 
in the sector. 
 
At the corporate level, policies increasingly compel organizations to integrate sustainability 
standards into their operational and investment strategies. In Europe, large corporations are 
required to publish regular reports detailing the environmental and social risks they face, as well as 
the impacts of their activities on people and the environment (European Union, 2021). These 
requirements were initially introduced under the Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD), 
which mandated disclosures on key environmental, social, and governance (ESG) metrics. 
Companies are required to track and report on specific frameworks, such as the OECD Guidelines 
for Multinational Enterprises, ISO 26000 for Social Responsibility, or the European Commission’s 
ESG guidelines (Branquart, 2024). 
 
However, the NFRD has now been superseded by the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive 
(CSRD), which came into force for the 2024 financial year, with reporting requirements applicable 
to 2025 onwards. The CSRD, one of the European Green Deal directives, strengthens previous 
regulations by making double materiality reporting mandatory, requiring companies to assess not 
only the impact of environmental factors on their operations but also their own impact on the 
environment (Branquart, 2024; European Commission, 2024b). This new directive demands a more 
rigorous and transparent approach, pushing for contextualization, companies must include risk 
associated with their emissions (Brans et al., 2024).  It challenges corporations to move beyond 
compliance, encouraging them to proactively integrate sustainability into their business models. 
From a resource-based perspective, CVC activities are increasingly linked to corporate social 
responsibility (CSR) initiatives. Battisti et al. (2022) illustrate how CVC investments contribute to 
a sustainable competitive advantage by bolstering environmental and social performance, building 
reputational capital, and extending beyond purely financial returns.  
 
As Ainhoa Segurola et al. (2024) note, the CSRD offers an opportunity for startups to attract CVC 
investment. These requirements can be turned into a competitive advantage, leveraging sustainable 
practices to enhance market reputation and investor appeal. A survey by Workiva supports this 
perspective, showing that 92% of investors consider ESG data critical for evaluating a company’s 
long-term financial health, with 88% advocating for ESG reporting to be treated with the same 
rigour as financial disclosures  (Dwillis, 2024). 
 
CVCs are uniquely positioned to align with these regulatory demands, leveraging sustainability 
reporting to drive strategic value. Robust sustainability disclosures have been shown to enhance 
market valuation and reduce the cost of capital, thereby attracting investor interest and intensifying 
corporate efforts toward sustainability (Fermann, 2024). Additionally, third-party assurance of 
ESG data is becoming a critical factor for investor confidence, with 88% of investors more likely 
to fund companies that secure such assurances (Dwillis, 2024). Findings are summarised in Figure

5. Navigating Investment Challenges: Aligning Metrics, Strategies, 
and Expectations to Investors 
Cleantech entrepreneurs face unique challenges in securing funding to overcome the Valley of 
Death. Venture capital and corporate venture capital investments are essential for overcoming this 
funding gap. This section explores how aligning with the decision-making criteria of venture 
capitalists and corporate venture capitalists can enhance investment appeal. By focusing on key 
evaluation metrics, integrating EPIs, and addressing due diligence requirements, startups can better 
position themselves for success in this competitive and evolving sector. 
5.1 Aligning Entrepreneurs with Investor Priorities: Bridging the Valley of 
Death in Cleantech Ventures  
 
This section focuses on the decision-making processes of investors, with particular emphasis on 
the critical role of alignment between entrepreneurs and investors during deal evaluation, which is 
essential for successful funding. To effectively bridge the VoD entrepreneurs must align closely 
with the expectations of VC and CVC firms. This alignment should focus on demonstrating 
scalable growth, highlighting clear market potential, and providing metrics that substantiate the 
ability to achieve these goals (Farshchi, 2018). 
 
==PAGE:44==
 
44 
Understanding VCs Decision-Making for Cleantech Energy Investment 
 
The VC model operates on the premise of exiting investments within a relatively short time frame, 
typically 5 to 10 years (Auris, 2024). Thus, from the outset, VCs prioritize investments that 
demonstrate strong exit potential and returns. While VCs need to balance supporting sustainable 
ventures with taking calculated risks, they increasingly prioritize startups with revenue models that 
reflect a “big vision”, focusing on revenue from large, sustainable markets with high customer 
retention and growth potential (Farshchi, 2018). 
Cleantech VC investments require a measurable financial return apart from a positive 
environmental impact, underscoring the need for startups to demonstrate tangible ecological and 
financial benefits (Bourbon, 2024). Key metrics that VCs look for include for instance decreasing 
customer acquisition costs (CAC), increasing revenue per user, and demonstrating efficiency 
improvements as the business scales. By presenting clear, concise metrics that underscore 
scalability and market fit, startups become more attractive to VCs, thereby enhancing their chances 
of securing essential growth capital (Farshchi, 2018).  
However, Cleantech startups face unique challenges due to the hybrid nature of their business 
models, which blend radical innovation with long-term environmental goals. This often leads to 
information asymmetry (IS), making it challenging for investors to fully understand and assess the 
risks involved (Gaddy et al., 2017). Entrepreneurs must communicate a convincing value 
proposition through well-crafted pitch proposals (Mason & Kwok, 2010; Harrison et al., 2020). For 
instance, disclosing intellectual property assets, such as patents, can help reduce information 
asymmetry by signalling the startup’s innovation quality (European Investment Bank, 2024a). 
CVCs' Decision-Making in Cleantech Energy Investments 
Corporate venture capitalists (CVCs) adopt a multifaceted approach to investment decision-
making, which contrasts with the relatively standardized practices observed among traditional 
venture capitalists (Hegeman & Sørheim, 2021). Unlike venture capital firms, CVCs often rely on 
diverse evaluation frameworks tailored to the strategic goals of their parent companies. 
Scholars have identified key decision-making criteria used by CVCs. For instance, Weilinghoff, 
(2018) highlights a multicriteria approach involving eight overarching dimensions, characterized 
by thirty specific attributes. These attributes include elements such as the startup’s go-to-market 
strategy, strategic alignment with the parent company, identification of unmet market needs, and a 
clear roadmap for developmental milestones. Other scholars indicate some CVCs emphasize 
thorough risk assessment as part of their due diligence process. Ferreira & Macedo de Melo (2022) 
underline the importance of evaluating integrity risk, this process typically involves deploying 
detailed questionnaires and mining publicly available data to classify and mitigate potential 
financial and reputational risks, ensuring the sustainability of the investment. 
Moreover, Parent company influence shapes CVC decision-making, with a tendency to prioritize 
alignment with the parent firm’s core business and long-term strategic objectives (de Lange, 2019). 
Unlike venture capitalists, who often rely on external referrals or networking for deal sourcing, 
CVCs frequently receive referrals directly from their internal business units, reducing the need for 
extensive external due diligence (Souitaris & Zerbinati, 2014). Additionally, CVCs often conduct 
technical due diligence in-house, leveraging their industry-specific expertise, whereas VCs may 
outsource such assessments (Souitaris & Zerbinati, 2014).  
 
This nuanced decision-making approach reflects the broader role of CVCs in driving innovation 
and strategic growth within the Cleantech sector, emphasizing both financial returns and alignment 
with long-term corporate objectives. 
==PAGE:45==
 
45 
 
Understanding the role of Green Metrics in bridging the VoD  
Investors are increasingly integrating “green” objectives into their investment strategies (Yan et 
al., 2021; Roundy, 2017). However, while VCs incorporate Environmental Performance Indicators 
(EPIs), there remains a gap between Cleantech goals and the market-driven metrics favored by 
VCs. This misalignment is compounded by Cleantech’s unique challenges as an industry with high 
capital requirements, long commercialization timelines, and dependence on regulatory support. 
Unlike fintech or biotech, Cleantech’s dual objectives of financial return and societal 
transformation further complicate its investment case (Bose et al., 2019). Research by Harrer & 
Owen (2022) indicates that VCs focus on impact metrics tied to market outcomes rather than long-
term behavioral change, which Cleantech ventures aim to achieve. This misalignment underscores 
the need for Cleantech entrepreneurs to understand VC expectations and present relevant indicators 
that resonate with their investment criteria. Likewise, CVC research indicates that emission 
reductions give parent firms a financial advantage over time and that there is a financial interest for 
corporate investors to drive green innovation (Benkraiem et al., 2023). CVC investors type, green 
vs. nongreen CVC, further impacts the selection and nurturing effects of investments in Cleantech 
ventures (Wunder & Maula, 2024). CVCs conduct due diligence on environmental aspects at 
different stages, for example, as part of their ESG assessment or in separate questionnaires. For 
instance, some of the investors interviewed emphasized the role of evaluating the startups’ ESG 
spectrum in their preterm sheet.  
Therefore, this research highlights the importance of entrepreneurs’ understanding of VCs and 
CVCs prioritisation and their focus on specific metrics. By aligning with these expectations, 
Cleantech startups can enhance their investment appeal and better navigate the VoD. As Kroner 
(2024) suggests, impact, regardless of the specific KPIs used, should be considered a core non-
financial metric that complements financial returns. This underlines investors’ need to focus on 
scalable business models that deliver both high impact and high return potential. 
A Focus on Deal Evaluation in the Investment Process 
In exploring how entrepreneurs can better align with both CVC and VC stakeholders, this research 
emphasizes the deal evaluation phase of the investment process (see Figure 13). Deal-making in 
venture capital is a multi-stage process, with various scholars outlining distinct phases. Tyebjee & 
Bruno (1984) identify five sequential steps: Deal Origination, Deal Screening, Deal Evaluation, 
Deal Structuring, and Post-Investment Activities, offering a comprehensive framework for 
understanding venture capital investment. Similarly, Klonowski (2022) highlights comparable 
stages, including deal generation, screening, evaluation, financial contracting, monitoring, and 
exiting.

The deal evaluation phase is critical and aims at determining the value of a startup to decide if it's 
a good investment. It involves assessing the perceived risks and expected returns of potential 
investments. During this stage, entrepreneurs present their business proposals to secure funding 
and access networks that can drive their commercial and financial goals (Petty & Gruber, 2011). 
This phase requires identifying optimal deal opportunities by evaluating a variety of factors such 
as the management team's capabilities, market attractiveness, product or service differentiation, and

conducting thorough due diligence to determine the company's valuation (Sakovska, 2022). These

unique methodology (Walton, 2024). Notably, the criteria influencing decision-making during this 
phase vary based on the startup's investment stage.  
This thesis focuses solely on the deal evaluation phase for its research because it is the critical

methodologies stakeholders use to evaluate Cleantech energy startups, offering insights into how

5.2 Key Evaluation Criteria in Cleantech Series A and B Due Diligence 
This section examines the criteria investors use to evaluate Cleantech startups during Series A and 
B funding. It provides a detailed analysis of risk assessment, metrics, and strategies critical for 
securing investment. 
Venture capitalists and Corporate venture capitalists conduct rigorous due diligence on Cleantech 
startups at Series A and B funding stages, where the primary focus is on concrete proof of viability, 
scalability, and potential returns (Gouedard, 2024). As mentioned in section 3, Cleantech 
investments, especially within energy, entail heightened risks and capital demands, making 
thorough due diligence essential. Due diligence processes combines both quantitative and 
qualitative assessment methods, utilizing Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) to evaluate the risk, 
value, and growth potential of each venture. 
Challenges in Valuation and Benchmarking 
 
Standard valuation methods are often lacking for early-stage Cleantech investments. Yang & Sollén 
(2013) note that VCs typically rely on benchmarks and custom valuation methods tailored to the 
startup’s context, reserving historical data analysis for later growth phases. This valuation 
flexibility reflects the complexities of early-stage Cleantech, where developing a fair market value 
can be resource-intensive. Harrer & Owen (2022) further highlight that diverse stakeholders in 
Cleantech prioritize different aspects of the venture’s environmental performance, leading to 
variations in EPIs that emphasize different value components without standardized metrics for 
alignment with environmental goals. 
 
Moreover, much of VC decision-making in Cleantech extends beyond formal analysis, involving 
a combination of intuition and “gut feel.”. Research highlights the importance of intuition in the 
VC process, where many decisions hinge on subjective assessments rather than rigid metrics alone 
(Hisrich & Jankowicz, 1990; Zacharakis & Shepherd, 2016). Gut feeling plays a significant role in 
investment decisions for venture capitalists, as intuition is often viewed as a natural component of 
executive thinking (Huang & Pearce, 2015). Many VCs rely on their intuitive judgments, which 
are shaped by their expertise and emotional experiences, to assess business viability data and 
perceptions of entrepreneurs, often prioritizing intuition over formal analysis (Sadler-Smith & 
Shefy, 2004).  
 
Finally, VC firm characteristics, including location, asset size, and team diversity, also influence 
investment criteria and impact-focused decision-making (EIF, 2020). For early-stage investments, 
this blend of intuition and structured analysis enables VCs to identify potential high-return ventures 
while managing inherent uncertainties (Huang & Pearce, 2015). 
==PAGE:47==
 
47 
Risk Assessment and Mitigation Strategies 
Cleantech investments further involve specific risk categories, market, technology, people, 
regulatory, and exit risks, that impact VC assessments (Wustenhagen & Teppo, 2006). Michelfelder 
et al. (2022) extend these categories to include time-to-exit risk, which relates to Cleantech’s longer 
pathways to commercialization and capital availability (Migendt et al., 2017). To manage these 
risks, entrepreneurs are encouraged to either reduce costs, boost revenues, or otherwise mitigate 
potential risks to increase their appeal to VCs (Michelfelder et al., 2022). Wustenhagen & Teppo 
(2006) explain, VC returns depend on three key variables: the purchase price of the initial 
investment, the exit sales price, and the time elapsed between investment and exit. 
Despite the subjective nature of early-stage Cleantech investments, a growing emphasis on robust 
quantitative metrics provides VCs with a more systematic approach to evaluate scalability, 
profitability, and long-term sustainability. 
Valuation Metrics and Decision-Making Process 
Besides qualitative considerations, investors often rely on more quantitative methods to achieve a 
clearer comparison among investment opportunities. Fernandez (2006) divides evaluation methods 
into six different groups: balance sheet, income statement, mixed (goodwill), cash flow 
discounting, value creation and options.  
For Series A and B investments, VCs prioritize KPIs that capture growth momentum and 
scalability, such as revenue growth, customer acquisition efficiency, and financial stability. Typical 
targets include rapid revenue expansion, 200-300% year-over-year or at least 20% month-over-
month growth, as indicators of market viability (Petrosyuk, 2024). Metrics like Annual Recurring 
Revenue (ARR) and Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) relative to Lifetime Value (LTV) further 
inform scalability and profitability expectations (Zemlyak, 2022). 
Different investors prioritize distinct metrics based on their strategic focus. Huang & Pearce (2015) 
emphasize the importance of core operating value, underscoring key performance indicators (KPIs) 
such as EBITA, revenue, and internal rate of return (IRR) as primary tools for assessing operational 
efficiency and profitability. Complementing these, Gompers et al. (2020) highlight metrics like net 
present value (NPV), multiple on invested capital, and average required IRR as critical for 
analysing long-term investment viability. While these quantitative metrics provide valuable 
insights, both sources acknowledge the role of non-financial metrics such as sales productivity, 
operating cost productivity, and capital productivity in assessing the broader operational 
effectiveness of a company (McKinsey & Company et al., 2010). Importantly, as highlighted 
previously, despite the reliance on structured financial evaluations, investors often incorporate 
qualitative assessments and gut instinct into their final decisions, reflecting the nuanced and 
multifaceted nature of Cleantech investment strategies (Gompers et al., 2020). This interplay 
between formal metrics and subjective judgment underscores the complexity of evaluating 
opportunities in a rapidly evolving sector. 
Thus, in Series A and B due diligence, balancing traditional financial metrics with sector-specific 
risks and growth potential is crucial for VCs.  
5.3 Integrating Environmental Performance Indicators (EPIs) with 
Traditional Financial Metrics in Cleantech Startups 
This section highlights the importance of integrating EPIs with financial metrics to align with 
investor priorities. It discusses sector-specific KPIs and regulatory compliance as essential 
components for bridging the funding gap. 
==PAGE:48==
 
48 
To align with VCs and CVCs priorities, Cleantech startups often integrate EPIs with traditional 
financial metrics. While core financial metrics, such as revenue growth and customer retention, 
remain fundamental, incorporating EPIs like carbon footprint reduction, energy efficiency, and 
sustainable supply chain practices is increasingly essential. These environmental metrics not only 
enhance a startup’s appeal to sustainability-focused VCs but also offer a clear perspective on its 
long-term environmental impact. According to the PRI Cleantech Guidelines, effective EPIs must 
be measurable and verifiable, indicating the startup’s alignment with Environmental, Social, and 
Governance (ESG) standards and regulatory goals, an essential factor for navigating due diligence 
and attracting investors focused on both returns and impact (PRI, 2024). 
Research further supports the value of environmental attributes in enhancing a startup’s financing 
opportunities. Hörisch & Tenner (2020) found that positive environmental factors improve the 
likelihood of securing both equity and debt financing, an effect amplified by the number of 
investors engaged. This relationship highlights the increasing importance of EPIs in facilitating 
growth-stage investments in Cleantech (Bianchini & Croce, 2022). 
Energy Startups KPIs in Cleantech Due Diligence 
Sector-specific KPIs are critical in evaluating Cleantech startups, particularly in areas like energy. 
For example, in enabling technologies, energy efficiency assesses the effectiveness of devices, 
systems, or processes in reducing energy consumption, underscoring a startup’s commitment to 
sustainability by optimizing energy input-to-output ratios (BXVentures, 2023). In the “Energy & 
Power” sector, renewable energy capacity tracks the installed capacity of renewable sources, such 
as solar, wind, and hydro, reflecting a startup’s commitment to decarbonizing energy systems. 
Additionally, Carbon Intensity, the measurement of CO₂ emissions per unit of energy produced, 
highlights the actual environmental impact and effectiveness of Cleantech solutions in reducing 
emissions (Di Lorenzo et al., 2012). 
Ikram et al. (2021) uses different indicators tailored to the energy sector. Their "Energy Utilization" 
category encompasses aspects such as the implementation of energy management systems, the use 
of energy-efficient appliances, cost reduction strategies, energy storage mechanisms, energy 
recycling, and energy conservation. It also addresses challenges like barriers to adopting renewable 
energy and cleaner technologies, as well as efforts to reduce reliance on fossil fuels to mitigate 
CO2 emissions (Ikram et al., 2021; Metson et al., 2020). 
Other scholars as Bianco et al. (2021),  highlight the variety of KPIs which vary depending on their 
application, whether to measure high-level objectives aligned with national or international energy 
strategies or to localized goals, such as those for microgrids and energy communities. Moreover, 
KPIs can be represented either as absolute metrics, such as energy production from renewable 
sources or carbon dioxide emissions or as percentage-based values relative to a reference scenario, 
such as reductions in carbon dioxide emissions or an increased share of renewables in the energy 
mix (Bianco et al., 2021).  
Proving Sustainability and Regulatory Compliance 
As startup, VCs and CVCs needs to look at LPs and parent corporation needs and track the adequate 
data to prove their compliance. Sustainable companies must prove the sustainability of their own 
business model, and this is crucial for entrepreneurs to consider (Bueno. & Glennon, 2023). When 
it comes to regulation on reporting, both the EU taxonomy provides classification for sustainable 
activities, namely the SFDR, defining sustainability disclosures for investors companies and the 
CSRD provide a type of non-financial disclosure to integrate. They serve a non-financial reporting 
directives bringing reporting needs complementary to financial metrics (Bueno, Glennon, 2023). 
As stated in section 4.2, CSRD allowed a shift from financial materiality to double materiality. 
==PAGE:49==
 
49 
Companies must extend the focus from measuring environmental impact towards their own assets 
to integrating a measure of their impact on the environment in return.  
SFDR and Metrics of Impact 
In the case of firms compliant with Article 9 of the Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation 
(SFDR), specific metrics are prioritized. In contrary to Article 8 products, Article 9 funds must 
continue to ensure that all the companies they invest in are EU Taxonomy-aligned (Bueno & 
Glennon, 2023). According to Harrer & Owen (2022), Art 9 VCs assess key indicators such as CO₂ 
equivalent savings, energy savings (in MWh), raw carbon material impact, food waste reduction, 
and water savings (in m³) to gauge a startup's contributions to environmental goals. This integration 
not only enhances transparency but also builds investor confidence by demonstrating the startup’s 
long-term viability in both financial and environmental terms. 
Aligning Impact Investing with Cleantech Success 
These regulations drive interest for such environmental KPIs by VCs and CVCs. A study by the 
European Investment Fund (EIF) in 2020 further indicated that 11% of surveyed investors 
considered impact investing due to regulations and initiatives like the United Nations Principles for 
Responsible Investment (UN PRI). According to the Principles for Responsible Investment, PRI 
(2024), funds or strategies using impact investing should align with section 2.A.19 of the “Global 
ESG Disclosure Standards for Investment Products Handbook”(CFA Institute, 2021). This 
alignment requires investment managers to define their impact objectives in measurable terms, 
identify primary stakeholders, and establish timelines for achieving these objectives. 
Additionally, investment managers must explain how impact goals interact with other objectives 
and disclose any trade-offs involved (CFA Institute, 2021). If the fund aims to support third-party 
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), this alignment must be specified, including the percentage 
of the portfolio dedicated to impact investments. Managers are also expected to outline their 
approach to tracking and reporting progress, managing any potential negative social or 
environmental impacts, and mitigating risks that could hinder their impact objectives (PRI, 2024). 
By integrating EPIs with traditional financial metrics, Cleantech startups can better align with VC 
expectations, emphasizing both financial success and measurable environmental contributions. 
This dual focus ensures Cleantech ventures meet the evolving demands of Cleantech investors, 
reducing the chance of failure in the VoD.  
Conclusion to the Literature Review 
The literature review served as a systematic research phase to identify the key barriers to energy 
startup growth and define the most relevant research perspective. This step-by-step approach first 
highlighted the energy sector’s dominant role in emissions reduction and examined the unique 
challenges faced by the European Cleantech ecosystem, including regulatory complexities, limited 
venture capital market maturity, and geopolitical factors. The focus then narrowed to identify the 
specific barriers affecting energy startup growth, emphasizing the difficulties in securing venture 
capital funding during the commercialization phase. 
Through this process, the literature review explored various Valley of Death (VoD) models, 
assessing how different frameworks conceptualize the funding gap. The review identified the Series 
A and B funding gap as the central challenge, confirming that the Commercialization Valley of 
Death is the most significant barrier for Cleantech energy startups. It further highlights that the 
critical challenge for energy startups stems from a shortage of VC funding opportunities, primarily 
==PAGE:50==
 
50 
due to reduced investor incentives. These hesitations are driven by high capital intensity, long 
development cycles, and uncertain returns. 
By analyzing different VoD models and aligning them with European-specific challenges, the 
review established a foundation for the research’s methodological approach, reinforcing the focus 
on investor-entrepreneur alignment as a key mechanism for overcoming this funding gap. A critical

Methodology 
 
This thesis seeks to address the funding gaps in the Cleantech energy sector by exploring the 
commercialization Valley of Death that inhibits startups' growth at Series A and B stages. Using 
an inductive, mixed-methods approach, it combines a cross-sectional survey for quantitative data 
with semi-structured interviews for qualitative insights. By deriving key research questions from a 
literature review and analyzing patterns emerging from the data, the study develops a conceptual 
framework to align investor needs with entrepreneurial strategies. The findings aim to generate 
actionable recommendations for Cleantech startups and advance theoretical understanding of 
investment dynamics in this sector. 
1. Research Foundation, Theoretical and Empirical Approach 
The research focus derived in the last prior sections was conducted in two complementary stages: 
theoretical and empirical. The theoretical study, detailed in the core literature review, focused on 
identifying the main barriers to energy startup growth by synthesising findings from a wide range 
of academic sources and industry-specific grey literature, such as policy briefs and market reports. 
This stage began with a broad exploration of Cleantech and the Valley of Death, progressively 
narrowing down to examine the specific role of venture capital (VC) in supporting energy startups. 
It focused on understanding the needs, drivers, and barriers influencing funding decisions for Series 
A and B stages. The review process continued until data saturation was reached, defined as the 
point at which no new insights were found on entrepreneurial strategies to align with investor 
expectations. 
The second stage, an empirical analysis presented in the PitchBook Data Analysis section, 
complemented the theoretical findings with quantitative insights. This stage employed Python-
based data analysis to validate the patterns observed in the literature. By analyzing funding trends, 
investor priorities, and startup performance metrics, the empirical study confirmed key gaps in 
Series A and B funding and helped refine the research focus. Together, these two stages provided 
the foundation for defining the thesis research question.  
2. Conceptual Model Components  
To address the thesis research question of how Cleantech energy startups can better align with 
investor expectations to overcome the Valley of Death, this study employs an inductive approach 
to explore the entrepreneur-investor relationship. The methodology follows a three-step 
exploratory approach, with each step guided by a specific research question.

Research Question 1: What are the drivers, risks, and challenges faced by both investors (VCs and 
CVCs) and Cleantech energy startups during the deal assessment and funding processes at Series

3. Focal Units 
 
Primary Focal Unit: Cleantech Energy Startups 
This thesis research primarily focuses on Cleantech Energy Startups. Cleantech is defined as 
innovations and technologies aimed at improving environmental sustainability, reducing carbon 
emissions, and enhancing energy efficiency. For this study, it specifically targets solutions that 
facilitate the deployment of green electrons. Within the energy sector, it narrows the scope to 
startups focused on: renewable energy generation (e.g., solar, wind, geothermal), energy storage 
(e.g., batteries, grid optimization solutions) and energy efficiency (e.g., smart grids, IoT-enabled 
technologies). This study is centred on enabling these startups to overcome the commercialization 
valley of death, the critical phase where startups struggle to secure sufficient funding to transition 
from proven prototypes to scalable commercialization. To address this challenge, the study targets 
startups in Series A and B funding rounds, which are seen as critical stages were funding gaps for 
energy startup growth. These are typically associated with startups operating at specific TRLs 6 to 
9, where technologies are advanced from prototype demonstration to full-scale deployment and 
market entry. 
 
Secondary Focal Unit: VC and CVC Investors 
The secondary focal unit of our study is Venture Capital and Corporate Venture Capital investors. 
VCs are the primary funding sources for Series A and B rounds and are defined as professional 
investment firms that focus on startups with high growth potential, typically evaluating financial 
returns, scalability, and innovative solutions. CVCs on the other hand, are investment arms of 
corporations that fund startups strategically aligned with the corporation’s business interests. 
Unlike VCs, CVCs often prioritize startups that can integrate into or enhance their parent 
company’s value chain. The study aims to understand how entrepreneurs can better conform to 
these two stakeholders’ expectations to facilitate funding opportunities. 
 
4.  Research Design and Methods 
 
This study employs a mixed-methods research design, combining qualitative data collection 
through semi-structured interviews and quantitative data from surveys to explore the funding 
challenges and opportunities faced by Cleantech energy startups. While the research is purely 
inductive in nature, the inclusion of multiple qualitative and qualitative methods allows for a 
comprehensive and multifaceted exploration of the research problem (see Figure 23). This 
approach ensures that the perspectives of both entrepreneurs and investors are captured in-depth, 
providing a robust foundation for understanding the commercialisation VoD and the factors 
influencing funding decisions. The study employs semi-structured interviews, which are open-
ended and exploratory, aiming to gather in-depth insights into investor challenges, expectations, 
and criteria. Parallelly, it incorporates surveys that involve structured questions and scales to 
measure alignment, understanding of investor needs, and funding challenges. These gather insights 
of entrepreneurs in pre- and post-Series A&B funding and investors’ expectations.

Cross-sectional surveys 
The primary quantitative component of this study involves cross-sectional surveys targeting two 
key groups: entrepreneurs and investors. These surveys were developed using Survey Xact, a 
Ramboll survey platform accessible to Denmark Technical University (DTU) students, which 
facilitates automated email distribution and participant reminders. The respondent list was derived 
from a PitchBook extract of 2,336 European energy startups that have received funding since 2000 
across various deal types. After cleaning the dataset of dummy variables, a refined sample was 
created to focus on relevant startups. 
The first survey targeted 1,092 startups whose last funding rounds were at seed or pre-seed stages. 
Its objective was to capture entrepreneurs’ perceptions of their funding needs, investor 
requirements, and anticipated challenges when approaching Series A and B funding rounds. The 
second survey, targeting 669 startups, focused on startups that had successfully raised Series A or 
Series A and B funding, gathering insights into their experiences, including how they overcame the 
funding gap and whether misalignments stemmed from a lack of knowledge about the rounds 
process or general misunderstanding by entrepreneurs. 
To complement the semi-structured interviews, a third survey was designed to target 669 VC and 
CVC investors. This survey collected data on their decision-making criteria, perceived risks, and 
expectations when evaluating startups at Series A and B stages. Respondents were identified using 
PitchBook and professional networks, with selection criteria including VCs and CVCs based in 
Europe who had completed at least one Series A or B investment in the energy sector since January 
1, 2020. To ensure comprehensive coverage of the cleantech energy sector, the study included 
categories such as Alternative Energy Equipment, Energy Exploration, Production and Refining, 
Energy Services, Electric Utilities, and other energy-related fields. The survey also explored 
challenges encountered by both entrepreneurs and investors during these critical funding stages. 
For entrepreneurs, the surveys were designed in two versions: one targeting startups preparing to 
raise Series A or B funding to understand their perceptions of investor expectations and anticipated 
challenges, and another focusing on startups that had already secured Series A or B funding to 
capture their experiences in navigating the funding gap. For investors, the survey aimed to collect 
qualitative insights into their decision-making criteria, with particular attention to the role of EPIs, 
as well as perceived risks and barriers to funding cleantech startups. 
By employing a survey-based strategy, this study ensures greater control over the research process. 
Surveys are particularly effective in understanding how a defined population, entrepreneurs and 
==PAGE:62==
 
62 
investors, thinks or behaves regarding specific issues, such as Series A and B funding. Moreover, 
the data collected using this strategy can be used to suggest possible reasons for relationships 
between variables and to construct models that explain these dynamics. 
Semi-structured interviews 
 
In addition to the questionnaires, qualitative data were collected through semi-structured interviews 
conducted with professionals from VCs and CVCs firms actively involved in the Cleantech energy 
sector. This method was chosen for its ability to delve deeply into the experiences and decision-
making processes of participants while allowing for flexibility in exploring emergent themes. The 
interviews were structured around five key areas: background information, challenges faced by 
Cleantech energy startups, risks and concerns during the investment process, the role of financial 
and EPIs, and expectations regarding standardized reporting and regulatory compliance. The 
interview questions, detailed in the Appendix 12, included open-ended queries to encourage 
expansive responses and probing questions to explore specific topics in greater depth. 
 
Respondents were selected through in-person connections at the Cleantech Forum Europe Annual 
Summit taking in Chantilly, France from November 5th to 7th 2024. This summit gathered over 
300+ industry leaders in Cleantech, including investors and corporations. Participants to the 
interview were reached during the summit and invited to participate in a following online interview. 
Additionally, targeted LinkedIn outreach was used to engage respondents from funds classified as 
Article 9 compliant under the SFDR specifically those investing in energy solutions. Data from the 
interviews were captured through audio recordings using the “Noota” translator application when 
consent was provided. For participants who preferred not to be recorded, detailed notes were taken 
to ensure accuracy. 
 
The surveys and interviews together provided a robust qualitative dataset, enabling the research to 
identify common themes, patterns, and relationships. This qualitative mixed approach ensures a 
rich and nuanced understanding of the funding dynamics in the Cleantech energy sector, 
contributing meaningful recommendations for both entrepreneurs and investors navigating the 
Series A and B funding landscape. 
 
The use of a mixed-methods approach was motivated by the desire to provide for triangulation, 
which enhances the credibility and reliability of research findings by integrating data from multiple 
sources. Triangulation allows for the validation of insights derived from surveys through the 
complementary data collected via interviews, ensuring that the findings are both comprehensive 
and well-substantiated. By combining these methods, the research design leverages the strengths 
of each to provide a more nuanced understanding of the funding dynamics in the Cleantech energy 
sector. This mixed-method approach ensures that the study captures a holistic view of the investors-
entrepreneurs alignment at the intersection of environmental performance and investment criteria.  
 
5. Sampling  
 
Sampling Population 
 
This research used probability sampling through its three surveys. In survey 1, the population 
comprised all VC and CVC investors who invested in energy startups at Series A or B since 2020. 
Survey 2 targeted European European energy startup entrepreneurs in pre-seed or seed stages likely 
to raise Series A and above in the future. In survey 3, the population consisted of all European 
energy startup entrepreneurs who raised Series A or B since 2020.  
 
==PAGE:63==
 
63 
As it is challenging to have an exact list of all startups, we relied on one of Europe’s main databases 
for startups, namely PitchBook. Unlike others such as Crunchbase or Dealroom, PitchBook’s 
extensive coverage of private market data makes it a valuable resource for this thesis. PitchBook 
offers comprehensive data coverage, encompassing the entire lifecycle of private capital globally, 
from seed funding to secondaries PitchBook, 2024). It includes granular details about deals, fund 
performance, team size, stages, and more, often providing more accurate and comprehensive early-
stage investor data, making it a valuable resource for startups seeking funding (Vendr, 2024). 
Therefore, the choice of a target population from PitchBook’s available data was made for all three 
surveys. 
 
This research follows a probability sampling procedure relying on a standard email to all 
respondents in the targeted population. This entails that the chance, or probability, of each case 
being selected from the target population is equal for all cases. The choice of sampling procedure 
allows population generalization without having to interview all European energy startups and 
investors. 
 
Sample size of Surveys 
 
Survey 1 
This survey targeted 436 VC and CVC investors who had invested in energy startups at Series A 
or B since 2020. Contact details were available for 375 of these investors. Non-responses due to 
refusal or ineligibility accounted for 29 respondents. Among this group, a further 33 emails were 
undeliverable. The initial dataset comprised 41 responses from investors, including representatives 
from various funding stages and investment categories. To maintain alignment with the research 
focus on Series A and B investments, the dataset was refined to exclude respondents who did not 
meet the following criteria primary involvement in Series A and B investments and classification 
as either VC or CVC investors. After this cleaning process, the final dataset included 35 responses. 
The survey yielded 30 completed questionnaires and 5 partially completed responses. 
 
Survey 2 
This survey targeted energy startup entrepreneurs who have received funding in earlier stages than 
Series A or B. Out of 1,092 potential contacts, only 739 were eligible and contactable. Thus the 
outreach was sent to 739 potential respondents, of which 144 were no longer active. Non-responses 
due to refusal or ineligibility accounted for 29 respondents. After two rounds of outreach to this 
group, we received 41 completed and 6 partially completed responses, which are both of value to 
this research. 
 
Survey 3 
This survey focused on entrepreneurs post-Series A or B, primarily those who had recently raised 
Series A or B funding. Filtering for having raised last round of Series A, B or C in PitchBook 
identified 669 potential respondents. Due to a lack of email access, 484 were targeted. We received 
25 completed answers, 6 partially completed responses, and 61 rejected emails (e.g., undeliverable 
or startup closure).

The interviews relied on a homogeneous sampling method, targeting a particular subgroup with 
similar characteristics to explore their perspectives in depth and identify subtle differences. 
Participants were selected through LinkedIn cold messaging outreach to all Article 9 funds in 
Europe focused on energy startups, as listed on Sifted (Pratty, 2024). Homogeneous sampling 
was prioritised, allowing for a detailed examination of participants' experiences. Additionally, 
purposive sampling was employed to include respondents who had participated in the Cleantech 
Europe 2024 annual summit. This event facilitated in-person connections and enabled follow-up 
interviews with relevant stakeholders. Purposive sampling, a non-probability method, targeted 
VCs and CVCs attending this cleantech-focused event, ensuring participants were directly 
relevant to the research objectives. These participants were chosen based on their presumed 
knowledge, experience, and engagement in the cleantech industry, making the event a convenient 
platform to access a concentrated group of relevant respondents. 
VCs interviewed include article 8 and 9 fund compliant as well as general investors in the energy 
sector. CVCs interviewed include investor branches of banks and energy company. The 
interviews provided qualitative insights into investor decision-making criteria, particularly the 
role of Environmental Performance Indicators (EPIs), alongside perceived risks and barriers to 
funding Cleantech startups. This dual approach of homogeneous and purposive sampling ensured 
a robust dataset aligned with the research objectives.

Hardware). Ordinal data points cover funding stages (e.g., Pre-Seed, Series A) and importance

qualitative data is gathered through open-ended responses addressing challenges during Series A

Control Variables  
To ensure a robust and nuanced analysis, this study incorporates control variables that account for 
external variations in the characteristics of startups and investors. These control variables are 
objective and measurable, including factors such as the sectoral focus and sources of funding 
(nominal data) as well as development and funding stages (ordinal data). By integrating these 
variables, the study isolates foundational differences, enhancing the reliability of the analysis and 
supporting a clearer understanding of the primary outcomes. 
The inclusion of control variables also allows for a deeper understanding of the dataset by mapping 
these variables to better interpret the characteristics of the surveyed respondents.

To answer this research sub-question, we perform a Chi-Square Test for Homogeneity to first 
analyze the distribution of perceived risks across the two groups: VC/CVCs and Entrepreneurs. 
This statistical test is used to determine whether the distributions of categorical data differ 
significantly between these two groups. Specifically, we examine five categories of risks: market 
risk (e.g., market adoption, competition), technology risk (e.g., scalability, innovation feasibility), 
financial risk (e.g., runway, revenue consistency), regulatory risk (e.g., compliance with ESG 
standards, policy changes), and execution risk (e.g., team expertise, ability to scale operations) 
identified in the literature section 5.3.

Interview insights confirm that addressing the unique challenges in Cleantech energy investments

these risks manifest differently across startups. Insights from investors like CVC_3, VC_4, VC_3,

Sectoral Risks 
Sector-specific risks in Cleantech are often characterized by long-term uncertainties compared to 
other industries. As CVC_3 notes, “There’s a bit more business risk due to capital intensity, high 
cash burn, and therefore higher risks.” These risks are further compounded by the need for 
substantial upfront investment and long payback periods, as CVC_1 highlights. This capital-
intensive nature of the sector demands robust financial strategies to ensure sustainability and 
growth. 
Regulatory and Policy Risks 
Regulatory risk emerges as a critical concern for startups reliant on evolving policies or government 
subsidies. CVC_3 identifies this as a double-edged sword: "Government subsidies can significantly 
boost certain technologies. However, when these subsidies end, companies that depend on them 
may become economically unviable, creating a 'false market.’". VC_4 provides a concrete 
example, citing a battery technology company that had to make costly adjustments to comply with 
updated EU safety directives: "Whenever there is a challenge, additional regulatory requirements 
can suddenly increase costs or complicate business models, making future projections uncertain." 
VC_3 adds that for some startups: “If that regulation somehow changes or gets delayed, they don’t 
have a business case anymore”. In sectors like Cleantech, where regulatory frameworks are 
dynamic, startups must proactively engage with policy developments to mitigate risk. This is 
particularly important in regions like the European Union, where policy shifts are frequent and 
impactful. 
Strategic and Timing Risks 
Timing plays a pivotal role in Cleantech, as solutions must enter the market at the right moment to 
achieve adoption. CVC_3 underscores this challenge: "Too early, and the market isn't ready; too 
late, and the opportunity has passed. Timing is critical.". Thus, market risks, such as dependency 
on subsidies, further exacerbate this challenge. Startups must align their strategies with market 
dynamics to navigate these risks effectively.

to find a well-sold company. People do not know how to put a pitch deck together. Most 
entrepreneurs don’t follow key structures, which does not inspire confidence in founders’ capacity 
to put 10 slides together.” 
Strike the right balance of information: VC_4 noted that the quality of information in initial 
decks varies significantly: “On the one hand, we have M&A advisors whose decks are super long 
and detailed. On the other hand, we sometimes see decks that are simply too vague. The more 
precise the data, the better.” Entrepreneurs should aim for a middle ground, concise but 
comprehensive presentations. Entrepreneur Survey respondents stressed the importance of 
organizing information well: “Get all elements arranged and consistent.” Additionally, VC_4 
emphasized the importance of data precision for effective decision-making. 
2. Include Key Data in the Pitch Deck 
Highlight critical business metrics and projections: Investors consistently pointed out the need 
for clear and relevant information in pitch decks. VC_3 outlined the fundamental elements: “What 
is the pain point you are solving? How are you solving it? How far along have you gotten? What’s 
your revenue and projections for this year and next year (not 2030)? How will your gross margin 
evolve over the next two to three years? And how much are you looking to raise?”. Investors prefer 
decks that allow them to evaluate opportunities quickly without needing to ask follow-up questions. 
==PAGE:98==
 
98 
“You might have a good idea, but if you don’t know how to present it or sell it, it makes your life a 
lot more difficult,” added VC_3. 
Avoid overcomplication: Entrepreneurs should ensure that their decks are straightforward and 
accessible. VC_3 warned: “If investors need to ask a lot of follow-up questions, or if NDAs are 
required to understand your pitch, you’re pretty much guaranteeing a ‘no’ from most venture 
funds.” 
Provide key evaluation criteria: VC_4 stressed the need for clarity on these elements: “What is 
the value proposition? How do you differentiate from competitors? What is the market size? All the 
classical KPIs and parameters for assessing the case should be clear.”. Entrepreneurial survey 
added: “Make sure the fundamentals of the business (financials, unit economics, ARR, etc.) are in 
order as well as environmental impact. It needs to be a solid, scalable business with returns in the 
future.” 
3. Focus on a Great Team 
Prioritize team quality and dynamics: A strong team can distinguish a great company from a 
good one. CVC_4 stated: “A good team with strong dynamics is more important than a great 
product. Confidence in explaining the product and demonstrating synergy is essential.”. VC_4 
identified challenges with technical teams: “A purely technical team often struggles with 
commercial aspects. While some investors like us can provide operational support, most cannot 
and will hesitate to rely solely on technical expertise.” 
Invest in talent development: Insights from the Cleantech Forum Conference emphasized the 
importance of attracting and developing talent through upskilling and training programs. These 
initiatives are crucial for long-term growth and competitiveness. 
4. Have a Structured Plan for Fund Use 
Demonstrate clear allocation of funds: Investors expect specificity in how funds will be used. 
CVC_4 explained: “The use of funds is crucial. Provide more than a pie chart showing 30% for 
personnel. Explain exactly what that 30% will achieve and where it will go.”. VC_3 emphasized 
aligning fund use with appropriate investment types: “You cannot invest venture money into project 
finance. It just doesn’t make sense.” 
5. Approach Investors Early 
Build relationships well before funding rounds: Investors value proactive engagement. VC_1 
advised: “Prepare well in advance of the round. That way, you can close the deal faster, focus on 
the business, and maintain growth.” CVC_1 warned against poor planning: “Coming for funding 
only six months before running out of cash is a red flag.” 
Start your funding round early: Survey respondents recommended: “Take over a year to close 
a deal. Start too early rather than too late.” 
6. Planning Fundraising timeline 
Develop a robust fundraising timeline: VC_1 emphasized the importance of planning fundraising 
timelines to accommodate market variations and avoid unnecessary delays. “Plan fundraising 
timelines to accommodate market variations.” Insights from the Clean Tech Forum Conference 
further highlighted the need to raise sufficient funds to provide a safety net during the growth phase 
==PAGE:99==
 
99 
and address potential complications. Entrepreneurs should aim for funding levels that reduce 
financial stress and enable strategic decision-making. 
7. Avoid Unrealistic Projections and Valuations 
Set achievable projections: CVC_3 noted: “Sometimes startups have very ambitious timelines. 
We bring a sense of realism about where the market is going. For instance, many hydrogen 
production assumptions linked to the Paris Agreement don’t hold true. Startups building on such 
assumptions face challenges when these projections prove unrealistic.” 
Provide realistic valuations: CVC_4 observed: “We occasionally see unrealistic valuation 
expectations, often influenced by existing shareholders”. VC_3 added: “Unrealistic growth 
projections waste time for both entrepreneurs and VCs. Misrepresenting facts in initial meetings 
that are easily verifiable is a mistake that undermines credibility.”. Investors prefer grounded 
projections. VC_3 stated: “If you claim to grow from $2 million this year to $15 million next year 
without strong evidence, it sounds suspicious. We’re more attracted to realistic projections with 
clear supporting data”. These findings are supported by survey responses in which investors 
emphasized honesty in pitches: “Be clear and honest about opportunities and challenges. We will 
always find out.” Another respondent highlighted: “Be realistic about commercial traction.” 
8. Target the Right Investors and Understand Their Models 
Align with investor focus: VC_1 recommended targeting funds aligned with the startup’s stage 
and focus area. “Startups should understand investor expectations around financial and 
environmental performance.”. VC_3 noted common mistakes Cleantech founders make: “Some 
business models are not venture fundable. Founders must ensure their business matches the venture 
return model and identify appropriate funds for high-risk, capital-intensive technologies.” 
Understand different investor goals: CVC_2 highlighted the importance of recognizing investor 
goals: “Financial funds prioritize cash-on-cash returns within a set timeframe, while CVCs often 
seek synergies with the parent company and long-term market impact.” Entrepreneur survey 
responses further stressed the understanding that some businesses do not fit the VC model and 
exploring alternative pathways if needed: “Encouragement for other pathways where needed.”. 
9. Inform Yourself About Venture Funding Mechanisms 
Educate yourself on venture funding: Investors emphasized the need for entrepreneurs to 
understand the mechanics of venture funding. VC_3 stressed: “Even Series A entrepreneurs often 
don’t understand that LPs expect at least 3x returns in 10 years. To achieve this, startups must 
demonstrate rapid revenue growth from $2 million to $60 million in five or six years.” 
Choose funding sources wisely: Cleantech conference forum speaker added “Entrepreneurs 
should understand the requirements and limitations of various funding types. If their business 
doesn’t suit venture funding, they should explore alternative options.” 
Read a book on the history of venture: VC_3 suggested entrepreneurs to read a book read a book 
on venture funding to understand when it fits to entrepreneurial strategies, stating: “If you want to 
go out the venture fund route take the time to understand what they need”.

Collaborate with industrial investors: CVC_2 emphasized the value of having other industrial 
investors at the table: “Engaging with other CVCs creates an industrial ecosystem favorable for

Seek investors willing to take risks: CVC_1 recommended: “Look for investors ready to accept 
higher risks for lower returns, such as foundations or sovereign funds. Hybrid models with public

Engage with public funders and corporates: Insights from the Clean Tech Forum emphasized

11. Technology 
De-risking technology: CVC_4 emphasized the importance of demonstrating traction to mitigate 
perceived technological risks: “We like to see some sort of traction, at least a pilot or something 
that proves the technology works under certain circumstances. Even though risks may remain, 
evidence of functionality eases concerns.”. VC_4 added that entrepreneurs must clearly present the 
challenges of scaling their technology or business. “We’ve seen many cases where a demo or pilot 
is provided, but the scaling, whether it’s the first real-size product or a factory, is too big a risk and 
too expensive.” They further add that “Hardware scaling is particularly tough.” 
Avoid approaching investors too early with CAPEX-heavy projects: CVC_1 warned against 
seeking investment prematurely for capital-intensive projects. Instead, entrepreneurs should ensure 
their technology and financial models are sufficiently validated before approaching investors. 
12. Adapt to the European Context 
Align with EU regulations: VC_4 advised startups to align their operations with existing and

Address regional challenges: VC_2 highlighted the difficulty European startups face due to fewer 
technology infrastructure funds compared to the U.S. This necessitates strategic planning and 
efficient use of resources. 
Prepare for policy shifts: A Clean Tech Forum speaker emphasized: “Companies must secure 
funding and plan for potential policy changes. State-level policies may maintain or even strengthen 
environmental protections, even if federal policies are relaxed.” 
13. Focus on Your Exit Strategy Early 
Integrate exit strategy into planning: CVC_4 emphasized that alignment on exit plans is critical: 
“We ask CEOs about their exit strategy from the very beginning. It’s crucial to align with investor

Understand investor goals and align exit strategies: CVC_2 highlighted the importance of 
tailoring exit plans based on investor models: “Financial funds prioritize cash-on-cash returns 
within a defined timeline, whereas CVCs often seek synergies with their parent companies, 
reinvesting in other ventures after exit or acquiring startups that align with their operational goals.”

Prepare detailed, realistic strategies: CVC_2 highlights that Entrepreneurs must go beyond

•

•

Prioritize profitability and governance: Insights from the Cleantech Conference stressed:

Spin challenges into opportunities: As one entrepreneur stated: “You can tell investors the wheels

Discussion 
 
This study provides a critical examination of the funding challenges faced by Cleantech energy 
startups in Europe, specifically the hurdles encountered during the Series A and B funding stages. 
By exploring the Valley of Death through the lens of investor-entrepreneur alignment, the research 
highlights the misalignments between startup needs and investor expectations, offering actionable 
strategies to bridge the funding gap. The findings underscore the importance of integrating 
Environmental Performance Indicators with financial metrics, improving risk assessment 
communication, and adapting to evolving regulatory frameworks. While the study offers 
meaningful insights into the challenges Cleantech startups face, certain limitations must be 
acknowledged, which also provide avenues for future research. 
 
One of the key constraints of this research lies in its scope. The study focuses exclusively on the 
European Cleantech landscape, which, while significant, limits the applicability of findings to other 
regions with distinct economic conditions, regulatory frameworks, and investment cultures. The 
unique challenges in Europe, including a reliance on debt financing over equity and a relatively 
less mature VC market compared to North America, may not be representative of the global 
Cleantech ecosystem.  
 
Moreover, the research scope was confined to the Commercialization VoD at Series A and B 
funding stages, excluding earlier and later less prominent VoDs, which may require different 
entrepreneurial strategies. This study was originally built upon the Valley of Death framework, 
which ranges from one to five valleys depending on the models. The findings confirm that Series 
A and B stages are particularly challenging for Cleantech startups, aligning with prior literature on 
the Valley of Death, particularly Osawa & Miyazaki (2006), who defined commercialisation as the 
most critical funding stage. While this focus allowed for a deep exploration of commercialization-
stage barriers, future research could expand to encompass the full startup lifecycle, including the 
technology VoD and the profitability VoD. These require analyzing different funding sources as 
Seed-stage startups, for example, rely more heavily on grants and angel investors, while later-stage 
ventures engage with private equity and strategic investors, each of which presents unique funding 
dynamics that this study does not explore in depth.  
 
While this thesis adopts a three-valley model of the Valley of Death, prior research has identified 
between one and five valleys, highlighting the variability in how these funding gaps are 
conceptualized. The findings reinforce the significance of the commercialization gap as the most 
critical funding challenge for Cleantech startups. However, future research could further examine 
whether the three-valley framework sufficiently captures the complexities of Cleantech financing 
or if alternative models, such as a more dynamic or sector-specific approach, would offer a more 
precise representation of these challenges. 
 
Next, despite employing a mixed-methods approach, the study is not without data limitations. 
While the survey response rate was high, and interviews provided deep qualitative insights, the 
overall sample size remains a constraint. A larger dataset could enhance the robustness of findings 
and allow for greater statistical generalization. Furthermore, the reliance on self-reported data from 
entrepreneurs and investors introduces potential biases, as respondents may unconsciously or 
strategically frame their responses to align with perceived expectations. While efforts were made 
to mitigate such biases through triangulation, a more extensive dataset incorporating third-party 
validation, such as investor portfolios or startup financial records, could offer a more objective 
evaluation of the funding landscape. 
==PAGE:106==
 
106 
 
A particular challenge of this thesis was the dual focus on venture capital and corporate venture 
capital. While studying both funding sources provided valuable insights, it also introduced 
complexities in the analysis. CVCs operate under different strategic priorities compared to VCs, 
often prioritizing synergies with corporate parents over financial returns. Although integrating 
CVCs into the study added depth to the discussion, a more focused examination solely on VCs 
could have yielded a more streamlined analysis. However, given the data constraints and the 
growing role of CVCs in Cleantech funding, their inclusion remains a necessary dimension of the 
research. 
 
Another key limitation stems from the inherently dynamic nature of the Cleantech sector. Policy 
changes, technological advancements, and market shifts continuously reshape the investment 
landscape. For instance, geopolitical developments, such as the recent U.S. elections, have the 
potential to either attract more entrepreneurs to the European Cleantech market or, conversely, lead 
to a deprioritization of global energy startup investment. While this study provides a snapshot of 
current trends, ongoing shifts in public and private sector commitments to sustainability could alter 
funding dynamics in ways not captured in this research. Future studies should consider longitudinal 
approaches to assess how these variables evolve over time and impact the Cleantech funding 
environment. 
Given these limitations, several avenues for future research emerge. First, comparative studies 
examining Cleantech startups in North America, Asia-Pacific, and other key regions could shed 
light on how different funding ecosystems shape the VoD. The impact of policy interventions, such 
as the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act, on Cleantech funding success could offer valuable lessons for 
European policymakers seeking to enhance investment in the sector (Cleantech Group, 2024). 
Second, longitudinal studies tracking Cleantech startups from inception to exit would provide 
deeper insights into how funding needs evolve over time. Understanding whether startups that 
successfully bridge the VoD sustain long-term growth or encounter additional financing roadblocks 
could refine strategies for supporting the sector.  
 
A critical gap identified in this study is the lack of tailored investor recommendations. This research 
prioritized entrepreneur-focused strategies to improve investor alignment, but future work could 
explore ways in which investors can adapt their models to better accommodate Cleantech energy 
startups. Given the long commercialization timelines of energy hardware ventures, investors might 
consider alternative funding structures with extended return horizons, such as funds designed to 
accommodate 15–20 year investment cycles. As VC_4 noted, high CAPEX investment cannot 
compete with software-as-a-service returns. However, the transition cannot happen with solely 
software solutions. He underscores that “It requires a different breed of investors, requires patience, 
it requires people.” and that their fund is trying to communicate that to LPs. 
 
Moreover, some recent studies highlight that VCs alone are not sufficient to help Cleantech startups 
overcome the VoD and question whether the traditional VC model is well-suited for Series A and 
B Cleantech startups. Researchers such as Gaddy et al. (2017) highlight that deep tech investments 
in Cleantech, with high CAPEX requirements, have too low a return for the VC funding model. 
They highlight the need for broader support from policymakers, corporations, and investors.  
 
In line with this, future studies could explore whether macro-level enablers, such as policy 
interventions at the European or governmental level or new financial instruments, might better 
address the VoD challenges. Researching the effectiveness of existing policies such as the 
European Green Deal in fostering Cleantech investment (highlighted in the literature review).

These sectors may share similar high capital requirements and VoD challenges, and a broader study

alignment strategies identified here are universally applicable or require sector-specific 
modifications. 
 
This research lays the foundation for understanding the funding constraints faced by Cleantech 
energy startups in Europe. By emphasizing micro-level enablers and practical alignment strategies, 
it offers actionable insights for entrepreneurs seeking to navigate the commercialization phase. 
However, the limitations underscore the need for continued research to refine and expand the 
understanding of Cleantech investment dynamics.

Conclusion  
Cleantech energy startups have substantial potential to drive global decarbonization efforts, yet 
many face significant challenges when transitioning from early-stage innovation to 
commercialization. Despite the energy sector being one of the most attractive for venture capital 
investment in Europe, this research identifies a lack of funding at the Series A and B stages as the 
primary obstacle hindering energy startup growth. Overcoming this gap requires specific 
entrepreneurial strategies that are aligned with venture funding mechanisms. 
By focusing on traditional and corporate venture capital as the main funding sources, this thesis 
investigated the risks and challenges that deter investors from supporting energy startups during 
their growth phases. While interest in Cleantech investments is rising due to sustainability goals 
and potential returns, market risks remain investors' main concern, followed by execution and 
financial risks. Energy startups face additional hurdles such as high capital expenditures, first-of-
a-kind technologies and scalability barriers. The uncertainty around exits and funding timelines 
further discourages investors from committing to Series A and B rounds. The core issue is the 
structural mismatch between the risk and capital requirements of energy startups and investor return 
expectations at Series A and B. Many Cleantech energy solutions, especially hardware-intensive 
technologies, require substantial capital, long development cycles, and extended market entry 
timelines. This contrasts with venture capital funds' short investment horizons, typically seeking 
exits within a decade, limiting energy startups' ability to secure the necessary growth funding. 
Entrepreneurs should take a proactive approach to reduce perceived risks and address information 
asymmetries. Through cross-sectional surveys and semi-structured interviews, this research 
highlighted the growing influence of regulatory frameworks such as the Sustainable Finance 
Disclosure Regulation and EU Taxonomy on investment drivers. However, financial returns remain 
core decision drivers, with VCs and CVCs heavily weighing traditional metrics like revenue 
growth, burn rate, and annual recurring revenue. Additionally, Environmental Performance 
Indicators, such as CO2 reduction and energy efficiency, are of growing interest to investors. Yet, 
entrepreneurs often fail to align their business models with these expectations. Mitigating investor’s 
perceived risks primarily revolve around demonstrating a strong product-market fit and presenting 
attractive performance metrics, a compelling business model, and strong accurate data. 
By providing practical guidance to help entrepreneurs understand the Commercialization VoD, this 
thesis improves alignment with investor expectations and effectively prepares for Series A and B 
funding. These insights aim to equip Cleantech energy startups with the tools needed to enhance 
their funding prospects and scale their innovations. However, beyond expecting entrepreneurs to 
adapt, systemic changes are needed in Cleantech investment models. Current venture capital 
structures favor short-term returns, which are not suited for the capital-intensive, long-development 
nature of many energy solutions. A more flexible, Cleantech-specific investment framework is 
needed, balancing financial returns with long-term environmental impact. Public-private 
investment mechanisms, blended financing models, and risk-sharing instruments must be further 
explored to enable more patient capital for Cleantech innovation.  
While this thesis advances understanding of the VoD, it acknowledges certain limitations. The 
findings are based on a European dataset, and future research could explore country-specific 
funding landscapes, alternative financing models, or non-European contexts. The future of 
Cleantech energy innovation requires a fundamental shift in how sustainable solutions are financed. 
European policymakers and financial institutions must develop more adaptive investment 
==PAGE:109==
 
109 
frameworks that accommodate longer return cycles, enabling high-impact technologies to reach 
commercialization at scale. Europe has a strong environmental agenda with the potential to position 
itself as a world leader in Cleantech energy solutions, particularly given the current challenges in 
the American investment landscape. Only through a united effort between entrepreneurs, investors, 
and policymakers can Europe unlock the full potential of its clean energy future.
