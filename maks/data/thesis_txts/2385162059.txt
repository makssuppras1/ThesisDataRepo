Abstract 
In the current thesis a parametric analysis of an existing office building, Copenhagen 
Business Park, with respect to energy and comfort performance optimization was 
conducted. For this reason dynamic simulations with IDA ICE software were performed 
in a typical floor for both the current building and renovation suggestion cases. The indoor 
conditions in the existing building were unsatisfactory during summer due to the absence 
of a cooling system. Regarding energy, the biggest part of heating energy use during 
winter came from the Air Handling Unit operation and not from the installed radiator 
units. Finally, the existing fluorescent lighting units used a large quantity of energy, too. 
The total yearly energy use according to IDA ICE simulation was 133.1 kWh/m2. Before 
the application of renovation measures, changes in the supply temperatures of the heating 
plant and ventilation system were made, existing CAV ventilation was converted to VAV 
and new lighting units were installed. After these changes, renovation measures which 
contained installation of radiant panels for heating and cooling and installation of fan 
coils, active and passive chilled beams for cooling were implemented. With the application 
of all these systems the total annual energy use was reduced to around 51 kWh/m2 for all 
cases, corresponding to a more than 50% reduction in relation to the existing building. 
Now, the building complied with the energy requirements of BR15 for Renovation Class 
I. The operative temperature was between the limits of thermal comfort Category II 
(EN15251), namely 20OC and 26OC, in all the rooms during the whole year, while the CO2 
concentration and relative humidity (RH) levels complied with the requirements of 
Categories II and III of EN15251 respectively. A separate case with three variations of 
implementation of Thermo Active Building System (TABS) was also examined. In the 
TABS case similar results with the previous renovation cases were observed, with the 
total yearly used energy being again reduced to almost 51 kWh/m2, the operative 
temperature belonging to Categories I and II and the CO2 and RH levels within the limits 
of Categories II and III respectively. Finally, a daylight analysis with Velux Daylight 
Visualizer and IDA ICE was performed. It was concluded that the current daylight 
conditions in the building were poor due to the building’s external architectural design, 
with the average daylight factor (DF) in the offices being around 1.5%. The measure of 
the external glass façade removal increased the average DF by 0.2% and only the 
implementation of windows with higher light transmittance led to acceptable average DF 
values of 2%.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Objective 
The current master thesis focused on the parametric analysis of an existing office building: 
Copenhagen Business Park, by performing dynamic simulations with IDA ICE simulation tool. 
The investigation was oriented in the optimization of the building’s energy performance as well 
as the occupants’ comfort. Therefore, a typical floor in Copenhagen Business Park was studied 
and various renovation suggestions were examined. These renovation suggestions included 
changes in the HVAC system and the building envelope. The alternative renovation scenarios 
of the HVAC system consisted of installation of radiant ceiling panels for heating and cooling, 
as well as installation of active and passive chilled beams and fan coils for cooling. A separate 
case with variations of implementation of Thermo Active Building System in the building floor 
was also examined, although it could not be applied in a renovation case. Regarding the building 
envelope, a case with change of windows was examined. Furthermore, a daylight analysis was 
conducted with Velux Daylight Visualizer and IDA ICE and the output from the two software 
tools was compared. Lastly, it was investigated whether the renovation cases were economically 
feasible and the most favorable solution was indicated.

2. Background  
2.1 Indoor environment 
People in industrialized countries spend a large majority of their time in indoor spaces. The 
indoor air quality and thermal comfort inside these spaces is of great importance not only for 
the occupants’ health but also for their performance.  
Regarding office buildings, where people spend around 1/3 of their day, it has been estimated 
that a percentage of occupants between 20% and 60% suffer from Sick Building Syndrome (SBS) 
symptoms, which among others are headache, eye, nose or throat irritation, fatigue and 
dizziness (US Environmental Protection Agency, 1991). On the other hand, recent (laboratory 
and field) studies performed by the Technical University of Denmark have shown that the 
productivity of office workers is increased when comfortable room temperatures, sufficient 
ventilation and decrease of indoor pollutants are achieved (Olesen, 2007). 
In typical office buildings the cost of people is a factor that accounts for 100 times more than the 
cost for energy (Olesen, 2007). Therefore, the optimization of the indoor environment, hence the 
well-being and effectiveness of office workers can lead to significant financial reductions for 
companies. 
2.2 Energy use 
From the mid-1970’s people got aware of the upcoming energy crisis and the first attempts were 
made to focus on energy reduction in the building sector. Today buildings are responsible for 
40% of the energy use and 36% of the CO2 emissions in the European Union, with 35% of the 
building stock being over 50 years old (European Commission, 2017).The required heating 
demand of older buildings compared to that of newly built outlines the need for a green strategy. 
Therefore, the EU has set a 20% energy savings target for 2020 and a 30% target for 2030. These 
targets include amongst others mandatory energy efficiency building certificates as well as 
energy efficient renovations to at least 3% of the buildings owned and occupied by the central 
government (European Commission, 2017). 
Towards this direction, the reuse of older buildings can save huge amounts of energy if it is 
methodically designed. What is important is to focus on the problematic areas that compose a 
low-efficient building and take actions in terms of the building envelope, heating and cooling 
strategies and ventilation systems. Regarding the building envelope, typical heat losses from a 
building include losses from walls, roofs, floors and windows (DS 418, 2011). 
2.3 Daylight importance and energy 
Energy for artificial lighting consists the second largest part of the total energy consumed in 
non-residential buildings after the energy used for heating, ventilation and air-conditioning 
(HVAC) systems. Specifically, lighting accounts for approximately 18% of the used energy in 
commercial buildings (Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy, 2010). However, 
except for improving the performance and efficiency of the artificial lighting systems 
themselves, a more careful daylight planning during the early building design process could 
reduce a significant part of this energy use by taking advantage of the natural resources that 
daylight provides (International Energy Agency, 2000).

Daylight offers the conditions for good vision, as it supplies spaces with high illuminance and 
allows exceptional colour discrimination and colour rendering. Thus, daylight is desirable in 
indoor spaces as it fulfils two primary human demands: the ability to see both a task and a space 
well and to experience some environmental stimulation (Boyce, 1998). It is generally considered 
that working by daylight leads to the perception of a more pleasant indoor environment as well 
as less stress and discomfort for the occupants (IEA SHC Task 21, 2000). 
2.4 Indoor terminal units and their characteristics 
Today various heating and cooling strategies are applicable in residential, commercial and other 
types of buildings. In the following pages a brief introduction to the most commonly used heating 
and cooling systems is made and some of their most significant characteristics are analyzed. 
The terminal units of these systems can be classified in three categories: all-air systems, passive 
and active beams and hydronic radiant heating and cooling systems (Kazanci, 2016). 
2.4.1 All-air systems 
All-air systems are mechanical ventilation systems which consist of a combination of appliances 
(air handling units, ducts and terminal units) that facilitate the supply of outdoor air and the 
extract of indoor air in interior spaces (Babiak, et al., 2013). In these systems, the main method 
of heat emission and removal is convection and the medium of energy distribution is air 
(Kazanci, 2016). 
Air-based systems have been broadly used for both heating and cooling purposes. Common all-
air systems are Constant Air Volume (CAV) and Variable Air Volume (VAV) which are 
centralized and fan coil units which are decentralized  (Le Dreau, 2014). 
Some of the most common methods of air distribution for total volume ventilation of rooms used 
by the above-mentioned systems are mixing ventilation, displacement ventilation, and 
personalized ventilation (Kazanci & Olesen , 2015).  
 
Fan coils 
Fan coils are simple air distribution systems which allow optimal allocation of the air in the 
examined zones and in that way the desirable thermal comfort conditions are achieved. In most 
of their applications they recirculate air. One characteristic of a well-designed fan coil system 
is that the optimal conditions can be achieved at very low speeds and as a consequence the noise 
in the environment is being reduced (Cammarata, et al., 2016). Also, fan coil units facilitate the 
local control of the different zones, thus the systems energy is reduced (Engineering Guide Fan 
& Blower Coils, 2011). A typical fan coil unit is illustrated in the following figure.

2.4.2 Passive and active beams 
Passive Beams 
Passive beams are only used for the cooling of spaces with typical sensible cooling capacities in 
the range of 40-80W/m2 (Kazanci, 2016). A passive beam consists of a coil, which includes a 
circuit copper pipe expanded into aluminum fins, and a casing. When cold water circulates 
through the coil, the fins are cooled down and the air in contact with the fins is cooled, becomes 
denser and descends into the room. Passive beams can be either exposed or recessed in a 
suspended ceiling depending on the available ceiling height and architectural preferences for 
the space (REHVA, 2015). 
Active Beams 
Active beams can be used for heating as well as for cooling buildings. They are connected both 
to the primary air and the chilled- and heated water systems. Primary air is provided through 
nozzles to ventilate and dehumidify the space, while inducing room air through the coil to 
control the balance of heating or sensible cooling load. Room temperature can be controlled by 
varying the water flow rate through the heat exchanger, the volume flow rate of primary air or 
both of them (REHVA, 2015).

2.4.3 Hydronic radiant heating and cooling systems 
Hydronic radiant heating and cooling systems are systems where water is the medium of energy 
distribution while at least 50% of the heat transfer takes place by radiation  (Kazanci, 2016). 
They are used in residential, commercial and industrial buildings. Apart from the traditional 
radiators, there are modern radiant systems which are widely applied today due to the reduced 
peak loads as well as their low energy use. These novel systems can be classified in three 
categories: radiant heating and cooling panels, pipes isolated from the main building structure 
(radiant surface systems) and pipes embedded in the main building structure (Thermally Active 
Building Systems, TABS) (Kazanci, 2016). In the figure below some examples of novel hydronic 
radiant systems are presented (Kim & Olesen, 2015).  
 
 
Figure 3: Water based radiant systems examples (Kim & Olesen, 2015) 
 
Radiators 
Radiators are the most conventional heating units. They have a direct impact on the thermal 
comfort due to surrounding air heating. Specifically, the warm surface of the radiator heats the 
air coming in contact with it and as a result heated air is produced through convection. Today 
radiators are often made of steel whereas iron, aluminum or copper casts may be also used. 
Some of the most common radiator types are: segment radiators, panel radiators, panel-
convector radiators, convector radiators, canal radiators, smooth and finned tube radiators, 
surface radiators (Muniak, 2017).

Radiant heating and cooling panels 
Radiant ceiling panels are heating and cooling panels with integrated pipes and they are placed 
under the ceiling surface. These radiant systems are installed after the initial ceiling 
construction and they are normally hanged.  Metal panels, rill placed pipes and seamless 
integrated panels are some of the most frequently met on market heating and cooling radiant 
panels (Babiak, et al., 2013). 
 
Figure 5: Example of two different constructions of cooling panels (Babiak, et al., 2013) 
Radiant surface heating and cooling systems  
Radiant surface systems work with low water temperature for heating and high water 
temperature for cooling. They are normally insulated from floor, wall and ceiling, which are 
used as surfaces that provide the space with heating or cooling. Hydronic radiant surface 
systems can only address sensible heating and cooling loads. Thus, a ventilation system is 
demanded with their application in order to cover the latent loads  (Kazanci, 2016). 
Thermally Active Building Systems (TABS) 
TABS is a heating and cooling system where water pipes are embedded in the building slab. In 
that case heating is stored in the slab and cooling is transferred over a longer period according 
to the thermal loads (Figure 6). They are widely used in multi-story buildings and some of their 
advantages are cited in this chapter (Babiak, et al., 2013).  To begin with, the TABS are low 
temperature heating and high temperature cooling systems which require smaller-capacity 
heating and cooling plants, and downsized ventilation systems as well as less space due to their 
construction details. It is of equal importance that a reduction of the total energy use, the heat 
losses and the peak loads is observed with TABS. Moreover, the indoor air quality can be 
sufficiently improved and the total cost (initial, operational and energy cost) can be significantly 
lower (Kazanci, 2016).

2.4.4 Advantages of radiant systems versus all-air systems 
One of the main objectives of radiant heating and cooling systems is to create a pleasant indoor 
environment and enhance the thermal comfort of the occupants. Radiant systems provide the 
occupants with higher air quality than convective systems, as less dust is transported during 
their operation. Also, the use of carpets, which can be allergen or pollutants’ sources, is not 
necessary when radiant systems are implemented.  
Another advantage of radiant versus air heating and cooling systems is the reduction of the 
noise level due to the air circulation. The ventilation system may actually have decreased duct 
size and air velocity, since it is only needed to maintain an acceptable indoor air quality and 
avoid dehumidification. In this way draft problems are also minimized. Finally, the higher 
heating surface temperatures in radiantly heated spaces decrease the possibility of 
condensation and mold growth. 
However, there are some issues that should be carefully addressed with the application of 
radiant systems. For example, special care should be taken in order to avoid condensation in 
radiantly cooled spaces with no natural ventilation. In addition, in buildings with thermally 
activated building systems special solutions for the space acoustics may be required, as the use 
of conventional acoustic ceiling panels would reduce the heat transfer and thus the TABS 
performance (Kim & Olesen, 2015). 
 
2.4.5 History of radiant heating and cooling systems 
The ancestors of radiant heating systems were recorded in ancient China in approximately 
10.000 BC with the use of a type of heated beds. In 5.000 BC Koreans developed heated floor 
systems too, while in 500 BC Greeks and later Romans implemented the application of 
conditioned surfaces through a system called “hypocaust” (Bean, et al., 2010).  
The use of radiant heating and cooling re-appeared more massively in Europe in the 20th 
century. Most of the cooling ceiling systems developed in the 1930’s failed due to condensation 
problems.  However, the simultaneous use of a small ventilation system in order to lower the 
dew-point of the indoor air “solved” the issue. This was the case in a department store built in 
Zürich in 1936-1937 and in a multi-story building built in Canada in the early 1950’s. In 1985 
floor heating became a common heating solution in houses in Europe and Nordic countries, 
while 10 years later the use of TABS was applied into the market of both residential and 
commercial buildings (Bean, et al., February 2010). 
Today radiant floor heating is widely used in Korea, Northern China and Japan. Furthermore, 
embedded radiant cooling systems have become common in mid-Europe and radiant based 
HVAC systems with low temperature heating and high temperature cooling are applied in many 
areas around the world (Bean, et al., February 2010). 
2.5 Recent studies 
The renovation of HVAC systems in office buildings has gained more popularity in the past few 
years due to the increase in energy use. Some of the most recent studies examine also the cost-
==PAGE:16==
9 
 
factor in the renovation cases apart from the effects on the indoor thermal comfort conditions 
and the CO2 emissions. 
In 1999 Pejtersen et al. conducted an intervention study in an office building with severe indoor 
climate complaints amongst the occupants. By renovating the HVAC system in both cellular 
and open-plan offices and by replacing the floor carpet with a low-emitting vinyl floor in cellular 
offices, they achieved improvement of the building indoor conditions’ perception of the 
occupants. This improvement was mostly declared in the cellular offices, where both the pre-
mentioned measures were implemented (Pejtersen, et al., 1999). 
Also in the same year, Imanari et al. compared a radiant ceiling cooling system to a conventional 
air-conditioning system with respect to thermal comfort, energy and cost performance. It was 
concluded that the total energy use was reduced by 10% with the radiant system in relation to 
the air-conditioning system. Moreover, an energy cost reduction of 9%-10% was observed and 
the estimated payback time for the implementation of the radiant ceiling panel system ranged 
between 1 and 17 years depending on the price of the used panels (Imanari, et al., 1999). 
In 2014 Zhu examined the potential for a zero-carbon renovation in office-educational buildings 
with Energy Plus computer simulations. The author found out that only 2.2% of the carbon 
emissions can be reduced by improving the building’s air-tightness and the windows’ U-value, 
while 40.8% of the carbon emissions can be reduced by implementing renewable technologies 
such as photovoltaics, solar, thermal etc. (Zhu, 2014). 
According to Kalz and Pfafferott, the investment costs for passive cooling were estimated to 
20€/m2, for mechanical night-ventilation to 32€/m2, for fan coils to 85€/m2, for radiant cooling 
ceiling panels to 138€/m2 and for TABS to 117€/m2 in a European office building (Kalz & 
Pfafferott, 2014). 
Simmonds proved that there was minor difference between costs for installation of a VAV and 
a radiant ceiling system in an office building in the US. However, the radiant system was 
associated with 7% lower maintenance cost and energy cost savings in comparison to the all-air 
system (Simmonds, 2014). 
In 2016 Thorsten et al. studied the energy use and potential of cost-efficient retrofit of 
institutional building stock in Luxembourg through measures which were applied to the 
building envelope (e.g. insulation or/and new windows) and by using more efficient HVAC 
systems. They came up with the conclusion that schools, offices and accommodation facilities 
with consumption of more than 190 kWh/(m2a) showed the highest financial potential with 
retrofit costs of 0.04-0.08 €/kWh if their energy use was lowered to approximately 90-100 
kWh/(m2a) (Thorsten, et al., 2016). 
Finally, in 2017 Niemelä et al. presented cost-optimal renovation solutions for office buildings 
built in 1970’s and 1980’s in cold climates. Specifically, they analyzed optimal combinations of 
renovation measures and HVAC system setpoints for optimization of thermal comfort conditions 
and users’ productivity. They concluded that 65% return on investment and 63% reduction of 
CO2 emissions could be achieved in owner occupied office buildings, when energy efficiency and 
indoor environment conditions were improved at the same time and reported a ground source

3. Regulations 
In this chapter a brief description of the relevant to the examined case regulations is provided.  
The given regulations refer to the energy use of the building and the indoor environment 
conditions. 
3.1 Energy 
According to Danish Building Regulations (BR, 2015) §7.4.3.1 an existing building can be 
categorized in a renovation class if its requirement for supplied energy is improved by at least 
30 kWh/m2 per year after a renovation measure. Specifically, according to §7.4.3.3 office 
buildings may be classified as renovation class 1 when their total energy consumption for 
heating, ventilation, cooling, domestic hot water and lighting per m2 of heated floor area does 
not exceed:

Moreover, according to §7.4.1(4) from BR15 structural measures may be considered “cost-

2015)). 
Finally, for buildings which are provided with combined types of energy and comply with the 
renovation classes a factor of 2.5 for electricity and a factor of 1 for district heating is used. The 
corresponding factors used for buildings, which comply with the requirements of BR15 are 2.5 
for electricity and 0.8 for district heating.

4. Methodology 
This chapter describes the procedure that was followed for the dynamic simulation of the 
building performance in IDA ICE software for all the examined cases. In the beginning of the 
chapter a general description of the studied building is provided, while in the second part all 
the input data for the simulation of both the existing and the renovation cases are presented. 
4.1 Building description 
Copenhagen Business Park (CBP) is an 8-storey (ground floor and 7 floors) office building 
located in Hvidøvre, Stamholmen 147-159, around 10 km southwest of Copenhagen. The 
building was constructed in 1982 and covers a total area of 67.000 m2 consisting one of 
Denmark’s biggest multi-user office properties. It is divided in 3 parts as it is seen in Figure 7, 
one central, the smallest one, above the street, one to the left (east side) and one to the right 
(west side). The current study was focused on the west part of the building, as it was going to 
undergo renovation during the period of the thesis conduction. Therefore, most of its floors (2nd, 
3rd, 4th) were unoccupied, which facilitated the inspection of the examined spaces, systems and 
components. However, it has to be noted that the building was studied as it was fully occupied 
so as to simulate realistic operation conditions.  
 
Figure 7: Northern view of Copenhagen Business Park 
The interior design of the examined building as it currently is was not demonstrated in the 
drawings that were provided. More specifically in the given drawings the building was pictured 
as it was initially constructed before it underwent minor renovations. In order to update the 
drawings of the studied building all the internal distances and spaces of the 2nd, 3rd and 4th floor 
(with total area of around 6000 m2) were measured with a laser distance measuring tool. 
The updated floor plans of the examined floors in AutoCAD software are illustrated in the 
following pictures (Figures 8, 9 and 10). As it can be seen, the selected floors had similar 
arrangements with repetitive modules. There were both open plan and single offices placed in 
the north and south facades and two long corridors running along the floors connecting the 
external offices with the internal common spaces. In these spaces meeting rooms as well as some 
multi-purpose rooms, such as kitchen, storage-paper and copy machine rooms were located. 
Toilets were located next to each staircase.

representative as possible- model of the current building. 
In the first meeting information about the current conditions of the building were obtained:

indoor air quality.

Internal heat gains 
Occupants 
Each of the studied offices was occupied between 08.00 and 17.00 with lunch break from 12.00 
to 13.00 from Monday to Friday.  In the two meeting-rooms an occupancy schedule of 2 hours 
per day was used. Regarding the occupancy density, the suggested values from Annex C (prEN 
16798-1rev5, 2016) were used. Specifically, 10 m2/occupant were recommended for single offices 
and 17 m2/occupant for open plan offices. Since there were both single and open plan offices in 
the examined building it was decided to conservatively use the value of 10 m2/occupant for both 
of them. The corresponding value for the meeting rooms was 2 m2/occupant. The activity level 
of the occupants was taken equal to 1.2 MET for sedentary work in offices and meeting rooms 
according to (DS/EN ISO 8996, 2004). 
Equipment 
The equipment was considered according to the recommended internal heat gain values from 
AHRAE load calculation applications manual (Spitler, 2014). Specifically, 1 desktop computer 
of 73 W for each occupant (Table 6.8 in the manual) was used to simulate the equipment heat 
gains in offices. The equipment schedule was set to 08.00 – 17.00 with lunch break from Monday 
to Friday. No equipment was taken into account in the meeting-rooms.

namely 36W, were installed in the offices, while only small lighting units of 36 W were installed 
in the corridors (Figure 19). The lighting schedule was set to 08.00 – 17.00 from Monday to 
Friday for the offices, corridors and internal stairs. No lighting was considered in the escape 
zone (flugt). In the meeting-rooms the lighting schedule was set to 2 hours operation per day 
(same as the occupancy schedule).

4.2.2 Renovation cases  
After the creation, simulation and evaluation of the initial model in IDA ICE, 7 updated models 
corresponding to 4 different renovation cases and 3 separate case were built, run and analysed. 
The aim of the suggested cases was the highest possible reduction of the energy use and the 
optimisation of the indoor environment in the building. The renovation scenarios included 
changes in the building envelope, namely the windows, as well as in the heating, cooling and 
ventilation system. Finally, the separate case included the implementation of Thermally Active 
Building System in the building. 
Changes applicable to all cases 
New lighting units 
Before proceeding with the renovation cases it was decided to replace the conventional lighting 
units since it was evident that their power consumption was too high (Table 12 and Table 13).  
The current incandescent lighting units were removed and new LED lighting units which 
combined high efficacy and visual comfort were used. The selected units were the DN570B 
LED20S/840 PSE-E C CU3 WH with an initial luminous flux of 2200 lm and an initial input 
power of 17.4 W (Philips Lighting, 2017). According to (DS/EN 12464-1, 2011) the acceptable 
illuminance level for offices and meeting rooms is 500 lux=500 lm/m2 and for corridors 100 
lux=100 lm/m2. These values were multiplied by the area of each space to find the total required 
number of lumens. Afterwards, the total number of lumens was divided by the luminous flux of 
each unit in order to identify the demanded number of units (Table 15 and Table 16).

5. Results & Discussion 
In this chapter results regarding the indoor environment parameters and the energy use of each 
simulated case in IDA ICE are provided. It is checked whether these results comply with the 
requirements of BR15 and EN15251, as they are mentioned in Chapter 3 Regulations. 
Comparative results between the various cases are also presented. It should be noted that the 
building´s indoor conditions are only presented for some representative rooms depending on 
their size, orientation and location in the floor-plan, as the results in the other rooms were 
similar. These rooms are Offices 2.01, 2.05, 2.13 and 2.19 and Meeting room 1 and can be seen 
in the building’s floor plan in Chapter 4 Methodology. 
5.1 Basic case (existing building) 
Regarding the indoor conditions in the given building, the thermal environment did not fully 
comply with the requirements of EN 15251. In most of the offices the percentage of time where 
the indoor temperatures did not fall within the limits of neither of the 3 suggested categories 
was quite high, reaching a value of 54% in southern Office 2.18. This high percentage was mostly 
due to overheating during the summer months. The percentage of occupied time where there 
was compliance with the thermal comfort categories of EN 15251 is illustrated for five 
representative rooms (Figure 35). It is observed that this percentage had the lowest value in 
Meeting room 1, where the operative temperature was above 27OC during 86% of the occupied 
time. This happened because the meeting room was cooled by the supplied air which had the 
same flow rate as the one in the offices, although the occupancy density of the meeting room 
was quite higher. In general, these high percentages could be justified by the fact that there was 
no cooling system in the building and also no scheduled opening of the windows (natural 
ventilation).

6. Cost estimation 
In this chapter a cost analysis of the provided renovation solutions was attempted. The aim of 
the analysis was to identify potential energy savings and to investigate whether the suggested 
measures were cost-effective. For this reason, the method suggested in (BR, 2015), with the 
calculation of the cost-effective factor, in combination with the method of Net Present Value 
(NPV), Internal Rate of Return (IRR) and Pay Back Time (PBT) indices were used.  
 
6.1 Estimation of investment cost 
The investment cost for each renovation suggestion was calculated by adding the costs of all the 
required operations for each case. 
In all the renovation cases an initial investment cost for the installation of new lighting units, 
for changing the system from CAV to VAV and for labor work was considered. The light 
installation cost and the labor cost were taken equal to 100 DKK/unit and 450 DKK/person/hour 
respectively, according to information provided by NIRAS. The number of workers was 15 and 
they were considered to work for 3 weeks (8 hours per day). The cost for changing the ventilation 
system from CAV to VAV was considered 2,255,722.5 DKK according to a relevant study case 
regarding the retrofit of an office building (PECI & U.S. Department of Energy, 2011). 
In the radiant panels renovation case, the total investment cost also consisted of the cost for the 
removal of the radiators and the cost for the installation of radiant panels. The cost for removing 
the radiators was estimated to 1000 DKK/radiator according to NIRAS’ data. The installation 
of radiant panels was estimated at 412 DKK/m2 (Uponor, 2017) .  
In the fan coils renovation case, the cost for their installation was taken equal to 400 DKK/m2 
according to information given by NIRAS. 
Finally, the cost for installing passive and active chilled beams was 400 DKK/ m2, provided from 
NIRAS’ information. The ducting cost in the case of active chilled beams was calculated at 
191,863 DKK (Homeadvisor.com:install ducts and vents, 2017).  
Regarding maintenance cost, for passive and active beams it was the cleaning of the beam and 
it was considered 803.2 DKK per five years based on a life cycle cost calculation (Virta, et al., 
2007). In the case of fan coils the maintenance cost was significant since it included both the 
filter change and the fan coil cleaning and was taken equal to 48,189.1 DKK (Virta, et al., 2007). 
In the case of radiant panels no maintenance cost was taken into account (Ducoterra LLC, 2014). 
It was considered that the renovation measures were applied to 2nd, 3rd and 4th floor.  
The results of these calculations are shown in Table 39. The highest investment cost was 
observed in the renovation case of active chilled beams while the lowest in the renovation case 
of passive chilled beams.

7. Conclusions 
In the current thesis a parametric analysis of an existing office building, Copenhagen Business 
Park, with respect to energy and comfort performance optimization was conducted. The study 
focused on the examination of a typical floor of the building and dynamic simulations with IDA 
ICE simulation tool were performed. In total ten variations were studied, including 
investigation of the current building and renovation suggestions.  The renovation suggestions 
contained change of the building’s HVAC system through installation of radiant panels, fan 
coils, active and passive chilled beams, as well as removal of the external glass façade and 
implementation of new windows. Also, a separate case with three variations of implementation 
of Thermo Active Building System was examined. The conclusions of this investigation are 
presented below. 
7.1 Existing building 
The current indoor conditions in the existing building were unsatisfying, as there was 
overheating in the offices and meeting rooms during summer. Specifically, the operative 
temperature exceeded the value of 27OC during around 50% of the occupied time. Regarding 
indoor air quality, CO2 concentration was below 900 ppm in all the offices but it reached rather 
high values in the meeting rooms. Relative humidity was also very high in the meeting rooms, 
while in the offices it was below 70% complying with the requirements of EN15251 Category 
III. The factor which caused this poor indoor environment was the absence of a cooling system. 
Moreover, although the mechanical ventilation had been adjusted to operate during 24 hours, 
it was not enough to cover occupants’ needs. The problem was even more evident in the meeting 
rooms where there was high occupancy density but the supply air flow rate was the same as in 
the offices. 
Regarding energy use, it was observed that although the building was equipped with radiator 
units for heating purposes, their operation consisted a very small part of the used heating 
energy. The biggest part came from the Air Handling Unit operation which supplied air at 22OC 
during winter. Therefore, a very high amount of energy was used by the ventilation system, 
since it was the system covering the building’s heating needs. Except for the AHU, the existing 
fluorescent lighting units used also a large quantity of energy. The total yearly energy use 
according to IDA ICE simulation was 133.1 kWh/m2. 
7.2 Renovation cases and TABS 
With the application of radiant panels, fan coils, passive and active chilled beams the total 
annual energy use was reduced to around 51 kWh/m2 for all cases, corresponding to a more than 
50% reduction in relation to the existing building. According to this, the building complied with 
the requirements of Renovation Class I (BR15). With all four systems, heating consisted the 
biggest part of energy use and then lighting (since new lighting units had been installed in the 
building). Regarding cooling and ventilation, small variations were observed in the operation of 
the various systems, with the radiant panels being the most efficient cooling choice as they used 
the lowest cooling energy than the other systems. Regarding fan coils, active and passive chilled 
beams, they used almost the same amount of cooling energy. The above results indicated that 
IDA ICE did not take into account significant variations in these systems’ operation.

The operative temperature was kept into the range of 20OC-26OC for the occupied time during 
summer and winter, belonging to thermal comfort Categories I and II of EN15251 for all the 
systems. CO2 concentration was below 900 ppm in the offices for all systems while it was 
approximately 950 ppm in meeting rooms due to their higher occupancy density and absence of 
operable windows. Finally, relative humidity levels in both offices and meeting rooms were 
within Category II during summer and Category III during winter due to values lower than 
20%. Condensation problems did not occur. 
When the installation of Thermo Active Building Systems in the building floor was examined, 
the annual total energy was again reduced to 51.5 kWh/m2. The operative temperature in the 
offices and meeting rooms complied also with the requirements of EN15251 Categories I and II. 
CO2 and relative humidity levels were within the limits of Category II and III respectively. 
Finally, when a lower slab thickness and a smaller temperature difference between supply and 
return water was tested in the TABS model, it was observed that IDA ICE did not take into 
account these differences providing very similar results of the building’s total energy use and 
indoor environment parameters. 
7.3 Daylight conditions 
The daylight conditions in the given building could be characterized poor for a work space, since 
the average daylight factor was around 1.5% in the offices. These conditions could be attributed 
to the building’s architectural design. Specifically, the slab of the upper balcony and the top part 
of the external glass façade, which both behaved as an overhang, covered the higher area in the 
height of each floor, from which in principle the larger amount of daylight penetrates the 
building. When the upper part of the glass façade was removed from the building model, an 
increase of 0.1%-0.2% was observed in the offices’ average daylight factor, which still did not 
comply with the requirements of BR15. Only when new glazing units with higher light 
transmittance were applied in the building, the average daylight factor increased by 0.4%-0.5% 
reaching values of 1.9%-2%, which were considered marginally satisfactory for a working space 
(BR15).
