Abstract
The age of technology has arrived and its numerous applications to real-world problems
are imminent. Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), commonly known as drones, are a subject
that has acquired a lot of prominence in the last years. Especially when Amazon revolutionized
the logistics industry by introducing drones to deliver goods. This idea has not been ignored
by many companies that have set to work to oﬀer similar services and to improve their
economic activity.
One of the most classic problems within Operations Research, and normally used for last-
mile delivery operations, is the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), whose deﬁnition can be
altered to incorporate the UAVs and formulate a problem combining the operation of the
trucks and these aircrafts. Firstly introduced by Murray and Chu [40] with its formulation
the Flying Sidekick Traveling Salesman Problem (FSTSP), propose a new mathematical
problem where a drone can cooperate with the truck, being launched and recovered at certain
visits on the truck route so that the drone can deliver goods simultaneously to customers.
This introduction considerably reduces the operational cost of the routes, being possible to
reduce the total fuel consumption for the truck and operate the routes more eﬃciently.
A mathematical model will be formulated deﬁning a problem similar to the FSTSP, but
with new considerations in the number of vehicles, the truck capacity and the completion time
of the routes. The corresponding problem will be named the Drone-Vehicle Routing Problem
(D-VRP). Due to the diﬃculty of solving large instances to optimality in reasonable time,
metaheuristics such as Simulated Annealing and Large Neighborhood Search will be tested for
the obtaining of close-optimal solutions. Finally, the developed algorithms will be compared
with the exclusion of the drones in the routes, among other analyses, to investigate how
beneﬁcial is the cooperation of drones and vehicles in the routes.

Introduction
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), better known as drones, have been of great importance over
the last decade. As the name given, these are vehicles that are able to stay in the air in an
automated and controlled way thanks to their engines of reaction. Initially, these vehicles, like
Operations Research, were created for military purposes, developed after the First World War to
improve military strategies, to develop innovative weapons of combat and to train the operators
of anti-aircraft guns [57]. With the advancement of today’s technology and the conclusion of
wars, Operations Research was no longer limited to the military sphere, but it expanded its
ﬁeld of study to other branches of knowledge, once scientists learned to apply their knowledge
to the civil sector. In the same way, the drones were used for other tasks, once the technology
was developed suﬃciently. Among the many applications, the transport of parcels, food or other
goods stands out, which is currently a fact, with many companies studying it [22][33][42].
Recently, the delivery of goods using drones reached a new high. It all started when JeﬀBezos,
Amazon’s CEO, announced on a 60-minutes CBS broadcast that the company was developing
the idea of using UAVs for the delivery of small commodities [54]. Arguing that it is not science
ﬁction, the Amazon company intends to launch its program for the delivery of goods from
warehouses to customers, or simply moving goods between warehouses, with the use of its
Prime Air drone for the year 2017 [66].
Figure 1: Latest prototype drone Prime Air from Amazon.
However, they are not alone in this commercial career. Later, DHL stated it was already de-
veloping a similar project for the delivery of medicines and other goods considered as urgent
on a small island in northern Germany, obtaining better results than Amazon Prime Air [25].
In addition, a similar project is being developed by Google X, developing the drone ’Project
Wing’ with related properties as a plane. Moving vertically and horizontally, it drops the mer-
chandise from the air, when it arrives to the location, with the help of a wire to guarantee a
safe landing [39]. But Amazon, Google and DHL are not the only companies looking forward to
the transport of goods with the use of drones. Many companies have begun joining this group
of parcel-deliveries with drones, highlighting UPS, FedEx or Domino’s Pizza [15][64][8][13].
The use of drones can lead to controversial issues, which can be dangerous for people, caus-
ing accidents or problems due to misuse or inexperience of pilots. It can also be detrimental
to aircraft, being able to be in conﬂict when ﬂying at certain heights. Moreover, drones could

has created legislation that limit the use of drones for commercial activities when ﬂying by the

Recently, the new rules that were presented by the FAA on the use of unmanned aerial vehicles
for commercial activities will be of larger ease for the companies, but will continue having a

to ﬂy commercial drones, which has been relaxed and is now easier to obtain a permit to ﬂy
these aircrafts. Also, with respect to the load that the drones can carry, the combined total
weight cannot exceed 55 pounds. Nonetheless, the drone being within the visual line of sight
during the operation is still a prevailing rule. The latter regulations will mean that the plans
for many companies working on drone delivery are extended in the long term. However, other
companies, such real estate, aerial photography, construction or other industries using drones
for basic functions, beneﬁt as new regulations facilitate these operations.
The rules of drone usage vary depending on each country, and many of them merge govern-
ment laws with other guidelines recommended by aviation authorities. However, commercial
operations are similar in many of these countries [38]. Furthermore, the current regulations for
the use of drones for commercial delivery activities make almost impossible for companies to
perform these tasks. The limitation on the drones’ weight, speed, ﬂight distance and altitude
means that these deliveries do not reach very far from the launch point [44]. Nevertheless, it is
still an interesting problem to consider, the advance of technology will come and the regulations
will change. It is a matter of time for the drones to work independently or together with trucks
for the delivery of goods.

problem addressed in this thesis is an extension of this case, where several vehicles are used.

2.1
Delivery Applications
Undoubtedly, technological progress has allowed drones to be increasingly used in the civilian
sector. One of the most immediate applications is the delivery of goods. This means that many
problems that address this issue are modiﬁed and studied to include the use of drones in the
delivery operations. The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) and the Vehicle Routing Problem
(VRP) are two of the most researched problems in Operations Research and, among the various
applications, they are closely related to delivery operations. There exists a numberless body of
literature for these problems as it can be seen in [5] and [17]. Among the several variants of
the problem, there are small works regarding these type of problems in combination with the
unmanned aerial vehicles. Nonetheless, there are conceptually related variants of these problems
in the literature that present many similarities with the aforementioned problems.
The main paper on which this thesis is inspired is the FSTSP formulation by [40]. This is an
optimization problem of parcel deliveries using a single truck with a single drone. The objective
is to reduce the duration time of the route to service all customers and return both vehicles to
the depot. The vehicles work in synchronization to deliver all the goods to the customers, being
able for the UAV to separate and reconnect with the delivery truck multiple times along the
route. In addition, the paper presents a diﬀerent problem that is applicable to scenarios in which
the distribution center is close to a signiﬁcant proportion of customer that may be serviced by
the UAV, the Parallel Drone Scheduling TSP (PDSTSP). In this case, a single vehicle and a ﬂeet
of UAVs work together to deliver the goods to customers, although synchronization between
the UAV and the truck is not needed since the truck operates independently to the remaining
customers. Furthermore, saving heuristics for both formulations are provided by the authors,
obtaining considerable improvements in the solution with respect to the truck-only case.
Drones will bring a new approach to Same-Day Deliveries (SDD), especially if the problem
presents the dynamic feature. Although drones cannot replace the entire ﬂeet of conventional
delivery vehicles, they reduce signiﬁcantly the operation costs in combination [28]. Similar to the
PDSTSP, [62] is the ﬁrst article to study the dynamic vehicle routing problem with the inclusion
of drones in a heterogeneous vehicle ﬂeet. It aims to maximize the expected number of customers
served with SDD and it is based on a Markov decision model, followed by a Parametric Policy
Function Approximation that leads to high quality solutions, where the parameter divides the
area into two zones, according to the travel distance, deﬁning a threshold from the depot for

the vehicles and for choosing the vehicle to preferably serve the zone. It was proven that the
heuristic is eﬀective serving nearby customers with vehicles and further customers with drones.
There are more problems within the last-mile delivery concept in which a truck collaborates
with a drone to make deliveries that are worth noting, such as the TSP-D [2]. The TSP-D is very
similar to the FSTSP, but it is assumed that the drone is faster than the truck by a factor α and

maximum attainable gains that can be achieved by using the two vehicles in unison versus the
simple case of using a truck exclusively. Moreover, this problem is considering that the truck can
wait for the drone in the same position that it is launched, aspect that was not considered in
FSTSP. An heuristic approach is presented, based in route ﬁrst - cluster second procedure with
a greedy and exact partitioning algorithm as well as the consideration of iterative improvement
procedure to ﬁnd a solution.
[24] considers another problem for the parcel delivery with UAVs, also called TSP-D, but not
essentially the same problem. Like FSTSP, the launch and recovery operations for the drone
are again restricted to diﬀerent locations. In addition, it is this problem with which it shares
the same features. Nevertheless, it presents a much more compact formulation, based on the
Column Generation. The paper provides two diﬀerent heuristics approaches, known as route
ﬁrst - cluster second and cluster ﬁrst - route second. The eﬀectiveness of both heuristics is
presented, outperforming in objective both the TSP and the FSTSP as the size increases.
There is another article that deals with the use in tandem of the truck and the drone [20].
Nonetheless, the objective of the problem is to determine the optimal number of launch sites
and locations of truck stops and how theses aﬀect the delivery time and the energy requirements.
Solutions approaches based on K-means clustering and Genetic Algorithms are proposed to solve
the problem. The paper states that triﬂing time improvements are obtained if the drone cannot
reach at least twice the speed of the truck. Moreover, for more real time savings, trucks should
be equipped with more than one drone.
Although there is not synchronization between the drones and the trucks in operation, it is
worth mentioning the Vehicle Routing Problem for Drone Delivery [14]. It is a variant of the
VRP developed speciﬁcally for drone delivery scenarios. This problem can be seen as an initial
step for the new approach, where the energy consumption model for multi-rotor drones is stud-
ied. A Simulated Annealing heuristic is proposed to ﬁnd sub-optimal solutions to the diﬀerent
scenarios. Moreover, an interesting approach for this problem could be considered in parallel
when solving the route problem for the trucks alone since it is a similar to the PDSTSP, where
there exist close customers to the distribution centers.
Another important paper recently published is the one presented by Choi and Schonfeld [11].
This article deals with an automated drone delivery system, rather than considering it in co-
operation with trucks. In this case, the drone can carry numerous packages according to its
maximum capacity and ﬂight range, thus being able to serve a visiting area more eﬃciently. In
addition, the model considers the endurance of the battery to provide a more realistic character
to the model, since it is the main source for a drone for ﬂying.
Finally, the drone literature is mainly focused on the use of drones exclusively or with the
combination of a single truck, when regarding delivery operations. There seems to be few articles

Surveillance and Data Gathering Applications
Drones also play a fundamental role in carrying out surveillance tasks. Due to their size and
ease of moving in the air, they can be equipped with cameras to perform the jobs in question.
There are numerous areas where drones can facilitate this type of task. As an example may be
highlighted the use of drones by the police. These gadgets can be used to patrol the streets of the
city to monitor the activity and being able to act quickly in case of disturbances more eﬀectively.
In addition, equipped with heat sensor cameras, drones can be instrumental in carrying out
search-rescue operations more successfully, as they can easily ﬂy over areas of limited access
and identify individuals. It is expected that in the future will be vital the assistance of the
drones in these missions to help teams on the ground.
However, the applications of the drones equipped with cameras are not conﬁned exclusively
to surveillance tasks. It is notable that these devices can bring new approaches and strategies
to many activities, such as taking photographs and/or videos for journalistic reporting, which
is a form of journalism that is closer and informative to viewers. Likewise, high quality, ther-
mographic and multispectral cameras can be added to these aircraft. Thermographic studies
and aerophotogrammetric surveys can be made more economically and with less impact for the
environment. Capturing images for the collection of information and inspection tasks with the
use of drones involves a considerable reduction of eﬀorts that helps the workers to complete
their tasks. In Spain, companies such as DroneScan oﬀer this type of services, being an example
of the large number of companies that arise and bet on these new types of technology [16].

problems deﬁned in the Delivery Applications Section 2.1. Some examples of problems deﬁned
for this application can be discussed in this section. Firstly, it can be seen the Mobile Target
Covering Problem [47], a surveillance problem using a set of UAVs that must constantly monitor
the movement of each target. It is observable that it is type of Covering Problem that can

Military Applications
Although drones presents numberless applications in the day-to-day life, these small devices
were ﬁrstly created for military activities. Due to their size and their ability to be remotely
controlled, drones have become a perfect machine to be used in warfare. This can be also seen

areas. Although military drones are often related to defense and surveillance strategies, where
the main objective is reducing the ﬂight time or visiting the greatest number of nodes and they
are more focused in the equations of motion of the UAV, the problems can be studied to be
applied to other areas.
Drones can be equipped with visual sensors, GPS radars and even weapons. In this way, drones
can be launched from the military base and inspect a conﬂict area more easily. However, their
main function is to help troops on the battleﬁeld, avoiding threats and minimizing the casualties
of civilians. Furthermore, because these machines are more precise when it comes to attack, can

method to compute rapidly the cost of many diﬀerent trajectories, decoupling the assignment
and the trajectory problems. This paper is interesting for future works, where the truck can be
equipped with several drones and planning can be operated more eﬃciently.

The Drone-Vehicle Routing Problem
In this chapter the general mathematical formulation for the Drone-Vehicle Routing Problem is
presented. First, Section 3.1 will provide a brief description of the problem in question in words.
Section 3.2 will present the ﬁrst formulation for the Drone-Vehicle Routing Problem with the
corresponding modiﬁcations and adjustments considered, describing each of the constraints as
they are presented. The formulation will be deﬁned as Drone-Vehicle Routing Problem Model I
(D-VRP-I). Similarly, Section 3.3 presents a mathematical formulation that extends the model
presented by Murray and Chu for the inclusion of the new vehicles in this thesis. This model
will be deﬁned as Drone-Vehicle Routing Problem Model II (D-VRP-II). A short discussion of
the main diﬀerences with the mathematical model of the Flying Sidekick Traveling Salesman
Problem presented in [40] can be found in Section 3.4, as well as a visual presentation of the
prohibited movements in the problem. In Section 3.5, the correct selection of the numerous
parameters present in the problem is discussed. Finally, Section 3.6 shows a small example
solved to optimality followed by a graphical representation of the problem in a time-line for a
better understanding.
3.1
Deﬁnition of the problem
A ﬂeet of driver-operated delivery trucks equipped each of them with a single Unmanned Aerial
Vehicle (UAV or drone) delivers packages to a given set of customers, each of whom must
be served exactly once by either the driver-operated delivery truck or the UAV operating in
coordination with the truck. Each truck with its corresponding UAV on board must depart
from, and return to, a single depot exactly once. The two vehicles may depart (or return)
either in tandem or independently to the depot. When the drone is not operational, it will be
transported by the truck, conserving battery power. The drones can be dispatched from the
vehicle and pickup-ed again by the same vehicle in a diﬀerent location multiple times along the
truck route. However, the drone can visit only a single customer due to the limited payload
capacity and there is a maximum ﬂying endurance due to battery capacity. It is considered a
time associated with the launch and recovery of the drone, as well as a service time for the
customers when delivering the packages. The trucks present a limited capacity that must be
respected and the route of the trucks should not exceed a certain time limit during the day of
operation. The objective is to minimize the overall cost of the operation of using the ﬂeet of
vehicles while respecting the capacity and time constraint and meeting the customers’ demand.

3.2
Mathematical Formulation for the D-VRP-I
In this section, a mathematical formulation for the Drone-Vehicle Routing Problem - Model I
(D-VRP-I) will be presented. The mathematical formulation is an alternative formulation to
the MIP problem The Flying Sidekick Traveling Salesman Problem presented in [40], it is taking
into account the time at which a truck and/or an UAV visits a customer. The truck and the
UAV must be synchronized in time during the truck’s route, except at the end, where the truck
and the UAV can arrive separately to the depot. Moreover, the capacity and the completion
time of the routes are now considered. The mathematical formulation is enriched with an extra
index regarding the truck assigned to the route. Furthermore, this formulation presents diﬀerent
binary variables indicating if a launch or recovery operation is performed in the particular node.
3.2.1
Sets
The following sets will be used for this formulation. Remark that there are several subset of
nodes, regarding some features about the location of the nodes in the problem.
• C = {1, 2, ..., c} ≡Set of customers.
• C′ ⊆C ≡Subset of customers that may be serviced by the UAV.
• D = {0, c + 1} ≡Depot nodes indicating the beginning and end of the route.
• N = {0, 1, ..., c, c + 1} ≡Set of all nodes.
• N0 = {0, 1, 2, ...c} ≡Set of nodes from which a vehicle may depart.
• N+ = {1, 2, ..., c, c + 1} ≡Set of nodes to which a vehicle may arrive.
• V = {1, ..., m} ≡Set of vehicles.

3.3
Mathematical Formulation for the D-VRP-II
In this section, another mathematical formulation for the Drone-Vehicle Routing problem will
be presented, corresponding to the second model. It is an extension of Murray’s and Chu’s
model for the multiple vehicles case. This formulation presents many similarities with the latter
formulation presented in Section 3.2, however, it is keeping the formulation as in [40]. The
mathematical formulation is enriched with an extra index regarding the truck assigned to the
route.
3.3.1
Sets
The formulation is deﬁned with the exact same sets as deﬁned in Section 3.2.1 but it requires
the addition of new notation for the identiﬁcation of the possible three-nodes sorties from where
an UAV can operate in the problem. Therefore, let P be the set of possible sorties, represented
by the tuples ⟨i, j, k⟩. The ﬁrst node represents the launch position of the UAV, the second node
represents the customer that is visited by the UAV, and ﬁnally, the third node represents the
recovery position of the UAV. Hence, an element ⟨i, j, k⟩belongs to the set P if the following
conditions hold:
• The launch position i ∈N0 of a tuple is the location from which an UAV can be launched,
corresponding to the location from which a truck can depart.
• The delivery position j ∈{C′ : j ̸= i} of a tuple is the set of customer that can be serviced
by the UAV diﬀerent from the launch position i.
• The rendezvous position or recovery position k ∈{N+ : k ̸= i, k ̸= j, τ ′
ij + τ ′
jk + SeD
j ≤e}
of a tuple is the location to which the UAV can be recovered by the truck while respecting
the battery’s life endurance.

The parameters used in the formulation are exactly the same as the parameters deﬁned in
Section 3.2.2.

4.1
Introduction to Metaheuristics
Normally, heuristics rely on search techniques to ﬁnd new solutions to an optimization problem.
Considering the solution space Ωas the set of all possible solutions that are feasible in an
optimization problem, an element s ∈Ωwill represent a possible solution, not necessarily the
optimal one. Therefore, it is deﬁned the neighborhood of a solution s as the set of solution in
Ωwithin a certain distance to the solution s. It is denoted as N(s). Figure 13 provides a visual
representation of the neighborhood of a solution.
Figure 13: Example of a neighborhood of a solution in the space solution
Hence, it is possible to deﬁne the local optimum of an optimization problem as the optimal
solution within a certain deﬁned neighborhood of the current solution, i.e. considering a cost
function f (and a minimization problem), it is said that s∗is the local optimum of an optimiza-
tion problem if there does not exist any solution in the neighborhood of the current solution s
with a more reduced cost. In other words, if f(s∗) ≤f(s′) for all s′ ∈N(s). The global optimum
is deﬁned as the optimal solution within the solution space, i.e. it is said that s∗is the global
optimum of an optimization problem if f(s∗) ≤f(s) for all s ∈Ω. Assuming a minimization
problem, Figure 14 shows an example of a local and global optimum in the search space.
Vehicle Routing Problems (VRP) are one of the most famous Combinatorial Optimization and
Integer Programming problems in Operational Research. It is known to be a generalization of

Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) and hence it is classiﬁed as an NP-hard problem. Obtaining
the optimal solution through exact methods can be time-consuming as the number of customers
increase. In particular, it can be seen that D-VRP is an extension of VRP, with the inclusion
of UAVs in the vehicles that can operate simultaneously with the trucks to visit the customers.
The computational complexity of NP-Hard problems causes its resolution to be a time-consuming
task. Various exact methods have been applied to solve VRP, among them can be found diﬀer-
ent methods such as Branch-and-Bound, Branch-and-Cut and Branch-and-Price among many
others. These methods are based on systematic enumeration of candidate solutions to obtain
the optimal solution, since the method is responsible for detecting in which branch the optimal
solution cannot be found in order to prune those branches. Techniques such as the Column
Generation or Dantzig-Wolfe Decomposition are common in these approaches. Nonetheless, if
the optimal solution or a good lower bound is not the ﬁrst interest, heuristics might be the way
to follow. Heuristics are methods that can provide good solutions within short computational
times, although there is no guarantee that the heuristic solution is optimal. It is possible to
distinguish diﬀerent type of heuristics:
• Constructive: Attempt to construct quickly a simple solution from scratch. Normally
the algorithm returns a poor quality solution, but this approach is mainly used to generate
initial solutions for more advanced heuristics.
• Improvement: From an obtained solution, the method attempts to improve the current
solution to a better solution. According to the problem, it is possible to deﬁne diﬀer-
ent types of neighborhoods, which may lead to diﬀerent solutions. However, only local-
optimum solutions are guaranteed. These algorithms provide better quality solution than
the constructive algorithms. Some examples of neighborhood that can be deﬁned in the
VRP are Relocate Customer, String Cross, λ-opt, String Relocate and String Swap [21][32].

4.2
Simulated Annealing
The Simulated Annealing (SA) algorithm is a metaheuristic based on stochastic processes that
is responsible for the search of global optimal solutions within a suﬃciently large search space.
This method was ﬁrst introduced by Scott Kirkpatrick [29] in 1983 and by Vlado Černý [9] in
1985, being an adaptation of the Monte Carlo method of the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm to
generate state samples of a thermodynamic system.
This algorithm is inspired by the annealing process of steel and ceramics in metal works. This
technique consists of heating and then slowly cooling the material so as to alter its physical
properties. At the beginning of the process, the atoms of the material are heated, increasing its
energy and allowing them to move from its initial position, and as it cools, new conﬁgurations
with less energy than the initial one are formed with greater probability, and thus to be able
to escape from a local optimum. The Simulated Annealing algorithm begins with a high tem-
perature, which is cooled slowly iteratively. When the temperature is higher, it is more likely
to accept solutions that are worse than the current one, however, these acceptances cause that
the algorithm can move through the search space being able to avoid the local optimum to
gradually ﬁnd a high-quality solution. This feature is essential for Simulated Annealing, and
makes it an appropriate algorithm for VRP and its variants. The algorithm can be described
generally as follows:

1. Generate an initial solution s0;
2. Save current and best solution s = s∗= s0;
3. Initialize temperature T = Tst;
4. Select randomly a solution from the neighborhood, s′ ∈N(s);
5. Compute the acceptance probability p(s, s′, T) of the current solution s′;
• If f(s′) < f(s), then s = s′;
• If f(s′) ≥f(s) and p(s, s′, T) > rand(0, 1), then s = s′;
6. Save the best solution if found. If f(s) < f(s∗), then s∗= s;
7. Cool temperature according to the cooling ratio. T = αT;
8. If stopping criteria is not met, return to step 4.
The Simulated Annealing Algorithm presents good properties to escape local optimums that
cannot be obtained with basic heuristics such as the Hill Climber [55]. Typically, the neighbor-
hood deﬁned in each iteration is quite limited since it is based on individual moves performed
in a small area of the current solution. The advantage of this algorithm is given by the pos-
sibility of realizing a large number of iterations that allows it to quickly explore a suﬃciently
large area of the solution space. Nevertheless, the initial solution may aﬀect the performance
of the algorithm. Although any initial solution can be deﬁned before calling the metaheuristic,
it may be diﬃcult to escape from the local optimum or ﬁnd the optimal solution if the initial
solution does not present similarities to the optimal solution, being necessary to carry out more
iterations.
Metaheuristics’ performance and deﬁnition are normally controlled by diﬀerent parameters, as in
the case of the Simulated Annealing, and the outcome may be dependent on the setting of these
parameters. The parameters for the Simulated Annealing depends on the initial temperature
(Tst), the ﬁnal temperature (Tf), the number of iterations (it), the cooling rate (α) or the time
(time) among many others. However, depending on how the algorithm conﬁguration is set, some
parameters may be given in terms of others. For this implementation, the Simulated Annealing
algorithm will be deﬁned according to the initial and ﬁnal temperature (Tst and Tf), therefore,
the cooling rate α can be determined from these parameters.
The stopping criteria is an important feature of the algorithm. The algorithm can be termi-
nated according to diﬀerent conditions such as reaching a ﬁxed number of iterations, when the
algorithm has spent a certain amount of running time or if the best found solution has not been
improved in a certain number of iterations. In this case, the chosen stopping criteria is the time
and it will be a ﬁxed parameter introduced by the user. Thus, the algorithm will run until time
has not run out.

of the current solution s and accept it with certain probability. In each iteration, a random

From the formula is possible to distinguish two diﬀerent cases, according the objective of the

• Case 1: If the random solution s′ is better than the current solution s (i.e., f(s′) < f(s)),

is a positive number, the result is always greater or equal to 1 (P ≥1). Therefore, the
new solution is always chosen.
• Case 2: If the random solution s′ is the same or worse than the current solution s (i.e.,
f(s′) ≥f(s)), then the exponential in Equation (80) is raised to a negative number and
the result will be a number between 0 and 1 (0 < P < 1). Therefore, the acceptance of
the new solution depends on the deterioration of the solution and the temperature.
Furthermore, it is observable that the acceptance probability is inversely proportional to the
temperature. Therefore, the algorithm presents higher probabilities to accept worse solution at
the beginning of the algorithm, when the temperature is initialized with high values, and this
probability is decreasing continuously as the temperature cools.

zero. This temperature aﬀects directly to the acceptance probability, therefore, it is another
important feature for the algorithm. Due to the selection of the parameters and the stopping

4.3
Large Neighborhood Search
Local Search heuristics investigate small changes in the current solution, usually constructed
with neighborhood movements such as the relocation of a node, the exchange of two nodes,
among many other small movements. This type of algorithms can be useful to examine in a
short time a large number of solutions from small alterations in the current solution. However,
it is observable that these algorithms will have diﬃculty moving from one promising area of
the neighborhood to another area of the solution space if the problem has strong constraints.
Therefore, it is necessary to deﬁne algorithms that allow to move in the solution space in a more
abrupt way. A larger neighborhood will be deﬁned, where possible changes from one neighbor
to another may be given by a large number of possibilities. This means that the size of the
neighborhood may be large enough to avoid or escape local optimum, one of the problems
facing local search algorithms with simple neighborhood movements. Subsequently, the search
can be improved with the use of metaheuristic techniques. This type of search is known as Very
Large-Scale Neighborhood Search (VLSN).
The basic idea behind VLSN algorithms is that the problems belonging to this class present a
large-sized neighborhood that increases with the size of the instance, and the searching through
the whole neighborhood is a time-consuming task [3]. It would be advisable to only heuristi-
cally visit those parts of the neighborhood that are most promising. One of the most appropriate
VLSN algorithms for mathematical optimization is the Large Neighborhood Search (LNS). This
metaheuristic, ﬁrst introduced by Shaw in 1998 [58], is based on progressively improving an ini-
tial solution through methods of destruction and repair in the current solution. Methods of
destruction destroy part of the current solution, while repair methods are responsible for re-
building the destroyed solution. Typically, the methods of destruction contain some randomness
to be able to destroy diﬀerent parts of the solution and thus to intensify the search for new
solutions, hence the name of large neighborhoods. On the other hand, the methods of repair
present some stochasticity and greedy algorithms associated, to search for the best current so-
lution at the moment but adding some noise to avoid building the same solution if the same
part of the solution has been previously destroyed. Another important feature of this algorithm
is the degree of destruction. If a small part of the solution is destroyed, it can be seen that LNS

4.4.1

To start running the algorithms, an initial solution is needed. This initial solution does not
necessarily have to be close to the optimum, but it is advisable for the best performance of

and easiest way to obtain a feasible solution is through a construction heuristic, which will be
improved by an improvement heuristic. Better solutions closer to the optimum can be obtained
with the application of metaheuristics.

• Construction Algorithm for the truck’s route (Algorithm 25);
• Local Search Algorithm for the truck’s route (Algorithm 26);
• Drone Addition Algorithm (Algorithm 27).

progressively looking for the nearest neighbor to the last visit added as long as the capacity

4.4.3
Computing the Cost Function
The objective function in D-VRP minimizes the cost of traversing the arcs in the problem.
The cost of these arcs is given as a factor of the distance between customers depending on the
consumption of gasoline, its price and the type of vehicle used. Once the solution is obtained,
it is not diﬃcult to compute the total cost of the solution, since it is given by a factor of
the total distance required by the vehicles, however, these calculations are crucial in some
algorithms. Especially during the calculation of the initial solution, the improvement heuristic
and the insertion of sorties in the current solution, which run until more improvements can
be found, and in each iteration performing small changes in the current solution. Although
these changes might not entail a great computing time, especially in small instances, the full
calculation of the solution every time a small change is performed can aﬀect the performance of
the algorithm. These changes will involve the alteration of a small part of the solution, keeping
the rest of the solution the same. It is therefore necessary to implement a delta evaluation of
the objective function and thus to evaluate the solution more eﬃciently. It should be noted
that these evaluations are only useful when calculating small changes, in other methods such as
those described in LNS or in some movements in SA, it does not represent an advantage, and
the objective function is computed after having destroyed and repaired the solution.
First, it can be seen that the delta function can be applied when calculating the relocation of
a visit to the current solution. However, the way in which the visit is returned to the solution
depends on the route and the position. When moving a visit to a forward position on the same

4.4.4
Feasibility Constraints
In the same way that the moves in the current solution are checked through the delta evaluation
in terms of the objective function, it is necessary to check additionally that the attempted moves
are feasible. Therefore, before examining whether a move is promising, one must check whether
it will lead to a feasible solution or not. Feasibility testers are given by the route capacity, drone
ﬂight endurance and the time it takes for a truck to complete its route.
The simplest test is to check if the capacity of the route will be exceeded or not, since it only
depends on the demand of the visit to insert and the current capacity of the vehicle. If when
adding the visit to the route the maximum capacity of the truck is exceeded, the move is not
performed. Likewise, when there is an exchange of visits between routes, the maximum capacity
of each route is checked with the exclusion of the visit to move and the inclusion of the visit
to insert. In this way, the algorithm is saved from computing numerous calculations that would
lead to a infeasible solution.

5.1
Test Instances
The Drone-Vehicle Routing Problem presents quite peculiar characteristics. Due to its deﬁnition,
many of the instances presented in other articles or instances available in the network cannot be
used. Parameters such as completion time, capacity of the routes, customer’s demand and times
associated to the drone, among others, provide the problem a special character. Transformation
of existing instances would not reﬂect correctly the real properties of the problem. It is therefore
that the test instances will be generated automatically following certain distributions according
to parameters and distributions obtained in the literature.
Some parameters related to the deﬁnition of the problem have been discussed previously in
Section 3.4. However, there are other parameters that must be taken into account, and these
are related to the generation of instances, such as the customer generation grid, total number
of customers and individual demand.
Firstly, the customer generation grid in the instances is considered. In order to adapt the
instance to make a more realistic approach, the grid should consider a central depot within a
big city, where the delivery activities occur in a big radius around the central depot. Amazon
argued that drones can operate with a range of about 10 miles from the distribution center
[54]. This statement considers the drone operating independently from the truck, performing a
tour from the distribution center to the customer and coming back to the distribution center.
Therefore, it can be argued that the drone has a dependency of approximately 20 miles from
the distribution center, since it can be picked-up by the truck in another position and continue
operating. Therefore, the customers will be generated in a grid of dimensions n × n around
the central distribution center, where each of its coordinates follows an uniform distribution
U(−n, n). In addition, the central depot is always generated in the coordinates (0,0).
Setting values for n greater than 10 miles might generate scenarios where the drone cannot
work independently, therefore the interaction of the drone with the truck is reﬂected. On the
other hand, generating bigger grids for n > 20 will expand the horizon and cover a fairly large
area, typical of rural areas. These scenarios may occur in reality but they are less likely. The

5.3
Selection of Random Customers in Simulated Annealing
The Simulated Annealing metaheuristic consists of stochastic processes and seeks solutions in
the neighborhood through small alterations of the current solution. Many of these movements
were deﬁned as relocating or swapping visits for other visits along the route. Nonetheless, the
way to select these visits is totally random. It can be seen that a move can be aﬀected by
the poor selection of the random position to relocate the customer by selecting positions that
unnecessarily increase the total travel of the vehicle. It is therefore necessary to get rid of this
random feature for selecting the position to move the customer. In order to deﬁne a smarter
space search in the moves, the selection of the position will be still chosen randomly but from
a set of close positions to the visit in question.
This new feature is present in several of the moves deﬁned above in Section 4.2.2. Whenever
a visit is to be relocated to another location, exchanged with another visit or inserted in the
current solution, the selection will no longer be totally random. An area of near visits will be
deﬁned and a visit will be randomly chosen from the deﬁned area.

location() in Algorithm 14 also presents this new quality. In this way, all methods that search
for a random position or a random route will not only require the current solution, but will call
the customer in question as well. As an example it can be observed in Algorithm 6 the method
to swap the position of a truck-only customer c with another customer in the route of the truck.
Previously, the new customer was found through the RandomTruckCustomers(s) method. With
the addition of this new function, the customer is added as input, searching for new customer in
a nearby area, i.e. RandomTruckCustomers(c,s). Similarly, if a relocation move is called, as in
Algorithm 4 for example, the method would no longer search the route and position randomly
in the solution through the functions RandomRoute(s) and RandomPosition(s,r), but would
get the route and position corresponding to a nearby customer.

Drone Speed Change
During the main setup, the drone speed had been set at 50 mph. This speed is obtained through
the FAA regulations and certain considerations of the companies when testing the ﬁrst proto-

objective function, because the problem minimizes the cost of traversing the arcs. Increasing
the drone speed, the set of feasible solution is expanded, since the drone can travel faster and
reach more customers within the same time. However, the drone has to operate in cooperation
with the truck, and although the number of possible sorties increases in the problem with a
faster drone, these combinations may not contribute to reducing the objective function. Accord-

the same speed as the truck (35 mph) to a speed superior to twice the speed of the truck (75
mph).

studied, being more or less beneﬁcial depending on the deﬁnition of the scenario. Three sce-

representing a rural area with not too many customers spread around the area; 100.10, repre-
senting a city area with more condensed customers; and 150.20, representing a bigger area with
more customers. For each scenario, 3 diﬀerent instances will be selected and the algorithms will
be run 10 times for each setting. Finally, the average performance for each scenario together

6
Conclusion and Future Researches
Amazon, DHL and Workhorse, among many other companies, have been studying the signiﬁcant
impact that the use of drones has on delivery activities. This new technology has drawn the
attention of a great number of researchers who have presented numerous mathematical models
and other mathematical techniques to solve this problem, and thus contribute with an analysis
to the possible beneﬁt that comes with the inclusion of these small aircrafts to the delivery
of merchandise. Especially, the cooperation of the drones along with the trucks on the day of
operation can revolutionize the last-mile delivery, since the routes can be programmed more
eﬃciently.
In this thesis has been studied the cooperation of drones and trucks through an extension of the
model proposed by Murray and Chu [40]. This new model is called the Drone-Vehicle Routing
Problem (D-VRP) and two mathematical models have been proposed to solve this problem as it
has been introduced in Chapter 3. Next, in Chapter 4, due to the diﬃculty of ﬁnding an optimal
solution in a reasonable amount of time when solving larger instances, the Simulated Annealing
(SA) algorithm and the Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) algorithm have been proposed as
possible metaheuristics for solving the new extension, being tested in diﬀerent scenarios with
greater density of customers in order to ﬁnd close-optimal solutions. The beneﬁts associated with
the use of drones in the problem can be observed in the analytical results obtained in Chapter
5, showing the performance of both algorithms in the diﬀerent scenarios and comparing them
with the case of using only trucks for the elaboration of the routes.
Analyzing the results obtained in the latter section, the clear advantage of the use of these
small aircrafts for this type of delivery activities is remarkable. Unlike the model presented
by Murray and Chu, that reduced the total completion time of the route, a new consideration
in the objective function is proposed, minimizing the total cost of the operation. This new
approach proposes a problem that is more focused on the operational cost, since it assumes that
the routes can be developed along the day of operation while respecting its maximum duration,
speciﬁed as a restriction on the problem. The algorithms are able to reduce on average about 20%
the operational cost with respect to the initial solution, demonstrating the eﬀectiveness of the
algorithms to obtain better solutions and escape local optimums. Comparing the performance
results with the case of only using trucks in the problem, the saving are also noteworthy, being
possible for both algorithms to outperform the VRP case. In addition, the initial solution already
by itself obtains good solution in a short time of computation. Remark that the initial solution
also surpasses in most cases the best known results for the problem when it is considered that
there are no drones involved in the routes. This results shows that a good feasible solution for
the D-VRP, corresponding to a local optimum, can produce important saving in the operation
planning of the routes with respect to the classic VRP, emphasizing the impact of drones for
delivery activities.
Due to the deﬁnition of the objective function in the D-VRP, it can be analyzed that experiments
in the changes of the endurance and the drone speed are not at all perceptible in some scenarios.
This may be due to the problem deﬁnition and the number of customers presenting a heavy
demand. The truck is forced to reach those customers, and if these customers are located away
105
==PAGE:115==
106
from the depot, the problem will incur a high cost in the objective. Additionally, some potential
sorties might become infeasible due to the dispatch of the truck those locations, causing that a
number of customers must to be visited only by the truck. On the other hand, by modifying the
maximum payload-capacity and the associated cost for using the drone, a reduction on the total
operational cost can be more easily observed. Routes can be built more eﬃciently by increasing
the number of customers that can be visited by drones. This increase in the transport capacity
of the drone might avoid the dispatch of a truck to visit some remote customers, being possible
to deﬁne better routes by sending drones to those positions. It is clear that better results can
be obtained in the ﬁnal solution if the drones could service all the customers in the problem.
The SA and LNS algorithms have been deﬁned and developed to solve this new problem.
From the results, it can be observed that both algorithms present good performance, with
the obtaining good results and considerable saving during their runs. The deﬁnition of the
neighborhood can aﬀect the performance of the algorithms and it is therefore important that
they are well deﬁned, in order to test neighborhoods of diﬀerent type in the problem. In the
case of Simulated Annealing, the neighborhood deﬁnes small alterations to the current solution
through customer relocations and swaps, while Large Neighborhood Search deﬁnes a larger
neighborhood by removing a larger percentage of customers in the solution and presenting
diﬀerent destroy and repair methods. From the results it can be seen that SA algorithm presents
generally better results in smaller instances (with respect to the number of customers and the
size of the grid), whereas the LNS dominates in larger instances. These neighborhoods can be
extended to include many more possibilities and to be able to hold many more possibilities
during the runs of the algorithms. However, the excessive number of possible moves in the
algorithms could aﬀect their performance, attempting moves that do not beneﬁt the search for
close-optimal solutions. In this way, it would be convenient to consider diﬀerent possibilities of
moving through the space of solutions and to consider only those moves that are beneﬁcial.
During this thesis the theoretical problem of the Drone-Vehicle Routing Problem has been
studied. Although many assumptions have been simpliﬁed to further deﬁne the problem raised,
this study can be extended to more complex cases. First, the static case has been considered. A
future approach to the problem may be the dynamic case, where routes can be altered during the
day of operation through cancellations, break-down of vehicles and other factors related to time
and capacity of the customers. These constraints pose a problem of greater complexity where the
optimal solution may be utopia. It is therefore necessary to develop diﬀerent heuristic methods
that help to solve the problem quickly to come back to activity as fast as possible without
aﬀecting much of the routes.
Other possible extensions to the problem that can successfully increase the parcel-delivery are
related to the number of drones per vehicle, its total transport capacity and the interaction
with the vehicle. It is already known that drone deliveries result in a reduction in the total cost
of the objective function when it is only considered a single drone per truck. The increase on
the number of drones per vehicle could signiﬁcantly impact the operational cost of the problem,
being possible to launch more drones per location to visit further customers. Similarly, the
activity of the drones is very limited to being able to transport only a single load when being
launched. A possible extension similar to increasing the number of drones per vehicle is the
106
==PAGE:116==
6 Conclusion and Future Researches
107
possibility that the drones increase their capacity of load, being possible to transport more
packages by trip and thus to be able to visit more clients when being launched. These extensions
can reduce the total execution time of the routes and considerably reduce the route of the
truck. However, when considering this new feature in the problem, energy consumption may be
aﬀected, inﬂuencing the speed and endurance of the drone due to the increased transport load.
One possible adaptation to this consideration in the problem is the inclusion of a factor that
aﬀects the speed and duration of the battery as more loads are considered to be transported.
Likewise, these parameters might depend as well on the scenario in which the drone is moving.
The movement of a drone inside a city can be more complicated, since it will have to ﬂy higher
to avoid great obstacles such as high buildings and other elements that can cause the drone
to be demolished during its ﬂight. Hence, taking this into account and the deﬁnition of the
objective function, it would be interesting to consider the case where the truck can wait in the
same location where it launches the drone, as it was considered in [2], for example. Therefore,
as the completion time is given as a constraint in the problem, several potential launches of
drones could be done from the same location while the truck is stopped, saving the total truck
cost, since the truck is just waiting to recover the drone in the same location.
It is currently being considered that the truck driver is in charge of recovering and launching
the drone. With the arrival of more modern technologies, new considerations on the interaction
of the truck and the drone can be done more eﬃciently. The drone can be carried on the roof of
the truck, where the battery can be charged automatically. In the same way, the replacement of
the parcel and the programming of the following route for the drone can be automated. These
considerations reduce the interaction of the driver with the drones, saving time to plan the
routes more eﬃciently. Many of these considerations were inspired by [36].
Finally, to conclude, this problem might be solved to optimality through exact methods, for
example, through Column-Generation and Branch-and-Price methods. The problem presents a
good structure for applying the Column Generation method with the Dantzig-Wolfe decompo-
sition. Deﬁning (e.g.) Ωas the set of possible paths for the truck and Θ as the set of possible
paths for the drone, D-VRP can be reformulated as a Set Covering Problem and thus facilitate
the formulation of the Master Problem. The most challenging part of this method is given with
the large number of feasible solutions that can be found. At the beginning, the method can
be initialized with a small set of feasible paths for both the truck and drone. Nonetheless, the
resolution of the Sub-Problem for the obtaining of new paths to add to the Master Problem
can be signiﬁcantly aﬀected as the number of customers increases. It is therefore interesting to
consider heuristic methods for the search for new paths to add to the Master Problem.
