Abstract
Conventional weight minimization of truss structures conﬁnes material distribution
to a network of bar members interconnected to a spatially ﬁxed set of discrete nodes
known as the ground structure.
Mesh reﬁnement of the ground structure permits force ﬂow through more favorably
located nodes, but a dense mesh comes at the cost of extreme computation time
and possibly impractical structures. A truly optimal nodal position is also only
obtained for a continuous set of nodes, i.e., inﬁnitely many. This thesis presents
an iterative local optimization method that includes position of nodes as design
variables in both a linear elastic and ideal plastic framework, incorporating state-of-
the-art global and local stability constraints. Local optimal points are approached
by solving linearized convex sub-problems.
The method suﬀers from the inevitable risk of ﬁnding local optimum points, but the
risk appears to scale with the amount and degree of non-convex constraints added
to the problem formulation. Premature convergence due to intersecting nodes was
found to be a problem speciﬁc to the present method and coupled with the issue
of local minimum points it limits the application to structures with a reasonable
spacing of nodes, previously subject to optimization analysis sorting out most of
the local minima. The analyzed examples and theory indicate that a decline in
material consumption is guaranteed and in any example, satisfying the requirement
to nodal spacing the decline amounted to a signiﬁcant percentage.

Chapter 1
Introduction and hypothesis
1.1
Material optimization of structures
Material optimization or weight minimization of structures generally concerns the
objective of locating the structural geometry that can be veriﬁed in design while
employing the least amount of material. This is as such a broad subject relevant
to anyone with the interest of saving resources, but will within the context of this
work be limited to enclose weight minimization through the concept of constrained
optimization.
For the uninitiated, optimization is typically associated with the search of points
where the independent variables imply a vanishing gradient of the corresponding
scalar function, i.e., unconstrained optimization. Constrained optimization, as sug-
gested by the name, extends the problem by constraining or restricting the vari-
ables. An illustrative example is constrained minimization of the function f(x, y) =
x2 + y2 −xy which level curves are depicted in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Constrained optimization

The constraints restrict the search space to be any point lying on the red circle

the global minimum point is easily located by inspection of Figure 1.1. Constrained
optimization inherently shares an eternal issue with unconstrained optimization,
that is, the risk of ﬁnding local rather than global optimal points. However, if the
objective function is convex and the search space or the set of feasible points is a con-
vex set, any local optimal point is a global optimal point, Boyd and Vandenberghe,
2004. Convexity is as such alpha-omega in optimization.
It may not appear trivial that constrained optimization can be applied in struc-
tural design, but the concept is widely used within the automotive and aerospace
industry to optimize mechanical elements and are within the ﬁeld of continuum
structures considered to be well established and matured, see Bendsøe and Sig-
mund, 2002. An exciting display of the possibilities rising when pairing extreme
computation power with optimization is Aage et al., 2017 where an entire aircraft
wing geometry that resembles the likes of bird beak bone structure was produced
using more than a billion elements. This thesis, however, concerns material opti-
mization of truss structures, i.e., structures characterized by a series of slender bars
assembled by pinned connections (referred to as nodes) to form a rigid framework.
1.2
Truss weight minimization
Truss weight minimization through the concept of constrained optimization dates
back to 1964 where Dorn, Gomory and Greenberg formulated a linear program
(constrained optimization problem with linear objective function and constraints)
capable of size optimization, see Tejani and Savsani, 2018. According to Assimi,
Jamali, and Nariman-zadeh, 2017, material optimization generally splits into three
categories; size, shape, and topology optimization. With regards to trusses, these
are respectively optimization of bar area distribution, the position of nodes and
node connectivity, i.e., how the bars are connected to the nodes. Figure 1.2 shows
the result of the linear program from 1964 applied to a simply supported common
truss subject to vertical loading.

The lines of the resulting structure are scaled linearly so the thickest bar repre-

and these bars can therefore be left out hereby altering the connectivity of the bars.
The linear program does by that also implicitly perform topology optimization but
does not perform shape optimization. After formulating the linear program, Dorn,
Goromy, and Greenberg pioneered the method and developed what today is known
as the ground structure approach.
1.3
The ground structure approach
The ground structure approach emphasizes the topology part of the optimization by
employing a ground structure or a dense ’mesh’ of bar elements that are intercon-
nected to a spatially ﬁxed set of discrete nodes. Figure 1.3 shows a ground structure
and the corresponding solution of the linear program.
==PAGE:16==
4

Figure 1.3: The ground structure approach
The ground structure improves the solution since the forces are allowed to take
more advantageous routes within the speciﬁed domain, hereby exposing the optimal
bars to assign an area. However even a relatively rough mesh of 75 nodes leads to
a vast number of elements as shown. The truly optimal position of nodes can also
only be achieved for a continuous set of nodes, i.e., inﬁnitely many. The computa-
tional time becomes unmanageable if too many nodes are used, especially for large
3D structures. There is also no guarantee that the solution resembles a practical
structure. In some cases, no space is available for connection maintenance or struc-
tures with too many connections to be economic are generated. Gilbert, Tyas, and
Shepherd, 2005 address these problems and list a series of issues related to practi-
cal design using the ground structure approach. These shortcomings are the prime
motivation for the development of the optimization method presented here. The
method will be covered in detail, but the essence is that it includes the position of
nodes as design variables to locate local optimal points or node coordinates. It is
the hope that this will allow a low-density mesh to be used as a ground structure,
thereby eliminating the problem of impractical structures while reducing computa-
tion time. Including position of nodes as design variables are not new within truss
weight minimization, but the particular method has not been found elsewhere in
the literature.

1.4. BACKGROUND
5
1.4
Background
A major part of the theoretical background forming a basis for this thesis is the
extensive work Boyd and Vandenberghe, 2004 who by a stringent mathematical ap-
proach covers convex optimization thoroughly. Convex optimization in a structural
context is presented in Bendsøe and Sigmund, 2002 with a focus on minimum com-
pliance design and will also serve as common reference.
There are a wide range of optimization schemes, methods and procedures to
be found in the literature of truss weight minimization or material optimization.
Examples that include positions of nodes as design variables are Descamps and
Filomeno Coelho, 2014 and Rahami, Kaveh, and Gholipour, 2008, but both diﬀers
signiﬁcantly from the present method. Descamps and Filomeno Coelho, 2014 for-
mulates a non-convex problem whereas present method is an iterative scheme that
solves many linearized convex sub-problems. A big advantage of the present method
is that it includes global and local stability constraints ensuring stable structures.
The literature holds various examples on how to include global stability, see for in-
stance Kočvara, 2002 but is more sparse with regards to local stability constraints,
although examples includes Rahami, Kaveh, and Gholipour, 2008 and Poulsen, Ole-
sen, and Baandrup, n.d. Even though Rahami, Kaveh, and Gholipour, 2008 include
position of nodes and local stability constraints, the generated solution, which are
found by use of energy methods, does not guarantee a globally stable structure. A
local optimization method that linearize the problem formulation to generate convex
sub-problems while including position of nodes as design variables and incorporating
both global and local stability constraints appears to be unique to present work.
1.5
Scope of thesis
The scope of this thesis is to present the theory and Matlab implementation of
the developed optimization method, illuminate its applicability and expose its lim-
itations.

Chapter 2
Theory - Material optimization of
trusses
This chapter presents the standard theory concerning material optimization of trusses
and gradually extends the problem formulations to include position of nodes and
stability constraints.
Every time the theory is extended, a problem overview is
presented along with validating examples.
2.1
Plastic material optimization
The most simple optimization problem presented here is a linear program and is the
same problem that was solved by Dorn, Goromy, and Greenberg in 1964.
The stress parameter of a bar element is the normal force. In element no. e it will
be denoted βe, while the collection of normal forces in every element is contained
in the vector β such that β(e) = βe. It will turn out that the stress ﬁeld or normal
forces will be chosen without any elastic constitutive relations and always be at the
yield limit. This corresponds to an ideal plastic stress ﬁeld, and the optimization
problem is therefore called plastic material optimization (PMO).
The goal of truss structure material optimization is to minimize the total volume
of the structure. The total volume of the structure can be described by the scalar
function
VT = wTm
(2.1)
With w and m vectors holding every element length Le and area Ae such that
w(e) = Le and m(e) = Ae. At this point minimization of VT is trivial. The solu-
tion is Ae = −∞for e = 1, 2...ne with ne the number of elements in the structure.
Clearly the problem needs to be constrained further to generate solutions that are
of any value.

2.2
Shape optimization and convexity
Before introducing the material optimization scheme that includes position of nodes
as design or optimization variables, a short note on convexity and its consequences
follow. Convexity is alpha-omega in constrained optimization because it guarantees
that any local minimum is a global minimum and that the solution can be obtained
in polynomial time.
Non-convexity is the main obstacle when trying to include
position of nodes as design variables. The following example illustrates the problem.

Validation example 2 : Prager’s cantilever
In 1904 Toby Michell published a paper containing analytical solutions to various
truss structures now known as Michell trusses, see Michell, 1904. Michell trusses are
optimal with respect to material usage, but it turns out that this implies an inﬁnite
number of bars. Such a structure cannot be constructed. Prager, 1977 realized that
and studied the eﬀect of adding a material cost to the connections with the hope
that this would lead to a structure with a ﬁnite number of bars. The weight of all
the connections were assumed to be proportional to the number of connections. For
an appropriate value of the constant of proportionality (weight pr. connection) he
found that for a cantilever truss with a height to width ratio of 2.5, eleven joints
were optimal. This structure is presented in Figure 2.6

2.4
Global stability constraints
The axial element stress has so far only been limited by the material yield stress.
This simpliﬁcation is not safe as structural collapse can happen due to global or
local instability, i.e., buckling before any element is loaded to this level of stress.
The inclusion of global stability constraints are considered in the following sections,
and the constraints massively complicate the optimization problem. The stability
constraints are based on properties of the matrix computed by adding the stiﬀness
matrix K and stress stiﬀness matrix KG of the structure see Olesen, Poulsen, and
Andersen, 2017 for a more detailed presentation of these. The global stability con-
straint converts the optimization problem into a semideﬁnite program that again
is solved by Mosek. A semideﬁnite program has a linear objective function and
includes a matrix variable, that can be subject to the constraint, that it must be
positive semideﬁnite. The syntax used by Mosek to solve semideﬁnite programs
is tricky and needs a fair amount of programming to generalize. The proof that
Ks = K + KG must be positive semideﬁnite (written as Ks ⪰0) and that this
ensures global stability is proven in Kočvara, 2002. The proof, however, is relatively
complex and compact, so an expanded version is provided in appendix D.

Thin hollow tubular sections
There is no unique relation between the moment of inertia of a tubular section and
the cross-sectional area. The argument is that one can imagine two cross-sections
with the same area but a diﬀerent moment of inertia. A thin cross-section with a
large diameter and a thick cross-section with a small diameter. To achieve a unique
relation, the diameter to thickness ratio is ﬁxed. The ratio is also necessary to gen-
erate usable solutions. With no restraints on the diameter to thickness ratio, the
most material economic solution is to have a super thin section with an extremely
large diameter. Such a section would be highly impractical concerning connections
etc. and would be prone to more localized instability eﬀects such as shell buckling.

2.6
Elastic Material Optimization
The plastic formulations presented so far does not involve any elastic constitutive
relations which eliminate the possibility of including constraints on the elastic defor-
mations of the structure. The plastic formulations are as such not suited for design
in the serviceability limit state, and many designs do furthermore not permit plastic
deformation in the ultimate limit state. This is the prime motivation for developing
an elastic formulation of the optimization problems.
This section serves to show how the constraints and objective function are approxi-
mated and assembled in order to include position of nodes as design variables now
in an elastic material optimization scheme. The elastic version of the optimization
method is harder to derive and implement but does not introduce any new concepts.
The following section is therefore brief and only concerns the derivation and assem-
bly of the linearized constraint matrices so that the problem formulations can be
presented.

Application examples
Five examples await after a short note on material parameters and appropriate units

The ﬁrst example uses the three-bar structure to analyze oscillations always present
at convergence. Secondly is an example demonstrating how optimization solutions

Material parameters and input units
The material parameters in the upcoming examples correspond to the modeling of
mild steel with a modulus of elasticity E = 210 GPa and σy = 210 MPa unless
otherwise stated. These parameters have been chosen to achieve a ratio of E/σy =
1000, which is crucial to get realistic results as demonstrated by Poulsen, Olesen,
and Baandrup, n.d. SI units are not recommended as input to the solver (Mosek) as
stated in Sønderskov and Mikkelsen, 2018. Parameters are therefore only entered
in the units resulting from using kN for force and cm for structural dimensions,
regardless of the units that they are presented in.

simply has too many closely spaced nodes. This results in nodes being trapped in

4.2
A local optimization method
The various optimization schemes applied all suﬀer from the common compromise
of any local optimization method, i.e., trading oﬀconvexity to include design vari-
ables such as position of nodes or local stability constraints. The consequences,
however, appears to be more severe in some problem formulations, the elastic ones
in particular. Take the three-bar structure example from section 2.2. The curve in
Figure 2.2 is exactly reproduced if an elastic version of the optimization problem
(Table 2.5) is run for every position of the loaded node of the three-bar structure.
Running the elastic scheme with movable nodes (Table 2.6) however produces local
minima such as the one in Figure 4.1.

The reason for this behaviour is that the constraints in elastic optimization are
’less convex’ than those of the plastic optimization problems. The elastic constraints
are a function of all three optimization variables ∆m, ∆V and ∆X and especially
the equality (equilibrium) constraints are very far from aﬃne. The subset of points
that can be searched for minima are hence more complicated and contains more
’valleys’ wherein the solver can become trapped resulting in local minimum points.
The problem is very pronounced in the examples presented here, where the elastic
optimization always ﬁnds solutions with a higher material consumption compared
to plastic optimization. The analysis indicates that the likelihood of ﬁnding min-
ima that are far from the global is related to the amount and degree of non-convex
constraints. An advantage of the method is that almost any constraints can be
incorporated in the problem formulation if the constraint function is diﬀerentiable,
but it comes at the cost of a less optimal solution. It is therefore important to try
and manipulate the constraints of the formulation to be as ’convex as possible’.

4.3. MERGING NODES AND CONSTANT STEP SIZES
67
4.3
Merging nodes and constant step sizes
As illustrated by the analysis of the column, premature convergence can happen
before an actual local minimum is found and the problem is speciﬁc to this partic-
ular method. There are a few options to circumvent the issue but none of them has
been implemented because of the uncertainty that they bring. The ﬁrst option is
to use a constant step size in change of nodal coordinates. The step size could for
instance be chosen as a fraction of the maximum structural dimension. Such a step
size would allow nodes to cross but invalidates the linear Taylor approximations for
any suﬃciently short bar element. The resulting solutions can potentially be out of
equilibrium, bars could be yielding or buckling, or the structure could be globally
unstable.
The second option is to have merging nodes. If a pair of node positions become suf-
ﬁciently close, they could merge to one which would remedy the problem of nodes
never crossing each other but would open the possibility of merging nodes that
coincidentally are passing close by, which is not desired. A sort of ﬁlter could be
implemented stating that nodes only were allowed to merge if they (in a given num-
ber of consecutive iterations) moves directly towards each other. The uncertainty
behind such an approach is that one may end up eliminating possible solutions only
obtainable by a structure with the original number of nodes.
4.4
On the subject of local stability
Adding local stability constraints to a formulation concerning truss structure opti-
mization can seem counterproductive. Take the column analysis in Figure 3.10 as
an example. The structural interpretation of the results is that material is moved
to the sides to create bending stiﬀness.
This implies that the bars at the sides
has a large cross-sectional area and given the local stability constraint they have a
huge diameter to prevent bar buckling. The bending stiﬀness of these potentially
huge cross-sections is not utilized to reduce the material needed to ensure global
stability, which seems like a waste, especially when the entire goal is minimization
of material consumption. An optimization model concerning beam elements would
as such be more appropriate, not only would the bending stiﬀness of individual bars
contribute to weight minimization, it may make the local stability constraints re-
dundant. Beam elements can represent individual buckling of elements, and as such,
global and local stability would be ensured by Ks being positive semideﬁnite. Not
==PAGE:80==
68

only does that make local stability constraints redundant it also removes the very
constraints that made the plastic truss structure optimization problem non-convex.
Another yet unaddressed problem of local stability constraints is that the diame-
ter can become too thin. Validation example 5 (Figure 2.14) yielded a 1.6 mm thick
section. EN 1993-1-1: Eurocode 3: Design of steel structures - Part 1-1: General
rules and rules for buildings n.d. only applies to a material thickness above 3 mm.
This problem could be solved by including the wall thickness in the problem for-
mulations, but as previously mentioned, it comes at cost of a less optimal solution,
and it is diﬃcult to predict the exact inﬂuence.
4.5
On the subject of global stability
The stiﬀness matrix and stress stiﬀness matrix refers to an elastic force distribution
among the bars, generated by elastic displacements collected in V. The generalized
eigenvalue problem (2.59) also ask for a load scaling factor that makes the elastic
stiﬀness of the structure vanish. Using a plastic force distribution β to describe
the reduction of the structural stiﬀness through KG does seem strange. It should,
however, be noted that no structural example has been found, where the global
buckling constraints were satisﬁed for a plastic force distribution β computed by
optimization, but dissatisﬁed for an elastic force distribution NF found by linear
elastic ﬁnite element analysis.
The solution structures generated by the optimization problems are often statically
determinate. This means that equilibrium only is satisﬁed for one force distribution
and as a result, β = NF. These examples, therefore, disqualify as examples that
can be used to check the issue of global buckling with a plastic force distribution.

Possible extensions
A weight minimization scheme concerning beam elements is, as mentioned, an in-
teresting topic, as local buckling may be incorporated into the global stability con-
straint governed by the matrix Ks being positive semideﬁnite. Extending a beam-
element version of the method to include position of nodes as design variables is
expected to bring about the same cons and pros as for truss structures. Unforeseen
complications that may arise with beam elements could also be illuminated.
Further work also includes techniques for dealing with the issue of closely spaced
nodes. As already discussed, there are a few ways to circumvent the issue, and
a study of the potential inﬂuences on the optimality of the results could be rele-
vant. At last is the extension or reformulation of the problems to be applicable to
three-dimensional structures.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
Solving linearized convex sub-problems to approach local optimal points has proven
ﬂexible in the sense that almost any diﬀerentiable constraint function can be in-
tegrated into the problem formulation. This allowed state-of-the-art stability con-
straints and position of nodes to be included in an optimization method capable of
ﬁne-tuning the nodal coordinates of truss structures. The algorithm was successfully
implemented in Matlab and validated through ﬁve examples.
The inevitable risk of ﬁnding local minimum points diminishes in the plastic case
as an initial feasible point can be generated by scaling the individual bar areas of a
structure found by solving the convex problem formulations. This procedure sorts
out a number of sub-optimal points but is not applicable in the elastic case as the
scaling would alter the stiﬀness and thereby force distribution. As the problem of
sub-optimal points is most prominent in the elastic formulation due to the degree of
non-convex constraints, this is a major shortcoming of the method. Combined with
the issue of closely spaced nodes the applicability of the elastic formulations are
challenged, while the applicability of the plastic formulations are limited to struc-
tures with a reasonable spacing between nodes that previously has been subject to
optimization analysis.
These application limits are within the framework of the design goals of the op-
timization method which were to develop a tool allowing a low-density mesh to
be used as ground structure, thereby eliminating the risk of generating impractical
structure while creating a less computational heavy optimization method, making
ground structures employing dense meshes to locate better nodal coordinates redun-
dant. As the material consumption of any of the investigated examples (excl. the
column) declined by a signiﬁcant percentage when subject to analysis with movable
nodes, this is considered accomplished.

Appendix D
Global stability theory
The following proof is based on Kočvara, 2002. The statement to prove is that if
matrix Ks = K + KG is positive semideﬁnite it follows that the structural load R0
is smaller than the buckling load calculated through a linear buckling analysis, i.e.,
the buckling load Rcr found from solving the generalized eigenvalue problem
(K −λKG)V = 0
(D.1)
Where λ is a load scaling factor deﬁned through
Rcr = λR0
(D.2)
If R0 is chosen as the structural load it follows from (D.2) that λ ≤1 implies that
the structural load exceeds or equals the buckling load. λ > 1 implies that the
structural load is lower than the buckling load and λ < 0 implies that the load
direction must be reversed for the structure to buckle.
It is shown that
(K + τKG) ⪰0
(D.3)
If and only if all eigenvalues of
(K −λKG)V = 0
(D.4)
Satisﬁes λ /∈[0, τ]. By choosing τ = 1 the proof is complete.
The ﬁrst step is to rewrite (D.4) by pre- and post multiplication with a matrix
satisfying
K−1 = CC
