Abstract
Proteins are extraordinary molecules that facilitate and regulate nearly every essential
process of life. Over the last century, the main aim of protein science has been to achieve
a complete and quantitative understanding of protein behavior, but that has proven exôÙ
tremely difficult as protein behavior is tremendously complex. An attractive method to
study protein behavior is sequence perturbative approaches. In these strategies, a proôÙ
tein sequence is systematically modified by protein engineering, followed by a detailed
biophysical characterization of the new sequence variants. A powerful method to invesôÙ
tigate sequence perturbation is mRNA display, a robust in vitro selection technique that
selects proteins with desired functions from libraries of up to trillions of variants. This
is done by covalently linking the protein to its encoding mRNA molecule, connecting the
genotype of a given protein variant to the phenotype, i.e., its biological function. However,
reliable ways to separate the different mRNAôÙdisplay variants depending on the interacôÙ
tion investigated are often limited. Previously, our lab has separated variants based on
their partition into condensates by separating the dense and dilute phases by centrifugaôÙ
tion. That method is, however, probably limited to studying condensate formation only.
Therefore, to study the sequence space of other systems, new methods must be develôÙ
oped for separating different mRNAôÙdisplay variants.
In this thesis, a microfluidic device was designed to separate mRNAôÙdisplay variants for
two different systems, the binding of öÝôÙsynuclein to lipid vesicles and the partition of anôÙ
alytes to Ddx4N1 condensates. Furthermore, the deviceãs ability to differentiate between
öÝôÙsynuclein at different binding equilibrium was shown, as well as its ability to differentiate
between differently partitioning analytes into Ddx4N1 condensates. Moreover, the device
was shown to be able to study the interaction from two different angles for both systems.
From the experience gained over the work of the thesis, new designs of the microfluidic
device were made and discussed. Finally, a mutant library of Ddx4N1 was produced,
and its propensity to form condensates characterized using TDIPS, to validate previously
acquired mRNAôÙdisplay data. A correlation was observed between the two, giving validaôÙ
tion to the mRNAôÙdisplay method to report on proteinsã propensity to form condensates.
Furthermore, interesting effects of Proline were observed, possibly shining a light on a
bigger role of prolines for condensate formation than previously was expected.

Introduction
Proteins are remarkable molecules that give rise to and govern almost every essential
process of life. Proteins are polymers formed by chaining together amino acids that come
together like beads on a string to create a larger molecule. In a biological setting, there are
20 common amino acids, termed the natural amino acids, that can be arranged in various
manners to form every protein in all living organisms, from bacteria to humans. Proteins
can be made up of anywhere from 11 to 27.000 amino acids, and the information for the
arrangement of the amino acids is stored in the DNA. That information is intermediated
by RNA molecules that are then read to form a chain of amino acids that can then fold, or
not fold, into functional threeôÙdimensional structures. In the human proteome, there are
over 20.000 different proteins that, together with other molecules, form an intricate web of
interactions that allow biological functions to be carried out. However, minor alterations
in proteins or their environment can often offset this interplay between proteins and other
molecules, resulting in various disease states [1].
1.1
Order and Disorder
For many years, the predominant view in protein science has been that proteinsã unique
ordered threeôÙdimensional structure gives rise to their function, often referred to as the
ãlock and keyã model proposed by Fischer in 1894 [2]. Furthermore, in 1950, Anfinsen
demonstrated that the thermodynamic driving force for a protein to fold is encoded directly
in the protein sequence of amino acids [3], which creates a direct link between genetic
information and function. While true for many proteins, it has recently been discovered
that an estimated ã¥30% of segments in human proteins do not have this intrinsic driving
force to fold [4, 5]. Such regions can span anywhere from small segments to the entirety
of the protein. These regions are described as being disordered rather than unstructured,
as they may convert rapidly between many structures of similar energy. Therefore, these
proteins that contain disordered regions lack one wellôÙdefined structure, and their function
cannot be described by structure alone. This has completely changed our understanding
of proteins. They are not only static, wellôÙdefined structures but dynamic ensembles rangôÙ
ing from folded proteins to proteins that stay completely disordered ôÙ even when carrying
out their function [6]. This disorder feature in proteins gives them increased complexôÙ
ity and versatility, often enabling them to have multiple functions and binding partners
depending on their location, environment, and postôÙtranslational modification [5].
1.2
Protein Folding and Misfolding
A proteinãs intrinsic thermodynamic forces govern if and how it folds. The folding process
can be viewed as the delicate balance between entropic and enthalpic energies. RestrictôÙ
ing the conformational freedom of a protein chain into an ordered structure comes with an
entropic penalty, but that loss in entropic energy is compensated by favorable enthalpic
interactions that result in a more stable overall structure [1]. However, this process is
extremely delicate and complicated and usually involves the formation and breakage of
many weak individual interactions, where the net change in free energy amounts to much
less than 1% of the total sum of interactions involved [7]. Protein folding has yet to be
fully understood, but several models have been proposed to explain this process, which is
better discussed elsewhere [8, 9, 10, 11, 12]. Most of them have in common that folding
proceeds in a highly cooperative manner, where the shielding of hydrophobic residues
and the formation of secondary structure elements, like alphaôÙhelices and beta sheets,

1.3
The Amyloid State of Proteins
Misfolding is particularly interesting when it converts ordinarily soluble proteins into agôÙ
gregates of intractable and highly stable amyloid structures. These amyloid structures
typically have the form of threadlike fibrils and are strongly associated with diseases like
Alzheimerãs, Parkinsonãs, and type II diabetes [13]. Unlike the native state of proteins,
amyloid fibrils have a core region formed by a common cross öý fold, where öý strands
are oriented perpendicular to the fibril axis with hydrogen bonding between the layers
[16]. Even though the cross öý fold is considered a joint or ãgenericã fold of amyloid fibrils,
their propensity to form, detailed structure, and properties vary significantly with amino
acid composition and sequence. It is also important to point out that although amyloid
fibrils are usually associated with diseases, experiments have shown that the ability to
form such aggregates is not a rare phenomenon only associated with a small number of
diseaseôÙrelated proteins but appears as an alternative wellôÙdefined structural form that
can be adopted by many, if not all, protein sequences under the right conditions. This
special semiôÙcrystalline conformation can then selfôÙtemplate and propagate, as seen in
infectious prion agents, which are all amyloids [17]. But not all amyloids are prion agents.
Even though prionôÙlike spreading of amyloids can occur in nonôÙprion diseases, they are
not contagious like prions, and the distinction between prions and amyloids remains unôÙ
clear. Furthermore, it has also been debated how amyloids might cause various diseases.
For diseases with deposits of amyloids broadly in the body, the buildup of large quantities
of amyloids can cause the inability of vital organs to function correctly. However, in neuôÙ
rodegenerative diseases, the amyloid burden can be quite low and does not always relate
to the severity of symptoms. In their case, small prefibrillar oligomeric assemblies probôÙ
ably cause toxicity [18]. Their toxicity arises because they are inherently misfolded and,
therefore, are likely to interact inappropriately with many of the functional components
of their highly complex and crowded environments through their exposed hydrophobic
surfaces [13]. Supporting this theory, it has been shown that many of the proteins in
pathways associated with neurodegenerative conditions are in ãsuperôÙsaturatedã levels,
making them particularly vulnerable to aberrant behavior [19]. Therefore, an essential
aspect of amyloid formation is that it is a nucleated phenomenon where multiple protein
molecules must misfold together. Significant efforts have been made to understand the
reaction kinetics of this phenomenon and how the fibrilãs elongation reaction can cause
secondary processes to speed up amyloid formation [20]. Still, much remains to be done
to understand amyloid behavior fully.
1.4
Protein condensates
Another interesting protein phenomenon is the formation of condensates through liquidôÙ
liquid phase separation (LLPS). These condensates are of high biological relevance as
they are pivotal in cellular organization.
These entities behave like condensed liquid
phases of the cytoplasm or nucleoplasm, which are crucial in regulating the growth and
development of cells and tissues [21, 22, 23]. This process of LLPS involves proteins
transitioning from a soluble state to a condensed, liquidôÙlike form, allowing specific bioôÙ
chemistry to be compartmentalized within the cell. These highly dynamic structures allow
for internal rearrangement and the diffusion of biomolecules into and out of the formed
condensates. Their lack of a surrounding membrane facilitates cellular regulation, as
droplet assembly or dissolution happens rapidly in response to surrounding stimuli or the
availability of binding partners [24, 25].
Protein LLPS generally occurs when transient proteinôÙprotein interactions become more
favorable than interactions with the bulk solvent [21]. Proteins that can undergo LLPS

1.5
Complete and Quantitative Understanding of Protein
Behavior
The main aim of protein science over the last century has been to achieve a complete
and quantitative understanding of protein behavior. A complete quantitative description
of protein behavior would enable us not only to predict the behavior of any sequence but
also to engineer novel sequences with specific and wellôÙdefined properties. However, that
has proven extremely difficult as protein behavior is tremendously complex, as discussed
above. Recent breakthroughs have been made in predicting the crystal structure of proôÙ
teins from the sequence alone with AlphaFold 2 in 2020 [30] and the joint crystal structure
while interacting with other molecules with AlphaFold 3 in 2024 [31]. However, structure
alone cannot explain proteinsã complete behavior and function. The AlphaFold models
are based on data from decades of structural experiments, with hundreds of thousands
of experimentally determined protein structures from the PDB and enormous sequencing
databases. Still, similar databases for protein interactions and higher assembly processes
have yet to be available. More experimental data must be generated if models are to be
built that can accurately predict these processes.
An attractive method to do so is sequence perturbative approaches, pioneered by Sir Alan
Fersht [32, 33, 34]. In these strategies, a protein sequence is systematically modified
by protein engineering, followed by a detailed biophysical characterization of the new
sequence variants. A powerful method to investigate sequence perturbation is mRNAôÙ
display, a robust in vitro selection technique that selects proteins with desired functions
from libraries of up to trillions of variants. This is done by covalently linking the protein to
its encoding mRNA molecule, connecting the genotype of a given protein variant to the
phenotype, i.e., its biological function [35, 36].
One of the aims of our lab has been to employ mRNAôÙdisplay to investigate the sequence
dependency of proteins for their interactions and higher assembly processes.

Essential principles of microfluidics
Microfluidics is the science that deals with the behavior, precise control, and manipulation
of fluids and particles on the scale of micrometers [38]. At that scale, fluid behavior differs
significantly from what is observed in bulk solution. The low ratio of inertial to viscous
force results in laminar flow. In laminar flow, fluid mixing only occurs through diffusion as
chaotic turbulence is completely suppressed, which is the primary contributor to mixing
in systems at the macroôÙscale. The laminar regime is characterized by the Reynolds

strated by Wright et al. in 2018 when they investigated the oligomerization of molecular
chaperone HSP70 where aggregation kinetics revealed a cooperative nature of subunit
association [45].
So far, only examples of assays utilizing fluorophore labeling have been discussed. It
is, however, well known that fluorophore conjugation can affect the biochemical properôÙ
ties of tagged proteins. Therefore, in recent years, many have sought to design assays
that enable labelôÙfree MDS. One approach has been a latentôÙlabeling strategy, where the
molecules investigated are separated based on the interaction of interest and then inôÙsitu
labeled before quantification [46] (Figure 2.1 c). This method has, for example, been
used for the observation of binding interaction between alphaôÙsynuclein and a nanobody
[43]. Another labelôÙfree strategy is the use of intrinsic fluorescence. By utilizing the intrinôÙ
sic fluorescence of the amino acids tyrosine, tryptophan, and phenylalanine, fluorescence
imaging of unlabelled proteins is possible. This method has been applied to study both the
aggregation of lysozyme and silk fibroin [47] and the selfôÙassembly of oligomeric alphaôÙB
crystallin [48]. A third labelôÙfree strategy that has been used is to utilize an electrochemical
readout to report a weak binding interaction between free and membraneôÙbound protein
[49].
Therefore, MDS could offer an attractive way to separate mRNAôÙdisplay variants for both
binding and partitioning into condensates, which will be explored in the work here below.
Variants that bind to a larger species, that does not diffuse significantly over the assayãs
time scale, will have different diffusion profiles depending on their binding affinity. This also
applies to partitioning into condensates, where the condensates are now the nonôÙdiffusive
species, and variants will have different diffusion profiles depending on their propensity
to partition into condensates. The variants could, therefore, be separated by splitting the
volume inside the microfluidic device into different outlets and the output to the outlet
sequenced.
2.1.2
Microchip electrophoresis analysis
In addition to passive analysis of protein interactions through diffusional sizing, assays
utilizing electric fields to induce molecular transport directly have been developed (Figure
2.1 d). Electrophoretic methods have been a stable technique within molecular biology for
a long time, but at bulk scale, they come with certain drawbacks. They typically take a long
time to run, making it challenging to study biomolecular interactions that do not persist over
that timescale. Furthermore, species of similar molecular weight can not be distinguished
as traditional electrophoretic methods offer poor resolution. Lastly, proteins might interact
differently with the gel matrix depending on solution conditions. Gel electrophoresis relies
on migration through the gel relative to a set of reference molecules, making results not
readily comparable between solution conditions. Microfluidic electrophoresis manages to
deal with all of these drawbacks. The slow mixing through diffusion alone precludes the
need for a gel separation matrix, allowing for molecules to be analyzed and fractionated in
their native state. The micronôÙscale dimensions can reduce the experiment time down to
subôÙsecond time scales, allowing analysis of weak and transient interaction. Finally, the
lack of matrix means that electrophoretic mobility can be determined quantitatively and

Although probably not applicable to the separation of mRNAôÙdisplay variants, another inôÙ

Taylor dispersion induced phase separation
Taylor DispersionôÙInduced Phase Separation (TDIPS) is a novel and powerful method to
study the phase separation behavior of molecules [60]. It is a microfluidic method that
utilizes TaylorôÙdispersion, which describes the spreading of solute bands in laminar flow.
In the experimental setup, smaller particles like salt and proteins will tend to diffuse beôÙ
tween layers and give rise to Gaussian concentration profiles. In contrast, larger particles
(Rh ãË100 nm), such as protein aggregates and condensates, will not diffuse significantly
under the applied pressure and timescale required to keep a laminar flow. Instead, large
particles will tend to remain in the same flow layer, resulting in an asymmetric distribution
[61].
The differences in molecular diffusion, when subjected to pressureôÙdriven laminar flow
through a capillary, are exploited in TDIPS to induce phase separation (Figure 2.2). Species
with significantly different diffusivity will undergo partial spatial separation which can reôÙ
sult in the removal of a phase inhibitor. An example of this is the removal of NaCl from
Ddx4. The large axial distribution of proteins compared to salt ions results in the physical
separation of proteins at the head and tailôÙend of the flow. Proteins will there move into
the surrounding buffer system, and by selecting a nonôÙinhibiting buffer system of lowôÙsalt,
proteinôÙprotein interactions can be induced that will result in condensate formation. The
phenomenon is illustrated in Figure 2.2 a, which depicts condensates forming at the head
and tailôÙend of the protein dispersion [60].

The Propensity of a Ddx4N1 Mutant Library to Form

Previously, the lab obtained data from large screenings of mRNAôÙdisplay libraries of Ddx4ôÙ

when doing these screenings, it can be hard to differentiate between the effects of the
mRNA tag and the actual peptide. The mRNA tag is much larger than the Ddx4N1 fragôÙ
ments, and usually, the partition of the mRNA tag into the condensates is stronger than
that of the fragment itself. Furthermore, the ability of a fragment to report on the behavior
of the full protein can be questioned. To assess how well the mRNAôÙdisplay method is
reporting the sequence dependency, a Ddx4N1 mutant library of 23 mutants was created.
The different mutations were picked based on the mRNAôÙdisplay data, along with a natuôÙ
rally occurring spliceôÙvariant (D135ôÙ162). Nine of them had small segments deleted from
the sequence, and the other tvelve had point mutations. Unfortunately, because of an erôÙ
ror in the script used to generate the mutations, the deleted segment was 15 amino acids,
while the fragments in the mRNAôÙdisplay libraries were 14 and 16 amino acids. NeverôÙ
theless, the phase separation of the different mutants was assessed using the TDIPS
method over a range of different salt concentrations, displayed in Figure 4.1 a. As disôÙ
cussed above, it can be tedious to analyze data from TDIPS. Here, the integral over the
flowôÙfocus area is compared to the peak of the monomeric peak (Both shown in blue in
Figure 4.1 b). This ratio is a good indicator of condensate formation, as the monomeric
peak decreases as more molecules enter the condensates, and the condensates are exôÙ

Conclusions
5.1
The Propensity of a Ddx4N1 Mutant Library to Form
Condenstaes
A mutant library of Ddx4N1 was produced, and its propensity to form condensate was
characterized. This was done to validate the ability of mRNAôÙdisplay mutational screenôÙ
ing of smaller fragments of a whole protein to report on the behavior of the whole protein.
A correlation (PCC = 0.495) was observed between the mutants with the deletions and the
corresponding mRNAôÙdisplay results, validating the ability of the mRNAôÙdisplay screening
to report on the behavior of the protein. Furthermore, some interesting proline point muôÙ
tations were observed, possibly shining a light on a bigger role of prolines for condensate
formation than previously was expected.
5.2
The microfluidic device
A microfluidic device was designed and shown to be capable of differentiating between difôÙ
ferent species, based on either their binding to DOPS vesicles or partitioning into Ddx4N1
condensates. Furthermore, the device was shown to be capable of studying these two
interaction from two different angles. The interaction can be studied for the binding to
lipid vesicles by observing and quantifying the difference in the diffusion of the preôÙmixed
solution of analyte and DOPS vesicles within the device. Another angle to study this inôÙ
teraction would be to add the analyte into the flanking buffer, where the interaction could
be induced inôÙsitu. It could, therefore, be observed from the very start of the interaction.
This angle could, for example, prove valuable in studying the collapse of lipid vesicles and
possibly aggregation events in relation to vesicle binding. For the partition into Ddx4N1
condensate, the partition interaction can be studied both where the analyte is present as
the condensates form and their partition into already formed condensate. If the analyte
is preôÙmixed with the Ddx4N1, it will be present when condensate formation is induced.
However, suppose the analyte is placed in the flanking buffer and is significantly bigger
than the salt that is inhibiting condensate formation. In that case, the condensates will
form before most of the analyte diffuses to the center. That angle also opens up the
possibility of studying the chemical potential needed to break the surface tension of the
condensates.
Furthermore, the deviceãs ability to estimate the volume fraction of the dense phase of
Ddx4N1 was explored. A substantially higher volume fraction was estimated than exôÙ
