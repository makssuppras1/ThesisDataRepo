Abstract 
 
Optimizing clothing lifespans and decreasing product replacement has been widely suggested strategies 
to mitigate the environmental impacts of the fashion industry. Exploring consumer behaviour on its 
influence on clothing utilization and lifespans unveils areas of improvement for clothing brands and 
enhances representativeness of Life Cycle Assessments (LCA) on clothing. This study, particularly 
focussed on trousers, explored the effect of the categories ‘wardrobe practices’, ‘garment specifications’, 
‘garment use’ and ‘brand characteristics’, on the expected total physical lifespan and the total active use 
time of a pair of trousers (RQ1). In addition, the influence of these parameters on the total wearing 
frequency where examined (RQ2). User data of 117 female trousers was gathered though a wardrobe 
survey focused on the European consumer. The average expected physical lifespan and total active use 
time of trousers were 7.4 and 10.4 years, respectively. Second-hand trousers were found to have a 56% 
shorter total active use period. Apart from ‘brand characteristics’, significant relationships were obtained 
between all categories with the total physical lifespan, the total active use time, and the total wearing 
frequency. A discrepancy between the expected duration of actively using a pair of trousers, compared to 
their estimated physical lifespans, implies a need for designing clothes that last, and outlines the need for 
brands to effectively communicate the lifespan of their garments, which would both be environmentally 
beneficial and may enhance customer satisfaction. Future research should aim at defining a method to 
optimally obtain a representation of the use phase of clothing, and subsequently structure the functional 
unit and reference flows which includes the dynamics of consumer behaviour.  Furthermore, brands and 
LCA practitioners must carefully define the duration of the service lifespan of each garment, as inaccurate 
service lifespans can result in largely skewed and unrealistic environmental impact assessments. 
 
 
Key words 
Clothing lifespans, Physical lifespan, Service lifespan, Clothing use, Life Cycle Assessment, Use phase, 
Wardrobe survey, Consumer behaviour, Trousers

EPL 
EPL stands for the ‘expected physical lifespan’, which describes where an item is in its 
physical lifespan, indicated as a percentage. EPL is one of the dependent variables in 
this study and is used to calculate the total physical lifespan (TPL).  
EAU 
EAU stands for the ‘expected active use time’, which, similarly to EPL, describes where 
an garment is in its active use time, indicated as a percentage, over the course of one 
possession span (1 consumer). EAU is one of the dependent variables in this study and 
is used to calculate the total active use time (TAU) and the total wearing frequency 
(TWF) of one garment.  
EWF 
EWF stands for the ‘expected wearing frequency’, which covers the total number of 
wears per year estimated by the consumer. It is used to calculate the total wearing 
frequency (TWF) of one garment.  
ELF 
ELF stands for the expected laundry frequency of a garment, describes by the number 
of wears in between washes.  
TPL 
TPL describes the total physical lifespan of a garment, which is calculated by dividing 
the item age with the ‘expected physical lifespan’ (%, EPL). The TPL indicates how long 
a garment can be put to use, before it is considered unaccepted to wear due to physical 
aspects of the garment due to e.g., wear and tear. This is related to the physical 
properties of a garment such as quality, strength, colour fastness etc. The TPL includes 
all users within the physical lifespan of a garment.  
TAU 
TAU describes the ‘total active use time’ of a garment, covering the period one owner 
uses a garment, starting at its first use until its last. TAU is calculated by dividing the 
item age with the ‘expected active use time’ (%, EAU). TAU excludes periods of inactive 
use, and only encompasses one possession span.  
TWF 
TWF stands for the ‘total wearing frequency’, describing the total number of wears of a 
garment over the course of the ‘total active use time’ (TAU) by one owner.  
TLF 
TLF stands for the ‘total laundry frequency’ of an item, throughout its entire lifespan. By 
knowing the number of wears in between washes, and subsequently calculating the 
total wearing frequency (TWF), the TLF can be calculated. TLF can either be calculated 
by dividing the TWF with the expected number of wears in between washes, also 
referred to as the ‘expected laundry frequency’ (ELF).

1. Introduction 
1.1 Project motivation  
The fashion industry is currently one of the most impactful industries to the environment (European 
Environment Agency, 2022; Leal Filho et al., 2022). From 2000 until 2015 the industry nearly doubled in 
size, releasing high amounts of CO2, greenhouse gasses and toxins to the atmosphere, and is generating 
an excessive amount of waste (e.g. Niinimäki et al., 2020; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017; Peters et 
al., 2021). Simultaneously, the march of fast and cheaper fashion with an accelerated turnover of clothing 
styles and an increased number of seasonal collections has resulted in a significantly lower utilisation rate 
of clothing (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017). This low utilisation and recyclability rate of clothing has 
resulted in increased demands of raw materials, which puts an enormous pressure on the earth's 
resources (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017). Thus, extending clothing lifespan and decreasing product 
replacement have been widely suggested key components in making the fashion industry more 
sustainable (e.g. Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017; Fletcher & Fitzpatrick, 2021; Klepp et al., 2020; 
Vesterinen & Syrjälä, 2022).  
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) studies have been an internationally accepted tool to study the impact of the 
life cycle of clothing and have shown that the use phase is one of the most contributing phases (e.g. Muthu, 
2015, Allwood et al., 2006). The impact of this phase is directly influenced by the user in the way they take 
care of and discard a textile. Additionally, there is environmental potential in increasing the number of 
wears per user and extending the active lifetime of a textile, and thus lifespan (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 
2021; Sandin et al., 2019; Wiedemann et al., 2020). If an item was worn twice as long on average, the 
GHG emissions could potentially be lowered with 44% (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017). Consequently, 
political attention to extending product lifetime has increased significantly in the past years.  
Optimised lifetime of textiles is highlighted in the EU's Circular Economy Action Plans (CEAP) and is 
included in the EU Ecodesign initiative. So far, these political instruments primarily highlight the 
significance of improving physical product lifespan (Heidenstrøm et al., 2021). However, the lifetime of a 
product is much more complex than just its physical properties (Connor-Crabb & Rigby, 2019), and is 
mostly decided by the user (Wiedemann et al., 2020). A user-centred approach is lacking, as the emotional 
relationship between a consumer and a product plays a crucial role in the overall lifetime (Heidenstrøm et 
al., 2021; Chapman, 2015). Brands need to encourage consumers to care for, repair and reuse their 
garments to extend the lifetimes of textiles (Cooper et al., 2017). Yet, with linear business models still 
being dominant, brands are challenged to influence the consumers to mitigate the impact of the use phase.   
There is no consensus within LCA research on the best approach to define the functional unit for clothing, 
and there is a need for improved knowledge about the use phase (Daystar et al., 2019). The functional 
unit is often determined by the expected physical lifespan of a product (Roos et al., 2017). Yet, for textiles 
this is highly dependent on the user(s), care, garment, style and many other variables (Laitala, Klepp, & 
Beverly, 2017). In addition, there is no common approach nor legislative requirements directly applicable 
to clothing to assess their physical durability (Cooper & Claxton, 2022). Moreover, it is challenging to make 
a realistic representation of the use phase of clothing, as this often includes active and passive use periods 
(I. G. Klepp et al., 2020) and strongly differs per individual or user group (Langley et al., 2013). 
Representative user data is limited (Laitala et al., 2018), therefore rough assumptions are inevitably made 
within LCA studies, increasing uncertainties, and leading to varying impact results of the use phase 
(Sandin et al., 2019). One part of clarifying the use phase and generating a consensus in defining a 
functional unit for clothing, is to gain more understanding of a realistic lifespan for clothing. Although 
numerous studies have focused on extensively investigating the use phase of clothing (e.g. Klepp et al., 
2020; Laitala et al., 2017a, 2018; Muthu, 2015b), it is still an area in need of further research.

1.2 Purpose 
This study aims to further understand variables in the lifetime of clothing, through a case study on trousers 
(excluding jeans). A focus on one garment category enables investigating the influence of garment style 
characteristics on clothing use patterns and their lifetime. New information obtained about the use phase 
of clothing in this study will contribute to defining a method to optimally structure the functional unit and 
the use phase in LCA studies which include the dynamics of consumer behaviour.  
This study is conducted in collaboration with Tekstilrevolutionen1 and Our Units2, to collectively investigate 
how the general consumer uses trousers, and examine which factors influence their lifespan. The results 
of this study will contribute to Tekstilrevolutionen’s extensive research on LCA’s of clothing and textiles 
and will provide a more holistic view on affecting variables in modelling the use phase in future LCA studies 
for their partners. Furthermore, uncovering the factors influencing clothing utilization is essential for 
clothing brands to enhance their comprehensions of potential areas of improvement and influence.  
1.3 Research questions and hypotheses 
Based on the above-described literature and the purpose of this study, a list of variables impacting 
consumer behaviour and the active use time of a garment was established for this study and can is 
addressed in section 3.  
 
RQ1 How do wardrobe practices, garment specifications, garment use and brand characteristics influence 
the total wearing frequency of a pair of trousers? 
 
H1: The garment specifications are expected to significantly influence the wearing frequency. Significant 
changes in the utilisation rate of clothing may be a result of seasonal changes in styles and trends (Ellen 
MacArthur Foundation, 2017). In addition, garment use is expected to influence the wearing frequency, as 
demonstrated in previous research (Laitala & Klepp, 2020). Moreover, wardrobe practices are expected to 
have an influence on total wearing frequency of a pair of trousers, as purchase priorities have appeared 
to influence wearing frequency (Laitala & Klepp, 2020). Lastly, we expect brand characteristics to influence 
the wearing frequency, as the role of brands has been highlighted in literature as important for optimising 
clothing utilisation (Cooper et al., 2021).  
 
RQ2: How do wardrobe practices, garment specifications, garment use and brand characteristics influence 
the expected total physical and total active use time of a pair of trousers?  
 
H2: Similar to H1, we expect that garment specifications influence the expected physical and active use 
time of a garment. Regarding the active use time of clothing, one can expect that style and colour are 
influential, as garments in a classic colour or style are often kept longer, as they are less prone to getting 
out of fashion (Niinimäki & Koskinen, 2011) where material properties might be more related to the 
expected physical lifespan. Next to that, wardrobe practices are expected to have an influence the physical 
lifespan and active use of a pair of trousers, as a relation between clothing practices and clothing lifespans 
emerged from previous research (Laitala & Klepp, 2020). Moreover, garment use is expected to be 
influential, as satisfying use experiences have appeared to be important factors for long-life clothing for 
consumers (Niinimäki & Koskinen, 2011). Lastly, it is expected that the brand characteristics are influential,

2. Theoretical background  
 
This section explores supportive literature of the selected variables of this study, that relate to consumer 
behaviour in the use phase of clothing. A detailed analysis of both technical and social aspects will be 
provided, which have been the key parameters for the development of our methodology. 
2.1  Functional unit & Use phase of trousers 
Life Cycle Assessments (LCA) have been widely conducted to examine the environmental impact of the 
life cycle of apparel and other products. LCA studies can vary in scope, focusing on different aspects of a 
product’s life cycle. However, the most comprehensive scope is known as "cradle-to-grave," which 
encompasses the entire life cycle of a product, from raw material extraction through manufacturing, 
distribution, use, and ultimately disposal.  
 
The functional unit within these studies serves as the measuring unit, establishing the reference of the 
assessment, facilitating impact comparisons of systems with similar functions (ISO14044, 2006). It 
describes the function or service fulfilled by a product over a specific period (Hauschild et al., 2018), and 
by defining a ‘reference flow’ the number of items required to fulfil the functional unit can be determined. 
For instance, if the functional unit assumes a timeframe of ‘5 years’, and a pair of trousers with a physical 
life expectancy of only 1 year is being evaluated, it indicates that five of those trousers are required to 
meet the functional unit, which is visualized in Figure 1.  
 
The physical lifespan is often used as a reference point for defining the functional unit (Roos et al., 2017). 
However, research has indicated that longevity of a garment is not primarily determined its physical 
lifespan (Fletcher & Toth-Fejel, 2014; Laitala et al., 2018), as factors such as user behaviour and care 
practices play significant roles (Laitala et al., 2017b; Wiedemann et al., 2020). Consequently, the duration 
of the use phase of a garment is closely linked to its lifespan, which in turn affects defining the functional 
unit.   
 
==PAGE:15==
 
15 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
To date, there is not a standardized method for defining the functional unit for cradle-to-grave LCA studies 
on clothing. Cradle-to-gate studies, however, often define the functional unit by the garment or fabric mass 
itself, leaving out the use of the product (Laitala et al., 2018). Specifically for clothing, it is challenging to 
make a realistic representation of the use phase, as this often includes active and passive use periods. 
Heidenstrøm et al., (2021) highlights the contrast in active use periods for clothing compared to products 
such as refrigerators.  The active use periods directly define the impact of the use phase and are therefore 
highly important to clarify for clothing. Moreover, the use phase of clothing can include multiple users with 
different use patterns, when i.e., a user decides to sell a garment, making the use phase even more 
complex to map.  
 
Klepp et al. (2020) discuss what a functional unit for clothing should contain, based on a consumer survey, 
and argue that the functional unit should state the number of wears and the service lifespan (see Table 1) 
for all users in the lifecycle of a garment. In addition, they discuss how garment lifespans can best be 
described and measured. They argue that the unit in which clothing lifespans should be measured depends 
on the type of clothing and the intended use. For casual clothing, that are worn on a daily basis, the lifespan 
can best be measured in the number of wears, whereas the number of years is more suitable for occasional 
wear. Trousers, however, can both be intended for regular wear and occasional wear. Watson & 
Wiedemann (2019) argue that the most optimal way to realistically define a functional unit and model the 
use phase of a garment, is through a robust consumer survey. Nevertheless, this a time-consuming 
method (Wiedemann et al., 2020), hence, different ways of modelling the use phase are applied in LCA 
studies, where either a comprehensive consumer survey is conducted (Wiedemann et al., 2020), generic 
data is collected from national statistical databases (Sandin et al., 2019), or hypothetical generic 
assumptions are made (Laitala et al., 2017a).

2.2 Clothing lifespans 
2.2.1 Expected physical lifespan & expected social lifespan 
 
Within literature plural of definitions for the lifespan of products can be found, covering different timeframes 
of the use phase in the lifecycle of one product. In this study the terms relevant to this study are defined in 
table 1 and are adapted from a study carried out by Klepp et al. (2020) that specifically adapted product 
lifespan terms (Murakami et al., 2010) to clothing.  
 
Table 1 Definitions of different stages in the lifespan of clothing, adapted from a study carried out by Klepp et al. 
(2020). 
Term 
Definition 
Total lifespan 
The duration a garment exists, regardless of i.e. the number of users or decreased 
functions. It starts from the manufacturing of the item, until its end-of-life.  
Service lifespan The period a garment functions and can be used, actively fulfilling a service, including 
all users in the lifespan. It begins at the first purchase of the garment and ends when 
the last user discards it. The service lifespan is dependent on the physical and social 
lifespan of a product.  
Physical 
lifespan 
The physical lifespan of a garment can also be referred to as physical durability in this 
study. It indicates how long a garment can be put to use, before it is considered 
unaccepted to wear due to physical aspects of the garment due to e.g., wear and tear. 
This is related to the physical properties of a garment such as quality, strength, colour 
fastness etc. The physical lifespan in this study is referred to as the ‘total physical 
lifespan’ (TPL).  
Social lifespan 
The social lifetime, which can also be referred to as emotional durability, describes 
how long a garment is socially acceptable by the user. A social lifespan can end before 
the physical lifespan of a garment, and thus both decide the service lifespan.  
Active use time 
The period one owner uses a garment, starting at the first use by the owner until the 
last. The term excludes periods of inactive use. The active use in this study is referred 
to as the ‘total active use time (TAU). 
Possession 
span 
The possession span describes the time a user owns a garment, including both active 
and passive periods.  
Item age 
The item age describes the age of a garment from when it is produced, until the time 
of the study.  
 
This study attempts at exploring the total physical lifespan (TPL) and total social lifespan, which together 
describes the service lifespan of a garment, as visualized in Figure 2. However, examining the total social 
lifespan of a garment is challenging as it requires investigating the use of multiple owners of one garment.

Hence, regarding exploring emotional durability, this study only addresses the total active use time (TAU) 
of a pair of trousers by one owner. Even though the acquisition method is an included variable in study, 
revealing whether an item is second hand, this does not indicate the number of previous owners of that 
item. Figure 2 depicts the scope of this study highlighted in blue, with respect to the introduced terms in

lifespan (Laitala et al., 2018).  
 
 
Studies on the expected service lifetime of a garment are limited, and consumers find it difficult to assess 
the durability of their clothes (Cooper et al., 2021). Consumer research in the UK by WRAP (2013) found 
that the average active use time of trousers is 2.8 years compared to 3.3 years for clothing on average. A 
follow up on this consumer study in the UK, found an active use time of trousers of 4.3 years (WRAP, 
2022). This suggests an increase in the overall active use time of trousers, however other studies have 
found average lifespans for trousers of 2.5 and 6.2 years (Laitala et al., 2017a), and an average first-life 
use period of 3.5 years (Daystar et al., 2019). These varying results therefore confirm the need for further 
research on the lifespan of trousers.    
2.2.2 Wearing frequency 
The wearing frequency describes how often an item is worn, and thus is the key measuring unit to defining 
the active use period. In addition, it shows if an item is in actual active use at the time of a study. It is 
directly related to the laundry frequency and care decisions, since these three factors all influence one 
another, and thus having an influence on the environmental impactful of a garment (Laitala & Klepp, 2020). 
In addition, wearing frequency is correlated with the total lifespan of a garment (Laitala & Klepp, 2020). An 
extensive study in the use phase of apparel showed that trousers and jeans are most likely to be worn 
more than once, before laundering the item (Laitala et al., 2017a).

2.3 The user 
2.3.1 Gender, Age, Employment, Geography 
Clothing lifespans are affected by user demographics (Laitala et al., 2017a), such as nationality (Daystar 
et al., 2019; Laitala & Klepp, 2020), gender, age, and employment status (Fletcher & Fitzpatrick, 2021; 
Langley et al., 2013). Cox et al. (2013) also found that women were more likely to estimate a shorter 
lifespan of clothing, compared to men. However, a consumer study in the UK conducted by WRAP (2022), 
found no differences in estimating clothing longevity between gender nor age. Where consumers live also 
influences the garment lifespan and wearing frequency. For example, the climate of Scandinavian 
countries is colder compared to a Spanish climate, potentially allowing a sweater to have more wearing 
opportunities in Scandinavia compared to in Spain (Laitala & Klepp, 2020). Nevertheless, differences in 
temperature significantly change consumers’ buying behaviour, as warmer temperatures either increase 
or decrease sales of seasonal clothing (i.e., summer dresses in summer and winter jackets in winter (Oh 
et al., 2021). This in turn also potentially increases the turnover of clothing, leading to shorter active use 
periods. A consumer’s country of residence also affects consumer care habits, as for instance consumers 
the UK, France and USA wash their jeans more frequently (number of wears in between washes: 2.5, 2.3, 
2.3 respectively) compared to China (3.9) (Levi Strauss & Co., 2015). Given the relevance of user 
demographics to consumer behaviour, it is important to include this in this study.  
2.3.2 Residential area 
To our knowledge, the residential area of consumers in relation to lifespan of clothing is not yet addressed 
in research. In an expert interview with NewRetex3 it was noted that from their observations the quality 
and content of the received batches with wasted textiles is dependent on in which regions in Denmark 
these textiles were wasted and collected. This suggests that even residential area could play a role in the 
lifespan of clothing. Therefore, this variable is valuable to consider amongst the independent variables of 
this study.  
2.4 Wardrobe practices 
2.4.1 Trouser amount 
The content of a wardrobe, or wardrobe size, is positively correlated with the lifespan of the items within 
that wardrobe (Klepp et al., 2019). With every 10 items added to a wardrobe, the average lifespan of the 
garments within that wardrobe increases with one month (Laitala & Klepp, 2020). In this study the wardrobe 
size is limited to just one garment category, and only scopes the amount of trousers in a wardrobe.  
2.4.2 Purchase values 
Our personal values are amongst other things defined by our age, gender, culture etc. and are strong 
drivers of our behaviour (Fletcher & Fitzpatrick, 2021; WRAP, 2022). The theory of planned behaviour 
(TPB) is a model often used in consumer behavioural sciences, that contributes to explaining and 
predicting human behaviour (Rausch & Kopplin, 2020). It describes how our attitudes, the subjective norm 
and perceived behavioural control, influence our intentions and consequently our behaviour. Rausch & 
Kopplin (2020) proposed a framework inspired by the theory of planned behaviour and the theory of 
reasoned action, focussing on environmental intentions and purchase behaviour of consumers. They found 
that the environmental concern of consumer contributes to their attitude, which subsequently appeared to 
be a driver in purchase intentions. However, environmental purchase intentions only moderately influenced

the consumer’s purchase behaviour. This gap between the consumers’ attitude and their behaviour is more 
often found in consumer behaviour studies (Mandaríc et al., 2022).  
 
Langley et al. (2013) and Laitala & Klepp (2020) studied other parameters influencing purchase behaviour. 
Langley et al. (2013) found that comfort was amongst the key parameters. In addition, consumers who 
value buying clothes with a long lifespan, also reported using clothes longer. Laitala & Klepp (2020) found 
that valuing garment fit and design/style positively contributes to how often a garment is worn. This study 
evaluates the influence of purchase values on the lifespan and use of trousers, and accordingly the extent 
to which purchase values are reflected in purchase behaviours will be examined.  
2.4.3 Care label practices 
Apparel within the EU must contain a care label, indicating garment characteristics (e.g. the fibre content 
material and percentage %, size, etc.) and the appropriate care instructions, to most optimally maintain a 
garment’s aesthetics and physical characteristics throughout its lifespan most optimally (Nayak & Padhye, 
2015). In addition, within LCA studies, the use phase is sometimes modelled according to the care 
instructions indicated on the care label (Laitala et al., 2017a).  
 
However, numerous studies have found that consumers often misinterpret or disregard the care 
instructions. A study in the UK performed by Cooper et al. (2021) found that only half of the consumers 
follow the care instructions indicated on a care label. Another study on 15 consumers in Ireland carried out 
by Maguire & Fahy (2023) revealed that only 1 regularly checked the care instructions, and only two 
participants consulted it occasionally. In addition, a consumer study in New Zealand on T-shirts by Wakes 
et al., (2020) found that the price of a T-shirt negatively correlates with the extent to which care label 
instructions are followed. Since lower priced garments are often assumed having shorter lifespans (Mcneill 
et al., 2020), reduced adherence to care label instructions potentially accentuates this issue.    
2.5 Garment specifications 
The garment specifications indicate the characteristics of a garment. In this study, the characteristics of a 
pair of trousers are defined according to type, fibre content, how old the garment is, style, and which brand 
produced the item.  
2.5.1 Trouser type/Category 
This study exclusively focuses on the use of trousers and excludes jeans from the assessment. One could 
argue that jeans serve the same function as casual trousers, thus having similar functional units. However, 
jeans have different use patterns and care habits compared to trousers (Daystar et al., 2019; Laitala et al., 
2017a). In addition, trousers are a more diverse clothing category, where trousers can be made of different 
colours, styles, materials etc. This diversity in styles leads to different wearing occasions, as trousers can 
be i.e. formal, casual etc.  
 
Within literature there is no consensus on clothing categorization, which challenges comparisons between 
studies. Clothing are either categorized according to garment type, fibre content (Laitala et al., 2017a),  
occasion or function (Klepp et al., 2020). However, even within the garment category ‘trousers’ a variability 
in categorizations can be observed. There are studies solely focussing on trousers, covering all types of 
trousers regardless of style of use occasion in one term (e.g. WRAP, 2017). Other studies, however, 
differentiate between i.e. casual and formal trousers, thus categorizing trousers according to the use 
occasion (Laitala & Klepp, 2020).  
 
==PAGE:20==
 
20 
Participants in the consumer study carried out by Laitala & Klepp (2020) reported that garments only worn 
for occasions had a lower wearing frequency, had shorter active use periods, yet had the longest lifespan. 
In contrast to this, in WRAP’s most recent consumer study (2022) in the UK, they reported opposite results 
where casual garments had longer estimated lifespans compared more occasional garments (5 years for 
a casual T-shirt, compared to 3.4-3.7 years for a more occasional one). Interestingly, for clothing worn 
from the waist down, such as a pair of trousers, this difference between lifespans was not found. Since 
this study exclusively focuses on trousers, these differences in reported lifespans emphasize the 
importance of taking garment categorization into consideration.   
2.5.2 Item age 
Including the item age in this study is necessary when calculating the overall social or physical lifespan of 
trousers. Laitala & Klepp (2021) found that the item age is an influencing factor in how often the item is 
used. They studied the active use periods of a garment in relation to the item age and the number of users 
and found that newer items are more actively used compared to older garments. In addition, they found 
that second hand garments are worn 30% less compared to those that are new.  
2.5.3 Fibre content 
The fibre content of a garment is indicated on the care label. In comparison to jeans, trousers can be made 
from a diverse set of materials with a varying fibre content. A garment’s fibre content influences the way 
consumers take care of their clothing (Laitala et al., 2018), leading to different lifespans (Laitala & Klepp, 
2020) and different laundry patterns. Garments made from cotton have the shortest expected lifetime, 
whereas woollen and silk garments have the longest lifespans (Laitala & Klepp, 2020). Due to its relevance 
to environmental impacts in the use phase, fibre content is considered in this study.  
2.5.4 Style, Fit & Colour 
Colour and style characteristics are sensitive to fashion trends and change seasonally (King, 2017). As a 
result, consumers are driven to buy new styles seasonally to be up to date with the latest fashion, which 
in turn results to shorter social lifespans of garments. According to Cooper et al., (2013) is a garment’s cut 
the primary design aspect influencing the expected lifetime. They found that tailored items and ‘classic 
styles’ had the longest expected lifetime.  
 
A consumer survey by Neto & Ferreira (2021) asked participants to select the oldest garment in their 
wardrobe and consequently consulted the determinants for the long life of this garment. As a result, 37.6% 
of the participants mentioned the design aspects of a garment to be a determining factor in the lifespan of 
garment. Garments were, amongst other things, described as simple, unique, different, classic etc. A third 
of this share was due to the colour of the item, either being more neutral (e.g. black, white, etc.) or more 
original (e.g. red, green, etc.). This suggests that design elements are an important determinant in the 
lifespan of clothing, and therefore highly important to include in the variables of this study. 
 
Our unit’s trouser portfolio consists of 5 styles, hence the name ‘Five Units’. These five styles differ in fit, 
which serve as the baseline for their designs. Based on these fits different styles are created varying in 
colour, fibre content, waist height and length. This study categorizes the fit of garments in the categories 
wide, straight, skinny, slim, tapered and flared, which are visualized in Figure 3.

2.7 Garment Use 
 
Garment use describes the variables during the use of a garment, that play a role in the use phase of a 
garment and potentially affect the lifespan of trousers.  
2.7.1 Acquisition method 
The way in which a garment is acquired, has an impact of how often it is used, and affects the expected 
lifespan. When a garment is acquired as a gift, this results in a decreased active use period (Laitala & 
Klepp, 2020). In addition, several consumer studies found that second hand garments have longer 
expected lifespans, compared to garments that were purchased new (Laitala & Klepp, 2021; WRAP, 2022). 
Moreover, WRAP (2022) addressed that even the way in which a second-hand garment is obtained affects 
the longevity estimations, as those bought in commercial settings were estimated to last 5.9 years 
compared to 4.9 years for those that were bought online. This could potentially be explained by the lack of 
physical cues from a garment (quality, fit, etc.) when purchasing an item online. However, estimating the 
lifespans of pre-owned and second-hand clothing is challenging (Laitala et al., 2017b). Most studies 
examining clothing lifespans are based on the reported behaviour of one consumer, where information 
regarding previous users is based on their estimations, thus resulting in uncertainties.   
2.7.2 Matchability 
In this study, the extent to which a garment matches with other garments in a consumers’ wardrobe, is 
referred to as ‘matchability’. To our knowledge, there is limited research investigating the extent to which 
the matchability of an item relates to the wearing frequency and the expected longevity. Given that 
matchability is influenced by individual preferences and values, such as the importance of appearing 
fashionable, which in turn is related to age, gender, culture, etc. (Fletcher & Fitzpatrick, 2021; WRAP, 
2022), it is plausible that matchability can impact the wearing frequency and the expected active use of 
clothing.

2.7.3 Wearing experience (Flattering, comfort) 
The way an item is experienced is largely subjective yet is related to how often an item is worn. The use 
experience of wearing a garment influences the extent to which a user is emotionally attached to the 
garment, which subsequently relates to how often and for how long the garment is worn (Cooper et al., 
2016). Fletcher & Fitzpatrick (2021) describe the fundamental role of our bodies in the wearing experience. 
Feelings about our constantly changing bodies, affect how we experience a garment on our bodies over 
time. Laitala & Klepp (2020) suggest further exploring the relation between the wearing experiences of 
garments, such as fit and comfort, in relation to their expected lifespan.  
 
Cooper et al. (2013) highlights that the importance of comfortable clothing, is largely dependent on the 
user group and the intended use of the garment. For example, consumers noted that comfort was of larger 
importance for sportswear and casual wear compared to occasion wear. Another consumer study in the 
UK carried out by WRAP (2022), found that a poor or uncomfortable fit was among the key indicators for 
not wearing a pair of formal trousers actively. This association between wearing experience and active 
wear, suggests that there is a relation to clothing lifespans as well.  
2.7.4 Laundry frequency 
How often a garment is worn directly influences the environmental impact of an item per wear, as increased 
active generates the need for care and laundry (Klepp et al., 2020). In addition, the laundry frequency is 
directly related to a garment’s physical lifespan measured in years and number of wears (Laitala et al., 
2020). Laitala & Klepp (2020) found that garments that were washed once after 30 wears or more, lasted 
up to 4.8 years longer and were worn 94 times more often compared to garments washed after every wear. 
Studies have highlighted the effect of user demographics, garment type, fibre content, and geography on 
laundry frequency as significant influencers on the number of wears in between cleaning cycles (Laitala et 
al., 2020; WRAP, 2022).  
 
The significant effect of consumer behaviour on laundry frequency, such as the experience of odour on 
increased laundry frequency (McQueen et al., 2019), underscores its relevance to explore in this study.  
This notion is also supported by Laitala et al. (2020), who found that the dominant factor influencing the 
laundry frequency is an item’s proximity to the skin. Since trousers are a garment category worn close to 
the skin, one would expect that this influences how often trousers are cleaned. WRAP (2022) concluded 
that trousers, on average, are worn 4.5 times in between washes, however, also outline that consumers 
between 18-34 are more likely to wear their garments less before laundering. Another study carried out by 
Daystar et al. (2019) found an average global number of 4.86 days in between cleaning cycles for pants, 
with a wide-ranging variability between countries (3.05-7.1 days for US and Japan respectively). However, 
the latter two studies, the category ‘trousers’ or ‘pants’ are used as an umbrella term, and therefore this 
number could differ for more specified garment categories i.e., casual pants, formal pants, etc.  
 
Studies examining the laundry frequency of consumers, mostly conduct consumer surveys to explore this 
topic. However, Klint et al. (2023) highlights the gap that arose in a Swedish consumer study, between 
stated domestic laundry behaviour based on memory and actual behaviour. A tendency could be observed 
in consumers underestimating the number of washes, and an overestimation the amount of laundry per 
wash, both affecting the environmental impact of a garment. Since the laundry frequency is directly 
associated with the environmental impact of a garment, and thus highly relevant for LCA studies, it is 
important to include this variable in this study.

2.6 The role of the brand 
2.6.1 Brand & Communication of Use 
Brands could play a crucial role in optimizing the use phase to mitigate environmental impacts (Cooper et 
al., 2017). Even though garment care labels contain relevant care information, retailers and brands should 
focus on more effectively communicating appropriate care of garments to properly maintain physical 
garment characteristics and extend physical and social lifespans (Cooper et al., 2016, 2017; Cooper & 
Claxton, 2022). Consumers lack knowledge of different fibre types, and the need for different laundry 
methods (Cooper et al., 2021). Despite ample research by organizations such as ISO (GINETEX), many 
consumers still do not fully comprehend the information indicated on the label (Nayak & Padhye, 2015). 
However, the task of educating consumers is acknowledged as a challenging endeavour (Cooper et al., 
2016), and thus far, no universally applicable approach has been proposed to enhance the general 
comprehension among consumers.  
 
Our Units actively promotes optimized use of their trousers in their marketing strategies and has included 
additional texts on their care labels. However, in their linear business model they (and many other clothing 
brands) are challenged to have a direct and measurable effect of these intentions.  
 
When estimating future use of a product, consumers rely on brand and price to formulate expectations to 
the lifetime (Cox et al., 2013). Regarding a garment, Laitala & Klepp (2020) found that valuing a brand in 
the purchase decisions of a consumer, appeared to negatively influence the number of wears of an item. 
High-cost brands and items are associated with a longer estimated active use time, compared to lower 
cost brands (Laitala & Klepp, 2020; WRAP, 2022). This relation between brand and garment lifespans is 
therefore relevant to include in this study.  
2.6.2 Environmental concern 
 
Within the UK, most fashion organisations value sustainability, yet this is not always reflected in the 
longevity intentions of their garments (Cooper et al., 2021). To enhance clothing lifespans, fashion brands 
need to adopt more circular and sustainable business models, that require developing marketing strategies 
that stimulate consumers to buy longer lasting clothing. On top of that, improving clothing longevity must 
be aligned with intentions to increase the active use periods of a garment (Cooper & Claxton, 2022).  
 
In this study, the extent to which an individual or a fashion brand is concerned with environmental issues 
is referred to as the ‘environmental concern’ of a brand (Rausch & Kopplin, 2020). A number of studies 
address the gap between positive consumer attitudes and intentions towards sustainable clothing and their 
purchase behaviour (e.g., Mandaríc et al., 2022; Rausch & Kopplin, 2020). Moreover, Rausch & Kopplin 
(2020) found that a consumer’s greenwashing concern towards fashion brands negatively influences the 
relation between sustainable attitudes and sustainable purchase intentions. This demonstrates that the 
environmental concern of a brand is a parameter that plays a role in consumer behaviour.  
 
In relation to lifespans, Laitala & Klepp (2020) found no significant correlation between valuing sustainable 
production when purchasing a new item and their expected lifespans. However, when assessing the quality 
of garment regarding the expected lifespan, a brand’s name and reputation play a significant role (Aakko 
& Niinimäki, 2022). Furthermore, Lundblad & Davies (2016) found observed longer estimated lifespans of 
clothing that was perceived as ‘sustainable’. These findings suggest that there is a potential relation 
between estimating the lifespan of trousers and a brand’s environmental reputation, which is relevant to 
examine and therefore included in this study.

3. Methodology 
The findings from the studied literature, have been translated to a set of variables that will be examined 
on its relevance to a trousers’ physical lifespan, active use time and wearing frequency, which supports 
answering the research questions.  
3.1  Measurement techniques  
To test the hypotheses, a wardrobe survey was designed addressing the independent and dependent 
variables, which are visualized in Figure 4. The proposed selection of parameters is built upon a framework 
presented in a study carried out by Laitala & Klepp (2020) that categorizes factors playing a role in the 
lifetime of clothing in user, clothing practices, garment, and garment use. In this study the variables are 
divided into five sections, wardrobe practices, garment specifics, use (pre-use, during use, expected use) 
and brand characteristics, where the latter variable mainly distinguishes this framework from the one 
presented by Laitala & Klepp (2020).  
 
According to Klepp et al. (2020) a constructed survey can provide key data to study the lifespan of clothing. 
Each independent variable was examined on the relationship with (1) the total wearing frequency, (2) the 
total expected physical lifespan (in years) and (3) the total expected active use time (in years). The 
complete questionnaire can be found in Appendix B.  
 
3.2  Experimental design/setup 
The questionnaire consisted of a maximum of 65 questions, with some questions queuing or skipped 
based on previous responses. Participants were requested to select three trousers from their personal 
wardrobe, which they had actively purchased themselves, and recently used at the time of participating in 
the study. These requirements aimed to reduce memory biases associated with the wearing frequency.

3.5 Statistical considerations 
Prior to recruiting participants for this study, the questionnaire was administered to a diverse test sample 
audience consisting of individuals from various backgrounds, including the target audience, students, and 
academics (n=17). This step aimed to enhance the user experience and minimize any potential 
misinterpretations within the survey. The official data collection phase resulted in a total of 44 participants 
responding to the survey (excluding participants from the test audience), and a total of 117 trousers were 
analysed. All statistical analyses were conducted on the group of trousers (n=117) since findings pertaining 
to trousers are the most relevant for the purpose of this study.  
 
The relationship between the independent variables (each with a distinct nature) and the dependent 
variables (TPL, TAU and TWF) were examined, by employing specific statistical analyses tailored to each 
variable. A visualization of the examined statistical analyses is depicted in Figure 6. Correlation analyses 
(Biserial, Pearson and Spearman) were performed when feasible to examine the significance and strength 
of the relationship between the variables. A positive correlation was observed when an increase in one 
variable corresponded with an increase in the other variable, while a negative correlation was observed 
when an increase in one variable was accompanied with a decrease in the other variable. 
 
Cases where a correlation analysis was not feasible due to the nature of the independent variable, a one-
way ANOVA was utilized to evaluate the significance of mean differences. In all analyses, the significance 
==PAGE:28==
 
25 
level p ≤ 0.050 was used as a threshold to assess the statistical significance of the relationships between 
the variables. If the calculated p-value fell below this threshold, it indicated that there was evidence of a 
statistically significant relationship. Additionally, trends between variables were observed and 
acknowledged when the p-value ranged from 0.050 to 0.060. 
 
Given that the total physical lifespan (TPL), total active use time (TAU) and the total wearing frequency 
(TWF) are obtained by using the item age as a reference, the item age specifically will not be examined 
on its correlation with these three dependent variables. Consequently, no correlation analyses between 
the dependent variables will be conducted. Yet, a correlation analysis between item age and expected 
physical lifespan (EPL), expected active use (EAU) and expected wearing frequency (EWF) will be 
assessed, to avoid misleading results. Additionally, the relation between EWF and TWF will not be 
assessed, since EWF is one of the variables used to calculate TWF.

4. Analysis of the results  
 
The results of this study will be discussed in this part of the report. First, the average results are discussed, 
followed by addressing the results of the statistical analysis. The average results per variable (per 
category) can be found in Appendix F, and the results of the statistical analysis in Appendix G.  
4.1 Average lifespans  
The results revealed that the average item age from the moment the 117 analysed trousers were 
purchased, of which 21 were purchased second hand, is 2.3 years. The total expected physical lifespan 
(TPL) and total active use time (TAU) at the time of the study were on average 7.4 and 10.4 years 
respectively. Figure 7 is a graphic summary of the difference between TAU and TPL. In addition, a large 
variance between the minimum and maximum values of each dependent variable can be observed.  These 
results suggest that there is a significant difference between how long consumers expect an item to last, 
compared to how long they expect to still want to use it. However, realistically, it is highly unlikely the 
expected end of use is longer than the expected end of life of a pair of trousers. 
4.2  Average wearing & laundry frequency  
The average total wearing frequency (TWF) throughout the total active use period of one consumer was 
521 wears. Additionally, the average laundry frequency of a pair of trousers is 4.5 wears in between 
washes. Subsequently, an average of 107 washes throughout the active use period of one consumer was 
obtained.

4.2.1 Hypothesis 1: Total wearing frequency (TWF) 
Both positive and negative correlations emerged between certain independent variables and the total 
wearing frequency (TWF). Most significant correlations were obtained with garment use, whereas no 
correlations between brand and garment specification emerged.  
4.2.1.1 User demographics  
Negative correlations emerged between user demographics and TWF, where a comparison between 
Danish consumers and consumers from The Netherlands reveals a connection between residence and 
TWF (r = -0.241, p = 0.032). Moreover, when grouping the data in such a way that Danish consumers are 
compared with consumers from the rest of the Europe, a similarly equal, yet slightly stronger, correlation 
occurs (r = -0.255, p = 0.014). These negative correlations indicate that Danish consumers tend to actively 
wear a pair of trousers more frequently and for a longer duration, although the strength of these 
relationships is moderate. The variables ‘age’ and ‘employment status’ did not significantly influence the 
TWF.  
4.2.1.2 Wardrobe practices 
One negative correlation was obtained between wardrobe practices and TWF. The purchase value 
‘environmental concern of a brand’ negatively correlates with TWF (ρ = -0.235, p = 0.023). These results 
indicate that the more a consumer values the environmental concern of a brand when purchasing a new 
pair of trousers, the less trousers are worn throughout their active use period.  
4.2.1.3 Garment use 
The majority of the obtained significant correlations with TWF, are in relation to the garment use. A positive 
correlation between the laundry frequency and TWF (ρ = 0.230, p = 0.027) emerged, demonstrating that 
the more wears there are in between washes, the higher the total number of wears throughout the total 
active use period.   
 
A link between the wearing experience also emerged, since a positive correlation was obtained between 
the extent to which a pair of trousers is experienced as ‘comfortable’ and the total wearing frequency (ρ = 
0.303, p = 0.003). However, no correlation between ‘flattering’ and TWF emerged. Another positive 
correlation emerged between the extent to which a pair of trousers matches with the rest of someone’s 
wardrobe and the total wearing frequency (ρ = 0.290, p = 0.005).

5. Discussion 
 
The significant relationships found between the independent variables with trouser lifespans and how often 
they are worn throughout their active life, are highly relevant for LCA research and the clothing industry. 
However, the small sample size of this study could be a possible explanation for the low to moderate 
strength of the found correlations obtained in this study. Hence, further exploration of parameters affecting 
clothing lifespans, is suggested to support the findings from this study. Nevertheless, our findings give a 
highly valuable image of preliminary areas of influence, which need to be considered when performing 
LCA studies.  
Average lifespan of trousers  
 
The general picture emerging from our data, is that a significantly longer active use time of a pair of trousers 
was obtained compared to its expected physical lifespan. Given that the total active use period merely 
contains one user, this might indicate that there is a drastic difference between how long consumers expect 
trousers to last, compared to how long they prefer them to last. This is a highly relevant finding for the 
industry, as this indicates that there is a strong need by consumers for trousers with longer lifespans, that 
can be used actively for a longer period of time. An alternative interpretation of this discrepancy could 
indicate the necessity for brands to effectively communicate the lifespan of their garments, or clothing in 
general. These findings suggest a potential mismatch between consumers' expectations and the actual 
durability of clothing, emphasizing the need for improved communication in this regard. Simultaneously, 
this needs to be supported by enhancing the consumers’ understanding in how to reach such indicated 
lifespans, by providing guidance for optimal care, repair, and reuse of their garments (Cooper et al., 2016).  
 
The average duration of active use for trousers obtained in this study (10.4 years) notably surpasses the 
finding of similar studies, which reported values from 2.8-4.3 years (Daystar et al., 2019; Langley et al., 
2013; WRAP, 2022). However, the average physical lifespan of trousers yielded in this study aligns more 
closely with values found in comparable research, which range from 2.45 and 6.2 years (Laitala et al., 
2017a), nevertheless is still higher (7.4 years). This could potentially be explained by an underestimation 
of where trousers are in either their physical lifespan or their active use time at the time of the study, which 
could be influenced by the wording of the survey question. Both questions strongly rely on the estimation 
capabilities of the participant, which could be a possible explanation for the large variance in answers (1-
55 years, for both TPL and TAU). In addition, the wording of the question could have been prone to 
misinterpretation errors, even though the survey has been reviewed by a sample audience. However, 
given that this study demonstrates that older trousers (at the time of the study) are associated with a 
shorter estimated remaining lifespan and active use time, suggests comprehension of the question.  
User 
 
Differences in lifespans based on a consumers’ country of residence could not be supported by the data 
from this study, which is not in line with observed relations in literature (Daystar et al., 2019). However, the 
obtained relationship between a consumes’ country of residence and a trousers’ wearing frequency, 
supports similar findings by Laitala & Klepp (2020). Danish consumers use their trousers more frequently 
when in active use, compared to other countries in Europe (and The Netherlands) is a highly relevant 
finding. Nevertheless, given that our data was not equally distributed amongst countries within Europe, 
future studies with larger sample sizes, equally distributed response rate throughout multiple countries, 
needs to further address differences between wearing frequency of European consumers. The trend 
emerging between the active user period of trousers and a consumers’ country of residence is a new 
==PAGE:43==
 
40 
finding to our knowledge, as studies examining the active use time of clothing (e.g. Langley et al., 2013) 
have excluded residence or nationality thus far. This potential area of influence therefore needs to be 
further understood in further research.  
 
Our results provide preliminary evidence that residential area is significantly related to both the physical 
lifespan and the active use of a pair of trousers, which is in line with our findings from an interview with an 
expert from the recycling company ‘NewRetex’. A possible interpretation of the finding that trousers owned 
by consumers living in the city are expected to last and be used for a shorter period of time, could potentially 
be explained by that these consumers might be more exposed to fashion trends, or might live closer to 
clothing stores. Thus, possibly having different values compared to those that do not live in the city. 
Interestingly, when comparing physical lifespans and active use periods of trousers by consumers living 
rural areas, equally to those living in the city low values were observed, which is not in line with our 
expectations. A misinterpretation of ‘residential area’ could be possible explanation for this, as the line 
between living in a rural area versus in a suburban area could be subjectively interpreted. Nevertheless, 
the number of trousers in this category is significantly low, hence further comprehension of differences in 
clothing lifespans based on residential areas needs to be obtained in future research.   
 
The identified significant correlations between ‘residential area’ and ‘employment status’ with ‘laundry 
frequency’ are relevant findings for LCA research, implying that these demographics should be considered 
when modelling the use phase of trousers. The reduced frequency of laundering among students may 
stem from financial considerations, while the lower frequency observed among full-time workers might be 
influenced by time constraints. The additional relation obtained between the age of a consumer and 
decreased number of washes, is aligned with previous consumer studies (WRAP, 2022). One could argue 
that this may be in relation to diverging values of younger consumers from older consumers, as research 
has highlighted the relevance of age to different use patterns of clothing (Laitala & Klepp, 2020).  
Wardrobe practices  
 
The found links between wardrobe practices and all three dependent variables are aligned with our 
expectations. While the number of owned trousers by the participants was relatively equally distributed, 
the lack of a significant link with trouser lifespans and wearing frequency, does not support the finding from 
similar studies (Klepp et al., 2019). This could potentially be explained by that this variable does not 
specifically indicate the total size of the wardrobe, as one could for example more frequently wear and own 
other bottom parts compared to trousers. 
 
The relevance of valuing comfort for increased active use periods, suggests that brands could pay more 
attention to promoting ‘Comfort’ as a key parameter when consumers consider buying new garments. 
Moreover, since another relevant finding demonstrates that when consumers value simple care 
instructions of a garment, longer active use periods are observed, brands should consider ease of care 
when designing new garments. However, testing if this value was reflected in the assessed trousers was 
not part of this study, and could be further addressed in subsequent research.   
 
In addition, 89% of the participants mentioned considering how often they expect to wear an item when 
purchasing new clothing. The positive association between this variable and the total physical lifespan of 
trousers is an interesting finding. It may imply that consumers are aware of the environmental benefits 
from enhancing the use of a garment. However, financial motivations could also play a role, in which the 
expected wearing frequency could be related to a price/expected wears ratio. Nevertheless, this effect is 
not observed when assessing the indicated wearing frequency of the examined trousers with physical 
lifespans. This discrepancy between intention and behaviour suggests a highly valuable area of further 
==PAGE:44==
 
41 
exploration. In addition, it highlights an opportunity for the fashion industry, to (perhaps even further) 
promote such purchase values to enhance clothing lifespans. 
Use of trousers 
 
Aligned with our expectations, our findings suggest a significant relation between ‘garment use’ and the 
wearing frequency, which is consistent with previous research (Laitala & Klepp, 2020). Regarding ‘pre-
use’, the finding that second-hand trousers have a shorter total active use periods is consistent with 
previous research by Laitala & klepp (2021). However, where Laitala & Klepp found that the active use of 
a second-hand garment is 30% less, compared to the first active use of a garment, our results suggest 
that this value is 56% for trousers, possibly implying that there is a difference between garment type.  
 
Similarly, the negative effect of purchasing a pair of trousers online on the expected physical lifespan has 
been demonstrated before (WRAP, 2022). With the growth of online retail of clothing (Lundblad & Davies, 
2016), the latter finding is concerning. While no effects on the active use time were observed, which to a 
certain degree might be considered as a better representation of consumer behaviour, the link with laundry 
frequency is highly interesting. Given that a pair of trousers is washed more often when a garment is 
purchased online, could be a result of possibly missing care or decreased care instructions on a web shop, 
which could have been provided when purchasing an item physically instore. These findings emphasize 
there lies potential in optimizing acquisition methods, to enhance both clothing lifespans and their active 
use time.  
 
During use, the obtained average number of wears in between washes in this study, 4.5, closely aligns 
with similar studies that reported values of 4.5 (WRAP, 2022) and 4.86 (Daystar et al., 2019). While the 
results show increased wearing frequency is associated with an increase of the number of wears in 
between washes, which seems understandable, it is not consistent with previous studies (Klepp et al., 
2020). However, when considering the positive influence of an increased number of wears in between 
washes on the estimated total physical lifespan, it suggests that consumers are aware of the beneficial 
effects of reducing laundry frequency on prolonging the lifespan of their trousers. Yet, more reasons than 
environmental, such as emotional or financial reasons, could be a driver in this type of use behaviour, in 
which it is economically beneficial or emotionally preferable to enhance the lifespan of a pair of trousers. 
Nevertheless, given that this study relies on a participant’s ability to memorize actions, such as laundry 
frequency, this data may come with uncertainties, as a recent study by Klint et al. (2023) highlighted the 
underestimation of the laundry frequency when this is based on memories. A subsequent study with a 
similar purpose could, for instance, apply a method in which a laundry diary is applied, resulting in accurate 
laundry data. Moreover, such a study could also further explore the influence of consumer behaviour on 
other parameters of the use phase, such as ‘drying’ and ‘ironing’. These parameters have not been part of 
this study yet are largely relevant for examining the environmental impact of the use phase of clothing.  
   
Even though, no associations between a trousers’ wearing experience and their lifespans could be 
observed, which was amongst our expectations, the significant contribution of a trousers’ comfort on the 
total wearing frequency is a highly interesting finding. This demonstrates that consumers should be more 
aware of comfort when purchasing new trousers, as valuing comfort was not reflected in consumers’ 
purchase behaviour. Additionally, clothing brands could pay more attention to designing for comfort, as 
could result in enhancing clothing utilisation. Moreover, the positive influence of the extent to which a pair 
of trousers matches with the rest of a consumer’s wardrobe, is a relevant finding. However, this study did 
not explore the rest of one’s wardrobe, hence no guidelines can be derived from this data on how to design 
for matchability most optimally. This is therefore a topic that needs further exploration in future research.  
 
==PAGE:45==
 
42 
The significant influence of reading a trousers’ care label for the material content and the care instructions 
on increased physical lifespans and decreased laundry frequency, creates a largely valuable area to 
further investigate. However, since merely three care labels were conducted in both ways (3 and 2, per 
analysis), a study with a larger sample size needs to further address this area. Nevertheless, this finding 
may suggest a focus area of improvement in the clothing industry in which brands need to emphasize the 
importance of consulting care labels to enhance clothing lifespans. Interestingly, trousers of which the care 
label was not consulted, resulted in having longer physical lifespans. A similar pattern was observed in 
laundry frequency, in which a low number of washes were indicated when a care label was not consulted. 
Given that the values obtained in this study rely on the participants’ ability to estimate lifespans, a possible 
explanation could be that consumers that do consult the care label are more conscious towards clothing 
lifespans, and thus might have different references when estimating physical clothing lifespans. 
Nevertheless, these findings emphasize the relevance of reading care labels, and therefore further 
research is needed to address this topic.  
 
Another noteworthy finding to discuss, is the correlation between wearing frequency and the projected 
remaining period of active use (regardless of the item age). While there wasn't a significant link found 
between the wearing frequency and the overall physical lifespan and active usage duration, the finding 
that a higher wearing frequency of a garment is associated with longer expected emotional attachment 
highlights the fundamental role of emotional durability in promoting more frequent and intensive usage.  
 
Trouser specifications 
 
Contrary to our expectations, the results provide no evidence of an association between trouser 
specifications with the total wearing frequency. However, the expected relation between trouser 
specifications with the total physical lifespan and the total active use time, is supported our results. The 
most significant effect was observed between a trousers’ age and the expected remaining physical lifespan 
and active use time, which is highly conceivable. In addition, the positive effect of the height of a trousers’ 
hem on the physical lifespan and active use time, is largely interesting. A possible explanation of this effect 
could be that shorter trousers are possibly more likely to be worn seasonally (e.g. in summer and spring) 
or their wearing frequency is more prone to weather influences. Hence, the trousers are worn less, and 
thus potentially laundered less, leading to longer expected physical lifespans. However, since seasonal 
items were excluded from the data analysis, other possible explanations might apply.  
 
Equally noteworthy is the significant relationship observed between the style parameter 'fit' and the total 
duration of active use. This finding aligns with the research conducted by Cooper et al. (2013), who 
similarly identified that the cut or fit of a garment influenced its anticipated lifespan. One possible 
explanation for the lack of influence of other style parameters, such as colour, on the physical lifespan and 
active use of trousers in this study, could be attributed to solely examining trousers that were actively used 
by the participants during the study period. It is conceivable that trousers with less active use might exhibit 
different usage patterns. Therefore, future research could further explore this aspect by conducting a 
similar study that encompasses the examination of trouser lifespans for all trousers within one's wardrobe, 
providing a more comprehensive understanding of the topic. 
 
Since the above-mentioned results demonstrate that design characteristics of a pair of trousers are 
influential on both the physical lifespan and active use duration of a pair of trousers, this indicates that this 
must be considered when estimating the serviceable life of clothing. However, due to the small sample 
size of this study, and a relatively narrow scope, it is suggested that this topic is further explored to fully 
comprehend its influence in future studies.  
 
==PAGE:46==
 
43 
In contrast to previous literature findings (e.g., Laitala & Klepp, 2020), no discernible impact of the type of 
fibre on the physical lifespan of trousers was observed in this study. However, it is important to note that 
the limited availability of trousers with intact care labels (to validate their fibre content), resulted in a 
significantly low sample size for this analysis, challenging obtaining significant differences between fibre 
types. Although the influence of a trousers' fibre content on the number of washes aligns with literature 
(Laitala et al., 2020), in which our results indicate that trousers with cotton as their main fibre are washed 
less frequently, it is necessary to conduct further research with a larger sample size to establish more 
compelling evidence for this relationship. 
Brand characteristics 
 
In contrast with our expectations, no relations between brand characteristics and the dependent variables 
emerged. One possible explanation for this could be that there is an uncertainty in a consumer’s ability to 
accurately judge whether a brand is genuinely environmentally friendly. While certification labels, such as 
‘Ecolabel’, may influence consumers’ perception of a brand’s commitment to sustainability, in an ideal 
scenario it would be beneficial for future research to explore and develop a proper grading system that 
objectively evaluates a brand’s environmental concerns. Subsequently, this could be applied to further 
examining the effect of a brand’s environmental intentions, as only 9% of the trousers were considered as 
being produced by brands that were environmentally concerned (5% agreed, 4% completely agreed). 
However, developing such a system is extremely challenging, as environmental consciousness of brands 
and individuals is complex and may be expressed in excessively diverse ways. Besides this, other brand 
characteristics that not were not a part of this study, could have a potential influence on clothing lifespan, 
since literature have demonstrated a relation between brand and formulating expectations to lifespans 
(Cox et al., 2013).  
 
Nevertheless, the demonstrated link between valuing a brand’s environmental concern and a trousers’ 
total wearing frequency and active use period, provides preliminary evidence that a brand’s sustainability 
intentions is of relevance to how a garment is worn. In addition, the trend emerging between purchase 
values and purchase behaviour, regarding the environmental concern of a brand, further reinforces its 
influence on usage patterns.  
 
The majority of participants in this study did not feel supported by the brands in how to optimally care for 
a pair of trousers (merely 20% felt supported). While no significant relations between use support and the 
three dependent variables were obtained, an unexpected negative relation emerged with laundry 
frequency. When consumers feel supported by a brand in terms of proper care instructions for trousers, 
an increase in the number of washes occurred. One possible explanation for this could be that consumers 
who indicate feeling supported may also place a higher value on maintaining clean clothes and ensuring 
proper cleaning practices compared to other consumers. Irrespective of this adverse association, the 
absence of consumer support in optimal care of a garments to enhance their lifespan, emphasizes the 
need for brands to effectively encourage and provide guidance for proper care, repair, and reuse of their 
clothing, as highlighted by Cooper et al. (2017). This notion is further substantiated by our research finding, 
which potentially underlines a desire to extend physical lifespans of garments. Furthermore, the results 
concerning the correlation between consulting care labels, longer lifespan estimations, and reduced 
frequency of laundry provide additional support for the notion that this is a critically relevant area that 
warrants further investigation.

6. Application of the results 
 
To demonstrate a possible application of the discussed results of this study, this section addresses its 
relevance to LCA research and outlines which areas still need further exploration. Based on four randomly 
selected samples of this study, 8 scenarios of the use phase are generated, all of which comply with the 
same functional unit, yet vary in reference flows. Accordingly, per each scenario the processes in the use 
phase are defined. Nevertheless, no environmental impact assessment will be performed, as the scenarios 
serve purely as illustrative and explanatory examples.  
 
6.1 LCA research  
6.1.1 Functional unit  
 
The functional unit in this case is based on obligatory and positioning properties (Hauschild et al., 2018). 
Obligatory properties refer to the essential characteristics that every product being assessed or compared 
must comply with or possess, which may include legal requirements or technical terms defining the 
fundamental attributes determining the product's classification. The positioning properties of a product are 
characteristics that define a product’s position in the market, which can include factors such as price, 
colour, comfort etc.  
 
Four inputs from the survey have been partly randomly selected, as the dataset was filtered from low to 
high either based EPL or EAU values. Accordingly, two samples from the data set within the lower 50% of 
the values were selected (EPL low, EAU low), and two from the higher 50% of the values (EPL high, EAU 
high), resulting in four scenarios. Table 7, outlines the variables of the four randomly selected samples 
from the survey, that correlated with TPL, TAU, TWF and LF, together with the obligatory and positioning 
properties of the analysed trousers. Yet, those used to calculate TPL and TAU are excluded from the 
visualization. Based on the survey data and the defined properties, two nearly identical functional units are 
defined that differ in the geographic location of where the trouser is used.  
 
6.1.2 Reference flows 
 
Accordingly, the reference flow of each pair of trousers is defined, which is depicted in Table 8. As can be 
observed, two reference flow scenarios are visualized, in which either the service lifespan is defined by 
the total active use time (TAU) or the total physical lifespan (TPL), calculated from the survey data. 
Consequently, the total wearing frequency (TWF), total laundry frequency (TLF), and the number of drying 
cycles are calculated within the service lifespan.  
 
For this example, certain assumptions similar to those in this study are made. First, it is once again 
assumed that TAU exclusively consists of a period of active use, thus not considering passive use periods. 
Secondly, it is assumed that each trouser will have two owners throughout its serviceable life. Since the 
calculated TAU values from our study, exclusively include the active use period of one user, the TAU is 
recalculated to compensate for the additional user. Given that our results demonstrate that a second hand 
trouser is associated with a 56% shorter total active use period, this value is considered when calculating 
the active use period per user of a pair of trousers. Additionally, while no correlation between a second 
hand trouser and its laundry frequency emerged in this study, an LCA study by Subramanian et al., (2022) 
found that, based on a consumer survey, second hand trousers are laundered and used 10% less. 
==PAGE:48==
 
45 
Additionally, this study calculated that a pair of trousers is machine dried two out of three washes. Both 
values are applied to the data from our survey, in which the same ratio of washing/drying is applied in the 
second use of the garment. Lastly, for the purpose of this illustration, ironing is excluded. It is important to 
note that it is likely that trousers are ironed as part of a cleaning cycle. However, specific data per garment 
type regarding the number of ironing cycles in a garment’s service life is limited (Laitala et al., 2017a; 
Munasinghe et al., 2021), and needs further investigation.   
 
To demonstrate how these obtained values influence the use phase of the trousers, in reference to the 
remaining life cycle stages, a generic life cycle diagram is visualized in Figure 15. Note that only the values 
are indicated in which TAU was used as a reference. The highlighted processes are based on the survey 
data, whereas the remaining processes have been inspired by a study investigating processes in the use 
phase (Munasinghe et al., 2021). While similar processes throughout the trousers’ life cycle are selected, 
a significant difference between number of use cycles per user can be observed, leading to different 
environmental impacts.  
 
What can be observed from these scenarios, is that it is highly important to properly define the service 
lifespan of a pair of trousers, or clothing in general. Table 8, outlines that the reference used for determining 
the service lifespan of clothing, can lead to extremely (up to 557%) different durations of the use phase of 
a pair of trousers. It is important to mention that a longer duration of the use phase, leads to higher impacts 
in this phase, while in reference to the functional unit, the overall impact per scenario to fulfil the same 
functional unit might result in different conclusions regarding environmental impacts (section 2.1). The 
extreme difference between the number of wears and cleaning cycles per scenario, emphasizes that it is 
highly relevant for companies to investigate use of their clothing to obtain a representation of the 
environmental impacts of the use of their garments most realistically. Moreover, it is highly important for 
brands to carefully define the duration of the service life of their garments, the define a functional and 
reference flow that matches a realistic scenario. As these differences directly lead to a different 
environmental impact of the use phase, it is essential to carefully investigate the use phase parameters as 
inaccurate data might skew the result to an unrealistic impact assessment.

6.1.3 Future research 
 
To further assess the impact of the results of this study, an LCA model could be generated based on the 
provided data in Table 7 and 8, in which the life cycle diagram in Figure 15 can be used as a reference. 
For this, a life cycle inventory (LCI) needs to be generated, which is a comprehensive list of quantified 
input and output values, i.e., the energy use of a residential laundry machine, that relate to the 
environmental impact of all life cycle stages within the studied product system that are associated with the 
functional unit (Hauschild et al., 2018). Data regarding additional input and output processes, i.e., 
detergents used during laundry, can be obtained from extensive LCA’s, such as a study on six garments 
in Sweden by Sandin et al., (2019) and a study on 15 garments (including pants) by Babel et al. (2019). 
Additional choices regarding the use phase can be supported with data from Laitala et al. (2017a). 
However, further research will have to explore ironing behaviour per garment type, to minimize the number 
of assumptions. By modelling an LCA system based on the findings from this study, there lies great 
potential in exploring the influence of the variables in the use phase on the uncertainty of the use phase 
model of a pair of trousers. These findings may have implications for further optimizing the uncertainty of 
an LCA model, in which the use phase is included.  
 
Furthermore, while this study solely focussed on the use of trousers, it is emphasizing the relevance for 
companies to investigate use of their clothing to obtain a representation of the environmental impacts of 
the use of their garments most realistically. Moreover, it is highly important for brands to carefully define 
the duration of the service life of their garments, the define a functional and reference flow that matches a 
realistic scenario. Future LCA research should aim at defining a method to optimally obtain a 
representation of the use phase of clothing, and subsequently structure the functional unit and reference 
flows which includes the dynamics of consumer behaviour.  
 
Moreover, subsequent research is needed to further examine the effect of aspects in relation to consumer 
behaviour on laundry behaviour. Besides its relevance of such a study to LCA research, brands could 
potentially design for optimized laundry behaviour based on these findings, to mitigate environmental 
impacts of their clothing. This research also points out that even within one clothing category, significant 
difference in use patterns occur, such as the influence of a trouser’ fit and the length of the leg’s hemline. 
This suggests that a more detailed categorisation of clothing is needed, which includes more variables 
than garment type, or its anticipated use, as no significant differences in use patterns between ‘casual’ 
and ‘formal’ trousers emerged in this study, which is inconsistent with previous research (Laitala & Klepp, 
2020).  
 
6.2 Brands & Industry 
 
The results of this study also have implications for brands and stakeholders in the clothing industry. Several 
findings suggest areas of improvements and further explorations to mitigate the environmental burden of 
the industry. To speed up such developments, legislations are needed that push for these changes to 
happen. 
 
The discrepancy between the expected duration of actively using a pair of trousers, compared to their 
estimated physical lifespans, implies a need for designing clothes that last, which would both be 
environmentally beneficial and may enhance customer satisfaction. In addition, it outlines the need for 
brands to effectively communicate the lifespan of their garments. Simultaneously, this needs to be 
supported by enhancing the consumers’ understanding in how to reach such indicated lifespans, as the 
==PAGE:53==
 
50 
results highlight that consumers do not feel supported in how to optimally take care of trousers. It is 
necessary to provide guidance for optimal care, repair, and reuse of their garments (Cooper et al., 2016).   
 
Given the influence of trouser design characteristics on clothing utilisation, brands could consider these 
parameters when designing new clothing. While style characteristics are prone to changes in emerging 
fashion trends, brands could consider exploring style that appear to be used more often, to enhance 
clothing utilization. In addition, factors such as comfort and the extent to which a garment matches with 
the rest of one’s wardrobe, suggests areas to further explore. Brands should have comfort as a key value 
in their design requirements, to optimize clothing use. In addition, while matchability is subjective to one’s 
wardrobe and preferences, it is worth exploring their target user and their preferences, to design clothes 
that would be worn more frequently.    
 
Brands and the clothing industry should also consider improving the online retailing of clothing, in such a 
way that consumers feel more informed about care requirements and physical clothing properties, i.e., 
qualitative aspects of the garment. Our results show that trousers purchased online, are washed more 
frequently, and are estimated to have shorter physical lifespans.

7. Limitations 
 
While the findings from this study provide preliminary evidence for variables influencing trouser lifespan, 
there are certain limitations to this study.  
 
Given that this study solely focused on female trousers, the results from this study are not generalizable 
to other clothing categories including male trousers. Subsequent research with a similar approach is 
needed to examine if our findings are supported in the other clothing categories, and to further explore 
which other variables play a role in clothing lifespans that were not part of this study. Moreover, as 
mentioned previously, the small sample size of this study limited finding significant relationships with large 
effects on the examined dependent variables. While significant relations between variables were obtained, 
a follow up of this study reaching a larger sample audience, which more equally distributed on user 
demographics such as country of residence, age, residential area, etc.  
 
In addition to the small size, the recruitment of participants could have created biases in the sampled 
audience. Participant recruitment through online platforms of DTU, such as DTU inside, possibly only 
targeted an academic audience. These participants could potentially be more conscious of their 
environmental impact in terms of clothing consumption. In addition, other participants recruited through 
platforms such as linked in, exclusively represents consumers that are 1) actively involved in these online 
platforms, thus excluding consumers that are not, and 2) are part of a professional or social network that 
is equal to the recruiters of this study.  
 
Even though a survey is a valid method to assess the lifespan of a garment (Klepp et al., 2020) a 
quantitative survey has its limitations. The survey only contained 1 open question, which was open to the 
interpretation of the participant. All other questions were categorical, which limits the participant to 
answering questions within these categories. Subsequent qualitative studies should aim to find a balance 
between obtaining quantitative and qualitative data regarding influential factors of clothing lifespans.  
 
Initially, this study aimed at distributing two identical surveys, where one targeted customers of OU and 
the other the general consumer. Since these surveys were spread on social platforms there was, however, 
a potential risk of customers from OU to participate in the survey directed to the general consumer. An 
increased risk to this appeared when the general survey link, distributed through LinkedIn, was reposted 
by OU, reaching their network including potential consumers possessing one or more of their trousers. 
This both could have created confusion between the participants. Nevertheless, the survey targeting OU’s 
customers was declared invalid, hence, minor effects of this confusion on the data analysis were expected. 
However, when further addressing differences between target users, this risk of confusion could be 
avoided by spreading the survey at a different time in the year. Yet, another potential risk might occur, as 
different trouser types are possibly worn during different seasons, influencing the results and comparability 
of the surveys.  
 
To scope the study and limit survey time, participants were asked to solely select 3 items that were actively 
purchased and worn actively and recently at the time of the study. By only having reviewed trousers that 
are in active use, those that are in passive use are not reviewed on this study. Investigating which factors 
contribute to infrequent and limited use of a garment is highly relevant to further examine, particularly 
regarding style parameters of trousers. Moreover, since our study exclusively concentrated on trousers 
that were actively worn, the timing of data collection may imply that the findings are exclusively applicable 
to trousers worn during the period when the study was conducted. However, when focussing on multiple 
countries, it is challenging to avoid differences between seasons during a study period, unless the study 
has multiple deployments within a calendar year. 
==PAGE:55==
 
52 
 
Furthermore, ‘sustainability’ is a topic that may be subject to social expectations and subjective norm. 
Hence, answers could have been prone to inaccurate representations of behaviour, as questions such as 
the participants’ purchase values, could have been answered to the positive end. While this study aimed 
at limiting biases, by investigating if intentions were met in their indicated behaviour, there is still a potential 
risk of the responses reflecting preferred behaviour rather than actual behaviour. A possible way to limit 
this risk is through an observation study or by conducting qualitative research. An observation study could 
also overcome the challenge of underestimating laundry and wearing frequency and estimating future use 
of a garment. Given that all calculated values in this study are based on the participant’s estimation, the 
value could be considered as merely an indication rather than a prediction.  
 
Lastly, the way in which the dependent variables of this study were calculated has led to some noteworthy 
limitations. The assumptions that had to be made to calculate the active use period and wearing frequency 
values, limit the representativeness of these values. For instance, it was assumed that a pair of trousers 
was used actively throughout the entire indicated period, thus, not including any passive periods. It is 
conceivable that a trousers’ wearing frequency is not equally distributed throughout the year, hence, the 
total active use period and wearing frequency are possibly overestimations compared to realistic values. 
Additionally, the indicated item age was a key parameter to calculate the dependent variables. However, 
when, for instance, a trouser was purchased a year prior to the study, but was decided to be first worn a 
month in advance the study, a huge overestimation of the active use period and wearing frequency are 
the result. Once again, an observation study would result in more accurate results, in which also the use 
of multiple users could be examined. However, this is a highly time-consuming method, yet leads to the 
most realistic results.

8. Conclusion 
 
Optimizing clothing lifespans and decreasing product replacement has been widely suggested strategies 
to mitigate the environmental impacts of the fashion industry (e.g., Fletcher & Fitzpatrick, 2021; Vesterinen 
& Syrjälä, 2022). By uncovering the factors influencing clothing utilization, clothing brands can enhance 
their comprehension of potential areas of improvement and influence. In addition, further comprehension 
of the use phase of clothing is essential for enhancing the representativeness of Life Cycle Assessments 
(LCA) on clothing. New information obtained about the use phase contributes to defining a method to 
optimally structure the functional unit and the use phase in LCA studies which include the dynamics of 
consumer behaviour. 
This study explored which factors are fundamental in the use phase and service lifespan of clothing, 
through a case study on trousers. A focus lied on unravelling how wardrobe practices, garment 
specifications, garment use and brand characteristics influence the expected total physical lifespan (TPL) 
and the total active use time (TAU) of a pair of trousers (RQ1). In addition, the influence of these 
parameters on the total wearing frequency (TWF) where examined (RQ2). Through a consumer survey 
targeted at the European consumer, between 18-64 years and wearing female trousers 44 participants 
were recruited, which collectively provided data of 117 trousers. Several noteworthy relationships between 
parameters were obtained, which have implications for LCA research and the clothing industry.  
The average expected physical lifespan and total active use time of trousers obtained in the study were 
7.4 and 10.4 years, respectively, indicating a large difference between consumers' expectations of how 
long an item will last versus how long they desire to use it. Moreover, second-hand trousers were found to 
have a 56% shorter total active use period. Additionally, on average, a pair of trousers is worn 521 times 
during one active use period, while the total number of wears in between washes, obtained in this study, 
is 4.5.  
A disparity in trouser utilization was noted among consumers based on their country of residence, with 
Danish consumers demonstrating more frequent use. Moreover, individuals residing in urban areas 
exhibited significantly shorter expected physical lifespans and active use times for their trousers compared 
to those living outside the city. Furthermore, the findings revealed significant influences of consumers' 
purchase values, yet not all purchase values were reflected in the analysed trousers, as only placing 
importance on factors such as a flattering fit and a brand's environmental concern appeared to be 
reasonable determinants of purchase behaviour. The characteristics of trousers, including their fit and 
length, emerged as factors influencing the estimated active use time and lifespan. Furthermore, trousers 
constructed with polyester as their primary fibre content were subjected to more frequent washing.  
Trousers purchased online, appeared to have shorter estimated physical lifespans, and are washed more 
frequently. Next to that, increased number of wears in between washes are associated with increased 
expected physical lifespans and utilisation. Moreover, wearing experiences, such as comfort and the extent 
to which an item is considered to match with the rest of the existing wardrobe, positively influences how 
often a trouser is worn. Lastly, consulting a trousers’ care label both for a its material content and care 
instructions, led to the highest physical lifespan estimations and lowest number of washes.  
Given that this study had a small sample size, with high variances in the results, a subsequent study is 
recommended reaching a larger sample audience, further exploring parameters of consumer behaviour 
affecting clothing lifespans, which would be more equally distributed on user demographics such as 
country of residence, age, residential area, etc. In addition, this study only scoped trousers that were in 
active use at the time of the study. Hence, future research will have to explore the factors playing a role in 
passive of use of clothing. Furthermore, subsequent research is suggested to examine if the findings from 
this study are supported in the use of other garment types.   
==PAGE:57==
 
54 
 
These results emphasize the relevance for companies to investigate the use of their clothing to obtain a 
representation of the environmental impacts of the use of their garments most realistically. Moreover, it is 
highly important for brands and future LCA practitioners to carefully define the duration of the service 
lifespan of each garment analysed. Given that the duration a garment is in service is defined by either the 
physical or social lifespan of garment, the disparity between these parameters obtained in this study, 
demonstrates that the service lifespan should be defined with precision, as inaccurate service lifespans 
can result in largely skewed and unrealistic environmental impact assessments. Future LCA research 
should aim at defining a method to optimally obtain a representation of the use phase of clothing, and 
subsequently structure the functional unit and reference flows which includes the dynamics of consumer 
behaviour.  
 
The discrepancy between the expected active use of a pair of trousers, compared to their estimated 
physical lifespans, implies a need for designing clothes that last, which would both be environmentally 
beneficial and may enhance customer satisfaction. In addition, it outlines the need for brands to effectively 
communicate the lifespan of their garments. Simultaneously, this needs to be supported by enhancing the 
consumers’ understanding in how to reach such indicated lifespans, as the results highlight that consumers 
do not feel supported in how to optimally take care of trousers.

Appendix C : Questionnaire invitation 
 
The following text were applied for distributing the surveys. Text 1 was used for recruiting the general 
consumer, whereas text two was targeted at customers from Our Units.  
 
Text 1: 
 
How do you wear and care for your trousers?  
  
We invite you to participate in a research study aimed to improve our understanding of the use and lifetime 
of female trousers. Understanding how people wear and care for their trousers is essential to optimising 
the use and prolonging the lifespan of trousers and will be used to inform future research into similar 
garments.   
  
This questionnaire has been developed as part of a MSc Thesis conducted at the Technical University of 
Denmark in collaboration with Tekstilrevolutionen and Five Units. Your input is extremely valuable in 
helping us understand the relationship between individuals and their trousers and contributes to mitigating 
the impact of the textile industry.  
  
Please note that upon participation your identity and results will remain strictly anonymous, and no further 
obligations are required after submitting your results. We kindly request that you complete the 
questionnaire by the 31st of May to ensure your valuable input is included in the analysis. Feel free to 
share this post with your network, colleagues, and friends who might also be interested in contributing to 
this research project. 
 
Text 2:  
 
How do you wear and care for your trousers?  
 
If you own a pair of Five Units trousers, this might be for you: Too many trousers don't find their right match

do better. Understanding your relationship with your Five Units trousers is essential to optimise the use 
and prolong the lifespan of our trousers. 
 
So, we've made this questionnaire in collaboration with TekstilRevolutionen and DTU. It's only worth the 
effort through the insights you can provide as a trouser user. Please help us out—a reward awaits you 
after answering the last question. We kindly request that you complete the questionnaire by the 31st of 
May to ensure your valuable input is included in the analysis. Please note that upon participation your 
identity and results will remain strictly anonymous, and no further obligations are required after submitting 
your results.
