Abstract
This project, made in collaboration with PURIFI, seeks to find why PURIFI have a deviation
of up to 20% between simulations and measurements in some cases. This report primarily uses
FEMM (Finite Element Method Magnetics) as the simulation software, because it is a free and
open source program. However, Simcenter MAGNET, by Siemens, were used by PURIFI when the
large deviation were found. Some differences between the two programs were not found, however
the error got minimized to the 2% to 6% range using FEMM.

Introduction
This project is made in collaboration with PURIFI, an industry leading driver manufacturer.
PURIFI has observed inconsistencies between simulations and measurements on magnet systems
for their loudspeakers. On some systems, simulations indicate up to 20% higher force factor than
measured.
This report seeks to investigate this issue.
The scope of the project is to identify
why PURIFI finds a difference between the simulated magnet systems and the measured magnet
systems of up to 20% in some cases. Magnet systems of different configurations will be examined,
with different magnet types and placements. The improved magnet simulation will help PURIFI
in the design process, which increases correlation between simulations and finished product.
Important aspects of the work include:
• Perform FEA of single magnetic parts and different configurations of magnets and iron
• Build the simulated configurations and make measurements for validating the simulations

Theory
This section governs the theory that is needed in later sections for a good understanding of the
subject.
3.1
The Lorentz force
The Lorentz force is a force acting on any moving charges in a magnetic field[4]. The Force on
a charge moving perpendicular to the magnetic field, will be perpendicular to both the magnetic
field and the movement direction of the charge, and have a magnitude of
F = Bqv
(1)
This can be turned into the three dimensional vector form
F = qv × B
(2)
3.2
The Hall effect
As described in [4], section 6.15. When a current carrying conductor is immersed in a uniform
magnetic field, an electrostatic field appears perpendicular to both the magnetic field and to the
current.
The electrostatic field arises due to the Lorentz force given by Equation 2 (see subsection 3.1).
The charge buildup, giving rise to the electrostatic field, continues until the force on the electrons
from the electrostatic field just balances the Lorentz force on the electrons. This is what is known
as the Hall field, and is given by
EH = v × B
(3)
Where EH is the hall field, v is the velocity of electrons, and B is the magnetic flux density.
From the hall field, the hall voltage can be found to be
VH = IB
nqel
(4)
Where VH is the hall voltage, I is the current, n is the density of carriers, qe is the charge of
carriers, and l is the width of the material in which the effect is present.
This effect is very useful in numerous applications, such as, with a known current and magnetic
field it can be determined if a semiconducting material is of the n-type, p-type or near intrinsic, due
to the different carriers. Another application is current measurement, if the material properties
is known and a constant known magnetic field is applied, the Hall voltage will be proportional to
the current. Another, more relevant for this project, application is the measurement of magnetic
fields, where the material properties and current is known, the voltage will be proportional to the
magnetic field, this is known as a Gauss meter.
3.3
Permeability and Susceptibility
Magnetic permeability is the ability of a material to be permeated by a magnetic field. This means
that a high permeability material can help make the magnetic field stronger, and a low permeability
material can make the magnetic field weaker. The symbol for permeability is µ. The permeability
is often given as the relative permeability compared to vacuum, µr =
µ
µ0 , where µ0 is the vacuum
permeability.

3.4
Magnetic materials
When it comes to materials there are three main categories, diamagnetic, paramagnetic and ferro-
magnetic, where the ferromagnetic is the most important in this project. Diamagnetic materials
slightly oppose the applied magnetic field, decreasing the overall magnetic field. Diamagnetic ma-
terials show no sign of temperature dependence. Paramagnetic materials contribute slightly to
the applied magnetic field, increasing the overall magnetic field slightly. Paramagnetic materials
vary with temperature, where the susceptibility drops as a 1/χ function. Ferromagnetic materials
contributes greatly to the applied magnetic field, thereby greatly increasing the magnetic field.
Ferromagnetic materials are also temperature dependant, and only show Ferromagnetic properties
below a certain temperature (depending on the material), this is called the Curie temperature. At
the Curie temperature, there are no magnetic properties left, and at higher temperatures these
materials act paramagnetic. Ferromagnetic materials, unlike diamagnetic and paramagnetic mate-
rials, have nonlinear relation between the B and H fields, and a hysteresis between the two fields.
The Ferromagnetic materials can be further subdivided into two groups known as soft magnetic
materials and hard magnetic materials.[4]
3.4.1
Soft magnetic materials
Soft magnetic materials are characterised by their tall but narrow hysteresis curve. this makes
them great for use as ”magnetic conductors” to guide magnetic fields along them, as they are easy
to change the magnetic direction in and can have a very high B field for a moderate H field. An
example of soft magnetic B-H curve can be seen in Figure 1a.
3.4.2
Hard magnetic materials
Hard magnetic materials are the materials we typically think of when we talk about permanent
magnets. these materials also have a tall, but much wider hysteresis curve. This means that a
much higher field is needed to magnetize the material in the first place, but also that the materials
holds on to its magnetization much better, even when small to medium strength fields are applied
to oppose them. The field strength that needs to be applied opposing the magnet to get a net zero
B field, is called the coercive force. This is where the hysteresis curve crosses the x axis (where B
is zero). An example of hard magnetic material B-H curve can be seen in Figure 1b.

4
Simulation
The Simulation in this project were made using FEMM, a free simulation software for Finite
Element Method Magnetics (FEMM). This were chosen as a good alternative to other paid software.
For the purpose of comparison, Simcenter MAGNET from Siemens, is also used, as this it the
software currently used by PURIFI, and it is what was used when the deviation were first observed.
4.1
FEMM
FEMM is quite an intuitive modelling software. To get started with the software I followed the
”Analysis of a Woofer Motor”[2] example on FEMM’s Web page.
Following the example, the
simulation were set up axis symmetric, revolving around the z axis. Then the system were drawn,
and the boundary conditions set up with the Open Boundary Builder wizard. With the system
set up, a MATLAB script can be created to move the coil in steps, thereby obtaining the Bl as a
function of x (or z in FEMM coordinates). There were also a template for this MATLAB script
on FEMM’s web page [2].
This script works by moving the coil to the bottom of the system, then running the simulation
and integrating the field over the coil to obtain the Bl. This is then stored in MATLAB, and the
coil is moved a small specified step size, where the simulation starts over. When the simulations
have run for all positions of the coil, the script saves the fluxdensity in the air gap. Finally the DC
resistance of the coil is also saved.
The material properties of magnets in FEMM can be entered as linear or nonlinear properties.
For the magnets, the nonlinear B-H curve provided by the manufacturer data sheet were used.
These curves can be seen in Figure 20. The manufacturer only supplies the second quadrant of
the B-H curves (as this is the most relevant part for permanent magnets). However FEMM only
takes positive numbers for the B-H curve. This means that the B-H curve have to be shifted from
the second quadrant to the first quadrant. The coercive force can then be set as a separate value
instead of being where B is zero.

Measurements
This section covers the equipment and methods used to test and measure the different systems
under different conditions.
5.1
Linear rail measurement
To measure the Bl of the motor in various states of assembly, a linear rail with a coil attached at
the end is used to drive the coil trough the system at a constant rate, so the changing magnetic
field will induce a voltage in the coil that can be measured. By knowing the speed of the coil,
the voltage measured, the length of the coil, and the number of turns in the coil, the Bl can be
calculated using the force on a wire [4] (this can be found from the Lorentz force).
F = BlI
(6)
If the wire is moved a small distance ds perpendicular to both B and l, the work done will be
dW = Fds. Since voltage is work per unit charge V = dW/dq, this can be substituted in to get
V = Blds

Since the current is the time rate of charge flow, I = dq

The velocity of the coil is known and constant, and the voltage can be measured and logged during
the movement. The setup can be seen in Figure 3.
This is a very robust measurement setup, as the coil goes all around the system, making the
positioning of the coil less important for a good measurement.

5.2
Gaussmeter
The linear rail system is also used with a gaussmeter (a hall effect sensor, see subsection 3.2), to
measure the flux density as a function of position. This measurement were taken multiple times
for each system, rotating it between measurements to account for tolerances making the field vary
slightly around the system. The gaussmeter used is Gauss meter HGM09s by Goudsmit magnetic
systems.
This setup can be seen in Figure 4.
However, the results from this setup is not really used, due to the fact that a slight difference in
positioning of the gaussmeter causes large differences in the data, making it unreliable unless some
form of fixture is made to ensure the correct position and orientation of the gaussmeter.

6
Procedure
The first thing to do is, verifying the large deviation found by PURIFI. This were accomplished by
assembling 4 different new speaker magnet motor systems and measure them with the linear rail
system. To obtain more useful data, the systems were also measured in between assembly steps to
get the data for partial systems. These partial systems, as well as full systems, can be seen illus-
trated in Figure 2. The partial systems were not used much, as there were no discernible difference
between the partial systems and the whole systems (in terms of error between measurements and
simulations). This, however, showed that the problem did not lie in the iron parts, as this would
have been reflected as higher errors in systems with more iron.
The simulations made in Simcenter MAGNET, supplied by PURIFI can be seen in Figure 6. Here
it is evident that there is a large difference between the simulation results and the measurements.
To confirm this, the material properties were copied from Simcenter, and pasted into FEMM, these
results can be seen in Figure 8. Here it can be seen that there is a significantly smaller deviation
than with the simulation made in Simcenter. This difference were investigated by making sure the
other parameters in the simulations, such as the coil and the dimensions, were the the same. The
source to this difference were not found, and the quest to get a simulation without (or with little)
error were continued using the FEMM models.
The flowchart seen in Figure 5, was made as a visual aid for which measurements were needed.
Figure 5: Flowchart for the data.
The material used for the iron parts is SPHC steel. SPHC steel is a commercial quality hot rolled
steel. SPHC is a specific grade and designation of material defined in JIS G 3131 standard, which
is a Japanese material standard for hot rolled steel[1]. However due to a lack of specified magnetic
properties for SPHC steel, another steel type is chosen from the FEMM library. Here 1018 steel,
1010 steel and 1006 steel were considered. As it can be seen in Table 1, 1006 steel is the one that
chemically resembles the specification of SPHC steel from PURIFI‘s supplier.

The full and partial systems were then Simulated using 1006 steel for the iron parts and the
manufacturer supplied data for the magnets. The results of this can be seen in Figure 10. This
difference is not as large as expected from the previous results, however lying in the 4% to 10%
error range, it is still not a negligible difference.
As a hypothesis the strength of the ferrite magnet were lowered in the simulations, without changing
the B-H curve. This were done by changing the coercive force parameter in the material. The
results can be seen in Figure 11. To verify that this is a good hypothesis, the ferrite magnets were
measured alone, these results can be seen in Figure 13. This verifies that the ferrite magnets are
weaker than expected, with the only other variable being the coil.
To further verify this hypothesis, a ferrite magnet, a neodymium magnet, and a piece of SPHC
steel were sent to Goudsmit magnetics, for them to measure the B-H curves of the material.
Unfortunately, they were not able to measure the materials in time, due to staffing limitations.
To confirm that the coil is as simulated, the DC resistance can be measured and compered with
the simulations, the physical dimensions can be measured, and it can be unwound to count the
number of turns. The results of this can be seen in Table 2, Figure 14 and Figure 15. Due to the
fewer turns and shorter length of the coil, the simulations were run again with the new number of
turns and length. The results of this can be seen in Figure 16 and Figure 18.
These new results show that the ferrite magnet probably is weaker than expected. However, now
the systems with both neodymium and ferrite magnets start to become weaker than expected, due
to the weak ferrite, this shows that the neodymium magnet might be stronger than expected, and
thereby compensating for the weaker ferrite in systems with both magnets. This was verified by
measuring the neodymium magnet alone, as seen in Figure 19. This could also explain why these
systems were so close to being correct despite having a weaker ferrite magnet than expected.

Discussion
Although the differences between the two different programs were not found, using the same
material parameters and and setup for the coil in FEMM, still gave large differences, and on this
basis the project continued only using the FEMM models.
It was unfortunate that Goudsmit were not able to provide the B-H curve data in time for the
project, as this could prove or deny the thesis that the ferrite magnet is weaker than the man-
ufacturer data sheet says. However the results of the lowered strength ferrite are close to being
without error, and on this basis the thesis have not been disproved yet.
The measurements with the Gauss meter would have been a valuable tool in this project, if it had
some sort of fixture to ensure that the measurements were taken at the exact position and rotation

There is a higher difference between the measurements and simulations for the 20 mm ferrite than

the other destroyed to verify the number of turns. This ensures that there is a specifically known
coil for measurements in future systems.

8
Conclusion
It can be concluded that the high error observed by PURIFI is rooted in many different parameters,
some of which were avoided by setting the simulation up in a different program, other due to wrong
parameters in the simulation.
It can be concluded that 1006 steel is a better approximation than the 1010 steel used by PURIFI,
by both comparing the chemical composition and the simulation results.
Furthermore, it can be concluded that these relatively complex systems are sensitive to many of
the small things that make up the parameters of the systems. It is therefore important to have
a good understanding of the system, to find and minimize the errors. The source of errors are
things such as, the dimensions of the system, the turn count and dimensions of the coil used and
the material parameters.

1
Introduction
PURIFI has observed inconsistencies between simulations and measurements on magnet systems
for their loudspeakers. On some systems, simulations indicate up to 20% higher force factor than
measured. An investigation into this issue would therefore be of great interest.
2
Problem Statement
I seek to answer the following.
1. Where do the differences between the simulations of the magnetic system and measurements
of the magnetic system come from.
• Is it possible to find and correct what makes the simulation differ from reality.
3
Scope of the project
This project is made in collaboration with PURIFI, an industry leading driver manufacturer.
The scope of the project is to identify why PURIFI finds a difference between the simulated
magnet systems and the measured magnet systems of up to 20% in some cases. Magnet systems
of different configurations will be examined, both of varying sizes and with different magnet types
and placements. The improved magnet simulation will help PURIFI in the design process, which
increases correlation between simulations and finished product.
Important aspects of the work include:
• Identifying magnetic properties of different components in the magnet system, such as iron
and permanent magnets
• Comparing measured data with supplier-provided data sheets
• Perform FEA of single magnetic parts and different configurations of magnets and iron
• Build the simulated configurations and make measurements for validating the simulations
Furthermore, it would be of great interest to PURIFI, to devise a BH curve measurement technique
to be used in future quality control of loudspeaker drivers, if time allows for it.

Project plan

4.2
Research
The project is heavily dependant on an in-depth understanding of electromagnetism, and therefor
requires some additional research.
This includes Reading books and articles that aids in the
understanding of the subject, and therefore provide important references to include in the final
report.
4.3
Simulations
Because the project relies heavily on the comparison between the simulations and real life, some
time is needed to get familiar with the simulation program used at PURIFI, Simcenter MAGNET
from Siemens. If there is sufficient time, and it is found appropriate, the same magnet system
can be modeled in another simulation software such as COMSOL, to compare more than one
simulation to real life. In the simulation software, multiple simulations will be made of different
magnet systems. This will provide a broader reference to compare to the measurements, and it
will give good insight in the modelling process and the parameters, such as geometry and material
properties, that can cause the difference that is observed.
4.4
Measurement
As an important part of this project lies in comparing the simulations to measurements, some good
and accurate measurements have to be made. Different measurement techniques will be considered,
to obtain the most accurate measurement results. Measurements will then be conducted on systems
representing the simulated magnet systems for comparison.
