Abstract 
 
Concrete is one of the most widely used structural materials, being a brittle material with low 
tensile strength and limited tensile strain capacities. Improving the weak characteristics of 
concrete can be achieved, among others, by using reinforcing bars and/or discontinuous fibers. 
Reinforcement bars and fibers are introduced in concrete to account for the low tensile strength. 
The reinforcement bars are activated when the concrete is cracked; due to bond forces the tension 
in concrete is transferred to the steel bars, thus a reinforced concrete can sustain flexural loads. 
Fibers, on the other hand, are introduced in the concrete mix as discrete elements, uniformly 
distributed in the concrete matrix, which tend to limit concrete cracking and improve strength 
and ductility.  
Fibers can be manufactured from different materials with different geometry and mechanical 
properties. Steel fibers are the most used type of fiber in concrete, the composite concrete 
obtained by addition of these fibers being called Steel Fiber Reinforced Concrete (SFRC).  
The determination of the pull-out resistance of fibers embedded in the cementitious matrix is 
done through various methods. Indirect tests entail the evaluation of the overall performance at 
the macro-scale, which is then used to calculate the mechanical properties of the composite 
material through mathematical models. Direct tests are based on the pull-out response of a single 
or multiple fibers from which the load vs. displacement curve is obtained for determination of the 
pull-out resistance.  
The most common research method developed to determine the pull-out response, is the single-
fiber pull-out test, in which a single fiber is pulled-out from a concrete or mortar specimen, while 
the load and fiber slip are recorded throughout the process. Note that the fiber can be embedded 
at different depths and at different angles.   
The variation of embedment angles simulates the behaviour of the fiber in a real concrete 
composite. Since the fibers are discrete and randomly distributed in the concrete matrix their 
angle with respect to the cracking plane varies, and in some isolated cases is straight to the plane 
of the crack. 
The pull-out experimental setups presented in the literature, are not developed to simulate the 
real behaviour of SFRC. In addition, there is currently no standard test specified in any code or 
specification for fiber pull-out characteristics.  
To study the effect of embedment angles, this project conducted pull-out experiments on fibers 
with two different geometries, straight and hooked-end, considering an increment of 15o for 
embedment angle. The embedment angle varied from 0o to 600, to obtain a better understanding 
of the pull-out behaviour with the increase of the embedment angles through pull-out force vs. 
displacement curves. Also, through the application of digital image correlation (DIC) and X-ray 
micro-tomography, this study is aiming to provide a better understanding of the extent and 
development of general and localized damage at the fiber and fiber-matrix.

 Introduction 
Concrete is one of the most widely used structural materials, being a brittle material with low 
tensile strength and limited tensile strain capacities. Improving the weak characteristics of 
concrete can be achieved, among others, by using reinforcing bars and/or discontinuous fibers. 
Reinforcement bars and fibers are introduced in concrete to account for the low tensile strength. 
The reinforcement bars are activated when the concrete is cracked; due to bond forces the tension 
in concrete is transferred to the steel bars, thus a reinforced concrete can sustain flexural loads. 
Fibers, on the other hand, are introduced in the concrete mix as discrete elements, uniformly 
distributed in the concrete matrix, which tend to limit concrete cracking and improve strength 
and ductility. 
Stress transfer provided by fibers is strongly dependent on the fiber pull-out mechanism. The 
bond mechanism that acts on the fiber-matrix interface provides the resistance to pull-out, the 
bond is influenced by the adhesion of the fiber to the cement matrix and the mechanical grip. The 
mechanical grip develops through friction between the fiber and concrete matrix and is also 
influenced by the shape of the fiber. If the fiber is smooth the mechanical grip is only given by 
friction, unless fibers get into clusters and mechanical clamping appears due to fiber to fiber 
interlock. 
The most common research method developed to determine the pull-out response, is the single-
fiber pull-out test, in which a single fiber is pulled-out from a concrete or mortar specimen, while 
the load and fiber slip are recorded throughout the process. Since the fibers are discrete and 
randomly distributed in the concrete matrix their angle with respect to the cracking plane varies, 
and in some isolated cases is perpendicular to the plane of the crack. The variation of embedment 
angle simulates the behaviour of the fiber in a real concrete composite, where fibers are randomly 
distributed. 
To study the effect of fiber embedment angle, this project conducted pull-out experiments on 
fibers with two different geometries, straight and hooked-end. The fiber embedment angle varied 
from 0° to 60°, to obtain a better understanding of the pull-out behaviour with the increase of the 
embedment angle through pull-out force vs. displacement curves. Further, through the 
application of digital image correlation (DIC) and X-ray micro-tomography, this study is aiming 
to provide a better understanding of the extent and development of general and localized damage 
at the fiber and fiber-matrix. 
 Aim and objectives 
The aim this project is to study the pull-out performance of steel fibers with various fiber 
orientation and embedded length, and to provide data on the local damage mechanisms at the 
fiber and fiber-matrix interface. 
To achieve these aims, the following objectives are defined: 
• 
Summarize and categorize the existing literature on pull-put performance of steel fibers, 
focusing on the influence of fiber orientation and embedded length. 
• 
Optimize the operation of existing test-setup for the execution of fiber pull-out testing, in 
combination with DIC measurements. 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
14 
 
• 
Characterize the pull-out performance of commercial steel fibers depending on the fiber 
orientation and embedded length and to combine standard measuring techniques with 
DIC analysis. 
• 
Evaluate the local damage at the fiber-matrix interface using X-ray tomography. 
 Methodology 
For the fulfilment of the project objectives, the methodology is based on the following steps:  
• 
Literature review by identification and classification of existing literature, summarizing 
of important aspects from literature such as tests used, results, conclusions. 
• 
Experimental study which include preparation of specimens, test setup, testing of 
samples and scanning of required samples. 
• 
Comparison of experimental findings from the present study and results published in the 
literature. 
• 
Analysis of experimental results with the help of descriptive statistics, DIC analysis and 
exploration of computerized tomography (CT) scans followed by discussion and 
conclusion based on the results. 
 Scope and limitation 
In relation to the abovementioned methodology, the scope of the project is as follows: 
• 
Development of a test setup to simulate realistic mechanical behaviour of a random fiber 
in cracked SFRC. 
• 
Determination of the pull-out resistance of steel fibers with different embedment angles 
and comparison of experimental findings with results published in the literature. 
• 
Interpretation and extrapolation of experimental results to contribute to further 
knowledge of SFRC. 
The scope of this project is limited by: 
• 
Standard experimental setups for single fiber pull-out tests at different angles do not exist, 
the authors developed a new system and worked with a new clamping system for fibers. 
• 
Only one-sided single fiber pull-out tests were conducted with cubic test specimens.  
• 
Smooth and hooked fibers have been investigated, other deformed fibers were 
disregarded. 
• 
Only one type of concrete was used.  
• 
Fiber embedment angles studied were limited to 5 particular values (0°, 15°, 30°, 45° and 
60°). 
• 
Variation of the embedment length was not investigated, being kept at 30 mm for all test 
specimens.

 State of the art 
 Introduction to fiber reinforced cementitious composites 
Concrete has a relatively low tensile strength; hence, it easily cracks in tension and through 
cracking becomes sensitive to environmental exposure. In low and medium importance buildings, 
such as residential buildings, moderate cracks (i.e. crack width below 0.5 – 0.7 mm) do not pose 
serious serviceability issues [1]. However, cracking becomes crucial in high importance buildings 
and structures, such as e.g. tunnels, special pavements and linings. In such structures cracks 
widths must be limited to maximum 0.05 – 0.50 mm, depending on the exposure conditions [1]; 
steel fiber reinforced concrete improves the performance of structures by reducing the cracking.  
The addition of fibers aims to create a pseudo-ductile behaviour for the hardened concrete, 
capable of limiting crack opening and to absorb a great amount of energy in case of a dynamic 
load. Fibers, which intersect a crack, will create a “stitch effect” and keep the crack from further 
opening. Fibers, if well bonded in the matrix, transmit stresses across the matrix cracks, and 
preserve the load-carrying capacity of the section [2]. 
Over the years, the evolution of reinforced concrete composites was also marked using different 
types of fibers – from natural fibers (straws) to polymer, glass and steel fibers. Furthermore, other 
types of materials also incorporated fibers to enhance their properties; from this category, 
ceramics, plastics and gypsum products can be mentioned. Steel fiber reinforced concrete was 
introduced commercially in the European market in the second half of the 1970s. Initially, steel 
fibers were mostly used as a substitute for secondary reinforcement or for crack control in less 
critical parts of the structure. Today steel fibers are widely used as the main reinforcement for 
industrial floor slabs, shotcrete and prefabricated concrete elements. Their use extends also to 
structural purposes as reinforcement of slabs on piles, full replacement of the standard 
reinforcing cage for tunnel segments, concrete cellars, foundation slabs and shear reinforcement 
in pre-stressed elements [3]. 
Experiments with discontinuous steel reinforcement elements, such as nails, wire segments and 
metal chips, date back to the 1910s. During the 1960s, major investigations took place in the 
United States, to evaluate the potential of steel fibers as reinforcement for concrete. Since then, 
there has been a substantial amount of research, development and industrial use of steel fiber 
reinforced concrete [4]. 
Stress transfer provided by fibers is strongly dependent on the pull-out mechanism. The 
effectiveness of the fiber is tested by the single fiber pull-out test, embedded in a cementitious 
matrix. The bond mechanism that acts on the fiber-matrix interface provides the resistance to 
pull-out, the bond is influenced by the adhesion of the fiber to the cement matrix and the 
mechanical grip. The mechanical grip develops through friction between the fiber and concrete 
matrix and is also influenced by the shape of the fiber. If the fiber is smooth the mechanical grip 
is only given by friction, unless fibers get into clusters and mechanical clamping appears due to 
fiber to fiber interlock. On the other hand, if the fiber has a varying geometry (i.e. hooks at the 
end, undulated shape), the additional energy needed to pull the fiber out of the matrix, is the 
energy required to straighten the fiber. Same analogy can be made regarding the fiber 
embedment angle; the pull-out energy for an angled fiber is greater than the pull-out energy for 
an uninclined, aligned fiber, due to the additional forces introduced in the concrete because of the 
angle. Leading in the end to a different pull-out response, with higher forces needed to pull the 
fiber out of the matrix [5].

 Pull-out behaviour of steel fibers 
Within this chapter, the pull-out behaviour of smooth and hooked-end fibers is discussed from a 
mechanical point of view. The most relevant factor that influences the behaviour of steel fibers is 
the relation between the pull-out force and the slip of the fiber. Explicitly, the slip of the fiber is 
constituted of the displacement of the fiber with respect to the matrix (Figure 2.1). For a better 
understanding of this behaviour, different parameters such as bond type and pull-out mechanism 
are discussed briefly and qualitatively in the following.

 Adhesive Bond 
Fiber reinforced concrete (FRC) is mainly composed of aggregate mixture, cement paste and 
fibers, which are bonded together at an interface. The interface surrounding the fiber can be 
referred to as the interfacial transition zone (ITZ), having a microstructure which is different from 
the bulk paste. This microstructure is dependent both on the nature of the matrix and on the 
processing of the composite.  
In general, the microstructure can have two different characteristics which affect the pull-out 
behaviour: it can cause either a porous and weak interface which leads to an overall reduction in 
bonding or it can cause a weak interface situated in the porous layer of the ITZ (not at the surface 
of the fiber). It is important to emphasise that for the first case, the characteristics of the 
microstructure influence directly the bond and pull-out resistance, whereas in the second case 
the characteristics of the microstructure influence the mode of debonding, having an indirect 
effect on the pull-out resistance [6]. 
The adhesive bond can be improved by decreasing the size and inhomogeneity of the ITZ as it was 
demonstrated by Igarashi et al. [6], which obtained through increase of mixing time a better 
microstructure (e.g. less porosity) leading to better adhesive bond and thus increased 
performance (Figure 2.2).  
Since the inhomogeneity of the ITZ microstructure is frequently present in conventional FRC, 
complex modes of cracking and debonding are induced. The main role of fibers is to increase the 
cracking stress of the cementitious matrix and to transfer stresses across the cracks once 
initiated.  
In general, in SFRC, the dominant pattern of cracking is that of multiply-branched and laterally 
offset cracks [7]. Investigations at higher resolutions, for example using a scanning electron 
microscope, showed that many cracks are blocked in the matrix just before interacting with the 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
17 
 
fiber or cracks being deviated at 90° to a new course approximately 10-40 μm in front of the actual 
paste-fiber interface [8], see Figure 2.3. Aforementioned observations are caused by the weak 
interface located away from the fiber surface in which debonding is not at the actual fiber surface. 
Experimental data shows that the adhesive bond influences the pull-out response. ITZ has an 
important impact on the adhesive bond when considering porosity and strength. One approach 
trying to improve the ITZ’s impact is increasing the densification by adding silica fume or by 
increasing the mixing time [6].

 Mechanical bond 
Necessity to develop adequate reinforcing efficiency has led to the development of mechanical 
bond by inducing deformations to fibers. Geometry modifications of the smooth shape to complex 
geometries such as hooked-end fibers, crimped or non-smooth, and others (Figure 2.4) are used 
to improve the anchoring of fibers. Aforementioned optimization of geometries with respect to 
fiber performance, induce significant improvements in the bond between steel fibers and the 
cementitious matrix, therefore the anchoring effect is generally more important than the adhesive 
effect [9]. 
Different and complex types of interaction between fibers and cementitious matrix can be 
observed in nowadays SFRC, which combine complex bond components. Important bonds 
include the following: physical and chemical adhesion, mechanical component induced by 
deformation complex geometries of fibre, friction and fiber to fiber interlock [10]. 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
18 
 
The first bond mechanism that is activated in SFRC is the adhesive and frictional bond, having a 
relative weak strength in comparison to the mechanical component. Adhesive and frictional bond 
exhibits important contributions to the overall fiber pull-out performance in case of composites 
with high surface area fibers (synthetic filaments such as carbon) and advanced cementitious 
matrices characterized by an extremely fine microstructure and very low porosity. In 
conventional SFRC, in which the high water/binder ratio induces a more porous microstructure 
and fibers have a greater diameter, adhesion and frictional bond do not have a significant 
contribution in anchoring of fibers, therefore this is one of the main reason for using fibers that 
offer mechanical anchoring [11]. It is assumed that during the pull-out process the anchoring 
mechanism involves two main processes:  the fiber undergoes plastic deformation (energy 
dissipation) and matrix cracking in the stressed vicinity of the fiber, these zones being larger in 
case of deformed fiber than for smooth fiber [11]. The last bond to be mentioned, is fiber to fiber 
interlock (i.e. clusters of fibers form and get entangled together), which is more pronounced in 
the case of high volume fraction of fibers. Homrich and Naaman [13] have shown in their study of 
Slurry Infiltrated Fiber Concrete (SIFCON) that fiber to fiber interlock can have a significant effect 
on the bond. SIFCON is a steel fiber reinforced concrete with a steel fiber content up to 20 volume 
percent, while usual SFRC has a maximum fiber content of around 2% volume fraction, therefore 
fiber to fiber interlock is not considered in this research.  
The bond has also been classified by Naaman et al. [14] and Bartos [10] according to the stress 
transferred across the interface in shear and tensile bond. Shear bond controls the transfer of 
stresses parallel to the longitudinal axis of the fiber. For an uncracked composite, the shear bond 
is transferring stresses from the matrix into the fiber, while when the crack is formed the shear 
bond transfers the stresses in the uncracked part of the matrix. Two kinds of shear bond are 
involved in stress transfer i) elastic shear bond, which includes adhesion (the existence of the 
elastic shear bond implies compatibility at the interface between longitudinal displacement of the 
fiber and matrix) and ii) frictional shear bond, which resists displacement along the interface 
parallel to the length of the fiber (local slip is allowed, during this stage the adhesion is broken 
and becomes friction in nature) [10,14]. Tensile bond withstands displacement caused by forces 
acting perpendicular to the interface. Tensile bond has a direct influence on the behaviour of 
composites with discontinuous fibers. Tensile bond is also affecting the strength of composites 
when subjected to radial tensile stresses, which can be caused by lateral contraction or spacing 
of fibers [10].

 Pull-out mechanism of fibers  
The fiber pull-out mechanism is dependent on the bonding types of the fiber, therefore a brief 
discussion about smooth and hooked fiber pull-out mechanisms is presented in this subchapter. 
Since the embedment angle of the fiber with respect to the load is also an important factor, the 
influence of the embedment angle in the pull-out mechanism will also be discussed here. 
Smooth fiber 
The overall pull-out mechanism for smooth (steel) fibers can be explained in two stages: 
debonding and frictional slip of the fiber. Failure of the fiber-matrix composite occurs when the 
embedment length is fully debonded followed by pull-out of fiber opposed by friction until 
complete pull-out. The overall behaviour can be seen in Figure 2.5, which presents a qualitative 
load-slip curve. In the first phase, a linear increase of the load-slip curve is observed until Fcrit, in 
which the adhesive bond is active. Between the peak load, Fmax, and the point defined by Fcrit, non-
linearity of the graph can be observed; this non-linearity can be explained by partial loss of 
adhesive bond and the initiation of cracks in the ITZ, also the stress transfer within this zone 
combines adhesion and frictional stresses [11]. When the response of the specimen reaches Fmax, 
the fiber embedment length can be considered fully debonded, in other words, the adhesive bond 
is no longer active. After the peak load, Fmax, only the frictional bond remains active, which is 
characterized by decreasing pull-out forces with increasing fiber slip.  
 
Figure 2.5 Theoretical load-slip curve of aligned straight fiber, after [15]. 
Hooked fibers 
The pull-out mechanism of hooked fibers is similar to the one of smooth fibers up to the complete 
loss of adhesion bond. Even though the frictional bond is playing a major role after loss of 
adhesive bond in this case, mechanical anchorage of the hook has the largest contribution to the 
pull-out resistance after the peak load, Fmax. The overall pull-out behaviour can be represented by 
a qualitative load-slip curve as illustrated in Figure 2.6. The first two phases, mentioned for 
smooth fibers, are also valid here. These phases consist of fully active adhesive bond and partial 
loss of adhesive bond combined with frictional bond. In contrast to the smooth fiber: after full 
debonding has occurred, an increase in the pull-out load is observed due to the mechanical 
anchorage provided by the hook. When the peak pull-out load is reached, both hooks are 
straightened out, forming two plastic hinges. Progressive deformation of the hook results in a 
decrease of the pull-out force. At the end of the pull-out process, the fiber is fully straightened, 
none of the plastic hinges being active, and only frictional bond contributes to the pull-out 
resistance [11,16–18] .

 Inclined fibers 
In structural application of SFRC, random orientation of fibers is most likely to occur; therefore, 
the pull-out behaviour of inclined fibers is of important significance for understanding the 
behaviour of SFRC. The pull-out behaviour described in the aforementioned sections accounts for 
most of the phenomena involved in the pull-out process of SFRC; however, in the case of inclined 
fibers the pull-out behaviour is different [17]. Additional complexity must be considered in this 
case due to bending of fibers and matrix spalling [19]. The fiber is resisting the pull-out force in 
the case of inclined fibers by two main components i) interfacial shear stresses including the 
debonding of the fiber and ii) bending of the fiber against the matrix [20] . 
For smooth fibers, the pull-out mechanism is similar to the one of aligned fibers. The general pull-
out behaviour of inclined smooth fibers is represented qualitatively in a load-slip curve in Figure 
2.7. The initial linear part represents the adhesive bond, which increases up to Fcrit (S1) marking 
the beginning of the debonding process. After the debonding starts, the fiber undergoes increased 
friction due to the supporting matrix. An important phenomenon, which influences the pull-out 
behaviour in this case, is spalling of the matrix, which increases the free length of the fiber. Due 
to this phenomenon, the pull-out diagram increases at a slower rate and the peak load, Fmax (S2), 
is usually lower than for aligned fibers. The behaviour after the peak load is different in 
comparison to aligned fibers, the drop being smoother because of the additional friction due to 
fiber bending against the matrix. Therefore, a higher load is observed at the beginning of the 
purely frictional stage (S3). The load decreases with the increase of the displacement until a very 
short length of the fiber remains embedded. Once this critical length is reached (S4), a fast load 
drop is observed, as the fiber is pulled completely. It has been observed that the zero load point 
(S5) is always reached at smaller displacements than in the case of aligned fibers due to spalling 
of matrix, which contributes to the reduction of embedded length of the fiber as the pull-out 
process develops [17].  
For inclined hooked fibers, the initial pull-out response is similar to the inclined smooth ones, 
representing the adhesive bond up to Fcrit (H1). After Fcrit, a more complex process is occurring 
than for aligned hooked fibers, due to imposed bending of the debonded fiber, which induces 
matrix spalling (H2). This phenomenon is more pronounced as the angle increases. Considering 
the results presented in literature, after the peak load, Fmax (H3), is reached, no further spalling 
occurs. Further on, the pull-out mechanism can be described by activation of plastic hinges (H4, 
H5 and H6) due to fiber deformation until the tip of the fiber enters the straight segment (H7) 
where frictional bond determines the pull-out resistance. As for the aligned hooked fibers, when

the critical embedment length is reached, the load decreases suddenly to zero (H8). The general 
pull-out behaviour of an inclined hooked fiber is described qualitatively in a load-slip curve in

 Fiber pull-out tests 
The preferred failure mode of cement-based composites is the pull-out of the fiber that bridges 
the crack, rather than fracture of the fiber. Fiber pull-out dissipates more energy than fiber 
rupture. Furthermore, the energy needed for failure with hooked fibers compared to smooth 
fibers, increases due to mechanical anchorage [23]. 
Pull-out test methods provide a considerable amount of information about the fiber/matrix 
interfacial bond, under the assumption that the boundary conditions in the test specimen are the 
same as in the composite [24]. Although several different direct test methods have been 
proposed, the most fundamental and easiest to interpret is the single-fiber, concentric pull-out 
test, see Figure 2.9. 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
22 
 
The single fiber pull-out test method in general, has the following advantages as opposed to other 
methods of testing: 
• 
The geometry of the test specimen simulates the actual composite material, through the 
single fiber and the surrounding matrix. 
• 
The residual stresses that develop in the specimen due to matrix curing are similar to 
those in the composite. 
• 
Initiation and progression of interfacial bond failure in the test specimen and in the 
composite are similar [24]. 
Research in the area of fiber pull-out has considered the fracture mechanics and shear stresses of 
fiber-matrix failure. Since the 1950s, embedding and removing a single fiber or filament from neat 
resin has been used to model the failure of a composite material [25].  
Pull-out tests have been the basis for interpreting the bond properties. The final output of the 
single fiber pull-out test is a load versus displacement curve. The interpretation of the slip-force 
curve has changed over the years along with the understanding of the failure events. For example, 
contrary to previous interpretation, nowadays it has been shown that the crack initiation in the 
ITZ does not, necessarily, correspond to the point of maximum pull-out load[25]. Recent reviews 
of various studies indicate a general agreement over the two processes describing the pull-out 
mechanism: debonding associated with an adhesion type of resistance, followed by frictional 
resistance in the debonded portion of the fiber [26], as described in the sections above.

 Test methods 
Multiple test methods, see Figure 2.10, have been used to carry out the single fiber pull-out test. 
These test methods can be categorized based on the type and configuration of the test specimen 
[24]. A first classification can be done by the number of fibers tested with a single specimen: 
● Single-fiber - only a single fiber embedded in the specimen is pulled from the matrix. 
● Multiple-fiber - two or more fibers embedded in the specimen are subjected to pull-out 
forces. 
A second classification may be according to the configuration of the test specimen: 
● Single-sided - fiber debonding and pull-out must occur only on one side of an artificially-
induced crack or free surface of the specimen. 
● Double-sided - fiber debonding and pull-out may occur on either side or on both sides of 
an artificially-induced crack or free surface of the specimen.  
However, the single-fiber double-sided pull-out test is not widely used due to the difficulties 
encountered in analysis and interpretation of results, as well as complexity of the experimental 
setup [24].

 Pull-out rate 
Generally, the fiber pull-out test may be affected by numerous factors; the most influential factors 
for the response of a pull-out test are: the fiber and matrix characteristics, environmental 
characteristics (test temperatures) and loading characteristics [27]. 
Loading characteristics, such as load rate, existence or not of sustained strains and load cycles are 
some of the factors that may affect the response of a fiber to pull-out force. In a study performed 
by Nemkumar [27], the influence of loading rate was assessed by conducting two tests, one with 
a cross-head speed of 0.508 mm/min and another with the speed of 127.2 mm/min. The study 
comprises several additional variables: different water/cement ratios, various matrices, smooth 
and deformed fibers (Figure 2.11). The study concluded that for smooth fibers the influence of 
the load rate on the pull-out behaviour was not significant, while for deformed fibers, increased 
load rates resulted in higher pull-out forces [27]. 
In another study conducted by Xu et al. [28], single fibers were pulled from an ultra-high 
performance matrix (UHPC) at different load rates. The study included four types of fibers, 
straight-smooth (S), straight-hooked (SH - straight fiber - obtained through cutting the hooks of 
a hooked fiber), half-hooked (HH) and twisted (T). Four different loading steps were tested: 0.025 
mm/s, 0.25 mm/s, 2.5 mm/s and 25 mm/s. The pull-out response of the fibers was higher with 
increasing loading rate. As a method to quantify the sensitivity to change of loading rate, the 
authors introduce the Dynamic Impact Factor (DIF), as the ratio between the dynamic response 
and the quasi-static response of the fiber. Comparing the load rate sensitivity for the straight-
hooked fiber and the straight-smooth fiber suggests that a decrease in diameter increases the 
sensitivity to change of loading rate, DIF for SH is 1.07 - diameter 0.38 mm, DIF for S is 1.19 - 
diameter 0.2 mm, the phenomena (lower diameter yields higher DIF) was attributed to matrix 
abrasion, fiber surface damage and minimal fiber end anchorage.

As for the HH-fibers, which are manufactured by cutting off, the plastic hinge at the hook, the DIF 
is the highest among all, 1.28. This supports the hypothesis of potential local micro-split cracking 
due to high tensile strength of the fiber, high local anchorage and short embedment length. For T-
fibers the pull-out load also increased with the loading rate resulting in a maximum DIF of 1.14. 
Results suggest that the inertia effect of crack formation could potentially increase the matrix 
active crack length, le,cr and thus the pull-out load (Figure 2.12). Comparing the load rate

 Fiber properties 
Fiber geometry 
The resistance to cracking of the composite material that the fiber provides is strongly dependent 
on the pull-out mechanism. Smooth fibers have been extensively analysed, and experiments have 
shown that mechanically deformed fibers are more effective in improving the pull-out resistance 
[5]. The two main mechanisms that control the fiber pull-out response are the debonding and 
frictional sliding for smooth fibers, while the straightening of the fiber can be added as a third 
mechanism for deformed fibers. The fiber straightening mechanism must be taken into 
consideration since it influences the response in a complex way [5]. Steel fibers can have different 
shapes from hooked to crimped and twisted, as shown in Figure 2.4.

Zile [5] published a study describing the advantages of mechanically deformed fibers, in this 
study, smooth, hooked and crimped fibers are tested. Crimped fibers were embedded 30mm, and 
failed by fiber rupture at loads around 900N, only those embedded 15mm were pulled out. This 
leads to the conclusion that the larger the embedded part of the fiber with mechanical 
deformations, the greater the pull-out resistance. Hooked-end fibers were pulled out regardless 
of the embedment length, although, fibers embedded 30mm showed about 50% larger response 
than those embedded 15mm.  
An experimental study is presented in [29], which discusses the bond-slip response of deformed 
metallic fiber pulled out from a cementitious matrix. Five types of steel fibers were studied: flat-
crimped (SFC), flat-end (SFE), hooked-end (SHE), round-crimped (SRC), double-anchored (SDA). 
Results showed that fibers with less geometrical deformation have a lower peak load but higher 
grade of dissipated energy at large strains (i.e. total slip of 25mm), on the other hand fibers which 
are more deformed show a much higher peak load but the dissipated energy at large strains is 
lower. Such behaviour is due to the fiber rupture occurring with highly deformed fibers, since the 
loads transferred through the mechanical bond exceeds the tensile capacity of the fiber.  
As can be seen in Figure 2.13, SFC and SRC fibers, with high grade of deformation have a steeper 
initial phase than hooked-end fibers. It can be observed that SFC fibers ruptured when the tensile 
strength was overcome. Researchers observed an equal amount of fiber rupture and pull-out for 
the SRC fibers [29]. 
 
Fiber tensile strength 
Mechanical strength or tensile strength for steel fibers vary considerably from 0.5 to 2.7 GPa [11], 
depending on the type of steel used. Fibers can be classified after their mechanical strength into 
low, medium and high strength. In the case of the pull-out process, fiber tensile strength can be 
seen as a limitation, which causes fiber rupture and premature termination of the pull-out 
process.  
The pull-out response is linked to the embedment length and orientation of hooked fibers. 
Explicitly, increasing the aforementioned parameters, the magnitude and toughness of the pull-
out response is also increased but limited by the tensile strength of the fiber. Furthermore, when 
the pull-out force exceeds the ultimate strength of the fiber, a fracture of the fiber will occur [22].

Published research of Breitenbücher et.al. [30] has shown that the pull-out resistance is generally 
increased with increasing fiber strength. Regarding fiber efficiency (ratio between the maximum 
normal stress and the yield strength), high strength fibers showed the lowest efficiency (42.7% 
when tested in normal strength matrix. However, when tested in a high strength matrix the 
efficiency was much higher (61.6%), i.e. almost the same level as normal strength fibers when 
tested in high strength concrete (64.5%) (Figure 2.14).

 Inclination angle and embedment  
Fiber inclination angle 
Fibers in FRC become active when the concrete matrix cracks. A crack can be crossed by a large 
number of fibers and each one may be oriented with an arbitrary angle to the normal surface of 
the crack plane. Thus, it is of great importance, and the subject of this study, to understand the 
behaviour of the pull-out response in this situation. For this, pull-out tests are conducted with 
fibers being embedded in the concrete matrix at certain angles relative to the pull-out force. 
Published research showed that the fiber angle with respect to the crack plane has direct 
influence on the pull-out strength of the fiber [30, 31, 32, 2, 16, 15, 22]. 
During the pull-out of an inclined fiber, the following mechanisms usually occur: debonding of 
fiber-matrix interface; slipping between fiber and matrix on the debonded interface; bending of 
fibers at the surface of the matrix crack; and spalling of matrix due to the bending of fibers [19]. 
An experimental study conducted by Ouyang et.al. [19], analysed the pull-out response of smooth 
fibers with four angles, 0°, 14°, 27°, and 37°, respectively. The specimens consisted of two parts 
of matrix separated by a brass plate. In order to obtain the desired angles for the fibers, the plates 
had holes in which fibers can be aligned. For all specimens, the curing age was 56 days, the 
embedded length 12.2 mm and the same mix design for the matrix was used. Two types of 
specimens were tested: (A series) with 16 fibers bridging the sample and (B series) with 8 fibers. 
It was observed that both the peak pull-out load and the pull-out load after the peak increase with 
fiber angle for series A. Matrix spalling was not observed in these experiments, possibly because 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
28 
 
of the maximum angle, i.e. 37° used in the study. Radial matrix cracking at the exit of the fiber 
from the matrix was observed. It is worth mentioning that for the A specimens, both the peak load 
(per fiber) and the corresponding slip value increased with increasing fiber angles. No such 
noticeable effect was observed for series B specimens, with 8 fibers. The mechanisms involving 
the influence of number of fibers on pull-out load are currently not clear. The study explains the 
increase in the response through the so-called pulley effect. The pulley effect is illustrated in 
Figure 2.15(b), showing that only one component in the direction of the fiber axis Px of the total 
pull-out force Pθ is involved in the pull-out of an inclined fiber. As for the Py component, there are 
several models proposed to explain its role. One model as presented in [19], is the bending 
mechanism in which the bending of the fiber at the exit point from the matrix accounts for the 
increased response; another mechanism presented by the same authors [19], is the yielding 
mechanism in which, the fiber yielding and matrix spalling are responsible for this increase in 
pull-out resistance. Finally, the last mechanism is presented as the increased frictional stresses 
mechanism, which appears due to the Py component, as it introduces additional normal forces 
between the fiber and the matrix, thus resulting in a larger friction force. 
Trends of experimental results reported in literature show that fiber angle has a complex 
influence on the pull-out response in cementitious composites. Published results have shown that 
for inclined fibers, the pull-out force is higher up to a certain angle than for the case of fibers 
aligned to the direction of loading. The optimum angle, until which this force is increasing, has 
different values in literature, being mentioned to be between 15° to 40° in experimental research 
done by Breitenbücher et.al. [30] and between 30° to 40° [2,31,32]. Decrease in the load after the 
optimum angle was observed for different types of fibers, being more significant in the case of 
smooth fibers [32].  In the case of fiber efficiency, mechanical work increases proportionally with 
the angle, while the spalling phenomenon at the exit point of the fiber is more pronounced [30].    
In published research, combined angle effect with other variables such as matrix strength, fiber 
tensile strength or ductility can be found. The influence of the matrix strength has been found to 
be less significant with the increase of the fiber angle [30]. Increase of the fiber angle significantly 
reduces the rupture load of the fiber in comparison to aligned fibers; this phenomenon is referred 
to as apparent strength degradation [33]. In the case of ductile fibers (low and medium strength 
steel), which are more flexible and work as dowels, additional pull-out resistance can compensate 
for the reduction of the pull-out force due to the fiber inclination angle, while high strength steel 
can produce premature fracture of fibers [16]. 
As seen in Figure 2.15 (a, b) fibers were tested in a high strength matrix and at 5 different angles. 
Fibers with normal strength have lower peak strengths and rupture occurs for 60° fiber angles, 
while high strength steel fibers have much larger peak loads. In general, from the graphs 
illustrated in Figure 2.16 it can be seen that increasing the fiber angle leads to an increase of the 
displacement at peak load and the stiffness of the pre-peak branch decreases.

Fiber embedment length 
Resistance to debonding and pull-out processes is mainly a function of the fiber-matrix interfacial 
bond shear strength and the interfacial bond area, i.e. embedded fiber length times fiber diameter 
[34]. 
A study by Shannag et al. [35], analysed the influence of the embedment length with a single fiber 
pull-out test. The tested embedment lengths were 6, 12 and 18 mm, with high strength fibers, 
normal strength mortar matrix and cement based DSP (densified small particle) matrix. Results 
of the study concluded that increasing the fiber embedment length from 6 to 18 mm, while 
keeping the other factors unchanged, results in the increase of the pull-out load, as seen in Figure 
2.17. 
Published research by Tuyan and Yazici [36] shows experiments with hooked and smooth fibers, 
embedded at 3 different depths. The study focuses on the influence of the embedment length in a 
SIFCON matrix on the pull-out response of a single fiber. Tested embedment lengths were 10, 20 
and 30 mm, fibers of length 60 mm and diameter 0.90 mm with tensile strength of 1160 MPa. The 
mix was poured into 50 mm cubic moulds; no compaction was used because of high fluidity of the 
matrix. It was observed that increasing the embedded length of the fiber resulted also in the 
increase of the pull-out peak load. Increase of the embedment length of hooked fibers from 10 to 
30 mm caused an increase in peak load by 26% (Figure 2.18), while the increase for the smooth 
fibers was 80% (Figure 2.18). The study concluded that the embedment length has a great 
influence on the bond strength for smooth fibers, which is in correlation with theories that 
describe the pull-out process, as a dependent mechanism on the interfacial shear forces.

 Concrete matrix  
Concrete strength 
One of the major parameters that affects the pull-out behaviour of steel fibers, is the compressive 
strength of the cementitious matrix. 
Experimental research concerning the influence of matrix strength was carried out by Shannag 
et al. [35]. In the study, the authors present experiments in which 2 types of concrete matrices 
are used: a normal mortar matrix with 40 MPa compressive strength and a densified small 
particles (DSP) matrix system, with 150 MPa compressive strength. The DSP matrix consists of 
special DSP cement, which contains silica fumes and the mix has a very low water to cement ratio 
of about 0.18. DSP concrete provides a major improvement to the adhesion and friction bond due 
to improved particle packing, as shown in Figure 2.19. The obtained pull-out curves provide 
information about the energy dissipated and the influence of the matrix strength on the pull-out 
response. Results show that the frictional bond strength for DSP range from 3.4 to 6.58 MPa, and 
from 1.41 to 4.60 MPa for conventional mortar. The debonding energy of the fiber/matrix 
interface for DSP ranges from 20.6 to 56.2 N/m and from 6.3 to 12.6 N/m for conventional mortar, 
as illustrated in Figure 2.20.

A comparison on the pull-out performance between a normal strength matrix and high strength 
matrix is made by Soetens et al. [31] in their research to predict the pull-out behaviour of inclined 
hooked-end steel fibers. Compressive strength after 28 days was 47.4 MPa for normal matrix and 
76.5 for high strength matrix. The study included hooked and smooth fibers embedded in the 
matrix at different angles, normal strength and high strength steel. The study revealed that for 
aligned hooked-end fibers, a high or low-quality matrix does not provide a considerable 
advantage when the fibers are made from normal strength steel. The gain in pull-out load was 
only 13% while if high quality steel was used the gain in capacity was 37% due to improving of 
mechanical bond. For smooth fibers, similar results were found proving that the high strength 
matrix improves the frictional bond.

 Pull-out test 
The pull-out test has been conducted with a MTS 810 machine. A tailored clamp has been 
designed to test inclined fibers. The clamp was fixed on a steel rod and in the upper part of the 
machine, the moving head of the hydraulic piston. To accommodate the cube, a steel frame was 
built and fitted in the lower part of the machine where the loadcell was localized. Prior to 
clamping, the fiber was cleaned and degreased with alcohol to prevent fiber slippage and glue 
was applied on the tip of the fiber to ensure bond between steel plates of the clamp and the fiber. 
The pull-out procedure followed predetermined steps in order to capture the behaviour of the 
pull-out process. Data acquisition was set to 100Hz. The procedure started with a preload phase 
(load control) up to 0N, to create the same testing conditions for each sample. After the preload 
phase, the pulling started (displacement control) with a rate of 50 µm/min until the displacement 
reaches a value of 0.1 mm. The procedure continued with a pulling rate of 250 µm/min until a 4 
mm relative displacement was reached, ending with a pulling rate of 2000 µm/min until complete 
pull-out occurred.  
For the pull-out test, slip and force were registered by the machine using an incorporated linear 
variable differential transformer (LVDT) and by a load cell. Since the loading of the specimen was 
displacement controlled, the LVDT measurements were backed up by external measuring 
devices, thus two auxiliary extensometers were employed. The testing machine is presented in 
Figure 3.4.

 Analysis 
 Material properties 
In order to obtain the compressive strength of the concrete, result from testing of each series 
were considered part of a standard distribution. Mean value and standard deviation was 
computed for each batch. Thereafter, all the samples were considered as one population, i.e. mean 
value and standard deviation for all specimens was computed.  
For the tensile strength of the steel fiber, as only one type of fiber was used and influence of the 
tensile strength in the pull-out process is not an immediate output of current project, 
documentation provided by the producer was considered reliable. No further analysis of this 
subject was performed.  
 
 Pull-out tests 
Processing of the acquired data was done through a MATLAB® code. Data was imported at first 
as raw data and each parameter (slip, force, time) was stored separately. The analysis was done 
in two steps, pre-processing and actual processing of data.  
In the pre-processing stage, median filters were applied in order to smooth each of the data sets. 
For the slip data set, an average between the two measuring devices (auxiliary extensometers) 
was calculated and a resampling procedure was applied for obtaining a 0.25 µm resolution with 
respect to fiber displacement. The force and time data sets were linearly interpolated according 
to the resampling resolution. During the processing stage, the work done or dissipated energy, 
was computed as the area below the curve in the force-slip graph. All data obtained were then 
plotted in force-slip and work-slip graphs for a better interpretation of the data. All data from the 
tests was fitted to a log-normal distribution considering that the data is non-centred, and taking 
the mean of the results as final output. For this distribution, a confidence interval of 95% was 
chosen.  
Additionally, points of interest in terms of slip were considered. According to [1, 37], 
recommended crack widths for most common exposure classes are 0.2, 0.3 and 0.4 mm, 
respectively. 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5 and 2.0 mm crack width are also considered as points of interest for 
which information regarding the force and work is computed. Also, the point of peak force and 
debonding force provide important information.  
In the end, a comparison of the results from the testing machine data with published results was 
performed. For this analysis, an image data acquisition tool provided by MATLAB® was used in 
order to reconstruct force-slip curve from literature. An error of 5% in data acquisition was 
observed.

 Digital image correlation analysis 
Digital image correlation analysis (DIC) was used in order to check the data acquired by the two 
extensometers. For analysis of the images, ARAMIS® software was used. Within the process of 
analysis, pictures were calibrated according to the known distance on the reference. By choosing 
two pixels, one on the reference and one on the fiber, it was possible to determine horizontal and 
vertical displacement of the fiber against the reference on the concrete surface. The process for 
data acquisition and processing is presented in Figure 3.7.  
After ARAMIS® data processing, displacement obtained from DIC analysis was compared to the 
one measured by the extensometers. Based on the timestamps of the acquired photos, the pull-
out test data was resampled allowing for comparison of force and slip at the same exact moment 
in time. More explicitly, the slip-force curve created using DIC data was compared to the slip-force 
curve from extensometer measurements. Analysis was performed for 2 tests at each fiber angle 
both for smooth and hooked fiber, except for 45° smooth fibers, were DIC analysis was not 
performed.

specimens with 0° fiber angle and 56 µm/pixel for specimens with 45° fiber angle.

In Figure 4.27, an illustration of the pull-out mechanism influenced by the clamping system is 
provided. The torque lever is defined as the distance between the clamping point and the point 
where the resulting force of the reaction of concrete appears. In the left figure (Figure 4.27), the 
fiber is clamped at a distance of approximately 10 mm from the concrete surface, thus, the fiber 
being subjected to a rotation due to torque. The torque is expected to become larger as the 
distance between the clamping point and the concrete surface increases. This configuration yields 
high lateral forces, which has a large influence on the pull-out curve and will not reveal a true 
pull-out behaviour of the composites. In this configuration, the clamping system must ensure that 
the lateral movement is restrained. 
In the right figure (Figure 4.27), a second configuration is illustrated in which the clamping point 
is close to the concrete surface. The two lateral forces will still develop because of the fiber angle, 
but the torque is of a much lower magnitude, because the distance between the clamping point 
and the concrete surface is much lower, resulting in a smaller torque lever. Such configuration 
may easily put in practice, and if enough stiffness is provided, the rotation of the fiber is inexistent 
and a true pull-out curve may be obtained.

This section contains information regarding damage of the concrete matrix observed during 
experiments through images acquired for DIC and local damage of the matrix through CT scans 
of selected samples. The images from DIC evaluate matrix spalling observed at the exit point of 
the fiber from the concrete matrix, whereas CT scans present internal cracking and local damage 
of the concrete matrix during the pull-out process.  
4.4.1 Digital image correlation 2D 
This section discusses the spalling observed at the exit point of the fibers. The images acquired 
for DIC analysis were used to illustrate the initiation of spalling phenomena. For specimens with 
0° fiber angle, no spalling was observed during the test. Spalling phenomena were observed with 
increasing fiber angle. At 15° fiber angle (see Figure 4.28), spalling was observed at the end of the 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
70 
 
test for a slip of 25 mm, which does not impact the overall performance of the material under 
service or ultimate limit state (i.e. cracks up to 2.5 – 4.0 mm). Therefore, the phenomenon will not 
be discussed further for 0° and 15° fiber angle. For specimens with fiber angles of 30°, 45° and 
60°, the spalling phenomenon was observed at earlier stages than in the aforementioned cases, 
therefore detailed discussion is presented below. 
The analysis of the development of spalling, is divided into 5 stages: stage I, corresponds to the 
initial condition, in which the specimen is not subjected to any forces; stage II and IV, correspond 
to the debonding and peak point respectively; stage III, describes the beginning of spalling; and 
stage V presents the end of the experiment. 
Figure 4.29 presents the development of spalling for specimens with a fiber angle of 30°. Spalling 
is initially observed between the debonding (stage II) and peak (stage IV) point, at around 1 mm 
slip, closer to the peak point. The development of the spalling, from start (stage III) until the end 
(stage V), continues in the post-peak branch of the slip-force curve. It is observed by comparing 
the peak point (stage IV) and the final point (stage V), that the volume of spalled material 
increased relative to 0° and 15° fiber angle. 
Figure 4.30 presents the development of spalling for specimens with a fiber angle of 45°. For this 
case, the first spalling (stage III) was observed immediately after the debonding point (stage II), 
at around 1 mm slip. For this case, the stage in which spalling starts (Stage III) is closer to the 
debonding point than to the peak point (Stage IV), in contrast to 30° fiber angle. It is observed 
that for the peak point (stage IV), the spalling is already pronounced. Development of spalling in 
the post-peak branch is less pronounced than in the case of 30° fiber angle, by comparing stage V 
with stage IV, one can observe that there is limited variation in the volume of spalled material 
relative to 30° fiber angle. 
Figure 4.31 presents the development of spalling at 60° fiber angle. In this case, spalling is 
observed to start earlier than for specimens with a fiber angle of 30° and 45°. Initiation of spalling 
(Stage III) is observed before the debonding point is reached (Stage II). The peak point (Stage IV) 
presents pronounced spalling, indicating the development of spalling from the first observed 
stage. Comparing Stage V and Stage IV, one can observe that small differences are present in the 
volume of material spalled, indicating that further spalling does not appear for larger slip 
distances.  
The general trend in initiation of spalling is shifting towards smaller values of slip as the angle 
rises. For 0° fiber angle spalling was not observed while for 15° fiber angle spalling was observed 
for large slip values, i.e. 25 mm. For specimens with fiber angles of 30°, 45° and 60°, spalling was 
observed to initiate close to the maximum peak force for 30° fiber angle and decreasing to the 
debonding point in the case of 60° fiber angle.

4.4.2 Local damage at the fiber-matrix interface 
For the analysis of local damage at the fiber-matrix interface, x-ray computed tomography (CT) 
scans of 2 samples were performed (Figure 4.32), i.e. one sample for 0° fiber angle and one sample 
for 45° fiber angle. Both samples were scanned twice, first scan served as reference, while the 
second scan was undertaken after the fiber was pulled up to a defined value and used to identify 
damage in the pulled samples.  
It is assumed that during the pull-out process, the pull-out force is transferred from the fiber to 
the concrete bulk paste through the interface transition zone (ITZ) which has a lower strength 
than the bulk paste. In the initial stage, i.e. pre-debonding, this force is transferred firstly as a 
shear force between the fiber and ITZ and secondly as compressive force, which leads to damage 
of the ITZ in the hook region. As the pull-out process develops, matrix spalling was observed by 
means of DIC, see Section 4.4.1 for inclined fibers. Therefore, damage is assumed at the fiber exit 
point where the fiber is subjected to rotation. Localized damage at the hooked region and at the 
fiber exit point has been investigated in this study as shown in Figure 4.33. Due to limitations in 
the scan resolution (i.e. 45 – 56 µm/pixel), detection of micro-cracks in the specimens was not 
possible.  
Figure 4.34 shows the CT scans for the 0° fiber angle sample. Due to the abovementioned 
limitations in the scan resolution, slip is observed in the figure from the right side without any 
apparent additional damage (i.e. micro cracking). For the 45° fiber angle sample, even after the 
fiber was pulled 1 mm, no displacement at the tip of the hook was observed, see Figure 4.35.  
The analysis of the damage at the fiber exit point (Figure 4.36) reveals cracks due to bending of 
the fiber. From these observations, it can be concluded that in the case of 45° fiber angle, pull-out 
resistance of the fiber is inducing bending deformations at the fiber exit point before the fiber is 
pulled from the initial position. This bending is accompanied by spalling of the matrix as primary 
damage mechanism. This damage phenomenon is influencing the fiber pull-out resistance by 
reducing the fiber embedment length and extending the vertical displacement of the fiber at the 
exit point.

4.5 Comparison of obtained results with solid mechanics theory 
This chapter aims at providing a theoretical understanding on the influence of the overall stiffness 
of the clamping system and the embedment angle on the debonding peak. An ideal situation is 
presented in Figure 4.37, where a fiber at 0° and 45° angle is pulled with a force aligned to the 
==PAGE:78==
 
 
76 
 
axis of the fiber. For this ideal situation, the debonding force is assumed to be 250 N (based on 
the observation of this study) with a slip of 0.1 mm. In the case of 45° fiber angle, slip is measured 
along two directions: vertically and along the axis of the fiber. 
In Figure 4.37, Slip1 represents the slip of the aligned fiber, Slip2 is the slip measured along the 
longitudinal axis of the fiber, while Slip3 is the slip in vertical direction. As the parameters 
determining the pull-out response of the fiber are the same, the debonding force is the same. In 
terms of slip, Slip1 and Slip2 are the same, but Slip3, which corresponds to the extensometers 
measurements, is smaller due to the fiber angle, as shown in Figure 4.38. One may observe that 
the pull-out distance normal to the crack surface for an inclined fiber is smaller for the same pull-
out stage as for aligned fibers due to geometry, therefore the stiffness should be higher.  
The presented case is just an idealization of the pull-out process, in a realistic structural 
application, e.g. an element subjected to pure tension, cracking of the element activates the fibers 
and the pull-out process starts. The pull-out process involves complex phenomena (mentioned in 
Section 2.2.4) especially in the case of inclined fiber. Among these phenomena, the most difficult 
to quantify is the spalling of concrete, which has a significant influence on the pull-out behaviour. 
Therefore, the following analysis is presented as a qualitative discussion.

Considering a more realistic situation, e.g. a cracked structural element, the fibers are 
subjected to forces perpendicular to the concrete surface as shown in Figure 4.39. An 
idealistic experimental approach implies that the debonding force would be the same as in the 
case of aligned fibers, if the spalling phenomenon is not accounted for. For increasing fiber angles, 
the slip at the same force is observed to decrease. According to solid mechanics [44], the stiffness 
of the system should increase for larger fiber angles, since the pull-out distance normal to the 
crack should be smaller in the case of inclined fibers compared to the case of aligned fibers. This 
is not the case for this study or published results, in which the slip stiffness is decreasing as the 
angle increases. One exception has been found in the results published by authors of [31] in which 
the stiffness is increased in the case of specimens with fiber angles of 15° and 30° in the pre-peak 
branch. A graph showing the increase in stiffness for this ideal approach is shown in Figure 4.40. 
In this study, a distance of 5 to 8 mm was kept between the fiber clamping point and concrete 
surface. Due to insufficient stiffness of the clamping system, horizontal displacement of the clamp 
and rotation of the fiber was observed (Figure 4.41). This additional vertical slip measured during 
the experiment is shown in Figure 4.42. The additional displacement was calculated for the case 
presented above by using the cosine theorem, the results being presented in Figure 4.43. It is 
observed that, by adding the calculated displacement, the slip-force curve shows similar 
behaviour as the one observed during the experiments of this study and published in the 
literature, i.e. the stiffness is decreasing as the fiber angle increases.

 Conclusions 
This study investigated the pull-out behaviour of steel fibers embedded in a concrete matrix for 
specimens with fiber angles of 0°, 15°, 30°, 45° and 60°, respectively and through single fiber, 
single-sided testing. The study furthermore presents an alternative approach in studying the slip 
of fibers by aaplication of Digital Image Correlation and x-ray computed tomography.  
The unprocessed results gathered directly form the test-setup showed that increasing the fiber 
angle affects the pull-out behaviour of steel fibers. For smooth fibers, maximum pull-out force 
was found for specimens with a fiber angle of 15°, followed by a decrease in pull-out force. In the 
case of hooked fibers, pull-out forces increase with the angle of the fiber, the additional pull-out 
resistance provided by the hooked fibers, is 1.8 times higher for 0° fiber angle, and increasing up 
to 2.5 times for 60° fiber angle compared to smooth fibers.  
The acquired data with the extensometers was influenced by bending of the fiber during the test 
and additional horizontal displacement of the clamping system. The comparison of the data and 
results published in the literature showed similar results for a similar test setup as the one used 
in this study. However, discrepancies were observed compared to other studies; this denotes the 
necessity for the development of a standardized test setup, which simulates realistic pull-out 
conditions and possess sufficient stiffness in order to avoid undesired rotations and 
displacements.  
The test data acquired with the extensometers was corrected with a coefficient obtained by DIC 
analysis. Differences in slip for the peak point for smooth and hooked fibers are situated in the 
range of 0.06 to 0.9 mm, for smooth, and 0.15 and 1.8 mm, for hooked fibers, respectively. The 
additional information acquired from the DIC analysis shows that DIC is a useful tool to check the 
testing set up and determine the actual fiber pull-out performance.  
A significant dispersion in slip-force curve has been observed, which can be attributed to the 
inhomogeneity of the concrete matrix due to the presence of porosity around the fiber, different 
aggregates of different sizes and to the inexact dimension of the hook, which may vary from 1-4 
mm as stated by the producer.   
There is significant matrix damage occurring during the pull-out of an inclined fiber. The damage 
is concentrated at the exit point of the fiber from the sample, due to the tendency of the fiber to 
align with the direction of the pulling force bending occurs. The bending of the fiber introduces 
an additional state of stress in the concrete and cracking occurs with the matrix being spalled.  
Matrix spalling was observed during all tests and analysis was possible using DIC. Through image 
analysis, it was concluded that the spalling phenomena increases with increasing fiber angle. Also, 
it was observed that with increasing fiber angle spalling initiates at earlier points. 
The computed tomography study shows that for specimens with 0° fiber angle, the fiber 
displacement was found around the tip of hook. However, for specimens with 45° fiber angle no 
fiber displacement was found at the tip of the hook, damage was rather localized at the fiber exit 
point. Fiber rotation induces damage to the matrix but without any slip at the tip, up to a certain 
point. This phenomenon may explain the increase in slip as the fiber angle increases.  
5.1 Further research 
This study presents an investigation of the pull-out response of steel fibers embedded in concrete 
at varying fiber angles. During the project, a number of discrepancies and limitations were found 
==PAGE:83==
 
 
81 
 
and were discussed previously. In the following, suggestions are presented to overcome some of 
these limitations. 
Firstly, differences in slip between the results from the test machine and auxiliary measurement 
devices are subjected to errors in the case of the present study. Therefore, the use of DIC is 
recommended for future research. Improvement of the test setup stiffness is important since 
horizontal displacement of the clamp was observed, which had a large impact on the pull-out-slip 
curve. 
In this project, an analysis of the spalling phenomenon was performed with the help of image 
analysis. To improve the spalling analysis development of a test setup for a 3D image analysis is 
necessary. Due to this limitation, the present study did not discuss any results concerning 3D DIC; 
However, a setup was build and tested, as can be seen in Appendix J. 
For local damage at the fiber-matrix interface, improvement of the CT scan resolution is needed.
