Abstract
Drug resistance has always been a big challenge in cancer therapy, often leading to non­effective
treatments. This resistance is often generated by mutations in target proteins and can interfere
with binding. This study aims to address this issue by being able to know in advance whether a
therapy will be effective. To achieve this, a computational pipeline has been developed to predict
mutation­induced drug resistance mechanisms. The pipeline integrates structural bioinformatics,
molecular dynamics, and experimental data to validate the method.
The workflow starts with the automatic identification of approved drug­target interactions using
public available databases. Then, depending on data availability, it retrieves or generates the 3D
structure of the protein­ligand complex. The next step is the addition of mutations into the pro­
tein structures using the PMX toolkit and the production of molecular dynamics simulations with
GROMACS to model the structural and energetic changes of the system. Finally, alchemical free
energy calculations are performed using a thermodynamic cycle to quantify the effects of muta­
tions on drug efficacy. These calculations are validated against experimental data from scientific
papers or databases like MdrDB, which will ensure accuracy, reliability, and reproducibility.
The project uses the BCL2­Venetoclax interaction as a case study due to the fact that it is a well­
studied interaction and its clinical significance. This will help demonstrate and explain how the
pipeline works. The results obtained show that this method has high predictive power and is capa­
ble of accurately detecting which mutations are going to generate drug resistance. The main aim
of this project is to create a generalizable framework to study drug resistance in any drug­target
complex and contribute to the development of more effective and personalized cancer therapies.

Introduction
1.1
Background on cancer and drug resistance
1.1.1
What is Cancer
Cancer is a complex group of diseases characterized by uncontrolled cell growth and division.
This unregulated proliferation occurs due to the accumulation of genetic and epigenetic alterations
that disrupt normal cellular processes such as cell cycle regulation, apoptosis, and DNA repair
[1]. Unlike healthy cells, cancer cells evade normal growth control mechanisms, allowing them to
proliferate indefinitely and, in many cases, invade surrounding tissues and metastasize to distant
organs [2].
Cancer can arise in virtually any tissue and is classified according to the tissue of origin and molec­
ular characteristics [1]. The disease is driven by mutations in oncogenes, tumor suppressor genes,
and other regulatory pathways that govern cellular behavior[2] . These genetic and molecular al­
terations enable cancer cells to survive under adverse conditions, evade immune detection, and
develop resistance to therapies, making treatment particularly challenging [2].
1.1.2
Drug resistance
Drug resistance generates a big challenge in cancer therapy, limiting the effectiveness of treat­
ments and often leading to disease recurrence and progression [3]. Resistance mechanisms allow
cancer cells to survive despite the presence of chemotherapeutic agents or targeted therapies [3].
These mechanisms can be broadly classified into intrinsic resistance, where tumors possess preex­
isting resistance prior to treatment, and acquired resistance, which develops during therapy due to
selective pressure [3].
Cancer cells can develop resistance through various biological pathways [4], including:
• Genetic mutations: Alterations in drug targets that reduce drug binding or efficacy.
• Epigenetic modifications: Changes in DNA methylation and histone modification that af­
fect gene expression.
• Drug efflux: Overexpression of ATP­binding cassette (ABC) transporters that pump drugs
out of cells.
• Alterations in apoptosis: Disruption of cell death pathways that allow cancer cells to sur­
vive treatment.
• Microenvironmental changes: Adaptations in the tumor microenvironment, such as hy­
poxia and stromal interactions, that contribute to resistance.
The first consequence of drug resistance is tumor recurrence after an initial response to therapy.
Even if a treatment successfully reduces tumor burden, a small population of resistant cancer cells

While many drug resistance mechanisms exist, they can be broadly categorized into two types:
target­related resistance and non­target­related resistance. Target­related resistance involves direct
modifications of the drug target that affect drug efficacy, whereas non­target­related resistance
includes alternative survival pathways that allow cancer cells to bypass the drug’s effect.
One of the most well­studied resistance mechanisms involves genetic mutations in drug targets,
which can alter the drug’s binding affinity and render it ineffective. However, resistance can also
arise through completely different pathways, such as activation of compensatory signaling path­
ways, increased drug efflux, or metabolic adaptations [6].
This study specifically focuses on target­related resistance mechanisms driven by mutations that
alter drug binding affinity. Although this is only one aspect of resistance, it is critical to under­
standing how mutations impact drug efficacy and for designing strategies to counteract resistance.
Moreover, this underscores the necessity of predictive tools to identify drug resistance mechanisms
early. By anticipating resistance, oncologists can design more effective treatment strategies and
explore combination therapies to delay or overcome resistance.
1.2
Binding affinity prediction methods for drug resistance
The need for predictive tools has led to the development of various computational approaches that
can be used to study drug resistance mechanisms. This study specifically focuses on methods that
predict changes in binding affinity caused by mutations, as these alterations can directly impact
drug efficacy, as previously mentioned.

Two main classes of computational methodologies will be discussed: machine learning­based mod­
els and molecular dynamics simulations. Machine learning approaches utilize large datasets and
statistical models to predict how mutations affect the binding affinity of a drug to its target. Molec­
ular dynamics simulations, on the other hand, provide atomic­level insights into the structural and
energetic changes induced by mutations, allowing for a more detailed mechanistic understanding.
It is important to note that these methods are not inherently designed for studying drug resistance.
However, they are applied in this context to evaluate how mutations influence drug binding, po­
tentially contributing to resistance. The combination of these approaches provides a more com­
prehensive understanding of mutation­driven changes in drug­protein interactions.
1.2.1
Machine learning and its limitations
Machine learning (ML) has gained widespread attention as a powerful tool for predicting protein­
ligand binding affinity, offering potential applications in drug resistance studies [7]. ML models
use large datasets and sophisticated algorithms to recognize patterns in molecular interactions and
predict binding affinities. These models range from traditional approaches such as random forests
to advanced deep learning frameworks such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and graph
neural networks (GNNs) [8].
Several ML tools have been developed for binding affinity prediction. Pafnucy is a deep neural net­
work model designed for structure­based binding affinity prediction [9]. AtomNet, a CNN­based
architecture trained on structural data, has been widely used in drug discovery applications [10].
Another approach, SFCNN (Spherical Fourier Convolutional Neural Network), applies spherical
convolutions to protein­ligand complexes in 3D space to enhance binding affinity predictions [11].
Despite their potential, ML­based methods face significant limitations. One of the main challenges
is the dependence on large and high­quality datasets containing experimentally validated protein­
ligand complexes and binding affinity measurements [12]. Due to the lack of such datasets, many
ML models struggle to generalize across different targets, mutations, and drug classes [13]. In
addition, these models often rely on simplified molecular representations that do not fully capture
the dynamic nature of protein­ligand interactions in biological environments. The lack of explicit
conformational flexibility and solvent effects limits the accuracy of ML predictions, particularly
in cases where mutations induce structural rearrangements or allosteric effects [13].
These limitations highlight the need for methodologies such as molecular dynamics (MD) simula­
tions, which provide a detailed, time­resolved representation of the structural and energetic effects
of mutations.
1.2.2
Molecular dynamics
Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations offer a detailed, physics­based approach for studying the
behavior of biomolecules, including protein­ligand complexes. Unlike ML models, which rely on
precomputed molecular structures, MD simulations capture atomic movements and interactions
over time by solving Newton’s equations of motion [14]. This allows for the investigation of con­
formational flexibility, binding site rearrangements, and thermodynamic properties that influence
drug binding.

MD­based approaches are widely used to study drug resistance because they allow the direct cal­
culation of free energy differences between wild­type and mutant proteins. In particular, alchem­
ical free energy calculations provide a rigorous framework for quantifying how mutations affect
protein­ligand binding affinity [15]. These simulations utilize non­equilibrium transformations be­
tween wild­type and mutant states to estimate binding free energy differences (∆∆G), which can
be directly correlated with experimental binding affinity data.
Despite the fact that MD simulations are computationally expensive and require extensive pa­
rameter tuning and workflow optimization, their ability to mimic and capture dynamic molecular
behaviors makes them an indispensable tool for generic drug resistance studies [15]. The contin­
uous advancement of GPU technology has significantly improved the efficiency and scalability of
MD simulations, making them increasingly accessible for large­scale studies [14]. Moreover, once
high­quality drug resistance data has been generated through molecular dynamics, it can be inte­
grated with machine learning models to improve their predictive power, combining the accuracy
of physics­based simulations with the efficiency of data­driven approaches [16]. This synergy be­
tween MD and ML has the potential to revolutionize drug resistance predictions by enabling faster
and more reliable screening of potential resistance mutations.
The subsequent sections will provide a detailed theoretical background on MD simulations and
alchemical free energy calculations, including key methodologies such as thermodynamic inte­
gration (TI), Jarzynski equality, the Crooks fluctuation theorem, and the Bennett acceptance ratio
(BAR) method.
1.3
Theoretical background of molecular dynamics
As mentioned above, molecular dynamics (MD) is a simulation technique that provides an atom­
istic framework for studying the dynamic behavior of biomolecules, including protein­ligand com­
plexes. Unlike machine learning models, which are based on static molecular representations, MD
simulations explicitly model atomic motions and interactions over time by integrating Newton’s
equations of motion [14]. This enables the exploration of conformational flexibility, binding site
rearrangements, and thermodynamic properties relevant to drug binding.
1.3.1
Principles of molecular dynamics
MD numerically integrates Newton’s equations of motion to describe the time evolution of a molec­
ular system:

each atom is derived from the spatial variation of the energy landscape. The force field used in MD
defines the potential energy surface, capturing both bonded interactions (bond stretching, angle

bending, torsional rotations) and non­bonded interactions (electrostatics, van der Waals forces,
hydrogen bonding) [17].
MD simulations operate at femtosecond (fs) time steps and can be run for nanoseconds (ns) to
microseconds (µs), depending on computational resources. By sampling multiple conformations
through ensemble­based approaches, properties such as binding energies, conformational changes,
and solvent interactions can be extracted, providing a dynamic perspective on molecular interac­

Simulations are conducted under different thermodynamic ensembles, which define the thermody­

• NVE (Microcanonical Ensemble): Conserves total energy; no exchange with surroundings.
• NVT (Canonical Ensemble): Maintains a constant temperature using a thermostat.
• NPT (Isothermal­Isobaric Ensemble): Maintains both temperature and pressure constant,
mimicking physiological conditions.
For studying protein­ligand interactions, the NPT ensemble is typically used, as it best replicates
experimental conditions by maintaining realistic temperature and pressure [18].
1.4
Alchemical free energy calculations
Accurate estimation of binding free energy differences (∆∆G) is crucial to understanding how
mutations affect protein­ligand interactions and contribute to drug resistance. One of the most
effective approaches for this purpose is alchemical free energy calculations, which leverage non­
equilibrium statistical mechanics to compute the free energy differences between different molec­
ular states [15]. These methods rely on the thermodynamic principle that free energy is a state
function, meaning that the computed free energy difference depends only on the initial and final
states, regardless of the transformation path.
To systematically compute these free energy differences, alchemical methods make use of a well­
defined thermodynamic cycle, which provides a rigorous framework for calculating binding free
energy changes in both wild­type and mutant systems.
1.4.1

In alchemical free energy calculations, the thermodynamic cycle serves as the foundation for com­
puting the free energy differences (∆G) between the wild­type (WT) and mutant (Mut) systems in
both ligand­free and ligand­bound states. Since free energy is a state function, the thermodynamic
cycle principle, illustrated in Figure 1.2, can be applied to derive the change in binding free energy
(∆∆G). This approach enables precise quantification of the impact of mutations on ligand binding,
offering valuable insights into mutation­induced drug resistance.

1.4.2
Thermodynamic integration and non­equilibrium work theorems
Thermodynamic Integration (TI) is a widely used approach in free energy calculations [19]. It in­
volves defining a non­physical (alchemical) pathway that gradually transforms one molecular state
into another. This is achieved by introducing a coupling parameter, λ, that interpolates between
the initial and final states:
U(λ) = (1 −λ)UA + λUB
where UA and UB are the potential energy functions of states A (wild­type) and B (mutant), and λ
varies from 0 to 1, effectively transforming one state into the other. The free energy difference is
obtained by integrating the derivative of the Hamiltonian with respect to λ:

can be computationally expensive. To circumvent this, non­equilibrium work methods such as the

a series of non­equilibrium transitions.

1.4.3
Jarzynski equality and Crooks fluctuation theorem
The Jarzynski equality provides a relationship between the work done on a system in a non­
equilibrium transformation and the free energy difference between two states[20]:
∆G = −kBTln
⟨
e−W/kBT⟩
where W is the work performed during the transformation, kB is the Boltzmann constant, and
T is the temperature. Instead of requiring full equilibrium sampling, this equation enables free
energy differences to be estimated from multiple simulations where the system is driven from
the initial state to the final state under non­equilibrium conditions. These non­equilibrium work
trajectories refer to individual simulation runs in which external perturbations (such as changing
the coupling parameter λ) are applied at a finite rate, meaning the system does not remain in
equilibrium throughout the transformation. By collecting multiple such trajectories and statistically
analyzing the work performed in each, accurate free energy estimates can be obtained.
The Crooks fluctuation theorem refines this approach by relating the forward (wild­type to mutant)
and reverse (mutant to wild­type) transformations [21]:

PR(−W) = e(W−∆G)/kBT
where PF(W) and PR(W) are the probability distributions of work values in forward and reverse
directions, respectively. The free energy difference can be estimated from the intersection of these
distributions. These relations form the basis for statistically efficient methods, such as the Bennett
Acceptance Ratio (BAR), which further optimizes the estimation of free energy differences.

1.5
Case study: BCL2­Venetoclax
For the test and demonstration of the pipeline, a case study was used. The BCL2­Venetoclax drug­
protein interaction, which is already well known and supported by extensive experimental data,
was chosen to validate the computational calculations.
1.5.1
BCL2: B­cell lymphoma 2
The B­cell lymphoma 2 (BCL2) protein is a central regulator of apoptosis, the programmed cell
death mechanism critical for maintaining cellular homeostasis. BCL2 functions by binding to
pro­apoptotic proteins, such as BAX and BAK, and inhibiting their activity, thus preventing the
initiation of the apoptotic cascade. Although this anti­apoptotic role is essential in normal cells,
overexpression of BCL2 is frequently observed in various cancers, particularly hematologic ma­
lignancies such as chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL). This overexpression enables cancer cells

1.5.3
BCL2­Venetoclax interaction mechanism
Venetoclax works by disrupting the interaction between BCL2 and pro­apoptotic proteins such
as BAX and BAK. As mentioned above, BCL2 under normal circumstances inhibits these pro­
apoptotic proteins and prevents permeabilization of the mitochondrial outer membrane (MOMP),
thereby blocking apoptosis. In the presence of Venetoclax, competitive inhibition occurs, where
Venetoclax specifically binds to BCL2, displacing BAX and BAK. This displacement frees BAX
and BAK, allowing them to trigger MOMP permeabilization, which in turn facilitates the release
of cytochrome c from mitochondria. The release of cytochrome c initiates the apoptotic cascade,
which ultimately leads to tumor cell death [26].
The mechanism can be seen in Figure 1.6. Venetoclax indirectly promotes apoptosis by increasing
the cellular concentration of pro­apoptotic proteins. This mechanism is what makes Venetoclax a
powerful drug for BLC2­dependent cancers.
Understanding the molecular determinants of Venetoclax binding is essential for deciphering its
interaction with BCL2. Venetoclax binds to the BH3­binding groove of BCL2, a hydrophobic
pocket formed by residues Phe104, Val146, Phe194, Ala149, Leu96, and Leu135. These residues
create stabilizing hydrophobic interactions that anchor Venetoclax within the pocket. Additionally,
Asp103, Arg107, and Glu152 contribute through critical hydrogen bonds, with Asp103 playing a
central role in stabilizing the ligand.

1.6
Aims
The primary objective of this project is to develop a computational pipeline capable of predicting
the effects of mutations on drug resistance mechanisms in cancer proteins. By integrating tools
from structural bioinformatics and molecular dynamics simulations, the aim is to create a robust
framework that can assess mutation­induced changes in protein­drug interactions.
Building on existing computational approaches for modeling drug resistance, this project focuses
on both the design and application of a computational pipeline tailored for alchemical free energy
calculations. The pipeline is applied to the clinically relevant BCL2­Venetoclax system, integrat­
ing structural modeling and molecular dynamics to assess the impact of resistance mutations. In
addition, validation against experimental data is incorporated to ensure the accuracy and reliability
of the predictions. Beyond this specific case study, the project aims to develop a generalizable
framework for studying protein­drug interactions, enabling its adaptation to other therapeutic tar­
gets.
Therefore, the primary objectives of this thesis are:
• Design and implementation of a computational workflow that integrates data retrieval, struc­
tural modeling, molecular dynamics simulations, and free­energy calculations.
• Applying the pipeline to the BCL2­Venetoclax system to quantify the effects of specific
mutations on drug binding and resistance.
• Validating computational predictions with experimental data from databases such as MdrDB
and scientific literature [27] [28].
• Establish a generalizable computational approach to support future studies on drug resistance
mechanisms.

Protein structure prediction and modeling
For cases where the experimentally resolved structures were unavailable or incomplete, computa­

RosettaFold All­Atom (RFAA) is an advanced structure prediction tool that extends the capa­
bilities of RoseTTAFold2 by incorporating atomic­level modeling of complex biomolecular as­
semblies [36]. Unlike previous deep­learning­based structure prediction models, RFAA integrates

RFAA employs a multi­track neural architecture that processes molecular representations at three
levels: one dimensional (1D) sequence information and ligand chemical properties, two dimen­
sional (2D) pairwise interactions and connectivity constraints, and three dimensional (3D) spatial
coordinates with chirality. This multi­level approach captures molecular interactions with high
accuracy.
The model predicts structures with atomic detail, ensuring a precise placement of the ligand. It has
been trained on a diverse set of biomolecular complexes, including protein­ligand, protein­metal,
and covalently modified protein structures from the PDB, allowing it to generalize across different
molecular systems. Additionally, confidence estimation metrics, such as predicted aligned error
(PAE) and local distance difference test (plDDT) scores, are provided to assess the reliability of its
predictions.
RFAA enables structure generation in cases where experimental structures are unavailable, allow­
ing accurate modeling of protein­ligand interactions for molecular dynamics simulations.

thermodynamic integration (TI).

dynamics simulations to compute free energy differences. PMX is fully compatible with the GRO­
MACS simulation engine, making it an accessible and efficient tool for studying mutation­induced
changes in protein­ligand interactions.

2.4
Thermodynamic cycle for alchemical free energy calculations
With the 3D structure of the drug­protein complex, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations can be
used to study the effects of mutations on drug binding. As described in the Introduction, alchemi­
cal free energy calculations use the thermodynamic cycle framework to estimate the binding free
energy differences (∆∆G) between wild­type and mutant systems. This approach allows for the in­
direct calculation of binding affinities by simulating alchemical transformations between different
molecular states. This section outlines the workflow used to implement these calculations within
molecular dynamics simulations.
2.4.1
Simulation workflow for calculating ∆G
To compute the alchemical free energy changes (∆G), a combination of equilibrium molecular
dynamics (MD) simulations and non­equilibrium free energy calculations is employed. Equi­
librium simulations ensure that the system reaches a stable conformational ensemble before the
non­equilibrium transformations are conducted. These transformations utilize fast­switching per­
turbations between the wild­type and mutant states to estimate free energy differences, following
the principles of non­equilibrium statistical mechanics. Figure 1.3 illustrates the workflow for
calculating alchemical free energy differences using this approach.
Equilibrium MD simulations are first performed for both wild­type and mutant proteins in both
ligand­free and ligand­bound states. These simulations allow the system to explore its confor­
mational landscape and ensure that structural and energetic properties stabilize before alchemical
transformations are initiated. Equilibrium is assessed by monitoring key system properties, in­
cluding temperature, pressure, and potential energy profiles. Once these properties remain stable
over time, the system is considered equilibrated. In this study, a simulation length of 20 ns was
selected based on this assessment, ensuring that sufficient sampling of equilibrium conformations
is achieved.
Following equilibrium simulations, non­equilibrium MD simulations are performed to compute
the non­equilibrium work associated with alchemical transformations between the wild­type and
mutant states. These simulations capture the energetic cost of perturbing the system from one state
to another, which is then used to estimate the free energy difference. Both forward (wild­type to
mutant) and reverse (mutant to wild­type) transitions are conducted to improve the accuracy of
free energy estimates and reduce bias by ensuring proper sampling in both directions.
Snapshots from the equilibrium simulations are extracted every 100–200 ps to initiate non­equilibrium
transformations. To avoid artifacts from early fluctuations, the first 2–3 ns of the equilibrium sim­

duration of each non­equilibrium transition varies depending on the complexity of the mutation,
ranging from 80 to 200 ps. Mutations involving minimal structural changes require shorter transi­

2.5
Molecular dynamics simulations
Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations form the computational backbone of this study, enabling the
calculation of alchemical free energy differences within the thermodynamic cycle. The workflow
consists of key preparatory steps, including ligand parametrization, mutation modeling, and system
assembly, followed by equilibrium and non­equilibrium simulations.
As mentioned before, equilibrium MD simulations are conducted to ensure system stability and
serve as the starting point for subsequent non­equilibrium transitions. The non­equilibrium simu­
lations then allow for alchemical transformations between wild­type and mutant protein states,
enabling the calculation of binding free energy differences. These simulations follow a well­
established workflow, ensuring consistency and reproducibility across all studied mutations.
2.5.1

1. Ligand parametrization
For accurate MD simulations, small molecules such as ligands must be parametrized using a com­

The protein structure was prepared to ensure compatibility with the simulation environment. Ini­

physiological pH. The structure was then converted into a format suitable for molecular dynam­

representation of the protein.
To investigate mutation­induced effects on ligand binding, targeted amino acid substitutions were
introduced using the pmx toolkit. The mutation was applied while preserving the structural integrity
of the surrounding residues, ensuring that conformational changes were limited to the intended
site. Topology files were subsequently generated for both the wild­type and mutant protein states
to enable alchemical free energy calculations. Care was taken to maintain consistency between
structural and topological representations, preventing discrepancies that could impact simulation
stability.
Finally, the processed structures were converted into a format compatible with GROMACS, al­
lowing seamless integration with the ligand and solvent system for further molecular dynamics
simulations.

After preparing the protein and ligand components, the complete system was assembled for molec­

proper formatting and atom count consistency in the coordinate files. The topology file was up­

2.5.2
Equilibrium simulations
Equilibrium molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were conducted to stabilize the prepared sys­
tems prior to non­equilibrium simulations. Each system was simulated in two distinct states: wild­
type (State A) and mutant (State B), both in ligand­free and ligand­bound conditions. These sim­
ulations ensure the system reaches a thermodynamically stable state before further analysis.
1. Energy minimization
Before running equilibrium MD, energy minimization was performed to eliminate steric clashes
and stabilize the system prior to equilibration. This step involved iteratively adjusting atomic
positions using a steepest descent algorithm while maintaining structural integrity. The potential
energy of the system was monitored to ensure convergence to a physically meaningful level.
For minimization, a stochastic dynamics (SD) integrator was used with a 2 fs time step, ensur­
ing energy dissipation without affecting system stability. A Verlet cutoff scheme was applied to
efficiently handle non­bonded interactions. The van der Waals interactions were treated with a
force­switch modifier to smoothly transition forces near the cutoff distance, minimizing artifacts.
Separate minimization configurations were applied for the wild­type and mutant systems, ensuring
consistency in force field parameters and initial energy landscapes before proceeding with equili­
bration.
2. Equilibration under physiological conditions
Following energy minimization, the system was equilibrated under an NPT ensemble to maintain
constant temperature and pressure. The equilibration process was conducted in two phases, first
focusing on temperature stabilization, followed by pressure equilibration. To regulate the system
temperature, a velocity­rescaling thermostat was employed, gradually adjusting the system to a
physiological temperature of 298 K. In ligand­bound systems, an additional coupling group was
introduced to ensure proper temperature regulation between the protein and ligand.
Once thermal equilibration was achieved, the system was further stabilized using an isotropic
Parrinello­Rahman barostat, maintaining a pressure of 1 bar. This allowed for the correct density
and volume adjustments required for a physically meaningful simulation environment. During the
initial equilibration steps, harmonic position restraints were applied to the protein backbone and
ligand, when applicable, preventing significant conformational changes before the system reached
equilibrium. These restraints were gradually released to allow natural fluctuations while preserving
overall system stability.

For ligand­bound systems, additional modifications were necessary to define the ligand as a sepa­
rate coupling group. Index files were generated to correctly assign molecular groups for temper­
ature coupling, and position restraints with force constants of 1000 kJ/mol/nm were introduced to
stabilize the ligand in all three spatial directions. The topology was updated accordingly to ensure

3. Production equilibration
After reaching thermal and pressure equilibrium, an unrestrained production equilibration phase
was performed to ensure full system relaxation. The system was allowed to fluctuate naturally,
and key properties such as temperature, pressure, and potential energy were monitored to confirm
equilibrium.
During this phase, the thermostat and barostat coupling constants were optimized to ensure minimal
fluctuations, maintaining a stable ensemble suitable for free energy calculations. The system was
simulated for a sufficiently long duration to achieve convergence, as indicated by stable potential
energy and RMSD values. For the ligand­bound simulations, the temperature coupling groups
were further refined to account for interactions between the ligand and surrounding solvent.
4. Equilibration assessment
To confirm that equilibrium was reached, temperature, pressure, and potential energy profiles were
analyzed over time. Convergence was determined based on the stability of these parameters, en­
suring that fluctuations remained within acceptable limits.

Non­equilibrium molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were performed to compute the work as­
sociated with the alchemical transformations between wild­type and mutant systems, as described
in the thermodynamic cycle (Section 1.4.1). These simulations correspond to the forward (wild­
type to mutant) and reverse (mutant to wild­type) transformations, allowing free­energy differences
to be estimated using the Jarzynski equality and Crooks fluctuation theorem.
To ensure adequate sampling of the non­equilibrium transitions while maintaining computational
efficiency, snapshots (frames) were extracted from equilibrium simulations at regular intervals.
Each extracted frame served as the starting point for a short non­equilibrium simulation, where the
mutation was gradually introduced or reverted.
1. Frame selection and system preparation
For each system, wild­type (state A) and mutant (state B), both with and without the ligand, equi­
librium trajectories were sampled at fixed intervals, skipping an initial relaxation period to ensure
that only well­equilibrated frames were selected. Frames were extracted every 100–200 ps from
the production trajectory, starting after 2.5 ns, to prevent biases from early non­equilibrated con­
formations. Each extracted frame was stored in a separate directory and served as an independent
starting structure for subsequent non­equilibrium simulations.
For ligand­bound systems, index files were generated from the first frame to correctly define tem­
perature coupling groups and maintain consistent thermostat behavior across all frames. These
index files were reused throughout the workflow to ensure consistency between ligand and protein
coupling groups.

2. Non­Equilibrium transitions
Each extracted frame underwent an independent non­equilibrium transition, following a protocol
designed to introduce or revert the mutation while maintaining system stability. The alchemical
transformation was performed over short timescales (80–200 ps), with the exact duration depend­
ing on the mutation and system complexity.
Separate simulations were conducted for:
• Wild­type to mutant (forward transition).
• Mutant to wild­type (reverse transition).
Work values were recorded for each direction to construct work distributions, improving the accu­
racy of free­energy estimates.
The non­equilibrium simulations used a stochastic dynamics (SD) integrator to enhance numerical
stability. A lambda schedule was applied to smoothly interpolate between the wild­type and mu­
tant states, ensuring that perturbations were introduced gradually. The Verlet cutoff scheme was
employed for non­bonded interactions, with a van der Waals force­switch modifier to minimize ar­
tifacts near the interaction cutoff distance. Electrostatics were treated using Particle Mesh Ewald
(PME) to account for long­range Coulombic interactions.
3. Free energy calculation and analysis
The free energy differences (∆G) for the alchemical transformations were computed using the pmx
toolkit, which processes the work values obtained from non­equilibrium simulations. The Bennett
Acceptance Ratio (BAR) method was applied to the collected work distributions to improve sta­
tistical convergence and ensure accurate estimation of free energy differences [22].
The free energy change (∆G) was calculated by applying the BAR method at a temperature of
298.15K. The resulting free energy profiles are visualized in Figures 2.2a and 2.2b, where for­
ward (0 →1) and reverse (1 →0) work distributions are shown. These plots represent the work
distributions from the non­equilibrium simulations. The ∆G value is determined at the intersec­
tion of the forward (green) and backward (blue) distributions, as indicated by the dashed lines. The
y­axis represents the work (W) in kJ/mol, while the x­axis corresponds to the snapshot number
from the non­equilibrium simulations.
This is how by calculating ∆G for both ligand­free and ligand­bound systems and substituting
these values into the thermodynamic cycle equations, the binding free energy difference (∆∆G)
was determined. This value quantifies the effect of the mutation on the binding affinity of the
ligand, completing the thermodynamic cycle described in Section 1.4.1.

3.1
BCL2­Venetoclax case study
BCL2 was selected as the target protein for this study due to its critical role in regulating apoptosis
and its well­documented overexpression in various cancers, particularly hematologic malignancies.
By preventing programmed cell death, overexpressed BCL2 contributes to tumor survival and
progression, making it a prominent therapeutic target in cancer treatment. Moreover, the majority
of approved drugs targeting BCL2 are designed to inhibit its wild­type form, as the therapeutic goal
is to suppress its anti­apoptotic function. This simplifies the demonstration of the pipeline because
the wild­type protein can be used as state A, eliminating the need to use an already mutated target
for initial simulations. With regard to the drug, the next section shows how it was selected.
3.2
Target identification and structure retrieval
To identify approved drugs targeting BCL2, a systematic database search was performed using
Pharos and RCSB PDB databases. This process was automated using a custom script, as described
in Section 2.2.1 of the Methods. The script facilitated the retrieval of approved drugs, their corre­
sponding ligands, and any available 3D structural data.
The search identified three approved drugs that interact with BCL2: Gossypol, Ixabepilone, and
Venetoclax. For each drug, both the SMILES representation and the structured data file (SDF)
were obtained, ensuring compatibility with the RFAA modeling tool. Furthermore, for Venetoclax,
four experimentally determined protein­ligand complexes were found in the RCSB PDB database:
6O0K, 6O0L, 6O0M and 6O0P. These structures provide valuable reference models for assessing
binding interactions and validating computational predictions.
The availability of these experimental 3D structures made Venetoclax the ideal choice for this
study. With the selection of Venetoclax, the pipeline structure generation step could be validated
by comparing computationally generated structures with experimentally determined ones. This
selection ensured a robust framework for testing and validation, laying the foundation for the next
stages of the workflow.

3.3
3D structure generation
Despite having valid experimental structures available for use in the subsequent steps of the pipeline,
this section demonstrates the capability of the workflow to predict a 3D structure computationally.
BCL2 presents challenges for structural modeling due to its flexible regions, particularly a large
disordered loop spanning residues 31–92 (as highlighted in Figure 3.1). This loop exhibited low­
confidence predictions in the AlphaFold structure, indicated by low pLDDT scores, suggesting
an intrinsically disordered region. Such flexible regions can lead to excessive fluctuations during
molecular dynamics simulations, which affect convergence and stability. To prevent this, the loop
was replaced with a shorter linker sequence, informed by previously resolved X­ray and NMR
structures of BCL2. Specifically, the NMR structure of BCL2 (PDB: 2O2F) was used as a ref­
erence, as its shorter linker has been experimentally validated to stabilize the protein conforma­
tion [43]. Furthermore, BCL2 contains a transmembrane α­helix, which serves as a membrane­
anchoring domain (see Section 1.5.1). When BCL2 is not membrane­bound, this domain has been
reported to compete with ligands for binding, potentially altering the interaction with Venetoclax.
To avoid interference in ligand binding, this helix was removed. These modifications were incor­
porated into the input FASTA sequence for subsequent structure generation.
To validate structural modifications, a new BCL2 structure was generated using AlphaFold3
(AF3) and an updated sequence (FASTA) as input. AF3 is capable of generating a predicted pro­
tein structure using its FASTA [37]. This approach allowed to assess the quality of the redesigned
linker and its ability to maintain the overall structural integrity of the protein. Figure 3.1 compares

4
Discussion
In this chapter, the effectiveness and validity of the designed pipeline for assessing drug resistance
mutations will be evaluated, with a particular focus on the BCL2­Venetoclax case study.
The discussion is organized into distinct subsections. First, the pipeline’s validation is examined
by comparing computational outputs with experimental data, highlighting strengths and areas for
improvement. Next, an evaluation of the pipeline’s adaptability and potential application of the
pipeline to other protein­ligand systems. Finally, the study’s limitations are addressed, and future
directions are proposed to improve the pipeline and expand its applicability.
Through these analyses, this chapter aims to contextualize the findings within the broader field
of drug resistance research, focusing on the utility and relevance of the developed computational
framework.
4.1
Validation of the pipeline
The pipeline developed for this study offers a systematic approach to identifying, simulating and
evaluating the effects of mutations on drug resistance. Using the BCL2­Venetoclax system as a
case study, the pipeline was validated throughout its key stages, from initial data processing to final
binding free energy calculations.
4.1.1
Data collection and experimental validation
The data collection and processing steps effectively used MdrDB and other approved drug databases
to identify relevant drug­target pairs and mutations. Using utility scripts, the pipeline efficiently
filtered for single substitutions and human proteins, while also integrating experimental data vali­
dation through manual literature searches. This rigorous filtering process ensured that only high­
quality data was used as input for subsequent steps. Due to the fact that for BCL2­Venetoclax
all MdrDB mutations provided were not directly from experimental sources, additional mutations
were obtained to ensure reliability. These mutations, extracted from the literature, were experi­
mentally validated using surface plasmon resonance (SPR). SPR is a highly sensitive technique
for quantifying molecular interactions, such as protein­ligand binding, in real time and without the
need for labels. This makes it a gold standard for experimentally determining binding affinities,
adding a robust layer of validation to selected mutations [45].
4.1.2
Structure generation and model accuracy
Structure generation was a critical stage, particularly for BCL2, which posed challenges due to its
large disordered loop and transmembrane helix. The RoseTTAFold All­Atom (RFAA) model was
used to generate the protein­ligand complex, ensuring accurate structural predictions of the docked
drug­target system. When using the SMILES representation of Venetoclax as ligand input, RFAA
produced a PAEinter score of 9.573. This score was evaluated against recommended cutoffs from
42
A framework for drug resistance prediction using structural bioinformatics and molecular dynamics
==PAGE:51==
the RFAA literature, where a PAEinter below 10 is considered indicative of high­quality protein­
ligand binding predictions [36].
Since BCL2 contains a large disordered loop and a transmembrane helix, additional modifications
were necessary to improve the quality of the input structure. AlphaFold3 was employed specifi­
cally to model the missing linker region, ensuring that these flexible regions did not interfere with
the simulations. The final protein­ligand structure was aligned with experimental PDB entries,
yielding a root­mean­square deviation (RMSD) of 0.514 Å. Since an RMSD below 2 Å is gen­
erally considered indicative of a close match between protein structures, this result confirms the
accuracy of the model [46].
These results validate the structural modifications and confirm the pipeline’s ability to generate
reliable input structures using RFAA for protein­ligand complex generation and AlphaFold3 for
linker modeling.
4.1.3
Equilibrium MD simulations
Equilibrium simulations provided further validation by demonstrating that with 20 ns, consistent
stability in temperature, pressure, and potential energy profiles could be reached in all systems
(Figure 3.4). It can be seen that is stable form the beginning because previously NPT equilibration
was performed. These metrics are critical indicators of system stability and equilibration, as fluctu­
ations in temperature and pressure can disrupt simulation accuracy, while potential energy stability
confirms the system’s energetic equilibrium [47]. Despite observing stable profiles from the begin­
ning of the simulations, the first 2.5 ns were excluded from frame extraction for non­equilibrium
simulations to ensure that snapshots were derived from fully equilibrated systems. This conser­
vative approach minimizes the risk of including transient behaviors that may not represent true
equilibrium. A simulation length of 20 ns was selected as a balance between computational cost
and accuracy; however, future studies could explore varying lengths to assess their impact on the
results.
4.1.4
Non­equilibrium MD simulations
Non­equilibrium simulations generated the work profiles necessary for calculating alchemical free
energy differences. For each mutation, ligand­free and ligand­bound systems were simulated un­
der standardized conditions. To evaluate the impact of parameter variations, iterative adjustments
such as increasing the number of frames or extending the simulation duration were explored, us­
ing F104C as a primary test case. The results, detailed in the results section, demonstrated that
variations in parameters, such as extracting snapshots every 100 ps versus 200 ps or extending the
non­equilibrium simulation duration from 80 to 200 ps, produced consistent ∆∆G values within
the margin of error. This consistency underscores the robustness of the pipeline and suggests that
parameter variations, while useful for testing, do not significantly alter the calculated binding free
energy differences.
4.1.5
Accuracy of free energy calculations
The Bennett Acceptance Ratio (BAR) method, used to analyze the work profiles, proved highly
effective for this study. BAR is a statistically rigorous approach for estimating free energy dif­
A framework for drug resistance prediction using structural bioinformatics and molecular dynamics
43
==PAGE:52==
ferences between two states by using the overlap in work distributions from forward and reverse
transformations [48]. Morevoer, BAR maximizes the accuracy of the calculated ∆G through min­
imizing the variance in free energy estimates. This is particularly advantageous in the context of
alchemical transformations, where forward and reverse work profiles are inherently noisy due to
the non­equilibrium nature of the simulations.
One potential issue that could arise is the absence of an intersection between the forward and re­
verse work distributions, which would make the estimation of ∆G impossible. To address this,
increasing the number of frames extracted from equilibrium simulations or extending the duration
of non­equilibrium simulations can enhance the overlap in work distributions, improving the relia­
bility of the results. Moreover, a transition that is too fast could dissipate excess work, potentially
leading to inaccurate ∆∆G values. However, as shown in the F104C tests, consistent results were
obtained across a range of tested parameters, further validating the reliability of the pipeline. For
practical purposes, using 100 ps between frames and extracting 88 frames was determined to be
an optimal balance between computational cost and accuracy for future applications.
The binding free energy calculations integrated the results of the ligand­free and ligand­bound sys­
tems, which allowed the computation of ∆∆G values. To compare the computational results with
the experimental data, binding free energy differences (∆∆G) were calculated using the experi­
mental values Ki provided in the literature. The relationship between the binding free energy and
Ki is given by:
∆G = RTln(Ki)
where R is the gas constant (1.987 × 10−3 kcal/(molK)) and T is the temperature in Kelvin
(298.15K). Using this equation, the experimental binding free energy (∆G) was calculated for each
system. The binding free energy difference (∆∆G) values were then determined by subtracting the
binding free energy of wild­type (∆GWT) from the binding free energy of each mutant (∆Gmutant):
∆∆G = ∆Gmutant −∆GWT
The experimental Ki and the corresponding ∆G values for the BCL2­Venetoclax interactions were

4.2
Insights on drug resistance mechanisms
The binding free energy calculations and structural analysis reveal distinct molecular mechanisms
underlying drug resistance in BCL2­Venetoclax interactions. This study evaluates the effects of
specific mutations on Venetoclax’s binding affinity, identifying key structural and energetic factors
that influence drug efficacy.

Mutations such as G101V and F104C showed significant reductions in binding affinity, consistent
with their classification as drug­resistant variants. The computational results aligned closely with
experimental data for these mutations, supporting their impact on the molecular interaction network
at the binding site. For G101V, the substitution of glycine for valine introduces steric barriers and
reduces flexibility in the binding pocket, disrupting key drug­protein interactions. Similarly, the
F104C mutation, involving the replacement of phenylalanine with cysteine, diminishes hydropho­

In contrast, mutations such as F104L and E200D demonstrated minimal or negligible effects on
binding affinity. The F104L mutation, which involves a substitution within the same hydrophobic
class of residues, maintained most of the structural integrity of the binding site, resulting in only
a modest reduction in binding affinity. E200D, a control mutation located at the protein’s tail,

value of no significant change in binding affinity [28].
The outlier, E152A, highlights the challenges of charged mutations in free­energy calculations.
The loss of charge at this position disrupts the electrostatic interactions critical to the binding in­
terface, potentially skewing the results. This aligns with observations in the literature: hydrogen
bonds typically contribute 1 to 2 kcal/mol to protein stability [49]. The calculated ∆∆G of −2.6
± 0.62 kcal/mol falls within this expected range, suggesting that the discrepancy arises from dis­
rupted hydrogen bonding networks. Addressing such effects will require specialized corrections
or alternative methods to improve the accuracy of charged residues. Moreover, a script from the
pmx toolkit is designed for these cases which will be tested in the future [41].
In general, these findings underscore the importance of understanding the structural and energetic
consequences of specific mutations to develop effective strategies for overcoming drug resistance.
They also validate the utility of the computational pipeline in exploring the effects of point muta­
tions on drug binding, offering a powerful tool to guide drug design and optimization efforts.
4.3
Pipeline adaptability
One of the primary strengths of the developed pipeline is its adaptability to various research con­
texts and experimental needs. This is because it is designed to analyze mutations in drug­protein
complexes and the workflow is not restricted to BCL2­Venetoclax, it can be easily applied to other
protein­ligand systems. The modular structure of the pipeline allows for easy customization at
each stage, from data collection and structural preparation to molecular dynamics simulations and
binding free energy calculations.
The pipeline supports mutations with various biophysical effects, such as steric hindrance, hy­
drophobic alterations, and electrostatic changes. For example, the successful evaluation of muta­
tions like G101V and F104C, which disrupt binding through steric and hydrophobic mechanisms,
demonstrates its applicability to a wide range of resistance mutations. Although the E152A mu­
tation presented challenges due to its charged nature, future adjustments, such as incorporating
advanced techniques for handling electrostatic changes, will enhance its robustness for such cases.

4.4
Limitations and future directions
Although the pipeline developed in this study shows significant utility and adaptability, several
limitations were identified, providing opportunities for future improvements. Addressing these
limitations will enhance the robustness and versatility of the workflow, expanding its applicability
in diverse research contexts.
One notable limitation is the handling of charged mutations, as demonstrated by the E152A mu­
tation. The loss of a charged side chain introduces artifacts in free energy calculations, resulting
in discrepancies between the computational and experimental binding free energies. To address
this issue, the pmx-toolkit provides a script specifically designed to fix charged mutations by
adjusting electrostatics during alchemical transformations. Incorporating this correction step into
the workflow will be essential for improving accuracy when studying charged residues and elec­
trostatically driven interactions.
Another limitation involves the computational requirements of the pipeline. High­performance
computing (HPC) resources are necessary to handle the simulations efficiently, particularly for
larger systems or high­throughput mutation analyses. Although the current workflow is resource
intensive, implementing optimizations, such as distributed computing, could reduce runtime. The

expansion of automation is another critical area for improvement. Currently, several commands
must be run manually to complete the pipeline. Future developments should focus on minimizing
user intervention by consolidating steps into a single, streamlined workflow.
Parameter selection, such as frame extraction intervals and non­equilibrium simulation lengths,
was optimized for the current case study, but may require refinement for other systems. Although
the results for F104C showed that different parameter sets yielded consistent ∆∆G values within
acceptable error ranges, more studies are needed to establish general guidelines. These guidelines
would ensure reproducibility and reliability across diverse protein­ligand systems and mutation
types.
In terms of its scope, the pipeline currently focuses on single mutations but could be extended to
analyze systems with multiple simultaneous mutations. This would enable the study of complex
mutation combinations and their collective impact on protein­ligand interactions. Furthermore,
integrating the pipeline with machine learning models could open up new possibilities for de novo

Expanding the pipeline to include more complex systems, such as multi­protein assemblies, mem­

Conclusion
This thesis presented the development, application, and validation of a computational pipeline
designed to predict the impact of mutations on drug resistance, using the BCL2­Venetoclax case
study as a model system. The pipeline successfully integrated structural modeling, molecular dy­
namics simulations, and alchemical free energy calculations, providing detailed insights into how
mutations influence protein­ligand interactions and binding affinities.
The main findings during this project are:
• The pipeline successfully quantified the effects of specific mutations on drug binding, with
computational ∆∆G values closely aligning with experimental data for most mutations.
• The validation process confirmed the reliability of the computational predictions, demon­
strating strong agreement with experimental results from the scientific literature.
• The workflow’s structured approach enables reproducible and generalizable analyses, mak­
ing it applicable not only to BCL2­Venetoclax but also to other protein­ligand systems.
• The adaptable design of the pipeline allows for seamless integration with emerging compu­
tational methods, positioning it as a versatile tool for studying drug resistance mechanisms
in various therapeutic areas.
The work also underscored the potential of computational pipelines for applications beyond can­
cer drug resistance, including enzyme­small molecule interactions and de novo protein design.
Despite its success, limitations such as challenges with charged mutations and the need for high
computational resources were identified, providing opportunities for future improvements.
In conclusion, the pipeline represents a valuable tool for structural bioinformatics, offering a ver­
satile and reliable framework for studying protein­ligand interactions and guiding experimental
efforts. Its adaptability, accuracy, and potential for further automation pave the way for advances
in computational biology and its applications in diverse fields.
