Abstract
With the rapid development of autonomous driving and advanced driver assistance
systems, efforts to enhance driving safety, especially in detecting traﬀic accidents
when natural driving states are unclear, have increased significantly. Generally, traf-
fic anomalies are defined as events that deviate from common occurrences and ex-
pected behaviors, such as vehicles and pedestrians disregarding traﬀic signals to cross
arbitrarily, or vehicles abruptly stopping ahead.
In real driving scenarios, timely detection of potential traﬀic accidents by au-
tonomous vehicles is crucial for prompt response and accident prevention. This pa-
per addresses the challenges in traﬀic accident detection by proposing a model for
predicting future vehicle positions. The model integrates an enhanced GRU module
and compares RGB pixel-level features of real frames with the actual positions of
objects to detect traﬀic accidents. To validate the model’s effectiveness, experiments
were conducted using the A3D dataset, demonstrating significant improvements in
detection performance.
Due to the dynamic camera motion and complexity of scenes during driving, de-
tecting traﬀic accidents in driving environments requires consideration of both global
and local features to measure the scope of predicting visual relational structures
among dynamic road participants (pedestrians, vehicles, non-motorized vehicles, etc.).
This paper proposes an adversarial learning-based accident detection model with an
attention mechanism.
The network comprises an attentional convolutional LSTM
encoder-decoder module, predicting future positions and frames of driving partici-
pants by inputting target boxes from video sequences and consecutive frames.
Keywords: traﬀic accident detection; video frame prediction; future bounding
box position prediction; adversarial learning

1.1
Motivation
In Denmark, a country renowned for its harmonious blend of green urban environ-
ments and a vibrant cycling culture, there is a profound commitment to environmental
stewardship deeply ingrained in societal values. Despite the widespread acclaim of
cycling as a preferred mode of transport, Denmark exhibits a surprisingly high rate
of automobile ownership, with 540 vehicles per 1,000 inhabitants, as shown in Figure
1.1. This dichotomy highlights the critical role of automobiles in daily life and un-
derscores the intricate challenges associated with mobility, economic eﬀiciency, and,
most importantly, road safety. By focusing on the development of innovative solu-
tions for traﬀic accident detection and prevention, this research aims to contribute
to a safer transportation environment, thereby enhancing the quality of life and up-
holding Denmark’s commitment to sustainability and safety.
According to data from the Danish Road Safety Agency, we witnessed a significant
reduction in traﬀic accidents, decreased from 7,126 to 2,563 incidents during the
period from 2002 to 2022 [22a], as illustrated in Figure 1.2. This reduction attests to
the effectiveness of Denmark’s road safety policies and public awareness efforts.[MH96]
However, a detailed analysis of the past decade reveals a stabilization in the number of
traﬀic incidents, indicating a persistent challenge in achieving further reductions. This
study draws inspiration from international research to explore the persistent causes of
traﬀic accidents in Denmark and to identify innovative solutions. Contributing factors
may include excessive speeding, driving under the influence, driver fatigue, the need
for ongoing improvements in traﬀic infrastructure, and adverse weather conditions
[22b].
Given Denmark’s dedication to safety and technological advancement, particu-
larly in transportation, this research aims to explore how neural networks and deep
learning algorithms can enhance road safety. The global advancement of science and
information technology has enabled machines to simulate human behavior and per-
form complex tasks, leading to significant strides in fields such as computer vision
and machine learning. These technologies have shown remarkable success in image
recognition and object detection, often surpassing human performance.
Many vision perception systems in autonomous vehicles use deep learning tech-
niques to detect and recognize the appearance and behavior characteristics of partic-
ipants in driving scenarios. However, several issues persist in the research process.

With further research on neural networks, artificial intelligence technology has made
significant progress.
An increasing number of researchers worldwide, including in
China and Denmark, are engaging in the field of intelligent driving. Intelligent driving
systems can gradually achieve object detection, tracking, and recognition tasks with
real-time testing and minimal human intervention. Additionally, they can perform
further analysis based on detected traﬀic characteristics and various types of traﬀic
accidents. Significant effects have been achieved in traﬀic incident detection research

divided into data pre-processing, behavior modeling, and incident detection mod-

Monitoring under the perspective of traﬀic incident detection is usually defined

classification models to evaluate the rarity of movement or path, sparsity, and irreg-

With the development of large-scale video anomaly detection work, another ap-
proach is to use an unsupervised learning method to reconstruct the normal train-
ing data, minimizing the reconstruction of input samples and the prediction error.
Samples with large errors are identified as abnormal.

2.1
Convolutional Neural Networks
In 1998, LeCun proposed the convolutional neural network (CNN) in his pioneering
work, LeNet-5 [Lec+98], applying it to handwritten digit recognition tasks and achiev-
ing high recognition accuracy. In 2012, Alex and colleagues utilized CNNs to build
AlexNet [KSH12], which defeated all other algorithms in the ImageNet recognition
competition, winning the championship and sparking the true explosion of deep learn-
ing. Inspired by the biological visual mechanism, CNNs emulate the receptive fields
of cells in the visual cortex through convolutional operations, extracting feature in-
formation by sliding convolutional kernels over input features. As the network depth
increases, the receptive field expands, enabling the association of more information.
CNNs are essentially a variant of multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) that significantly
reduce model parameters by weight sharing, making the model easier to optimize and
effectively preventing overfitting. A typical CNN consists of multiple layers, typically
including input layers, convolutional layers, pooling layers, activation function layers,
and normalization layers. Next, this section will introduce two-dimensional convo-
lutional neural networks and three-dimensional convolutional neural networks from
these structural aspects.
2.1.1
Convolutional Layer
The convolution process is illustrated in Figure 2.1, where a convolutional kernel slides
over the input feature map with a certain stride. At each position, the corresponding
elements are multiplied and summed, generating a new feature map. Convolutional
operations exhibit characteristics of parameter sharing and sparse interaction. Param-
eter sharing allows the same feature detector to extract features at different positions,
reducing the number of parameters and computational complexity, thereby enhanc-
ing model generalization. Sparse interaction enables convolutional layers to effectively
learn and extract local features from input data, reducing redundant interactions and
improving computational eﬀiciency.

2.1.2
Activation Layers
Activation functions introduce non-linear characteristics, enabling neural networks to
learn complex data patterns. Without activation functions, a neural network would
remain linear, rendering additional layers ineffective. Commonly used activation func-
tions include the Sigmoid activation function, the Tanh activation function, and the
ReLU activation function.
The sigmoid function maps any value to a range between 0 and 1, providing a
degree of stability for network training. It constrains the output of the output layer,
preventing excessively large values during optimization. However, when the input to
the sigmoid function saturates, its derivative approaches zero, causing gradients to
diminish rapidly during backpropagation and potentially hindering effective weight
updates. The mathematical expression of the sigmoid function is shown in Equation
(2.1):

layers during backpropagation. The mathematical expression of the tanh function is

2.1.3
Pooling Layers
The pooling layer is an essential component of deep convolutional neural networks
(DCNNs), used to reduce the spatial dimensions of feature maps, thereby decreasing
network parameters and computational complexity while enhancing feature robust-
ness. Typically following convolutional layers, the pooling layer performs downsam-
pling on the output of the convolutional layer. Common pooling operations currently
used include max pooling and average pooling, as illustrated in Figure 2.2. In max
pooling, the size of the feature map is reduced by selecting the maximum value within
each pooling window. Specifically, max pooling divides the input feature map into
non-overlapping rectangular regions, selecting the maximum value within each region
as the output value. This process retains the strongest features while reducing the
size of the feature map. The main advantage of max pooling is its ability to extract
local invariance features, making the network more robust to changes in position and
scale.
In contrast to max pooling, average pooling calculates the average value of all
pixels within the pooling window as the output value. Average pooling reduces the
size of the feature map while preserving more feature information. Due to its use of
average values, average pooling is more robust to noise and helps reduce the risk of
overfitting. Both max pooling and average pooling achieve spatial downsampling by
performing regional statistics on the feature map. This downsampling improves net-
work computational eﬀiciency and generalization while reducing the risk of overfitting.
Therefore, pooling layers are widely applied in deep convolutional neural networks
and have been successful in tasks such as image recognition, object detection, and
semantic segmentation.
2.1.4
Fully Connected Layers
The fully connected layer is used to aggregate and classify features extracted by
convolutional and pooling layers, enabling advanced reasoning or decision-making.
Leveraging information from all preceding layers, the fully connected layer integrates
these features, adjusts weights, and maps them to final class labels or regression
values.

a to point b, while the motion field represents how objects move from point a to point
b in the actual image. Optical flow generation requires three conditions: (1) motion,
(2) regions with optical features, and (3) imaging projection.

Bounding Box Encoding Branch
The bounding box encoding branch is shown in Figure 4.2. First, the optical flow map
is processed through the ROIPooling module. Then, the target detection image with
bounding boxes and the output of the ROIPooling module are fed into the F-GRU
encode module. This approach aligns the features in the optical flow map with those
in the target detection image through the ROIPooling module. The optical flow map
captures the motion information of pixels in the image, while the target detection
image provides the precise location of objects. By aligning these two types of features
on the same spatial scale, subsequent feature fusion and processing become more
convenient and effective. The purpose of feature alignment is to ensure that features
from different sources can be processed on a unified spatial scale.
The optical flow map provides temporal dynamic information about the motion
of objects, while the target detection image provides spatial location and appearance
information of objects. By combining these two features, the model can more compre-
hensively capture the motion state and spatial position of objects. The optical flow
map captures the direction and speed of objects’ movement, while the target detec-
tion image provides the precise location and bounding boxes of objects. Combining
this information enables the model to understand the motion trajectory of objects
more comprehensively.
After feature alignment and fusion, the F-GRU encode module further processes
these fused features. F-GRU effectively retains and updates important information
in the time series. Using the previously fused features, the F-GRU encode module
performs more refined feature extraction and information enhancement based on the
time series. This approach helps improve the accuracy of predicting the positions of
objects in future frames, as it captures hidden motion patterns and trends in the time
series.
The optical flow map provides motion information within the time series, which,
when processed by the F-GRU encode module, allows for better capturing of the
vehicle’s motion trajectory. The F-GRU module utilizes the motion information pro-
vided by the optical flow map, combined with the spatial information in the target

detection image, to perform long-term dependency modeling and prediction. In this
way, the model can better reflect the motion patterns and trends of objects in the

4.3.4
Predicting Future Vehicle Bounding Box Positions
By summing the optical flow encoded features and the vehicle position encoded fea-
tures, the model can simultaneously utilize motion information and spatial position
information. This fusion method enhances the richness of feature representation, en-
abling the model to comprehensively understand the vehicle’s motion state and spatial
distribution. The fused features are then fed into the convolutional decoder. The role
of the convolutional decoder is to decode the input high-dimensional features layer by
layer, progressively reconstructing the vehicle’s position in future frames. Through
deconvolution operations, the decoder gradually increases the spatial resolution of
the feature maps and generates the predicted vehicle bounding boxes.

5.3
Accident Generative Adversarial Network
AcciGAN (Accident Generative Adversarial Network), as illustrated in Figure 5.2,
consists of two prediction pathways. The upper pathway begins with optical flow
maps processed by a convolutional encoder to generate flow feature maps, which are
sequentially fed into an attention convolutional LSTM network. The lower pathway
involves vehicle bounding boxes processed through convolutional encoding to extract
texture and spatial feature information from the images. These inputs are then input
into an attention convolutional LSTM network to establish long-term dependency
relationships, recursively generating new hidden features. Finally, outputs from both
attention convolutional LSTM networks are concatenated and fed into a convolutional
decoder to predict the positions of vehicles in future frames. The predicted future
frames and corresponding real frames are jointly input into a Patch GAN (Markovian
discriminator) for real versus fake classification.

5.3.1
Optical Flow Prediction Pathway
The optical flow maps are computed from adjacent frames of the original RGB im-
ages, and these mappings are individually processed by the optical flow convolutional
encoder to generate their respective feature maps. Therefore, only the feature maps
generated by the convolutional encoder on the optical flow pathway are sequentially
input into the attention convolutional LSTM network based on their temporal order.
Since there is no convolutional decoder generator in the model to generate optical flow
maps based on the output of the attention convolutional LSTM, the iterative genera-
tion occurs at the level of optical flow feature maps. Here’s the process: The attention
convolutional LSTM network outputs the optical flow feature map at the final time
step. This map is concatenated with subsequent optical flow feature maps, excluding
the first one, and then both are input into the attention convolutional LSTM network.
This iterative process iteratively generates multiple optical flow feature maps, which
combine semantic feature representations fused from RGB. These fused dynamic and
static feature representations are then input into the convolutional decoder generator
to generate multiple future video frames.
5.3.2
Vehicle bounding boxes pathway
The vehicle bounding boxes, as shown in the lower pathway of Figure 5.2, are pro-
cessed by inputting sequences of frames containing detection boxes into a convolu-
tional encoder.
The convolutional encoder transforms the information from each
frame into semantic and spatially structured feature maps, preserving critical infor-
mation about vehicle positions and movements. These extracted feature maps are
then sequentially fed into an Attention Convolutional LSTM (ConvLSTM) network.
ConvLSTM combines attention convolution and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
capabilities, effectively handling image sequences while maintaining temporal depen-
dencies. Within this network, the feature maps are gradually updated and propagated
over time steps, enabling the model to understand and learn the temporal dynamics
of video sequences.
Simultaneously, features from optical flow maps are extracted and processed in
another pathway. Optical flow maps, generated by analyzing pixel changes between
adjacent frames, provide directional and speed information about object movements
within the images. These optical flow features, along with the feature maps from
the ConvLSTM network, are fused during the feature integration stage. This fusion
process effectively integrates spatial and temporal information, enhancing the model’s
understanding of vehicle movements in complex traﬀic scenes.
Finally, the fused features are input into a convolutional decoder network for
decoding.
The convolutional decoder network reconstructs the feature maps back
into image space, generating predicted bounding boxes for future frames of vehicles.
This integrated approach not only combines image feature extraction, temporal de-
pendency modeling, and motion analysis effectively but also demonstrates significant
potential and practical value in traﬀic accident detection and prediction.

5.5
Conclusion of this Chapter
This chapter proposes a traﬀic accident detection method based on the GAN frame-
work, integrating Convolutional LSTM and attention mechanisms to achieve multi-
frame prediction tasks. We provide a detailed description of the process for multi-
frame prediction: gradually incorporating newly generated video frames into the input
sequence, removing the first frame from the sequence, and then inputting the updated
sequence into the network for prediction. This process is implemented in two predic-
tion pathways. This method combines various advantages of deep learning, such as
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), Convolutional Long Short-Term Memory
networks (Convolutional LSTM), and attention mechanisms, enabling more precise
detection of accidents in complex traﬀic scenarios.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion and Future
Work
With the continuous increase in the number of automobiles, the pressure on road
traﬀic has become increasingly severe, making driving safety a topic of utmost concern.
Researchers are now focusing on intelligent driving to enhance driving safety and
reliability, aiming to reduce casualties and property losses.
This thesis addresses
several key areas in this domain.
6.1
Summary
6.1.1
Construction of Driving Dataset
Most existing traﬀic driving datasets are collected from a static surveillance perspec-
tive, which does not reflect real driving scenarios. Additionally, the rarity of accidents
means these datasets mostly consist of normal driving video sequences, which are not
conducive for experimental comparisons and accident detection research. To address
this issue, this study has constructed a large-scale driving perspective traﬀic accident
dataset, DADA-2000.
6.1.2
Advanced Methodologies for Vehicle Position Prediction
and Anomaly Detection
This study explores advanced methodologies for vehicle position prediction and anomaly
detection in driving scenarios.
Key innovations include the application of Fusion-
GRU, an advanced encoder-decoder architecture designed to enhance the accuracy of
future bounding box localization. This model integrates optical flow information and
detection box outputs, enabling robust predictions of vehicle trajectories in dynamic
environments.

6 Conclusion and Future Work
6.1.3
Anomaly Detection Using Generative Adversarial Networks
with Recurrent Residual Structures
To further enhance the extraction of spatial and temporal features from videos, struc-
tural optimizations are applied to the generative network. This includes leveraging
recurrent structures, residual structures, and cyclic structures within the convolu-
tional modules. Residual units are integrated into the sampling layers to maximize
feature extraction, minimizing the loss of feature information while optimizing the
extraction of shallow visual features.
FlowNet is employed to extract temporal characteristics of input sequences by
predicting the optical flow estimation for the subsequent frame. The generated images
and predicted optical flows are then fused and input into a discriminator network for
detection.
Ablation experiments and benchmark model comparisons demonstrate
significant improvements in AUC scores and image generation quality, validating the
effectiveness of introducing cyclic residual structures into the generative network.
6.2
Challenges and Future Prospects
Researchers in abnormal behavior detection have made significant contributions, of-
fering new ideas and methods for studying abnormal behaviors. Despite numerous
experiments and breakthroughs, the field continues to face challenges. The recon-
struction of feature maps by deep learning models is often comprehensive, which
may prevent models from detecting small amounts of abnormal behavior. This study
employs a predictive approach within a generative network framework, comparing
generated images with real images. If a generated frame does not match an actual
frame, it indicates an abnormal behavior frame.
However, the complexity of theoretical research models and the stringent require-
ments for model training environments pose significant challenges for practical imple-
mentation. Moving forward, there are three key areas for future research:
• In-depth research into identifying complex behaviors. Real-world be-
haviors are often complex and involve continuous actions that can interfere with
current algorithmic detection. Addressing the detection of continuous complex
behaviors remains a significant research challenge.
• Decoupling network structures. Current network structures are complex
and redundant. Only simple, rapid algorithms have the potential for practical
application. Developing lightweight and fast computing networks capable of
performing multiple functions while remaining compact is essential for effective
abnormal detection methods.
• Integrating multi-modal information for abnormal detection. Abnor-
mal behavior characteristics are not only evident in action and characterization
