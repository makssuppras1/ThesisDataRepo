Abstract 
 
Significant advancements have been made in the last 20 years in creating hardware for quantum 
computing that can solve issues that are beyond the capabilities of traditional computers. 
For the development of dense, reasonably priced integrated photonic circuits for a range of uses, 
including quantum networking, silicon photonics has become a top platform. Electro-optic (EO) 
modulating switches that are quick and energy-efficient are essential to these applications.  
The continuous development of integrated silicon photonics, a technique that uses silicon structures to 
manipulate infrared light, emphasizes the need for EO modulators that are small, energy-efficient, and 
able to operate at high speeds.  
For many applications, the electro-optical Pockels effect, a crucial nonlinear optical phenomenon, is 
essential. For optical modulators used in photonic circuits, the capacity to quickly vary the refractive 
index is very crucial. Silicon does not have a strong Pockels effect, despite being a good platform for 
integrating small photonic devices.  
In this regard, lithium niobate (LiNbO₃ or LN) has demonstrated excellent performance and can be 
included into silicon photonic devices. However, complicated and energy-intensive wafer bonding 
methods are required to incorporate thin-film LN onto silicon. 
According to recent research, when barium titanate (BaTiO₃ or BTO) is grown epitaxially on silicon 
or on intermediate layers based off silicon substrates, it shows a notable electro-optic response 
through the Pockels effect, much higher than traditional materials like LN, and at the same time 
consuming less power. This characteristic is essential for developing portable energy-efficient 
effective phase modulators for silicon photonic systems.  
This thesis focuses on the deposition of BTO films, including their epitaxial growth on strontium 
titanate (STO) and silicon dioxide (SiO₂), aiming to investigate and document the deposition 
characteristics, behaviors, and optimal processing parameters. 
Overall, the research carried out in this project enabled DTU Nanolab to enhance its understanding on 
depositing epitaxial BTO thin films by RF magnetron sputtering.

adfærd og optimale behandlingsparametre.  
 
Samlet set gjorde forskningen i dette projekt det muligt for DTU Nanolab at forbedre sin forståelse af 
aflejring af epitaksiale BTO-tyndfilm ved hjælp af RF-magnetronsputtering.

6. Introduction and Objectives 
 
Complex oxides are becoming essential components for photonic integrated circuits (PICs) that 
operate on-chip. The capacity to regulate the phase of optical modes is a key prerequisite for 
integrated photonic technology. This control makes it possible to integrate into a variety of 
optoelectronic devices, including optical frequency combs [1-7], phased-array emitters, and 
modulators.  
 
Typical techniques for modifying optical phase usually depend on the plasma effect (refractive index 
varies with carrier concentration) or the thermo-optic effect (refractive index shifts with temperature) 
[8-10]. Both approaches have made it possible to perform a number of optical functions, but they have 
limitations, such as large optical losses (plasma effect) or limited bandwidth (thermo-optic). [1], [11].  
 
Consequently, there has been a drive for techniques that enable quicker refractive index modification, 
especially those that make use of optical nonlinearities. The electro-optic effect is one such technique 
that quickly modifies a material's refractive index by introducing an electric field. Particularly 
encouraging is the linear electro-optic or Pockels effect, in which the refractive index changes in 
direct proportion to the electric field. This nonlinear response allows for more effective phase control 
by allowing the phase (actual component of refractive index) to be changed without changing the 
amplitude (imaginary part). [12-13] 
 
Lithium niobate (LN) has gained prominence in optoelectronics during the last decade because of its 
high Pockels coefficients (r₃₃ = 31.45 pm/V, r₄₂ = 33.96 pm/V) and low optical losses [2-3], [13-14], 
[15-16].  Because of these characteristics, it works well with a variety of optoelectronic devices.  Its 
large Pockels coefficient, which permits effective phase modulation with lower voltages and permits 
more compact electro-optic systems, is the primary factor contributing to its success [12].  
 
With the help of this powerful Pockels effect in LN, Mach-Zehnder interferometer (MZI) modulators 
with millimeter-long lengths (such as 3 mm [17]  and 5 mm [16-18]) have been developed. These 
modulators achieve phase shifts at voltage-length products of VπL = 2.2 V·cm [17] (with simple 
capacitor designs) and as low as VπL = 0.64 V·cm [19-20] (with optimized capacitor structures).  In 
spite of these developments, LN devices are still somewhat big in comparison to those built on 
modern photonic or CMOS architectures.  Furthermore, wafer bonding is required to combine LN 
with silicon since lithium-containing materials are incompatible with conventional CMOS production 
[21]; this method reduces yield while increasing complexity and expense. 
 
With even higher Pockels coefficients than LN (r₄₂ = 923 pm/V [13] for thin-film and r₅₁ = 1000 pm/V 
[22-23] for bulk forms), barium titanate (BaTiO₃ or BTO) is another intriguing material.  Significant 
changes in refractive index under electric fields are also permitted by BTO.  Crucially, CMOS-
compatible techniques including RF sputtering, pulsed laser deposition, and molecular beam epitaxy 
(MBE) can be used to deposit it.  The development of BTO devices is still in its infancy, but a number 
of investigations have shown promise, especially when employing MgO templates, lanthanum 
aluminate (LAO) substrates, or a Strontium Titanate (SrTiO3 or STO) base.  
 
In order to confine light and reach the nonlinear BTO region, certain BTO-based photonic devices 
employ a hybrid architecture in which the waveguide and electro-optic layers are separated.  Strong 
performance has been attained by this design [13], [24-27] (e.g., VπL = ~0.23 V/nm) [28-30]; 
nevertheless, modulation efficiency (VπL) has been somewhat reduced by limiting the optical mode 
overlap with the nonlinear layer.  
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
 
 
 If everything else is equal, monolithic BTO-based devices could improve device performance and 
streamline production.  Recent reviews offer a comprehensive overview of the materials and device 
architectures being investigated and include comparative studies on current silicon photonics 
modulators, including LN, BTO, and other systems like SiGe, III–V semiconductors using the Franz–
Keldysh effect, and various organic and 2D materials [12], [31].  
 
Compared to BTO grown by molecular beam epitaxy (MBE), RF-sputtered BTO offers substantially 
quicker deposition rates and allows high-quality epitaxial growth [32]. For large-scale, high-
throughput manufacturing of monolithic BTO optoelectronic devices that require materials with 
thicknesses on the order of the optical wavelength, this advantage makes it more feasible [28].  
 
Here we report attempts to grow BTO films on first, Silicon (100) substrates, to check for crystalline 
phases at desired locations using preferred RF magnetron sputtering parameters. We discuss the 
characterisations done by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), X-ray diffraction (XRD). The 
crystallinity effect studies on annealing the samples were also carried out. We then report on the 
crystalline growth behaviour exhibited by BTO grown on pure STO crystal substrates using the same 
deposition technique. Epitaxy was then attempted by depositing SiO2, STO, and BTO respectively on 
Si substrate. Thickness measurements extrapolated and obtained from thin film reflectometry have 
also been documented.  
Consequently, this MSc thesis was devoted to understanding the deposition, characterization and Si 
integration of BaTiO3 thin films for quantum networking based on electro optic transducers with the 
following main objectives: 
- 
Reveal the best parameters to deposit BTO thin films by radio-frequency (RF) magnetron 
sputtering. A range of different parameters (power, pressure, target to substrate distance) on 
glass, fused silica, silicon, and pure STO substrates were tested.  
- 
Assess the RF Magnetron sputtering operation method to deposit high quality BTO, STO and 
SiO2 thin films, and to perform Si integration of BTO.  
- 
Perform analytical characterization (spatially distributed) using tools such as XPS, XRD and 
Raman spectroscopy.  
- 
Evaluate the grain structure by SEM and possibly AFM.  
- 
Investigate the effect of thermal annealing on thin film properties.  
- 
Analyze data from the characterization tools.  
- 
Report on findings in the form of report and presentation.  
 
- 
Understand to what extent the acquired knowledge on barium titanate can be used to improve the 
performance of BTO thin films for electro optic transducer applications.

7. Applications and Methods 
 
7.1. Introduction 
The white, crystalline ceramic substance barium titanate (BaTiO₃) is distinguished by its perovskite 
structure.  It has a number of exceptional qualities that make it ideal for both photonic and electrical 
applications.  Ferroelectricity, in which the material displays spontaneous electric polarization that 
may be reversed by an external electric field, is one of its distinguishing features.  This characteristic 
is essential to its operation in capacitive devices and non-volatile memory.  Furthermore, barium 
titanate is piezoelectric, which means that when mechanical stress is applied, it can produce an electric 
charge. This property makes it useful for sensors and actuators [33].  
 
Barium titanate (BaTiO₃) has a history that dates back to the early 1940s, when World War II and 
post-war industrial growth fueled an era of fast advancement in materials science.  In 1941, scientists 
at the National Bureau of Standards (now NIST) in the United States synthesized it for the first time.  
Because of its excellent thermal stability and high dielectric constant, it was first investigated as a 
potential mica substitute for capacitors [34].  
 
A significant turning point was reached in 1945 when Von Hippel and associates at MIT discovered 
ferroelectricity in BaTiO₃.  Ferroelectricity in a ceramic oxide was first demonstrated in this way, and 
its characteristics sparked a great deal of curiosity.  One of the first and most researched ferroelectric 
materials was BaTiO₃.  Its ability to reverse electric polarization in the presence of an external electric 
field opened up new opportunities for sensors, adjustable capacitors, and electronic memory systems. 
[35].  
 
Barium titanate was widely used in industry in the 1950s and 1960s, especially in multilayer ceramic 
capacitors (MLCCs), which are still widely used today.  Its dielectric and ferroelectric properties were 
enhanced by the tight control over grain size and domain structures made possible by advancements in 
ceramic manufacturing.  Research on its application in piezoelectric devices and sonar transducers 
also increased during the Cold War [36].  
 
As optical technologies advanced by the late 20th century, scientists started investigating the 
nonlinear optical and electro-optic characteristics of BaTiO₃, particularly the Pockels phenomenon.  
As a result, BaTiO₃ was identified as a potential contender for optical modulators and integrated 
photonics.  Advances in thin-film deposition methods, including as sputtering and pulsed laser 
deposition, made it possible to develop high-quality BTO films on silicon substrates in the 2000s and 
2010s, overcoming significant obstacles related to thermal expansion and lattice mismatch [37]. 
 
High-speed, low-power electro-optic modulators that work with CMOS fabrication are now possible 
because to BaTiO₃, which is leading the way in hybrid photonic integration.  Its development from a 
material used as a capacitor during the war to a key component of contemporary electro-optic and 
photonic systems illustrates its ongoing scientific and technological importance [28]. 
 
Its high dielectric constant is another important characteristic that makes it a common material used in 
the manufacture of energy storage components and capacitors.  The most significant feature of 
BaTiO₃ for optical applications is its strong Pockels effect, which is a linear change in refractive index 
upon application of an electric field.  For integrated photonic circuits to modulate light quickly and 
effectively, this electro-optic behavior is necessary. These characteristics collectively establish barium 
titanate as a fundamental component for the creation of transducers, capacitors, and nonlinear optical 
devices, especially when considering next-generation electro-optic modulators that are integrated with 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
silicon platforms.  
 
7.2. Applications 
 
Metal oxide thin films have been used in a wide range of generic applications, including 
microelectronics, optics, and renewable energy, among others. Several concrete examples, including 
the main properties of materials exploited for device functionality, are presented in this chapter.  
 
 
Capacitors and High Dielectric Applications 
 
Thin films of barium titanate are an essential part of multilayer ceramic capacitors (MLCCs), which are 
found in many electronic devices, ranging from automobile electronics to cellphones.  BTO's very high 
dielectric constant, which enables designers to fit large capacitance into tiny spaces, is its main benefit in 
this situation.  This reinforces the current trend of electrical components being smaller without 
compromising functionality. BTO's significance in advanced capacitor design is further increased by its 
temperature stability and compatibility with thin-film fabrication methods [38].  
 
Ferroelectric Memory (FeRAM) 
 
The ability of BaTiO₃ to sustain a spontaneous electric polarization that can be reversed by an external 
electric field is known as ferroelectricity.  Ferroelectric random-access memory (FeRAM), a kind of non-
volatile memory, takes advantage of this characteristic.  Information is kept in a ferroelectric layer's 
polarization state in FeRAM devices.  BaTiO₃ is a great option for memory applications due to its non-
volatility, quick switching speeds, and low power consumption, particularly in embedded systems where 
energy efficiency and durability are essential [39].     
 
Piezoelectric Devices (Sensors and Actuators) 
 
BaTiO₃ may also transform mechanical stress into electrical signals and vice versa thanks to its potent 
piezoelectric qualities.  Because of this, BaTiO₃ thin films can be used in MEMS (microelectromechanical 
systems), pressure sensors, accelerometers, ultrasonic transducers, and inkjet printer heads.  These devices 
are essential in a variety of industries, including industrial automation, aerospace, healthcare (e.g., medical 
ultrasound), and automotive systems (e.g., airbag deployment sensors) [40].  
 
Electro-Optic Modulators and Photonic Devices 
 
One of the most promising applications for BaTiO₃ thin films is photonics, namely electro-optic 
modulators. BTO's potent Pockels effect allows it to change light very effectively when subjected to an 
electric field.   This enables the employment of high-speed, low-power optical switches and modulators for 
next-generation optical communication systems, including fiber optics and data centers. BTO sheets have 
been effectively integrated into silicon photonics platforms to close the gap between the nonlinearity of 
BaTiO₃ and the scalability of silicon.   This hybrid integration is necessary for the development of scalable, 
CMOS-compatible photonic integrated circuits (PICs) [41].  
Energy Harvesting 
 
BaTiO₃ thin films are being researched for use in energy harvesting applications, which use the 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
piezoelectric effect to transform ambient mechanical energy—such as vibrations or pressure changes—into 
electrical energy.  When battery replacement is problematic or impossible, these applications are especially 
useful for wearable devices, remote sensing, and autonomous wireless sensor networks.  BaTiO₃-based 
energy harvesters are promise for self-powered, sustainable microsystems due to their efficiency and 
scalability [42]. 
 
7.3. Deposition methods 
Choosing the right deposition technique is an efficient strategy to modify the chemical and physical 
characteristics of thin films [43]. The crystalline structure, which in turn affects the optical, electrical, 
and mechanical properties of the film, can be greatly impacted by the deposition procedure selection. 
Furthermore, the particular technique employed is intrinsically linked to crucial characteristics 
including homogeneity across the surface, surface roughness, layer thickness, and deposition rate. 
Certain material properties can be obtained by carefully selecting or refining the deposition method. 
It's crucial to remember that not all applications can benefit from every deposition procedure. 
Therefore, choosing the best approach or improving on current methods is crucial to creating thin 
films of superior quality.  
Chemical and physical procedures are the two primary categories into which deposition techniques 
are often divided. In this discussion, however, we will focus solely on physical deposition methods 
here, as they are most relevant to this work.  
 
 
7.4. Physical deposition methods 
 
Physical deposition techniques use physical procedures, usually carried out in high vacuum, to 
transfer material to a substrate, usually from a solid source.  Inorganic compounds that do not degrade 
in a vacuum are the primary targets of these techniques.  With exact control over their chemical and 
physical properties, the technique enables the fabrication of high-purity, high-quality thin films.  
Before or during deposition, a high vacuum environment (between 10⁻² and 10⁻⁷ mTorr) is frequently 
created in order to remove undesirable gases from the chamber, reduce contamination, or promote 
plasma formation.  These techniques are sometimes referred to as physical vapor deposition (PVD) 
procedures since the material is often transferred via vaporization [44].  
 
Pulsed Laser Deposition 
Thin films of metals, semiconductors, and insulators can be deposited using the Pulsed Laser 
Deposition (PLD) method.  It entails applying high-energy laser pulses to a target material, as the 
name implies, in order to cause ablation.  Following ejection, the material is moved in a gas phase and 
applied to a substrate as a thin layer [45]. Through the scattering of electrons and atoms, the particles 
created by the ablation process interact with the surrounding gas and the incident laser light to form 
plasma.  Ionized species with energies ranging from a few to millions of electron volts are propelled 
toward the substrate by the electric field produced by this plasma. [46]. PLD typically uses a laser 
light in the ultraviolet region at around 250 nm of wavelength. The pulse of light emitted is absorbed 
at the top surface of the target, leading to a rapid vaporization and its ablation. Typical parameters are 
pulses of 20 – 30 ns with a pulse energy of tenths of a joule [44].  
 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 1: Schematic representation of a PLD process. 
As long as the produced metal oxide thin film is undamaged, the PLD approach has a number of 
advantages over conventional PVD deposition procedures, most notably the ability to maintain the 
film's stoichiometry [47]. PLD films usually show higher crystal quality leading to a superior material 
properties. PLD films usually have better crystal quality, which leads to better material qualities. 
Notwithstanding PLD's many benefits, a fundamental drawback still exists: the laser's tiny ablation 
spot size of 0.01 cm² makes it difficult to achieve uniform layer deposition over broad substrate 
surfaces like 4 inches (~10 cm) in diameter. The use of PLD in large-scale manufacturing has been 
hampered by this problem.  
Magnetron sputtering is a plasma-based technology that can be utilized to overcome the difficulty of 
producing homogenous PLD films over vast areas and the low crystalline quality frequently observed 
in evaporated films.

8. Magnetron sputtering 
 
The deposition process known as sputtering occurs when high-energy particles or ions hit a solid 
target material, causing atoms or molecules to be expelled. A target atom will be released from the 
target and deposited onto a substrate if it possesses enough energy and outward velocity to 
overcome the binding forces securing it to the surface [48]. An inert gas ion is usually the particle 
that imparts momentum and energy to the target atom, but any ion, atom, molecule, or even 
photon can do this as long as it imparts enough energy to cause the target atom to come loose [49].  
 
The vacuum chamber used for magnetron sputtering is usually kept at pressures between 10⁻³ and 
10⁻² mTorr. To ensure a clean environment for deposition, the chamber can be evacuated to 
pressures lower than 10⁻⁷ mTorr prior to the sputtering process starting. This will eliminate 
pollutants, impurities, moisture, and any leftover gases or wall deposits. [50-53]. An extraction 
valve is used to keep an eye on the pressure while an inert noble gas, often argon (Ar), is 
continually delivered to the chamber.  
 
Both a cathode and an anode are needed to create an electric field in order to produce a glow 
discharge, which is an electric current passing through the gas. The target material is placed on the 
negatively charged cathode, while the substrate, which is usually grounded or positively charged 
but occasionally receives a bias voltage, acts as the anode. [54]. As shown in Figure 2(a), argon 
gas, sometimes referred to as the working gas, is injected into the sputtering chamber and fills the 
gap between the two electrodes. A self-sustaining discharge occurs when the voltage applied to 
the cathode above the working gas's breakdown threshold. At high energy, electrons are repelled 
from the negatively charged target surface due to their negative charge, and they collide with the 
argon atoms in the chamber. Ar⁺ ions are created when the argon atoms are excited and ionized by 
these collisions. Through radiative decay, some of these ions decay back to a lower energy state, 
releasing visible light that is sometimes referred to as the discharge's glow. Dislodging atoms from 
the target and creating secondary electrons through momentum transfer, the Ar⁺ ions strike the 
target surface while being drawn to the negatively biased cathode. As seen in Figure 2(b), the 
electric field also accelerates these secondary electrons away from the cathode, giving them 
enough energy to further excite and ionize the nearby argon atoms in the working gas. [55] 
 
The material ejected through this sputtering process travels across the chamber and eventually 
deposits onto the substrate. This arrangement is known as the diode sputtering configuration, 
where secondary electrons are essential in maintaining the direct current (DC) discharge. 
However, in 1965, W. D. Gill and Eric Kay discovered a method to make better use of these 
secondary electrons by introducing small magnets into the cathode. This innovation led to the 
development of what is now known as magnetron sputtering, significantly improving the 
efficiency of the process [55-56]. Primary and secondary electrons are trapped by a static 
magnetic field created by inserting magnets into the cathode immediately below the target. As 
seen in Figure 2(c), this confinement intensifies the production of Ar⁺ ions close to the target 
surface, creating a high-density plasma zone with a distinctive torus-like structure. This 
configuration greatly increases the sputtering efficiency by prolonging the residence period of 
electrons close to the cathode. Higher deposition speeds, lower discharge voltages, and the 
capacity to function at lower pressures are just a few benefits that magnetron sputtering has over 
its predecessor [55]. Since ions are much heavier than electrons, they are far less affected by the 
magnetic field [57]. Ultimately, the Ar⁺ ions transfer their momentum to the target surface,

Main configurations 
 
Target shape, cathode location, and magnetic field arrangement are some examples of the 
variables that can be used to classify magnetron designs. The many sputtering magnetron 
configurations range from planar circular configuration, rectangular planar configuration, rotatable 
cylindrical magnetron sputtering configuration, unbalanced magnetron sputtering, and hollow 
cathode. The relavant main configuration, which is the planar circular configuration, is described 
below.  
 
Planar circular configuration 
 
This arrangement places the target parallel to the substrate and shares a vertical center axis. 
Although this configuration is similar to the conventional diode sputtering setup, it adds two 
stationary, concentric cylindrical magnets that are placed underneath the target. A single magnet 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
 
with a distinct polarity is positioned in the cathode's core, while a second magnet with the opposite 
polarity is positioned close to the edge [58, p. 5]. Figure 2(c) displays the magnetic field lines 
produced by this configuration. 
 
In this configuration, the combined effects of electric and magnetic fields control electron flight. 
Electrons move perpendicular to the magnetic field and parallel to the cathode surface as a result 
of this interaction, which creates a Hall Effect drift. Consequently, electrons travel along the 
magnetic field lines in a helical pattern. By allowing electrons to move around several times, this 
closed-loop trajectory improves the energy transfer from electrons to the plasma and raises the 
efficiency of ionization [59]. 
 
High electron concentrations in the area between the two magnets cause the working gas to be 
intensely ionized, creating a dense, torus-shaped plasma [60]. The distinctive concentric erosion 
rings that are frequently observed on the target surface are mainly produced by this concentrated 
plasma eroding the target material directly beneath it [61]. The magnetic field has a substantial 
effect on electrons, but because of their larger mass, heavier particles like ejected ions, neutral 
atoms, and ionized species are mostly unaffected. 
 
Additionally, cathode arrangements can be categorized as either on-axis or off-axis depending on 
how they are oriented with respect to the target. The cathode is positioned parallel to the target and 
directly opposite it along the same central axis in the on-axis configuration (as seen in Figure 2). 
On the other hand, with the off-axis configuration, the cathode is positioned some distance away 
from the target and at a 90° angle to it. Effective deposition on the substrate is made possible by 
this orientation, which benefits from the wide angular distribution of the sputtered particles [62]. 
 
Operation modes 
 
Magnetron sputtering techniques can also be categorized based on the mode of operation used 
during deposition, specifically by the type of potential applied to the target. The primary modes 
include direct current (DC) magnetron sputtering and radio frequency (RF) magnetron sputtering. 
These two modes are the main focus of this thesis, with a detailed discussion presented in the next 
chapter. 
 
DC 
 
The process of DC magnetron sputtering involves grounding the chamber walls, which serve as 
the anode, and supplying a steady negative voltage to a cathode that is electrically insulated from 
ground. Electrons in the chamber and on the target surface are excited by the negative bias, which 
accelerates them away from the target. Argon (Ar) atoms in the working gas are ionized when 
some of these electrons collide with them. The resultant Ar⁺ ions are attracted to the negatively 
charged target, where they attack it and release secondary electrons along with atoms or clusters of 
atoms. The distinctive light discharge produced by the ionized argon gas is maintained by these 
secondary electrons, which carry on the ionization process.  
 
Neutral atom sputtering is the main driver of the process, as evidenced by the relatively low 
ionization percentage generated by DC discharge, which is on the order of 10⁻⁴ . Electrons can no 
longer interact with the working gas if they escape to the substrate or the grounded chamber walls. 
In order for the sputtered atoms to condense and form a thin layer on a substrate, the substrate is 
usually positioned close to the target.  
 
Conductive materials, such as metals or conductive metal oxides, are well suited for DC 
sputtering. Despite its widespread usage in industrial settings for the deposition of different metal 
oxide thin films, DC sputtering can be challenging due to the dielectric nature of some oxides 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
 
Arcing may happen if the target surface has dielectric areas or is heavily oxidized. This can harm 
the film or sputtering apparatus and occurs when impurities, flaws, or dielectric particles build up 
electric charge until it becomes unstable and releases as arcs. The radio frequency (RF) magnetron 
sputtering technique was created to address this issue. 
 
RF 
 
 
The process is referred to as radio frequency (RF), alternating current (AC), or mid-frequency 
(MF) magnetron sputtering when a voltage that alternates between positive and negative is given 
to the cathode. Usually, AC sputtering works at frequencies lower than 50 kHz. Ions have enough 
energy and mobility at these lower frequencies to produce alternating discharges that resemble DC 
at both electrodes. The previously deposited thin layer might "backsputter" or "resputter" because 
to its alternating action.  
 
In contrast, RF magnetron sputtering uses alternating currents with higher frequencies (over 500 
kHz), with 13.56 MHz being the typical frequency in commercial systems. Even if DC-like 
discharges between the electrodes are inhibited at these higher frequencies, the electrons 
nevertheless acquire sufficient energy to ionize the working gas. An alternating surface potential is 
produced in this method when the RF voltage delivered to the target couples capacitively with the 
plasma. Similar to DC sputtering, positive ions are propelled toward the cathode during the 
negative half of the cycle, impacting the target and ejecting atoms. 
 
In the positive half-cycle, the positively charged target attracts electrons from the glow zone and 
plasma sheath, neutralizing surface charges and preventing electrical arc generation. RF sputtering 
is appropriate for treating dielectric materials because of its self-neutralizing effect. Nonetheless, 
RF sputtering's relatively lower deposition rate in comparison to DC sputtering is one of its 
drawbacks. The propensity of dielectric targets to crack as a result of their high coefficients of 
thermal expansion (CTE) and restricted ductility presents another difficulty when using them. 
High power application can speed up sputtering, but it also causes overheating, which can lead to 
thermal shock, cracking, and ultimately target degradation or failure.

9. Barium Titanate Thin Films 
 
Magnetron sputtering 
 
An extensively researched ferroelectric perovskite oxide with superior dielectric, piezoelectric, 
and ferroelectric qualities is barium titanate (BaTiO₃, BTO). It has been used in a number of 
electronic parts, such as non-volatile memory devices, sensors, actuators, and capacitors [63]. The 
quality, crystallinity, and stoichiometry of the BTO thin films have a significant impact on these 
devices' performance. Radio-frequency (RF) magnetron sputtering is a dependable and scalable 
deposition technology that can be used to create high-quality BTO thin films. [64-65] 
 
It is especially well-suited for high-speed optical modulators, waveguides, and other photonic 
devices due to its large Pockels (linear electro-optic) coefficient. Next-generation optoelectronic 
components can be fabricated in a scalable, CMOS-compatible manner by integrating BTO thin 
films onto silicon substrates using magnetron sputtering. [29] 
10. Materials  
 
10.1. Introduction to BTO 
 
The ferroelectric perovskite oxide barium titanate (BaTiO₃, BTO) is well-known for its large 
linear electro-optic (Pockels) coefficient, notable piezoelectric sensitivity, and high dielectric 
constant. These characteristics make BTO an attractive material for use in high-speed 
optoelectronic and quantum photonic devices, as well as capacitors, sensors, and actuators. 
The strong Pockels effect of BTO is used in this work for electro-optic modulators in quantum 
networking, enabling quick and adjustable light control for qubit connections. [29], [66] 
 
 
Barium Titanate 
 
The quality of BTO's thin films and how they interact with underlying substrates are 
inextricably tied to the technology's performance in integrated photonic systems. Radio-
frequency (RF) magnetron sputtering, a scalable and CMOS-compatible physical vapor 
deposition method, is used in this work to manufacture BTO thin films. Device-grade 
ferroelectric and electro-optic performance requires the creation of homogeneous, 
stoichiometrically regulated BTO films with tunable crystallinity, which is made possible by 
RF magnetron sputtering. [28] 
 
Properties 
 
BTO has a tetragonal crystal structure at ambient temperature, but at its Curie temperature 
(around 120°C), it changes to a cubic phase. Its ferroelectricity, which is characterized by 
spontaneous polarization that is modulable by an external electric field, is supported by this 
phase transition. This non-centrosymmetric ferroelectric structure gives birth to the linear 
electro-optic (Pockels) phenomenon, which facilitates effective light modulation. [67] 
 
 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
 
 
Structural Properties 
 
High crystalline quality and accurate orientation are required for BTO thin films to exhibit optimal 
electro-optic performance. Polycrystalline growth that is epitaxial or highly orientated reduces 
optical losses and improves the electro-optic responsiveness. Grain orientation, interface quality 
with underlying layers, crystallite size, and film tension are critical characteristics. Annealing after 
deposition can decrease defect densities and enhance crystallinity even more. [68] 
 
Lattice Matching 
 
 
BTO is applied on a buffer layer of strontium titanate (SrTiO₃, STO) in order to produce high-
quality epitaxial growth. By sharing a perovskite structure with BTO and having a nearly matched 
lattice constant (BTO: ~4.00 Å; STO: ~3.905 Å), STO functions as an efficient lattice-matching 
layer, reducing interfacial strain. In order to isolate the structure from the silicon (Si) substrate and 
preserve compatibility with the CMOS process, the STO layer is RF sputter deposited on top of a 
thick silicon dioxide (SiO₂) layer [69-71].  
 
The integration of superior BTO thin films onto silicon platforms is made easier by this layer 
stack, which consists of Si substrate, SiO₂, STO, and BTO. This opens the door for the scalable 
manufacture of electro-optic modulators, which are crucial for future quantum communication 
networks. Compact, fast, and low-loss modulation platforms that are perfect for qubit 
interconnects and quantum information transfer are made possible by the combination of BTO's 
strong Pockels effect with the well-established silicon photonics infrastructure.

11.2. Reflectometry for Thickness 
 
By measuring the reflection of light from the sample surface, reflectometry—a non-contact, 
non-destructive optical technique—is frequently used to determine the thickness of thin films. 
The fundamental idea depends on light waves partially reflected from various interfaces within 
a thin film stack interfering with one another. When a thin film is exposed to a broadband light 
beam, some of the light bounces off the top surface, while the rest passes through the film, 
bounces off the substrate interface, and then returns through the top surface. The optical path 
length determines the relative phase difference between the reflected beams, which determines 
whether they interfere constructively or destructively. The reflectance spectrum exhibits 
distinctive oscillations due to this interference phenomenon. 
 
At normal incidence, the condition for constructive interference is given by Equation 1: 
 
2nd=mλ 
 
 
 
 
Eq [1] 
 
where m is the interference order, λ is the vacuum light wavelength, d is the film thickness, 
and n is the film's refractive index. The thickness of the film and its optical constants 
(extinction coefficient k(λ) and refractive index n(λ)) have a direct impact on these 
interference fringes. The film thickness can be precisely calculated by measuring the intensity 
of reflected light as a function of wavelength and fitting the obtained spectrum to a theoretical 
model. 
 
A reflectometry system normally operates in the following order: 
 
1. Light Source: Light directed toward the sample at normal or near-normal incidence is emitted 
by a broadband light source, which usually covers the UV to NIR range.  This guarantees a 
straightforward and consistent geometry for modeling. 
 
2.  Reflection and Detection: The light bounces off the film's upper surface as well as the 
substrate-film contact.  The reflected beam that results from these interferences is gathered. 
 
3. Spectral Analysis: After the light is gathered, it is transferred to a spectrometer, which creates 
a reflectance spectrum by measuring the intensity of the reflected light in relation to 
wavelength. 
 
4.  Spectrum Features: Interference fringes can be seen in the reflectance spectrum.  Broad 
fringes are produced by thin films, whereas closely spaced fringes are produced by thicker 
films.  The film thickness and refractive index affect these fringes' amplitude, form, and 
spacing. 
 
5. Mathematical Modeling: A model based on thin-film interference and the Fresnel equations is 
used to create a theoretical reflectance spectrum.  The film thickness d, refractive index n(λ), 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
 
extinction coefficient k(λ), and angle of incidence θ all affect the modeled reflectance R(λ), 
which can be written as follows: 
 
 f(n(λ),k(λ),d,θ) = R(λ) 
 
6. Curve Fitting: The model spectra and the observed spectrum are contrasted.  To reduce the 
discrepancy between the observed and modeled data, a nonlinear least-squares fitting 
algorithm modifies the model parameters, mainly d, and optionally n(λ) and k(λ).  After that, 
the best-fit thickness is given, usually with a fitting error or confidence interval. 
 
A variety of film types, such as transparent, semi-transparent, and absorbing films, can be 
measured using this technique. With the right modeling, it can also be applied to multilayer 
stacks.  When the surface is uniformly smooth and the optical characteristics of the film are 
fully understood, reflectometry's accuracy is at its peak. 
 
 
11.3. X-ray diffraction (XRD) 
 
An analytical method called X-ray diffraction (XRD) is used to examine the arrangement of 
atoms, determine the phases found in thin films, and evaluate structural characteristics like 
internal stress, lattice constants, crystal orientation, and grain size. At least three fundamental 
parts make up a typical XRD system: 
 
First, a glass or metal tube with a tungsten filament inside is usually used as the X-ray source. 
The filament releases electrons when heated. A high-voltage electric field accelerates these 
electrons in the direction of a copper (Cu) anode. The high-energy collision creates X-rays 
upon interaction with the anode, which are subsequently focused on the sample. 
 
The sample is then placed on a goniometer, or stage, which may tilt and rotate. In order to 
maximize its exposure to the incident X-rays, the sample is positioned. The regularly 
organized atoms in the crystal lattice interact with the monochromatic X-rays when they strike 
the sample. As seen in Figure 4, under specific circumstances, the diffracted X-rays stay in

12. Experimental methods 
Magnetron sputtering 
BaTiO3 thin films were sputtered on a system that employed short target to substrate distance 
using a 2-inch single phase BTO target. There was no substrate heating on this system.  
A target-to-substrate distance of 45 mm was kept short in order to increase the number of 
sputtered species that reached the substrate surface. On amorphous substrates, such as glass, 
where epitaxial growth is not supported, film quality depends on uniform coverage and intense 
nucleation. The small distance encourages the production of smooth, compact films with fewer 
voids and imperfections by increasing the adatoms' arrival rate and local energy. Furthermore, for 
volatile elements like oxygen and barium, which are prone to scattering or re-sputtering at longer 
throw distances, this design aids in maintaining stoichiometric transfer [74], [75]. Early on in the 
deposition process, when the film's shape and substrate adherence are established, these factors are 
crucial.  
 
The change of the DC self-bias voltage as a function of working gas pressure was studied in order 
to characterize the plasma conditions during RF magnetron sputtering of BaTiO₃ (BTO) thin 
films. In order to assess the impact on the plasma-sheath dynamics, we systematically altered the 
shutter opening diameter (Ø = 50 mm, 60 mm, and 100 mm).

Figure 5 displays the DC self bias as a function of pressure. All showed increased DC biases at 
low pressures (<5 mTorr), which is in line with what is expected in low-pressure RF plasmas with

The ion energy of species bombarding the substrate decreases in proportion to the decreased DC

resputtering effects, it is beneficial to reduce incident ion energy during deposition. Overly high

XRD 
 
The first studies were conducted on amorphous glass substrates to validate the sputtering process

phase formation because glass lacks a crystalline template. Before moving on to the more intricate 
and resource-intensive epitaxial architecture incorporating a Si/SiO₂/STO stack, this first 
evaluation was essential [77]. We confirmed that the deposition process yielded no film

crystallinity.  
Therefore, it was appropriate to proceed for additional optimization on the deposition parameters,

characterization for the chosen choice.  
 
Annealing 
 
It was reported that the crystallization starts at around 420°C in ambient atmosphere.  
As the temperature rises above 420°C, two distinct crystalline phases begin to form: the cubic 
perovskite BaTiO₃ phase (c-BaTiO₃) with Pm3̅m symmetry, and the hexagonal BaTiO₃ phase (h-
BaTiO₃) with P6₃/mmc symmetry. As the annealing temperature continues to increase, the 
proportion of the cubic phase becomes more dominant [78].  
It was therefore decided to anneal them at 600°C for 2 hours, but under vacuum instead of air to 
prevent outgassing of contaminants from the walls of the annealing chamber. [78] 
XRD was again performed and no crystallinity was found at the center of the deposited wafers.

From the range of ~0.3-0.55 Pa (~2-4.2 mTorr) the plasma discharge is in the plume-like mode 
The plasma typically takes on a plume-like shape at lower working pressures, with its 
concentration along the central axis between the substrate and the target [79]. Sputtered species 
can move primarily in straight-line trajectories under these circumstances due to the decreased 
collision frequency, producing a flux that is strongly directed along the normal to the target 
surface. The sputtered flux's directionality increases the rate of deposition near the substrate's 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
center in comparison to its edges.  
 
Resputtering effects can have a major impact on film uniformity in RF magnetron sputtering of 
BaTiO₃ using argon (Ar) as the working gas, especially in the substrate's center, at higher 
pressures of around 4mTorr or higher. The plasma sheath can accelerate negatively charged 
oxygen ions from the BaTiO₃ target surface back toward the substrate, whereas Ar ions are in 
charge of sputtering material from the target. The sheath potential gives these oxygen ions a high 
kinetic energy, and they often follow paths that are normal to the target surface.  
 
This effect is amplified at low target-to-substrate distances by the geometric confinement, which 
concentrates the ion flux close to the substrate center. Material, especially volatile components 
like titanium or barium, might be respewed from the developing film by the ensuing barrage of 
powerful oxygen ions. In addition to lowering the net deposition rate, this resputtering may result 
in stoichiometry variations near the substrate's center. Central thinning of the deposited film 
therefore resulted in compositional inhomogeneity, even at low RF power applied.  
The erosion track is also visible, marked (yellow line) in the thickness measurement plots

~120nm/hour, so deposition time was ~17 hours, at room temperature with no substrate heating,

under parameters of 100W RF power and 5mTorr total pressure with Ar as working gas.  
For STO, the deposition rate was found to be quite low at 40nm/hour, so the deposition time was 
~12 minutes, at 150W RF power and 5mTorr total pressure.  
 
For BTO, the thickness at that location was of the order of ~400nm which is close to the ~400-
500nm we would want, so the preferred deposition parameters for BTO, especially the deposition 
time, was left unchanged from the original 2 hours planned.

Conclusions 
 
This MSc thesis focused on the deposition, characterization, and silicon integration of barium 
titanate (BaTiO₃, BTO) thin films with the goal of enabling their application in electro-optic 
transducer applications for quantum networking.  
 
The study started by investigating BTO deposition utilizing RF magnetron sputtering on both 
crystalline and amorphous substrates. No discernible crystallinity was found in early depositions 
on glass substrates, which led to additional substrate selection and deposition parameter 
optimization. Better lattice-matched substrates are required, as annealing at 600°C under vacuum 
conditions did not produce crystalline coatings on glass either. 
 
Although crystallinity was poor and restricted to the wafer's edge, further experiments employing 
silicon (Si) substrates shown that crystalline phases could be produced under ideal circumstances, 
most notably, deposition at 200 W, substrate heating to 700 °C, and a working pressure of 10 
mTorr. Due to their superior lattice matching with BTO, deposition on single crystal SrTiO₃ 
(STO) substrates demonstrated enhanced but still weak crystallinity in the vicinity of the target-
facing edge and wafer center. 
 
Si/SiO₂/STO/BTO was used to create a multilayer stack in an effort to try epitaxial integration 
with silicon. Reflectometry was used to calibrate the deposition rates for each layer, and BTO 
reached thicknesses of 400–500 nm. EDS mapping corroborated the elemental composition of the 
BTO layer, whereas cross-sectional SEM imaging validated the gradient profile of the deposition. 
 
Film stoichiometry and crystalline quality were revealed by XPS and XRD investigations, 
whereas plume shape and sputtering system-specific resputtering effects were connected to spatial 
changes in thickness and composition.  
 
In summary, this study's results show that RF magnetron sputtering can be used to create BTO 
thin films with partial crystallinity on Si and STO substrates. The study found important process 
parameters that influence crystalline quality and spatial uniformity, even when epitaxial 
integration on Si was not entirely accomplished. High-quality epitaxial BTO layers that work with 
silicon photonics for quantum networking applications may result from additional parameter and 
process improvement.
