Abstract
This master thesis constitutes a systematic investigation of thermonuclear bursts on neu-
tron stars in low-mass x-ray binary systems, observed by NuSTAR from 2018 to 2021. It
adopts a comprehensive approach to investigate these highly energetic events and the re-
lated phenomena. Time-resolved spectroscopy, with a variable persistent emission approach,
is implemented in XSPEC to extract information about these bursts, such as their dura-
tion, temperature, photospheric radius expansion (PRE), peak flux, and fuel composition.
Twelve bursts in total were explored, from SLX 1735-269, 4U 1636-536, 4U 1728-34, and
GRS 1741.9-2853, all sources are known to exhibit X-ray burst activities. The findings of
this research are largely on par with theoretical predictions, with observed values of τ and
α aligning with those documented in the literature. The derived peak flux, temperature,
and fa values were also largely in line with those previously reported in scientific literature.
However, the analysis revealed a consistently small blackbody radius and limited availability
of photons that inherently restricted the resolution of the spectroscopy and resulted in high
uncertainty in many cases.

Introduction
1
Introduction
Neutron stars are incredibly dense objects that form after a massive star undergoes a super-
nova explosion. They are typically about 10 kilometers in radius but can have twice the mass
of our sun. Due to their extremely high densities, neutron stars generate strong gravitational
fields that make them ideal natural laboratories to study the behavior of matter in extreme
conditions.[5]
These characteristics contribute to occurrences such as thermonuclear bursts, or Type-I X-
ray bursts, which arise from rapid and unstable nuclear reactions in accreted stellar matter
on a neutron star’s surface. The accretion matter is mainly composed by Hydrogen and/or
Helium derived from the outer layers of a low-mass companion star. The matter accretes
on the surface of the neutron star, forming a layer under increasing pressure due to the
intense gravitational field. Once the pressure and temperature at the base of this layer reach
a critical point, the conditions can lead to a sudden onset of thermonuclear burning. This
burning rapidly spreads across the entire surface, producing a bright flash of X-rays.[14]
Over recent decades, advances in space-based X-ray observatories have significantly improved
our ability to study neutron stars and thermonuclear bursts. NuSTAR, launched by NASA
in 2012, has been instrumental in advancing our knowledge in this domain, especially due
to its ability to observe high-energy X-rays in the band of 3-79 keV.[25]
This thesis explores the Type-I x-ray bursts on accreting low-mass x-ray binary system
neutron stars, as observed by the Nuclear Spectroscopic Telescope Array (NuSTAR) from
2018 to 2021.
The x-ray bursts were identified by fellow bachelor student, Astrid Lind
Bouquin, through light curve analysis. The primary objective is to study these bursts using
spectral analysis and extract time-resolved information about the properties of the neutron
stars during these bursts, such as their duration, temperature, photospheric radius expansion
(PRE), flux, and, if possible, the composition of the fuel, either hydrogen or helium-rich.
The data will be initially analyzed to produce the source files into distinctive time segments
corresponding to different phases of the neutron star burst, namely the rise, peak, and
decay phases. This approach will guarantee sufficient photon counts for accurate analysis.
Moreover, the examination will not be limited to the burst itself. Attention will also be paid
to the persistent emission preceding the burst, a process crucial for defining an accurate
model.
Spectral analysis will be implemented through the use of the XSPEC software, measuring
the energy distribution of the X-ray radiation emitted during the burst. By fitting the data
to a model specifically determined for each burst, the temperature and photospheric radius
can be determined as well as the evolution of the persistent emission during the burst. In the
past, the persistent part of the burst was thought to remain constant throughout the burst,
but this has been shown to not be accurate and instead, the persistent emission typically
increases.[32]

Theory
This section provides an overview of the theory and concepts fundamental to the understand-
ing of neutron stars. The purpose of this theoretical part is to base the ensuing observations
and analyses on established scientific background.
After a small introduction, the basic structures around low-mass X-ray binary systems will
be described followed by important associated phenomena. Finally, the role of spectroscopy,
NuSTAR, and relative literature will be discussed.
2.1
Neutron star overview
Neutron stars are extremely compact celestial bodies that are predominantly composed of
matter with a density exceeding the nuclear threshold, largely constituted by neutrons, hence
their name. These compact stars have a similar mass to the sun, approximately 1.4 solar
masses (M⊙), but a radius of about 10 kilometers. This is an enormous difference from the
radius of the Sun, which is approximately 105 times larger, at 6.96 × 105 kilometers. This
significant disparity in scale results in several unique characteristics of neutron stars, such as
their enormous gravitational field.[15] In Figure 1a and Figure 1b, the structure of a neutron
star’s inner and outer layers is shown for reference.

Neutron stars are typically remnants from a supernova explosion. The mechanism capable of
producing such objects, which compresses matter to the point where protons and electrons
combine to form neutrons, is the gravitational collapse of a star’s core, typically a type II or
Ib/Ic supernova. When a star nears the end of its life cycle, the balance between two main
forces - gravity pulling inward and the outward radiation pressure from energy generated by
nuclear fusion - starts to change. Once the star exhausts its nuclear fuel, the gravitational

forces overcome the radiation pressure, resulting in a collapse. This event releases a signifi-
cant amount of energy, transforming into heat due to the change in gravitational potential
energy. As this collapse comes to a stop, thanks to the strong nuclear force, it triggers a
supernova explosion with a dense, compact core remaining, referred to as a neutron star.[29]
In this research, data from binary systems are analyzed. Binary systems, particularly those
emitting X-rays, were a significant discovery in the field of astrophysics during the 1960s.
No known physical process at the time could account for the immense X-ray luminosity they
exhibited.[29]
Two primary types of binary systems have been identified:
High-mass X-ray binaries (HMXB): Here, the material is provided from a relatively young
star and is then transferred to the neutron star through stellar wind. In these systems, we
primarily observe the star due to its luminosity.[29]
Low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXB): In these systems, a comparably older companion star
goes over its Roche lobe, a region around a star in a binary system within which orbiting
material remains gravitationally bound to that star. By going over the Roche lobe, the
material is transferred to the neutron star. For this process to be balanced, the donor star
has to have less mass than the neutron star.[29] Low-Mass X-ray Binaries are the type of
systems analyzed in this research. A visual representation of this process can be seen in

2.2 Accretion Disks in Low-Mass X-ray Binary Systems
2.2
Accretion Disks in Low-Mass X-ray Binary Systems
In LMXB systems, the main components are the neutron star and a companion star of
lower mass. Due to gravitational influence, matter from the companion star is drawn toward
the neutron star.
This process, termed accretion, initiates when the outer layers of the
companion star go over the Roche lobe.[10][29]
The matter drawn from the companion does not directly descend onto the neutron star.
Instead, due to angular momentum conservation, the matter spirals inward, forming an
accretion disk around the neutron star. The material spirals towards the center due to the
loss of angular momentum caused by internal friction within the disk. [29]
The process of mass transfer in an accretion disk is characterized by high energies.
As
material moves inward in a spiraling path, it experiences friction, which generates heat and
increases the temperature of the matter drastically. This causes the emission of X-rays,
mainly from the inner parts of the disk where the temperature is higher. An additional
process of X-ray emission comes into play when the matter from the disk reaches the surface
of the neutron star. The extreme gravitational strength of the neutron star pulls the matter
onto its surface at incredible speeds, which results in more heat. This additional heating
results in the emission of additional X-rays.[23][8] A layer of Hydrogen is formed on the
surface of the star due to accretion, that will undergo steady fusion to create Helium, under
the extreme pressure and temperatures, also emitting x-rays. This continuous X-ray emission
processes will be called persistent emission.[29]
This layer of Helium, which now resides beneath the hydrogen layer, will eventually undergo
an unstable thermonuclear explosion, known as an x-ray burst.[21]
It is worth highlighting that in High Mass X-ray Binary (HMXB) systems, neutron stars
often have potent magnetic fields. These intense fields interact powerfully with the accretion
disk, steering the matter being pulled in along the magnetic field lines, towards the neutron
star’s magnetic poles. Because of this effect, the X-rays emitted by the accumulating matter
predominantly come from the magnetic poles. This emission pattern appears as observable
pulsations. Hence, neutron stars in HMXB systems are frequently referred to as pulsars.[29]
In contrast, in Low Mass X-ray Binary (LMXB) systems, neutron stars typically have weak
magnetic fields.
These less powerful fields insignificantly affect the process of accretion.
Consequently, the matter being drawn in forms a disk around the neutron star and spirals
inward with minimal interruption. This means that these systems usually do not exhibit
pulsations, unlike their high-mass counterparts[29] As a result, this investigation assumes
that the processes involved in accretion and emission are not magnetically influenced.
Another important consideration is the orientation of the accretion disk in relation to the
observer.
The observational line of sight might be obstructed due to the disk’s relative
inclination, which can significantly impact the data.

High inclination sources cause the observation of the accretion disk through changing absorp-
tion impacts, resulting in noticeable fluctuations in X-ray emissions during different phases.
Given a high enough inclination, the neutron star may be completely hidden and these sys-
tems become identifiable only through their coronae that emit X-rays, extending above and
beneath the disk.[29]
As indicated in Figure 3, the position of the donor star can also obstruct the observer’s
view of the neutron star. However, this obstruction tends to coincide with the angles at
which the accretion disc itself disrupts the neutron star’s visibility, thus making the disc
the problem. The disc, being thickest at its edges, can cause further obstruction, regardless
of the donor star’s position in relation to the observer since it surrounds the neutron star.
Consequently, to facilitate an unobstructed observation, the system inclination must be such
that it overcomes the accretion disc edge. This requirement defines the range of observational
angles that can provide clear sight of the neutron star.[29]
2.3
Corona
In LMXB systems, hard X-ray emissions are often detected. These emissions are in a power-
law pattern, with energy levels up to tens or hundreds of keV. These hard X-ray emissions
do not originate from the standard accretion disk that generates thermal X-ray emissions
but instead, they come from what is known as a corona, a structure close to the star, which
extends over a large region around it as seen in Figure 4, but radiation seems to pass through
without being significantly absorbed or scattered.[10]

In neutron stars that are accreting at a relatively low rate, the key source of X-ray radiation
that illuminates the accretion disk might not be the corona. Instead, it could be the layer
where the material meets the neutron star’s surface. In other words, it is the region where
the accreted matter spreads out and slows down to match the star’s rotation that could be
primarily responsible for lighting up the disk with X-rays.[10]
The theoretical physics and relative research around the corona is a very complex subject that
lies outside the scope of this investigation. That being said, whether the corona significantly
influences emission processes could impact the modeling of the spectral data, as will be
explored in subsequent sections.
2.4
Thermonuclear Explosions on the Neutron Star Surface
As matter from the accretion disk shifts and settles, it progressively covers the surface of
the neutron star. This results in a thickening layer of accumulated hydrogen and/or helium,
with its depth continuously increasing due to ongoing accretion processes.
As the material accumulates, the conditions at the base of this layer grow increasingly
extreme. High pressures and temperatures at the bottom of these layers stimulate the onset
of nuclear reactions. Initially, these reactions occur sporadically and create heavier elements
like carbon through a series of fusion processes. However, as accretion continues, the base of
the layer becomes hotter and denser. Once a critical pressure and temperature are reached, a
runaway nuclear reaction can occur. This leads to a thermonuclear explosion on the surface
of the neutron star. This kind of explosion is identified by a sudden surge in luminosity and
is classified as a type I X-ray burst. If the fuel was burned steadily in a controlled fusion
instead of this burst process, it would not be observable as the surrounding emissions would
render it invisible.[29]
As the visual representation suggests in Figure 5, this process is circular. Following a burst,
the process of accretion resumes, setting the stage for another explosive event. The interval
between such bursts can often exhibit a certain regularity, typically spanning from hours to
days. However, some instances of smaller bursts have been observed to occur within minutes

2.5
Eddington limit
As material falls towards the neutron star, it interacts with its radiation pressure, which can
decelerate it. This pressure correlates directly with the intensity of the emitted radiation.[29]
If the luminosity of the star during a burst is high enough, the radiation pressure can coun-
terbalance the gravitational force, stopping the accretion process. When these conditions
are met, the ensuing luminosity is referred to as the Eddington luminosity. This value is

2.6
Photospheric radius expansion(PRE)
As stated in the previous section, the Eddington limit sets the upper limit for the maximum
flux of a burst for any given source. When that limit is reached during a burst, the intense
radiation pressure counterbalances the gravitational forces and rises the outer layers of the
neutron star increasing the photospheric radius while simultaneously, the temperature de-
creases. This is described as photospheric radius expansion (PRE) and is only possible close
to the Eddington limit.[29][1]
As described in subsection 2.4, the duration of accretion before a burst is directly related to

the accreted mass. As a result, a longer accretion period will typically produce a brighter
burst, possibly close to the Eddington limit, that may result in PRE. A double peak in the
temperature can be observed close to the peak phase of the burst, where the temperature
initially increases as the fusion process peaks, followed by the immediate reduction due to
the PRE, only to rise again at touchdown. Finally, the temperature will gradually decrease
over the cooling phase.[29][6]
Recognizing a PRE through this temperature pattern can be a challenge due to the rapid
nature of its occurrence, requiring short time intervals to be detected. Using short intervals
inherently limits the number of available photons for spectroscopic model fitting, which can
prevent precise analysis by producing large error bars. This issue and its implications will
be examined in greater detail in the forthcoming section.
2.7
X-ray time-resolved spectroscopy
Time-resolved spectroscopy serves as an essential tool in the study of various astronomical
phenomena, including thermonuclear explosions on neutron stars. This technique provides
valuable temporal information about the spectral changes associated with these events, fa-
cilitating a more comprehensive understanding of their nature and underlying physical pro-
cesses.
LMXBs mainly emit X-rays, and the ratio of their luminosity in the optical band to that
in the X-ray band usually falls below 0.1. Due to the large distances to LMXB systems,
current X-ray telescopes cannot obtain the spatial resolution of the central X-ray emission
from sources of this size. Consequently, the study of these objects in X-rays relies on the
time-resolved analysis of their spectra.[7]
The issue with time-resolved spectroscopy lies in the collection and analysis of spectra over
a defined period, with each individual spectrum corresponding to a distinct time interval.
Smaller time intervals enable better time-resolved analysis that could potentially reveal pro-
cesses that would otherwise not be observable, but there is a caveat. As the time intervals
get smaller and smaller, so does the photon count. This makes the results less reliable and
difficult to analyze. Therefore, a balance has to be found between the two, a balance that is
unique for each case as it depends on the duration and the intensity of the burst at hand.
By combining temporal and spectral data, time-resolved spectroscopy paints a more com-
plete picture. Specifically, it illuminates details of energy transfer, as well as structural and
compositional changes happening in short timescales. In the context of this investigation,
time-resolved spectroscopy will provide information about the evolution of temperature, ra-
dius, flux, fuel composition, and the effects of persistent emission during the burst phase.

2.8 Relative literature & Physical parameters
2.8
Relative literature & Physical parameters
In this investigation, as it is common, the neutron star emission spectra will be modeled
using a blackbody function as it is proven to approximate their physical processes quite
accurately.
A blackbody is a theoretical construct that completely absorbs all incident
radiation, regardless of frequency or angle of incidence. Consequently, it is also a perfect
emitter of thermal radiation. Given the extremely high densities and temperatures found
within neutron stars, it is reasonable to consider their emission as approximating blackbody
radiation.
A fundamental principle in this study is the Stefan-Boltzmann Law, which is mathematically
represented by j∗= σT 4. In this equation, j∗stands for the blackbody radiant emittance,
which is the energy radiated per unit area per unit time, T is the blackbody temperature, and
σ represents the Stefan-Boltzmann constant. Essentially, this law underscores the concept
that a blackbody’s total radiated energy is proportional to the fourth of its temperature.[29]
Derived from this law, we can compute essential properties related to the emission from
neutron stars. For instance, the luminosity (L) of a neutron star can be computed from
the Stefan-Boltzmann Law, assuming that the distance is known, by considering the surface
area of the star. If R represents the radius of the neutron star, then L = 4πR2σT 4. The
observed luminosity and temperature can then be used to estimate the radius of the neutron
star. This also shows that for a given luminosity, the radius and temperature are inversely
proportional.[29] As the radius of the neutron star increases, the temperature must decrease
to keep the luminosity constant, and vice versa. In the case of a PRE burst, this stands
exceptionally well. As described in subsection 2.6, during the PRE the radius will increase,
inducing a cooling due to the expansion, and then reheats during touchdown. In addition,
the comparative tool in this investigation will be the bolometric flux, which is the estimated
flux, as observed at the source, after accounting for interstellar absorption.
It is important to note, however, that the blackbody model is an approximation. Neutron
stars are not perfect blackbodies, and the high-energy environments lead to deviations from
the ideal blackbody spectrum. Another important assumption is that the whole surface of
the neutron star is visible to the observer. As mentioned before, that is not always the case
and depends on the orientation of the system. Nonetheless, these models provide a useful
first-order approximation and their shortcomings will be discussed later.
The persistent emission is also fitted using the blackbody function. It has been common
practice to subtract the persistent emission prior to the burst as background radiation. This
assumption proposes that the persistent emission is not affected during the burst.[6][1][19]
In light of recent studies, the conventional approach of utilizing pre-burst spectra as a back-
ground for modeling X-ray burst emissions has been found to have its drawbacks. It has
been observed that, when dealing with high signal-to-noise ratio data, the technique results
in spectral residuals.
These findings question the effectiveness of this standard method,
suggesting potential instability in the accretion process.[19][1][32][33][10]
Introducing a new constant can help modify the model according to the effects of the burst

The investigated data are provided by NuSTAR, specifically during the period from Septem-

June 2012, is a space-based X-ray observatory that operates in the high-energy X-ray range
(3-79 keV). This thesis will utilize data collected by NuSTAR, specifically on its observations
of low-mass X-ray binary systems.[16][27]
Figure 6: NuSTAR[16]
As seen in Figure 6, NuSTAR operates with two co-aligned X-ray telescopes, each having
an identical design that serves to enhance the overall sensitivity of the instrument. These
matching pieces of equipment work in parallel to capture and analyze the high-energy X-ray
emissions independently. The data from the two telescopes can then be combined to increase
the photon count and enable more precise measurements.
The two identical optics modules are equipped with Cadmium Zinc Telluride (CdZnTe)
detectors that achieve a spectral resolution of approximately 400 eV (FWHM) at 6 keV.[26]
The dual instrument design significantly improves photon collection efficiency, facilitating
the detection and spectral characterization of both faint and bright X-ray sources.
Each module in NuSTAR is equipped with a quadruple detector, as depicted in Figure 7.
This design essentially combines four individual detectors into one unit. To ensure optimal
accuracy in measurements and minimize potential variations, it is crucial to keep the source
of interest in a single detector as much as possible. This ensures reliable and consistent
collection of high-quality data, vital for the robust analysis and interpretation of observations,
and will be important in section 3.
A distinctive feature of NuSTAR is the extendable mast that separates the mirrors from
the detectors. This 10-meter mast, which was deployed once the spacecraft was in orbit, is
fundamental to the operation of the observatory. This extension creates a physical separation
between the optics and the detector, enabling the telescope to achieve an effective focal

NuSTAR to attain an exceptionally high angular resolution, enabling detailed observations
of X-ray sources with great clarity.[16]
In addition, a metrology laser system is incorporated to maintain the precision and accuracy

and gravitational effects, and the inertial displacement after locking on a new target, can in-

When two models produce indistinguishable results, the model
with the fewer model parameters is selected for simplicity. The aforementioned results are
numerically evaluated based on chi-squared statistics. The chi-square value is automatically
printed when fitting along with the degrees of freedom. The metric of goodness is given
however by the ratio between those two numbers and ideally as close as possible to 1, called
reduced chi-squared.
It is important to mention that in all the cases for the persistent emission, re-binning of the
channels was performed to at least 50 counts per bin, to ensure that each bin has a sufficient
amount of counts.
This was always proven to produce better results.
The rebinning is
performed outside the XSPEC environment with the following set of commands:
grppha nu[OBSID]A01.pha
group min 50
exit
Although the process for determining the persistent emission model ends here, the same
methodology is used for the burst phases. The differences start with the model definition.
In the case of the burst phases the following model format is used:
Page 24 of 63
==PAGE:32==
Methodology
3.3 Time-resolved spectroscopy using XSPEC
mo TBabs(bbodyrad+cons([PERSISTENT_MODEL]))
where the [PERSISTENT_MODEL] is defined by the aforementioned process and all its model
parameters are kept frozen. As analyzed in the theory the bbodyrad model is the blackbody
approximation from which the temperature and radius are extracted, as it includes a normal-
ization proportional to the area. Fitting this model will allow only these three parameters,
including the constant cons, to vary.
The errors for three parameters are also produced by using the command:
err 1.0 2 3 4
where 1.0 sets the confidence level to 1σ. Then the model is modified to include another
set of parameters in order to calculate the bolometric flux from 0.01 keV to 200 keV:
editmod mo TBabs*cflux*(bbodyrad+cons([PERSISTENT_MODEL]))
With the normalization parameters frozen, the model is fitted again and the bolometric flux
is calculated alongside its errors.
This whole process has to be repeated for each phase to acquire the temperature, radius, fa,
and bolometric flux at each time interval, thus providing all the necessary data to create the
burst evolution plots.
This process was originally carried out manually, however, as familiarity with the method-
ology increased, this was eventually translated into an automated script. However, despite
this automation, manual intervention was almost always required. Ensuring the appropriate
energy range and validating the success and acceptability of the fitting, as XSPEC would
encounter problems in fitting all variable parameters. An example of the formulated script
can be seen in A.2.
The data was saved in a specific format as an XLSX file to ensure easy handling and ma-
nipulation. To visualize the evolution of the burst, a MATLAB script was developed. This
script accepts an Excel file as input and outputs a single multi-plot depicting the change in
temperature, radius, fa, reduced chi-squared, and bolometric flux over time. The MINBAR
catalog was also used to derive the values of distance, used to calculate the radius and Ed-
dington limit which is noted in the bolometric flux figure. The MATLAB script can be seen
in A.1.
Final noteworthy comments:
• The possibility of re-binning the burst spectra was also considered during the analysis.
However, this approach ended up providing less satisfactory results. This may be due
to the already low count rates in the spectra. While re-binning is a common tool for
improving fit in cases with low counts, it seems that in this case, the counts were
already too low to support a further reduction in the number of bins.
This could
negatively impact the results and make them less reliable due to the very low degrees
of freedom. Regardless of the minimum grouping value that was used, the original,
un-binned data always provided a better fit.
Page 25 of 63
==PAGE:33==
Methodology
3.3 Time-resolved spectroscopy using XSPEC
• When dealing with a very low amount of data, using the c-stat (Poisson data) is often
more suitable, as chi-squared statistics assume a Gaussian distribution. If the counts
are indeed very low, this Gaussian assumption might not be correct. The potential use
of c-stat was explored, but it did not offer any advantages over chi-squared statistics.
Additionally, determining the goodness-of-fit in c-stat is not as straightforward. It
involves running simulated Monte Carlo realizations of the spectra based on the model,
a procedure that is computationally expensive and therefore time-consuming.

Discussion
5
Discussion
Overall, the physical parameters were successfully extracted from the time-resolved spec-
troscopy and the results largely show consistency with the theory. The temperature displays
the expected typical values of 1-3 keV during the bursts within uncertainty.[19]. The flux
also displays the same expected patterns overall, while the fa largely remains between 1-10,
which is in accordance with the literature, although occasionally with large error bars.[33][1]
The fa parameter, as expected, tends towards a value close to one in the late decaying
phases, which is the prior persistent emission. The reduced chi-squared values were typically
within the acceptable limits of 0.8-1.2.
The use of only one of the instruments (FPMA), proved to affect the time resolution of the
spectroscopy, especially for 1728-34, where a lower amount of photon counts was available.
A note should be made on the, at times, significantly low radius provided by the fit. Some
possible explanations for the unusually low radius across all different sources are the follow-
ing:
• The inclination of the system, as discussed in the theory.
• The assumption that the burst is isotropic may not be true.
• Relativistic effects from the gravitational field and the distance (redshift) are not taken
into account.[6]
• Photosphere absorption may affect the perceived emission.
• The assumption of distance used to calculate the radius may be incorrect.
All, or some of the above have affected the resulting radius in all of the sources, some more
than others. As a result, the discussion on the radius at any given point is made in reference
to its preceding and succeeding value and not to realistically estimate the size of the neutron
star.
In addition, the radius exhibits a significant increase in the last decay phase which is expected
due to the aforementioned blackbody theory, as the temperature drops. However it is possible
that this increase is not related to a physical increase in the photospheric radius of the neutron
star, but rather an influence from other emission components that might start to contribute
more significantly as the temperature drops below 1 keV. For example, cooler surrounding
areas may now also emit observable amounts of radiation. This radiation would have a lower
temperature, and could possibly cause an increase in the inferred radius, as the fit tries to
balance the decreased temperature with an increased radius to maintain the observed flux
level.
While the above observations hold significance for all bursts, in the following subsections the
results for each source will be discussed separately.

5.1 SLX 1735-269
5.1
SLX 1735-269
The results in Figure 14, show consistency with the theory. In the decay phases, with an
increased photon count, there is also an increase in the reduced chi-squared goodness. The
flux and temperature parameters demonstrate the expected patterns, peaking during the
peak of the light curve before reducing during the decay phases. However, large uncertainties
are seen in the fa parameter, especially in phases with fewer available photon counts.
The bolometric flux reaches, within error, the Eddington limit, as observed for this source
by MINBAR, but in the absence of further indication, there is no sign of PRE. Finally, the
calculated τ = 7.76 s indicates an abrupt burst. Unfortunately, without prior information
for the persistent duration, this result cannot be compared with α but given the very low
τ it can be an indication that the fuel is pure Helium.[12] The persistent emission flux
was measured at 8.07 × 10−10 ergs cm−2s−1 which is considerably higher from the reported
average of 3.1 × 10−10 ergs cm−2s−1 but still a slow accreting source.[31][2] The relative
literature for this source is limited, as there is a low number of observed bursts, of which
most are intermediate-duration or superbursts.[2][17] This is not the case here.
5.2
4U 1728-34
All seven bursts examined in this source exhibit the same patterns described in terms of
temperature and radius: Temperature between 1-3 keV, within uncertainty, and a sudden
increase in the radius at the last decay phase.
The assumption required for the calculation of α, was that all the bursts that occurred during
the observation duration were indeed recorded. This assumption is supported by the time
between two consecutive bursts which average at 4 hours for the first OBSID and 4 hours
and 50 minutes for the second OBSID respectively.
5.2.1
30401020002
Overall, there is no sign of a PRE as the Eddington limit is not reached, except for the
third burst, as can be seen in Figure 19, where there is some indication. The radius is also
increased during the peak indicating a PRE, nevertheless within a considerable uncertainty
level. However, it is possible that other bursts, in Figure 17, Figure 18 and Figure 20 might
have reached the Eddington limit but appeared normalized due to the combined time interval
of the peak and rising phase. This could potentially render any likely PRE undetectable.
The third burst shows the smallest τ, as can be seen in Table 2, while the rest are displaying
relatively higher values. This is believed to be because of the PRE of this burst. In accor-
dance with the literature, α > 60 for all three bursts with low τ, typical for this source,
indicating low hydrogen composition, possibly pure helium.[13][14][30]

5.3
4U 1636-536
In this observation set, the first burst possibly reached the Eddington limit within uncertainty
as seen in Figure 26. There is good indication of PRE, with the radius expanding during the
peak phase, while there is a drop in the temperature, leading to the characteristic double
peak.[29][6]
A second weak burst that can be seen in Figure 27, occurs only minutes after the initial
one, suggesting that there is unspent fuel that is being used in the subsequent burst.[29] Due
to the close proximity of these two bursts, the average time interval between consecutive
bursts throughout the entire observation period was calculated to be approximately 4 hours
and 30 minutes, as can be seen in Table 4. This average, however, does not account for
exceptionally closely-spaced bursts such as the pair in question. This observation supports
the assumption that this is not a normally occurring burst, but rather caused by incomplete
burning from the first burst.
In accordance with the literature, the characteristic timescale τ is typically larger for this
source when compared to 1728-34, with lower values of α as can be seen in Table 5, indicative
of a hydrogen-rich fuel. Notably, α experiences a significant reduction in the second burst,
further supporting that the fuel for this burst was largely provided by the previous one.[29]
In addition, the short recurrence time is also indicative of mixed H/He accretion.[14][9]
5.4
GRS 1741.9-2853
The first burst seen in Figure 29, does not reach the Eddington limit although there is an
increase in radius with a simultaneous reduction in temperature leading to the characteristic
double peak induced from a PRE.[29][6]
The second burst, in Figure 30, does, within flux uncertainty reach the Eddington limit. The
large errors in temperature and radius parameters, hinder the ability to definitively provide
an answer.
The values of τ for both bursts, as seen in Table 6, fall within the literature reported mean of
τ = 11±2 s.[13] With short τ alongside the extremely high α value observed, the composition
of the fuel is assumed pure helium.[14][24]

Conclusion
6
Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis has provided a comprehensive investigation of thermonuclear bursts
on neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binary systems.
The methodology employed in this
research allowed for the manipulation and analysis of data, as well as the extraction of
spectra, leading to the intended time-resolved spectroscopic analysis of the bursts with the
use of XSPEC and variable persistent emission approach.
By exploring twelve bursts from the sources SLX 1735-269, 4U 1636-536, 4U 1728-34, and
GRS 1741.9-2853, the study has contributed to the existing knowledge in the field. The
findings of this investigation have been compared with previous studies and theoretical pre-
dictions, with the implications and shortcomings being discussed accordingly.
The derived peak flux, temperature, and fa values were also largely in line with those previ-
ously reported in scientific literature as well as the resulting values of τ and α. Certain PRE
bursts were identified within uncertainty levels. However, the low amount of photon counts
limited the resolution of the spectroscopy and potentially rendered the identification of some
PRE bursts impossible. The combination of data from both instruments of NuSTAR would
inherently improve the photon count, and possibly double the resolution of the spectroscopy
revealing previously invisible PRE bursts.
In summary, the investigation although successful, was completed with certain shortcomings
in the implementation process that are documented for improvement in future work.
Page 50 of 63
==PAGE:58==
Future Work
7
Future Work
Transitioning to the section on future work, it becomes apparent that the completed research,
though successful, leaves room for improvement.
As already discussed, leveraging the capabilities of both instruments could effectively dou-
ble the number of time intervals implemented. This would provide a more comprehensive
understanding of the spectral evolution, enhancing the time resolution of the analysis. Such
an approach would enable the observation of minor fluctuations, as is required for a PRE,
that were potentially overlooked when relying on a single instrument. This would not only
support the investigation but also contribute to the robustness of the results.
Another way for expanding this study lies in integrating data from multiple instruments as
discussed in subsection 2.9. For instance, combining high-energy data from NuSTAR with
softer X-ray data from NICER or XMM-Newton may facilitate a better understanding of
the spectral changes during the burst. This would not only extend the energy range of the
investigation, but would also increase the number of available photons.
The use of the combinations mentioned above would significantly improve the time resolution
and robustness of this investigation.
