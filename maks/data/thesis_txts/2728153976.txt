Abstract
In a highly competitive sport such as flatwater sprint kayaking every slight improvement
counts. In this thesis, a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulation is set up to model
the flow around kayak rudders in conjunction with a moving hull. A scanned model of a
Nelo K1 Vanquish L1 is used in combination with a designed rudder, or one of its three
variations. The flow is modeled as a multi­phase flow, using the Volume­Of­Fluid (VOF)
method, and as a turbulent flow, using the k­ω Shear Stress Transport (SST) model. The
description of the motion of the kayak through calm water is based on experimental motion
data.
During the mesh convergence study, untypical low and diverging drag values were dis­
covered that were caused by numerical ventilation. This was prevented by improving the
mesh quality close to the hull surface. To compare with reference data, the simulations
were run steadily without a rudder. Good agreement was found with experimental data,
despite using different kayak models, in total hull drag at lower speeds but at a higher
speed of 5 m/s a larger deviation was found. The comparison to a reference simulation
using the same kayak model and size, showed large deviations in hull drag, which were
attributed to a significant difference in the determined draft.
In a fixed hull simulation, the rudder was evaluated to contribute 2.20 % to the total kayak
drag at a flow speed of 4.26 m/s. No separation was visible on the rudder in moving kayak
simulations. The mean lift­drag ratio of the rudder was found to be 8.99. A rudder with a
higher aspect ratio but the same length led to a slightly higher lift­drag ratio of 10.07 but
an overall smaller lift force. No significant difference was found between a rudder with
and without a sweep angle in lift and drag. A shorter rudder led to reduced lift and drag
but a similar lift­drag ratio. The developed simulation setup can be used in the future to
optimize the shape of the rudder.
Keywords: Kayak, Rudder, CFD, Drag

Introduction
In the Olympic Games 2021 in Tokyo, the difference between the silver and bronze medals
in the men’s 1000 m flatwater sprint single kayak final was 47 milliseconds. The gap to
the fourth place was 76 milliseconds [1]. These results show how minor the differences
between the kayakers can be in competitions and how a slight performance improvement
can hence decide medals.
In international competitions, kayaking is put into one category with canoeing. There are
different disciplines such as canoe sprint, canoe slalom, and canoe marathon. All these
disciplines are then subdivided into canoes and kayaks. In canoeing the athlete kneels
and uses a single­bladed paddle, whereas in kayaking the athlete is seated and uses a
double­bladed paddle to propel the kayak [2]. In flatwater sprint kayaking, the athletes
compete over 200 m, 500 m, and 1000 m. Furthermore, they compete either in boats of
single, double, or quadruplet athletes [2].
Figure 1.1: Danish kayaker Emma Aastrand Jørgensen in a K1 [3]
Not only does the performance of the athletes play a role in competitions, but consider­
ing the minor differences in race time, so does the equipment. The hull shape and the
paddle’s geometry are important when optimizing the boat’s performance. In both as­
pects, research has been conducted, both experimentally and numerically. Some studies
focused on the hull’s performance and the flow around it, whereas others aimed to inves­
tigate the interaction between kayak, paddle, and athlete.
Another component of the kayak that could be optimized is the rudder. The rudder is used
for steering and is essential for the boat’s stability as it counteracts the roll motion induced
by the athlete’s paddling. Team Danmark has noticed that other teams use different rud­
der designs based on race conditions. They also saw designs different than the ones
produced by the typical kayak manufacturers. So far, only one study has been published
on the kayak rudder and its influence on the flow and performance of the kayak. This study
from 2012, however, focused purely on the rudder and neglected interaction with the hull,
as it was deemed to be too computationally heavy [4]. As kayaking is a highly competitive
sport, little research is published and Team Danmark has yet to investigate the influence
of the rudder on the boat’s performance. These facts yield potential for research on the
influence of the kayak rudder on the overall performance.

Objectives
This thesis aims to gain an understanding of what the flow around a kayak rudder looks like
and what the magnitude of the forces acting on it is. Moreover, its purpose is to investigate
different rudder designs and give a first insight into what influence these design variations
might have.
A Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is set up to investigate the flow around the rud­
der. The increase in computational power in the last few years makes it possible to now
study the rudder in conjunction with the kayak hull. A single kayak, abbreviated as K1,
is studied to save computational power as it requires the least amount of cells due to its
short length. The final simulations, in which the different rudder designs are investigated,
are conducted with the kayak moving based on measurements obtained during a kayak
sprint. An optimized rudder design could possibly improve race times.
Thesis Outline
In the theory section, the thesis gives an overview of how a kayak is built up and what
rules are given by the International Canoe Federation (ICF) regarding its design. The
section also describes the flow around the kayak and the forces acting on the rudder
and hull. It then explains chosen concepts of CFD that are important for this multiphase,
turbulent flow problem. A literature review of theoretical, experimental, and numerical
kayak research is also presented.
In the next chapter, the preparations for the simulations are shown. This includes the CAD
model of the kayak, the design of the rudder and variations to it, and the post­processing
of experimental motion data. Thereafter, the simulation setup, including the mesh and
the physic models, is explained. This section also describes the occurrence of numerical
ventilation discovered in the mesh­generation process and how this undesired effect was
avoided. It is also explained how the initial position of the kayak was determined.
In the result section, fixed hull simulations and simulations with free heave and trim are
compared to reference data. It is furthermore investigated what difference allowing free
heave and trim makes regarding the total drag. A fixed hull simulation with the rudder
is used to show the influence the rudder has on the total kayak drag. The results from
performing a simulation with a moving kayak are shown and the results from using different
kayak rudders are presented.

The boat is propelled by the kayaker applying force against the water with their paddle
blade, and subsequently, the kayak moves forward [6]. The kayaker paddles alternately
on both sides of the kayak. One stroke is defined from the moment the paddle enters the
water on one side, also called catch, to the start of the catch on the other side. The stroke
includes a pull phase, where the paddle is submerged in water, and a recovery phase
when the paddle is out of the water and the kayaker prepares for the next stroke [6]. The
kayak is accelerated during the pull phase as the kayak is pulled under the paddler’s
body, and the force is greater than the drag force. During the recovery phase, the hull’s
drag slows down the kayak, and the kayak decelerates. The motion of a kayak is thus
time­dependent on its oscillatory motion of acceleration and deceleration.

Forces Acting on the Hull
Drag is the force acting on the kayak moving through the fluid. It can also be described
as the resistance of the hull. The goal is to minimize the resistance as it counteracts the
propulsive forces produced by the athlete. The hull producing the lowest drag for a kayak
with a given weight and length at a fixed speed is the optimal one [10].
The drag of the kayak can be divided into active drag when the kayaker is paddling and
passive drag when only the hull is moving. The latter would be the case in a towing tank or
a deceleration phase. The active drag is higher than the passive drag due to the variations
in velocity and the alternating paddling on the two sides, which causes yawing [11].

2.2.3
Turbulence Modeling
The flow around the kayak’s hull is turbulent, and therefore, a turbulence model is required
for the simulation. Different classes of turbulence models exist that can, among other
things, be distinguished by their computational effort. The most computationally heavy
one is the Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS), which resolves all turbulent length scales
and returns a time­dependent solution of the governing equations (Equations 2.10 and
2.11). A less computationally expensive model is the Large­Eddy Simulation (LES). In
this model, large­scale motions are resolved, and the effect of small­scale motions is
modeled [8].
The solver used in this thesis is a Reynolds­Averaged Navier­Stokes (RANS) solver.
RANS solvers are the least computationally heavy type of turbulence models, while still of­
fering good accuracy, and are therefore popular turbulence models in the CFD community
[8]. In this approach, the quantities, such as the flow velocity, are Reynolds decomposed,
i.e., divided into a mean and a fluctuating part. After that, the continuity equation and
the Navier­Stokes equations are time­averaged. The Reynolds decomposition and the
time­averaging lead to an additional stress tensor in the Navier­Stokes equations, the so­
called Reynolds stress tensor. This tensor is symmetric and thereby introduces six more
unknowns. This means ten unknowns are now present, while still, only four equations are
available. This is known as the closure problem [8].
There are different approaches on how to close the equations with different complexity.
One approach is using linear eddy viscosity models. These models rely on the Boussinesq
hypothesis, which assumes that the turbulent shear stress in the Reynolds stress tensor
depends linearly on the mean rate of strain and, thus, can be modeled similarly to molecu­
lar stress. This linear dependency is described with the eddy viscosity. The eddy viscosity
can be modeled using no, one, or two transport equations for the eddy viscosity. There
are algebraic or also called zero­equation models, such as Prandtl’s mixing length model,
one­equation models, such as the Spallart­Allmaras model, and two­equation models,

Below a y+­value of five lays the viscous sublayer. In this region, the relation between the

if it is above thirty, it lays in the logarithmic layer. In case the y+­value lays in the vis­

is needed to describe the wall­bounded flow. In case the y+­value lays in the logarithmic
layer, wall modeling can be applied [19].

2.2.5
Courant Number
It is important to consider the Courant number C regarding stability and the time step to
ensure convergence. The Courant number C is defined as the following [20]:
C = u · dt
dx
(2.28)
u is the magnitude of the velocity, dx a characteristic cell size and dt the time step. The
Courant number can be seen as a ratio between the time step and the characteristic con­
vection time, which is dx/u. This characteristic convection time describes the ”time re­
quired for a disturbance to be convected over a distance dx” [20]. In general, the Courant
number should be lower than one. In CFD, it can also be described as ”a fluid particle
should not move more than one grid length in a single time step” [20].
2.3
Literature Review
This project was the first project on kayak hydrodynamics by Team Danmark. Thus, it
was of interest to learn what kind of research had already been done on kayak hydro­
dynamics, especially regarding CFD simulations in the scientific community. Most likely,
more research has been conducted by the different national teams which has not been
published due to kayaking being a highly competitive sport. Experimental, theoretical,
and numerical research was taken into account for this literature review.
2.3.1
Theoretical and Experimental Research
The earliest study on what influences kayak hull performance was performed by Jackson
in 1995 [10]. Many authors mentioned in the following section (e.g., Prétot et al. [21])
refer to this seminal work. In this study, Jackson analyzed which factors influence the
speed of a kayak and how sensitive the speed is to small changes in these factors. This
was done after estimating the hull drag, i.e., friction, aerodynamic and wave drag, and
the propulsion produced by the paddle. The most influential parameters he found were
total weight, power, and skin friction drag. The weight affects the wetted surface area and
the wave drag. Reducing the wetted surface area or alternatively, the friction coefficient,
reduces the friction drag. Decreasing the friction coefficient is rather difficult because the
ICF prohibits applying substances to the hull [5]. Jackson assumed it would be unlikely
to reduce the wetted surface area more since a lot of hull optimization had already been
conducted [10]. He also concluded that the aerodynamic drag has little contribution to the
overall drag.
A literature review on the influences on kayak performance was published by Michael
et al. [6]. It describes more thoroughly the kinetics and kinematics of the kayak. The
forces acting on the different parts of the kayak are considered, as well as the boat’s
motions in the six degrees of freedom. The development of kayak equipment and design
is covered. The authors state, for example, that changes in paddle design and boat design
improvements in the late 1980s, led to faster race times. In 2000, beam width restrictions
from the ICF were withdrawn and led to innovations in boat design. Nowadays, kayaks
have a very narrow hull that decreases wetted surface area and frontal area.
A theoretical approach was combined with experimental measurements to determine the
active drag composition in a study by Prétot et al. [21]. Two athletes in two different boats
CFD Simulation of the Flow Around Sprint Kayak Rudders
11
==PAGE:24==
were measured and filmed in different situations; a standing start, pure deceleration, and
ten 50 m sprints. This data was then used to determine a model describing the velocity of
the kayak. The pure deceleration corresponds to a situation where only passive drag is
present, so they could show that drag increases when the kayaker paddles actively [21].
Several studies investigated passive drag, meaning the paddler’s movement is not con­
sidered. In a study by Gomes et al. [11], the passive drag was measured for different
kayak sizes and weights. This was achieved by towing a kayak at different constant ve­
locities, typical in international competitions, and measuring the drag using a load cell. It
was found that the weight specification given by the manufacturer for a kayak size did not
always lead to the lowest possible drag. The authors suggest that the race distance and
hence velocities must also be considered when choosing the kayak size.
The same research group investigated the effect of the wetted surface area on the passive
drag in the same experimental setup. The passive drag of the same boat was measured
with three different weights placed in it. As they initially had expected, they found that
the passive drag increased with a larger weight. At higher velocities, a larger increase in
passive drag was observed. It could furthermore be seen that the friction drag contributed
the largest to the total drag. The wave drag increased vastly with a larger wetted surface
area. It was concluded that the wetted surface area is important when trying to reduce
friction and wave drag [22].
The passive drag in calm water and low regular waves on a single kayak was reported
by Tzabiras et al. [23]. The drag was measured in a towing tank. The calm water results
were used to validate numerical models. From the low regular wave measurements, it
was concluded that at wavelengths larger than the boat, the added resistance to the hull
is negligible. In contrast, the resistance could increase by up to twelve percent for shorter
waves.
One study focused on the effect of wind on kayaking. The study by Barber [13] measured
the wind conditions on a race track and verified that it is valid to assume a logarithmic
wind velocity profile, which is typically used in the atmospheric boundary layer, within the
height of a kayaker. With that information, the aerodynamic drag arising from the local
atmospheric winds can be added to the drag arising from the forward motion of the athlete.
In the study, the drag coefficient of a sprint kayaker was determined using a 1­to­6 model
in a wind tunnel. It was concluded that the local winds have a larger effect on the race
finish time than the body­shaped aerodynamic drag [13].
2.3.2
CFD Research
Most research on kayak hydrodynamics using CFD simulations has been conducted with
constant inflow, multi­phase, and fixed hull simulations. In these simulations, a constant
inflow velocity is set, and the kayak cannot move freely in any direction. One study by
Mantha et al. [24] used this approach to investigate the development of a Nelo K1 Van­
quish kayak. Three models (LI, LII, LIII) were compared based on the viscous and pres­
sure drag. A drag reduction was found in both iterations. Barros [25] conducted a similar
study in which the LIII model of the Nelo K1 Vanquish and the follow­up model, the LIV,
were compared to the results from Mantha et al. [24], unpublished experimental results
from Gomes and experimental results from Tzabiras [23]. A significantly large deviation
was found at higher velocities which was assumed to be due to the fixed hull. In experi­
mental studies, the kayak can heave and pitch.
Two different solvers, a non­linear potential flow solver, and a RANS solver, were used by
Tzabiras et al. [23] for a constant inflow fixed hull simulation. The results of the two solvers
CFD Simulation of the Flow Around Sprint Kayak Rudders
12
==PAGE:25==
were compared to the towing tank tests conducted in the same paper. It was found that for
up to 3.5 m/s, the results from the potential flow solver are in good agreement. After that,
viscous forces were dominant, and it was concluded that the RANS solver should be used.
The authors deemed both solvers appropriate for calculating the calm water resistance of
a flat water racing kayak. Another study by Banks et al. [26] developed a model for the
paddle motion that was validated using experimental measurements. The paddle motion
was then included in the fixed hull simulation. A slight increase in resistance could be
found, and it was shown that the computational effort only increased by eight percent.
The authors state that as the hull is fixed, the full impact of the paddle motion has not
been captured in the study.
An unsteady moving­hull simulation was performed in a study by Leroyer et al. [27]. The
unsteadiness was implemented by using measurements of the motion of a kayak hull in
the six degrees of freedom that were then prescribed to the kayak hull. The study verified
that the forces obtained with this motion resulted in the same motion again when the
forces were applied to the hull.
Other previous studies have performed simulations where the biomechanics, i.e., the body
movement of the kayaker and the fluid mechanics, have been connected. A dynamic, cou­
pled biomechanical­Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics model was developed by Harrison
et al. [28]. The paddle, the hull, and the kayaker can be modeled. Within the study, the hy­
drodynamics of the naked hull were validated by comparing the total drag of the kayak hull
at different speeds to the experimental results mentioned in the previous section, namely
[26], [10], [23], and [29]. The results were found to be in a range of seven Newton, which
the authors argue to be acceptable as this is smaller than the large differences between
the experimental results. It is also stated that the results presumably vary due to factors
such as athlete mass, boat model, and the draft of the kayak. Besides validation and
verification, more advanced influences, such as the stroke rate and the left­right stroke
symmetry, were investigated in the study.
Another model, which takes the entire system of motion of the body, kayak, and paddle
into account, was developed by Nakashima et al. [30]. The model is based on a hu­
man swimming model that connects the hull, paddle, and athlete by virtual springs and
dampers. It can model the boat’s movement and the forces acting on it. Its accuracy was
determined by comparing the simulated forward speed to the measured forward speed
and was found to have room for further improvement. The model was later used to study
three modified paddling motions that were considered bad compared to the original motion
[31].
The previously mentioned studies did not focus on the kayak’s rudder; some even did not
include the rudder in their studies. One study by Willmann [4] investigated the lift and
drag of a kayak rudder. First, an analytical approach using the slender­body theory and
the lifting line theory was used. These results were later compared to the CFD simulation
of the rudder. The simulations took only the rudder into account, leaving out the hull
and its possible influence. The inflow velocity was fixed, and the rudder was positioned
at different angles of attack. It was found that the rudder lies in the critical transitional
regime, which makes it difficult to simulate as transition needs to be captured by the CFD
model [4].

3.2
Rudder Design
The CAD model of the kayak did not include a rudder; thus, it was necessary to design
a rudder that could also be varied. A kayak rudder can be defined by its width, i.e., the
airfoil’s chord length c, thickness t, depth d, length r, sweep angle θ, and shape. The
planform area Sp can also be considered, especially when one is interested in the aspect
ratio AR = r2/Sp. A sketch of a kayak rudder and its parameters is shown in Figure 3.2.
The chord length c and either the depth d or the length r can be varied freely, whereas
the thickness t depends on the airfoil choice. The thickness t is here considered to be the
maximum thickness of the airfoil at the top of the rudder. If the sweep angle is zero, the
length and depth of the rudder are the same. A kayak rudder often has a sweep angle as
it is of great importance to avoid getting seagrass stuck on the rudder in a competition;
this vastly slows down the kayaker.
The rudder is a hydrofoil, i.e., a lifting surface; therefore, an airfoil must be included in the
design. Usually, airfoils are chosen based on the highest lift­drag ratio and the angle of
attack. In the case of the kayak rudder, rather moderate angle­of­attacks are expected

Simulation Setup
The following chapter describes the setup of the CFD simulation. It contains the domain
size, the boundary conditions, and the mesh. The mesh section also describes how nu­
merical ventilation occurred in a preliminary mesh and what was done to prevent it from
happening. Furthermore, the chapter explains which models were used and which simpli­
fications were done. Lastly, a section describes how the initial position of the kayak was
determined.
4.1
Domain Size and Boundary Conditions
Two domains were needed for the simulations, a background domain and a kayak domain.
This was necessary because the movement of the kayak was modeled as a rigid­body
motion. The kayak domain was moving through the background domain in the unsteady
simulations.
4.1.1
Background Domain
The size of the background domain was based on the kayak length l. Inspiration for the
values of the length, width, and height of the domain was drawn from previously published
simulations such as [24], [25], and [27]. The length was defined such that one kayak length
was added upstream of the kayak and two kayak lengths downstream of the kayak. The
width was defined as one kayak length on each side of the hull as a kayak rolls, and waves
also develop at an angle behind the kayak. The height was chosen to be one kayak length,
where the kayak was positioned such that two­thirds of the domain is below the origin and
one­third above. In preliminary simulation runs, it was discovered that the domain size
changed the total drag of the hull, and thus, the width and depth were doubled. The length
was modified such that the kayak lengths upstream and downstream of the kayak were
doubled. The domain size given in Table 4.1 was used.

4.1.2
Kayak Domain
A second region close to the kayak was needed as the kayak was moving and the overset
technique was used for that reason. The overset technique allows a region with its own
mesh to move through the domain. The kayak region was created using the offset function
in StarCCM+. In this function, the original surface of the kayak is kept and offset, in this
case, by 0.05 m. The boundary on the offset was then defined as an interface boundary.

4.2
Mesh
Each of the two domains has a mesh that fits its purpose. The background domain cap­
tures the free surface and the waves, whereas the kayak domain captures the boundary
layer. In this section, the detailed setups are described including refinements in the back­
ground mesh and the prism layers in the overset mesh. It furthermore describes the mesh
convergence studies conducted on the rudder and the hull.
4.2.1
Background Mesh
A trimmed mesh was used for the background region because this aligned the cells with
the free surface. Several refinements in the form of volumetric controls were applied in the
background mesh. Three refinement levels were used for the free surface as is shown in
Figure 4.3. In these volumetric controls, the y­size of the cells was varied, i.e., the vertical
size was changed. The change in vertical cell size was done to capture the free surface
with a higher refinement.
A wake refinement was implemented to capture the waves behind the kayak. This refine­
ment was also divided into three refinement levels. The coarsest one spanned to the end
of the domain, whereas the second one spanned two kayak lengths downstream of the
kayak, and the finest one extended one kayak length downstream. The wake refinement
is shown in Figure 4.4.
One refinement was applied close to the kayak as shown in Figure 4.5. This rectangular
refinement had a size of 6 m in the flow direction (x­direction), 0.6 m in the vertical direc­
tion (y­direction), and 0.8 m in the z­direction. This volumetric control ensured a smooth
transition from the larger cells in the background to the overset mesh.

Overset Mesh
In the overset region, a polyhedral mesh with prism layers was used. Initially, the aim
was to set the number of prism layers, thickness, and growth ratio on the hull and the

y+­values around one would capture the wall shear stress with a higher accuracy.

100 on the submerged hull. More details on the occurrence of numerical ventilation are

Numerical Ventilation
Finally, it was found that air got dragged underneath the kayak in the simulation, which
then produced an air layer around the kayak. This effect is known as numerical ventilation
and is often observed when modeling planning boats with the VOF method [34]. In the
case of numerical ventilation, the free­surface interface is not captured sufficiently, and
particles get thereby trapped in the boundary layer and transported below the hull [34].
Figure 4.14: Volume fraction of water on the bottom of the kayak for the coarse mesh
mentioned in Table 4.5
The first prism layer contained a mixture of water and air on the bottom of the hull. Since
the prism layers were so thin compared to the rest of the mesh, the air had not been
noticed earlier. The numerical ventilation also explained why the drag values were so
much lower than those found in other papers.
Figure 4.15: Prism layers on a yz­plane (normal to the kayak surface) where numerical
ventilation, i.e., volume fraction of water lower than one, is observed (the brown area is
the submerged kayak hull)
Solution
When looking for solutions, it was decided that the numerical ventilation problem was
most likely due to poor mesh quality close to the hull. The transition from the fine prism
layer cells to the polyhedral cells was too abrupt, as the volume changed too rapidly.
This could among other things result in problems capturing the gradient. The fine layers
had been needed to obtain y+­values of around one, and it had only been possible to
use a limited amount of prism layers as the number of cells otherwise increased rapidly.

layers, ensuring a smoother transition. To save computational effort, it was decided to
instead aim for y+­values between 30 and 100, such that the high y+­treatment would
be used. Thus, the number of prism layers was decreased, and the total thickness of
the prism layers was increased to get higher y+­values. The final settings are shown in

4.3.1
Simplifications
A kayak is propelled by the propulsion forces created by the paddle’s motion through
the water. Thus, modeling the entire system would include modeling the interaction of
the paddle with the water. This would make the simulation complex and computation­
ally heavy. Furthermore, the rudder was the focus of this thesis. Therefore, instead of
computing the motion from the paddle motion, the experimental data described in Section
3.3 was used in the unsteady simulations. The motion was prescribed as field functions
to the overset region. The mean forward speed was set as a flat wave in the negative
x­direction in the VOF Wave model. From the ten­second period from the experimental
data, a mean forward speed of 4.26 m/s was determined. In the simulations, calm water
situations were assumed.
The total weight was needed in the Dynamic­Fluid­Body­Interaction (DFBI) simulations.
For that case, the weight of the kayak was assumed to be the minimum of 12 kg and the
kayaker was set to weigh 85 kg. Hence, the total weight was 97 kg. The weight of the
paddle was neglected in the simulation, as this would be minor compared to the weight
of the kayaker.

rudder is used to show the influence of the rudder on the total drag. Secondly, results
are presented from simulations with a moving kayak including a rudder. The influence
of side wind is investigated. Furthermore, the four different rudder designs are analyzed
and compared with each other.

The total drag values obtained with the fixed hull simulation vary greatly from the refer­
ence data for the Vanquish L1 kayak by Mantha et al. The discrepancy could be attributed
to a difference in the draft. Mantha et al. determined the draft to be 184 mm [24], whereas
a draft of 134 mm was determined in Section 4.4 of this thesis. Furthermore, the displace­
ment volume varies in the two simulations. In this thesis, the displacement volume was
determined to be 0.0976 m3. Mantha et al. found the submerged kayak hull volume to
be 0.152 m3 [24]. In both simulations, the athletes’ weight had been set to 85 kg and the
kayak’s weight to 12 kg. The different drafts and displacement volumes result in different
wetted surface areas. The wetted surface area in Mantha et al. was found to be 2.223
m2 [24] and 1.72 m2 in this thesis. An increased wetted surface area results in larger skin
friction drag and thereby total drag [22].
The results of the DFBI simulations are in the range of the experimental data. At 3 m/s
and 4 m/s the deviations from the drag values from Tzabiras et al. are within 5 %. The
values are also within 5 % of Gomes et al.’s data. At 5 m/s the drag value is 15 % lower
than the one found by Gomes et al.’s. Gomes et al. reported a wetted surface area
of 0.173 m3, which is similar to the wetted surface area in this thesis [22]. The data is
therefore assumed to be a good reference despite the difference in kayak model. The
wetted surface area influences the skin friction drag which was found to be responsible
for 60 % of the total drag by Gomes et al. [22]. The trend shown by the DFBI simulations
resembles the one found by Tzabiras et al. more closely than the one found by Gomes
et al.

5.2
Simulations with Motion
In the previous fixed hull simulations, the rudder only experienced flow at an angle of
attack of zero at a constant speed. In reality, the rudder experiences flow at different
angles of attack and different speeds. Therefore, the forces on the rudder and the flow
around it were studied by imposing the motion determined in Section 3.3 on the kayak.
The kayak thereby moved as a rigid body in all six degrees of freedom and since a function
had been fitted to the motion, it did so in a regular periodic motion.
In the simulations with motion, the rudder was placed in a fixed position on the hull. This
implies that the rudder did not change its position relative to the hull during the simulations
and therefore, no active steering was done to counteract any motions. The rudder acted
like a fin ensuring stability. The rudder ensures stability by producing lift which thereby
counteracts the roll induced by the athlete moving from side to side with the paddle.
In this section, the results from the simulation with the standard rudder are shown in detail.
Thereafter, it is investigated what influence crosswind might have on the forces acting on
the kayak. The results from the simulations with the different rudder designs are also
shown and compared to each other.

influence the forces on the rudder particularly. The heave and pitch are not expected to

5.2.3
Impact of Crosswind on Forces
Crosswind poses a challenge for the athlete as it causes an additional force acting on the
kayak. Depending on the rudder size, the crosswind forces the athlete to steer the rudder
to counteract the additional side force. By steering the angle of attack changes and the
lift increases as long as stall has not been reached yet. In this thesis, the rudder acts
like a fin and thus, no steering would be possible. A simulation was conducted including
crosswind to investigate whether the hull was affected by it and how it would be visible
in the results. The simulation was missing the athlete who would be responsible for the
largest side forces due to the aerodynamic drag. A crosswind of 4 m/s was imposed at
a 90­degree angle, i.e., coming directly from the side in the negative z­direction. The
crosswind combined with the forward motion results in the air coming diagonally on the
kayak.

Final Considerations on Comparing Different Rudder Designs
In this analysis of the different rudder designs, the focus was, due to time constraints, on
the lift and drag produced by the rudders. It could be shown that an increased aspect ratio
when keeping the same depth resulted in a higher lift­drag ratio. The overall lift, however,
decreased. The straight rudder was found to have a similar drag and lift behavior as the
standard rudder. It was, however, also shown that there are slight differences in the torque
around the z­axis between the two rudders. Given its reduced length, the short rudder
produced less lift and drag.
Considering that the lift force counteracts the roll induced by the athlete moving from side
to side, the question is how much lift force is actually required for ensuring stability. This
might also depend on the particular athlete and the wind and water current conditions.
Therefore, it can be said that this analysis has given a first insight into different rudder
designs but a more detailed analysis is required. It would be of interest to investigate the
forces and torques in combination with the respective motion to gain a better understand­
ing. This could be, for example, done by investigating the power needed to overcome the
forces and torques or by investigating the added mass coefficients.

Conclusion
This project was undertaken to gain a better understanding of the flow around sprint kayak
rudders, as well as the magnitude of the forces acting on a rudder. For this purpose, a
CFD simulation modeling the flow around a K1 hull in conjunction with a rudder was set
up. The final simulations were set up as unsteady simulations with the kayak moving
according to experimental motion data. The flow was modeled as a multi­phase flow
using the VOF method and as a turbulent flow using the k­ω SST turbulence model.
Before running simulations, a CAD model of a scanned Nelo K1 Vanquish L1 was obtained
from a research group in Portugal and a rudder model was designed based on a K2 rudder
used by Team Danmark. The rudder model design was varied three times in its length,
sweep angle, and aspect ratio, respectively, resulting in a total of four rudder designs.
Experimental motion data from a 500 m sprint was post­processed such that a smooth
description for all six degrees of motion was available for the simulations.
Setting up the simulations included creating a mesh and performing a convergence study
on the mesh to ensure that the error caused by the discretization was bounded. Numer­
ical ventilation occurred while performing a convergence study on the mesh around the
hull. Numerical ventilation meant that air was dragged underneath the kayak hull in the
simulation and thereby, the hull drag values did not converge. Convergence could be
obtained by increasing the prism layer size on the hull surface at the cost of having higher
y+­values on the hull surface. During the setup process, the initial draft of the kayak was
determined to be 134 mm.
After the setup, steady simulations were performed where the kayak was not moving.
The simulations were first conducted without a rudder to be able to compare the resulting
drag values to available numerical and experimental reference data from different kayak
models. The rudderless, steady simulations were once run with the hull position fixed and
once with allowing free heave and free trim. Allowing free heave and free trim increased
the total hull drag by up to ten percent. These results also matched the experimental
data more closely. At flow speeds of 4 m/s and 5 m/s, the drag results were within a
five percent range of the experimental data. At 5 m/s, the deviation was larger at around
15 %. The results from the fixed hull simulation varied largely from numerical reference
data obtained with the same kayak model in a fixed hull simulation. This was attributed to
the fact that the simulations used different draft values, which resulted in different wetted
surface areas, and thus in different drag.
Steady flow simulations with the rudder were used to give an estimate of the rudder’s
contribution to the total kayak drag. It could be shown that the rudder is responsible
for 2.20 % of the total kayak drag. This is only a minor contribution but considering the
small differences in race time it might still be of importance to consider the rudder when
optimizing the kayak.
The final simulations were run with the kayak moving according to the experimental motion
data for all four rudder designs. It was seen that no separation occurs on the rudder. The
rudder designs showed similar lift­drag ratios ranging between 8.66 and 10.07. Increasing
the aspect ratio with an otherwise equivalent depth and length as the standard rudder
design, led to the highest lift­drag ratio while having an overall smaller lift than the standard
rudder. The rudder without the sweep angle showed similar results as the standard rudder,
CFD Simulation of the Flow Around Sprint Kayak Rudders
54
==PAGE:67==
with slightly increased pressure drag values. The short rudder led to the lowest lift­drag
ratio of 8.66 and to the least drag and lift due to its reduced depth and length.
This thesis is limited by the quality of the mesh. High wall­y+ treatment is used for the
flow around the hull, which limits its accuracy, as the flow is governed by skin friction drag.
Moreover, the prism layers at the trailing edge of the rudder were collapsing and thus, a
lower resolution is present in this region. Another limitation is the neglect of aerodynamic
forces and the body of the athlete. Although they are minor compared to the hydrodynamic
effects according to Jackson [10], they might especially be important when crosswinds are
present such as was tested in this thesis.
Future work could include improving the mesh quality on the rudder and refining the mesh
on the hull to better capture the skin friction drag. A more extensive mesh convergence
study on the kayak in motion could also improve the accuracy of the results. It is also
highly recommended to analyze the influence of the different rudders in more detail by,
for example, investigating the work required to overcome the torques and forces or by
investigating added mass coefficients. Further research might explore the influence of
adding a person to the simulations and thereby investigating aerodynamic forces. A nat­
ural progression of this thesis is to optimize a kayak rudder using an optimization algorithm
in combination with CFD such as, for example, has been done for surfboard fins [35].
