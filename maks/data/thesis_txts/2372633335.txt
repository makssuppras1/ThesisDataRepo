Abstract
Rostering of personnel is a hard and time-consuming problem solved manually most
places in the world. The study of rostering problems have however increased drasti-
cally the last 40 years making it possible to classify the rostering problems and build
mathematical models to solve them and automate the procedure. This thesis concerns
a hospital speciﬁc rostering problem, called the Nurse Rostering Problem. The aim is
to build a mathematical model that can solve the Nurse Rostering Problem for a small
unit at Rigshospitalet with the goal of ﬁnding attractive and fair rosters for the nurses.
In this thesis two models are constructed, a Mixed Integer Programming model and a
Column Generation model. The result is that the Mixed Integer Progamming model
ﬁnds the best solutions for the small unit at Rigshospitalet, while the Column Gen-
eration model ﬁnds operable solutions in a short time. Evaluating a roster plan from
Rigshospitalet, we ﬁnd that the Mixed Integer Programming model ﬁnd signiﬁcantly
better solutions.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Rostering or scheduling of personnel is a common problem that arises in a variety of
working industries such as the airline industry, transportation companies and hospitals.
The problem is how to assign personnel to diﬀerent shifts or tasks that satisﬁes the
regulations and the requirements in the industry.
This project concerns the rostering of nurses at the Intensive Care Unit of Liver
Medicine at Rigshospitalet (ICULMR), a hospital based in Copenhagen, Denmark.
The Nurse Rostering Problem (NRP) is a very well investigated problem as it has been
studied since before 1969. The complexity of the problem is NP hard, nevertheless,
it is solved and applied diﬀerent places in the world to optimize and automate the
scheduling practice. Belgian hospitals have implemented solutions to NRP with great
success [1]. Utilizing Operational Research techniques is a common approach to solve
the NRP which we will also deploy in this project.
There are three main stakeholders to consider when a nurse roster is generated at a
hospital: The patients, the nurses and the hospital itself. The patients usually require
that the number of nurses at work and the level of competence of the nurses should
meet a certain standard. The nurses need fair and reasonable working conditions in
order to perform at job and to live a regular life next to their job. The hospital wants to
minimize operational costs and ensure quality of operations.
During the last couple of years, a key problem for ICULMR has been to satisfy the
nurses as stakeholders. The reason for this is primarily because of unsatisfying work
conditions due to a high amount of hard shifts (evening and night shifts) combined
with a low number of personnel which results in little ﬂexibility. The results is, unfor-
tunately, that many experienced nurses with a high level of competence have left their
jobs at Rigshospitalet. Therefore, this project prioritises the nurses as stakeholders and
will not as many other projects minimize operational costs or maximize proﬁt; instead
it will maximize the nurse satisfaction. The two other stakeholder, the patients and
the hospital, will still have an important role in this project. However, it will be a less
important role and the nurses will be the main concern.
At the moment, the head nurse is manually creating the rosters in an iterative pro-
cess, where she starts out satisfying the weekend shifts, then the night shifts followed
by the evening shifts and ﬁnally the day shifts. This process takes several days of work
6
==PAGE:7==
every month and many solutions are obviously overlooked as the head nurse has lim-
ited time to operate in. As an alternative to this manual process, we want to utilize
Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) to look at all possible allowed rosters and choose
the best solution of those that satisfy the speciﬁc rules and demands of the hospital.
My personal motivation for choosing this project is ﬁrst of all the enjoyment and
challenge of building a comprehensive and complex MIP model which is required for
the NRP. Furthermore, I want to help improve the working conditions for the nurses
who have a tough job with long and shifting hours. The practicality of the project have
been a great motivation from the beginning, knowing that my contribution potentially
could have a positive impact on peoples life.

Chapter 2
Problem Description
The scope of this project is to establish a well balanced nurse roster for the Intensive
Care Unit of Liver Medicine at Rigshospitalet. The rosters will include all part time and
full time personnel (28 to 37 hours/week), but only those who have had an introductory
course at the hospital will count as staﬀ-covering-personnel.
We will consider the planning phase of a scheduled period and, furthermore, neglect
sick-days, maternity-leave, public holidays and regular holidays in order to simplify the
problem. The schedule of a single nurse consisting of diﬀerent shifts will be denoted
as a roster or as an individual roster. A feasible solution to our problem will then be a
collection of individual rosters, which we will call a roster plan, that satisﬁes the hard
constraints in Section 2.2 in form of collective agreements, work environment regula-
tions, etc. A good solution will contain attractive work patterns and a fair distribution
of unattractive shifts as explained under soft constraints in Section 2.3. As the amount
of data from the hospital is very small, it will be displayed during Section 2.2.
2.1
Shift Types
The ICULMR generally works with four shift types: Day, evening, night and oﬀshift.
The ﬁrst three types are work-shifts which are between 8 to 8.25 hours. However, to
simplify the problem, we assume a length of 8 hours. The deﬁnition in Danish and

2.2
Hard Constraints
There are four main areas that has to be considered when the head nurse manually plans
the rosters:
1. The collective agreement between Rigshospitalet and DSR1 [2] [3]
2. Work environment regulations
3. Local arrangements at Rigshospitalet
4. Individual nurse agreements
2.2.1
Collective Agreement
Three rules are important from the collective agreement: Weekly oﬀperiods are re-
quired, the eleven-hour rule and a rule about weekly reduced rest time between shifts.
Weekly oﬀperiods can consist of either one long oﬀperiod of more than 55 hours
or two short oﬀperiods of more than 35 hours each. The purpose of this rule is to make
sure that the nurses gets enough weekly rest to keep performing at job. See Figure 2.1
for an illustration of the impact of the rule.
Figure 2.1: The top roster is not allowed since the oﬀstretch is only 48 hours. The
bottom roster has an oﬀstretch of 56 hours which corresponds to a long oﬀperiod.
The eleven-hour rule states that every nurse must have a oﬀperiod of eleven hours
between each work shift. This makes it impossible to have consecutive work shifts and
impossible to have the following work shift combination: Work shift - break - work
shift, where the nurse has 8 hours of rest in between the work shifts.
The reduced rest time rule is made to give the planner more ﬂexibility when making
the roster plan. It states that a nurse once a week can have reduced rest time either
between two close shifts from eleven to eight hours or between two work shift periods
such that the oﬀperiod is reduced from minimum 35 to 32 hours. There are four cases
for utilizing reduced rest time, these are given in Table 2.2.

2.2.2
Work Environment
For the importance of a healthy work environment, two rules are established. The ﬁrst
constraint is a maximum of two types of work shifts per week. This creates more sta-
bility and structure for the nurses, which help to perform at job. The second constraint
is a maximum of six consecutive work days because rest is important for the nurses
and the quality at the hospital.
2.2.3
Local Arrangement
The local arrangement at Rigshospitalet consists of ﬁve elements which we will go
through one by one: Minimum staﬀcoverage, minimum competence level, minimum
and maximum work shifts allowed, oﬀweekend and ﬁxed weekend patterns.

quality in the unit, it is important never to have a shortage of nurses on work. Further-

rush hour of the institution, meaning that many nurses are needed, and at the same time
it is the most attractive shift. The minimum amount of nurses or exact demands for

The number of hours that each nurse works should be as close to his or her norm time
as possible over a scheduling period (e.g. 4 weeks) in order to avoid loss of operational
costs. As we will operate in work shifts rather than hours, we can sometimes encounter
fractional values (e.g. 37 hours/week in 4 weeks gives 148 hours corresponding to 18.5
shifts). Thus, we allow for a diﬀerence in work shifts and the corresponding shifts of
the norm time of plus or minus 1 during the scheduling period.
Based on continuous requests from the nurses, the head nurse now keeps every
second weekend as an oﬀperiod, such that a nurse only is allowed to work in even or
odd weekends. The request is based on the hard time the nurses have planning their
life with no systematic weekend shifts, especially for divorced parents.
The rule of ﬁxed weekend patterns means that if you start destroying your weekend
with a hard shift Friday, you might as well work the whole weekend. On the other
hand, if you are oﬀFriday, you should be oﬀthat weekend as well.
2.2.4
Individual Agreements
At the moment, the most experienced nurse in the department has her own ’home made’
roster that she insists to follow. Two other nurses are seniors with certain beneﬁts,
preventing them from having night shifts. As these are very few individual agreements,
we will not infer them into the model, but instead test them in the test section to see if
their individual arrangements have an impact on the quality of the rosters.

2.3
Soft Constraints
After ensuring that the hard constraints are satisﬁed, we can add the knowledge about
satisfactory work patterns, which the head nurse also wants to implement in order to
establish good working conditions for the nurses. The soft constraints can generally be
put into two categories:
1. Attractive work patterns
2. Fair distribution of hard shifts
2.3.1
Work Patterns
Instead of rewarding attractive work patterns, we will mostly penalize unattractive work
patterns. The unattractive work patterns includes few single-night shifts and few four-
night shifts. It is tough to convert your circadian rhythm to staying awake at night, thus
when you do so, you want to have some continuity. Having many night shifts in a row
is also tough for your body, which explains the four-night shift penalty. Lastly, it is
much appreciated if one can get two days oﬀafter night shifts: One for sleeping and
one to recover.
A pattern that is rewarded is consecutive days with the same type of shift (including
consecutive days oﬀ) as the nurses get into a daily rhythm. Consecutive work days are
great, but too many work days aﬀects your energy level, which is why we want work
stretches below 5 days. Thus, ﬁve consecutive work days are punished.
2.3.2
Distribution of hard shifts
Finally, an important element for the complete roster plan is the fairness of the distri-
bution of hard shifts. Hard shifts are evening, night and weekend shifts (excluding D
shifts on Fridays) and are the most unpopular shifts. The attitude on Rigshospitalet is
that everyone should participate equally on the weekend shifts whereas evening and
night shifts should be split according to the norm time of each nurse.
To summarize, the problem we will solve consists of some hard constraints that has
to be kept and some soft constraints which will penalize a solution when they are not
met. We will go into more details with the constraints in the MIP model chapter, but
before that we want to establish an overview of how the Nurse Rostering Problems are
solved today.

Chapter 3
Literature and Modelling
Techniques in the NRP
In this section we will try to give a brief picture of how comprehensive the study of
NRP is and show some of the main modelling techniques and methods used in solving
the problem. To do this, we make use of a Survey about the NRP written by Cheang
et al in 2003 [4]. This survey has gathered information from more than 85 papers
from recent time that studies the NRP. Two other great articles collecting and bringing
overview of passed literature and summing the latest state of the art techniques are
Ernst et al 2004 [5] and Burke et al 2004 [6]. All these surveys published within a year
from each other are in itself a statement of the need to categorize and bring overview of
the many studies of the NRP, and with 193 references in [5] and more than 100 in [6]
it has not been an easy task to get that overview.

3.3
Objective Functions
In the NRP the objective function is often a minimization of operational costs or a
maximization of nurse satisfaction or a combination hereof. In [10] Bjørk primarily
minimizes operational costs and in our project we maximize nurse satisfaction, which
makes both of these objective functions rather simple. Other papers have more complex
objective functions as in [11] where the cost of schedules are minimized, but with
penalties for violating shift balances.
3.4
Methods
Three main methods are used to solve the NRP: Mathematical Programming (MP),
Heuristics and Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI). MP is an exact approach and is used to solve
optimization problems, although they can also be used in combination with heuristics.
Sometimes the problems can have too many constraints to allow for an MP approach
and instead Heuristics and AI methods are used. In this section we will give a few
examples on how MP, heuristics and AI methods are used in to solve the NRP in recent
papers.
Miller et al. [12] used an MP model with an objective function that balances staﬃng
demands and nurse preferences subject to some hard constraints on the nurse schedules.
Violation of the soft constraints incur a penalty cost in the objective function.
A two-phased Column Generation Model, consisting of a master problem solved
by MP and a subproblem solved by heuristics was used by Balakrishnan and Wong in
1991 [13] to solve the NRP. They use a network model, where they deﬁne a non-cyclic
graph with work shifts or days oﬀas nodes and feasible paths as edges with respect to
all the incorporated constraints except the staﬃng coverage constraint that stays in the
master problem. They use LP dual values from the solution to the master problem to
ﬁnd the k-best paths through the network.
Within the category of heuristics we ﬁnd metaheuristics which are more generic
and less problem speciﬁc heuristics that can be used as black box tool for various
15
==PAGE:16==
problemns. As for heuristics, metaheuristics does not guarantee optimality of the solu-
tion, but can often be used to ﬁnd good solutions. Metaheuristics such as Tabu Seach
(TS), Genetic Algorithm (GA) and Simulated Annealing (SA) have been increasingly
used during the last 20 years for many NP hard problems, including the NRP. Burke
et al. [14] used a hybrid of a TS approach and manual scheduling techniques for a
commercial nurse rostering system in 1998. Furthermore, Burke et al. [15] developed
a memetic algorithm in 2001 incorporating TS into a GA by the use of steepest de-
scent on each nurse. The latter algorithm produced better results than that of [14] with
respect to the NRP.
AI techniques have also been applied to solve NRPs. Chun et al. [16] used AI for a
hospital in Hong Kong in the year of 2000 solving the NRP by combining look-aheads
and intelligent scoring to determine which nurse to be scheduled next and what shift is
preferably compared to the soft constraints.
In our project we will start out using an MP approach to the problem. Later we will
decompose the problem into two smaller problems, solving one part with an MP based
approach and the other part with a problem speciﬁc heuristic.

4.6
Challenges in the MIP model
One of the unique things for our speciﬁc NRP is that we want to maximize nurse
satisfaction. This is truly not an easy task to do, as there are many things to consider
in order to end up with a roster plan that is attractive for the nurses. Computationally
it is even worse, because of the many diﬀerent counting variables needed, which leads
to high amounts of variables and constraints.
In the beginning, the goal was to get as close to a 14 weeks scheduling period as
possible, since that is the norm scheduling period at Rigshospitalet, i.e. the period of
which the norm time for each nurse should be met. However, it was quickly found that
this period was too long to generate solutions in a reasonable time frame. Eight weeks
was pursued next and this period was used in most of the process building the MIP
model. Having the head nurse investigating our MIP solutions increasingly helped
us ﬁnd new things to improve, but improvement comes at a cost of more counting
variables, which in turn increased the run time to more than 24 hours to ﬁnd solutions
that was even far from optimal. This is the main reason, we started working with a
scheduling period of 4 weeks instead, as we could ﬁnd solutions within a reasonable
time frame.
A major break through happened after a few months, where we came up with the
constraint (4.31) that use the precalculated number ϵi of fair evening shifts based on the
norm time of nurse i and penalize only if the nurse gets above this limit. Before this
constraint was a penalty on the diﬀerence between the highest amount of evening shifts
compared to that of the lowest. This also worked, but was a lot more computationally
demanding as the constraint went across all rosters instead of along each roster. This
new idea was also used for night shifts and weekend shifts.
At this point, the run time was about 4 hours to ﬁnd reasonable good solutions
qualitatively measured. Our main concern, however, was that even a small increase in
nurses theoretically would create a huge increase in the solution time, i.e. the current
model is not scalable.
This lead us to search for alternative solution approaches to speed up the process.
We implemented a ﬁx and dive strategy ﬁxing the variables vi jk to 0 or 1 if they were
close to either one in a LP relaxation of the MIP model. Many trials were made; the
ﬁrst by ﬁxing the N closest variables to 0 or 1 and the last by ﬁxing just one variable
per iteration, but all without luck. The model had big problems with the ﬁxed weekend
patterns which eventually lead to infeasible solutions, but also assigning night shifts
was very rare, probably because of the single night shift counter.
It seemed obvious that our next step should be to create a scalable model with po-
tential to ﬁnd solutions fast. Inspired by former NRP projects described under Methods
in section 3, we found out that an interesting method with these characteristics is the
Column Generation model, which we will investigate in the next chapter.

Chapter 5
Column Generation
As an alternative to the large and complex MIP Model we developed in the last chapter,
we can address the problem with a method called Column Generation (CG).
5.1
The concept of CG
Column generation is often used when a Mathematical Programming model becomes
too comprehensive in size, making solutions hard to ﬁnd within reasonable time. The
reason for this, is that CG utilizes the idea of decomposing the original problem into
two smaller optimization problems that can be solved independently. These optimiza-
tion problems are deﬁned with diﬀerent names throughout the literature. We will be
using the naming convention from Anders Dohn’s Ph.d. in Rostering and Task Schedul-
ing [17]. The ﬁrst optimization problem is called the master problem and the second
is called the subproblem also known as the pricing problem. Before diving into the
two problems, we need to deﬁne the meaning of a column. A column corresponds to
a work-line, roster-line, network path or similar depending on the exact setting. This
means that we can think of a column as a work schedule for a single worker.
The master problem is formulated as a MIP Model constituting of only a subset
of the constraints from the original MIP model from the last chapter, meanwhile the
rest of the constraints is put into the subproblem. The decision variables of the master
problem (often denoted λ) decides which and the amount of each column that we bring
into the solution. This means that the purpose of the master problem is to choose the
best columns of the columns available in the problem.
However, we cannot generate all possible columns, since it would be too time con-
suming. Instead we want to ﬁnd the columns that can improve the solution most. The
idea here is to utilize dual information from the solution to the Linear Programming
(LP) relaxation of the master problem to ﬁnd columns with negative reduced cost, i.e.
columns that can improve the solution in the master problem. The negative reduced
cost has the general form C −π < 0, where C is the total cost and π is the sum of dual
values. This leads us to the subproblem.
The subproblem is an optimization problem with the goal of ﬁnding the column(s)
30
==PAGE:31==
Figure 5.1: Visualization of the process of Column Generation as used in this project.
with most negative reduced cost. The subproblem is formulated as an exact shortest
path problem, but can also be solved via heuristics to ﬁnd the N best paths in a problem
speciﬁc graph. The remaining constraints not put into the master problem are placed
in the subproblem, and are used to setup the graph. This ensures that the columns or
paths through the graph are feasible columns for the master problem.
Having decomposed the original MIP model into two smaller problems, we can
start the iterations back and fourth between them. First the LP relaxation of the master
problem is solved with an initial feasible solution. Next, we use the dual information
from the solution to ﬁnd columns in the subproblem that can improve the objective
function in the master problem. When there are no more columns with negative reduced
cost, the process terminates and we have a ﬁnal LP solution.
The last step is to enforce integrality of the LP solution, since we cannot have frac-
tional columns. This can be done by combining the column generation procedure with
a branch-and-bound algorithm yielding what is called a branch-and-prize framework.
Theoretically the optimal solution can be found via this approach. However, in many
cases a good integer solution close to the optimal solution exists already by solving the
master problem with the added columns as a MIP. A visualization of the process of
Column Generation and enforcing integrality by solving the original MIP is given in
Figure 5.1.

Subproblem
For our problem, the subproblem is a resource-constrained shortest path problem of a
directed graph. The nodes in the graph are shift types and the edges between two nodes
exists only if is feasible to have the second shift directly after the ﬁrst shift given all the
former shifts on the path and given the constraints transferred to the subproblem. As
nodes and shifts are inherently the same in the subproblem, we will use both names,
although nodes will be used preferable as the subproblem is in a graph setting. A path
through the graph corresponds to an individual roster or a column.

constraint, which was put into the master problem. However, two of the constraints are
simpliﬁed: The rule of reduced rest time will be neglected, since it is rarely used in the
MIP model and it is complicated to implement, and the rule of oﬀperiods (one long
or two short) is changed to two days oﬀwork each week instead, since the rule of oﬀ
periods is too hard to implement.

5.4.4
Heuristic choices
The idea of the heuristic is to avoid searching the part of the graph where the nodes
already are included in many rosters or where the nodes are very costly to bring into
the partial rosters, such that the roster becomes too costly to bring into the solution.
The heuristic choices consists of two parts. Adding only few good nodes per iteration
and controlling the depth of which we investigate the graph with some terminating
parameters. The choice of good nodes should is based on the dual values from the
master problem and the cost for the feasible nodes. During testruns in the beginning,
we quickly found out that 3-7 nodes were too many to add in each iteration as the
subproblem ran for hours without adding any columns. Thus, we choose to add two
good nodes in each iteration in the partial roster. Again, based on testruns, we choose
the ﬁrst node to be based on lowest negative reduced cost and the second to be based
on lowest cost. We note that in theory the best result should come from choosing the
two lowest negative reduced cost nodes, however, choosing one node based solely on
cost has proven to be more eﬀective in our project.
The second part of the heuristic is the parameters controlling the depth of which we
investigate the graph. This is the parameters maxRosterCost, maxNodesSearched and
minLimitReducedCost. The maxRosterCost is the maximal cost that a partial roster

==PAGE:41==
can get before we stop iterating the path of the partial roster. The reason for using

is the maximum number of nodes we search in the graph before we stop all node
iterations. The minLimitReducedCost is the minimum limit as a percentage of the
scheduling period, where the partial rosters need to have a negative reduced cost. If
it is not the case when the partial roster reaches the limit, we stop the iteration of the

Recursive Algorithm
The recursive algorithm or the recursive roster generator is a depth ﬁrst algorithm that
iterates through the graph using partial roster objects and incrementing them with nodes
based on the heuristic choices explained in Section 5.4.4. Only nodes that are returned
by the feasible nodes function can be used for incrementing. After ﬁnding the two best
nodes, the algorithm copies the partial roster object and increments the objects before
it re-runs the recursive algorithm on the partial roster objects.
The algorithm continuous the iteration of the partial rosters until either one of the
heuristic depth parameters stops it or if the variable current day reaches the scheduling
period. In the latter case, the partial roster is stored into a list we call rosterCollection.
If the size of rosterCollection reaches the heuristic parameter maxRosters or if the num-
ber of nodes searched reaches maxNodesSearched, we terminate the algorithm. Below
the algorithm is described in a algorithmic manor. The dual values α jk is assumed
known for each iteration. The partial roster object is indicated with square brackets to
reduce confusion between objects, functions and variables.

5.5
Column Generation Algorithm
This section is about how we put it all together and iterate between the master problem
and the subproblem in the column generation algorithm.
As mentioned in Section 5.3.6 the highest value in βt indicates what nurse type
that is most likely to improve the LP solution as explained in Section 5.3.6. Thus, we
choose the nurse type t with the highest βt value in the next subproblem iteration. We
run the recursive roster generator algorithm two times for the chosen nurse type; one
for the graph of even weekends oﬀand one for odd weekends oﬀ. Afterwards we add
the rosters to the master problem.
Furthermore, we have some parameters to control the runtime of the algorithm:
– maxRostersPrIter
– maxIt
– maxTime
– minGain
– minLimitReducedCost.

The purpose of minLimitReducedCost is already explained in the recursive algorithm
section, but in this algorithm we control the increase of the limit starting at a low per-
centage and ending at 100 percent. The maxRostersPrIter is the maximum number of
rosters that we add to the set t for each run of the recursive algorithm. The param-
eters maxIt and the maxTime are stopping parameters, stopping the algorithm if the
number of iterations reaches maxIt and the run time reaches maxTime. The last param-
eter, minGainLP, is a minimum gain in the solution to the LP relaxation of the master
problem. If the gain does not reach minGainLP, the last nurse type t is put into the
list lowGain, where it will stay until another gain exceeds minGainLP. If this does not
happen for all the nurse types, we extend minLimitReducedCost by 20 percent untill it
reaches 100 percent. If lowGain gets full again, we pass the 100 percent limit and stop
the algorithm, as there will be no further gain solving the subproblems.
Having the ﬁnal solution to the LP relaxation of the master problem, we solve the
original MIP of the master problem hoping for a good solution without any pseudo
shifts (high cost shifts from the initial solution). The column generation algorithm is
given in an algorithmic form below.

5.6
Challenges in Column Generation
First of all, we highlight the amount of work it takes to establish a column generation
model. Everything has to be created from the buttom compared to the original MIP
model, where we added small pieces to the model as we developed it. Especially the
subproblem was challenging as it consisted of a lot of coding and testing. The master
problem was not hard to setup as it was just a slightly changed, smaller version of the
original MIP model.
It did not take long before we found out that it was impossible in a realistic time
setting to iterate through all of the graphs; even for a scheduling period of two weeks.
This fact was based on both theory with 7 shifts to choose over 14 days corresponding
to 714 = 6.78 · 1011 nodes to search and practise where the recursive algorithm ran for
hours without returning any rosters. The solution was to use a heuristic, which made
it possible to solve the problem for a scheduling period of two weeks, meanwhile 4

weeks still was too hard a problem.
The part of the heuristic where we choose the best nodes was also problematic
for us. The best nodes should be the nodes with most negative reduced cost, but the
algorithm gave poor results choosing nodes only based negative reduced cost. It was
only when we combined the most negative reduced cost node with the cheapest cost
node that we got reasonable good results.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In this project, we have speciﬁed and solved the Nurse Rostering Problem with two
diﬀerent mathematical models for a small unit of 14 nurses at Rigshospitalet. Both
the Mixed Integer Programming model and the Column Generation model found good
and operable solutions to the problem. The MIP model found impressive solutions for
a scheduling period of 4 weeks within 5 hours. The MIP solutions were praised by
the head nurse and signiﬁcantly superior to the evaluated roster plan from Rigshospi-
talet. The CG model found operable solutions very quickly for a scheduling period
of 2 weeks. A comparison between the two models showed that the MIP model were
superior for a low number of nurses, thus, matching the unit at Rigshospitalet. Mean-
while, the CG model were able to ﬁnd operable solutions for a higher number of nurses
without increasing the runtime.
To summarize, the MIP model has been the most successful model in the project,
and the head nurse at Rigshospitalet has said that she is interested in having the model
implemented. The CG model was found to be scalable as predicted and could poten-
tially be used for larger hospital units.
7.1
Future work
As of now, we do are not ensured that the cost of individual rosters is distributed fairly
across the rosters, meaning that we potentially could end up a single roster with a
signiﬁcant higher cost than the rest. If this turned out to be a problem for some nurse
setting, we have a two suggestions to solutions. One solution would be to implement a
variable for the diﬀerence between the highest cost roster and lowest. This was already
pursued in this project, but was removed as the runtime to ﬁnd solutions increased
by many hours. The reason for this is probably because this type of constraint goes
across all the individual rosters. Another way of approaching the problem would be to
implement variables that went along the individual rosters penalizing if the roster cost
has passed a predeﬁned roster threshold. This approach is very similar to the extra night
shifts for each nurse, which turned out to work great. The problem is how to decide
the value of the roster threshold , but given the model run on a ﬁxed scheduling period
65
==PAGE:66==
with ﬁxed objective function coeﬃcients for some time, the head nurse will probably
get an idea of the value of the roster threshold for her unit.
