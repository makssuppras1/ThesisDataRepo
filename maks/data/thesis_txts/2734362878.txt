Abstract
Hempel is currently undertaking efforts to decrease its GHG­emissions, and a significant
focus lies in the decarbonization of its car fleet. This area is of utmost importance since
car emissions account for 40% of Hempel’s scope 1 emissions. However, addressing
this matter proves to be quite complex and extensive. As a global company operating
in numerous countries, Hempel faces the challenge of navigating diverse energy sys­
tems, varying electric car accessibility, and distinct cultural considerations in each region.
The project will be structured into four primary sections and ,initially, will focus on just
some regions where Hempel operates. Firstly, there will be the technical section, which
will involve conducting an LCA study (with a focus on the use phase). This study aims to
analyze various types of vehicles, examining their energy usage and CO2 emissions. Fol­
lowing the technical section, the project will move on to the economical section. Here, an
analysis of investment costs associated with CO2 savings will take place, with a specific
focus on the expenses involved in transitioning from existing leases to more environmen­
tally sustainable vehicles. After the economical section, the project will investigate the
psychological section. This phase aims to explore cultural aspects and examine poten­
tial barriers arising from cultural perceptions. It will use LCA as a scientific foundation
to facilitate a shift in perception, leveraging its findings to address and overcome these
cultural obstacles. In conclusion, the project will culminate in the ”Conclusions and Rec­
ommendations” section. This phase will involve researching opportunities to minimize
environmental impact further. Additionally, it will explore the implementation of systems
to manage vehicle distribution, such as promoting the use of company buses or encourag­
ing employees to cycle to work. The ultimate goal is to create a decision tree that can be
universally applied to every country where the company operates, ensuring a consistent
and sustainable approach across the board.

1.2
Thesis Purpose
The primary purpose of this thesis is to meticulously analyze and propose strategies for
the decarbonization of Hempel’s car fleet, a critical initiative given that car emissions con­
stitute 40% of Hempel’s scope 1 emissions. Recognizing the complexity and breadth of
this task, especially in the context of Hempel’s global operations, the thesis aims to nav­
igate through the diverse energy systems, varying electric car accessibility, and distinct
cultural considerations in different regions where Hempel is active. The thesis endeavors
to provide a holistic understanding of the environmental, economic, and cultural dimen­
sions of fleet decarbonization.
The ultimate goal is to develop a comprehensive decision­making framework, which guides
Hempel in implementing a consistent and sustainable approach to decarbonizing its car
fleet across all operational countries. This thesis not only seeks to contribute to Hempel’s
environmental sustainability goals but also aims to provide a model that can be adapted
and applied by other global entities facing similar decarbonization challenges.
1.3
Thesis Structure
The thesis on Hempel’s car fleet decarbonization is structured methodically into five pri­
mary sections, each addressing a pivotal aspect of the decarbonization initiative. The
initial chapter serves as an insightful introduction, showcasing the critical role of car emis­
sions in Hempel’s overall scope 1,2 and 3 emissions, and the intricate challenges posed
by its worldwide operations. This pivotal chapter provides a solid framework for the thesis,
outlining its objectives and rationale for its geographical emphasis.
Progressing to the second chapter, the thesis delves into a technical analysis. This section
is dedicated to conducting a comprehensive Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) study, with an
emphasis on the use phase of various vehicle types in three specific regions: Denmark,
UK and Middle East. These regions have been chosen for the analysis as they play a key
role in the fleet decarbonization process. Denmark is considered an example in terms of
maturity toward sustainability solutions. The UK is involved in this analysis as it has the
highest number of vehicles in its fleet. Finally, the Middle East is taken into consideration
because of its complexity both in the feasibility of the decarbonization process and in the
cultural shift towards non conventional types of vehicles. This LCA meticulously analyzes
the energy consumption and CO2 emissions of different vehicles, providing an empirical
basis for understanding their environmental impact.
The third chapter transitions into an economic assessment, a crucial aspect of the de­
carbonization process. It scrutinizes the investment costs associated with transitioning to
environmentally sustainable vehicles, and shows the connection between costs and CO2
savings. This economic perspective sheds light on the financial implications of moving
away from existing leases to greener vehicle alternatives.
In the fourth chapter, the focus shifts to psychological and cultural considerations. This
unique section explores the cultural barriers and perceptions across different regions
where Hempel operates. By leveraging insights from the LCA study, this chapter aims
to facilitate a perceptual shift and strategize to overcome cultural hurdles in implementing
decarbonization measures.

1.4
Vehicles Analyzed
1.4.1
Electric Vehicles (EVs)
An electric vehicle is an vehicle that is propelled by one or more electric motors, us­
ing electrical energy stored in rechargeable batteries or another energy storage system.
Electric vehicles do not rely on internal combustion engines like traditional gasoline or
diesel­powered vehicles; instead, they use electric motors to generate power and move
the vehicle.
1.4.2
Internal consumption Engine Vehicles (ICE)
An ICE vehicle refers to a conventional vehicle powered by an internal combustion engine.
These vehicles use the combustion of fuel, such as gasoline or diesel, within the engine
to generate power. The engine’s combustion process releases energy that drives the
vehicle, turning the wheels and enabling movement. In an ICE vehicle, the engine’s power
is transmitted to the wheels through a transmission system. Traditional gasoline­powered
and diesel­powered cars, trucks, and motorcycles are examples of ICE vehicles.
1.4.3
Full Hybrid Vehicles (HEVs)
A Hybrid Electric Vehicle (HEV) is a type of vehicle that combines an internal combustion
engine (typically gasoline­powered) with an electric propulsion system. HEVs use two
sources of power to drive the vehicle: an internal combustion engine and an electric mo­
tor powered by an energy storage system, such as a battery. The vehicle’s control system
manages the power sources, utilizing the electric motor for low­speed or cruising condi­
tions and the internal combustion engine for higher speeds or when additional power is
required. HEVs are designed to improve fuel efficiency and reduce emissions by utilizing
electric power during certain driving conditions, thereby complementing the conventional
engine and conserving fuel.
1.4.4
Plug ­ in Hybrid Vehicles (PHEVs)
A Plug­in Hybrid Electric Vehicle is a type of hybrid vehicle that combines an internal
combustion engine with an electric propulsion system and a rechargeable battery. What
sets PHEVs apart from conventional hybrids is their capability to be charged by plugging
into an electrical power source, such as an electrical outlet or a charging station. These
vehicles can operate in an ”all­electric” mode using the electric motor and the energy
stored in the battery. Once the electric charge is depleted, the vehicle seamlessly switches
to the internal combustion engine, which can either power the vehicle or recharge the
battery, or both, depending on the vehicle’s design. PHEVs offer the flexibility of using

2.1
LCA Introduction
In today’s drive towards promoting environmental sustainability, the automotive industry
recognizes the paramount importance of Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). This study uti­
lizes LCA as a fundamental tool to examine the ecological implications of different vehicle
models within Hempel’s fleet in Denmark, the United Kingdom, and the Middle East. Em­
phasis is placed on the use phase, which is widely regarded as the stage in a vehicle’s
life cycle that has the most significant impact on the environment. This includes the emis­
sions and energy consumed during the operation of the vehicles. By concentrating on
this phase, the research aims to gain a deep understanding of the operational environ­
mental impact of different vehicle types, such as electric vehicles (EVs), hybrids, Plug­in
Hybrid Electric Vehicles (PHEVs), and Internal Combustion Engine (ICE) vehicles. As a
company dedicated to environmental responsibility, Hempel understands the importance
of gaining key insights to inform its fleet decisions. By focusing on the use phase, this
research delves into critical factors such as fuel usage, energy efficiency and emissions
in real­world driving scenarios. This depth of analysis is crucial in accurately assessing
the environmental impact of different types of vehicles, especially considering the vary­
ing driving habits, fuel options, and energy sources across different regions. Moreover,
this focus aligns with Hempel’s practical needs in managing its vehicle fleet. The use
phase is directly influenced by how vehicles are operated and how drivers behave. In­
sights gleaned from this phase of the LCA are expected to enable Hempel to implement
more environmentally friendly practices in their vehicle operations, thereby reducing the
overall ecological footprint. Additionally, the study of the use phase is highly relevant in
light of evolving environmental regulations and growing public concern about the impact
of transportation on the environment. By offering clear insights into emissions and energy
usage during the use phase, This crucial information can then inform the company’s ap­
proach to meeting environmental regulations and meeting public demand. Additionally,
the project will carefully investigate the ecological impact of battery production and dis­
posal in non­ICE vehicles. This exploration will be supported by thorough online literature
research.

2.2.6
LCI Modelling Framework
The selected LCI modeling framework is an attributional approach, where the goal is to
estimate the portion of the global environmental impact that can be attributed to the prod­
uct. The primary objective is to differentiate the product’s environmental impact from the
broader technosphere. To achieve this, Hempel needs to comprehensively monitor all
aspects of the environmental impact within the system and identify the areas with the
highest impact under the existing conditions at a specific point in time. Additionally, mul­
tifunctional processes will be managed in accordance with ISO 14044 guidelines, which
provide a hierarchy for addressing multifunctionality issues.

In the whole project, a literature study will also be incorporated to cover the production
phase and end­of­life considerations, emphasizing the significance of battery reuse or
recycling.

2.3.1
HEV and PHEV Literature Research and Assumptions
Assumptions were made for each region by combining observations of driver behavior,
regulations (like speed limits), and thorough research into existing studies on driver ten­
dencies and actions. These insights were used to build conclusions that encompassed
the data gathered through calculations based on the initial assumptions for the four dif­
ferent types of vehicles in the three countries being studied.
HEV ­ Denmark
In Denmark, the operation of HEVs is significantly influenced by the varied speed limits.
These limits are set at 50 km/h in urban areas, 80 km/h on motorways outside urban
zones, with most motorways allowing 130 km/h and certain areas limited to 110 km/h.
Moreover, dual carriageways and regular roads outside urban zones maintain an 80 km/h
limit [1].
This diversity in speed limits allows for a detailed analysis of HEV fuel consumption.
Models such as the Toyota RAV4 or Corolla, highlighted by Hempel’s procurement data,
demonstrate that at speeds of 40­48 km/h or lower, these vehicles predominantly operate
on electric power. Above 60 km/h, the internal combustion engine becomes the primary
power source. Given that most Danish employees use their cars for shorter, often non­
motorway, distances, it is assumed that HEVs operate on electric mode approximately
60% of the time, a figure influenced by regional speed limits and traffic conditions [2].
PHEV ­ Denmark
For PHEVs in Denmark, the trend, particularly for company cars, often leans towards a
higher usage of the combustion engine [3]. However, Denmark’s strong sustainability
commitment and advanced infrastructure suggest a different pattern. Analyzing the daily
kilometers driven by Hempel employees and the battery range, and assuming regular
charging at home and work, a high rate of battery usage is anticipated. This analysis
leads to the assumption of around 90% battery usage for PHEVs in Denmark, a reflection
of the country’s commitment to sustainable transportation and its well­developed support
for electric vehicles. It is important to note that prices are not considered here as an
influential factor, as it is the company that pays for it so it doesn’t affect the consumer
behavior. This is valid throughout all the project.
HEV ­ UK
The year 2021 saw a distinct divergence in driving speeds across the UK: average speeds
were around 28 km/h on urban Local ’A’ roads and about 55 km/h on rural Local ’A’ roads
[4]. This variation in speed, alongside driving patterns, notably influences the operation of
HEVs. An additional critical factor impacting HEV performance is acceleration. In models
used by Hempel, such as specific versions of Toyota’s lineup [2], the vehicle’s mode of
operation (electric or engine) is not only dictated by speed but also by the pattern of
acceleration. At speeds of 40­48 km/h or below, and with moderate acceleration, these
vehicles tend to rely on electric power. However, higher speeds or rapid acceleration
prompts the engagement of the internal combustion engine. However, higher speeds or
rapid acceleration prompts the engagement of the internal combustion engine.

tainability indicate a greater reliance on electric mode. This is reinforced by the likelihood
of companies facilitating charging options at homes and workplaces, promoting the use
of the vehicle’s electric features.
Given these considerations, a higher use of the electric mode in PHEVs is assumed.
However, as many vehicles are used for more extensive city routes rather than fixed
commutes, the extent of battery use might not be as pronounced as in Denmark. It is
estimated that PHEVs in the UK operate on electric mode about 80% of the time.
HEV ­ Middle East
The Middle East, specifically Abu Dhabi and Dubai, boasts higher speed limits than many
European countries. In Abu Dhabi, drivers are expected to adhere to a 140 km/h limit,
while Dubai and the Northern Emirates allow for a slightly lower maximum of 120 km/h
[5]. This is accompanied by a speed cushion of 20 km/h before fines are issued. Due
to lighter traffic and longer distances, driving in these regions is typically a smoother and
faster experience. For example, urban areas in Dubai boast an impressive average speed
of 59 km/h.
Given these factors, the operation of HEVs in the Middle East, such as those offered by
Hempel, is expected to be predominantly reliant on the internal combustion engine. The
high speed limits and long distances mean that the electric battery is used less frequently.
It is estimated that in the typical Middle Eastern setting, HEVs operate on electric mode
only about 20% of the time, leading to a correspondingly high level of emissions.
PHEV ­ Middle East
The driving behavior for PHEVs in the Middle East is influenced by a variety of factors,
including country­specific policies, the availability of charging infrastructure, and regional
driving patterns. Each country in this diverse region has its unique approach to trans­
portation. Some Middle Eastern countries, like the United Arab Emirates, have introduced
incentives to encourage the use of electric and hybrid vehicles, such as reduced fees and
free charging stations. However, the overall adoption of EVs and PHEVs has been rel­
atively slow. The availability of charging infrastructure is a significant factor; where it is
well­developed, PHEV owners may opt more frequently for electric mode, especially for
shorter trips. Additionally, the region’s hot climate can adversely affect battery perfor­
mance, limiting the electric­only range of PHEVs.

To ensure consistency and accuracy in the analysis, this value of 0.195 kWh/km is uni­

ICE
When assessing LCI for Internal Combustion Engine vehicles, the fuel consumption rate

of vehicle models.
These fuel consumption values vary depending on the proportion of city driving compared

kilometers per liter (km/l) and then to liters per kilometer (l/km) to determine the quantity of
fuel needed per kilometer. Thus, the total fuel consumption for each region is calculated
as follows:
• In Denmark: The total fuel consumption is calculated at 11.9 km/l, which equates
to 0.084 liters per kilometer (l/km).
• In the United Kingdom: The total fuel consumption is calculated at 10.63 km/l,
translating to 0.094 liters per kilometer (l/km).
• In the Middle East: The total fuel consumption is calculated at 13.18 km/l, equating
to 0.076 liters per kilometer (l/km).

Life Cycle Impact Assessment
This Life Cycle Impact Assessment study was simulated in Simapro using the Recipe

categories. It primarily utilized data from a professional database such as Ecoinvent 3.8 or
other LCA literature, This section will emphasize the influence of these categories, with a
primary focus on the Global Warming category, in alignment with the goal to reduce Scope

the endpoint results, to illustrate their association with the midpoint findings.
Midpoint Results
The midpoint results within an LCA represent a critical juncture in the assessment process.
These results correspond to the inventory of environmental outputs — such as emissions
to air, water, and soil, as well as resource consumption. They are termed ’midpoint’ be­
cause they occur before the translation of these outputs into actual impacts, which is the
’endpoint’ of the LCA.
The interpretation of midpoint results is critical in shaping the conclusions of the LCA and,
by extension, the recommendations for environmental improvement strategies. There­
fore, the accuracy and relevance of these findings are of paramount importance in the
broader context of sustainable product development and environmental policy formula­
tion.
Endpoint Results
Endpoint results in an LCA are the culmination of the study, where the data collected and
the impacts measured at the midpoint are translated into more understandable outcomes,
such as damage to human health, ecosystems, and resource availability. These results

provide a more comprehensive picture of the potential environmental impacts associated
with a product’s life cycle from a holistic standpoint.
Endpoint results would allow us to see beyond the emissions or resource use and under­
stand the actual implications for human well­being, natural environments, and resource
sustainability. For example, global warming potential at the midpoint translates to poten­
tial impacts on human health, ecosystem quality, and economic resources at the endpoint
level. The objective of this LCA is to furnish insights into emissions stemming from the
company’s fleet. While the endpoint results will be recognized and compared with the
midpoint results for a consistency assessment, they will not be the central focus of the
project’s outcomes.

2.5
Scenario Analysis
2.5.1
Introduction
This chapter presents an in­depth scenario analysis for HEVs and PHEVs across three
different countries, shedding light on their environmental performance under various us­
age patterns. Central to our analysis is the exploration of diverse operational scenarios,
specifically focusing on the differential use of battery and fuel in these vehicles. These
scenarios take into account factors such as the proportion of battery usage versus fuel
consumption, the availability of charging infrastructure, and the driving behaviors preva­
lent in each country.
Additionally, this analysis includes a comparison of these hybrid vehicles against the per­
formance analyzed in the base case of traditional ICE vehicles and EVs. This compara­
tive approach provides a comprehensive perspective on where HEVs and PHEVs stand
in terms of environmental impact relative to the other vehicle types. Through this chapter,
the study aims to offer a clear understanding of how the varied use of HEVs and PHEVs
across different scenarios influences their environmental footprint. This understanding is
crucial in evaluating the role of these vehicles in the broader context of sustainable mo­
bility and in shaping policies and strategies for their adoption and use in different regional
settings.
2.5.2
HEV: Factors Influencing Use Patterns
Factors influencing the use patterns of Hybrid Electric Vehicles (HEV) are multifaceted,
stemming from both vehicle­specific characteristics and external environmental condi­
22
Car Fleet Decarbonization
==PAGE:31==
tions. Primarily, the design of HEVs, which combines a traditional internal combustion
engine with an electric motor, dictates their operational flexibility. The efficiency of the re­
generative braking system, which recharges the battery during braking, plays a significant
role in determining how often the vehicle relies on its electric motor versus the combustion
engine.
External factors include driving habits and conditions. Urban driving with frequent stops
allows for more effective use of regenerative braking, enhancing the HEV’s electric op­
eration. Conversely, prolonged highway driving may lead to increased reliance on the
combustion engine.
In summary, HEV use patterns are shaped by a combination of vehicle design, driving
conditions and policy environments, all of which contribute to how effectively the vehicle
balances its electric and combustion engine operations.
2.5.3
PHEV: Factors Influencing Use Patterns
Factors influencing the use patterns of Plug­in Hybrid Electric Vehicles are diverse and
encompass both vehicle­specific features and external environmental and infrastructural
elements. A key characteristic of PHEVs is their ability to be recharged externally, which
significantly impacts their usage. The capacity and efficiency of the battery determine
the electric­only range of the PHEV, influencing how often drivers need to rely on the
combustion engine.
Charging infrastructure availability is a critical factor. In areas with widespread and ac­
cessible public charging stations, drivers are more likely to utilize the electric mode of
PHEVs, maximizing their electric­only driving range. Conversely, in regions with limited
charging options, users may depend more on the combustion engine, reducing the po­
tential environmental benefits of PHEVs.
Driving patterns and distances also play a significant role. For shorter, urban commutes
within the electric range of the PHEV, drivers can operate mostly in electric mode, minimiz­
ing fuel consumption and emissions. On longer journeys that exceed the electric range,
the vehicle automatically switches to hybrid mode, utilizing the combustion engine.
Economic incentives and environmental regulations can further influence PHEV use. Fi­
nancial benefits such as tax reductions, subsidies for electric vehicle purchases, and lower
electricity costs compared to fuel can motivate drivers to favor the electric mode. Envi­
ronmental policies like low­emission zones or restrictions on ICE vehicles in city centers
also encourage electric mode usage in PHEVs. In this project this is not an influencing
factor as the company pays for fuel/electricity.
In summary, the use patterns of PHEVs are shaped by a complex interplay of techno­
logical capabilities, infrastructure availability, driving habits, and policy frameworks, all
contributing to the degree to which the electric component of these vehicles is utilized.

2.6
Literature Study on Production Phase
The production phase of electric vehicles, including battery electric vehicles, hybrid elec­
tric vehicles, and plug­in hybrid electric vehicles, presents a multifaceted and significant
aspect of their overall environmental footprint.
This phase encompasses a series of
energy­intensive and resource­demanding processes, as detailed in studies by the In­
ternational Council on Clean Transportation (ICCT) [10], MIT Climate Portal [11], and
McKinsey [12].
The production of these vehicles begins with the extraction and refining of raw materi­
als, particularly lithium and nickel, which are critical for battery production. This process
alone accounts for about a quarter of the total emissions in battery production, with vari­
ations due to geographical location, ore type, and processing technology. For instance,
the emissions associated with battery­grade nickel can vary significantly, influenced by
factors such as the method of mining and refining. Lithium and nickel extraction not only
contributes to greenhouse gas emissions but also poses environmental challenges such
as water usage and ecological disruption.
Moving to the manufacturing of active materials for anodes and cathodes, this stage is pre­
dominantly characterized by high­temperature processing. The energy intensity of these
processes, primarily driven by electricity consumption, is a major contributor to emissions.
The source of this electricity is crucial; a shift to renewable energy sources can substan­
tially reduce the carbon footprint of this phase.
In cell manufacturing, which is central to battery production, traditional processes often
rely on non­renewable energy sources, particularly in the electrode­drying process. Inno­
vations in this area, such as electrifying the production process and adopting new tech­
nologies like dry coating, can significantly reduce emissions. The potential for reducing
32

==PAGE:41==
environmental impact in this phase is notable, as it forms a considerable portion of the
overall battery manufacturing emissions.
For HEVs and PHEVs, the manufacturing process also includes components for both
electric and internal combustion engines, adding layers of complexity and potential en­
vironmental impact. The production of these additional components, such as internal
combustion engines and related systems, contributes to the overall emissions of these
vehicles. However, the dual powertrain nature of HEVs and PHEVs often results in lower
overall emissions compared to traditional internal combustion engine vehicles, particularly
in the use phase where the electric motor can significantly reduce fuel consumption and
emissions.
Overall, the studies emphasize that despite the emissions associated with their produc­
tion, the lifecycle emissions of EVs, HEVs, and PHEVs – inclusive of production, use, and
disposal – are significantly lower than those of conventional vehicles. This reinforces the
environmental advantage of these vehicles, particularly in the context of a gradual shift
towards cleaner energy sources and advancements in battery and vehicle manufacturing
technologies. The research also suggests that future improvements in battery technol­
ogy, increased use of renewable energy, and enhancements in recycling processes will
likely lead to further reductions in the environmental impact of the production phase of
these vehicles.
2.7
Literature Study on Disposal Phase
In the disposal phase of electric vehicles, which includes battery electric vehicles, plug­in
hybrid electric vehicles, and hybrid electric vehicles, the environmental implications and
challenges of battery disposal and recycling become increasingly complex.
The potential for second­life applications of EV batteries, including those from PHEVs and
HEVs, such as in energy storage systems, represents a significant opportunity. Research
from Nature [13] highlights a growing market for repurposing used EV batteries, driven
by economic incentives. This reuse is particularly pertinent for PHEV and HEV batteries,
which may have different usage patterns and degradation rates compared to BEVs. How­
ever, as the study notes, the eventual surplus of retired batteries from all types of electric
vehicles necessitates recycling as the ultimate solution.
Recycling these batteries, however, is fraught with challenges. Nature’s study outlines the
complexities involved in the disassembly process, which is hazardous due to high voltage
and the risk of short­circuiting. This can lead to thermal runaway and the release of harm­
ful byproducts. The varied chemical compositions of batteries across BEVs, PHEVs, and
HEVs, including flammable electrolytes and potentially toxic materials, further complicate
the recycling process.
A Science Advances research article [BatteryDisposalScienceAdvances] delves into
how battery chemistry impacts the overall environmental footprint. It underscores the
importance of designing batteries with recycling in mind, a consideration that is crucial for
all types of EVs. The study also points out that certain materials, such as cobalt, while
environmentally intensive to mine, can enhance the lifespan and recyclability of batteries,
suggesting a nuanced approach to material selection.

This is especially relevant for PHEV and HEV batteries, where their varied energy capaci­

their second life remain a concern, necessitating advancements in recycling technologies

Furthermore, policy plays a crucial role in this phase. Effective management of the grow­

friendly handling of retired EV batteries from all types of electric vehicles.

Method Description
In the methodology section of this thesis, a comprehensive and detailed approach is em­
ployed to accurately assess the cost involved in reducing 1 ton of CO2 by transitioning
to more sustainable vehicle options within Hempel’s fleet. This multifaceted process en­
compasses several critical steps, each contributing significantly to the overall accuracy
and reliability of the analysis.
The initial phase involves pinpointing the vehicle that emits the most CO2 per kilometer
within a specific country, leveraging data meticulously sourced from the procurement de­
partment. Concurrently, the emissions of the least polluting vehicles are also ascertained.
These crucial emission figures, denoting the kilograms of CO2 emitted per kilometer, are
extracted from the LCA results section, ensuring data accuracy and consistency.
Following this, the total emissions saved per vehicle is calculated using equation 3.1:

In regions where electrically powered vehicles emit less CO2 compared to their fuel coun­
terparts, the transition from ICE to EVs or PHEVs can lead to substantial savings. These
savings primarily stem from the lower cost of electricity relative to fuel. To meticulously
calculate these savings, the study first determines the cost of electricity that Hempel would

Total Cost of Electricity = Electricity Factor (KWh/km) × Years Remaining

Total Fuel Cost = Fuel Factor (kg/km) × Years Remaining

× Cost of Fuel (euro/kg)

The difference between the fuel cost and the electricity cost gives the operational sav­
ings. For PHEVs, the calculation is adjusted to account for some fuel costs, although
the methodology remains consistent. While for HEVs, as the battery is not charged with
electricity from the grid, the cost savings come from a reduced fuel consumption derived
from this type of vehicle.
After determining the operational savings and the early termination cost, the final cost of
transitioning to a less polluting vehicle is established. To ascertain the cost per ton of

4.2.1
Historical Context and Evolution of EVs: An In­Depth Look
The transformation of electric vehicles from a niche offering to a mainstream preference
is a remarkable testament to technological progress. In their early days, electric vehicles
faced skepticism for being impractical ­ their limited range, expensive prices, and lack
of charging infrastructure were major deterrents. While the early models were ground­
breaking, they could only handle short commutes. However, the tide turned when major
advancements were made in battery technology, specifically the emergence of lithium­
ion batteries. These batteries not only had greater energy storage capabilities, but also
boasted extended lifespans and quicker charging speeds. This evolution was not just
about the batteries; it involved a holistic approach including more efficient electric motors,
regenerative braking systems, and lighter, more aerodynamic vehicle designs. All these
factors contributed to enhancing the range and performance of EVs, making them a viable
option for a broader audience.
4.2.2
Understanding Range Anxiety: A Psychological and Technological
Perspective
Range anxiety is a complex phenomenon that intertwines psychological apprehension
with technological limitations. From a psychological standpoint, the fear of the unknown
plays a significant role. For many potential users, transitioning from a traditional internal
combustion engine vehicle to an EV represents a leap into unfamiliar territory. Concerns
about running out of charge mid­journey, coupled with the perceived scarcity of charging
stations, contribute to this anxiety. On the technological front, while the range of EVs has
improved significantly, inconsistencies in battery performance under different conditions
(like extreme temperatures or varied terrains) add a layer of unpredictability, fueling this
anxiety. Addressing range anxiety requires not just technological solutions but also efforts
to build consumer trust and confidence in EV technology.
4.2.3
The Role of EV Infrastructure in Mitigating Range Anxiety: A Closer
Examination
The expansion of electric vehicle charging infrastructure is a crucial step in addressing
the issue of range anxiety.
However, it’s not simply a matter of increasing the num­
ber of charging stations; the strategic placement, reliability, and speed of these stations
are equally important factors. While urban areas and major highways are experiencing
a significant growth in charging stations, rural areas often remain underserved. A key
challenge is ensuring that all these stations are well­maintained and equipped with fast­
charging technology, minimizing the time drivers must spend at the station. Additionally,
the integration of charging stations with smart grid technology can lead to more efficient
energy usage and improved management of power demand, alleviating strain on existing
resources caused by the ever­increasing number of EVs on the road.
4.2.4
Charge Anxiety: Beyond Range
As the electric vehicle market continues to evolve, the focus is shifting from vehicle range
to the overall charging experience, also known as ”charge anxiety.” This form of anxiety
stems from concerns about the availability, accessibility, and reliability of charging sta­
tions. Drivers often worry about the frustration of searching for a charging spot, only to
64

particularly heightened in areas where EV density is high, but the charging infrastructure
has not kept up. Resolving charge anxiety requires a multi­pronged strategy, including
increasing the number of chargers, ensuring compatibility between charger types and ve­
hicle models, and implementing rigorous maintenance protocols for the charging stations.
4.2.5
Technological Advancements and Innovations: Paving the Way
Forward
The world of EVs is constantly evolving thanks to boundless innovation and technological
progress. One of the most exciting areas of development is in battery technology, as
researchers strive to enhance energy density, decrease charging time, and extend battery
lifespan. However, the transformation reaches far beyond just batteries, encompassing
the entire EV infrastructure. For example, battery swapping stations are emerging as a
promising solution to address concerns around limited range and charging time for EVs.
Meanwhile, the prospect of wireless charging technology, although still in its early stages,
has the potential to revolutionize the charging process by eliminating the need for physical
connections.
4.2.6
Psychological and Behavioral Aspects: Navigating through
Perceptions
The transition to EVs is as much a psychological journey as it is a technological one.
Consumer perceptions, formed by a combination of personal beliefs, societal norms, and
media portrayals, play a crucial role in the adoption of EVs. Range anxiety, at its core, is a
manifestation of these perceptions. Addressing this requires concerted efforts to educate
potential buyers, not just about the capabilities of EVs but also about the realities of daily
vehicle use. Most daily commutes are well within the range of modern EVs, a fact that,
when well communicated, can significantly alleviate range anxiety.
4.2.7
Future Prospects and Challenges: Charting the Path Ahead
As we move towards a future dominated by electric vehicles, there is great promise, yet
also significant challenges ahead. In order to truly drive forward with this new mode of
transportation, the development of a sustainable ecosystem must be prioritized . This
means not only perfecting the vehicles themselves, but also implementing the necessary
supporting infrastructure, utilizing renewable energy sources, and establishing fair and
effective policies. The successful resolution of issues such as equal access to charging
infrastructure, affordability for all, and integration into renewable energy grids is crucial for
the advancement of EVs.

than a quick sprint. It demands a multifaceted approach that encompasses technologi­

insights into citizens’ mobility behavior and requirements.
Micro Mobility and Urban Planning

limited to individual passengers, or used as a connection to other forms of public transit.

4.4.1
Comprehensive Exploration of EVs, HEVs, and PHEVs Sustainability
Deconstructing Misconceptions
Many people overlook the sustainability of EVs, HEVs, and PHEVs due to persistent
myths. One of the most widespread falsehoods is the idea of a ”break­even” mileage
point for EVs, suggesting that their environmental advantages don’t outweigh the emis­
sions produced during battery production until they’re driven for tens of thousands of miles.
Yet, this belief is simply inaccurate. In fact, these vehicles greatly reduce lifecycle green­
house gas emissions in various situations. Their importance in reducing emissions in road
transport is crucial and recognized by top environmental authorities. Despite this, certain
segments of the media persist in disseminating myths, painting EVs in an unjustly neg­
ative light, which can hinder public perception and adoption of these sustainable vehicle
options.
Grid Integration and Energy Management
The prospect of EVs transforming power grids has sparked widespread debate and con­
jecture.
Despite the perception that EVs will disrupt grid functioning, these cars are
equipped with clever charging tactics to prevent overload. With advancements like Vehicle­
to­Grid (V2G) technology, EVs can even aid in stabilizing the grid by supplying power
during peak usage periods. Though EVs will increase electricity consumption, this can be
efficiently managed through intelligent grid control and infrastructure improvements. The
move towards a grid capable of accommodating a substantial number of EVs is not only
possible, but already underway in many parts of the globe.
Superiority over ICE Vehicles
Not only are EVs, HEVs, and PHEVs better for the environment, but they also offer a
wide range of other benefits. These alternative vehicle options provide more flexibility,
improved energy efficiency, and simpler manufacturing processes when compared to tra­
ditional ICE vehicles. In fact, governments and policy­makers in developed countries are
actively promoting the shift towards these cleaner options, recognizing their undeniable
advantages. This is evident through various initiatives such as financial incentives, tax
reforms, and investments in charging infrastructure. The increasing number of EVs on
the roads serves as a testament to the rising acceptance of these vehicles and the global
dedication to reducing emissions and combatting climate change.

Elevating Biking as a Mainstream Commute Option

The key to creating sustainable cities lies in effective public transportation. To truly entice

forms of transportation such as biking and public transit. With a deliberate approach to
dispelling misconceptions, building essential infrastructure, and implementing effective

able and economically feasible while also promoting social inclusivity. This journey to­
wards a better future necessitates embracing innovation, fostering partnerships between
the public and private sectors, and committing to long­term planning and investment.

PHEVs

longer journeys where charging infrastructure may be sparse.

towards sustainability, showcasing the environmental and economic benefits of PHEVs
and other green transportation modes.
Hempel can make a considerable impact in both reducing its fleet’s carbon footprint and
