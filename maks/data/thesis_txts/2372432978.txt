Abstract
In the present study, a 3R-compliant method for studying the innate immune response
to inﬂuenza A virus infections using high-throughput quantitative real-time Polymerase
Chain Reaction was applied. The aim was to elucidate innate immune factors involved
in the antiviral immune defence of the upper respiratory. This was conducted by inves-
tigating the transcriptional immune response to viral infection of two diﬀerent inﬂuenza
strains; A/swine/Denmark/75-12687/2003 (H1N2 2003) and A/swine/Denmark/10845-
1/2012 (H1N2 2012) in nasal mucosal porcine explants. Nasal mucosal porcine explants
were isolated from 6 piglets and subsequent inoculated with one of the two strains of
inﬂuenza A virus or medium as control and samples were collected at time points 0h, 24h
and 48h after inoculation. Expression signatures of 61 genes including six reference genes
were included in the data analysis. Nine genes involved in the antiviral innate immune
response were found to be signiﬁcantly regulated after H1N2 2003 infection whereas 19
genes involved in the antiviral innate immune response towards H1N2 2012 were signiﬁ-
cantly regulated after infection of the explants. The regulated genes belonged to several
functional groups; pattern recognition receptors (RIG-I and TLR3), genes stimulating
interferons (MAVS, STAT1, IRF8 and IRF2), cytokines (INFA, INFB, INFE, IL1A and
CSF2), interferon stimulated genes (MX2 and ISG15) and genes associated with apop-
tosis (TRAF2, TRAF6, FAS, FOS and CASP9). Among these genes, seven were found
to be signiﬁcantly regulated following experimental infection of both viruses. The results
showed that the investigated transcriptional antiviral immune response following experi-
mental infection with inﬂuenza A virus mimics the human antiviral response described in
literature, suggesting that nasal mucosal explants from porcine can be applied as a model
for studying inﬂuenza A viruses.

INTRODUCTION
2
Introduction
Throughout the years many animal species have been used for experimental research, test-
ing and education. It was the increasing concern of using experimental animals, animal
welfare and ethical problems of having to add pain and related stress to the animals, that
allowed for Russell and Burch’s to introduce the 3R concept in 1959. A concept which
is still being applied today. The three principles behind the 3R concept, is to replace
experimental animals, with non-sentient material as, cell, tissue cultures or organs. To
reduce the number of animals used, including those cases where fewer animals can be
used in each study to obtain the same knowledge and ﬁnally, to reﬁne experiments in
order to inﬂict minimum pain and distress to the animal (Flecknell, 2002).
For studying human inﬂuenza A virus (IAV) infection, a variety of animal models e.g.
mice, rats, hamsters, ferrets, dogs and pigs have been applied. Animal models are ap-
plied to insure a more complex and realistic replication of host factors and conditions
compared to other types of laboratory in vitro studies (Rajao & Vincent, 2015). The IAV
enters the human host by binding to α-2,6-linked sialic acids receptors of the epithelial
cells of the lung and upper respiratory system (Trebbien et al., 2011), this applies for pigs
as well. The human and the pig share remarkable similarities regarding the respiratory
tract, making the pig an excellent model for human respiratory diseases i.a. IAV. The
pig and human share several other similarities as; size, genetics, anatomy and physiology
(Rajao & Vincent, 2015).
The aim of the present study was to elucidate innate immune factors involved in antivi-
ral immune defence of the upper respiratory system, using a 3R-compliant method for
studying IAV infections and broadening the knowledge within the ﬁeld. The transcrip-
tional immune response to viral infection of two inﬂuenza strains; A/swine/Denmark/75-
12687/2003 (H1N2 2003) and A/swine/Denmark/10845-1/2012 (H1N2 2012) was investi-
gated using high-throughput quantitative real-time Polymerase Chain Reaction (qPCR).
The two viruses were isolated from swine populations in Denmark, the 2003 H1N2 strain
was isolated prior to the H1N1 pandemic in 2009 whereas H1N2 2012 was isolated later
than the H1N1 2009 pandemic.
The high-throughput qPCR tested expression levels of mRNA, coding for 61 diﬀerent
genes chosen based on previous studies and literature. The 61 genes included several
functional groups of innate immune response; pattern recognition receptors (PRRs), genes
stimulating interferons (IFNs), cytokines, interferon stimulated genes (ISGs) and genes
associated with apoptosis. The 61 genes and the six reference genes are shown in appendix
A and B.

BACKGROUND
3
Background
3.1
Inﬂuenza virus
Inﬂuenza viruses give rise to contagious respiratory infections, resulting in annual epi-
demics or in rare occasions pandemics. Inﬂuenza viruses are classiﬁed into four types of
inﬂuenza, three types which infect humans; type A, B and C and one type which is mainly
known to infect cattle, inﬂuenza D. In humans inﬂuenza A and B viruses can cause sea-
sonal epidemics whereas Inﬂuenza virus C cause mild respiratory illness and is not thought
to cause epidemics. IAV is known to infect a range of mammals and avian species and is
therefore more likely to cause pandemics due to avian disease carriers, whereas inﬂuenza
B and C mainly aﬀect humans, which limits the number of disease carriers (CDC, 2016).
In the human population inﬂuenza viruses usually cause short-term disease. However,
in some population groups inﬂuenza viruses can cause severe illness. These population
groups are; infants, who have not yet acquired an adaptive immune response to inﬂuenza
viruses, elderly, who lack mobility and resistibility and people with an existing health
condition, resulting in a weakened immune response (CDC, 2016). Symptoms caused by
inﬂuenza virus include: respiratory distress, fever, coughing, sneezing and increased mu-
cus formation.
The IAV belongs to the Orthomyxoviridae family, which are, enveloped RNA viruses
with a single-stranded negative sense RNA ((-)ssRNA) genome (Peiris et al., 2010). The
envelope of the IAV particle, consist of host derived lipid bilayer membrane, which con-
tains three viral proteins; the glycoproteins Hemagglutinin (HA), Neuraminidase (NA)
and matrix 2 (M2) ion channels, where HA is the most abundantly expressed and M2 the
least abundantly expressed on the viral particle. The matrix 1 (M1) encloses the core of
the viral particle by forming a coat inside the envelope, see ﬁgure 3.1 (Bouvier & Palese,
2008). These enveloped proteins play an essential role in infecting the host cells, regard-
ing penetration of the cell and releasing new viral particles from the cell surface. The
(-)ssRNA genome is located within the IAV particle. The genome consist of eight sepa-
rate and unique segments which code for functional parts of the IAV and non-structural
proteins, which functions still remain to be fully determined, see ﬁgure 3.1(Kuntz-Simon
& Madec, 2009). The ﬁrst three segments in ﬁgure 3.1 are; PB2, PB1 and PA these seg-
ments encode the RNA polymerase, which is necessary for a (–)ssRNA to replicate within
the cell. The fourth segment encodes HA, this glycoprotein binds to receptors on the host
cell, enabling the virus to enter the cell and is involved in severity of the host range. The
ﬁfth segment encodes the nucleoprotein (NP), which encapsulates the genome of the virus
for transcription and replication. The sixth segment encodes the second glycoprotein NA,
which enables the IAV to be released from the host cell after replication. The seventh
segment encodes the matrix proteins M1 and M2. These are involved in the replication
of the IAV. The eighth and last segment encodes the non-structural proteins (NS) which
are also required for viral replication (Hutchinson & Fodor, 2013)(Bouvier & Palese, 2008).

The diﬀerent subtypes of inﬂuenza virus vary genetically in HA and NA. These viral
antigens located on the surface of the virus particle are major targets of the protective
host antibody response, which is part of the adaptive immune response (Peiris et al.,
2010). IAV strains are subtyped based on HA and NA. Currently 16 HA types and 9
NA types have been described. A natural infection with inﬂuenza can provide immunity
to the same inﬂuenza virus due to HA and NA being used as antigenic markers in the
adaptive immune response. Therefore, novel subtypes and strains of IAV have to emerge
in order for the IAV to survive (Peiris et al., 2010). These novel subtypes can happen
due to genetic changes. Minor genetic changes by antigenic drift occur frequently by
accumulating mutations over time causing the inﬂuenza virus to evade immune recognition
by the adaptive immune system. Major genetic changes by antigenic shift are caused by
gene segment reassortment, happening when diﬀerent subtypes of inﬂuenza infect the
same host cell at the same time. Subtypes which has undergone antigenic shifts are more
likely to cause pandemic outbreaks due to lack of host immunity in the populations (CDC,
2016)(Pizzolla et al., 2016). The most common IAV subtypes within the population of
pigs in Europe are subtype H1N1, H1N2 and H3N2, these subtypes have also been found
circulating in humans and avian populations (Janke, 2013).
3.2
Viral life cycle
The infulenza virus enters and infects the host through either oral or nasal cavities result-
ing in an infection of the upper respiratory tract. The virus then binds to and invades the
respiratory epithelial cells, where it can start to multiply and spread (Iwasaki & Pillai,
2014)(Bouvier & Palese, 2008). The inﬂuenza virus binds to the host cells by attaching

==PAGE:12==
3
BACKGROUND
its HA to the sialic acids receptors expressed on the membrane of the epithelial host
cells (Bouvier & Palese, 2008)(Stevens et al., 2005). The aﬃnity of the binding between
inﬂuenza virus strains and sialic acids depends on the chemical structure of the sialic
acids. These chemical diﬀerences further determine which animal species can be infected
with the inﬂuenza virus. In avian IAV the HA preferably binds to the α-2,3-linked sialic
acid receptors of the intestinal epithelial cells, whereas human and swine IAV preferably
binds to α-2,6-linked sialic acids receptors of the epithelial cells of the lung, including the
alveolars and upper respiratory tract (Trebbien et al., 2011). For an avian inﬂuenza virus
to infect a human or a pig a switch of the viral HA should occur before it is able to bind
to the α-2,6-linked sialic acid receptors (Watanabe et al., 2014). This switch can occur
due to single amino acid mutations within the sequence, permitting the HA to bind to
the α-2,6-linked sialic acid receptors (Yamada et al., 2006).
When HA proteins of the inﬂuenza virus bind to the sialic acid receptors on the cell surface,
it facilitates a receptor mediated endocytosis of the inﬂuenza virus, forming an endosome,
see ﬁgure 3.2. Within the endosome the inﬂuenza virus facilitates conformational changes
due to the acidic conditions, provided by the M2 ion channel, resulting in a fusion between
the membrane of the endosome and the inﬂuenza viral membrane, hence releasing viral
RNA segments into the cytoplasm. The viral RNA segments are transported from the
cytoplasm to the nucleus. Once the viral RNA segments are inside the nucleus, the virus
exploits the host cell’s machinery for replication (Harrison Jack & Connors, 2008). Since
IAV is a (-)ssRNA, it needs to be converted into (+)ssRNA before being able to replicate
its genetic material. The transcription from (-)ssRNA to (+)ssRNA is done by a viral
RNA-dependent RNA polymerase carried by the viral ribonucleoproteins (RNPs) complex
(Rehwinkel et al., 2010). The RNA polymerase then translates the viral RNA segments
into viral mRNA. The viral mRNAs are transported to the cytoplasm where they are
translated into viral proteins. The three viral RNA polymerase subunits (PB1, PB2 and
PA) and the NP are then transported back to the nucleus where the viral RNA segments
are used to create new viral RNPs. The new viral RNPs are then transported back to the
cytoplasm where it is be assembled into an inﬂuenza virus particle and is then ready to
leave the cytoplasm. The release of the inﬂuenza virus happens by an accumulation of the
eight segments, hence assembling and budding oﬀfrom the plasma membrane of the cell
(Aartjan et al., 2016). Budding is facilitated by NA, which cleaves the sialic acids of the
surface of the cell forming an envelope around the inﬂuenza virus resulting in the virus
already possessing the viral receptors, see ﬁgure 3.2. After the release the inﬂuenza virus
is ready to infect neighboring cells. This is necessary for the inﬂuenza virus to maintain
infection (Nayka et al., 2009).

3.3
The innate immune system
The immune system is fast reacting and a coherent system including; organs, tissue and
diﬀerent cells and their products. The immune system comprises of two defense systems,
the innate immune system and the adaptive immune system. The innate immune system
is the non-speciﬁc and inborn response, the level and mechanism of reaction is therefore
the same each time it encounters a pathogen, regardless of previously encounters whereas
the adaptive immune system is the speciﬁc and acquired immune response (Chaplin, 2010).
8
==PAGE:14==
3
BACKGROUND
The main role of the innate immune system is the response and early recognition of in-
vading pathogens. It is furthermore the ﬁrst line of defense the pathogens encounter.
The innate immune response lasts from 0 to approximately 96 hours after infection and
uses basic mechanisms to prevent pathogens from entering and replicating in the host
(Melchjorsen, 2013). The ﬁrst line of defense the pathogens encounter is the anatomical
barriers, these barriers keep the pathogens from entering the host and consists of physical
and chemical barriers. An important anatomical barrier is the epithelial surfaces of the
skin. The epithelial surfaces have several defense mechanisms; sweat, desquamation and
acidic environments, which challenges the pathogen, thereby reducing its chance of surviv-
ing. The upper respiratory system is another anatomical barrier. The upper respiratory
system includes; the nose, mouth, sinuses and throat, lined by mucosal membranes. The
defense mechanisms in this system are; cilia, mucus, saliva and diﬀerent types of enzymes
i.a. amylase, peptidase and protease. The lower respiratory tract including the lungs is
also an anatomical barrier, which e.g. uses pulmonary surfactants as a defense mecha-
nism. The eyes serve as another anatomical barrier with tears as their defense mechanism.
Finally there is the gastrointestinal tract. This anatomical barrier uses several kinds of
defense mechanisms including; digestive enzymes, peristalsis, bile acids, gastric acids and
the gut microbiota itself (Murphy, 2014).
When the pathogens breach one of the anatomical barriers the second line of defense
begins, see ﬁgure 3.3. Phagocytes start killing the invading pathogens directly, as well
as inducing an inﬂammatory response recruiting new phagocytic cells. The ﬁrst type of
phagocyte the pathogens encounter are the resident macrophages. The second type of
phagocyte are the monocytes circulate in the bloodstream. These monocytes diﬀerenti-
ate into macrophages or dendritic cells after migrating from the blood to the tissue. If
macrophages and monocytes are unable to control the pathogens, a second kind of phago-
cytes, the granulocytes, are activated. Granulocytes include; neutrophils, eosinophils and
basophils. Of these three types of granulocytes, neutrophils are the most abundant type
of phagocyte involved in the innate immune response to viral infections. Neutrophils have
a short life span in order to prevent tissue damage in the host, due to their ability to kill
healthy cells as well as pathogens. If the invasion of the pathogen is still not stopped, the
last type of phagocytes, the dendritic cells are activated. There are two main functional
types of dendritic cells, conventional dendritic cells and plasmacytoid dendritic cells. The
conventional dendritic cells generate peptide antigens after phagocytosis, which can acti-
vate the adaptive immune response by T cell activation. The plasmacytoid dendritic cells
are major producers of the antiviral IFNs (Murphy, 2014).

The innate immunity mechanisms act instantaneous after the succeeded breach of the
pathogens. The mechanism of the ﬁrst defense include several soluble molecules present
in the body. These molecules can either kill the pathogen directly or weaken the eﬀect of
the pathogen (Murphy, 2014).
The innate immune system is triggered by the recognition of simple molecules known
as pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs). These PAMPs originate from the
bacterial cell wall, lipopolysaccharide (LPS) and peptidoglycans (PGN) or viral nucleic
acids or viral proteins. The PAMPs bind to the PRRs either on the cell surface or within
the infected cells. The PRRs can be divided into transmembrane PRRs or cytoplasmic
PRRs.
The transmembrane PRRs include the Toll-like receptors (TLRs) and the C-
type lectin receptors (CLRs). The CLRs will not be further described. The cytoplasmic
PRRs include the retinoic acid-inducible gene I (RIG-I)-like receptors (RLRs) and the
nucleotide-binding oligomerization domain (NOD)-Like receptors (NLRs). The PRRs are
located on and in a number of various cells, including; macrophages, dendritic cells and
epithelial cells (Takeuchi & Akira, 2016).
The TLRs described in humans are located on the cell surface (TLR1, 2, 4, 5, 6, and 10)
or in intracellular compartments such as endosomes (TLR 3, 7, 8, and 9). The function
of TLRs are to initiate and shape cytokine signaling cascades involved in innate immu-
nity and modelling of the adaptive immune system including antigen presentation to the
lymphocytes (Pizzolla et al., 2016)(Akira et al., 2006).
There are three types of RLRs, RIG-I, Melanoma Diﬀerentiation-Associated protein 5
(MDA5) and Lipophosphoglycan 2 (LGP2). The RLRs can distinguishes between host
RNA and pathogen RNA in the cytoplasm. Recognition of pathogen RNA initiate an in-
nate immune signaling cascade which induces the activation of type I IFNs and activation
of ISGs (Pizzolla et al., 2016).
These NLR proteins form complexes called infammasome complexes which functions are
to regulate inﬂammatory and programed cell death response (apoptosis). The NLRs are
activated not only when sensing an infection but also upon cell stress and apoptosis (Piz-
zolla et al., 2016).
Recognition of PAMPs by TLRs, RLRs and NLRs activate signaling cascades, leading to
induced levels of cytokines, inﬂammatory caspases and apoptosis. Cytokines are small

INFs can be divided into three types, type I IFN and type II IFN and type III IFN.

Secondly IFNs stimulate the expression of hundreds of genes which is known as ISGs

state, preventing the virus from producing and replicating. Thirdly, IFN enhances the
phagocytic activity of macrophages (Schoggins & Rice, 2011)(Ivashkiv & Donlin, 2014).

and inhibition of viral replication. Chemokines are involved in the migration of lekocytes
and the inﬂammatory response (Murphy, 2014)(Pizzolla et al., 2016).
3.4
The antiviral innate immune response
For IAV to successfully infect its host, the virus is dependent on infecting the epithelial
cells of the respiratory tract. The epithelial cells are the main target of the IAV. The
ﬁrst defence system the IAV will encounter is the mucosal surface. The function of the
mucosal defence system is to prevent and limit the infection of the IAV. The mucosal
defence system consists of non-speciﬁc and speciﬁc mechanisms. Secretion of mucus and
physiochemical factors e.g. enzymes and acid are some of the non-speciﬁc mechanisms
whereas the speciﬁc mechanisms include T-cell and antibody responses (Freiborst et al.,
2001). After the mucosal defense system the IAV will have its ﬁrst encounter with the
viral innate immune system.
The innate immune system senses the IAV through the PAMPs, which for IAV is mainly
ssRNA. As low levels of double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) is found during an IAV infection,
it may also represent as an important inﬂuenza virus PAMP (Mohsan et al., 2015). The
inﬂuenza viral PAMPs are recognised by diﬀerent classes of intracellular PRRs within
e.g. macrophages, dendritic cells and epithelial cells. These PRRs are the RLRs, TLRs
and the NLRs, see ﬁgure 3.4. The RLRs involved in viral detection are; RIG-I, MDA5
and LGP2, detecting dsRNA and ssRNA (see ﬁgure 3.4a). The TLRs involved in viral
detection are; TLR3, which detects dsRNA and TLR7 and TLR8, which detects ssRNA
(see ﬁgure 3.4b). Lastly, the NLR which detects viral PAMPs is NLRP3. NLRP3 is an
inﬂammasome complex which plays a role in the innate immune response to viral infection,
after the release of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in stressed or infected immune cells, see
ﬁgure 3.4d (Guo et al., 2015). TLR3, TLR7 and TLR8 recognise the viral RNA when it
has entered the endosomes of the sentinel cells whereas, RIG-I, MDA5, LGP2 and NLRP3
recognise viral RNA present in the cytoplasm of the infected cells (Pizzolla et al., 2016).

apoptosis regulating molecules, which activate and induce the caspase cascade involving
caspase 9 and caspase 3. The caspase cascades result in a degradation of the infected cell
(Murphy, 2014) (Shigekazu & Masato, 2017).

3.5.1
Quantity, quality and purity of the RNA
The NanoDrop ND-1000 spectrophotometer measure the quantity of a given RNA sample
through the concept of Lambert-beer’s law, which predicts a linear change in absorbance
with concentration. Nucleic acids have an absorption maximum of 260 nm., proteins at
280 nm and phenols and other components as guanidine used RNA extraction at of 230
nm. These absorptions are used to calculate the A260/A280 ratio and A260/A230 ratio
and these ratios of absorbance are used to assess the purity of RNA. Both ratios should
be approximately 2 for a pure RNA sample. A low A260/A280 indicates a protein con-
tamination of the RNA (Nanodrop, 2017)(Taylor et al., 2010).
The Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer evaluates the quality of a RNA sample. The Agilent 2100
Bioanalyzer interprets an electropherogram by a software algorithm on a scale from 1 to 10,
with one being the most degraded RNA to ten being the most intact RNA. These numbers
are called RNA integrity numbers (RIN). A RIN from 1-6 is low quality RNA, while
RIN from 6-8 is medium quality RNA and a RIN 8-10 is high quality RNA. Due to the
fragility of RNA, RIN is depended on the time and management of tissue sampling. The
quality of the RNA has shown to have a large impact on the subsequent gene expression
analysis. RIN serves as a good indicator of the physiological status of the tissue (Agilent,
2017)(Taylor et al., 2010).

3.5.2
qPCR
qPCR is a technique applying simultaneous ampliﬁcation and detection of speciﬁc tran-
scripts. Total RNA is extracted and validated as described above. In reverse transcribed
(RT)-qPCR RNA is reverse transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA) prior to pre-
ampliﬁcation in order to increase stability and to act as a template for the DNA poly-
merase used during pre-ampliﬁcation and qPCR. During qPCR the amount of ampliﬁed
DNA is measured in real time after each PCR cycle by ﬂuorescence (Taylor et al., 2010).
It is in this step the quantiﬁcation of the qPCR lies, by the measurement of ﬂuorescence
emitted by the ﬂuorescent DNA binding dye or by a gene-speciﬁc probe. The unspeciﬁc
DNA binding dye will bind to accumulated double stranded DNA in each ampliﬁcation
cycle emmitting a ﬂuorescent signal. Therefore, the amount of ﬂuorescence is correlated
with the amount of PCR product produced again correlated to the initial amount of cDNA
in the sample (Bustin et al., 2009) (Taylor et al., 2010).
When using unspeciﬁc DNA binding dye, control samples should be included to insure
the signal is from the targeted cDNA. A –RT control sample will test for the extent of
genomic DNA (gDNA) and a negative template control (NTC), only containing the mas-
ter mix, will test for contamination of the diﬀerent components used during the qPCR.
The controls help set the threshold cutoﬀcriteria for contamination (Bustin et al., 2009).
When the qPCR is ﬁnished, melting curves are generated and used to establish if the
qPCR has produced one single product or if the assay is contaminated, see ﬁgure 3.5a.
Ampliﬁcation curves are also obtained after the ﬁnished qPCR, see ﬁgure 3.5b.

citrate buﬀer pH=5, to remove some of the bobbles produced during the washing steps.
the next step was performed in a laminar ﬂow cabinet. 100 μl. of sterilﬁltrated 3-amino-9-
ethyl carbazole (ACE) (diluted in 99% ethanol and added 2.5 μl. H2O2 per 5ml. solution)
was added to each well and incubated in the dark for 30 min. at room temperature. The
ACE-dilution was removed from the samples and 30 μl. miliQ water was added, before
the samples were analysed in the microscope. The virus titers were calculated by the
Reed–Muench method, using Excel.
An IAV speciﬁc RT-PCR assay was also performed, this assay was applied to screen for
IAV speciﬁc RNA in the NE. This assay will not be further described and was performed
by another department.
4.5
RNA extraction and puriﬁcation of nasal mucosal explant
Total RNA was extracted from the NE with the miRNeasy mini kit (Qiagen) and ge-
nomic DNA was degraded by the RNase-Free DNase set (Qiagen), the following step was
performed in the fume hood. First, 1 ml. QIAzol lysis reagent (Qiagen) was added to
the M-tubes containing the NE. The NE were then homogenized in M-tubes using the
gentleMACSTM (Milteny Biotec). When the NE were homogenised 200 μl. chloroform
was added and the samples were centrifuged at 1200 g for 15 min. at 4◦C, leading to a
separation of the samples in three phases; an aqueous phase, an interphase and an or-
ganic phase. The RNA is located in upper aqueous phase. The upper aqueous phase was
carefully removed and 100% ethanol was added at the ratio 1:1.5. The samples were then
transferred to RNeasy Mini Spin Columns, where the contaminants were washed away.
100% ethanol was used to improve the binding of the RNA to the silicone membrane of
the column. The samples were subsequently eluted in RNase-Free water.
Following extraction of total RNA, quantity and purity of the total RNA was assessed
by applying 1 μl. to the lower pedestal of the NanoDrop ND-1000 spectrophotometer
(Saveen and Werner AB). The assessment of the quantity and purity was based on the
absorption of 230 nm., 260 nm., and 280 nm. including the OD 260/280 and OD 260/230.
The quality of the RNA was determined by using the Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent
Technologies) and the Agilent RNA 6000 Nano Kit (Agilent Technologies).

4.6
cDNA synthesis and pre-ampliﬁcation
Extracted RNA was converted into cDNA by using the QuantiTect Reverse Transcrip-
tion Kit (Qiagen). Duplicates of each RNA sample were performed to insure technical
replicates. The cDNA reactions were conducted by adding a total of 500 ng RNA, 1.5 μl.
Wipeout buﬀer and RNAse-free water, ending at a volume of 14 μl. The samples were incu-
bated on the thermal cycler applying the DNAse program (2 min. by 42 ◦C and then 4◦C.
or on ice), to remove gDNA. Afterwards 6 ml. of RT master mix (Reverse Transcriptase,
RT primer Mix, RT buﬀer, H2O) (Qiagen) was added to all samples (the RT enzyme was
not added to the -RT samples). The samples were incubated on the thermal cycler at 42
◦C for 15 min. and then at 95 ◦C for 5 min. subsequently the samples were cooled to 4◦C.
Prior to the pre-ampliﬁcation step the samples were diluted 1:10 in low Ethylenediaminete-
traacetic acid (EDTA) Tris-EDTA (TE) buﬀer (VWR R
⃝). The pre-ampliﬁcation step was
performed to ensure a quantiﬁable amount of the qPCR product and was conducted with
TaqMan PreAmp Master Mix (Applied Biosystems) in the following manner; 2.5 μl. of
200nM primer mix (combining all selected primers for the subsequent qPCR reaction,
see appendix B), 4 μl.
low TE buﬀer and 1 μl.
TaqMan PreAmp Master Mix, sub-
sequently adding 2.5 μl. diluted cDNA. The Pre-ampliﬁcation samples were incubated
on the thermal cycler for 21 cycles of 95◦C for 10 min. followed by 95◦C for 5 seconds
and 60◦C for 4 min. The number of cycles was based on the results from the pilot project.
subsequently, the samples were exonuclease treated in order to remove the excess of
primers. The exonuclease treatment was conducted by diluting the exonuclease to 4U/μl.
(3.5 μl. MiliQ, 0.5 μl. reaction buﬀer (New england biolab), 1 μl. Exo 20U(New england
biolabs))/cDNA. 4 μl. exonuclease was then added to 10 μl. of pre-ampliﬁed sample and
incubated on the thermal cycler at 37◦C for 30 min. followed by 80◦C for 15 min. The
samples were then cooled to 4◦C. Finally, the exonuclease treated pre-ampliﬁed samples
were diluted 1:10 in low EDTA TE buﬀer and stored at -20◦C until the qPCR.

4.8
Data pre-processing
Pre-processing of the data set allows the analysis of true biological variation between the
samples and removes technical induced variation. The data set generated for the 96.96
dynamic array was visually inspected by using Fluidigm real-time PCR analysis software
4.1.3 (Fluidigm R
⃝) before further analysis. Sorting the samples were done by evaluating
and removing samples based on melting and ampliﬁcation curves. To insure valid data
the samples were compared to the negative controls; -RT and NTC. The samples were
considered valid if the measured samples quantiﬁcation cycle (Cq) value was 5 Cq values
lower that the negative controls.
The sorted data was analysed by using GeneEx R
⃝software 5.4.4.119. The ﬁrst step of pre-
processing the data was to correct the data set in relation to the eﬃciency of the RT-qPCR.
Samples with an eﬃciency of 90-110% and R2 > 0.98 were accepted. Technical variation
was then removed from the data set by normalization with ﬁve reference genes (ACTB,

the samples missing data were given a Cq value +1 from the highest Cq value in the data

4.9
Data analysis
The data set was converted into log2 for statistical analysis.
Statistical signiﬁcantly
diﬀerentially expressed genes between control and infected groups were determined by
a two-tailed paired t-test for the data at 24 hours and at 48 hours. Furthermore, the
standard deviation (sd) was calculated by using standard error mean (SEM). The changes
in the mRNA expression levels were considered statistically and biologically signiﬁcant if
p-values <0.05. The FC criteria was set to FC > 1.5 up- or down-regulated. Regardless
of statistically and biologically signiﬁcant. The samples were mean center scaled before
performing the Principle Component Analysis (PCA). The samples were marked according
to treatment or hours of inoculation and data was analysed by (PCA). The PCA analysis
was used to compare the mRNA expression proﬁles of all samples based on the entire data
set. This analysis group or cluster individually samples based on similar gene expression
proﬁles, distance indicates variance within the diﬀerent groups or samples of the data set.

DISCUSSION
6
Discussion
In the present study the investigated transcriptional antiviral immune response after in-
ﬂuenza viral infection of porcine nasal mucosal explants was found to mimic human antivi-
ral innate immune response described in the literature (Van Poucke et al., 2010) (Rajao
& Vincent, 2015). Porcine models have previously shown to be successful when study-
ing human inﬂuenza infections, due to similarities of genetic and physiologic properties
of the pig i.e. organ size, organ composition and the immune response (Meurens et al.,
2011)(Rajao & Vincent, 2015). The immune response of the pig encompasses most of
the proteins involved in the human innate immunity with up to 80% resemblance, in-
cluding both structural and functional similarities to human counterparts (Dawson et al.,
2013)(Iwasaki & Pillai, 2014).
6.1
Time as a parameter
The results from the PCA analysis (ﬁgure 5.1), showed that inoculation time accounted
for most of the variation within the full gene expression data set. Samples inoculated
with medium, H1N2 2003 and H1N2 2012 clustered mainly according to inoculation time.
Mechanical damage, degradation of the tissue or adjustments to the new in vitro environ-
ment could be some of the main reasons for gene expression diﬀerences and similarities
shared among the diﬀerent treatments over time.
The results from the Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer assay indicates that a time duration of 48
hours does not aﬀect the quality of the RNA. This is in accordance with other in vitro
studies using similar material. A study by Glorieux et al. (2007) has shown that porcine
nasal mucosal explant could be maintained in culture in an air-liquid interface for 60
hours after inoculation and without replacement of medium and without any signiﬁcantly
damages to the quality of the explants based on morphometric analysis (quantitative
description of form, shape and thickness). Another in vitro study conducted in horse nasal
mucosal explant by Vandekerckhove et al. (2009) showed that no signiﬁcant changes of
morphology and viability of the explants were seen during cultivation and that the nasal
mucosal explants could be maintained in culture for a time period of 96 hours.
Even though the gene expression changes over time could not be explained by RNA
degradation (RIN), an abrupt change of environment (in vivo to in vitro) is assumed to
result in a signiﬁcant change of the transcriptional response. However, by comparing the
expression signatures of the three treatments at the same time points (24h and 48h) after
infection the time eﬀect should be minimal.
6.2
Regulation of the antiviral innate immune response follow-
ing experimental infection
The studied expression of 55 genes involved in antiviral innate immune response showed
that host transcriptional response to infection were relatively similar at time point 48h for

after infection with both type of viruses, compared to the medium controls were found

6.3
Regulation of PRRs following experimental infection
mRNA coding for classical virus-speciﬁc PRRs, TLR3 and RIG-I, were signiﬁcantly up-
regulated at time point 48h after experimental inoculation. Both TLR3 and RIG-I are
intracellular located supporting that the IAV was able to enter cells of the NE. However
whether the virus replicated or not cannot be concluded based on the regulated PRR.
TLR3 is primarily localized to endosomes of macrophages and dendritic cells whereas the
PRR, RIG-I is located in the cytoplasm of a most cells, (ﬁgure 3.4a and 3.4b) (Koyama
et al., 2008). RIG-I was found to be more up-regulated compared to TLR3. As a response
to viral infection an explanation for this could be that RIG-I is expressed by a wide variety
of cells compared to TLR3. In agreement with the present study, Skovgaard et al. (2013)
also found both RIG-I and TLR3 to be signiﬁcantly up-regulated at day 1, 3 and 14 in
lung tissue of H1N2 infected pigs.
Based on the mRNA expression of RIG-I and TLR3, IAV is able to enter the cells of the
NE and be recognised by the PRRs of the antiviral innate immune response suggesting
that NE as a model for studying antiviral innate immune response is applicable.
6.4
Regulation of genes stimulating IFNs following experimen-
tal infection
Transcripts coding for genes stimulating IFNs; MAVS, STAT1, IRF8 and IRF2 were
signiﬁcantly up-regulated after experimental infection compared to medium controls. Up-
regulation of MAVS and STAT1 will be discussed in the following section.
MAVS is localized on the surface of mitochondria and has an important role in relation
to inﬂuenza A induced type I IFN production (ﬁgure 3.4a). MAVS is activated by the
cytoplasmic PRR RIG-I and is involved in the RIG-I/MAVS signaling pathway which
induces the type I IFNs and ISGs upon viral infection (Wu & Hur, 2015) (Ohman et al.,
2009). The signiﬁcant up-regulation of MAVS is stimulated by RIG-I, an important PRR
of dsRNA and ssRNA found during inﬂuenza virus replication in the cell. The mRNA

expression of MAVS supports that both viruses were able to successfully enter the epithe-

A gene expression in vitro study by Fujita et al. (2017) was conducted in a human
bronchial epithelial cell line.
The cell line was stimulated with an H3N2 IAV for 24
hours. The results showed that MAVS was constantly expressed in the cell line, however

that MAVS was highly expressed, however not signiﬁcantly up-regulated. Sirén et al.

up-regulation. In all the three studies above, inﬂuenza A H3N2 was used for infection,
whereas in the present study two diﬀerent strains of H1N2 were used for infection. The
up-regulation of MAVS found in the present study is to be suspected during antiviral
immunity but it cannot be explained by strain type, as RIG-I would recognise both types
of strains through their PAMPs (ssRNA). Neither the Fujita et al. (2017), Ohman et al.
(2009) or Sirén et al. (2006) study sampled at time points later than 24 hours. This could
be why the other studies fail to observe MAVS as highly regulated, whereas this study
observes a signiﬁcant up-regulation of MAVS.
Transcripts coding for STAT1 were also signiﬁcantly up-regulated following infection com-
pared to medium controls. STAT1 is a cellular transcription factor activated by a cascade
involving type 1 IFNs, inducing ISGs and IRFs (ﬁgure 3.4c) and STAT1 is an important
factor for the IFN induced antiviral state (Young et al., 2000). STAT1 is inducing a
positive feed-back loop of type I IFNs in a complex with IRF7 (ﬁgure 3.4c) (Honda &
Taniguchi, 2006). The up-regulation of STAT1 at both time points following infection
is likely a results of this positive-feedback loop. The signiﬁcant up-regulation of PRR,
RIG-I, also contributes to the positive feed-back loop due to its capability of stimulating
type I IFNs (Honda & Taniguchi, 2006).
In agreement with the present results, both Skovgaard et al. (2013) and Li et al. (2011),
have found STAT1 to be signiﬁcantly up-regulated in lung tissue of inﬂuenza infected pigs.
In the study by Skovgaard and co-workers (2013), the pigs were infected with H1N2 and
euthanised 24 hours, 72 hours and 14 days after infection. STAT1 was found signiﬁcantly
up-regulated at all three time points. Skovgaard et al. (2013) furthermore used the same
type of inﬂuenza A strain as in the present infection study. In Li et al. (2011) piglets were
infected with H1N1 and euthanised at day 3 and 7. The study showed that STAT1 was
signiﬁcantly up-regulated at day 3. Both Skovgaard et al. (2013) and Li et al. (2011) in-
fected living animals, however after euthaniseing the lung tissue and the NE were treated
by the similar means.
The agreement of STAT1 regulation after viral infection both in vivo and in vitro in-
dicates that respiratory mucosal explants might be used as a promising model to study
host pathogen interaction and antiviral immune response short time after inﬂuenza virus
infection.

6.5
Regulation of cytokines following experimental infection
Transcripts coding for the cytokines; IFNA, IL1A, INFB, IFNE and CSF2 were signiﬁ-
cantly up-regulated following experimental infection compared to medium controls. The
type I IFNs includes IFNA, INFB and IFNE and are induced due to stimulated PRRs
(RIG-I and MDA5, TLR3, TLR7 and TLR8). Type I IFNs are important classical an-
tiviral cytokine playing major roles in the the early stages of the innate immunity to
viral infection by inducing several cascades stimulating ISGs and other cytokines, thereby
creating an "antiviral state", restricting the IAV from replication and spreading (ﬁgure
3.4)(Killipa et al., 2015) (Xi et al., 2012). IFNE was more up-regulated compared to IFNA
and INFB. An explanation could be due to IFNE having a unique role and involvement
in the respiratory mucosal immunity compared to the other type I IFNs (Xi et al., 2012).
The study mentioned previously by Skovgaard et al. (2013) has found type I IFNs to be
up-regulated in lung tissue of H1N2 infected pigs at day 1, 3 and 14 after infection.
IL1A and CSF2 are cytokines involved in inﬂammation and IL1A is activated by cell
damage (To et al., 2010). In agreement with the present study To et al. (2010) also found
both IL1A and CSF2 signiﬁcantly up-regulated in human respiratory tract samples at day
4 after infection with inﬂuenza A strain H1N1. The NE model mimics several genes of
the antiviral innate immune response as in agreement with the above mentioned studies,
further supporting that NE could be applied as a functional model for studying the innate
immune system upon viral infection.
The results from the NE model mimics the gene expression of cytokines present in this
study in accordance with the above mentioned studies. This supports the applicability
using NE as a model for studying innate immunity upon an inﬂuenza infection.
6.6
Regulation of ISGs following experimental infection
ISGs are induced by a signaling cascade activated by the PRRs upon recognision of viral
PAMPs. ISGs have several functions. Most ISGs interferes with required pathways and
functions during the viral life cycle inducing viral detection and/or innate immune signal-
ing (Schneider et al., 2014). Two transcripts coding for MX2 and ISG15 were signiﬁcantly
up- and down-regulated, respectively. MX2 interferes with the viral replication and be-
longs to the family of large GTPases, enzymes which can hydrolyze GTP, an energy source
for RNA translation (Murphy, 2014) (Schneider et al., 2014). ISG15 is a ubiquitin-like
protein, which interferes with newly translated viral proteins by targeting the NS1 protein
of the virus (Melchjorsen, 2013) (Iwasaki & Pillai, 2014).
In accordance with the present study, MX2 was found to be signiﬁcantly up-regulated
in lung tissue from ferrets infected with H1N5 at day 2. The ferrets were infected with
the IAV strain H1N5 and euthanised at 2, 4 and 6 days post infection. The study was
performed by Cameron et al. (2008).
Surprisingly ISG15 were signiﬁcantly down-regulated, which was unexpected due to cur-
rent knowledge of viral innate immunity and former inﬂuenza gene expression studies
performed in lung tissue of inﬂuenza A infected pigs and ferrets Skovgaard et al. (2013),

CONCLUSION
7
Conclusion
Based on the present study the 3R-compliant model was proved to be successful when us-
ing nasal mucosal porcine explants for studying innate immune factors following infection
with IAV by mimicking results found in lung tissue of infected pigs and infected human
bronchial epithelial cells. The model is reducing the amount of experimental animals,
obtaining up to 14 explants per pig if both sides of the nasal mucosal layers of the septum
are used. The model is replacing the in vivo experimental animals, thereby reﬁning the
experiment by inﬂicting minimum pain to the experimental animals, while obtaining reli-
able results. The viruses H1N2 2003 and H1N2 2012 were however not able to eﬃciently
replicate within the nasal mucosal explants, thereby leaving room for improvement with
regards to the model.
The aim of the present study was to elucidate innate immune factors involved in antiviral
immune defence of the upper respiratory system using a 3R-compliant method, based
on porcine nasal mucosal explants. The mRNA expression analysis of the nasal mucosal
explants showed that nine genes involved in the viral innate immune response towards
H1N2 2003 and 19 genes involved in the viral innate immune response towards H1N2 2012
were signiﬁcantly regulated. These genes included; PPRs, genes stimulating interferons,
cytokines, ISGs and genes associated with apoptosis. A lot of the genes signiﬁcantly up-
regulated were in agreement with previously described knowledge of the subject. Many of
the signiﬁcantly up-regulated gene were in agreement with other IAV studies conducted
in human cell lines, humans, pigs and ferrets. The mRNA expression results were estab-
lished by comparing the gene expression between the infected explants versus medium
controls.
Several quality control tests were performed concluding that time did not aﬀect the RNA,
purity or quality of the NE. Based on the mRNA expression analysis, PCA and quality
control tests, the results obtained from this study are shown to be reliable.

PERSPECTIVES
8
Perspectives
To further improve the present study a second dynamic array should be integrated, thereby
allowing two primer pair of each gene of interest in the set up. The primers should be
designed to amplify diﬀerent fragments of the gene. Two primer pair targeting each gene
would strengthen the results and insure and sustain a broad screening of genes, in case
one of the primers should be discarded from the analysis. Conducting the study on two
dynamic arrays would additionally allow for medium samples at time point 0h and for
duplicates of the medium sample at time points 24h and 48h.
Future work including this method, could include a time duration study, testing the
quality of the NE upon 96 hours, which is the duration of the innate immunity.
If
the duration study provided reliable results, a study investigating the transcriptional
viral innate immune response during the ﬁrst 96 would be interesting, due to the "late"
regulation of immune genes in the present study.
Another study could include investigating the mechanical response of innate immune genes
after harvesting the NE. This could eliminate doubt in the results being regulated due
to viral infection or mechanical damages. A study focusing on eﬃcient viral replication,
using diﬀerent combinations of ingredients and concentrations in the diﬀerent media used
throughout the entire process could provide optimal conditions for viral replication in
NE.
Lastly, a study investigating the viral innate immune response to several other strains
viruses, including other subtype of IAV would be relevant. This would fully establish
that NE could be applied as a model studying the innate immune response in respiratory
pathogens. Additionally, the study would hopefully broaden the use of the 3R-compliant
method.
