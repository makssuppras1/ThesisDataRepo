Summary
The aim of this thesis is to examine how a Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) approach
to tiling a rectangular area compares to the use of Genetic Algorithms (GA). The mo-
tivation behind this comparison is that the scientiﬁc consensus in tiling antenna arrays
has been to apply GA to ﬁnd the best antenna layout. We aim to challenge this scientiﬁc
agreement.
In order for the reader to get a basic understanding of the problem faced in this
thesis, the ﬁrst two chapters lay the foundation of the tiling problem.
First of all,
polyominoes are described in detail. This includes an explanation of how each polyomino
is rotated and/or mirrored on the plane, and how diﬀerent polyominoes can have diﬀerent
symmetries.
To wrap up the polyominoes, a detailed description is given of how a
polyomino is stored in the computer’s memory.
After a thorough description of polyominoes, we describe how polyominoes are used
to tile rectangular areas. It is demonstrated that even with simple setups, the problem
of tiling rectangular areas is combinatorial. To ﬁnd one of many solution two models
are set up, one for MIP and one for GA, respectively. When one such model is solved,
it returns a possible tiling and a description of how such a tiling is shown.
We also discuss the implementation of both GA and MIP in detail. In particular,
results from tiling an area of varying size with a single polyomino are compared. Here
the MIP shows superior performance over GA in both quality and time. Comparisons
for tiling a ﬁxed area with varying number of polyominoes are also done, with MIP again
showing results with better quality tilings and far lower computation time than GA.
After testing the MIP against the implemented GA, the thesis compares the MIP
against current state-of-the-art variants of GA from scientiﬁc literature. In all compar-
isons, with cases taken form the literature, using MIP provides results of better quality
than any of the GA.
Lastly, another great advantage of using MIP is demonstrated by extending the MIP
model such that the manufacturer is now able to specify where he/she want holes to
appear, and where on the rectangle the optimizer should place polyominoes. A simple
example of how these extensions help the design process is provided.
This thesis aims for being published [12] in the future, as the results show improve-
ment to the current scientiﬁc consensus.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In today’s world our dependence of digital communication has increased a lot with time.
We also see more and more antennas in our daily lives.
Many of today’s cars have
antennas of diﬀerent types, from the standard radio antenna, which receives signal from
all angles, to the phased antenna used to detect people around the car to improve road
safety. A phased antenna is an antenna that is able to change the scanning direction
despite being is stationary. When many of these antennas are combined we call it an
antenna array.
An example of such is shown on Figure 1.1, where each of the grey
squares is an antenna, we call them antenna elements, and the entire rectangle is called
an antenna array.
Figure 1.1: An antenna array, the grey squares are individ-
ual antennas, also called antenna elements. The combination
of many controllable antennas forms an antenna array.
Such an antenna array has the advantage that each one the array elements can be
excited individually. This would be an expensive, heavy, and energy ineﬃcient installa-
tion, as the number of controllers will be high. At least the weight and energy eﬃciency
will be problematic when designing satellites. So a question we asked ourselves was how
to work around these problems, by studying what others in the ﬁeld have done. The
conclusion was to split the antenna array into smaller subarrays [20].
By dividing the antenna array into to smaller subarrays the weight, energy consump-
tion, and price of installation is reduced due to the reduction of controllers. But we still
==PAGE:12==

have not addressed how to divide these subarrays. An antenna array has the ability to
send a "focused" signal, also known as a main lobe, and there will be some unwanted
signal besides the main lobe. These unwanted signals are called grating lobes, shown on
Figure 1.2. If the division of subarrays is symmetric the amount of grating lobes will
be too high. To reduce the level of grating lobes we will be dividing the antenna array
into asymmetric subarrays. As stated previously, cars use phased antennas to detect
people or obstacles - where low precision is not a problem. However, if we consider the
applications in space, where precision is a keyword, reducing the grating lobes is of high
priority. An example of why this is of so high priority, is when satellites scan the oceans
for salinity. If the antenna had high grating lobes, and one of these was scanning land,
the measurements would be compromised.

and the other lobes are known as grating lobes. The radius

We will use polyominoes to divide the antenna array into asymmetric subarrays since
the antenna will be of the type depicted in Figure 1.1. Examples of polyominoes are
shown on Figure 1.3. By using the asymmetry of polyominoes the grating lobes can be
reduced, and higher precision can be achieved. To achieve this reduction we will model
the polyomino tiling as a mathematical optimization, where the objective is to maximize
the number of antenna elements in these asymmetric subarrays. If an array element is
not in one of these polyominoes, the antenna element will be excluded.

CHAPTER 2
Polyomino
The word polyomino was introduced in 1954 by Solomon Golomb in his paper Checker
Boards and Polyominoes [10]. The name came as a generalization to the domino. The
late Martin Gardner popularized this paper in November 1960 by citing Golomb in his
column Mahtematical Games in the journal Scientiﬁc American.
In this chapter the building blocks, i.e. the polyominoes, to our problem will be
deﬁned. Polyominoes are the ﬁrst thing that are deﬁned in the following section. The
second thing that will be introduced is how we will be using linear algebra to rotate/mir-
ror the polyominoes. At the ending of this chapter we will describe how the polyominoes
are deﬁned in a data structure.
2.1
Theory
Consider a 8 × 8 chess board with only one rook on it as illustrated in Figure 2.1. A
rook can only move up, down, left, or right, which is illustrated in Figure 2.1 with grey
arrows. If we are allowed to take a ﬁnite number of moves, we will be able to track a
path on the chess board. Figure 2.2 shows a arbitrary path made by a rook, we say
that the sqaures on this path are rookwise connected. Any set of rookwise connected
squares deﬁne a polyomino, where the number of squares, n, on the path is the order of
the polyomino.
Moving away from the chess board analogy and looking at polyominoes from a gener-
alized perspective; we deﬁne a polyomino as a set of squares that are rockwise connected.
In Figure 2.3(a) a polyomino of order 12 is depicted whereas in Figure 2.3(b) an example
of a shape which cannot be described as a polyomino as the squares not are rookwise
connected.

2.1.2
Symmetries
When a polyomino has symmetries it means that some of the eight conﬁgurations cor-
respond to the same conﬁguration. We will consider the ﬁve symmetries; symmetry,
persymmetry, left-right, up-down, and 2-fold rotational symmetry. We say that P is
left-right symmetric if P = PJnP , and that P is up-down symmetric if P = JmP P. If
P = JmP PJnP we say that P is 2-fold rotational symmetric. If P is square (mP = nP),
we say that P is symmetric if P = P T, and P is persymmetric if P = JnP P TJnP .

CHAPTER 3
Tiling
With the deﬁnition of a polyomino in place, we turn our attention to tiling problem, i.e.,
how polyominoes can be used to tile a ﬁnite rectangular plane. In this chapter we will
introduce the mathematical term tiling. After deﬁning a tiling, there will be a short
part about the fact that if a rectangle has a tiling, it is probably not a unique tiling.
We will formulate the tiling problem as an optimization problem with focus on how
to model the constraints. In the modelling section we will focus on how the constraints
are modelled.
First we consider how to manually model the constraints for a small
problem, and then secondly how to generalize this for larger problems. Lastly, we will
describe how to implement the tiling problem in practice and describe how the solution
should be presented.
3.1
Theory
In order to be able to introduce the concept of tiling, we need to have a deﬁnition of such.
A tiling is deﬁned [14] to be a region R (a rectangle) which is covered with polyominoes
that do not overlap except along their edges. In this thesis we will turn our attention to
tiling ﬁnite rectangles with polyominoes, an example of such is shown in Figure 3.2.
It is not always guaranteed that a set of polyominoes will tile a rectangle R. If we
are given a free polyomino P, it is possible to test whether P tiles R ∈RM×N. If n does
not divide MN we know that P does not tile R. If p polyominoes are used (3.1) [9,
Thm. 2.5(i)] is used to see whether the set of polyominoes tile R.

Genetic Algorithms
Where MIP is a method of solving optimization problems, GA is an algorithm for search-
ing for an optimal solution. In stead of having a model to solve, the algorithm uses a
ﬁtness function to evaluate each of the points in its population. The GA algorithm is
based on Chirikov’s Snowball algorithm [4]. Chirikov used two diﬀerent ﬁtness func-
tions, one where he used common edges and successfully placed polyominoes, and one
where he along the previously mentioned features also used side lobe levels. We will be
considering the ﬁrst ﬁtness function, in the form
Ψ (⃗xq, q = 1, . . . , Q) = αΨP + βΨB,
(3.6)
on the left hand side we have the ﬁtness function Ψ as a function of the chromosome
⃗x = {µ1, µ2, . . . , µQ}, where each gene µi is is the orientation of a polyominom and Q
is the number of genes. Normally in GA the number of genes is the dimension of the
space, but in our case Q is the number of polyominoes we try to ﬁt in R. There are cases
where there will be used less than Q polyominoes. On the right hand side we have the
weights (α, β) ∈[0, 1]2, where α + β = 1, and the features ΨP, for successfully placed
polyominoes, and ΨC for common edges among the placed polyominoes. The ﬁtness
function in the Snowball method is updated for each placed polyomino.
The method works like rolling a snowball in the winter time, it starts in the mid-
dle, and then like rolling through the snow, polyominoes will be added in the circular
outwards manor. This is described in more detail in Section 4.1 and Figures 4.6-4.8.
GA has a given number of chromosomes, this number is called the population. The
user tells how many generations GA has to evolve, and in each generation the ﬁtness
of all the chromosomes is computed, and then the strongest are selected for the next
generation.
The algorithm has no convergence criterion, and thus it will run until
the maximum number of generations.
If a global optimum is found early, the best
chromosome which contains the genes having the coordinates for an optimum will be
kept.
Compared to the MIP, where the result is a binary vector x∗, the result from GA is
the ﬁttest chromosome, where the value of each gene is not binary. The resulting tiling
is found by computing the ﬁtness.

CHAPTER 4
Methods
Now that the polyominoes and tilings are deﬁned, we will investigate how two diﬀerent
approaches can solve a tiling problem. Many of the tiling algorithms used today in tiling
antenna arrays, are based on the work performed by Gwee and Lim [11] in 1996. Their
idea was to use GA to tile the rectangle R from the corners and inwards. The results were
promising as their implementation was shown to have fewer function evaluations than a
Steepest Ascent Hill Climbing method. But the number of holes were never discussed,
as the setups they used in all cases had a tiling. In the following years research was
conducted in order to improve on Gwee and Lim’s work. A group of researchers chose
to work on designed tilings, and they came up with a layering technique [19]. Another
group of researchers chose to question the tiling method, as they believed that tiling
from the centre and outwards would yield better tilings, among these researchers was
Chirikow [4], Roberts et al [20], and Rocca et al [22].
4.1

as maritime protection [3]. In this thesis we will be looking at how a GA can be used
to solve tiling problems.

After the crossover- or mating procedure has been performed, the next step
is mutation. Mutation usually has a very low possibility, but as in nature it still happens
from time to time. If we have a mutation, a random gene will mutate and be assigned a
random value, in our case this value is in the interval [0; n −1], where n is the number
of ﬁxed polyominoes, which is found by counting the number of elements in the result
of Algorithm 1.

of generations, Ng. When the algorithm has gone through Ng generations we say that
the ﬁttest chromosome is the "optimal" result.

Implementation
The implementation of a genetic algorithm follows the work done by Chirikov [4], the
so called Snowball algorithm. If we follow the general structure given in Algorithm 5,
implementing the ﬁtness function is most time consuming, as it is not evaluated for each
placed polyomino.
We previously stated that the ﬁtness function denoted Ψ, describes how each chro-
mosome is evaluated. First we state that the chromosome is not a point in space, it is
a ordered list of ﬁxed polyominoes that we will try to ﬁt in the rectangle R.
We start by describing how the evaluation of each chromosome is performed. First
we denote the ﬁrst n genes of ⃗x as ⃗xn. Hence the evaluation is based on how many of
these ﬁrst n polyominoes which have been successfully placed, how many common edges
the polyominoes have, and how many polyominoes share edges with the border. This
form of evaluation is proposed in [21], and has the form
Ψ (⃗xq) = αΨP + βΨB + γΨC for q = 1, . . . , Q,
(4.2)
where ΨP denotes how many polyominoes have been successfully placed, ΨB denotes
how many edges are shared with the border, and ΨC describes how many shared edges
there are among the placed polyominoes. α, β, and γ are weights that tell how much
we prioritize each of the previously mentioned values. We have that the weights must
be non-negative and sum to 1. The value of the ﬁtness is denoted as C.
The Snowball method works by adding a new polyomino to previously placed poly-
ominoes. The placement of the new polyomino depends where it has most common edges
with the previously placed polyominoes. The ﬁtness (4.2) is updated after a polyomino
is placed. This procedure is repeated until no more polyominoes ﬁt in the rectangle.

The history of using mathematical optimization in tiling problems is not that long. We
only encountered one paper that brushed upon the subject [9], where they did not accept
partial tilings, and then [7] did some work where holes were allowed. This inspired us
to work further on tiling with mathematical optimization, also as there are possibilities
for future work.
When using mathematical optimization we are able to solve the tiling problem using
optimization software such as GLPK, CPLEX and Gurobi. In this section we will look
at an implementation of Gurobi’s optimizer, and since it is not our software, we will be
describing the basics of how a MIP solver works.
Mixed Integer Programs (MIP) can be in the form of (4.6). In order to be a MIP at

In integer programming, a common way to solve the MIPs is to use the Branch-and-

denoted z. Wolsey proposes that the lower bound in the ﬁrst iteration should be z = −∞,

4.2.2
Coping with the complexity
The issue faced when using optimization methods when solving a tiling problem, is the
NP-hardness inherited from MIP problems in general. As shown in Section 4.2.3 there
are cases where a candidate for an optimal solution is found, but it takes a long time
to verify the global optimality.
This is due to the vast number of leafs in the tree
produced in the Branch-and-Bound method that need to be checked in order to prove
the optimality.
In these special cases where there must be holes, the "optimal" result is not veriﬁed,

to some stopping criterion. There are two criterion in focus here, i.e., the maximum
number of iterations and the maximum running time.
In order to set a maximum number of iterations, we want it to depend on the total
number of variables Nt. Thus this maximum number of iteration is set to be κ · Nt,
where κ ∈N. Another parameter that can be set is the maximum running time, again
this is to ensure that the solver does not run in an eternity, just to verify a potential
optimal solution. In the implemented solver we will be ﬁxing the maximum running
time independent of the size of the tiling problem, this is merely a last line of defence.

• Easy to parallelize
• The ﬁtness function f does not have to be diﬀerentiable
• Can escape a local minima
As with so many other types fo implementations, as soon as the basics are understood it
can often be easily implemented. In the case of GA, the basics are easy to grasp, and to
implement the main structure of the the algorithm is therefore easy. And continuing on
the implementation, GA is easy to parallelize as there are often a large number of func-
tion evaluations, and for each generation all of the function evaluations are independent
computations. The method does not rely on diﬀerentiable functions, as the strength of
a chromosome is directly connected to the ﬁtness-value. If a function has multiple local
minima, then GA is able to escape one of these in order to ﬁnd a better local minima,
or perhaps the global minima. This is due to the mutation part of the algorithm.
Disadvantages:
• No convergence criterion
• Uses many function evaluations
• Output is not guaranteed to be a local minima
• Hard to tune parameters
As GA does not have a convergence criterion, it has to run the maximum number of
generations. Even if it has reached the optimum in one of the ﬁrst generations, it still
has to run to its maximum number of generations, this is a major drawback if one has
to run a large optimization problem an example of this is shown in ??. The fact that
GA always will run to its maximum number of generations, means that it has Ngen ·Npop
function evaluations, where Ngen is the maximum number of generations (iterations)
and Npop is the population size. Take the scaling experiment for GA in Section 4.1.2
as an example, where Ngen = 50 and Npop = 75, which gave 3750 function evaluations,
and these were just numbers we chose in order to have a comprehensible computation
time.
As GA is based on the number of generation, there is no guarantee that the
algorithm has converged to an optimum after the maximum number of generation has
been reach.
A counter argument to this, is just to increase population size and/or
maximum generations. The last disadvantage is the fact that it is hard to tune the
parameters used. How to set these is not well deﬁned, and they seem in some cases to
be set by trial and error.
In the case of tiling a ﬁnite rectangle R, we will not consider any local minima, as
these is not well deﬁned for integer problems. GA’s greatest advantage must be the
ease of implementation. Furthermore the running time is predictable, as the number of
function evaluations is easy to ﬁnd. The fact that GA is easy to parallelize, is also based
on the number of function evaluations in each generation, this all helps in estimating
the computation time.
In our implementation of GA in Chapter 4, we used a non-
diﬀerentiable ﬁtness function, and this can give the designer more control over designing
the tiling.

The disadvantages of using GA for tiling is the absence of a convergence criterion,
and thus running large problems will in many cases take longer time than necessary. An-
other problem when using GA is the diﬃculty of tuning the parameters for the diﬀerent
operations in the algorithm.
4.3.2
Mixed Integer Programming
The MIP approach is based on several heuristics, depending on what software is used.
But common for most of the software packages is that they use the Branch-and-Bound
method to solve the integer problem. Using MIP means that many heuristics are used
in order to improve the convergence.
Advantages:
• Convergence rate is known
• Much more control over the model
• Many software packages are available
When using a MIP solver, it is easy to estimate an upper limit to computation time, as
the worst case complexity is known. Even though in most applications the convergence
rate is much lower than worst case scenario. When the model is derived, there might
be some natural constraints, which are easily handled by the MIP method, and adding
these constraints gives more control over the model. A great advantage of using MIP
methods is that many software packages available, hence one is able to chose between
open source and commercial. The modelling setup is similar across these packages, thus
if a model is made for one package, it can, in most cases, with ease be solved using
another package.
Disadvantages:
• Complexity in worst case scenario is of order O(2n)
• Implementation of method is hard
The time complexity of using Branch-and-Bound on a model with only binary variables
gives the worst case complexity of O(2n), where n is the number of variables. In most
cases however, there are heuristics in order to choose which variable to branch upon, for
a reduction the computation time. Where GA was easy to implement, the coding of the
MIP method is a bit more technical. Furthermore if one decides to work independent of
any software packages, much more implementation is needed in order to also have any
heuristics.
When using MIP to solve a tiling problem, it is a clear advantage the have the ability
to add extra constraints, in order to have more control over the model. Compared to GA,
modelling a problem with MIP gives the designer the ability to set mounting brackets,
or screws, where ever he desires. This can all be modelled in the constraints, by setting

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The main objective of this thesis was to eﬃciently tile a rectangular area with poly-
ominoes, which is a generalization of dominoes. This is a key step in designing and
controlling antenna arrays, as controlling polyominoes in antennas are more energy eﬃ-
cient than controlling each antenna individually.
The motivation of this thesis was to challenge the current state-of-the-art algorithms
used in tiling antenna arrays. Current methods are based on Genetic Algorithms (GA),
and these do not allow the designer to specify any geometrical constraints. Further GA
is slow, and produces results of poor quality. This is a problem because the antenna
arrays are key components in many satellites, and the quality of the initial antenna array
layout will be inherited through out the design and construction process.
In order to investigate how MIP would compare with any of the GA-methods used in
current literature, an implementation of Chirikov’s Snowball method [4] was performed
in Chapter 4.
This led to a scaling experiment, where a single free polyomino was
chosen and the size of the rectangular area varied. The solutions generated by MIP had
a quality 1-2 orders of magnitude better than the results from GA.
As the quality was better, a potential trade-oﬀin computation time was expected.
Contrary to the expectations, MIP was also 1-2 orders of magnitude faster than the GA
method implemented, making the choice between MIP and GA easy.
To further examine how the approaches compared, an experiment where a square
area was ﬁxed and the number of polyominoes varied. This experiment showed that the
computation time was 3-4 orders of magnitude higher when using GA compared with
MIP. Further the quality of the results from GA was one order of magnitude lower than
the solutions from MIP.
When comparing the implemented MIP tiling method to some of the current state-of-
the-art GA-based tiling results taking from scientiﬁc literature, the tilings from MIP were
again superior to the current standard. MIP demonstrated solutions with signiﬁcantly
better quality than what [19] could produce.
Implementation of a GA method is far simpler than MIP. As MIP problems are
known to be NP-hard to solve, one has to implement multiple heuristics in order to
avoid worst case complexity of MIP. Therefore there are commercial optimization soft-
ware on the market, where these heuristics are implemented, and the user only has to
focus on the modelling part. These software packages are often expansive, but have
strong and reliable performance.
==PAGE:70==
60
7 Conclusion
After having concluded in Chapter 4 that MIP is able to produce better solutions,
and also on a faster timescale, the design aspect was addressed in Chapter 6. By in-
troduction of constraints on each of the cells in the rectangular, the designer is in early
stages able to specify where antenna array elements cannot be installed. This gives an
a-priori guarantee that the layout of the antenna array will be implementable.
Combining all of these partial conclusions, an implementation of MIP might be more
challenging than GA. However MIP yields better tilings, and with a costly antenna in
mind, the ability to have a strong performance is increased, due to the better quality of
polyomino tilings.
