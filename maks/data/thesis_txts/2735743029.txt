Abstract 
In this project experiments with epoxy-based intumescent coated steel samples are first subjecting to tensile 
force at 20˚C, whereafter exposed to high temperatures of maximum 600˚C in an electric oven. The 
temperature profile of the coated samples is measured in the oven and compared with a coated reference 
sample. After each of the experiments, a visual inspection of cracks and detachment is conducted, in order to 
evaluate the performance of the coatings. The visual inspections and the temperature profiles of the samples 
are analysed, and the results are used to investigate the correlation between the deformation of the steel 
samples and loss of integrity of the epoxy-based intumescent coatings.  
In general, no correlation is observed between deformation and grade of detachment when comparing 
samples with the same paint and different thicknesses. The same applies when comparing samples with the 
same thickness but different paints. Similar, no correlation is observed when the measured temperature 
profiles are compared with the grade of detachment.

1. Introduction 
Fire protection of steel constructions is widely used across different industries, as steel loses its strength at 
high temperatures. To avoid high temperatures passive protection systems are used, which can be either by 
encasing in concrete or by using different materials such as gypsum or mineral wool. Another very effective 
method for fire protection of steel is spray encapsulation with polymeric materials due to their outstanding 
properties, simple processing, low cost, and ease of use. 
Different types of polymers are used together with several different additives that act as fire retardants. The 
application of the polymer is based on mixing two components, one component (the base) consisting of a 
reactive molecule and the other component (the hardener) consisting of a reaction initiator. When the base is 
mixed with the hardener, a polymerization reaction starts. The reaction leads to a matrix of crosslinked 
molecules that cause the mixed material to solidify. 
The fire-retardant additive can be incorporated into the polymers in two different ways, either by being 
mixed into the polymeric matrix or by being incorporated as part of the matrix. There are different 
advantages of the two methods, but the mixed compound method is the most widespread due to greater use 
and lower costs. The fire-retardant additives are added to achieve a number of effects, such as promoting 
endothermic reactions when the polymers are exposed to heat. In this way, a cooling effect can be obtained 
on the surface of the object while the reactions proceed. Another effect is the development of inert gases that 
can prevent radical oxidation processes and thereby prevent flame formation on the surface of the object, or 
the formation of an impermeable protective coating that prevents heat transfer. Special effects can also be 
achieved, such as the reduction of smoke or toxic substances that develop during a combustion process. 
Flame retardant polymers are advanced and complex composed materials that work by a series of chemical 
reactions. To compare the different polymers, they can be categorized according to the additives they 
contain, where the following are the most used in the literature [1]: 
1. Halogen-based flame retardants 
2. Phosphorous-based flame retardants 
3. Nitrogen-based flame retardants 
4. Intumescent systems 
5. Mineral flame retardants 
6. Silicon-based flame retardants 
7. Nanoparticles 
Intumescent systems (4) are a special category of its own. Intumescent systems can either be water-based, 
solvent-based or epoxy-based, where the latter is the focus in this project. Epoxy-based intumescent coatings 
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
are typically used for steel protection in petrochemical and offshore oil industries, where rapid fires can 
occur. 
In case of elements protected with intumescent coating, the design method implicitly assumes that the 
intumescent coating can follow the deformation of the steel up to 2% without losing adhesion and detaching. 
However, the possibility that the intumescent coating can actually follow such large deformations of the steel 
without cracking or detaching is not investigated. Previous studies conducted on different types of sprayed 
fire-resistant materials (SFRM) [2] indicated that cracking and even detachment of such insulations might 
occur at strain as low as 3 times the yielding value of the steel. This is a much lower strain than the 2% 
needed to achieve the effective yielding strength and suggests that the current fire design of beams protected 
by SFRM may be unsafe. 
Previous studies at DTU have investigated the response of steel samples protected with epoxy coatings to 
tensile solicitations at 20˚C. The studies have established that detachment of the paint is possible before 2% 
and the likelihood increases with the thickness of the paint and is also much more significant in case a 
reinforcing mesh is present. Nevertheless, a proper quantification of the damage of the paint has not been 
done and it is possible that most of the cracks would close during fire, due to the expansion of the 
intumescent coating during heating. Such expansion depends on many factors, including the heating rate and 
the type of the coating, and should therefore be taken in consideration, in order to establish whether the 
cracking and detachment of the coating registered in the test would in fact cause a higher heating of the 
sample in fire or could be counteracted by the coating expansion. 
1.1 Project objective 
The objective of this project is to advance the status of knowledge on the mechanical resistance of epoxy-
based intumescent coating. This is done by investigating steel samples coated with epoxy-based intumescent 
coating, which are subjected to large deformation and subsequently exposed to heat, after which the damaged 
coating’s capability to effectively protect the steel samples is investigated. 
The work performed in this project will therefore consist of painting some of the samples prior to the 
experiments. Prior to painting the samples, a mandatory two-day course called “Personal safety when 
working with epoxy and isocyanate” was completed and passed. Furthermore, experiments are carried out by 
first subjecting the steel samples to tensile force at 20˚C, whereafter exposed to high temperatures of 
maximum 600˚C in an electric oven. The temperature profile of the coated samples is measured in the oven 
and compared with a coated reference sample, as well as with the theoretical steel heating temperature, 
derived from the declared thermal resistance of the coating (only valid for standard fire exposure). After each

2. Theory 
In this section the theory of the conducted experiments and calculations are described. The following derived 
equations are used to validate the experimental set-up and further used in the analysis of the experimental 
results. 
2.1 Intumescent systems 
Intumescent systems are a special system of fire-retardant polymers. This kind of fire-retardant system has a 
complex composition and is special in terms of being an expanding polymer, when subjected to heat. 
Overall, the system consists of 3 components, which are an acid source, a carbonization component, and a 
blowing agent [1].  
When an intumescent coating is exposed to heat, the initial heating of the coating is inert, where the 
temperature rises in the coating. At a given temperature the acid source starts to decompose and releases an 
acid, which starts a decomposing process of the polymer matrix. During this process the polymer matrix 
starts to melt, and the formation of a char layer is initiated. At the same time the blowing agent starts a 
thermal decomposition where inert gasses are released which are incorporated into the chard layer, by which 
the expansion of the coating is initiated. 
These processes are happing simultaneously, where the thermal decomposition of the blowing agent is an 
endothermic reaction, which consumes heat and thereby act as a cooling agent during the process of gas 
release. Thereby a decade of the temperature rises occur, where a temperature plateau is expected in the 
temperature profile of the substrate. When the endothermic reaction in the coating is ended a temperature rise 
in the substrate is expected. 
The gas release also has a flame-retardant effect due to the inert gasses preventing oxidisation reactions in 
the chard layer and on the surface. 
The processes in the intumescent systems occur at a certain temperature interval of approximately 280-340 
°C [1] [3], which depends on the composition of the intumescent system.

General requirements defines the heating conditions in terms of a specified temperature-time curve [4]. This 
temperature-time curve is also called the standard temperature-time curve or a cellulosic fire curve, which is 
expressed by the following equation:

3. Experimental section 
This section describes the methods used to conduct the experiments, where two different epoxy intumescent 
coatings are tested under tensile force and afterwards exposed to heat in an electric oven. 
3.1 Intumescent coating 
In this study two different epoxy intumescent coatings are used, which are called C and D respectively. The 
epoxy coatings consist of two components, a base (resin) and a hardener (cure) respectively, that are mixed.  
According to the product data sheets of the epoxy coatings from the suppliers, both of the coatings are tested 
in accordance with the standard ANSI/UL1709 – Rapid Rise Fire Tests of Protection Materials for Structural 
Steel. 
3.1.1 Fire-resistance rating and thermal resistance 
To investigate the behaviour of the epoxy intumescent coatings, different thicknesses are investigated. The 
steel plate samples used from a previous project, are coated with two different thicknesses of 4 mm and 10 
mm respectively. In relation to these thicknesses a thickness of 7 mm is chosen for the coating on the pipes. 
As mentioned, the two epoxy-based intumescent coatings are tested in accordance with the UL 1709 
standard. On the basis of the UL 1709 standard, the fire-resistance ratings of the coatings are determined 
based on a limiting temperature of the steel by which the time duration for different thicknesses of the 
coatings is determined. From a supplier, a table of the fire-resistance rating of a coating has been handed out,

3.2.2 Steel pipes 
The steel pipe samples used for this study are seamless boiler tubes, with the dimensions of 26.9x2.3 mm and 
a length of 1 m. The steel pipe samples have been coated with one type of epoxy intumescent coating, type

3.2.2.1 Coating of steel pipes 
Before being able to work with the epoxy intumescent coating I, the undersigned, have completed the course 
“Personal safety when working with epoxy and isocyanate” and obtained a certificate. Through the process 
of painting, the necessary personal protective equipment has been used and the painting work has been 
carried out at a special epoxy working room at DTU Structural Lab. 
As a preparation for painting the steel pipes a painting test was made, where two small rectangular steel 
samples were coated.  This was done for gaining knowledge about the viscosity of the coating when the two 
components were mixed. In that context it was concluded that the mixed coating has a high viscosity and that 
it is difficult to apply a uniformly thickness of the coating onto the test samples. 
On this basis, it was decided to make custom-made casting forms for the steel pipes and thereby be able to 
ensure that the thickness of the coating was uniformly distributed around the pipe. Because of the velocity of 
the coating, it was decided to make the casting form in a way that one half of the steel pipe would be casted 
at a time. Thereby the steel pipes are casted with the coating in two steps. 
The casting form is made of a plastic pipe with a diameter of 50 mm, a thickness of approximately 2 mm and 
a length of 50 cm, which is divided in two parts lengthwise. Because of some inner tension in the plastic 
pipe, the diameter of it was reduced to approximately 40 mm. The target value of the coating was 7.0 mm 
and thereby the inner diameter of the casting form had to have a diameter of 41 mm. To make sure the 
diameter of the plastic pipe was kept in place with a diameter of 41 mm and that the steel pipe was placed in 
the middle of the casting form, wooden blocks were made to be placed at the ends and some to support under 
the casting form, as shown in Picture 3.2. 
 
Picture 3.2 - Casting form for steel pipes

3.4 Experimental set-up MTS machine 
The tensile tests of the coated pipes are carried out at DTU Structural Lab and a static MTS machine is used 
to conduct the tensile tests. MTS machines are used to determine material mechanical properties, by for 
example performing a uniaxial tension test. This is done by applying tension force to a test sample, of which 
the properties of the material can be obtained, e.g. the strength.  
The MTS machine is controlled by a servo hydraulic system, by which it is possible to control the crosshead, 
actuator, and grips in the MTS machine, by using the program ‘Station Manager’ on a connected computer. 
The specific MTS machine used at DTU structural lab has a maximum actuator force of 500 kN and a 
maximum displacement of 15 cm. 
Some preliminary tensile test was conducted prior to tensile testing the coated pipes, to make sure the grips 
would not compress the ends of the pipes and thereby slide in the grips. From the preliminary test it was 
concluded to use wooden plugs at the ends of the pipes. Thereby pinewood plugs with a diameter of ca. 22 
mm and a length of 10 cm was inserted into the ends of the pipes as a preparation before the tensile tests 
were carried out. 
To measure the displacement of the coated pipes an extensometer was used, which is custom made by the 
personal at the Measuring Technic Department at DTU structural lab, and beforehand it was calibrated with 
help from them. The extensometer has two measurement points and when mounted onto the sample, the 
measurement points are placed on each side of the coated area on the pipe. When mounted onto the pipe it 
has a fixed gauge length of ca. 63 cm, as shown in Picture 3.5, and can measure a maximum displacement of

3.5 Experimental set-up electric oven

used, which has inner dimensions of ca. 68x80 cm (width and depth) and a hight of 93 cm. The electric oven 
has coil elements at the two sides, the bottom, the back and the front, which are heating the oven. At the top 
of the oven a hole is placed, with a diameter of ca. 10 cm and an adjustable lid is placed above it. 
Furthermore, an exhaustion hood is placed above the lid, to exhaust heat and potential smoke from the oven. 
Before the oven tests are carried out, the samples are prepared by shortening the length of the samples to ca. 
58 cm, because of the width of electric oven, and holes of ca. 1.5-2.0 mm are drilled into the steel sample at 
specific point, where thermocouples can be placed in order to measure the steel temperature.

5. Conclusion 
In connection with the experiments carried out in this project, visual inspections have been made, where the 
grade of damage is noted based on how damaged the samples are. This is based on a grade of damage 
system. Based on these visual inspections, the grade of detachment after the tensile tests is compared to the 
deformation of the individual samples. From this, minor correlations were observed between deformation 
and grade of detachment, which primarily applies to samples with a coating thickness of 4 mm. However, 
these samples also had a few deviations, which can be argued to be within the limits of what is reasonable.  
In general, no correlation is observed between deformation and grade of detachment when comparing 
samples with the same paint and different thicknesses. The same applies when comparing samples with the 
same thickness but different paints. 
Furthermore, the measured temperature profiles are compared with the grade of detachment of the samples 
after the tensile test, and a calculated thermal resistance. No correlation between the calculated thermal 
resistance and the grade of detachment was observed. 
In general, the results for all the samples do not have statistically significance, due to few samples in each set 
and many variables. Thereby no correlation is concluded. 
5.1 Further work 
Based on the experiments performed in this project, it has been concluded, that the number of samples need 
to be reevaluated for experiments in the future. The experiments should consist of more samples with either 
the same type of coating but with different thicknesses or with one coating thickness with different type of 
paints. However, to get representative results, more sample within each set of samples are recommended for 
future studies, as errors like the unexpected detachment of both thermocouples can occur, resulting in a 
sample being eliminated. 
In connection to the thermocouples, various reasons could result in the thermocouples being detached from 
the sample such as the expansion of the paint, blast waves, and the fact that they might not be attached good 
enough in the sample in the first place. It would therefore be recommended to increase the number of 
thermocouples, while also focusing on improving how well they are attached to the samples. 
Another aspect which would be interesting to further investigate is the age of the paint, as the set of samples 
with painting C with a paint thickness of 7 mm, showed a higher deformation than the other two sets of 
samples with paint C. This is interesting because of the fact that this sample set was newly painted compared 
to the ones with thicknesses of 4 mm and 10 mm. It could thus indicate that the age of the paint might have 
==PAGE:57==
48 
 
an impact on the performance of the paint. Furthermore, another reason for the high deformation could be 
because of the shape of the sample was different. And thus, different shapes of steel samples could also be 
interesting to further investigate. 
Regarding the deformation of the samples, it is recommended to either focus on one deformation with 
different type of coatings or several deformations with one type of coating.
