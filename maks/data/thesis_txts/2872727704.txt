Abstract
Autonomous floor cleaning is a topic which has only been studied recently. In this paper, we will
work on the Fejemis robot, a cleaning rover designed by the DTU Electro department to clean
the ground of the Autonomous Systems Test Arena. This problem requires a precise control of
the robot and obstacle detection to maximise the covered area. Three thesis already worked
to develop components that will be useful in the final software, but they were all scattered in
different folders. This thesis focus on the system integration for this particular case with ROS2
to make a coherent and simple architecture, building a baseline for the next students. More
specifically, we will tackle USB communication with micro-controllers, localization with a planar
LiDAR and a 2D SLAM algorithm, and we will design a draft of a behaviour tree to initiate the
development of an intelligence.
Keywords: ROS2, system integration, coverage, localization, cleaning

Introduction
1.1
Context of the project
Researching and developing new technologies require a lot of theoretical studies, simulations
and real-life experiences. And this is especially true for the robotics fields. While the first two
parts can be done in classic rooms, testing will often be limited due to the lack of open spaces
sufficiently large and empty for the regular operation of the robots.
The tests could happen outside, however for most preliminary tests we need stable environmen-
tal conditions and an enclosed building will provide them all days. Moreover, the DTU Electro
department is specialized in several types of mobiles robots: rovers, drones, submarine, all with
their own requirements.
In response to these needs, the DTU Electro department decided to build the Autonomous
Systems Test Arena (ASTA), an inflated dome with a surface of 756 m² for an height of 13m
- making it one of the biggest indoor robotics testing arena - providing a testing ground for any
projects [1].
Figure 1.1: The ASTA building (outside and inside)
The facility host several spaces: two areas for land and air operations, one pool and one zone
with a motion capture system (cf. fig. 1.1). This building can host projects for DTU, other
universities or even European-level researches. It also provides experimental floors for several
courses of the department.
Many students and researchers thus visit the building during the year and dust, dirt and small
wastes accumulate on the floor. Moreover, as the area usages may vary from project to project,
the environment is likely to change often. While it could be cleaned by humans, having an
autonomous rover to clean the whole building is a nice idea. This is the mission given to the
Fejemis robot, an in-house designed robot made of an industrial cleaning cart on which two
motorized wheels have been attached (cf. fig. 1.2).
However, the robot is still far from being really autonomous as we will see in the next chapter.

1.2
Objectives
As said before, the main mission of the Fejemis robot is to autonomously clean the building.
This should be achieved through localization, mapping and cleaning planning. We can then
define some high-level objectives for this project:
⋄The robot should be able to move and clean autonomously and periodically the ASTA
building,
⋄The robot should prevent any manual intervention unless absolutely necessary,
⋄The robot should prevent any damage or injury to objects and humans.
1.3
Limitations
The project has some constraints imposed by the usage of a mobile robot:
⋄Finite amount of energy available for both the computers, the wheels and the brushes,
⋄Minimal computing power: we use Raspberry Pi 5 boards for sensors processing, map-
ping and planning,
⋄Hardware limitations: we need the most lightweight robot as possible to save energy, and
it is difficult to change any piece of the hardware without triggering a chain of modifications
in other parts of the project.
However, these limitations are not that restrictive. The two Raspberry Pi 5 proved to be enough
for the algorithms run in this thesis, and the batteries give enough autonomy to move for several
dozens of minutes.
1.4
Thesis outline
In the following chapters, we will first analyse the problems: where is the project at, what is the
state of the art and what should be done. We will then proceed to detail what has been done
on the several topics defined during the analysis.
Then, a chapter will convey the results obtained throughout the project. And a last chapter will
provide a conclusion about this thesis.

Analysis
Autonomous cleaning is conceptually part of a bigger topic that is area covering by robots. Even
if they may have different requirements, the problem is not that far from automatic fields work on
farm, oceanic seabed mapping or even CNC machining and 3D printing. The mission always
comes down to the following tasks:
⋄Having an autonomous mobile robot that can move according to the precision require-
ments,
⋄Being able to localize the robot in a possibly changing environment,
⋄Using an algorithm that can compute a path that will cover an area,
⋄Having sensors and/or logic to know if the robot is able to continue its task.
The solving of these tasks may be very different depending on the case and the usable tech-
nologies.

Constraints of the project

sensors, the resources limitations or even time. We need to identify these limitations for our
project so that we can link the previously expressed tasks and objectives with hardware or
software technologies, theories and algorithms.

State of the art
2.2.1
A bit of history
Cleaning has always been a tedious task and while new technologies were developed to make
it easier with vacuum cleaners and other machines it stayed manual work. After the emergence
of automation and its applications, people began to dream about machines that could ease their
life. However, autonomous cleaning was hard to put in practice because it required energy and
computing power that could not be met before the 70s.
In 1969 came the first autonomous lawn mower Mowbot [5]. This is one of the first self-driving
rovers that would do one’s chores. However, the robot was not really intelligent: the path was
randomly generated and after a certain time the lawn should be fully covered. If the robot works
well outdoor in an empty area, the efficiency of its path planning in a indoor room is less certain
because of the many obstacles.
In parallel, research was done on the topic on environment-awareness for robot. For example,
Ralph Hollis’ robot Newt of the University of Colorado was an autonomous rover with a computer,
sensors and a manipulator [6]. Its tasks were to roam freely in a room, detect the walls and
obstacles, and detect the outlets to charge itself when the battery is nearly flat. However the
tests were made in defined tests area and could not be used in any other room. The creation
of autonomous vacuum cleaner was still far away.
At the end of the 70s, some ideas began to appear about indoor cleaning: either with manual
wired control [7] or remote control [8]. These two projects were the first iterations of rover
vacuum cleaner integrating the mechanical and electrical parts for a three wheeled robot, a
vacuum pump and a battery. Nonetheless the rover still lacks a computer to give it autonomy.
The first real domestic autonomous vacuum rover prototypes appears in 1986: the HCR-00 (aka
Robby) from Hitachi which embedded a gyroscope, infrared sensors and a rotating sonar [9].
Many companies followed the initiative and released their own vacuum cleaner as Panasonic’s
Brownie (1991) and Minolta’s Cleaning Robot (1997). Year after year the robots became lighter,
more efficient with the addition of other sensors and brushes. Experimentations on the global
shapes and types of sensor continued until 1999. After that, the companies ended building only
disc shaped rovers (as we know them today) and decreased the complexity and sophistication
of the system for simpler sensors to reduce the costs [10].
On the industrial cleaning robots side, products began to be released during the 90s as Ro-
boSoft’s AutoVacC 6 or Cyberworks’s Cybervac II [9]. They worked on the same principles,
only having lighter constraints.
2.2.2
Current cleaning robot solutions
If we look at the current domestic floor-cleaning robots on the market they all seem to use
either cameras and/or LiDAR combined with other sensors (infrared, edge and pitfall sensors,
IMU, ...) [11]. This is justified by the improved performances of embedded processors of the
latest years which can process point clouds in real-time. Moreover, most of them seem to
use AI technologies to do segmentation and identify some classical objects and even stains.
Nonetheless, the companies seem to restrict information about the technologies they use.
Some robots are also thought for companies with big surfaces. In this case it seems there are
two trends: small disc shaped rover similar to the domestic ones (maybe with bigger capacity),
and cart-like ones which provide even more capacity and performance possibilities. Neverthe-
less, all of them use similar technologies as the domestic one: LiDAR, 3D cameras, ultrasonic
perception, edge detection, ... [12][13].

2. Analysis - State of the art
2.2.3
State of the art of coverage path planning
The development of several fields of robotics such as agricultural automation and watching,
ocean study or lawn mowers all participated in rising the problem of coverage path planning
(CPP). Until now, general path planning were mainly focusing on arms movement or obstacle-
free trajectory in the plane between a point A and B. On the other hand, CPP was not really
studied as the use cases of these algorithms were few.
For agricultural-related and lawn mower automations, other solutions were used as under-
ground cables, and with improving technologies other solutions were found as computer vision
to follow the crop lines [14]. However, these solutions does not provide computed path, it relied
on human intelligence to construct the path.
Nevertheless, starting from 2001 algorithms began to emerge [15]. Nowadays, papers are still
published on the topics to find ever more efficient ones. They can be classified in two categories:
⋄Discrete ones: the world and obstacles are converted to an occupancy grid and the
planners will try to cover the whole space by moving cell by cell [16],
⋄Non-discrete: they try to compute a path in a geometrical shape (polygonal or curved)
while covering the maximum area.
The most simple one of them is by generating Boustrophedon path in a finite area [17]. This
algorithm will try to split the space into trapezoidal cells tangent to the obstacles perimeter and
then generate a path with parallel lines. Some improvements were made by using mathematical
theories such as Dublin’s lines [18] or Voronoi graphs [19]. Nevertheless, they all rely on the
generation of alternated parallel lines.
For this project, we need an algorithm that is easily integrable to our system, preferably available
as library and which provide efficient computations. Fields2Cover is an open-source library that
fills all of these requirements [20], and will be the one we will use in this project. Moreover, the
newer version (2.0) supports non-convex fields that can contain obstacles, which is perfect for
our application [21]. According to the comparison they provide (cf. fig. 2.2), this library seems
to be complete and configurable. Of course, this comparison might be biased and should not
be taken as ground truth.

3.5
Remote access
As this robot is a rover, it is obviously bound to move. However, to display the data in real-time
we cannot rely on ROS2. Indeed, the usage of multicasting on DTU network is disabled and it
prevents the proper work of the DDS discovery protocol. We could have two solutions to use
unicast instead of multicast [25]:
⋄We could setup a DDS broker either on the robot or on the network. However that would
both go against the paradigm of the decentralized system and induce a failure point. All
DDS vendors does not provide this functionality either (e.g. FastDDS has it, but not Cy-
cloneDDS). Moreover it might slow down the system only for the sake of adding the pos-
sibility of remotely checking data from time to time.
⋄We could setup a static list of IP for discovery. But here again this would requires that
every participant has a static IP, which is not the case most of the time (even the robot
might have a change of IP).
Moreover, some people without a ROS2 installation might need to interact remotely with the
robot, meaning we may not rely on the DDS communications.
The choice was made that, instead of using restrictive solutions, using a VNC server is a better
alternative. No big set-up is needed for the client (except for a VNC client), and he can use all
ROS2 tools on the robot directly. However, this methods is less efficient as desktop sharing is
communication hungry to keep everything responsive.
A VNC server has then been set-up with a Xfce desktop, and uses the display of id 2 (to keep
the id 1 for screen connections). Sadly, the several ROS2 visualization tools cannot be used.
This issue has not been solved yet. However, this can still be useful to run several terminal
operations simultaneously without opening several SSH sessions.

4.2.2
The objectives for the new bridge
Before making a new bridge we need to define some objectives to guide the development:
1. Simplify the writing of translation functions as I/O declaration, ROS2 ⇌Serial mappings,
and (de)serialization of the messages.
2. Simplify the message pipeline down to a minimal number of actors,
3. Make it a working shared library with a maximum flexibility and reusability of the compo-
nents.
These goals should ensure simplicity and readability. Indeed, to add a translation, one should
not need to know how the message channelling works, and if one needs to modify deeper parts
it should remains simple enough to understand the processing.
4.2.3
The new bridge architecture
The new organisation is not stranding far from the original code, it just reduces the number of
elements and every component works in a top-down organization rather than a flat hierarchy.
We now have only four elements to consider:
⋄The bridge: the bridge is the class hosting the whole process, from ROS2 to the initial-
ization of sources.
⋄The sources: the sources are the objects that manage communication between the serial
devices and the translators.
⋄The proxies: these components are the heart of the bridge. They are declaring the inputs
and outputs, mapping the serial messages to ROS2 components and also take care of
calling the right translation function. They should be implemented source-independent.
⋄The translating functions: these functions are proxy-independent. They either take a
serial message or a ROS2 message and return the equivalent one.
We also limit the scope of this program to USB communication and (de)serialization of messages
with the Teensy board. We thus removed the TCP/IP related components, the gamepad input
and the terminal console. These could be made as other ROS2 nodes.

4.2.4
Translation & variants
The conversion of messages between C strings and ROS2 objects is made through the usage
of two template functions:
template <typename T, ProxyVariant_t V>
bool dev2ros(ProxyBase *, const char *, T &);
template <typename T, ProxyVariant_t V>

The template needs two arguments: one being the ROS2 type to either produce or read, and the
other is a variant. The variants are simply integer values that could be used to define alternative
translations of messages (e.g. the twist of a rover could be 2D where a drone would use a 3D
space) or to support the merging of several messages into one unique ROS2 object.
The use of templated functions helps increasing the flexibility as a standardized translation API.
This further helps the making of a general proxy interface which implements the logic and only
leave little work to the implementations. This partially completes objective 1 and objective 3.

4. The control chain - Command mixer
4.3
Command mixer
Another part of the control chain is the merging and prioritization of the commands. Several
sources can send commands to the robot, but only one should be executed. The component
responsible of this task is what we call a mixer.
4.3.1
Motivation
A mixer is already implemented on the drive Teensy along with the driving computations. It
works by reading command messages where an index is given alongside the command. This
index is an unsigned integer that can be used as a way to identify the source, and also prioritise
some commands above other. In the current design the smaller the index, the bigger the priority
is.
However, transmitting velocities commands from several sources may consume a lot of band-
width, where it will be discarded in the end due to lower priorities. Another mixer was thus
implemented on the ROS2 side to filter all these messages.
Moreover, the Teensy mixer is made such that when a velocity command is received, the com-
mand is applied until another valid message is received. In the event of a controlling source
crashing or any other behaviour that would stop the sending of command messages, the robot
would not stop. We then need to add a validity timeout to the commands. It would be best to
implement it in the Teensy mixer in case the bridge itself crash, but we will assume here that
this possibility will be rare.
4.3.2
The implementation
The implementation of the mixer is simple. It follows several ideas:
⋄Action Server
The node exposes a public action server2 that let other components interact with the mixer
(open input command topics with a defined priority, or close it). This was made to follow
what the bigger project did (e.g. the navigation2 package) for simpler integration and to
have a global homogeneity of the elements’ architecture.
⋄Dedicated channels
Each command source will have its own input topic. As long as they all have a unique
ID the mixer will publish at the same rate as the input topic which can avoid undesired
behaviours compared to direct communication with the bridge.
⋄Timeout on message stop
When a source stops sending messages, the mixer waits for a certain time and switch to
lower priority channels.
⋄Zero-value output when no input
If no source has sent a message in the configured timeout, then the mixer will send a null
twist as output. This will prevent uncontrolled robot behaviours if one part of the program
crashes.

elaborate a test protocol that would measure several indicators:

4.5.2
Range
The gamepad is using Bluetooth to wirelessly communicate with a dongle on the robot. The
communication between Bluetooth devices with low power antennas and receivers are typically
rated for ranges up to about 20m. Obviously this range actually depends on the Bluetooth
technology used, whether there is a direct line of sight between the devices, how far are the
walls and the number of elements that could either block or reflect the electro magnetic waves.
In our case, the building is quite large (about 20m) and is mostly empty so it is likely that the
robot and the controller will have a direct line of sight with each other. However, the dongle
is placed near the computing units and the power management boards that might generate
interferences.
In practice, the effective control range is about 10 to 15 meters in open areas which is slightly
more than the given specifications [27]. In cramped area however, the range decreases to
around 4 to 6 meters and sometimes even less. This was tested by moving the robot in front
of and behind vertically placed wooden pallets. The communication was often severed when
moving the robot behind the obstacles and required getting closer or around to restore the
control.
4.5.3
Manoeuvrability
The nominal speed of the robot is fixed at 0.2 m · s−1 to ensure a proper cleaning and localiza-
tion (c.f. section 5.7). The robot is then moving slowly enough to prevent any uncontrollable
behaviour. The analogue joystick usage for speed and turning rate control is greatly helping
inputting precise changes.
Nevertheless, contrary to in-games driving where you can change nearly instantaneously the
command through the joystick, a real robot has inertia and sudden change in input will result
in high acceleration in the motors. These abrupt accelerations should be soften to prevent any
hardware degradation. There are two ways of limiting it:
1. Being gentle with the joystick to slowly increase the linear velocity and turning rate com-
mands. This is the simple solution and only requires taking a new habit.
2. Adding a acceleration-limiting node to soften the manual command before sending it to the
mixer. While this could be implemented, it is not imperative as carefully using the gamepad
prevents the problem, especially when the system is designed to be fully automatic in the
end.
4.5.4
Responsiveness
We need the manual control to be as responsive as possible to provide the best feeling and a
safe system. Indeed, it is easier to move an object that reacts instantaneously than one acting
after a noticeable delay. Thus, we want to know how long it takes for an input to be processed
and for the motors to start spinning. We do not care about the timing of the gamepad specifically
yet, we are more interested in the timing of the whole control chain.
It could be hard to make a computerized system that would precisely press a button or move
the joystick and measure the movement of a wheel. However, a simpler solution would be to
film both the gamepad and the wheels and measure the time span between the two actions.

Localization & Mapping
The development of mobile autonomous robots needs most of the time means of localization,
whether it is relative (i.e. based from its starting point) or absolute (i.e. based on known points
in the world). This project does only require a relative localization system to map and navigate
in the environment.
In this chapter we will firstly talk about the implementation details of the localization pipeline and
try to validate the system in a second time.
5.1
The embedded localization means
The robot has several sensors mounted and some may be used for localization purposes:
⋄IMU (Gyroscope, Accelerometer, Magnetometer)
⋄Driving wheels relative encoders
⋄Steering wheel absolute encoder
⋄Planar LiDAR
⋄Depth Camera
These different sensors give different information about the current state1of the robot. Typically,
the wheels’ encoders will be used for computing an odometry based on the kinematics equations
of the robot. The LiDAR and the depth camera can on the other hand be used with SLAM or
Monte-Carlo localization algorithms to provide a position estimate. It would also be possible to
achieve visual odometry with the depth camera.

The ROS2 environment provides a well-known and tested project which implements a 2D SLAM
algorithm: the slam_toolbox package [30]. The main node provided by this library accepts laser

the algorithm has been tested for areas up to 2700 m², which is far greater than the surface of

⋄The package is well-known and tested in the ROS2 community,
⋄The implementation is made for 2D scenarios, which is the case here,
⋄The program should run smoothly even for big areas.
5.6
Sensor fusion with robot_localization
The Fejemis robot has several pose estimation sources (wheels, LiDAR, IMU) and they might all
give slightly different results. To get a good odometry, we need to merge all these estimations:
we need sensor fusion, and the classical mean of achieving this is through the usage of a
Kalman filter. Nonetheless, as the robot may have non linear behaviours, it may be better to
use an extended Kalman filter instead.
Once again, the ROS2 community already worked on this subject and a package is now the
reference for Kalman filters implementations: the robot_localization package [31]. It has
been developed since 2014 and became de facto the first choice for the ROS2 community.
Although the package does also provide an unscented Kalman filter implementation, we choose
to use an extended Kalman filter for this project.
The configured odometry sources for this node are: the computed position by the SLAM node
from the LiDAR scans, the IMU and the wheel odometry.
Sadly, the robot_localization package is getting deprecated in favour to more modern solu-
tions as Fuse [32][33]. However, the current state of Fuse is not yet mature. Nonetheless, in
the future years, it may be interesting to switch technologies.

5.7.1
Impact of the speed on the mapping result
The SLAM algorithm has to correlate laser scans and compute the relative displacement of the
robot in real time. Even though we are working on a 2D space with scans of only 720 points,
this process is quite resource-hungry. The current implementation uses displacement criteria
on the wheel odometry to trigger a new correlation computation. If the robot is moving too fast
and the trigger criteria are too small, the computation may be requested before the last one
finished. The node may even skip many scans which will decrease the correlation precision,
giving a wrong position estimate and a failed map.
This can be tested easily by modifying the robot’s speed limit. For example, if we run the robot
at a good pace of 0.5 m · s−1, we get the map as fig. 5.6c. The obstacles borders are seen as
several lines instead of one. Moreover, the position of the elements are quite blurry (e.g. the
walls are not seen as a solid line but as a point cloud). This map is unusable for our application
as we need to see precisely where the obstacles are in order to move as close as possible to
them.

fig. 5.6a). This map should be exploitable for moving around the building, but maybe not for
going close to the obstacles. Indeed, we can note that some obstacles are still hard to tell

Behavior & Planning
The next step after moving and localizing the robot is to build the behaviour logic that will de-
termine what and when the actions should be executed. This program will rely on planning
algorithms to compute the paths, and actions needed for these actions.
6.1
Architecture of behavioural module
Every robot need some level of intelligence: from simply executing a simple sequence of actions
based on sensors readings, to complex systems relying on artificial intelligence. Our application
is complex enough to require a better paradigm than actions sequence but does not need to
integrate artificial intelligence either.
There are several traditional paradigms for building control architectures [35]:
⋄Decision trees: the decision trees are a kind of control system which execute actions
depending on the value of internal or external variables. This system is extremely simple
to implement as it is mainly execution of nested conditions. However, despite being simple
to implement, it is also too basic for many cases making its use very uncommon.
⋄Finite state machine (FSM): the FSM is the most known and used behaviour paradigm
due to its simplicity and implementation. It works by defining several states, and transitions
between them. Under some conditions the transition can be triggered and the system
switch to a new state.
⋄Hierarchical actions modules: the system is composed of several concurrent modules
that all want to control the robot. Each module compute plans for the actions it wants to
execute along with a score (i.e. priority). The higher (or lower) scored one will be executed
and other plans will be discarded. This was the architecture proposed by the first master
thesis [2].
⋄Behaviour trees (BT): a more complex one but more flexible and cleaner than FSM are
BT. As the name suggests it works by defining a tree of actions that should be done in a
hierarchical manner. In this case we do not have transition, we simply enable or disable
the execution of some branches. It also has the advantage of being able to reuse parts
of the tree elsewhere with the concept of sub-trees.
This project may consist of many actions and states and writing everything as a FSM could be
heavy and hard to maintain. A hierarchical actions module could solve the problem but would
be in fact hard to implement and not efficient. Indeed, each task would need its own program
and new plan would be computed endlessly by all action. Moreover the score computation also
brings other issues like how they should be computed and scaled so that no module overwhelms
the others.

The BTs seem to be heavily considered in the future of robotics as a good alternative to FSM
[35][36]. It does provide flexibility, reusability and readability that other architectures were lack-
ing. However, its syntax and logic can be hard to apprehend as it is really different to other
classical means.
Moreover, the ROS2 environment has several implementations of BT libraries [37]. We will use
the one defined in the navigation2 package that is a wrapper around the open-source behav-
ior_tree_cpp library as well as some custom-made nodes dedicated to mobile robot navigation.
The used library provides a BT engine, interfaces for several node types, the writing of custom
plugins and supports the XML format for the tree description [38]. It also provides a graphical
interface for editing the BT that also support custom nodes.
For all these reasons, we chose to use behaviour trees as our control architecture.
6.2
About the behaviour trees
A BT is composed of a root and many nodes of different types [35]:
⋄Actions
Action nodes in BT are leaf nodes that will trigger some functions (either by doing it itself
or calling another program).
⋄Control
Control nodes in BT must have one or several children and will execute them in a particular
order. For example, the sequence control node will execute all its children sequentially.
⋄Condition
Condition nodes are similar to action node as they are terminal and are used as a way to
enable or disable branches.
⋄Decorators
Decorator nodes have exactly one child. They are created to modify the execution by
modifying the result, or to act as a barrier for a node execution.
All nodes have three possible return types: SUCCESS, FAILURE or RUNNING. Depending on
these values, the parent nodes might decide to either continue, return or wait. It is a great tool
for designing the execution flow, and with the usage custom decorators we can also modify the
behaviour of the BT.
By combining nodes descending from these interfaces we have a great freedom on how the
execution flow will go and a great control on which action to run and in which order.

6.3
Design of the BT
6.3.1
Baseline of the BT
The BT controlling the robot should implement several things:
⋄Request and/or directly initialize some components or internal variables,
⋄Watch for external safety signals to prevent any damage or injury,
⋄Run the several missions of the robot, which can be implemented as subtrees.
The project’s BT was made based on this requirements
and an conceptualized version can be found as fig. 6.2. It
is composed of three subtrees: initialization, passive (i.e.
safety and other elements needing frequent refresh), and
runtime (i.e. the description of the missions).
The first branching is happening in the top sequence. We
first call the initializing subtree, then we switch the runtime
loop (with the KeepRunningUntilFailure decorator). Then,
thanks to the ReactiveSequence control (which executes
all components up until a failure is thrown) we run both
the passive and the runtime subtrees at each BT server
iteration.
This BT architecture allows the implementation of a gen-
eral purpose control loop containing both safety checks
and active mission execution.

6.3.2
Active mission implementation
The Fejemis will have several missions to execute: go to an area, clean it, go to base, stay in idle
mode, ... Hence we need a way of storing and switching missions in the BT. The implementation
of this functionality was done as a set of action nodes that can be used to manipulate internal
registers. Moreover, it also provides a ROS2 interface for receiving external mission switch
commands.
The mission is stored as a tuple of string: the mission class (e.g. calibration, cleaning, idle, ...)
and a mission specialization that specify which part of the mission we are currently executing.
Compared to a just one string mission ID, using a tuple allows one to group similar missions
(as for the calibration), or to subdivide a big task into smaller tasks. This follows the modularity
idea of the BTs.

Traditional cleaning robots works in enclosed spaces and usually are able to clean at least a

Partial conclusion
The control logic and the covering path computation is the heart of the problem and must be
built with care. We chose to use modern technologies with already existing ROS2 integration
to ease the process:
⋄Behaviour trees: for the flexibility, readability and reusability. They provide a modern
alternative to finite state machines when using many states.
The ROS2 implementation we will use in this project are the behavior_tree_cpp package
and its navigation2 integration with nav2_behavior_tree.
⋄Covering path planning: many algorithms exist with their own specific requirements and
focus. The ROS2 integration being important, the choice was directed to existing ROS2
library. The only realistic candidate is the opennav_coverage package which is a ROS2
wrapper around the Fields2Cover library as other packages were not or not fully migrated
to ROS2 yet.
These two packages give a baseline for fulfilling the main objective of the project, but we still
need components to define the cleaning areas.

was made yet. This chapter exists solely to make a baseline for future works on the robot on
the topic of planning. This is the point where the robot needs more focus to be operational.

7.3
The results of this thesis
7.3.1
State of the system
The system currently has the control and localization working. The new implementation of the
serial bridge described in chapter 4 is robust and reliable, even though it has a limited byte
rate. The objective of simplifying the writing of translations functions was also achieved by
the definition of a shared library which handles of all the logic, leaving only a few declarations
to the developers. This abstraction is a double edged sword: it allows for a simpler first-time
interaction but hides a more complex mechanism to build the objects in the background. Overall,
this objective was fulfilled.
The rest of the control loop is for now composed of the command mixer and the manual gamepad
input. We validate both components with a delay analysis between a user input and the move-
ment of the wheel. We measured a delay of around 60 ms between these two actions, which is
acceptable since the human reaction time is about 200 ms. However, the system would require
more tests for software generated movement. As we remove the remote control and its logic
we should get a smaller delay, but a proper analysis will definitely validate the components.
The localization setup follows what has been done in the previous thesis: the computation of
the wheels odometry and the usage of a LiDAR [4]. These estimates are inputted in a SLAM
implementation to get a map and hopefully a better odometry. For the current setup, we added
an extended Kalman filter to merge the output of the LiDAR with the IMU measurements to
refine even further the robot position estimation.
After some calibrations, the wheels odometry was improved compared to the previous work with
only 50 to 60 cm in position errors and around 10° of angle error, after driving around the 7 x 7m
square. The obtained maps were also improved compared to the former system (cf. fig. 7.2). If
we drive at a slow pace (≈0.2 m · s−1), we have a usable map.

Hardware problems
The robot had some hardware problems during this thesis that had to be fixed:
⋄Former computer: the former central computer was an Intel NUC which was sufficient
for the scope of this project. However, we could not get it to connect to the DTU device
network (the dedicated network for the robots and other projects). Moreover, it had limited
performances and was showing some weakness (especially in term of RAM) for running
the SLAM algorithm. These two reasons motivated the change to two Raspberry Pi 5
which could support the workload by dedicating one of the board to heavy-duty computa-
tions for computer vision.
⋄Power management: at one point, one of the relay of the batteries charge management
circuit failed and paralysed the whole robot. Since these components were custom made
and in small batches, it may be hard to replace them if they fail again.
⋄The LiDAR: because of vibrations and manipulation, poor designing from the vendor or
being a defect, the USB connector of the LiDAR communication board had contact issues
that kept disconnecting it. After a while the connector broke and it had been soldered back
in place. This repair currently fixed the issue.
However, one of them is still an ongoing problem that may requires a bigger redesign: the
current steering wheel system is what make the robot not usable. During the few months of this
thesis, several problems were related:
⋄Rigidity: the front wheel is mounted on the carter of the industrial carter on which the robot
is based. However, the plastic of this piece is thin and not very rigid. Added to the margins
in the several joints, this makes the steering mechanism bend under the weight of the
robot, especially when turning. This twisting also destroys the odometry result because it
does not represent the reality of the movement. This is why the wheel odometry heading
computation is now based on the gyroscope measurements (cf. appendix D.1).

7.4.2
Possible improvements
During this thesis we proposed several improvements that can be made to the software or the
hardware to get closer to the project objectives. We will present them below:
⋄Additional sensors: the system is quite simple in term of sensors with only an IMU, a
LiDAR, a depth camera and some wheel encoders. Some other sensors might be inter-
esting to have as some distance sensors on the side to help moving around obstacles (or
even for detecting if the robot can turn without hitting something).
⋄Absolute localization: if the final odometry is not good enough for this application, switch-
ing to an absolute localization system could improve the overall performances. However
this kind of system can be expensive and requires precise placing of the antennas. An-
other idea could be placing ArUco codes as an alternative to antennas, which is cheaper
but does require as much precision, especially giving the fact that the walls move under
strong winds and that the plastic fences can be moved anytime.
⋄Local and global maps: the navigation2 package which is the reference for navigation
with mobile robots supports the concept of local and global maps. A way to improve the
mapping could be to manually define a static global map with the walls and use the laser
scans to make a local map in order to avoid objects. This way we could prevent the issues
of the badly defined walls and blurry elements edges.
⋄Laser scan matching: we rely on SLAM to provide an estimate of the robot position.
However, the estimate can be drifting because it is based on the wheel odometry. Other
methods exist to estimate a localization from a point cloud as AMCL, but only if the pro-
vided map is good enough [34].

8
Conclusion
Autonomous floor cleaning has for long been a dream for humans. However, this dream became
reality in the 90s when the first autonomous domestic rovers have been released by some
companies. Starting with limited and expensive technologies, their solutions evolved with time
and now integrate better sensors while keeping the price relatively low.
The ASTA is a building on the DTU campus that hosts robotics experiments in all kinds. Its
large dimensions and the available equipments make it a great testing ground, even at the
European level. However, as many experiences happen in this space, dirt, dust and other
waste accumulate on the floor: the space must be cleaned regularly in order to provide a nice
space for everyone. While it could be done by a human and a vacuum cleaner, it would be more
interesting to develop a custom robot dedicated to this task. This is the objective of the Fejemis
project.
For four years, the DTU Electro department and several students have been working on making
this project real. Some topics have already been tackled: self and environmental awareness,
obstacle detection with computer vision, and localization. However, these works were done
independently and we need to start integrating everything into one unique software.
This thesis tackled the integration of several parts of the software and their validations. We firstly
defined the new architecture that the project will follow. Then we built the software part by part:
firstly the command pipeline, then the localization and we ended on the subject of the planning.
Even though everything could not be done in one thesis, we now have a working software
movement control, and a working LiDAR SLAM implementation. We achieved better results on
these points as we got an odometry that does not diverge too quickly, and the generation of
obstacle maps with a realistic shape.
Nevertheless, many things have yet to be implemented to achieve a first working prototype.
It is essentially is about the design of the BT governing the robot behaviour, but also in the
improvement of the localization system allowing the robot to get close to obstacles.

C.2
Frame rate of the videos
The precision of the processing delay of the control chain depends on the elapsed time between
two frames, and thus on the recording frequency. The standard video capturing mode of the
phone explicitly says the video feed is at 60 fps. However, for the slow-motion the capturing
rate nor the slowdown factor are known, we only know that the output video is a 60 fps. In order
to use these videos, we need to compute how much time passed between frames to get the
precision of the measurements.
For both videos we counted with an editing software the number of frames needed for a certain
amount of time to pass (1s for the regular slowed-down one, 380 ms for the super slowed-down).

D
Localization
D.1
Steering angle vs Gyroscope for heading computation
During odometry testing and calibration, the behaviour and the results given by the steering
wheel were strange. Indeed, the front wheel mounting is not very rigid and it often bends which
induce errors in the actual measured turn rate. Professor J.C Andersen realized a test to check
whether the front wheel could actually be used for odometry computations.
The protocol is fairly simple:
1. Go straight for 3m,
2. Turn 180° around a table,
3. Go back to start
The following results were then obtained:
(a) Path taken by the robot (starts at (0,0))
(b) Turn rate given by the gyroscope, steering angle
and computed heading
Figure D.1: Results obtained after running the previous protocol on the real robot
As we can see, the relation between the steering angle and the gyroscope values (which should
give more trustworthy orientation indications) are not strongly correlated. This is especially true
around 82s where the robot is reversed and the steering angle and gyroscope gives opposite
data. We can conclude that the steering angle is giving a bad estimate of the actual turning
rate of the robot and should not be used for odometry computation. The use of the gyroscope
values seems to be a good alternative.
