Abstract 
Background: Despite the growing implementation of sustainability initiatives into 
businesses there is still a lack of understanding how initiatives causally lead to beneficial 
business performance outcomes. A System Dynamics (SD) approach is intended to bridge 
this gap of knowledge, and provide it through a set of guidelines to companies and 
researchers. 
Method: A literature study was applied to define a coherent methodology of SD 
principles and techniques to model Causal Loop Diagrams (CLD) through the approach of 
best practice Group Model Building (GMB) exercises. A systematic literature review was 
conducted for defining the most recurring business performance outcomes (BPO) in 
scientific literature, which were later validated by companies. The guidelines were 
developed, tested and iterated in collaboration with test users and experts. 
Results: A set of guidelines with a coherent methodology was validated by a System 
Dynamics expert. The systematic literature review identified 360 BPOs of which majority 
(76%) are in the economic area, of which all were categorised and validated by four 
companies. A set of generic guidelines and a manual were instantiated to fit two use 
contexts, on which tests and iterations were based. 
Conclusion: The results indicate that companies primarily focus on financial BPOs and 
secondarily on value proposition related BPOs. The use tests and developed CLDs 
indicate that the guidelines are usable for qualitative modelling that later can be 
simulated in quantitative models to support business case creation of implementing 
sustainability initiatives. This will have to be tested in future research to prove its 
usefulness. 
 
Key words: Guidelines, Business case, Sustainability initiatives, System Dynamics, Causal 
Loop Diagrams, Group Model Building.

Executive Summary 
Intro 
This master thesis report explores and documents the development of a set of guidelines 
which support the creation of business cases for implementation of sustainability 
initiatives in companies. Through a systematically structured modelling process, the 
guidelines facilitate groups of specialists, middle managers and chief-level decision 
makers to develop a qualitative system model, which provides key insights about 
feedback mechanisms, trade-offs and leverage points in an implementation context. 
 
Uncertainty of pursuing the sustainability business case 
The expansion of the environmental agenda, in the past few decades 
has caused more and more stakeholders to expect that companies meet the triple-
bottom line (TBL) of economic, environmental and social value creation instead of just 
short-term profits (Elkington, 2004), which is by some researchers termed as the 
sustainability revolution for global business (Starik & Kanashiro, 2013). 
Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) initiatives have been proven to create 
positive impact on corporate reputation (Lombart & Louis, 2014; Maden et al., 2012; Park 
et al., 2014), influence competitiveness and firm performance (Saeidi et al., 2015) and in 
some cases increase companies’ profits (Plouffe et al., 2011). But despite this there is a 
lack of understanding how the implementation of sustainability initiatives lead to business 
benefits. Therefore, companies face crucial barriers and risks if committing to create a 
positive impact for the economy without harming the ecological environment or social 
rights. 
 
A prescriptive method for quantifying sustainability business cases 
The guidelines bridge this gap of knowledge through the System Dynamics (SD) 
approach, which includes tools and techniques for sharing and developing stakeholders’ 
==PAGE:11==
 
xi 
different perspectives on a complex organisational system. This has proved to be 
successful for prescriptively quantifying and simulating costs and benefits of complex 
projects, such as the Flour Corporation Change Management Project, which has provided 
business benefits exceeding $800 million for Fluor and clients (Godlewski et al., 2012). In 
the context of sustainability initiatives that encompass all three TBL-dimensions, the SD 
approach seems to be particularly well suited for investigating how these three 
dimensions can be integrated into the business activities and corporate strategy to make 
a substantial business case. 
The guidelines facilitate the development of a qualitative model, which can be 
further quantitatively simulated to decide whether a sustainability business case should be 
pursued or not. The modelling process of the guidelines consists of an initial goal & 
scope meeting, three incremental modelling workshops and finally an external validation 
meeting. The methodology is based on a literature study of SD principles and techniques 
to develop qualitative system models through consolidated best practice group 
exercises. The coherence of the methodology was developed, evaluated, tested and 
iterated in collaboration with test users and experts. 
  
A systematic literature review of 199 studies defined 21 categories of the most 
recurring business performance outcomes in literature, which were validated by four 
companies. The results indicate that companies have a primary focus on financial BPOs 
and secondary focus on BPOs relating to their specific value proposition, all dependent 
on which key decision makers are targeted. This is a crucial foundation of modelling that 
the guidelines need to consider. 
The use tests and developed CLDs indicate that the guidelines are usable for 
qualitative modelling that later can be simulated and quantified. Ultimately, this is likely 
to support business case creation in the general context of implementing sustainability 
initiatives, as exemplified by the EcoM2 use context. The usefulness of the guidelines will 
have to be tested further in future research.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Research Structure & Purpose 
1.1.1 Theoretical Motivation 
The theoretical motivation of this master thesis is based on the urgent need for 
sustainable development, as defined by the (Brundtland, 1987): 
“Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations 
to meet their own needs”. More concretely the motivation revolves around the need for 
supporting the creation of business cases for sustainability initiatives in companies. 
 
Growing implementation of sustainability initiatives 
The expansion of the environmental agenda in the past few decades 
has caused more and more stakeholders to expect that companies meet the triple-
bottom line (TBL) of economic, environmental and social value creation instead of just 
short-term profits (Elkington, 2004). This has been denoted by some researchers, as the 
sustainability revolution for global business. It is characterised by the movement of 
individuals, organisations and societies, committed to creating a positive impact for the 
economy without harming the ecological environment (Starik & Kanashiro, 2013). 
On a company-level (Lozano et al., 2015) emphasises the term corporate 
sustainability as the alternative to traditional, short-term, profit-oriented approaches of 
management that balances the triple bottom-line in a sustainable way. It implies that 
sustainability initiatives, programs and projects can be implemented in the many different 
business processes of the company (Lozano, 2012). 
Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) initiatives have a positive impact on 
corporate reputation (Lombart & Louis, 2014; Maden et al., 2012; Park et al., 2014), and 
reputation and competitive advantage are found to be mediators in the relationship 
==PAGE:20==
 
2 
between CSR and business performance (Saeidi et al., 2015). Even though eco design 
products have been shown to increase some companies’ profits (Plouffe et al., 2011), 
there is a lack of understanding of the quantitative side of the benefits of sustainability 
initiatives. Moreover, the positive association between environmental and financial 
performance depends on the specific firm (Clarkson et al., 2011). 
 
Need for prescriptive method for quantifying business case 
On this basis, it seems that business cases for sustainability need to target 
aforementioned benefits more effectively. A business case can be defined as the 
arguments or rationales supporting or documenting why the business community should 
accept and advance a certain cause (Carroll & Shabana, 2010). 
However, business cases for sustainability seldom reach a strategic level. Rather, 
they are mostly “considered to be an ad hoc measure, an add-on to core business, or 
simply a coincidence.” (Schaltegger et al., 2011). This means that sustainability initiatives 
(e.g. eco design approaches) are not sufficiently integrated into companies’ product 
development processes or strategies (Maxwell & van der Vorst, 2003). 
Perceived risks are one of the barriers that hinder sustainability business cases of 
being pursued, despite companies’ perception of sustainability as a “good thing” (Short 
et al., 2012). In addition, the difficulties of capturing and measuring business benefits of 
sustainability initiatives (e.g. eco design) play an important role (Rodrigues et al., 2016). 
Researchers argue that key conditions for effective implementation of sustainability 
initiatives in businesses is provided by a strategy level approach, which is integrated into 
the corporate business, its sustainability/environmental systems and product 
development systems (Hallstedt et al., 2010; Maxwell & van der Vorst, 2003). 
Some strategies include redesigning the business model to deeply integrate the 
business case into the business activities and corporate strategy to make it substantial 
(Schaltegger et al., 2011). 
==PAGE:21==
 
3 
Lozano (2012) highlights the lack of understanding how initiatives influence the 
different parts of the company system. These include e.g. operations & processes, 
management & strategy, organisational systems, procurement & marketing, assessment & 
communication, as well as “how they are linked, or how they contribute to sustainability’s 
four dimensions (economic, environmental, social, and time)”. 
To bridge this gap of understanding about business cases for implementation of 
sustainability initiatives a prescriptive approach can be taken - for quantifying potential 
business benefits, risks and trade-offs in an early stage, before implementation. And by 
building a compelling set of arguments that are business-oriented and targeted at top-
level management (Pigosso et al., 2013). 
The EcoM2 maturity model prescriptively develops roadmaps for eco design 
improvements (Pigosso et al., 2013). Likewise a systems thinking perspective (Meadows & 
Wright, 2009; Sterman, 2003) can provide the prescriptive methodology for simulating a 
“to-be” business case. As the comprehensive task of transforming organisations towards 
more sustainability requires more than product, process and technological innovations, it 
can be assisted by System Dynamics thinking to clarify the dynamics of companies’ 
business models in relation to e.g. environmental impacts (Abdelkafi & Tauscher, 2015). 
 
System Dynamics and Causal Loop Diagrams as a prescriptive methodology 
The Systems thinking perspective, in this context the field of SD enables understanding of 
the structure and dynamics of complex systems (Meadows & Wright, 2009; Sterman, 
2003). 
The scientific field of SD historically emerged from J.W. Forrester’s work in the 
mid-1950s with an objective to more efficiently solve corporate/managerial problems 
(Radzicki & Taylor, 1997). The premise of SD is that the attempt to solve a problem often 
create unanticipated side effects due to our inability to grasp the dynamic complexity of 
systems. Forrester called this the phenomena of “counterintuitive behaviour of social 
==PAGE:22==
 
4 
systems” (Forrester, 1971). The application of SD has since expanded to include 
environmental and social issues, e.g. within change management. 
The methodological field of SD includes tools and techniques for sharing and 
developing mental models (i.e. interpretations) of system structures, which through 
simulation, can provide insights for improving mental models and make better decisions. 
SD has been applied in industry for prescriptively quantifying and testing costs and 
benefits of complex projects, such as in the Flour Corporation Change Management 
Project, which since 2005, have provided business benefits exceeding $800 million for 
Fluor and clients (Godlewski et al., 2012). Furthermore, the case of General Motors’ 
understanding of the complex system dynamics in the used car industry made them 
choose the appropriate leasing strategy for their new cars, which provided an estimated 
$500 million savings compared to other car manufacturers (Sterman, 2003, p. 42). 
In the case of sustainability initiatives in a TBL-perspective, SD thinking can be 
effectively employed (Lee et al., 2012). Quantifying business cases through simulation 
models will require preliminary qualitative SD modelling. 
 
Need for supporting companies in applying SD 
SD modelling can effectively be practiced through the process of Group Model Building 
(GMB), which is a participatory process that can be defined as “the initiative to support a 
decision-making group in structuring a messy problem and designing effective policies to 
deal with it”, (Vennix, 1996) according to (Otto & Struben, 2003). 
Small separate exercises, called scripts, have been developed and consolidated in 
the GMB field as best practice templates. These are useful if aligned with each other to 
form a coherent methodology. In addition, the many techniques and principles in the 
application of SD modelling often require expertise, which is why SD modelling projects 
are usually facilitated by experts/consultants (Vennix, 1996, p. 266). Herein there is a need 
for directly supporting companies to apply SD in quantifying business cases for 
==PAGE:23==
 
5 
sustainability initiatives. 
More concretely, the prospect of this master thesis is to fulfil this need through a set of 
guidelines to elicit dynamic interdependencies through GMB of qualitative SD models 
(See subchapter 2.2.1). The guidelines should consist of a coherent methodology (See 
subchapter 1.2), and aim to be usable for professionals without prior experience in SD. 
The usability of the guidelines (whether they can be used for their intended task) 
depends on the intended use context, which makes the guidelines an object of user-
oriented design. The use context will determine both the form and content of the 
guideline, while its form will have appeal to the user types. 
The users are defined as specialists, middle managers and key managers, all of 
whom can participate by contributing useful knowledge in the task of modelling the 
dynamic problem. Moreover, the deliverables of the guidelines should be targeted 
towards relevant key managers with decision-power relevant to the specific business case. 
Requirements and criteria on the guidelines’ content, use context and user types will be 
further elaborated and specified through the systematic 
design process of developing the guidelines.

supporting business cases (Figure 1), the knowledge is

dynamic behaviour (and trade-offs) relevant for creating

1.1.2 Research scope and boundaries 
Scope definition (Included vs excluded): 
• 
The research revolves around the qualitative modelling through CLDs, which will 
create awareness and understanding of System Dynamics (Vennix, 1996, p. 108) 
related to the implementation of sustainability initiatives. The conjugated effects of 
several feedback mechanisms cannot be understood without quantitative 
simulation of the system model, but this is outside the boundaries of this research 
project. Hence, it is not possible to test how well the qualitative model supports 
business case creation. 
• 
The research revolves around the development of a set of guidelines (in whatever 
format it might be) and testing the usability (whether they can be used for the 
intended task) of qualitatively modelling.  
• 
The GMB methodology will be the main methodology applied for facilitating the 
modelling process, since this area is a consolidated area with a specific objective of 
SD approach (Hovmand et al., 2011; Vennix, 1996). 
 
1.1.3 Relation to PhD project 
The thesis plays a role in the PhD project “Framework for measuring sustainability 
performance of eco design implementation”. The outcome of this master thesis helps to 
systematise and put a logic and structure behind the relationships between Eco design 
Management Practices, their outputs, initial outcomes and the business performance 
outcomes, for business case creation (See subchapter 3.3.3)

1.1.4 Research objectives and process 
The main objective of this research is to develop a set of guidelines to support the 
creation of business cases for implementation of sustainability initiatives through Causal 
Loop Diagrams. 
 
Research Question #1 
“What is the most coherent methodology (guidelines) for supporting the creation of 
business cases for sustainability initiatives through Causal Loop Diagrams?” 
The premise is that qualitative modelling through CLDs will be crucial support for creating 
business cases for sustainability initiatives. The most coherent procedure for modelling in 
the given context will have to be developed and tested. 
 
Research Question #2 
“What are the most important business performance outcomes, when considering 
implementation of sustainability initiatives?” 
The objective of creating a business case, posits that business performance outcomes 
(BPOs) play a crucial role to the audience and framing of the implementation of 
sustainability initiatives. The most important BPOs will be defined deductively through a 
systematic literature review based on how frequently they recur in literature. The results 
will later have to be validated by companies with the objective of implementing 
sustainability related activities.

Research Question #3 
“How do the guidelines support the development of a business case for sustainability 
initiatives?” 
The guidelines’ usability will be tested in two use contexts of qualitative modelling to 
specify how they concretely support business case creation through their instructions and 
case specific approach.

Definition of “Business case” 
The term business case is defined as the set of arguments or rationales that supports and 
documents the reasons why the business community should accept or advance a certain 
cause (Carroll & Shabana, 2010). 
 
Definition of “Sustainability initiatives” 
The term sustainability initiative covers the many organisational management practices 
that can be implemented in a business for achieving sustainable development as defined 
by the Brundtland Commission (Brundtland, 1987): “Meeting the needs of the present 
without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs”. This 
definition is put in the perspective of the triple-bottom line of economic, environmental 
and social value creation. Among others, these initiatives include Eco design practices, 
Corporate Social Responsibility, Life Cycle Assessment, Design for Sustainability etc.   
 
Definition of “System Dynamics” 
The definition of the System Dynamics term used in this master thesis, is defined by 
(Sterman, 2003, p. vii): “System dynamics is a perspective and set of conceptual tools that 
enable us to understand the structure and dynamics of complex systems. System 
dynamics is also a rigorous modelling method that enables us to build formal computer 
simulations of complex systems and use them to design more effective policies and 
organizations.” 
 
Definition of “Group Model Building” 
In this master thesis Group Model Building is defined as “the initiative to support a 
decision-making group in structuring a messy problem and designing effective policies to 
deal with it” (Vennix, 1996) according to (Otto & Struben, 2003). 
==PAGE:28==
 
10 
 
Clarification of “Coherent methodology” 
The meaning of a coherent methodology encompasses both the usability of the 
guidelines (whether the guidelines can be used for the intended task) and the usefulness 
(whether the guidelines have the expected impact) (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009, p. 37) 
of which the latter is not able to be thoroughly evaluated in the scope of this master 
thesis project. 
The applied methodologies include System Dynamics modelling of Causal Loop 
Diagrams through the approach of Group Model Building in workshops with several 
relevant stakeholders. 
The coherence of the methodology can be defined by: 
• The alignment of GMB exercises, which determines how well the modelling 
workshops can be facilitated and knowledge can be shared and elaborated 
between participants. 
• The appropriate use of relevant CLD modelling techniques, which elicit key insights 
about the dynamic feedback mechanisms of the system model. 
 
The usability of the guidelines is indicated by whether the CLD: 
• Captures the most important dynamic mechanisms 
• Precisely identifies leverage points in the system 
• Communicates dynamic mechanisms and leverage points in a simple way 
• Can be simulated in a quantitative model later in the modelling process, which is 
not part of the scope. 
The usefulness of the guidelines is indicated by how well the CLD includes the above-
mentioned properties.

2.1.1 Defining the business case scope through Business 
Performance Outcomes 
The term business performance outcome (BPO) is in this context defined as an overall 
strategic indicator or measure, e.g. a Key Performance Indicator (Rodrigues, Pigosso, & 
McAloone, 2016b) that the company’s chief level managers use for prioritising activities in 
the company. Several terms fit this definition, such as internal/external drivers, strategic 
drivers (Pigosso, 2012, p. 146), business case drivers (Schaltegger et al., 2011) or business 
benefits (Grayson & Howard, 2011) and they range from measurable to qualitative. The 
important criterion is that the BPOs are considered on a strategic level rather than on the 
tactical and operational levels of the organisation. As framed in the introduction, taking a 
strategy level approach for a sustainability initiative can make it part of corporate strategy 
and make it a core business criterion to be implemented in many of the organisation’s 
business functions, such as the method of Sustainable Products and Service 
Developments addressed in (Maxwell & van der Vorst, 2003).

Taking a strategy level approach should ensure that companies with various preconditions 
and reasons for implementing sustainability initiatives will more easily be convinced with a 
business case (See Chapter 1). 
Modelling-wise, an important part of the scope definition for a business case will

to be targeted by the modelling project needs to be included in the guidelines.  
More concretely, a list of consolidated BPOs should form the scope definition 
exercise in the guidelines. Therefore, a brief systematic literature review was conducted 
with the objective of providing an overview of the most generally applied BPOs in 
businesses and later validated by a few companies working with sustainability initiatives.

2.2.1 Literature Review on existing CLD guidelines and GMB

A brief literature review was conducted to identify the key principles for qualitative Causal

A known item search provided the theoretical starting points of each methodological

• J. D. Sterman’s “Business Dynamics” (Sterman, 2003) concerning fundamental SD

• J.A.M. Vennix’s “Group Model Building” (Vennix, 1996) concerning the practice of

("guidelines" OR "best practice" OR "hints" OR "tips" OR "method") AND ("causal loop

Furthermore, many relevant sources were identified in the reference lists of the 
primary sources, which is also known as the “roll a snowball” approach. The databases of 
the literature review include Web of Science, Scopus and DTU Find it. 
 
Identification of the key principles for qualitative CLD modelling 
The brief literature review identified the key principles of the GMB approach and gave a 
general set of overarching GMB activities necessary for qualitative modelling of CLDs. 
The review identified several CLD guidelines that clearly stated a list of procedures to 
follow without the use of GMB. These were related to the overall System Dynamics

Developing the Design Specification 
The Design Specification (DS), also called a “Performance Specification” is reported to 
emphasise “… performance that a design solution has to achieve, and not any particular 
physical components which may be means of achieving that performance” (Cross, 2008, 
p. 106-116). The performance is represented by product attributes, which are prioritised 
in the categories of requirements (need to do) and criteria (nice to do), and ideally 
specified in terms of precise limits (Appendix A). 
In this master thesis project, the DS helped to manage the different aspects of the 
guidelines, namely the “content” (e.g. GMB exercise goals) to be included in the 
==PAGE:45==
 
27 
guidelines and the “format” of the guidelines, which influences the outcomes of the GMB 
exercises. 
Initially, the DS was general and comprised of different “guidelines types”, such as 
a protocol, poster and a slideshow. Later in the design process the DS was delimited to 
comprise more concrete guideline features based on an idea generation session and 
several evaluation meetings. 
 
Developing the initial guidelines 
In order to develop the initial set of guidelines the Morphological Chart method (MC) was 
applied. The purpose of the MC method is according to (Cross, 2008, p. 138-149) to 
identify feasible combinations between different sub solutions. Setting up the functions 
and means in a chart (See figure 12) enables the designer to draw possible combinations 
and get an overview of different concepts of a product, which fulfil the same core 
functions in different ways. 
This method can easily be aligned with the DS by listing the specified requirements 
and criteria (i.e. functions) against the different means by which these can be achieved 
(i.e. sub solutions). In the case of compiling and aligning GMB scripts into a coherent set 
of guidelines, the various scripts can be seen as different means to achieve the same 
GMB exercise goals (i.e. functions). 
The objectives of each GMB phase, or in other words functions to be achieved by 
scripts, were based on the database of Scriptapedia (“Scriptapedia”, 2017). From which 
the scripts were extracted and revisited through their primary source. 
 
Selection criteria 
Scripts were selected for the MC based on two qualitative criteria, they should be: 
a) Considered a best practice script by Scriptapedia 
b) Should mainly be targeted towards qualitative modelling

were: 1) Indirectly touches upon fulfilling the goal, 2) Partly relates to fulfil the goal, 3) 
Directly relates to fulfil the goal.

The second work package was presented initially in the methodology chapter. To

2.3.1 Contextual inquiry approach 
To test the guidelines’ usability in their intended use context, several application cases 
were set up and observed through the contextual inquiry approach. This is an “in situ” 
approach for gathering data/user statements that hint at where and why work processes 
breakdown or do not live up to their requirements. The observation/interview form of this 
approach, called The Master/Apprentice Model, relies on a relationship between the 
interviewee and interviewer, which resembles the partnership of a Master and an 
Apprentice (Beyer & Holzblatt, 1998, p. 42-55). It enables the interviewer to find issues, 
by observing details and structure of the work process and probe curiously to let the 
interviewee shape the interviewer’s understanding. The method should be understood in 
the light of the usual pitfalls of interviews, where interviewers solely get a list of questions 
answered, answer questions himself about the correct conduct of a work process or 
==PAGE:49==
 
31 
politely abstain from being nosy, which all could hinder useful insights about the work 
process under investigation. 
In the case of testing the guidelines through contextual inquiry, the user has an 
objective to model a useful qualitative CLD with the purpose of understanding a complex 
dynamic problem. The guidelines attempt to support the user in fulfilling this objective, 
by prescribing a set of theoretically based work processes for qualitative CLD modelling 
through GMB. 
During the tests, the role of the observer role was a combination between pure 
observation and being a modelling participant (apprentice). Users were observed and 
inquired through simultaneous verbalisation (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009, p. 262) with 
support from audio recording of interviews and note taking of the most important 
statements. The facilitator role alternated between the participants, and the observer 
stepped in as a “Master” when instructions could not be interpreted and understood by 
the participants. 
The focus of the observations was on participants’ behaviour and opinions when 
following the guidelines. The questions tried to clarify the participants’ opinions on 
whether the guidelines’ instructions, visualisations and educative steps were clear to the 
users during the modelling process. In this manner, even though the participants were 
novices, they were sometimes considered as “Masters”, who should explain their use of 
the guidelines. 
 
2.3.2 Data gathering and processing  
The data gathering was inspired by the Affinity Diagram method, which is a bottom-up 
categorisation of user statements, gathered during tests and evaluations. It is a structured 
approach for defining issues and key elements of a work process (Beyer & Holzblatt, 
1998, p. 154-163). The method is intended to be used for 200-1500 user statements. The

2.3.3 Use testing 
The validated set of initial guidelines had a generic and wide spanning set of scripts. They 
were tested during two pilot tests, to evaluate usability and evaluate the coherent 
methodology to continue the development. Based on results from the pilot test and 
several DTU evaluation meetings the generic guidelines would need to be instantiated by 
modifying the process and the scripts. This would provide more coherent and usable 
guidelines for the modelling processes. 
 
The guidelines and manual were tested in two different use contexts through 
two so-called instantiations of the generic guidelines. The “instantiation” analogy is 
borrowed from Object-Oriented Programming, where an instantiation is the creation of 
an instance of a generic object. Each instantiation is unique depending on the variations 
of the elements within the generic object (“Techopedia”, 2017). The main differences 
between the two versions will be covered in chapter 3 (Results). 
The tests were related to the overall research objective of Research Question #3: 
“Supporting the development of a business case creation for sustainability initiatives”, 
both from two concrete business cases of sustainability initiatives. In both use tests the 
usability of the guidelines (whether the guidelines can be used for the intended task) was

merely be indicated through the tests. The intended task of the guidelines is fulfilled 
through the coherent methodology defined in subchapter 3.2.2.

3.2.1 Identification of key principles for qualitative modelling 
From the literature review it was evident that CLDs are mostly applied as qualitative 
precursors for quantitative modelling (Sterman, 2003, p. 191). The key principles 
consolidated in this review stay within the qualitative scope, where it is possible. 
Meadows (Meadows & Wright, 2009) provide a consolidated overview of Stocks & Flows-
diagrams, which is the quantitative modelling methodology of SD, and although it relates 
to many qualitative aspects, majority of the principles are quantitative and not relevant in 
this context. Moreover, it is evident that many parts of Sterman’s representation of SD in 
his “Business Dynamics” book, integrates important aspects from Meadows’ research. 
According to Sterman, CLDs are excellent for: 
“Quickly capturing your hypotheses about the causes of dynamics; 
Eliciting and capturing the mental models of individuals or teams;  
Communicating the important feedbacks you believe are responsible for a problem.” 
(Sterman, 2003, p. 137). 
 
A Causal Loop Diagram (CLD) is a system model which encompasses the cause and effect 
chains between variables, such as population size and birth rate. The CLD provides a 
visual and qualitative overview of the system’s structure, which can be used to understand 
the dynamic behaviour of the system and show points of intervention that create a 
desired outcome. In the following simplified example of population growth dynamics 
==PAGE:62==

(inspired by Sterman (2003)), the basic idea behind qualitative CLD modelling will be 
demonstrated. 
Through causal links, variables either affect an increase or decrease on each other. 
The polarity of an arrow (i.e. causal link) is either “+” or “-“,  which describes the structure 
of the system; if the birth rate increases (under the premise that everything else is held 
equal) it will increase the population: 
 
And vice versa: any aspect that decreases the birth rate, e.g. the use of prevention, will 
decrease the population size below what it otherwise would have been: 
 
Notice that the behaviour in the system is dependent on the case, i.e. if use of prevention 
increases. The polarities of the causal links are the same (the system structure), illustrating 
what would happen if the variables were to change. E.g. a decrease in the use of 
prevention will increase the birth rate above what it would otherwise have been. 
When several variables form a closed chain of causal relationships it forms a feedback 
loop. In the case of birth rate and population, it is a reinforcing loop (R), because a bigger

The literature review provided several examples of CLD guidelines with an alternative (or 
little) relation to GMB approaches. Some approaches are highlighted to exemplify how 
diversely CLD modelling can be fitted to specific use contexts. 
a) The guidelines in Kim (1992) are visual and precise and relate to the 
technicalities of drawing a CLD. They could be targeted towards an individual, although 
the use context is not specified. The guidelines are in line to most of the basic SD 
principles stated by Sterman, but if they were used in a group modelling context without 
further notion, they would risk developing biased mental models and promote a sense of 
false confidence during problem solving (Hovmand et al., 2011). 
==PAGE:67==
 
 
49 
b) Jafari et al. (2008) creates a preliminary CLD model based on several individual 
interviews and a systematic approach for identifying the common sense between different 
stakeholders’ interpretations through a decision-making evaluation method called 
DEMATEL (Gabus & Fontela, 1973) according to (Jafari et al., 2008). Developing a 
preliminary model, a so-called “concept model”, before assembling participants, is 
considered a best practice script (Hovmand et al., 2011; Richardson, 2013), however the 
merge with the cause-effect method of DEMATEL is interesting in its systematic and 
mathematical properties. As stated by the authors the intended context of the script is 
characterised by uncertainty and disagreement about the problem formulation and the 
potential solution types. But in contexts where this is not the case, the many individual 
interviews might be more time consuming than basing a preliminary model on a meeting 
with one participant with key insights and bring this to the GMB session with more 
participants. 
c) In (Otto & Struben, 2003), the non-GMB specific “standard method” adapted 
from (Hines, 2001) was used as a framework for GMB sessions of quantitative scope. The 
authors conclude that their approach provides a beneficial “flexibility for intuition” in an 
otherwise highly structured GMB process. However, the approach conceptualised a CLD 
that exceeded the level of comprehension for the participants and pushed the system 
boundaries too far, and that could probably have been avoided if following a more 
consolidated GMB approach. 
d) In (Vermaak, 2016) five rather intuitive steps (besides a preparation and post-
phase) are intended to provide an effective CLD in a change management context. The 
first two steps rely heavily on intuition, e.g. “use your gut feeling” and “sense a 
storyline”, which can lead to false confidence and biased models. However, the 
guidelines purposefully apply basic CLD principles with emphasis on attaining a visually 
clear and impactful diagram through redrawing techniques. The guidelines integrate an 
influence analysis framework (Probst & Gomez, 1991) to identify the intervention points 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
50 
with the highest leverage, which seems to be a useful way of communicating the CLD 
results in a more presentable and applicable way to the audience, also relevant for a 
business case. 
(Vermaak, 2016) highlights the important balance between the properties of a 
CLD: “They need to be rich enough to capture underlying mechanisms, precise enough 
to spot leverage, but also simple enough so that most important dynamics clearly stand 
out”. 
Considering the various outcomes of examples a)-d) the balance between these 
properties is possible to achieve with a coherent set of GMB to support the CLD 
modelling. The richness of the underlying mechanisms can be increased by sharing 
mental models, e.g. through the divergent GMB activities. The precision and simplicity 
can be achieved through the educative parts of the presentation activities, which ensures 
that the CLD techniques are followed appropriately, and especially through a 
combination of the convergent and evaluative activities of GMB, which ensures that the 
mental models are consolidated and ranked accordingly. 
It is reasonable to assume that this balance can be achieved through some of the 
intuitive approaches seen in the examples of CLD guidelines, but intuition relies heavily 
on the experience of the modellers. A CLD based on intuition is unreliable and makes 
GMB less scientific and more of a craft. This issue was already articulated in 1997 by 
(Andersen et al., 1997b) as a need to “…identify what aspects or combinations of 
activities within facilitated group model-building conferences lead to more successful 
outcomes”, which sparked a structured theory development within GMB research. 
Among other things promoted through the invention of scripts (Andersen & Richardson, 
1997) and a consolidation of best practice scripts on the database Scriptapedia (Hovmand 
et al., 2011).

Facilitating a coherent methodology 
For GMB efforts to be effective, several facilitative roles have been defined by 
(Richardson & Andersen, 1995) in a context of a consultant team and a client company, 
the roles and their primary responsibilities are: 
a) The Facilitator: Facilitates the GMB process 
b) The Modeller/Reflector: Helps the group to reflect on and recognise issues and 
insights, which become evident during the modelling. 
c) The Process coach: Observes the group process with focus on participants’ 
experience of the session, to evaluate how the session is going. 
d) The Recorder: Takes notes of definitions and terms used in the conversations 
during the modelling session. 
e) The Gatekeeper: Ensures that the modelling project meets the needs of the 
client organisation/community, and communicates the results to the client. 
The roles are usually delegated to consultants or persons with responsibility for the 
modelling project, and often several roles can be performed by one individual 
(Richardson & Andersen, 1995). The roles cover different aspects of the "right attitude", 
which will contribute to the effectiveness of the group process (Vennix, 1996, p. 147). 
These include having an attitude of inquiry when sharing mental models in the search for 
the most objective representation of reality, and having a helping and neutral attitude 
towards others' interpretations, e.g. so divergent activities will provide as many mental 
models as possible.

roles by communicating their aspects through instructions, so one individual can facilitate 
the modelling process as a main facilitator. 
 
3.2.3 Elements from User Interface design 
Based on the visual communication of CLD modelling and the incremental learning 
process of GMB, an important aspect of the guidelines’ usefulness (whether the 
guidelines have the expected impact) (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009, p. 37) is how the 
information can be presented to the users, which is one of two key issues in User-interface 
development (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009, p. 295). 
The guidelines’ communicative capabilities are therefore in focus, so besides 
classic product design methods, relevant aspects of User Interface (UI) and User 
Experience (UX) design theory were applied. Although UX theory is mostly applied in the 
field of software products, these aspects can be relevant to other types of products, 
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009, p. 295). 
==PAGE:76==
 
 
58 
In short, user interface creates user experience (“Interaction Design Foundation”, 
2017) while UX methods can generally be useful for achieving user-friendly solutions, e.g. 
defined as: “A person's perceptions and responses resulting from the use and/or 
anticipated use of a product, system or service” by ISO 9241-110:2010, (Vermeeren et al., 
2010). 
More concretely, UI elements, such as the goal & task completeness, flexibility, 
learnability, user satisfaction and modifiability (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009, p. 296) are 
relevant properties of the guidelines, which are specified in the DS (Appendix A). 
 
Developing a Design Specification 
The well-defined set of GMB activities and sources of consolidated best practice scripts 
(Hovmand et al., 2011) helped to predefine many requirements and criteria that were 
implemented in the DS.  Under the category “GMB exercise goals” they include absolute 
outputs such as “elicit variables” and “identifying feedback loops”. The quality of these 
outputs depends on attributes related to the “format of the guidelines” as well as how 
successfully the facilitative GMB roles are integrated in the guidelines. Especially the 
intangible and soft UI aspects, are the most unspecific yet important factors for 
supporting companies by means of a coherent methodology, because they help 
communicate the details of the GMB and CLD aspects. The difficulty of specifying these 
aspects is one of the important limitations of how the DS methodology was applied in this 
research project (See Limitations chapter 6).

Flexibility within the time frame 
The time frame of modelling projects is difficult to restrict without compromising the

feature of the methodology, because scripts can help practitioners engage in double-
loop learning (Schön, 1983) according to (Hovmand et al., 2011). The guidelines already 
included a Goal & Scope definition step, so the choice to extend the process could be 
considered in relation to the scope of the modelling project.

As a proposal to meet the above-mentioned time frame issue, RL suggested a need for 
dividing the modelling process in several workshops. This is a well-known strategy in

Intuitive nomenclature 
Since the theoretical SD jargon is naturally hard to grasp for participants who are 
unfamiliar with the theory, RL suggested to use an easily understandable nomenclature. 
 
Inspiration for guidelines format 
RL reported that he applies incrementally progressing slideshow presentations during 
lectures when modelling with participants, who are unfamiliar with the SD approach. 
According to him, benefits from this format includes:  
1) Higher degree of visual communication of the SD approach,  
2) Keeping participants at the same step of the procedure, which ensures a stepwise 
process, and  
3) The opportunity for clarifying details of the techniques along the modelling.

4 Discussion of results 
Developing a tailor-made set of guidelines targeted for an audience who are 
inexperienced in the methodological area of SD is ambitious. The founders of 
Scriptapedia emphasise the risk of promoting SD “lite” and false confidence when 
increasing access to the methodology while not developing deeper knowledge in SD 
(Hovmand et al., 2011). The intention of making an accessible and widely appealing set of 
generic guidelines is similar to the purpose of the accessible “scripts”. The guidelines 
take this intent a step further by its synthesised chain of scripts that merely require the 
users to read through the manual and apply them. The guidelines intend to be in line with 
Scriptapedia to “make it easier to delineate where professional judgment is required and 
applied” (Hovmand et al., 2011) and based on test results, this seems possible to a 
certain degree. Integrating an external validation in the modelling process will decrease 
the risk of developing erroneous models. 
The guidelines’ format, user interface and communicative capabilities should 
enable access for a broad user audience, but the slideshow format is only one design 
path out of many. The notion of another interesting path (poster format) emerged from 
suggestions from the test evaluations and the experiences from the BPO validation 
workshops. The poster format proved efficient for communicating exercises, capturing 
participants’ attention and facilitating discussions. This could be relevant for the repetitive 
modelling tasks (i.e. reviewing and extending each causal links systematically) during an 
extensive model such as the EcoM2 model. Thus, it is suggested for future research to 
investigate the opportunities of combining slideshow facilitation with modelling on 
posters. 
  
The guidelines can potentially enable the identification of trade-offs and barriers 
through the understanding of complex feedback mechanisms. But these mechanisms 
obviously rely on the data behind, which in the EcoM2 context seem to be focused on the 
==PAGE:116==
 
 
98 
opportunistic side of the sustainability implementation. If the model solely focuses on the 
opportunities, the final business case simulation may not address important barriers for 
pursuing the business case, e.g. perceived risks (Short et al., 2012). The guidelines 
explicitly address barriers in the goal & scope definition, and furthermore touch upon it 
through the modelling process. But to substantiate possible risks of a business case more 
emphasis will have to be put on this in future research, e.g. by implementing an exercise 
for eliciting capability eroding loops. 
How effectively the guidelines support business case creation in the field of 
sustainability initiatives is not possible to conclude from the results of this master thesis. 
However, considering that a qualitative model is an important step for grasping feedback 
mechanisms and potential trade-offs, its relevance for business cases is clear. If the 
guidelines are targeted for a much broader spectrum of implementation projects rather 
than sustainability initiatives is not unlikely, due to the widely covering methodology of 
the generic set of guidelines. 
  
Compared to problem modelling method called Decision Making Trial and 
Evaluation Laboratory (DEMATEL) the strength of this statistical approach is that a 
problem model can be substantiated by considering the perceptions of many 
participants. As seen in (Jafari et al., 2008) the DEMATEL method seem to be useful in 
the context of drawing CLDs, which makes it relevant to consider for applying in future 
research, e.g. in the quantitative modelling phase. 
As exemplified by the tests in the two use contexts, the SD methodology enables a 
more comprehensive understanding of the complex organisational systems. In this light, 
taking the whole TBL value creation into consideration, while being aware of the SD 
perspective, seems like a useful alternative to traditional, short-term, profit-oriented 
approaches of management (Lozano et al., 2015). As long as the key selling point of a 
sustainability business case encompasses all three TBL-dimensions, the SD approach 
seems to be particularly well suited for investigating how these three dimensions can be 
==PAGE:117==
 
 
99 
integrated into the business activities and corporate strategy, which would make the 
business case more substantial (Schaltegger et al., 2011). It is up to future research to 
provide tangible or measurable results to underpin how useful the SD approach implies 
to be.

5 Conclusion 
Work Package 1 
The set of guidelines were defined based on the literature review with consolidated 
sources of the methodological fields of System Dynamics, Causal Loop Diagrams and 
Group Model Building. The validation by an SD expert and a subsequent proof of 
concept through a pilot test suggest that the guidelines encompass a coherent 
methodology. The coherence of the methodology is defined as the alignment of GMB 
exercises, which determines how well the modelling workshops can be facilitated and 
knowledge can be shared and elaborated between participants. A coherent methodology 
also includes the appropriate use of relevant CLD modelling techniques, which elicit key 
insights about the dynamic feedback mechanisms of the system model. 
 
Work Package 2 
A systematic literature review in the Web of Science database provided 199 articles of 
which 54 were selected. 360 strategic level Business Performance Outcomes were 
identified and extracted from the sources. These were categorised into 21 subcategories 
based on their recurrence in literature. Majority of them (76%) were directly related to 
economic goals due to the dominant research area of business economics among 
studies. To include BPOs related to environmental and social value creation a low nominal 
boundary for recurrence was set to 6. 
During validation process of the BPOs, companies primarily prioritised BPOs of the 
financial category, but the “secondary BPOs” seem to reveal the identity of the specific 
company and are at least as important when creating a business case, because they relate 
to the companies’ value propositions. BPOs are also dependent on which key decision 
maker the business case targets. From this empirical standpoint, it seems that business 
cases for sustainability initiatives need to be financially substantiated and should also 
==PAGE:119==
 
 
101 
consider BPOs related to the company’s value proposition to be successful. 
Research Question #2 “What are the most important business performance 
outcomes, when considering implementation of sustainability initiatives?” cannot be 
answered unequivocally, since the validation of BPOs were highly dependent on the 
specific company. Thus, the guidelines should include the process of identifying context 
dependent BPOs as a part of the goal & scope definition step.  
 
Work Package 3 
The test results from use test#1 were clusters of causal links, consisting of clearly defined 
EMPs, outputs and initial outcomes in knowledge area 7 of the EcoM2 model. These 
results were brought to use test #2, where the manual and guidelines were tested 
through facilitation by one of the participants unfamiliar with SD. The basic SD and CLD 
principles were incrementally introduced to the participants and enabled them to 
systematically review and extend the predefined causal links of selected EMPs. 
  
The successful development of feedback loops in use test#3 suggests that the step 
can be used successfully in the EcoM2 context as well. The usability of the guidelines was 
indicated by the CLD developed in use test#3, which a) captured important dynamic 
mechanisms, b) precisely identified leverage points in the system, c) communicates 
dynamic mechanisms and leverage points in a simple way and d) can be simulated as a 
quantitative model. 
  
The Research Question #3, “How do the guidelines support the development of a 
business case for sustainability initiatives?” can be answered through the concrete use 
context of the EcoM2 instantiation. The guidelines support above mentioned through 
scoping the business case (Step A), which based on the BPO validation seems important 
to be aware of for sustainability business cases with a TBL balance to fulfil. 
  
Furthermore, supported by the instructions for modelling the core aspects of a 
CLD through a bottom-up approach, including unambiguous variables (Step B), reviewed

Synthesis 
Based on the results from use test#1, #2 and #3 the guidelines are usable for qualitative 
modelling because they encompass a coherent methodology of SD principles, CLDs 
techniques facilitated through best practice GMB exercises. 
Although the test size has not built statistically significant claims, the results 
indicate that the coherent methodology in the instantiated guidelines can be used for 
modelling qualitative models that later can be simulated in quantitative models. 
Ultimately, this is likely to support business case creation in the general context of 
implementing sustainability initiatives, as exemplified through the EcoM2 context.

6 Limitations 
Empirical 
The size and coverage of the Systematic literature review is a clear limitation of this 
master thesis, since Web of Science does not index all journals. More sources could 
beneficially substantiate the categorisation of BPOs, e.g. by including the Scopus 
database. 
 
The categorisation process of the identified BPOs is susceptible to the qualitative 
judgements of whether BPOs fit one subcategory or another. The overlap between some 
subcategories due to their intentionally flexible definitions also contributes to uncertainty 
in the validation results, however the validation results were concluded to be relevant and 
sufficient. 
The use of the wikibook-database Scriptapedia (“Scriptapedia,” 2017) is 
susceptible to subjective iterations of the scripts, which are applied in the guidelines. To 
counter-balance this source of error, the primary sources behind the scripts were revisited 
for the scripts. 
Besides, user evaluations could have been increased by including focus groups, 
which would have generated discussion during evaluations, contrary to the individual and 
small group evaluations mainly setup in this project.  
 
Methodology 
The DS of the guidelines encompasses many qualitative measures of functionalities, which 
are susceptible to subjective evaluation, especially in relation to the format, e.g. the User 
Interface aspect, learnability: “Ensure that participants learn about the system dynamics 
of sustainability initiatives in the company”. The DS could have been even more specific, 
but in the scope of the master project, the aggregation level was kept on a general level 
in relation to the format, to prioritise the functionality of the coherent methodology. 
==PAGE:122==
 
 
104 
The bottom-up categorisation of improvement areas, mostly considered the means 
to solve issues. So, the sub solutions for each area are susceptible to the small group of 
user suggestions and my own creative capacity. In a project with a longer time frame each 
iterative loop should include improvement areas with a defined functionality. This would 
enable the development of several alternative means of solving an observed issue, which 
would result in a set of guidelines with a more substantiated functionality.

7 Reflection 
I have learnt a lot from the process of the master thesis project, both in terms of new 
theoretical areas and knowledge, and in terms of new methodological skills, such as 
conducting a systematic literature review. 
Managing a project of this size has been the biggest meta-challenge for me as a 
team-player character. The project plan was separated into three phases with a distinct 
work package structure, which should ensure a work load spread evenly out through the 
process. Although deadlines were mostly met at the due date, the quality of the 
deliverables could have been better, e.g. at the first milestone presentation where the 
initial guidelines were merely a concept of a set of guidelines. They were not ready for 
validation as planned, which meant that the process was delayed 1,5 weeks, which is 
evident from the Gantt-charts before and after (Appendix K). It was a classic trade-off 
between progressing in the process vs. documenting the work outputs as more than 
bullet point notations. During in the project there was uncertainty about what specific 
work should be documented for the report, which was impossible to decide at the given 
moment, and had to be decided in the end of the process. To keep up with the pace of 
the plan the effort of documenting the development process was decreased, which lead 
to a high work burden in the end. 
One of the factors that contributed to the uncertainty is presumably a general 
challenge of project management; the difficulty of estimating the time required for each 
task. Encouraged by my supervisors, the plan was “operationalised” by specifying the 
different activities and specifying them carefully into tasks, which helped steering the 
project towards deadlines. Retrospectively, the operationalisation could have been more 
effective if updated more often and followed more strictly – nevertheless it is certainly a 
technique I will use in my future work life.
