1. Abstract 
 
The aim of this Master’s Thesis is to validate a new technique for measuring the isobaric heat 
capacity. The measured heat capacities are combined with density results either from literature or 
from experimental results in the lab, in order to generate Joule – Thomson coefficients.  
A differential scanning calorimeter (DSC) apparatus Seratam C80 Calvet calorimeter, which 
operates up to 10 MPa, is used for the heat capacity measurement. In order to perform 
measurements at higher pressures, we designed and machined new cells, which can operate up to 
150 MPa. However, our current study is conducted at pressures up to 60 MPa.  
During the experimental study, first it was measured the heat capacity of n-tetradecane with the 
original cells derived with the C80 calorimeter, as a verification procedure of the technique. The 
results of these experimental heat capacity values are compared with those of literature and then 
combined with bibliographic density values in order to calculate the Joule – Thomson coefficient. 
After this measurement, the new high pressure cells were tested and used for heat capacity 
measurements of n-decane up to 60 MPa. In addition, experimental density measurements were 
conducted with a densimeter apparatus in the lab at pressures up to 140 MPa and temperatures 
up to 463.15K. Both experimental heat capacity and density results are used together in order to 
determine the Joule – Thomson coefficient of n-decane. In order to obtain from the measured 
density values, those density values in the corresponding temperature and pressure conditions of 
the heat capacity tests (and subsequently of the JT coefficient), a modified form of the Tait – 
Tammann equation is applied.  All these three determined properties are compared with existing 
literature data. Moreover, a methane - n-decane mixture (xmethane=0.8499 mole fraction) is 
measured with the DSC apparatus at 40MPa and 60MPa (Psat,mix=35.98MPa at 298.21K) and then 
the JT coefficient was determined. Again from bibliographic references, both heat capacity and JT 
coefficient are compared with the experimental measurements.

2. Introduction 
 
The growing demand for oil has resulted in on the one hand different techniques used to produce 
more oil (chemical flooding, gas injection, steam flooding, etc.), and on the other hand, production 
from reservoirs with unique characteristics (low permeable layers, shale formation reservoirs, 
higher depth of reservoirs leading to high-temperature and high-pressure conditions). Because of 
the improvements of technology, the unconventional reservoirs which are economically non-
profitable a few years ago, can become worthy of production. Upon this opportunity, it is 
inevitable the better understanding of the reasons that influence the oil production, regarding also 
the temperature changes of the reservoir fluids.  
The temperature variations in an oil or gas field, are strongly connected with the Joule – Thomson 
(JT) effect. This phenomenon is observed when a fluid passes through a contraction valve, a 
restriction or a capillary tube (Pinto et al., 2013), (Perry & Green, 1984), (Escobedo & Chen, 2001) 
and the isenthalpic pressure drop results to a temperature change of the fluid, either upwards or 
downwards, depending on the PVT conditions.  JT coefficient (JTC) is a property – tool of great 
assistance in cases such as the clarification of the water and gas intervals in reservoir drilling 
exploitation procedures, through a temperature log data (Pinto et al., 2013). In this log readings, 
the JTC has been used as the input characteristic. Nonetheless, the accuracy of the JTC influences 
a lot the reservoir behaviour during depletion and the magnitude of this property has been used 
for temperature prediction of the exploited fields.  
App (2009) has reported that the non-isothermal behaviour of the reservoir fluids is because of 
the JT effect. More specifically, it is mentioned that this effect is more pronounced at large 
pressure drawdowns during depletion. When the pressure is high enough (mostly for deep 
reservoirs), then due to JT effect, it is usually observed an increase of the reservoir temperature 
(negative JTC values), whereas when the pressure is low (such as in case of the gas reservoirs) then 
the reservoir fluids’ temperature, decreases (positive JTC values). In another study (App 2010), 
there have been performed experiments in order to investigate more extensively the dependence 
of temperature profile transients. When the production rates are changing and especially after the 
beginning of a build-up test, there have been noticed significant temperature changes over time. 
This changing rates are quite high in the beginning of the procedure and it is reported that they 
are due to local pressure transients that influence the productivity of the wells. Temperature 
transient profiles can change significantly among low permeable reservoirs (Sui et al. 2008). 
Results of this study revealed that pressure and temperature transient profiles are influenced 
differently by formation characteristics, such as the skin factor and the permeability of the layer.  
The direct method of predicting the JTC is by measuring the temperature changes upon a 
throttling process, knowing, of course, the resulted pressure expansion. In addition, Bessières et 
al. (2006) have used the Monte Carlo simulations combined with a pressure – controlled scanning 
calorimetry to measure experimentally JT inversion curves upon a large temperature and pressure 
range.

In this thesis project it was performed an indirect method of calculating the JTC by experimental 
measuring of other two thermodynamic properties; density and heat capacity. According to basic 
thermodynamic laws, it was derived (section 4.4) the basic equation that is being used and it a 
function of density, heat capacity and the derivative of density with respect to temperature. The 
estimation of JTC by measuring experimentally other properties has the advantage of better 
acknowledgement of the system, as they are presented more thermodynamic properties. In 
comparison with the direct method, it is a simpler procedure, the result of which depends on the 
uncertainties of the corresponding measurable properties. In general, these properties have been 
measured for some oil compounds but not a wide range of literature is available at high pressures 
and high temperatures, in which range this project thesis is based on. Heat capacity experimental 
measurements have also been studied with lubricants and oil derivatives such as paraffin or 
naphthenic mineral oils (Larsson & Andersson, 2000).

3. Literature review 
 
Literature for experimental density and heat capacity measurements of n-alkanes is not very 
adequate in the case of high pressure and high temperature conditions. From the bibliographic 
research that has been conducted, not many references were found for heat capacities of n-
tetradecane. Except abundant research for binary mixtures of n-tetradecane, only Khasanshin TS & 
Shchemelev (2002) have measured bulk density and isobaric heat capacity of this n-alkane at 
temperature range between 293.15 K until 433.15K and pressures up to 140MPa.  
Density of n-tetradecane has also been introduced by a model calculation of Cibulka and 
Hnédkovský (1996) in the range 293 K – 358 K and 0.89 MPa – 366.8 MPa. From the same source, 
there are reported also parameters for calculating density of n-decane in temperatures 248.15 K – 
613.15 K and pressures 0.2 MPa – 784.6 MPa. 
In the case of heat capacity for n-decane, experimental data up to 10 MPa and 318.15 K – 373.15 K 
has been reported by Banipal et al. (1991) and up to 300 MPa and 673 K by Gehrig & Lentz (1983). 
In addition, Gerasimov et al. (2001) have proposed a model that calculates isobaric heat capacities 
from the triple point to the critical with a temperature applicable range 243.5K – 617.6K. Also a 
model has been proposed for n-alkanes, including n-decane, for heat capacity measurements by 
Růžička et al. (1991) for temperatures 247K – 462K. Furthermore, there have been proposed two 
wide range suitable models of calculating heat capacities and other thermodynamic properties. 
The first is called GERG 2008 by Kunz and Wagner (2012) and it is an equation of state pertinent to 
calculating properties for compounds of natural gas. It involves two applicable ranges, one strict 
which varies from temperatures 90K – 450K and pressures up to 35 MPa and one extended with 
larger uncertainties and validity range up to 700K and up to 70 MPa. The other model has been 
introduced by Lemmon and Span (2006), which is an equation of state. Lastly, there has been 
noticed a reference from Finke et al. (1954) for low temperature range validity (12.28K - 318.62K).  
In addition, no much available literature data was available for heat capacities/JTC of the mixture 
methane – n-decane, expect Regueira et al. (2016) and the GERG 2008 model of calculating 
thermodynamic properties of gas/oil fluids.

4. Experimental section 
 
In this section, the materials used during the experimental procedure are presented as well as the 
methodological sequence that has been followed and also the principles behind the techniques.  
More specifically, after mentioning the materials that have been used throughout the procedures, 
there are described the experimental apparatus, including their measuring principles of their 
operation.  Therefore, there are underlined the experimental setup and procedures for both 
techniques, such as the cleaning method, the preparation of the samples and last but not least the 
calibration procedure which is always necessary for the apparatus reliability. In the final part of 
this chapter, there is given a short theoretical background and the physical meaning of the Joule – 
Thomson coefficient (μJT), the property on which this project thesis is based on. More specifically, 
after a sequence of mathematical equations, it is revealed why and how an indirect experimental 
procedure of this property has been implemented, instead of a direct measuring method in a 
throttling pipe system.

The above-mentioned densimeter is a typical apparatus of a vibrating tube densimeter assembly. 
The oscillator is a U-shape tube containing the measuring fluid. The principle behind this technique 
is based on the fact that the frequency of the vibration of the tube is dependent on the mass of 
the oscillator and as a result on the density of the liquid (Lagourette et al. 1992). During the 
experimental procedure the oscillation period, τ (μs), of the vibrating tube is measured and 
through an equation, density ρ of the fluid is obtained.  This technique is proved by many works 
(Regueira 2013, Comuñas et al. 2008) to provide reliable results of liquid densities (estimated 
uncertainty ±0.5 kg m-3 or ±0.05%), as mechanical oscillators are quite accurate machines.

4.2.2 Calorimeter 
 
The Setaram C80 Calorimeter was used for measurements of heat capacity. The Differential 
Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) or Calorimeter is the thermo-analytical technique, where a differential 
heat flow 𝑞𝑞̇ (mW) is measured over time between two cells; one cell is always empty and the 
other contains the measuring sample.  
In figure 4.3 and 4.4 below, a schematic graph and a picture of the experimental setup are 
illustrated, respectively. The calorimeter device consists of one chamber, in which there are placed 
two cells; one reference and one measurement cell.

connections is the reference cell and on the left there is the cell with the sample 
The reference cell is always empty, whereas the measurement cell contains our sample. The cells 
are located inside the chamber from two holes at the top of the device, so as only the bottom part 
of the connection, which is the cell (no. 3 in figure 4.5), is placed inside the furnace of the 
chamber. The 2nd part of the connection of figure 4.5 is also inside the chamber but no furnace is

outlet.

In every experiment, the results from the pressure transducer SIKA are electronically recorded 
(maximum pressure at 150MPa). The ISCO pump keeps the pressure in constant level throughout 
the procedure by pumping in or out water to the metal high-pressure cylinder with the sample. 
The variations of the pressure occur because of the temperature increase of the fluid in the

close to the furnace which is only in the level of the cell (no.3), is influenced the most by this 
temperature difference. Because of the density change of the sample due to the temperature 
change, the pressure is being adjusted by the pump in order to remain constant. This 
phenomenon of convection, which is happening due to temperature difference of the sample 
between part no.2 and no.3 of the connections due to the heating transport process, is affecting a 
lot the measures, as it will be discussed later.

4.3 Methods 
 
In this section there are going to be described the methods that have been followed during the 
experimental procedures. These are the cleaning procedure of the apparatus between different 
fluid measurements, the preparation of the sample for each experimental test as well as the 
calibration procedure that was followed in order to generate more precise results. 
4.3.1 Cleaning procedure 
 
In order to start a new experiment, each time we have to be sure that our system does not contain 
any other substances that will contaminate our sample. As a result, an extensively good cleaning 
procedure is required. This process is resolved in three stages:  
1. Charge with toluene all the wiring/tubing of the assembly that the sample will be displaced 
through. The flowrate should be low in order for toluene to dissolve all of impurities  
2. Charge the same part of the assembly with ethanol, also in low flow rate 
3. Let all the wiring dry (if possible also pump in some air) and then make vacuum in the 
system for a couple of hours 
After the system has been in vacuum condition (20 – 30 Pa) for some hours, the sample is loaded 
in the system and left until it is in steady state condition.  
 
4.3.2 Filling procedure 
 
In this section, the procedure of sample preparation in both apparatus densimeter and 
calorimeter is described and it is focused on the experimental procedure that has been followed.   
 
4.3.2.1 Densimeter 
 
As it has already been mentioned in the cleaning procedure (section 4.3.1), the sample is loaded in 
the apparatus, after the latter has been in vacuum for some hours. Once the 1st valve is shut off 
(V1 in figure 4.1) and the vacuum pump is disconnected, the glass funnel with the sample is 
connected. Before opening the valve again, a few drops are left to leak out in order to make sure 
that no air is left between the valve and the funnel. After the sample has been inserted in the 
22 
 
==PAGE:23==
apparatus, the piston pressure intensifier is opened slowly enough in order for the sample in the 
funnel to remains abundant (by refilling it) and no air comes in the apparatus. Once the piston is 
completely opened, valve 3 opens in order to make sure that the sample is inserted in the whole 
assembly of the apparatus. After some fluid has leak out of this valve, both valves 1 and 3 are shut 
down and the sample is left to reach the desirable temperature and pressure condition for the 
measurement.  
In order for a measurement to start, the temperature and pressure have to be stable for at least 
10 mins. By stable, there are implied the same accuracies as described in the apparatus section 
(4.2.1). The criterion of keeping a good experimental measurement in order to use it in the 
average within a constant temperature (±0.05K) and pressure (±0.01MPa) condition, is the 
resulted period between those measurements, not to exceed the difference (between minimum 
and maximum value that is measured) of 0.007 μs. 
 
4.3.2.2 Calorimeter 
 
The sample is first placed in the cylinder, after the latter has been cleaned with toluene and 
ethanol and also after an appropriate volume of sample has been considered to be filled in. By 
appropriate here, it is implied that the volume of the sample will be abundant for the assembly, 
regarding the tubing connection plus the volume of the cell, but not excessively abundant in order 
to reduce the cost of the procedure and of course the unneeded use of chemicals. So, after the 
sample has been placed in the cylinder, then the latter gets connected to the system and all the 
valves get open, except the first one (V1), connecting the refilling reservoir and the ISCO pump.  
In order to place the sample in the cell and also to keep the process isobaric throughout the 
measurement, a pump is connected to a cylinder and the latter is connected to the calorimeter 
cell, as shown in figure 4.3. The cylinder contains a piston interface that separates the two liquids 
contained inside; there is water in the side connected to the pump and the sample in the other 
side. Because of the pump, there can be achieved constant pressure in the cell during the whole 
experiment, by pumping in or out water in the cylinder. More specifically, there is a cell in the 
pump of specific volume (equal to 103 mL) that is refilled from a small reservoir of water and it is 
connected to the cylinder. When the valve between the refill reservoir and the cell of the pump is 
closed, the pump can control a steady level of constant pressure (maximum 650 bar) or constant 
flow (maximum 50 ml/min) by sucking out or in water between cell and cylinder because of the 
constant volume cell of the pump.  
The container is filled up with the fluid of measurement until liquid comes out from the outlet 
(valve V5 in fig. 4.3). Then the valve before the outlet is turn off and the sample is left over until it 
reaches the appropriate temperature in the furnace. 
To begin with, a constant flow is applied to the system, in order to ensure that the sample has 
been displaced through all the tubing and the calorimeter cell. Eventually, after some liquid from 
the outlet (figure 4.3) is observed, the flow is cut off. Then, if the experiment is taken place in 
23 
 
==PAGE:24==
ambient pressure, the assembly remains with all the valves open and the measuring cell is placed 
inside the calorimeter furnace. The cell will be left there until the flowrate signal between the 
measuring and reference cell is very low (typical values are between -0,07 mW and -0,12 mW) and 
constant for at least half hour (usually this takes ca. 3 hours). If the pressure though is not 
ambient, the outlet is been sealed and a constant pressure is applied to the pump, subjective to 
succeed the required pressure in the cell. Then a pressure test is implemented, in order to check 
any leak of fluid throughout the assembly, before the cell is placed in the furnace.  
In the case of the mixture methane – n-decane (xc1=0.8499, mole fraction), its saturation pressure 
is Psat,mix=35.98MPa at 298.21K. As a result, in order to fill the tubing system of the calorimeter 
with the mixture and to have one phase (liquid) flow, the filling procedure of the sample has to be 
in higher pressure than the saturation. For that reason, a cylinder in high pressure is connected to 
the outlet. The pressure of this cylinder is also controlled by another ISCO pump. The pressure in 
the cylinder in the outlet is slightly lower than the one in the cylinder of the sample in the inlet. 
For instance, in order to parch the sample inside, the inlet cylinder is at 41 MPa and the cylinder in 
the outlet at 39 MPa. With that way, it is assured that the flow will be towards the outlet.

implemented on that.

5. Results & Discussion  
 
In this chapter, the experimental results of measurements of density and heat capacity of the 
fluids that have been described in the methods’ section (4.2), are presented. More specifically, in 
the beginning the results of the heat capacity of n-tetradecane operated with the industrial cells of

5.1 Verification of the procedure 
 
In order to verify the experimental procedure of the indirect calculation of Joule – Thomson 
coefficient through the experimental measurements of density and heat capacity, first there is 
presented an experiment of measuring the heat capacity of n-tetradecane, which was 
implemented with the industrial – original cells of the calorimeter C 80 apparatus in pressures up 
to 10MPa. The results of this experiment are combined with literature values of density in order to 
result in calculations of Joule – Thomson coefficient of n-tetradecane. 
 
5.1.1 Heat capacity results of n-tetradecane with the industrial cells  
 
As it has already been described in 4.2.2, in order to extract measurement from the DSC 
apparatus, there are needed to be conducted three different experiments. Firstly, one with empty 
cells, secondly one with water as a reference fluid and then lastly one with your measurable fluid 
component. The reference cell remains empty in all the cases.  
As it has already been commented the empty - empty experiment is taken place at ambient 
pressure only and its results are used for every pressure afterwards. Additionally, because the 
saturation temperature of water at ambient conditions is 373.12 K, which is much lower than our 
maximum measuring experimental temperature of 483.15 K, the empty - water test was only 
implemented at 10 MPa. At this pressure the saturation temperature (boiling point) of water is 
584.15 K (Wagner and Pruß 2002) which is much higher than our measuring borders.

5.3 High pressure heat capacity tests for a mixture methane – n-decane  
 
The next experimental task was to measure the heat capacity of a mixture of methane - n-decane 
and then calculate the Joule - Thomson coefficient (JTC). The mixture has mole fraction 
xmethane=0.8499 and at ambient temperature has saturation pressure equal to 35.98 MPa (at 
298.91K) (Regueira et al. 2016), which decreases while the temperature increases. So in order to 
be in the liquid phase region, the mixture was stored in a cylinder in pressure around 40MPa.

6. Conclusions  
 
In this project, we further validate the capacity measurement method used for the Seratam C80 
calorimeter and extended its measurement range to higher pressures. In the meantime, we 
performed high pressure density measurement using the Anton Paar density meter, and combined 
the capacity results and the density results to produce the corresponding Joule – Thomson 
coefficients (JTC). Some detailed conclusions are summarized below: 
As far as the density experiments of n-decane are concerned, the parameters A and B followed the 
magnitude and trend of the literature (Regueira, 2013) for this specific apparatus (DMA –HPM), so 
the calibration procedure was successfully implemented. Also the trends of density seem to have 
normal dependence with respect to temperature and pressure; density increases with pressure 
and when temperature decreases. What is more, it is not observed any pressure and temperature 
trend in the deviation plots between experimental and literature values (Lemmon & Span 2006, 
Cibulka & Hnedkovský 1996). Furthermore, in both deviation plots with pressure and temperature, 
the relative deviations are both negative and positive, i.e. the distribution of the experimental 
error is not biased to one direction. 
As for the heat capacity, the verification procedure (heat capacity experiments of n-tetradecane) 
has successfully ensured the validity of our experimental DSC apparatus (Calorimeter C80) for 
measuring the property of isobaric heat capacity. More specifically, the relative deviation 
percentages (0.35% max.) between our results and those that were extracted from a correlation of 
the experimental results of Khasanshin et al. (2002), were varying beneath the acceptable 
experimental uncertainties (up to 0.6%), as given by Bessières et al. (2000) performing 
experiments with the same type of apparatus.  
Moreover, the experimental results of n-decane performed with the new cells, which were built by 
the workshop, have revealed maximum AADs 1.11% at 60 MPa and lower than 1% at the other 
pressures (compared with Lemmon & Span, 2006). These percentages are (slightly above only in 
one case) within the accepted limit of 1% (given by the same source). This agreement validates the 
newly developed cells for measuring heat capacities under high-temperature high-pressure 
conditions. What is more, because of the low percentages (max 1%) of deviations between 
experimental measurements performed with new cells and literature values, it can be concluded 
that even if the volume of the new cells is significantly decreased (from 8.37 cm-3 to 1.87 cm-3), the 
validity of the experimental results does not seem to be compromised. 
Also, during the continuous Cp zone of the DSC (where the heating rate is constant), we noted two 
zones of the heat flow records, where deviations from the linear trend are observed. The first zone 
is detected at elevated temperatures (higher than 420K) due to convection phenomena that 
influence a lot the heat flow records. The second zone, where the same consideration should be 
paid on, is in the temperature range up to 330 K. The reason of this repeatable diversion from 
linearity at this area might be due to the apparatus behaviour for temperatures close to the 
starting point of the heating rate. As a result, an overestimation in the heat capacity results in the 
65 
 
==PAGE:66==
mentioned temperature intervals must be considered in order to avoid experimental errors. 
Furthermore, the estimated volume of the cell, which is subtracted after the processing of the 
heat flow records of the empty – water experiments, should be normalized for the same 
temperature intervals (extrapolation of the calculated volume of the cell).  
In addition, slight pressure trend has been noticed during the heat capacity measurements of both 
n-alkanes compounds (n-tetradecane and n-decane) and of the mixture methane – n-decane. A 
maximum reduction of 3% for the heat capacity values of n-decane over an increase of pressure by 
60MPa at the same temperature, shows a small pressure dependence. Moreover, the measured 
heat capacity shows a clear linear dependence on the temperature. For instance, n-decane heat 
capacity was increased at 10 MPa by almost 27%, within a temperature trend from 313.15 K to 
463.15 K. Consequently, future measurements of heat capacity should involve just a few pressure 
steps for long intervals or even only measurements at the edges for short pressure intervals.  
As far as the JTC is concerned, the pressure drop seems to increase the magnitude of the JTC, for 
both n-alkanes and the mixture. The negative value of JTC in all three cases implies a temperature 
increase within a drawdown (pressure decrease). This temperature increase should be considered 
in cases when the magnitude of the JTC is large in absolute value, due to safety reasons. However, 
the magnitude of our experimental data is low. With other words, a JTC value of -0.4 K MPa-1 
implies a 4 K temperature increase for a pressure decrease of 10 MPa, which is not very 
significant.  
Additionally, the differences between two sets of results provided for the JTC of the mixture 
methane – n-decane, imply the need for improvement of the derivative of density with respect to 
temperature. Also, because these results are compared with some calculated values generated by 
a model for gas/oil fluids (GERG 2008), the validity of the reference cannot be compared with 
some other direct experimental measurement. After also considering that the magnitude of the 
JTC at these pressure stages is small (ca. 0.04 K MPa-1 – 0.4 K MPa-1), even a marginal difference 
can give high deviations in percentages between our data and the values of the model.  
In conclusion, the new designed cells for the DSC apparatus performed successfully heat capacity 
measurements up to 60 MPa for n-decane and one mixture of n-alkanes. This procedure could be 
operated up to 150 MPa likewise. No need of small pressure measuring step is required for heat 
capacity experiments; only a few pressures stages can be enough. These results can be combined 
with density experimental measurements at high-pressure high-temperature conditions to obtain 
indirectly the JTC of the mixture/compound. Future work can be performed correspondingly for 
more binary or ternary multicomponent mixtures and reservoir fluids.
