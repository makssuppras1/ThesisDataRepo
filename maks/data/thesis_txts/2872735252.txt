Abstract
Accurate and efficient estimation of wave-induced loads is critical for the safe design,
maintenance, and life extension of offshore structures. Traditional high-fidelity computa-
tional methods, such as CFD, offer detailed insight but are often too computationally ex-
pensive for routine engineering use. This thesis presents a novel and efficient methodol-
ogy for calculating wave-induced loads, with distinct treatment of non-breaking and break-
ing wave conditions, aimed at bridging the gap between accuracy and practicality.
For non-breaking waves, the velocity field is derived using a stream function formulation,
which captures the sharper crests and milder troughs observed in wave profiles. The re-
sulting flow field is coupled with the NAMISWAN solver to compute hydrodynamic loads,
which are then applied to a finite element model for structural response evaluation. Com-
parisons with experimental data show that the proposed approach achieves an error of
less than 10% in most cases of non-breaking waves, validating its accuracy for quasi-
static loading.
For breaking waves, classified as spilling breakers, the same methodology is extended by
introducing an empirical correction to the horizontal velocity component based on exper-
imental observations. This correction significantly reduces errors in the predicted shear
forces, particularly in the direction of wave propagation. While the corrected method still
underestimates peak slamming loads, NAMISWAN demonstrates acceptable accuracy in
capturing impact effects. More than half of the breaking wave case results had an error
of less than 30%.
The proposed method combines physical fidelity with computational efficiency, achiev-
ing results comparable to high-fidelity CFD simulations at a fraction of the computational
cost. This makes it especially suitable for preliminary design, load envelope estimation,
and maintenance planning of offshore structures. The research also highlights the impor-
tance of accurate velocity field representation in slamming load prediction and provides a
foundation for future development of more advanced models.
Keywords: offshore structures, wave loads, slamming load, breaking waves, stream
function, finite element model, velocity field.

Introduction
1.1
Project background and objectives
Offshore jacket structures are widely used to support oil and gas platforms, as well as off-
shore wind turbines. These structures are typically composed of tubular steel members
arranged in a truss-like framework, anchored to the seabed to ensure stability under var-
ious environmental loads. Among the primary forces acting on offshore structures, wave
loads are often the most dominant and complex to model accurately.
Wave loading plays a critical role in determining the structural integrity, serviceability, and
fatigue life of offshore jacket structures. The oscillatory nature of waves induces both
quasi-static and dynamic responses in the structure, potentially leading to vibrations, res-
onance, and long-term fatigue damage in structural members. Moreover, extreme wave
events can generate large hydrodynamic forces, which may threaten the overall safety
and stability of the structure.
Accurate calculation of wave-induced loads is therefore essential in offshore engineering.
Underestimating wave loads can compromise structural safety, leading to unexpected
failures or service disruptions. Conversely, overestimating them—due to conservative
assumptions or inadequate modeling—can result in excessive material usage and higher
construction and maintenance costs.
Traditional wave load models, such as Morison’s equation and linear wave theories, pro-
vide useful approximations but may fall short when applied to nonlinear wave conditions,
breaking waves, or complex sea states. As offshore developments move into more chal-
lenging environments, the limitations of these models become more pronounced. This
necessitates the development and validation of more advanced and accurate wave load
prediction models that can capture nonlinear, unsteady, and potentially breaking wave
effects.
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has become an increasingly powerful tool for mod-
eling wave-structure interactions recently, especially for complex flows involving breaking
waves, turbulence, and viscous effects. While CFD offers detailed insights into the hydro-
dynamic forces on offshore structures, it also comes with significant drawbacks that limit
its practicality in many engineering applications. One of the primary challenges is the high
computational cost; accurately resolving free surface dynamics, turbulence, and bound-
ary layers requires fine spatial and temporal discretization, often resulting in simulations
that demand extensive computational resources and time. Additionally, CFD models are
sensitive to numerical settings, including turbulence models, interface tracking methods
(e.g., VOF), mesh quality, and boundary conditions, which can introduce uncertainties if
not properly calibrated.
In addition to analytical methods, experiment-based models have been developed using
physical testing data to estimate global wave forces on structures, especially for complex
scenarios where theoretical solutions are difficult. These models offer empirical force ap-
proximations that are more aligned with observed behavior. However, they also introduce
limitations: they typically simplify the dynamic time history of wave loads into idealized
shapes or parameters and are often applicable only under the specific test conditions for
which they were developed. As such, they lack the generality, flexibility, and resolution
required for modern offshore structural analysis, particularly at the component level.

Therefore, it is essential to develop a wave load calculation model that strikes a balance
between accuracy and computational efficiency. While high-fidelity CFD simulations can
provide detailed insights, their high cost and complexity make them impractical for routine
engineering design. A reliable, lower-cost alternative is crucial for the design, construc-
tion, and maintenance of offshore structures. By improving the accuracy of wave load pre-
dictions without incurring excessive computational expense, engineers can ensure struc-
tural safety, reduce unnecessary conservatism in design, and optimize material use. In
particular, accurate modeling of wave loads is critical not only at the global structure level
but also for estimating internal forces and moments in individual structural components,
which govern fatigue life, joint design, and local failure mechanisms. The simulation re-
sults of the model need to be compared with experimental data to verify the accuracy of
the model. The verified model may be used in the fields of structural design and mainte-
nance in the future. This approach supports both the structural integrity and longevity of
offshore platforms while also enhancing the overall economic viability of marine energy
and coastal infrastructure projects.

2
Literature review
Wave load estimation is a fundamental component of offshore and coastal structural de-
sign. Accurate assessment of wave-induced forces is essential for ensuring the safety
and durability of marine structures subjected to dynamic ocean conditions. Among var-
ious types of wave actions, breaking waves are particularly significant due to their high-
energy impact and associated nonlinear characteristics. A breaking wave is defined as
a wave whose amplitude grows to a critical level, causing it to become unstable and
dissipate a considerable amount of wave energy into turbulent kinetic energy [1]. This
energy dissipation typically results in violent hydrodynamic behaviors, such as splashing,
air entrainment, and strong fluid-structure interaction, which can generate impulsive and
highly localized slamming forces on offshore structures. As a result, breaking waves are
considered critical scenarios in the structural design process, especially for components
exposed near the free surface or situated in shallow water zones.
Slamming loads induced by breaking waves can cause severe local damage or fatigue in
structural members, and they are difficult to predict due to their transient nature, spatial
and temporal variability, and sensitivity to local wave and structural geometries. These
complexities have prompted extensive research efforts aimed at understanding the mech-
anisms behind wave slamming and developing reliable predictive tools and models.
The Morison equation remains one of the most widely adopted engineering models for
estimating wave-induced forces on slender structures, such as monopiles, tubular mem-
bers of jacket platforms, and risers [2]. This semi-empirical approach decomposes the
total hydrodynamic force into two components: an inertia force that is proportional to the
fluid acceleration and a drag force that is proportional to the square of the fluid velocity.
The inertia term accounts for the added mass effect, while the drag term captures the
resistive force caused by flow separation and vortex shedding around the body.
One fundamental assumption of the Morison equation is that the flow field surrounding
the structure is uniform and the structure does not significantly disturb the incoming wave
field. Therefore, it is most applicable when the characteristic diameter of the structure is
small compared to the wavelength (typically when D/λ < 0.2). For vertical cylinders or
similar members within these limits, this assumption is generally valid. However, when
the structure’s size becomes comparable to the wavelength, the wave field is altered due
to diffraction effects, and the Morison equation may no longer be accurate.
In such cases, diffraction theory is used to account for the wave-structure interaction more
precisely. Faltinsen suggests that diffraction effects become significant when the ratio
D/λ exceeds 0.2 [3]. To bridge the gap between Morison-type models and fully diffracted
solutions, international standards, such as those for offshore wind turbines, recommend
using empirical corrections to the inertia coefficient based on MacCamy-Fuchs theory.
This hybrid approach allows the inertia term to be modified to partially account for diffrac-
tion without requiring a full diffraction analysis [4].
To apply the Morison equation, knowledge of the water particle kinematics—specifically
the horizontal fluid velocity u and acceleration ˙u at the point of interest—is necessary.
These kinematics are usually obtained from wave theories. Linear (Airy) wave theory
is the simplest among them and assumes sinusoidal wave motion. It applies to small-
amplitude, deep-water waves, where the wave height is much smaller than the wavelength

and water depth [5]. However, it neglects nonlinearity and becomes less accurate in
shallow water or near-breaking conditions.
Stokes wave theory improves upon linear theory by including nonlinear terms through a
perturbation expansion in wave steepness. It captures higher harmonics and can rep-
resent wave asymmetry and crest sharpening more accurately, especially in intermedi-
ate and shallow water depths [6]. Both Airy and Stokes theories are based on potential
flow assumptions, implying irrotational, inviscid, and incompressible flow. While these
assumptions hold for regular wave propagation in many cases, they break down under
breaking wave conditions, where vorticity, turbulence, and strong nonlinearity dominate
the flow regime. Therefore, such classical wave theories are not suitable for directly mod-
eling breaking waves or slamming loads.
Several dedicated models have been developed specifically for slamming load estimation.
One of the most frequently referenced models is the semi-empirical slamming force model
proposed by Wienke and Oumeraci [7]. Their approach characterizes the impact force as
a function of impact velocity, wave parameters, and structure geometry, offering a practical
method for estimating the magnitude and duration of slamming events. However, these
models often require calibration with experimental or high-fidelity numerical data due to
the stochastic nature of breaking events.
In recent years, Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has emerged as a powerful tool
for simulating wave-structure interaction, particularly in regimes involving complex flow
phenomena such as wave breaking, vortex formation, turbulence, and air entrainment.
CFD models based on the Navier-Stokes equations, coupled with free surface capturing
techniques such as the Volume of Fluid (VOF) method, can simulate wave propagation,
deformation, and impact in high fidelity. These tools are especially valuable for analyzing
the behavior of structures under extreme wave loading, where linear or semi-empirical
models fall short.
CFD has been used successfully to simulate wave impacts on a variety of structures, in-
cluding vertical walls [8], monopiles [9], and other offshore components. These studies
demonstrate the ability of CFD to resolve fine-scale fluid dynamics, predict localized pres-
sures, and provide insights into the load distribution over time. Moreover, CFD models
have shown superior accuracy in capturing peak impact forces compared to traditional
models [10]. To improve computational efficiency, Li et al. developed a hybrid method
based on the Spectral Wave Explicit Navier-Stokes Equations (SWENSE), which sepa-
rates the incident potential flow from the viscous perturbation field, significantly reducing
the computational cost while maintaining accuracy [11].
Experimental methods continue to play a critical role in validating numerical models and
providing empirical data for the development of wave load prediction techniques. For ex-
ample, Stansberg et al. conducted experiments on a vertical cylinder subjected to irregular
waves to study high-frequency load components resulting from steep random waves [12].
Kyte and Tørum carried out experiments on a vertical cylinder located on a shoal and an-
alyzed the total and local forces generated by plunging breaking waves. Their work led to
the development of engineering formulas for predicting horizontal forces and overturning
moments [13].
Huseby and Grue investigated wave loads on a vertical cylinder in a laboratory wave
flume and evaluated the performance of theoretical models against experimental data.
Their results showed good agreement for small to moderate wave amplitudes, but indi-
cated that theoretical models tended to be conservative for larger waves [14]. Chen et al.

key characteristics of slamming force evolution on jacket-type structures [20].

Theories
3.1
NAMISWAN
NAMISWAN is a model to calculate wave loads on jacket structures at the component
level, proposed by Anastasiadis et al. [21].
The calculation method for the slamming load has four steps. In the first step, each mem-
ber is discretized into a specific number of elements. In the second step, the impact of
each element is identified. The third step is to calculate the impact force based on the
identification results. Finally, the load curve of each member is obtained through an inte-
gration process.
Step 1 - Member discretization
After selecting the element size ∆s, each member is discretized into several elements of
length ∆s, as shown in Figure 3.1. ∆s should be sufficiently small so that the curvature
of the water surface at the time of impact can be ignored, and the time interval between
adjacent elements being impacted should be significantly lower than the time required for
their cross-sections to be fully immersed.
Figure 3.1: Member discretization [21]
Step 2 - Impact identification
Impact loads occur when elements pass over the water surface at high speed. NAMISWAN
proposed an exclusive criterion for the slamming of each element, defined by its entry into
the water surface. This criterion ensures that the impact load on the component is dis-
tributed temporally and spatially, depending on its position and direction relative to the
wave and the breaking wave itself.

NAMISWAN assumed that the slamming load is caused by the interaction between the
water surface and the object, so the velocity required to calculate the impact force is
the velocity of the object relative to the water surface. In addition, it was assumed that
member deformations can be neglected. Based on the above assumptions, the velocity
can be expressed as the projection of the water particle velocity on the surface normal

3.3
Kinematics of breaking waves
A breaking wave is a wave for which the particle velocity at the crest becomes equal to or
exceeds the phase speed of the wave, leading to instability and collapse of the crest [23].
The breaking criterion is expressed by the wave height and depth at the breaker point
Hb = KDb
(3.25)
where Hb is the wave height at the breaking point, Db is the water depth at the breaking
point, and K is the empirical breaker index. K = 0.78 is widely used [24].
Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) methods can calculate the wave kinematics of break-
ing waves. However, simulating breaking waves using CFD is computationally demanding
[25] and sensitive to some initial settings, such as meshing. Compared with CFD, study-
ing the velocity field characteristics of breaking waves directly is a feasible method. Xu et
al. experimentally studied the velocity field characteristics of different types of breaking
waves [26]. In the study, critical waves, spilling breakers, and plunging breakers were
generated in a water flume, and the velocity field was obtained using image velocimetry
technology.
The study found that the velocity field of the critical wave is relatively symmetrical, with
the maximum horizontal velocity umax = 0.66Cd, where Cd is the dominant wave phase
velocity. The velocity before the breaking wave crest is greater than that after the crest,
showing a certain asymmetry. The maximum horizontal velocity of the spilling breaker
is umax = 0.84Cd, which occurs 0.7ηmax before the leading wave crest, where ηmax is the
maximum height of the wave surface. The maximum horizontal velocity of the plunging
wave is umax = 0.83Cd, which occurs at the top in front of the peak.
The study also obtained the characteristics of the gas-liquid mixing zone. The maxi-
mum thickness of the gas-liquid mixing zone of the spilling wave is Dmax = 0.75ηmax,
the maximum existence time is tmax = 0.6T, where T is the dominant wave period, and
the maximum horizontal velocity is um

8
Conclusion
This thesis set out to develop and validate a computationally efficient methodology for
evaluating wave-induced loads on marine structures, with explicit treatment of non-breaking
and breaking waves.
8.1
Non-breaking waves
A stream-function formulation was employed to obtain the velocity field for regular, non-
breaking waves. Shape analysis of the computed free surface revealed characteristic
sharper crests and milder troughs, which guided the choice of series terms in the stream-
function expansion. The resulting velocity field was coupled to the NAMISWAN solver to
derive quasi-static loads, which were subsequently transformed to internal forces using a
finite-element model (FEM) of the test structure.
Across the experimental campaign, the predicted quasi-static member forces differed from
measurements by less than 10% in 60% of the cases, thereby confirming both the ad-
equacy of the stream-function representation and the validity of the Morison-type load
model for the present conditions. The few outliers were possibly traced to malfunctioning
transducers, not to deficiencies in the method itself.
8.2
Breaking waves
Breaking-wave events were classified as spilling breakers based on visual records and the
computed Iribarren number. A direct comparison with the non-breaking cases showed that
modeling assumptions for pressure, inertia, and added-mass terms remain valid, and that
the main source of error resides in the under-prediction of the horizontal particle velocity
u.
Building on recent laboratory observations of breaker kinematics, an empirical correction
was introduced to the stream-function velocity field. The corrected field reduced the error
in member shear forces in the wave-propagation direction. Although the method still un-
derestimates peak slamming velocities, the NAMISWAN predictions of resulting impact
loads are within the acceptable error range, demonstrating the practical usefulness of the
approach.
8.3
Contribution and significance
The proposed framework combines the physical transparency of classical wave theories
with the computational economy of NAMISWAN, yielding turnaround times significantly
faster than two-phase CFD while retaining engineering accuracy for both regular and
breaking waves. The method therefore enables rapid load envelopes during concept
design and efficient update analyses during operation and maintenance planning.
8.4
Limitations
The present validation was confined to intermediate-depth spilling breakers acting on a
slender-member jacket structure. Applicability to plunging or surging breakers, very shal-
low water, and bluff bodies remains to be demonstrated. Furthermore, the empirical ve-
locity correction was derived from a single test series and may not capture scale effects
or complex bathymetries.

8.5
Recommendations for future work
1. Broaden the experimental database to include a wider range of breaker types,
wave steepness, and water depths, allowing the velocity-field correction to be gen-
eralised and non-dimensionalised.
2. Incorporate local air-entrainment effects and pressure-pulse duration into the
slamming load model to further improve impact-force prediction.
3. Conduct coupled hydro-structure simulations to assess the method’s perfor-
mance under dynamic amplification and multi-directional sea states.
4. Develop an open-source implementation of the corrections to facilitate adoption
by the offshore engineering community.
8.6
Closing remarks
Fast and reliable assessment of wave loads remains a bottleneck in the design and life-
extension of offshore assets. This thesis demonstrates that, with carefully chosen correc-
tions, depth-integrated solvers such as NAMISWAN can meet this challenge, delivering
engineering-level accuracy for both non-breaking and breaking waves at a fraction of the
computational cost of high-fidelity CFD. The findings offer a viable path towards more
resilient and cost-effective offshore infrastructure.
