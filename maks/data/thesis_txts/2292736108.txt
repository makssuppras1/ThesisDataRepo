Abstract
In the current thesis project, a general study of the microbial enhanced oil recovery has
been achieved. More speciﬁcally, this thesis project includes an extensive observation of
the MEOR process and a general understanding of how the MEOR works and according
to which mechanisms. Also, an incorporation of the indigenous bacterial population in a
model which was made by the petroleum engineering department of DTU.
Microbial EOR is a method that acts according to three main mechanisms. The two of
these mechanisms (reduction of oil-water interfacial tension and ﬂuid diversion) have been
modelled through a program that is made using a one dimensional scheme. This scheme
consists of isolated models such as a reactive transport model and the bacterial reactions
that are combined. These bacterial reactions are the microbial growth, the consumption
of nutrients, the sporulation - reactivation process and the production of surfactants. This
program is created by S.M Nielsen (2010) and modiﬁed in 2012 by I. Nesterov in order
for the ﬁnal version to be formed.
One of the main purposes of the current thesis project is the incorporation of the in-
digenous bacterial populations in the model. Thus, the program is modiﬁed in a way
which is described explicitly in the following chapters. Through these modiﬁcations, the
user can analyze a case of a certain reservoir by taking into consideration the indigenous
bacterial populations. In this way, the ’stimulation of the indigenous bacteria’ process
can be studied and the delineation of its eﬃciency in the oil recovery can be achieved.
Moreover, an extensive parametric analysis has been made in which a general discussion
is presented, describing how the stimulation of indigenous bacteria technique works. Also,
the importance of the distribution of the indigenous bacterial population in the ﬂowing
and the attached phase in the MEOR process was observed and a general comparison of
this technique with processes such as injection of metabolite and waterﬂooding was held.

Chapter 1
Overview of MEOR
1.1
Introduction
Enhanced oil recovery is a fast growing ﬁeld of the petroleum industry which aims to
improve the oil recovery from the already developed reservoirs. In general, EOR or tertiary
recovery promises the future potentiality of the oil ﬁelds that can no longer be productive
using the conventional ways of oil extraction. Various EOR methods have been developed
and some of them present extraordinary results in terms of oil recovery. One of the most
promising methods in the tertiary recovery is the Microbial enhanced oil recovery which
optimizes the oil production using microorganisms and cheap molasses as their nutrients.
This type of EOR presents some advantages that will be analyzed in the following sections
of the thesis project.
Extensive laboratory and ﬁeld tests have been implemented in diﬀerent types of reservoir
around the world, from the middle of the previous century until now. The main discrep-
ancy of the MEOR method lies in its eﬃciency between the ﬁeld trials and the laboratory
tests. This discrepancy is caused because of the wide variety of the factors that have to
be taken into account when MEOR process is modeled. These factors can be biological,
chemical, or physical and they are related to the interaction between the microbial sys-
tem that is set and the reservoir. Thus, extensive observation of the reservoir as well as
the indigenous biota has to be taken into consideration when a certain MEOR model is
formed.
One of the promising techniques of microbial enhanced oil recovery is the stimulation
of the indigenous bacterial population. According to this technique, the utilization of
certain indigenous bacterial populations occurs. The bacteria that are picked in order
to enforce them into growth are carefully picked. The choice of the appropriate type
of bacteria depends on a variety of diﬀerent factors that there will be mentioned in the
following sections. This type of MEOR has stimulated the scientiﬁc consideration because
it promises an additional portion of oil recovery in a very low cost. In the following sections
of this chapter there will be an explicit description of all the aspects of the microbial EOR,
as well as its applicability and the parameters that inﬂuence its eﬃcacy in a positive or
negative way.

1.2
General
In the MEOR method the ability of the microorganisms to convert cheap nutrients to
surfactants, acids, gases, solvents and other beneﬁcial for the recovery substances is uti-
lized. Each substance activates diﬀerent mechanisms which will be explicitly explained
in the following chapters. Microbial enhanced oil recovery is considered a revolutionary
type of EOR, mainly because it is a low cost process. However, it is a process that the
mechanisms of functionality have not clearly been discovered. Even though, extensive
laboratory work and ﬁeld procedures have been implemented worldwide, resulting to the
general contribution in the optimization of the procedure.
1.2.1
History of MEOR
The ﬁrst suggestions that microbes can be used for the extraction of oil came from Beck-
mann at 1926. After 1926 no progression in the MEOR ﬁeld was noted until 1940 when
initially ZoBell and subsequently his research team (1947) began an extensive laboratory
investigation. The ﬁrst breakthrough in MEOR occurred at 1946 where a patent was given
to ZoBell on his process to increase the extraction of oil from underground. However, at
the year 1947, ZoBell’s results were controverted by Beck’s laboratory experiments which
were based on ZoBell’s research. In 1953 ZoBell received another patent that was based in
experimental work utilizing the genus ’Clostridium’. ZoBell’s experiments were continued
at 1954 (Updegraﬀand Wren 1954, Davis and Updegraﬀ) leading to Updegraﬀ’s patent
(1957) according to which an obligate anaerobe genus is injected with sugars as substrate
and specifying the number of the bacteria that there were employed. Updegraﬀpatent is
considered a breakthrough in MEOR as it was the ﬁrst time that a complete system that
leads to optimization of the recovery was presented.
Until in this point, all the results were based on experimental work and no ﬁeld exper-
iment was tested.
At 1954, in the Lisbon ﬁeld in Arcansas of USA the ﬁrst MEOR
test implemented (Yarbrough and Coty, 1983). This year Kuznetsov discovered that oil
deposits contain bacteria that can convert oil into gas under anaerobical conditions. Af-
ter that, Kuznetsov worked extensively in the activation of the reservoirs microbiota.
The following decade signiﬁcant research activity occurred in Europe (Poland, former
Czechoslovakia, Hungary) and USA as well as ﬁeld trials using a variety of anaerobic bac-
teria such as Clostridium, Bacillus, Pseudomonas, Micrococcus and Mycobacterium. The
second breakthrough in MEOR happened in 1958 when Heinningen and his colleagues
proposed microbial plugging with biomass produced by microorganisms. From 1970 to
2000 the MEOR research boosted by the 1970’s oil crisis and it expanded worldwide. In
more than 30 countries research projects were implemented and there was a constant op-
timization of the existed techniques of microbial EOR, focusing on the microbial ecology.
In 1995, a survey about MEOR eﬃciency (Portwood) shows that 322 ﬁeld projects had
been developed and 78% of them were marked by success, achieving high recovery. Nowa-
days, as oil and gas demand increases gradually, a lot of companies dare to use MEOR in
ﬁeld projects, especially at the periods that oil and gas prices are high.

1.2.2
Application of the MEOR
Microbial EOR is a targeted ﬁeld in petroleum engineering which incorporates the cog-
nitive background of diﬀerent sciences such as chemical and environmental engineering,
geology, ﬂuid mechanics and microbiology. All these ﬁelds of science have contributed
in order for MEOR to reach the level that is nowadays. The main concept in microbial
enhanced oil recovery is the use of the microorganisms for the extraction of larger ’oil
portion’ from the reservoir. According to this technique the utilization of the natural
by-products of the microorganisms, such as gases, biopolymers and surfactants occurs.
The interaction of these substances with the oil, inside the reservoir triggers some reac-
tions according to which the oil that is trapped in the reservoir begins to ﬂow towards
the production well.
Microbial EOR method can be distincted in in-situ and indigenous depending on the
source of microorganisms. Except for the optimization of the oil recovery, MEOR can be
applied in the ﬁeld of diﬀerent production problems such as:
• Well Stimulation which is achieved by the process that is called ’Cyclic Microbial recov-
ery’. In this process a solution of microbes and nutrients are injected in the reservoir. The
microbes produce carbon dioxide gas, surfactants and other by-products that mobilize the
residual oil
• Well Cleanup where Wax removal and Paraﬃn Inhibition occurs. As a result, substances
that diminish the production of the oil decreases and the production is optimized
• Selective plugging, according to which porosity clogging occurs that results to ﬂuid
diversion using mechanisms that there will be explicitly described in the following chapters
• Acidizing/Fracturing
In conventional Microbial Flooding recovery, the selected microbes are injected to the
reservoir as well as the appropriate nutrients for the feeding of the microorganisms. After
the injection, the well closes for approximately 20 days. During this time the microbes
consume the nutrients, they reproduce and produce by-products. When the well opens,
water is injected that drifts the mobilized oil and the products of the microbial reactions to
the production well. As the production continues the same process is repeated (Ezeanya
et al, 2010). The process has to be supported by the injection of nutrients inside the well
in speciﬁc time frames, according to the schedule which have been planned. One of the
most beneﬁcial parts of the microbial EOR is the abandonment phase. The abandonment
phase is achieved by simply stopping the nutrient ﬂow into the reservoir. By creating a
scarcity of nutrient in the environment of the reservoir, the amount of microbes decreases
as they die or they form spores. The abandonment phase seems to be one of the major
advantages of this method, especially from the economical point of view.

1.2.3
Advantages and Disadvantages of MEOR
Advantages
Microbial EOR is one of the most attractive oil recovery methods from the perspective
of economics, especially for marginal producing ﬁelds (Ollivier, B. et al, 2005). This lies
on the fact that the expenditures of the necessary materials are low (simple mollases as
nutrients), while the surface equipment for waterﬂooding is adequate for the needs of
this EOR method (Lazar et al, 2007). Furthermore, the cost of the microbial injection
ﬂuid is totally independent on the price of the crude oil. This is very convenient for the
enhanced oil recovery and it dominates the EOR methods which are related to injection
of hydrocarbons, such as gas injection EOR methods, especially when the oil price is high.
As it is mentioned above the abandonment phase of this method is as simple as stopping
the feed the microorganisms in the reservoir. In addition, because of the fact that the
process utilizes microbes, it tends to be considered as environmental friendly method of
EOR (Bryant et al, 1989). Microbial EOR can be applied eﬃciently in a wide range of
crude oils, either heavy or light type of oil and the choice of the injected microbes can be
adjusted in the conditions of the reservoir in order to have the optimal results in terms of
recovery (Bryant, 1989).
Disadvantages
The main constraints of this EOR method are derived from the sensitivity of the microbial
strains that are injected. The microorganisms cannot survive in adverse environments
with high temperatures and high salinity and this is one of the major drawbacks of the

This could be shown by the constant progress that MEOR present the last century.
1.3
Petroleum Microbiology
One of the main distinctions of the microorganisms depends on the type of respiration.
Microorganisms are distinguished in aerobic, anaerobic and facultatively aerobic. The
aerobic microorganisms use oxygen as O2 in order to respire, on the contrary with the
anaerobic in which oxygen function negatively. The facultatively aerobic microorganisms
can survive in both aerobic and anaerobic conditions depending on the concentration of
the nutrients as well as on the environmental conditions of the reservoir. Some of the
microorganisms that are used in such processes are illustrated in the Table 1.1 (Bryan &
Burchﬁeld, 1989). Taking into account that its diﬃcult for the oxygen to reach high levels
of depth it can implied that the potentiality of the microbial EOR process is enforced by
the anaerobic bacteria (Jang et al., 1984).
In general, a wide variety of laboratory work and ﬁeld trials are tested using Bacillus
and Clostridium with more or less positive results. Depending on the mechanisms that
it is preferable to stimulate the appropriate type of bacteria is picked. The products of
some of the bacteria that are commonly used are illustrated in the Table 1.1 (Bryant
and Burchﬁeld, 1989). Some of the bacteria present some additional portion of eﬃciency
because of their ability to form spores (such as Bacillus). This occurs by the sporulation
process and the way that function will be described in the following sections.
The physiology and the metabolic activity of the bacteria is highly inﬂuenced by the con-
ditions that lives in. The level of salinity, the temperature and the pH are some of the
most signiﬁcant factors that may boost or inhibit the growth of a certain bacterial pop-
ulation. Moreover, the substrate is another important parameter that actually functions
decisively in the growth of the bacterial populations. Diﬀerent types of nutrients boost
diﬀerent types of populations.

oil, as microorganisms consume the carbon from the heavy components of the oil phase
and optimize its quality (Peihui, 2001).
As nutrients are consumed by the microorganisms, energy is stored in the systems. This
major part of this energy is used for the growth of the microorganisms and the production
of their metabolites. However, in some cases a part of the energy is consumed by the cells
in order to maintain the proper physiology of the cell and resist against the adverse

condition of the environment.
The metabolites are dependent not only on the conditions of the environment that the
microorganisms live in, but also on the the type of the nutrients. There are six main
byproducts:
Bio-surfactants
The bio-surfactants are amphipathic molecules with both hydrophilic and hydrophobic
parts which are produced by microorganism such as Bacillus sp., Clostridium sp. and
Pseudomonas sp.. Bio-surfactants have the ability to reduce the surface interfacial tension
by accumulating at the interface of immiscible ﬂuids and increase the mobility of the
organic compounds (Singh A, 2007). They can be distinguished in ﬁve diﬀerent types
such as lipopeptides, phospholipids, glycolipids, fatty acids and neutral lipids.
Biopolymers
The biopolymers are polysaccharides which are produced by the bacteria such as Bacillus
sp., Xanthomonas campestris, Enterobacter sp. in order to protect them by temporary
desiccation and predation. Moreover biopolymers are used by the bacteria in their ad-
hesion to pore wall surfaces (Brown F., 1992). The functionality of the biopolymers in
Microbial EOR method is the selective plugging of the high permeable zones that leads
initially to permeability modiﬁcations and thus, to ﬂuid diversion. Furthermore, they can
improve the sweep eﬃciency and the mobility ratio as they have the ability to function as
mobility control agents by increasing the viscosity of the displacing water (Akit R., 1989).
Biogases
Biogases such as carbon dioxide, hydrogen and methane are the products of the fermen-
tation of carbohydrates by some bacteria. The main mechanism that biogases contribute
to the optimization of the production is the re-pressurization of the reservoir and the
heavy oil viscosity reduction (Brown F.G., 1992). This type of metabolite is produced
by species such as Clostidium sp, Enterobacter aerogenes, Pseudomonas sp. and certain
methanogens (Behlulgil K, 2003).
Bio-acids
Bio-acids are produced by species such as Clostridium sp. and Enterobacter aerogenes
when they consume certain types of nutrients. In the category of bio-acids are included
acids such as lactic acid, acetic acid and butyric acid (McInerney M.J., 2003). Bio-acids
seem to be beneﬁcial in increasing porosity and hence the permeability of the carbonate
reservoirs as they cause dissolution of the carbonate rocks (Gray M., 2008).
Bio-solvents
Bio-solvents such as ethanol, acetone and butanol are produced by some species of Clostrid-
ium (acetobutylicum and pasteurianum) and Zymomonas mobilis. They can contribute in
the reduction of the surface interfacial tension as they can function as co-surfactants and
they help in the reduction of the oil viscosity leading to an increase of the oil production
(Youssef N., 2007).

Microbial biomass
Microbial biomass consists of bacteria that reproduce themselves and they start to form
a considerable amount of mass. Some of the genera that produce biomass are Bacillus
licheniformis, Leuconostoc mesenteroides and Xanthomonas campestris. The microbial
biomass contribute to the porosity clogging process as bacteria start to accumulate to-
gether with other polymer, bioﬁlm and slimes in the large pore throats of the porous
media (Al-Sulaimani, 2001).
1.3.2
Bacteria attachment and Bioﬁlm formation
A variate of diﬀerent processes occurs inside the porous media of the reservoir. One of
these processes is the bacteria attachment in the pore walls of the rock. The components
that the reservoir consists of tend to stick on the surfaces and subsequently they are accu-
mulated in formations that are called bioﬁlm. The bioﬁlm is a combination of water and
diﬀerent immobile constituents such as sticky polysaccharites and dissolved components
(Nielsen et al, 2010). The substrate as well as the bacterial metabolite that ﬂow in the
reservoir tend to adsorb by the pore walls but in relatively less extent than the bacterial
population (Kim et al, 2006). Bioﬁlm functions as a separate ecosystem that bacteria
which are stuck there present growth and produce metabolites. However, because of the
fact that it is more diﬃcult for the substrate to penetrate deeper in the bioﬁlm and feed
the bacteria, lower bacterial growth is indicated.
The composition of the bioﬁlm depends in a certain extent on the bacterial population, the
diﬀerent substances of the local biota as well as on other factors regarding the reservoir.
Some types of bacteria form multilayer bioﬁlms (such as Pseudomonas) (Characklis and
Wilderer, 1989). The pore size is a factor that actually aﬀect on the process of attachment.
The smaller the pore sizes the larger the eﬀects of ﬁltration in the process (Characklis and
Wilderer, 1989). Laboratory studies have shown that bioﬁlm tends to reduce the eﬀective
porosity of the reservoir and subsequently the permeability of the porous media (Pintelon
T.R.R., 2009).
The detachment of the bacteria from the bioﬁlm occurs mainly because of the erosion
that caused by the shear forces. These forces caused by hydrodynamics and the general
interaction of the ﬂowing components with the bioﬁlm. Furthermore, it has been inves-
tigated the inﬂuence of the matrix-degrading enzymes and lyctic bacteriophages in the
bioﬁlm detachment (Orgaz et al, 2006). In general, erosion makes the bioﬁlm smoother
and results to the facilitation of the ﬂow in the reservoir (Gikas and Livingston, 2006).
1.3.3
Physical and Chemical Constraints
Microbes are very conditions-dependent organisms. That means that every environment
depending on various physical and chemical factors can facilitate the growth of speciﬁc
types of species. Indigenous microorganisms are the organisms that have been adapted
perfectly with the local environment of the reservoir whose conditions cannot function
inhibitively for them. By studying these factors, scientists can outline the appropriate
==PAGE:16==

9
genera for the application of MEOR method in a speciﬁc type of reservoir. These factors
can be summarized in physical and chemical ones.
Physical Constraints
From the physical point of view, reservoir is a very adverse place for the microorganisms
with temperature which in many cases exceed 150 ◦C. In general microbes that are se-
lected for MEOR applications present maximum growth rate at temperatures of 80 ◦C.
However, there are some species such as Bacilus that can survive in temperatures up to
121 ◦C (KasheﬁK, 2003). According to Marshall (2008) high temperature can disrupt the
enzyme function that microorganisms depend on in a large extent due to denaturation
or disruption around the catalyzing sites. The eﬀects of high pressure seem to be more
indirect (Marshall, 2008) as high pressure aﬀects on the protein synthesis and changes in
gene expression occur.
The size of the microorganisms and the pore size have to be evaluated when microorgan-
isms are chosen for a speciﬁc reservoir for the application of MEOR. Pore size distribution
and the geometry of the microorganisms are very important parameters and they have to
be taken into consideration during the planning of the MEOR. In general, bacteria are
commonly used in the microbial enhanced oil recovery because of its small size and the
easiest penetration into the reservoir. Furthermore, some types of bacteria form spores
that make its penetration in the porous media even easier facilitating its transportation.
Chemical Constraints
One of the major chemical constraints for the microorganisms that lie on the reservoir is
salinity. High salinity of the water, inside the reservoir, has the tendency to inhibit growth
of microorganisms. When the salinity of the water is high, microbes have to conserve
the appropriate salinity of the cellular ﬂuids in order to maintain the enzymatic action
(Madigan et al, 2003). Thus, higher portion of energy is consumed for the conservation
of the salinity rate inside the cell. Because of the fact that salinity is increased with
depth (Nmegbu, 2004), the deeper the reservoir is lie, the harder is the growth of the
microorganisms.
Another important chemical factor is the pH. According to Donaldson (1989), although
that pH of oil bearing reservoir does not seem to be inhibiting for microbial growth, the
control of speciﬁc metabolic processes of microorganisms that are required for MEOR
could be complicated by pH gradient or with pH changes inside the reservoir. In general,
the acidity aﬀects on the surface change of the microorganisms and as a result the charge
aﬀects on the transportation of them in the reservoir.
Moreover, the evaluation of hydrophobicity of reservoir’s microorganisms is from one
hand diﬃcult to be accomplished but essential for the MEOR processes, because cell
surface hydrophobicity is one of the most important factors governing the transport of
microorganisms (Jiamin Wan, 1994).
According to Crescente (2006) in cases of pure
hydrophobic bacterial surfaces, the bacteria tend to form accumulations and transport as
ﬂocks. On the contrary, the bacteria with hydrophilic surfaces transport as single units.

1.4
Mechanisms of MEOR
After extensive laboratory work and plenty of ﬁeld trials in diﬀerent types of reservoir
and diﬀerent reservoir conditions there can be concluded three major mechanisms that
microbial EOR functions.
• Reduction of oil - water interfacial tension by the utilization of the surfactants that
microorganisms produce
• Fluid diversion that comes as a result of the permeability modiﬁcations that occurs
because of the produced bacterial biomass
• Reduction of the oil viscosity resulted from the degradation of the heavy oil components
by the microorganisms
The latter can be considered as a beneﬁcial side eﬀect, as it optimizes the quality of the
oil that lies on the reservoir and it enforces its extraction (Bryant and Burchﬁeld, 1989,
Sarkar, 1994). The mechanisms that they will be analyzed in detail below, act all of
them at the same time in a certain reservoir by applying the MEOR method. It can
intentionally be picked to promote the appropriate conditions in order to enforce one of
these mechanisms. However, all of them act in a certain proportion and they contribute
to the overall increment of the recovery factor.
1.4.1
Reduction of oil-water interfacial tension
Microorganisms which live in the reservoir produce some metabolites that function as sur-
factants. These surfactants are involved in processes such as transport of water-insoluble
compounds into the cell, bioﬁlm formation as well as adhesion of cell on diﬀerent surfaces.
In general, surfactants are biphilic molecules that interact with each other and with the
reservoir. These substances have the tendency to reduce the interfacial tension between
the oil and the water inside in the reservoir. As a result, the oil mobility increases and
more residual oil start to be recovered (Zekri and Almehaideb, 1999). In order for the
surfactant to be eﬀective, it has to exceed a certain concentration. That means that only
when the concentration of surfactants reaches a speciﬁc threshold can actually aﬀect the
interfacial tension between oil and water. The main problem of the surfactant ﬂooding is
that surfactant is adsorbed by the pore wall in the reservoir. Thus, a proportion of the
surfactants escape the free pore and the concentration of which decreases. In some cases
the concentration decreases more than required to achieve increased oil recovery. How-
ever, this phenomenon is noticed less often in microbial EOR method when surfactants
are produced in situ (Zekri and Almehaideb, 1999).

1.4.2
Porosity Clogging
After the waterﬂooding stage, the water forms some permeable paths underground across
the reservoir, between the injectors and the producers. When these paths are formed the
water which is injected by the injector crosses the reservoir and after a certain time point,
the eﬃciency in the oil recovery stops. However, there is still a portion of residual oil in
the reservoir, in places that water cannot be directed. By utilizing bacteria, a system is
created that bioﬁlm closes the permeable paths of the reservoir. As a result, the water is
directed to other paths and it drifts the oil that is trapped. More speciﬁcally, after the
adaption of the microorganisms in the reservoir, they start to reproduce and if scarcity of
nutrients occurs the bacteria convert to spores. A part of these bacteria and spores attach
to the pore walls of the porous media forming a thin layer of bioﬁlm. The production
of bacteria and sticky polysaccharides increase the volume of this layer. Consequently,
the porosity decreases due to bioﬁlm formation and reduce the ﬂow of the water. Thus,
the ﬂow of the water is diverted and the sweep eﬃciency increases with an increase of
the recovery. Generally, ﬂuid diversion is applicable to oil reservoirs with heterogeneous
permeability distribution. In order for the ﬂuid diversion to be successful, the following
criteria have to be satisﬁed.
• Controlled penetration of microorganisms throughout the reservoir
• Controlled transport of nutrients for microbial growth and metabolism
• Reduction of the apparent permeability of the reservoir rock as a result of microbial
growth and metabolism
The main risk of this mechanism is that the bacteria accumulate in the well bore region
where they form the bioﬁlm. This may leads to damages in the reservoir and reduction
of the production (Gray et al, 2008).

1.6
Stimulation of indigenous bacteria process
The stimulation of the indigenous bacteria is considered a kind of microbial EOR process
according to which the stimulation of the local bacterial population of the reservoir is
achieved, using the appropriate types of nutrients. This is a very demanding process as it
is required to understand how the local ecosystem of the reservoir functions as well as to
fully being aware of the bacterial (or other) populations that inhabit the reservoir. In this
process nutrients are injected in the reservoir and they are consumed by the indigenous
bacteria which present growth and they produce metabolite. A large number of diﬀerent
bacterial and other populations inhabit the reservoir. Therefore, in order for the MEOR
process to have the optimal results, the interaction of the bacterial genomes and the
reservoir has to be studied (Xiang TingSheng, 2009). Moreover, information has to be
collected regarding the local population and there is need for analysis which of these
populations present resilience in the conditions of the reservoir as well as which kind of
nutrient optimize their growth.
During the stimulation of the indigenous bacteria process, some types of the indigenous
bacteria which are chosen to be boosted using speciﬁc nutrient to prevail in terms of
growth compared to the others. This knowledge is gathered with extensive laboratory
work using samples from the ﬁeld which are analyzed for the extraction of the information
that are facilitative for the process. The samples are gathered not only from the injectors
but from the production wells in order to acquire a clear cognitive background of the
ecosystem of the oil reservoir (Fan-Zhang, 2011). However, as Fan Zhang (2011) notes,
microbial EOR is a method of tertiary recovery, which means that waterﬂooding and other
processes, are preceded in a certain reservoir. In some cases diﬀerent types of chemicals

• The sporulation and reactivation processes are triggered only by the scarcity and the
abundance of the substrate and not by other factors such as the environmental condition
of the reservoir.

4.2.2
Discretization Scheme
In order to model the MEOR system, discretization process occurs that facilitates not
only the scheme but the calculation as well. The main two parameters which are the
time and the space are splitted in intelligible segments which separately are dealt with in
order to solve the system. As space in this model is one-dimensional, the whole system
is described with a two dimensional matrix in which the x axis and y axis describe the
spacial position (with step ∆x) and the changes of time (with step ∆τ), respectively. In
order to distinguish the speciﬁc spacial and time step, indices are used. The k indice
refers to distance and the n indice refers to time.

terms of time (vertically)
In the ﬁrst version of the program, implicit discretization was occurred as a ’tanks in
series’ approach applied. In this approach, calculations achieved in a sequential manner,
using a system where the spacial loops are nested in a time loop. In such a way, the

calculations was utilized. According to the parallel approach, calculations occur not only
in spacial manner but in time as well. In this way the system begins taking into account
the initial conditions and the calculations proceed in x axis and y axis simultaneously.
In terms of distance the outﬂow of the certain block is the inﬂow of the following one.

The saturation and the mass concentration of a block that represents a speciﬁc state is

calculations for 8 core processors CPU as the program predestinates one calculation of
a block to each processor. Thus, the program splits and it implements calculation of 8
diﬀerent blocks at the same time.
In the parallel approach the calculations begin in the ﬁrst block that represents the state
of reservoir in zero time and zero distance (injector). The calculations proceed diagonally
until the upper right block of the matrix that represents the end of the reservoir (pro-
duction well) at the time that the process ends. The ﬁrst column as well as the ﬁrst row
contains values of the initial conditions as it is described in Chapter 3. These conditions
represent what is injected and what the reservoir initially contains.

4.2.4
Constraints and Errors
The program is created in such a way to proceed to the solution of the MEOR process
regardless the validity of the given inputs. This means that the user is responsible for the
data that is input in the system as the data that describe the system have to be between
the correct typical ranges. However a mistaken value (out of the typical range of a speciﬁc
property that is considered unreasonably high or low) leads to mistaken and unreasonable
results. The program is formed in order to trace some of the mistakes of the process and
display errors that orientate the user to correct the initial input values.
One of the main constraints is the input number of the blocks in terms of distance and
time. The program tends to output more realistic results as the number of spacial and
time blocks increases. Also, the ratio between the spacial step to the time step requires not
to exceed a certain limit as it is described in the Section 3.2.2. Otherwise the calculations
are not implemented properly and the run terminates with unreasonable results.
Furthermore, one of the crucial parameters that the program takes into consideration is
the porosity of the reservoir. The program is designed to continue the run as the porosity
of the system (in all the blocks) remains larger than zero. As the bioﬁlm is formed the
porosity tends to reduce. In general, when it is taken into account 2-dimensional or 3-
dimensional systems and the porosity of a speciﬁc block is clogged, the ﬂuids tend to
penetrate deeper into the reservoir through the alongside ways of the block. However in
this model the system is 1-dimensional which means that the ﬂuids cannot escape from a
speciﬁc clogged block and to proceed to the following one. Consequently, the program is
designed to terminate the calculation when porosity clogging of a block occurs, signifying
the end of the process.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
Using the model that is described in the Chapter 2 and 3 and by incorporating the
indigenous bacterial populations of the reservoir, an extensive parametric analysis was
held. According to the model that was applied, including the assumptions that was taken
into consideration and through this parametric analysis, the following conclusions was
extracted:
• Stimulation of indigenous bacteria is achievable, economical but it presents low addi-
tional (to the waterﬂooding) oil recovery.
• Not the higher concentration of metabolite but the fastest produced one presents higher
oil recover factor.
• The distribution of the indigenous bacterial concentration (bacteria and spores) in the
ﬂowing and in the attached phase actually inﬂuences the oil recovery.
• In a certain reservoir, the higher the proportion of the indigenous bacterial concentration
in the attached phase (bacteria and spores) the lower the oil recovery. This is because of
the isolated growth of the bacterial population which are not distributed uniformly inside
the reservoir. Thus, the produced metabolites are drifted by the injected water phase and
they spread later in the rest of the reservoir. As a result, when the metabolite is produced
the Sor reduces in an extensive time frame that procrastinates the oil recovery.
• In a certain reservoir, the higher the proportion of the indigenous bacterial concentration
in the form of spores (spore concentration) the lower the oil recovery. This is because the
spores need some extra time to be reactivated in bacteria, in order to produce metabolite.
As a result, the production of the metabolite comes later as well as the oil recovery.
• In a certain reservoir the higher the proportion of the indigenous bacterial concentra-
tion in the ﬂowing phase the higher the oil recovery. This is because the bacteria are
transported along the reservoir as well as their metabolites (dispertion of metabolites).
This dispersion of the metabolites reduces the Sor more eﬀectively (along the reservoir)
and increases the oil recovery.
• The injection of a certain mass of exogenous bacteria appears to be more eﬃcient in
111
==PAGE:119==
the oil recovery compared to the stimulation of the same mass of indigenous bacteria.
This is because when bacteria and substrate are injected, the reaction between these is
instantaneous as they are at the same distance point. On the contrary, when substrate
is injected in the reservoir, there is needed some extra time to overpass the reservoir and
feed the bacteria (activate the bacterial growth) that lie along it.
• The smaller the time frame of the injection of a speciﬁc mass of exogenous bacteria in
a certain reservoir, the higher the oil recovery.
• The injection of surfactants tends to show better eﬃcacy compared to the MEOR
technique. However, the process is very expensive.
• In order for an ’injection of surfactants’ process to be cost eﬀective, the appropriate
concentration of surfactants have to be investigated which from the one hand, reduces
the residual oil saturation and from the other hand, do not make the process economi-
cally ineﬃcient. This means that when the concentration of the surfactant exceeds this
appropriate level not only do not aﬀect the oil recovery but put extra expenses (due to
the surfactant material) to the process.
• Injection of spores can boost the bacterial population deeper into the reservoir because
of their small size. This can avoid a possible clog of the pores at the region close to the
injector that the bacterial mass tends to attach (and close the pores).
• Strategically planned sequence of slugs can actually lead to an optimization of the oil re-
covery. For this process, a large number of parameters have to be taken into considaration
regarding not only the reservoir (porosity) but also properties regarding the bacteria.
