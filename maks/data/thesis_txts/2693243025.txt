ABSTRACT 
There is a paradigm shift in the building industry towards off-site manufacturing of building 
components (e.g., walls, roofs, floors) or large sections of buildings (e.g., modular rooms, flat-pack) 
that can be assembled on-site. This shift is often driven by housing shortages, demands for better 
quality constructions, or a lack of skilled labor in the construction sector. Greenland is battling 
similar challenges, and thus it seems logical to move towards off-site manufacture Greenlandic 
buildings.  
Greenlandic construction companies are interested in the prospect of off-site construction, but they 
need a clearer outline of the costs, benefits, and risks. This thesis, therefore, aims to deliver a 
holistic overview of the implications of using off-site constructions in Greenland.  
The research is characterized by a mix of qualitative and quantitative methods, with qualitative-
data-analysis forming the foundation of knowledge and understanding of sociocultural and 
industrial context. I went on a 6-week field trip to Sisimiut in Greenland and lived among people in 
a Greenlandic community to observe and learn about Greenlandic customs and ways of life. This 
lead to a collection of qualitative data from interviews, observations, and literature reviews. I 
analyzed this data using a qualitative data analysis software called MAXQDA to identify patterns 
and highlight different perspectives. I applied quantitative methods to gather and visualize climate 
data and highlighting different costs. 
The findings in this study confirm that off-site manufacturing of Greenlandic buildings will reduce 
the need for specialists, create buildings of higher quality, and enable shorter construction times. 
However, the findings also reveal that off-site construction will increase transport and material 
costs, and therefore I do not expect off-site construction to initially reduce the construction cost 
compared to a conventional construction method.

1 INTRODUCTION 
There is a paradigm shift in the building industry towards off-site manufacturing of building 
components (e.g., walls, roofs, floors) or large sections of buildings (e.g., modular rooms, flat 
packs) that can be assembled on-site. This shift is often driven by housing shortages, demands for 
better quality constructions, or a lack of skilled labor in the construction sector. Off-site 
constructions have so far proven their value in many counties around the world, and Greenland 
seems like a prime candidate for a similar transition, as they battle with the same driving forces. 
Furthermore, the Greenlandic climate provides challenging conditions for builders, with snow, low 
temperatures, and strong winds. This can make it difficult to transport and store building materials 
and to plan and manage workflows. Consequently, constructions and renovations are often 
delayed and have significant cost overruns.  
The local construction companies in Greenland are interested in the prospect of off-site 
manufacturing, but they need a clearer outline of the costs, benefits, and risks. Therefore, this 
thesis aims to deliver a comprehensive overview of the implications of transitioning to off-site 
constructions in Greenland. The analysis forms a holistic view with consideration to the social- and 
industrial context.  
I designed a conceptual framework to help answer the research questions and view the problem 
statement from multiple aspects. Qualitative methods are the primary information source as there 
is limited information regarding the subject of this thesis. I gathered this information through a 6-
week field trip to Sisimiut in Greenland, where I lived among people in a Greenlandic community to 
observe and learn about Greenlandic customs and ways of life. This lead to a collection of 
qualitative data from interviews, observations, and literature reviews. I analyzed the data through a 
deductive qualitative data analysis tool called MAXQDA to identify patterns and highlight 
perspectives.  
The thesis starts with an introduction to the Greenlandic setting and a literature review of off-site 
constructions, leading to the background for the problem statement. The problem statement is 
followed by an elaboration of relevant theory and an elaboration of the research framework. The 
methodology section then explains the research steps and methods for qualitative and quantitative 
data collection and analysis. The analysis and findings are presented in subsections that 
correspond to the perspective from which they are studied. The results reveal how the findings 
relate to the problem statement. The method and results are debated in the discussion, and the 
conclusion is explained in the last chapter.

2.1 THE GREENLANDIC SETTING 
Greenland is the world's largest island located between the Arctic and Atlantic oceans at the 
northern polar circle. Given its massive size, the population is relatively small, with only 56,421 
(2021) inhabitants and the world’s least densely populated territory of only 0.028 people per km2 
(Greenland Statistics, 2021). Furthermore, roughly 15% live in smaller villages and cities across 
the south, east and western coast, with boat or plane being the only option to travel between 
locations (Greenland Statistics, 2021). The largest city is the capital Nuuk, with a population of 
18,800 (2021), and Sisimiut is the next largest city with 5,622 (2021) people (Greenland Statistics, 
2021).  
Due to Greenland's large size and relatively small population, there is a general shortage of 
specialists within the construction sector.

The distance between Greenlandic cities and settlements is often large with impassible terrain and 
without interconnecting roads. This makes transport of workers and material challenging as it can 
only happen by ship or aircraft. Consequently, commuting between settlements is not an everyday 
activity, as plane tickets are expensive, and sailing is time-consuming. 
Goods are moved around via the Greenlandic freight company Royal Arctic Line (RAL). They use 
large container ships to transport goods around the larger Greenlandic cities, Island, USA, 
Canada, and Denmark. Smaller container ships called “Bygdeskibe” can, in some cases, sail to the 
smaller settlements, but the inhabitants often have to visit the larger cities to receive goods.  
The Arctic climate in Greenland means that temperatures can get very low, especially in the north 
and inland, where the lowest temperature ever recorded reached -69.6°C. In Nuuk, the mean 
lowest temperature for February between 1991-2020 was -10.9°C (DMI, n.d.). Greenland is 
extensive; hence there is considerable variability across the Greenlandic climate. The temperature 
can have significant daily variations creating optimal conditions for frost and thaw.  
The wind can be very harsh in Greenland. Gusts can come from the ice sheet or high-lying 
mountain plateaus, where accumulations of cold air crash like avalanches over the terrain towards 
the coast. The Inuit term for this is Piteraq, which means ‘an assault’. In a few minutes, it can crash 
down, and the most terrific have average winds of 40 meters per second and gusts of 70 m/s, i.e., 
double hurricane strength (Danmarks Meteorologiske Institut, 2019). 
 INUIT CULTURE 
The traditional Inuit way of life was adapted to the Arctic tundra's harsh climate and stark 
landscapes. The Inuit’s invented tools, gear, and shelters from naturally available materials to help 
them survive in this environment. They also used sleds made of animal bones and skins that dogs 
pulled. This tradition of dog sledding is kept today north of the polar circle. They also invented 
kayaks to scout and hunt for food, while larger boats called “umiaq” were used to transport people, 
dogs, and supplies. Since most plants cannot grow in Greenland, the Inuit diet consisted of almost 
entirely meat from whales, walruses, seals, and fish (Anderson et al., 1999). 
The Inuit religion was a form of shamanism with rituals integrated into people's daily lives. They 
believed that a pantheon of supernatural entities could influence the spirits when one required 
some animal or inanimate being to act in a certain way. According to a customary Inuit saying,  
“The great peril of our existence lies in the fact that our diet consists entirely of 
souls.” – Attributed to Aua, Inuit Sharman 
 DANISH COLONIZATION 
The priest Hans Egede initiated Greenland's change towards a Christian society and sought to link 
the island closer to Denmark. In 1721 he arrived in Nuuk, where he began his mission. He went up 
there to find the Norsemen, whom he reckoned must be Catholics and wanted to missionize and 
convert them to Protestantism. The Norsemen were no longer there, and Egede was not 
enthusiastic about the Inuit religion, although he also believed that the conversion of the Inuit

should respect the culture. During the mission, he translated Christian scriptures into Greenlandic, 
which was the first step in the Danish colonization of Greenland (Simon Mølholm Olesen, 2018).  
 MODERN GREENLAND 
Denmark set up the ‘Great Greenland Commission’ after World War II. In 1950, the commission 
compiled the ‘G-50’ report: Denmark had to raise the standard of living, the level of knowledge, the 
production, and expand the infrastructure and not least merge the county councils. The 
Greenlandic national councils agreed with the proposals, and the Danish state covered 50% of the 
reform costs. This industrialization of Greenland has rapidly changed the Greenlandic way of life 
(Hedtoft, 1953).  
In order to optimize society in the areas proposed by the G-50, it was believed to be necessary to 
implement a policy of concentration. Therefore, the Greenlandic community was built around larger 
cities, e.g., Nuuk and Sisimiut, where factories, schools, and homes were gathered. The by-
product became an increasing population, limiting overcrowded apartment blocks and gave a 
negative social spiral (emu, 2020). 
After being elected to the National Council in 1971, Jonathan Motzfeldt proposed establishing a 
committee to investigate Greenland's possibility of home rule. The committee came to consist of 
Greenlandic politicians, and it was proposed to make Greenlandic the primary language of 
instruction in schools, soften the birthplace criterion, and improve democratic conditions. All this 
without secession and without renouncing subsidies from Denmark (emu, 2020). 
 COLONIAL BUILDINGS 
The peathouses became a standard construction for Greenlandic housing after Danish colonization 
in 1721. These houses' roof was disassembled each spring to air out when the Inuits moved into 
tents and prepared for the summer travels. Archeologists found that the peathouses along the 
Greenlandic West coast changed during 1700 from housing one family to larger houses for multiple 
families (Greenland National Museum & Achieves, n.d.). 
When the "Bergenskompagniet" came to Greenland in 1730 and started creating trade-posts, it 
was natural to use the Norweigan building concept of "Laftehus" or "stokværkshus," as housing for 
the traders. This house was an early example of off-site construction as it could be completely 
disassembled, then moved, and reassembled in a short time. The wall consisted of logs that were 
cut and placed horizontally on top of each other. About 50 of these houses were created between 
1734-1847, but the production stopped after 1847 (Nunnatta Katersugaasivia Allagaateqarfialu, 
2016). 
The Greenlandic houses evolved over 1800 with the influence from Europe and access to new 
building materials. The traditional long entrance tunnel was replaced with an entryway, a small 
passageway connected with the front door to the house. This room has the purpose of reducing 
the houses' heat loss by preventing wind, cold, and moisture from penetrating directly into the 
building and creating a draft. The facades started to be constructed of stone or timber with an 
insulating peat wall at the exterior (Nunnatta Katersugaasivia Allagaateqarfialu, 2016).

A new replacement and supplement to the "stokværksusene" were constructed from the1880s. 
The replacement was a type of boarded half-timbered house with three layers of boards. This 
construction style has been kept with only a few alterations till recent times. 
 CONTEMPORARY BUILDINGS 
"Today's Greenlandic housing construction is no messenger of originality, traditions, 
and regional building customs. It has come from Denmark and is completely 
dominated by changing Danish housing ideals. However, it has not been carried out 
without the care of the Greenlanders."- Steenfos & Taagholt, 2013. Page 313. 
There is generally no overarching building style in modern Greenland. The architecture is 
characterized by various building compositions, from the colonial "stokværkshuse" to the traditional 
multistorey blocks (Kommuneqarfik Sermersooq, 2014).  
Crawl spaces are a widely used foundation method due to the terrain conditions; however, mold 
growth can often be observed in these spaces. Other foundation methods, e.g., Terrain decks, are 
rarely seen as appropriate alternatives due to the risk of flooding in the event of thaw.

2.2 THE GREENLANDIC HOUSING SITUATION 
Apart from the mountainous landscapes and extreme climate, one of the major challenges is the 
lack of naturally occurring building materials. Most things, from everyday items to building 
materials, must be imported (Møller & Lading, 2020). Since Greenland is an island-operated 
community, transport is a significant challenge as there is no interlinking road infrastructure. 
Therefore, transport in between cities and settlements happens by boat, plane, helicopter, and 
snowmobiles.  
Moisture and mold are other issues that are common in Greenlandic homes (Kotol, 2012). People 
spend more than 70% of their time indoors and have gotten costumed to stuff airgaps, ventilation 
hatches, and leaks to stop the draft from entering the building. A consequence of this behavior is 
that the relative humidity and CO2 build-up create mold and poor air quality (Kotol, 2012). Figure 3 
shows a newspaper article from INI A/S, which is the Greenlandic autonomous building 
administration. The text in the upper left corner reads as follows:  
"Take responsibility – take good care of your home.  
Mold development can be self-inflicted, and you may be held liable for irresponsible 
behavior."

The housing shortage in Greenland is an ongoing topic reinforced by recent demands for better 
quality housing. Modern housing demands have put the construction sector under pressure by 
lacking skilled specific labor to produce and maintain modern buildings. Other related issues are 
undoubtedly rooted in Greenlandic building politics, which has been called complicated and 
unbalanced. 
"Although the intentions have been good, a very complicated housing policy has 
contributed to a reduced housing stock and given an imbalance in the economic 
distribution policy" - Kristoffer S. Pedersen (2019). 
The government finances most buildings in Greenland (Møller & Lading, 2020), and constructions 
thus must be balanced with the public budget. The governmental strategy is to create cheap 
buildings quickly to mitigate the housing situation. However, the consequence is that the buildings 
are often poorly constructed where cold and drafts pour into the buildings, consequently reinforcing 
the demand for better-quality housing (BygTek, 2019). 
The building shortage in Greenland is evident from multiple official reports and newspaper articles. 
The municipality Qeqqata Kommunia sent out a public press release in 2010, mentioning that they 
are making efforts to relieve the issue. The following is a translated snippet from that press 
release: 
“Due to continued housing shortages, joint efforts and cooperation between the 
Government and Qeqqata Kommunia will ensure coordinated multi-year plans for 
the construction of houses, thus ensuring a better framework for planning and 
development of housing and residential areas in Sisimiut.” 
- Press release from Qeqqata Kommunia (2010). 
The coordinated construction plan is well underway in Sisimiut, with new houses being built in 
Akia. However, reports suggest that even more homes are needed in the coming years. According 
to a report by the economic advisory of Greenland Aningaasaqarnermut Siunnersuisoqatigiit, it 
seems that the building shortage will increase in the larger cities during the following decades 
(Siunnersuisoqatigiit, 2014). This shortage happens because people are starting to move from 
smaller settlements to the larger cities Nuuk, Sisimiut, Ilulissat, and Qaqortoq. In Nuuk, there are 
currently (2020), alone at the municipal company Iserit, more than 2,000 people on the waiting list 
for public rental housing (Petersen, 2020). On average, there are 7,5 years waiting lists for public 
rental homes. In Sisimiut, the report projects that the housing demand will increase from ~3,000 to 
~10,000 homes between 2013-2040 through a linear growth rate. In Nuuk, the growth rate seems 
exponential and is projected to increase from ~2,700 in 2021 to 10,500 in 2040 
(Siunnersuisoqatigiit, 2014).  
There is also a significant renovation backlog, and some buildings stand empty for a long time 
before they can be demolished. Some people live in homes that do not fulfill housing living 
standards. It is homes that cannot be demolished because there are no substitute homes for the 
residents (Kristoffer Seidelin Pedersen, 2019).

In 2014, the Economic Council of Greenland produced a report describing Greenland's housing 
policy problems. The report states that the housing policy in Greenland creates more problems 
than it solves. The council based its criticism on an untransparent policy and a misconception that 
housing can be made cheaper for everyone. The policy intends to make it cheap for everyone by 
granting a high allowance and low rent for public housing. The rent in public housing is artificially 
set below the market price and thus below the construction costs. Therefore, it is difficult to 
maintain a turnover where renovations and reinvestments are affordable. The policy also involves 
several favorable taxation and loan conditions for homeowners to create an incentive for the 
resourceful to move out of public housing and into owner-occupied or cooperative housing.

2.3 OFF-SITE MODULAR CONSTRUCTIONS 
Off-site manufacturing refers to the manufacturing, planning, design, fabrication, and assembly of 
building components at a location other than the final building site. The idea behind the concept is 
to boost the speed and efficiency of constructing a permanent structure. The building components 
may be prefabricated at a different location off-site or prefabricated on the building site and then 
moved to their final location.  
There are various purposes for off-site constructions, single-family homes, multi-storage housing, 
rowhouses, health care, and commercial applications. The buildings can consist of a few modular 
units to several hundreds of flatpacks. 
Figure 4: Three types of off-site modular constructions 
PANELIZED BUILDING SYSTEM 
Panelized building systems consist of structural and non-structural wall panels, floor- and ceiling 
cassettes. Open panels are typical without insulation and include only the structural elements and 
often sheeting on one side. Closed panels include everything from insulation to installations with 
sheeting on both sides. They can also have internal linings, external cladding, windows, and doors 
fitted in the module. Structurally insulated panels (SIPS) are solid insulation sandwiched with glue 
between two layers of oriented strand boards (OSB). 
VOLUMETRIC BUILDING SYSTEMS 
Volumetric building systems represent 3D units that enclose a usable space. They are typically 
fully finished internally but can also be equipped with an external finish. Some of these systems 
even contain multiple rooms in connection to each other, like an entrance and bathroom.  
Pods are a smaller non-structural type of volumetric building system often used for bathrooms or 
plant rooms, especially in hotels and student accommodations.  
HYBRID BUILDING SYSTEMS 
Hybrid Building Systems cover various construction techniques where panelized- and volumetric 
systems and different materials are used in combination.

 THE HISTORY OF OFF-SITE CONSTRUCTION 
Off-site manufacturing can be traced back to the Middle Ages when carpenters would often 
assemble the frames of buildings in the yard to ensure that all elements fitted together. The frames 
were then marked and dismantled to be transported to the building site and reassembled. This 
system was known as the quick frame. In the 19th century, the technique developed into the 
familiar timber balloon and platform framing.  
During the Crimean War in the 1850s, British engineer Isambard Kingdom Brunel designed the 
Renkioi hospital, with timber-framed structures manufactured in the UK that were then flat-packed, 
shipped, and assembled in Turkey by unskilled workers in under six months (Bektas, 2017). 
 
Figure 5: Renkioi Hospital designed by Isambard Kingdom Brunel

historical buildings from the colonial times in 1700-1800, initially constructed in Denmark or Norway 
before they were disassembled, numbered, and shipped to their destination in Greenland.

4.4 COMPLEXITY: WORK BREAKDOWN STRUCTURE AND COST ESTIMATION 
The development of an off-site manufacturing process and pairing that with the Greenlandic needs 
is a complex task. It calls for integrating diverse tools and knowledge bases across multiple parties, 
calling for the collaboration of people with different backgrounds and the reconciliation of 
stakeholders' different views and interests. This integration and coordination are what the 
complexity aspect is referring to. The complexity is about all the practical aspects of how, when, 
and where the project can be realized.  
 Work Breakdown Structure 
Estimating the costs and benefits of Greenlandic off-site construction will start by understanding 
the scope of the work. A tool for this is a work breakdown structure (WBS). The WBS works by 
breaking the project down into several substantial chunks. Each of these chunks of work is then 
further broken down into its principal components. Then each of these principal components is 
further broken down until we end up in the lowest end of the hierarchy. Here we have individual 
tasks or activities that the person who carries out the tasks recognizes as a single coherent task 
that they fully understand (Cerezo-Narváez et al., 2020).  
Once we have the WBS, we can estimate the time it will take to deliver each component of work, 
and from that, we can allocate people into the roles to do the work. From this, we can start building 
up the cost estimate through a cost breakdown structure (CBS). The CBS represents the costs of 
the components in the WBS. A CBS is thus a hierarchical representation of the various costs in a 
project, where the WBS is a hierarchical representation of the various tasks in a project (Cerezo-
Narváez et al., 2020). This thesis compares a WBS for an off-site construction approach to a 
conventional construction approach to highlight the difference. I have not created a CBS as it 
requires substantial knowledge about each component of work, like the number of bolts used, 
which is not within the scope of this thesis.  
A common way to represent the WBS is by numbering the principal activities, and for each 
component attached to the principal activity is given a sub number. In the end, we end up with a 
comprehensive list of the work that needs to be done e.i., the scope of the project.

 Cost Estimation 
Estimating and highlighting the costs and benefits is more reasonable for this type of thesis than 
attempting a complete cost-benefit analysis. The purpose of any cost-benefit assessment is to 
determine if an action/project is sound and ascertain if and how much the benefits outweigh the 
costs. In a cost-benefit analysis, as far as possible, all consequences of the investigated action or 
project must be calculated in monetary units and added together.  
A complete cost-benefit analysis can also measure a project's societal value by quantifying the 
project's societal effects and making costs and benefits comparable in monetary terms (Koopmans 
& Mouter, 2020). If the result is a net gain and increases societal and economic welfare, the action 
should be completed (Rosen & Gayer, 2014). The full cost-benefit analysis often involves a 
discount of costs and benefits, a Net Present Value (NPV) calculation, and a sensitivity analysis.  
This thesis is using empirical studies with quantitative data to highlight and exemplify the following 
cost sources: 
• 
People: Workers, contractors, and consultants 
 
• 
Property: Offices, facilities, and warehousing 
 
• 
Assets: Equipment and transport 
 
• 
Materials: Construction materials 
Other costs (i.e., licenses, foreign exchange, insurance, etc.) also affect the costs and benefits. 
However, this thesis will try to quantify these aspects due to limited resources and high 
uncertainties.  
Estimation Methods 
There are multiple techniques to estimate costs. This paper relies on Parametric Forecasting 
together with published and available pricing tables. This technique is best described with an 
example: 
If we know that a worker can lay 100 bricks an hour, and if we know we need to lay 200.000 bricks, 
we can work out how many hours of work it takes. If we then have to fit the job in a specific 
timeframe, that tells us how many workers we need. If we also know the average salary of a 
bricklayer from a pricing catalog, we have all the data needed to estimate the costs of laying bricks.

4.6 PURPOSE: SWOT ANALYSIS 
The purpose is about identifying why we should do this project? - followed by what would it deliver? 
Although purpose seems vital to projects, it has not traditionally been covered in best practices and 
guidelines for project management (J. Geraldi et al., 2017). Tools have been developed in the 
interface between organizational context and projects and are considered outside project 
management.  
The classic form of measuring a project's success is evaluating whether it meets time, cost, and 
scope constraints. However, researchers suggest that this traditional way of evaluating project 
success is not optimal on its own. Alternatively, one could consider and combine the three 
constraints with stakeholder satisfaction. 
"The purpose should provide the direction to a "future state," that people can believe 
in and subscribe to" - J. Geraldi et al., 2017 
The purpose is associated with the benefits of off-site manufacturing. These benefits will reveal 
themselves by outlining the Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats of off-site 
manufacturing. The analysis of the purpose aspect is done through literature reviews, quantitative- 
and qualitative data research.  
 SWOT MODEL 
The SWOT stands for Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats, and it is a tool used to 
identify internal and external factors that affect an organization’s performance. The SWOT model 
can be used in any decision-making situation when the desired objective is defined, and it is not 
limited to profit-seeking organizations (Westhues et al., 2001).  
Strengths and weaknesses are internal factors, and opportunities and threats are external factors.  
Strengths in the SWOT analysis are internal capabilities and positive factors relevant for the 
company to achieve its objectives and serve its customers efficiently (Culp III et al., 2016). 
Weaknesses are internal factors or constraints which might impede or hinder the performance of 
the organization.  
Opportunities are external factors or features in the environment that the company can exploit to its 
advantage.  
Threats deal with negative external factors, which can hinder or delay goals.

5.2 QUALITATIVE METHODS 
Qualitative research distinguishes itself from quantitative research by analyzing non-numerical data 
to understand concepts, opinions, and experiences. This type of research is helpful to gather in-
depth insight into topics not well understood. It focuses on exploring ideas to formulate hypotheses 
through open-ended questions. Engineers generally tend to quantify data to compare and validate 
the research. Therefore, there is a common lack of qualitative methods and social science in civil 
engineering, and it has been advised to incorporate more of these social sciences into the civil 
engineering education (Evans, 2020).  
Social scientists have experience developing community structures and relationship models, and I 
have been consulting experts within this area to help guide the project. This thesis's qualitative 
data handling is driven by knowledge gathered from interviews with seven separate social 
anthropology and ethnography professionals (from Cold Climate Housing Research Center 
CCHRC, Technical University of Denmark DTU, and NORDEC Consulting and Design). From 
these interviews, it became evident that the best way to gather qualitative data and understand a 
foreign culture is to go there to live and breathe in the environment.  
Interviewing is an excellent tool to gather information on concepts, opinions, and experiences used 
as qualitative research. This is done by identifying patterns throughout the interviews which can 
say something about the social and industrial context. Unlike quantitative research, qualitative data 
is presented by describing data without numerical values but instead using quotes and highlighting 
narratives. The qualitative research in this paper is descriptive research that informs about human 
motives, opinions, experiences, and barriers that are unfeasible to quantify statistically.   
Given Greenland's small population size and the fact that research data is scarce, it is not easy to 
make reliable statistics. Off-site manufacturing of Greenlandic buildings is a topic with limited 
documentation. Qualitative data analysis is thus a necessary means for understanding the 
Greenlandic context of off-site constructions. Other data regarding the construction sector in 
Greenland can be found in official legislation, regulations, reports, and from companies working in 
Greenland.  
Evaluating the feasibility for off-site manufacturing of Greenlandic buildings is based on how it is 
expected to contribute to Greenlandic society. Projects in small remote societies like the cities in 
Greenland are highly dependent on local engagement and interest (Francisco de Oliveira & 
Rabechini, 2019). Projects can seem economically viable from a quantitative perspective, but they 
will die out if the project is unwanted by the local community.

5.3 QUANTITATIVE METHODS 
Quantitative research is a form of analysis based on measurable numeric data. It is used to 
statistically express data, make predictions, test causal relationships, and generalize results. 
Correlations and experiments are ways within quantitative research where the researcher can 
formally test hypotheses or predictions using statistics. If the sampling size is large enough, then 
results may be generalized to a broader population (Jung, 2019).  
Quantitative data uses operational definitions that translate abstract concepts into observable and 
quantifiable measures, e.g., rating a person's energy level on a scale from 1-10. Once such data is 
collected, it needs to be processed into numbers used to express statistical answers to various 
research questions. The cost estimations in this thesis are based on quantitative data from 
measurements, pricing catalogs, and reports, while the climate descriptions are made from 
external hourly measurements. 
Descriptive statistics are summaries of the data and include measures of averages and variability. 
It often includes graphs, different plots, and frequency tables to visualize data and check for trends 
and outliers. I used measurements from DMI and Asiaq to visualize the Greenlandic climate in 
different graphs. I calculated the mean daily values and the 30-day moving average to visualize the 
air temperature and relative humidity in Sisimiut for 2021. I also calculated the average low, mean, 
and high air temperatures in Sisimiut between 2011-2021 to visualize the great temperature 
variances. Descriptive statistics are also used in the cost estimations. Here I calculated the cost of 
an off-site manufacturing facility. I simulated the facility's heating energy consumption and derived 
an average from five simulations. I also estimated the cost of transport options per m3 of goods to 
compare different transportation options.

6 ANALYSIS AND FINDINGS 
This chapter presents the data and results used to answer the research question.  
Most construction efforts can reduce housing shortages by simply building more homes; however, 
the attractiveness of the construction method is guided by its implications relative to other 
solutions. The success criteria for this project are based on off-site manufacturing's ability to 
reduce housing shortages by creating socially desirable homes of high quality that are faster and 
more affordable to construct relative to a conventional construction method. The following priority 
list elaborates on the success criteria 
1. To be socially attractive, off-site manufacturing of Greenlandic buildings must be adaptable to 
the Greenlandic context, respect Greenlandic culture, and account for Greenlandic challenges. 
 
2. The off-site constructions should be of high quality and live up to modern housing standards. 
They should be airtight but ventilated and be durable but easy to assemble and disassemble 
 
3. The off-site constructions should reduce construction time and make it easier to plan and 
manage timetables.  
 
4. The total construction cost of off-site constructions should be lower than with the conventional 
method, and the homes should consequently become more affordable for the residents.

 POLITICAL ASPECTS 
Greenland has a rich history of being subject to Danish colonization, and it was first in May 1979 
that the area became a sovereign state within the kingdom of Denmark (Naalakkersuisut, n.d.). By 
vote in 2008, it was decided to move more power from the Danish government to the Greenlandic 
government; however, Denmark is still responsible for foreign affairs, military, police, and the legal 
system. Furthermore, Denmark is still controlling the monetary policies as Denmark is yearly 
contributing with a regular block grant of DKK 3,469.6 billion. The subsidy is set at the same level 
as the state's subsidy before the introduction of self-government in 2009. The subsidy is adjusted 
annually with the price- and wage index but was DKK 3,911.3 million in 2020 (Folketingets Social- 
og Indenrigsudvalg, 2020). 
The political factors that intervene in the economy and building industry have a particular impact on 
the proposed construction process. The public institutes are financing most building projects in 
Greenland and thus significantly influence what is being constructed (Møller & Lading, 2020).  
“It is often a requirement to use concrete as a building material in government 
building tenders” – Greenlandic contractor 
Current political forces are working on advancing the use of concrete in buildings. This 
advancement challenges the freedom for off-site construction, as wooden constructions are lighter 
and easier to transport and assemble. The reasoning behind this is to limit mold as mold needs 
organic material to grow. 
“The trade relations with the outside world must be taken into account through the 
continuous development of the infrastructure.”  
- (Ministry of Foreign Affairs of Denmark, 2020). 
The official foreign trade policy is governed by Denmark and built on a free trade agreement with 
the Arctic, Scandinavia, and EU. The Danish government wishes to establish better infrastructure 
in Greenland to accommodate foreign trade. 
 ECONOMICAL ASPECTS 
The Gross domestic product (GDP) for Greenland has steadily increased since the year 2000 and 
was 18.5 billion DKK in 2018 (Danmarks Nationalbank, 2019). However, the progress has led to a 
shortage of labor in many areas, professionally and geographically. Public finances reflect the 
favorable economic situation through rising revenues from taxes and duties. With 2014 as an 
exception, there has been a surplus on the operating and construction balance in recent years, 
although balances have generally been regulated (Danmarks Nationalbank, 2019).  
The distance between different cities and settlements is often large with impassible terrain and 
without interconnecting roads. This makes transport of workers and material difficult as it has to 
happen by boat or aircraft. Building in Greenland is therefore costly and challenging to plan and 
manage. Commute between settlements is not an everyday activity, and consequently, there might 
be shortages of workers in one city and a surplus in another. Also, due to the large size and low 
population number, there is a shortage of specialists.  
==PAGE:49==
 
 
41 
 
“We tend to lack specialized labor. When i.e., a new HVAC system fails in a new 
built, it can be necessary to fly in specialists from Nuuk or Denmark to fix the issue.” 
– Engineer 
The available specialists are mainly situated in Nuuk, making it a costly affair to transport 
specialists between cities and settlements. The same is true for building materials, and it can often 
take much time and money before they can be delivered.  
“It is difficult to create a timetable that lasts” - Contractor 
Time scheduling is difficult to manage, and waiting on materials or human resources combined 
with bad weather can quickly stagger any construction project. 
Greenland has a current unemployment rate of 2.6% among people aged between 17-64 
(Høgedahl & Krogh, 2020). This unemployment rate is relatively low compared to the average in 
Europe and Denmark. Moreover, Greenland ranks among the countries with the lowest 
unemployment rate from an international perspective (Høgedahl & Krogh, 2020). 
 SOCIAL ASPECTS 
The Greenlandic population has lived in a society that has been in rapid change from local 
communities with an economic base in hunting and fishing to today's international wage worker 
society.  
“[…] changes to a predominantly monetary economy, where all major consumer 
goods or means of production had to be imported. The Greenlanders thus became 
strongly dependent on the economic relations with the outside world.” 
- (DIIS, 2007) 
This rapid change comes with some frustrations and with many people yearning for a simpler 
lifestyle. However, they do not want to be without modern technology and welfare, and thus 
Greenlanders look to become self-sufficient before becoming a sovereign nation (DR1, 2020).  
"I think Greenland will become independent in the future. It will be good to decide 
over our own country, but we must be realistic and look at where we stand now. I 
first think it will happen in many years" - Greenlander 
Greenlanders tend to feel excluded from certain decisions and policy-making regarding Greenland 
and feel Denmark is setting the agenda on the Greenlanders' behalf. This is evident from multiple 
newspaper articles and observations made in Greenland. There are also multiple current media 
discussions regarding Greenland and independence, like the documentary by DR1 in 2020 “The 
Fight for Greenland". In this documentary, the viewer is presented with multiple Greenlandic 
statements about sovereignty.  
"I want an independent Greenland, where we cooperate with countries from all over 
the world. It can also be Denmark. Independence does not mean we should never 
have contact. I mean, it's unrealistic and irresponsible with independence today. It 
requires thorough preparation. We must also lift our own people. Too many people

suffer today. But in the end, independence means the world to a country. How many 
other countries in the world belong to other countries?” - Greenlander 
High qualifications and great adaptability are needed to fill several existing jobs and many of the

lower secondary schools. Today, Greenland spends many resources on primary and lower 
secondary schools (Danmarks Nationalbank, 2019), so it is more important to ensure a good return 
on the many resources than adding more funds. 
"Many Greenlanders want to 'reclaim' - take back their identity. Some do it through

The Greenlandic education system is a replication of the Danish system without much adaptation

Danish as a second language in elementary school and prefer speaking Greenlandic. Numbers

“The reason why we do not teach about Greenland in Danish classes (in Denmark)

The average Dane's knowledge about Greenland seems to be very limited. A survey by EPINION

shows that many Greenlanders living in Denmark are well integrated into Danish society and that 
most have lived in Denmark for many years. However, many Greenlanders experience being 
exposed to discrimination, stigma, and prejudice from Danish society because they are 
Greenlanders. It also shows that most Danes do not feel a tight connection to Greenland and that 
the average Dane’s knowledge about Greenland is limited to basic facts like demographics, nature, 
and climate (EPINION, 2019).

 TECHNOLOGICAL ASPECTS 
Building Information Modeling (BIM) tools are generally only used on a fundamental level, if at all, 
by Greenlandic construction companies. When I asked an anonymous construction manager from 
a large Greenlandic-based construction company about their use of simulation software, the 
person answered with the following imperative. 
"Our architect has designed the homes out of his experience […] I know that you 
learn a lot about simulation tools in school, but we don't use them up here (i.e., in 
Greenland)." - Contractor 
This illustrates that this specific company heavily relies on experiences and expertise. Relying on 
expertise is evident for most Greenland and Inuit communities, and the construction sector is 
generally highly conservative. One reason for this is that there are many examples of bad “new 
initiatives” that have previously been brought to the north and arctic by southerners. These 
examples have consequently made people less open to new solutions.  
“People come to Greenland, and think they know how to build houses, but they 
don’t. The conditions up here are much different from Denmark […]”  
– Architect based in Greenland 
It has been proven in many literature studies that the use of BIM and CAD (Computer-Aided 
Design) tools enhances collaborative scheduling and improves construction management. 
However, it does require some investment in equipment and training to use this kind of software. 
For example, Zhang et al., (2018) state the following: 
"It is widely accepted that BIM can be used as a tool to help reduce project cost; 
shorten project duration by optimizing the construction sequence; improving the 
coordination of project teams; providing a communication platform for different 
disciplines; etc.” 
Greenland has access to all the necessary technology needed for off-site construction. The only 
limiting factor is thus the skill level that is needed to design and manage the production. Modern 
technologies are standard and utilized in numerous professions in Greenland, but personal 
technologies and IoT are not used as extensively as in the rest of the North. Communication 
happens mainly over the phone, email, or the social media platform Facebook. I was told that the 
rural community of Qeqertat only has one cell phone, which the 26 inhabitants share: 
“ I have a friend who has a family in Qeqertat, and they do not have any electricity 
there. The only way people from Qeqertat can communicate with the outside world 
is with one phone which all the residents share” – Greenlander from Sisimiut.

 ENVIRONMENTAL ASPECTS 
The climate in Greenland varies with the latitude, but it is primarily categorized as an arctic climate. 
The deep fjords in southern Greenland are the only exception with its subarctic climate. The 
northern part has a close bond to the North American continent, and only a small icy passage 
separates the two from the arctic ocean. The arctic climate, frozen soil, and bedrocks mean that 
trees do not naturally grow in Greenland. (DMI, 2021). 
Temperatures in Greenland can get very low during winter, especially in the northern and inland 
ice areas. At Kap Morris Jesup, the most northern part of Greenland, the average yearly mean 
temperature in 1981-2010 measured -17.6 °C. It is estimated that temperatures reach below -50°C 
in the middle of Greenland during the winter months. Temperatures in Greenland have generally 
shown an increasing trend. 2001-2010 was the warmest decade in all series since 1784, and 2010 
had record-high annual temperatures in several places in Greenland (DMI, 2021).

temperatures creating additional shifts between frost and thaw.

 LEGAL ASPECTS 
The Greenlandic regulations do not complicate off-site manufacturing more significantly than 
conventional construction methods. However, the municipal plan can include policies regarding the 
following aspects: Location of the building, external appearance of the building, preservation of 
existing buildings, economic conditions in connection with communal facilities, limits for 
environmental impact on the surroundings, and requirements to be met by targets for low-energy 
buildings (information gathered from city planner). These conditions are specific for each 
municipality and need further investigation to ensure there are no complications. 
Greenland has its own building regulation based upon the Danish building regulation from 1995 but 
adjusted to Greenland. The Greenlandic building regulation divides the island into two zones, 
regarding limits for building energy use, north and south of the polar circle at latitude 66°33′48.5N. It 
states that higher energy consumption is allowed north of the polar circle (Pisortaqarfik, 2006).

The government and municipality are financing most of the building projects in Greenland, and 
updating the regulation has not been a priority for a couple of years. However, in multiple 
interviews with local construction- and engineering companies, it became apparent that there is a 
new regulation in consultation waiting on final approval at the time of writing. It is expected that this 
new building regulation will be like the Danish building regulation 2018 with some adjustments for 
Greenland. One concern that came up in interviews was that the new regulation will be too similar 
to the Danish and not account for the Greenlandic conditions. 
“The new ventilation requirements could increase the total energy consumption and 
risk a worse indoor climate despite good intentions.” - Lindstrøm (2021) 
The new regulations introduce an explicit requirement for ventilation in all homes with rules that 
fixed air volumes must be ventilated around the clock. Increased ventilation will bring more fresh 
air into the homes and minimize the CO2 levels and the risk of mold growth. However, the outside 
air in Greenland is much dryer than in Denmark, and that must be accounted for. Another concern 
is that updated construction requirements will create an initial poorer building performance as 
companies need to get accustomed to the new regulation. 
“I agree that we need to build more energy-efficiently, but GBR21 (the building 
regulations ed.) is sloppy and will work against the intention. It will bind our hands 
and feet so that we are unable to improve our existing homes” – Justus Hansen in 
(Schultz-Nielsen, 2021) 
The background for the committee chairman Justus Hansen’s criticism is a constraint that makes 
buildings subject to further new and more comprehensive rules after any type of renovation. A rule 
that will make all renovation projects more expensive (Schultz-Nielsen, 2021). 
“You do not own plots of land in Greenland, but you can get the rights to use the 
area on which the house stands by applying for an area allocation through the 
municipality.” – City planner 
In an interview with the city planners in Sisimiut, it became apparent that all land in Greenland is 
publicly owned. The government distributes area allocations to applicants who, in return, receives 
a right to use that area.  
First, the city planners publish an available building area that must be applied for. The recipient will 
then be chosen by a lot if there are multiple applicants. The area is then reserved for the recipient 
in three months, in which the recipient must send documentation for the planned construction. The 
documentation is in the form of floorplans, elevations, and building sections which the municipality 
will assess to check if it complies with the building regulation. Upon approval, the user has two 
years to start the construction and apply for building approval within six months. The planned 
construction is then once again evaluated regarding the building regulation and special planning 
guidelines. A new building area generally costs around 230,000 DKK allocated to site development 
(rock blasting, roads, pipe systems, foundation, and electricity).

This approach of owning a building with user rights to the ground that the building is built upon 
comes with a few challenges. People will rather wait for new areas to be available instead of 
paying for empty, dilapidated buildings that need to be demolished to build a new home. 
“In some cases, the municipality may choose to take back a building plot by force 
with a plan to demolish an abandoned house and making the plot more attractive to 
new users. However, this rarely happens as the municipality then have to pay for 
the demolition.” – City planner 
This results in many buildings standing empty and taking up building areas as owners of these 
buildings would rather wait for someone to purchase the house and consequently gain the rights to 
the allocated area instead of paying for demolishing themselves to return the house area rights to 
the municipality.   
“The workhours here are from 07-17, so that is 10 hours Monday through Friday, 
and then it is common also to work 07-12 on Saturdays.” – Greenlandic contractor 
The official Greenlandic working environment law is, for the most part, like the Danish (The Ministry 
of Employment, 2005). However, workers in the construction sector in Greenland often work six 
days a week, while in Denmark, it is typically five days a week. 
6.2 FINDINGS WITHIN GREENLANDIC SOCIAL CHALLENGES 
The social context and challenges that need to be considered can be summed in the following 
points: 
 
1. Greenlandic empowerment. 
” Historically there have been many Danes coming to Greenland with new ideas and 
methods that do not work for Greenland” – Local from Sisimiut 
Greenland has a rich history of colonization and foreign interference, which has led to distrust 
towards foreign ideologies and plans. Greenlanders are tired of being told what to do and how to 
do it. The problem is that people in Greenland are usually never asked. Involvement and the option 
to decide for oneself is precious to the Greenlanders, and the prospect of off-site manufacturing of 
Greenlandic buildings is dependent on understanding this fundamental fact.  
 
2. A small population with relatively few scholars. 
“There is a great shortage of skilled labor in both the public and private sectors. [...] 
An excessively large part of Greenland's population will be spectators to the 
country's development, and the need for imports of labor from other countries will be 
even greater than if we ourselves could occupy the many exciting and well-paid jobs 
that offered everywhere in the community.” - B. B. Pedersen (2019). 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
50 
 
Greenland is a large country with a small population, but people in Greenland do not get as highly 
educated as the rest of the north and EU, and therefore, there is a shortage of Greenlandic 
specialists. The shortage means that foreigners possess most decision-making positions. This 
creates a need to increase the national supply of skilled labor so that Greenland becomes less 
dependent on foreign labor (B. B. Pedersen, 2019). In addition, a larger national supply of skilled 
labor will increase the productivity and per capita income of permanent residents in Greenland. 
However, given the low unemployment rate, I am concerned that educating more people could 
create labor shortages in other sectors. 
6.3 FINDINGS WITHIN GREENLANDIC BUILDING INDUSTRY CHALLENGES 
The industrial challenges and context that needs to be considered can be summed in the following 
points: 
 
1. Resistance. 
” It is best to build on-site. That is what we have equipment for and experience with”. 
- Lead contractor 
The local contractors have expressed some resistance towards off-site manufacturing as 
their business is running very well. A Greenlandic contractor mentioned that they currently 
had a profit margin of 12%, which is relatively high compared to 4,6%, which is the 
comparable average Danish profit margin (Deloitte & Dansk Byggeri, 2019).  
The engineering firms appreciate the idea of off-site manufactured buildings. Masanti A/S 
expresses good relations and experiences with an off-site construction company called Grønfeldt 
Elementer, who produces modular buildings in Denmark and ships them to Greenland. 
 
2. Planning and scheduling. 
A stable workforce seems hard to come by in the Greenlandic building industry. Workers 
sometimes work construction as a side job and might have other priorities. Multiple contractors and 
engineering firms have mentioned that it is challenging to plan construction projects due to 
spontaneous worker sick days. They say that it is common for people in the construction industry 
to work 60 hours a week from Monday-Saturday, but consequently, take one sick day a week.  
“I work between 55-60 hours a week […]. I try to take a day off in the weekend, but 
many of my coworkers take off days of between Mon-Fri to get time with their 
families.” – Local Greenlandic carpenter 
“I am satisfied with my workweek. I have tried working 37 hours a week in Denmark, 
but it cannot be done in Greenland. The hourly rate will be too expensive. […] If I 
had a 37-hour workweek in Greenland, I would find a side job to earn more money.”  
- Local Greenlandic carpenter 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
51 
 
Construction workers appear to be satisfied with their long work week. They even express that a 
shorter workweek would not be possible in Greenland and that if they were to work 37 hours in 
construction, they would most likely find a side job. A local contractor has even experimented with 
a 37-hour workweek for its construction workers by offering a monthly salary comparable to the 
hourly rate, meaning workers would get paid the same. It was not a success for unspecified 
reasons, as the construction workers preferred to work more hours with an hourly rate. 
“We experience significant arbitrary absence within the craftsmen trades; 
carpenters, painters, masons, site, and foundation. It can be a real challenge to do 
tight scheduling. We have started incorporating something called location-specific 
planning on our constructions, but if one link fails, then the entire chain falls apart. 
So, it can be complicated to create a timetable that lasts” - Lead contractor  
The challenge with the preferred long workweek is the consequential arbitrary sick days workers 
take to have more time with their families. If the construction manager cannot count on when the 
workers will come to work, it is challenging to plan tasks, especially when tasks are dependent on 
each other.  
 
3. Transport. 
“Transport is properly not the major cost, but one of the most important ones since 
transport of materials is much more limited and expensive than in other more 
developed areas.” – Local Engineer 
The lack of natural resources is a big challenge as building materials need to be shipped to the 
country by boat. The prefabricated building components also need to be able to handle transport to 
various remote locations. Most big cities in Greenland have roads and access for cargo ships; 
however, there need to be alternative transport options for the smaller settlements without roads 
and access by large ships.  
 
4. Mold. 
“Mold growth is a big problem in Greenland. To avoid this, the government has a 
strategy of not using organic materials, as mold need organic material as nutrient to 
grow.” - (Møller & Lading, 2020) 
Mold has historically been a prominent issue in Greenland, partly why concrete has become the 
favored building material. However, the standard guideline to avoid mold is associated with user 
behavior rather than material compositions. Limiting organic materials to prevent mold is only a 
partial solution. One needs to consider the root cause of mold: humidity caused by poor ventilation 
from human behavior. Ventilating correctly is the apparent challenge, and that should not limit 
wood as a construction material

 COST ESTIMATION 
Traditional methods for estimating cost tend to be based on direct resource cost, which does not 
consider other relevant issues. Off-site work increases predictability, reduces risks, reduces 
temporary works and other elements depending on the context (Fraser et al., 2021). The following 
will compare the conventional construction method with off-site construction through examples, 
cost highlights, and empirical arguments.  
To start a new off-site construction company or to change from a conventional construction method 
to off-site construction will require some investment in manufacturing space. 
Manufacturing Space 
The manufacturing facility for the off-site constructions can vary from a temporary sheltered area to 
a fully automated production facility with robots and high-tech solutions. It depends on the purpose 
and investment. Increased initial investment leads to a more controllable environment and thereby 
faster manufacturing time, better quality, fewer injuries, and cheaper buildings as production 
efficiency increases. However, this is only true as long as the demand is high and the 
manufacturing company can utilize the facilities.  
In the new build area of Akia in Sisimiut, the local contractor Permagreen A/S utilized simple off-
site manufacturing without actively being aware of it. Permagreen was building cluster houses 
where the gables had been constructed at a platform near the final buildings and then lifted and 
attached at their final location. The gables are a closed panelized building system with cladding, 
doors, and windows, as seen in Figure 36.

Recommendation

burning oil.

Material 
The construction industry is facing an uprise in demands for “green buildings” and minimize waste. 
Innovate UK states that 32% of the UK’s landfill waste comes from the construction industry alone 
(Outram, 2021).  
Waste efficiency and productivity are to be prioritized in the construction industry. Excess offcuts, 
over-ordering, and on-site packaging are some of the aspects that can be reduced by using a 
systematic approach as off-site manufacturing requires. Extra material can be reused and recycled 
in-house, significantly reducing waste that otherwise had gone to a landfill. Assembling materials in 
a warehouse also lessens the packaging on-site, as materials are delivered ready for assembly.  
Labor 
Labor costs come down to wages, the number of workers, and the time spend. According to the 
2018 Off-Site Construction Industry Survey, skilled labor shortages have continued to make off-site 
construction attractive to developers (NIBS, 2018).  
“With the ongoing shortage of skilled craft workers, prefabrication in a controlled, 
off-site environment may become a necessity […]” 
A report from McKinsey & Company found in 2019 that more recent off-site construction projects 
“have consistently been completed 20%-50% faster than conventional on-site builds”. This is partly 
because building components are built inside a manufacturing facility concurrently with any site 
work. It then requires less time and people on-site to put them into place compared to on-site work 
using the conventional method. 
Quality 
Although difficult to quantify, there seems to be evidence pointing towards an increased quality of 
construction using off-site manufacturing. The literature and suggestions from interviewees state 
that it is easier to do quality assurance on smaller building components than an entire building 
envelope. Therefore, building components from off-site constructions can be very airtight as 
sealing can be precise with special tape over the connections. 
“Off-site manufactured buildings are more airtight, better constructed, and easier to 
repair and modify.” – Engineer 
Dodge Data & Analytics found in their qualitative survey in 2020 that off-site constructions improve 
quality by reducing post-handover defects and rework. In Fraser et al., (2021) it is stated that: 
“Manufacturing and assembly in off-site facilities is able to provide the benefits of 
better quality products that fit well and rapidly on site.” 
And 
“There are also, of course, substantial knock-on benefits in terms of the quality of 
the delivered product in these environments with less risk of contamination, better 
and easier quality control and the ability to inspect, test and commission under 
known conditions.”

6.6 QUALITATIVE ANALYSIS OF RISKS 
With uncertainty comes risk. Risks are neutral elements that we cannot fully project or forecast. 
These external factors can either be opportunities (positive) or threats (negative).  
There may be some risks associated with off-site manufacturing: 
1) By only having one company capable of single-handed manufacturing all units, rather than a 
series of smaller contractors constructing the building on-site, there is an increased 
vulnerability due to the narrow supply chains. If one of the links in the supply chain goes under, 
it can cause issues for practical completion, costs, and delays.   
 
2) Off-site modular manufacturing is a newly reinvented construction strategy, which affects how 
insurance writers assess risk. The uncertainty surrounding the risks makes insurance 
companies uncomfortable and thus increases the insurance costs (Morse, 2015).  
 
3) Off-site construction, like any innovation, is subject to sudden problems caused by the steep 
learning curve (Morse, 2015). Therefore, it is expected that there will be an initial learning 
period before a manufacturer can harvest the benefits. This period will most likely be subject to 
errors and mistakes, but they will even out with time, knowledge, and experience. 
 
4) Even relatively small projects will need a transport and assembling strategy. Transport and 
assembly can require advanced or large equipment like cranes and trucks. The Greenlandic 
infrastructure makes transport of larger building components challenging, especially in the 
smaller settlements. Modules can be damaged or destroyed during transport which will cause 
significant construction delays.

7.1 SOCIALLY DESIRABILITY OF OFF-SITE CONSTRUCTION 
People need to be given the agency and freedom to select and build their homes. However, this 
can complicate the production process as production needs to be agile to comply with various 
customer demands. A solution could be to have a component catalog with multiple components 
within each category that fit together with the other categories. For example, you could do like 
Grønfeldt Elementer in the example below and have multiple façade elements that fit together in 
various combinations:

It will need to be explained to people that off-site constructions can give further opportunities for 
self-reliance through choice and options for both construction professionals and residents.  
Off-site manufacturing requires a few specialists for the designs, while the manufacturing workers 
could consist of initial unskilled labor. The unskilled manufacturing workers need an introduction 
program for their tasks, consisting of routine tasks without much variation. As workers obtain more 
experience and skills, they should be encouraged and allowed to reach higher responsibilities. 
I have identified scheduling and planning as the most significant challenge for construction 
companies in Greenland through the interviews in this project. Since this is such a challenge, it 
seems that BIM tools would be an excellent solution. However, due to a lack of skill and 
specialists, BIM implementation has not yet manifested itself in Greenland.  
The construction sector tends to rely on past experiences and is generally skeptical of new ideas 
and solutions. However, having a building model that can be used for different simulations can be 
extremely helpful to understand and test different scenarios. Zhang et al., 2018 state that: 
“It is widely accepted that BIM helps improve the coordination of project teams [..]”. 
The off-site constructions should have a component catalog to choose and mix from. A benefit 
from this is that the BIM models can be replicated and reused from other construction projects. 
This will make it easier to work with BIM as models do not have to be created from scratch.

The best relationship with contractors is established through mutual understanding and keeping 
things simple. Their job should not be complicated by off-site manufacturing but rather simplify the 
process, thus creating positive responses. It is therefore recommended to arrange workshops and 
include contractors in the design phase. They should be listened to for guidance and optimization 
opportunities regarding the assembling and handling of modules.

7.2 TIME SAVINGS WITH OFF-SITE CONSTRUCTIONS 
Planning and scheduling have been identified as the main challenge for the Greenlandic building 
industry. It is challenging to stick to a timetable because the weather and workers can, at times, be 
unpredictable. A way to solve this is by minimizing time spent on-site and prefabricating the 
building components in a controlled environment. The many components can be built concurrently 
in a sheltered environment, thus making the construction process more resilient to worker absence 
and weather days.  
Off-site construction allows BIM models to be reused or replicated as the buildings comprise 
specific prefabricated components, e.i. the components only need to be created once in a BIM tool, 
and they can then be copied to other projects. This will save time on designing BIM models as 
previous models, and results can be recycled. 
7.3 QUALITY OF OFF-SITE CONSTRUCTIONS 
Off-site manufacturing can deliver more airtight buildings with great insulation coverage i.e., 
minimizing spaces in the envelope without insulation.  
Having a more airtight building makes it easier to control the indoor air quality. However, airtight 
buildings can sometimes lead to poorer indoor air quality if the building is not ventilated correctly. 
The off-site constructions should be designed with modern mechanical ventilation systems to 
==PAGE:77==
 
 
69 
 
ensure an appropriate ventilation strategy. In addition, people should be informed about the 
consequences of poor ventilation and be reminded to air out. Poor ventilation can e.g., lead to 
mold growth, and the most effective way to control mold's root cause is with an effective ventilation 
strategy. Measuring relative humidity has been done for many years and is now a market standard 
in Europe (VELUX, 2014). The homes can also be equipped with sensors that detect CO2 levels 
and relative humidity and remind people to air out. 
7.4 COST OF OFF-SITE CONSTRUCTION 
An effective off-site manufacturing strategy can help relieve some of the garbage production from 
the construction sector. Houses can be built for easy assembly and disassembly to be reused and 
rebuild. Buildings also can be designed to fit the standard measurements of timber to minimize 
waste from cuts. Studies by Jaillon et al. (2008) and Tam et al. (2005) examined records of the 
waste generated by off-site and conventional building projects. The results suggested that off-site 
processes reduced waste from timber formwork by 74-87% and concrete works by 51–60%. The 
authors concluded that net waste could be reduced on average by a factor of about two by 
adopting off-site manufacturing approaches. Krug (2013) suggests that material waste corresponds 
to 3-5% of the construction cost. Applying a waste reduction factor of two to the overall 
construction cost thus suggests that a direct saving of 1.5-2.5% of the conventional tender price 
might be achieved. In addition, materials can be chosen from a life-cycle assessment to minimize 
the carbon footprint.  
Simulations of the annual energy consumption used to heat an ~866 m3 insulated light-structured 
off-site manufacturing space revealed an acceptable energy consumption similar to heating the 
average Greenlandic household. The facility provides a sheltered environment that is resistant to 
wind, rain, and snow.  
The limited stock of skilled labor in Greenland drives up specialists' wages and the budget for 
wages (Danmarks Nationalbank, 2019). Surveys like (NIBS, 2018) and (Dodge Data & Analytics, 
2020) state that off-site construction significantly reduces the need for skilled labor, and the cost for 
wages can thus be reduced with off-site construction.

8 DISCUSSION 
With limited initial knowledge and experience within the area of off-site manufacturing in 
Greenland, it was necessary to do much preliminary research before establishing the scope. The 
scope of this thesis can thus seem broad and with many aspects. However, as there are such 
limited data on construction in Greenland, it was necessary to consult various aspects around the 
Greenlandic context to comprehend the opportunities for off-site construction. 
8.1 BIAS 
Ensuring trust in any study is essential to ensuring that its findings are credible and have 
meaningful applications. The cross-cultural dimension in this thesis requires extra vigilance, and 
trustworthiness cannot be achieved without adapting the research methods in a culturally 
meaningful way (Pelzang & Hutchinson, 2018).  
“In contexts where researchers lack in-depth knowledge and understanding of the 
sociocultural and political dynamics of the research setting, there is a risk of 
inadvertently imposing their beliefs, values, and patterns of behavior upon the 
cultural settings and participants in which the study is being conducted.”  
- Pelzang & Hutchinson (2018) 
I did not have prior in-depth knowledge and understanding of Greenland's socio-cultural and 
industrial aspects. Therefore, I traveled to Greenland and do literature reviews to build up this 
knowledge and understanding. However, the socio-cultural and political dynamics are so complex 
that I cannot say that I fully grasp them, and therefore there is a risk that I have imposed my 
beliefs, values, and patterns of behavior upon the research.  
It is important to mention that all data is biased to some degree. Every study ever made has been 
done for a reason by people with some degree of bias. People are often not aware of their biases 
which are called unconscious or implicit bias. I am aware that I might have unconsciously 
introduced some bias to this research. Having other people reviewing and criticizing the statements 
will, therefore, be valuable to validate the results further.  
Having minimal prior knowledge can also come with some benefits. Being a stranger to the 
Greenlandic environment allowed me to take the unspoken less into account and avoid taking 
things for granted. It made it possible to minimize confirmation bias and gave me a fresh view of 
the challenges.  
Kallio et al., (2016) state that the primary way to avoid researcher bias is to follow an interview 
guide as a predetermined structured approach towards interviewing. To create such a guide, I 
created interview questions categorized by different themes before traveling to Greenland. These 
preliminary questions can be found appendix 11.8. I did not strictly follow the guide as I changed 
the questions with increasing knowledge. I always aimed towards asking standardized questions 
==PAGE:81==
 
 
73 
 
without presumptuous explanation, which is another essential factor when avoiding researcher bias 
(Kallio et al., 2016).  
"Researcher bias is a difficult issue to address and attempt to resolve." 
- Hunter, (2012) 
I have tried to avoid researcher bias by asking the questions in the form of, "… tell me about …"; or 
"… please describe …". When trying to identify the industrial challenges of building in Greenland, I 
would, e.g., ask, "Tell me about the challenges of building in Greenland." However, this way of 
questioning is often unstructured, and people will give many different answers. Incomparable 
answers often lead to the researcher probing for more details, which will introduce some bias 
(Hunter, 2012). To avoid this bias, I aimed to ensure that the research participant had every 
opportunity to relate their story to the questions without forcing an answer. See appendix 11.9 for 
an example of the questioning form in an interview with a local carpenter. 
 CODING BIAS  
The qualitative data was gathered in the form of transcriptions, literature, and observation. This 
data was then processed by a code system that helps identify patterns, opinions, and arguments. 
However, even apparent objective data like this is often biased, particularly when it is gathered by 
a single researcher (Knobloch-Westerwick et al., 2020). The classifying of stakeholders was a 
subjective process, and the occurrence of bias must be expected here. 
Confirmation bias occurs when the researcher uses the data analysis to prove a predetermined 
assumption. Going into this project, I had minimal knowledge about Greenland and building in the 
Arctic; thus, it was easy and difficult to avoid confirmation bias. It is easy to fall into the trap of 
projecting the first interviewee's answers to a more extensive opinion base than what it is. I have 
tried to mitigate this by interviewing people from different social layers and keeping the questions 
open. However, selection bias is evident in this paper as the qualitative data gathering was limited 
to one city (Sisimiut).

8.3 POPULATION PROJECTIONS AND HOUSING SHORTAGES 
A report by Greenland’s statistics and the Economic Council suggests a rise in the population's 
average age. As the proportion of 30-59-year-olds increases, more and more people will live 
together, which will dampen housing demand, measured in the number of housing units 
(Siunnersuisoqatigiit, 2014). There are significant regional differences in population development 
and thus also in the potential housing need. The adult part of the population has increased in the 
four major cities, especially in Nuuk, while the rest of the country has had a stagnant or declining 
population (Greenland Statistics, 2021). 
The Economic Council has made a projection of the future population of Greenland towards 2040 
based on data from Greenland’s statistics. The main result of the projection is that the total 
population is expected to fall from 56,541 (2021) to 53,400 (2040). In 2040, there will thus be 
around 3,000 fewer people living in Greenland than today if there are no unexpected changes in 
fertility, mortality and immigration, and emigration (Greenland Statistics, 2021). However, the 
population in the three largest cities Nuuk, Sisimiut, and Illulisaat is projected to grow, with current 
assumptions about migration within the country and continued urbanization (Greenland Statistics, 
2021).  
The housing shortage is a product of increased demands for better quality homes and the 
significant renovation backlog. Off-site constructions can produce homes of high quality with total 
insulation coverage and minimal air leakage. Furthermore, the constructions are designed to be 
quickly assembled and disassembled, making renovation and reconstruction an easier task. 
Therefore, on the basis of the assumptions presented in this report, I conclude that off-site 
construction can help relieve housing shortages.

9 CONCLUSION 
The housing shortage in Greenland is mainly a problem in Nuuk, Sisimiut, and Illulisaat, where the 
population is expanding, and the renovation backlog is increasing together with demands for higher 
quality homes. Off-site manufacturing of Greenlandic houses can relieve these housing shortages 
by producing socially desirable homes of high quality that are fast to construct. However, 
transporting building components is more expensive and challenging than transporting materials 
and equipment, and off-site manufacturing is therefore not a more affordable solution than 
conventional construction methods.  
Social desirability in Greenland is related to providing people the opportunity for self-reliance with 
more choices and options. Off-site manufacturing can do this by allowing Greenlandic building 
professionals to design the components and processes. The prefabrication process is fully scalable 
and can vary from a single wall construction to a fully operational manufacturing process. 
Everything can be done in Greenland, and the tasks do not require as many specialists, which 
makes foreign interference less likely.  
People can have the opportunity to decide how they want their homes to be built by designing a 
component catalog with multiple components. The components can be quickly assembled and 
disassembled, making renovation and reconstruction simple. Assembling materials in a facility 
makes it easy to replicate components and lessens the packaging on-site, as components are 
delivered ready for assembly. Extra material from excess offcuts, over-ordering can also be reused 
and recycled in-house and thereby reducing waste. 
Off-site manufacturing allows building components to be constructed concurrently with any site 
development, which reduces the total construction time. Individual components are easier to 
quality control than an entire building envelope, resulting in greater insulation coverage and lower 
air leakage.  
9.1 FURTHER WORK 
This thesis focused on explaining and highlighting the different aspects of off-site manufacturing of 
Greenlandic buildings so that others can make more informed decisions and plan further research. 
The next step is to create a proof of concept that can be used to draw learnings and quantify the 
specific costs and benefits in monetary value. 
I would apply for research funds to have a Greenlandic-based company design an off-site 
manufactured single-family home that a local contractor should build. While doing this, I would 
overview the processes and document requirements, times, costs, quality, collaboration efforts. A 
test case like that would give valuable experiences and learnings for further investigations.

 PRELIMINARY INTERVIEW STRATEGY AND QUESTIONS 
These interview questions were used mainly in the first month of the research where I had limited 
knowledge around Greenland and qualitative data. 
 
Interview Strategy 
• 
Set aside at least 30 minutes for each interview. 
• 
Do research on the candidate before the meeting. 
• 
Have all information on the candidate available. 
• 
Know what direction the interview should go. 
• 
Follow a consistent interview structure. 
• 
Determine how to group each candidate. 
• 
Simplify the answer scheme. E.g., use a ruler to have candidates answer in percentages. 
• 
Ask the important questions first when the candidates are most energized. 
• 
Do not prioritize asking all the questions but rather the right questions and prepare those 
beforehand.

INTERVIEW QUESTIONS IN INTERVIEW WITH LOCAL CARPENTER 
Questions have been translated from Danish for the purpose of this appendix. Answers was 
manually noted as background noise made recordings inaudible.  
Q) How long have you been a carpenter? 
A) Håndværker udlært siden 2005 (16 år) og er glad for sit arbejde. 
Har arbejdet i Danmark i 11 måneder, og var meget glad for det. Arbejdet i Danmark var mere 
simpelt, da man der fokuserede på én ting, som at sætte vinduer i, hvor i Grønland skal man 
lave det hele.  
 
Q) Tell me about what kind of work you do? 
A) I Grønland laver man det hele fra bunden. I Danmark laver man ofte én ting som tømrer. 
Vi bruger mest tid på beton og betonfundament som vi får fra Permagreen. 
 
Q) How does a typical Greenlandic façade element look like? 
A) Fra ind til ud.  
Gips/Krydsfiner – dampspærre – isolering – vindplade – afstandsliste – beklædning.  
Man er begyndt at bruge crembit plader (metal) da de er ”vedligeholdelses frie” og ikke skal 
males hvert år. 
 
Q) How would you describe your work week? 
A) 9-10 timer man-fre og 5-6 lør. (55-60 timer) 
Jeg tager prøver at holde fri om weekenden. 
Kollegaer kan godt holde 1 sygedag om ugen for at få tid til familie 
Jeg er tilfreds med min arbejdsuge 
 
Q) Have you ever worked 37 hours a week instead of 55-60 hours? 
A) Ja i Danmark.

 CONCRETE AS A BUILDING MATERIAL 
Work with concrete forms an essential part of the contemporary building practice in which the 
Greenlandic construction industry specializes. The casting of concrete can usually be carried out 
all year round, with special precaution in the winter. In an interview with Permagreen A/S, they 
explained the following: 
"We can cast concrete down to -20°C by covering it with tarpaulins and connecting 
diesel heaters." 
The same is not evident for assembling precast concrete panels with concrete or grout as sealing 
between connections. Here the same interviewee was vouching that it was durable while others 
questioned the quality of the final product. A representative from Masanti A/S replied: 
"Buildings are constructed all year; however, the foundation and groundwork must 
be done during summer when the ground is not frozen. The same is true for 
concrete assembling since concrete needs +0°C temperatures to harden." 
However, these connections can be made in many ways using either: foam, welded connections, 
bolts, or dowel/anchor connections. It is thus expected that concrete buildings can be constructed 
in the same climatic conditions as wooden buildings. Just as wood is sensitive to wet weather, 
concrete is sensitive to cold weather as concrete, depending on the curing period, generally needs 
positive temperatures to cure.

Mold growth has historically been a big problem in Greenland. Non-organic building materials like 
concrete are thus favorited by the Greenlandic building sector as mold needs organic material to 
gow.  
Utilizing own resources and preventing mold are the two prime arguments the government used to 
encourage the use of concrete. Interviews with both Permagreen and Masanti suggested that 
government projects often necessitate concrete in public tenders. As the government is financing 
most of the construction in Greenland, it is subsequently evident that most modern buildings are 
using concrete. In Nuuk and Sisimiut, there are small concrete element factories owned by 
respectively MT Højgaard (Nuuk) and BJ Enterprise (Sisimiut).
