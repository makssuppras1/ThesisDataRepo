Abstract
It is well known that the resonance frequency of a moving coil loudspeaker
changes with the input level of the driving signal. Since the resonance frequency
depends on the total moving mass and the total mechanical compliance of the
transducer system, a certain level dependency must be inherent in one of these
two quantities. As it is assumed that the moving mass does not change with
level it is concluded in most studies that the level dependent quantity is the
mechanical compliance.
Diﬀerent studies from the ﬁeld of structural mechanics show, that ﬁlled elas-
tomers exhibit a level dependent compliance and level dependent damping. In
structural mechanics this material behaviour is usually modelled by using fric-
tional elements. Since the same material is used for the outer loudspeaker sus-
pension, an approach for including such a material model into the well known
lumped-parameter loudspeaker model is made in this thesis. The thesis presents
the characteristic behaviour of such a model and points out opportunities and
problems. Furthermore measurements of the transfer function and the electrical
impedance of a loudspeaker were done to compare the model behaviour to data.

Chapter 1
Introduction
A loudspeaker transforms electrical signals coming from a source, for example an
audio-ampliﬁer, into acoustical signals. The transformation from an electrical to
an acoustical signal is done via an mechanical system. The mechanical system
is characterized by a mass, a compliance and a certain amount of damping. The
behaviour and operation range of the loudspeaker is determined by the resonance
frequency of the mechanic system which is deﬁned by the moving mechanical
mass and the total mechanical compliance. A precise knowledge of the resonance
frequency is for example crucial in the design process of loudspeaker enclosures,
especially in the case of vented box systems.
In several studies [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], a dependency of the compliance on the
input level was found, causing a shift in the resonance frequency. Regarding
to the results of those studies the mechanical compliance increases with input
level, causing the resonance frequency to decrease. This eﬀect is also seen in
measurements done in previous master projects at DTU [6], [7]. Most of the
existing loudspeaker models use parameters that do not depend on the input
levels. It is very clearly illustrated in [6] that this can lead to deviations between
measurement and simulation when predicting the loudspeaker response at dif-
ferent levels than the one were the ﬁxed parameters were found. In order to use
a lumped-parameter model a new ﬁt to the measurements at every new level
is necessary in order to predict the correct behaviour of the loudspeaker. This
indicates that the present models do not describe the physical behaviour of the

loudspeaker precisely enough, pointing out the need of a better understanding
of the inherent physical processes.
The eﬀective mechanic compliance and damping of the loudspeaker are made
up by the inner suspension, the so called spider, and the outer suspension of the
loudspeaker. The outer suspension of the loudspeaker is usually made out of
rubber material. The rubber material is characterized by a certain compliance
as well as a certain amount of damping. Studies from the ﬁeld of structural
mechanics [8], [9], [10] indicate a level dependent behaviour of the compliance
and the damping of rubber material: The compliance increases with level while
the damping is showing an increase for small input level and starts decreasing
at larger input levels. The models introduced in [8] and [9] suggested frictional
elements to capture the level dependent behaviour.
Since the material models used in [8] and [9] seem to show the behaviour which
is also seen in the compliance of the loudspeaker, an approach for incorporating
a frictional element into the existing lumped parameter model is made, in order
to introduce a level dependency to the loudspeaker model. Such a model uses
only one set of parameters, but gives diﬀerent responses at diﬀerent input level.
Report Structure
Chapter 2 introduces two material models using frictional elements. In the fol-
lowing chapter, chapter 3, the lumped-parameter model of the loudspeaker is
explained and the main three non-linearities of the loudspeaker are presented.
Furthermore, a model for a level-dependent suspension is derived, which is based
on the frictional models introduced in chapter 2. Chapter 4 presents the results
of the measurements done. It is followed by chapter 5, where the simulation
results obtained from the model for the level-dependent suspension are shown.
In the discussion, chapter 6, the measurement and simulation results are com-
pared and discussed. The report ends with an conclusion and ideas for future
projects which could further improve the model derived in this project.

Chapter 2
Modelling of Filled
Elastomers using Frictional
Elements
The models described in this chapter are from the ﬁeld of structural mechanics
and try to capture the change of compliance and damping of the rubber material.
They are not chemical models, but since the level dependency of the material
is caused by changes in the chemical structure of the material a short overview
is given in the following paragraph.
From a chemical point of view, rubbers are elastic polymers which are also often
termed elastomers [9]. They consist of long molecular chains which are linked
together after a process called vulcanization. Fillers like carbon-black are added
to the material to reinforce certain mechanical characteristics like stiﬀness and
damping [11].
The ﬁllers are added to the rubber before the vulcanization
process where they ﬁnally build agglomerates and are connected to the polymer
chains with crosslinks [9]. The eﬀect of the increasing compliance of the material
with increasing strain is called Panye eﬀect [9], [12], [10] and is highly connected
to the adding of the ﬁller material [11]. It is shown for example in [11, p.14] that
unﬁlled rubber is more compliant and less energy is dissipated in the material
than compared to a ﬁlled sample.
The Panye eﬀect is mostly explained by
the change of the ﬁller network that is built by the chemical links between

2.3
Material Models using Frictional Elements
The models describing the behaviour of ﬁlled elastomers are often split into a
elastic and a plastic part [8], [9]. The elastic part is usually capturing the vis-
coelastic behaviour of the material, as for example frequency dependent damp-
ing, and can be modelled for example with a generalized Maxwell chain or
fractional order derivatives [8], [9]. The plastic part captures the level depen-
dency of the material. Since the thesis is about capturing the level dependency
of the loudspeaker the focus will be on the plastic parts of the material models.
The theory of plasticity is concerned with material behaviour that is time
independent and shows a non-linear relationship between force and displace-

Moving-Coil Loudspeaker
A moving-coil loudspeaker transforms an electrical signal into an acoustic signal.
The cross section of a moving-coil loudspeaker is shown in ﬁgure 3.1. The electric
voltage supplied for example by an audio ampliﬁer causes a current ﬂowing
through the voice coil. Since the voice coil is placed in a magnetic ﬁeld a force
regarding to the Lorentz force is acting on the coil causing a movement of the
voice coil, the suspension parts consisting of spider and outer suspension, and
the diaphragm [17]. The movement of the diaphragm causes a pressure change
and produces an acoustic wave.

3.2
Loudspeaker Non-Linearities
The linear model of the moving-coil loudspeaker is a good approximation espe-
cially at low input levels where the displacement is small. Larger input levels
causing larger displacements make the loudspeaker behave non-linear.
This
means that the input and output of the loudspeaker do not necessarily contain
the same frequencies, making the linear model introduced in the previous sec-
tion inapplicable, since superposition is a requirement for that model. One way
of capturing the non-linear behaviour is to describe the system in the time do-
main by means of it diﬀerential equations. The diﬀerential equations can then
be represented in a state-space model and a solution to the equations can be
found by numerical methods.
In the following sections the state-space representation and the three major non-
linearities of the loudspeaker are introduced. Finally the model developed to
capture the level dependency of the suspension is presented.

usually equal to the state variables.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The simulation results presented in chapter 5 show that the loudspeaker model
developed in section 3.2.4 is level dependent. By using only one set of parame-
ters a shift in resonance with increasing level is obtained. Since the developed
model does not change the mechanical mass of the system, according to equa-
tion 3.5 it is concluded that the shift in resonance is caused by an increase
in compliance. For simulations done with pure tones an clear upward shift of
the transfer function with increasing level below the resonance is seen. This
behaviour is in agreement with the measurements presented in section 4.1 and
also with measurements done in [6] and [7].
Using multi tone signals for the simulation of the transfer function gives diﬀerent
results than using pure tones. The main reason for the diﬀerence is assumed to
be the distortion caused by the frictional element in the model. When using a
multi tone signal, the system is excited by several frequencies at the same time.
Driving a non-linear system with multiple frequencies causes intermodulation
products [21]. Since the frequencies of the multi tone signal used for the sim-
ulations are multiples of each other at low frequencies, those intermodulation
products occur also at frequencies that are contained in the initial input signal
causing increased energy at this frequencies. The eﬀect is stronger at the very
low frequencies since the frequency resolution is equal to the distance of the
third order diﬀerence intermodulation products. The ﬁrst four frequencies of
the multi tone are 1mHz, 2mHz and 3mHz. The third order diﬀerence products
==PAGE:70==
52
Discussion
caused by 2mHz and 3mHz are given by fIP 3,1 = 2·2mHz−3mHz = 1mHz and
fIP 3,2 = 2 · 3mHz −2mHz = 4mHz showing that the products occur exactly at
the frequencies 1mHz and 4mHz which are already contained in the signal. At
higher frequencies the neighbouring frequencies are not multiples of each other
so that the eﬀect is not seen. On the other hand, all frequencies are multiples
of 1mHz so that intermodulation products produced from high frequencies in-
ﬂuence the very low frequencies. The level-dependency of this eﬀect is probably
due to stronger distortion products at larger levels. For the simulation of the
impedance obtained from multi tones the eﬀect is not seen since the frequencies
very close to the resonance where the inﬂuence of the mechanic system is largest
are not multiples of each other.
The resonance peak of the electrical impedance shows the desired shift towards
lower frequencies for increasing levels. Anyhow, the mechanical damping de-
termining the height of the peak at resonance is not showing the behaviour
expected from the measurements. The damping increases from the lowest level
to the medium level, but decreases again from the medium to the highest level
for the ﬁrst parameter set. For the second parameter set the damping of the
resonance is decreasing with level. For the measurements the resonance peak
decreases with increasing level which corresponds to an decrease in damping
with level. The damping for a viscous damper is deﬁned in equation 2.9. For
the viscous damper the dissipated energy increased equally fast than the square
of the displacement amplitude, causing a constant damping. For the model in-
cluding the frictional element this does not seem to be the case. In order to
obtain an increase in damping at a given frequency the dissipated energy would
need to increase faster than the square of the displacement magnitude. Since
the compliance is increasing with level it might be that the displacement after
a certain level growths faster than the dissipated energy, causing a decrease in
damping. In order to make a signiﬁcant statement further simulations investi-
gating the damping of the frictional element alone and in combination with the
loudspeaker model at diﬀerent levels and frequencies would be necessary.
The simulations shown in ﬁgure 5.6 and 5.7 show that a diﬀerent set of loud-
speaker parameters gives a diﬀerent level dependency, even if the parameters
of the frictional element are the same. The compliance Cms of the second pa-
rameter set shown in table 5.3 is approximately three times larger than the one
shown in table 4.1. The spring Cms and damper Rms are connected in series to
the frictional element as shown in ﬁgure 3.4. If the compliance Cms is changing,
the distribution of the total displacement xd between the frictional element and
the spring and damper will change as well. For a larger compliance Cms the
spring and damper will contribute more to the total displacement than for a
smaller one. This would mean that the frictional element is less displaced for a
larger compliance, resulting in a smaller eﬀective compliance for the frictional
element and therefore in a smaller total eﬀective compliance. Furthermore the
==PAGE:71==
53
dissipated energy in the frictional element would be diﬀerent, explaining why
the trend in the resonance peak in the electrical impedance is diﬀerent for the
two parameter sets. Still, this is only an assumption, since the other loudspeaker
parameters Bl, Re, Le, and Mms are also diﬀerent and have a inﬂuence on the
total displacement as well. To further justify the assumption those parameters
should be kept constant, changing only Cms and Rms. Due to time limitations
such a study was unfortunately not realized until the deadline of the report.
The measurement of the transfer function shown in ﬁgure 4.2 shows a slope
below the resonance, indicating a frequency dependency of the compliance. This
slope is not seen in the simulations, where the transfer function approaches a
constant level below the resonance frequency for pure tones. This is expected
since no viscoelasticity is included in the presented loudspeaker model. In the
future a generalized Maxwell chain as shown for example in [16] or a fractional
derivative approach as shown in [22] could replace the spring and damper in
order to accord for the frequency dependency of the compliance.
It is seen from ﬁgure 5.8 in section 5.2.1 that the model with the frictional ele-
ment adds distortion to a pure tone input signal. Since the frictional element is
the only non-linear element in the model it can be concluded that it causes the
distortion. Harmonics at odd multiples of the fundamental are reasonable, since
the plastic modulus Hp in equation 2.12 is symmetric in positive and negative
load direction. The model shows distortion even at a relatively small input peak
voltage of 0.5V. In the measurements done at the same voltage shown in ﬁg-
ure 4.6a the harmonics are less pronounced. Furthermore the measurement sys-
tem could contain non-linearities causing the distortion of the input signal. To
be certain about the measured non-linearities at low level a second measurement
of the pressure done in anechoic conditions would be necessary. A combination
between the non-linearities described in section 3.2.3 and the level dependent
suspension could give insight whether the frictional element could improve the
prediction of the harmonics compared to a model using only polynomials for Bl,
Cms and Le. The prediction of a model using only those non-linearities seems
to become better with increasing level(see ﬁgure 4.5). This indicates that the
non-linear behaviour at lower levels might be caused by a diﬀerent mechanism.

Future Work
In the initial time plan a development of a ﬁtting algorithm for the model de-
rived in section 3.2.4 was included. Since the derivation of the model for the
level-dependent suspension took a large amount of time the ﬁtting algorithm
could not been implemented. In [8] a ﬁtting algorithm for the frictional element
shown in ﬁgure 2.11 is proposed. This ﬁtting algorithm is ﬁtting the continuous
frictional element to measurements of the resulting force when a rubber speci-
men experiences a certain displacement. The main diﬃculty of ﬁnding a ﬁtting
algorithm for the model developed in this report is the long simulation time to
obtain a transfer function and impedance plot. A reduction in computation time
would be necessary to use the classical curve ﬁtting algorithms, which adjust
the parameter models to obtain the closest ﬁt to the measurements.
As mentioned several times no viscoelasticity is included in the model yet. This
could be done by replacing the spring and the damper in ﬁgure 3.4 by a gener-
alized Maxwell chain [16] or by a fractional derivative approach [22].
Furthermore the change in damping with changing level introduced by the fric-
tional damping is not fully understood yet and needs further investigation.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
Initially, two frictional models where presented giving an overview over the
functionality and characteristics of those models. In the following a loudspeaker
model including a frictional element was developed in the time domain using
the state-space representation.
The transfer function and the electrical impedance were measured showing the
necessity of such a model. By use of the Klippel Analyzer, parameters for a
linear loudspeaker model were measured as well as the non-linearity curves for
the force factor, the compliance and the inductance of the loudspeaker. The
total harmonic distortion of the loudspeaker was measured and compared to
the prediction given by a model including only Bl, Cms and Le non-linearities
modeled as polynomials. The prediction seems to be quite good above and at
the resonance and at large levels. Below the resonance the polynomial model
predicts a smaller THD for low levels. This indicates that the given model is
not describing the loudspeaker precisely. Since the compliance is dominating
at frequencies below resonance the frictional model could be beneﬁcial for the
prediction of the harmonic distortion. The simulation results of the transfer
function and the electrical impedance presented in chapter 5 show that the fric-
tional element indeed introduces a level dependency to the loudspeaker model.
Two diﬀerent parameter sets indicate that the relation between the change in
level and the change in total eﬀective compliance depends on the interaction
of the frictional element and the spring and damper. The mechanical damping
==PAGE:76==
58

of the model does not necessarily increase with level. A faster growth of the
displacement compared to the dissipated energy might be the reason for that.
Overall the frictional element approach seems to go in the right direction, but
still diﬀerences between the measurements and simulations are seen, making
further investigations of the applicability of this method in loudspeaker models
necessary.
