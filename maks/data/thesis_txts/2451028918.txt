Abstract
Abstract
Most of the simple wake models to date only consider steady state operation. No dynamics are
taken into account. A steady state model cannot account for the propagation velocities that are
important for control matters.
The aim of the present thesis is to make a model that accounts for wake propagation velocities.
This means that the time delays in the event of a change in wind or operation need to be accounted.
In order to parametrize the model, Large Eddy Simulation data has been used. With this data,
a cross-correlation analysis has been performed in order to determine the time delays and
consequently propagation velocities. A diameterwise cross-correlation has resulted in better
correlation levels than the total distance cross-correlation. The values for the total distances can
be obtained by addition of the time delays obtained per diameter.
The propagation velocities have been represented in different forms: total propagation velocity re-
spect to freestream (VpropTOT /U0), local propagation velocity respect to freestream (VpropLOC/U0)
and local propagation velocity respect to local velocity (VpropLOC/ULOC). This last one shows a
clear tendency in which the near wake and the far wake can be represented. Therefore, it has
been the chosen form for the model.
Once, the case studies of the model are compared with a model with a ﬁxed propagation ratio, it
is observed that the model over-estimates propagation velocity.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Wind energy has been used for centuries with different purposes, from grinding cereal to pump-
ing water. However, since wind energy started being harvested to generate electricity in 1887
[21], the industry has evolved in leaps and bounds. What started with turbines producing a few
kilowatts is currently on its way to reach 12 megawatt turbines with blades that are 107 meters
long [20]. Not only are the turbines increasing in size, but many turbines are being mounted in
groups to increase energy production and reduce costs as wind farms. Thanks to the transition
towards offshore, wind farms can be bigger and accommodate larger turbines [14].
However, the layouts of the turbines in the wind farms bring a new set of problems. When wind
turbines are mounted in farms, the turbines located upstream will affect the turbines downstream,
by what is known as the wake. This effect happens as a wind turbine extracts kinetic energy
from the wind. The air mass that goes through the rotor disc will decelerate due to the loss of
energy. Consequently, the velocity of such air mass will be lower than the unperturbed wind
that did not go through the rotor. Due to this difference in velocity, a boundary layer is created,
which will prolong downstream and can be illustrated as a stream-tube with a circular cross-
section, denominated as the wake. A wake can be divided in two parts: a near wake and a far wake.
The torque that drives the turbine rotation requires a reaction torque in the opposite direction,
which will introduce a rotational component to the air making it rotate in the opposite direction.
This rotation can be pictured as helical vortexes arising from each blade [5]. The loading of the
turbine will increase the turbulence levels downstream, therefore, making the wake become more
turbulent. The turbulence will entrain air from outside of the boundary layer, thus re-energizing
and increasing the velocity of the wake When the mixing is high enough, the velocity deﬁcit
proﬁle will start to take a Gaussian shape, it is at this point where the near wake and far wake
can be separated [7].
The effect of the upstream turbines in the downstream turbines results in a lower available
wind speed downstream, which will affect the power output. On the other hand, the turbine
will affect the turbulence levels of the ﬂow, which will affect the loading of the turbines. These
two parameters, the downstream velocity and the turbulence levels, are strongly linked with the
propagation velocity of the wake, which is the focus of the current thesis.

1.1. Motivation
1.1
Motivation
The goal of the present thesis is to model the dynamic behaviour of a wind turbine wake, by
extending a one-dimensional model that accounts for temporal variations. So, for a given inﬂow
and control parameters, the model should deﬁne the state of the wake for a certain array of
turbines.
Currently, most of the wake models of wind farms are performed in steady state. The dynamics
are too complicated to be included and a steady state calculation of the Annual Energy Production
(AEP) is considered a good enough estimation. However, the dynamics become fundamental if a
wake control is to be implemented.
The modeling of the wake will be approached by extending existing engineering wake models.
The wake of a turbine causes a reduction of the available energy for the downstream turbines,
resulting in a velocity deﬁcit. However, the wake effects are not felt instantaneously, the wake has
a propagation velocity and it will take some time before it covers the distance in-between turbines.
Knowing the time it will take for a certain wind ﬂow to propagate through a wind farm allows
adjusting the operation of the downstream turbines to the conditions of the ﬂow in the corre-
sponding instant, when said ﬂow reaches the turbine.
Another case in which the delay is important would be to determine the energy availability in
the near future (by accounting for the delay) for the grid codes, given the increasing part taking
of wind turbines as active regulators of the grid [1].
The turbulence level at the wake is higher than the atmospheric one. A higher turbulence intensity
means that there will be a changing wind speed, which will induce varying loads in the turbine.
This loads affect the fatigue of the turbine, so in some cases it may be necessary to control the
turbine to better deal with such loads. Therefore, it is necessary to know how long it will take for
the wake to propagate so that action is taken at the estimated moment.
The modeling process will be carried out by increasing the level of complexity. Starting with
constant winds, followed by step changes in wind velocity and ﬁnally using a time series.
Even though, to date, there are already existing models that compute the previous scenarios, the
objective of the model developed on this thesis is to describe the wake by using low computa-
tional time, but still including the necessary dynamics. The model will be derived from existing
LES data and compared to the available measurements. Depending on the progression of the
project, further parameters could be considered, such as the effect of yawing.

1.3
State of the art for wake models
There are plenty of models that account for the velocity deﬁcit behind a wind turbine. The
level of complexity of such models varies from simple 2D models to CFD models using the
Navier-Stokes equations. Nonetheless, since this project is targetting low computational times,
the simpler models are more relevant.
One of the most well-known wake models is the N.O. Jensen model (1983) [16]. It calculates
the velocity deﬁcit at a certain distance behind the turbine, dependant on the axial induction
factor (a) and assuming a linear expansion on the wake. The Jensen model has been the starting
point for other models that have followed, such as the Park model. This is the model used in
WAsP [19], originally developed by Katic et al. [8], which estimates the available wind speed
by quadratic summations of the velocity deﬁcits of the isolated individual turbines in a cluster.
Nonetheless, the Park model by Katic et al. has been revised and some improvements have been
performed by Rathman et al. [9], substituting the quadratic summation by a linear summation of
the deﬁcits.
When it comes to simple propagation models, F. González-Longatt et al. [6] deﬁne a propagation
velocity that is the mean of the available wind speed at 2 consecutive turbines. Bingöl et al.
[12] determine that the propagation speed is lower than the ambient ﬂow and Rolf-Erik et al.
[22] state that the appropriate value of the propagation velocity is in between the mean velocity
of the wake deﬁcit and the freestream velocity. De Mare et al. [18] say that the propagation
velocity should be around 0.8U0. In T. Göçmen et al. [10], a wake propagation velocity of x−2/3
is yielded. J.N. Sørensen et al. deﬁne the convective propagation velocity as Uc=0.73 respect
to freestream wind speed, obtained from simulations. Machefaux et al. present an advection
velocity of Uw = √1−CTU0 from turbine to 3 diameters downstream, obtained from the 1D
momentum and vortex tube theory. They also present an advection velocity Ua=Uin f-0.4∆u ,
where 0.4 is a coefﬁcient determined from empirical results [11].

Chapter 2
Methodology
2.1
Overview
The followed methodology in this thesis has been the following. First of all, different steady state
velocity deﬁcit estimation methods have been studied. They have been compared and contrasted
with literature and one has been chosen.
The parametrization of the propagation velocity in this thesis is going to be based in the avail-
able Large Eddy Simulations (LES). There are two simulation batches available: one for the
NM80 turbine [4] from NEG Micon and one for the V27 [2] from Vestas, with several cases for
different operation points of the turbines. The analysis of the LES data is going to be based in a
cross-correlation study of the rotor averaged wind speeds. From this, the time delay of the ﬂow
after covering a certain distance will be studied.
Using the delays from the cross-correlation analysis, a normalized propagation velocity spread
is going to be obtained for different cases.
There are available measurements ,for the S97 turbine from SUZLON, in TEXAS and they are
going to be analyzed with the same cross-correlation analysis that has been used for the LES data.
Afterwards, both are going to be compared with the measurements that are to some extend usable.
Once all the LES data has been analyzed, the obtained results for the propagation velocity are
going to be introduced in a matlab model for a row of turbine, where various case scenarios are
going to be analyzed.

2.3
LES Data
Large Eddy Simulation (LES) is a mathematical model of the turbulence. Turbulent ﬂows are
numerically solved by the Navier-Stokes equations, which are computationally costly to solve.
Therefore, LES reduces the computational cost by leaving the smallest length scales out, which
are the most costly computationally, with a low pass ﬁlter.
Turbulent ﬂows contain eddies of several time and length scales. Theoretically, it is possible to
resolve the whole spectrum of the turbulent scales by using direct numerical simulation, but if all
the scales want to be solved, the mesh size is too big. In LES, the large eddies are solved, while
the small eddies are modelled, since they are more universal and less dependant on geometry.
Thus, the mesh resolution is not as restrictive, but highly ﬁne meshes are still required [15].

Time scales
A time scale is the time it takes an eddy to rotate for one revolution (eddy turnover time). In
order to deﬁne a timescale, it is necessary to use a measuring time or an observation time that is
longer than the timescale itself, otherwise it cannot be detected. The following analysis shows
the level of correlation (ρ) depending on the window size and a moving average.

boundary layer and starts recovering. Hence, VpropLOC/U0 reaches higher than for case 2. This
shows the importance of the local time scales in the recovery of the wake.

Model
The developed model is going to simulate a cluster or a row of turbines. The aim of the model,
which has been implemented using Matlab, is to account for the time delays of the wind ﬂow on
the turbines.
Whenever there is either a change in the incoming wind speed or in the operation of a turbine,
there will be a change in the propagation velocity of the wind, which will make the ﬂow reach the
downstream turbines sooner or later than it was. In order to determine such delay, the following
process is followed.
Estimation of the downstream local wind speed

to be used. This allows the determination of the local wind speed at set positions downstream. In
this case it has been every diameter (as shown in ﬁgure 3.20), given that the local propagation

propagation velocities can be seen. The point of transition is the point where the near wake
breaks down. There is a part with a descending slope and a part that could be considered constant.

3.7
Limitations
Local velocity estimation model
As it is perceivable, the utilized park 2 engineering model has limitations. First and foremost,
it does not take the near wake into account and directly treats the downstream as if it were the
far wake. This will result in an overestimation of the near wake propagation velocity, as seen in
ﬁgure 3.50. Given that it is used to obtain the diameterwise propagation velocity, this will be an
aggregated overestimation, because in the calculation of the total delay in between two turbines,
the diameterwise delays are added.
In addition, the velocity deﬁcit after the turbine is a step change, whereas in reality, this is not
possible due to the accelerations and forces this would require [5]. So, the velocity reduction
should be gradual. The decrease to the incoming wind velocity induced by the rotor is not
represented here either.

Chapter 4
Conclusions
A quasi-steady wake propagation model has been implemented based on a cross-correlation
study of the available LES simulations.
A diameterwise cross-correlation study results in higher correlation levels compared to a total
distance cross-correlation analysis. The total propagation velocities, obtained by addition of
the diameterwise time delays, agree with the highest densities of the data spread obtained by
the total cross-correlation analysis. In addition, in the propagation velocities that come from
total cross-correlations analysis, the levels of correlation are low in the furthest diameters. This
contributes to having a larger spread and consequently, the propagation velocities are erroneous.
Therefore, the addition of diameterwise delays is used to determine the total delays over a certain
distance.
The propagation velocity analysis of the total propagation velocity respect to freestream
(VporpTOT /U0) does not show any clear tendencies. However, when the local propagation veloci-
ties are normalized by freestream wind speed (VporpLOC/U0), the values on the far wake start to
converge towards a value between 0.85 and 0.9, regardless of the CT magnitude.
When the local propagation velocity is normalized by the local velocities (VporpLOC/ULOC), two
very clear trends appear. A downwards linear trend and a constant trend. The point where both
intersect, determines the point of the near wake breakdown. This has been observed for all the
cases, whether there is a shear, no shear or different values of atmospheric TI. Nonetheless, in
the cases without shear, the propagation velocity in the far wake has a slight decreasing trend
within the analyzed downstream space.
These clear trends allow a simple model composed by a 1st degree equation, for the near wake
region, and a constant, for the far wake region. The slope of the linear part is going to vary
depending on the CT. It has been observed, that for a certain atmospheric turbulence, the variation
of the slopes that depend on CT also vary in a linear manner.
When determining the near wake breakdown, accounting for the added turbulence is necessary.
With the atmospheric values alone, the breakdown does not correspond to the LES.
From the case studies it has been concluded that the model accounts for the propagation delay in
a coherent manner, having longer delay times as the wind speed decreases and the opposite when
51

it increases. However, the magnitudes of the propagation velocity are overestimated, because the
ﬂow propagates faster than the propagation ratio proposed by De Mare [18]. One of the reasons
for the shorter delay times is the aggregated overestimation of the diameterwise propagation
velocity.
In the current project, the ﬁxed propagation ratio of 0.8 has been observed in the far wake.
However, given that the spacing of the turbines is closer to the near wake distances than the far
wake, the total propagation ratio observed for these distances is lower, and that should have been
reﬂected in the propagation velocity.

Chapter 5
Future work
In order to improve the work carried out during this project, more LES data is necessary. The
more data the model is based on, the more reliable it will be.
To be more speciﬁc, LES data for higher turbulence intensities should be targeted. Not only
regarding the atmospheric turbulence intensity of the inﬂow, but also due to the added turbulence
intensity downstream. Most of the turbines in a wind farm will be affected by the added turbu-
lence intensity , so the data should be obtained in these ranges of TI.
Having more LES data could also help establish a clear relation on the equations for the near
wake region by observing the effect of the atmospheric turbulence intensity on the linearity. If
all the coefﬁcients varied to some extent linearly, a relation should be found for the effect of TI
in the coefﬁcients of the near wake equation for different CTs.
Once the model is built with sufﬁcient information, it should be compared to LES simulations of
clusters or reliable measurements.
Finally, the model should be able to perform with more challenging inﬂows, such as a real time
series. Therefore, a time slot needs to be set in which the propagation time will be allowed to
vary. Otherwise, the abrupt differences in time delay will be represented as steps.
