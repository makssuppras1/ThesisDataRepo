Abstract
This project explores the novel integration of text descriptions with automated 3D ob­
ject generation and material assignment. This work advances the field by automating
the generation of 3D models from simple text descriptions. By leveraging text­to­image
generation and novel view synthesis, the project aims to produce not only accurate 3D
geometries but also appropriately assigned materials, thus enhancing the realism and fi­
delity of the rendered objects. This thesis contributes to computational creativity in 3D
graphics, offering a scalable method that can potentially transform how digital assets are
created across various industries.

1
Introduction
In the rapidly evolving domain of artificial intelligence, the ability to create 3D models
that can be implemented in computer games, virtual reality(VR), and augmented reality
(AR) is increasingly crucial. This project seeks to bridge the gap between textual descrip­
tions and visual representations, enhancing the capabilities of 3D modelling and rendering
technologies.
Traditional methods of designing 3D models are time­consuming, often requiring exten­
sive manual effort and expertise in complex modelling software. By automating the gener­
ation of 3D models from simple text descriptions, this project aims to make 3D modelling
more accessible and efficient, thereby simplifying the creation process and empowering
designers, artists, and even novices to bring their visions to life with ease.
The field of text­to­image generation has made significant progress by leveraging a pre­
trained diffusion model on text­image datasets. in contrast, 3D content generation devel­
oped at a slower speed. The initial 3D generation models are categorical, the model can
be used on objects for a single class, for example, a set of chairs. So the limitation largely
depends on the size of the text­3D dataset. Therefore, researchers try to achieve this by
applying powerful text­to­image generative models.
However, since the diffusion model cannot produce consistent multi­view outputs suit­
able for subsequent tasks, many works use distillation way to get 3D models, but some
challenges occur when lifting from 2 dimensions to 3 dimensions. Firstly, the diffusion
model always lacks multi­view constraints, which leads to a phenomenon named ’multi­
face Janus problem’, which is a common issue in text­to­3D generation techniques where
an object, particularly faces or heads, may appear with multiple instances of the same
feature in different views, causing inconsistency, as shown in Fig.1.1.
Figure 1.1: The squirrel has three ears and two faces
Some works attempt to keep the results consistent by introducing some innovative meth­
ods. MVDream[1], inspired by DreamBooth[2], user their multi­view diffusion model ab­
sorbs identity information from a set of 2D images and exhibits strong multi­view consis­
tency after fine­tuning. X­Dreamer[3] introduces two innovative ways: Camera­Guided
Low­Rank Adaptation (CG­LoRA) and Attention­Mask Alignment (AMA) Loss. CG­LoRA
dynamically integrates camera information into the pre­trained diffusion model to enhance

1
==PAGE:10==
the alignment. The AMA loss uses the binary mask of the 3D objects to guide the attention
map of the pre­trained diffusion model to prioritize foreground objects.
Secondly, Surface geometry, texture, and illumination are coupled in images, and directly
using a 2D diffusion model to infer geometry and texture simultaneously is considered sub­
optimal. This leads to a new two­stage decoupled approach: first, the geometry is gener­
ated, and then the texture is generated. The method Fantasia3D[4] shows good promise
in this decoupling strategy, significantly improving geometric reconstruction. However,
Fantasia3D relies on a 2D diffusion model to optimize normal maps, which results in differ­
ences in data distribution, compromising the quality of geometry generation and causing
optimization instability.
This project aims to advance the integration of AI in the field of computer graphics by
employing AI techniques for both the generation of 3D models and the assignment of
potential materials, to showcase the potential of AI in enhancing the fidelity and realism
of computer­generated imagery. We try to reduce the occurrence of the ’Janus problem’
and predict the object’s materials, then we could assign material for different parts of the
object later by employing the Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function(BRDF) or
another way.
Figure 1.2: The issues we want to solve in this system
Fig.1.2 shows the functionality of our system, users provide descriptive text detailing the
object they want to model, which employs generative models to convert textual descrip­
tions into initial 2D images. This image is then used by the Multi­View Generation com­
ponent to create multiple views of the object. The 3D Reconstruction from 2D Images
component utilizes these multi­view images to construct a detailed 3D model, ensuring
the geometry and perspective are consistent across all views. Finally, the Material As­
signment and Rendering part assigns appropriate materials to the reconstructed 3D model
and renders it, enhancing the visual realism and fidelity of the model based on the text
input.

Background
Integrating AI with 3D modelling technology holds great promise but is also confronted
with rigorous difficulties. One major challenge is the lack of comprehensive 3D datasets
and assets, which are essential for training accurate models. Additionally, the complexity
of training these models and the limitations of real­time AI rendering further complicate
progress in this area.
Research in text­to­3D model technology has rapidly developed. Some companies like
Meshy, Luma AI, and CSM are leveraging advanced AI technologies to streamline the
creation of 3D content, transforming how professionals and hobbyists design digital as­
sets across various platforms. Notable examples include Google’s DreamFusion[5] and
NVIDIA’s Magic3D[6], which can be used for designing 3D assets in games and the meta­
verse. Researchers have also employed multi­model learning strategies, they support a
variety of input modalities that can be easily provided by humans, including sound, im­
ages, text, partially observed shapes, and combinations of these, to achieve Interactive
generation of 3D models
These technologies demonstrate significant advancements in generating detailed and re­
alistic 3D models directly from textual descriptions. By utilizing state­of­the­art AI algo­
rithms, these platforms not only improve the accuracy and detail of 3D renderings but also
streamline the design process, making it more efficient and accessible to developers and
creators across various industries.

The first three methods are explicit representation methods, which are generally easy
to edit but consume resources or are difficult to reconstruct; the latter two methods are
implicit representation methods, which are generally convenient to reconstruct but difficult
to edit, which emerged following the rise of the deep learning method.
The methods of 3D generation can roughly be divided into two factions. One is the point
cloud diffusion method based on the 3D point cloud and diffusion model, such as Point­E,
which generates 3D point clouds from input images, starting with a coarse set of points
representing the object’s surface, which is then refined to produce a dense and high­
quality point set. This method excels in reconstructing 3D objects with detailed spatial
distribution from single images.
On the other hand, some method like Shape­E[7] uses an implicit surface representation
through a signed distance function (SDF) to generate continuous 3D shapes. It predicts
SDF values for points in space, defining the object’s surface as the zero­level set, al­
lowing for smooth and detailed surface reconstruction suitable for complex geometries.
While Point­E focuses on discrete point clouds for simplicity, Shape­E offers higher detail
and smoothness with continuous implicit surfaces, catering to applications requiring fine
surface representations.
The other kind of method is the more promising, using 2D generation large model and
Neural Radiance Fields(NeRF)F[8] to get a 3D model from 2D. It uses the prior information
of 2D pictures to reconstruct the 3D scene representation. Some methods are developed
based on this technology, for example, DreamFusion, One­2­3­45[9], prolificdreamer[10],
etc. Recently, 3D Gaussion Splatting[11] uses 3D Gaussians as a flexible and efficient
representation, thereby taking advantage of implicit and explicit radiation fields. Gaussian
accurately expresses scenes through neural network­based optimization but with explicit,
structured data storage.

3
Related Work
In various applications, creating high­quality 3D assets becomes more and more impor­
tant. The generative model learns the data distribution directly from 3D data to achieve
data sampling. The currently existing methods use many kinds of representation methods
including voxels, point clouds, meshes and implicit fields to construct scenes, which have
had promising results. However, due to the limited size of 3D training datasets, these
methods can only demonstrate generative capabilities within a limited class of objects or
train a text­conditioned 3D generative model from scratch, for example, SDFusion[12] by
Cheng et al.
3.1
Single­view Reconstruction
Besides using text­3D object pair datasets like ShapeNet[13][14] to train the deep learning
model to get the 3D objects, other methods include using pre­trained text­to­image models
and then employing score distillation sampling(SDS)[5] to generate the shape from image.
The milestone is the occurrence of DreamFusion which achieves zero­shot text to 3d by
using SDS to modify NeRF. They leverage a pre­trained 2D text­to­image diffusion model
as a prior for synthesizing 3D objects from text prompts without the need for 3D training
data. By optimizing a randomly initialised 3D model using a loss function based on prob­
ability density distillation, the approach enables the generation of 3D models that can be
viewed from various angles, relit by different illuminations, and composited into diverse
3D environments. The optimization process involves sampling cameras and lights, ren­
dering images of the NeRF model, computing gradients of the SDS loss, and updating
NeRF parameters iteratively. The method demonstrates the effectiveness of transferring
2D image diffusion models to the 3D domain and highlights the potential of text­to­3D
synthesis without the reliance on explicit 3D data.
Some researchers modified the NeRF model to get better results. For, example, Neu­
ral Radiance Factorization(NeRfactor)[15], when given a set of images captured from a
different view with an unknown illumination source, NeRFactor can factorize the scene
into 3D neural fields of surface normals, light visibility, albedo, and material represents by

3.3
Multi­view Reconstruction
Multiview reconstruction is a process in which 3D structures are derived from multiple
2D images taken from different viewpoints. The procedure begins with image acquisi­
tion, capturing numerous images of the scene or object from various angles to ensure
Generating an Object Shape with Assigned Materials from Text
5
==PAGE:14==
comprehensive coverage. These images are then processed for feature detection, iden­
tifying distinctive points such as edges and corners using algorithms like Scale­invariant
feature transform(SIFT)[16] or Oriented Fast and Rotated Brief(ORB)[17]. Once features
are detected, feature matching aligns these points across different images to establish
correspondences.
Next, camera calibration is essential to determine both intrinsic parameters (internal cam­
era specifications like focal length and optical center) and extrinsic parameters (camera
positions and orientations relative to each other). With these parameters, Structure from
Motion(SFM)[18] techniques estimate the 3D coordinates of the features by solving the
structure and motion of the cameras and scene simultaneously. After obtaining a sparse
set of 3D points, dense reconstruction methods like multi­view stereo (MVS) are employed
to generate a complete 3D surface by filling in the gaps between sparse points.
The resulting dense point cloud is then used for surface reconstruction, creating a mesh
or surface model through algorithms like Poisson surface reconstruction or Delaunay tri­
angulation. Finally, texture mapping applies textures from the original images onto the
3D model, enhancing its realism.
Multiview reconstruction faces challenges such as occlusions, illumination variations, and
computational complexity. Accurate camera calibration is crucial for a successful out­
come. Despite these challenges, this technique has vast applications, tools and libraries
like OpenMVG[19], COLMAP[20][21], and Meshroom[22] to facilitate the implementation
of these processes, making multiview reconstruction a powerful tool in creating accurate
3D models from 2D images.
3.4
Material Estimation and Assignment
The estimation and assignment of materials are central to simulating realistic visual effects
in academic research and commercial applications.
3.4.1
Computer Graphics Material Models
In the field of Computer Graphics, the representation of surface materials is a fundamen­
tal concern. The most widely used model for this purpose is the BRDF. This function
describes how light is reflected at an opaque surface, detailing the change in light inten­
sity due to incident and viewing angles. It is critical for achieving realistic rendering of
objects in CG. Notably, the BRDF model captures the interaction of light with surfaces in
a way that can be empirically measured and reproduced.
3.4.2
Material Prediction in Computer Vision
Moving into the realm of Computer Vision, material properties are typically predicted using
machine learning models that can interpret and classify material characteristics from 2D
images. For instance, techniques like those employed in the NeRFactor utilize the MERL
BRDF database[23] to train neural networks, specifically Multilayer Perceptrons (MLP),
to predict the BRDF properties at different surface points. This allows for the synthetic
generation of realistic materials under varied lighting conditions.
Despite the advances in material prediction, these methods generally fall short of the
capabilities offered by professional 3D modeling software, such as Keyshot or Blender.
These advanced tools allow users to not only define geometrical structures but also to
intricately specify materials and textures. Most AI­driven methods in CV focus primarily
on shape and structure and often omit detailed material properties, necessitating manual
intervention for realistic rendering.

In contrast, our project aims to bridge this gap by predicting materials from 2D planar ge­

3.4.3
Material Generation
Recent advancements in material generation have enhanced the ability to intricately model
the properties and interrelationships of different materials within a scene. A notable ex­
ample is the development of ControlMat[24], a diffusion­based material generation model
introduced by Vecchio et al. This model generates high­resolution materials from a sin­
gle input photograph, even under uncontrolled lighting conditions. By leveraging diffusion
models, the authors effectively address challenges such as flash­based acquisition and
the handling of unknown illumination conditions. Building on different approaches, Liang
et al.[25] have developed a method that uses differentiable building blocks to construct
programmatic materials. These blocks can convert large­scale programmatic models into
differentiable node graphs. The method employs deep neural features to predict coarse
parameters and select the most suitable programmatic graph. Once selected, the node
graph is treated similarly to a neural network, which facilitates the recovery of accurate
parameter values from the captured images. This approach addresses the limitations of
traditional pixel­based generative models, which struggle with the irregular structures of
material graphs due to variable node counts and parameter types. Furthermore, the Mat­
Former[26] model overcomes the shortcomings of existing sequence generation models
like L(LSTM)[27] and (GRU)[28], which are less effective at handling the special struc­
tures of material graphs. MatFormer utilizes a three­stage generation strategy based
on the Transformer architecture, optimizing the process to first generate the node type
sequence, then the parameters for each node, and finally the connections between the
nodes. This method ensures a more coherent and effective generation of realistic mate­
rial graphs, capturing complex inter­node dependencies and facilitating the generation of
materials that accurately reflect real­world textures and properties.
3.4.4
Material­based Object Segmentation
Material­based segmentation involves recognizing different parts of an object and seg­
menting it based on materials. Jeppe et al.[29] achieve a data­driven method for semantic
segmentation of images based on material properties, specifically targeting complex ma­
terials like glass, chrome, plastic, and ceramics. Utilizing synthetic images and training
through the DeepLabv3+[30] convolution neural network, their approach successfully seg­
ments these materials from both synthetic and real­world images. This method leverages
the advantages of synthetic data to train models that are effective in real­world applica­
tions, highlighting the potential of using advanced rendering techniques and deep learning
for sophisticated image processing tasks.
Furthermore, to achieve dense segmentation, Paul et al.[31] introduce a large­scale dataset
of 3.2 million dense segments on 44,560 indoor and outdoor images. Then, researchers
extended the dataset and thought of some new methods. The MaterialSeg3D[32] frame­
work utilizes the MIO dataset to infer the underlying materials of 3D assets and generate
precise surface material information, including properties such as metallic and roughness.
Researchers conduct segmentation on textures and generate new UV maps, proposing
the utilization of human prior knowledge of 2D material information to guide the surface
material generation for 3D assets. MatAtlas[33] proposes an innovative method to gen­

Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models
The basis of no matter text­to­image or multi­view image generation is the Diffusion Model.
Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models(DDPMs)[36] provide an effective generative model
by progressively adding noise to data and then systematically removing the noises, en­
abling the generation of high­quality data samples. The core idea is to learn the reverse
denoising process, allowing the model to recover real data from noise. This method ex­
cels in tasks like image generation and video generation, making it a significant approach
in the field of generative models.

noise to it, ultimately ending up with a pure noise distribution. This process is repre­
sented as a series of steps where a small amount of noise is added. Specifically, for real

4.2.1
Perceptual Image Compression
The technology for implementing image latent space compression is Variational Autoen­
coder (VAE)[38]. The VAE neural network consists of the encoder and the decoder. The
encoder compresses the image into a low­dimensional representation in the latent space,
while the decoder restores the data in the latent space to the original image.
As shown in Fig.4.2, for an image x in pixel space(512x512x3), Stable Diffusion uses
Encoder ε to encode x into a 4x64x64 latent space to get ε(x), which is 48 times smaller
than the image. All forward diffusion and back diffusion steps are performed in latent
space. The training process is the same, but instead of generating a noise image, creating
a tensor in the latent space (i.e., a 4x64x64 noise map).
4.2.2
Generative Modeling in Latent Space
Latent Diffusion Model
Latent Diffusion Models[37] build upon the principles of DDPM
by transferring the diffusion process to a latent space. The diffusion process is partitioned
into forward and reverse diffusion processes as same as the stable.
To address the resource consumption issue of DDPM. Robin et al. introduce an autoen­
coder, such as the VAE discussed earlier, where the original image is first compressed into
a lower­dimensional representation ε(x). This ε(x) is then used as the input for DDPM.
The DDPM generates results that are subsequently decoded back into images by the
decoder. Since ε(x) is compressed and has much smaller dimensions than the origi­
nal image, this approach significantly reduces the resource consumption of DDPM. The
compressed data ε(x) resides in a latent space, thus it can be referred to as latent data.
In the forward diffusion process, Gaussian noise is incrementally added to ε(x) over sev­
eral time steps, transforming it into completely noisy data. This process is simulated dur­
ing training by adding noise to real data, allowing the model to learn the characteristics of
Generating an Object Shape with Assigned Materials from Text
11
==PAGE:20==
noise addition and its impact on image quality.
The reverse diffusion process is where the actual image generation occurs. Starting from
an initial noise sample drawn from a standard Gaussian distribution, the model iteratively
refines the noise sample step by step to generate an image. At each time step t, the
model predicts the noise component in the current noisy image xt conditioned on the text
encoding. This predicted noise is subtracted from xt to obtain xt−1, gradually denoising
the image and refining its details to match the text description.
4.2.3
Conditioning Mechanism
Stable Diffusion needs instructions to guide it when generating images, otherwise it is
not a complete text­to­image model. Without instructions, it will be impossible to control
whether Stable Diffusion generates images of cats or dogs.
At this time, the concept of ”conditioning” needs to be introduced. The purpose of condi­
tioning is to guide the noise predictor so that it can predict the appropriate noise so that
after subtracting it from the image, the generated image evolves in the desired condition.
4.2.4
Encoders for LDMs
Fig.4.2 shows how text prompts are processed and fed into the noise predictor.
First, the Tokenizer converts each word in the instruction into a token represented by a

Text Encoder: CLIP
Contrastive Language­Image Pre­training(CLIP)[39] is a commonly
used text encoder that converts input text descriptions into dense vector representations.
These vectors capture the semantic information of the text and serve as conditional inputs
for the image generation process. CLIP is particularly effective due to its ability to under­
stand and encode complex textual semantics, making it ideal for generating coherent and
contextually accurate images.
Tokenizer
After the text instruction is processed by the CLIP tokenizer, it will be broken
down into a set of tokens. Tokenizing text is the first step to making it understandable
to computers. Humans can read words, but computers can only read numbers, so it is
necessary to convert the text into tokens corresponding to a set of numbers.
The tokenizer can only perform tokenization on words it has seen during training. For
example, in the CLIP model, there are two tokens, ”California” and ”beach”, but do not
have token ”Californiabeach”. When processing, the tokenizer will split the word ”Cali­
forniabeach” into two tokens it knows, ”California” and ”beach”. Therefore, a word does
not necessarily correspond to only one token. In addition, spaces are also part of a to­
ken. The word ”California beach” generates two tokens, ”California” and ”[space]beach”.
These tokens are different from the two tokens parsed from ”California” in the previous
example because the tokens in the previous example did not contain spaces.

4.4
3D Reconstruction
To get the object’s geometry, we directly train the neural surface reconstruction method
NeuS, because we can generate consistent multi­view images.
Different from NeRF,
NeuS offers more accurate surface reconstructions through its use of Signed Distance
Function (SDF) and a novel unbiased volume rendering method, which provides high­
quality reconstructions for complex structures and self­occlusions without needing fore­
ground masks for supervision. While NeRF is robust for novel view synthesis by learning
a volumetric radiance field, it struggles with extracting high­fidelity surfaces due to insuffi­
cient surface constraints in its density­based representation. NeuS’s approach addresses
these limitations by combining the benefits of surface and volume rendering, ensuring ro­
bustness against abrupt depth changes and better handling of self­occlusions, making it
superior for detailed 3D surface reconstruction.

Hierarchical Sampling
The stratified sampling strategy is very close to NeRF. Firstly,
sampling is performed uniformly over the rays, and then importance sampling is iteratively
performed at the peak of the coarse probability estimate. But different from maintaining
two networks in NeRF, only maintaining one network, computing coarse sampling prob­
abilities based on ϕs(f(x)) with fixed standard deviations and fine sampling probabilities
with the learned s.
The crude probability at the ith iteration is fixed to set the value of s to 32×2i. In addition in
each iteration, we additionally go to sample 16 more points. Thus we sampled a total of
128 points. For the no­mask setting, we sample an additional 32 points outside the unit
ball. The representation of the sampled points outside the unit ball is implemented based
on NeRF++[44].
Specifically, the network further includes two MLP (Multi­Layer Perceptron) networks,
which are used to encode SDF and color respectively. The MLP for SDF encoding con­
sists of 8 hidden layers, each containing 256 hidden neurons. Additionally, the original
ReLU activation is replaced with Softplus (β = 100) for each hidden layer. Skip connec­
tions are used between the input and the output of the fourth layer. As for the MLP that
encodes color, it utilizes 4 hidden layers, each containing 256 neurons. It inputs not only
the spatial position but also the viewing direction, the normal vectors from the SDF, and
the 256­dimensional feature vector output by the previous SDF MLP. Position encoding is
used with six frequencies for spatial positions and four frequencies for viewing directions.
4.5
Material Prediction and Assignment
Logically, we have two conditions. First condition, we write the specific material’s in­
formation in the text prompt. Second, we don’t provide the stable diffusion model with
information about the object’s materials.
The first condition, the stable diffusion model can understand the text prompt and show the
material in the generated image. In the second condition, the appearance of the material
will be decided by the stable diffusion model. Based on its training sets, it will assign
the corresponding materials to the object as they appear in real life. Regardless of the
condition, we then use a neural network to predict the material and save the result in a
2­dimensional matrix.
4.5.1

ding natural image patterns in synthetic data to achieve learning zero­shot segmentation.

For material prediction, we trained a ResNet network[46] to classify each segmented re­

image, isolating each region, and then feeding these masked regions into the model indi­
vidually. The model outputs a material label for each region. These predicted labels are
then mapped back to the corresponding pixels in the original image dimensions, creating
a material matrix.

Discussion
This project achieves the lower bound of generation of a 3D model, but there still are some
limitations that need to be improved.
6.1
Limitation
The primary limitation encountered in this project is the generation speed, particularly
evident during the multi­view image synthesis and 3D reconstruction phases. The se­
quential generation of images in the second step relies on using the generated images as
feedback to guide the generation process. What’s more, we require additional memory to
store these images, and the sequential generation of images will result in additional time
consumption.
Furthermore, while NeuS provides high­quality reconstructions of static scenes, its train­
ing phase is considerably time­intensive. Another notable issue is the resolution compro­
mise; to expedite processing, images were generated at a lower resolution (256x256),
which adversely affected the detail fidelity in complex objects, as seen in the poorly ren­
dered tree in Fig.5.5. Opting for higher resolutions would enhance detail but at the cost
of increased processing time, posing a challenge for real­time applications.
Another problem is that we have not tested the system on complex objects like gastropod
shells. So it is hard to say the performance on complex material.
6.2
Future Work
To address these challenges, future work will focus on optimizing the multi­view 3D re­
construction pipeline.
3D reconstruction speed
We plan to incorporate NeuS2, which integrates multi­resolution
hash coding into neural surface representations, drawing inspiration from Instant­NGP[54].
This approach is expected to significantly reduce reconstruction time, with 15k iterations
taking about 5 minutes, achieving significant speedup without compromising quality.
Generation Quality
In addition, we aim to keep the original high­resolution image (1024*1024)
obtained from the stable diffusion model during the new view generation process or just
compress the image to 512*512. Although this requires longer processing time, it will be
able to preserve key details and thus improve the quality of the final 3D model.
Material Prediction
During the process of material prediction, we use FMD database,
but it does not have too many classes of materials, recently, Materialized Individual Ob­
jects (MIO)[32] was introduced by Li et al. It contains 14 material classes under different
camera poses which are collected from the AmbientCG[55] and Adobe dataset[56]. In
future work, we could train the model on this new dataset, and then get a better accuracy
of the material.

Conclusion
This project embarked on a journey to bridge the gap between textual descriptions and
their corresponding 3D models, leveraging advanced techniques in AI­generated content.
The integration of Stable Diffusion and Neural Implicit Surfaces models showcased the
potential of AI in transforming simple text into intricate 3D representations. the initial
results are promising, and show the project’s capability to reach the requirements of 3D
model generation.
The use of multi­view generation techniques and the employment of the NeuS model for
3D reconstruction on high­performance hardware such as the Tesla H100­PCIE demon­
strated the project’s technical viability and scalability. The project encountered significant
challenges related to generation speed and computational efficiency, particularly during
the sequential generation of images and the training phases of NeuS. The compromise
on image resolution to expedite processing times resulted in a loss of detail in complex
objects, indicating a need for a balanced approach to resolution settings.
Looking forward, the project aims to incorporate NeuS2 for faster 3D reconstruction, po­
tentially reducing time costs drastically without sacrificing output quality. Furthermore,
maintaining high­resolution images throughout the processing chain is planned to improve
the quality of the final 3D models, albeit at the expense of increased computational de­
mand.
The exploration of these advanced techniques opens up new avenues for research and
development in AI and 3D modelling. Continuous improvements and innovations in this
domain could soon enable even more sophisticated applications, from virtual reality to
personalized 3D printing, marking a significant step forward in the digital representation
of complex objects and environments. This project stands as a testament to the evolving
capabilities of AI in the creative and design industries, paving the way for future advance­
ments that could transform how we interact with and visualize digital content. As AI tech­
nologies continue to mature, the potential applications of such tools are bound to expand,
promising exciting developments in the fields of computer graphics and beyond.

A.1
Matrics in Section4.3
Structural Similarity Index Measure(SSIM)
SSIM is used to measure the similarity
between two images. It is based on the computation of three factors: luminance, contrast,
and structure. SSIM takes into account the visibility of errors (or differences) based on
the structural information in an image. It provides a value between ­1 and 1, where 1
indicates perfect similarity. This measure is particularly useful in assessing the quality of
images after compression or other types of processing where preservation of structural
information is crucial.
Peak Signal­to­Noise Ratio(PSNR)
PSNR is another widely used metric in image pro­
cessing, often for assessing the quality of reconstruction or compression. It measures
the ratio between the maximum possible power of a signal (represented by image pixel
intensities) and the power of corrupting noise that affects the fidelity of its representation.
It is typically expressed in a logarithmic decibel scale. A higher PSNR generally indicates
that the reconstruction is of higher quality, but it doesn’t always correspond perfectly to
the perceived visual quality
Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity(LPIPS)
LPIPS is a newer metric that uses
deep learning to estimate perceptual similarities between images. Unlike traditional met­
rics like SSIM and PSNR which focus on pixel­level errors, LPIPS evaluates images in
a manner that aligns more closely with human visual perception. It uses features ex­
tracted from pre­trained neural networks to compute similarity, offering a more nuanced
assessment of visible differences in image quality.
LPIPS is used to measure the perceptual similarity between images. The lower the LPIPS
value, the higher the consistency between views.
SIFT
SIFT is a feature detection algorithm used in computer vision to detect and de­
scribe local features in images. The method involves identifying keypoints in images and
computing descriptors that are invariant to image scale and rotation, and partially invariant
to changes in illumination and 3D camera viewpoint. These features are then often used
for various tasks such as object recognition, image stitching, and scene classification.
SIFT features can be matched between different images to find corresponding points,
often using metrics like Euclidean distance of their descriptors.
SIFT is used to measure the number of matching points between images. More matching
points means higher view consistency.
CLIP
CLIP is a model developed by OpenAI that efficiently learns visual concepts from
natural language supervision. It’s trained on a variety of (image, text) pairs and learns to
associate images with captions, facilitating tasks like zero­shot classification, where the
model can correctly classify images into categories it has never seen during training. In
applications, one might use similarity scores computed from CLIP to evaluate how well
an image matches a textual description, or vice versa. These scores are often derived
from the cosine similarity between the image features and text features produced by the
model.

BCE Loss in NeuS training process
The BCE loss of mask is:

Here, N is the number of samples, yi represents the actual label of the i­th sample, and
ˆyi is the predicted probability that the i­th sample belongs to the positive class.
When the actual label yi is 1, i.e., the target category is positive, the loss function primarily
computes through yi · log(ˆyi), focusing on the model’s probability prediction for the posi­
tive class. The loss increases if the predicted probability ˆyi is far below the actual value
(nearing 0). Conversely, when the actual label yi is 0, i.e., the target category is negative,
the loss is calculated by (1 −yi) · log(1 −ˆyi), focusing on the probability prediction for the
negative class. Here, the loss increases if the predicted probability ˆyi exceeds the actual
value (approaching 1).
The objective of this loss function is to minimize the total loss across all training samples,
thereby encouraging the model to predict probabilities that are as close as possible to the
actual labels, whether they are positive or negative.
A.3
Get Parameters of BRDF
To store the materials in a ’.mtl’ file, we also need to store parametric for each material.
Except for getting the parameters online, we also provide a way to compute the parame­
ters of a certain material from the MERL Database. In short, The calculation formula uses
the Bidirectional Scattering Distribution Function (BSDF) multiplied by the lighting param­
eter (denoted as Light) to compute the Material reflectance data (MERL). The formula is
expressed as: BSDF × Light = MERL
Upon observing the results, it was noted that the color of MERL appeared dark. To address
this, a corrective factor, termed MERL_fix, was introduced to enhance the brightness of
the image. Consequently, the final formula was adjusted to: BSDF × Light = MERL ×
MERL_fix This adjustment ensures that the resulting image maintains an appropriate
level of brightness.
