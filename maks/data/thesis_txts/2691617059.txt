Abstract
Carbon fibre reinforced pultruded profiles are characterized by their exceptional mechanical
properties. Their superiority against composites fabricated with different manufacturing tech­
niques is favourable when they are intended to be used as main loading carrying components
at the spar caps of wind turbine blades. Their very few defects render difficult and doubtful
the determination of their behaviour when they are tested under compression.
Two and three­dimension numerical models are employed for predicting the compressive be­
haviour and validating experimental results from two specific pultruded profiles, named S and
Z profile. Their material properties needed as input for the numerical models are determined
and simulations considering different material systems and fibre orientations are conducted
as a parametric study for the compressive strength and stiffness predicted from the models.
Furthermore, the impact of the resin properties, the temperature, the element size, and the dif­
ferent fibre imperfection assumed in the numerical models on the compressive behaviour of
the composites are also investigated. Additionally, a comparison between the 2D and 3D user
material subroutines (UMAT) is performed, showing how the plane strain condition is handled
by each model.
The numerical predictions are in good agreement with the experimental results. The Z pul­
truded profile performed better compared with the S profile, by showing higher strength both
when it is mechanically tested and numerically simulated. Finally, the inferior tensile ma­
trix properties caused by elevated temperatures and generally low­performance resins, and the
level of the fibre misalignment are the primary parameters that undermine considerably the
performance of the pultruded profiles under compression.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Pultruded profiles in wind turbine blades
Wind energy is indubitably one of the most rapidly expanding industries nowadays. Over the
last decades, the industry has quickly matured, with innovative turbine designs, advanced oper­
ations and increased performance having considerably decreased the cost. The power from the
kinetic wind energy where a wind turbine can extract is analogous to the effective area covered
from the rotors, according to Betz’s law with a maximum efficiency of 59.3%. Therefore, the
wind energy trends focus on the rotor blade’s design, production, and materials to maximize
power by increasing the blade length and thus the area occupied by the rotor.
However, constructing longer and consequently heavier blades with conventional materials
(glass fibres), introduce higher loads which must be undertaken both by the blade itself and
the support structures together with the turbine drive components. As a result, the installed
capital cost of the system increases. Additionally, as the life span of wind turbines is twenty
years, reliability and exceptional performance are necessary.
While these requirements were primarily met by manufacturing blades from glass fibres rein­
forced composites, modern blade designs require the use of carbon fibre. Nowadays, blade
designers are aiming to bigger, lighter, and slimmer blades. With lesser favorable high­wind
speed sites available, especially onshore, wind turbines with lighter and longer blades are crit­
ical to presume upon lower­wind speed sites. Moreover, slender blade profiles provide better
aerodynamic response and require less material for the build.
For constructing slimmer and longer blades, manufacturers have begun to replace crucial load­
carrying blade areas from glass fibre to carbon fibre. As a result, the blades become longer,
lighter, and stiffer due to the higher stiffness and lower density of the carbon fibre. Further­
more, lighter blades can be accommodated by a less concrete and robust tower and turbine
components decreasing considerably the cost. However, these savings are counterbalanced by
the significantly more expensive carbon fibre used in the blades.
Carbon fibre reinforced composites were started to be employed in the spar cap by manu­
facturers since the latter consists the main load­carrying element of the blade. It is essential
that these unidirectional fibre­reinforced parts are composed by straight and perfectly aligned

fibres in order to ensure exceptional performance. Defects in the composite, like voids, wrin­
kles, and fibre misalignment, undermine the composite’s performance under tension and com­
pression. Currently, the main processes used for manufacturing spar caps are the infusion
or pre­impregnated (prepreg). While both techniques introduce a significant amount of wrin­
kles in the final product, they also require high levels of manual operations increasing the cost.
Another way to manufacture the spar caps is through pultrusion process. Pultrusion is a contin­
uous and consistent method that provides high quality unidirectional composite profiles. The
controlled resin content, the higher fibre volume fraction and the precise unidirectional fibre
alignment attained with pultrusion provide consistently superior overall mechanical properties
compared with other manufacturing techniques. The process also enables customization, since
various profile geometries and lengths can be produced depending on the need of the applica­
tion. The pultruded spar caps are supplied prefabricated for installation to the rest of the blade
structure, thus giving the opportunity to the blade manufacturers to minimize the production
time and increase the quality. Pultrusion is a cost­competitive manufacturing method of uni­
directional continuous fibre composites and as a result, has gained high interest from leading
turbine producers over the last years Ennis et al. (2019).
1.2
Pultrusion process
Pultrusion process is a relatively recent method of manufacturing fibre reinforced polymer
composites. The first patent was issued at 1951 from Brandt Goldsworthy who is considered
the ’father’ of pultrusion technology Starr (2000). Pultrusion is undoubtedly a proven man­
ufacturing process for acquiring high performance composites. The consistent repeatability
of the exceptional mechanical properties seen in pultruded profiles makes this manufacturing
method favourable for structural components production. An illustration showing the pultru­
sion process is depicted in Figure 1.1.
First, the reinforcements in the form of continuous strands like rovings are supported by racks
placed in the beginning of the production line. The reinforcements are then continuously fed
through a guiding system into the resin bath where they are impregnated with the required
liquid resin system. The impregnated reinforcements are then progressively directed such that
approximately forming the desired final profile before they enter the heated die. The resin
cures within the die resulting in a solid and fully shaped profile. Before the profile is be­
ing gripped and pulled by the reciprocating puller units it has first sufficiently being cooled.
In most cases, the pullers follow a hand­over­hand movement such that ensuring a constant
pulling speed. Finally, the profile is cut by a cut­off saw into the desired lengths before the
final product is being taken care of in the take­off unit.

1.3 Research Objective and Motivation
3
Different types of reinforcement materials can be used such as carbon roving, mat or cloth.
The carbon reinforcements in the roving form allow the determination of the location and
amount of the carbon fibres in the design stage. The mechanical properties of fibre reinforced
polymer composite are not only dependent on the mechanical performance of the constituents
(fibre and matrix) independently but also on the fibre alignment and content seen in the final
product. Since the pultrusion process allows a consistent control of these two key parameters,
leads to superior composites and makes them both interesting to be investigated and more
favourable for use as loading carrying components in the spar cups.

1.3
Research Objective and Motivation
The investigation of the compressive properties of polymer fibre reinforced composites is cru­
cial since comprising the key design limit when they are used as load­carrying components
in wind turbine blades. Given that the industry is heading towards larger, slimmer and stiffer
blades, carbon based composites are preferable due to their high mass specific stiffness and
strength. Manufacturing cost is the main barrier for blade construction from entirely carbon
reinforced composites with conventional manufacturing practices e.g. infusion, thus giving
the advantage to lower cost material manufacturing processes e.g. pultrusion.
Main objective of this work is to numerically investigate the compressive behaviour of carbon
fibre pultruded profiles intended for use in wind turbine blades by employing a user defined
material model in the commercial finite element code Abaqus. The principal reason behind
this research is the challenging investigation of the compressive behaviour of the profiles when
they are mechanically tested. Pultruded profiles demonstrate exceptional tensile and compres­
sion properties with minor defects inside them, thus having a few failure initiation points. This
combined with the inferior shear properties compared to their tensile and compressive perfor­
mance often leads to conservative results when the profiles are being mechanically tested. As

2.1
Failure mechanisms
Various mechanisms can lead to fibre reinforced composites failure under compressive loading.
One of the first study on the compressive failure mechanism of fibre composites was conducted
by Rosen (1965). Later, Fleck (1997) provided a detailed overview of the mechanisms respon­
sible for the compressive failure of unidirectional fibre composites. More specifically, in his
work, he highlighted six distinct mechanisms as shown in Figure 2.1.
In Figure 2.1 (a) elastic micro­buckling failure mechanism is depicted. This failure mode is a
shear buckle instability governed mainly from the matrix shear properties and initially studied
from Rosen (1965).
Sketch in Figure 2.1 (b) illustrates the plastic micro­buckling failure mechanism or kink band
formation which is a plastic shear instability and was studied initially from Argon (1972). This
instability appears when the matrix deforms non­linearly, namely at substantially large strains
in the matrix. This failure mode is going to be discussed further in this report since constitutes
the dominant failure mechanism in polymer­matrix composites, Fleck (1997).
Fibre crushing is the third failure mode suggested by Fleck (1997) as presented in Figure 2.1
(c). In this failure mechanism, the fibres fail prior to the matrix, requiring a sufficiently stiffer
and stronger matrix system. The initiation point occurs when the intrinsic crushing strain ϵfc
of the fibres equals the uniaxial strain in the composite. However, depending on the fibre ma­
terial, different mechanisms (e.g. longitudinal splitting, fibre yielding, and microscopic fibre
kinking) could accommodate fibre crushing.
Matrix splitting is another failure mechanism as shown in Figure 2.1 (d) where the matrix
cracks along the fibres. Essentially, consists of a fracture failure of the matrix similar to the
tensile mode I cracking. Composites prone to this failure mode are those where the stiffness
of the matrix exceeds that of the fibres, combined with low toughness and high porosity. An
extensive study on matrix splitting was conducted by Sammis and Ashby (1986).
Buckling­delamination is shown in Figure 2.1 (e). It is an out­of­plane buckling mode that ap­
pears when a surface layer demonstrates weak or insufficient bonding. Then split­debonding

was studied by Hutchinson and Suo (1991).
Finally, Fleck (1997) discussed the shear band formation as depicted in Figure 2.1 (f). The cur­

2.2
Kink Band
The models used to describe the plastic micro­buckling failure mechanism are based on the in­
stability approach, meaning that the failure load is equal to the load required for the instability
to occur. The point at which the instability appears is the so called critical point. Is essentially
the loading point beyond which the stiffness is no longer positively defined. By this approach,
it is assumed that the compressive strength of the composite is given by the load at which the
critical point is reached.
A cutting edge model based on the instability approach was first proposed by Rosen (1965). He
developed an elastic analytical model, where the fibres are initially perfectly aligned, and as­
sumed that the elastic bifurcation occurs through two possible modes i.e. a transverse buckling
mode and a shear buckling mode. However, he highlighted that the elastic instability is mainly
caused by the shear buckling mode at which the fibres deflect into a sinusoidal shape. Then,
based on the assumption that the kinking is an elastic bifurcation­buckling phenomenon, Rosen
derived an analytical expression equating the compressive strength with the elastic shear mod­
ulus of the whole composite as shown in Equation 2.1. Moreover, he showed that the lowest
predicted strength is given for an infinite fibre wavelength (straight fibres undergoing uniform
rotation of an angle ϕ). The key conclusion from his work was that the shear properties of the
matrix play a crucial role in the composite’s compressive behaviour.

The main conclusions from both Argon (1972) and Budiansky and Fleck (1993) work is that
the key parameters impacting substantially the composites strength prediction under compres­
sion are the fibre misalignment and the matrix plasticity. The compressive strengths predicted
from Budiansky and Fleck (1993) expression as shown in Equation 2.4 are in good agreement
with numerous experiments.
The models developed by both Argon (1972) and Budiansky and Fleck (1993) assume the pre­
existence of an idealised kink­band geometry thus determining the critical stresses through a
static equilibrium approach. However, Jensen and Christoffersen (1997) introduced a plane
strain constitutive model considering the matrix and the fibre nonlinear response for predicting
the compressive strength, based on an energy approach till stability. The way to do so is by de­
termining the load beyond which the stiffness matrix is negatively defined. By this approach,
they showed a strength dependency on the fibre material parameters regardless of the huge
difference with the matrix material properties. The finite geometry, the layering of the mate­
rial and the deep derivation of the constitutive material behaviour by means of both J2­flow
and J2­deformation allowed Jensen and Christoffersen (1997) to develop a model way more
accurate compared to the hardening behavior model suggested by Budiansky and Fleck (1993).
Another very important parameter associated with the superiority of the pultruded profiles
against conventional polymer composites is the fibre volume fraction. Its affection on the
compressive strength of the composite was not investigated from Argon (1972) and Budiansky
and Fleck (1993). However, Pane and Jensen (2004) highlighted its impact on the compressive
strength prediction.
The so far discussed studies consists of the fundamental approaches for modeling and pre­
dicting the compressive strength of polymer fibre reinforced composites and investigating its
dependency on the initial fibre imperfections and material properties. Accurate material mod­
els for the fibre and matrix constituents are crucial when modelling such a failure mechanism
with specific concern placed on the matrix plasticity.

As initially proposed by Argon (1972), in order to model the local kinking instability namely
initiate the plastic micro buckling failure mechanism, fibre imperfection has to be considered
in a specific region of the mean. A local idealised imperfection of the composite was devel­
oped by Sørensen et al. (2009) and it was implemented in ABAQUS/Standard through a user
ORIENT subroutine by defining the material local directions. As illustrated in Figure 2.3 the
imperfection assigned in the composite material is a specific bandwidth in which the fibre ori­
entation varies gradually from the outside bandwidth (α angle) to the center of the band width
(ϕm angle). The deviation of the fibre angle misalignment is the highest in the middle of the
imperfection. The parameters describing the initial kink band geometry (b , ϕm , α , β) are
used from the ORIENT subroutine for calculating the initial local fibre direction. At the start
of the simulation ABAQUS/Standard calls the ORIENT subroutine at each material point and
utilizes the local geometry information (Sørensen et al. (2009)).

The nonlinear material behaviour of the constituents mainly due to the plastic deformation
which required for the kink band formation, render impractical a solution using implicit finite
element codes because of the instabilities that will occur in the equilibrium path. As a result
the use of nonlinear arc­length solvers are appropriate for solving nonlinear problems due to
snap­back and snap­through behaviour. ABAQUS/Standard ® provides a built­in non linear
solver called Static,Riks based on the Riks method, Riks (1979).
Despite the fact that ABAQUS offers a huge variety of material models for modeling various
material behaviours, the material modelling of such a failure would require a user­defined
constitutive law for the material. Sørensen et al. (2009) developed a 2D UMAT subroutine
(UserMATerial) written in FORTRAN, defining the material constitutive behaviour. The 2D
UMAT version used in this work was an updated version of the initially developed subroutine.
More specifically, the latest UMAT, models the material behaviour for both the constituents
as non­linear elastic and not as elastic­plastic making use of the J2 deformation theory instead
of the J2 flow theory seen in the first version. The material is also assumed to be isotropic
and incompressible. The 3D UMAT subroutine used in this work is also based on the same
principle and the final version has been developed by Skovsgård (2019). Additionally, both
theories assume von Mises yield surfaces, for which the equivalent stress is based on the sec­
ond deviatoric stress tensor invariant J2. While conventional von Mises plasticity is available
in ABAQUS as in­built capability, requiring as material input a table of true yield stress and
logarithmic plastic strains, the UMAT subroutine used in this work, enables a direct (without
tabular data) definition of the power law isotropic hardening. One can find more details re­
garding the constitutive law and its implementation in the UMAT subroutine in Sørensen et al.
(2009). The 2D UMAT subroutine uses 78 solution­dependent state variables SDV s needed
for the analysis while the 3D UMAT utilizes 320 SDV s. These are variables that are updated
as the simulation progresses. The rotation of the fibres (SDV3) and the initial direction of the
fibres (SDV4) for the 2D and (SDV7) for the 3D were investigated mostly in this work for both
2D and 3D models since the first indicates where the failure occurred during the simulation and

Numerous studies were conducted by utilizing this user­defined material model and this ideal­

directional carbon or glass reinforced composites (Sørensen et al. (2009), Skovsgård (2019),
Koutsos (2015)). The work done by Sørensen et al. (2009), focused on the effect of initial
misalignment of the fibre and the load direction. According to Sørensen et al. (2009), the
critical load is highly dependent on the maximum imperfection angle ϕm with a decrease on
the predicted peak load by increasing the sinusoidal fibre misalignment. However, the com­
pressive composite’s strength is less sensitive to the band inclination β. Sørensen et al. (2009)
also showed, that the nonlinear response of the matrix impacts the strength of the composite.
By varying the harden exponent nm from 1 (elastic) to higher values (almost perfectly plas­
tic), a significant decrease of the peak load was observed. Moreover, the angle between the
load and the fibre orientation outside the bandwidth α affects the critical load and the over­
all response. A snap­back behaviour is observed for small α angles, while peak load fades
away at the load­deformation curves for increasing the α angle. Furthermore, Sørensen et al.
(2009) highlighted the mesh size dependency with the formulated bandwidth. The localised
bandwidth is observed to be extended approximately within one element width. Moreover,
Budiansky and Fleck (1993) also state that the bandwidth is typically 10 times the fibre diame­
ter. Additionally, Koutsos (2015) investigated the matrix material properties on the prediction
of UD composite’s strength. He showed that the inferior matrix material properties extracted
from matrix testing with elevated temperature, are notably decreasing the composite’s strength
prediction.
When the local instability is modelled through the finite element code ABAQUS, the compos­
ite shows a softening response beyond the critical load by a snap­back behaviour, regardless
modelling the constituents as hardening materials. It has to be mentioned that the snap­back
response can be captured only in the finite element model simulation and be traced by using
the Riks method as solving scheme. During mechanical compression tests, the real response
of the composite is highly dynamic since sudden failure follows after the peak load is reached.

the regions undergoing elastic unloading far from the band, is larger than the axial shortening
of the band, leading to a net elongation of the composite instead of contraction. Beyond point

response. This response is seen in many other works e.g. Kyriakides et al. (1995), Guynn et

2.3.1
Approach for the material properties evaluation
Before modelling the material behaviour and predicting the compressive properties of the pro­
files numerically, mechanical tests had first to be conducted for determining the constituents
material properties and content needed as input in the numerical model. The method followed,
was to fit a hardening law equation on the experimental data and determining the Young’s
==PAGE:32==
16
2 State of the art
moduli E, the initial yield strength σy, and hardening parameter n, based on the mechanical
measurements. This parameters would contain the information for the material behaviour of
the constituents at any stress level ensuring that the numerical model would ’know’ how to
model the materials even at stress states where the samples never reached during the mechan­
ical tests.
For the matrix constituent these parameters were determined by fitting the hardening law on
tensile stress­strains curves. This is rather contradictory since the load mode of interest is
compression. However, Rosen (1965) highlighted that the shear properties of the matrix are
driving the compressive failure of the composite thus making possible to use tensile data for
the matrix. A more detailed explanation for modeling the matrix by using its behaviour under
tension and not shear is presented in a later chapter of this report.
Tensile tests were also conducted on the fibre and the whole composite individually in order to
back­calculate the fibre volume fractions by employing the Standard Rule of Mixtures (ROM).
The measured stiffness from the tensile tests of the composite was also employed to correct
the strain measurements from the compression tests that were performed on the profiles.
Regarding the fibre parameters needed as input for the modelling, the hardening law had to be
fitted on their compressive stress­strain curves as this is the load mode that they experience
when the whole composite is loaded under compression. Since the fibres cannot be tested
individually under compression, their compressive stress­strain curve was extrapolated by us­
ing the experimental compressive stress­strain curve of the composite. For that, the already
determined fibre volume fraction and the tangent modulus of the compressive curve of the com­
posite were utilised. Except from the compressive fibre curve extrapolation, the composites
were tested under compression also in order to compare their performance with the predictions
of the numerical model.

Favimat+, Germany) Kumar et al. (2020). Studies have reported a strong dependency of the
Young’s Modulus to the gauge length of the tested fibre. The testing machine crosshead’s posi­
tion performs an inaccurate approximation of the fibre strains since does not take into account
the contribution of the strain of the other components in the testing system e.g. glue, paper
tabs. Therefore, tests of various fibre gauge lengths are necessary in order to account for the
testing machine’s compliance. A more detailed explanation of how the measurements were
corrected based on the compliance of the testing machine can be found in Yang and Thomason
(2013).

cleaned, namely removing all the unnecessary stress­strain data points. Then, representative
stress­strain curves based on the tensile measurements were computed for the two carbon fibres

CHAPTER 4
Material Properties
In the current chapter, the constituent’s material properties will be analysed to determine the
material inputs needed for the numerical modelling. More specifically the curves determined
in the previous chapter from the experimental data will be further analysed to retrieve the
required information needed for the numerical models. The outcome of the tensile and the
compressive mechanical testing of the profiles is also being used for back calculating the fibre
volume fraction of the two materials and for extrapolating the compressive curves of the fibres.
Finally, the representative data are fitted based on the Ramberg­Osgood hardening law, and
the fitting parameters needed as input to the UMAT model are extracted and compared.
4.1
Matrix
As stated in a prior chapter of this report, the plastic micro buckling or kink band instability
requires plastic deformations of the matrix. The matrix plasticity controls the fibre rotation
and thus the failure of the composite under compression. The load is carried mainly by the
fibres until the point at which they start to rotate, leading to a decrease in the composite’s
strength. This point is essentially the matrix’s yielding point and as a result, the matrix has to
be modeled as elastic­plastic or nonlinear elastic in order to study the kinking instability.
It is rather unreasonable to use tensile data as material inputs while the loading model of in­
terest is compression. However, the reason for that lies in the mechanism under which the
kink band failure occurs. As Argon (1972) highlighted, for the composite to fail by kinking,
fibre misalignment has to be present. As a result, while the fibres undertake the compressive
loading, the angle that they have from the loading direction introduces shear stresses in the
interface of the matrix and the fibres. Thus, is the shear properties of the matrix that drives the
failure as initially mentioned from Rosen (1965).
Recent study performed by Zike (2015) showed that the plastic effective stress­strain relation
from experiments is in a fairly good agreement for tension and shear as shown in Figure 4.1.
Thus, this fit between the shear and tensile response renders possible the use of tensile data for
shear modelling.

Finite Element Model
In this chapter, the numerical models used in this work will be described. First, their geometry,
the material properties, the boundary conditions, and the mesh of the models will be presented.
Then, the difference between the 2D and 3D UMAT models will be validated and discussed
based on an analytical solution. It has to be mentioned that the models were not developed
in this work but they were only used and tuned accordingly for each case. The simulation
running procedure and the post­processing will be also explained. As mentioned, the finite
element models were employed through the commercial simulation software ABAQUS/Stan-
dard ®Standard.
All the simulations were held on Jess CPU cluster located at DTU Risoe and the Abaqus
jobs were submitted by using scripts written in Python programming language. Finally, the
post­processing was performed by utilizing Python as programming language.
5.1
Models
The models used can be divided into three main categories as Single−Element model, Kink
model, and Mapping model. Their primary differences are relying upon their geometry, their
mesh and their material orientation scheme. Then, each model is further divided into a 2D and a
3D version. More details regarding their differences and why they were employed in this work,
will also be presented in this chapter. It has to be mentioned that for all the simulations, the
integrated into ABAQUS Static, Riks solving method under Steps was used. The parameters
needed for the solver were tuned accordingly to the case such that to follow the equilibrium
path effectively thus avoiding numerical instabilities and acquiring steady solutions.

a stiffer overall material response when compared with the 3D model without the additional
boundary conditions since the former can expand or contract only in two directions. The co­

6.1.1
Maximum Imperfection Angle
As described in a prior chapter of this report the maximum imperfection angle ϕmax in the
middle of the initial kink band geometry, impacts considerably the numerically predicted com­
pressive properties as shown in Figure 6.2. It can be seen that by increasing the maximum
imperfection angle the predicted strength and the predicted stiffness with the same material
system are decreasing. Moreover, for angles 8° and 10° the snap­back behaviour is not present
anymore and the sharp peak of the critical load fades away. A local imperfection with so high

fibres are misaligned globally. The way stiffer regions with perfectly aligned fibres, are es­

whole geometry are uniformly not aligned with the direction of the loading. Furthermore, the
elastic energy stored in the perfectly aligned regions of the model until the critical load, is not

S and Z profiles modelling
Aim of this section is to present the compressive properties of the two pultruded profiles of
interest, predicted from the Map −L007 model. These results, are then compared with the
compressive mechanical test measurements of the two profiles.
Based on the findings of the previous section, the choice of the element size used in Map −
L007 model was crucial. The element size was chosen based on the real fibre diameter and the
fact that the bandwidth seen in the FE­model is strongly dependent on the element size. As the
bandwidth seen experimentally is typically 10 times larger than the fibre diameter (Budiansky
and Fleck (1993)), and considering an average carbon fibre diameter of 7 microns (0.007 mm),
this model is expected to simulate a more realistic bandwidth. In order to perform a straight
comparison between the numerical prediction and the measurements, the dimensions of the
model should have the same dimensions as with the tested samples. However due to numer­
ical instabilities, it was decided the Map −L007 model to be scaled down, as described in
subsection 5.1.3.

termined in chapter 4 were considered. The material parameters describing each material sys­
tem are summarized and presented in Table 6.3. As described in subsection 5.1.3, two main
mapping cases per pultruded profile, namely center and side mapping were employed. More
specifically, the slices from the X­ray scan used for the mapping, were from the center and
side scans respectively. As there were three samples per profile used for X­ray scans, the
center and the side scans used for the mapping, were from the Z01 and the S02 samples for
the Z and the S profile respectively. In each case, two slices from different positions from
each scan (center or side) were stitched side by side. In that way, it was ensured that different
fibre orientations are simulated without performing more time­consuming scans in the length
direction of each sample. Essentially, this time shortcut regarding the scans made possible a
fibre orientation mapping in a model whose length is 5 mm and ensuring at the same time that
different fibre orientations are considered throughout the whole length of the mapped region.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Undoubtedly carbon pultruded profiles are characterised as premium materials with excep­
tional properties under tension and compression compared with materials fabricated with other
manufacturing methods. As the compression strength constitutes the design limit for blade
designers when pultruded profiles are intended to be used as the main loading carrying com­
ponents, the characterisation of their compression properties is necessary. The higher volume
fibre fractions and the high degree of fibre alignment seen in pultruded profiles enhance signif­
icantly their mechanical properties. However, the repeatedly consistent and almost flawless
fabrication of such profiles, renders difficult the characterization of their compressive mechani­
cal properties through mechanical testing. As a result, numerical models are used for validating
and predicting the compressive strength and stiffness of the UD composites. By employing
numerical models, parametric studies can also be performed, having as purpose the investi­
gation of the impact of different parameters on the final predictions. This thesis makes its
contribution to better understand how a numerical model has to be employed in order to most
accurately predict the compressive behaviour of UD fibre reinforced polymer composites by
investigating two different pultruded profiles, named Z and S.
7.1
Work completed
Initially, a literature study was performed in order to first investigate which is the main failure
mechanism in UD fibre reinforced polymer composites under compression. Several studies
showed that the main failure mechanism is a local out­of­plane instability named plastic­micro
buckling or kinking instability. Pioneering works which studied the kinking instability in com­
posites showed that the plasticity of the matrix and its performance in shear together with the
initial fibre misalignment are the main factors that dominating the failure.
Then, a discussion of the numerical models that have been employed so far for predicting the
compressive behaviour by modelling the kinking instability was conducted. A novel numer­
ical model developed by Sørensen et al. (2009) is discussed by addressing the way of how
to numerically model the constituent’s material behaviour through a user­defined subroutine
UMAT and the initial fibre imperfection for initiating the kinking failure. Findings of studies
that used this model were also discussed and conclusively the matrix properties and the degree
of fibre misalignment are the main parameters that affect the numerical predictions.
==PAGE:94==
78
7 Conclusion
Data from mechanical tests were analysed in order to both compare the different candidates
and determine the material properties needed as inputs for the numerical modelling. Differ­
ent models were employed by varying their geometry, mesh, material properties, and fibre
imperfections. A comparison between the 2D and 3D UMAT models was also conducted hav­
ing as purpose the better understanding of how the plane strain condition is applied in each
model. Finally, the numerical predictions among different cases and models are presented and
compared together with the experimental results.
7.2
Mechanical tests
Data from mechanical tests on the constituents as well as the composites under investigation
were analysed and investigated. The (Ve) vinyl ester matrix system seen in the Z profile had
the best performance under tensile testing. The (Ep) epoxy used in the S profile showed infe­
rior properties, being under the (EpRoomA) epoxy. Moreover, when the same epoxy is tested
at an elevated temperature (Ep50A), its tensile properties are undermined significantly.
The single­fibre tests on the fibre showed that the carbon fibre used in the S profile was stiffer
and stronger. The composite’s tensile tests showed that the S material is stiffer mainly due to
the stiffer fibre. The compressive tests showed a similar result regarding the stiffness. How­
ever, Z profile samples were failing later compared the S composite’s specimens, primarily
due to the superior resin and the lower degree of local fibre misalignment.
7.3
Numerical predictions
The different numerical models made possible a parametric study by varying the initial fibre
imperfection angle, mesh, and fibre orientation as well as a straight comparison between the
two pultruded profiles.
First, the extensively used from prior studies 2D Kink model was employed, in order to inves­
tigate the impact of the resin properties by varying fibre imperfection angle ϕmax. As expected,
based on the theory and previous works, the resin used in the composite together with the level
of fibre misalignment impact greatly the strength predictions. The composite’s stiffness is also
undermined from both parameters but is mostly affected by the fibre misorientation. An inter­
esting conclusion is that the low quality resins increased the deviation of the predicted strength
between the different ϕmax angles. The resin measured in elevated temperature decreased the
composite’s strength considerably, highlighting the tremendous impact of the temperature on
the composite’s performance under compression.
==PAGE:95==
7.4 Future work
79
Then, Mapping models considering the real fibre orientations of the two pultruded profiles
were employed. The resin properties affect with the same way the strength and the stiffness
prediction of the composite when the real and same fibre orientation is considered. However,
the model with the denser mesh under­predicts the strength and the stiffness compared with the
coarser mesh model. The same is observed when the sinusoidal fibre imperfection is taken into
account in the 2D kink model by decreasing to half the element size. Nevertheless, this under­
prediction is way smaller than the real fibre orientation. However, this smaller difference has
to be validated when similar element sizes are considered on the model with the sinusoidal fi­
bre imperfection, in order to investigate if the smaller difference is due to the fibre orientation
scheme or the absolute element size.
Taking into account this difference, the final numerical predictions were made with a model
where its element size would simulate more realistically the formed bandwidth. So the Map−
L007 model was employed by using the constituent’s material properties and the back calcu­
lated fibre volume fraction seen in the two profiles. By that, a ’clearer’ comparison to the
experimental findings and the numerical prediction was possible. The numerical predictions
were in very good agreement with the experimental results in all the mapping cases, namely
center, and side mapping. Overall, the models with the fibre mapping from the center scans
predicted a stronger and stiffer material, since the level of the fibre misalignment was lower.
The stiffness of the S profile was found to be higher compared with the Z profile, due to the
stiffer fibre, regardless of the superior properties of the Z profile’s resin. However, the strength
of the Z profile was higher compared with the S profile, both when it was mechanically tested
and numerically simulated, mainly due to the superior matrix properties.
Finally, the numerical predictions from the 3D Kink model by assuming a ϕmax angle which
was determined based on the strength predictions of Map −L007 model showed a less stiff
response overall. This was rather expected since the 3D Kink model can expand in all direc­
tions.
7.4
Future work
The numerical models used in this thesis can be further used and developed for studying the
compression behaviour of UD fibre reinforced composites. More specifically, a 2D Mapping
model with dimensions equal to the average dimensions of the samples used in experimental
tests can be developed. As the predicted strength is sensitive to the element size and the fibre
distributions, a model with real sample dimensions and with fibre orientations from the whole
sample would undoubtedly simulate the experimental conditions more realistically. Another
study can be done by developing a 2D mapping model by considering the same boundary con­
ditions with the experiments. In that way, the effect of the friction in the interface between
the sample and the clamping devices could be taken into account in the numerical model sim­
ulating the experimental conditions more accurately. Moreover, a study by varying in a large
==PAGE:96==
80
7 Conclusion
range the element size on the 2D kink model could also be possible in order to show how
much the strength prediction is affected by changing this parameter. Of course, this would be
very interesting to be compared with a similar study on the 2D Mapping model. However,
the latter would require different mapping information between the element size cases.
Even if the kink band failure can be modelled by considering plane strain condition, a 3D
Mapping model by assuming the same real fibre orientation in the width direction would
simulate and predict more realistic results. So an investigation of a 3D model with real fibre
misalignment could be considered as a future study.
Finally, further investigation and validation can be made on the 2D and 3D UMAT models by
tuning the 3D UMAT model, in such a way, where the plane strain condition (when considered)
is applied independently on the fibre and the matrix as with the 2D UMAT model, enabling a
more fair comparison between the two models predictions.
