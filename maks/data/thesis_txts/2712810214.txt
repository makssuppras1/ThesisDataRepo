Abstract
In this thesis, the prospect of frequency conversion of single-photons in a fiber is explored.
After establishing the theoretical basis for such a conversion scheme based on four-wave mixing
in the Bragg-scattering configuration, an experimental implementation and characterization
of classical four-wave mixing in a highly nonlinear fiber is carried out. We demonstrate the
construction of a heralded single-photon source with low degree of second order quantum
coherence, g(2)
H = 0.08, and ultra-high coincidence-to-accidental ratio of 1119.36 based on de-
generate four-wave mixing in a silicon straight waveguide. Finally, we integrate the heralded
single-photon source into a frequency conversion scheme in the attempt to spectrally trans-
late quantum states within the telecom C-band. In this, we outline and investigate how to
overcome central challenges in single photon frequency conversion in fibers; such as temporal
alignment and polarization control of single photons, broadband noise from fiber-based optical
amplifiers, and the issue of spontaneous Raman scattering.

Chapter 1
Introduction and motivation
At the turn of the 20th century, physicists trying to describe black body radiation stumbled
upon the foundations of quantum mechanics which would revolutionize our understanding
of the foundations of nature. Although at times counter-intuitive, confusing, and seemingly
paradoxical, quantum mechanics has stood the test of time and countless experimental trials
and provided the world with technologies like the transistor and laser. This has allowed light-
speed global communication and launched the world into the information age. All of this falls
under what has been called "the first quantum revolution". Now, we are fast approaching "the
second quantum revolution" (some would say we are living in it) where individual quantum
systems can be more easily engineered and precisely controlled. Even so, many of the anti-
cipated technologies that we expect to emerge as a result of increasingly efficient quantum
manipulation techniques have yet to come to full fruition. Quantum computing, and quantum
information technology (including quantum cryptography and communication) is the center
of much interest both from mainstream media, researchers all over the world, and increasingly
from national governments. The recent announcement from NATO of the creation of a center
for quantum technology here in Denmark as part of its defense strategy is a salient example
of this[1].
Photons - single quanta of excitation in the electromagnetic field - are obvious candidates
upon which to build a quantum system since they exhibit many desirable properties: chiefly
their weak interaction with their environment and with other photons. The vast amount of
technology and infrastructure that research within photonics has spawned in the 60 years
since the invention of the laser[2] is of course also a boon.
Indeed, the ability to create,
manipulate, detect and transmit classical1 light over long distances forms the backbone of
the modern information society. However, there are naturally some challenges that must be
overcome as we try to expand this existing infrastructure to include quantum communication

networks based on single (or few) photons.
1.1
Producing and transmitting single photons
In a sense, producing single photons is easy.
Producing exclusively single photons is the
hard part. In recent years, various schemes have been envisioned and implemented for the
production and manipulation of single photons. Currently, there are many different systems
being used as sources of single photons - including ion traps, quantum dots, diamond defects,
and atoms.
Sources like these rely on the excitation and subsequent relaxation of single
emitters.
Of particular interest in areas such as quantum cryptography[3, 4] are schemes
that involve the use of correlated pairs of single photons such that one photon can be used
to herald the existence of another[5]. Sources that emit pairs of photons can be realized via
nonlinear optical processes. Optical crystals exhibiting second order non-linearities have seen
extensive use in sources of correlated photon pairs as these pairs are produced in second order
processes such as spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC)[6]. However, the sources
so-far mentioned share one key draw-back in that they typically operate in the 550 - 780
nm range [7]. Although higher wavelength sources have been realized based both on second
order processes[5, 8] and single emitters[9], the fact remains that most mature technology for
processing, detection, and generation of single photon states is found to operate in or close
to the visible spectrum - especially for deterministic sources[10]. This poses an issue for the
transmission over long distances of the generated quantum states as visible light does not
benefit from the low transmission loss in optical fibers found in the telecom bands (1260 nm
- 1625 nm).
One potential solution to this issue is to frequency convert the quantum states pre- and post
transmission; translating the single photon frequency to the telecom band before coupling into
the transmission line (down-conversion) and then shifting back at the end of the transmission
line (up-conversion) before further processing/detection. Up-converting before detection is
especially desirable, as most photon detectors which operate in the C-band (one of the telecom
bands) suffer from higher noise and lower sensitivity than those used to detect the visible
spectrum[11, 12].
Such up-conversion can be achieved in second order materials like the
aforementioned optical crystals, but the optical power required to pump the process is a
limiting factor - multiple watt laser fields are usually needed to demonstrate high conversion
efficiency[12].
A promising alternative is to utilize higher-order nonlinear effects such as
the third order effect of four-wave mixing (FWM). These third order effects naturally occur
in fibers and thus conversion steps that exploit them are easily integrated into fiber-based
transmission lines. Specifically, the effect known as Bragg-scattering (BS) FWM is especially
well-suited for frequency conversion since - in contrast to other FWM effects (parametric
amplification) - it does not produce excess noise in the frequency converted output[13, 14].

Utilizing BS-FWM in a highly nonlinear fiber (HNLF), single photon conversion efficiencies
upwards of 94% have been reported recently for signals in the O-band (1260 nm - 1360 nm)[6].
The efficiency of FWM is contingent on achieving good phase matching between the constitu-
ent fields taking part in the nonlinear interaction. This has traditionally made it impractical
to perform conversion across wide frequency ranges, but improvements in the fabrication of
fibers that allow stable propagation of higher order optical modes has sparked new (or rather
renewed) interest in the field of intermodal nonlinear effects [16, 15, 17] - one component of
which is the facilitation of phase matching across unprecedented wavelength ranges afforded
by the modal degree of freedom. While it remains a challenging task to achieve phase match-
ing for long-range frequency conversion, intermodal BS-FWM across more modest frequency
ranges has been experimentally demonstrated repeatedly within the last decade[19, 18].
1.2
On the scope of this project
The previous section contextualizes the scope of this Master’s thesis project. Our aim is to
start on the path towards long-range frequency conversion of single photons using intermodal
BS-FWM and like you do with any journey, we begin with the first step. In our case, this
first step takes the form of short-range single photon frequency conversion within the telecom
C-band (1530nm - 1565 nm) in a single-moded (SM) scheme. To the best of our knowledge,
this feat has yet to be experimentally demonstrated.
In attempting to achieve this, our
experimental work is comprised of 3 main components: building a suitable setup to facilitate
frequency conversion via BS-FWM, setting up a single photon source, and finally combining
the two prior components in an attempt to detect converted single photons. Our frequency
conversion scheme will be based on BS-FWM in a HNLF. The single-photon source will
be realized in the heralded fashion discussed previously, however it will actually be tailored
for generation of photon pairs within the C-band.
This is made possible by utilizing the
third-order effect of spontaneous degenerate four-wave mixing (SpFWM) in a straight silicon
waveguide - a method of producing telecom-compatible single photons that has also seen a
lot of research focus within recent years[20, 21, 22].

In classical four-wave mixing (FWM), we usually consider the generation or amplification of
light at a specific frequency due to the interaction of three other frequency components of an
electric field described by the third order susceptibility of some nonlinear material, i.e. the
process encompasses four frequency components in total (hence the name). A distinction is
usually made between degenerate (also termed "modulation instability"[23] or "modulation
interaction"[24]) FWM in which two of the four frequency components are identical, and

2.1
Non-degenerate four-wave mixing
In non-degenerate FWM, the interaction between frequency components oscillating at the
idler frequency (ωi), the signal frequency (ωs), and the two pump frequencies (ωp and ωq)
is treated in two separate cases: phase conjugation (PC) and Bragg scattering (BS). In the
former, energy is transferred from the two pump fields to the signal and idler fields whereas in
Bragg scattering, the energy transfer is between respectively one of the pumps together with
the signal field and the other pump together with the idler field. A further delineation splits
the PC case in respectively the outer-band and inner-band configurations, and the Bragg
scattering case into the nearby frequency and distant frequency BS configuration[25]. All 4
configurations are shown schematically in fig. 2.1.
When it comes to frequency conversion, BS FWM offers the considerable advantage over PC
FWM that noise in the pump field is not mapped to the generated signal (idler) field, since
the creation of a signal (idler) photon is linked to the annihilation of an idler (signal) photon
- and not directly to the annihilation of any pump photon1. In other words, the BS FWM
dynamics entail energy flow between signal and idler and between the two pumps. Thus,
any excess noise in the amplified pump fields is isolated from the generated signal (idler)[13].
Hence, we will be utilising BS FWM for the frequency conversion in this project.

2.1.5
Phase matching
The phase mismatch is arguably the most important parameter in achieving efficient FWM,
so an in-depth understanding of the fundamental physical mechanisms and how to manip-
ulate them to decrease the phase mismatch is necessary. The total phase mismatch can be
represented as
κ ≈∆βM + ∆βW + κNL,
(2.1.35)
where the first two terms on the right hand side represent the phase-mismatch due to material
dispersion and waveguide dispersion respectively. These two terms are often combined into
one term called the linear phase-mismatch, denoted ∆β in previous sections, however denoting
it as a sum of two separate effects is only approximately accurate since it ignores cross terms.
For a given fiber, it is impossible to distinguish exactly how much of the observed dispersion
at a given wavelength is due to the material and how much is due to waveguide design,
which is why the two terms are often combined. However, for standard single mode fiber
designs ∆βW is typically quite small compared to ∆βM except near the ZDW where they
are of comparable size[31]. A total of zero phase-mismatch can be obtained if one of the
three contributing terms in eq. (2.1.35) have opposite sign compared to the others. There
are various schemes for realizing this. By using a multi-mode fiber for instance, it is possible
22 of 126
==PAGE:28==
Chapter 2. Classical four-wave mixing
to make ∆βW negative through a combination of higher order and fundamental modes since
higher order modes have a lower effective refractive index [32]. The modal dependency of ∆β
is the main feature that makes inter-modal FWM viable for long-range frequency conversion.
In single mode fibers there are still plenty of options for obtaining a low phase mismatch. ∆βM
and ∆βW are strongly frequency dependent so placing all fields closely together in frequency
minimizes the linear phase mismatch. This in conjunction with equal (or low) pump powers
(which minimizes the non-linear phase mismatch) is one way of achieving low total phase
mismatch.
It is also possible to work in either a normal or anomalous GVD regime and
counteract a nonzero linear mismatch, ∆βM + ∆βW ̸= 0, by judiciously choosing the input
pump powers to tune the magnitude and sign of the nonlinear mismatch, κNL = γ(Pp −Pq),

birefringence. Lastly, its possible to operate near the ZDW so that ∆βM and ∆βW are of

Since we will be working with amplified narrow-width lasers1 to pump nonlinear processes
in a 500 m PM HNLF, a characterization of the stimulated Brillouin scattering (SBS) in the
fiber is in order. SBS is a process by which an incident light wave (the pump) incites an
acoustic phonon wave in the fiber (via electrostriction) and is then in turn scattered by the
phonon. The frequency of the scattered wave (the Stokes wave) obeys the energy conservation
requirement:
ωs = ωp −ων,
where ωs is the Stokes wave (angular) frequency, ωp is the corresponding pump frequency and
ων is the angular acoustic phonon frequency. The corresponding phase matching condition
(or momentum conservation) is given by:
⃗βs = ⃗βp −⃗kν

In a fiber, the wave vectors are limited to the forwards and backwards direction (parallel to
the fiber axis), i.e. ⃗β = βˆez and ⃗kν = kνˆez. In a dispersion diagram, the acoustic phonon
line (ων ≈vνkν) lies everywhere to the right of the light line of the pump (ωp ≈cβp) since
the phonon velocity is much slower than the speed of light in the medium (vν ≪c). For this
reason, phase matching for SBS in a fiber can only be achieved for βp = −βs. In other words,
the SBS only occurs in the backscattered direction. It thus serves to lower the fraction of
useful pump power in the fiber and essentially imposes a limit on the input power that can
effectively be utilized to drive the BS-FWM frequency conversion.[41]

absolute power difference between the input and backscattered light.
For this reason, we
define the SBS threshold as the crossing of the line defined by the linear (Rayleigh) part of
the backscattered light and the exponential (small signal SBS) part (indicated in fig. 4.8).
This definition gives us an SBS threshold of 20.65 dBm. This definition refers directly to the
way we measure input power in our experiments.
There are many SBS threshold manipulation techniques which are highly effective and could
be implemented to achieve higher conversion efficiency. It is apparent from fig. 4.8 that SBS
certainly is a limiting factor in achieving higher conversion efficiency as it starts to rapidly
increase already at 20.5 dBm input power corresponding to 23.5 dBm EDFA output power.
The EDFA can output up to 33 dBm but much of the additional power would simply be
backscattered without implementing additional SBS compensation mechanisms.
The first
fitted linear relation in fig. 4.8 indicates the low power regime where Rayleigh scattering dom-
inates the backscattered power, which is expected to be linear in input power corresponding
to a slope of 1 in a loglog plot. At 20.5 dBm input power, the backscattered power begins
to be dominated by the SBS before reaching a saturation regime in which the input pump is
significantly depleted by SBS. The SBS power is expected to increase asymptotically towards
a theoretical max as seen in early SBS papers such as [41].

single photon regime.
The silicon strip waveguide in the chip is not in itself a single photon source as it produces
photon pairs. However, upon detection of one photon from the SpFWM process of frequency ωi
in one output arm, we are immediately made aware of the existence of another single photon
at frequency ωs = 2ωp −ωi in the other output arm. The procedure is called "heralding"
and the photon emitter a "heralded" single photon source. In the measurement of the second
order quantum coherence function at zero time delay, g(2)(0), a third InGaAs/InP free-running
detector (of type IDQ ID220) with a dead time of 25 µs and quantum efficiency of 20% is
employed in the idler arm to act as a heralding device. This is detector DH in fig. 5.1. The
signal arm is split using a 3 dB coupler with each of the two outputs going to one of the two
ID230 devices - detector D1 and D2 in fig. 5.1 respectively. Thus, we have a heralded HBT
setup in which all detection events in the two HBT detectors (D1, D2) are discarded unless
accompanied by a simultaneous detection event in the heralding detector (DH). As mentioned
previously, the detector jitter is on the order of a few hundred picoseconds. Compared to this,
the potential temporal walk-off in the straight silicon waveguide due to the difference in
group velocities between the signal and idler is around 1 ps[48]. Equivalently, the signal-
idler coherence time, τ ∼λ2

to select the signal and idler frequencies (fig. 5.5). Thus, by choosing a coincidence window
of 2.5 ns we should more than ensure that we are not discarding any heralded single photons
due to the uncertainty in their arrival times at each detector.

Note on two-photon absorption in silicon
Figure 7.4b shows a deviation from the respective fitted polynomial scaling of both the co-
incident and accidental counts for high input pump powers going to the chip which was not
clearly present in measurements performed in the previous SPS setup (chapter 5). One might
expect nonlinear loss in the silicon to be the explanation, since the deviation is observed at
higher input powers. The dominant nonlinear loss mechanism would be two-photon absorp-
tion (TPA) in the silicon material. TPA is a process by which an electronic transition is
induced in the waveguide across the silicon band gap by the simultaneous absorption of two
pump photons at a frequency that satisfies: 2ℏωp > Eg (where Eg = 1.12 eV is the silicon
band gap). This condition is satisfied for the pump wavelength we are using to drive the
SpFWM in the silicon waveguide. Being a nonlinear process (involving the simultaneous ab-
sorption of two photons that must collide spatially and temporally), the loss due to TPA can
be described as1:
dI
dz ∼−βI2,
where I is the optical intensity of the pump. In [53], it is suggested that TPA in silicon at
wavelengths of 1.55 µm is given by a loss coefficient of β ≈7 · 10−10 cm/W. Other sources
suggest slightly different values - for example β ≈4 · 10−10 cm/W (at 1560 nm) is suggested
in [54]. Ideally, the coefficient for our specific waveguide should be estimated by tracking the
ratio of input to output pump power, Pin/Pout, for different input pump powers (a procedure
outlined in [54]). However, such a measurement was not done for the silicon chip we used.
Regardless, using the highest of the two suggested values of the coefficient ([53]) and assuming
an effective mode area of Aeff ≈0.06 µm2 (based on results in [55]), we find that the input
power necessary for the nonlinear loss to reach 1% of the pump is Pp > 28 mW. Hence, we do
not expect this loss mechanism to account for the observed deviation of the quadratic scaling
in Ncc seen in fig. 7.4b for input powers Pp > 2 mW.

7.2
Intrapulse spontaneous Raman scattering
In chapter 6, we discussed how the rate of Raman photons that are spontaneously generated
by the pumps in the HNLF would saturate the APDs under CW operation. This can be
somewhat alleviated by switching to pulsed pumps that are triggered by the detection of a
herald photon at one of the outputs from the SPS. This can ensure that the photon rate at the
APDs is below saturation, however it will not reduce the rate of intra-pulse Raman scattering
that occurs when the pumps are on. In fact, if the pulses obtain a peak power higher than
their CW equivalent, the peak rate of Raman scattering will be higher as well. For this reason,
we still expect most of the intra-pulse noise photons counted at the HNLF output to be due
to intra-pulse SpRS. The following is an outline of the results of measurements that were
performed to rule out other candidates for noise production.
There are a plethora of alternative processes that could yield noise which would be indistin-
guishable from Raman photons if not systematically ruled out. The longpass filters allow for
much of the ASE from the EDFA through (above their filtering wavelength), including a lar-
ger "bump" in the spectral power density near the pump wavelengths and a long tail of lower
spectral power density at higher wavelengths. This tail could act as the seed for degenerate
FWM with either of the pumps to generate photons at the idler/signal frequency. It could
also seed a phase conjugation scheme but this can be quickly ruled out by turning off one
of the pumps. Since the seeded degenerate FWM requires energy conservation, its impact in
the form of noise photons at the idler wavelengths would be drastically reduced by filtering
the ASE spectrum ∼20 nm above the pump wavelengths before the HNLF (since it is this
region of the ASE that would contribute to noise at the idler wavelength due to seeded degen-
erate FWM). Such filtering can be done with a TBF centered around the pump wavelengths.
This was tested and yielded no decrease in raw detection counts or in the coincidence-time
histogram peak counts - aside from the decrease associated simple insertion loss in the filter.
Another potential source of noise is cascaded FWM. When the holding beam is removed,
the pump pulses reach around 29 dBm peak power exiting the EDFA which yields strong
cascaded FWM, as evident in figures like 7.18b. These cascaded peaks would cause noise
issues if the spectral separation of the pumps is an integer times the spectral distance to the
signal/idler. The spectral separation (and thereby central frequencies) of the cascaded peaks
can be changed by changing the wavelength of one of the pumps or turning one of them
off. Both these things were tested and neither yielded any noticeable difference in the noise
counts. Figure 7.7 shows both the coincidence-time histograms and OSA spectra with all
permutations of the lasers being on/off. The slight difference in the peak bin count in the the
coincidence-time histogram is due to the EDFA amplifying slightly differently depending on
if the lasers are turned on together or independently. The EDFA ASE emission peak around
1560 nm also changes slightly (fig. 7.7b) depending on if the pumps are turned on. This also

Any APD coincidence-time histogram measured over a period of time is a series of superim-
posed single detection events where the final count rate is a reflection of the probability of
detecting a photon at a certain time delay. This means that a rising edge of a powerful pulse
can contain more photons than the entirety of a weaker pulse and therefore blind the detector
to any surplus photons. The APD is not a power meter and cannot determine the difference
between a pulse containing 100 or 1000 photons unless averaging over an absurd amount of
time since one of the first few photons will with increasing likelihood be the photon to trigger
the detection and thereby with a very high probability blind the detector to all the remaining
photons. This can deceptively move what appears to be the pulse center onto the rising edge
of the pulse. To accurately determine the center of the pulse then, it’s required to operate in
a regime where the photons per pulse are low enough that an ensemble average traces out all
parts of the pulse.
The reason the powerful pulse appears to decrease even while increasing in power is again due
to detector deadtime which decreases the likelihood of determining a photon on the ’back’
side of the pulse. This poses a very large issue for the purposes of detecting an idler peak,
when large amounts of Raman noise is present which is most clearly seen in fig. 7.18a. The
signal peak which appears around -7435 ns is gradually pushed from being at the front of
the pump pulse to behind the apparent peak of the higher power peaks. The idler photons
will therefore be essentially invisible to the APD since so many Raman photons arrive at
the detector first. The signal can be placed on the front of the rising edge so that it was is
detectable for higher powers but the potential conversion efficiency would suffer as a result of
the much lower optical intensity experienced by the signal at the edge of the pulse.

7.8

In chapter 6, it was estimated that cooling the PM HNLF to 77 K could ideally yield an

noise photons we observe at the HNLF output at the idler frequency. To test to what extent
the observed noise photons are due to anti-Stokes SpRS - and to what extent their presence
can be reduced by cooling - we tracked the evolution of the idler frequency noise counts as
the PM HNLF was cooled in liquid nitrogen. Figure 7.23 shows the impact of cooling as
expressed in the herald-idler output coincidence-time histogram. The observed reduction in
the measurable noise photon peak is less than theoretically predicted 8 dB. The reduction of
the peak value seen in the "raw" data (fig. 7.23a) is ∼600/200 ≈4.5 dB. However, due to
the peak-distorting effect brought on by APD saturation illustrated in fig. 7.19, we propose
that the extrapolated pulse shapes seen in fig. 7.23b are a more accurate representation of the
magnitude of the noise photon rate at the output detector. Using instead the extrapolated
peak value, we get an estimated 4.8 dB reduction of the peak coincidence rate by cooling to
77 K.

results in a slight deviation in the propagation constants of the constituent fields of the FWM

To account for this shift, a classical characterization like the one detailed in chapter 4 should
be performed for the cooled fiber to ensure optimal conversion efficiency. Even so, we suggest
that optimization of the noise performance of the FC setup take primacy over fine-tuning the
conversion efficiency in a noisy scheme. If some of the remaining noise photons are also caused
by SpRS in the HNLF, a possible avenue for further noise reduction is cooling with liquid
helium. The reduction of phonon occupancy at 4 K (liquid helium temperature) compared to
room temperature is 125 dB. Liquid helium is however more expensive and harder to handle
since it requires pressurized systems to contain and transfer the fluid.

Discussion
Throughout the project, various challenges have been highlighted that need to be overcome in
order to tackle the experimental demonstration of frequency conversion within the C-band in
the single-photon regime. The considerable difference in performance between the iterations
of the heralded single-photon source showcased the importance of filtering/noise suppression
and loss reduction. Improving out-coupling efficiency from the chip led to a direct increase in
the coincident detection rate. In our case, this did not come at the cost of a higher accidental
detection rate since the ratio of bi-photons to noise photons was improved by further spectral
filtering. Comparing fig. 5.9 and fig. 7.4a, we see how this allowed us to reach a record high
CAR and decent heralded single photon generation rates. We stress that the difference in
the two setups was not just a 4 dB increase in coupling efficiency but an increase in SPS
side band rejection from -120 dB to -130 dB - that extra order of magnitude in the filtering
matters. This ties very closely to the issue of loss in the SPS output arms. While our SPS
has a high ratio of coincident to accidental counts, its heralding efficiency is low and peaks
at 0.00035 for an SPS input pump power of 2.8 mW. This is a direct consequence of the high
losses associated with the necessary filtering of the SPS output. When incorporating the SPS
in a single photon processing scheme like FC using classical pumps, the loss of heralded signal
photons becomes detrimental to the SNR of the processed output since it leads directly to
time-correlated noise from the classical pumps that are triggered by the herald. Similarly,
the loss of a herald photon entails a loss of signal - although this effect can be somewhat
overcome by measuring for a longer time since it does not directly produce noise in the
output. The high losses reported for the signal and herald SPS output arms (22.65 dB and
22.43 dB respectively) were necessary to produce a heralded source with good single photon
performance as regards the usual metrics (CAR, g(2)). However, even though we retained
some tunability in the signal photon and pump noise SNR in the frequency conversion scheme
(see section 7.5), these aforementioned losses proved a large obstacle to detecting converted
idler photons in the FC scheme. A high CAR is not sufficient as a sole figure of merit for a

further effort into minimising spurious FWM and Raman scattering processes should be made.

this comes with its own set of additional challenges. It might also be beneficial to deviate
from the co-polarized scheme that has been attempted in this project to reduce the coupling
of the pump fields to the part of the Raman-scattered anti-Stokes field that shares the same
polarization state as the idler. A polarizer can then be used to filter cross-polarized noise

efficiency and might require more power in the pump fields.

itself it is probably fruitful (and less costly) to optimise principally on the first two issues (SPS

Chapter 9
Conclusion and outlook
We have introduced the basic theory of and concepts in classical FWM including our own
model for FWM in specifically the BS-FWM configuration. We have shown how some of
those classical principles extend into the quantum mechanical domain to form the theoretical
foundation of the SP FC we sought to demonstrate experimentally. In the effort to perform
quantum mechanical frequency translation, a classical characterization of several highly non-
linear fibers has been carried out. In the characterization, we extracted dispersion properties
and the Green’s function for frequency conversion through measurements based on the theory
of FWM and we demonstrated conversion efficiencies in a classical regime of up to 10% for
pump powers as low as 19.5 dBm. This is promising for SP FC with fields that are closely
spaced spectrally, since it has proven crucial to obtain high conversion efficiency at low pump
power to perform this feat.
We have successfully constructed a setup for a heralded source emitting single photon pairs
in the C-band based on SpFWM in silicon. In this, we have demonstrated how sufficient
spectral filtering on the SPS output can result in a high CAR of 1119.36 when operating in
the heralded single photon regime with a low second order quantum coherence, g(2)
H < 0.08.
When integrating the SPS setup with the setup for BS-FWM frequency conversion, we have
outlined the importance of combining the right set of spectral filters for sufficient suppression
of noise even outside the intended operational frequency band of the setup. The emphasis on
filtering has proven a recurring theme whenever single photon detection was involved. In the
attempt to reduce continuous background noise by employing quasi-CW pulsed pump sources,
it was shown how heralded single photons can be timed to co-propagate with classical pumps
in a coincident SPD detection scheme. In a similar way, we have illustrated how time-of-flight
differences can be evaluated by the construction of a coincidence-time histogram to facilitate
the fine tuning of single photon polarization in a PM HNLF.
Despite such seemingly positive results, the nearby frequency conversion of single photons in
the C-band has proven elusive. Operating with signals that are closely spaced in frequency
104
==PAGE:110==

(and close to the ZDW of our PM HNLF), our frequency translation attempt has been accom-
panied by a range of noise sources as well as competing nonlinear processes taking place in the
fiber. Although our SPS seems to be the silicon straight waveguide based photon-pair source
with the highest reported CAR, its construction has come with some drawbacks that make
it a less ideal candidate for the purposes of demonstrating SP FC (the overarching purpose
of the project). Chief among these are the high coupling losses which have proven to be a
fundamentally limiting factor in the usefulness of the SPS for fiber based frequency conver-
sion - made only worse by the filtering losses suffered to suppress the pump that is spectrally
adjacent to the SPS output. We made efforts to cool the PM HNLF in liquid nitrogen in
order to reduce SpRS photons at the FC output. This proved partially successful but was
not a one-and-done solution to eliminate all background counts. The pervasive noise coupled
with the low heralding efficiency of our SPS meant we were unable to definitively demon-
strate the frequency conversion of single photons. The slightly disproportionate irregularity
we observed in the coincidence-time histogram for the idler output SPD and herald SPD for
signal photons polarized along the fast axis could be due to frequency converted photons, but
a measurement in a regime with much less noise (or conversely much longer integration time)
would be needed to confidently determine whether detectable SP FC occurred.
Going forward, we suggest that for future attempts at demonstrating single photon frequency
conversion in the C-band, emphasis is put on incorporating a source that accommodates a
coupling mechanism with higher coupling efficiency. Although it eluded us, better coupling is
definitely attainable for on-chip silicon waveguides[56]. SPS based on SpFWM in fibers might
also become a viable low-loss option in the future but it seems to be plagued by the same
problem of SpRS that we have encountered in this project [57][58]. Alternatively, photon-pair
generation through spontaneous parametric down-conversion in χ(2) crystals (such as period-
ically poled lithium-niobate) has been used in SPS schemes with CAR- and g(2)-values similar
to ours but with heralding efficiences of upwards of 4% (two orders of magnitude better than
for our SPS)[6]. The caveat is of course that such SPDC sources do not produce photons
in the C-band "out-of-the-box". Although often significantly more cumbersome to set up1,
there is also the option of using SPS based on single emitters such as atoms, quantum dots
or colour centers[59]. These emitters usually entail photon generation close to the visible
spectrum, but SP generation in the C-band has also been reported within recent years[60].
Moving from the source side to the detection side, we note that photon counting with su-
perconducting nanowire single-photon detectors (SNSPDs) - which can have have dead times
of few nanoseconds - would allow multiple photons per pulse to be detected with quantum
efficiencies of over 80%. Combined with low SNSPD timing jitter (which can be below 25 ps),
this might allow for the further rejection through post selection of a lot of the noise that has
degraded the results in this project.[61] Improvements on the aspects mentioned above might
finally allow for the conclusive demonstration of frequency conversion of single photons in the
