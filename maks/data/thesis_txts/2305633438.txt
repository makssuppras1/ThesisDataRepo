Abstract
As with other manufacturing processes, modern casting is aiming at higher
precision. To this, establishing a reliable measurement procedure for di-
mensional measurements on green sand molds is a prerequisite for analysis
of geometric deviations in mass production of quality castings. The surface
of the green sand mold is soft and not suitable for dimensional control via
tactile Coordinate Measuring Machine (CMM) which is well-known, estab-
lished and traceable to the meter unit.
Optical 3D systems are used increasingly for dimensional metrology with-
out damaging the surface but suﬀer from the lack of international standards
as well as reference artifacts that make it complicated to establish trace-
ability of their measurements and compare them to tactile instruments, in
particular when the object is sand.
In this project, three reference objects were developed for performance char-
acterization of the optical 3D scanner. Namely, Sand cylinder, which was
presented as metrological approach for length measurement on green sand
molds using a 3D-optical scanner with fringe projection. A new sand sam-
ple was developed with a hard binder to withstand the contact force of
a touch probe while keeping optical cooperativeness similar to green sand.
The sample was calibrated on a dial gage set-up and implemented in optical
measurements. In order to determine the uncertainty of optical measure-
ments, the procedure of using calibrated workpieces (ISO 15530-3) was used.
Results conﬁrm that the uncertainty of the optical scanner on the substi-
tuted hard sample is similar to that of the soft sample, so the hard sample
can successfully represent the soft sample in the ISO 15530-3 procedure.
Another reference object were step-heights (Step sand cylinder) ranging
from 0.1 to 5.0 mm, were made on six customized sand samples with an av-
erage grain size of 230 µm. The samples were produced using a hard binder
that can be measured both by tactile and optical probes. The step-heights
were calibrated using a CMM with ø8 mm tactile probe.
A 3D-optical
scanner using fringe pattern was also used to measure the step heights re-
sulting in a point cloud for each scanning. From this investigation it can

be concluded that CMM cannot be used to ensure traceability of optical
measurements on this type of surface.
The cone plate is the third reference object developed in relation to the
metrological performance characterization of optical 3D scanning. The ob-
ject features a 5x5 array of 25 conical holes, which are formed on a furan
sand. The general concept of the cone plate is similar to the ball plates
and hole plates used in classical coordinate metrology. The calibration of
the cone plate was carried out using tactile CMM and used for performance

In addition, the inﬂuence of the scanning factors on the optical 3D scan-
ner by using a commercial 3D optical scanner (ATOSIII) was investigated.
The eﬀects of some scanning factors aﬀecting the measurement process were

eﬀect on how accurate scanner represented the surface of the object.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter provides a brief description of the general research area and
how to accommodate discovered demands with existing new technologies.
Moreover, the problem deﬁnition and project structure are presented.
1.1
Description of the general research areas
Metrology is the backbone of precision manufacturing. A measurement is
said to be more accurate when it oﬀers a smaller measurement error. Ac-
cording to ISO/IEC Guide 99 [1] the deviation from the true value is com-
posed of random and systematic errors. The two kinds of errors assumed to
be always distinguishable, have to be treated diﬀerently. Dimensional accu-
racy of cast parts has been in focus for many years [2]. Dimensional errors
in cast parts may originate from diﬀerent areas. The systematic deviations
are mainly considered in the pattern design phase, which is due to con-
traction during solidiﬁcation [3]. There may be some systematic diﬀerences
between the horizontal and vertical arrangement of cavities of a pattern [4].
Random error of measurement is the component of measurement error that
in replicate measurements vary in an unpredictable manner [1]. A factor
causing the dimensional errors of the cast part is the sand mold geometry
(cavity). The green sand used in most mass production casting lines is a
mixture of sand, water and Bentonite. Typically, mass production casting
factory uses a machine similar to the DISAMATIC®, which can be seen
in ﬁgure 1.1 [5]. Thermal distortion of sand due to contact with the melt
can be critical for the production of near-net-shape castings with even more
requirements of stringent casting dimensional reproducibility [6]. Forces ex-
erted on the sand mold by melt during the cooling phase can also be the
main reason for dimensional errors [7]. In the literature, it is customary

to discuss the dimensional deviation of the cast parts and the melt-mold
interaction, rather than focusing on the dimensional measurement of the
sand mold itself.
Figure 1.1: A DISAMATIC® casting machine.
The novelty of the current work is to establish a dimensional measure-
ment system, which can be directly applied to sand molds before pouring.
This leads to a rather new ﬁeld for analysis of dimensional deviations of
cast parts. However, there are certainly some challenges to open up this
ﬁeld in a satisfactory way.
The fundamental steps of a DISAMATIC® process can be seen in ﬁgure
1.2. It is a highly automated process developed in 1957, which is still the
dominant method used for the sand casting today. To put it brieﬂy, the
process starts by compacting sand so that it takes on the geometry of the
mold. It is important to note that the mold cavity is split between the front
side and the back side of the sand mold. Then, the mold is pushed out of
the machine and aligned with identical sand molds. The mold cavity on the
two neighboring sand molds makes up the complete mold cavity where the
melt is then poured into to create the cast part.
The surface of a green sand mold is soft and not suitable for dimensional
control via tactile Coordinate Measuring Machines (CMM). The tactile sys-
tems are well-known, established and traceable to the meter unit and have
been used for decades to measure three-dimensional engineering compo-
nents. Development of new optical dimensional measurement instruments
has a great advantage of measuring complex geometries without any con-
tact, which makes them attractive for 3D dimensional measurement [8]. In
automatic mass production casting lines, time is the main issue and it is
not practical to hold the production of sand molds for a measurement task.
Moreover, it is important to note that the green sand mold is fragile and
not easy to transfer for oﬀ-line measurement purposes. In such a limited

The project is designed to develop a metrological artifact for measurements

3.1
Introduction of measurement traceability
in optical 3D measurement system
To prove the validity of the measurement, traceability to standard units
of length, i.e. the meter, it is necessary for any measurement instrument.
The International Vocabulary of Metrology (VIM) [1] deﬁnes metrological
traceability as a "property of a measurement result or the value of a stan-
dard whereby the result can be related to a reference through a documented
unbroken chain of calibration, each contributing to the measurement un-
certainty". Regarding the inﬂuence of many diﬀerent parameters through
scanning process chain and the lack of practices and comparative data which
is still almost in-existence, measurement uncertainties are almost unknown.
In this work, main eﬀort is to develop a particular reference object, same
as used in classical coordinate metrology, to identify the error source and
recover them.
General speaking, for tactile and optical coordinate metrology, to check the
accuracy and precision of obtaining measurement traceability, two steps are
deﬁned:

Preparation of reference object
In casting industry, green sand molding is developed vaster than another
process and it is replacing many of the more expensive molding meth-
ods. Reasonable cost, environmental friendly, high productivity and readily
adaptable to manual, semi-auto and automatic molding machine, are the
advantages of using green sand molding process.
A clear disadvantage,
however, is that the sand mixture makes for indigent use in repeatable ex-
periments. The water will just evaporate, leaving the green sand sample
even more fragile than in already was before. Therefore, the ﬁrst step is to
==PAGE:47==

ﬁnd a substitute sand mixture and to do this; it is important to decide on
some parameters on which to evaluate such a substitute sand mix. Since
the aim of the project is to evaluate the use of 3D scanners for green sand
molds, a natural choice would be to characterize the surface of the samples.
This can be done in many ways, but the choice was made to use optical
roughness measurements, primarily because of availability and the fact that
the method is contact-free. Another important parameter for the samples
is the strength. This parameter is maybe the most important one since no
experiments can be made if the samples are not strong enough to keep their
geometry throughout the measurement. No actual experiment was used to
evaluate this parameter. Instead, the samples were evaluated by a simple
touch-and-look strategy. Here, the samples were picked up by hand and the
feel and number of loose sand grains were evaluated. It is important to note
that the strength of the substitute samples should be higher than that for
regular green sand.

can be avoided [36]. But due to the original black colour of the test object,

Chapter 8
Conclusion
8.1
Summary
This project presented a general introduction to optical 3D scanning, includ-
ing the description of each step of the scanning process chain. Then, prob-
lems concerning measurement traceability, containing discussions about
available standards and guidelines, identiﬁcation of inﬂuence factors, and
the survey of the current reference objects were discussed. Furthermore, the
developed reference objects and their usage for characterization of optical
3D systems presented. Finally, the eﬀects of some scanning factors which
can aﬀect the measurement process in optical 3D scanning was investigated.
According to the issues explored in this work, the following conclusions are
drawn.
Sand cylinder
The main limitation with the optical instruments is how to demonstrate
their traceability to the meter unit. Compared to tactile instruments, which
have been used successfully for decades in the 3D measurement of industrial
parts, the traceability of optical instruments is not completely established.
Currently, there is a lack of standard procedures as well as reference artifacts
for the optical instruments. In this regards, the application of ISO 10360-2
has been helpful to study the performance characterization of optical 3D
systems and ISO 15530-3 to obtain measurement uncertainty. Concerning
the developed reference objects and their application for the characteri-
sation of optical 3D systems, have made it possible to open discussions
about the dimensional measurement of the green sand molds. Accordingly,
a metrological approach was presented to measure the length of green sand
samples by optical instruments. Using the concept behind the substitution
74
==PAGE:90==
CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSION
75
method (ISO 15530-3), a sample with hard binder replaced the ordinary
green sand. A surface comparison study conﬁrmed a good agreement be-
tween the substituted surface and the original surface concerning roughness
(Ra = 48 µm) and surface cooperativeness (under the optical microscope).
The substituted sample (hard sample) was used as traceability element be-
tween an optical scanner with unknown uncertainty and a calibrated dial
gage with a ﬂat probe. The dial gage measurements on the ordinary green
sand showed a drift in the range of 5-10 µm during the measurements due
to the probe force. After each probing, the optical instrument scanned the
same area and simulated measurements were performed using GOM-Inspect
software. The results indicated that the optical scanner was able to detect
the drift in the range of 5-20 µm. The eﬀect of changing the measurement
volume of the optical scanner was examined. Optical scanner measurements
were 42 µm and 56 µm below the dial gage readings for measurement vol-
ume of 320 and 560 respectively. The dial gage measurements on the hard
sand sample were stable. The total expanded uncertainty was estimated to
2 µm. The measurement bias of the optical scanner was 12 µm (STD = 4
µm) for MV 320 and 41 µm (STD = 6 µm) for MV 560. The total expanded
uncertainty for the length measurement using the presented procedure with
optical scanner was 10-12 µm for both measurement volumes.
Afterward, the optical 3D scanner with fringe pattern projection was used
to measure the length of a green sand sample (soft sample) with traceability
transfer through the hard sample. Results conﬁrm that the uncertainty of
the optical scanner on the substituted hard sample is similar to that of the
soft sample, so the hard sample can successfully represent the soft sample
in the ISO 15530-3 procedure. The expanded uncertainty (k=2) of length
measurements on sand was estimated to 10 µm.
Step sand cylinder
The step sand cylinder was used in connection with the metrological per-
formance characterization of optical 3D scanners. The general concept of
the object is to test the structural resolution of the optical 3D scanner
and compare the performance of measurement obtained using the optical
scanner and CMM. In this regards, a comparison has been made between
optical scanning and CMM on sand surfaces, which represent the green
sand surfaces (Ra 60 µm) commonly used in the casting industry. Results
of parallel measurements on 12 ﬂat sand surfaces, each with ﬁve repetitions
shows that the maximum STD of the CMM with Ø 8 mm probe and the
scanner was in the same range (STD = 30 µm for CMM and STD = 37
µm for the scanner). The results of step height measurement showed that
==PAGE:91==
CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSION
76
between steps ranging from 0.1-5 mm made of sand, the maximum error
of CMM was ± 12 µm while scanner showed only ± 4 µm. The diﬀerence
between the optical measurement and CMM measurements was between -5
µm (below CMM readings) to 27 µm (above the CMM readings). The main
conclusion is that CMM results show a higher variation than the optical
scanner and so cannot be used for traceability of the optical scanner.
Inﬂuence of the scanning factors on the optical 3D
scanning
Finally, the investigation on the inﬂuence of the scanning factors on the
optical 3D scanner (ATOSIII) were done. Through a custom DoE approach,
it appeared that the selection of two parameters had signiﬁcant eﬀect on
the Number of Point.
The result showed that the optimal selection for
optical 3D scanning of the green sand surface was obtained as Angle (45
degrees) and lower ETM (350 ms), while scanner showed 5 µm variation of
ﬁve repetitions. In order to achieve lower amount of ETM, its interaction
with angle, ETR and ambient lighting should be considered.
8.2
Outlook
Based on the results obtained in this project, in the ﬁeld of optical 3D
scanning, the following points are suggested for further research work:
• The development of an object for correcting of all inﬂuence factors,
during scanning or post-processing, is almost impossible. Therefore,
more investigation needs to use of more calibrated workpieces for task-
speciﬁc uncertainty estimation for dimensional measurements.
• As discussed in chapter 4, ﬁnd a substitute sand mixture is the pri-
mary key for reference objects. Therefore, the eﬀect of the binder
need to be further surveyed with this theme that what amount of
binder should be used and optimize the ratio of binder and catalyst.
It would be done by full-size DoE for the binder.
• For calibrating the vertical proﬁle component of optical 3D scanner,
the step sand cylinder reference object can be enhanced with respect
to ISO 5436-1 [39]. (Type A: Wide grooves with ﬂat bottoms)
• More accurate representative scanned surface requires further inves-
tigation.
