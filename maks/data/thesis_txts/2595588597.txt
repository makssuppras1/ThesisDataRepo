Summary
The objective of the project is to present a contactless method for discharge estima-
tion by deploying the UAV platforms. This implies contactless velocity and water
depth measurements as well as the merge of them without the use of any Ground
Control Points (GCPs). Water surface velocity (vsurg) is estimated by implement-
ing the Particle Image Velocitmetry (PIV) technique. A sonar (SOund Navigation
And Ranging) unit tethered on drone is used for water depth measurements. The
trivial part of the thesis is the combination of the data at the same cross-section, for
accurate and independent results. Therefore, the image georeference is implemented
and the sonar’s Cartesian coordinates from RTK GPS transformed into Image coor-
dinates. Then, the velocity profile can be estimated across the same line with the
bathymetric data. The final product is the stream’s discharge and is given by the
solution of a system of two equations; Manning’s equation and mid-section method
for discharge computation. This approach needs the assumption that flow conditions
at cross sections are approximately normal and that Manning’s equation can be used
to compute discharge from the observed water surface slope.
Apart from the discharge estimation with UAV-borne measurements, is also tested
the ADCP method, an in-situ method with many advantages. ADCP method gives
velocities profiles in depth with high spatial resolution. Furthermore, the operator
can handle the instrument from the shore since it is tethered. The fact that ADCP
gives velocity profiles in depth, it helps to observe the relation between the water
surface velocity (vsurf) and the mean velocity (Um). For comparison and evaluation
of the results, the water surface velocity is also measured with the electormagnetic
water flow meter, the OTT MF pro. The field campaign took place in Denmark’s
longest river, Gudenå. The methods are tested in three different XS.

Introduction
Water resources is a study area that should not concerns only the hydrologists but
each of us individually since water play a significant role in our lives. Climate change
as well as extreme weather conditions increase the interest for monitoring inland water
resources including the rivers. For example, the accurate data in flood monitoring
can contribute to predictions for extreme flows and save lives [1] [2] [3] [4].
All these years, several methods and techniques have been implemented for the
collection and analysis of the data regarding the water flow. In-situ measurements give
undoubtedly very accurate river discharge estimations and some of these methods such
as ADCP (Acoustic Doppler Current Profiler) and electromagnetic flow measurements
are used in this project as well. However, the size of the investigated areas, the lack
of resources, the meteorological conditions and the inaccessibility to stream sections
makes them impractical [5] [6].
The technological progress in connection with the need for flow observations has
led to the the solution of remote sensing [7]. In recent years, a lot of research has
been made for the non-invasive discharge estimation [8] [9] [10]. The development of
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) and aerial photogrammetry give the opportunity
for measurements with higher spatial and temporal resolution than satellites images
can provide [11] [4].
A camera mounted on UAV records high-resolution videos of water surface. Pic-
tures extracted and with the use of optical based softwares estimated the surface
velocities [8].
The technique that calculate the velocity distribution within parti-
cle images pairs is called PIV (Particle Image Velocimetry) and for this project is
used the PIVlab toolbox [12] while there other softwares as well such as PTVlab,
KU-STIV [13] and DischargeKeeper [14]. Another method use thermal cameras and
take advantage of the thermal signal which is not affected by illumination conditions
like RGB imagery [6] [7].There are several parameters that can affect the accuracy
of PIV, like the density of particle seeding, the camera stabilization and the image
processing [15] [16].
One limitation of the PIV method is the use of GCPs [8]. This project attempts
the removal of the GCPs (Ground Control Points) by implementing the image geo-
reference. The aerial photogrammetry helps to replace the need of GCPs and as a
result to minimize the time and the cost (no need for survey control points) [17].
The bathymetric data are obtained remotely with a tethered floating sonar con-
trolled by the UAV [5] [18]. The UAV payload includes a radar that measures the
distance from water surface, and is used for water level estimations and as a result
for the slope estimation on the crosssection [11].
The estimation of water discharge is a method that combines the mid-section

uniform flow. Therefore, under the assumption that the XS have uniform flow the

UAV-borne image velocimetry
2.1
Video acquisition
The first step for the vsurf estimation with UAV is the video acquisition. This project
describes the PIV (Particle Image Velocimetry) method, where the water surface is
seeded with tracer particles and their motion is used to calculate the velocity (speed
and direction) of water surface [19].
Drone payload
For the needs of the project were used two kind of drones. The first
one is the phantom 4 from dji [20], with video-camera recording in FHD: 1920x1080
mode, and gimbal for the stabilization and to keep the camera plane parallel to the
water surface. The payload of the second drone includes: a Go-PRO camera [21]
which acquires video with 4K resolution (3840×2160), a GNSS antenna, a radar that
detects the range between the camera and the water surface and an IMU (inertial
measurement unit) which measures the motion, the angular rate and the orientation
of UAV by using accelometers, gyroscopes and magnetometers [22] [5] [2].
The camera remains the most important type of equipment. The accurate position
of the drone is given from the GNSS antenna and it is used for navigation and
for image georeference. The radar measurements are used for the conversion of the
velocity from pixel to metric units and the estimation of the Sw (water slope).
Seeding Particles
The seeding particles have an important role for computing
surface velocity with PIV. For example, the color, it is advisable to be bright to absorb
enough light and be distinguishable beside the water surface (Figure 2.1a). Regarding
the size, it should be large enough to scatter the light and be visible in images but
small enough for accurate and with high spatial resolution results [23]. Last but not
lest, a homogeneous distribution along the crossection will give the entire information
for the water flow (Figure 2.1b). Figure 2.1 shows three different situations where
the seeding particles cannot give optimal results for the above mentioned reasons.
Seeding materials for the experiments in this project are woodchips and released by
manual operators from bridge close to crossections (upstream).

There are several parameters should be taken into consideration during
the field work. First of all, the weather is a critical component; the wind has impact
on water’s surface velocity but also it is a problem for drone to fly. Even if the wind
speed is not high, it can result in a shaky video. The sun may also cause problems

2.2
Video processing
This section describes the workflow from the raw data (video) to the final product
(undistorted images) which is imported in PIVlab. The video starts recording a little
before the drone takes off and stops after it lands. Thus, the video is trimmed at
the moment when the drone is up and the maximum number of seeding crossing the
XS. The duration of the trimmed video ranges from 1 to 5 seconds, depends on how
many image frames are needed for the PIV analysis. For the video trimming is used
the software AVS VideoRemaker [24]. Subsequently, the image frames are extracted
depending on the FPS (Frames Per Second) of the camera (23.98 for Go-Pro, 29.97
for Mavic).
2.2.1
Video Stabilization
The video is stabilized to reduce displacements in horizontal plane which may affect
the accuracy of the velocity estimation. The stabilization algorithm is using stable
features to eliminate the vibrations [25]. The movements in vertical direction, which
cause scaling effect in the transition from pixel to metric units, can be limited by the
drone operator by controlling the flight height [2].

2.2.2
Camera calibration
This section presents the intrinsic parameters as well as the radial and tangential
distortions as a result of the camera calibration. The intrinsic parameters include
the focal length, the skew coefficient and the optical center, all in pixel units. Skew
coefficient is non zero when the axes px, py of each pixel are not perpendicular.
Tangential distortion represents the angle between the lens and the image plane [26].
Figure 2.2 shows a straight bridge that is distorted by displacements caused by radial
distortion. The radial distortion is based on the geometry of the lens. For the camera
calibration is used the software OpenCV where inserted images from multiple views of
a checkerboard with known distances [2]. Camera calibration is only for the Go-Pro
camera and the estimated coefficients are shown in Table 2.1. The distances in frames
should be uniform and independent from the camera location. The topographical
variations of the area as well as the tilted camera affect the distances and the angles
between the image points [27]. The process of orthorectification removes the effects
of these two by adding a elevation model of the ground surface [28]. In this project,
orthorectification is avoided because the gimbal keeps the camera facing nadir and
the terrain is almost flat.

2.3
PIVlab analysis
PIVlab [12] is an image toolbox in MATALAB environment and computes the surface
velocity by implementing image cross-correlation techniques.The particles captured in
images and the velocity is derived by the distance that the particles traveled from im-
age A to B. The particle displacement has spatial resolution the selected interrogation
area.

The Region of interest (ROI) is selected according to the line of

Analysis
In PIV analysis is selected the PIV algorithm for analyzing the data (here

it can been in Figure 2.3 the magnitude and the direction of the velocity vectors
are smoothly varying when there are seeding particles, but in other case there are
obviously erroneous vectors.
Post - processing
In the output of the PIVlab analysis is applied a 3 sigma thresh-
old to remove the outliers and replace them with interpolated values. Even though
the vectors with magnitude higher than 3 times the st dev. are detected (orange color)
there are still vectors that do not depict the true values. Thus, the implementation
of mask is decided (Section 2.3.1), where only the pixels with seeding particles are
selected.
For each frame is exported an ASCII file which consists of 4 columns: x coordinate,
y coordinate, velocity in x and y direction, respectively.

Conversion from pixel into metric units
The data processing in PIVlab gives the surface velocity (vsurf) in pixel units, specifi-
cally estimates the travel distance of particles in pixels per frame. Unless the velocity
is converted in metric units, the result is useless.
The conversion can be done in two ways and it depends on the availability of
GCPs. Thus, in case there are GCPs (poles that mark the XS), it is measured the
real distance in meters between the two poles with measurement tape or by estimating
the Euclidean distance of the poles’ coordinates which requires surveying with RTK
GPS. Then, the pixel distance of the two poles is computed from the undistorted
images. The ratio, meter per pixel (mpp), as it is shown in Equation 2.1 gives the
relation between the pixel and metric units.

2.5
Applying contact-less method
At this section is implemented the main goal of this thesis, the contactless stream
discharge estimation from UAV, namely without the use of any GCPs. This means
that the velocity and bathymetry data should be along the same XS without the use
of any marks.
Drones give the opportunity to access remote areas [8] and collect velocimetry
and bathymetry data for streams. It is high time to use the full potential of UAV
and proceed in image georeferencing and assign ground coordinates to the pixels of
any given image. In this way, it is succeeded the merging of the velocity data (image
plane) and bathymetry (ground plane). Another advantage of the method is that it
is not require the survey of GCPs which saves time and money.
For the image georeferencing is needed:
• UAV’s accurate position (GNSS antenna)

5.1
ADCP (Acoustic Doppler Current Profilers) method
The Acoustic Doppler Current Profiler (ADCP) uses acoustic energy to measure ve-
locity and direction over a range of depths, while measuring river bathymetry and
boat speed. Discharge data is computed in real time as it crosses the river. A pulse
of energy with high frequency is transmitted into the water and is reflected back from
particles into the water column. The reflected energy has a different frequency, the
ADCP measures the Doppler shift and estimates the water velocity relative to the
ADCP [33]. For each XS are measured at least four transects that their mean of all
the samples should be in agreement within 5%.
Τhe ADCP is a widely used method for discharge estimation [7] [34], since it is
characterized with high spatial resolution, it is time efficient and the results are in
real-time. In comparison with other in-situ measurements, the instrument is tethered
and is controlled by the operator from the shore. Especially for larger streams with
strong currents this is a great advantage. Additionally, it provides data for the whole
profile of the XS and not only point measurements. One of the limitations of the
ADCP is that there is blanking distance under the surface because of the time delay
between the transmitted and the received signal [7].
5.1.1
Velocity profiles
The processing of the ADCP measurements gives a graph with the velocity as a
function of depth. A water column is divided into discrete segments, which called
depth cells. Figure 5.1 shows a contour plot with the velocity as a function of depth
that is produced in WinRiver II Software. It can be seen that there are no data close
to water surface as well as to bottom surface. The blank close to water surface is due
to the fact that there is only one transducer which transmits and receives the pulse.
Thus, some time must pass to receive the first pulse. For the example in Figure 5.1
this distance is 23 cm. Regarding the bottom region, ADCP while transmits acoustic
pulses, some energy has direction other than vertical and side reflections near the
bottom can reflect into invalid velocities. The height of the depth cells corresponds
to the measurements density in depth, for this example is 12cm and it depends on
instruments design and the frequency of the pulse. The width of the cells is constant
and represent one ensemble number (one reading). The relation between the ensemble
and the width for the same example can be seen in Figure 5.2. [33]

The river depth information for the discharge estimation comes from the setting

5.1.3
Dishcharge
The final discharge is the combination of the unmeasured top and bottom parts
and the Middle Layer Dishcharge (MidQ). The Middle Layer Dishcharge is given by
the sum of the individual bins. For the areas without information, it is used the
Power law method among others. The power method combines Chen’s power law
and Manning’s formula for open channels and it takes into consideration the distance
and the velocity of the first layers bins and the last layer bins for the top and bottom
areas, respectively. [33]

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
The fact that PIV method provides water surface velocity remotely, with high spatial
resolution and reliably results makes it increasingly popular.However, there are many
parameters that can affect the final accuracy of the method.
The thesis tried to minimize the limitations from seeding particles. Firstly, with
the good selection of seeding particles (bright color, right size) during the field work.
Secondly, by the mask implementation, the vsurf is estimated only from pixels with
good information. So, even if there is a small amount of seeding particles, the final
result is not affected by noisy data. Furthermore, in second site were used combined
data from two different sample times, and as a result the seeding density increased.
Last, the scatter plots with the PIV data gives an overview how velocity varies on
water surface and help to detect outliers.
The image georeference gives the opportunity to merge the velocity and bathy-
metric measurements without the use of GCPs and the advantages are many. It is
not mandatory to have access to XS or to survey ground marks with extra instrumen-
tation (e.g. RTK GPS). Thus, we save money and time while at the same man-made
errors are avoided (survey wrong poles, poles have been moved during the measure-
ments, poles are not visible in images etc). Regarding the accuracy of the method,
there is room for improvement: 1.By applying orthorectification: a digital elevation
model of the area can improve the result of the image georeference while is manda-
tory for areas with rough topography 2. with a good IMU system since the method
is quite sensitive to azimuth angle.
The ADCP velocity profiles in all cases showed that the velocity did not vary in
depth. On the other hand, for the discharge estimation both with Q2eq and Q85 is
regarded that the velocity decrease in depth. Maybe this is the reason why in all
three cases PIV gave lower discharge estimations even though the velocity results
look similar.
The OTT MF pro gave lower values and this may be due to operating errors.
For wide streams the small rubber boat is unstable and it is quite difficult to take
measurements. Thus, the method is proved weak for large streams with high flow.

Conclusion
The work done in this project intended to answer the challenges in estimating the
water discharge contactless with drone-borne data. The velocimetry data are given
by the PIV method. It was attempted to optimize the result from PIV analysis by
implementing an algorithm that masks out all the information that come from pixels
with no seeding. The bathymetry is obtained by tethered sonar. The fact that image
georeferencing is implemented, the velocity data are merged with the bathymetry at
the same XS. The discharge estimation based on a system of two equations:Manning’s
equation and the mid-section method. The discharge is estimated for three different
XSs.
The fact that in-situ measurements also conducted helps to end up in the following
results (for the comparison with ADCP results is not taking into consideration the
third site due to the mentioned limitations that makes the PIV results unreliable):
• The ADCP method gives higher values in discharge estimation and they differ
from PIV between 19% and 24%
• The ADCP and PIV velocity profiles look similar both in pattern and absolute
values
• The bathymetry data from sonar are in agreement with the ADCP, apart from
the 3rd site where the sonar penetrates deeper (may due to high sediment
concentration)
• The OTT MF pro gives lower values in comparison with the other two methods
