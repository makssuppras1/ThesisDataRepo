Abstract
The heavily overconsolidated clay till in the Copenhagen area pose a number of interesting challenges
in relation to the numerical modelling of problems related to this soil. A proper choice of constitutive
model must be made for such modelling. Therefore a description and evaluation of the details of
the BRICK model as formulated by Brian Simpson in [Simpson, 1992] and later modiﬁed by several
authors is done in order to evaluate the BRICK models capabilities in modelling Copenhagen clay
till.
Based on general knowledge about the clay till from the Copenhagen area and few relevant samples
of the soil, a calibration of the model parameters is performed. This initial calibration is used
as a basis for evaluating the inﬂuence on the results from diﬀerent known modiﬁcation to the
formulation of the BRICK model.
The sensitivity in the BRICK model towards the parameter choice is investigated for each parameter
individually to assess the inﬂuence from each parameter on the model output.
From these assessments and calibrations a ﬁnal suggestion of BRICK parameters for use in modelling
Copenhagen clay till will be made. Also suggestions as how to calibrate the BRICK model eﬃciently
is made based on the experiences from the study of the model.
Further than a calibration the ﬁndings made during the calibration process will be presented in
order to make an evaluation of the BRICK models capabilities and shortcomings.
Expanding the analysis from simulations in one gauss point to a full ﬁnite element model will show
how well the BRICK model performs compared to other more well known models. The BRICK
model is already incorporated in the software called SAFE. This implementation is compared to
the single gauss point analysis and ﬁnally used in modelling an example case.
Through modelling an excavation case both the general implementation of the model is investigated
and its ability to simulate the interaction with soil and structure in a geotecnical construction works.
Along with the evaluation of the BRICK model, an evaluation of the SAFE software is made.

INTRODUCTION
1
Project description
The main purpose of the project is to establish a better numerical description of the soil structure
interaction in complex soils, such as the heavily overconsolidated clay till in Copenhagen.
With triaxial and consolidation tests (on such soil) as a reference, a calibration of the BRICK
model as implemented in the ﬁnite element program SAFE is to be done. The BRICK model has
capabilities to incorporate overconsolidation, small strain behaviour and creep more realistically
than other models presently applied to the clay till. It is thus of interest to investigate whether the
BRICK model can capture the observed behaviour. By understanding of the theory used in the
modelling, a description of the inﬂuence on the output from the diﬀerent input parameters is to be
established - qualitatively as well as based on sensitivity studies of governing material parameters.
Additionally, the stress ranges and loading conditions for which the calibrated model is applicable
will be assessed using selected geotechnical boundary value problems, such as bearing capacity
surface footings and performance of deep excavations supported by multipropped retaining walls.
Comparison with simpler constitutive models will be included in this assessment.
2
Introduction
Large amounts of the the top soil in the Copenhagen area consist of heavily overconsolidated clay
till stratiﬁed in layers. The properties of the Copenhagen clay till are quite unique since the soil
has been deposited as an end moraine during the latest ice age. The complex loading history along
with the unique combination of ﬁnes and grains up to the size of boulders make this soil hard to
represent by a soil model.
Due to the previous glaciations covering Denmark extreme pressures combined with shearing have
resulted in the clay till compacting very much. This has made the clay till of Copenhagen obtain
great strength and stiﬀness compared to other soils. The high strength and stiﬀness of the clay has
made it easy to build regular buildings on it, but with an increasing demand for more challenging
geotechnical construction works the need for a better soil model representing the behaviour of the
clay till has occurred.
As discussed in [Potts et al., 2002], one of the common problems in geotechnical engineering relates
to the subjectivity of modelling. Opposite to structural problems the domain of the analysis is ill
deﬁned in geotechnical engineering why the choice of domain for an analysis will vary and inﬂuence
the solution. Further the interaction between soil and structure poses a number of numerical
problems, solved in diﬀerent ways in diﬀerent softwares through diﬀerent types of interface elements,
also leading to subjectivity in the solution. Even the general numerical solution code may vary
depending on the software in use.
Along with these programming problems are the problems of representing the details of a construction
case in both space and time and the problem of choosing the right model for the right kind of soil.
When analysing geotechnical construction works, The choice of model pose a great inﬂuence on the
result from an analysis and since there have not been chosen any speciﬁc soil models as standards
for speciﬁc soils there is a constant need for development of new, better and simpler soil models.
In urban construction works the inﬂuence on neighbouring constructions from settlements caused
by excavations or tunnelling is of great economic and environmental importance. Deﬂections in a
retaining wall or volume loss from tunnelling will inﬂuence the soil surface close to the construction,
and might cause major damage to surrounding buildings. The ability to predict the settlements and

deformations occurring in geotechnical construction works is therefore a key feature to minimizing
this damage.
The most frequently used constitutive model for these problems is the Mohr-Coulomb model (MC),
a linear elastic, perfectly plastic model. The use of this model leads to greater area of inﬂuence of
settlements with less magnitude than what is seen in experiments [Hejazi et al., 2008]. If a better
prediction of such settlements is to be made a more sophisticated model is needed.
Diﬀerent models with varying complexity and capability have been proposed through the history of
geotechnical engineering. Examples of these are the elastoplastic Duncan & Chang model (DC) by
[Duncan and Chang, 1970] and the Modiﬁed Cam-clay model (MCC) presented in [Wood, 1990].
These two models have diﬀerent capabilities, the DC model being able to simulate non-linear
stress-strain paths and the MCC model being able to simulate peak eﬀects.
These models both have a stress state dependant stiﬀness and change of stiﬀness in unloading
compared to virgin loading, virgin loading being formulated through a yield criterion. These models
show better results with regards to the mentioned problem of settlement and deformation modelling,
but are still lacking some observed behaviours.
It has been noticed that the stiﬀness of soils not only depends on the stress state in that soil and
information on previous loading, but also the amount of strain occurring in the soil. This eﬀect
was discovered by measuring the stiﬀness of soils subdued to a rotation of the stress path, where it
was observed that the stiﬀness would rise under such conditions. This led to the formulation of a
number of diﬀerent model, among these the BRICK model initially described in [Simpson, 1992].
The BRICK model deviates from other models by being formulated in strain space opposite to
the traditional formulation in stress spaces. Its closest resemblance is to a multi surface kinematic
hardening model. The model is capable of simulating peak eﬀects along with overconsolidation
behaviour and non linear stress-strain paths. The stiﬀness in the model varies both with stress
state and amount of straining. The BRICK model should therefore have the capability to include
all the advantages from the MCC and DC models plus adding a strain dependant stiﬀness.
The BRICK model seems to have great potential. The details of the BRICK model are not very
well described in the literature, why the model is not used very much. Further the model has
mostly been used to model soft clays like the London clay. For the applicability of this model in
Danish practise a more through investigation of the model, its shortcomings and capabilities, and a
calibration to a more relevant Danish soil type is needed.

COPENHAGEN CLAY TILL
3
Copenhagen clay till
In this section a description of the deposition and loading history of the Copenhagen (CPH) clay
till will be presented. The geotechnical consequences of this will be described for the purpose of
using this knowledge in soil modelling of the CPH clay till.
3.1
The geological history of the Copenhagen area
The movement of the glaciers during the ice ages transported a mixture of a great variety of soils
from the north and eastern parts of Scandinavia to Denmark. These soil mixtures were pushed
into piles and mixed up by the movements of the glacier. This it what is know as the clay till of
Copenhagen [Sand-Jensen, 2006].
When discussing CPH clay till it is important to mention its characteristic high Pre Over Pressure
(POP). This POP is caused by the weight of the ice cover during the ice ages of the quaternary
period reaching from ∼2.6 million year before present until now.
The ice cover, resulting in the POP experienced by the CPH clay till, have been of a thickness of
2000-3000 meters during the latest ice age (Weichsel). The ice cover in the latest ice age only just
reached Denmark and did therefore not cover it entirely as can be seen on Figure 3.1. The ice made
its ﬁnal retraction ∼10000 year before present.
Figure 3.1: Maximum reach of the last three ice ages (from [Woldstedt, 1958])
The deposits from this period are classiﬁed as end moraine since they are from the border of the ice
cover. Clay till is already a soil with great variability in its composition with particle sizes ranging
from clay minerals up to boulders. Being an end moraine does further complicate the matter since
the deposition method in this region also varies, causing diﬀerent POPs and stiﬀness.
The glacial deposits are commonly divided into glaciogenic till and glacioﬂuvial deposits. Glaciogenic
till is soil moved directly by the ice from the Scandinavian mountains, as push moraines, and
deposited under periglacial permafrost [Foged and Steenfelt, 1992]. This concept is shown on Figure
3.2.
Glacioﬂuvial deposits are accumulations from melt water streams, caused by the melting of the
glacier in the spring, ﬁlling depression in the terrain in front of the glacier. These deposits can also
be created in the warm contact zone between the moving ice and the soil/rock. This part of the
ice is warmed due to friction and heat ﬂow from below. Due to the high pressure of the ice cover
the melting point of ice if lowered with ∼

exhibit almost no overconsolidation.

The clay till was deposited in two layers called the lower and upper clay till. The lower clay till is
the strongest showing undrained shear strengths (cu) in the range of 500-1500 kPa where as the
upper clay till is in the order of 100-700 kPa (still strong compared to other clays) [COWI et al.,
2009]. The thickness of both clay till layers increase from 2-3 meters on Amager to 6-8 meters for
the upper clay till and more than 12 meters for the lower clay till in the west parts of Copenhagen
[Frederiksen et al., 2002]. In between the two clay till layers a layer of melt water sand is found,
and below the lower clay till, limestone.
This is what will typically be found, but great variation in the thickness of the clay till layers exist
and locally one or both layers may not exist.
3.2
Characteristics of CPH clay till
Due to the complicated deposition method discussed in Section 3.1, the CPH clay till usually
appears as an unsorted mixture of silty and sandy clay with particles as large as boulders. It is
often of low plasticity, and with high POPs related to the overburden of the ice cover from the ice
ages [Foged and Steenfelt, 1992].
Even though the variation in the thickness of the ice cover is small when considering an area the
size of Copenhagen, the POP can vary greatly. This is caused by the water pressure created in the
sole of the glacier due to the melting of the warm ice shown in Figure 3.2. This water pressure can
reduce the eﬀective pressure with up to 90%. Crevasses in the glacier assist the dewatering of the
surface and reduce the pore pressure resulting in higher POPs. In general the POP is said to be in
the order of 1-10 MPa [Foged, 2015] in the glaciogenic tills.
When the glacier is retracting, the melting of frozen layers in front of the glacier causes ﬂow through
unfrozen soil resulting in upwards gradients of water pressure that can become of such magnitude
that they will cause destructurisation of the soil. In these cases the POP is “forgotten”.
The soil of main interest, in this project, is the heavily overconsolidated clay till. This soil have not
lost the information on the overconsolidation and thus has the before mentioned POP in the order
of 1-10 MPa.
Shear in the contact zone between ice and soil can further increase the strength of the soil. This
serves to also increase the measured POP even though the actual POP is not increases.
In some cases the extreme compaction of the CPH clay till causes it to resemble fractured soft rock
more than actual soil. Due to the high levels unloading when the ice retracted, the soil in some
cases has experienced passive failure leading to this analogy to fractured soft rock.

3.3
Use of geological history in soil modelling
The reason to investigate the geological history of the soil is to include the information obtained by
it in a soil model. The BRICK model, which is to be discussed in Section 4, include the eﬀects of
loading/deposition history but needs an initial modelling of these actions.
The concluding remark from this section will therefore be: The POP experienced in the clay till in
the Copenhagen area is caused by one loading:unloading cycle, namely the adding and removal of
the ice during the latest ice age. Even though the POP is of great variety it can generally be said
to be above 1 MPa.

BRICK
4
BRICK
In this section a review of the BRICK model, with respect to both the analogy formulated by Brian
Simpson in the late 70’s and the math behind the model, will be done.
The BRICK model was originally formulated by Brian Simpson at Arup Geotechnics. It has since
been described in detail in [Simpson, 1992], [Clarke, 2009] and [Ellison, 2012] among other works.
4.1
The analogy of the BRICK model
As described in [Simpson, 1992], the analogy of the model is that of a man walking through a room,
pulling behind him a set of bricks tied to him by separate strings of diﬀerent lengths. The room
represents a strain space and the position of the man represents the strain situation in the soil
element. A brick then represent a proportion of the soil, and the length of the string connected to
that brick represents the amount of strain needed to mobilize plasticity in that part of the soil. In
other words the moving bricks represent the plastic part of the total strain whereas the weighted
sum of the diﬀerence of the movement of the man and the bricks represent the elastic part of the
strain.
When the man is walking continuously in one direction more and more strings get taut, therefore
an increasing larger proportion of the soil acts plastically and the stiﬀness of the soil decrease in
accordance with Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: The S-shaped curve of degradation of stiﬀness and the connection to bricks on strings (from
[Simpson, 1992])
As seen on Figure 4.1 the degradation of the stiﬀness will happen in steps with the size of the brick,
the sting of which is getting taut.
Initially the strain in the soil element is completely elastic since no bricks are moving. As stings
begin to become taut a larger part of the strain is getting plastic and in the end when all the bricks

line up in a straight line behind the man (at constant volume as introduced later) the soil element
will fail. This corresponds to the earlier deﬁnition of the elastic part of the strain in the soil element.
Since there is no diﬀerence in the movement of the man and the sum of the movement of the bricks
all of the strain experienced by the soil element will be plastic thus generating a stiﬀness of 0 and
therefore leading to failure [Clarke and Hird, 2012].
Up until now only the movement of the man in one direction starting with all the bricks at the
same point as the man has been described. The strain path can oﬀcause diﬀer from this. In Figure
4.2 some possible paths for the man is illustrated.
Figure 4.2: A man pulling bricks on strings (from [Simpson, 1992])
When changing the path of the man, some of the strings might slacken causing the stiﬀness to rise
again. When only moving in one dimension this can be thought of as the initial loading curve of a
normal consolidated soil in the oedometer apparatus with possible unloading “loops”.
As it can be seen from Figure 4.2 (a and b) all the strings will slacken when the path of the man is
reversed. After continuing in this opposite direction the shortest string will eventually get taut
again and reduce the stiﬀness once again. As also shown in Figure 4.2, turning the path of the man
by approximately 90o will not make any of the strings slacken but will according to the deﬁnition
of the elastic part of the strain, mentioned earlier, generate more elastic strain since the sum of the
movement of the bricks is reduced at the same movement of the man.
4.2
The mathematics of the BRICK model
The original BRICK theory is described in [Simpson, 1992]. As mentioned earlier further descriptions
of the model is done by other authors developing the theory further. The original theory is formulated
in 2D plane strain. There exist four versions of the BRICK theory available in SAFE according to
Oasys [2009a]. These are:
1. Original plane strain theory as presented in [Simpson, 1992]
2. Same as 1., but with linear v −ln (s) line for swelling and some other minor modiﬁcations

BRICK
3. 3D theory, with linear v −ln (s) line for swelling, and modiﬁed Drucker-Prager shape for the
failure envelope, determined by ratios of current stress.
4. Same as 3., but with modiﬁed Drucker-Prager shape for the failure envelope, determined by

4.2.2

correct some model behaviours. The changes from theory 1 to 2 are described by [Clarke, 2009].

be diﬀerent from the increase in stiﬀness.

5.3.3
Bricks and strings
A key feature of the BRICK model is the stiﬀness degradation curve (also known as the S-curve),
the principal of which is shown in Figure 4.1.
As shown in Figure 4.1 the information needed to draw the S-curve comprises of the string data.
Therefore, to be able to choose representative string data, the shape of the S-curve must be
determined.
The ﬁrst part of the S-curve can be determined by performing small strain stiﬀness tests with
bender elements, cyclic triaxial testing or shear wave velocity tests, thereby ﬁnding a representative
value of Gmax. The tail of the S-curve can be determined from regular laboratory tests.
When determining the string and brick data the total shape of the S-curve is rarely known. Some
parts may have been mapped through test as just mentioned, but the overall shape of the S-curve
is a subject for calibration to make the model show the wanted behaviour [Yeow, 2015].
No direct measurements leading to the determination of the shape of the S-curve is available for the
CPH clay till, why empirical relations by [Sun et al., 1988], [Vucetic and Dobry, 1991], [Ishibashi
and Zhang, 1993] and [Massarsch, 2004] are compared and used to establish an initial guess of the
general shape.
Both [Sun et al., 1988], [Vucetic and Dobry, 1991], [Ishibashi and Zhang, 1993] and [Massarsch,
2004] state that the main parameter to aﬀect the shape of the S-curve is the plasticity index (Ip), if
the shear stiﬀness modulus (G) is normalized with the maximum shear stiﬀness modulus (Gmax).
It is discussed by [Darendeli, 2001] that the OCR might have some eﬀect on the shape of the
normalized S-curve, but since no well known studies are available describing this eﬀect, the eﬀect of
OCR is not included.
As shown in Table 5.3 the average plasticity index for the four samples of CPH clay till is 8.5 %.
The S-curves are aimed at a plasticity index of 10 % based also on the general knowledge about
CPH clay till from Section 3.2 and the fact the the empirical expressions used to generate the
S-cures have no better precision.
It is described by [Ishibashi and Zhang, 1993] and [Kramer, 1996], that the conﬁning pressure also
change the shape of the S-curve. The eﬀect of this is most prominent at small Ip’s.
It can be found that by using a conﬁning pressure of 20 kPa in the formulation by [Ishibashi and
Zhang, 1993] the four diﬀerent S-curves ﬁt reasonable well, with each other. The comparison is
shown in Figure 5.5. It is therefore reasonable to think that the S-curves formulated by [Sun
et al., 1988], [Vucetic and Dobry, 1991] and [Massarsch, 2004] are based on a tests with a conﬁning
pressure close to 20 kPa.

5.3.5
Initial stress and strain condition
The initial vertical stress is easily found through the weight of the soil and the ground water condition,
while the rest of the stress and strain components have to be determined through modelling of the
geological history. This is of cause not necessary if another manner of determination is possible, but
since the position of the bricks are highly dependant on the strain path of the“man” the preferred
way of determination is through modelling of the stress:strain history.
From the geological history described in Section 3.1 it is known that the clay till of Copenhagen
has undergone a long series of complex loading and unloading. Since no exact knowledge about the
loading history is available, the stress:strain history is modelled through one dimensional loading
from deposition of the soil to the maximum previous vertical load followed by one dimensional
unloading to the current vertical stress, like it is usually done in a triaxial test. This will simulate
the POP that can be interpreted in experimental tests of the soil.
Through such modelling it is possible to obtain the initial stress and strain condition along with
the positions of the bricks.

CHOOSING BRICK THEORY
6
Choosing BRICK theory
In this section an evaluation of the diﬀerences in the BRICK theory 1 and 2, described in Section
4.2, will be carried out to access which implementations are best suited for modelling CPH clay till.
This evaluation is based on simulations with the parameters obtained in Section 5, modelling with
these, a one dimensional loading followed by an unloading and lastly a reloading.
Brick movement along with the stress path in the s−t stress space and behaviour in the compression
plane will be discussed.
6.1
Establishing a reference simulation
It can quickly be realised that the improved calculation of the ﬁrst (volumetric) component of the
plastic strain reduction is needed in order for the BRICK program to converge when changing state
from a NCL to an URL or the other way around, at least with the soil parameters for CPH clay
till. If not implementing the improved calculation method, extremely small strain increments are
needed in order for the equilibrium iterations to be able to converge, thereby an enormous amount
of global iteration steps are needed making the iteration process very lengthy. This improvement is
therefore implemented as a standard.
To make a comparison of the diﬀerences in theory 1 and 2, a 1D consolidation with a unloading
reloading loop is modelled. All strain paths are done with equal v and (εy −εx) and with γxy = 0 as
suggested by [Clarke, 2009]. The initial loading is stopped when s = 1000 kPa is reached. Unloading
is then performed to 5 kPa, followed by reloading to 10000 kPa.
This test will show both the NCL and an URL along with the transition between the two in both
directions. This test does not show the failure phase. This will be discussed in Section 7.
Running this test gives the results shown in Figure 6.1. This will be the reference simulation for
comparison of the diﬀerent changes from theory 1 to 2.
As mentioned, the change of the calculation of the ﬁrst (volumetric) component of the plastic strain
reductions is incorporated in the results in Figure 6.1 to enable the simulation of the URL. The
brick movements are only shown for the longest and shortest string, since plotting all of them
would make reading results very diﬃcult, and since plotting the two extremes gives a adequate
representation of what is going on.
Before implementing any changes (other than that for the ﬁrst component of the plastic strain
reduction) some issues with the results can be noted. The movement of the bricks show a strange
curl during unloading caused by the plastic strain reductions. This eﬀect is clearly seen on the
longest string in Figure 6.1. Further an “overshoot” of the NCL is seen in Figure 6.1 where the
unloading reloading loop causes a parallel shift of the NCL.
The ﬁrst part of the loading also have a tendency to curl a little bit in the compression plane, in
the beginning. It then slowly approach the true NCL as higher stress are reached.
Lastly there seem to be a divergence in the stress path when reaching the overshoot in the
compression plane resulting in the stress path further from this point generating more shear stress
instead of keeping the same K0.

Choise of theory
What have been shown from the comparison of the two theories is that they both have some
diﬃculties in modelling high Over Consolidation Ratios (OCRs) in CPH clay till for the parameters
from Section 5. The BRICK model was originally developed to model London clay which is much
softer and have not been subject to the same levels of POP, why a diﬀerent approach to the model
is needed for it to model CPH clay till properly.
The diﬃculty in modelling these levels of OCR is addressed by [Lehane and Simpson, 1999] ﬁnding
that the best way to use the BRICK model on the very similar DBC is to set β = 0 and then do
the parameter calibration for a limited stress range.
It should also be noted that the simulation presented in this section is testing the model at its limits
by unloading to a mean normal stress level of 5 kPa. There will at all times be some meters of top

ADJUSTING THE BRICK PARAMETERS
7
Adjusting the BRICK parameters
In this section the obtained soil parameters from Section 5 will be discussed. In the determination
of the BRICK parameters in Section 5, the lack of data have an inﬂuence on the conﬁdence in
the choice of some parameters. The BRICK parameters will therefore be evaluated and the model
sensitivity to the diﬀerent parameters will be assessed.
In trying to calibrate the BRICK model to the soil behaviour shown by the four oedometer and
triaxial tests similar situations are modelled in MATLAB using the scripts from Appendix A.
Of the parameters presented in Table 5.9, those related to the most lack of knowledge are the string
data, β and ι∗since non of these are based on any direct tests of the soil but rather on empirical
expressions relating to the general plasticity index and a calibration to obtain the right angle of
shearing resistance.
In conclusion the only parameters in Table 5.9 that can be said to be the real model parameters
are λ∗, κ∗and ν, since they are all found directly from the test results. And even these parameters
are subject to some amounts of scatter due to the small data base.
It can be seen from the plots in Section 6 that the chosen λ∗and κ∗give the right inclinations of
the NCL and URL compared to the results from the oedometer test.
7.1
Assessing choice of S-curve
The problems mentioned in Section 6 with the overshoot and the curl, due to plastic strain
reduction, might be related to a poor choice of the above mentioned parameters instead of only
being a programming issue. The BRICK model was originally developed to model the much softer
London clay, why subjecting the model to these very diﬀerent parameters might show some problems
with the model that were “invisible” with the London clay parameters.

δve = δv −δvp

any shearing the bricks lie in a straight line behind the man on the volume strain axis. As shearing
commences the bricks will move closer to the volumetric strain position of the man thus generating
positive plastic volume strain countered by equal and opposite elastic volume strain in accordance
with Equation 7.2. When inserting this negative elastic volume strain in Equation 4.1 it can be
seen that negative increments of mean normal stress are produced.

7.4
The use of βφ
As discussed previously, changing βφ can make the BRICK model fail at the right failure line
comprised of a cohesion and friction term without the BRICK model having incorporated cohesion
directly. Depending on, at what mean normal stress failure is expected and what the previously
highest mean normal stress has been, the calibration of βφ should be made.
Based on the failure situations in Figure 7.13 it can be seen that the failure at low OCR reaches
the right failure line with βφ = 11, whereas the failure at high OCR fails at a bit lower stress state
for the same value. The choice of βφ is therefore in the right order of magnitude.
For a better calibration βφ should have some variation with OCR instead of a constant value. This
could also be done in a more primitive way through making a calibration of βφ for diﬀerent depths
and incorporating this when using the model in a Finite Element Method (FEM) model or other
calculations beyond a single gauss point calculation.
7.5
Eﬀects of the Poisson ratio
By changing ν the development of t can be controlled. To keep a constant K0, the direct calculation
of δt in Equation 4.2 is unchanged by changing ν, since ι∗also is changed to keep the term (1−2ν)/ι∗
constant for the same string data. The eﬀect of changing ν occurs in the plastic strain reduction in
Equation 4.5 where a larger ν will result in higher capacity for elastic shear straining. This eﬀect is
clearly shown in Figure 7.14.
More than changing the generation of shear stress, also the decrease in mean normal stress is
changed by the choice of ν. With a higher ν it is possible to generate a stress path that reaches
much further back compared to the lower ν. Since values of ν outside the interval of 0.2 to 0.3
would be unrealistic choices for the CPH clay till the extremes shown in Figure 7.14 would not be
reached, but the eﬀect of the choice of ν becomes clear from this ﬁgure.

7.6
Evaluation of the calibration process and model sensitivity
The conclusions from this and the previous sections in regards to the calibration process of the
BRICK model are brieﬂy summarized.
λ∗and κ∗can be determined as they would be regularly for a MCC calibration, though it should
be kept in mind to change them for a volumetric strain axis instead of speciﬁc volume or void ratio.
The conversion formulas for this purpose works ﬁne, both those for conversion from oedometer tests
and from speciﬁc volume to volumetric strain.
The BRICK model is very restricted in the choice of almost all the other parameters due to the
direct interconnection of the parameters ν, ι∗, φ, K0 and the string data. The connection of these
parameters put up a huge restriction in the calibration of the model since what appears to be ﬁve
parameters in reality is only three (If String data, ν and ι∗is chosen, φ and K0 is locked, and
likewise for other combinations).
Further it has been found that there are some restrictions to the choices of ι∗and the cut-oﬀin
therms of the parameters K and Sκ, which should be kept within certain limits. There is still some
freedom of choice of these parameters, but it puts up another restriction in the calibration.
Oﬀthese parameters a lot of attention should be put in making a good choice of string data. This
“parameter” have proved to have a great impact on the results generated by the BRICK model. The
calibration of the string data has this far only been based on previous calibrations and empirical
expressions, for a better calibration some parts of the S-curve should be mapped preferably the
value of Gmax and some parts of the tail of the S-curve should be found. It is not necessary to map
all of the S-curve since it largely also is a calibration factor to make the model perform as wanted.
The last BRICK parameters, the β parameters, are largely independent of the other parameters,

9.2
Excavation case for comparison
For the comparison of the four models a ﬁctive excavation case is modelled. The case is comprised
of the installation of a diaphragm retaining wall followed by staged excavation, supported by struts.
The geometry of the excavation case and model domain is based on one of the model examples
presented in [Potts et al., 2002].
The soil stratigraphy will consist of only one layer with the CPH clay till. No water will be present in
the model to minimize uncertainties generated from the diﬀerent pore pressure calculation methods
in the diﬀerent models.
The stages in the excavation case will be as listed below.
1. Initial equilibrium calculation with modelling of POP when needed
2. Installation of wall and interfaces (Wall wished in place)

==PAGE:79==
9

3. Excavation to level -4 m
4. Excavation to level -8 m and installation of strut in level -3 m
5. Excavation to level -12 m and installation of strut in level -7 m
The geometry of the model domain and construction for the analysis is shown in Figure 9.4
Figure 9.4: Geometry of the example excavation case
The retaining wall in the excavation is a diaphragm wall of 0.8 m width reaching from the ground
level at 0 m to -20 m (8 m below the base of the excavation). Since SAFE only uses continuum
elements, the wall is modelled by change the material in the elements in the region of the wall to a
relevant reinforced concrete material.
The wall material is modelled as a linear elastic isotropic material with the same stiﬀness of 21
GPa as speciﬁed in [Potts et al., 2002]. The Poisson’s ratio of the wall material is 0.15, and the
density of the wall is 22 kN/m3.
The struts are modelled through ﬁxing the horizontal degree of freedom in the node in the middle
of the wall, thus allowing for rotation in the wall but restricting it from horizontal translation.
It is suggested by [Oasys, 2009b] to model interfaces as a thin layer of elements next to the wall
with reduced strength. More speciﬁc with half the cohesion and 2/3 the angle of shearing resistance.
Since the four models do not all have a direct term of c and φ the interfaces are modelled with a
Mohr-Coulomb material in all models so that the eﬀects in the interfaces should remain the same
through the models.
When using the mentioned half c and 2/3 φ for the interface elements it is found that, due to the
lack of a tension cut-oﬀin the general SAFE code, the cohesion will result in the wall moving
into the soil (For the models with an explicit term of cohesion) when excavation commence. The
interfaces are therefore modelled with c = 0 to try and overcome this problem.
The suggestion of a interface thickness of 100 mm by [Oasys, 2009b] is also found to be to large
in the sense that to much of a diﬀerence in the movement of the outside of the interface elements
compared to the wall will occur with 100 mm of interface thickness. A thickness of 10 mm is found
77
==PAGE:80==
9
COMPARING BRICK WITH OTHER MODELS
to give better results even though the interface elements will end up having a poor aspect ratio.
It is argued in [Oasys, 2009b] that the stiﬀness of the interface elements will have no inﬂuence on
the behaviour of the model. This is found to be quite the opposite. It has been tried to make the
stiﬀness 100 times harder, the same and 100 times softer. With the softer interfaces a relatively
large diﬀerence in the movement of the wall and the movement of the outside of the interfaces
happen. The interfaces are therefore modelled with 100 times the stiﬀness of the surrounding soil
to make them follow the wall during its deﬂection.
In SAFE the voids generated during excavation (the excavated parts of the soil) must also be
modelled through changing the material. This material has a non zero, but very small, stiﬀness as
the only parameter. The stiﬀness is set to 0.001 kPa for the void material used in all models.
The boundary conditions of the model are those normally applied to plane strain models, with
vertical boundaries restricted in horizontal movement and the bottom boundary restricted from
any movement.
Generally the only thing changed from model to model is the material of the soil. Everything but
this is modelled in the same way to isolate the eﬀects the diﬀerent models have on the deﬂections
in the wall, settlements behind the wall and earth pressures. These three distributions are plotted
for each model for phase 1, 3, 4 and 5. Phase 2 is neglected since no change should occur from
installing the wall. The changes that are observed from Phase 1 to 2 are within the boundaries of

10
Conclusion
In this section a summary of the most important conclusions made throughout the project will be
presented. Based on the ﬁnding, suggestions regarding further work will be given.
10.1
Summary of conclusions
A lot of eﬀort has been put into describing the details of the BRICK model. The main source of
information about the details in the model has been drawn from [Simpson, 1992], [Ellison, 2012]
and [Clarke, 2009]. Even though the concept of the BRICK model is well known in geotechnical
communities it is rarely used, partly due to the limited description of the details and partly due to
the lack of implementation of the model in a more frequently used FEM software. The details of
the model has been described in order to overcome the ﬁrst problem.
It has been found that the BRICK model is very simple in regards to the mathematics and coding.
This is caused by the model being formulated in strains instead of stresses, as is usually done. This
way of formulating the model expels the need for formulating a yield surface and no ﬂow rules are
needed.
Having a formulation based on strains does simplify a lot of formulations, but in turn the stress
behaviour of the model is more unpredictable compared to models formulated in stresses. The
calibration of the BRICK parameters is therefore more complicated since a number of the models
parameters does not relate directly to output, and therefore should not be determined directly from
soil tests but rather through calibration to wanted behaviour.
Trying to calibrate the BRICK parameters based only on soil tests is, as mentioned, complicated.
Rather than ﬁnding the parameters directly from experiments an initial guess should be made and
this guess should then be calibrated through ﬁtting simulations to wanted behaviour. Of cause, if
more advanced test are performed a better initial guess can be made and the calibration process
eased.
The BRICK model introduce another layer to the well known λ, κ relations from the Cam-clay
framework, called ι. This parameter controls the behaviour of the soil material at initial very small
strains. With the introduction of this parameter follows a number of correlation between diﬀerent
soil properties caused by the formulations in the BRICK model. This makes the new parameter ι
correlated to the ν, φ, K0 and the S-curves discretization into bricks and stings.
The BRICK parameter calibration is therefore restricted to a calibration of the string data, ν and
K0 then ﬁxing ι and φ. To assist this calibration two new “parameters” are introduced: K and Sκ,
and suggestions for the interval of valid values of these are made.
From the correlation of the values it has been found that the parameter that has the most inﬂuence
on the model behaviour is the string data. The string data must be chosen carefully both in regards
to the S-curve it represents and the strain chosen for which no more stiﬀness is present.
The low values of λ and κ for the Copenhagen clay till restricts the choice of S-curve in such a way
that very little freedom of choice is left for its shape and cut-oﬀvalue.
From the calibration process a most optimal BRICK parameter choice is made based on the limited
number of soil samples tested. This parameter choice is tested in a ﬁnite element simulation with
the program SAFE, from where it is found that the model implementation in it self works ﬁne
compared to the simulations of one gauss point. Some diﬀerences exist but due to the lack of
capability to use the same BRICK theory in SAFE as used in the calibration process, it is diﬃcult
to determine if these diﬀerences are caused by the BRICK version or the implementation.
From comparing the BRICK model to the Mohr-Coulomb, Modiﬁed Cam-Clay and Duncan &

CONCLUSION
Chang models it is found that the capability of the BRICK model to generate a OCR dependant K0
condition is superior to the other models. The comparison of the models is unfortunately hampered
by general modelling complications in SAFE. Nevertheless the comparison shows that the performed
calibration of the BRICK model has a to early decline on the S-curve making the model degenerate
almost all of its stiﬀness before developing any stress when exerted to the excavation stages of the
example case.
All in all it can be said that the most attention in the calibration process should be put into the
shape and values of the S-curve. It is suggested that a determination of Gmax and the shape of the
tail of the S-curve should be performed for a better calibration. There is no need to determine the
entire S-curve, as some freedom for calibration should be left. Also a better representation is not
necessarily gained by having perfect calibration of all parameters as is also shown for the choice of
φ.
10.2
Suggestions for further work
It is suggested that the next couple of steps in making the BRICK model usable for modelling of
Copenhagen clay till is to investigate the full 3D versions of the BRICK model and reformulate the
model if necessary to make available a lager range of choices for shape of the S-curve. The model
seem to be best suited for soils with higher λ and κ compared to ones found in Copenhagen clay
till, why the mentioned reformulation might be needed.
Due to the problems with modelling in SAFE it would be very interesting to dig deeper into the
SAFE programming and investigate the exact details in the calculation processes of this software.
An alternative would be to incorporate the BRICK model into a more used (and well documented)
FEM software such as PLAXIS or ABAQUS.
The calibration of the BRICK parameters is, in this project, based on relatively few experiments,
why it would also be of interest to obtain a larger data base and recalibrate. Adding to the
performed triaxial and oedometer test not only more of these but also drained triaxial tests and
measurements of both Gmax and the tail of the S-curve would be of interest to use in a calibration.
The BRICK model does indeed show capabilities in regards to small strain modelling but its initial
purpose of modelling the very diﬀerent London clay makes it diﬃcult to calibrate for the use of
modelling Copenhagen clay till.
