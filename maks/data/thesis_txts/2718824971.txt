Abstract
With the advancement of artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) and deep learning (DL) within more crit-
ical use cases like autonomous driving and medical imaging, the need for trustworthy AI has
become ever so evident. While methods today exist for improving uncertainty quantiﬁcation
in DL, it is still largely an unexplored problem. In this thesis, I present a novel method called
Bayesian Triplet Loss for image classiﬁcation (BTL), which aims at improving the uncertainty
quantiﬁcation by inherently modeling the aleatoric uncertainty. With a basis in deep met-
ric learning, in conjunction with applying a Bayesian approach, the aleatoric uncertainty is
captured in the variance estimate of a stochastic metric embedding. The variance estimate
proves a good estimator for this, as it seems to consider not only the image quality (blurriness)
but also the potential interference of the actual labeled object in the image and class-speciﬁc
diﬀerences. By introducing two novel probability extraction methods to the stochastic metric-
embedding space of the BTL model, it is shown that within uncertainty quantiﬁcation, the
BTL model outperforms the non-calibrated DNN softmax classiﬁer (Vanilla). While it also
proves evident that the BTL model can neither compete on predictive performance nor on
uncertainty quantiﬁcation with a temperature-scaled Vanilla model, there are still plenty of
possibilities for further research. This is not only within the simple optimization of the model
architecture and training strategies.
The BTL model also shows promising results within
zero-shot learning both in predictive performance and, equally importantly, within uncer-
tainty quantiﬁcation.

Introduction
Motivation
Artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) have become a progressively more inte-
grated part of our everyday. With the exponential growth of computing power (from Moore’s
Law)[1] and the emergence of GPU-powered computing and deep learning, the potential of
AI within various use cases have become almost unlimited. Today artiﬁcial intelligence, in
the form of deep learning, has enabled the invention of self-driving cars (like Tesla[2]), multi-
functional language models (like GPT-3[3]), self-learning agents that, after only nine hours of
training, could beat not only the best human chess player but also the largest chess engine in
the world (AlphaZero[4]).
However, with great power comes great responsibility, which is an area of struggle for
deep learning models. Deep neural networks (DNN) are notoriously known for impressive
predictive performance but poor uncertainty quantiﬁcation[5]. This signiﬁcantly impacts the
potential of deep learning in a multitude of use cases where good uncertainty quantiﬁcation
is indispensable. This is, for instance, the case within medical imaging and self-driving cars,
where misclassiﬁcations can be a matter of life or death. Misclassifying medical images of
cancer might result in wrong treatment, or misclassifying a kid as a plastic bag might result
in a traﬃc accident. In both cases, it is about trusting the model, and if the model proclaims
that it is a plastic bag with 99.999% conﬁdence, even though 5% of the time it is, in fact, a
kid, we can and should not trust the model. In these cases, we would much instead prefer a
predictive-wise, worse performing model, as long as we can trust what it says, because then
we, as humans, can just take our precautions. It is imperative for further utilization of deep
learning in these use cases that we construct models with better uncertainty quantiﬁcation.
Uncertainty in Deep Learning
In modern classiﬁcation models using DNNs with a softmax-activated output layer, the en-
tropy of the output probability distribution over the classes is typically used to estimate
the predictive uncertainty. However, DNNs with softmax output are often overconﬁdent in
their predictions[6], which consequently aﬀects the uncertainty estimates and thus fails to
make good uncertainty quantiﬁcations[5]. Generally, it is diﬃcult to make good uncertainty
quantiﬁcations in deep networks because of the high number of parameters. Low computa-
tional methods exist for post-training calibration of the probabilities, like Platt scaling and
Temperature scaling[6], and they are considered good and simple baselines. More advanced
methods include Bayesian approximations like Monte Carlo dropout, deep ensemble models,

and Laplace approximations, which have all proven scalable to deep networks and, in practice,
work well[5]. There is, however, still room for improvement.
Deep Metric Learning
Deep Metric Learning (DML) has become a cornerstone in many computer vision applica-
tions[7]. It has been applied successfully within various types of image retrieval problems [8,
9, 10], face/car recognition/veriﬁcation problems [11, 12, 13], and clustering problems [14].
Although DML might not be the most commonly used model for image classiﬁcation, espe-
cially when you have plenty of labeled training data, it has still proven interesting from a
predictive performance standpoint as seen in [15, 16]. Within zero/few-shot learning, it has,
on the other hand, been applied very successfully in a multitude of cases like [10, 17, 18].
Conventional DML models are non-Bayesian models, which means that the latent em-
beddings are point estimates. Hence, DML for image retrieval and face/car recognition is
commonly Nearest Neighbor (NN)/distance based, like in [8, 12]. Within image classiﬁcation,
one has proposed to train a k-NN classiﬁer on the embeddings [16] and then use that for
predictions, while another bases predictions on k-NN with a Gaussian kernel[15].
Bayesian-like Deep Metric Learning
For better uncertainty quantiﬁcation in DML, one could use Bayesian approximations, as
described earlier, which should capture some of the epistemic uncertainty in the model. How-
ever, the aleatoric uncertainty would go unaddressed, which might be problematic for noisy
input. Stochastic rather than deterministic embeddings have been proposed in various articles
to address this [19, 20, 21]. All three proposals model the latent embeddings as multivari-
ate Gaussian distributions with a diagonal covariance matrix. [19] and [20] both concerns
themselves with image matching, [19] with face recognition, [20] with matching noisy MNIST
pairs. Shi Y. et al. [19] try to maximize the mutual log-likelihood that two embeddings stem-
ming from positive pairs of images are the same. Oh S. J. et al. [20] use a soft contrastive
loss between positive and negative pairs to create the embedding space, with a Monte Carlo
sampling approach to determine match probability on test images.
Bayesian Triplet Loss
My proposed model bases itself on a paper by Warburg F. et al. [22]. Warburg F. et al.
propose reformulating the deterministic triplet loss-based DML model to a stochastic version
called Bayesian Triplet Loss. Within image retrieval, they show promising results for uncer-
tainty quantiﬁcation without compromising on the predictive performance compared to the

conventional triplet loss-based model. By modeling the embeddings as probability distribu-
tions, they better capture the input-dependent heteroscedastic uncertainty. I will refer to this
type of model as Bayesian Deep Metric Learning (BDML).
Many diﬀerent metric loss types exist, with most types having a basis in contrastive loss
and triplet loss[23].
The more complex metric losses try to incorporate the structure of
the embedding space better, such as quadruplet loss[24], n-pairs mc-loss[25], lifted structure
loss[26], ranked list loss[27], etc. However, as also pointed out by Warburg F. et al., it has
been shown by Musgrave K. et al. in [23] that these more complex metric losses only provide
a marginally better performance, if any, than conventional contrastive and triplet loss. Thus,
for the same reason as Warburg F. et al., I will only focus on the triplet loss extension.
A Bayesian Triplet Loss Model for Image Classiﬁcation
Warburg F. et al., at test time, retrieve images using a NN approach and use the embedding
covariance of the squared Euclidean distance between the anchor and nearest neighbor as
an estimate for the uncertainty of that retrieval. I propose two novel ways of extracting a
full probability distribution over the class space, utilizing the entire approximate distribution
of the embeddings rather than the mean and variance separated. As such, the uncertainty
can be estimated by the entropy of the predictive distribution. One approach is based on a
Monte Carlo sampling strategy, while the other is inspired by a nearest neighbor Gaussian
kernels approach as done by Meyer B. J. et al. in [15]. Ideally, it should improve conﬁdence
and uncertainty calibration compared to baseline models, as this method inherently models
the aleatoric uncertainty. I refer to this model as a Bayesian Triplet Loss model for image
classiﬁcation (BTL). To the best of my knowledge, this image classiﬁcation approach has not
been investigated yet. I will compare this model to the Vanilla softmax DNN with and without
conventional calibration methods like Bayesian approximations and temperature scaling.

Background and Theory
2.1
Bayesian Deep Metric Learning
In this section, we will dive deeper into the theory behind Bayesian deep metric learning,
its origin in metric learning, and then the adaption to a Bayesian style formulation. This
section will also brieﬂy touch upon uncertainty in Machine Learning (ML), leading up to the
deﬁnition of Bayesian ML (BML). It is expected for the reader to have a broad understanding
of Neural Networks, especially Convolutional Neural Networks and Deep Neural Networks,
an understanding of transfer learning, variational inference, and a basic understanding of
probability theory for multivariate random variables.
2.1.1
Uncertainty in Machine Learning
In ML, predictive uncertainty is composed of two types: aleatoric and epistemic. Aleatoric1
uncertainty refers to the notion of inherent randomness in data[28], stemming from the vari-
ability in the outcome of experiments. Aleatoric uncertainty can be divided into homoscedastic
(constant for all input data) and heteroscedastic (variable depending on the input). In the
case of image classiﬁcation, the former could refer to the image resolution of the input images.
The latter could refer to the sharpness of the image, if the object is by itself or not, the
position of the object, and many more. Aleatoric uncertainty is said to be irreducible (given
the data).
Another type of uncertainty in ML is epistemic uncertainty2. Epistemic uncertainty is
caused by a "lack of knowledge/data"[28]. Epistemic uncertainty could stem from a lack of
training samples in certain areas of the domain3, but it could just as well stem from lack of
information for each sample4 (see illustration in Figure 1). An ML model that tries to model
the former is called a Bayesian ML model. This is achieved by Bayesian Inference, which in
short, is applying a prior distribution over the model parameters, P(✓), and then estimating a
posterior distribution over the model parameters, P(✓|x) from data x, using Bayes theorem.
Often it is impossible to ﬁnd the posterior distribution analytically, leading to approximations
such as Laplace approximations, Markow-Chain Monte Carlo, or variational inference (VI).
The main reason for estimating the posterior distribution is to get a better estimate of the

2.1.2
Metric Learning
The goal of metric learning is to learn a task-speciﬁc similarity function from data. The
aim is to ﬁnd a function that can embed objects into a latent space, where semantically similar
objects should be close to each other and dissimilar objects far away. The absolute position of
an object in the latent space is of no importance, in contrast to more "conventional" learning,
where the feature embeddings are often considered to express speciﬁc discriminative properties.

To design and train a metric learning model, you need to choose a metric[32] that de-

loss. So it does not matter how "spread" out a class is, as long as it can be distinguished with
some margin m to the other classes (see Figure 3 for an illustration).

2.1.3.3
The Intuition behind the Likelihood
In this section, I will provide a short heuristic explanation to give the reader an intuition
about the mechanics of the likelihood (9). Figure 4 shows a few cases of triplets (a −p −n)
before and after training. Suppose it is easy to distinguish the anchor and positive from the
anchor and the negative (easy distinction). In that case, it must be because their respective
location in the embedding space is correct. Hence we force upon a smaller variance for both
the anchor, negative, and positive. If it is not that easy to distinguish the pairs (medium
distinction), then we are not quite sure about their respective positions. The positive will
have a decrease in variance since it is on "the better side" of the negative and anchor. It
seems to be in a more optimal location. The anchor, on the other hand, we are not so sure
about. The location might be good but also bad, so we keep the variance as is. The variance
of the negative will increase because we are more convinced that this is in the wrong location
since we have two embeddings from the same class nearby. When it is tough to distinguish
the pairs from each other (hard distinction), we increase the variance of all of them, as we are
unsure about everything. In theory, these heuristic explanations and illustrations in Figure 4
should also result in an increase of the likelihood deﬁned in (9).

Data
For testing my implementation of the Bayesian Triplet Loss model for image classiﬁcation
(further referred to as the BTL model), I am using the Pascal VOC12 dataset[44]. This dataset

be problematic from a few standpoints regarding this model type. See Section 4.1 and 6 for

4.1.1
Hard-negative Mining
Hard-negative mining refers to extracting those negatives that are the most similar (in terms
of embedding) to the anchor. These negative samples consequently contribute most to the
loss function and are, as such, optimal for training the model.
Opposed to traditional Triplet Loss models, hard-negative mining might not be as crucial
for the BTL model. The reason is that for Triplet Loss, a triplet only contributes to the loss
when the embedding distance between the positive and negative pair is within the margin
(see (4)). Without hard-negative mining, one might experience inadequate training because
the optimizer is constantly fed triplets with "no information." For the BTL model, a triplet
always carries "some information" because the likelihood is probabilistic. Needless to say,
those negatives that are very dissimilar to the anchor carry very little information.
I perform hard-negative mining similarly to Warburg F. et al. The only diﬀerence is that
Warburg F. approximates the similarity measure between samples during mining by simply
considering the dot-product. In contrast, I use the more precise, albeit slower, true Euclidean
distance.

4.1.2
Hyper-parameter Notes and Optimization
In this section, I will brieﬂy describe some of the essential hyper-parameters of this model and
general training notes.
Margin and Variance Prior
I consider these hyperparameters concurrently because they, in conjunction, play a role in
shaping the embedding space. I found that increasing the margin will increase the eﬀective
variance of the embeddings (and their respective distances from each other), regardless of the
prior, and vice versa. Changing the prior variance eﬀectively changes the distances between
the embeddings (and the variances, naturally). During training, the model seems to adjust
the embedding space to the margin and prior variance. All other things equal, I found that
changing either of these did not signiﬁcantly change the model performance. However, the
prior variance, in particular, did aﬀect the conﬁdence and uncertainty calibration performance
to some extent. If one were to optimize this model to its fullest, the prior variance is worth
looking into.
I found that setting the margin to 0 and prior variance to 1/D, with D being the dimen-
sionality of the embedding space, yields decent results for both methods. This choice of prior
variance imposes an implicit l2−normalization on the embeddings, which has proven eﬀective
in other metric learning use-cases, like image retrieval[48].
KL Divergence
Since the KL divergence measure and the likelihood (9) are not on the same scale, I adjust
the KL divergence contribution to the loss function with a parameter β ⇡10−3. I found
that the model embeddings collapsed to the origin of the KL divergence contribution was too
high, leading to sub-optimal distinguishing of the classes. Furthermore, to avoid convergence
to a non-optimal local minimum at the beginning of training, I also use linear annealing of
the value of β for the ﬁrst few epochs to ensure the model learns to distinguish the diﬀerent
classes before being regularized.

4.1.3
Constructing the Model Database
Post-training of the model, we need to construct a model database with embeddings and their
corresponding labels to make predictions. The performance of both probability extraction
methods relies very much on this construction. Since this dataset is very imbalanced, we
have to address this issue as we would otherwise favourize some classes over others simply
because some classes are overrepresented. For the kNN-MC-min-dist method, the chance of
an overrepresented wrong class being the closest embedding to the query embedding increases
by the law of large numbers. For the Bayesian-kNN-Gauss-kernels method, the overall density
of such class is naturally larger.

samples as motorbike (see Appendix E), which gives the person class relative to motorbike
an unfair possibility of being predicted. To address this, I found that upsampling all classes
to a minimum of the average sample size per class yields decent results. This is done using
the same data augmentation strategies as when training the model, performed on randomly

Detecting OOD Samples
A well-calibrated model should, in theory, also be able to distinguish between in-distribution
(ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) samples. These models were only trained on 18 of the
total 20 classes, leaving out horses and cars. Horses and cars, however, are semantically fairly
close to cows/sheep and busses, respectively, which the model was trained on. This should
pose a somewhat challenging task for the BTL model, which is also what we see in Figure 21.
The BTL does not do as good of a job detecting OOD samples as the Vanilla model. The
Figure 21: These plots display the distributions of the unnormalized entropy for ID and OOD
samples across the BTL and Vanilla models. The Vanilla models manage much better to
distinguish between ID and OOD samples. The values annotate for each plot indicate the
Hellinger Distance between the ID- and OOD-entropy distributions. It is a metric returning
a value between 0 and 1, and the closer to 1, the more dissimilar.
value annotated with the plots is the Hellinger distances[51] between the ID- and OOD-entropy
distributions. The Hellinger distance is a metric function that returns a value between 0 and
1, and the closer to 1, the more dissimilar the distributions are. These values support the
claim that the BTL model struggles to diﬀerentiate between ID and OOD samples compared
to the Vanilla model.
This is an inherent weakness of the BTL model in combination with the probability ex-
traction methods used, as they do not take into account absolute dissimilarity/similarity to
samples in the model database, only relative. As seen in Appendix K, the OOD car samples
are frequently predicted as [bus, motorbike, boat, or person], and the OOD horse samples as
[cow, sheep, or person], all of which makes intuitive sense, given their semantic similarity and
the amount of person samples in the database. In order to detect OOD samples for the BTL
42 of 72
==PAGE:49==
Master’s Thesis
22-01-23
model, one would most likely also have to take into account absolute distances to the other
classes in the embedding space19.
Suppose we would have instead excluded semantically very distinguishable classes, like
aeroplanes and tv-monitors, and then trained the model. In that case, we see in Figure 21
that the BTL model does a much better job detecting OOD samples but is still not at the
same level as the Vanilla model.
However, while the nature of the BTL model has it struggling, natively, to detect OOD
samples, it is also one of its core strengths! This strength originates from the principles of
metric learning. The BTL model has never been optimized to classify speciﬁc objects but
instead learned a similarity function that should diﬀerentiate between the speciﬁc objects
trained on. This similarity function is not restricted to a ﬁxed number of classes, and as a
result, it is possible to do zero/few-shot learning. This has earlier been proven eﬀective[52].

6
General Thoughts and Future Work
This section will cover some previously discussed things, my general thoughts about the model
design, the model training, the model testing, and ideas for future work.
Data and Backbone Model
Have I had more time, it would have been a more fair evaluation of the BTL model if I had
made a more comprehensive study by also testing it against other datasets and with diﬀerent
backbone models. In section 4.1, I argue that the training procedures used are not meant
to optimize training for this speciﬁc dataset but rather procedures that are intended to work
in the general case. Of course, I never actually test this hypothesis, which is worth looking
into for future work. It would be interesting to see how the BTL model would have held up
against the Vanilla model with a (much) smaller backbone model. Naturally, the predictive
performance of both models would decrease. Still, I have a feeling the Vanilla model would
struggle more with uncertainty quantiﬁcation than the BTL model, as less information about
the image would pass through the backbone model. Needless to say, this is just speculation.
Training a BTL Model and Hyper-parameter Optimization
One of the cons of the BTL model is that it is tricky to do good hyperparameter optimization.
As explained earlier, the validation loss depends very much on some model design choices,
making it impossible to use for optimizing those parameters. Also, the validation loss might
not be a good indicator of the model’s performance, as it only considers the distinction between
the very challenging sample pairs (i.e., the hard negatives). Besides, there are a lot of design
choices, such as the network architecture of the mean and variance head, the output dimension,
learning rate, KL divergence contribution, prior variance, how to design the triplet sampling
procedure, etc. Testing all combinations is infeasible in practice, which was the main reason
why I wanted to come up with a general rule of thumb for some of these parameters and
procedures.
If I were to continue working with this type of model, I would start with the procedures
explained in Section 4.
Then to do proper hyper-parameter optimization of the ’simpler’
design choices, I would use performance metrics rather than the loss function. It does take a
bit of time to extract the probabilities of a validation/test dataset since, at each extraction,
you ﬁrst have to construct the model database (which is a model design choice by itself). Of
course, one should, for the sake of computational time, only consider those objects already
in the sample pool (memory) at each epoch (see Section 4.1.1 for further explanation), this
47 of 72
==PAGE:54==
Master’s Thesis
22-01-23
way avoiding passing all the images through the large ﬁxed backbone model again. Note that
these samples are often augmented, so it would by no means be an optimal database, but
hopefully, good enough to give an idea of the actual performance. I did not implement this,
but if I were to continue working on this type of model, I would deﬁnitely consider doing so.
Evaluation Metrics and Model Performance
Within predictive performance, many established metrics exist, for instance, the accuracy, the
mean Average Precision, the Receiver Operating Characteristic, and many more. However,
for measuring the conﬁdence and uncertainty calibration performance of multi-class classiﬁers,
there still is no agreed-upon "best practice". Variations of the ECE are commonly used for
measuring the conﬁdence calibration performance, whereas the relatively new invention, UCE,
has been used for measuring uncertainty calibration performance. Both measures require the
user to deﬁne a ﬁxed set of bins for the probability/uncertainty interval, which impacts how
the ECE variants and UCE measures are calculated. These metrics depend very much on the
choice of bins. Fairly early in the process, I chose these bin splits:
Bconf = {[0, 0.01], ]0.01, 0.05], ]0.05, 0.1], ]0.1, 0.2], ]0.2, 0.3], ]0.3, 0.4], ]0.4, 0.5], ]0.5, 0.6],
]0.6, 0.7], ]0.7, 0.8], ]0.8, 0.9], ]0.9, 0.95], ]0.95, 0.99], ]0.99, 1]}
Buncert = {[0, 0.0025], ]0.0025, 0.005], ]0.005, 0.01], ]0.01, 0.025], ]0.025, 0.05], ]0.05, 0.1],
]0.1, 0.2], ]0.2, 0.3], ]0.3, 0.4], ]0.4, 0.5], ]0.5, 0.75], ]0.75, 1]}.
The bins are smaller for the conﬁdence measures when the conﬁdence is very high or low
since the density of the predicted probabilities is higher here. The same reasoning goes for
the selection of the uncertainty bins. However, have I had chosen these bins diﬀerently, the
outputs would have been diﬀerent. To what extent, I did not investigate. Still, to mitigate
this possible randomness in the outcomes, I chose to include multiple measures for both the
conﬁdence and uncertainty calibration performance, hopefully reducing the randomness and
resulting in a more accurate picture of the diﬀerent models’ performances.
Constructing the Model Database and Probability Extraction Methods
Constructing the model database post-training properly is crucial for good model performance,
especially if the dataset is imbalanced, as we would otherwise unfairly favor some classes over
others. As discussed earlier (Section 4.1.3), in some cases, it might be okay to favor a few
classes by overrepresenting them in the model database. Preferably one should consider the
48 of 72
==PAGE:55==
Master’s Thesis
22-01-23
construction of the model database as a hyper-parameter tuning task, i.e., using a training
and validation dataset. It sometimes would make sense to upsample some classes more than
others to increase the probability of predicting those classes. This is something I did not
investigate thoroughly either, but something worth looking into going forward!
Furthermore, it is essential to keep in mind how many nearest neighbors are considered
for both probability extraction methods.
It should very much depend on the number of
samples from each class. I found that using half the average sample size per class in the model
database (prior to upsampling of the classes) yields a good balance between utilizing the most
information available and, at the same time, not favoring the overrepresented classes. I found
from testing that, for instance, using all samples in the model database for both methods
critically increased the prediction rate for the overrepresented person class, resulting in a
useless model.
BTL as an Autonomous Open-World Image Classiﬁer?
Where the BTL model has shown most promising is within zero-shot learning. Here, the
BTL model performs well when including OOD samples in the model database, both within
predictive performance and uncertainty quantiﬁcation.
To me, this raises the question of
whether the BTL model could be used as a self-learning open-world classiﬁer, i.e., being able
to identify and include new objects in the model all by itself. Of course, it would not be able
to label new objects meaningfully, and human supervision would indeed be advised!
It would undoubtedly require a better method for detecting anomaly/OOD samples, not in
the database, than just using the entropy as seen in Section 5.6. In [52], Jun Cen et al. propose
a method for detecting anomaly/OOD samples in deep metric learning for image segmentation
by considering a mixture of the predictive conﬁdence and the Euclidean distances of the query
embedding to the other class embeddings. This method is based on point estimates in the
embedding space. It would be interesting to make a stochastic adaption of this procedure for
future work. And if it turns out helpful, maybe expand to a self-learning classiﬁer.

Conclusion
The main goal of the Bayesian Triplet Loss model for image classiﬁcation was to improve
uncertainty quantiﬁcation, compared to conventional softmax classiﬁers, by inherently mod-
eling the aleatoric uncertainty. The quality was measured using conﬁdence and uncertainty
miscalibration metrics, such as Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and Expected Uncertainty
Calibration Error (UCE). With a basis in deep metric learning, in conjunction with applying
a Bayesian approach, the aleatoric uncertainty is captured in the variance estimate of the
stochastic metric embedding. The variances prove a good estimator for this, as it seems to
consider not only the image quality (blurriness) but also the potential interference of the ac-
tual labeled object in the image and class-speciﬁc diﬀerences. Needless to say, the variance
estimate should not be used as a direct measure of predictive uncertainty but instead thought
of as a mediator of the aleatoric uncertainty to either the two probability extraction methods
presented, both of which had their pros and cons. Within uncertainty quantiﬁcation, the
BTL model outperformed the baseline Vanilla DNN model without post-training calibrations.
When also accounting for the epistemic uncertainty, by applying conventional Bayesian ap-
proximations to the BTL model (MC Dropout, SWAG, or Deep Ensemble), the predictive
performance and uncertainty quantiﬁcation were further improved.
While it proved evident from the results that this implementation of the BTL model, how-
ever, could neither compete on predictive performance nor on conﬁdence/uncertainty quan-
tiﬁcation with the conventional softmax DNN model with temperature scaling calibration, at
least on this dataset, there are plenty of possibilities for further research. Just naming a few,
it would be worth looking into a better optimization of the network architectures (mean and
variance heads), the output dimension size, the training optimizer used, the hard-negative
mining procedure, and the construction of the model database post-training. For the latter,
there might even be a better probability extraction method. However, suppose any improve-
ments are found negligible in the grand scheme of things. In that case, the BTL model has still
shown promising results within zero-shot learning, both in predictive performance, which was
already a known fact for metric learning models, but equally important, within uncertainty
quantiﬁcation. Expanding model capability in just the matter of minutes it takes to include
OOD samples in the model database and still being able to trust the probabilities returned
by the model can prove very useful in various autonomous tasks. Here the BTL model should
deﬁnitely be considered an exciting model.
