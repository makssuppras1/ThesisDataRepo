Abstract
Wrinkle defects are prevalent in wind turbine blades and are a leading cause of blade failure. Accurate mod-
eling of the compression strength of wrinkled composite laminates is needed by manufacturers.
Based on the creation of a parametric ﬁnite element model of a through-thickness wrinkle defect, along with a
series of geometric and material parameter studies, this research seeks to determine the compressive strength
of various kinds of wrinkled composite laminates. A 2D FE model of alternating layers of elastic-plastic matrix
and elastic orthotropic ﬁber laminae is used. Plastic microbuckling (kinking) strength is given by the limit
load, while delamination strength is determined from a maximum shear stress criterion.
The main conclusion is that compression strength of a wrinkled laminate is strongly dependent on the max-
imum initial ﬁber misalignment angle. There is also strong sensitivity to the height to length ratio. Further-
more it is found that higher matrix yield strength increases compression strength. Finally, it is found that in
hybrid wrinkles, an increasing amount of carbon increases the compression strength.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Defects in Wind Turbine Blades
Wind turbine blades are critical components of wind turbines, not only because they are responsible for
collecting energy from the wind, but also because their size and weight dictate how the other components
such as the hub, tower and foundation are built. With the push towards larger wind turbines, minimizing
the weight and cost of wind turbine blades is crucial. Furthermore, the longer blades must be designed to
withstand even greater extreme loads without failure. Thus a good design must achieve a balance between
the structural integrity of the blades and their cost.
Unfortunately, modern blades do not always live up to their designed lifetime. According to Griﬃn and Malkin
(2011, p.1), "in recent years numerous wind turbine manufacturers have been aﬀected by signiﬁcant blade
problems that range from catastrophic failure to extensive remediation and retroﬁt campaigns." Failure of such
large blades can be extremely dangerous. Furthermore, it can become immensely expensive to replace these
blades after failure. According to Nelson et al. (2011, p.1), preventing failures "has a profound economic impact
on the continued development of the wind turbine industry." As such, wind turbine blade manufacturers spend
a signiﬁcant amount of energy in trying to prevent blade failures, but despite this, failures do occur.
Griﬃn and Malkin (2011, p.2) examined many cases of blade failures across diﬀerent manufacturers and own-
ers and found that, in their experience, the leading cause of failure in terms of "frequency and severity" are
manufacturing defects, such as wrinkles in laminates, defective bond lines and dry ﬁbers. The presence of
these ﬂaws in blade components degrade the structural integrity of the blade as a whole and can lead to
catastrophic failure in the right conditions.
The prevalence of such ﬂaws are an inherent aspect of the manufacturing process of wind turbine blades.
As Mandell et al. (2003, p.1) states, "since blades must be produced at a ﬁnished cost in the range of $10/Kg,
only relatively inexpensive resins, reinforcements and processes are competitive. The low cost materials and
processes combined with strength-driven design produce a signiﬁcant concern with the eﬀects of ﬂaws on the
various strength-based properties." Essentially, the focus on low-cost in the wind industry clashes with the
stringent strength requirements. Ultimately, the result is that the "acceptable defects often included in wind
turbine blades far exceed what would be acceptable in other industries, such as aerospace" (Nelson et al. 2011,
p.2).
Nelson et al. (2011, p.2) goes on to note that further research of the criticality of these ﬂaws is required. A
main goal of this thesis paper is to explore the criticality of one of the most typical ﬂaws in wind turbine
blades: ﬁber waviness. In the following section, a background of ﬁber waviness is presented.

1.2
Fiber Waviness
Fiber waviness defects, also known as wrinkles, "are one of the predominant sources of problems for wind
turbine blade structural integrity" (Griﬃn and Malkin 2011, p.3). Fiber waviness in composite laminates is
deﬁned by the presence of a group of ﬁbers whose orientation is inclined with respect to the nominal ﬁber
direction of the laminate. Waviness is often divided into two groups: out of plane waviness, where the ﬁbers
are inclined with respect to the plane of the laminate, and in plane waviness where the ﬁbers are oriented
within the plane of the laminate. Fiber waviness ﬂaws, or wrinkles, can occur in both the face sheets of
composite sandwich structures in the blades, as well as in the main laminate, also known as the spar cap.
Wrinkles which occur in only a fraction of the thickness of a laminate are known as partial wrinkles. Wrinkles
which occupy the full thickness of the laminate are known as through-thickness wrinkles. An out of plane
partial wrinkle is shown in Figure 1.1. The plane of the laminate in this case extends left to right and through
the page, while the waviness occurs out of this plane, hence the name out-of-plane waviness.
Fiber waviness, as it occurs in wind turbine blades, "has three main causes: waviness introduced in processing,
as by resin ﬂow in an infusion process...waviness inherent in woven or stitched fabrics...and ﬁbre misalign-
ment associated with ﬂaws and structural details" (Veers et al. 2003, p.250). As Mandell et al. (2003, p.14)
states, wrinkles are essentially an inherent aspect of the "low-cost composites typical of the wind turbine in-
dustry." Their prevalence is one key reason for further research, and the other is the severity of their eﬀect on
structural integrity. Several investigations have shown that laminates with through-thickness wrinkles can
experience a reduction in compression strength of up to 50% (Mandell et al. 2003, p.14). While tensile strength
may also be an issue, it is mostly compression which is critical.
Because these ﬂaws are so critical and common, it is important for wind turbine blade manufacturers to obtain
an in depth understanding of their eﬀect on compression strength. The most severe case would be a through-
thickness wrinkle that occurs in the unidirectional main laminate, which acts as the primary load carrying
structure.
1.3
Research Objective and Relevance
The main objective of this thesis is to determine the compression strength of unidirectional ﬁber laminates
with through-thickness wrinkles by implementing a parametric ﬁnite element model of the wrinkles. There
are several relevant applications for such a model when designing and manufacturing a wind turbine blade.
For example, an obvious solution to wrinkles is to detect them and make repairs to remove them. However,

Chapter 2
Literature Study: Modeling of Compression
Strength
This literature study aims to review the modeling and analysis of composites with small defects under com-
pressive loading for strength determination. The main question driving this study is, what is an appropriate
model for characterizing the compression strength of wrinkled ﬁber composite laminates? The goal is to
examine the various analytical and numerical models of defects in composites, and consequently determine
which failure modes and physical mechanisms must be modeled to get a suﬃciently complete representation
of the wrinkled ﬁber laminates that are typically found in wind turbine blades.
First, a very brief overview of the most common compressive failure modes found in composites is presented
in section 2.1. Subsequently, a variety of models are examined. These are broken into two main categories:
instability approaches and failure criterion approaches. The main diﬀerence between these two categories
is how the authors deﬁne compressive failure. In this review, models that employ each approach will be
evaluated with regards to their assumptions and their results. Diﬀerences and overlaps between models in
both categories will be analyzed. A discussion of the speciﬁc conclusions reached by this review will be given.
Finally, a consolidated theoretical position concerning an appropriate model will be presented, based on the
analysis of the diﬀerent models
2.1
Compressive Failure Modes of Polymer Fiber Composites
According to Fleck (1997, p.44), "The [compressive failure] mode is particularly sensitive to the shear proper-
ties of the matrix and to the degree of imperfection of the composite (typically, ﬁber waviness)." Thus most
analyses require modeling of multiple failure modes at the same, in order to determine which of the failure
modes prevails in a given situation.
As such, Fleck (1997) presents an excellent overview of the most common failure modes found in composite
laminates under compression. Figure 2.1 presents a good a schematic of these various overviews.
In Figure 2.1a, elastic microbuckling is depicted, where a global elastic instability is the cause of failure. A
model by Rosen (1965) of elastic microbuckling is presented in detail in section 2.2. Figure 2.1b describes
plastic microbuckling, which is a localized instability. A model by Budiansky (1983) of this is also described
in detail section 2.2 as it is a very important failure mode, and occurs often in the type of composite laminates
found in wind turbine blades.
Figure 2.1c depicts ﬁber crushing. This is deﬁned as failure of the ﬁbers themselves at the microscopic level.
This occurs when the axial strain in the ﬁbers reach an intrinsic crushing strain εfc. The exact failure here is
dependent on the ﬁbers: glass ﬁbers tend to crush by longitudinal splitting, while carbon ﬁbers are crushed
due to local microbuckling of the individual ﬁbers (Budiansky and Fleck 1993, p. 187).

Matrix splitting, which is depicted in Figure 2.1d is deﬁned as the development of matrix cracking parallel to
the axial direction due to low matrix toughness. Fleck (1997, p.55) notes, "This failure mode dominates when
the stiﬀness of the matrix exceeds that of the ﬁbers, and when the composite has a low toughness and high
porosity." This is not typically the case for the polymer ﬁber composites used in wind turbine laminates, and
will not be considered.
A buckle delamination, depicted in Figure 2.1e is buckling of a surface layer, which can be understood as
splitting at the surface when there is already a large delamination present. Because the separated layer would
be so thin, its eﬀective buckling load would be very low.
Finally, as shown in Figure 2.1f, shear band formation can occur. This is in fact ductile failure of the matrix,
which occurs when the ﬁber volume fraction is so low that it does not dictate the failure of the composite.
Again, this is not the case for the composites of interest, and is thus not relevant.
In summary the most relevant failure modes to consider for wind turbine composite laminates are microbuck-
ling, ﬁber crushing and buckling delamination. Following is a discussion of several studies that aim to model
these failure modes in polymer ﬁber composites.
2.2
Instability Approach
In this section, models that employ an instability approach to strength determination are examined, wherein
the point at which stability is lost is considered the failure load. This point is called the critical point, and it
is deﬁned as the point in loading at which the stiﬀness of the structure is no longer positive deﬁnite. Only
when the applied load reaches this point, can an instability occur.
==PAGE:25==
2.2 Instability Approach
7
A classical example of an instability is the buckling of an Euler-Bernoulli column, which undergoes bifur-
cation. At the critical point, the structure has two possible equilibrium paths to follow, which can lead the
structure to undergo drastic deformation. The buckled conﬁguration may or may not be able to sustain further
loading, however in many cases, structures are designed to not buckle.
Another example of instability is the existence of a limit point, beyond which the structure cannot support
any additional load. This may lead to a collapse load, or a snap-back/snap-through type behavior, wherein
the structure dynamically adopts a new conﬁguration. In either case, failure can occur.
By using an instability approach in assessing composite compressive strength, an assumption is made that
the compressive strength is given by the load at which the critical point is reached. This is the underlying
assumption of the models that are discussed in this section.
These models are divided into two subcategories: those that analyze kink-band micro-structures, and those
that analyze ﬁber waviness.
2.2.1
Kink-Band Analysis
A pioneering model that employs the bifurcation approach was developed by Rosen (1965). The main result
of his work is that failure occurs in the form of elastic bifurcation caused by matrix shear buckling parallel
to the ﬁbers. In this analytical model, the composite is considered as a perfectly aligned layered structure,
composed of elastic inﬁnite ﬁbers embedded in an elastic matrix material. He determined that the mode of
failure with the lowest compressive strength was in phase buckling of the ﬁbers into a sinusoidal waveform.
Furthermore, the lowest compressive strength is found for an inﬁnite wavelength buckling mode, i.e a uniform
rotation of all the ﬁbers by an angle φ. He obtained an analytical expression of the compressive strength as
equal to the elastic shear modulus of the full laminate, as shown in Equation 2.1. A result to take away from
the work of Rosen (1965) is that the shear behavior of the matrix plays an important role in the overall failure
of the composite structure. This result was derived even when assuming completely elastic material, and thus
it seems that any appropriate model for the compressive strength of wrinkles should place importance on
matrix shear behavior.

It can be concluded from the work of both Argon (1972) and Budiansky and Fleck (1993) that the key parame-
ters for predicting kinking stresses are the ﬁber misalignment angle and the plasticity parameters. Budiansky
and Fleck (1993) use a fully nonlinear matrix model, and the resulting analytical expression, given in Equa-
tion 2.4, is shown to be reasonable when compared to experiments.
These studies presented so far have a clear relevance for modeling wrinkled laminates. It is clear that an
accurate matrix plasticity model is necessary to predict microbuckling failure in wrinkled composites, and the
microbuckling stress is a function of the ﬁber misalignment. Furthermore, all of the above analyses determine
that compressive failure occurs through matrix shear, and thus the shear behavior of the composite laminate
is important to model accurately.
However, both Argon (1972) and Budiansky and Fleck (1993) assume that an idealized kink-band structure
is already present in the composite. This is thus an equilibrium approach to determining the critical stress.
Jensen and Christoﬀersen (1997) employ an energy approach to stability, in which they build a detailed plane-
strain constitutive model based on the ﬁber and matrix nonlinearities and determine the compressive strength
by ﬁnding the load at which the stiﬀness matrix ceases to be positive deﬁnite. They show the properties of
the ﬁbers are important as well, despite the large stiﬀness diﬀerence between matrix and ﬁbers. Pane and
Jensen (2004) also employ the same constitutive model. Unlike Budiansky and Fleck (1993), a ﬁnite geometry
is considered as well as a layered material. The constitutive behavior of the layers were derived in depth by
means of both J2-deformation and J2-ﬂow. Thus, the resulting material modeling is highly accurate compared
to the hardening behavior described Budiansky and Fleck (1993).
Pane and Jensen (2004) then conducted parametric studies to determine the eﬀect of ﬁber volume fraction
and other material properties. Their ﬁnal result was that "The eﬀects of the ﬁber to matrix moduli ratio and
the ﬁber volume fraction appear to be important" (Pane and Jensen 2004, p.371), which is a result that is not
found in the analysis of Budiansky and Fleck (1993) or Argon (1972).
Thus far, it is clear that kinking failure is a very important failure mechanism in polymer matrix compos-
ites. Modeling of such failure requires accurate material models for both the ﬁber and matrix constituents,
with particular importance placed on the plasticity of the matrix. Finally, the eﬀect of ﬁber misalignment is
signiﬁcant to the compressive failure strength, and thus is an important parameter.

2.2.2
Fiber Waviness
Kyriakides et al. (1995) investigated the compressive strength of ﬁber composite laminates with sinusoidal
imperfections. The geometry of these imperfections is very similar to the patterns of ﬁber waviness found in
wind turbine laminates, and thus this study is highly relevant. Similar to the work of Budiansky and Fleck
(1993), a fully nonlinear analysis is conducted, including matrix plasticity and geometric nonlinearity. The
geometric nonlinearity analysis is done by employing the ﬁnite element method (FEM) and using the arc-
length method for tracing the equilibrium path past the load peak. Another diﬀerence between this work and
the work of Budiansky and Fleck (1993), Argon (1972) and Rosen (1965) is that a ﬁnite geometry is assumed.
This is a matter of necessity when implementing an FE model, however, it only extends the generality of the
results to any ﬁxed geometry. The model determines the limit load of the structure, beyond which a snap back
behavior is found. This load is considered the failure load. Interestingly, beyond the limit load, it is found that
"highly deformed inclined bands develop nautrally through a process of localization of deformation following
the limit load" (Kyriakides et al. 1995, p.733). Thus, Kyriakides et al. (1995) were able to predict the onset of
kink-bands with this model.
In the model of Kyriakides et al. (1995) a layered material is used of alternating ﬁbers and matrix. The matrix
is modeled as a Ramberg-Osgood hardening plastic, with a J2 ﬂow theory of plasticity used. The compressive
strength is found for a varying set of parameters using the FE model.
Another model that investigates sinusoidal ﬁber waviness with the FE method is given by Vogler et al. (2000).
The approach of Kyriakides et al. (1995) is extended by using 3D elements instead of 2D. This extension makes
the results more general than the results of all the previously mentioned models, where the assumption of
plane strain was inherent. An additional investigation done by Vogler et al. (2000) is the eﬀect of an applied
shear stress. It was determined that the direction of shear can have a weakening or strengthening eﬀect.
An interesting FE model of ﬁber waviness that includes material and geometric nonlinearity is given by Liu
et al. (2004). The focus in this study is random distribution of waviness, in contrast to the uniform/linearly
distributed waviness found in the model of Kyriakides et al. (1995). The results show how the macroscopic
compressive strength is sensitive to the distribution parameters of the defects.
In all, ﬁber waviness models are directly related to wrinkled laminates, and thus are highly relevant. It can
be concluded from the results of these models that material and geometric nonlinearity are essential aspects
of these models, and thus should be included in any model of a wrinkled ﬁber laminate. Finally, they show
the beneﬁt of creating parametric FE models, which allow for easy manipulation of the input parameters to
obtain a variation of the kinking strength.
2.3
Failure Criteria Approach
In contrast to the previously mentioned models, the ones in this section employ failure criterion to deﬁne
failure. This means that the state of stress in the structure is compared to known material strength properties
to determine if the structure has failed. Thus, the following models require more material speciﬁc information

than the previously mentioned models. Two sets of models are presented in this subsection, linear elastic
models and fully nonlinear models.
2.3.1
Linear Elastic Models
A signiﬁcant model that employs a failure criterion is presented by Hsiao and Daniel (1996). Similar to Kyr-
iakides et al. (1995), the model geometry is one of a sinusoid of variable amplitude and length, and variable
distribution within the laminate. However, unlike all the models that employ a bifurcation approach, this
model is purely linear. It makes use of classical laminate theory to predict the behavior of the structure under
compression. Expressions for the local compliance matrix for small elements in a representative volume are
developed, and assumptions of plane stress in each layer are used. Similar to Budiansky and Fleck (1993) the
model assumes homogeneous material properties, and ignores the eﬀects of discrete layers. An incremental
analysis is used to compute strains from stresses using the compliance matrix. The model was veriﬁed with
a very limited number of experiments, but showed good agreement.
In contrast to the previous studies, a Tsai-Wu failure criterion was used to predict when failure occurred.
Thus, the model predicted distinct failures in each ply. To model the behavior after failure of the ﬁrst ply, a
bilinear progressive damage model was used. This aspect was not included in the analysis of either Budiansky
and Fleck (1993) or Kyriakides et al. (1995). In those models, material degradation is only represented by the
plastic behavior of the matrix.
Finally, the model exhibited gradual failure between the ﬁrst ply and the last ply. This is in contrast to the
failure found in other studies in this section. This failure took the form of delamination between layers. The
gradual propagation of these delaminations leads to global buckling of the individual separated layers.
The study by Hsiao and Daniel (1996) is relevant because it shows the importance of the distribution of the
wrinkles within the laminate, as well as the defect size. The use of a progressive damage model is interesting,
as it assumes that the failure in wrinkles occurs in distinct phases. However, the limited linear analysis of this
study neglects material and geometric non-linearity, which was shown to be important in other work.
Another model that relies on the Tsai-Wu failure criterion to predict compressive strength is described by
Garnich and Karami (2005). Similar to Kyriakides et al. (1995), a ﬁnite element model of wavy ﬁbers is used,
however, with homogeneous properties that are derived from micro-mechanics considerations. The main
focus on the paper is the eﬀect of localization on strength reduction, speciﬁcally, when the waviness pattern
is not continuous. The results showed that "when the waviness is localized in both transverse directions, the
predicted strength reductions are in the order of 15%" (Garnich and Karami 2005, p.1244), which is signiﬁcantly
less than for the case of continuous waviness. Thus, the addition of undisturbed material to the structure
would greatly aﬀect the strength reduction.
These two models are simpliﬁed by the assumptions of linear elasticity, and thus do not require excessive
computational expense. However, it is clear from the discussion of section 2.2 that nonlinearity plays an im-
portant role. In the following section, nonlinear models that employ the failure criteria to identify compressive
strength are discussed.
2.3.2
Nonlinear Models
Takahashi and Chou (1987) describe a nonlinear incremental analysis used together with the maximum stress
failure criterion to predict the failure strength of carbon/glass hybrid laminates. In particular, nonlinearity in
the lamina shear-stress strain relation and reorientation of the ﬁbers under large deformation are considered,
as was done in the model of Kyriakides et al. (1995). However, a ﬁnite strain formulation is used instead to pre-
dict the eﬀect of large strains. This is a unique approach, and typically not considered by other authors due to
the complexity of the constitutive relationship. The results of the study were in agreement with experimental

tests conducted by the authors. Strength was found by using a maximum stress criterion, in contrast to the
cohesive zone models and progressive damage models presented later in this subsection. The work empha-
sizes the importance of the in-plane shear behavior of the composite laminate, in particular the nonlinearity
of this response.
A detailed experimental and numerical study of wrinkle defects in glass ﬁber reinforced sandwich structures
is described by Leong et al. (2012). Of relevance to this review is the analysis of facesheet failure. Plies in
the facesheets were modeled as homogeneous materials. A 3D model was used, as in Vogler et al. (2000). It
was found that in conjunction with the NU failure criterion (Northwestern University), that the FE model was
capable of predicting the initiation of layer-wise delamination. This mechanism was taken to be the primary
failure mode, in contrast to the models in section 2.2. The model’s reliance on a failure criterion requires
detailed material strength properties, and unfortunately it can be diﬃcult to obtain such data accurately.
However, the focus on the interlaminar shear damage is interesting. From their experimental results, it was
found that this is indeed the main failure mode. This is extremely relevant, since they are modeling exactly
the wrinkles found in wind turbine blades. In the model to be made of a wrinkled laminate, the interlaminar
shear stress could be given as an output to see if it is a limiting factor.
A novel approach to modeling failure is presented by Mukhopadhyay et al. (2015), in which they make use
of cohesive zone elements and continuum damage models in a 3D FE model. The modeling was informed by
a series of experimental tests, which found that the two main modes of failure were ﬁber compression and
interlaminar shear failure (as found by Leong et al. (2012)). The study found that for imperfection angles larger
than a certain threshold, the failure mechanism changes (Mukhopadhyay et al. 2015, p.142). In a sense, this
result is similar to the found by Kyriakides et al. (1995), who demonstrated that the post-instability behavior
becomes softer with increasing misalignment angle. An interesting aspect of this study is the usage of cohesive
zone modeling and progressive damage. The progressive damage model, like the one used by Hsiao and Daniel
(1996) allows for an examination of the structure past the point of initial fracture. This is not necessarily an
aspect of the models in section 2.2, where material degradation is only present due to plasticity eﬀects. This
in conjunction with cohesive zone elements to model the region in between fractured plies, could provide
greater insight, albeit with added computational expense.
On the other hand, the approaches of Leong et al. (2012) and Mukhopadhyay et al. (2015) required detailed
knowledge of the failure mechanisms before the modeling began. Lemanski et al. (2013) attempt to overcome
this by creating a model that can predict a variety of failure mechanisms for specimens that appear superﬁ-
cially similar. The main focus then is evaluating strength by allowing for "continuum failure within the plies
and for delamination failure between plies" (Lemanski et al. 2013, p.35). By using this generalized approach, it
was shown that all failure mechanisms are typically present in any specimen, but it is the sequence of events
that is of most importance when determining the ultimate failure load. The model is well suited for situations
"where multiple possible failure mechanisms exist and the actual mechanism of failure is not known a pri-
ori." (Lemanski et al. 2013, p.35). One drawback, however, is that the model has a strong dependency on the
cohesive zone strength and fracture energy inputs.
It is of interest consequently to see how cohesive zone methods can be used in composite structures. Abrate
et al. (2015) oﬀers a survey of cohesive zone models (CZM) and their relation to both impact and delamination.
Delamination is of particular interest in wrinkled laminates, as was shown by the experimental tests of Leong
et al. (2012). The main advantage of the cohesive zone method is that it can "be implemented in a general
purpose ﬁnite element code in a way that is not problem-dependent" (Abrate et al. 2015, p.2608). This is
indeed useful for the proposed work, as it lends itself to parametric studies. However, it is noted that "a
reﬁned ﬁnite element model is required to determine interfacial stresses accurately and small size elements
are also needed to avoid stability problems with cohesive elements" (Abrate et al. 2015, p.2608). This is a
problem faced by both Lemanski et al. (2013) and Mukhopadhyay et al. (2015). The solution then would be to
have a reﬁned mesh covering the interlaminar regions.

2.4
Research Methodology
From this discussion a few key conclusions can be made regarding the compression strength of composite
laminates with wrinkle defects. First, the key failure mechanisms are plastic microbuckling (kink band for-
mation) and delamination, which can lead to Euler buckling of the individual layers. Various experimental
studies have found these failure criteria in diﬀerent geometries and materials. Several attempts to model them
have also been undertaken, both numerical and analytical.
It is clear that to model failure by plastic microbuckling, a non-linear analysis is required, which must include
the eﬀect of plastic deformation of the matrix. Nonlinearity of the ﬁbers can have an eﬀect on these results,
and the eﬀect of ﬁnite ﬁber stiﬀness can be signiﬁcant. Most models have been 2D in nature, which was
deemed suﬃcient.
To model delamination, a variety of methods can be used including stress-based failure criteria, or the use of
cohesive zone modeling incorporated in a ﬁnite element model. In either case, prediction of the delamination
strength is quite dependent on the model inputs, i.e the materials strengths used in a failure criterion, or the
constitutive behaviors of the cohesive elements.
In this study, both failure mechanisms will be modeled, since they are both pertinent. A ﬁnite element (FE)
model will be created of a unidirectional through-thickness wrinkle defect. Through-thickness wrinkles have
the greatest impact on reducing compression strength, and thus are a good starting point, to which modeling
of partial wrinkles can be added. Also unidirectional laminae are the most relevant types of laminates to
model, since the main load carrying structure of the wind turbine is made of UD laminae. The FE model will
be made of 2D continuum elements, as 3D eﬀects are beyond the scope of this thesis, and can be investigated
in subsequent studies. A layered structure similar to Kyriakides et al. (1995) will be used, with an elastic plastic
matrix model to allow for the microbuckling instability. However, in this thesis, instead of individual ﬁbers
or ﬁber bundles being modeled, each "ﬁber layer" will instead be composed of an orthotropic unidirectional
laminae, with an average set of properties. This is a novel approach not used before, and is much more relevant
to the macroscopic wrinkles found in wind turbine blades, (where several laminae form the wrinkle). The ﬁber
laminae will initially be considered linear elastic, but the model will be setup such that laimna nonlinearity
can be easily added.
By adopting the layered structure with sinusoidal waviness and elastic-plastic matrix, the model should be
able to predict microbuckling ("kinking") failure, the strength for which can be determined from the limit load,
as presented in Kyriakides et al. (1995). The delamination failure requires more consideration. As discussed,
cohesive zone models can be very useful in modeling the initiation and propagation of cracks or delaminations.
This is particularly feasible in a layered structure, where the interface between the ﬁber laminae and matrix is
clearly deﬁned. However, cohesive zone models require some material information in the form of a traction-
separation law, and this information is not available at the moment, making CZM infeasible. On the other
hand, there is not much interest in modeling the propagation of delaminations, as several experimental studies
have shown that failure occurs quickly and without any warning. It seems likely that the load at which any
delamination develops is the compressive failure load, since the wrinkle would fail catastrophically after that.
Determining the load at which a delamination would develop could instead be accomplished by means of
a failure criterion, such as the Tsai-Wu criterion used in Hsiao and Daniel (1996). However, this particular
failure criterion requires several material strengths for the composite laminate in all of its principal material
directions, again making this infeasible for this study. Instead, it is noted that most authors mentioned in this
literature review denoted the importance of the interlaminar shear stress towards the development of delam-
ination in wrinkle defects. As such, a maximum shear stress criterion will be used to indicate delamination
failure.
The entire numerical model will be implemented in Abaqus CAE, by using its interface with the python
programming language. This approach has several advantages. First, using a veriﬁed commercial software
prevents the possibility of programming errors being made when creating an FE code from scratch. However,
with the python scripting, it will be very simple to run parametric studies of the diﬀerent parameters to
==PAGE:31==
2.4 Research Methodology
13
determine their impacts on strength. Finally, it is very easy to extend the model capabilities in this manner,
because several of the routines written can simply be reused, and others can be added to.
The main hypothesis of this work is that for low values of misalignment angle, microbuckling failure will
occur, as often found in aligned laminates without wrinkle defects. However, there exists a misalignment
angle for a ﬁxed geometry and material system, at which the failure mechanism smoothly transitions to
delamination. This hypothesis is supported by the results found by Mukhopadhyay et al. Throughout this
paper, several parametric studies of the misalignment angle will be conducted to explore the implications of
this hypothesis. The hypothesis is then tested by comparison to experimental results, the results of which are
shown in a separate report (Manikarnika 2016).
Because of the simple geometry used to model a through-thickness wrinkle, simple analytical expressions can
be developed to predict the model behavior in the elastic case. This can be used to verify the model, and also
to help understand the mechanics of strength reduction phenomenon. In the next chapter, these analytical
expressions are derived, using classical beam theory and the theory of beam columns.

infusion occurs, resin impregnates the small spaces between individual ﬁbers in the ﬁber bundle. However,

in between thin layers of pure resin.

and pure resin phases. Figure 4.3 shows the full model, with the ﬁber bundle phases in green and the pure resin
phases highlighted in red. The partitions are made by using the partition by sketch feature in Abaqus CAE.
The layer boundaries are deﬁned by curves as in Equation 4.1, with varying vertical oﬀsets, which depend on
the thickness of each phase.
Figure 4.4 shows the geometry of two adjacent layers, as made in Abaqus CAE. The thickness of the ﬁber

of the ﬁber lamina tﬁber/tply is an input to the model. This input is called the ﬁber lamina volume fraction

is typically about 20%. However, in most cases, the resin layer is divided into two smaller layers, which go on
the top and bottom of the ﬁber lamina in order to make a symmetric cross-section.

4.2
Material Model Inputs
The model described in this paper is a 2-phase layered model. One phase is the elastic-plastic matrix phase.
The other is the elastic ﬁber lamina phase. Following are descriptions of how each material is modeled, and
how that description enters Abaqus CAE.
4.2.1
Elastic-Plastic Matrix
The matrix material is considered to be an homogeneous isotropic material. In order to obtain the kinking
instability as described by Budiansky and Fleck (1993) and Kyriakides et al. (1995), the matrix material must
have some plastic behavior. This is because the instability cannot occur unless the matrix stiﬀness is reduced
over the course of loading.
In the approach of Budiansky (1983), an elastic-perfectly plastic model was used for the matrix material. This
was suﬃcient for the kinking instability to occur, however this sort of model is unrealistic. The matrix in
composites often exhibit strain hardening behavior, and as such, Budiansky and Fleck and Kyriakides et al.
make use of a Ramberg-Osgood power law hardening description to model the matrix material. This is the
approach taken in this thesis as well.
The default Abaqus plasticity model is used to model the matrix material. It assumes an associated plastic ﬂow
theory, with a von Mises yield surface, for which the equivalent stress is based on the second deviatoric stress
tensor invariant (J2). Isotropic hardening is used, as there is no interest in cyclic loading and unloading. The
input required for the default isotropic hardening plasticity model in Abaqus is a table of true yield stresses
vs. logarithmic plastic strains. This can be input from experimental data, or from a hardening law, such as
the Ramberg-Osgood formulation. In this study, experimental data is used up until the maximum stress, after
which the hardening relation is used to extrapolate to higher stresses.

relation between the total strain and the total stress. The ﬁrst term on the right hand side of Equation 4.5 is the

4.2.2

Unlike the matrix material, the ﬁber laminae are modeled as linear elastic. Although there may be some
nonlinearity in the ﬁbers, especially in carbon, this is neglected in this model. However, as Yokozeki et al.
(2005) points out, using a non-linear ﬁber model results in a larger strength reduction than in the linear case.
However, in the future, it would be easy to implement such a nonlinear elastic constitutive model for the ﬁber
laminae.
Furthermore, while the matrix material is isotropic, the ﬁber laminae are transversely isotropic. This means 5
engineering constants are required to fully characterize the material behavior of the lamina. In many cases, it
is often possible to conduct a variety of mechanical tests on a lamina itself to obtain these constants. However,
another approach is to use simple formulae that combine the properties of the constituents to obtain the
properties of the lamina. Thus, with separate data for the ﬁbers and the resin, one can determine the lamina
properties as a function of the ﬁber volume fraction in the lamina.
An extensive amount of data can be found on the properties of various glass and carbon ﬁbers. For this thesis,
ﬁber properties are derived from Soden et al. (1998), speciﬁcally corresponding to E-glass and AS4 carbon

4.3
Boundary Conditions
The same boundary conditions described in section 3.2 are used in the actual model. Instead of an applied
load, a uniform displacement in the global ˆx direction is applied to all nodes on the left boundary. The
value of the displacement is denoted δ, and is set to 5% of the half-length L/2 in most cases (because of
symmetry this leads to an overall end-shortening of 5% as well). This displacement-driven loading emulates
the static compression tests. It also ensures that the stiﬀglass layers and soft matrix layers undergo uniform
deformation, which would not be the case with a pressure load, under which the left edge would not deform
uniformly.
Since the uniform horizontal displacement is applied to all nodes on the left side, the left edge of the wrinkle
cannot rotate, and is thus "clamped." Typical wrinkles are constricted from rotating on the left and right sides,
where they are attached to undisturbed laminate, so a clamped condition is realistic.
Due to the Poisson eﬀect, the left edge will expand in response to the applied compressive load. Thus, all the
nodes on the left edge are allowed to translate in the global ˆy direction to accommodate this. The exception
is the bottom-left node, which is constrained in the ˆy direction to prevent rigid body motion of the wrinkle.
The right edge of the Abaqus model corresponds to the half-length of the full wrinkle, i.e x = L/2. A
symmetry boundary condition is applied at this edge. This means that the right edge is free to translate in
the ˆy direction, but ﬁxed in the ˆx direction. A consequence of this boundary condition is that no shear load
can be supported there, which is expected.
No other loads or boundary conditions are present. The four boundary conditions are summarized in Table 4.4.

4.5
Analysis
The parametric model is set up so that either the geometrically linear or nonlinear analysis in Abaqus can be
used. The former is only used for veriﬁcation purposes, and all other simulations are done with geometric
nonlinearity turned on. This is necessary for the kinking instability to be present, which occurs due to the
ﬁnite rotation of the ﬁbers as the matrix loses shear stiﬀness.
However, Abaqus standard’s static general solver cannot resolve the post instability behavior. This is because
the material begins to soften, and the maximum load is actually a limit load. Past this, it is expected that a
real specimen would undergo signiﬁcant deformation in a very short amount of time. This would apply the
eﬀects of inertia are important, and thus an explicit analysis would be necessary. However, of interest in this
thesis is mainly the limit load itself, and the kink band formation. To obtain the latter, instead the Abaqus
version of the arc-length method is used (Riks 1979), to trace the equilibrium path past the limit load through
the snap-back behavior.

4.7
Summary of Model Assumptions
There are several implicit assumptions made in this parametric model. First, we have a 2D continuum. Fur-
thermore, with the assumption of plane strain, it is assumed that the wrinkle’s depth in the ˆz direction, is much
larger than the others. This can certainly be the case for some wrinkles in the wind turbine main-laminate,
however, is not general to all wrinkles.
Secondly, the Abaqus model assumes an idealized sinusoid shape and material orientation. Real wrinkle
defects can have any shape, but typically it is easy measure a characteristic amplitude and wave-length. This
==PAGE:60==
42
Finite Element Model
lends itself to a sinusoidal representation, which is why this basic geometry was chosen. However, there can
be some discrepancies between the model and real wrinkle as a result.
Furthermore, the plasticity model used for the thin matrix layers assumes J2 ﬂow theory. This is a basic
assumption made in several studies (Budiansky and Fleck 1993),(Kyriakides et al. 1995), (Pane and Jensen
2004) and has been shown to yield good results for predicting kinking strength.
Finally, it is assumed that homogeneous smeared-out representation of the ﬁber lamina is suﬃcient. Because
we are considering it macroscopic wrinkles, it is not of particular interest how the individual ﬁbers behave,
and thus modeling the ﬁber bundles as a single material is a good assumption. However, this means the matrix
plasticity behavior can only occur in the thin resin layers between ﬁber laminae. One way to solve this while
still modeling homogeneous ﬁber lamina would be to use the UMAT model described in Sorensen et al. (2007).

5.1
Kinking Instability
As discussed in chapter 2, microbuckling is an important compression failure mode for ﬁber reinforced com-
posites. The term microbuckling implies a localized instability, which can take on many forms. However,
most of the existing theories and models predict a collapse/limit load beyond which the composite cannot
support any further loading. This is taken to be the compression strength. When including the eﬀects of
matrix plasticity, the model described in this thesis also predicts a limit load. Following is a discussion of
the instability’s representation in the parametric model and what this means for predicting the compression
strength.

lamina acts as an Euler beam column, and undergoes large deformation similar to elastic buckling. In reality,
the delamination would occur between the layers, and as the cracks run across the length of the wrinkle,
the individual laminae would separate, and each would indeed buckle elastically, due to the reduced bending
stiﬀness.

that the initial beam-column behavior is important to the development of the kinking instability, and the
two are related. As already mentioned, it is the plastic deformation which causes the kinking instability to
occur. The reason the plastic matrix deformation occurs is the high shear due to bending, which is predicted
in the theory of beam-columns. Figure 5.7 shows a comparison of the shear stress τ12 at the very center of
the wrinkle (lengthwise and thickness-wise) as a function of the applied load for both the purely elastic and
elastic-plastic cases. Up until point (1), the responses are very similar. However, the matrix begins to yield
afterwards, precisely because the shear stresses are growing. The shear stress grows further until the limit
load. The conclusion is that the shear behavior is very important when determining the compressive strength.
The shear behavior also plays an important role in the onset of delamination, as discussed in the next section.

5.2 Delamination

5.2
Delamination
5.2.1
Maximum Interlaminar Shear
As pointed out by studies such as Hsiao and Daniel (1996) and Mukhopadhyay et al. (2015), it is often interlam-
inar delamination that is the failure mechanism for macroscopic wrinkles, especially at large misalignment
angles. In this failure mode, cracks within the small layer between individual lamina begin to grow until
the individual laminae are fully separated from one another. This then allows the individual laminae to un-
dergo elastic buckling, which leads to the ultimate failure. Modern ﬁber reinforced composite laminates are
susceptible to this failure mode due to the low strength of the ﬁber matrix interface.
As Hsiao and Daniel notes, it is in fact interlaminar shear that causes the delamination. Hsiao and Daniel
modeled the wrinkles using classical laminate theory, combined with the tsai-wu failure criterion and a pro-
gressive damage model. They note that the interlaminar shear stress is "far more signiﬁcant than other stress
components" (Hsiao and Daniel 1996, p.589). By using their failure criterion, they are able to predict the onset
of damage.
In contrast to the Tsai-Wu criterion, which requires many material strength inputs, a maximum stress criterion
could be used. As noted by Hsiao and Daniel, the shear stress is ﬁrst stress component to reach its maximum
allowable value. The hypothesis made in this thesis is that if maximum interlaminar shear stress reaches a
threshold value, it can be considered to have failed, as delamination often leads to rapid failure.
It is not certain that this approach is accurate. It relies solely upon the state of stress to indicate failure and
neglects any modeling of the fracture mechanics. However, the beneﬁt of this approach is that it requires
only one value as input and is very simple to test. Thus as a starting point, the interlaminar shear stress will
be examined and compared to the threshold value. This threshold value can come from the often used short-
beam test to identify the strength of a composite laminate in interlaminar shear τILSS. A typical value for glass
ﬁber polyester matrix composites is 45 [MPa]. This value will be used in the rest of the thesis. Therefore, any
statements about the delamination strength, σdelam, are made assuming that the threshold shear stress value
is 45 [MPa]. The model predictions about delamination strength would be diﬀerent for a diﬀerent threshold.
This maximum shear stress criterion can be applied to the current baseline case. The shear stress is highest
at the wrinkle center, and thus it is only necessary to compare the value there to the threshold. It should be
noted that the value is obtained from a ﬁber lamina layer, where the shear stress is obtained in the correct
coordinate system. Figure 5.9 shows the shear stress vs. the applied load along with the threshold. The shear
stress does not reach the threshold until after the limit load (only the pre-limit load response is shown). As
such, it can be stated that the overall compression strength for this case is given by the kinking strength. Here,
no value for the delamination strength can be given since delamination does not occur before the limit load.
In other geometries, it can indeed be that delamination occurs ﬁrst, and σdelam is the overall compression
strength.
Throughout the rest of this report, the center point shear response τmid as shown in Figure 5.9 will be given.
The assumption here is that the center point shear is the maximal value of shear, which is true. This response
helps show when delamination will occur. Also, analyzing the shear stress response helps determine why
kinking occurs, since the shear stress is partly responsible for matrix yielding. In all cases, the shear stress
response is taken from a lamina layer, not a matrix layer. The local coordinate system in the matrix layers
rotates separately from the lamina coordinate system, and thus can give unreliable stress results, when using
the element local coordinates.

5.2.2
Other methods
Finally, we note that this maximum shear criterion is very simple, and thus might not yield the correct delam-
ination strength. It does however provide a simple understanding of when the state of shear in the wrinkle
becomes critical to the strength.
In the future, this same model can easily be augmented to allow more complex delamination models. This
can include a more complex quadratic stress-based failure criterion such as the Puck criterion for inter-ﬁber
failure. This requires more material inputs, but is more detailed in nature. This failure criterion can be
implemented on an element basis, so that failure can be determined anywhere in the model, not just at the
center point.
Also, fracture mechanics based approaches could be implemented. A small crack could be simulated in the
lamina matrix interface, and a J contour integral could be computed to determine the energy release rate. This
could be compared to a threshold value to determine if the crack would propagate and lead to delamination
failure.
These other methods are more complex and require more material information, and thus will not be pursued
in this thesis. But the model has been created in a way that such further modeling would not be diﬃcult to
implement.

6.1
Varying Misalignment Angle
In this section, the height and length of the wrinkle are ﬁxed to 12.5 and 100 mm, respectively, while the
misalignment angle is varied by changing the amplitude A. As noted by Argon (1972), the initial misalignment
of the laminate should contribute greatly to the strength prediction. Thus the purpose of this study is to
demonstrate how the wrinkle behavior changes with varying initial misalignment angle.
Figure 6.1 depicts the end shortening response of the wrinkle for varying maximum misalignment angles.
As predicted by Argon (1972), the kinking strength decreases with greater misalignment. As mentioned in
section 5.1, a sharp cusp can be seen in the response for small misalignment angles as a result of elastic
unloading. This does not occur with the higher misalignment angle wrinkles, where the kink band takes a
larger percentage of the overall length of the structure. For large θmax, the elastic unloading does not elongate
the structure enough to compensate for the shrinkage within the kink band, so no cusp is seen. This exact
behavior was also found by Kyriakides et al. (1995).
It should also be noted that for small misalignment angles, the pre-limit load behavior is nearly linear. How-
ever, for large misalignment angles, there is a signiﬁcant amount of non-linearity before the limit load. This
non-linearity is mostly a geometric non-linearity, which is predicted by the theory of beam-columns. Fig-
ure 6.2 compares the model behavior to the theoretical buckling behavior of beam columns with various
initial imperfections. On the y-axis, the far-ﬁeld stress is normalized by the critical buckling stress, and on
the x-axis, the normalized vertical deﬂection at the beam center is given. Initially, the model follows the the-
oretical behavior, however, eventually, plastic deformation begins to occur, causing the model response to
diverge from the elastic theory. As the matrix deforms plastically, the load reaches a peak value, while the
center cross-section begins to translate vertically. Thus the kinking deformation can be seen in Figure 6.2 as
the large increase in v at constant load.

increases. This behavior is explained by Figure 6.3, which depicts the maximum shear stress as a function of
the far ﬁeld axial stress. According to beam theory, the higher amplitude wrinkles experience greater shear

load than for the lower amplitude wrinkles, as shown by Figure 6.4. Thus, the shear modulus degrades quicker

6.3
Strength Results
Finally, the eﬀect of slenderness ratio and misalignment angle are examined simultaneously. In Figure 6.8, the
overall compression strength is given as a function of θmax and h/L. The trend is that strength is reduced
as θmax increases. Also, strength is reduced as h/L decreases, i.e for more slender wrinkles. Clearly both
parameters are very important when using this model to predict strength.
In this parameter study, the failure mode transitions to delamination at angles between 1 and 3 degrees as
seen in Figure 6.9. Note that the delamination strength cannot always be deﬁned as the limit load may occur
before the ILSS threshold is reached.
Kinking is the failure mode for smaller wrinkles, and is depicted in Figure 6.10. The strength decreases dra-
matically with misalignment angle, which is similar to the predictions of Argon (1972) and Budiansky (1983).
In all cases, a smooth transition occurs between failure modes. However, the transition is dependent on the
input delamination strength.
Finally, Figure 6.11 depicts the strength as well, however instead normalized by the critical stress. For very
small θmax, the strength value is near σcr. This can be examined using the theory of beam-columns.
As the load approaches the critical load, the shear stresses begin to amplify greatly, as seen in Equation 6.1.
This causes the matrix to yield and deform plastically, which induces the kinking instability. In a sense, the
elastic buckling is connected to the kinking/microbuckling phenomenon. Budiansky (1983) found the kinking
strength to essentially be the elastic buckling strength predicted by Rosen (1965), multiplied by a plasticity
knock-down factor. Similarly, the microbuckling/kinking strength determined by this model is related to its
own elastic buckling strength, namely the Euler load.
As such, the critical Euler stress is a good approximation for the kinking strength for very small imperfections.
However, it is not necessarily an upper limit. Even in the purely elastic case, a beam-column can continue to
be loaded past the Euler load, without instability (this can be predicted by using Euler’s theory of elastic for
large deformations).

Chapter 7
Model Input Sensitivity
In the previous chapter, it was found that the wrinkle failure strength is very dependent on the wrinkle
geometry. In this chapter, the goal is to investigate what other parameters are inﬂuential for the strength
determination. In each section, a diﬀerent parameter is varied, along with the maximum misalignment angle
θmax, and the kinking and delamination strength predictions are compared.
In all the following cases in this chapter, the ﬁber laminae are glass-polyester composites and the matrix layers
are polyester (using data from the 2.5 mm/min strain rate test). Also, the following geometric parameters are
ﬁxed, while A is changed in order to vary θmax: h = 12.5 mm, L = 100.0 mm, w = 30.0 mm.
7.1
Number of Layers
The model consists of n identical layers of material, subdivided into ﬁber lamina and matrix phases. It is of
interest to determine the eﬀect of the number of layers on the response of the model. In this study, the number
of layers is changed between 1, 3, 7 and 13. The layup of each layer is given as [M,G,M], where M denotes
a polyester matrix layer (with) and G denotes a glass ﬁber lamina layer. In each case, the volume fraction of
the wrinkle that is made of ﬁber lamina is kept at 78%. Thus any diﬀerences will occur simply by how this
volume is split up and spread throughout the wrinkle.
Figure 7.1 shows the end shortening response for varying number of layers. It can be shown that one layer
is not suﬃcient to obtain an accurate result. In the one layer model, all the resin material is positioned
toward the upper and lower edges of the beam, where the shear stress is the lowest. Also, due to its lower
stiﬀness, the matrix does not carry much axial stress either. Consequently, the lightly loaded matrix does not
undergo much plastic deformation, which prevents the kinking instability from occuring at a lower load. This
erroneous result is due to the layered nature of the model. To prevent this plasticity could be introduced into
the lamina layers as well, or the number of layers could be increased to eﬀectively simulate a homogeneous
material.
Figure 7.2 shows the kinking strength as a function of misalignment angle for various number of layers. 3
layers yields a slightly diﬀerent result for the same reason 1 layer was not suﬃcient: too much of the matrix
is at the edges, where the shear is lowest. It can be seen that there is virtually no diﬀerence in strength
prediction for 7 or 13 layers. A similar trend is shown in the delamination strength. There is a diﬀerence
in post instability behavior depending on the number of layers used. For a constant height, the more layers
are used, the thinner each ﬁber lamina layer is in absolute thickness. This reduced thickness reduces the
buckling load of each lamina as well, which reduces the post instability equilibrium load. However this is
not of interest for the strength determination. Thus, the conclusion is that if a suﬃcient number of layers are
used (approximately 7), the model is not very sensitive to the number of layers.

7.3
Laminae Volume Fraction
In each of the n layers, a certain proportion of the volume is occupied by a ﬁber lamina. This volume fraction
is denoted as Vlam. In real laminates, this value varies from layer to layer, and thus an average result must be
used. In this study, ﬁber laminae volume fraction is varied to see its eﬀect on strength and compliance.
Figure 7.4 shows clearly that the laminate with the higher Vlam has both a greater kinking strength and initial
stiﬀness. Increasing Vlam reduces the total amount of elastic-plastic matrix material. The matrix material
holds less of the load, and thus undergoes less plastic deformation. Thus, the stiﬀness reduction of the matrix
material occurs at a higher load, which delays the instability as well. It can be seen in Figure 7.4 that the total
end shortening is nearly constant at the peak load regardless of Vlam. This implies that the end-shortening
determines the limit load, when all else is equal.
Figure 7.5 shows the kinking strength as a function of misalignment angle, normalized by the values obtained
for Vlam = 0.80. What is seen is that a 10% increase in Vlam leads to a 10 to 12% increase in kinking strength.
A 10% decrease in Vlam leads to a 7 to 10 % decrease in kinking strength. It is often the case in real wrinkle
defects that Vlam is not exactly known, but must be approximated. It is typically on the range of 80%. While
the relationship is not very simple, and includes a large amount of non-linearity, a simple rule of thumb could
be that a change in Vlam is directly proportional to the change in strength. Thus there can be a great deal of
variability in the kinking strength in any experimental data.
On the other hand, Figure 7.6 shows that the delamination strength is however unaﬀected by the laminae vol-
ume fraction at higher angles. This can be better understood by examining Figure 7.7. At high misalignment
angles, the shear threshold is reached during the linear portion of the shear response, which is determined
entirely by geometry. Thus the strength at higher angles is completely unaﬀected by Vlam. At low angles,
plasticity has an important eﬀect on the strength. The overall compressive strength (minimum of the two) is
given in Figure 7.8. For θmax > 3 failure is by delamination. However, at θmax = 1◦and Vlam = 0.88, the

7.5
Laminae Shear Stiﬀness
The shear modulus of the laminae G12,lam is also typically given by an inverse rule of mixtures, similar to
Equation 7.1. In order to see the sensitivity of the model to this input, a rule of mixtures formulation is
considered. This again is not physical, but is used to determine the bounds of the possible eﬀect on the model
response.
Figure 7.10 shows the end shortening response for the two diﬀerent values of the lamina shear modulus at two
misalignment angles. The eﬀect on the limit load is pronounced at low angles. The kinking instability occurs
when the matrix shear stiﬀness is reduced, and the matrix can no longer support the ﬁbers. The greater the
matrix shear modulus is to begin with, the more plastic deformation is required to reduce this to a critical
level. Thus, the wrinkle with the higher shear modulus has the larger kinking strength, as seen in Figure 7.11.
This is in line with the theory of Rosen (1965), who said the compressive strength is proportional to the shear
modulus. Interestingly, the eﬀect is reduced for higher misalignment angles, which is in conﬂict with kinking
theory. However, it is not kinking, but delamination which is the failure mode for θmax > 1◦. The overall
strength is given in Figure 7.12.
Similar to previous studies, the delamination strength at high angles is also, again, unaﬀected by the lamina
shear modulus. This can be seen in Figure 7.13. For higher angles, the shear stress threshold is reached before
any signiﬁcant nonlinearity occurs, and thus the delamination strength is dictated purely by the geometry of
the wrinkle. Since the delamination strength is always lower than the kinking strength, it can then be said
that the overall compressive strength for moderate to high angles is unaﬀected by the shear modulus.
Finally, the kinking strength at low misalignment angle (when kinking is the failure mode) is compared to the
shear modulus of the entire laminate, Glaminate. As mentioned, according to Budiansky (1983), the compres-
sive strength should decrease linearly with Glaminate. This value was varied to see if indeed there is a linear
relationship.
Glaminate can be calculated from the lamina shear modulus G12,lam and the matrix elastic shear modulus
Gm using the inverse rule of mixtures as presented in Equation 4.13. However, there is a limit to how large
Glaminate can be. Assuming the laminae have inﬁnite shear stiﬀness, the maximum value for Glaminate is
given by Equation 7.2. Keeping Gm = 1.24 and Vlam = 0.78, this maximum value is 5.64 GPa. To get a value
of 5 GPa for Glaminate, G12,lam would have to equal 35.1 GPa. This value is greater than the value given by
the rule of mixtures estimate (20 GPa), and is therefore completely theoretical.
Gmax
laminate =
Gm
1 −Vlam
(7.2)
Figure 7.14 shows the kinking strength for a θmax = 1◦for varying Glaminate. It is clear that the trend
is not linear, as would have been predicted by Budiansky. However, there is a strong dependence on this
value. The discrepancy can exist for two reasons. First, this model allows for the ﬁnite stiﬀness of the ﬁber
lamina, whereas Budiansky (1983) assumes the ﬁbers are rigid. Secondly, the geometry of the problems are
quite diﬀerent. Budiansky considered a kinked structure, with a portion of the ﬁbers nominally straight, and
portion uniformly misaligned, whereas a sinusoidal waveform is considered here. The two geometries have
diﬀerent buckling modes, and as such should have diﬀerent buckling loads, that have diﬀerent dependence
on the parameter Glaminate.
In conclusion, the parameters Vlam and G12,lam are very important inputs to the parametric model when
predicting compression strength. Both of these parameters are often diﬃcult to obtain. Vlam can be measured
using optical techniques from typical samples, but the result of this is an average value across samples. In fact,
Vlam can vary within a single sample, so a representative value must be used. It was shown that the error in
Vlam might be proportional to the error in the kinking strength. An easy way to account for this is to adjust
Vlam to match experimental results for longitudinal stiﬀness E1.

Chapter 8
Inﬂuence of Matrix Plastic Behavior
The kinking instability is only possible if the matrix can lose stiﬀness. This behavior is a function of the
matrix plasticity hardening law, which is deﬁned in this study by two parameters: the yield strength σY and
the hardening exponent N.
In this chapter, the eﬀect of these two plasticity parameters are examined. The purpose is to determine what
resin parameters are important in determining the kinking strength. In this way, in the design process, resin
systems can be selected that would increase kinking strength.
In all of the following studies, the ﬁber lamina volume fraction is ﬁxed to 80%, and the remaining 20% is
polyester matrix. The following geometric parameters are again ﬁxed, while A is varied to change θmax:
h = 12.5 mm, L = 100.0 mm, w = 30.0 mm.
8.1
Varying Power Law Fit
In the ﬁrst study, three diﬀerent matrix hardening laws are used, pertaining to tensile test results from a
typical polyester resin for three diﬀerent strain rates. The test data was ﬁt to a power law, to obtain the yield
strength and hardening exponent, as explained in subsection 4.2.1. It was found that both yield strength and
hardening exponent increase with strain rate.
The model is purely static, so any rate dependence only appears in the plasticity inputs. Thus, while this
analysis gives some preliminary insight into how the strength changes with loading strain rate, it is not
suﬃcient. Further analysis of the eﬀect of high strain rates would require a dynamic analysis.
Figure 8.1 shows the response for the three diﬀerent strain rates. All three responses have the same initial
linear stiﬀness because the plasticity has no eﬀect on the elastic properties. However, the wrinkle with the
higher yield strength (corresponding to the higher strain rate data) has a higher limit load as seen in Figure 8.2.
This is intuitive, as a higher yield strength means that the matrix cannot deform plastically until a higher
applied load. This higher kinking strength exists regardless of the misalignment angle.
The delamination strength is only eﬀected by the plasticity inputs at smaller angles. Figure 8.3 shows that
the misalignment angle at which delamination becomes the failure mode changes depending on the plasticity
inputs. This can be better understood by examining Figure 8.4, which shows the center point shear response
for θmax = 10◦. For this misalignment angle, the shear threshold is reached before any plasticity occurs.
However, for the smaller angles, plastic deformation does occur before the shear threshold. The wrinkles
with the higher yield strength are able to withstand higher applied compression loads without kinking. The
higher compression loads mean a higher shear stress, which increases the likelihood of delamination.

8.2
Hardening Exponent
In the studies discussed thus far, both N and σY were varied simultaneously. The eﬀects of these two pa-
rameters were also examined independently. It was determined that the hardening exponent in and of itself
does not have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the strength prediction, as can be seen in Figure 8.5. A lower harden-
ing exponent would mean less plastic strain at a given load above the yield stress. This is why the the the
very low values of N yield a higher kinking strength. However, the eﬀect of N on σkink only causes a 20
[MPa] variation in strength in the range of reasonable values of N (4-10). Beyond a hardening exponent of 10,
the plasticity model is essentially elastic-perfectly plastic, and the kinking strength rapidly converges to one
value.
8.3
Yield Strength
The yield stress on the other hand is very important because it determines the applied compressive load at
which the matrix will begin to yield. Low resin yield strengths cause wrinkle kinking failure at very low
loads. However, can the strength of the laminate be increased indeﬁnitely if the yield strength is increased?
Figure 8.6 shows that there is an asymptotic limit to how much the compressive strength increases with the
yield point. By using power law hardening, there will always be a ﬁnite amount of plastic deformation below
the yield point. Thus, even for an extremely large yield strength, the matrix would undergo some stiﬀness
reduction at lower loads, which should be enough for the kinking instability to occur. If on the other hand, a
perfectly plastic formulation was used as in Budiansky (1983), there would be no plastic deformation below
the yield strength, and thus no kinking until yield occurred. However, real materials do not exhibit perfect
plasticity, but rather, some form of strain hardening.

Hybrid Materials
Finally, it is of signiﬁcant interest to explore the strength of hybrid wrinkles. These are laminates which have
a combination of glass and carbon ﬁber, and are being used more in modern wind turbine blades. This study
aims to determine how the wrinkle compression strength varies with volume fraction of carbon laminae,
Vcarbon.
As in the previous studies, each of the 13 layers is divided into a number of separate phases. In this study, the
layup of each layer is given by [G,M,C,M], where M is the matrix material (polyester in this case corresponding
to a strain rate of 2.5 mm/min) and G and C are the glass and carbon layers, respectively. The volume fraction
of the wrinkle which is made up of matrix is ﬁxed to 20%. The remaining 80% is variably split with glass and
carbon ﬁber laminae. Vcarbon indicates the fraction of the 80%, which is made of carbon (i.e. Vcarbon = 1
indicates the laminate is 80% carbon lamina and 20% resin).The following geometric parameters are again
ﬁxed, while A is varied to change θmax: h = 12.5 mm, L = 100.0 mm, w = 30.0 mm.
It is important to note that this analysis neglects any nonlinearity in the ﬁbers, which for the case of carbon,
can be signiﬁcant in reality (Yokozeki et al. 2005).
Figure 9.1 shows the end shortening response for wrinkles with varying volume fractions of carbon laminae.
The stiﬀest and strongest wrinkle corresponds to the carbon only laminate, whereas the glass only laminate
is the weakest/least stiﬀ. The increase in stiﬀness is simply a result of the rule of mixtures: the more carbon
ﬁber is present, the stiﬀer the structure is overall.
An increasing amount of carbon increases the kinking strength. This is not necessarily an obvious result, so
to understand this, one must again examine the elastic-plastic matrix behavior. The kinking instability occurs
when the matrix shear modulus degrades suﬃciently to allow the ﬁbers to rotate under the applied load. The
shear modulus degrades as a result of plastic deformation, which occurs when the equivalent load in the matrix
exceeds the yield value. This equivalent load, is a combination of both the axial and shear stresses on the
matrix. Since the carbon ﬁbers take up more axial load, increasing Vcarbon reduces the axial load on the matrix,
and thus delays the onset of yield. This is especially true for the case of small misalignment angles, where the
eﬀect of shear does not dominate the response. As in Figure 9.2, the laminate strength increases non-linearly
with Vcarbon. This could be because the equivalent load is typically deﬁned by a quadratic combination of the
stress components, such as in J2 ﬂow theory. Thus, the applied compressive load at which matrix yielding
occurs is a nonlinear function of Vcarbon.
Figure 9.3 shows the kinking strength variation as a function of θmax for a pure glass, a pure carbon and an
equal parts carbon/glass laminate. The trend is that is that the diﬀerence in kinking strength is greatest at
low misalignment angles. At small angles, shear is not important to the response, so the matrix yields due
to axial compressive stress. The compressive stress in the matrix is less in composites with carbon than with
glass. At higher misalignment angles, shear becomes important to yielding. The presence of carbon ﬁbers
increases the critical buckling stress, which causes a very minor decrease in the shear stress compared to
glass, as indicated in Equation 6.1. This diﬀerence in shear at the center point for a given load can be seen in
Figure 9.4. This minor decrease in shear stress for a ﬁxed load means the matrix yields at a higher load in the

Chapter 10
Conclusion
The driving motivation behind this research is the drastic eﬀect wrinkle defects have on the structural integrity
of wind turbine blades. As mentioned in the introduction, the leading cause of wind turbine blade failure is
defects, and the most common and severe of all defects is out-of-plane ﬁber waviness (wrinkles) (Griﬃn
and Malkin 2011, p.2). Blade failure is extremely dangerous and costly and should be prevented at all costs.
However, the problem is many wind turbine manufacturers deal with wrinkles in a way that is not grounded
in research. A detailed understanding of the eﬀect of wrinkle defects would help wind turbine manufacturers
make safer blades in a more eﬃcient manner.
The objective of this thesis has been to determine the compression strength of composite laminates typi-
cally found in wind turbine main laminates, when including the presence of an out-of-plane wrinkle defect.
Compression is considered because it is the strength value which is most degraded when wrinkles are present.
10.1
Work Completed
A literature survey has been conducted of various models used to determine the compression strength of
composite laminates with and without wrinkles. It has been determined that the two most important failure
mechanisms to consider are plastic microbuckling and delamination. It has also been shown that the most
important modeling aspects to predict these failures are plasticity of the matrix and accurate representation
of the composite’s behavior in in-plane shear.
By considering an idealization of the wrinkle defect’s geometry, analytical expressions have been derived
from elastic beam theory for the behavior of the wrinkle, including the shear response. It has been shown
that the misalignment angle and the elastic critical load are important parameters in that response.
With all this theory in mind, a parametric model of a wrinkle defect has been successfully made. The model
includes the eﬀects of matrix plasticity, and is 2D continuum layered structure, made of alternating layers
of linear elastic orthotropic ﬁber laminae and isotropic elastic-plastic polymer matrix. The model takes in
as input geometry and material parameters, and is capable of outputting a plastic microbuckling (kinking)
strength and delamination strength. The kinking strength is found in the form of a limit load, while the
delamination strength is determined by means of a maximum interlaminar shear stress criterion.
The parametric nature of the model has allowed for simple execution of parameter studies of the various
inputs. A parametric study of the eﬀect of misalignment angle has shown that it is inversely proportional
to both the kinking and delamination strengths. This is because an increase in misalignment angle leads
to an increase in shear stress. This means the shear stress threshold is reached at a lower load, indicating
delamination, and also that matrix yields at a lower load, causing the microbuckling. It has been determined
that the failure mode transitions from kinking at low angles (≲1◦) to delamination at higher angles.
The height to length (slenderness) ratio of the wrinkle has also been shown to be important, because it eﬀects
==PAGE:104==
86
Conclusion
the elastic critical load, of which the shear stress is a function.
A sensitivity analysis has also been conducted, which showed that the volume fraction of ﬁber laminae and the
lamina shear modulus are important parameters. They greatly eﬀect the kinking and delamination strengths
at low to moderate angles, but at higher angles (≳10◦), the delamination strength is independent of these
parameters. This has been shown to be because the shear stress at such high angles is only a function of the
geometry.
A parameter study of the matrix plasticity power law model has shown that an increase in yield strength
produces an increase in the kinking and delamination strengths at low and moderate angles. This is because
increasing the yield strength delays the onset of plastic deformation, which consequently delays microbuck-
ling. This also eﬀects the shear stress in the laminate, which means for moderate misalignment angles (≲10◦),
the shear stress threshold is reached at higher loads. This shows that resins with higher yield strengths are
better, when considering wrinkles.
Finally, a study of laminates with both glass and carbon laminae (hybrids) has been conducted to determine
the strength dependence on the volume fraction of carbon. It has been shown that Laminates with a greater
volume fraction of carbon have higher kinking and delamination strengths. This is because the carbon laminae
take load away from the matrix, which causes a delay in the onset of yield, and thus a higer kinking strength.
The carbon laminae also increase the elastic critical load of the whole laminate, which causes a reduction in
shear stress at a given load. This means the delamination strength is increased as well.
From all these studies, the most important conclusion is that matrix plasticity has a very important eﬀect on
a wrinkle in compression, and the wrinkle’s shear response is very insightful in determining the compression
strength.
10.2
Further Work
Unfortunately, the model as is has not been successfully veriﬁed by experimental results. This is necessary
before further work on the model can be done, otherwise it is not sure that the model’s predictions are rel-
evant. In order to get the model successfully veriﬁed, it should be adjusted to accurately reproduce a static
compression test. This includes adding the undisturbed laminate to the left and right edges of the wrinkle to
simulate a test-sample. It may also be relevant to change the model to 3D continuum elements, in case this
eﬀect is important when modeling the experiments.
Also for veriﬁcation, the simple failure criterion used for delamination must most likely also be upgraded
to a more complex failure criterion, such as the Puck criterion, or Tsai-Wu criterion. This would require
some laminate material inputs, which can be obtained by experimental testing. A fracture mechanics based
approach could also be used, for example: including a crack singularity and evaluating a J contour integral
to compare to a critical fracture energy value, or using cohesive zone elements to model the matrix-lamina
interface.
If the model can be successfully veriﬁed, it is of great interest to investigate partial wrinkles, where there is
undisturbed laminate present below and/or above the wrinkle itself. This could allow modeling of in-plane
waviness, where the surrounding material is very important. Furthermore, partial out of plane wrinkles are
much more common than the through-thickness wrinkles investigated thus far, and their strengths should be
much diﬀerent. The parametric model could be easily extended so that the proportion of the laminate that is
misaligned is a variable as well as the relative location of the defective material in the whole laminate. After
this fully functional model is further veriﬁed with experiments, it could be used in a manner analogous to
Liu et al. (2004) to investigate random ﬁber waviness. This sort of model would be a very powerful tool in
determining the reliability of a wind turbine blade design.

10.3
Final Remarks
Wrinkles are an obstacle in the pursuit of safe and cheap wind turbine blades. This research does not help
in preventing them, but instead it provides useful information for wind turbine blade manufacturers. They
can use this information in order to set up better criteria for determining when blades need to be repaired.
This ensures safe blades while limiting workers’ exposure to dangerous chemicals during repair and reducing
unnecessary mold down time. It can also potentially be used by designers when creating design models that
ensure high reliability.
With wind turbine blades that are safer, cheaper, and more reliable, the wind energy industry can expand to
become an integral part of the energy revolution that is so greatly needed today.

def laminaProps(Ef,Er,nuf,nur,Gf,Gr,Vf,Vr):
# Rule of Mixtures
E1 = Ef*Vf + Er*Vr
nu12 = nuf*Vf + nur*Vr
# Inverse rule of Mixtures
E2 = Ef*Er/(Ef*Vr+Er*Vf)
G12 = Gf*Gr/(Gf*Vr+Gr*Vf)
return [E1,E2,nu12,G12]
def matrixPlasticity(inputType,input):

table = [[float(item) for item in row] for row in rows]
elif(inputType == ’hardening’):
N = input[0]
K = input[1]
table = [(1.0,0.0),]
# First yield must have 0 plastic strain
for i in range(2,200):
sigma = float(i)
strain = K*(sigma)**(N)
table.append((sigma,strain))
table = tuple(table)
return table
