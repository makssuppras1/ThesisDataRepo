Abstract
As smartphones have become a great part of our everyday life, it is necessary to study
their influence. Sleep is an important function of the human body and thus it would
be interesting to explore whether there is a relationship with smartphone usage. Recent
studies examine how phone usage affects sleep and differences based on gender or age.
This study focused on phone usage before and after bedtime based on data acquired
from smartphones and wristbands and explores differences among gender, age, and BMI
groups. Results from smartphone usage and sleep analysis separately showed that gender
and age can be important factors, however, BMI doesn’t seem to have a big influence.
Finally, there are some indications that phone usage after bedtime could have a positive
impact on sleep duration but a negative on sleep quality.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Throughout the last decade, smartphones have become part of our everyday life to a
great extend. The number of smartphone users increases year by year reaching around
3.5 billion in 2020 [El-+21]. Smartphones are a new type of technology that allows users
not only to communicate but also to perform calculations[MYS12] and have applications
of various aspects such as health and fitness, education, travel, and more[Hig16; YC12;
WXF16]. However, their popularity has raised concerns about addiction[PC18] as peo-
ple think that they can’t be separated from their phone[Lep+15a]. More specifically,
research conducted in the USA showed that 46% of the participants feel that ”they could
not live without” their smartphone [SMD15].
As for every new technology presented what most concerns us is the negative effects.
Problematic smartphone usage can lead to depression, anxiety, stress, and even low
self-esteem[Elh+17]. Studies have shown that it can have negative effects on academic
performance[Jud14; Kar+13] and the use of specific applications while studying might
be related to the decrease of college grades[JC12]. Also, the number of times students
use their phone might affect leisure[Lep+15b] and generally the level of satisfaction with
life[LBK14].
A study focused on gender differences in smartphone usage showed that the negative
effects on males are associated with game applications while on females with social net-
work and multimedia ones[Che+17]. Phone addiction seems to be related to age and as
shown from studies the younger the user is, the more prone is to phone addiction[Alh+18;
Csi+19].
Smartphones and sleep
Sleep is one of the most important functions of the human body and it can be affected by
smartphone usage. Research among university students showed that there is a positive
correlation between smartphone addiction and sleep quality[DAA15]. It is also shown
that among young adults 68.7% with phone addiction had poor sleep quality compared to
57.1% of those with no addiction. Furthermore, as users prolong the time before sleeping
when they don’t use their phone, the fraction with poor sleep quality decreases[Soh+21].
In this project, I investigate the relationship between smartphone usage before and after
bedtime and sleep.

1.1
Scope
The goal of this thesis is to understand smartphone usage before and after bedtime and
explore if it affects our sleep. The dataset contains information for 4 years, from June
2015 to May 2019 for 4,176 users from Denmark. It contains records regarding applica-
tion usage, sleep, and user data such as gender and age, as explained more analytically
in chapter 3.
Firstly, we emphasized the exploration of smartphone usage and sleep separately and
explored differences among social groups defined by gender, age, and BMI. The analy-
sis is focused on the duration of smartphone screen time for each application category
contained in the dataset and the sleep duration. The next part is to explore any rela-
tionship between sleep and phone usage. For this analysis, we examined the correlation
between different evaluation variables for sleep and application usage respectively both
individually, to identify trends, and general in the users of the dataset.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
This chapter presents an overview of the literature related to this project. Following
the structure of the analysis, which is presented in the following chapters, similarly,
the literature research was split into three parts: smartphone usage, sleep, and their
combination in the sections that follow respectively. The points of the total research are
summarized in the final section.
2.1
Smartphone usage
In 2020, the number of smartphone users was estimated to be around 3.5 billion [El-+21].
A study conducted by Parasuraman S. et al. with participants from 18 to 55 years old
showed that 95% of them use smartphones and 81.7% own at least one device. The
majority of them(64.3%) use their phone for around 1 hour per day while 36.7% check
it between sleep. Furthermore, a vast majority of the participants(87.8%) stated that
they use their phone for communication [Sub+17]. Another research by Falaki H. et al.,
which included participants who use Windows and Android smartphones, revealed that
users have longer sessions but few in a number other than many and short. Also, game
sessions tend to be longer compared to other applications [Fal+10].
Demographics play an important role in how users use their phone, with gender and
age being two main factors. Bisen S. and Deshpande Y. performed research among en-
gineering students to spot differences in smartphone usage between genders and showed
that males tend to spend more time in communication applications than females who
spend slightly more in entertainment applications [BD16]. Zhao S. et al. tried to iden-
tify the different kinds of smartphone users and group them. The results showed that
the main kind of users is defined as ”night communicators”, as they use communication
applications frequently especially at night. This cluster includes slightly more females
than males and 40% of them are older than 35 years old [Zha+16].
2.2
Sleep
Sleep is a neurophysiological state which is considered important for resting and restoring
brain and body functions [JRE15] and a lot of studies have been conducted around this
subject. Roenneberg T. et al. examined sleep times based on questionnaires and they

showed that there are important differences between work and free days [RWM03], thus
a part of this project concerning sleep is separated in weekdays and weekends.
Some studies show differences that depend on age. Floyd J.A. et al. showed that as
age increases, the ability to initiate sleep decreases [Flo+00]. A few studies were focused
on specific sleep stages.
Ohayon M.M. et al.
explored general age-related changes
in sleep and whether stages 1, 2, and Rapid Eye Movement(REM) depends on age.
The results showed that significant changes in children-adolescents and adults. In the
children-adolescents age group, total sleep duration decreased but only in studies that
were performed on school days. Also, slow-wave sleep is negatively correlated with age
while the fraction of stage 2 sleep and REM changes significantly. Regarding adults,
total sleep time and the fraction of slow-wave sleep and REM decreases with age but
the fraction of stage 1 and stage 2 sleep increases [Oha+04].
Furthermore, some researchers examined the morningness-eveningness effect and
whether it is dependent on gender.
Morningness- eveningness refers to the individ-
ual preference in a sleep-wake pattern and when a person feels more eﬀicient [UMR11].
Adan A. and Natale V. showed that females are more likely to be morning types [AN02],
in contrast with Merikanto I. et al. who proved that eveningness is more common to
females [Mer+12]. Finally, Paine S. et al. conducted research that confirmed gender
independence [PGT06].
Sigga S.J et al. examined sleep in non-shift workers and their dependence on gender,
age, or BMI. In their analysis, they explored sleep duration, onset, and offset generally
and separately on weekdays and weekends. Their research showed that the time people
wake up becomes earlier as age increases and they tend to go sleep later on weekends,
however, the differences between weekdays and weekends are constant between genders
for all age groups. Furthermore, sleep duration decreases as people get older with the
difference between weekdays and weekends being insignificant.
Finally, BMI affects
little sleep timing [JML21]. The difference in this research is that wearable devices were
used to measure sleep while in the previous studies mentioned the main method was
self-report.
2.3
Mobile phones & sleep
As smartphones have become an important part of our everyday life, there are some
studies conducted to explore whether and how they affect our sleep quality. Initially
what concerned researchers the most was the effects of radiation and electromagnetic
fields emitted by phones. Nakatani-Eminoto S. et al. showed that a 3-hour exposure to
the electromagnetic fields by mobile phones has no significant impact on sleep [Nak+13]
while Hossmann K.-A. and Hermann D.M. proved that the current effects of phone-
emitted radiation are very small [HH03]. On the other hand, Heo J.-Y. examined the
effect of blue light emitted by smartphones’ screens in adults and showed that it can
negatively affect sleep [Heo+17].
Some studies try to connect the way we use our phones before and after bedtime with

sleep quality. Akçay D. and Akçay B.D. explored the impact of mobile usage on the
sleep of high school students. The research showed that 65.5% had poor sleep quality
while 19.2% delayed their sleep because of their phone usage. The main conclusions
were that as mobile phone usage increases, sleep quality decreases and that students
who keep their phones near them while sleeping tend to use them more [KAL18]. A
similar analysis was conducted by Sahin S. et al. to explore the relationship between
mobile phone addiction and sleep. The results showed that female students use their
phones mostly for communication while males perform various tasks such as playing
games and watching TV. The general conclusion was that sleep quality decreases as
the addiction level increases [Sah+13]. What is worth mentioning is that both of these
studies used the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) to evaluate sleep quality. The
PSQI is a questionnaire filled by the survey’s participants to assess the quality of sleep
[Buy+89].
More general research by Orzech K.M. et al. explored the use of digital media 2 hours
before bedtime among university students. Their findings proved that as the duration
of media usage increased, sleep duration decreased and the participants reported later
bedtimes. Moreover, earlier bedtimes and increased sleep duration were noticed when
the variety of the digital media used before sleep time increased [Orz+16].
From the studies that investigate the connection between sleep and phone usage,
the larger part concerns young people. Exelmans L. and Van de Bulck J. focused on
the relationship between mobile phone usage and sleep in adults. Of the participants,
almost 60% replied that they take their phone to the bedroom while sleeping while
14.7% might send messages after their bedtime and 2.7% make phone calls. Most of the

4.2.3
Kolmogorov-Smirnov test
Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (KS-test) is a non-parametric statistical test that determines
the statistical difference between two distributions. Non-parametric tests don’t assume
anything about the underlying distribution but it has to be continuous. The Kolmogorov-
Smirnov statistic is defined by the maximum difference of the two distributions and based
on its value we can determine whether the samples are drawn from the same distribution.
The hypothesis that the two samples are drawn from the same distribution is defined as
the null hypothesis and is rejected based on the p-value, if the p-value is lower than the
significance level then we reject the null hypothesis and state that there is a statistical
difference between the distributions [Mas51]. The Kolmogorov-Smirnov test is sensitive
to the shape of the distributions [LRH07] and works for continuous samples [EC11]. In
this project, the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test is applied for all continuous variables.
4.2.4
Mann-Whitney U test
Similar to the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, the Mann-Whitney U test is a non-parametric
test that determines the statistical difference between two distributions.
As a non-
parametric test, it assumes nothing about the underlying distributions, however, it re-
quires that the samples are independent. The null hypothesis states that the samples
come from the same distribution and is rejected based on the p-value, if the p-value is
low, comparing to the significance level, then we can reject the null hypothesis and say
that there is a significant difference between the two samples [Nac+08]. Mann-Whitney
U test is used for statistical testing on discrete variables as the Kolmogorov-Smirnov
test works better for continuous samples, as mentioned above.

4.3.1
Change Point Detection
Even after filtering based on the WASS value, we notice sudden jumps that can be
considered as noise in the signal (B.3). A way to identify the changes is change point
detection.
Change point detection methods can be divided into two categories: online methods,
where we acquire the signal in real-time and try to identify the changes, and offline
methods when we have received the entire signal and find the changes afterward. In
Python, there is a package that implements the offline change point detection methods
called ruptures. As is presented by Truong C. et al. [TOV20] the ruptures package is
based on the principles of change point detection. The methods included trying to solve
two kinds of problems, those where the number of changes is known and those with an
unknown number of changes. Furthermore, the methods consist of a cost function, a
search method, and a constraint, which are defined by the kind of problem we want to
solve.
In our case, since we don’t know the number of changes in a signal the most appro-
priate search method is the pelt method which uses a linear penalty β and the model
used is rbf, which uses a kernelized cost function to identify changes in the mean of the
signal. The most important parameter in the function is β, a smoothing parameter that
affects the number of changes found by the algorithm. Low values of β identify many
change points while large values have the opposite effect. In figures B.4, B.5, B.6, B.7
and B.8 we can see how the change points are modified by changing the β parameter
and we choose to use β = 3 in the change detection algorithm.
The result of the algorithm applied is the index of each change thus creating sub-
periods of data. For each sub-period created we check the variance and the length and
finally choose these with the smallest variance and largest length. The final result of the
filtering can be seen in figure B.9.

7.4
Summary
Five experiments have been performed to explore the relationship between sleep and
application usage. To begin with, application usage before bedtime doesn’t seem to
affect sleep duration as there is no significant correlation between the variables. On the
other hand, using our smartphone after sleeping might positively impact the duration
of sleep, especially on weekdays as the fraction of users with a positive correlation is
half. Furthermore, it was shown that smartphone usage before going to bed does not
influence the sleep interruption an individual might have.
As wristbands are not very accurate in detecting the sleep states, the Weighted
Average Sleep State(WASS) metric was presented and calculated by formula 7.1. WASS
is not affected by the use of the phone before onset time while it could be negatively
influenced by its usage after bedtime. Finally, it was shown that the number of sleep
interruptions doesn’t have an impact on the quality of sleep, as it doesn’t change the
WASS significantly.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
This chapter includes a discussion on the main findings of this project. The first three
sections discuss the results on smartphone usage, sleep, and how sleep is affected by
phone usage before or after bedtime. The last section discusses some limitations raised
during this study.
8.1
Smartphone usage
This research has shown how users distribute their phone usage into different applications
before and after their bedtime.
We haven’t looked into specific applications but on
categories to make the study more general. Overall, there is not much literature studying
application usage before and after sleep, however, it was proved that of the users that
use a smartphone, a vast majority uses it for around 1 hour per day while one-third
checks it between sleep [Sub+17]. As it was shown in figures C.2 and C.1 although there
is no difference in the number of sessions we can see in screen time as it was proved from
another research that users tend to have longer sessions but not many [Fal+10].
To be able to compare screen time between groups, we used the Kolmogorov-Smirnov
statistical test to evaluate the results based on the p-values acquired. We observed that
users are more likely to spend around 5 minutes on their phone before and after their
sleep and the preference appears to be in Games, Communication and Browsing. This
finding is validated by research trying to cluster smartphone users into categories and
showed that most of them are defined as ”night communicators”, as their use their phone
for communication during night [Zha+16].
Finally, we examined the distribution of screen time between groups defined by de-
mographic characteristics such as gender, age, and BMI. To begin with, females appear
to use slightly more their phone comparing to males and they prefer Games, Communi-
cation and Movie/TV while males show an additional preference on Browsing. Further-
more, screen time decreases as age increases, a meaningful observation as younger ages
are more familiar with the new technology and use their phone for various activities.
However Games is an application category preferred by all age groups but for younger
ones on a greater scale. On the contrary, seniors appear to like more Books and Music.
Finally, we haven’t proved dependence on BMI.
==PAGE:61==
8.2 Sleep
50
8.2
Sleep
The larger part of the sleep exploration is divided into weekdays and weekends as ac-
cording to one study there are differences between the two parts of the week [RWM03].
In our study, we focused on the time people sleep and wake up but mostly on the sleep
duration and the heterogeneities among groups.
First of all, we have seen that people wake up early on weekdays, most probably
because of work or university obligations (as users are older than 18 years old), and also
go to sleep early. On weekends we observed that users wake up at similar times and
a small fraction sleep later. Regarding gender dissimilarities in sleep duration, it was
proved that females sleep slightly more compared to males however age is an important
factor. Depending on the age group, we notice that the difference in sleep duration
among the genders changes with the minimum gap being at the age 25 to 35 years old.
This period could be related to motherhood and early parenthood when women especially
sleep very little. According to some studies, the ability to initiate sleep decreases as age
increases [Flo+00] meaning that older people have diﬀiculty in falling asleep and one
could expect that they would sleep less.
However, in this study, it was shown that
seniors sleep a little more than middle-aged adults. In that case, we could suspect that
everyday life obligations play an important role as seniors are retired and they are not
limited by morning alarm clocks. Finally, we proved that BMI doesn’t affect sleep.
8.3
Smartphone usage & sleep
As smartphones have become part of our everyday life, it is necessary to understand how
they affect their different aspects. As sleep is an essential function of the human body
[JRE15] it is important to understand whether the use of smartphones before or after
sleep time affects it and how.
Firstly, we explored the correlation between sleep duration and smartphone usage
before and after bedtime. What we showed is that phone usage before sleep doesn’t seem
to affect the total duration of sleep while its use after bedtime might have a positive
impact, meaning that the more we use our phone when a sleep interruption occurs, the
more we want to sleep. On the other hand, previous studies have shown that digital
media can decrease sleep duration [Orz+16] and that phone usage after onset time
doesn’t affect it at all [EV16].
Additionally, there is some work done examining how phone usage impacts sleep
quality. To evaluate sleep quality with the variables included in the dataset, we presented
the Weighted Average Sleep State (WASS) and examined the relationship between screen
time and sessions. From the literature we expected to prove a negative correlation among
the sleep and application metrics [KAL18; EV16] however our study proved that using
the phone before sleeping doesn’t influence the sleep quality.
Finally, we had some
findings indicating that usage after onset time could affect WASS negatively but this
tendency appears to apply to a small fraction of users.

8.4 Limitations
51
8.4
Limitations
What makes this study novel compared to the ones mentioned in chapter 2 and explore
the relationship between phone usage and sleep, is that we obtain the sleep data from
wristbands while in the previous studies the sleep evaluation is made with the Pittsburgh
Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) [Buy+89]. In PSQI the participants of the research fill a
questionnaire on their own to evaluate their sleep. The use of wristbands detects the
total sleep duration as well the duration of the sleep states based on the heartbeat and
includes also the exact onset and offset time. However, this could be considered a large
limitation of the project as the detection of the sleep states is not accurate as we have
seen in figure B.1, with sudden jumps to states indicating deep sleep. For this reason,
it was diﬀicult for us to evaluate the sleep quality by grouping the states in light and
deep sleep. However, as the dataset used in this project is the same as the one used in
Sigga’s et al. research [JML21], we have some findings regarding the validation of sleep
data. In their research, they compared the sleep data acquired from wristbands with
sleep measures across countries and they found consistency. Thus, we consider that data
regarding Denmark specifically are valid.
Furthermore, we are not aware of other user characteristics. In our dataset, we have
information regarding gender, age, and BMI and this is why we studied heterogeneities
among different groups. But other factors can bias the dataset such as exercise, since
people who exercise are more willing to track their heartbeat or sleep and thus, using
wristbands. If the dataset is biased we can extract safe conclusions for the entire pop-
ulation we study but for groups of users with specific characteristics. Also, we don’t
know if users spend time on other digital media such as computers or tablets. We only
have information regarding the time spent on smartphones however, it’s common that
people use tablets and computers before sleep and as it was mentioned in chapter 2
[Orz+16] the use of other digital media can influence sleep. Last but not least, some
others aspects influence sleep and could not be controlled during this project. The most
important of them are exercise, drinking, and stress. In chapter 7 we showed that sleep
interruptions are independent of screen time so there could be other factors that affect
them, and thus sleep quality, but couldn’t be examined during this project.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
As smartphones become part of our everyday life with the number of users constantly
increasing, the need to study their influence has raised. So far, studies showed that they
can affect various aspects of our life such as academic performance [Jud14; Kar+13;
JC12] or our psychology [Elh+17]. As sleep is an important function for resting and
restoring the brain and body [JRE15], there is the need to explore how smartphone
usage affects sleep. More specifically, we focused on application usage before and after
bedtime.
Our dataset consists of smartphone usage and sleep data, acquired from wristbands,
and thus this study was divided into three parts: smartphone usage, sleep, and their
relationship. Exploration of the smartphone usage data revealed some interesting trends
among users. Users appear to spend around 5 minutes on their phone and the most pop-
ular categories are Games, Communication and Browsing. Analysis by gender showed
that females pass 2 more minutes on their phone than males before bedtime while after
bedtime there is almost no difference. Furthermore, we have seen that as age increases,
screen time decreases, considering that older people are not familiar with this kind of
technology. On the other hand, there is no dependence on BMI. Regarding sleep, users
tend to sleep and wake up early on weekdays while larger fractions report later times
for both sleep onset and offset times on weekends. These points are meaningful if we
take into consideration that work obligations. Moreover, a sleep duration between 6 and
8.5 hours seems to be normal for most of the users with females sleeping around 7.5
hours, approximately 30 minutes than males. Age is an important factor influencing
sleep duration. The difference between the two genders is large at ages smaller than 25
and afterward, it appears to close since at this point women appear to sleep less than
before. Finally, we haven’t seen any clear dependence between BMI and sleep duration.
The most important part of this project is to explore the relationship between sleep
and smartphone usage before and after bedtime. To evaluate smartphone usage we used
screen time while as sleep metrics we used sleep duration and the Weighted Average
Sleep State (WASS) for sleep quality.
To begin with, phone usage before going to
sleep doesn’t affect either sleep duration or WASS as it was shown from the analysis
performed. Individual exploration revealed some cases where a rise in screen time can
cause a decrease in sleep duration but after generalizing the exploration in all the dataset
we have seen no correlation between the two metrics. Also, the same tendency appears
to be between WASS and screen time or sessions. On the other hand, there are some
good indications that using our phone after bedtime affects our sleep. First of all, sleep
duration rises which makes sense as users need to make up for the time they dedicated

to their phone especially if there is no alarm clock to bother them the next morning.
Additionally, there is a negative correlation between screen time and WASS meaning that
sleep quality is negatively influenced. Finally, we have examined sleep interruptions and
phone usage before onset time and we showed that passing time on our phone before
sleeping doesn’t have an impact on the number of times we might wake up in the middle
of time. Overall, sleep interruptions appear to be a measure that is independent of phone
usage and also doesn’t affect the sleep quality, as disturbances on WASS are irrelevant
with the number of sleep interruptions a user might have.
9.1
Future Work
There are a few directions we could follow for future work. The most important is to find
an accurate way to detect the sleep states to be able to evaluate the sleep quality. Using
wristbands to acquire sleep data makes this research novel, however, their accuracy in
detecting the sleep states and input noise in the data is a major drawback.
Furthermore, we could scale up the research by adding users from different coun-
tries so to study heterogeneities based on geography. Finally, it would be interesting
to examine the relationship between smartphone usage and onset and offset times, to
understand whether waking up or sleeping times are affected by the way we use our
smartphone.
