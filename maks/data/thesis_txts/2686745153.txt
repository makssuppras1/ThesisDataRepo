Abstract
This study aimed to investigate whether it is possible to use borehole cuttings as a cheap
alternative to cored samples for estimating the swelling properties of clays found doing oil
explorations.
The petroleum industry often uses salts and other chemical compounds in their drilling
fluid mixtures to prevent clays and shales from expanding and affecting the stability of
the well. A salt commonly used for this purpose is potassium chloride [1]. As it is well
established that salts can be used to change a clay particles properties significantly [2][3].
The samples obtained from drill cuttings were therefore affected by the stabilizing agents
used when drilling the well, whereas it was necessary to clean the samples before they
could be used for geotechnical experiments.
To clean the samples, a simplistic cleaning procedure was established. This procedure
relied upon diffusion to remove diffused and free ions from the samples. This cleaning
involved repeatedly suspending the drill cutting particles in deionized water, replacing the
deionized water between each suspension.
Nine primary drill cutting was chosen for the purpose of testing their swelling properties
and cleaned using this approach. Four additional samples were used to run a separate
test, with the aim of evaluating the cleaning procedures effectiveness, and to establish
whether the ions released into the suspension solution could be related to the samples’
ability to absorb water.
The experimental results for the nine drill cutting samples showed a striking similarity,
even though their clay content varied significantly. The results furthermore showed a rel-
atively poor correlation with theoretical well-established parametric relations. At the same
time, the tests evaluating the cleaning procedure showed little evidence of the cleaning
having any effect. Based on the results obtained, it was therefore not possible to deter-
mine whether drill cuttings could be a suitable substitute for cored sample. As there was
found no clear indications that the simplistic washing procedure used in the present study
had any significant effect on the removal of the drilling fluid chemicals.

Introduction
The oil and gas industry has over the last couple of decades, steadily been shifting from
oil-based drilling fluid towards water-based drilling fluid. The changing of drilling fluid
has, both been economical- and environmentally beneficial. However, the use of water-
based fluids gives rise to problems with soils and rocks that exhibit swelling properties,
as any induced swelling in a borehole is likely to cause stability problems. The typical
cause of swelling doing drilling is shales and clays. To inhibit the swelling of shales and
clays, various chemicals are mixed in the drilling fluid. A common method of inhibiting the
swelling behavior of clays are to reduce the clay particles ability to absorb water. This can
be achieved in different ways, but a commonly used technique is to saturate the swelling
inducing particles with ions and replacing the in-situ ions with ions of higher valence or
smaller atomic radius [1].
When drilling a well for oil exploration, the drill cuttings are mainly used to estimate the
geological layer and test for oil traces. The material is otherwise considered as a by-
product that needs to be exposed of. This makes the drill cuttings a plentiful sample
material source that maybe could be used for parametric mapping. However, for drill
cuttings to be of any reliable use for this purpose, it will be necessary to consider the
effects that the stabilization agents used, has on the soil properties.
1.1
Aim of the Thesis
The purpose of this study will be to investigate whether it is possible to use borehole
cuttings as a cheap alternative to cored samples to estimate the swelling properties of
clays found doing oil explorations.
The drilling cuttings used for this study report are from the danish oil well N-11 placed in
the field Gorm, showed in Figure 1.1.
The field Gorm is placed in the Danish part of the Central Graben and well N-11 is placed
at coordinates: UTM zone 31: E 610904 N 6160885.
As the drill cuttings from Gorm were sampled in 1981, it is assumed that the primary
mechanism used to inhibit swelling is ionic exchange. Whereas this thesis will look into
the possible removal of these ions and whether the drill cuttings from oil exploration can
be used as a representative alternative to core sampling.

The Paleogene and Neogene in the
Danish Central Graben
This chapter will briefly present the two most prominent late Cenozoic formations in the
Danish Central Graben, which is the origin of the drill cuttings. After that, the drill cutting
samples will be presented.
In the Jurassic period, rifting occurred in the central part of the North Sea due to continen-
tal drift. This created grabens such as the Viking Graben and the Danish Central Graben.
These Grabens formed sedimentation basins, where poor circulation made the formation
of oil possible. The oil in the Danish North Sea is typically located in anticlines in the chalk
layers from the upper Cretaceous and early Paleocene, meaning that to reach the oil, the
well has to go through the younger sedimentary layers [5].
Throughout the Paleogene and Neogene, the sedimentary supplied varied greatly, but the
sedimentary environment in the North Sea did not vary as significantly as onshore Den-
mark [4][6]. In the North Sea, the sedimentary layers from the Paleogene and Neogene
are divided into six different formations, as shown in Appendix A.1. The two most promi-
nent formations is the Horda- and the Lark formations, made of sediments from a period
of approximately 39 million years. However, the thicknesses of these two formations vary
greatly depending on the location in the central North Sea, as seen on the isochore maps
in Figure 2.1 and 2.2

Throughout the Paleocene and Eocene, the sediments were generally dominated by clay,
with an upwards increasing amount of silt and sand [6]. A high Illite content is found
along the back then the southern coast of Norway, with decreasing Illite amounts further
out in the North Sea. Throughout the Paleocene and Eocene, an upwards increasing
percentage of Kaolinite is found as the supply of volcanic material decreased [7]. Likewise,
the Smectite content decreases throughout the Paleocene and Eocene.
The changes in the clay mineralogy are related to factors such as the distance to the
shorelines, the direction of the sedimentation transportation, the climate, and the access
to volcanic material. For the formation of Kaolinite minerals to be favored over the other
minerals, there must be an abundance of alumina and a scarcity of silica. Furthermore,
the optimal conditions for the formation of Kaolinite is a low pH environment with a low
salinity, which is often found in areas with good drainage[8]. Kaolinite is often found in

more significant quantities in shallower water, near the shorelines[7].
For the formation of Smectite to be favored, the environment has to have high pH, low
drainage and an abundance of silica, and a high salinity with a low potassium content[8].
These conditions are primarily found in periods with high volcanic activity, which is why
the Paleocene and Eocene generally have a high content of Smectite minerals, due to the
volcanic activity from the widening of the Norwegian-Greenland Sea [7]. For the formation
of Illite to happen, the conditions need to be similar to those needed to form Smectite, with
the addition that potassium needs to be present [8].
In the Oligocene, the lithology is dominated by silty clay [6]. The Smectite content in the
clay further decreased, while the amount of Illite and Kaolonite increased, likely supplied
by weathered material from Norway [7].
In the Miocene, the sea level in the North Sea decreased due to an increased uplifting of
Scandinavia [6], as shown in Figure 2.3.

2.1
The drill cuttings
The drill cuttings were obtained from the rig Maersk Explorer in the Gorm field from well
N-11 which surface coordinates is UTM zone 31: E 610898 N 6160816. The cuttings
were sampled by Dansk Boreselskab every 30ft starting from approximately 500ft.
When it comes to the sampling of drill cuttings, there is a significant uncertainty involved.
The drill cuttings are essentially lumps of materials transported with the drilling fluid up to
the drilling rig. The time it takes for these lumps to reach the rig might vary depending on
the size of the lumps, the viscosity, and the flow speed of the drilling fluid. The sample
depth should therefore be seen as an estimation. The lumps making up the drill cutting
will not precisely match the in-situ soil at the given depth but will instead represent the
broader tendencies found in the given layer.
The samples used in this study were chosen based on three factors. The on-site ge-
ologists’ estimate the soil type and composition, the approximate depth of the samples
and the sample size. The samples were chosen so that three ”continuous” samples all
contained sufficient material to properly map the parameters that affect a soils swelling
properties. Finding three continuous samples proved difficult as the estimated minimum
material quantities needed was 450g, and the sizes of the drill cuttings varied roughly
between 200g-600g.
The idea behind choosing three continuous samples was to increase the chances that
these samples would have somewhat similar parameters, making it possible to group
them. Whereas each group chosen aimed to have varying clay content in between the
group.
Two test series was performed. These will be elaborated upon in section 4. The samples
chosen for the primary test series was from the depths of 5530ft, 5500ft, 5440ft, 4030ft,
4000ft, 3970ft, 3040ft, 3010ft and 2920ft. These samples were grouped into three groups,
and these groups will going forward be referred to as either the upper group, which con-
tains sample for the depth 3040ft, 3010ft and 2920ft. The middle group which contains
the samples form the depth of 4030ft, 4000ft and 3970ft, or the lower group containing
the samples form the depth of 5530ft, 5500ft and 5440ft.
For the secondary test series samples from the depths of 5380ft, 3220ft, 3190ft, 3160ft,
3100ft and 3070ft were chosen.
All the sample depths are the measured depth(MD) and not the true vertical depth(TVD).
Nevertheless, going forward, the different samples will be referred to by their measured
sample depth with the feet denotation ’.

3
Clay Theory
This chapter will describe the mineralogy of clay and the theory behind how clay behaves.
This chapter will give a brief introduction to the most common clay minerals. The chapter
will furthermore give a short introduction to the mechanical properties clay particles and
how these properties are linked to the water content.
3.1
Clay mineralogy
A clay mineral consists of individual units of either a 1:1 or 2:1 structure of tetrahedral
sheets and an octahedral sheet, stacked together in layers, as shown in Figure 3.1. The
exact atomic configuration of these sheets varies, depending on the clay mineral.
The typical tetrahedral sheet is comprised of a silicon atom and four oxygen atoms locked
together in a tetrahedron. However, isomorphic substitution may lead to the silicon atom
being replaced by an aluminum atom. Likewise, the octahedrons in a sheet structure are
in their most stable configuration when they consist of an aluminum and six hydroxide
groups, forming gibbsite. Due to isomorphic substitution, the aluminum atom can be re-
placed by lower valence atoms such as magnesium, forming Brucite. These substitutions
of higher valence atoms with atoms of lower valence will influence the net charge of a
clay particle. Where sheets that have undergone extensive isomorphic substitution will
have a higher negative surface charge than sheets that have not undergone isomorphic
substitution [9].
The individual sheets in the unit are held together by primary bonds in the form of cova-
lent bonds. However, depending on the charge of the individual unit surfaces, different
combinations of the interlayer bonds can occur. These different combinations of interlayer

3.1.1
Kaolinite minerals
The Kaolinite minerals consist of units with a 1:1 structure with a basal spacing of 7.2Å,
as shown in Figure 3.2. Where each unit is bound together by hydrogen bonds formed
between the hydroxides on the surface of the octahedral sheets and oxygens on the tetra-
hedral sheets [9].
Most of the clay minerals in the Kaolinite group experience very little isomorphic substitu-
tion. This combined with the relatively strong interlayer bonds, means that these minerals
generally is very resistant to weathering and can therefore become large relative to the
other clay minerals.

3.1.2
Smectite minerals
The Smectite minerals consist of units with a 2:1 structure which generally undergoes a
significant amount of isomorphic substitution. As a result, the surface of the individual
units has a high negative surface charge, which attracts cations and the positive dipole of
the water. The interlayer bonding between each unit will generally be weaker as Smectite
units have a greater negative surface charge relative to Kaolinite or Illite, whereas the
repulsion between the individual Smectite units is greater. The Smectite structure has a
basal spacing of 14-15Å, as illustrated in Figure 3.3, where the forces keeping the units
together mainly consist of Van der Wall forces bonding with the cations and water in the
interlayer space [9]. Due to the weak interlayer bonding, Smectite clay minerals stacks in
considerably fewer layers than Kaolinite particles. Whereas a Smectite mineral often is
a lot thinner and smaller than Kaolinite or Illite minerals. Unlike the Illite, the size of the
interlayer spacing in Smectite is very susceptible to changes in the porewater salinity.

3.1.3
Illite minerals
The Illite minerals consist of units with a 2:1 structure but have undergone less isomor-
phic substitution than Smectite. As the surface charge of the Illite units is less negative
than Smectite, fewer cations are needed to satisfy the charge difference, and less water
will be attracted to the surface. Furthermore, as the Illite interlayer gaps are filled with
potassium ions, as illustatred in Figure 3.6, the interlayer gap configuration is very stable,
with a basal spacing of 10Å. An Illite mineral is thereforr not very susceptible to changes
in porewater and salinity [9].

3.1.4
Chlorite minerals
Chlorite differentiates itself a bit from the other clay minerals considered, as its unit struc-
ture consists of a 2:1 with an additional octahedral sheet held in between the unit layers.
The basal spacing chlorite is 14Å, and as the interlayer gap contains an octahedral sheet,
the mineral structure is not affected by changes in pore water salinity [9].

modification of the clay as old as the Paleogene has happened in the North sea.

3.2.2
Consistency limits
The consistency limits were initially defined by A.M. Atterberg and are therefore often
referred to as Attenberg limits. The consistency limits are used to differentiate between
fine grained soils consistency states. Where the transition from one state to another is
directly related to the effect the water content has on the mechanical behaviour of a fine
grained soil. In geotechnical engineering four different soil states are used, the solid-, the
semisolid-, the plastic- and the liquid state. A soil in the solid and semisolid state will act
brittle to semi brittle when exposed to deformations, while a soil in its plastic state will
deform plasticity, and a soil in its liquid state will act as a viscous fluid. The limits between
each state are analytically defined based on different standardized tests, and can from a
holistic point of view, therefore seem arbitrary. The consistency limits of interest in this
report are the plastic- and the liquid limit.
3.2.2.1
Plastic limit
The plastic limit defines the boundary between the semisolid state and the plastic state.
The transition from the plastic state to the semisolid state happens gradually as the water
content is reduced, which physically manifests in decreasing the soils moldability. Where
the plastic limit indicates the water content at which the soil loses its ability to be remoulded
without affecting its volume and cracking [9]. The exact point at which a soil reaches its
plastic limit varies between standards. According to DS/EN ISO 17892-12:2018 the plas-
tic limit is the water content at which the soil crumbles as it is rolled into a threat with a
diameter of 3mm.
3.2.2.2
Liquid limit
The liquid limit defines the boundary between a fine-grained soils plastic- and liquid state.
The transition from the plastic state to the liquid state also happens gradually, whereas
this analytical limit describes the point at which the soil starts acting like a viscous fluid
under a standardised dynamic shearing force.
A. Casagrande attempted to relate the liquid limit to the shear strength of the soil, as it was
observed that when the water content increases, the shear strength of a soil decreases.
He suggested that a soil at its liquid limit on average has a shear strength of 2.65 kPA. In
a later study done by [16], it was suggested that the shear strength at the point where a
soil reaches its liquid limit is on average 1.7 kPa. This was partly based on the findings

of [17], where 29 soil samples of remoulded clay were tests. The clays had liquid limits
ranging from 30-200%, where a linear relationship between an increased liquid limit to a
decreased undrained shear strength of the clays was found.
The decreasing shear strength with increasing, water content results from of the water
filling up the inter-particle spacing, increasing the distance between particles. As the dis-
tance between the particle increase the strength of the inter-particle forces will decrease.
As the liquid limit indicates that the amount of water a fine grained soil can absorb, the
liquid limit is related to the electronegativity and the specific surface area of the particles
in the soil [18]. Meaning that a soil rich in Smectite minerals will have a significantly higher
liquid limit then to a soil rich in Kaolinite or Illite. The liquid limit of a fine grained soil will
therefore also be affected by the salinity of the soil. As a high concentration of ions in
the inter-particle space will decrease the inter-particle forces, decreasing the soils overall
ability to absorb water[19].

3.2.3
Soil fabric
The charges of the edges of the clay particles will influence the orientation of each indi-
vidual particle relative to the other. A positively charged edge will lead to a face to edge
orientation, and a negatively charged edge will lead to deflocculation. Edge to edge floc-
culation will typically occur if the charges of the edges are relatively balanced. However,
these relative orientations can also be effected by pressure, pH, and salinity, and can
furthermore affect the relative association between particles, making the microstructures
either dispersed or aggregated.
The location and the magnitude of the charges on the clay surface, and the sedimentary
environment, therefore, govern microstructure of the soil Which influences the porosity,
the surface area and the stiffness of the soil [22][9].
3.2.4
Swelling
The swell potential describes the volumetric change that soil will undergo if saturated. The
swell potential is strongly related to the swelling pressure, as the soils ability to change vol-
ume will be highly dependent on the confining pressure. McCormack, D.E. and Wilding,
L.P [23] found that by applying a confining pressure equal to 10% of the swelling pres-
sure, 50% of the volumetric change would be prevented. Soils that undergoes swelling is
often referred to as expansive soils. The swelling property is mainly associated with clays
and highly organic soils, where swelling occurs if a not fully saturated soil is subjected to
moisture. The water will be pulled into the pores of the soil and into the interlayer spaces
of clay particles, increasing the water content in the soils. This increase in water content
will result in a volume increase, sometimes multiple times greater than the volume of the
solids themselves.
A soils ability to pull water into its pore is typically measured as a suction force. The total
suction force a soil can exert is dependent on the charge of the particle surface, specific
surface area, the microstructure of the soil, mineralogy, salinity, clay content, organic con-
tent, hydraulic conductivity, compaction and the saturation degree of the soil.[23][24][9][25][22]
Some parameters have a more significant effect on the suction than other parameters.
However, all the parameters that affect the soil suction are strongly intertwined.

4.3.8
X-ray diffraction
X-ray diffraction (XRD) was used to estimate the mineralogy of the samples. X-ray diffrac-
tion uses ionizing radiation, specifically X-rays, which has an electromagnetic spectrum
ranging from 0.01 Å to 100 Å. The shorter wavelengths will be more energetic, while the
longer wavelengths have a greater penetrating effect. As the x-rays passes through the
atomic structure of the crystal lattice, the photons can either be adsorbed in a given atomic
layer or pass through the layer. If the x-ray passes through a given atomic layer of the crys-
talline structure, the nearby electrons will still absorb some energy from the x-ray through
electromagnetic forces. This energy adsorption will not be large enough to change the
electrons atomic orbit, and will instead be readmitted as heat and x-rays. As each atomic
layer absorbs energy from the photons passing through, each atom in the different layers
will radiate x-rays and heat. The x-rays radiated from the atoms in the lattice will be in
random directions, where some of the x-rays will destructively interfere with each other,
while other x-rays will constructively interfere, amplifying the intensity. Depending on the
atomic arrangement, i.e. the structure of the crystal lattice, strong amplifications of the
x-ray wave can occur at specific angles. By varying the angle of the incident wave relative
to the sample, the angles at which these strong amplifications occur can be mapped. By
controlling the x-ray emitted wavelength, the distance between atoms in the crystal lattice
can be calculated based on the angle between the incident wave and the diffracted wave
[13].

4.3.9
Soil reconstitution
As samples taken from drill cuttings are highly disturbed, there is little hope of obtaining
an intact soil fabric using this sampling method. Furthermore, the nature of the washing
procedure used in the report will affect the soil structure and any soil lumps that might
have had an intact soils structure would also be dispersed. Whereas to test swell pres-
sure of the nine primary soil samples, it was necessary to reconstitute the them. The
basic procedure for this was to dry the samples at 50◦C for two days before grinding the
soil to a powder mixing the powder with water, and consolidating the samples [45].
To determine the most optimal reconstitution procedure, preliminary reconstitution tests
was performed on blue clay samples. For these tests, two different slurry consistencies
and two different pore fluid combinations were tested, it was furthermore tested whether
it would be possible to increase the height of the consolidated samples by filling addi-
tional material into the oedometer ring after an initial consolidation. The original idea of
increasing the height of the sample was to perform the swelling tests perpendicular to the
consolidation direction, whereas a smaller sample for the swelling test was to be taken
horizontally through the consolidated sample. A swelling performed perpendicular to the
consolidation direction would have been more representative of the swelling occurring in
a borehole out in the Danish North Sea due to the layer orientation found there. However,
this consolidation procedure proved to require a lot of material and was therefore not ex-
plored further.
The primary purpose of the slurry consistencies tested was to figure out the most optimal
consistency for filling the oedometer ring. The water contents tested was 45% and 60%,
where even though approximately the same amount of material was consolidated in the
two tests, the variation between the final void ratios was 6.2%. This variation was initially
thought to be due to a bad filling technique solely, but B. Shipton and M.R.Coop[46] also
found that when reconstituting a natural clay material with different initial water contents,
it was difficult to achieve a consistent relation between the initial- and the final specific
volume. Although the relative difference between the final specific volumes decreases at
larger stresses.
For the pore fluid tests, two blue clay samples were reconstituted identically, one us-
ing deionized water and the other using a relatively strong sodium chloride brine with a
concentration of 1 mol/L. These tests were performed to estimate the significance of the
salinity of the fluid used for the reconstitution.
The final void ratio of the blue clay sample reconstituted with the brine only had a void
ratio 1.2% smaller than the blue clay sample reconstituted with deionized water.

4.3.10
Swelling potential
To test the swelling properties of the samples, a swelling pressure test was chosen over
a free swell test. The data obtained from an experiment such as a free swell test would
not be as representative of the conditions in the subsurface, as the overburden pressure
will be one of the controlling factors for the in-situ swelling.
The definition of swelling pressure is the pressure that is required to keep the soil sample
at a constant volume when water is added[24]. This will directly reflect that if the in-situ
pressure is greater than the swell pressure, the soil will not be able to swell, while if the
in-situ pressure is smaller, a volumetric increase might occur.
The test was performed as a constant volume swell pressure test, where the volume
was constrained in an oedometer ring with the help of the hydraulic piston in a triaxial
4.3. TESTS USED

frame as shown in Figure 4.24. The force induced by the swelling was measured with a
force gauge, that was locked in between the loading cap of the oedometer cell and the
triaxial frame. The test was performed directly after the consolidation of the samples.
The oedometer cells were placed in the triaxial frame, and a force of 6500 N was applied
with the piston. After the 6500 N had been applied, the load was slowly lowered down
to approximately 150 N, at which point the swelling test was started by adding deionized
water to the bath in the oedometer cell. The duration of each of the swelling tests was
24 hours. The preloading of the sample to 6500 N and the subsequent relaxation to 150
N was to ensure a proper connection between the loading frame and the loading cap to
prevent volumetric changes of the samples.

5.1.1
Changes in conductivity of the washing fluid
The conductivity of the washing fluid after each washing of the nine primary samples
is shown in Figure 5.1. These measurements seem hectic and random, which is likely
connected to the solids to washing fluid ratio not being controlled. The point at which a
solution reaches an equilibrium will depend on the volume of the solvent relative to the
amount of solute. If it is simplistically assumed that the samples contain a somewhat
similar ion content, the variation in the sample sizes means that the solvent to solute ratio
will vary. This variation increases even further as the volumetric expansion of the different
samples also varies. When the volume of a sample expanse the fluid replaced in between
washes will decrease, whereas the solvent to solute ratio will decline, leading to a smaller
change in conductivity in between washings.
In Figure 5.1, there is also a clear difference between the initial conductivity from the
samples in the lower group relative to the shallower samples. However, this difference in
conductivity decreases with the washings for all samples but not for 5440’. For sample
5440’ there is a decrease of the 46.7% in the conductivity between the first and the second
washing, as shown in Table 5.1. However, after the second washing, the decrease in fluid
conductivity between washings suddenly flattens out. This is likely due to the volumetric
increase shown earlier in Figure 4.9. This increased volume reduces the amount of free
fluid that can be replaced and will decrease the solid to washing fluid ratio. The reduced
amount of deionized water added in the following washing will therefore reach equilibrium
faster. This indicates that the solids to washing fluid ratio might be an important factor in
the washing. However, when considering samples such as 3970’ and 4030’, which both
were large samples that experienced a volumetric increase throughout the washing, this
ratio does not seem to affect the washing of these samples significantly.

The samples were consolidated with a pressure of 4.9 MPa, which barely is equal to a

contrast, the suction induced by the in-situ compression most likely will be slightly higher.
However, these tests showed that the soil samples only would induce a swelling pressure
of around 1 MPa. Meaning that if the overburden pressure is larger than 1 MPa, the soils
tested likely would not swell, even without taking into account the soil fabrics effect on the
swelling. Which is implausible to take into account when using drill cuttings.
Effectiveness of the washing
The initial washing test of sample 5380’ generally showed promising results, with both an
increase in its liquid limit and the specific surface area. However, when considering these
two parameters’ nature, it becomes debatable whether the washing had the intended ef-
fect. The increased ability to absorb water might not have been caused by the removal
of diffused ions as first hypothesised but might instead result from the increased surface
area. The increase in the specific surface area might result from the washing procedure
dispersing the natural particle association through the sedimentation, opening up addi-
tional surface area.
If the dispersion of aggregates caused the presumed effect, it is likely not through the
breakage of cementitious bonds created by calcium carbonate, as the washing was found
to have no significant effect on the carbonate content equivalent.
This would also coalesce into the conductivity of the washing fluid poorly correlating to all
the parameters effecting the swelling properties. The correlations found might be a result
of the numbers of washings both effecting the amount of ions diffusing into the solution
and the number of particles separated from their aggregates. As the sand, silt and clay
particles was presumably separated throughout the washing procedure. The settlement
speed of the different particles sizes, would results in the finer particles settling on top of
the coarser particles. This would free up the clay particles to settle and form flocculated
structures as they sediment, thereby increasing the volume occupied by particles. Which
would explain the volumetric increase of the samples observed throughout the washing
procedure.
The reason why the washing seemingly only affected sample 5380’ in the washing test
can only be postulated, as no clear tendencies were found in the data collected for this
report. However, it is plausible that it could be connected to the in-situ soil salinity or the

original soil fabric. In aggregates containing different fraction sizes, sand and silt particles
can be clothed in clay particles, where the clay particles form shells around these larger
fractions [9]. Freeing up this shell of clay particles might increase the specific surface
area in the sample and thereby its ability to absorb water.
It might also be that the lumps of material in the lower group samples was a bit larger,
as they were packed harder, which would result in the drilling fluids to a larger degree

recommended practice for using stabilization agents for road constructions, where it is
recommended to reduce the particle size of the soil down to as close to the tactoid size
as possible to avoid the stabilization agent reacting with the lumps of clay, instead of the
tactoids [50].
No matter what caused the initial promising results, little other evidence was found to
support that the washing had any effect. The second test washing series found that the
number of washes did not affect the tested samples ability to absorb water. Likewise, the
primary test showed a poor correlation between clay content and the specific surface area.
Furthermore, a poor connection between the clay mineralogy and the specific surface area
was found. The results indicate that the Kaolinite content gives a greater contribution to
the surface area than Illite, which suggest that the specific surface areas measured are
mainly linked to the external surface area, as the Kaolinite minerals often form larger
plates structures than Illite. It, therefore, seems likely that the washing procedure did not
remove the drilling fluid chemicals.

Conclussion
The project aimed to assess whether drill cuttings from oil exploration could be a feasible
cheap alternative to cored samples. As samples obtained from drill cuttings are affected
by the stabilizing agents used for drilling the well, it is necessary to clean the samples
before they reliably can be used for determining geotechnical parameters. For this pur-
pose, a simplistic cleaning procedure was established, which relied upon the removal of
diffused ions and ions in the voids through diffusion. This procedure involved repeatedly
suspending the drill cutting particles in deionized water, replacing the deionized water be-
tween each suspension.
Using this approach, nine drill cutting samples, taken from oil field Gorm, well N-11, was
prepared for geotechnical testing. While additional four samples were washed with a sim-
ilar procedure for the purpose of evaluating the effectiveness of the washing and establish
whether the ions released into the suspension fluid could be related to the soils ability to
absorb water.
The nine drill cutting samples were chosen based on their estimated clay content to evalu-
ate whether the grain size distribution influenced the cleaning procedure’s effectiveness.
The clay content was also of importance as the goal of the geotechnical experiments
was to assess the samples’ swelling properties and whether they were affected by the
drilling fluid. The tests used to determine the samples’ swelling properties were Liquid
limit, Plastic limit, Grain size distribution, Calcium carbonate equivalent, Loss on ignition,
BET surface area analysis, X-ray diffraction and a volumetric restrained swelling pres-
sure test. The samples were furthermore reconstituted before performing the swelling
pressure tests, as the cleaning procedure had dispersed the soil particles.
The experimental results for the nine drill cutting samples showed a striking similarity,
even though their clay content varied significantly. The consistency limits for all the sam-
ples was found to be roughly similar, and the swell pressure induced by all the samples
was approximately one-fifth of the consolidation pressure.
The results furthermore showed a relatively poor correlation between theoretical well-
established parametric relations. Where factors such as the clay content seemingly hav-
ing a quite small contribution to the samples’ specific surface area. Likewise, the XRD esti-
mated clay mineralogy indicated that the samples contained approximately equal amounts
of Kaolinite and Illite, and little to no Smectite or Chloride. However, the results showed
that Kaolinite seemingly contributed significantly more to the samples’ specific surface
area than Illite.
The washing of the four additional samples furthermore did not show any noticeable ef-
fect of the cleaning procedure and found no relations between the ions released into the
85
==PAGE:94==
suspension fluid and the samples ability to adsorb water.
These results suggest that the assumption about ionic exchange being the driving mech-
anism for the stabilizing in well N-11 was incorrect. It is instead plausible other stabilizing
agents, such as polymors, were used.
Based on the present study, it has not been possible to determine whether drill cuttings
could be a suitable substitute for cored samples. There are no clear indications that the
simplistic washing procedure used in the present study had any significant effect on re-
moving the drilling fluid chemicals.
If drill cuttings are to replace or act as a supplement to cored samples, quite a few chal-
lenges need to be sorted out. Just removing the stabilization agents from the samples is
a challenging procedure that might require chemicals, that could compromise the sam-
ple’s integrity even further. Combining this with the sampling uncertainty, the disturbed
soil fabric and the unknown porewater chemistry might significantly limit the applicability
of drill cuttings as samples for determining reliable geotechnical parameters.
6.1
For further studies
For the second test washing series, which was not finished, it was hypothesised that by
washing the drill cutting samples in a strong sodium chloride brine, it would be possible to
replace ions such as potassium from outer-sphere complexes. As sodium has a greater
hydration radius than potassium, the basic washing procedure might be able to make the
ions diffuse into the solution. The logic behind this washing procedure is based on the
agricultural use of fertilizer. Where nutritional ions lower the Lyotropic series replaces ions
higher in the series.
This hypothesis is furthermore supported by the findings of Caterina Di Maio who writes
Under low values of axial stress and high void ratio, potassium and calcium effects can
be revereted by exposing the material to concentrated NaCL solution[51]
This hypothesis should be tested further, and it should be investigate whether its possible
to remove ion bound in an inner-sphere complex.
It should furthermore be investigated if its possible to remove other common stabilizing
agents such as polymers, and whether the removal of these would require such radical
measures, that any trace of sample authenticity would be destroyed.
