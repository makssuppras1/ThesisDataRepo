Abstract
Climate change is one of the biggest challenges that human kind has ever faced. In order
to adapt to it and mitigate it as much as possible, governments and institutions like the EU are
promoting regulations to decrease CO2 emissions.
Energy consumed in buildings accounts for 40% of the total energy consumption in the EU
and the US. Danish government goal for a fossil free society by 2050 and the strict building codes,
create the need for innovation to reduce the building energy consumption at a reasonable cost.
Windows are the weakest point of the building envelope regarding heat loss. However they
are yet very necessary to provide a view to the exterior and natural daylight, which contributes to
reduce the energy use in electric lightning.
Roof windows duplicate the daylight factor for the same opening area compared to façade
windows. However, the low energy efﬁciency of traditional roof windows has made their usage
decrease due to the demanding regulations for energy savings, in spite of their high daylighting
efﬁciency. In order to provide enough daylight in deep rooms where façade windows cannot meet
the daylight recommendations, at the same time as reducing the energy consumption for space
heating, the improvement of roof windows seems to be a more cost-effective solution compared to
the increment of the whole building envelope insulation thickness [2].
This thesis tries to develop a class of improved roof windows able to provide the recommended
daylight inside deep rooms while offering a low heat transmittance.
Five alternatives to improve the roof window have been developed. From them, the alternative
using an aerogel window was the best energy performance solution to provide the necessary
daylight into a deep room. The study demonstrates that this aerogel solution for a roof window
would have lower energy cost compared to the same deep room with no roof window on it.
It was found that the use of a reﬂecting coating for the lightwell surface, increased the light
transmissivity of the roof window, helped to decrease the window opening size and therefore, the
energy losses.
The study found that the use of the developed class of improved roof windows offers a lower
space heating consumption and fewer overheating hours while complying with the recommendations
for minimum daylight in deep rooms compared to the solution in which only a big façade window
covers the minimum daylight recommendation.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In the recent years, the scientists from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)
have shown their consensus on the unprecedented global warming on the earth during the last
century [3]. Also, they have shown their consensus about that the rising levels of greenhouse gases
and other human activities have been the most likely dominant cause. Climate change supposes
one of the biggest dangers humankind has ever had, affecting millions of people. For this reason, it
is already one of the biggest challenges to face. To reduce and mitigate the global warming below a
safety threshold, IPCC scientists advised of the need of drastically reducing CO2 emissions.
In this scenario, many governments and international institutions as the EU are legislating to
reduce the human footprint on the environment, cutting down the greenhouse gases emissions and
promoting energy saving alternatives. In Denmark, there is an ambitious target for 2050 when
100% of the energy used for transport and energy sectors must be renewable. But in the short term,
regulations are also becoming strict; the Danish Building Code for 2020 requires nearly zero energy
buildings (NZEB) for all new constructions.
The housing sector is responsible for 25% of the energy consumption only in the EU [4].
According to the EIA, about 40% of the energy is consumed in residential or commercial buildings
2015 [5]. These statistics show that there is a big global potential of improvement in the buildings’
energy and of economic growth in the ﬁeld. This makes any development on energy savings for the
housing sector interesting.
Inside the buildings, one of the main sources of losses and gains are windows. They comprise
an important part of the building, as they have the function of providing natural daylight and
allowing a view to the exterior. Because of this, windows are a big ﬁeld of research nowadays.
When it comes to the illumination of a building, natural daylight is the most common preference,
and it also cuts down on energy from electrical lightning. One of the current trends in buildings is
the use of roof windows for their many advantages: they provide a larger amount of daylight for a
smaller opening, reducing the losses; they can provide natural daylight to a deeper room, etc.
In this context, the investigation on roof windows with improved energy performance appears
to be interesting. Given the thick insulation applied in the roofs of the new NZEB, when placing a

not need any modiﬁcation of the existing products, as its addition is separate from the outer rooftop
window structure. The easiness of the solution applicability could be interesting for the building
sector if the studies prove that it is cost effective.
1.2
Aim
The proposed improvement for the energy performance of the ﬂat roof windows consists of
applying an extra glazing at the inner ceiling level to prevent energy losses. Given the context and
the motivation for the realization of this research, the main goals of this project are:
• To ﬁnd appropriate software tools to perform the evaluation of the solution in a reliable and
accurate way.
• To evaluate the energy performance of the proposed improved rooftop windows.
• To evaluate the light distribution on this new solution and its differences for the several
proposed solutions.
• To evaluate the indoor comfort of the new solution.
• To ﬁnd an optimal combination of the optical characteristics for the window solution.
• To provide a solution for the airtightness of the window, the maintenance and the possible
solar shadings to avoid overheating of the closed space.
• To investigate the applicability of the solution and the scope of it.
• To evaluate the cost effectivity of the proposals.
1.3
Limitations
The main limitations of work carried out in the thesis are,
• The scarcity of data about the performance properties of granular aerogel glazing units from
different and standardized sources.
• The translucent windows daylight distribution modelling is limited by the capability of the
software tool and the lack of standardization in the building industry.
• The thesis does not study the heat ﬂows through the walls, frames and assemblies on a 3D
model due to the complexity of a 3D model creation.
• The thesis does not study the behaviour of the heat ﬂows though structural materials in the
roof slab. An ideal roof only composed of insulation is considered.

Aerogel glazing differentiates from usual glasses on its really low U-value. This U-value can

One of the most interesting applications of aerogel glazing units could be precisely horizontal
roof windows. Usual Argon ﬁlled glazing units signiﬁcantly decrease their performance when
placed in a horizontal position. This is because the glazing units are designed to isolate with a
convective movement of the gas inside the window cavity when they are placed vertically. For
this reason, the insulated windows have a big increment in U-value when changing the glass to a
horizontal orientation [15]. Aerogel could keep the extremely low U-value regardless of the glazing
position. The advantages of this characteristic must be investigated for horizontal fenestration
applications.
The windows with gas ﬁlled cavities isolate from the exterior with long convection cycles
between the panes when they are placed vertically. The inner gas travels from the bottom to the top
of the window cavity while exchanging heat. In the case of the windows horizontally installed, this

Similar studies, tools, and used methodologies
Regarding the really speciﬁc nature of the project, not so many previous relevant studies have
been found. Several researched studies measured the performance of skylights, simulating them
with daylighting software tools or experimentally. From them, some interesting results can be taken
into consideration.
As an example, Acosta, Navarro and Sendra [21], simulated the illuminance variation depending
on the reﬂection index of the lightwell and determined that it is a decisive factor in the interior of
the room. Also compared the different illuminance with the variation of the height and area of the
lightwell, determining that the illuminance generated by a lightwell is almost directly proportional
to its size. For their calculations, they used the simulation program Lightscape 3.2 (Autodesk).
Furthermore, several simulating software tools have been collected. Daylighting tools are
generally divided between radiosity and ray-tracing tools. Radiosity tools consider all surfaces
as diffuse surfaces and divide all surfaces as small patches. In radiosity tools, the illuminance is
calculated from the geometrical interaction between all the small patches, named form factors.
Ray-tracing tools, on the other hand, consider the different optical properties of the real surfaces,
whether if they are specular, diffuse or transparent. The simulation process is carried out tracing
each individual ray of light. From the result of this simulation, photo-realistic images can be
obtained [22].

3.1
Practical description of the problem
In the present time, lawmakers aim to increase the energy savings and the new building codes
come with stricter regulations in this matter. This is increasing the insulation thicknesses in building
walls and promoting research on energy efﬁciency for every building need. Daylighting helps
lowering energy consumption and it is a relevant health factor for the building occupants [25].
Rooﬂights provide more illuminance per aperture size than wall windows. This is useful because
the glazing area is a weaker surface for energy savings than the opaque walls. This scenario leaves
roof lights as a very interesting solution for daylighting. But the increasing thicknesses of the roofs
creates a potential problem that will be analyzed and an opportunity to solve it.
In the case of the roof window of study in the thesis, the Flat Glass Rooﬂight from VELUX
is placed around 20 cm over the ﬂat roof structure, as it can be seen in Figure 3.1. The purpose
of this elevation is to keep the roof opening perfectly water tight. This necessary outer frame
height produces extra thermal losses. The addition of the roof thickness and the window outer
elevation lead to a big and long lightwell, which can increase the heat losses of the house through
air convection.
Inside the room with a lightwell, a convection process takes part. The warm air heated inside
the room raises due to the buoyancy force because of its lower density. The air reaches the open
lightwell and raises until the top side of it. When this air gets in touch with the cold surface of the
outer glazing, its temperature drops, its density increases and descends again into the room. When
the air is close to the ﬂoor, it is heated again and a convection loop starts. This natural convection
loop increases the energy losses of the house.
Roof windows are especially worsening this process because their horizontal position reduces
their U-value and this makes the inner glass surface colder. Increasing the heat transfer with the
warm room air.

3.2.1
Light distribution calculations
First of all, the light distribution of the rooﬂight into a room must be modelled. For this
calculation, it is necessary to choose a standard room to obtain a comparable output among all
variations and similar studies. The room’s façade window and skylight dimensions must be designed
so a speciﬁc amount of daylight is always provided.
Before facing the lightwell modelling in a daylight calculation software, one main difﬁculty
is spotted. The lightwell geometry could be difﬁcult to model because of its double glazing layer.
Daylighting software are mainly designed for one only window as input for the interiors light
distribution. But the case of study requires a study of the light along the lightwell and through
the second glazing, to know exactly how much light is traveling inside the room and how is its
diffusion.
The lightwell geometry will be tried to be modelled in several daylighting software, among the
previously mentioned, in order to obtain a reliable output for the light distribution inside the room.
Ray-tracing tools will be prioritized.
The validated software tool Radiance will be used for the daylight analysis. The software works
together with Autodesk Ecotect 2011, which can be used to model the test room. Radiance acts as
its daylighting engine and does not provide a modelling tool by itself.
Once the geometry of the lightwell is modelled for the light distribution calculations, the values
for the annual distribution for the several possible sky conditions must be obtained. The daylighting
software DAYSIM is a tool able to evaluate the light distribution annually in the same way Radiance
calculates it for a single moment.
3.2.2
Heat transfer calculations
Once the dimensions of the window units are known, the heat transfer characteristics of the
whole lightwell must be analyzed. In this way, a U-value for the complete glazing unit could be
extracted and the overheating simulations could be performed. It is also relevant to check the model
for any possible thermal bridges or unexpected behaviours.
The lightwell behaviour and performance will be characterized by the heat balance inside the
lightwell of all the heat ﬂows crossing it. The thermal transmittance of the glazing units is known
from the manufacturers, but the heat transfer through the lightwell walls for the designed system
must be studied.
To carry out this heat transfer analysis, HEAT2 is the chosen tool. A 2D model can be used in
calculating the heat ﬂows in the two main lightwell sections, the vertical and the horizontal. The
vertical can measure the heat loss through conduction in the insulation and up and down the corners,
where the assemblies and frames are located. But the horizontal one can measure the geometry
effect of heat transfer on the corners of the lightwell.

The glazing in the room was dimensioned following the methodology of G. Skarning et al. in
[2]. Basically, the dimensioning is based on the daylight distribution results for the chosen test

room geometry.
For a ﬁrst approximation, the windows were dimensioned assuming that sufﬁcient daylight can
be obtained by combinations of façade windows with 20% glazing to ﬂoor area or by roof windows
with 10% horizontal projected glazing area to ﬂoor area. Basing the calculations on glazing with
75% light transmittance. The daylight supply in the test room is wanted to be split in half for the
rooﬂight and the façade window, so the initial sizes of the glazing units are half of the previously
mentioned glazing to ﬂoor area ratios.
When it comes to standardization of daylight in buildings, there are two main parameters in use:
daylight factor and daylight autonomy. Daylight autonomy provides the annual illuminance for
all the climate conditions over a year and daylight factor calculates the percentage of illuminance
inside a room for overcast conditions.
Danish standards very vaguely deﬁne the daylight requirements, but in the draft of the future
Daylight of buildings from Dansk Standard prEN 17037 [30] some recommendations for daylight
are offered. In this standard, the daylight recommended to be met in the building is also divided
in the two parameters, daylight factor, and annual illuminance. But there is a distinction whether
the daylight comes from a façade window or from a rooﬂight, being more amount of daylight
demanded when supplying the daylight from a rooﬂight (50% of the ﬂoor area is recommended to
be provided with 300 lux during 50% of the hours, for a room covered with façade windows while
100% of the ﬂoor area is recommended to be covered when providing the light with a rooﬂight). As
in the case of study there exists a mix of daylight provided from both façade window and rooﬂight,
the criterion in use will be the one from G. Skarning et al. [2]. This criterion was 75% of the ﬂoor
area covered by 300 lux during at least 50% of the hours. It seems fairer for the test room and it
is a middle point between both initial criterions from the standard. It is important to remark that
the criterion from the standard deﬁnes the recommendation for the minimum daylight, and higher
daylight recommendations are also deﬁned in the standard for applications in which the user could
beneﬁt from it.
The glazing dimensioning is ﬁnally arranged after the daylight calculations from the software
DAYSIM. The criterions to meet are both based on daylight autonomy and daylight factor. Both
are complementary and necessary to assure a good daylight quality inside the room. The goal level
set in this project is:
• An annual illuminance level of 300 lux for 50% of the daylight hours in 75% of the space
(Spatial coverage of DA 50% ≥75%) [2]. 60% of the daylight hours is preferred if possible.
• A daylight factor of 2.1% in 50% of the space (in overcast conditions).
The ﬁrst criterion is based on the recommendation for Spatial Daylight Autonomies established
by IES. This daylight autonomy is measured for the ofﬁce hours (8:00 to 17:00) of an ofﬁce located
in Copenhagen.
Usually, daylight factor index is more demanding than daylight autonomy. The useful daylight
index between 100 and 2000 lux is also taken into account in order to choose between alternatives
with tight results.

4.3
Calculation of window thermal transmittance
After the light distribution of the room is modelled and calculated, so the glazing units have
their dimensions assigned, the thermal transmittance of the whole roof window system must be
obtained. Knowing the thermal transmittance (U-value) of the whole roof window as a unit,
will allow making the necessary calculations for the room energy performance, like the energy
consumption and the number of overheating hours.
The U-value of the window is calculated equalizing the heat transferred through the total
window system to the heat transferred through the inner glazing unit, as equation 4.4 shows.
Awindow system · Uwindow system · (Tin −Tout) = Abottom window · Ubottom window · (Tin −TLW )
(4.4)
From equation 4.4, the only two unknowns are the total U-value of the window system,
Uwindow system and the temperature inside the lightwell, TLW . Awindow system is the area of
the window system roof opening, which is equal to Abottom window. Tin and Tout refer to the
temperatures inside and outside the room. Finally, Ubottom window refers to the U-value of the inner
glazing unit, in this case, the aerogel one.
Therefore, to obtain the U-value of the total window system, Uwindow system, the temperature
inside the lightwell, TLW , needs to be calculated.
The temperature inside the lightwell can be obtained with a heat balance through the lightwell
window units and the lightwell walls around it.
To introduce this heat balance, ﬁrstly, due to the difﬁculty of modelling the top and bottom roof
window units in three dimensions for the heat transfer study, several assumptions and simpliﬁcations
have been taken:
• The upper Velux window is included in the heat balance as a total thermal transmittance
value for the opening, combination of the frame and the pane (provided by the manufacturer).
• Second, the lightwell structure is considered to be ideal and only composed of insulation, to
provide a linear temperature proﬁle across it (no structural materials considered).
• Third, the air inside the lightwell is assumed to be fully mixed and its temperature constant
around all the lightwell volume. This temperature is not known from the beginning; therefore,
the simulations will be repeated until the convergence is reached for a given set of inside and
outside temperatures.
The heat balance of the complete window system is explained with the simple equation stating
that the same heat ﬂow that comes inside of the lightwell, leaves it; as it is considered a steady state
calculation. The heat coming into the lightwell through the aerogel unit, the lightwell walls, and the
assembly is equal to the one leaving the lightwell through the top glazing. The heat is considered
to come from inside the room, where the temperature is the highest.

Calculation of window thermal transmittance
Once the dimensions of the glazing units are assigned according to the recommended illu-
minance and daylight factors, the thermal transmittance of the whole roof window system must
be obtained. Knowing the thermal transmittance (U-value) of the whole roof window as a unit,
will allow making the necessary calculations for the room thermal performance, like the energy
consumption and the number of overheating hours.
The U-value of the window is calculated exactly with the same methodology than in the
previous alternative. This is, according to the heat balance inside the lightwell with the room
temperature, the outdoor temperature and the window system between them. The losses across
the insulated lightwell were obtained with the help of the two dimensional heat transfer software
tools Heat2. The losses through the window system, frames and the insulation in the opening are
calculated.
The base simpliﬁcations from chapter 4 have been assumed and an additional one for the glazing
unit : the upper Velux window is included in the heat balance as a total thermal transmittance
value for the opening, combination of the frame and the pane (provided by the manufacturer); the
lightwell structure is considered to be ideal and only composed of insulation; and the air inside the
lightwell is assumed to be fully mixed and its temperature constant around all the lightwell volume.
Additionally, the argon between the double-glazing panes is also considered to be fully mixed for
the two dimensional simulations in which it is included.
The temperature inside the lightwell is again not known from the beginning; a ﬁrst guess for
this temperature is taken and the simulations will be repeated until the convergence is reached

CHAPTER 9
Discussion
The present chapter will analyze the obtained results, their dependence to the methodology and
taken assumptions and the possible variations due to criteria modiﬁcations. Also, their reliability
is discussed. Contrary to typical discussion chapters, some new calculations and results used as
sensitivity analyses to comment the global results are presented here.
9.1
Light distribution discussion
The criteria for the light distribution assessment in the rooms for every alternative directly
inﬂuence the whole performance of the room and the roof window. On the ﬁrst hand, the different
criteria could modify the aperture dimensions for the roof and façade windows, which would cause
different light distribution in the rooms. On the other hand, the different aperture dimensions would
inﬂuence the thermal performance of the room, increasing the window area, with generally a lower
heat transmittance value than the walls, at the same time that the heat gains from the sun are also
increased.
From the draft of the future Danish Standard prEN 12037 for Daylight for buildings, the
minimum daylight recommendation was obtained for the two different kinds of windows: roof
windows and façade windows. The level of recommended daylight in the room in the Standard draft
varied depending on the kind of windows illuminating the room. For rooms illuminated with façade
windows, the requirement was: a daylight factor of at least 2.1% covering 50% of the ﬂoor area and
a daylight autonomy of 300 lux over 50% of space during 50% of the daylight hours. For rooms
illuminated with roof windows, the requirement was: a daylight factor of at least 2.1% covering
100% of the ﬂoor area and a daylight autonomy of 300 lux over 100% of space during 50% of the
daylight hours. This distinction of criteria for different kind of windows was considered unfair for
a research that aims at a reduction of roof window heat losses close to the best performance façade
windows.
Due to the indeﬁnition on spaces illuminated with both façade and roof windows, the criteria
followed was the one from G. Skarning in [25]. This paper has been taken as a reference in many
aspects, as this thesis continues the economic research on improved roof windows with a technical

9.2
Thermal calculations discussion
From the 2D method used to calculate the heat ﬂow through the walls of the lightwell, con-
sidered as the most consistent way to evaluate the rooﬂight with the tools, time and resources in
availability at the time of the thesis realization, the dependencies and uncertainties from the taken
assumptions are examined.
At the end of the thesis development, it was discovered that there exists a standardized method
to calculate the heat transfer between several environments using thermal coupling coefﬁcients. The
thermal coupling coefﬁcient method could not be applied to the ﬁnal results, but it was investigated
to obtain the thermal performance of the roof slab for some of the alternatives. This way a sensitivity
analysis could be carried out over the developed method carried based on a standardized calculation.
9.2.1
Thermal coupling coefﬁcients method for the heat ﬂow across the lightwell
walls
The used 2D method could be more intuitive than the standardized thermal coupling coefﬁcients
method, but it turned out to be the same simple to implement. The standard deﬁning the thermal
coupling coefﬁcients method is DN/EN ISO 10211.

The standard calculation is used to obtain the heat ﬂow through the walls of the lightwell to be
part of the total heat balance from equation 4.8. Once this heat ﬂow through the lightwell walls is
known, the heat balance leads to the equation for the temperature of the lightwell, necessary to
calculate the U-value of the window system.
TLW = Ubottom window · Abottom window · Tin + QLW walls
(Ubottom window + Utop window) · Atop window
(9.1)
From the Standard, considering the roof slab from section 4.3.1, the expression to calculate the
heat ﬂow through the lightwell walls based on the thermal coupling coefﬁcients is extracted,
QLW walls = Llw−in · (TLW −Tin) + Llw−out · (TLW −Tout)
(9.2)
Where, Llw−in and Llw−out are the thermal coupling coefﬁcients between the lightwell and the
interior of the room; and T refers to the different temperatures (inside the lightwell ’LW’, inside
’in’ or outside ’out’).
The thermal coupling coefﬁcients are calculated for the set of a number temperatures depending
on the many environments in contact. In the case of study, three different temperatures in the
real case, require three different calculations. The thermal coupling coefﬁcients between each
environment are always calculated with a difference of temperature of 1 K. The temperature matrix
for the coupling coefﬁcients calculation is presented on Table 9.2.

9.2.2
General comments about the method
One of the main assumptions regarding the calculation of the heat transfer through the lightwell
walls was that the frames and assemblies effect on the three dimensional model was negligible
compared to their 2D effect. Different than other assumptions of the thesis, this could not be
checked due to the difﬁculty of modelling the frames in 3D.
Another result to comment is the linear loss coefﬁcients, Ψassembly for every alternative.
The values of the assembly losses are unrealistically low. The main hypothesis is that they are
under-dimensioned because of the ideal insulation roof slab considered for the simulations.
In every Heat2 model, there could be small pixel deviations between the initially designed
dimensions of the model and the Heat2 model. But these should not modify the ﬁnal results.
Thinner structures as frames and panes were carefully dimensioned pixel by pixel.
Regarding the previous light distribution discussion, in which the strictness of the criteria was
discussed in cases where the daylight coefﬁcient results are close but under the minimum, the
results for the total heat transfer of the ﬁrst alternative are presented for a smaller aperture size to
give an idea of how much the variation in aperture size can inﬂuence the losses.
The different sizes of the aperture modify the heat ﬂow through the lightwell walls and therefore,
the temperature inside the lightwell. This directly impacts the U-value of the total window system
and therefore, the total losses.

Energy performance discussion
A sensitivity analysis for the designed room with the façade window facing all different
orientations was carried out. This comparison helps to obtain a global picture of the problem. The
results for all rooms and all orientations are presented on Table 9.6.
The total results of all alternatives for all orientations are explained and compared. Results of
the room are evaluated respect to the limit of 100 hours of overheating and the limit of 12kWh/m2
for annual consumption on space heating on a created room. The test room properties have been
modelled according to some guidelines stated in chapter 4, but the focus is not so much on the total
results of the overheating hours and space heating consumption of the rooms with the roof units.
The focus was put on the comparison between the different roof window alternatives and the façade
window alternatives and the comparison of their contribution to meet the performance goals. This
is because all the rooms can be optimized, and a better performance for every alternative could be
obtained to meet the speciﬁc goals.
Regarding the results for the overheating hours, the best results are obtained for the room
’equivalent sf’, which is normal because this room is the one not fulﬁlling the minimum recommen-
dation for daylight in the room and the glazed area is the smallest. The purpose of this room was
having a comparison point for the roof window models with a room in which the only change is the
addition of the roof window and the façade window is kept at the same dimensions. For this model,
the overheating hours were below 100 for all the façade window orientations.
On the other hand, the room having the worst overheating problems was the room covering all
the daylight recommendations with an increment of the façade window size (model ’equivalent bf’).
In this model the overheating went ﬁve times over the limit for the south orientation of the room.
The improved roof window models had an increment of the overheating hours for all orientations
compared to the ’equivalent sf’ model, but they kept below a reasonable number of hours. For the
designed orientation, i.e. the North orientation, all the rooms with improved roof windows fulﬁlled
the 100 hours limit. For the rest of orientation, the overheating hours over passed the limit in all
these models but kept below reasonable values of less than 50% more. For all these rooms the
overheating hours do not suppose a problem because they could be mitigated easily with a better
optimization of the venting, with shading or with a cooling system. The overheating hours result
was especially low for the room with the clear double glazed window on a reﬂective lightwell,
from the last alternative. In this room, the smaller roof glazing area beneﬁted the performance
and got almost acceptable results with no need for optimization. Between the other roof window
types, the one with the highest overheating hours is the model with the aerogel window due to the
higher g-value of this pane with increases the heat gains. This higher g-value does not only have a
negative impact because it also improves the energy performance of the room during the heating
season for a low U-value.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusions
The conclusions extracted from the accomplished work are:
• Reﬂective lightwell walls increase the performance of all kinds of roof windows increasing
the total light transmissivity and reducing the required window opening size for a better
energy performance.
With the application of the reﬂective lightwell, the ﬁfth clear roof window alternative reduced
its aperture size and energy loss from 1.159 × 1.159 m2 and 19.71 W, to 0.959 × 0.959 m2
and 13.83 W, respect to the second alternative (with the same inputs except the reﬂective
lightwell). It achieved reductions of 31% and 30%, respectively.
• Diffusive properties in roof windows help to spread the light in a more even way than clear
glasses but they reduce light transmission. Their combination with reﬂective lightwells helps
to increase their light transmission to levels comparable to the clear glass while getting a
slightly improved daylight factor due to the better distribution of the light.
• The addition of an aerogel window to seal the lightwell opening is found to be the best energy
performance solution to provide the necessary daylight to a deep room with no energy cost
compared to the solution with no roof window on it.
The space heating consumption of the room with the aerogel alternative was 10.24 kWh/m2
and for the solution without roof window, 10.69 kWh/m2.
• The addition of clear double glazed windows to seal the bottom of the lightwell provides
a high light transmissivity value and they are a cheaper solution than aerogel windows. It
offers a good energy performance improvement compared to the existing technology.
The space heating consumption of the room with the clear double glazed window from the
second alternative was 11.28 kWh/m2.
• If the combination of clear double glazed windows and reﬂective lightwells is proven not
to cause glare problems in a real model, an excellent energy savings performance and cost
efﬁciency could be obtained from this solution.

CHAPTER 10. CONCLUSIONS
The space heating consumption of the room with the clear double glazed window and the
reﬂective lightwell from the ﬁfth alternative was 11.05 kWh/m2.
• Third and second alternatives, both using clear double glazed roof windows, are compared.
The roof window alternative with the sealing unit placed on the top of the lightwell walls
(under the existing Velux window), and the regular alternative with the sealing unit located
at the bottom part of the lightwell, respectively. From their difference it can be concluded
that: the position of the window unit at the bottom of the lightwell actually increases the
energy savings potential of the roof window because it reduces the conductivity from the
lightwell to the exterior; in spite of this, the big energy savings potential of these improved
windows comes from the addition of a second bottom window unit more than its location on
the bottom of the lightwell1.
• The developed class of improved roof windows increase the energy savings potential of
existing roof windows and help them to comply with the energy consumption for space
heating and overheating hours limits.
The existing roof window model performs with an annual space heating consumption of
13.27 kWh/m2 and 153 overheating hours. These values are over the limits in the standards
and they are improved by any of the developed improved roof window models.
• The use of the developed class of improved roof windows offer a better energy performance
and less overheating hours while complying with the recommendations for minimum daylight
in deep rooms compared to the solution in which only a big façade window covers the
minimum daylight recommendation.
For the North orientation, the room with a big façade window has an annual space heating
consumption of 15.54 kWh/m2 and 93 overheating hours. This is improved by any of the
developed improved roof window models.

CHAPTER 11
Future work
The present chapter enumerates the work lines to continue with the development of this project
in the future:
• The temperature inside the lightwell must be calculated for summer conditions in order to
discard any overheating and high pressure problems, and if existing, evaluating proposals to
mitigate them.
• The total cost of the improve roof windows production and installation must be estimated
to evaluate the attractiveness of the proposals. Knowing the total cost and the energy
performance of these roof window solutions, the cost of its application to reduce the space
heating consumption of buildings compared to the cost of an insulation increment on the
building envelope can be studied.
• Improved roof windows with an aerogel unit on the bottom seem to be the best energy
performing solution from the ones compared in the thesis. The economic viability of this
product must be investigated and an accurate pricing for the material must be obtained.
• The study of the behaviour of a more realistic roof slab including structural elements in it
which could modify the thermal conductivity.
• Improving the bottom window frame model with a more detailed construction, including
insulation inside or reducing its volume to reduce its thermal conductivity.
• The development from the building industry of a standardized method to characterize the
translucent materials and their behaviour.
• The development of a full 3D model of the roof windows for the thermal calculations
including every component and assembly in order to fully characterize the 3D effect through
it.
• The creation of real models to measure the real performance of the improved roof windows
and evaluate their light distribution.
