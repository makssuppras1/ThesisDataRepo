Abstract
This report is part of a wider project regarding optimization performance of residential blocks in Sisimiut
where the building envelope and ventilation systems are studied. In this project a new block with improve-
ments regarding energy savings was developed. This part is developed by Eleni Pavlidi (s14214) and Juan
Sardar Mohamad Abdulla (s142956).
Renewable energies are growing and developing worldwide. The increase of their efﬁciency and their
cost-competitive prices make them the ﬁrst solution to replace the fossil fuels for a cleaner way of energy
supply. This shift is even more important if fragile environments are involved, as in the case of Greenland.
Greenland is a country in a northern latitude where the climate conditions are rather extreme. This project
studies the use of sustainable resources for providing energy to a residential area in Sisimiut, Greenland.
For this purpose, different simulations software were used. Some of the required data for the analysis were
acquired during two weeks of ﬁeldwork in Sisimiut. In this period, interviews to the households of the blocks
in the residential area and visual inspection were made. The ﬁeldwork include as well meetings with energy
and housing companies and data acquisition. With all of the information, energy systems based on solar
power were design. Firstly, a PV grid-connected system placed in the roof and the facade of the refurbished
block was evaluated as a partial power contributor. Secondly, a thermal collector system was designed in
order to supply heat and hot water to the building, together with the district heating system already operating
. Both systems were simulated in order to observe if their performance were good enough for being used as
an energy suppliers. It was found that the use of simulations programs for this kind of latitudes can give
inaccurate results. Finally, a discussion of geothermal heat energy is made at the end with the conclusion of
being a kind of energy that can be developed in Greenland in the future but not in the present.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
It is understood by sustainable or renewable energies those kind of energies that are able to provide and
satisfy the power needs resulting in minimal environmental impact. More and more countries are trying to
supply its industry and citizens with energy sources that are efﬁcient and reliable but at the same time clean
and respectful with their environment. Around 60 per cent of the greenhouse gases emissions are caused by
energy systems [7]. Therefore, one of the targets of the shift to sustainable energies from fossil fuels is to
reduce these emissions [8].
The rise and expansion of new renewable energy manufacturers in the last decade is due to the declining
costs in this energy market. A lot of different sustainable technologies are able to be cost-competitive with
other kind of traditional technologies[9].
Nowadays, renewable energies are not exclusively used to reduce climate change effects, but to decrease
dependence on fossil fuels that can give advantages as having more control on a country economy. This
means, creating new jobs or having access and more control on their own energy[9].
Looking towards the future, the AGECC (Advisory Group on Energy and Climate Change) has two
main goals. The ﬁrst one is to give universal access to new energy services by 2030. This services must
be efﬁcient and sustainable, giving a boost of economy to the resources-poor countries. Thanks to this, the
energy costs to the users will be according to their income levels and necessity and independent from the
traditional energy sources prices [7].
The second one is to reduce global energy intensity by 40 per cent by 2030. New effective policies, business
models and regulations regarding energy, need to be set in developing countries. The reach of this goal will
include the cooperation within several countries to achieve the standardization of energy-consuming devices
and to share knowledge regarding sustainable and efﬁcient energy [7].
1.1
Greenland and renewable energy
Greenland is the largest island of the world and the country with the least population density. Its
population is around 56.000, most of them living in small settlements spread across the west coast. During
the last decades, Greenland has been producing energy through diesel-driven power plants distributing it
via micro-power grid and district heating networks, being these ones the main source of greenhouse gas
emission in the island [10].

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
In past years, Greenland has been starting to replace the diesel energy generators for hydropower plants
supplied by glacial meltwater. This way the country will be less dependent on fuel imports and the level of
greenhouse gas emissions will be decreased. Nukissiorﬁit, the energy provider owned by the government, is
the proprietor of the hydropower plants [11]. However, only large towns have access to they energy from
the hydropower plant. The rest of the population live in isolated areas where the energy can be provided
exclusively by their own generators and this can be expensive [12].
Due to the ﬂuctuating prices of fuel, the number of people requiring more renewable energy in order to be
independent on fuel imports is increasing[12].
The place studied is a residential area in Sisimiut, Greenland, a town located in an Arctic environment
and it can be observed in ﬁgure 1.1. The climate in the Arctic is considered Polar and come deﬁned by cold
temperatures, long winters, and short summers. In Sisimiut in December, the shortest day of the year has a
length of 1h 37min in Sisimiut. On the contrary, between the 3rd of June and 10 the of July, the sun is up all
day. This event is called "midnight sun". The hours of darkness and the hours of sunlight are very important
factors to take into consideration while designing a system that involves solar modules [13].
Figure 1.1: Greenland with Sisimiut pointed in red [1]
Regarding Sisimiut’s energy supply, the main power supplier is a hydropower plant located 27 km North
of Sisimiut. This plant was open in 2009 to supply electricity and heat in a more sustainable way than before
when diesel generators where used for this matter.

1.2. LIMITATIONS
3
Although a hydropower plant is already a renewable source of energy, the systems that will be proposed
have less impact and will be less harmful for the environment than the plant. Not to mention the difference
between solar power and oil. Apart from the prices, the CO2 emissions are much lower using solar than oil.
There are of course some CO2 emissions when constructing for instance solar collectors or when shipping
and installing them, buy they can be offset withing few years. Regarding the costs, the maintenance and the
annual servicing can be decreased up to 1.5% of the investments costs. Furthermore, the operating costs are
low. Only 1kWh of electricity it is needed to provide between 40 and 50 kWh of heat[14].
The purpose of this project is to study how different types of renewable energy systems can cover a part
of the consumption of a refurbished block in a residential area in Sisimiut.
While renewable off-grid system are starting developing in small settlements, grid-connected systems
such as PV systems connected to the grid have not been really considered. The hydropower plant is already
a renewable energy source that can provide clean energy to the town but it has still a huge impact on the
surrounded environment. Furthermore, some discussions regarding the enlargement of it had been started.
The need of energy is increasing as more people are moving from small settlements to towns.
Therefore, in this report the possibility of using a PV system connected to the grid to provide power to a
refurbished block in a residential area will be simulated and analyzed in the fourth chapter. On the other
hand, there are solar collector systems already working in Sisimiut. Installing this kind of system to provide
heat and hot water to the refurbished block will be study and simulated as well in the ﬁfth chapter. Finally,
a discussion about geothermal heat pumps and how they can ﬁt in a residential area of Sisimiut will be
described in the sixth chapter.
A description of the case of study, including the development of the ﬁeldwork in Sisimiut and an analysis of
the data collected was made in the second chapter. Some theory was presented in the third chapter as it is
common background for the energy systems based on solar power.
1.2
Limitations

the proposed ideas might be hard to introduce in the Sisimiut and Greenlandic energy plans. Besides, the
house company that owns the apartments in the residential area has to agree with the new energy supplied
proposals.

CHAPTER 3
Theory
In this chapter some theory that can be applied to all the proposed energy supply systems will be
presented. When designing a system that involves solar energy it is essential to understand the patterns and
the behavior of the sun. This way the maximum potential of it will be utilized. There are elements that can
highly inﬂuence the output of a solar energy system. These elements have to be analyzed in detailed as they
highly inﬂuence the performance of the system.
3.1
Solar irradiance
Solar irradiance is the volume of solar energy that reaches a point on Earth at one speciﬁc moment.
It arrives to Earth as an electromagnetic radiation that has its wavelength and it can be measured with a
pyranometer.
The total (or global) solar radiation, IG, is the result of the addition of the beam (Ib), the diffuse Id and
the reﬂected radiation Ir [22].
IG = Ib + Id + Ir
(3.1)
The beam radiation is the solar radiation that comes to Earth directly from the sun. The diffuse radiation
is the sunlight after being scattered by the molecules in the atmosphere. The reﬂected radiation is the one
reﬂected back to the atmosphere when it reaches the ground [22].
3.2
Solar azimuth, solar altitude and apparent solar time
There are two main factors that can describe the position of the sun: the azimuth and the altitude. These
two combined are very useful when studying irradiance and where the sun is positioned in every moment.
Both of them are measured in degrees.
The azimuth is an angle between an element in the sky (the sun in our case) and the south vector. In
another words, is the direction in which the sun travels during the day.
The solar altitude is related to the angle between the sun and the latitudinal position of an object on Earth.
When the sun is right above the equator the altitude will be 90º. It increases at the sunrise and it decreases
when the day approaches to the sunset [23].

3.3
Albedo
The albedo is related with the reﬂected radiation. It measures the reﬂectivity of a surface’s object on the
Earth. The range that is usually applied goes from 0 to 1. The albedo average on Earth is 0.39. For instance,
a forest area will have an albedo value of 0.17 and sea water has an albedo of around 0.6. On the other hand,
snow and ice are some of the surfaces with higher albedo effect [24].
This effect is one of the reasons why Greenland is a crucial place to regulate the global climate together
with Antarctica. The ice cap inside the island reﬂects most of the light coming from the sun back to the
space. This way there is a cooling effect in the planet prevents partly the global warming [25].
During the months of April and May in Sisimiut there is still snow in the ground and the hours of sun are
increasing. Very little light is absorbed so almost most of it will be reﬂected. Sun and snow or ice combined
together could be good to increase the production in the panels. In order to measure albedo some factors that
have to be taken into consideration are explained in the next section.

4.1
Brief description of a grid-connected PV system
A grid-connected PV system is a set of solar modules connected so they can produce energy from the
sunlight.
The output from the PV is DC power that has to be converted to AC at a speciﬁc frequency, as is the one

4.3
Consumption vs Production
The behavior pattern study of the households is important in a way that the power production matches its
highest peaks with the moments of highest consumption. The moments of higher consumption are in general
during the mornings and during the evenings. In the middle hours the needed of energy is lower but on the
other hand, production can be higher. The sun during midday reaches its most powerful point.
If a solar panel is facing south during the midday on a sunny day, the production will be at its maximum.
There are other ways to achieve good production too. If a panel is facing East during the morning, the
production will be high as well. The same happens if the panel faces the West during the evening hours. It is
important to noticed that this scenario is during summer when the daylight hours are higher.
The ideal would be to have the possibility of having a system that tracks the sun, so the panels are able
to move and follow the sun with the proper angle. As these systems are rather expensive and complex one
proposed solution is given below.
The two sides of the roof are facing east and west, so the PV can be placed on both sides. This way,
during the ﬁrst peak of consumption of the day, the panels facing the east side will have their optimum
production and during the evening, the modules facing west will be the ones providing the higher amount of
power. During midday when the demand is lower, the panels mounted in the facade facing south will be
working at their maximum performance . The modules in the roof will have 45º of tilt but for the tilt of the
modules placed in the facade there are two options that will be discussed:
1. The PVs are mounted in the facade in vertical position: the production in the summer will not be
the optimum but there will be some production in winter. If the panels are in vertical position, the
snow will not remain on the top of them. Another interesting thing to have in mind is the reﬂection
of the snow. If the ground is covered by snow, the reﬂection on the modules will have to be taken
into consideration. For instance, in spring there is still snow and thus, there could be an increased
in the production. This condition adding to the cold temperatures makes it the perfect place for PV
modules. Furthermore, a low tilt in winter is a good choice as the sun is in a low position. To sum up,
the advantages of the vertical installation are:
• More production in the winter period if they are in a vertical position than if they are tilted.
• No shading.
• Due to reﬂection of the sun in the snow, the production in Spring can be good enough. There is
sun and the temperatures are still cold.
• There will no be snow covered the panels during winter.
• Easier installation.
The disadvantages would be:
• Less production in summer as the tilt angle is very low.
2. The PVs are mounted in the facade with a determined tilt: This will be achieved by mounting some
holders in the facade. The degrees of the tilt will be the one that gives the best performance from the
solar panels. This way in summer the production will be the maximum that the panels can provide in
accordance with the weather conditions. In the case of winter the panels in the roof will have snow,
==PAGE:42==
26
CHAPTER 4. GRID-CONNECTED PV SYSTEM
but the ones in the facade as there is anything below like a roof to hold the snow, perhaps it will go
away. In this case, the number of panels will be less due to shading. The design has to be made being
aware of this, placing the panels with a determined distance between them. This distance depends on
the panels tilt. The advantages are presented below:
• More production in summer.
• There will few or no snow covered the panels during winter.
The disadvantages would be:
• Fewer panels due to shading.
• The possibility of having snow during winter.
• More complex installation.
• More expensive due to holders.
The vertical panels would be a good choice for the south facade. It is true than in summer the PV
production would be less but during midday is when less production is needed. Besides, more panels
can be installed and the money of the PV holders is saved.

Different inverters were attempted to ﬁt with the different arrays design for the vertical and the roof

northern locations (snow, the altitude of the sun etc...)[32]. There is not a ﬁxed number for our location so a
value used in northern latitudes was selected.

The losses based on The European Commission Joint Research Centre (JRC) guidelines proposed in 1993

YR represents the reference yield, which is the result of dividing the irradiated energy (kWh/m2) by
irradiance (1kW/m2) under STC conditions for one year. In other words, it is the-the performance that the
system would have if the solar modules convert the irradiance that is reached by them in useful energy
depending on their estimated capacity. Then, the capture losses (Lc) will be subtracted to YR. These
losses represent thermal losses causes by cell temperatures higher than 25◦and also not depending on the
temperature as wiring losses, pollution, snow accumulation etc... Earray ( called YA too) is the array yield
or the effective energy at the output of the array (DC-). The next losses are the system losses LS that are
produced due to the inverter conversion (DC-AC). Finally, the ﬁnal yield YF is obtained which is the useful
output energy that can be used. A lossless system would have YR=YF [34].
To quantify the effect of the system losses the Performance Ratio is used (PR). This is useful to compare
systems regardless of the solar resource or the system capacity. It is described as the ﬁnal yield YF (AC) and
the reference yield YR that could be achieved. It is normally given in percentage. The higher the PR is the
better is the performance of the system.

4.7
Discussion of the results
4.7.1
Vertical modules
It was expected a smaller speciﬁc production as the average achieved by the PV systems already installed
in Sisimiut is less than the simulated one. The speciﬁc production in our simulation was 1651 kWh/kWp/year
which is a higher value.
Snow and ice can reduce the power generation substantially. However, the simulation software does not
take into account the snow in the meteo data. The effect of snow on the performance of a PV system is very
difﬁcult to anticipate so ﬁnally they were not included in the losses. This could be one of the reasons why the
simulated production is higher than the actual power production from PV systems in Sisimiut. Nevertheless,
the snow problem in the vertical panels is expected to be reduced.
The generated power reduction due to snow and ice could be one of the explanations, but such a difference
between the speciﬁc production in the simulation and in reality should have another reason. The aforemen-
tioned problem with the radiation data could have something to do with it. As in some months the radiation
had to be reduced due to the clearness index factor and ﬁnally the highest available value was taken, the
software might understand than for such months the radiation going through the atmosphere was the highest
possible. This could have an increasing power effect in the output of the PV system.
Regarding the graph of the speciﬁc production, it is observed the highest production during spring and
autumn months. During those time periods, the hours of sunlight are getting higher and the albedo effect
takes part in the ﬁnal result. Due to the vertical tilt, the solar modules can take advantage during these
months of the sunlight reﬂected in the snow. However, a real approach of how the albedo affects the power
production could not be done due to the uncertainty of the global radiation values previously stated.
During the months of summer, even if the radiation is higher, a vertical tilt is not the appropriate one for this
season. The altitude of the sun is higher and thus, the sun rays will arrive with a low incidence angle.
The performance ratio expresses how efﬁcient is the PV system during a speciﬁc period of time. In
general, the highest performance ratio is given during summer months due to the higher radiation. In the case
of the vertical system the PR is quite homogeneous during the whole year being November the most efﬁcient
month. This could be due to the combination of some factors as the low temperature or the convenient angle
of incidence of sunlight on the panels.
4.7.2
Roof modules
The speciﬁc production of the modules placed in the roof is closer to the one expected in Sisimiut. But
as in the case of the vertical modules, the snow is not taken into account and the problem with the clearness
index is repeated. Therefore, the real power production should be again, smaller.
The normalized production graph has the expected shape: higher production during summer and lower
or none during winter. The collection losses are high during summer as the orientation of the modules is not
the optimum one for this season. The modules with 45º with 45º and -45º of azimuth would have a better
performance.
It is noticeable the higher PR during January. Almost an 18% of the energy collected by the array is
transformed into energy. This is not realistic as the modules will be covered by snow and as mentioned

4.7.3
Overall system
Having solar modules installed facing different orientation can give maximum peaks of energy production
at different times of the day. This can be very useful for instance, in summer when there is sunlight 24
h. It could be useful to analyze the possibility of sharing the electricity with other blocks when there is

modules have to be mounted in a way that this air ﬂow is created and strong enough to get rid of the snow [36].

5.1
Parts of a solar thermal system
5.1.1
Solar collectors
The main types of solar collectors for residential uses are:
• Flat plate collectors: They consist in an insulated box covered by plastic or glass on top and an absorbed
plate underneath attached to a copper pipe where the liquid that transfers the heat is circulating. They
have a good performance in warm places but it is reduced in cold places.
• Evacuated tube collectors: They consist of one or two array of glass tubes containing an absorber
plated welded to a heat pipe. The tubes are surrounded by vacuum that avoids really well the heat loss.
The heat produced is transferred to a ﬂuid in a heat exchanger. Sometimes if the collector has only
one array of tubes it is covered with a sheet as the ﬂat plate collectors.
For our system ﬂat plate collectors were chosen as snow can go down the collector easily due to the
smoother surface. In the evacuated tube collectors the snow defrost slower as it can strain between the tubes
and remains longer [38]. In the high school of Sisimiut they are combining ﬂat plate solar collectors and
evacuated tube collectors in order to obtain solar hot water and space heating. The collectors are placed in
vertically on the top of the roof as it can be seen in picture 5.1. There were some problems some years ago
due to the lack of use during summer.
5.1.2
Storage tank
The function of these tanks is to stored the hot water produced by the solar collectors during an speciﬁc
time. A good tank must has an sufﬁcient volume according to the need of the users, high heat capacity and
a fast demand response. Normally the have a cylindrical shape to help the thermal stratiﬁcation. This is a
natural process based on the density of the water due to the temperature. The hot water is lighter than the
cold water so it will go to the top while the cold water will remain in the bottom.
The charging of the tank can be done by a heat exchanger in two ways: building up the thermal
stratiﬁcation with a three way valve or an inlet stratiﬁer. A second way to do is with an internal coil heat
exchanger. The discharging of the heat from the tank is done by using an external plate heat exchanger or by
a tank-in-tank solution. The most commonly used is the discharge by external plate heat exchangers [14].
5.1.3
Heat exchanger
There are different ways of how a heat exchanger transfer heat but the one that was of interest for this
project was liquid to liquid. The heated ﬂuid by the sun in the collectors will be transmitted trough the
heat exchanger to another liquid that in this case will be the water in the tank. The most relevant factors
for a heat exchanger are the material conductivity, the surface area and its conﬁguration. The material
conductivity is referred to the thermal conductivity which is essential for a good performance of the system.
The surface should be careful designed since a too small exchanger could lead to a poor performance an
and big exchanger one could have bigger losses. Regarding the conﬁguration, there are three types of heat
exchanger design: plates, tubing or tube in shell. The ﬁrst one was the one used for the analysis of the
system. They can be placed inside or outside the tank[39] .

Unlike PV grid-connected systems, solar collectors to provide energy for Hot Domestic Water and Space
Heating are already used in Sisimiut. Their efﬁciency and reliability is currently being tested in two main
buildings, the high school and one of the students dorm.
It would be expected a better performance of the system if this one was facing both east and west. The
reason why only one orientation was chosen is the less complexity of the system. One thermal system like
this, facing both east and west, would need a independent pump for each side collector array. Furthermore,
the pumps would not work at the same time. The ﬁrst pump would work in the morning, both pumps would
work in the afternoon and the second pump would work in the evening.
From the simulation and results it was observed that meeting the heating needs of the apartments area
required more energy than the one required for the DHW. The days that the heating is needed is almost
all year long but until the summer months it is hard to be covered. A solar collector system with the only
purpose of being used for DHW in a building would required less energy and thus the system would be less
complex.
Furthermore, the ration between Qsol and Esol is very low. This could be due to the orientation of the
panels as it is not the optimum one and to the large number of collectors in the roof. When an array is formed
with a high amount of solar collectors it tends to have higher losses.
Solar collectors efﬁciency decreased when the solar collector ﬂuid has a higher temperature, creating more
heating losses.
As the technical room in the refurbished building is on the west side, the system will installed facing
west. This way the heat will have to travel less distance and some piping losses will be avoided. In addition,
the system will need some maintenance for instance in the collector loop. The collector ﬂuid should be
replaced for time to time (around 3 years) so the antifreeze property is not lost.

CHAPTER 6
Geothermal heat pump
In this chapter a brief introduction to geothermal heat pumps (GHSP), the future of them in Greenland
and more speciﬁcally, in residential areas of Sisimiut will be discussed.
6.1
What is a geothermal heat pump?
The main parts of a geothermal heat pump are the ground heat exchanger, the heat pump unit, a compres-
sor and the system where the heat is delivered that can be an a air ductwork or a storage tank in the case of
the proposed building. The heat exchanger is placed in the ground at a certain level below the ground. It
consists of a set of pipes ﬁlled with an antifreeze ﬂuid that could be a mixture of water and Glycol. This
ﬂuid goes through the pipes taking in the heat from the ground[46].
Figure 6.1: View of a vertical geothermal heat pump[4]
Regarding the way of which the pipes in the ground are installed, there are two kind of geothermal
pumps: vertical and horizontal. The horizontal ones is placed in the ﬁrst layers of the ground. On the other
hand, the vertical ones require the drilling of a deep hole in the ground for its installation.
57
==PAGE:74==
58
CHAPTER 6. GEOTHERMAL HEAT PUMP
A GHSP works in the following way: the heat pump will send the ﬂuid coming from the ground to a
compressor that will extract the heat, transforming this one into gas. Later, in a condenser, the ﬂuid will
release heat by becoming liquid again. After this, the ﬂuid will go through an expansion valve. Then, the
ﬂuid will go back to the borehole where will collect the heat again repeating the same process[46]. It is
important to have in mind that these systems need power supply for working.
It depends on the kind of installation, but the money invested could return in form of energy savings
between 5 and 10 years. Besides, these systems does not require a big maintenance and the lifetime is
estimated in 25 years for the inside components and more than 50 years for the ground loop[4].
6.2
GHSP in cold climates.
There are some background of successful GHSP in places with cold climates. Actually the forth leading
country worldwide using geothermal heat pumps is Iceland. Being a volcanic area is the perfect place for
installing this kind of heating systems. Over 90% of all houses in his country are heated by geothermal en-
ergy [5]. Although Greenland is not a volcanic zone, it has in common with Iceland that they are both islands.
Figure 6.2: Geothermal power station in Nesjavellir, Iceland.[5]
Another region with similar conditions to Greenland is Alaska. There are already companies settle there
working exclusively on the installation of heat pumps. The industry is still small but is growing. In order to
start being fossil fuels-free, Alaska has set a goal in which the 50% of the used energy has to be provided by
renewable sources. Thanks to this initiative, systems like GHP are developing [47] [48]. Since Greenland
and Alaska share a similar kind of ground, the studies made in Alaska can serve as a ﬁrst overview for future
projects in Greenland.
6.3
The geothermal heat pump in Nuuk
A few months ago the ﬁrst geothermal heat pump in Greenland started running. It is a vertical heat pump
placed in the capital, Nuuk, that provides energy to an ofﬁce building owned by Nukissiorﬁit. The system is
working with low temperature radiators that were installed after a remodeling of the building. The system so
far was running successfully and owners and user are satisﬁed with its performance [49].
==PAGE:75==
6.4. IS IT FEASIBLE TO INSTALL ONE IN GREENLAND?
59
Drilling a hole in the ground can be expensive and even more if the place is isolated. This is somehow
the case of Greenland. The country does not have the appropriate tools to drill boreholes in the ground
for geothermal heat pumps so the machines were brought from Sweden exclusively for this project. The
shipping from Sweden to Nuuk lasted 3 weeks and the trip back, another 3 weeks. However, the actual work
in the place lasted less than one month. In short, the shipping took longer than making the job in Nuuk [49].
The Swedish company that made the work was renting out the machinery per day. Plus, as there are
not skilled workers in this ﬁeld in Greenland, workers from Sweden had to be hired as well. Therefore, the
accommodation in Nuuk for them had to be provided too [49].
6.4
is it feasible to install one in Greenland?
There are not yet many studies about geothermal heat pumps in Greenland. From the short experience
of the one installed in Nuuk, it can be said that the cost of installation of this kind of systems high. This is
due to the nonexistent market of GHP in Greenland. The fact that all the machinery has to be shipped from
somewhere else increases the costs a lot.
This systems require pipes buried in the ground and this is something avoided in the places with per-
mafrost. Heat pipes in arctic places are placed above the ground as the heat can damage the stability of the
soil. This is one of the questions that need to be solved in order to minimize the problems in the system [48].
Additionally,if more places in Greenland decide to use geothermal heat pumps the installation costs
could decrease. Even in the machines have to be rented from somewhere else, they could be used in more
than one place so the it would be more exploited [49].
Furthermore, installing a geothermal heat pump requires also a previous evaluation of the area. People and
companies with the knowledge for doing this kind of studies are not always in Greenland, so they have to be
hired from somewhere else.
6.5
How can this be applied to the residential area?
As previously mentioned, a study of the ground, heating loads etc should be done in order to evaluate
which geothermal heating system would be the appropriate one. There is space and it could be a possibility
to be able to use a geothermal heat pump for one building or maybe more in the residential area.
A combination between solar thermal panels and a geothermal heat pump for Space Heating and
Domestic How Water could be possible . Both of them can be connected to a heat storage tank. As the
geothermal energy does not fully depend on weather conditions like the solar thermal energy, the mix of this
too to have power all over the year is a good option.
District heating could be as well connected to the tank as an auxiliary heating. This way the demand of
district heating could be reduced.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
In this report different kind of renewable systems have been proposed to partially provide energy to a
residential block in the town of Sisimiut. The main proposals included a PV grid-connected system, a solar
collector system and a discussion regarding geothermal heat pumps in cold climates.
PV grid-connected systems for residential areas is still does not exist in Greenland. The hydropower plants
provide clean energy to the inhabitants of the largest towns. However, the population is moving from the
small settlements to the towns so the energy need will be bigger and these systems could help to provide this
extra energy if it is necessary.
The use of solar collectors for DHW in Sisimiut can be a good option as it is something using through the
whole year. Sunlight hours during summer in places above the Arctic circle can beneﬁt a lot the performance
of a solar collector ﬁeld. On the other hand, it was found that a great amount of energy is needed for SH.
Combining SH from solar collectors and district heating can be difﬁcult due to losses regarding different
temperatures.
Nevertheless, simulation softwares with any value related with the sun position as an input for these kind of
latitudes and climates, are not prepared for giving an accurate result. Problems regarding radiation data were
a challenge as there is not documentation showing a unique solution for this matter.
Sisimiut society and speciﬁcally the residential area studied need to be informed about the way they
consume energy and how can they save money in, for instance, the electricity bill. Small gestures as turning
off the radiators if the windows are going to stay open or if they are just not needed, can make a difference in
energy consumption and savings. The community is responsible for this as well and some changes in they
way the electricity and heat is provided and how the citizens are charged for them, could reduce the waste of
energy.
Greenland is still strongly dependent on fossil fuels to provide energy to the inhabitants. It is specially impor-
tant that the international community make big efforts to preserve places with such a delicate environment
and crucial for the world climate.

could help to the development of sustainable energy systems in these places.
