Preface 
The thesis was submitted to the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) for partial 
fulfilment of the requirements for the candidature of a master‘s degree in Aquatic Science 
and Technology (cand.polyt.) from the National Institute of Aquatic Resources (DTU Aqua). 
The project is divided into two parts affiliated with different geographical places; 1) 
collection of samples, 2) genetic work, analysis and writing. 
Samples were collected at the Natural History Museum of Copenhagen, Denmark, and all the 
practical work associated with the genetic studies took place at the population genetics lab at 
DTU Aqua in Silkeborg, Denmark. Genetic analysis and the thesis write-up were conducted 
at DTU Aqua, in Silkeborg and Lyngby, Denmark. The goal of the thesis was to investigate 
the global population structure of the Tiger Shark (Galeocerdo cuvier). Briefly, findings 
included new insights into the genetic connectivity of tiger sharks in the Indo-pacific, as well 
as discoveries of the origin of archived tiger sharks from the 18th century.

Thesis summary 
This study aims at investigating the population structure of an iconic globally distributed 
shark species, the Tiger Shark (Galeocerdo cuvier), by applying genetic tools. This study 
adds to (Bernard et al., 2016a) and (Holmes et al., 2017) findings for global distribution of 
the tiger shark, in order to obtain a better overall resolution of the population composition and 
spatial dynamics for tiger shark. In the Introduction section the study species will be 
introduced (Study organism: The Tiger Shark (Galeocerdo cuvier) considering first general 
perspectives of the biology, fisheries exploitation and genetics, followed by basic population 
genetics. Here, background of Molecular Markers, Statistical Tests and Population Structure 
of Marine Fish, as well as a walk-through of experiment setup in the present study is 
presented, in order to understand the application of genetics in conservation and management. 
The second section defines practicalities in relation to the research conducted, where also 
methods involving the genetic tools are described. Results (both technical and genetic) are 
presented in the third section (Results) followed by a summarization and discussion of the 
findings, as well as future perspectives (Discussion).   
Introduction  
Molecular-based approaches on fishes has contributed significantly to the understanding of 
the processes of evolutionary change and diversification for more than 60 years. Fishes 
display extraordinary cases of explosive speciation in vertebrates, displaying great potential 
for adaptive radiation and persistence across diverse environments. These traits makes fishes 
great model organisms for genetic and genomic studies of local adaptation, genetic structure 
in the wild as well as in aquaculture, and response to environmental stressors and 
overexploitations (reviewed in (Carvalho et al., 2016)). 
One quarter of the 1,041 chondrichthyan fishes; sharks, rays, and chimaeras, are currently 
considered threatened according to International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) 
Red List criteria, due to targeted or incidential overfishing. Overall chondrichthyan extinction 
risk is substantially higher than for most other vertebrates (Fowler et al., 2005) 
In order to provide the best possible management unit for at given (exploited or threatened) 
species, detection and understanding of the mechanisms shaping intraspecific genetic 
population structure of a given species needs to be known. The level of gene flow and the

level of genetic independence among populations are key parameters influencing the health 
of the population as well as their persistence towards environmental change. 
Population genetic approaches can not only be applied to answer questions of population 
subdivision but also in relation to movement ecology of individuals concerning contemporary 
dynamics of natural populations. In addition, genetic tools can be used to determine the 
species in question, but also the geographic origin of catch and thus be an important tool for 
Fighting IUU fishing. 
To determine the population structure of a given species, large sampling sizes are required for 
robust statistical results. The longer they migrate, the larger a sampling area is needed, to 
conduct studies as informative as possible, and to then make the best possible management 
decisions. 
Study species: Tiger Sharks 
The tiger shark Galeocerdo cuvier (Peron & LeSueur 1822) is a large, circumglobal coastal-
pelagic shark. It is omnivorous, displaying a very generalist, opportunistic diet (Ferreira et 
al., 2017): A stomach content study revealed that the number of prey items is the largest of 
any elasmobranch (n=193), including fish, seabirds, marine mammals, and even the small 
antelope blue duiker (Dicken et al., 2017), showing that these sharks exploit multiple shelf 
and offshore habitats. Accordingly the trophic role of tiger sharks is both context- and 
habitat-dependent  (Ferreira et al., 2017), and they are in the position to structure marine 
ecosystems through top-down processes (Bernard et al., 2016a). 
Tiger sharks can reach a total length (LT) up to 5.5 m. (An especially large male of 7.5 m. LT 
has however been documented from the Persian Gulf (Assadi et al., 1997)). Males reach 
maturity at approximately 292 cm LT, whereas females mature between 330 and 430 cm LT, 
where they are assumed to be 8-11 years old. The tiger shark is the only species of the 
Carcharhinidae family that is ovoviviparous, with large litter sizes ranging from 3 to 57, with 
a mean of 32.6 embryos per litter (Whitney and Crow, 2007). Tiger sharks display discrete 
pupping areas in the North West Atlantic, from the Gulf of Mexico, to North Carolina (Fig.3 
in (Driggers et al., 2008). Data from 23 litters from various months of the year indicated that 
the size of the pups at birth are 80–90 cm LT, however female tiger sharks give birth only 
once every three years, implying an overall low fecundity compared to other species of 
elasmobranchs (Whitney and Crow, 2007).

So how do ecosystems respond to removal of an omnivorous shark that feeds in several

Population Genetics 
Alongside molecular ecology, phylogenetics, phylogeography and taxonomy, population 
genetics is part of the large field of conservation genetics. It’s an applied science that utilizes 
molecular tools to help solve problems in species conservation (Larson and Daly-Engel, 
2017). Population genetics is a dialog between predictions based on principles of Mendelian 
inheritance and results obtained from empirical measurement of genotype and allele 
frequencies that form the basis of hypothesis tests (Hamilton et al., 2009). 
The concept of what a population is, is central to the academic fields of ecology, 
conservation- and evolutionary biology, and a population can hence be defined within a 
statistical, an ecological or an evolutionary paradigm (Waples and Gaggiotti, 2006). The first 
definition focuses on an aggregation of individuals one wants to draw inferences about by 
sampling (Snedecor and Cochran, 1977), the second on demographic cohesion and co-
occurrence in space and time, and the third emphasizes reproductive interaction between 
individuals, and genetic isolation or independence where there is no interaction.  As there is 
not one objective definition, it is important to clarify which definition of a population is being 
used, as for other researcher to apply the same definition to a common problem in order to 
achieve the same results (Waples and Gaggiotti, 2006). 
In this thesis, I apply a definition of the evolutionary paradigm, saying that a population is a 
group of individuals within a species who are reproductively isolated (to some degree) from 
other individuals of that same species. Populations of especially marine fish are often referred 
to as ‘’stocks’’, which is something to harvest from, or manage in a fisheries assessment 
perspective. A stock is geographically defined, and does not take biological populations into 
account, as several stocks can consist of one population, and several populations can be in 
one stock. Hence, ‘stocks’ is not a population genetic term.

Differentiation of populations can be considered as in Fig. 2, where two extreme scenarios 
can be detected according to divergence. To the far right in Fig. 2D, is a species consisting of 
one large homogenous population (panmixia) containing all individuals. Here, mating is 
random within the entire metapopulation, and potential subpopulations are arbitrary. The 
other extreme in Fig. 2A is a species consisting of several populations, completely isolated 
from direct genetic and demographic linkage.  
Real populations in nature are typically intermediate between these two structures of 
populations (Fig.2B and C) (Waples & Gaggiotti 2006). The departure from panmixia occurs 
along a continuum of time, and could be regarded as evidence for the existence of separate 
populations. The level of departure from panmixia is determined by the genetic distance 
between samples, when the evolutionary definition of what a population is, is chosen.  
Selective forces 
Several factors determine the genetic level of structuring within and among populations. 
Changes in allele frequency are caused by four evolutionary forces: Migration/gene flow, 
random genetic drift, mutation, and selection. The degree of genetic population 
differentiation is shaped by a combination of these four factors.  
Migration is defined as changes in allele frequency due to mixing individuals from 
genetically different populations. Reproductive cohesiveness is determined by levels of gene 
flow, and the evolutionary consequences of gene flow equal the absolute number of effective 
migrants (Nem), which is the product of the effective population size, Ne and the migration 
==PAGE:11==
Master’s Thesis, DTU Aqua 
Matilde Sort 
01.10.2017 
 
 
 
11 
 
rate, m. In popular terms Ne is the number of individuals in a population that contributes with 
offspring to the next generation, or more correctly; the number of individuals that would give 
rise to the calculated loss of heterozygosity, inbreeding or variance in allele frequencies if 
they behaved in the manner of an idealized population. The population definition under the 
evolutionary paradigm requires Nem < 25 of genetic exchange between populations pr. 
generation, in order to say that the exchanged individuals constitute a different population.  
Random genetic drift is when allele frequency change due to random change alone. It is a 
stochastic process where an allele will be fixed or lost from the population during 
reproduction from one generation to the next. Populations with a small Ne are much more 
likely to be subjected to genetic drift than populations with a large Ne (Frankham, Briscoe 
and Ballou, 2002).  
 
Selection helps fixation of alleles that increase fitness. If a mutation occurs in a gene that 
increases the relative fitness of that individual compared to the average fitness of the 
population, this gene can be selected for, increase in allele frequency, and become fixed in 
the population over generations. This is also referred to as a selective sweep (Frankham, 
Briscoe and Ballou, 2002). Drift and migration can grouped together be classified as neutral 
processes, in contrast to selection. The identification of the relative effects natural and 
selective forces, is important in studies identifying adaptive response to global change, or 
genetic consequences of e.g. selective harvesting of fish (Nielsen et al., 2009). 
Mutations are mistakes in the copying process of DNA at any site of the genome that creates 
new alleles. Mutations can be either lethal, recessive lethal, neutral, nearly neutral, and/or 
advantageous (Frankham, Briscoe and Ballou, 2002). Mutation occurs at a relatively low rate 
and can therefore only shape population differentiation over a large number of generations in 
the time span of several thousands of years.  In contrast, allelic richness can be lowered 
substantially over one or a few generations if enough individuals from a population are 
removed (Thaulow Pedersen et al., 2009) 
All finite populations (independently of their finite size) will be affected by mutations and 
genetic drift, as well as selection, which will overall cause allele frequencies change. 
However, populations with a small Ne will be affected by genetic drift at a stronger intensity 
than populations with a larger Ne, because in these large Ne populations genetic drift is 
counteracted by selection.

In order for adaptive differentiation to occur, the intensity of selection s has to be stronger 
than migration m and genetic drift ¼ Ne. s > m and s > ¼ Ne. 
 
Molecular markers 
A genetic marker is a genetic unit that has the properties of being somewhat easily identified 
or measured. Polymorphic markers are markers that show some degree of variability in a 
population (e.g. individuals are expected to have different values of that marker.)  
Several different markers have been developed over time, as no individual marker is ideal for 
all applications and aspects of population genetics (Thaulow Pedersen et al., 2009) 
For many years, allozymes were the universal genetic marker, making many valuable 
contributions to basic and applied conservation and management (Waples, 2008). mtDNA 
analysis were introduced around 1980, and microsatellites followed in the 1990s, and gained 
popularity as they represent independent loci (if not linked), opposed to mtDNA which is one 
locus (all variation linked), and because microsattelites are highly abundant within the 
genome (Larson and Daly-Engel, 2017), and have hence alongside SNPs largely replaced 
allozymes as the marker of choice for population genetics studies. 
Genetic markers can very roughly be said to be divided into neutral and adaptive markers: 
Neutral markers have high mutation rates, making them advantageous in studies of genetic 
population structure, reproductive relationships (Storz, 2005), and demographic population 
relationships, such as estimating contemporary population parameters of Nem. These 
parameters would be obscured if non-neutral genetic markers subject to selection were used 
(Nielsen et al., 2009). It is therefore important to identify and remove markers that may be 
linked to functional genes, as the allele frequencies might be affected by natural selection. On 
the other hand, markers subject to selection can be used to identify local populations even in 
cases where gene flow is too high to allow neutral divergence to build up (Waples & 
Gaggiotti 2006). 
For studies of adaptive genetic variation, the interest is in identifying molecular genetic 
markers under selection. (Beaumont, 2005), but population genomics commonly include, or 
build on, previous large sequencing efforts created by a large number of anonymous markers 
(e.g. microsatellites). ‘Genomics’ is a broadly defined term for studies that target either the 
==PAGE:13==

entire genome, or large parts of the genome, of an organism. Population genomics is hence 
population genetics writ large; population genetic analyses of a large number of loci, 
distributed throughout the genome, allowing for discrimination between locus-specific 
(selection) and effects on a genome-scale (drift and migration) (Stinchcombe and Hoekstra, 
2008). 
Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) 
The mitochondria possess several copies of a single small loop of maternally inherited DNA 
(mtDNA). The size of elasmobranch mitochondrial genome is similar to those of other 
vertebrates, ranging from 16.707 to 16.783 base pairs (bp) (Heist, 2008) 
Because mtDNA is haploid and maternally inherited, it has an approximately fourfold lower 
effective population than nuclear genes (both parent contributes with each diploid loci (=4 
loci) in nuclear DNA, in mtDNA only one parent contribute with one copy). MtDNA 
therefore undergoes genetic drift at a faster rate than nuclear genes. This accelerated rate 
results in higher levels of genetic differentiation, i.e. highler FST values, (see section on FST) 
and greater power for detecting population structure, however it is important to keep in mind 
that mtDNA-derived FST values only indicate female mediated gene flow or lack hereof 
(Heist, 2008). 
MtDNA has been used for DNA barcoding, where species of unknown origin are identified 
through the sequencing of small fragments of mtDNA. Barcoding is useful when working 
with unidentified fins or body parts, and when identifying cryptic species, immature 
specimens, and hybrids (Dudgeon et al., 2012), and has been a great tool in Fighting Illegal, 
Unreported and Unregulated (IUU) fishing (Nielsen et al., 2012; Xiong et al., 2016) 
Microsatellites 
Microsatellites are short, non-coding two to four base pair length tandem repeats (e.g. 
(CTC)4) found in the nuclear genome, meaning that they are co-dominantly inherited from 
both parents. They evolve primarily through replication slippage, and are sometimes also 
referred to as Short Tandem Repeats (STRs). The length of the repeated sequences is highly 
variable from organism to organism, as microsatellites are subject to high levels of mutation 
rates ranging from 10-5 to 10-2 mutations pr. generation (Heist, 2008), and hence commonly 
display a high amount of allelic diversity within populations (Ashley & Dow, 1994). 
Flanking regions surrounding the microsatellites are constant and non-repetitive, and are used 
==PAGE:14==
Master’s Thesis, DTU Aqua 
Matilde Sort 
01.10.2017 
 
 
 
14 
 
to design complementary unique primers to amplify individual microsatellites using the 
Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR). The primers are sequences of generally 18 to 24 base 
pairs long (Portnoy, 2010). Some loci have 20 or more alleles within a single population, and 
heterozygosities exceeding 95% (O’Connell, M. Wright, 1998). Data from microsatellites can 
be obtained from very small pieces of tissue (e.g., blood, biopsies, or fin clips), enabling non-
lethal sampling (Heist, 2008). The use of microsatellite markers relies on the assumption that 
all size variation is due to changes in the number of repeated sequence units. Changes in 
number of sequences are thought to occur via “slip-strand mispairing” where the repetitive 
segment aligns improperly during DNA replication, because of their propensity to mispair, 
resulting in excision or addition of a repeat unit (Levinson and Gutman, 1987). The high 
polymorphism across multiple loci provide each individual with a ‘’genetic fingerprint’’, 
making microsatellites ideal for studying kinship, relatedness, and recent population structure 
(Selkoe and Toonen, 2006). 
A common error type when working with microsatellites is null alleles (Pompanon et al., 
2005). Null alleles have three definitions. They are 1) alleles with a mutation that prevents 
the production of a functional gene; 2) alleles that produce functional products, which are 
however not detected by the analytical method used, and 3) DNA sequences that are not 
detected because of a mutation in the primer-binding region. All three definitions of errors 
will cause true heterozygotes to be miss-scored as homozygotes, leading to false heterozygote 
deficiency and positive Fis.(see section on FST) 
Null alleles needs to be statistically accounted for in genetic datasets, as genotyping errors 
can significantly affect the conclusions drawn from a particular study (Ellis et al., 2011). 
Another problem with microsatellites is homoplasy. The high mutation rate makes it possible 
for two populations to contain the same allele derived from two different mutations, resulting 
in falsely inferred levels of relatedness between two populations (Thaulow Pedersen et al., 
2009). At least for now, microsatellites continues to be the most effective and easily applied 
genetic tool for characterizing relatedness (Larson and Daly-Engel, 2017). 
SNPs 
SNPs are single base pair substitutions in a DNA sequence, caused by a point mutation or 
indels (Morin, Luikart and Wayne, 2004). SNPs are densely distributed throughout the 
genome, and are surrounded by flanking regions to both sides (Portnoy, 2010). Because SNPs 
==PAGE:15==
Master’s Thesis, DTU Aqua 
Matilde Sort 
01.10.2017 
 
 
 
15 
 
are single base pair substitutions, they have a very simple mutational model and a low 
probability of homoplasy, due to the low frequency of these substitutions at a given locus. 
SNPs are biallelic, meaning that fast genotyping with low error rates and high comparability 
across labs is possible (Morin et al. 2004). SNPs also have a lower mutation rate than that of 
microsatellites (10-8 to 10-9 mutations pr. generation). This means that an estimated 4-10 
times more SNPs are required to obtain the same level of genetic information as that of 
microsatellites on levels of genetic diversity, and 40-100 times as many SNPs may be 
required to asses relatedness (Morin, Luikart and Wayne, 2004; Jones et al., 2009). Analysing 
thousands of SNPs generated with next-generation sequencing techniques (NGS) requires a 
large amount of processing power and bioinformatics knowledge, but applying more loci it is 
more likely to identify outlier loci subjected to selection, and thereby identify areas in the 
genome possibly subjected to selection (Morin, Luikart and Wayne, 2004) 
With fast advances in next-generation sequencing techniques, SNPs have become 
increasingly popular, and it is likely that within the next decade, whole-genome SNPs will 
become a more commonly used marker type, perhaps replacing microsatellite loci in 
effectiveness on studies on assessments of adaptations and recent population structure 
(Larson and Daly-Engel, 2017) 
Statistical tests for genetic divergence within and among population 
Determination of whether a group of individuals of the same species belongs to the same 
population (i.e. is homogenous), or are divided into subpopulation is crucial for the 
conservation and management of that species. Obtainment of the allele frequency of samples 
of a population or subpopulation can be used to test if the samples are related or consist of 
statistically different combinations of allele frequencies, and thus can be divided into 
subpopulations.  
It could be said that statistics sits at the intersection of theoretical and empirical population 
genetics (Hamilton et al., 2009). In 1908 both Godfrey Hardy and William Weinberg 
simultaneously, although independently, developed an equation that describes how under the 
right circumstances, mendelian genetics work at the population level (Hardy, 1908; 
Weinberg, 1908). The Hardy-Weinberg law is a principle stating that genotypic frequencies 
can be expressed as a simple function of allele frequencies, after a single episode of random 
mating, and that genotypic- and allele frequencies remain constant over time, in the absence 
==PAGE:16==
Master’s Thesis, DTU Aqua 
Matilde Sort 
01.10.2017 
 
 
 
16 
 
of the disturbing action of evolutionary forces. Genotypic- and allele frequencies will remain 
constant because they are in Hardy Weinberg equilibrium (HWE). It constitutes five basic 
assumptions: No mutation, no gene flow, no selection, but random mating and infinite 
population size is neccesary. Moreover, random sampling from the population as a whole is 
an implicit assumption. 
HWE shows the frequency with which you could expect to find different alleles within a 
hypothetical population that is not evolving, and where the frequency of alleles in a 
population remains constant from generation to generation. If the assumptions are not met for 
a gene, the allele frequencies of that gene may change.   
From HWE, it is possible to predict allele frequencies explained by a simple 2-allele (A1 and 
A2) model. If allele A1 and A2 with frequencies p and q (p+q=1), respectively, exist in a 
population, the probability that an A1 gamete joins with an A1 gamete to produce the zygote 
A1A1 is p*p = p2 and the probability of an A2 gamete joining with an A2 gamete to produce 
the zygote A2A2 is q*q= q2. There are two ways that a heterozygote can be produced (A1A2 
or A2A1) and the probability of joining an A1 gamete with an A2 gamete is therefore 2 p*q = 
2 pq. The expected frequencies of A1A1, A1A2, and A2A2 zygotes are thus p2, 2pq, and q2. 
These are also referred to as the Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium genotypes (Frankham, Briscoe 
and Ballou, 2002; Thaulow Pedersen et al., 2009). The simple p+q = 1 provides the 
opportunity to calculate the frequency of one gamete if you have the other one, so if q is 0.90 
then p is 1 – 0.90 = 0.10 
Results of two-locus analysis are typically expressed in terms of linkage disequilibrium (LD), 
defined as non-random association of alleles at different loci, independent of where the 
different loci is located (Waples, 2015) 
Due to the fact that all of the evolutionary forces are commonly operating in nature, HWE is 
not a true equilibrium, when applied to natural populations (Waples, 2015).  HWE describes 
an idealized state or a baseline, and genetic variations in nature can be measured as changes 
from this equilibrium state. This makes comparison of populations possible, as well as 
monitoring and differentiating the factors that change those populations.

Departures from HWE can be caused by a number of factors such as inbreeding, which 
should normally lead to an increase of homozygotes, null alleles, selection or Wahlund effect.  
The Wahlund effect is the reduction in heterozygosity (observation of more homozygotes 
than expected by HWE) due to the mixing of two or more semi-isolated subpopulations with 
different allele frequencies. When such a mixed sample is collected, the frequency of 
heterozygotes will be lower, compared to that of the expected frequency of heterozygotes in a 
panmictic population of the same total size with the same average allele frequencies 
(Wahlund, 1928; Hamilton et al., 2009; Thaulow Pedersen et al., 2009). 
By testing for HWE it is therefore possible to get an estimate of the state of the population, 
but it is often not possible to directly see which of these factors are causing deviation from 
HWE.

fundamentally important to focus on whether departures from HWE could be attributed to 
any of the selective forces / population mixture, or reflects scoring errors or other non-genetic 
artefacts (Waples, 2015). That includes acknowledging that failure to reject a hypothesis does 
not prove that the hypothesis is true, taking issues of multiple testing into account when 
sampling several loci in several populations, and remembering to distinguish between 
statistical significance and biological significance, as statistically significant differences may

Statistical tests of HWE tries to answer the questions “After accounting for sampling error, 
are genotypic frequencies observed in a sample compatible with those expected under 
HWE?”. The agreement or lack of agreement of observed and expected genotypic 
frequencies can be expressed using Sewall Wright’s F statistics (WRIGHT, 1950; Waples, 
2015), which is defined as the correlation between two alleles chosen at random within 
subpopulations relative to alleles sampled at random from the total population (WRIGHT, 
1950). It can also be expressed as the proportion of the total genetic variance, which is 
distributed among populations (Weir and Cockerham, 1984).

Population structure of marine fish 
Populations can diverge genetically from one another through interaction of the four 
evolutionary forces on populations. Molecular markers are identifiable pieces of DNA, and 
can be used to measure this genetic diversion when applied on samples from the population. 
(Waples, 1998; Thaulow Pedersen et al., 2009) 
‘Classical marine fish’ are defined as fish with large population sizes, high fecundity, pelagic 
larvae, and wide distributions (Nielsen and Kenchington, 2001). Many species of the classical 
marine fish category are finfish of commercial importance e.g. scombrids (mackerel, tuna, 
and bonito) and pleuronectids (plaices, soles and flounders), that have been extensively 
studied using population genetics, aiming to determine the discreteness of specific 
populations with the goal of determining fisheries management units (Hauser and Carvalho, 
2008; Nielsen et al., 2009).  
In general, marine fishes display high intra-population levels of genetic diversity, and weak 
neutral genetic differentiation at the inter-population-level (low FST, see section on statistical 
tests), when compared to freshwater and anadromous species (Waples, 1998). This is due to 
the fewer physical migration barriers, and large potential spread of pelagic eggs and larvae in 
the oceans compared to freshwater systems (higher migration m), coupled with general large 
Ne’s in marine fishes, rendering genetic drift a relatively weak evolutionary force (Nielsen et 
al., 2009). 
 
Even though geographical barriers are fewer in the oceans, factors such as distance, depth, 
temperature variation and salinity gradients, current pathways and patchy distribution of 
suitable habitat, structure populations on a genetic scale (Grant and Bowen, 1998). 
 
An important feature of population structure of classical marine fish, is that as Nem is of a 
substantial size, Nes is typically large too. Nes is the efficiency of a population to adapt 
depending on the coefficient of selection. Selection is the strongest force, leading 
evolutionary change in a population, if that population has a large Ne. 
Nem can prevent or slow down formation of small populations to adapt locally, but as Nes is 
large too, these local populations are little affected by random genetic drift, meaning that they 
are likely to respond positively to selective sweeping. Nem and Nes counteracts each other,

meaning that small levels of selection can override the effects of random genetic drift, 
thereby being a more powerful evolutionary force (Nielsen et al., 2009). 
Population genetics of sharks 
A thourough review of the application of molecular genetics for fisheries management and 
conservation of sharks and rays  have been conducted by (Dudgeon et al., 2012). In this 
review identification, phylogeography, reproductive philopatry, effective population size and 
molecular evolutionary rates are highlighted. 
Large elasmobranchs display a different reproductive strategy than marine finfish. Generally,  
slow growth and late sexual maturity, very long reproductive cycles, low fertility and 
relatively extended longevity are aspects of their reproduction (Ebert et al., 2013). That 
means that the effective population sizes are much smaller, resulting in lower levels of 
genetic variation, compared to marine fish (Dudgeon et al., 2012). 
Genetic diversity in sharks may be lower than that of other vertebrates, simply due to slower 
substitution rates. A study comparing synonymous substitution rates estimated for the 
mitochondrial cytochrome b gene, as well as three nuclear-encoded genes in mammals and 
sharks, revealed that mtDNA substitution rates are seven to eight times slower, and nuclear 
genes substitution rates are 10-20 time slower in sharks, compared to the substitution rates for 
mammals (Martin, Naylor and Palumbi, 1992; Martin, 1999)This relatively slow rate of 
genetic change in shark genomes, could imply that shark populations take longer time to 
recover from i.e. bottlenecks (Martin, 1999). 
It is therefore important to take the nature of a given species into consideration, when 
measuring their genetic diversity. Low levels can be due to low levels of effective population 
sizes, caused by overexploitation, but could also be indicative of lower mutation rates. This 
was for example observed in deepwater shark Broadnose Sevengill Shark (Notorynchus 
cepedianus) with average observed heterozygosities (Ho) of 0.417 and expected 
heterozygosities (He) of 0.531 (Larson et al., 2015), and the obligate deep water tope shark 
(Galeorhinus galeus) with average Ho of 0.40 and He of 0.43 (Chabot, 2015).

Assignment methods 
Most traditional population genetic methods are based on equilibrium assumptions, 
meaning that they attempt to characterize long-term genetic processes that have achieved a 
stable balance between opposing evolutionary forces.(Manel, Gaggiotti and Waples, 
2005). Contemporary dynamics of natural populations that occur on much shorter time 
scales, are typically considered ‘noise’ in these equilibrium models, so instead these 
contemporary events can be studied using assignment methods. Here, genetic information 
is used to ascertain population memberships of individuals (Hansen, Kenchington and 
Nielsen, 2001; Nielsen, 2016). 
One commonly used method for inferring population origin is “individual assignment 
tests”. The test approach the hypothesis that the individual in question arose from a 
particular population, based on the its multilocus genotype, and the probabilities of 
observing that genotype in each of a set of potential source populations is calculated 
(Manel, Gaggiotti and Waples, 2005). The individual is then assigned to the population 
where its multilocus genotypehas the highest likelihood of occuring 
The fundamental assumptions are that all potential source populations are defined in 
advance, sampled randomly, and are in Hardy-Weinberg- and linkage equilibrium. (Manel, 
Gaggiotti and Waples, 2005). 
 
Assignment tests can be used to assess the genetic distinctness of populations under study, in 
order to identify immigrants or individuals of immigrant ancestry, and thereby study patterns 
of dispersal and gene flow among populations (Hansen, Kenchington and Nielsen, 2001). For 
example the method can provide answer to questions such as ‘’How many populations are 
found to be mixing in my study area?’’ (Manel, Gaggiotti and Waples, 2005). As the 
==PAGE:25==

requirement for traceability of fish in relation to illegal, unreported and unregulated (IUU) 
fisheries increases, assignment methods have been applied extensively in fisheries forensic, 
(Ogden, 2008; Nielsen et al., 2012). 
A key feature of all assignment methods is that they make use of the large amount of 
information available in multilocus genotypes of individuals, meaning the combined 
information from several loci within an individual. The approach has traditionally focused on 
neutral microsatellite markers as neutral markers capable of reflecting regional reductions in 
gene flow, but more recently moved over to SNP’s. 
The ability to accurately assign fish to their population, relies on a number of different 
factors. First of all the level of underlying genetic structure or level genetic differentiation 
among the population needs to be known, the quality of the comparative datasets (size, 
representation and data accuracy, the number and variability of the molecular markers 
applied, and the relative genetic uniqueness of the individual sample (Manel, Gaggiotti and 
Waples, 2005; Ogden, 2008). Generally, assignment accuracy is greatest when using a 
modest number of loci with a modest number of alleles (Bernatchez, 2016); however, 
evaluating and selecting the best combination of loci for population assignment through 
empiric analysis of data drawn from natural populations is beneficial for assignment power 
(Banks, Eichert and Olsen, 2003) 
If the populations are sufficiently genetically distinct, we should expect that most or all 
individuals are assigned back to their correct population of origin. However, the population 
of origin does not necessarily exist in the populations included in a study, but the nature of 
individual assignment procedure entails that all individuals are assigned to a baseline sample, 
no matter how small their likelihoods are (Nielsen, 2016). 
To avoid miss-assignment, each assignment test has an exclusion-method counterpart, which 
measures the confidence associated with individual assignment tests, using Markov Chain 
Monte Carlo (MCMC) simulations based on the sample allele frequency. The MCMC is a 
technique to solve the problem of sampling from a complicated distribution, and the 
statistical procedure of the technique is repeated e.g. 10.000 times, until the expected 
distribution of genotypes in that population is obtained (Manel, Gaggiotti and Waples, 2005). 
This information can be used to generate a distribution of genotypic likelihood values.

Genetic variation and statistical analysis 
Genotypes of both contemporary and archived sharks were checked for scoring errors and 
duplicates using the Microsoft Excel extension program Microsatellite Toolkit. Tests for 
Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium (HWE) and Linkage Disequilibrium (LD) among pairs of loci 
in each population were performed in the GenePop package (Rousset, 2008) in RStudio, 
using the following Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) parameters for all tests 
(dememorization: 100000, batches: 100, and iterations per batch: 10000) (see Appendix 3).  
Population pairwise standard diversity indices were estimated for the contemporary samples 
using GenAlEx v. 6.5, including number of alleles (Na), number of effective alleles (Ne), 
expected (He) and observed heterozygosities (Ho), and inbreeding coefficient (Fis) (See 
Appendix 2). 
Pairwise FST values among all samples from the two studies were estimated using Arlequin v. 
3.11 (Excoffier, 2010), to determine the level of genetic. The level of significance was 
calculated using permutations to test for significance set at 100, and the number of 
permutations for Mantel test set at 1000.  
False Discovery Rate (FDR) corrections using RStudio were applied to control 
the expected proportion of (rejected null hypotheses) that are false positives, when testing for 
HWE, LD and FST. FDR was applied, as this correction calculates the expected number of 
false discoveries divided by the number of discoveries. (Yoav and Yosef, 1995). Following 
FDR correction, the screening of genotypes of the contemporary samples revealed no linkage 
disequilibrium for any of the loci of any of the populations. Likewise, the genotype 
frequencies at all loci did not deviate from Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium. 
GeneClass (Piry, 2004) was used to perform individual assignment to population. Self-
assignment of the individuals in the baseline samples was carried out an assignment threshold 
of scores of 0.05, employing Bayesian Methods by Rannala and Mountain (Rannala, 1997). 
Assignment of individuals of unknown origin was done with the same threshold.

Another challenge in aligning the work of different groups, is that different labs display 
different habits of scoring ‘’upwards’’ or ‘’downwards’’, meaning that if an allele is situated

two base pairs ‘’upwards’’, while another laboratory would tend to score that same allele one 
or two base pairs ‘’downwards’’. These habits should however always be consistent and 
hence easy to detect for accurate calibration for a third part. 
Method considerations 
When computing statistical output, it is highly important to be aware of the fact that different 
methods (different mathematic equations) estimate the same parameter, but provide different 
results. When estimating FST I initially used both GenePop in Rstudio and Arlequin, and 
found that their output estimated were slightly different, but following the same pattern: 
Genepop estimated FST by analysis of variance, following (Weir and Cockerham, 1984), and 
Arlequin computes Nei’s average number of pairwise differences within and between 
populations (Nei, 1987), as well as RST developed by Slatkin (1995) (See section on FST).I 
decided to run all three methods to evaluate if the Slatkin method build for microsatellites 
would provide a different output, but it was identical to that of Nei’s. Comparing this 
Arlequin output to Weir and Cockerham in Genepop, all three methods displayed the same 
pattern, but both Arlequin methods were more conservative, meaning that a lot more p-values 
were insignificant compared to the GenePop methods. Both the Bernard et al. and the Holmes 
et al. study used Arleqiun for FST comparisons, and it should be carefully evaluated which 
method provides the most appropriate, and in this case comparable, result, especially when 
working with as small and marginally significant FST as in this study.  
When several dependent or independent statistical tests are being performed simultaneously 
on a single data set, it is wise to apply a measure that reduces the chances of obtaining false-
==PAGE:51==
Master’s Thesis, DTU Aqua 
Matilde Sort 
01.10.2017

positive results (type I errors), as the probability of identifying at least one significant result 
due to chance increases as more hypotheses are tested. (Rice, 2008) 
Two different correction measures for adjustment of significance were applied in the two 
studies: Bernard et al. used False Discovery Rate (FDR), and Holmes et al. used sequential 
Bonferroni corrections. As sequential Bonferroni can be viewed as very conservative (Here, I 
would divide 0.05 with 732 = 0.000068, and this would be the new threshold that 
discriminates truly positive tests at alpha = 0.05). Correction of false positives were 
calculated using both FDR and Bonferroni, but as Bonferroni output was much more 
conservative, FDR was chosen here. For future comparisons with other tiger shark dataset, 
one needs to be aware of the whole analysis pipeline, in order to evaluate if differences in 
estimates can be caused simply by different methods applied. 
FST  
Sampling sizes matter when conducting statistical analysis. The absence of significant 
structuring allows for samples, obtained at the same locality, to be pooled. A potential 
problem arises when the samples are very small, as the power for detecting genetic 
differentiation directly depends on sample size. The power of both sets of pairwise FST 
analysis resembles the same pattern, but differs between pairwise comparison of 18 sampling 
sites compared to the 7 reporting groups, most likely due to the size of samples in each case: 
Bernard et al. conducted their analysis based on n = 65 from Hawaii (sampling site HW, 
number 18 in Fig. 5 and Table 7), whereas Holmes et al. (sampling site HAW, number 17 in 
Fig. 5 and Table 7) performed the analysis based in n = 21 only. Thus the difference in 
sampling size could be the reason for lack of significant differentiation, but when pooled 
together with the other 65 Hawaiian samples from Bernard et al., ntotal = 86, the statistical 
power is large enough to differentiate Hawaii from the remaining Indo-Pacific reporting 
groups (Table 8). Holmes et al. conducted their Hawaiian findings based on the 21 samples 
and in situ calibration of the genotypes from the Bernard group. Moreover, two loci (tgr_348 
and tgr_891) were unable to be reliably calibrated in silico between studies and were 
therefore excluded from the pairwise comparison analyses by Holmes et al. Eliminating 2 out 
of 9 loci represents a large fraction. This study indicates that actual calibration of genotypes 
scored in a different lab with all available loci provide a more robust approach for evaluating 
the genetic differentiation between reporting groups, from data derived from different studies.

Assignment 
Overall, the statistical power between sampling locations within reporting groups is 
diminishable, which follows in line of the FST values, that are either very small or zero. 
Contrary, the FST values between the two Ocean Basins is relatively large, implying that 
assignment of an individual into either the Atlantic or the Indopacific Ocean basin can be 
determined with relatively high statistical power. 
Two of the three mis-assigned individuals in the preliminary “raw” assignment displayed a 
similar pattern of being assigned 71% to a population in the Indo-pacific, and with a small 
likelihood score of 9% and 10% to be derived from the Atlantic Ocean Basin. As well as with 
the un-assignable individual USVI_14 in the 14 extra Bernard et al. samples, these 
individuals may represent a less informative genotype (admixed), which is not elsewhere 
present in the study, they could be migrants, or samples could have been mixed between 
hands or labs due to labelling failure etc.  It could be worthwhile to look at their genotypes in 
GeneMapper again, and see how the size standards have behaved. Alternatively, the raw 
assignment can be performed in a more detailed manner with different cutoff values as seen 
in (Henriksen, 2015), in order to develop a threshold value that eliminates mis-assignments. 
Mis-assigned can have different causes, as mentioned in the introduction chapter. In Fig. 12, 
the 18 sampling sites are compared to overall distribution of the tiger shark, in order to 
briefly evaluate the likely occurrence of “ghost populations” (i.e. unidentified populations not 
incorporated in the baseline). Sampling from sites in Western Africa, Mexico, Columbia and 
Peru or all the small islands in South East Asia between New Caledonia and Hawaii could be 
useful in future studies, to evaluate the possible number of ghost populations and their 
potential importance for the inferences assignments in this study.

 Another root of mis-assignment is ascertainment bias. This is however most likely when 
using SNPs, as microsatellites typically are polymorphic in all populations, whereas SNPs 
can be monomorphic. Nonetheless, potential problems can arise when attempting to estimate 
the genetic variation for population with markers developed for different populations from 
another geographical range (Li and Kimmel, 2013). The nine microsatellites loci used in this 
study was characterized by Bernard et al, using gDNA from 40 tiger sharks all captured 
within the waters of the Hawaiian archipelago (Bernard, Feldheim and Shivji, 2015). They 
amplified well in all contemporary and most archived samples, but as microsatellites 
characterized by tiger sharks captured elsewhere is available (the west coast of Brazil and the 
Fernando de Noronha archipelago (Mendes et al., 2016), or cross-amplification from bull 
sharks from Reunion Islands (Pirog et al., 2014)), it would be worthwhile to see how a 
combination of microsatellites from different origin will work when combined.  
The practicalities of conducting population genetic assignments on species of fish that does

In order to evaluate the genetic differentiations of the tiger sharks sampled, biological 
information should be incorporated. Holmes et al provides length information as the only 
factor. Coordinates for the specific sampling as well as date of sampling could be 
incorporated. If knowledge on how far apart in space and time tiger sharks from e.g. 
“Hawaii” were sampled in both the Holmes (2017) and the Bernard (2017) study, we would 
be better equipped to explain the genetic differentiation of sharks from these two sampling 
sites, and why they differ in relatedness to sharks sampled at sites in the Indian Ocean. 
Moreover, information on nursery grounds should be taken into consideration when choosing 
sampling site, in order to sample as few siblings as possible, as sampling of kin can influence 
the genetic analysis if undetected. 
Extracted DNA of the archived tiger sharks could be analyzed on the BioAnalyzer to check 
for foreign DNA, but as six out of nine Natural History Museums samples assigned back to 
the area (North West Atlantic) where they are most likely to have derived from. The 
remaining samples assigned exclusively within the Indian Ocean Basin. The reason for why 
they did not assign to the other ocean basin can be ascribed to migration, mislabeling, faulty 
genotyping or perhaps contamination of DNA from other near-related sharks in the museum 
due to handling over the years. It could be interesting to go back and perform a cluster 
analysis before the assignment tests, to perhaps get a clearer idea of actual population of 
origin for these old sharks. 
However significant, most of the values of genetic differentiation are very small. That could 
imply, that sampling of closely related siblings has been done. As the breeding grounds for 
tiger sharks are withint the sampling area, sampling of siblings could have taken place. As the 
genotypes was cleared for HWE, LD, and null alleles, none of the two studies mentions 
siblings. For future studies it could be interesting to statistically evaluate siblings, and then 
infer how excluding them, if at all present, would interefere with the FST values. 
Concluding remarks 
This study proofs that if lab procedure is conducted with the outmost care and precision, 
calibration of two independent studies can be successful. A calibrated database provides 
further insights into the genetic differentiation of tiger sharks on a global scale, due to the 
greater statistical power for identification of populations. It can also be used as a baseline for 
assignment of samples of unknown origin. However, due to the low genetic differentiation 
within the Indo-pacific and the West Atlantic, it can only as of now be certain to say from

APPENDIX 1 
PROTOCOL FOR SAMPLING OF ARCHIVED SHARK JAWS  
Hereby, the document aims to provide a clear procedure to extract powdered material from Shark 
jaws. The extracted material will be used for DNA extraction and it will contribute to the GenoJaws 
project. Thus, the samples obtained will be part of high-resolution retrospective genomic study for 
demographic inferences of shark populations worldwide.   
By the end of this procedure, the desired outcome would be a sufficient amount of powdered material 
collected in a plastic tube.  
Focus is to avoid cross-contamination between different individuals. As sampling may take place in 
the archives of a museum, and not in a clean lab, follow these precautions:  
1. If possible, use different drills bits for each individual. If using the same drill bit, clean it in 
fullstrength (100 g/L sodium hypochlorite) ‘HYPO 10’ bleach and sterilized water (DNA and 
RNA free water, e.g. Millipore water) between different samples to avoid cross-
contaminations. The same bleach can be used for all cleaning steps, but change water each

2. Clean Table with 1) bleach  2) alcohol  3) sterilized water between each drilling session 
of each individual. Use disposable paper towel for each cleaning session.  
  
3. Clean the area of sampling (on the jaw) with a cotton swab dipped in (1) bleach and (2) 
sterilized water, and discard the external layer of swarf (namely, powdered cartilage 
material).   
Particularly old and delicate jaws can be placed on a sheet of bubble wrap for protection during 
drilling (Figurea). This plastic material can however increase static electricity, making the sampled 
material stick to everything else than the collection tray.  
Figureb) shows suiTable spots for drilling. Two or three holes of about 3.5mm proved to be the 
optimal solution for obtaining sufficient cartilage material. Focus on drilling in places where the 
cartilage is as thick as possible, and from angles least visible from the front of the jaw. The spot 
identified as n.5 - the articulation between the palatoquadrate and the Meckel’s cartilage – proved to 
be the best solution providing enough high-quality cartilage bio-swarf and making as little visual 
damage as possible to the specimen [1]. For vertebrae, that can be further used for age determination 
[2], drill in a radial direction from the outer circumference towards the centre of each vertebra to 
minimize visual damage and allow for possible future use of the specimen [1].  
4. Place collecting tray beneath the jaw’s spot chosen for the drilling (Figurec). Screws are 
preferred to flakes as they are less prone to static electricity, and easier to handle for further 
analysis. Use a moderate speed to not damage the jaw and carefully collect the cartilaginous 
powder collected.   
  
5. Collect the bio-swarf in a small Zip-lock bag (one for each individual) or in a plastic tube of 
at least 2mL. If possible, collect enough material to fill up the 2mL tube. No limit on the 
maximum amount of material are imposed, feel free to fill up the zip-lock bag!  
If the sampled material is sticking to sampling equipment and tubes, place each tube in its separate 
plastic bags (Figured).  
6. Clearly label each bag/tubes with the collecting data - species, date, location and any capture 
details available. In addition to a photo of the jaw with cm scale, plus a close-up of teeth with 
scale. This is so we can infer animal length (and hence age) from jaw and teeth size.
