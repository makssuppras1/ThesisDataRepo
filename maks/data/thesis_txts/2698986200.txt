Abstract
A common choice of bearing for industrial applications such as turbomachinery and rotating comВӯ
pressors is the TiltingВӯPad Journal Bearing (TPJB) due to its excellent stability properties. TPJBвҖҷs
are however limited by the reduction of damping in the fluid film at high velocities. In order to
overcome this, the Active TiltingВӯPad Journal Bearing (ATPJB) has been developed. By adding the
possibility of highВӯpressure radial oil injection through servoВӯvalves which can be controlled via
a feedbackВӯloop control system, the classically purely mechanical TPJB becomes a mechatronic
device called ATPJB.
The objective of this project is to conduct an experimental evaluation of the dynamical behavior
of the ATPJB test rig located at the Technical University of Denmark, use the experimental reВӯ
sults to modify the previously developed dynamical model which is to be used for the calculation
of a modelВӯbased control system. The control system is to be implemented and experimentally
validated at high velocities.
Improvements made to the test rig in order to achieve high velocities have been documented and
described in this work. The mathematical modeling of the individual components, reduction methВӯ
ods, and the global system assembly is covered with an extensive overview. Parameters of the
model have been made frequency dependant in order to have a working model, resulting in good
agreement with experimental data over a wider operational range.
With the implemented Linear Quadratic Gaussian controller it is shown that ATPJB has extended
operational range compared to TPJB and shows reduction of vibrations over rotational speeds
spanning from 1000 RPM to 10,000 RPM. The ATPJBВӯtechnology, as it is implemented in this
project, does not improve frictional losses in the system.
It is argued that the added sensing and actuating systems inherited in the ATPJB technology make
the technology highly suitable for the ideas of Industry 4.0 and also allows for the implementation
of Early Fault Diagnosis which gives the industry an economical incitement to invest in ATPJBВӯ
technology.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Tribology is the science of interacting surfaces in relative motion, and the science of allowing them
to do so in a controlled and frictionВӯless manner, often implemented with bearings [1]. According
to Broman [2], a third of all the energy in the world used for technological purposes is lost due
to friction. This argument alone is good enough to motivate the importance of tribology and the
advances in the field. In rotating machinery the bearings are used to transfer the radial and axial
forces into a supporting structure while ensuring low friction and stability [3]. These bearings exВӯ
ist in various designs, each with properties better or worse suitable for different applications. For
cases with high loads and rotational speeds, fluid film lubricated bearings are often preferred due
to the low friction, stability properties, and longevity. Fluid film lubricated bearings for rotating
machinery with oil as the lubricating fluid exists in two main categories: Hydrodynamic bearings
and Hydrostatic bearings [1]. By combing the two, the hybrid and active lubrication regime can be
obtained [4]. Hydrodynamic bearings utilize the relative motion between two surfaces to generate
a pressurized oil film to eliminate friction and support the load [1]. Hydrostatic bearings use an
external pump to inject pressurized oil into the bearing, meaning that no motion is needed to genВӯ
erate a fluid film. Hybrid bearings are a combination of hydrostatic and hydrodynamic bearings
[4]. The most common version of hydrodynamic bearings is the journal bearing which consists of
a rotor or a journal, rotating inside of a bearing with a diameter slightly larger than the one of the
rotor and the fluid film exists in the small gap between the two [1].
Fluid film bearings can however present unstable behavior due to destabilizing crossВӯcoupling
forces caused by the fluid film [3]. To overcome these problems more advanced versions of journal
bearings can be used, one of which is the tiltingВӯpad journal bearing (TPJB) [5]. The TPJB is built
with separate bearing pads with the possibility to tilt, or pivot, to adjust their equilibrium position
under the current load case[5]. This decouples the forces acting on the journal, resulting in reduced
crossВӯcoupling of the stiffness [5]. Even though TPJBвҖҷs solves the issue of destabilization due to
the crossВӯcoupled forces, it does not solve another issue inherited in fluid film lubricated bearings,
namely the fact that they are all very dependant on the damping of the fluid film which decreases
with increased angular velocities and increased oil temperature [5]. It is mainly this problem that
is sought to be solved with the work presented in this thesis.
The idea of Active TiltingВӯPad Journal Bearings (ATPJB) was presented during the first half of
the вҖҷ90s by Santos [6] with the hypothesis that the great improvements in the fields of computers
and electronics could open up the possibility for feedbackВӯbased control via a TPJB. This could
reshape the view of a bearing as a purely mechanical machine element into a mechatronic one [5].
Similar ideas that have been realized and are industry standards today are electronic valve timing,
fuel injection, and ignition in combustion engines, all of which used to be purely mechanical [3].
Active magnetic bearings are another implemented idea of controllable components within the
field of tribology [3].
The main idea of ATPJBвҖҷs is that the damping of the oil film can be altered in a controlled manner
by injecting highly pressurized oil into the bearing and this modifying the bearing clearance [5].
This could reduce vibrations, reduce friction, and optimizing the load capacity which results in less
vibration and frictionВӯinduced fatigue, the ability to match your component to the current running
conditions, and improve the ability to monitor the operation of the machine. This project focus
on an ATPJB with four tiltingВӯpads, injection of lowВӯpressure oil in between the pads to feed the
hydrodynamic lubrication, and highВӯpressure injection directly into the radial clearance through
the center of the pads. The highВӯpressure injection is controlled by two servo valves.

1.1.1
Industry 4.0 and Early Fault Diagnosis
The term Industry 4.0 was established to describe a planned fourth industrial revolution, introducВӯ
ing an era of efficient and modular manufacturing systems where products can control their own
manufacturing process. The main concepts of Industry 4.0 relevant to this project, are the soВӯcalled
вҖқSmart FactoryвҖқ and вҖқCyberВӯPhysical systemsвҖқ. The smart factory concept relates to equipping
manufacturing processes with sensing, acting, and thinking systems in order to create autonomous
factories. The concept of cyberВӯphysical systems describes a merger between the digital and the
physical level. This can be interpreted as well as for production, as for products. The idea is that
digitization gets fundamentally integrated into the physical system, making it impossible to difВӯ
ferentiate a purely digital or physical representation of the system anymore. Two of the aspects
included in the latter concept are the ones of predictive maintenance and early fault diagnosis (EFD)
[7].
EFD in particular is a subject that recently has caught a lot of attention from both the academic
world and the industry. EFD is the idea of using sensing equipment and developed strategies for
detecting problems with systems before the point of critical failure is reached. The reasons for
the industry to wanting to pursue EFD are many, but to avoid catastrophic failures and reduce
financial losses are the main ones. The two main EFDВӯtechniques for rotating machinery are fault
frequencyВӯbased diagnosis methods (FFD) and artificial intelligence methods (AI). It has been
shown that severe faults in rotating machinery have obvious impulses in the frequency spectrum
related to the angular velocity. This knowledge is used in FFD by acquiring data, denoising it, and
via demodulation analysis detecting faults. The AI approach to EFD is to via machine learning,
teach a computer how data during normal operation looks and how to detect anomalies in the
operational data. The two methods have the need for data acquisition of some sort in common [8].
1.2
Previous Work
TPJBвҖҷs with active lubrication have been investigated for over two decades with several different
contributors to the understanding and improvement of the technology. The Modified Reynolds
Equation (MRE) was introduced by Santos and Russo [9] as a method to calculate the oil film
pressure with the inclusion of radial oil injection. The dynamics of the servo valves for highВӯ
pressure oil injection, reduce to a secondВӯorder differential equation, were also included but the
dynamics of the piping between the servo valves and the injectors were considered negligible. At
this point, the thermal effects on the pads and the pad flexibility were also neglected. However,
the validity of the idea to modify the oil film pressure was demonstrated theoretically.
The work has been continued both theoretically and experimentally at the Technical University
of Denmark (DTU) by numerous contributors, both master, and PhD students. Most of the work
has been based on test rigs built at DTU, including the one used for this project. The geometry
of the ATPJB concerning the bearing damping was made by Kristiansen [10], showing that the
optimal pad orifice configuration was obtained using four injection orifices, each with a diameter
of 4 mm, symmetrically around the pivot line and near the axial edges. He also showed that the
damping increases with the injection pressure. MГёnsted [11] included the pivot supports radial
and transverse degrees of freedom based on Hertz contact theory, showing that the pivot support
stiffness has a significant effect on the ATPJB dynamics. By investigating the optimization of
the dynamic properties of the ATPJB, Kurt [12] concluded that for a 4Вӯpad LoadВӯBetweenВӯPad
(LBP) configuration, oilВӯinjected only through the two bottom pads with the two upper pads opВӯ
erating only hydrodynamically would provide the optimized dynamics. Kurt also concluded, in
agreement with previous work, that high injection pressures provide the best suppression of viВӯ
brations. He also concluded that the correct injection pressure could have a great effect on the
system damping and mode shapes. In [4], Santos and Salazar displayed experimental proof that
the lateral dynamic response improves with the inclusion of oil injection, even without a feedback

3
==PAGE:17==
control system, in comparison with the passive TPJB. They also concluded, in agreement with
the work done by Kurt, that the best performance is obtained with the oil injection from the botВӯ
tom pads. Further on, Salazar and Santos designed a linearВӯquadratic Gaussian (LQG) controller
based on the dynamic model of the ATPJB for feedbackВӯcontrolled lubrication [13]. The LQG
controller was tested experimentally and compared with active operation with a PIDВӯcontroller,
TPJB with passive lubrication, and TPJB with hybrid lubrication. The actively controlled lubricaВӯ
tion showed promising signs of reducing vibrations, however, the LQG controller was generating
a new resonance at lower frequencies. This unwanted behavior was suspected to be due to the
unmodelled dynamics of the foundation. Hence work was to be done to include the foundation
in the dynamic model to further investigate the possibilities of a functioning LQG controller. The
first attempt to capture the test rig dynamics was made already in 2016 by Simonsen [14] by using
operational modal analysis methods instead of experimental modal analysis to avoid taking the
system out of service. The results from this method were however to inaccurate to be considered
useful and further investigation was needed to include the foundation dynamics. The first attempt
to include the test rig foundation dynamics into the mathematical model was made by Rasmussen
[15] by using a finite element approach with plate elements. With this model, the pedestals and
stiffeners were included using beam elements. This effort did not manage to provide satisfactory
results when compared with experimental data. To improve the foundation model, Gani replaced
the plate elements with solid 3D elements [16] and validated the model with experimental data.
The dynamic model showed significant improvement but to make the model fit the experimental
data, it was needed to set the mechanical properties of the stiffeners and pedestals to nonВӯphysical
values. Therefore it was suggested that further investigation of the foundation dynamics should
be conducted to ensure optimal performance from a modelВӯbased controller. Since the foundation
is very large the output matrices were very large, thus the CraigВӯBampton reduction method was
used to reduce computational time. Svensson conducted an investigation into the dissipation of
energy into heating of the oilВӯfilm [17]. The goal of SvenssonвҖҷs work was to conclude if the preВӯ
viously made assumptions of isothermal and isoviscous conditions could be justified. This was
made by solving an energy equation alongside the MRE and he concluded that the thermal effects
were significant to the system dynamics, especially at higher rotational speeds. The investigation
of the thermal effects was not complete since the inclusion of the energy equation and the radial oil
injection caused numerical instability and problems with convergence. Therefore it was concluded
that further investigation was to be made regarding the thermal influence on the system dynamics.
In addition to the previously mentioned work made by Svensson, he also added an approach to
determine the bearing dynamic coefficient. Since the previously used Alliere Perturbation Method
which is base on numerical differentiation struggled when including the thermal effects, PerturbaВӯ
tion Theory which is based on analytic expressions were implemented into the model. A further
investigation into the foundation dynamics and modelВӯbased control was made by Jensen [3]. The
dimensions and positions of the bearing pedestals were modified to more accurately represent the
physical system. The inclusion of the front sensor pedestal in the model was also made to comВӯ
pensate for sensor movement due to foundation movement. The foundation model showed good
agreement with the experimental data and the model as a whole showed satisfactory agreement
with the experimental data at lower rotational velocities. At higher velocities, the model and the
experimental data diverged which was assumed to be due to the thermal effects not yet being inВӯ
cluded in the model. Jensen managed to design an LQG controller which showed a great reduction
of vibrations for angular velocities of 1000Вӯ2000 RPM. He concluded that the controller requires
more work since it seems to amplify the vibrations at frequencies around 25 Hz.
The latest contribution was made by Гҳsterby Rasmussen [18] by including the thermal effects to
the dynamic model. This was made by modifying the MRE to include film thickness gradients
in z direction. This is because, with the thermal expansion of the pads, the fluid film thickness
also changes and this effect must be taken into consideration. His work showed that the highest

2.1
Fluid Film Lubricated Bearings
On the topic of fluid film lubricated bearings, two classical subВӯtypes are the most commonly
used, hydrostatic and hydrodynamic fluid film bearings. Both bearing types utilize the decrease of
friction that comes with a thin film of lubrication fluid between the surfaces of interest.
The third bearing type discussed in this section is a combination of these two fluidВӯfilm lubricated
bearings which is called a hybrid bearing.
2.1.1
Hydrostatic Lubrication
Hydrostatic bearings produce the lubricating fluid film by having oil being pumped into the bearing
by an external pump. The hydrostatic bearings have some fundamental advantages and disadvanВӯ
tages that are listed below [1].
вҖў Extremely low friction.
вҖў Great load carrying capacity at low speeds.
вҖў Good positional accuracy for high speed, light load applications
вҖў External pumping system needed, which requires physical room, extra operational energy
and adding complexity to the system.
Hence, hydrostatic bearings are often used in applications where the loads are very high and the
rotational speed is low.
2.1.2
Hydrodynamic Lubrication
Hydrodynamic bearings utilize the rotational motion together with the viscosity of the oil, the noВӯ
slip condition present with fluids in contact with solids, and optimized geometry of the bearing
surface.
The theoretical characteristics of such a bearing are often described by a simplified NavierВӯStokes
equation called the standard reduced form of the Reynolds equation [1], shown in equation 2.1.

2.1.3
Hybrid Lubrication
To overcome some of the disadvantages and to utilize the advantages from the two classical types
of fluidВӯfilm lubricated bearings one can combine the two. This combination allows for great loadВӯ
carrying capacity at both low and high speeds, reduced wear at the start and stop, and very low
friction [19].
Hydrostatic, Hydrodynamic, and Hybrid fluidВӯfilm lubrication all have one issue in common. At
very high speeds they tend to lose stability due to the lack of damping [20]. This causes vibrations
that are unwanted for several different reasons.
2.1.4

Journal bearings are a type of bearing that is commonly used to handle the radial load in heavy
machines such as rotation compressors and gas turbines. A principle sketch of a hydrodynamic
journal bearing is shown in figure 2.1 and a nomenclature belonging to the principle sketch is

2.2
Active TiltingВӯPad Journal Bearing
With the foundations of fluidВӯfilm lubrication and TPJB, the concept of Active TiltingВӯPad JourВӯ
nal Bearings can be explored. ATPJBвҖҷs are based on a synergistic relationship between tribolВӯ
ogy, classical mechanics, electronics, and control systems. The aim with ATPJBвҖҷs is to overcome
some of the classical problems associated with fluidВӯfilm lubricated bearings by combining them
with sensing, signal processing techniques, control theory and thereby lending them вҖқsmartnessвҖқ,
вҖқmultiВӯfunctionalityвҖқ and вҖқhigher reliabilityвҖқ [20].

2.6.1
Experimental testing
To get a good understanding of the test rig behavior at different velocities it was concluded that
a good resolution would be to conduct an experimental investigation at every 100 RPM for pasВӯ
sive lubrication, and every 1000 RPM for hybrid lubrication to create waterfall diagrams of the
frequency response with angular velocity, frequency, and receptance represented in the three difВӯ
ferent axis directions. All the plots used for the waterfall diagram were obtained using excitation
with a downВӯsweeping chirp function. The experiments with passive lubrication were conducted
over 15 minutes for 1000Вӯ4100 RPM, 20 minutes for 4200Вӯ4900 RPM, 25 minutes for 5000Вӯ5400
RPM, and 30 minutes for all velocities above 5500 RPM. For hybrid lubrication, the experiments
were conducted over 15 minutes for 1000Вӯ5000 RPM, 20 minutes for 6000Вӯ8000 RPM, and 30
minutes for 9000Вӯ10,000 RPM. The increase in measurement time was due to the expectation that
longer measurements allowed for more averaging in the signal processing, hence better results.
A runВӯout compensation was made at an angular velocity of interest before every measurement
to eliminate measurement noise due to mechanical runВӯout. Noticeable for the measurements at
passive lubrication is that for velocities over 4000 RPM, the coherence becomes very bad and the
measurement very noisy. This is expected to appear due to high sensitivity to excitation, making the
response behave with a nonВӯlinear character, and mechanical noise traveling to the measurement
equipment due to this nonВӯlinear behavior. The coherence gets worse at higher angular velocities
for the hybrid lubrication as well but not as significant as for passive lubrication.
The passive lubrication was measured up to 8200 RPM, after which the system got unstable and got
into a state of selfВӯexcitation. The hybrid measurements could be made up to 10,000 RPM without
any issues. However, due to the limitations of components in the test rig, no measurements at
higher velocities were conducted.

At around 60 Hz there is a resonance located at approximately the same position throughout the

the rotor and bearing dynamics. This will be referred to as the RBВӯresonance.
The frequencyВӯlocation of the foundation resonance is fairly constant over the full spectrum of

velocities the response of this mode varies a lot. This could be due to the bad coherence of the
measurements, which is explained in chapter 2.6.1.
The RBВӯresonance acts more dynamically over the velocity spectrum which is to be expected since
the dynamic behavior of this section is influenced by the gyroscopic effect of the rotor and the
damping and stiffness in the bearing, all of which are highly dependent on the angular velocity.
At around 3000 RPM this mode splits up into two distinct resonances. It is suspected that these
are the backward and forwards whirl of the rotor, however, one coincides with the second bending
mode of the foundation which could explain the very amplified response at around 70 Hz for
3500Вӯ4500 RPM. Just as for the foundation resonance, at higher velocities, the poor quality of the
measurements makes it hard to properly analyze the behavior of the RBВӯresonance.

Experimental investigation of system linearity
To detect if the system is showing any signs of nonВӯlinearity at different angular velocities, it was
chosen to make a comparison between the frequency response with excitation in both upsweep

to the direction of the chirp function, it can be assumed to have nonВӯlinear characteristics in the
region where the deviation occurs. This knowledge about the system is of importance both in the
case of choosing controller design and when evaluating the quality of the controller. In this project,
only linear control is considered, but for future work where more advanced controllers could be
implemented the knowledge about the behavior of the system is of great importance.
As can be seen in figure 2.21, the system is showing great linearity between 1000Вӯ4000 RPM for
the horizontal excitation. The FRF is almost identical with only small deviations for the different
excitation functions. For angular velocities greater than this the FRF is starting to show nonВӯ
linearity in the frequency range where the dynamics of the rotor and the bearingВӯdynamics are the
dominating ones.

2.7
Torque analysis via current sensing
A big problem in industrial applications with heavy machinery is the one related to starting torque
and starting currents. When a heavy machine needs to start from a standstill, the torque needed
to overcome the static friction in the system and accelerate it to normal operational speed can be
Active Control of HighВӯSpeed Flexible Rotors on Controllable TiltingВӯPad Journal Bearings
31
==PAGE:45==
much greater than the torque needed to keep it at operational speed. From a pure efficiency point
of view, this is problematic since friction losses always are to be kept at a minimum. The industrial
impact of this problem is often expressed as high starting currents for the electric motors driving
the system. Some of the industrial problems related to starting currents are listed below [?].
вҖў Interference with the power supply in the form of voltage drops, which can cause problems
in voltageВӯsensitive systems connected to the same power grid.
вҖў Starting current will add to the heating of the motor and can damage the motor itself, as well
as the connecting cables.
вҖў The starting time increases, which can affect not only the stability of the electric motor but
also the stability of the whole power grid.
Problems regarding starting torque are not limited to the ones regarding electrical motors, these
problems are relevant in almost every application involving heavy machinery. However, since the
experimental setup used in this report is driven by an electrical motor, it was chosen to limit the
problem description to only electrical motors.
Other than the starting torque, the operational torque is also of importance. The torque input needed
during normal operation is related to the friction within the system if the operation is kept at a
constant velocity, or the friction within the system and the mass/inertia for an accelerating system.

3.1
Rotor and Rigid Discs Model
The mathematical modeling of the rotorВӯbearingВӯfoundation model and the thermal effects have
been developed over several years with several contributors. The latest major addition was the
inclusion of the thermal effects on the tilting pads by Rasmussen [18]. To have an accurate model
of the system is of great importance when designing a controller with a Kalman filter since such
controllers are based on the estimation of the system behavior.
In the work by Jensen [3] the foundation was included in the model. With this inclusion, the
modeled FRF and the experimental FRF were consistent with each other at low angular velocities.
At higher angular velocities the experimental and the modeled results started to deviate. It was
assumed by Jensen that the deviations could be explained by the thermal effects on the ATPJB
since higher velocity also comes with higher energy dissipation in the oil. This hypothesis was
analyzed and tested by Rasmussen by including the thermal effects, concluding that the thermal
effects are most likely not the cause of the great deviations. Rasmussen however showed that small
changes in the properties of the flexible rotor and the rigid discs could have a great influence on
the resulting FRF due to the gyroscopic effects.
Due to these conclusions, work has been put in to further analyze the rotor and disc properties to
better match the model with the experimental data.
3.2
Flexible Rotor
The rotor shaft, or the rotor as it is most commonly referred to in this report, is a component deВӯ
signed to transfer torque to various components. It is a vital component in most rotating machines
and often contributes massively to the machinery dynamics. Because of this, it is of great imporВӯ
tance to properly model the rotor.
The rotor used in the test rig was designed by Гҳstergaard [24] and is shown in figure 3.1. It is
made of steel, more accurately St 60Вӯ2, which is supposed to have a density of ПҒ вүҲ7800 kg/m3
and elasticity modulus E вүҲ200 GPa. The total length of the rotor is 1150 mm with the excitation
bearing together with the mounting of the rigid discs and the ATPJB on one end, and the support
bearing on the other. The rotor is composed of sections with different diameters, with the largest
diameter of 99.6 mm inside the ATPJB.
Figure 3.1: CADВӯrendering of the rotor shaft in the test rig made in Autodesk Inventor.
For the modeling of the rotor, small characteristics like keyseats, threads, drilled holes at the ends
and the thin flanges near the center were neglected. Neglecting these creates the assumption that

the rotor is completely axisymmetric which simplifies the modeling significantly. The main work
of the rotor model was presented by Santos and Salazar [4] and it is a finite element model based on
EulerВӯBernoulli beam theory with the inclusion of gyroscopic effects. The rotor is discretized into
26 elements, as shown in figure 3.2. The choosing of the locations of the nodes is such that each
element has got a uniform thickness. Additional nodes are placed at the locations of the proximity
sensors which is the reason for the irregularity in element length for some sections.

The theory of the rotor model is based on [30], where a similar rotor is described. Important
assumptions for the model are that the rotor is considered flexible with small deflections and itвҖҷs
considered long and slender. The second assumption allows for shear stresses to be neglected and
for the EulerВӯBernoulli beam theory to be considered suiting. Two additional assumptions are that
torsional and axial deflection is considered negligible. These deflections should not be affecting
the dynamics of the system noticeably.

degrees of freedom (DOF) which are divided into two translational DOF noted V and W, and

26 + 1 = 27 nodes and 4 В· nr = 108 DOF. The length of each element is denoted Le and the
diameter of the rotor for each element is denoted de. The axial coordinates within each element

YoungвҖҷs modulus, E, and the density, ПҒ are considered constant for each element. To describe the
deflections V , W, ОІ, and О“ along the axial coordinates in each element, the nodal deflections qi are
calculated. As shown in figure 3.3, the minimum amount of nodal coordinates for describing the

3.4
ATPJB Model
The ATPJB is the heart of the system and the main focus of this project. The concept is to turn
the conventional and pure mechanical TiltingВӯPad Journal Bearing into a mechatronic machine
element with the ability to actively keep the performance of a machine as reliable and efficient as
possible. The ATPJB can be used in many different ways. It can be utilized as a hydrodynamic or
вҖқpassiveвҖқ bearing where the motion of the rotor pressurizes the fluid film and only lowВӯpressure
hydraulics are needed to supply the bearing with oil to compensate for the side leakage in the
bearing. It can also be used as a hybrid bearing where a constant highВӯpressure oil injection adds
hydrostatic characteristics to the bearing, making it more reliable at lower velocities and reduces
friction wear at the start and stop. However, the innovative and very interesting part of the ATPJB
is the active component of the bearing. By utilizing modelВӯbased feedback control, the bearing can
optimize the running conditions of the machine, extend the operational range, and reduce friction
and vibrations. The industrial applications of this technology are many since vibrationВӯinduced
fatigue is a costly and sometimes dangerous problem in heavy machinery. The additional sensing
systems used with this type of bearing could also have the potential of being used as measurements
of machine performance and have the potential to detect defects before they reach a critical point
which in its turn could reduce downtime and optimize service intervals in the industry. Another big
advantage of this bearing is that if the active system were to fail, and if the highВӯpressure hydraulics
Active Control of HighВӯSpeed Flexible Rotors on Controllable TiltingВӯPad Journal Bearings
59
==PAGE:73==
were to fail, the bearing still has all of the mechanical properties intact which means that system
failure is not equal to machine failure. A rendered 3D image of the bearing used for this project
can be seen in figure 3.13 with the two servoВӯvalves mounted on top.
Figure 3.13: Rendered image of the ATPJB used in the testВӯrig at DTU.
The initial idea o the ATPJB with radial oil injections was proposed by Santos in 1994 [6]. He
then continued to develop a model of the ATPJB and to do so he needed to modify the Reynolds
equation 2.1 stated in chapter 2.1.2 to include the injection term. This version of the equation is
called the Modified Reynolds Equation (MRE). The model Santos created is the same one used in
this project, but it has seen numerous improvements over the years by many contributors at DTU,
the latest of which was the inclusion of thermal effect done by Гҳsterby Rasmussen [18].
3.4.1
Dynamics of ATPJB
The dynamics of the ATPJB are more complicated to obtain than for the other components in this
project. Mainly because there are so many coupled domains in fluid mechanics, solid mechanics,
and thermodynamics. Another obstacle of the task to obtain the dynamics of the ATPJB is that
the coefficients depend on the journal equilibrium position which initially is unknown. To find
the equilibrium position of the bearing an iterative method is used, for which a flowchart can be
seen in figure 3.14. A short description of the method is that first the dynamic model of the pads is
calculated using a FEM approach. Then an initial guess of the parameters needed to calculate the
fluid film thickness is made, this initial guess is most likely wrong but the fluid film thickness is
calculated. With this guess, the MRE is assembled and solved using finite difference approximation
to obtain the pressure field for the pads. Since pressure times area equals force, the pressure field
is integrated over the pad areas and a check is made if the equilibrium position is found. If not, a
new and improved guess is made for the equilibrium positions and the process repeats itself until
Active Control of HighВӯSpeed Flexible Rotors on Controllable TiltingВӯPad Journal Bearings
60
==PAGE:74==
the equilibrium position is found. When the equilibrium position is found, itвҖҷs time to include the
thermal effects. This is done by solving the finite difference approximation of the energy equation
and coupling it to the FEM model of the pads to obtain the temperature field in all three Cartesian
coordinates. With this, the change in the viscosity field is calculated and a check is made if an
equilibrium is obtained. If it is not, a new calculation of the temperature field based on the new
viscosity field is made until equilibrium is found. With this, an equivalent thermal load on the
pads can be calculated and the pad FEM model can be calculated again after which a new film
thickness can be obtained by adding the thermal expansion. Now the last check is made if an
equilibrium is obtained. If it is not, new coefficients for the MRE are calculated and the process

3.8.2
After Thermal inclusion and modifications
The focus of the tuning of the model was put into trying to make вҖқsmartвҖқ changes according to the
disagreements between the model and the experimental data in figure 3.27. The biggest problem
with the model was that it did not show as much gyroscopic effects as the physical system. To cope
with this issue, work was put into the parts where small changes could have large effects on the
gyroscopic effect. The first part of the process was to doubleВӯcheck all the implemented models
to make sure that there were no coding errors or dissimilarities with the theory presented in this
work. After ensuring that the implementation of the model is correct, small changes were made to
the physical properties of the system. The bearing model was left untouched, but the program for
inclusion of thermal effects by Гҳsterby Rasmussen [18] got implemented.
The only change made to the foundation model was by lowering YoungвҖҷs modulus for the stiffeners
from of the bearing and pedestals 190 GPa to 175 GPa to allow the discs to move more in the
model and hence, have more gyroscopic influence on the dynamics.
One of the issues faced in the tuning of the model was the inability to make it fit over the whole
spectrum of angular velocities. To overcome this, a model in which parameters for the rotorВӯdisc
model dynamically changed with the angular velocity was created. This allowed for getting a better
match over the full spectrum, which allowed for better accuracy when calculating the modelВӯbased
controller. In table 3.9 the varying parameters for the model can be seen. The parameters have
been set for every 500 RPM by using a trial and error method. For all velocities in between, the
parameters get set by interpolation. In figure 3.28 the results of the tuned model in comparison
to the experimental results can be seen. The results seem to resemble the system fairly well over
the full spectrum, except for 7000 RPM. The quality of the model fitting is very dependant on
the quality of the experimental measurements, and with better quality of the measurements, an
optimization function could be utilized to find the best fit.

Design of Controller
This chapter covers the design of the control system that is to be implemented by using the ATPJB
servoВӯvalves as actuators and the two front pedestal proximity sensors as feedback. The chapter
describes the modal reduction of the system, describing the board work of limiting the model dyВӯ
namics to the dynamics of interest. The system is then separated into real and complex states to
implement the controller with the dSpace module used in the test rig. The continuousВӯtime sysВӯ

get evaluated and tuned before a Kalman filter gets designed and implemented. Finally, the conВӯ
troller gets optimized by implementing a linear quadratic Gaussian regulator. The controller gets
evaluated by simulation before implementation and experimental validation is to be made. The
theoretical foundation of this chapter is mainly based on the theory in [34] and the work made by
Jensen [3].

variance, or some other criterion defined as optimal. The Kalman filter is obtained by solving the

6
Discussion
6.1
Test Rig Improvements
One of the main aspects of this project that is not highlighted as a result was the work done with the
test to even be able to achieve the aim of the project. The test rig in the state of which it was when
this project started was nowhere close to being able to run at 10,000 RPM. Improvements were
made to allow for easier maintenance and reconstruction in future projects, but some alterations
were made due to pure necessity. Throughout the project, the hydraulics system has been fully
rebuilt due to a hose breaking. The encoder, the flexible coupling, the two front sensors, ball
bearings, and some drive belts broke during the project and needed replacement. The original
drive motor and frequency converter lacked the power needed to reach 10,000 RPM and needed
to be replaced. All of these small things added up to a lot of standstills during the project and stole
time that could have been put into other things, but they were all necessary to allow the test rig to
operate in such high velocities.
6.2
Active TiltingВӯPad Journal Bearings
The frequency response of the test rig was measured for the full range of three different lubrication
types with the passive lubrication showing instabilities after 8200 RPM while hybrid and active
lubrication could operate at 10,000 RPM without any signs of instability. This shows that hybrid
and active lubrication allows for the test rig to extend its operational range. The poor quality of
the measurements at high velocities weakens the impact of any conclusions, however, the results
indicate great improvement of the operational conditions for the test rig. Other than the results
shown, it can be noted that the reduction of vibrations was noticeable when being in the same
room as the test rig, which further induces confidence in the viability of the technology.
There is still something missing in the mathematical model to properly describe the system dynamВӯ
ics. The influence of the thermal expansion of the rotor could be greater than initially assumed and
would be a fairly simple extension to the model. The investigation on nonВӯlinear behavior showed
some nonВӯlinearity while crossing the critical speeds, which also could be further investigated.
To further improve the model, the quality of the experiments at high velocity would need to be
improved. Suggestions of experimental improvement would be to detach the sensors from the
foundation, to add weight to the excitation to minimize the risk of it vibrating with the rotor, and
to put time into carefully crossing the sensitive modes of the system with the excitation. Another
improvement that could be made would be a proper oilВӯcooling system to be able to control the
inlet temperature of the bearing. Getting highВӯquality measurements in a system with such intense
levels of vibrations is a very difficult and timeВӯdemanding task. In this project, the time needed to
improve the quality of the measurements further was not sufficient.
The quality of the implemented controller is, as earlier stated, dependant on the quality of the
model which implies that an improved model would yield better results with the controller as it is
designed today. This does however not exclude the possibility of improving the controller itself.
The amplification at lower frequencies for low speeds should be investigated, and the performance
of the controller in the range of 5000Вӯ7000 RPM could be improved. With the current control
design, a new controller needs to be calculated for each angular velocity which is not optimal in
terms of industrial applications. This calls for an investigation of the design and implementation
of a more advanced, adaptive control system.
The results are clearly in favor of the viability of ATPJB at higher velocities. It allows for a wider
range of operational velocities and indicates a reduction of vibration over the full operational range.

==PAGE:121==
With better quality of the experimental measurements a better model can be created, from which a
better controller could be calculated and even better results could be obtained.
6.3
Influence on frictional losses
The results from the current analysis show that passive lubrication is more efficient in terms of
frictional losses. This makes sense since the main reason for energy loss in a fluid film bearing is
overcoming the viscous shearing of the oil, and with hybrid/active lubrication the film thickness
is thicker by design, which makes for larger shear forces. This is a tradeВӯoff between efficiency
and low vibration operation, one is not always better than the other. In the case of start currents,
the hybrid and active lubrication are far superior. In some industrial applications, the classic TPJB
might not even be a valid option only because the power needed to start the machine is too great.
Some clear advantages of ATPJB regarding energy efficiency can be mentioned. The possibility
to choose between passive, hybrid and active lubrication will allow for the possibility to apply the
correct lubrication type for every given situation instead of being limited to one single lubrication
type. Since the control system is modelВӯbased, the current of the drive motor could be included in
the model and used as a parameter for optimization, which could create a good tradeВӯoff between
power loss and allowed vibrations.
6.4
Industrial applications
As this project has shown great indications towards the viability of ATPJB at high velocities it is
also valid to discuss the possibilities of industrial applications for the technology. Some drawВӯ
backs with the implementation of the technology in comparison to classical hydrodynamic and
hydrostatic bearings are the more complicated design of the bearing itself, the cost of additional
sensing systems, servoВӯvalves, extended hydraulic systems, and the development cost of a more
complex system. With more complexity and more components, the risk of failure also increases.
This drawback limits the possibilities of implementation, at least right now, to heavier industries
where the investment can be accounted for by the improvements the technology provides.
However, some of the mentioned drawbacks can also be listed as benefits by revisiting the ideas
of Industry 4.0 and Early Fault Diagnosis, explained in chapter 1.1.1. By adding sensing and
actuating systems, ATPJB allows for smart and independent adaptation to system operation. For
example, ATPJB allows for acting as a classical TPJB under normal conditions and only uses
the active lubrication when it is necessary. This possibility of smart operation fits well into the
very definition of Industry 4.0. The addition of sensing and actuating systems also allows for the
implementation of EFD. By detecting failures in the early stages, before they become critical, the
initial investment of implementing the technology could be accounted for by avoiding unplanned
standstills and emergency repairs.

7
Conclusion
The overall goal of this project was to experimentally implement, analyze and validate the viaВӯ
bility of ATPJBВӯtechnology in highВӯspeed applications, in terms of vibration control and system
efficiency. The work included reconstruction of the test rig to achieve high enough velocities.
Current sensors were added to enable the possibility to analyze the effect of different lubrication
types on frictional losses in the system, and thermocouples were added to enable the evaluation of
the thermal properties of the system.
Experimental investigations on the dynamics of the test rigвҖҷs frequency response in the horizontal
direction, both for passive and hybrid lubrication were made. Experiments were conducted with
excitation through an electromagnetic shaker and a uniВӯdirectional chirp function between 20Вӯ250
Hz with a period of 10 seconds.
With passive lubrication, the quality of the experiments drops significantly at velocities greater than
5000 RPM which makes the analysis of the experiments very difficult, and at velocities beyond
8200 RPM, the system becomes unstable. The drop in quality is suspected to be related to nonlinear
behavior and mechanical disturbance in the data acquisition equipment. With hybrid lubrication,
the overall measurement quality is better and the operational range is extended until at least 10,000
RPM. Due to mechanical limitations in test rig components, no tests beyond 10,000 RPM were
conducted. With both passive and hybrid lubrication, an amplified response at around 60 Hz is
present. This is assumed to be related to the second torsional mode of the foundation. At low
velocities, two modes seem to coincide at around 100 Hz, but they separate into separate modes at
higher velocities. These are assumed to be the backward and forwards whirl of the rotor, with the
backward whirl coinciding with the foundation second bending mode and therefore becomes very
amplified.
It is shown that the maximum oil temperature of the bearing with passive lubrication is approxВӯ
imately 54в—ҰC but with hybrid lubrication, with 100 bar supply pressure the oil temperature can
reach 78в—ҰC. It is therefore concluded that a proper cooling system should be fitted to the test rig to
control the oil temperature.
The reduced dynamical model for the rotorВӯbearingВӯfoundation system used in this project has
been developed in stages by contributors at the Technical University of Denmark as described
in the work. Small modifications in the model parameters have been made to fit the simulated
and experimental results. The most significant alteration to the model was the implementation
of angular velocityВӯdependent parameters for the rotorВӯdisc model. The dynamical model fits the
experimental results fairly well over the full range of the investigated velocities, but the poor quality
of the experimental data at high velocities makes the model fitting uncertain.
The dynamical model is then reduced with the use of modal reduction to 14 modes and discretized
to be fitting for the design and implementation of a realВӯtime controller. Using the controller design
used in previous projects, only changing the controller weight matrix, LQGВӯcontrollers could be
calculated for each angular velocity. By tuning the controller wights with a trial and error method it
was shown that it is possible to reduce vibrations with the use of ATPJBВӯtechnology for highВӯspeed
flexible rotor systems. The controller shows some problems related to amplified vibrations for
lowВӯfrequency excitation at angular velocities below 4000 RPM, but it shows great improvement
between 8000Вӯ10,000 RPM.
In terms of friction loss, the hybrid and active lubrication display almost identical performance, but
the passive lubrication shows less friction loss than the other two at velocities greater than 3000
RPM. This is discussed to be a drawbackВӯbenefit situation where stability is gained with the cost
Active Control of HighВӯSpeed Flexible Rotors on Controllable TiltingВӯPad Journal Bearings
109
==PAGE:123==
of efficiency. It is also argued that by including the power consumption in the model, it is possible
to optimize a controller with both power consumption and vibrations as inputs.
It is also argued that the technology of ATPJB has a place in the ideas of Industry 4.0 with builtВӯin
opportunities to also implement Early Fault Diagnosis strategies to avoid catastrophic failures and
to optimize the maintenance of machinery.
7.1
Future Work
The test rig needs maintenance with new ball bearings all around and minor oilВӯleakage issues need
to be dealt with. It would also be a good idea to inspect the bearing pads and evaluate the state of the
ATPJB. More work needs to be put into experimentally evaluating the dynamics of the frequency
response with passive lubrication to better fit the model. This could be done by experimenting
with excitation methods, sensor placements, etc.
It is believed that the thermal expansion of the rotor inside the bearing could be significant to the
system dynamics at higher velocities and the inclusion of this in the dynamic model should be
investigated. A proper cooling system for the oil could have reduced this effect and increase the
accuracy of the model validation.
The possibility to design and implement a more advanced adaptive control system that works over
the full operational range without issues of amplification at lower frequencies should be investiВӯ
gated.

Current Sensors
A new addition to the rig is current sensors used monitor the power consumption of the motorВӯ

differences in startingВӯcurrent between passive and active lubrication since starting currents are
