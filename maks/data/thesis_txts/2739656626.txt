Abstract
Atlantic Salmon aquaculture is a big industry in the country of Norway, where more than
50% of world supply of salmon fish is produced. Aquaculture systems include flow­through
and recirculating aquaculture systems. The latter are situated on land and recycle big
portion of the water they use. This is achieved through wastewater treatment processing.
The solid residue from this processing is called fish sludge. Sludge can be air dried to
facilitate transport and general use. Another option of sludge processing is slow pyrolysis
in order to produce biochar. Biochar has applications such as nutrient rich fertilizer, soil
upgrade among others. Production of biochar is a carbon sequestration technology, as it
contains chemically stable carbon that does not create global warming emissions. Con­
sequentially, carbon credits are attributed to it, in the emerging carbon emissions market.
Alumichem A/S is a company active in the wastewater treatment sector in Norway that
wants to assess the economic feasibility of a slow pyrolysis system for biochar production.
This thesis aimed to answer the question of the potential of feasibility for such a system
with capacity of 1000 metric tons of processed dried fish sludge per annum. For this
reason a slow pyrolysis with sludge drying process design was developed in Aspen Plus
software. It included air drying of the fish sludge which was led to the pyrolysis reactor,
producing biochar and pyrolysis gas. The pyrolysis gas was combusted producing 0.381
MW of thermal energy, enough to facilitate fish sludge drying, and maintaining pyrolysis
temperature, while the excess heat was delivered to a district heating system. For the cal­
culation of the process’ mass balance, a fish sludge and its biochar elemental characteri­
zation experiment was run. The biochar was produced from a pyrolysis experiment. The
biochar yield for pyrolysis at 650 oC temperature and 600 oC/h heating rate, was found to
be 36% wt. The (C,H,N,O,Ash) ultimate analysis composition of fish sludge was found to
be (40.2%,5.7%4.8%,28.8%,20.6%) and for biochar (37.6%,1%,1.9%2.3%,57.2%). Their
respective higher heating values were calculated from a formula in scientific literature and
found equal to 17.27 MJ/kg for fish sludge and 12.81 MJ/kg for biochar. The tempera­
ture in the process design was set to 650 oC in order to eliminate harmful substances,
and a screw conveyor/auger reactor design was considered in the simulation. From siz­
ing & costing the process design’s equipment a free­on­board purchased equipment cost
of 0.869 million $ was calculated. Three scenarios were considered, one basic called
Scenario 1, one basic without operating labour assuming full automation called Scenario
2, and one without operating labour and minimizing cost factors for the total capital in­
vestment and annual total production cost called Scenario 3. These had a total capital
investment cost of 4.446, 4.446, and 2.649 million $ respectively, and an annual total pro­
duction cost of 1.055, 0.613, and 0.139 million $. Only Scenario 3 demonstrated feasibility
potential with payback period equal to 11.5 years, return­on­investment 4.1 % per year,
net present value of ­1.92 million $ and break­even price of 1587 $ per kg of biochar un­
der 15% discount rate, and applying discrete biochar product and biochar’s carbon credit
valorisation. Sensitivity analysis showed that the purchased equipment cost and biochar
product market value as the most sensitive variables of the economic evaluation. A con­
sideration of a 66.70 $/ton of fish sludge gate fee for sludge processing provides a more
financially promising option for the fish sludge pyrolysis plant’s feasibility, with payback
period of 9.9 years, return­on­investment 5.3%/ per year, ­1.73 net present value and
break­even price of 872 $ per kg of biochar. Comparison of the latter with a heat pump
sludge air drying system, showed that the pyrolysis facility demonstrated lower purchased
equipment cost and lower operational costs.

1
Scope of the report & Case Study
The scope of this thesis report is to present a process design for slow pyrolysis of Atlantic
salmon fish sludge and conduct a feasibility assessment to ascertain its economic poten­
tial.
Alumichem A/S is a company that is active in engineering projects of environmental inter­
est, especially in the wastewater treatment sector. Among others, they provide engineer­
ing solutions for aquaculture systems and sludge treatment. [1]
The aquariums used for smolt hatching of juvenile fish, otherwise called fry, are mostly
land­based and operate in a recirculating aquaculture systems’ (RAS) manner. Thus,
through the non­consumed food waste and the excretions of fish, big quantities of sludge
are produced. This is why sludge management system must be incorporated in the RAS
tanks that are used in land­based aquaculture.
As stated in their website, a Norwegian salmon producer was expanding their fish farm­
ing facility and was worried about new stricter rules for waste water and what to do with
the increasing amount of sludge effluent, produced from the aquariums. Alumichem A/S
worked closely with the fish farmer and their equipment supplier to create a system that
effectively dried the sludge to 90% solids and cleaned the water. The drying of the sludge
was achieved through air drying. A heat pump was used to heat the air going through
the drying system. With this system, the water met the new discharge limits, and the
dry sludge produced was safe, stable, and valuable. Alumichem A/S also arranged for
the dry sludge to be used by a Norwegian organic fertilizer company, who received the
phosphorous­rich material for further processing. This solution ensured the water was
clean for the environment and the sludge was reused in a sustainable way. [2]
Even though the dried sludge can be sold as fertilizer for organic fertilizer production or
even direct land use, the question if it can be furthermore valorized arises. The option for
pyrolysis of sludge presents with an solution that has a fertilizer product of higher value,
that of bio­char, a second product, that of carbon credit as bio­char production is charac­
terized as as carbon emissions­negative technology, while possibly demonstrating lower
sludge treatment costs. As the excess heat produced from pyrolysis could be used for the
sludge drying step, lower requirements of energy are expected. Thus, it is evident that
the sludge pyrolysis process should be technologically and economically assessed.
The scope of this thesis project is to do the feasibility study of this pyrolysis process, us­
ing dried sludge as feedstock and producing bio­char. While Alumichem A/S is a Danish
company, they are active in aquaculture wastewater treatment projects close to the city

2.1.2
Atlantic Salmon aquaculture
Atlantic salmon species naturally occurs on both sides of the Atlantic Ocean. Eggs are

The pre­smolt phase most usually takes place in recirculating freshwater tanks. Due to

Fish sludge for bio­char production
As with most sludge waste, such as municipal waste, sewage sludge, fish sludge could be
a feedstock for bio­char. For the production of bio­char the pyrolysis process is employed.
2.3.1
Pyrolysis thermal process
Pyrolysis is a thermal decomposition process of biomass, that occurs under total absence
or limited supply of oxidizing agents, so that gasification phenomena are not possible. The
products of pyrolysis are gas, liquid and solid. Three main variables determine the prod­
uct distribution of pyrolysis. Specifically, these are the maximum temperature or pyrolysis
temperature, the heating rate which determines the rate of temperature escalation during
the process, and the gas residence time in the pyrolysis chamber. [23]
Pyrolysis processes can be generally categorized in two categories, the first being slow
pyrolysis and the second being fast pyrolysis. Slow pyrolysis mostly takes place at lower
temperatures (200­400oC), having also lower gas residence times (several minutes ­
days) and heating rates. The opposite for all process variables is the case for fast py­
rolysis. [23]
The liquid product of pyrolysis is called bio­oil. It is a mixture of complex hydrocarbons
with big amounts of oxygen and water. Compounds found in bio­oil fall into the categories
of hydroxyaldehydes, hydroxyketones, sugars and dehydrosugars, carboxylic acids and
phenolic compounds. [23]
The gas product of pyrolysis consists of a condensable part, characterized as primary gas

part can be considered part of the bio­oil in gaseous form. the primary gas part consists
mostly of volatile gases such as carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO2), methane
(CH4), ethane (C2H6) etc. This pyrolysis gas has a heating value within the range of 11­20

2.3.2
Slow pyrolysis
According to Nachenius et al.
(2013), a decreased pyrolysis heating rate (below 80
°C/min) that aims the formation of solid product or biochar is indicative of slow pyroly­
sis. The usual temperature range is between 350 and 750 oC. With slow pyrolysis, the
biochar’s composition approaches that of coal, with a comparatively higher carbon con­
tent at the expense of hydrogen and oxygen. Slow pyrolysis necessitates lower heating
rates or longer vapor residence time in order to maximize the solid product yield. The solid
product yield and quality are significantly influenced by the solid reaction time and peak
reaction temperature. By definition, the gases that remain after the reaction vapors have
gone through a condensation step to separate the permanent gases from the bio­oil are
the non­condensable gases that are generated during pyrolysis. Biomass has been py­
rolyzed to create char since prehistoric times, and this char has been utilized for lighting,
heating, and even creating art. [18] [23] The economics of the pyrolysis process are neg­
atively impacted by the water content of biomass. Eliminating water from the biomass, but
without initiating thermochemical conversion, is the primary goal of a drying stage as part
of a slow pyrolysis process set­up. As a result, 150°C or below should be the temperature
of the drying step in order to keep pyrolysis thermochemical reactions from happening.
The pyrolysis vapors will have a higher calorific value when a drying processed is em­
ployed outside the reactor, because of the much smaller amounts of water vapor. [24]
2.3.3
Bio­char
The solid product of pyrolysis is called bio­char. Its molecular composition mainly com­
prises of 50­90 % fixed carbon, 0­40% volatile organic compounds, 1­15% moisture and
0.5­5% inorganic ash. Bio­char has a heating value within the range of 25­32 MJ/kg dry
basis, which is bigger than that of its parent biomass and co­produced bio­oil. In most
cases the mass yield of pyrolysis bio­char does not exceed 35%. Favourable conditions
for maximization of bio­char production are those of slow pyrolysis or carbonisation, under
low­temperature (around 400 oC), and with long residence time of pyrolysis gas within the
pyrolysis chamber (10 min ­ days), and slow heating rate (0.01­2 oC/s). This is because
time is needed for all condensable gas product to be converted into bio­char. [23]

Screw conveyor/auger reactor design
The reactor design that is used in Aquagreen pyrolysis design appears to be a screw
conveyor design. This type of reactor is called auger reactor in scientific literature. The
reactor’s design can be seen in Figure 2.8 below. The solids go through a heated tube
where they are pyrolyzed. An inert gas, most usually nitrogen, is regularly used to purge
the vapour that is emitted from the biomass during the process. In large scale production
volume applications, a pre­heated heat carrier such as ceramics or steel pellets can be
used to provide better heat transfer conditions. The auger, otherwise called a screw, is
guiding the pyrolyzed biomass through the tube. The produced biochar is collected at
the bottom at the end of the tube. The condensable part of the produced vapours can be
collected using a condenser, either it be directly used for energy production through com­
bustion along the non­condensable part. The speed control controls the residence time
of the biomass, thus deciding whether fast or slow pyrolysis will take place. Additionally,
the control of speed can provide flexibility in the pyrolysis process, in case of fluctuations
to the biomass input due to over­ or under­ supply of biomass feed input. [34]
The advantages of the auger/screw pyrolysis reactor include the fact that it is suitable for
mobile applications or locations with limited infrastructure or accessibility. Also it is suit­
able to use heat carriers (steel shot, ceramic balls etc.) to ensure complete and uniform
heat transfer Finally a big advantage is its low specific reactor size, which leads to less
energy demand for conducting the pyrolysis reactions and reducing the capital cost of the
pyrolysis plant. [35]
Bridgewater (2012) has made a review on fast pyrolysis. he states that virtually any
biomass resource can be considered suitable for fast pyrolysis. Among others he men­
tions sewage sludge. This is important information as sewage sludge has similar char­
acteristics with fish sludge. Also, he shows a list table with fast pyrolysis industrial scale
reactors. In this list he includes the auger reactor design among many. He lists two fast
pyrolysis systems that both have a maximum working mass feed rate of 500 kg/h. one
of Lurgi LR in Germany and one of KIT (FZK) in Germany again. The first is of industrial
scale, having 4 working units and the latter is a research reactor. He mentions another in­
dustrial auger reactor of 200 kg/h capacity by Renewable Oil Intl in USA. These numbers
are important as they shown the capabilities of the auger system in terms of handling big
amounts of biomass. [36]
Brassard et al. (2017) have made a literature review with the aim to list and describe
auger pyrolysis reactors that are currently used to produce biochar and bio­oil, mainly at
laboratory and pilot scale. They argue that among the available pyrolysis units, auger
pyrolysis is a polyvalent and promising technology for producing both bio­oil and biochar.
These reactors are simple to operate and can be mobile, they require little or no carrier
gas and low energy. [37]
Another literature review paper discussing auger reactors has been published by Moser
et al. (2023). They aimed to compare auger versus rotary kiln reactor types. Auger reac­
tors, compared to rotary kilns, are a lot more established with the use of solid heat carriers,
such as sand, making them the easier choice for very fast types of pyrolysis. Moreover,
auger reactors offer easier access to the products during the process, due to the static
reactor walls. In addition to that, auger reactors need less space, because they are usu­

Data from literature
In this section several data from literature will be gathered. As not all necessary data could
be be found by experiment literature findings can provide data with good approximation

3.2.1
Reactor type
As discussed in chapter 2 the auger reactor design can effectively handle mass feed rates
of up to 500 kg/h. As the working feed rate of the planned salmon sludge pyrolysis plant
is equal to 1000 tn/y = 114.16 kg/h the auger design is sufficient to handle the sludge
processing needs.
3.2.2
Temperature T of pyrolysis
It is known from literature that in order to maximize char production, an operational regime
of using a slow heating rate (<0.01–2.0°C/s), a low final temperature, and a long gas resi­
dence time is recommended. [23], [20] In Figure 3.1 this pattern can be seen clearly. The
char production decreases with increasing pyrolysis temperature while gas product yield
increases. Upward is the course for the heating value of the char product as temperature
increases, only with a small dip close to 700 oC. [41]
Findings in literature confirm that this is also a fact for biochar produced from pyrolysis of
fish waste. [42].
Despite these facts, a temperature of 650 oC is chosen as for the pyrolysis process. This
is the temperature currently used in AquaGreen’s industrial­scale sludge pyrolysis mod­
ule. This is a compromise between maximum bio­char yield and contaminants’ presence
mitigation. Contamination of the final bio­char product is possible through the presence
of PFAs (Per­ and poly­ fluoroalkyl substances), PHAs (poly­aromatic hydrocarbons), mi­
croplastics and medical residues. [33]

Process Design
The pyrolysis process will be simulated in the Aspen Plus software environment. Aspen
Plus is a chemical process simulator widely used in the chemical, petrochemical, and
pharmaceutical industries for designing, modeling, and optimizing chemical processes.
It offers a comprehensive library of thermodynamic models and unit operations, allowing
accurate prediction of phase behavior and process performance. With its user­friendly
graphical interface, engineers can build and visualize process flowsheets, optimize pro­
cess parameters, and identify efficiency improvements.
4.1
Set­up & assumptions
The starting Aspen plus set­up model used for the simulation was found in the teaching
material of the Green fuels and Power­to­X course of DTU. The starting model included
the thermodynamic model, the components list, and the selection for custom input of
higher heating value and heat capacity, for special components. [50]
Thermodynamic model
This starting model has the Peng­Robinson method set as its thermodynamic model.
The Standard Peng­Robinson equation­of­state is the original formulation of the Peng­
Robinson equation of state with the standard alpha function. It is the basis for the PENG­
ROB property method and it is recommended for hydrocarbon processing applications
such as gas processing, refinery, and petrochemical processes. [51]
Ever since the pyrolysis process will include gas formation of substances usually met in
a petrochemical refinery (methane, carbon dioxide, hydrogen etc.) it is considered suffi­
cient to model the process that will be laid out in this report. In literature Peng­Robinson
is used with its variation of Boston­Mathias alpha function in biomass gasification simu­
lations in Aspen Plus software. In the work of Abdul­Azeez et al. (2023) and in the work
of Pilar­Gonzalez et al. (2021) biomass gasification is modelled in Aspen Plus using the
Peng­Robinson method with the Boston­Mathias alpha function. [52] [53] Shahbaz et al.
modelled a slow pyrolysis process for cellulose, hemi­cellulose and lignin biomass com­
ponents in Aspen Plus, using the Peng­Robinson with Boston­Mathias modifications as
well. [54] Al­Rumaihi et al. (2023) use a standard Peng­Robinson model to simulate co­
pyrolysis of camel manure blends, date pits and plastic waste into value added products.
[55] This information is enough to prove the suitability of the Peng­Robinson thermody­
namic model’s use for pyrolysis and generally thermochemical process simulation.

Drying

The latter is the input stream for the pyrolysis process sequence. [56]

The third part of the flow chart includes the pyrolysis and can be shown as a hierarchy

raise in temperature of all components inside the auger reactor, namely the biochar that

District heating
A number of parameters in the simulation were set in order to match real conditions. The

4.3.2
Temperature in the pyrolysis reactor
As discussed in subsection 3.2.2 the temperature inside the pyrolysis hierarchy was set

4.3.3
Vapor fraction
Attention was given to the vapor fractions in the exit stream WET­AIR of the separator
SEP­DRY and the exit flue gas streams FLUEEXIT. For both streams their respective
vapor fractions were equal to 1 meaning there was no liquid phase. The vapor fraction in

The payback period (PBP) is the time needed for an investment to recover its initial cost.
It’s calculated by dividing the initial investment by the annual cash inflows. The shorter

Net Present Value (NPV) measures the profitability of an investment by calculating the
difference between the present value of cash inflows and outflows, discounted to account
for the time value of money. A positive NPV indicates that the investment is expected to
generate more value than its cost, while a negative NPV suggests a loss. NPV is widely
used in capital budgeting to assess long­term projects, as it accounts for the timing and

Break­even price
The break­even price is the minimum price at which a product or service must be sold to
cover all costs, including fixed and variable expenses, without making a profit or loss. It’s
calculated by dividing total costs by the number of units produced or sold. At this price
point, the revenue generated equals the costs, so any price above the break­even price
results in profit, and below it leads to a loss. [82] The break­even price will be backwards
calculated by employing the GOAL SEEK function in MS Excel. More specifically, this is

viability of the facility, and it would be interesting to assess its potential variations. Prices
for biochar vary based on the grade of the product.
Different prices of biochar product are found in literature. O’Boyle et al. (2024) in their
feasibility study of co­pyrolysis of sewage sludge and biomass waste, state that biochar’s
stability, efficiency in sequestering carbon, and low concentration of heavy metals make
it classified as high­ or medium­grade biochar from single and co­pyrolysis. On the other
hand, because to its high heavy metal content, single­pyrolysis biochar is regarded as low­
grade. [93] Also, wide variations in biochar quality, application, packaging, and purchase
volume are reflected in the stated price variability. [94]
Two reports, that of Thengane et al. and that Elias et al. (2022) state that biochar’s price
in the U.S. fluctuates between 700 ­ 1000 $. [77] [78] Groot et al. (2018) conducted a
survey among producers of biochar in the United States of America. In their survey they
found that values of biochar ranged from 600 $/ton of biochar as the lowest, to 1039 $/ton
of biochar as the average, and up to 1600 $/ton of biochar. The latter was the most often
cited among the participants of the survey. [95] Campbell et al. (2018) presents a table list

report on the worldwide state of biochar industry and market provide a table for prices of
unblended and pure biochar prices, reported by companies of the sector. They found that

7
Conclusions
This thesis project aimed at gathering data for the pyrolysis of for Atlantic Salmon fish
sludge, developing a functional process design in Aspen Plus chemical process simula­
tion software, and conducting a economic feasibility assessment of the latter.
The higher heating values of the fish sludge and the produced biochar were calculated
with a formula found in literature and were found equal to 17.27 MJ/kg and 12.81 MJ/kg
respectively. Due to the fact that the fish sludge composition of food waste and faeces
is fluctuating during the season and depending on the age of fish, better accuracy on its
higher heating value needs to be achieved. Though many formulas exist than can predict
the sludge’s higher heating value, regular experiments are suggested for accurate simu­
lation results.
The pyrolysis process design presented in this study completely covers its thermal en­
ergy needs without the use of utilities by using the available 0.381 kW of heat produced
by combustion. Excess heat is directed to a district heating network and thus valorised,
producing revenue. This is important as in different case where auto­thermal process
could not be achieved a different design should be introduced. this design would include
alterations in the equipment i.e. an electrically heated auger reactor, which would bring
changes and complexity to the equipment costing, and certainly increase the operational
expenditure due to higher needs of electricity utility. Additionally, in the case where the
dryer’s thermal needs were not covered as well, and heat pump produced heat would be
needed, no process design could provide an argument for shift of sludge processing from
simple drying to pyrolysis for Alumichem A/S. Thus, as an auto­thermal process is en­
sured with the process design provided in this report, a good starting point for deepening
into other sectors of feasibility assessment is possible.
The free­on­board equipment costing proved uncertain when compared with data from
literature,and was found equal to 0.869 million $. The small scale capacity of the pyrol­
ysis plant in this report makes reason to take into account this uncertainty furthermore.
Only in one case, that of the furnace, was the exponential rule costing method used de­
livering comparable results from two different sources of data. The auger reactor was
costed using Guthrie’s method, correcting for the needed temperature­resisting material.
As there was no costing formulas in literature for an auger reactor, more meticulous work
should be conducted on its costing, using exponential rule. The best costing case would
be achieved if direct industry pricing data were able to be retrieved, same as with the dryer
equipment cost, which was equal to 0.665 million $ free­on­board, given by Alumichem
A/S in this report.
It is important to revise one by one the cost factors of TCI and TPC for a more accurate
result. Despite the fact that the default and minimum factor values were considered in this
report, costs like yard improvements, contractor’s fee, property tax, legal expenses and
administration costs may be different in reality, changing the capital and operational costs.

The best scenario judged by its feasibility metrics of those presented in this report is Sce­
nario 3, but with the addition of a gate fee for the fish sludge, having PBP = 9.9, ROI =
5.3 %/y, NPV = ­1.73 million $, and biochar nutrient BEP = 872 $/tn. In that case, the

the carbon credits. Alumichem A/S requested that the basic scenario of the feasibility
analysis would not include a gate fee, and in that case the metrics were PBP = 11.5, ROI
= 4.1 %/y, NPV = ­1.92 million $, and biochar nutrient BEP = 1587 $/tn. Thus it seems that
this fee is of big importance for the enhancement of feasibility metrics. The discussion
part revealed that biochar’s breakeven price could be within the most biochar markets’
price spectrum. As price uncertainty is high due to conflicting findings in literature, direct
communication with industry officials about the pricing of biochar from the specific slow
pyrolysis of fish sludge process presented in this report, would resolve this issue.
The comparison of the heat pump dryer XVP150 system versus its alternative, pyroly­
sis of fish sludge using SV150 was limited due to the fact that revenue data for a dried
sludge fertilizer could not be obtained. Thus profitability comparison using feasibility met­
rics could not be presented. Despite this it was shown cost­wise the pyrolysis system has
a lower equipment cost and consumes three times less electricity consumption then the
heat pump sludge drying set­up, with 0.018 million $ of utility cost for the former versus
0.054 million $ of the latter. The auger reactor and air compressor do not contribute a lot
to these utility costs in the pyrolysis set­up with 0.01 kW and 2.79 kW electricity consump­
tion respectively.
Ever since feasibility could not be clearly established with the information presented in this
report it would be interesting to explore different configurations of the process. Bio­oil from
pyrolysis is a product of pyrolysis that comes from condensation of the condensable part of
the pyrolysis gas. Collection of bio­oil would shift change the heat integration of the plant,
as the new pyrolysis gas would be of lower heating value. Despite this the exploration of
different process design alternatives would be interesting, as to how the maximum value
from the difference in bio­oil revenue and additional capital and operational expenditure,
could be obtained.
