Abstract
Department of Mechanical Engineering
Section of Materials and Surface Engineering
Master of Science
by Cecilie Vase Funch
This thesis concerns the non-equilibrium hierarchical microstructure of austenitic stain-
less steel (AISI 316L) manufactured using the selective laser melting (SLM) 3D printing
technology. The microstructure of diﬀerent locations in the sample is characterised and
variations are observed based on cooling conditions. When the temperature gradient is
steep the microstructure consists of melt pools, elongated austenite grains and a cellular
sub-grain structure. This microstructure is observed in the bulk of the sample. In the
bottom layer the cooling conditions were diﬀerent and the temperature gradient more
shallow resulting in equiaxed grains free of the cellular sub-grain structure.
The inﬂuence of diﬀerent treatments were tested, speciﬁcally heat, low temperature
surface hardening (LTSH) in the form of gas nitriding and deformation hardening (DH)
through burnishing. Austenitisation was found to remove the cellular structure, lower
the hardness and cause grain growth, while a low temperature treatment at 420◦C for
16 h had no measurable inﬂuence. The LTSH treatment formed a case of expanded
austenite with a much higher hardness than the bulk. It was found to form a large case
when treating directly on an as received (AR) sample compared to austenitised (AU).
Both hardness proﬁles have a tail of increased hardness, but particularly the AU sample.
The etching performance of the expanded austenite zone is improved, which indicates
improved resistance to localised corrosion. The burnishing treatment creates a gradual
hardness increase with eﬀects reaching 400 µm into the material. The microstructure
consists of a nano-grained zone closest to the surface followed by a heavily twinned
zone underneath. No strain-induced martensite was formed during deformation. The
etching performance of the nano-grained zone is severely diminished, indicating loss of
protection against localised pitting corrosion.

Introduction
Chapter 1
Introduction
Over the last decade 3D printing and Additive Manufacturing (AM) have changed from
over-hyped buzzwords and prototyping technologies to advanced industrial tools. AM
was invented less than 40 years ago in the early 1980’s with several new technologies
appearing over the years.
Metal Additive Manufacturing (MAM) in particular is a
recent phenomenon invented in 2000 [1]. Where AM was previously restricted to poly-
mers, being able to directly manufacture high complexity metallic components is highly
promising for a wide range of industries, such as aerospace, medical, automotive and
countless others.
Iron-based materials have been used for thousands of years and in this context stainless
steel is a relative newcomer, having been invented just over 100 years ago. Today, more
than 100 grades of stainless have been made commercially available in four diﬀerent
families: austenitic, martensitic, ferritic and duplex. It quickly became popular and
truly revolutionised the modern world and is applied in almost any industry imaginable
today from construction to cutlery and surgical tools [2]. Implementing one of the most
widespread engineering materials in this new and exciting manufacturing technology
holds immense potential for future applications.
1
==PAGE:24==
Introduction
1.1 Problem
1.1
Problem
AM is a layer-based process, which always has been associated with anisotropic mechan-
ical properties, heterogeneous elemental distribution and sample-to-sample variation.
These qualities of the AM process prove challenging for industrial scale implementation
as this requires the components to be reliable in use.
While stainless steel has been applied in MAM previously, it still faces several issues be-
fore it is fully implementable. The reason why stainless steel is so favoured in industries
is its excellent corrosion properties, but there have been problems with lowered pitting
potential and increased reactivation current when using AM. This makes the stainless
steel lose its inherently most important property: corrosion resistance.
This project concerns characterising the unique hierarchical microstructure produced
when 3D printing stainless steel with the Selective Laser Melting (SLM) technology and
how the microstructure changes heterogeneously through the sample thickness. This
is done in order to understand how the microstructural features are inﬂuenced by the
diﬀerent cooling conditions depending on their location in the sample. The second part
of the project concerns improving the microstructure through high and low temperature
annealing. As the SLM microstructure is non-equilibrium, it will be highly inﬂuenced by
temperature exposure. Lastly, the mechanical performance is improved through surface
hardening treatments in the form of low temperature nitriding and deformation harden-
ing. Micro hardness is the only mechanical property in scope in this project, so this will
be the basis for evaluation of the treatments. It is also investigated how the diﬀerent
surface treatments inﬂuence the underlaying microstructure as these treatments have
primarily been investigated on conventionally manufactured stainless steel.

Theory
This chapter contains an overview of the theory relevant for this project. Firstly, the
additive manufacturing (AM) technology will be presented, speciﬁcally the selective laser
melting (SLM) process. It will be investigated how the microstructure of an austenitic
stainless steel is inﬂuenced by the process as well as a review of the current research

2.1
Metal Additive Manufacturing
Additive manufacturing has some very clear advantages over conventional manufac-
turing methods, especially with regards to geometrical freedom. AM is a layer based
process, where the geometry is not restricted to a mould or die. This reduces the need
for specialised tooling and low-volume production and even full customisation become
economically feasible.
Today, several AM technologies for producing parts in a variety of materials exist, from
metals to polymers, however all have the same general process chain, as seen in Figure
2.1.
Figure 2.1: General AM Process Chain, adapted from [3] p. 59.

==PAGE:26==

2.1 Metal Additive Manufacturing
The process starts with a Computer Aided Design (CAD) model, which is saved as a
stereolithography (STL) ﬁle. The model is then sliced into appropriate layers suitable
for the speciﬁc AM equipment by the use of dedicated software. The machine setup
is entirely dependent on the speciﬁc AM technology and also varies between manufac-
turers. The optimal settings depend on technology, machine, material, part geometry
and acceptable lead time. After the part is built, it is removed from the printer and
cleaned. The ﬁnal step is the post-processing of the part. Depending on the geometry
and technology this can include: further curing of thermoset polymers, support removal,
heat treatments, mechanical polishing, surface hardening and more [3].
The ﬁeld of Metal Additive Manufacturing (MAM) has received a high level of media
attention over the past 10 years due to its unique design freedom and capability of
producing ﬁnished parts in well-known engineering metals, such as steel, stainless steel,
aluminium and titanium [4]. Over the past 30 years AM has changed from its initial
rapid prototyping applications to being capable of consistently producing almost fully
dense parts due to technological advances and lowered cost in the ﬁeld of reliable indus-
trial lasers, high performance computing software and hardware as well as high quality
feed stock. This has allowed several MAM technologies to become critically accepted
processing methods [5].
MAM has many potential areas of application from aerospace to medical and from
customised art to tools and moulds. This wide range of applications also gives the tech-
nology an enormous potential for growth. In fact, both the AM market in general and
MAM speciﬁcally have seen an exponential growth since 2010 [6].
2.1.1
Selective Laser Melting
Most of the MAM technologies in use today are based on more conventional powder
based fabrication, such as sintering. The powder-based methods are usually preferred
for their good surface ﬁnish and ability to manufacture intricate geometries due to the
size of the powder particles, even though wire-based processes have a higher deposition
rate and are capable of producing large parts [5].
The main distinction between the diﬀerent powder-based technologies lies in whether a
powder bed is used or if the powder is directly deposited where needed. The powder bed
technologies are the most widespread as they usually require less ﬁnishing operations [5].
4
==PAGE:27==
2.1 Metal Additive Manufacturing
Theory
Furthermore, a distinction is made between the power source with the most common
being lasers or electron beams. Selective Laser Melting (SLM) is a powder bed process,
where a thin layer of metal powder is spread over the build plate and consolidated by
a laser rastering over the surface and raising the local temperature above the melting
point, so the powder is selectively melted. Upon completion of one layer, the z-stage is
lowered by the distance equivalent to one layer and additional powder is distributed and
the scanning process is repeated [5].
Figure 2.2: Schematic of the SLM process [7].
Normally, the volume of parts built is signiﬁcantly lower than the chamber volume,
which results in a lot of unused powder. As the powder for AM is costly, it is recovered
and recycled in subsequent builds. This helps to keep the costs down, however the eﬀects
of continuous reuse is not fully understood. The limited research available suggests that
the elevated temperature and energy could cause both chemical and physical changes to
the powder particles [6].
The movement of the laser can follow several diﬀerent patterns or scan strategies that
overlap with a certain hatch spacing. One of the most important factors for the quality
of the SLM process is the volume energy, which is supplied to the powder. Too low
volume energy results in pores owing to insuﬃcient fusion and too high energy results in
gas porosity. The volume energy is calculated from the hatch spacing, hs, the eﬀective
laser power, PL, the scan speed, vs and the layer thickness, ds, as seen in Eq. 2.1 [4].

Powder Feedstock
In the powder-based MAM processes, the quality of the powder feedstock is critical to
the quality of the ﬁnished part. The homogeneity of the layer of powder on the build
plate should be high in order to prevent porosity from lack of powder or incomplete
fusion due to too thick areas.
Generally, the quality of the powder depends on the manufacturing method. For SLM
the most common method is gas atomisation (GA) due to its ability to produce powders
from a wide range of alloys and in a wide range of sizes. Also, powders produced using
GA are more spherical than the ones produced by water atomisation and the process is
capable of atomising reactive materials. Furthermore, GA is much more cost-eﬀective
than for example plasma atomisation. In the GA process, the molten metal is poured
into the chamber and is allowed to fall. The stream of metal is atomised by high pressure
jets of usually argon or nitrogen. Due to the lower conductivity of the gases compared
to water the solidiﬁcation is slower, so the particles become more spherical, as seen in
ﬁgure 2.3 [6].

In general, SLM is carried out in a protective atmosphere. For stainless steel, the shield-
ing gas can be either inert or non-inert gas. An inert gas, such as argon, will not react
with the metal, neither in liquid nor as vapour and is practically insoluble in stainless
steel. Nitrogen is also commonly used for shielding during SLM of stainless steel, even
though it is not inert, particularly at high temperatures. Research results on laser weld-
ing show that nitrogen can react with chromium to form chromium nitrides and is able
to dissolve in the stainless steel melt, which leads to a reduction of gas pores compared
to inert shielding gases [10]. Most likely, this research result can be transferred to SLM
[5].
Generally, an increased nitrogen content in solid solution in austenitic stainless
steels stabilises the austenite phase and improves the corrosion resistance and mechan-
ical properties without loss of ductility by solid solution strengthening [11]. Increasing
the nitrogen content also improves the grain size hardening eﬀect [12].

microstructure depending on the undercooling. Cast 316L typically has a grain size of
20-40 µm. A larger undercooling of welding reduces the grain size by a factor 2, while

In cases where texture is observed, there is a preferential growth along the (100) plane
[13, 19]. Other research results ﬁnd that no texture is formed during printing [8, 17].
It is not obvious why texture is observed in some results and not in others. In certain
research, small amounts of ferrite are detected in the samples, which most likely are
δ-ferrite formed at very high temperatures during SLM [13, 20]. A local misorientation
is observed across the grains, which is correlated with the low angle grain boundaries
(LABs) [18].
Wang et al. [18] studied the tensile properties of 316L parts and found that the yield
strength of the SLM samples was up to two or three times higher than wrought or cast
materials. Similarly, the SLM parts show an improved tensile elongation, which proves
SLM to be a useful hardening technique that avoids the ductility trade-oﬀassociated
with most hardening processes. They attributed this strengthening eﬀect primarily to
the cellular solidiﬁcation structure as well as LABs. The increased nitrogen content
in the parts will most likely also have an impact, as solid solution strengthening being
nitrogen known to increase strength without loosing ductility [21]. Saedi et al. [13]
found the hardness of SLM 316L to be 1.5 times higher than the one obtained with
conventional processes and attributed this primarily to the dislocation network. Zhong

Eﬀect of Heat Treatment
Austenitic stainless steels can generally not be hardened by heat treatments, however
they can provide beneﬁcial eﬀect in terms of stress relief and homogenisation as well
as other eﬀects, such as recrystallisation, phase transformation and grain growth. Ka-
mariah et al. [22] conducted 2 h heat treatments at 650, 950 and 1100 ◦C and found the
hardness to be reduced at the two treatments at higher temperatures due to a reduced
dislocation density and loss of the cellular sub-grain structure. No change in hardness
was detected at the lower temperature. A small amount of δ-ferrite was detected in the
as received sample, which was reduced by employing heat treatments. This also leads to
a reduction in hardness as the body-centred cubic (BCC) structure is harder than the
face-centred cubic (FCC) austenite.
Saeidi et al. [20] investigated heat treatments of 6 min and 1 h at 800, 900, 1100 and
1400 ◦C and found that a dual phase austenite-ferrite microstructure was formed after
annealing at 1100 and 1400 ◦C. This ferrite formation is promoted by small ferrite nuclei
present in the as received sample. The cellular structure disappears when annealing at
900 ◦C and higher as the cell walls have a high molybdenum concentration, which cannot
be dissolved at low temperatures. The dislocation density is reduced with all treatments.
This all leads to lowered tensile properties due to a combination of grain growth, strain
relief and phase transformations. The hardness is reduced by heat treatments, but has a
slight increase from 1100-1400 ◦C as a large degree of twinning is observed at the highest
temperature treatment.

2.2 Low Temperature Surface Hardening
2.2
Low Temperature Surface Hardening
Stainless steels inherit their excellent corrosion resistance from a passive chromium oxide
ﬁlm on their surface. This passive ﬁlm is susceptible to local breakdown as a result of
continuous wear or the presence of chloride solutions. Alloying with nitrogen both sta-
bilises the austenite phase and provides a strength increase without reducing toughness.
As the solubility of nitrogen in the liquid state is limited, the alloying with nitrogen is
usually a subsequent solid state process [23]. The corrosion properties rely on chromium
in solid solution. The formation of chromium nitrides during conventional nitriding of
stainless steel at 490-950 ◦C will adversely impact the corrosion resistance [24].
Figure 2.7: TTT diagram of austenite with a high nitrogen content [24].
The time-temperature-transformation (TTT) diagram in ﬁgure 2.7 shows how an austenitic
material with a high nitrogen content behaves as a function of time and temperature.
The formation of chromium nitrides can be suppressed by utilising a 12-24 hour, low
temperature treatment or a high temperature treatment followed by rapid cooling. Tra-
ditionally, most low temperature surface hardening (LTSH) processes with nitrogen were
plasma-based, however more recently, gas-based processes have been developed with a
particular gas mixture capable of both surface activation and nitrogen/carbon supply. At
low temperatures, the interstitials, such as nitrogen, can diﬀuse relatively long distances,
while the larger metal atoms are considered immobile, which prevents the formation of
nitrides. Up to 38 at% of nitrogen can be dissolved, producing a supersaturated case of
expanded austenite with a high hardness and considerable residual compressive stress.
The hardness improves wear and galling properties, while the compressive stress im-
proves resistance to fatigue. The expanded austenite is also corrosion resistant [24].

2.3
Deformation Hardening
Due to the composition of the 316L stainless steel, hardening by heat treatment is not
a viable option. Instead, these steels are often work hardened. During work harden-
ing, a metal is deformed and the dislocation density increases as new dislocations are
formed. This increased dislocation density limits dislocation movement and the me-
chanical strength and hardness increase. This hardness increase usually comes with a
trade-oﬀin the form of reduced ductility [26].
As the surface quality of AM parts, and MAM in particular, is often poor, burnishing
becomes an interesting choice for deformation hardening. Burnishing has been shown to
greatly improve surface roughness during deformation, while simultaneously increasing
the hardness and fatigue behaviour of the material due to an increase in residual com-
pressive stresses [27]. Burnishing is a process where force is applied to the part by using
a hard and smooth ball or roller as seen in ﬁgure 2.8.
Figure 2.8: Schematic illustration of the burnishing process [28].
In this example, the workpiece is stationary while the burnishing tool is moving, how-
ever for practical applications, the part will be mounted on a lathe and rotated for an
even deformation. The movement of the burnishing tool creates a cold ﬂow of material
from the peaks to the valleys of the proﬁle, so the overall roughness is reduced. As a
contrast to other ﬁnishing operations, such as grinding, the burnishing process creates a
micro-displacement of material. The main advantage over other work hardening surface
processes is the high-quality surface ﬁnish and controlled deformation [28].

Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) provides better resolution beyond the limitation

on a smaller scale. For the heat treated samples, it is also used to avoid etching, due to

In principle, SEM requires no special metallurgical preparation. For the AR, AR+LTSH
and AR+DH samples, the SEM investigations were carried out on etched samples, while
the heat treated samples and AR+DH sample were investigated in the unembedded and
electropolished condition. When metallic samples are investigated, they usually provide
suﬃcient electric contact to prevent charging, but when the samples are embedded, this
connection is lost. Surface charging results in blurring and image distortion and needs
to be prevented. In this project, a thin carbon coating is employed for this purpose.
Carbon is inexpensive and provides good conduction when applied to non-conducting
specimens. Furthermore, carbon is a light element and has therefore less interaction
with the material in the form of backscattered electrons. The carbon coating is done on
a Q150RE carbon coater with a rotary pump, which uses a carbon ﬁbre ﬁlament and a
vacuum evaporation process. To further improve the electrical contact, adhesive copper
tape is used to connect the metallic sample to the sample holder.

SEM imaging was done in both secondary electron (SE) and backscatter electron (BSE)
operational mode. SE mostly contains topographical information, while BSE carries

Secondary Electron Imaging
The basic principle of a SEM is identical regardless of the mode of operation. A beam of
electrons is focused to a point using electromagnets. The electron beam is rastered over
the sample surface and information from each pixel is collected by a detector. Secondary
electrons are a result of inelastic scattering of the incident electron beam. SEs are low
energy electrons and can only escape the specimen from a small volume near the sur-
face. When a high energy electron scatters inelastically, it transfers kinetic energy to an
electron from an atom in the sample. If this electron in the sample receives suﬃcient en-
ergy to overcome the bonding energy, it will escape and become a secondary electron [30].
SEs mostly provide topographical contrast from two eﬀects: trajectory and electron
number. Trajectory eﬀect is due to the physical location of the detector. Electrons
emitted from a surface facing the detector will provide a stronger signal and show up
brighter than surfaces facing away from the detector. Furthermore, due to the pear shape
of the interaction volume, more electrons will escape from edges than ﬂat surfaces, which
is the electron number eﬀect [30].
Backscatter Electron Imaging
Backscattered electrons are the result of elastic scattering of the primary electrons.
When electrons are elastically scattered, they loose no or very little energy and BSEs
are therefore high energy electrons and can escape from much deeper in the interaction
volume, which leads to a poorer spatial resolution. The backscatter detector is placed
directly above the electron beam and will detect BSEs being scattered back in the same
direction.
The compositional contrast is present because the backscatter coeﬃcient
(the ratio of backscattered electrons to incident electrons) increases with higher atomic
number making regions with a high concentration of heavier elements show up bright in
a BSE image [30].

Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectroscopy
Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS) is a characterisation technique used to
determine the chemical composition of a specimen by using the characteristic x-rays of
the elements. When a high energy particle, such as an electron, interacts with an atom,
it can knock out an inner shell electron and the atom becomes excited. The excited
state is highly unfavourable, so the atom will ﬁll the inner shell vacancy with an outer
shell electron and emit the excess energy as a characteristic X-ray or an Auger electron.
The energy of a characteristic X-ray is well-deﬁned and depends on the atomic number
of the atom. As X-rays have a much higher penetration depth than backscatter and
secondary electrons, they will be emitted from the entire interaction volume as well as x-
ray ﬂuorescence radiation even from beyond the electron interaction volume, which leads
to a poor spatial resolution [30]. In this project, both stationary and scanning modes
were used. In the stationary mode, a speciﬁc point or area is selected and the electron
probe is stationary while measuring. This was used for the overall chemical composition
of the powder and built samples. In the scanning mode, the probe moves over the sample
surface in a rastering motion while measuring the intensity of characteristic X-rays at
each pixel. This mode is useful for generating compositional maps or line proﬁles. In this
research, the line proﬁle was primarily used for investigating the compositional changes
across interesting features. EDS was carried out on a Phenom ProX with a 25 mm2
silicon drift EDS detector using 15 kV acceleration voltage. On the JEOL JSM-5900
SEM ﬁtted with a 50 mm2 silicon drift detector, an acceleration voltage of 20 kV and a
11 mm working distance were used.
3.6.5

X-Ray diﬀraction (XRD) is a characterisation technique used to identify phases in a
material based on crystallography. XRD can also give information about the lattice
spacing of the crystals, the grain size, the strains present in the material and preferred
crystallographic orientation. 316L is supposedly a single phase, austenitic material, so
XRD was used to detect whether δ-ferrite was formed during printing and how the mi-
crostructure changes as a result of the heat treatments. Furthermore, XRD was used
to investigate whether nitrides form during LTSH as well as characterise the expanded
austenite by measuring directly on the nitrided surface.
In order to understand the
diﬀerences between diﬀerent locations in the sample, both bottom and centre of each

3.6.6
Electron Backscatter Diﬀraction
Electron backscatter diﬀraction (EBSD) is a material characterisation method, which
provides information about the crystallographic orientation of individual grains, grain
sizes and grain boundary types and the crystallographic texture of a sample. It is also
used for identifying separate phases in a multi-phase material. EBSD was used to inves-
tigate the eﬀect of the chosen heat treatments, particularly austenitisation, on the grain
size, density of twin boundaries as well as crystallographic texture and misorientation
within the grains. The crystallographic heterogeneity of the AM samples was investi-
gated by conducting the same EBSD measurements on the top, centre and bottom of

the samples.
EBSD is usually conducted with a special detector in an electron microscope (SEM or
transmission electron microscopy (TEM)) and uses the same electron beam. EBSD re-
quires elastic scattering of electrons in a material. When the incident electron beam
interacts with the sample, there will be elastic scattering in all directions, which means
some electrons will scatter with an angle θ that fulﬁls Bragg’s Law (Eq. 3.1). The
diﬀracted beam is then recorded by a detector. Due to the specimen always being three-
dimensional, the diﬀracted beams form a symmetric cone and the intersection between
the cone and the detector plane creates two parallel lines, known as Kikuchi bands. Each
pair of bands represents a speciﬁc crystallographic plan (hkl) and indexing all bands in
a EBSD pattern reveals the crystal structure of the specimen and its orientation with
respect to the detection plane [30].
The EBSD measurements were carried out on a Zeiss Supra 35 FEGSEM SEM with an
EBSD detector, using a 15 kV acceleration voltage and 13 mm working distance. The
sample was tilted 70◦with respect to the detector in order to improve the signal by
allowing more electrons to escape the free surface and to limit the information depth
to a shallow region below the exposed surface. Texture analysis is done on a 1×1 mm
(215×196 pixels) area with a step size of 5 µm. 2-4 maps were collected from each area.
Grain size and boundary analysis was carried out on a 300×300 µm2 area with a 1 µm
step size. The degree of misorientation within a grain was measured on a 50×50 µm2
area with a step size of 0.2 µm. High angle grain boundaries (HABs) were deﬁned as
¿15◦, while low angle grain boundaries were deﬁned as ¿1.5◦.

measurements.

3.9.1
Powder Particle Size
The image analysis software Image-J was used to evaluate the size of the powder par-
ticles. The SEM images of the ﬁltered and unﬁltered powder at 800x magniﬁcation are
investigated by making the image binary by setting a threshold value where all pixels
above the threshold will be white and all pixels below will be black. This was done so
that the particles can easily be distinguished from the background of the image. The
automatic particle analysis tool was used to measure the average particle size. Particles
are included in the size range 0.00001 mm2-∞and a circularity of 0.1-1. The particles
on the edges of the images are excluded, as their size was not representative. One main
source of error in this size analysis was that the software cannot distinguish two powder
particles from each other when they were touching in the image and therefore sees them
as one particle, which might result in a larger measured particle size.

were counted with the multi-point tool and the density was calculated. Five sections on
two images were investigated for each point of interest.

Chapter 5
Discussion
The discussion brings together the results of diﬀerent experiments and attempts to
identify the eﬀects of diﬀerent treatments: heat, low temperature surface hardening
and deformation hardening. These eﬀects are described and compared to ﬁndings from
literature.
5.1
Through-Thickness Heterogeneity
The results of the experiments on the AR sample (see section 4.1) all indicate a through-
thickness heterogeneity, as the bottom layer appears and performs obviously diﬀerent
from the rest of the sample with regards to changes in microstructure.
The bulk of the sample consists of elongated austenite grains with a sub-grain cellular
network. The appearance of these cells is consistent with microstructures observed in
literature [8, 16] and they are likely a result of the steep temperature gradients in the
material and high cooling rate. This is a highly non-equilibrium microstructure and
it is thermodynamically unstable, as seen from the endothermic reaction during DTA
investigations (ﬁgure 4.11). As the walls show up brighter than the matrix in BSE SEM
on unetched samples (ﬁgure 4.13), they most likely have a higher concentration of the
heavier elements, such as molybdenum and chromium, which is also consistent with
ﬁndings in literature [13, 17, 20, 34]. Furthermore, the cell walls are likely austenitic
like the bulk material, but could also be ferritic, as a trace amount of ferrite was found
using XRD. The bottom 200-300 µm layer consists of grains with a markedly diﬀerent
morphology.
91
==PAGE:114==
Discussion
5.1 Through-Thickness Heterogeneity
The bottom grains are equiaxed and free of the cellular structure seen in the elongated
grains. As the bulk microstructure results from the very rapid cooling conditions, it
is probable that the microstructure in the bottom of the sample is a result of diﬀerent
cooling conditions. As the grains are equiaxed, this region has had a high growth rate
in combination with a shallow temperature gradient compared with the cellular regions
(ﬁgure 2.4). The laser power is slightly lower during printing of the support compared
to the part (table 3.1), but not suﬃcient to completely change the morphology of the
solidiﬁcation structure. Therefore, the more likely explanation lies within the geome-
try of the part and the principle of the SLM process. Initially, a lattice-like support
structure is printed surrounded by powder, which is not fully dense. As air is contained
in the powder due to the degree of packing, its insulation properties are much better
than solid metal. As the heat cannot escape as eﬃciently from the bottom and support
structure, the temperature gradient is signiﬁcantly lowered and the morphology changes.
The nano-scale particles (ﬁgure 4.18) have a much higher density in the centre and top
of one sample. In the bottom area of equiaxed grains, barely any particles are present.
This indicates that the formation of these particles is dependent on the thermal condi-
tions such as cooling and thermal exposure during printing of subsequent layers. As the
particles are too small to investigate with EDS in SEM and do not appear in XRD, it
has not been possible to identify the chemical composition or crystal structure of the
particles. Based on literature, they could be oxides in particular silicon oxides [17]. Due
to the high nitrogen content in the powder and printing in a nitrogen atmosphere, it is
also possible that the particles are small nitrides.

Implementation Perspectives
The two diﬀerent hardening techniques employed in this research showed very diﬀerent
eﬀects on the 3D printed material. The surface hardness of the LTSH is more than
twice the surface hardness of the burnished sample, which makes LTSH ideal for high
wear situations, where the hardness is vital.
Furthermore, the etching behaviour is
improved using LTSH compared to the bulk and burnished sample, which also indicates
that the LTSH will be preferable for implementation in corrosive environments. The
hardening eﬀect for the burnished sample is signiﬁcantly deeper than the LTSH sample
and the change in hardness is more gradual, which makes the deformation hardening
treatment preferable in fatigue situations. Also, the expanded austenite is rather brittle,
as seen by the cracks in the layer. This makes deformation hardening favoured in impact
conditions.

Conclusion
Chapter 6
Conclusion
The initial as printed microstructure of SLM 316L has been characterised using LOM,
SEM, XRD and EBSD as well as nitrogen combustion, DTA and micro hardness. A
through-thickness heterogeneity was observed on several parameters. The centre and
top sections correlated with the microstructure observed in literature consisting of semi-
circular melt pools with elongated austenite grains stretching through several melt pools.
The austenite grains contain a cellular sub-grain structure with the cells having a colum-
nar shape and appearing equiaxed or as parallel lines, depending on the sectioning plane.
The cells have an approximate size of 500-1000 nm and a length of several 10’s of microns.
Furthermore, the bulk of the sample contains nano-scale particles fairly homogeneously
distributed through the sample. It has not been possible to identify the type of particles
with the characterisation methods employed in this project. The bottom 200-300 µm
consist of a radically diﬀerent microstructure with equiaxed austenite grains that are
free of the cellular sub-grain structure and nano-scale particles. Instead, this area con-
tains annealing twins, which have not been observed in other regions. Using XRD and
EBSD, it was observed that the crystallographic texture changes through the sample
from randomly oriented crystals in the bottom to a weak texture towards the top. This
diﬀerence in microstructure is most likely due to changing cooling conditions during
build as the initial layers are well insulated by the surrounding powder.
The eﬀect of annealing was investigated by an austenitisation treatment and a low
temperature treatment at 420◦C. The low temperature treatment had very little de-
tectable inﬂuence on the microstructure. The XRD peaks show less broadening due to
strain relief, but the microstructural constituents and hardness appear unchanged. The
austenisation treatment results in a dissolution of the melt pools and cellular structure.
The ﬁnal microstructure consists of larger equiaxed austenite grains and more than 50%
109

annealing twins in the bottom and elongated austenite grains in the bulk, with the den-
sity of nano-scale particles being obviously diminished. The austenitisation also reduces
the peak broadening even further by removing the cells. Grain growth is observed in all
areas of the sample, but particularly in the equiaxed grains.
The LTSH treatments create a layer of expanded austenite on both the AR+LTSH and
AU+LTSH samples. The expanded austenite has a hardness of about 1100-1200 HV,
while the bulk has a hardness of about 250-280 HV. The layer of expanded austenite
is thicker on the AR+LTSH sample and the hardness proﬁle has a plateau before the
interface. This behaviour is not observed on the AU+LTSH sample, where the hard-
ness changes gradually through the expanded austenite. Both samples have a tail of
increased hardness into the bulk, but it is more pronounced on the AU+LTSH sample.
This eﬀect is not fully understood, but is is most likely due to the heterogeneously dis-
tributed alloying elements. Also, the AR+LTSH sample showed no expanded austenite
on the unpolished surfaces, most likely due to the formation of surface oxides, which are
removed by austenitisation. The expanded austenite layer was more resistant to etching,
indicating improved corrosion properties.
Deformation hardening in the form of burnishing proved eﬀective at increasing the hard-
ness to about 470 HV, with a gradually decreasing hardness towards the bulk. The
microstructure after deformation consists of a thin surface layer of nano-grains followed
by a deeper region of deformation twins. It seems like the preferred deformation mode is
twinning, possibly due to the nitrogen content. The nano-grained area showed severely
diminshed etching performance, while the twinned regions were consistent with previous
samples. No strain-induced martensite was detected as the austenite phase is very stable
in this alloy.

Future Work

Future Work
7.1
Transmission Electron Microscopy
Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) would be a valuable tool in understanding
the microstructure of SLM 316L. Due to the size of the nano-scale particles found with
SEM, it was not possible to determine their chemical composition with EDS or crystal
structure with XRD. The same issue is present with the cellular walls as it has not been
possible to completely determine which element segregates in the walls during printing
or if they are the same crystal structure as the matrix. The thin samples in TEM lower
the interaction volume considerably.
Therefore, investigating nano-scale features be-
comes feasible. Furthermore, it would be interesting to understand how the dislocation
network changes with heat exposure as well as deformation hardening.
During this project, two methods of TEM sample preparation were attempted, but both
showed similar issues. Initially, twin-jet electropolishing was used, as this is a common
preparation technique for TEM. The main issue here was the porosities as the polishing
equipment automatically shuts oﬀwhen a hole is formed. When the samples already
contain holes in the form of porosities, the samples are not properly polished and will
not become electron transparent.
Instead, mechanical dimpling and ion milling was
tried, but this method had similar issues as it also uses light through the sample as an
indication of having reached the desired thickness. It is possible to continue treatment
past this point, but with large variations in quality.
As there were issues with one
ion gun during this project, only one side was ion milled and the thickness became
rather uneven resulting in unsatisfactory REM results. Therefore, the optimal TEM
preparation technique for this material in this quality would be using a focused ion
beam (FIB) to extract the samples.

austenitisation at the same speciﬁed location.
7.3
Glow Discharge Optical Emission Spectroscopy
As the quantiﬁcation of light elements is unreliable in EDS, it would be interesting to
use a technique like GDOES to create a depth proﬁle of the nitrogen content through
the expanded austenite layer. This proﬁle can then be compared to the hardness depth
proﬁles and microstructural changes within the layer. It would be interesting to investi-
gate the nitrogen content and how it correlates to areas of expanded austenite and solid
solution strengthening as well as areas of compositional gradients. This would also help
determine if a detectable carbon accumulation occurs in front of the nitrogen diﬀusion
front.
7.4
Corrosion Test
As the LTSH treatments of both the AR and AU samples improved the resistance to
etching in the expanded austenite layer, it would be relevant to investigate their corro-
sion resistance. Also, the deformation hardened layer showed a decreased resistance to
etching, which could indicate reduced corrosion resistance. As the samples are provided
in standard corrosion test specimen geometry, it would be straight forward to conduct
corrosion tests on them and compare with untreated AR and AU samples and LTSH of
conventional 316L. Furthermore, the chemical inhomogenities and elemental segregation
in the AR microstucture may lead to lowered quality of the passive layer, as suggested
by Trelewicz et al. [34]. The porous nature of the SLM samples and poor printing
quality may also lower the corrosion resistance. Therefore, it is relevant to compare the
behaviour of the AR and AU samples with conventional 316L.

7.5 Carburising and Nitro-Carburising
Future Work
7.5
Carburising and Nitro-Carburising
Another LTSH treatment, such as carburising or nitro-carburising, would be interesting
to investigate in order to understand if the behaviour of the AR and AU samples is
consistent with other LTSH treatments. These treatments use carbon or a combination
of nitrogen and carbon as interstitial atoms in the austenite lattice. The processes are
similar but the dissolution of carbon results in a lower hardness and a gradual transition
between bulk and case. The good corrosion resistance and hardness of nitriding can
be combined with the gentler transition of carburising by adopting a two-step process
resulting in a hard outer layer of nitrogen expanded austenite followed by an underlying
transition layer of carbon expanded austenite [24].
7.6
Deformation
As the burnishing treatment provides a fairly complex loading situation, it would be
interesting to investigate how the deformation occurs and progresses until the observed
microstructure is reached. Therefore, a simpler loading should be used, for example pure
compression. The microstructure should be evaluated at diﬀerent levels of strain.
Due to the geometry of the provided samples, it was not possible to burnish directly
on the printed cylindrical surface. Therefore, it would be interesting to print longer
samples and burnish them in order to investigate how the surface quality changes. On
conventionally prepared samples, burnishing has shown to improve ﬁnish [28].
