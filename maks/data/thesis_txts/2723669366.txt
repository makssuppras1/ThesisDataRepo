Abstract 
The ventilation conditions are fundamental for the fire dynamics in an enclosure. In living accommodations, the oxygen 
flow is primarily from door and window openings. The properties of the windows influence the ventilation conditions, 
and it is important to investigate their behavior during a fire to better understand the process of glass breakage and 
fallout.  
This study focuses mainly on comparing the fire and breakage properties of traditional double-glazed windows to low 
energy windows and investigates whether low energy windows are more resistant to the effects of a fire. The study 
compares four difference types of windows including traditional double-glazed windows with wooden frame, 2-layer 
low energy windows with wood/alu frame, 3-layer low energy windows with wooden frame and with wood/alu frame. 
For all four types of windows the time to first crack, the breaking patterns and the fallout of glass is compared.    
14 windows with dimensions of approximately 600 x 600 mm have been set up in a frame system and exposed to 
radiation from a gas-driven heating panel using the H-TRIS (Heat Transfer Rate Inducing System) at the DTU Fire 
Laboratory. Each test specimen has been exposed to a heat flux consisting of five steps (5, 10, 15, 20 and 25 kW/m2) in 
a growing phase of 10 min followed by a steady phase of 25 kW/m2 for a total of 60 min. During the experiments the 
time to first crack, breaking patterns and the fallout is registered and the temperature on the inner and outer glass is 
measured using thermocouples. All experiments are registered with video, photos, and manual notes. 
The results of the experiments indicate that there was no significant difference in the time to first crack of the inner 
glass on the four different types of windows included in this report. Also, there was no indication that the type of frame 
or coating influences the time to first crack of the inner glass. There was a difference in the time to first crack of the 
outer glass. For the traditional double-glazed windows, the time to first crack of the outer glass is shorter than for all 
the low-energy windows both 2-layer and 3-layer. There is a considerable difference in the number of cracks on the 
outer glass when comparing the traditional 2-layer windows to both types of low energy windows. This indicates that 
the thermal property of the pane has an influence on the integrity during heat radiation. Based on the windows of these 
experiments, their dimensions, frame types, and coating combined with the experimental setup, it was evident that the 
exposure to heat radiation alone does not result in significant fallout from all glass layers on either traditional double-
glazed windows, the 2-layer low energy windows or the 3-layer low energy windows.

Resumé 
Ventilationsforholdene er grundlæggende for branddynamikken i et rum. I rum indrettet til boligformål er 
ventilationsåbninger primært døre og vinduesåbninger. Vinduernes egenskaber påvirker derfor ventilationsforholdene, 
og det er vigtigt at undersøge hvordan vinduerne opfører sig under en brand for bedre at forstå processen med brud i 
glasset og udfald af glas fra et eller flere lag. 
Denne rapport fokuserer på at sammenligne egenskaberne under brand for traditionelle termovinduer med 
lavenergivinduer og undersøger, om lavenergivinduer er mere modstandsdygtige over for virkningerne af en brand. 
Undersøgelsen sammenligner fire forskellige typer vinduer, herunder traditionelle termoruder med trækarm, 2-lags 
lavenergivinduer med træ/alu-karm, 3-lags lavenergivinduer med trækarm og med træ/alu-karm. For alle fire typer 
vinduer sammenlignes tiden til første brud, brudmønstre og udfald af glas. 
14 vinduer med mål på ca. 600 x 600 mm er opsat i et rammesystem og udsat for stråling fra et gasdrevet varmepanel 
ved brug af H-TRIS (Heat Transfer Rate Inducing System) på DTU Brandlaboratoriet. Hvert vindue er blevet udsat for en 
varmeflux bestående af fem trin (5, 10, 15, 20 og 25 kW/m²) i en vækstfase på 10 minutter efterfulgt af en stabil fase på 
25 kW/m² med en samlet varighed på i alt 60 min. Under forsøgene registreres tiden til første brud, brudmønstre og 
udfaldet af glas, samt temperaturen på inderste og yderste glas, som måles ved hjælp af termoelementer. Alle 
eksperimenter er registreret med video, fotos og manuelle noter. 
Resultaterne indikerer, at der ikke er nogen væsentlig forskel i tiden til første brud på det inderste glas af de fire 
forskellige typer vinduer, der indgår i forsøgene. Der var heller ingen indikation af, at typen af karm eller belægning på 
glasset påvirker tidspunktet for første brud i det inderste glas. Resultaterne viser, at der var forskel på tiden til første 
brud på det yderste glas. For de traditionelle termoruder er tiden til første brud på yderste glas kortere end for alle 
lavenergivinduerne både 2-lags og 3-lags. Der er en væsentlig forskel i antallet af brud på det yderste glas, når de 
traditionelle termoruder sammenlignes med begge typer af lav energi vinduer. Det indikerer, at rudernes termiske 
egenskaber har indflydelse på integriteten under varmestråling. Baseret på vinduerne i disse eksperimenter, deres 
dimensioner, karmtyper og belægning kombineret med forsøgsopstillingen, var det tydeligt, at eksponeringen for 
varmestråling alene ikke resulterer i væsentligt udfald fra alle glaslag på hverken de traditionelle termoruder, 2-lags 
lavenergiruder eller 3-lags lavenergiruder.

1. Introduction 
The ventilation conditions are fundamental for the fire dynamics in an enclosure. In an enclosure fire mainly two factors 
affect the energy released and the burning rate. One is that the enclosure will result in hot gasses at the ceiling level 
and the enclosure surfaces will be heated. The radiation from the hot surfaces and the hot gas layer will radiate heat 
towards the burning item and enhance the burning rate. Second is that the openings (doors and windows) in the 
enclosure may restrict the availability of oxygen causing a decrease in the energy release rate (Karlsson & Quintiere, 
2000). Building structures are affected by the enclosure fire dynamics, and changes in the ventilation conditions could 
influence the building structures and the fire safety level of the building. The improvement of glazing in building facades 
and the energy optimization of older building leads to questions such as, is it correct to assume that the glass in windows 
break and leave a vent to the outside? And questions like when and why does the glass fail, how much fails and what 
are the consequences? And what influence does the number of glass layers and the thermal properties of the window 
have on the breaking time, breaking pattern, and fallout? 
These topics are relevant both in designing new buildings and in renovating older buildings. New buildings only accounts 
for a small part of the total building mass, and demolition of existing buildings is limited (KEB, 2014). There is an 
increasing focus on sustainability and on limiting energy consumption, and in relations to this there are many 
advantages to energy renovation, including an improvement in the value and quality of the buildings, as well as an 
improvement in the indoor climate and daylight conditions. One of the most easily accessible areas of energy 
optimization is window replacement. However, it is also important to keep an eye on whether an energy renovation 
affects other aspects, such as fire safety in a property. 
As fire safety engineers, we see different kinds of projects where knowledge on breaking conditions of windows is 
relevant. When designing buildings many assumptions related to the ventilation conditions in enclosure fires are made. 
In the design of new buildings there is often a fire safety engineer involved, but even in these cases it can be difficult to 
determine what is correct in relations to ventilation of enclosures during fire scenarios. In the case of energy 
optimization there might not be a fire safety engineer involved but changing the existing windows with one or two layers 
of glass to energy efficient windows with three layers of glass may have an impact on the fire safety of the building in 
relations to the duration of the heating of structural elements, the working conditions for the firefighters and the safety 
for the occupants.

1.1 Opening factor 
The behavior of windows during a fire is relevant both in designing new buildings and in relation to energy renovation 
of existing buildings. For renovating existing buildings, the investigation of whether new and more energy efficient 
windows are more resistant to the effects of a fire, is important to ensure the desired fire safety level. Research is 
needed to establish whether replacing windows leads to a change in the opening factor and a change in the enclosure 
fire dynamics.

The equation for calculation of the opening factor contains the values for the total area of vertical openings in all walls,

conservatively be set as 0,02 m½ for constructions made of concrete, wood, and fire protected steel. The opening factor 
is to be conservatively set as the most critical value in relation to heating of the constructions. This makes it interesting 
to investigate how varying opening factors affect the gas temperature and duration of the fire in the enclosure, and 
thus also affecting the constructions. In Figure 1-1 the gas temperature-time curves for an enclosure with thermal inertia 
b = 1160 J/m²s1/2K and fire load q = 200 MJ/m² are plotted for varying opening factors. The gas temperature is based on 
Equation 1-1. A thermal inertia of 1160 J/m²s1/2K is often used and represents a standard enclosure in a traditional 
masonry building. For buildings with highly insulated internal surfaces the thermal inertia could be close to 100 J/m²s1/2K 
and if the building only consists of concrete structures the thermal inertia can be close to 1400 J/m²s1/2K (Hertz, 2006).  
For the graphs in Figure 1-1 the thermal inertia and fire load is the same for all opening factors. When comparing the 
graphs, it is evident that the opening factor has a great influence on the temperature and the duration. A high opening 
factor results in an increase in the temperature and a decrease in the fire duration. A low opening factor results in a 
decrease of the temperature and an increase in the fire duration. A longer fire duration results in a longer period of 
heating the building structures, which could result in failure.

According to Hertz, a fire with a low opening factor and longer duration in general has a greater influence on the building 
structures because it takes time for the heat to penetrate a cross section by conduction. For most concrete structures 
and most insulated steel structures, the low opening factor will result in a more severe effect, because the duration of 
the fire is longer and results in a long exposure time. For these structures the effect of the heat exposure time is greater 
than the effect of the gas temperature in the enclosure. Some light steel structures might be more affected by a fire 
with a large opening factor, which results in a higher gas temperature. For uninsulated steel structures the temperature 
of the steel will follow the gas temperature in the enclosure, but with some delay (Hertz, 2006). 
 
For wooden structures a larger opening factor results in an increase in the charring rate. But the total charring depth is 
decreased when the opening factor increases because the total fire duration has a direct influence on the total charring 
depth.

1.2 Literature study 
This literature study describes a selection of prior relevant research which has been done in studies on glazing and the 
reaction to fire, with a focus on the methods, equipment and test specimens and in particular multiple layer glazing. 
Furthermore, literature in form of articles and books have been used in the Theory chapter to explain the relevant 
theory in the report.  
According to Jørgensen the investigation on glazing related to fire began with a study done by two students from 
Harvard in 1977, they irradiated glazing with a shaded frame using a radiant source. The study stood alone for a long 
time, but after a decade Emmons in 1986 published an article and highlighted the need for development within this 
topic, because of the high impact on compartment fire dynamics (Emmons, 1986). From the previous study from 1977 
Emmons suggested that glazing failure happens because of the poor conductivity of glass, which leads to tension in the 
cold glass hidden in the frame due to the thermal expansion of the heated central part of the glass. After the suggestions 
from Emmons, Keski-Rahkonen in 1988 performed a theoretical study which established criterions for the breakage of 
the brittle glass based on the temperature difference between shaded part of the glass and the irradiated part of the 
glass and the glazing’s tensional strength (Keski-Rahkonen, 1988). After the study done by Keski-Rahkonen, Skelly et al. 
did an experimental study in the beginning of the 1990s on the breaking of single layer glass with an aluminum frame 
in a compartment fire (Skelly, 1990). In the following years several other studies were made, both experimental and 
theoretically, on this subject. Since then, numerous studies have been performed by Hassani, Shields, Pagni and Wang 
among others investigating multiple glasses, laminated glass, coated glass, and other aspects of glazing in fires 
(Jørgensen, 2018). For double layer glazing, the investigations in general showed that they lasted longer than single 
layer glazing, partly because the first layer of glass will act as a radiant filter to the subsequent layers. 
The study of relevant literature shows that different test methods have been used. As a heating source both pan fires, 
radiation panel, and compartment fires have been used in various experimental setups. Furthermore, a variety of 
different test specimen has been used with different window dimension, shading areas, layers of glass, coating, and 
types of glass.  
In the studies done by Shields, Silcock, and Flood in 2001 the experimental program included 19 experiments with single 
layers of float glass 6 mm thick. Glass specimens with dimensions of 845 x 845 mm and 1897 x 845 mm were placed in 
an enclosure 3,6 x 2,4 x 2,4 m high instrumented to ISO room standards. Experiments were conducted with five different 
sizes of pan fires and with a ventilation opening in the room. In the setup the fire source is liquid fuel in a pan placed in 
the corner of the room. The glass specimens with the dimension 845 x 845 mm were placed above each other so that 
one was in the cold layer and the other in the hot gas layer, beside them the glass specimen with the dimension 1897 
x 845 mm positioned both in the hot and cold layer. To measure the temperature thermocouples was used. Video 
cameras was recording the fire growth, crack patterns and the fire induced damage. The results showed that fallout 
happened in 14 out of 19 experiments for the small window in the hot layer and in 5 out of 19 experiments for the 
small window in the cold layer. The window placed in both layers had fallout in 18 out of 19 experiments (Shields, 
Silcock, & Flood, 2001).  
In 2002 Shields, Silcock, and Flood did a study including 14 experiments with similar glass specimens and method, but 
this time the fire source was placed in the center of the room instead of the corner, and with four different pan sizes 
as the fire source, only the four largest pan sizes from the former study was used. The results showed that fallout 
happened 6 out of 14 times for the small window in the hot layer and 4 out of 14 times for the small window in the cold 
layer. The window placed in both layers had fallout 13 out of 14 times (Shields, Silcock, & Flood, 2002). 
Some years later in 2005 Shields, Silcock, and Flood did 15 experiments with double glazing windows and the same 
setup as earlier. The dimensions of the windows were the same as in the two studies mentioned above, but the windows 
consisted of two pieces of 6 mm glass separated by 12 mm air. The results showed that fallout happened 10 out of 15

Another study that investigates multiple layers of glass is from Wang in 2016, where specimens with the dimension 600 
x 600 x 18 mm (two 6 mm glass, separated by 6 mm air) were tested. In this research nine double glazing units were 
heated by a 0,5 x 0,5 m pool fire, and data on the incident heat flux, temperatures, breakage time and cracking behavior 
was collected. In the experiments the distance between the pool fire and the test specimens was gradually decreased 
from 750 mm to 450 mm. The glass was supported in a frame of stainless steel and had a shaded edge of 20 mm. The 
temperatures were measured on four sides by attaching type K sheet thermocouples on each side of the glass with high 
temperature resistant adhesive. Thermocouples were attached before assembling and sealing the glazing units. Because 
of direct radiation heating on these thermocouples, uncertainties in temperature measurement were estimated to 5 %. 
For measuring the incident heat flux a water-cooled heat flux gauge was placed flush to the glass surface. Results 
showed that the glass at the ambient side did withstand significantly longer than the glass closest to the fire. The heat 
flux at the time of crack initiation at the fire side glass was 6 kW/m², and more than 25 kW/m² for the glass at the 
ambient side. Only in 1 out of the 9 tests did both layers of glass crack. In the test with a burner distance of 450 mm the 
glass at the fire side cracked after 96 s and the ambient side glass after 617 s (Yu Wang, 2016). 
The experiments done by Jørgensen in 2018 focuses on investigating the influence of shading width one glass specimens 
exposed to radiation. In Jørgensens study the investigated test specimens were 400 x 400 x 4 mm float glass, and they 
were tested as single layers of glass. Four different shading widths (10, 15, 20 and 25 mm) were exposed to radiation 
levels of 5, 10, and 15 kW/m². The radiative source was a H-TRIS and levels of heat flux was achieved by adjusting both 
the burner intensity and the distance between the burner and the test specimens. The glass was mounted in a window 
frame replica in front of the H-TRIS. To measure the temperature type K thermocouples were used and a water-cooled 
heat flux gauge was used to measure the heat flux during the tests. The results showed that for the low heat flux levels 
the shading width influences the time to first crack. But for the heat flux levels above 10 kW/m² the shading width does 
not have a significant impact on the time to first crack. For heat flux higher than 15 kW/m² the time to first crack is less 
than one minute (Jørgensen, 2018). 
When reviewing previous studies on glass breakage in fires they include both single layers of glass and multi-layer 
windows. Based on the variety of the tested specimens in previous studies it would be interesting to investigate and 
compare 2-layer windows with 3-layer windows. Based on the literature study the most widely used setup in the 
research included are radiation of glass by a pan or pool fire, and in some of the experimental setups the glass was 
placed in the wall of an enclosure. Also, in one study the glass specimens were heated by a radiation panel. The literature 
study and additional research on breakage of glass shows that previous research used either pool fires resulting in 
varying heat flux levels imposed on the glass or used steady heat flux levels in the range from 5 - 25 kW/m². The aim of 
this project is to replicate the same test several times and compare the behavior of different types of windows. At the 
DTU Fire Laboratory it is possible to use a Heat-Transfer Rate Inducing System which gives the possibility of controlling 
the heat flux and creating the same conditions for all experiments. The literature study shows that the most commonly 
used method for measuring temperature was by using type K thermocouples and the measuring of the heat flux with a 
water-cooled heat flux gauge. To register the cracks and fallout during the experiment previous studies used video 
cameras.

1.3 Project limitations  
This project is based on an experimental study of the fire and breakage properties for traditional double-glazed windows

2.1.1 Traditional double-glazed windows  
Traditional double-glazed windows were the preferred type of window from the 1960´s onwards but since the 1990´s 
the traditional double-glazed window has been phased out in favor of low energy windows (Bolius, 2023). They are 
however still produced and can be used in buildings where the respective building codes has little or no requirements 
with regards to the building’s energy saving capabilities. 
The traditional double-glazed windows consist of two layers of glass separated by a cavity filled with air as illustrated in

A low-emission coating is characterized by minimizing the amount of ultraviolet and infrared light that can pass through 
the glass, without compromising the amount of visible light transmitted. The solar energy spectrum consists of 
ultraviolet light, visible light and infrared light and the difference between them are determined by their wavelengths. 
Ultraviolet light (UV light) has wavelengths, λ, of about 10 to 400 nanometers. The visible light has wavelengths of about 
400 to 700 nanometers and the infrared light begins at wavelengths of about 700 nanometers (UCAR, 2023). Infrared 
light is heating energy and heat radiating from a warm object is referred to as long-wave infrared. Solar infrared is 
known as short-wave infrared energy. For a material the ability to radiate energy is known as emissivity and highly 
reflective materials have a low emissivity. The low-emission coating reduced the emissivity on the glass surface and 
reflects long-wave infrared energy (heat) which results in improved insulating properties. The coating reflects the heat 
from inside the room and minimizes heat loss to the outside. Some low-emission coatings also reflect significant 
amounts of short-wave solar infrared energy and minimize the solar heating of buildings. 
There are two types of coating commonly known as “hard coat” and “soft coat”. The hard coat is applied to the glass in 
the production process and the soft coat is applied postproduction. Low-emission coating is mainly soft coat and is 
mostly placed on the surfaces facing the cavity in the window to protect the coating (Vitro, 2023).

2.2.2 Fracture mechanics and fallout 
Unlike most of the other construction materials used, glass consist of an irregular network of silicon and oxygen atoms 
with alkaline parts between them. This structure of the molecules has no slip planes or dislocations to allow plastic ﬂow 
before fracture, thus in normal temperatures glass will behave as a perfectly elastic material and the inability to yield 
plastically before fracture will make it fail without warning, when exposed to the critical level of stress.  
An important factor in the breakage of glass are small pre-existing flaws, referred to as Griffith flaws. These are flaws 
on the surface and they are not necessarily visible to the naked eye. The flaws produce a very high stress concentration 
and have a severely weakening effect on the brittle solid. This is the reason why the strength is so unpredictable and 
low, (Haldimann, Luible, & Overend, 2008).  
When the fracture starts, it will quickly spread across the glass, and sometimes it divides. After the first fracture in the 
glass, additional fractures continue to develop. Pieces of glass falls out when “islands of glass” is isolated from the frame, 
and when the glass falls out a vent in the compartment will be created. From limited data Pagni indicates that both the 
frame details, window aspect ratio and the heating intensity all are factors when dealing with fallout of glass, (Pagni, 
2002).

the same pattern can be found on both sides after the glass has cracked. The fracture surface for glass is divided in three

2.3 Heat transfer theory 
There are three types of heat exchange conduction, convection, and radiation. The three types of heat exchange are 
briefly described in the following, mainly based on chapter 8 in Fire Safety Engineering and performance based codes, 
(Sørensen, 2014).  
• 
Conduction is a molecular energy transport, for example heat travelling through a wall. 
• 
Convection is the heat transfer between a moving fluid, for example hot gasses, and a solid. Convection is a 
type of conduction between a moving fluid and a solid boundary combined with the energy transport due to 
the movement of the fluid, for example hot gasses moving up from the fire.  
• 
Radiation is an electromagnetic energy transfer, for example radiation from a fire towards a window or a wall.

radiated to a point target as shown in Figure 2-10. 
The equation only applies to a target looking directly at the center of the flame. The calculations have been shown to 
have uncertainties up to +/- 50 %, but typically within +/- 30 %.

2.3.5 Transparency of glass influencing the absorbed radiation 
When radiation from an emitter radiates towards a receiver e.g., a windowpane, it will either be absorbed, reflected, 
or transmitted as shown in Equation 2-14. The fraction of incident energy absorbed is known as absorption coefficient, 
αλ. The fraction of incident energy reflected is known as reflection coefficient, ρλ, and the fraction of incident energy 
transmitted is known as transmission coefficient, τλ. Only the energy absorbed will result in heating of the glass. All three 
coefficients depend on the material, the wavelength, λ, of the incident energy and on the temperature of the material 
(Sørensen, 2014). Figure 2-11 illustrates the transmitted, reflected and absorbed radiation. 
 
𝛼𝜆+ 𝜌𝜆+ 𝜏𝜆= 1 
Equation 2-14 
For all wavelengths the reflection coefficient can be assumed to be 10 % for soda-lime glass. With a typical flame 
temperature of 1250 K, 25 % of the incident radiation will be transmitted, and since 10 % is assumed to be reflected, 
the remaining 65 % will be absorbed (Pagni, 2002).  
 
Figure 2-11: The absorbed, reflected, and transmitted radiation. 
Because of the transparency of the glass a fraction of the incident energy will be transmitted through the glass. The 
transmitted energy and the reflected energy will not contribute to the heating of the glass.  
In multi-layer windows where all layers of glass are made of the same type of glass, the first glass will act as a band pass 
filter. The incident radiation transmitted through the first glass will also be transmitted through the subsequent layers 
of glass. Therefore, the incident radiation will only heat the first exposed glass in multi-layer windows. Although the 
incident radiation will not heat up the next layer of glass, until the first layer fails, it does not mean that the subsequent 
layers will not heat up. The temperature in the subsequent layers will increase due to conduction and reradiation from 
the heated layer.

2.4 Heat release rate 
Heat release rate (HRR) or energy release rate is the amount of energy or heat released per unit time from a burning 
object. It is usually given in kW (kJ/s) and denoted, 𝑄̇. The heat release rate in an enclosure depends on variables such 
as the type, quantity, and geometry of the fuel and on the characteristics of the enclosure (materials and vents). The 
fire curve consists of three phases; the growth phase, the steady phase, and the decay phase as illustrated in Figure 
2-12. This fire curve does not take the initial ignition time into account, and the fire is assumed to start growing from t 
= 0 (Karlsson & Quintiere, 2000).

- 
Compare traditional double-glazed windows, and low energy windows with 2 and 3 layers of glazing. 
-

-

3.1.2 Heating panel (H-TRIS) 
To expose the samples to radiation a Heat Transfer Rate Inducing System, referred to as H-TRIS, is used. This test method 
enables studies of how different test specimens reacts to heating by radiation. The system consists of a gas burner 
(radiation panel) mounted on a railing system that allows the burner to move back and forth towards the test specimen, 
see a photo of the heating panel in Figure 3-6. The burner measures 0,5 x 0,5 m and is supplied by natural gas which is 
supplied by the gas system in the laboratory, and the fan supplies the air for the combustion. The burner on the H-TRIS 
is controlled by a small panel on the top of the box, see photo in Figure 3-5. The burner intensity can be varied from 0

multiple repeats of tests on several specimens within a relatively low economic range compared to larger scale 
experiments in a test laboratory (Maluk, Bisby, Krajcovic, & Torero, 2016). In front of the burner a flexible framing 
system makes it possible to create a frame to hold the test specimens in place during the test, see photo in Figure 3-7.

3.1.3 Device for measuring temperature 
The temperature on the inner and outer glass during the experiment is measured with the use of K-type thermocouples 
(TC), which has a measuring point in the end of a wire. The wire consists of two conductors and is measuring the voltage 
in the end of the wire which is placed on the glass. The TC is easy to use and can withstand high temperatures. The 
choice of using the TC is based on the previous study of measuring the temperature on glass carried out by Jørgensen 
(Jørgensen, 2018). 
3.1.4 Device for measuring heat flux

Excel file, which makes it possible to visualize the changes in heat flux over time for the duration of the experiments.

the related datasheet, the value for the specific sensor is S = 0,477 ∙ 10-6 V/(W/m²) with a calibration uncertainty ± 0,031 
∙ 10-6 V/(W/m²). 
The Hukseflux is connected to a water-cooling system, which consists of a water pump placed in a tub filled with water.

was done to expose an old window to heat in a longer period. 
Since the flames on the window frames, in some of the windows tested in the original experimental plan, spread from 
the inside to the outside of the window frame, an extra test with a window installed in a wall was planned and 
performed. This was to test if the window behaved differently when installed in a wall, this test is named WA.2.5. 
3.3.2 Description  
The aim of this research was to compare traditional double-glazed windows to low energy windows. To compare the 
windows, it was a high priority that the test specimens had comparable sizes, and that there were at least three test 
specimens of each type, so the results could support each other for each window type. Furthermore, it was interesting 
to compare different types of frames.  
The experimental setup has limitations in terms of the size of windows that can be placed in the frame. Also, the 
radiation panel has a size of 0,5 x 0,5 m as described in sections 3.1.2. This means that when testing specimens exceeding 
the dimensions of the radiation panel, only a part of the specimen can be directly irradiated.  
It proved quite difficult to procure the test specimens. In particular the traditional double-glazed windows were rather 
difficult to get a hold on. In the process several different companies were contacted, including window manufacturer, 
carpentry companies, window replacement companies and thrift shops. Some window replacement companies offered 
to donate windows that were on the way to being discarded, but they were all too large to fit in the experimental setup.  
The result of it was that the traditional double-glazed windows was bought from a thrift shop. All the traditional double-
glazed windows have dimensions in the range of 0,6 m x 0,6 m. Specimens of this size was able to fit in the experimental 
setup, and the entire glass area could be heated by the radiation panel on the H-TRIS. In this size it was possible to get 
several specimens of the same type within the economical range of the project. The traditional windows have been 
installed in a building and therefore has been used before included in this project. This is not seen as a disadvantage as 
it replicates the windows in a building in a real fire, but also results in traces of use on the specimens. One of the 
disadvantages is that it has not been possible to contact the manufacturer of most of the traditional windows. 
Furthermore, the storage of the windows before they were bought have not been documented.

3.4 Experimental setup 
In this section the setup and method used for the final experiments is described. From the pilot experiments described 
in section 3.2 a to-do list was made which described the procedures to follow before and during each experiment, this 
was composed to ensure that the same procedure was followed for each experiment. This list can be seen in Appendix 
A.  
Based on the Danish Building Regulations it was decided that the duration of the experiments should be at least 60 min. 
For a multi-storey apartment building (top floor max 12 m above the ground) the requirements for the load-bearing 
constructions are 60 min according to Danish legislation. This proved to be a challenge during the first tests (specimen 
A.2.1, A.2.2 and A.2.3) because the window frames ignited, and the tests was stopped for safety reasons and to ensure 
no damage was done on to the radiation panel and the frame system. The first three tests were stopped earlier than 
planned and the setup was reviewed. Changes were made to protect the framing system that held the window in place. 
A protective plate was added on the top frame to prevent the flames affecting the framing system. The remaining nine 
windows were then tested for 60 min according to the plan, after the heating period the radiation panel was turned off 
and the windows were left in the frame for a cooling period.  
After completing the original experimental plan two extra experiments were added. A traditional double-glazed window 
with wooden frame (A.2.4) was tested to get data for a 60 min test of a traditional double-glazed window. This 
experiment continued for approximately 40 min until the risk of the window falling out of the frame system made it 
necessary to stop the experiment. The upper part of the window frame had deteriorated, and the fixation of the window 
was compromised.

4.1.2 Breaking patterns and fallout 
In all the experiments conducted every glass in every window cracked at some point during the heating phase. The 
breaking pattern for the first crack of the inner, middle and outer glass are shown in Figure 4-3, Figure 4-4, and Figure 
4-5. The breaking patterns at test stop of the inner, middle, and outer glass are shown in Figure 4-6, Figure 4-7, and

The results show that in one experiment out of 14 there is fallout at first crack of the inner glass (specimen B.2.2). None 
of the experiment with three-layer specimens has fallout from the middle glass at first crack and in one of the 
experiments the outer glass has fallout at first crack (specimen B.2.1). 
The breaking patterns seems to have an influence on whether glass falls out. Fallout happens when pieces of glass are 
separated from the frame by the breaking pattern. In the study Thermal Glass Breakage, Pagni concludes that glass 
fallout occurs when cracking isolates “islands” of the glass away from the frame (Pagni, 2002). This is also confirmed by 
Wang in an interview about glazing in fire in the podcast series Fire Science Show (Wang, 2022). This can be recognized 
in the experiments done in this project. The data collected and the video recordings of the experiments show that glass 
fallout occurs from “islands” of glass and later in the experiments when the frame deteriorates this also leads to fallout.  
The data indicate that the breaking patterns are completely random. This corresponds well with previous studies 
describing that cracks occur where there are small flaws in the glass from cutting or the edge of the glass is accidentally 
damaged (Mecholsky, Rice, & Freiman, 1974) (Jørgensen, 2018).

frames influences the probability of glass falling out. The sealant and joints holding the glass in place could affect the 
probability of glass pieces falling out after the glass have cracked, because the pieces along the edges are fixed to the 
frame. The larger fallouts from specimens in this study are seen after 20 min which means that the windows have been 
heated by 25 kW/m2 for approximately 10 min, this could have caused the sealant and joints to fail.  
The gas filling in the cavities does not seem to make a difference for neither the cracking pattern nor the fallout. 
Jørgensen proposed whether pressure would build up in the gas filled cavities between layers of glass, preventing glass 
pieces from leaning inwards (Jørgensen, 2018). The experiments in this report does not indicate that pieces of glass are 
hindered in leaning inwards. This could be explained by the multiple cracking in the glass releasing any pressure from 
the cavity as soon as the inner glass cracks. It was observed that some of the fallouts of glass landed between the 
middle/outer and the inner glass.

When comparing the measured heat flux, differences are seen. The differences can be explained by the fact that even 
small changes in the distance to the heating panel when moving the H-TRIS will give a variation in the measurement. 
Furthermore, if the windows were not placed in perfect level this will also result in variations in the measurements. The 
angle of the Hukseflux sensor also results in deviations between the different tests.

As described in section 2.3.3 the heat flux can be calculated by Equation 2-11. For calculating the heat flux, it is 
necessary to know the emissivity. Since the emissivity for the radiation panel is not known it becomes an equation with

references for the emissivity.

5. Conclusion 
A series of experiments were conducted to investigate the difference in fire and breakage properties for traditional 
windows and low-energy windows, when exposed to radiation from a heating panel. Four different types of windows 
were included in the study: traditional double-glazed windows with wooden frame, 2-layer low energy windows with 
wood/alu frame, 3-layer low energy windows with wooden frame and 3-layer low energy windows with wood/alu 
frame.  
Pilot experiments showed that the use of a Heat Transfer Rate Inducing System (H-TRIS) enabled replicating of a fire 
growth phase. Based on a design fire for living accommodations from the Danish Building Regulations a heat flux curve 
in 5 steps was designed for the growth phase, and after this a steady phase for a total duration of 60 min. The pilot 
experiments also showed that the methods for measuring temperatures and heat flux found in the literature study were 
applicable to this study.  
All the windows were exposed to the same heat flux curve, but the experiments on the traditional double-glazed 
windows was terminated before 60 min. The results of the experiments indicate that there is no significant difference 
in the time to first crack of the inner glass on the four different types of windows included in this report, and there is no 
indication that the type of frame or coating influences the time to first crack of the inner glass. This could be because 
the frame types in these experiments are very similar. All frame types are wood, and on the wood/alu frames the 
aluminum is only a protective cover mounted on the outside of the wooden frame. For 9 specimens the first crack on 
the inner glass happens within 350 s where they are exposed to a radiant heat flux of 5 kW/m², and for 5 specimens the 
first crack happens in the time range with a radiant heat flux of 10 kW/m². For all middle and outer glass layers crack 
initiation happens with a radiant heat flux of 25 kW/m². There is a significant difference in the time to first crack of the 
outer glass. For the traditional double-glazed windows, the time to first crack of the outer glass is shorter than for all 
the low-energy windows both 2-layer and 3-layer. Even when comparing the two types of windows with 2-layers, the 
low-energy window has a longer average time to first crack of the outer glass. The results indicate that the time to first 
crack of the outer glass is affected by the insulating ability of the pane including the low-emission coating and this could 
cause the heat transfer to the outer glass to be slower. This indicates that the thermal property of the pane has an 
influence on the integrity during heat radiation. 
In all the experiments conducted every glass in every window cracked at some point during the heating phase. The 
results show that in one experiment out of 14 there is fallout at first crack on the inner glass (specimen B.2.2 – 2-layer 
low energy window). None of the experiment with three layer-glass has fallout from the middle glass at first crack and 
in one of the experiments the outer glass has fallout at first crack (specimen B.2.1 – 2-layer low energy window). The 
data shows that the breaking patterns are completely random. The breaking patterns seems to have an influence on 
whether glass falls out. The data collected and the video recordings of the experiments show that glass fallout occurs 
from “islands” of glass that are separated from the frame by the breaking pattern. Fallout from all layers occurs on one 
specimen. Since the fallout of the outer glass is less than 1 % of the visible glass area, the results indicate a very low 
opening factor based on the experiments of this report. There is no indication that the different frame types in these 
experiments influences the probability of fallout or the percentage of glass falling out. When comparing the fallout from 
the four different types of windows there is no substantial difference even when considering the fact, that the duration 
of the experiments on some of the traditional double-glazed windows were reduced.  
Based on the test specimens of these experiments, their dimensions, frame types, and coating combined with the 
experimental setup, it is evident that the exposure to heat radiation alone does not result in significant fallout from all 
glass layers on either 2-layered or 3-layered test specimens.

6. Future work 
Based on the investigations, experiments, and results of this project a variety of other aspects and subjects to be 
investigated further has come to our attention. In continuation of this project several subjects will be relevant to 
investigate in future work: 
 
• 
It would be interesting to investigate a greater number of specimens to get a more valid indication on the 
results.  
 
• 
Most windows used today have one or more layers of low emissivity coatings and could also have solar control 
low emissivity coatings. It is relevant to investigate and understand how different types of coatings affect the 
breakage and fallout of glass. 
 
• 
The windows included in the experiments in this report all have dimensions of approximately 600 x 600 mm. It 
is highly relevant to investigate whether larger windows lead to a larger percentage of fallout.    
 
• 
How does different frame types affect the heating and breakage of the glass. In this report only two types of 
frames were included, and they are very similar because they are both made of wood. One type has a protective 
cover of aluminum on the exterior side. Which difference does it make if the frame contains a larger amount 
of alu, consists of PVC or is made from other materials? 
 
• 
The experimental setup in this report limited to the effect of radiation imposed on the windows, and it does 
not include the pressure and hot gas layer that would be present in an enclosure fire. The influence of a fire in 
an enclosure is relevant to investigate further. Does the pressure in the room affect the breakage and fallout 
of glass? It is also relevant to study if it affects more than one window in the same enclosure or if the pressure 
will be equalized after fallout from one window.

Calculation of the temperature 
During the experiments the temperature on the inner and outer glass is measured as described in section 3.2.4 and 3.4. 
The resulting temperatures at the middle of the inner and outer glass are presented in section 4.2. In this appendix hand 
calculations of the temperature are presented and discussed in section 4.2 in relation to the measured temperatures 
and the calculation method.  
To calculate the temperature Equation 2-5 is used in a modified version. Equation 2-5 as given in section 2.3.1 is for a 
building element facing outdoors, for example a window. In the experiments the windows are not facing the outdoors 
but the lab, and therefore the transitional thermal resistance factor for other building elements is used. 
Test specimen B.2.3 
The measured temperature on the inner glass is an average of approximately 375 °C in the time range 2500 s – 3500 s. 
The temperature measured on the outer glass in the same time range has an average of approximately 110 °C. The 
thermal resistance for the pane of the test specimen is given by the manufacturer as Ug = 1,19 and this value includes 
the transitional thermal resistance factors Ri = 0,13 and Ro = 0,04 for a building element facing the outdoors, which gives 
a thermal resistance of 0,67 without the transitional thermal resistance factors. The temperature on the outer glass can 
then be calculated as

Appendix F. Scope 
The scope submitted and approved at project start-up, October 2022.  
Among the 17 global goals listed by UN, there are headings such as Sustainable cities and local communities, Responsible 
consumption and production and Climate action. Energy renovation of existing buildings can contribute to both energy 
optimization and lower consumption and there by address several of the global goals.  
When energy renovating existing buildings, it is common to replace the existing double glazed windows with more 
energy optimized products. This means that the existing windows are replaced with low energy glazing. In connection 
with an energy renovation few are aware that by replacing the windows it can influence the fire safety. 
Therefore, it is interesting to investigate whether new and more energy efficient windows are more resistant to the 
effects of a fire, and whether it leads to a changed course of a fire in the room or building and how the conditions for 
the fire are affected. It is therefore relevant to investigate when low energy windows break and their breaking pattern 
and compare it with breaking time and breaking pattern of double glazing.  
At the same time this knowledge is relevant in connection with the design of new buildings, as the expected course of 
the fire and opening factor in the glazing is important to determine which impacts the load-bearing structures must be 
able to withstand. 
In this assignment we want to investigate the following points experimentally: 
1. At what time do the double glazed windows break? 
How long must the windows be affected by a given radiation before it leads to breakage in one or more layers 
of glass and how much glass will fall out? Do different frame types make a difference? 
2. What breaking pattern is there for double glazed windows? 
Which fracture patterns appear and on which layer? Does it cause one or more pieces of glass to fall out? How 
large a proportion of the area falls out? 
3. At what time do the low energy windows break? 
How long must the windows be affected by a given radiation before it leads to breakage in one or more layers 
of glass and how much glass will fall out? Do different frame types make a difference? 
4. What breaking pattern is there for low energy windows? 
Which fracture patterns appear and on which layer? Does it cause one or more pieces of glass to fall out? How 
large a proportion of the area falls out? 
 
To investigate the points above experimentally, the H-TRIS (Heat Transfer Rate Inducing System) setup in the DTU Firelab 
will be used, where the test items can have a size of up to 1 x 1 m. The goal is to test different window specimens 
introduced to a uniform imposed heat flux and measure temperatures on the inner side (heated side) of the glass and 
on the outer side (ambient side). For each test the breaking pattern and area of glass falling out will be documented. 
The setup and the radiation level, for which the final tests will be carried out with, will be determine in an initial 
preliminary study. 
From the experiments it is interesting to look at the differences in the windows and how, for example different frame 
types affect the windows behavior in a fire, and based on the results from the experiments, how does it influence the 
course of the fire in the building and why?
