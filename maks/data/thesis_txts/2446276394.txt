Abstract  
During the last decades, the prevalence of IgE-mediated allergic diseases has increased more than two-fold, 
with approximately 30% of the population in industrialized countries having developed an allergy. The 
cause of this increase has been associated with several aspects of the western lifestyle, including a diet low 
in dietary fiber. A diet high in dietary fiber has been shown to provide a protective effect against the 
development of allergic diseases. Often, this protective effect has been linked to the metabolites produced 
as end-products of dietary fiber fermentation by certain species of gut microbes. These metabolites include 
the short-chain fatty acids acetate, propionate, and butyrate. Particularly butyrate has been well-
documented to regulate immune cell function by various means, including the modulation of cytokine 
secretion and the induction of regulatory T cells. In this master thesis project, the in vitro 
immunomodulatory effect of butyrate on PBMCs obtained from house dust mite allergic patients were 
studied. PBMCs were isolated and stimulated with both non-specific T cell and allergen-specific stimuli in 
the absence and presence of butyrate at different concentrations. It was found that butyrate inhibited 
PBMC proliferation in a dose-dependent manner. Furthermore, it was found that butyrate at 
concentrations above 1 mM induced apoptotic cell death, likely through activation of the caspase-cascade. 
At a non-toxic dose, butyrate was found to downregulate the secretion of IFN-γ and IL-5, while upregulating 
the secretion of IL-10 from PBMCs of eight HDM allergic donors. In preliminary experiments, butyrate was 
found to alter the phenotypic differentiation process of monocyte-derived dendritic cells. Altogether, the 
results indicate that butyrate regulates immune cell function through inhibition of proliferation, induction 
of apoptosis, modulation of cytokine secretion and through an altered activation state of dendritic cells. 
Thus, the findings of this thesis support further the interest for butyrate as a modulator of allergic 
responses.

1. Introduction  
1.1 Allergy and the western lifestyle  
During the last decades, a decrease in the prevalence of infectious diseases such as tuberculosis and measles 
has been observed[1]. Conversely, the incidence of immune-related disorders such as IgE-mediated allergic 
diseases has increased more than two-fold with approximately 30% of the population in industrialized 
countries having developed an allergy towards allergens such as pollens and house dust mites[2]. Often, this 
increase has been explained by the hygiene hypothesis. The hygiene hypothesis is based upon the idea that 
excess sanitation and antibiotic use have led to a dysregulated immune system, which has resulted in an 
increase in the prevalence of allergic- and inflammatory diseases[3]. However, contradictions to the hygiene 
hypothesis have been highlighted, including how the incidence of asthma in Japan, considered to be highly 
hygienic, is relatively low compared to the United States and Australia. In addition, despite a higher level of 
infectious diseases such as tuberculosis, an increased incidence of asthma is observed in economically 
disadvantaged populations in the United States[4][5]. Therefore, other factors of the western lifestyle, such 
as a diet generally high in fat and sugar while low in dietary fiber, has been suggested to be a contributing 
factor to the increasing incidences of allergic- and inflammatory diseases[6][7]. Dietary fibers are complex 
carbohydrates consisting of both soluble and insoluble components. While insoluble fibers provide important 
faecal bulking functions, soluble fibers can be fermented by certain species of gut bacteria, resulting in 
physiologically active metabolites; the most abundant being Short-Chain Fatty Acids (SCFAs). Various studies 
have shown that diets high in dietary fiber, resulting in increased SCFA production, provide a protective effect 
on the development of allergic- and inflammatory diseases in both mice and humans[8][9][10]. Due to the 
association of SCFAs to protection against allergic disease, an increased interest in the biology of dietary fiber 
and SCFAs is seen.  
1.2 The immune system and the allergic response 
The human body is protected from various pathogens, including bacteria, viruses, and parasites by a 
collection of effector cells and molecules that comprise the immune system. Ideally, the immune system is 
maintained in a balanced state of fighting pathogen-induced infections, while inducing tolerance to 
harmless proteins as well as commensal microbes within the microbiota. Although the immune responses 
initiated upon antigen recognition are crucial in providing host defence, in some instances, antigens not 
associated with infectious agents can elicit immune responses. An example hereof is hypersensitivity 
reactions towards harmless antigens such as pollen and food proteins. These immunologically mediated 
hypersensitivity reactions are known as allergic reactions, and most of these reactions occur after an 
individual has become sensitized to a harmless antigen – an allergen – leading to the production of specific 
==PAGE:10==
4 
 
IgE antibodies against it. Allergic reactions include allergic rhinitis, allergic conjunctivitis, asthma, atopic 
eczema, and food allergy[11]. 
The immune response leading to allergen-specific IgE production is initiated upon antigen-presenting cells 
(APCs), e.g., a dendritic cell (DC), capturing an allergen in the mucosa. APCs process and present fragments 
of the allergen in a complex with the human leucocyte antigen (HLA), encoded by the major 
histocompatibility complex (MHC), to naïve T cells. The naïve T cell responding to the allergen:MHC 
complex develop into an effector subset depending on various factors. These factors include the cytokines 
it is exposed to in the surrounding environment, the intrinsic properties and dose of the allergen, as well as 
the route of presentation. The development of Th2 cells is favoured by exposure to IL-4, IL-5, IL-9, and IL-
13. IL-13 and IL-4 secreted by Th2 cells provide the first signal for activated B cells to undergo class 
switching to IgE production. A co-stimulatory interaction between CD40 ligand on the surface of the T cell 
and CD40 on the surface of the B cell provides the second signal for IgE production. Allergen-specific IgE 
producing plasma cells travel back to the mucosa, in which secreted IgE binds to the high-affinity IgE-
receptor, FcεRI, present on mast cells and basophils[11][12][13]. 
The allergic inflammation following allergen sensitization can be divided into three phases; an early-phase, 
late phase, and a chronic allergic inflammation. In an early-phase reaction, occurring within seconds to 
minutes after a second encounter with the allergen, the allergen binds to IgE, crosslinking the FcεRI 
receptors, leading to degranulation of mast cells and the release of allergic symptom mediators, such as 
histamine. In many sensitized subjects, the early phase reaction is followed by a subsequent late-phase 
reaction, which peaks at approximately 6-9h after allergen exposure. The reaction is initiated by some of 
the released mediators from mast cells promoting local recruitment and activation of leucocytes including 
Th2 cells, eosinophils, and basophils. Chronic allergic inflammation can develop when exposure to the 
allergen is continuous or repetitive, which in many cases leads to changes in the structural cells at the 
affected sites, as well as an altered function of the affected organs. An example hereof is the development 
of allergic asthma, which is often based upon sensitization to allergens from house dust mites 
(HDM)[11][13][14]. 
HDM allergens are some of the major elicitors of allergic responses in westernized countries, and in Europe, 
an estimated 20% of the population is sensitized to HDM allergens[15]. HDM allergic patients are 
predominantly sensitized to allergens from the mite species Dermatophagoides pteronyssinus (Der p) and 
Dermatophagoides farinae (Der f). The mite allergens comprise of body proteins and of digestive enzymes 
of cells that have been detached from the gut wall of the mites and are discharged within faecal pellets. 
The allergens are so far characterized in groups from 1 to 24 based upon their molecular profile and their 
==PAGE:11==
5 
 
likely mode of action. The two most dominant groups are group 1 comprising cysteine proteases and group 
2 comprising lipid-binding proteins[14][16].

1.3 Immunomodulation 
The importance of a well-functioning and balanced immune system is essential for the maintenance of 
good health. The concept of modulating the immune system to improve health has been conducted for 
decades, and various pharmaceuticals are developed with the sole purpose of modulating immune 
responses. Immunomodulation can both aim to suppress unwanted immune responses, such as allergic 
responses, and to stimulate protective immune responses[17]. An example hereof is the use of allergen-
specific immunotherapy (AIT); a well-established method of modulating underlying immunologic 
mechanisms of allergic disease[18]. In addition to AIT, research within immunomodulation has primarily 
been focused on applying antibodies or kinase inhibitors to block cytokines and receptors involved in a 
particular immune response. Furthermore, having an essential role in maintaining the balance between 
immune activation and tolerance, regulatory T cells (Tregs) continues to be of high interest within the 
research of various immune-related disorders, aiming to develop new and efficient therapeutic 
interventions[17][19]. 
Recently, there has been a focus and interest in immunomodulation via dietary strategies. Various 
nutritional components have shown to be capable of interacting with the immune system and to be of 
importance for specific immune functions. Immune function is the target of the development of the so-
called functional foods, which aim to provide a beneficial effect to the consumer[17]. Some of the 
immunomodulatory dietary supplements that have gained awareness, and which have been studied 
extensively, are pro- and prebiotics. Probiotics are, by definition, “non-pathogenic microorganisms that, 
when given in adequate numbers, exert beneficial effects on the host.” Prebiotics are, by definition, 
“nondigestible food ingredients that beneficially affect the host by selectively stimulating the growth and/or 
activity of one or a limited number of bacteria in the colon, and thus improve host health”[20]. Synbiotics is 
a term covering the combination of pro- and prebiotics, and this combination is often applied in the 
development and production of functional food due to their capabilities of interacting with the 
microbiota[21]. The beneficial effect of synbiotics is highly related to the promotion of the growth of 
beneficial bacterial species (Lactobacilli and Bifidobacteria particularly) and the increased production of 
metabolites such as SCFAs. SCFAs have in many instances shown to have immunomodulatory properties, 
making them a target of interest in the study of various diseases and conditions, including allergy[22].  
1.4 The human gut microbiome 
The human microbiota comprises a wide-ranging collection of microbes, including bacteria, viruses, and 
fungi, colonizing several sites of the human body including the skin, oral cavity, genital, the respiratory tract, 
and the gut. Out of these sites, the human gut comprise the most extensive and dense collection, being 
colonized by up to 1014 microbes, which is the highest density recorded for any microbial habitat[23]. This 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
microbial habitat within the gut has co-evolved with the host providing a stable environment for the microbes 
to live, while the microbes in return provide the host with various functions such as digestion of dietary fiber, 
production of vitamins, defence against pathogens as well as development and maintenance of the immune 
system[24]. These are all properties that are essential for the health of the host. The microbiome community 
is dominated by bacterial members of the phyla Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes, and exhibits a high level of 
variation at the species and strains level, harbouring over 4000 different strains. Despite the fact that some 
bacterial phyla dominate the gut community, the gut ecosystem is a dynamic and competitive environment. 
In this regard, some microbes have established commensal status and will persist within the host. Some 
microbes, on the other hand, are transient members originating from, e.g., ingested food and water[23][25].  
The role of the human gut microbiota in health and disease is a subject that continues to be of high interest, 
as many links have been drawn between microbiota composition and several diseases including 
inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), metabolic- as well as allergic disorders. The dense microbial content of 
the gut poses a significant challenge for the mucosal immune system, as tolerance to commensal bacteria 
and harmless dietary proteins is required to avoid a constant state of inflammation[25]. Maintaining this 
tolerance while being able to initiate an immune response to eliminate pathogens is of high importance. 
Some of the key players in fostering immune homeostasis are Tregs, and though the exact underlying 
mechanisms of Treg induction in the colon remains unclear, various microbial components have been found 
capable of enhancing their expansion and function, including SCFAs[19]. The production of SCFAs is highly 
associated with microbiota composition, as gut bacteria produce variable amounts and types of SCFAs. The 
diet is furthermore highly associated with microbiota composition, diversity, and richness. Consumption of a 
diet high in fat and low in dietary fiber, characteristic of the westernized lifestyle, may alter the composition 
of the gut microbiota away from microbes efficient at digesting fiber, to that of microbes more suited to 
metabolise fat and sugar-rich foods. This alteration can ultimately lead to a reduction in the levels of SCFAs 
in the colon and at peripheral tissues[26][27]. 
1.5 Short-chain fatty acids 
SCFAs are metabolites produced as a result of dietary fiber fermentation by certain bacterial species within 
the host gut microbiota. Dietary fiber has been defined as “Carbohydrate polymers with three or more 
monomeric units that are resistant to the endogenous digestive enzymes and are thus neither hydrolysed 
nor absorbed in the small intestine”[28]. There are both soluble and insoluble components of dietary fibers. 
While insoluble fibers have important faecal bulking properties, soluble fibers can be fermented by certain 
gut microbes in the large intestine and thus lead to the production of SCFAs[8]. Chemically, SCFAs are 
composed of a carboxylic acid part and a small hydrocarbon chain. The most common and well-studied of 
the SCFAs are acetate, propionate, and butyrate, composed of two, three and four carbons, respectively, in

Due to a dense population of microbial producers, SCFAs are found in the gut lumen at high concentrations 
of approximately 70 to 140 mM. Conversely, the concentration of SCFAs in peripheral blood has been 
estimated to be much lower at 100 to 150 µM for acetate, 4 to 5 µM for propionate, and 1 to 3 µM for 
==PAGE:15==
9 
 
butyrate. This significant difference is due to an extensive uptake of SCFAs by intestinal epithelial cells 
(IECs)[29][30]. SCFAs are considered an essential energy source for IECs as they utilize them as a source of 
ATP[31]. However, a small proportion of SCFAs diffuses from the lumen through the epithelium and enters 
the peripheral circulation via the portal vein[32]. Being absorbed into the peripheral circulation, SCFAs can 
exert their effects (e.g., immune regulation) at peripheral tissues beyond the digestive system[21]. However, 
the level of SCFAs reaching and affecting peripheral tissues has yet to be fully elucidated[33]. 
SCFAs are known to be able to modify and regulate several cellular processes affecting host metabolism, 
immune system and cell differentiation and proliferation (e.g., by enhancing expansion and function of 
Tregs). These cellular modifications are often described to be based on both intra- and extracellular activities 
such as the inhibition of histone deacetylases (HDACs) and the activation of G-protein coupled receptors 
(GPCRs)[31]. 
1.6 Butyrate 
Among the three mentioned SCFAs, butyrate has been studied most extensively. Regulating epithelial cell 
metabolism, proliferation, and differentiation, butyrate is a highly essential metabolite in the human colon. 
Furthermore, butyrate is a main contributor to gut barrier function maintenance and has shown to have 
immunomodulatory properties[34]. These have been described to be based on various mechanisms, which 
are outlined below.  
1.6.1 Effect on cytokine secretion 
Cytokines are small soluble proteins secreted from various immune cells that can act on the secreting cell 
itself, nearby or distant cells. There are both pro- and anti-inflammatory cytokines and they play a major role 
in directing naïve T cell differentiation into an effector subset. Different patterns of lineage-specifying 
cytokines are associated with the development of different effector subsets of CD4+ T cells, described thus 
far, such as Th1, Th2, Th17, Tfh, and Treg cells. Downstream of the lineage-specifying cytokines is a specific 
pattern of STAT activation, programming a unique transcription factor network, which further defines the 
gene-expression profiles of mature effector T cells, as can be seen in figure 3[11]. 
==PAGE:16==
10

activated downstream of cytokines, determining the fate of the CD4 T cell subset. Figure modified from [11]. 
The differentiation into a given effector T cell subset leads to further secretion of cytokines that can signal 
and affect the behaviour of other immune cells. For some of the effector subsets, the secretion of cytokines 
can result in a positive feedback loop, reinforcing the signal for the differentiation of more cells of the given 
effector subset. Thus, the secretion of e.g., IL-4 from Th2 cells stimulates the differentiation of more Th2 
cells[11]. 
Butyrate has in several studies been found capable of regulating cytokine secretion of immune cells. More 
specifically, butyrate has been found to induce a shift in cytokine milieus to that of an anti-inflammatory one. 
Downregulation of the secretion of inflammatory cytokines such as IFN-γ, TNF-α and IL-12, and an 
upregulation of the secretion of anti-inflammatory cytokines IL-10 and TGF-β have been observed in several 
studies in which human immune cells have been stimulated with butyrate in addition to a bacterial stimulus 
such as LPS. Conversely, in these studies, no clear effect of butyrate on cytokine secretion has been observed 
in immune cells stimulated in the absence of bacterial stimulus[21][35]. 
1.6.2 Effect on DC maturation 
DCs are specialized APCs playing a major role in the induction of adaptive immune responses, by inducing T 
cell responses. The differentiation process of DCs leads to mainly two types of DCs: immature DCs (imDCs) 
and mature DCs (mDCs), providing different functions depending on their stage of maturation. ImDCs are 
found in most tissues where they are capable of capturing antigen, but they lack the essential expression of 
co-stimulatory receptors and MHC molecules on the cell surface[36]. For optimal T cell activation by DCs, at 
least two signals are required; stimulation via the T cell receptor (TCR) by antigen in a complex with MHC and 
a costimulatory signal. An absence of a co-stimulatory signal has been shown to be insufficient for T cell 
activation and instead induces clonal anergy in T cells[37]. The most well-characterized co-stimulatory 
molecules are the B7.1 (CD80) and B7.2 (CD86) molecules, found on the surface of DCs. The receptor for the 
==PAGE:17==
11 
 
B7 molecules is CD28 found on T cells and ligation of CD28 by the B7 molecules is essential to induce T cell 
responses. Due to the lack of co-stimulatory- and MHC molecules, the imDCs are poor T cell stimulators. 
However, once the DCs encounter a sufficient immunological stimulus, imDCs mature to become mDCs, thus 
going from antigen-capture mode to an antigen-presenting and T cell-stimulating mode. mDCs express high 
levels of co-stimulatory- and MHC molecules as well as pro-inflammatory cytokines and are thus efficient 
APCs[36].  
The effect of butyrate on DCs has been studied vastly on monocyte-derived DCs (moDCs) in vitro. Studies 
have found that butyrate is capable of altering moDCs morphology, cytokine production and metabolic 
activity. Furthermore, butyrate has been observed to downregulate the expression of DC maturation markers 
such as CD80, CD86 and MHC II, thus hampering the activation of naïve T cells[35][36][37][38]. 
1.6.3 Induction of regulatory T cells 
Regulatory T cells (Tregs) are a subset of CD4+ T cells crucial for maintaining a balance between immune 
system activation and tolerance. Tregs are characterized by the expression of specific markers, including the 
transcription factor Forkhead box protein 3 (Foxp3) and CD25[19]. Two complementary classes of 
CD4+CD25+Foxp3+ Tregs have been described: thymus-derived Tregs (tTregs) and peripherally induced Tregs 
(pTregs). While tTregs are highly involved in the maintenance of immune self-tolerance, pTregs play an 
important role in sustaining peripheral immune homeostasis. Both types of Tregs are involved in the 
regulation of several immune disorders including allergic responses through mechanisms affecting both DCs 
and effector T cells[39]. Directing the development and shaping the function of tTregs and pTregs is based 
on various factors, including stimulation by cytokines secreted by innate and adaptive immune cells. 
Additionally, recent and emerging data suggest that SCFAs, particularly butyrate, also play an essential role 
in directing the development of Tregs[19]. Butyrate has been found to induce pTregs both in vivo and in vitro, 
an effect of butyrate that has been proposed to be based on HDAC inhibition of the Foxp3 gene[40][41]. 
1.6.4 Induction of apoptosis in immune cells 
Apoptosis is a term covering a well-defined and highly regulated series of mitochondrial morphological 
changes resulting in programmed cell death. Programmed cell death is a mechanism essential in the 
development and homeostasis of metazoan organisms, as well as in several diseases such as cancer, acquired 
immunodeficiency syndrome, and neurodegenerative disorder. The activation of apoptosis can be based 
both upon the lack of survival signals and on signals originating from the intra- or extracellular milieu. The 
process leading to apoptotic cell death involves evolutionarily conserved genetic mechanisms, which have 
been elucidated through genetic studies in Caenorhabditis elegans. Through these genetic studies, three 
major components of the apoptotic pathway have been defined; membrane-bound regulatory proteins, 
==PAGE:18==
12 
 
cytosolic regulatory proteins, and apoptotic proteases. Apoptotic proteases are termed aspartic acid-specific 
cysteine proteases, or caspases, in vertebrates, and they can be divided into two classes; initiator and effector 
caspases. While initiator caspases (caspase-8, -9 and -10) promote apoptosis by cleavage and activation of 
other caspases, effector caspases (caspase-3, -6 and -7) initiate the cell degradation phase of apoptosis. This 
phase of the apoptotic pathway is characterized by the cleavage of various proteins critical for cellular 
integrity and the activation of enzymes promoting cell death[42][43][44]. 
Several studies on butyrate and its mechanism of action have shown that the compound is capable of 
inhibiting cell proliferation and inducing apoptosis in various target cells. For example, butyrate has been 
found capable of inducing apoptosis and cell cycle arrest in several cancer cell lines, and is thus widely studied 
in the field of cancer therapy[45][46]. Studies on the effect of butyrate on proliferative responses of human 
immune cells to various stimuli have found that butyrate inhibits these in a dose-dependent manner. 
Furthermore, butyrate has been found to increase caspase-3/7 activity in immune cells, and thus, it has been 
suggested that butyrate may trigger the activation of the caspase-cascade leading to apoptotic cell death. 
This has been suggested to be the underlying inhibitory mechanism of butyrate in immune cell 
proliferation[44][47]. 
1.7 SCFAs as therapeutics 
Due to the described immunomodulatory properties of SCFAs, particularly butyrate, utilizing them as 
therapeutics is of high interest. However, obtaining and maintaining adequate millimolar (mM) 
concentrations in the colon, as well as at peripheral tissues constitutes a significant challenge. Several 
approaches to deliver SCFAs have been described, and some of them are highlighted in figure 4.   
One mode of delivering SCFAs is by the application of a prodrug. An example hereof is the prodrug of natural 
butyrate, Tributyrin, an SCFA triglyceride. The aim of applying this prodrug in therapeutic settings is to 
overcome the pharmacokinetic drawbacks of natural butyrate, such as the difficulties in achieving and 
maintaining milimolar concentrations in the blood[48]. A clinical and pharmacologic study of Tributyrin found 
that Tributyrin given as an oral dose of 200 mg/kg three times daily may increase serum butyrate to a median 
level of 52 μM. However, drawbacks in clinical use of tributyrin have been observed in terms of significant 
intra‐individual variation in obtained serum butyrate. Furthermore, the high number of capsules required is 
another drawback to applying this prodrug in therapeutic settings[49]. 
Another mode of delivery is the dietary consumption of fermentable fibers. Examples of such fermentable 
fibers are inulin and several sources of resistant starch, which are frequently applied as prebiotics. These 
dietary components have, in varying doses, shown to increase faecal levels of total SCFA and to induce a 
significant increase in plasma SCFA levels after consumption. However, a gap between recommendations (30 
==PAGE:19==
13 
 
g/day) and intake of dietary fiber is observed worldwide, and it has been shown challenging to encourage 
the general public to consume an adequate dose of fermentable fiber leading to a sufficient boosting of SCFA 
production. Furthermore, the nature of SFCA production observed in response to dietary fiber intake is highly 
variable, likely due to variations in gut microbiota profiles. This variation constitutes a significant limitation 
of utilizing a dietary approach in SCFA delivery, and for future applications, matching the dietary fiber intake 
with microbiota profile may lead to a more beneficial effect on the host[28][50].  
A postbiotic delivery of SCFAs involves the direct delivery of SCFAs in the gut, circumventing the need for 
microbial fermentation. Postbiotic delivery by enemas containing SCFA solutions has been studied, mostly in 
regards to IBD and colitis, where disease pathology has been linked to impaired SCFA-epithelial 
interactions[51]. The results of the studies using SCFA enemas have been mixed, likely due to variations in 
concentration and frequency of the enemas applied. For instance, butyrate enemas given twice a day into 
the distal colon of rats showed no effect over saline infusions on aberrant crypt formation (potential early 
stage of colon cancer), but have significant effect when given five times a day[52]. Thus, a long period of 
contact between SCFA and mucosa may be required for therapeutic effects, making clinical efficacy of SCFA 
enemas challenging to achieve. Another route of administering postbiotic butyrate is by the oral route. 
However, most likely due to unpleasant taste and odour, studies on oral administration of postbiotic butyrate 
is limited in humans. Due to this limitation, a more palatable butyrate derivative, the N-(1-carbamoyl-2-
phenyl-ethyl) butyramide (FBA), is being studied as a potential postbiotic butyrate compound in which the 
unpleasant taste and odour is avoided[53].  
Another approach to deliver SCFAs is the promotion of endogenous processes leading to increased SCFA 
production. These endogenous processes include the starvation-associated ketosis in which long-chain fatty 
acids and pyruvate are converted to acetyl-CoA, which is then further broken down to acetate through the 
action of acetyl-CoA hydrolase[54]. Another endogenous process that may lead to acetate production in the 
liver is the exposure to ethanol through the actions of alcohol dehydrogenase and aldehyde 
dehydrogenase[55]. A further method of increasing endogenous SCFA production is the consumption of 
probiotic bacteria known to be butyrate producers. A recent study[56] has suggested a Clostridium cluster IV 
isolate, Butyricicoccus pullicaecorum, to be a safe and well-tolerated potential next-generation butyrate-
producing probiotic.

2. Project aim and experimental strategy 
With the prospects of the immunomodulatory function of butyrate, this metabolite is considered a potential 
therapeutic agent in the regulation of allergic responses elicited by immune cells. Some of the major elicitors 
of allergic reactions worldwide are HDM allergens. As the prevalence of allergy is steadily increasing, it is 
essential to continue to gain knowledge of the underlying immunologic reactions and potential regulation of 
these. This constitutes the motivation to carry out this master thesis project, in which butyrate and its effect 
on immune cells obtained from HDM allergic patients were studied in vitro. As has been described in the 
introduction, butyrate is an extensively studied metabolite, and its effects have been well-documented in 
various target cells. However, its potential immunomodulatory effect on HDM allergy remains uncertain.  
This master thesis project aims to investigate the ex-vivo HDM-specific immunomodulatory effect of butyrate 
in terms of: 
 
Apoptosis induction  
 
Proliferative responses 
 
Cytokine secretion 
 
DC activation  
 
The cytokines measured were IFN-γ, IL-5 and IL-10, which were chosen as they each represent a specific type 
of immune response. IFN-γ is a cytokine highly involved in the Th1 immune response, IL-5 is one of the major 
Th2 cytokines and IL-10 is involved in the regulatory immune response. Assessing these properties of butyrate 
will contribute to a further understanding of butyrate and its role in modulating HDM allergic immune 
responses as well as to elucidate whether butyrate is an interesting therapeutic agent in the field of allergy.   
The experimental strategy includes isolation of PBMCs from HDM allergic individuals and setting up human 
ex-vivo cell-based assays in the presence and absence of butyrate and different HDM extracts and allergens. 
An overview of the experimental strategy and the methods applied is provided in figure 5.

Human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) have been widely used in studies on the immune system, 
as they comprise several cell types playing a pivotal role in immune responses, including lymphocytes (NK 
cells, B cells, T cells), monocytes and dendritic cells[11]. In this project, PBMCs were isolated from blood 
drawn from HDM allergic donors. The subsequent ex-vivo PBMC stimulations included the use of various 
HDM allergens and butyrate at different concentrations.  
The butyrate applied in this project was sodium butyrate (NaB), the sodium salt of butyric acid. The 
concentrations of NaB applied were chosen in order to stay within a physiologically relevant range and based 
on several studies finding butyrate at a concentration of 0.1-2 mM to be sufficient to exert its 
immunomodulatory function[57][58], including Aitoro et al. [59] who observed butyrate at 0.75 mM in breast 
milk capable of modulating cytokine secretion.  
The allergens applied in this project were chosen based on the fact that HDM allergic patients are 
predominantly allergic to allergens from the mite species Der p and Der f. Therefore, aqueous allergen 
extracts derived from whole-mite cultures of Der p and Der f, (shortened Der p X and Der f X, in this thesis) 
containing both body and faecal material, as well as purified single-allergens Der p 1 and Der p 2 were applied 
in the PBMC stimulations.  
==PAGE:23==
17 
 
In addition to HDM allergens, PBMCs were stimulated with non-specific stimuli such as anti-CD3/anti-CD28 
(aCD3/aCD28) antibodies and the mitogen Phytohemagglutinin (PHA). These were applied in order to 
stimulate all T cells within a sample, in an antigen-independent manner. While PHA activates T cells by 
binding to cell membrane glycoproteins, including the T cell receptor (TCR)–CD3 complex, aCD3/aCD28 
activates both the TCR-CD3 complex as well as the co-stimulatory protein CD28 on T cells[60]. These 
stimulants constitute positive controls and were applied to compare non-specific and allergen-specific 
immunomodulatory effects of NaB.  
The effect of NaB on the stimulated immune cells was assessed through several immunological readouts, 
including proliferation, apoptosis, cytokine secretion, and DC activation markers measurements.

3.2 Methods 
3.2.1 Isolation of human PBMCs 
100 mL blood from HDM allergic donors was collected in vacuum VENOJECT glasses containing sodium 
heparin (Terumo, Japan). 50 mL Leucosep tubes (Greiner, Austria) were prepared by adding 15 mL 
Lymphoprep (Axis Shield, Scotland) and centrifuging at 1000 rpm for 30 sec. Donor blood was poured into 
Leucosep tubes, and tubes were centrifuged at 760xg for 15 min without brake, allowing the separation of 
plasma, PBMCs, red blood cells and granules. The plasma layer was discarded and the white interphase 
containing PBMCs was poured into new 50 mL tubes. The PBMCs were diluted 1:2 with PBS followed by three 
washes in PBS (1 wash á 20 min and 2 washes á 10 min, 300 g). The cells were resuspended in 10 mL complete 
RPMI (5% human serum and 1% Pen/Strep) and counted using NucleoCounter® (ChemoMetec, Denmark). 
The cells were adjusted to the appropriate cell number in complete RPMI.

3.2.3 Cell proliferation measurement 
To assess the effect of NaB on the proliferative response of PBMCs, two different cell viability assays were 
conducted using the following kits: CellTiter-Glo® 2.0 and RealTime-Glo™ MT (Promega, USA). Both of the 
assays were used to quantify metabolically active cells, but are based on different chemistries.

3.2.3.1 CellTiter-Glo®2.0 
CellTiter-Glo® 2.0 is an end-point “add-mix-measure” format and based on quantifying the amount of ATP 
present in a cell culture, as an indicator of metabolically active cells. The assay is based on the properties of 
a thermostable luciferase (Ultra-Glo™ Recombinant Luciferase), which generates a stable luminescent signal. 
This luminescent signal is generated upon cell lysis and a subsequent luciferase reaction. The luminescent 
signal is proportional to the amount of ATP present, while the amount of ATP corresponds to the number of 
viable cells in culture[61]. The principle of the assay can be seen in figure 6.  
 
Figure 6: Principle of CellTiter-Glo® 2.0 assay. Luciferase catalyses mono-oxygenation of luciferin in the presence of Mg2+, ATP and 
molecular oxygen. The result is a stable luminescent signal that can be detected by a luminometer. Figure from [61]. 
Procedure: 
Following cell incubation, the cells were transferred to 96-well opaque-walled tissue culture plates (with a 
clear bottom) compatible with GloMax® luminometer (Promega, USA). CellTiter-Glo® 2.0 reagent was added 
to the cells in a 1:1 ratio. The contents were mixed for 2 min on an orbital shaker (600rpm) to induce cell 
lysis. The plates were incubated at room temperature for 10 min to stabilize the luminescent signal, and 
luminescence were recorded on GloMax® luminometer.  
3.2.3.2 RealTime-Glo™ MT 
RealTime-Glo™ MT is an assay kit used to measure cell viability in real time. The assay is based on adding a 
combination of the provided MT Cell Viability pro-substrate and NanoLuc® luciferase enzyme to cells in 
culture. The MT Cell Viability pro-substrate is cell-permeable and is reduced by metabolically active cells-only 
to a substrate for the NanoLuc® luciferase enzyme. The NanoLuc® substrate diffuses from cells into the 
surrounding culture medium, in which it chemically reacts with the NanoLuc® enzyme resulting in the 
production of to a luminescent signal, corresponding to the number of viable cells[62] (Fig. 7).

3.2.4 Cytokine measurement 
In order to investigate whether butyrate can influence cytokine secretion in vitro, the cytokines IL-5, IL-10 
and IFN-γ were measured by the multiplex techniques Luminex® and Mesoscale®. Furthermore, a FluoroSpot 
assay was used to analyse cytokine secretion at the single cell level. 
3.2.4.1 Luminex MagPix 
A ProcartaPlex® Immunoassay Kit (Affymetrix, USA) was used to perform multiplexed cytokine 
measurements in cell culture supernatant (SN) samples using the magnetic beads technology from Luminex® 
on the MagPix® platform. The assay is based upon adding a sample of interest to a 96-well plate containing 
magnetic beads pre-coated with cytokine-specific capture antibodies. These capture antibodies bind to 
cytokines present in the sample and can be detected by adding cytokine-specific biotinylated detection 
antibodies onto the plate, followed by the addition of phycoerythrin (PE)-conjugated streptavidin that will 
bind to the biotin on the detection antibodies (Fig. 8). The plate can be read on a Luminex® MagPix® 
instrument, in which a magnet captures the magnetic beads. The beads are illuminated by two spectrally 
different light-emitting diodes (LEDs); one identifying the analyte being detected and a second determining 
the magnitude of the PE-derived signal[63]. This generates raw data as mean fluorescence intensity (MFI) 
and the concentration of each analyte for each sample is calculated using a 5-parameter logistic fit curve 
generated for each analyte from seven standards[64].

followed by two washes on the magnet. 25 µL Streptavidin-PE was added per well and the plate was

3.2.4.3 Standard curve evaluation 
Both of the multiplex techniques Luminex® and Mesoscale® rely on the quantification of cytokine levels 
based on a standard curve generated from seven standards and a blank. The quality of the generated 
standard curves can be evaluated through several parameters. Assay analytical sensitivity can be evaluated 
through measures of the lower limit of detection, a measure which is often defined as the lowest 
concentration on the standard curve having a readout greater than 2.5 standard deviations above that of the 
background (blank), and that can be reliably detected. A low limit of detection within an assay indicates high 
sensitivity. Assay precision can be assessed through a measure of the percent coefficient of variation (%CV) 
for replicate standard samples. The %CV is calculated by the formula: standard deviation / mean x 100. A 
%CV below 25% is often considered acceptable and an indication of a precise assay. The quality of the 
standard curve fit can be evaluated through the recovery percent by comparing the expected concentration 
of the standards to the calculated concentration which is obtained by backfitting to the standard curve using 
the formula: Calculated Concentration / Expected Concentration x 100. A curve describing the experimental 
data well will have a % Recovery value between 80 to 120 % of the expected concentration[64][66]. 
3.2.4.4 FluoroSpot 
With the purpose of analysing cytokine secretion at the single cell level, a FluoroSpot assay was conducted 
using two separate kits; a human IFN-γ/IL-5 kit and a human IL-10 kit (MabTech, Sweden). The principle of 
the FluoroSpot assay is similar to the principle of the enzyme-linked immunospot (ELISpot) method, but the 
assay differs by having the capacity to analyse the secretion of more than one analyte from the same cell[42]. 
Cells are incubated on a 96-well plate containing low-fluorescent Polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF)-based 
==PAGE:32==
26 
 
membranes pre-coated with specific capture antibodies. (PVDF)-based membranes are highly hydrophobic 
and have a high binding affinity for proteins and nucleic acids. For the detection of cytokines, fluorophore-
labelled detection antibodies are applied. For instance, a biotinylated detection antibody is used for one 
analyte, and a tag-labelled detection antibody for the other analytes, allowing for the analysis of two or more 
analytes within a well. Spot analysis is conducted using an automated fluorescence reader generating 
separate images for the single fluorophores. The spots that are derived from cells secreting more than one 
analyte can be identified by analysing co-positioned spots in a digital overlay of the single-fluorescent images. 
The definition of what is counted as a spot can be adjusted from assay to assay, and a ‘golden standard’ for 
what should be counted as a spot does not exist. For each assay, an algorithm and threshold value for the 
size and intensity of the image elements the program should recognize as spots should be adjusted[67][68].

3.2.5 Apoptosis measurement 
In order to assess apoptosis induction in PBMCs by NaB, two different methods were applied; caspase-3/7 
activation measurement and Annexin V staining.  
3.2.5.1 Caspase-3/7 activation 
An ApoTox-Glo® kit (Promega, USA) was applied to measure caspase-3 and -7 activities. As caspases play an 
essential role in the cell degradation phase of apoptosis, caspase activity can be used as an indicator of 
apoptotic cells. The principle of the assay includes the use of a Caspase-Glo® 3/7 reagent comprising a 
luciferase prosubstrate containing a Z-DEVD-Aminoluciferin sequence specific for caspase-3 and -7, and a 
thermostable luciferase (Ultra-Glo™ Recombinant Luciferase), formulated to generate a stable luminescent 
signal. Addition of the Caspase-Glo® 3/7 Reagent in an “add-mix-measure” format leads to cell lysis, followed 
by recognition and cleavage of the substrate by activated caspase-3 and -7 and the generation of a 
luminescent signal produced by luciferase. Luminescence is proportional to the amount of caspase activity 
present[69]. The principle of the assay can be seen in figure 11.  
 
Figure 11: Principle of Caspase-3/7 activity measurement as an indicator of apoptotic cells. The Caspase-Glo® 3/7 reagent is added 
to cells in culture, resulting in cell lysis and caspase cleavage of the Z-DEVD-Aminoluciferin caspase 3/7 substrate, leading to free 
aminoluciferin which is consumed by luciferase to generate a luminescent signal proportional to the amount of caspase activity 
present in cell culture. Figure from [69]. 
Procedure: 
Freshly isolated PBMCs from one donor were adjusted to 2*105 cells/well and cultured in NaB in duplicates 
at concentrations ranging from 0.125 mM to 4 mM. PBMCs cultured in 10% DMSO was applied as a positive 
==PAGE:35==
29 
 
control. A total of three plates were prepared in order to measure caspase activation at three different time 
points (24h, 48h and 72h). Cells were cultured in a humidified incubator at 37°C and 5% CO2 for 24h, 48h and 
72h, respectively. Following incubation, Caspase-Glo® 3/7 Buffer and Caspase-Glo® 3/7 Substrate were 
thawed at RT. Caspase-Glo® 3/7 reagent was prepared by transferring the contents of the Caspase-Glo® 3/7 
Buffer bottle into the amber bottle containing Caspase-Glo® 3/7 Substrate. 100 μL of Caspase-Glo® 3/7 
Reagent was added to all wells, followed by orbital shaking (300–500rpm for ~30 seconds). The plate was 
incubated for 30 min at RT and luminescence was measured on a GloMax luminometer.  
3.2.5.2 Annexin V staining 
The second method performed to assess apoptosis induction was Annexin V staining and subsequent 
Annexin V expression analysis by flow cytometry. Annexin V staining can be used to detect apoptotic cells 
in terms of exposed phosphatidylserine (PS) within the external cellular environment, which it binds to with 
high affinity. In viable cells, PS is usually located on the cytoplasmic surface of the cell membrane. However, 
when cells are undergoing the intermediate stages of apoptosis, PS is translocated from the inner to the 
outer surface of the membrane, and can thus be detected by fluorochrome-conjugated Annexin V binding 
to PS[70]. Flow cytometry allows cell analysis based on physical and chemical cell characteristics such as 
size, granularity and surface marker expression. The stained cell suspensions are injected into a flow 
cytometer, in which cells flow through a laser beam, scattering light characteristic to each cell. Forward and 
side scatter provides an estimation of the size and granularity of the cells, useful to differentiate between 
populations of, e.g., lymphocytes and DCs and to exclude cell debris. The fluorophore-conjugated 
antibodies excite and emit light in specific ranges of wavelengths, detected by bandpass filters within the 
cytometer, allowing the differentiation of cells based on cell surface markers[71]. Analysing the expression 
of Annexin V on cells stimulated by NaB at various concentrations by flow cytometry allows for 
simultaneously staining for immune cell subsets such as T cells, B cells, and DCs. This is an advantage, as it 
can then be further assessed which immune cell subsets are affected by butyrate in terms of apoptosis. 
Procedure: 
Cryopreserved PBMCs from two HDM allergic donors were thawed 24h prior to culturing in order for the cells 
to rest. Cells were harvested, counted and adjusted to 2*105 cells/mL. Butyrate solutions were prepared in a 
concentration range of 0.125 to 4 mM and added to the PBMCs. The cells were cultured in a humidified 
incubator at 37°C and 5% CO2 for 72h. Following incubation, the plates were centrifuged for 3 min at 500xg 
and SNs were discarded. The cells were washed once with 200 µL FACS buffer (PBS 1%BSA) and SNs were 
discarded. A mastermix containing the following cell surface markers: CD4 (APC-H7), CD8 (PerCP-Cy5), LD 
(eF506) and Annexin V (PE) was prepared for staining. 100 µL of the staining mastermix was added to each 
==PAGE:36==
30 
 
well, except for controls, followed by incubation for 20 min at 5°C. Cells were washed twice with FACS buffer, 
followed by a wash with 200 µL 1X binding buffer. Cells were resuspended in 100 µL binding buffer and 100 
µL fluorochrome-conjugated annexin V (PE) (1:20) was added to the cell suspension followed by an 
incubation for 15 min at RT, protected from light. The cells were washed once and resuspended in 200 µL 1X 
binding buffer before analyzing them by flow cytometry. Acquired data were analysed using FlowJo Software 
(LCC, USA).  
3.2.6 DC activation markers  
In order to assess the effect of NaB on activation marker expression on DCs, monocyte-derived DCs

markers CD80, CD86 and MHC II by Flow Cytometry.  
3.2.6.1 Generation of human monocyte-derived DCs 
moDCs were generated from PBMCs by first isolating monocytes (CD14+ cells) by magnetic-activated cell 
sorting (MACS) technology (Miltenyi Biotec, Germany) using CD14 MicroBeads. This method allows for 
isolation of monocytes based on the expression of surface CD14. The CD14+ cells were cultured in media 
containing the cytokines GM-CSF and IL-4 to direct monocytes to become DCs.   
The MACS technology is relying on the combination of nano-sized beads and a high magnetic gradient within 
a MACS column. The basic principle of the MACS technology can be seen in figure 12.  
 
Figure 12: Principle of the MACS technology. Cells are labeled with magnetic MicroBeads and placed in a MACS column containing a 
high magnetic gradient in which the magnetic labelled cells bind to. Once all of the cells have run through the column, the labelled 
cells attached to the column can be eluted into a new tube. Figure from [72].

follow a Gaussian distribution, a non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis test followed by Dunn’s multiple 
comparison test was performed. Statistical differences were tested on a 95% confidence interval and the 
differences between groups were considered significant when P values were 0.05 or below. Statistical 
significance was indicated by stars where *P < 0.05, **P < 0.01, ***P < 0.001, and ****P<0.0001

4.4.2 FluoroSpot 
A FluoroSpot assay was conducted in order get a preliminary glance at cytokine secretion on the single cell 
level. Three HDM allergic donors were included in the assay, in which the secretion of IL-5, IFN-γ and IL-10 
was analysed in terms of spot-forming cells (SFC) per 106 cells. As the three donors were also a part of the 
eight donors used in the U-PLEX assay, they were given the same donor number and labelling in terms of 
dot-color, for comparison.  
From the FluoroSpot readout it was observed that several wells were oversaturated with spots, as can be 
seen in Appendix C. This was particularly observed in the wells containing aCD3/aCD28 and PHA 
stimulations, which could be expected for the positive controls, as they stimulate all T cells directly. The 
spot counts from the wells that were oversaturated were considered unreliable, and therefore set to 2000 
SFCs to visualize that the number of spots in these wells were higher than what the reader could count. 
From these, it was not possible to conclude anything on the spots counted, other than that the result was 
positive, e.g., that many cells were found to be spot-forming.

5. Discussion 
During the last decades, the prevalence of IgE-mediated allergic diseases has increased more than two-fold 
in industrialized countries[73]. The cause of this increase has been associated with several aspects of the 
western lifestyle, including a diet low in dietary fiber[7]. Several studies have found a diet high in dietary fiber 
to provide a protective effect on the development of allergic diseases[8][9][10]. This protective effect has 
often been linked to metabolites such as SCFAs resulting from dietary fiber fermentation by certain microbes 
within the host gut microbiota. The SCFA butyrate has been well-documented to regulate immune responses 
through modulation of cytokine secretion, Treg induction, DC maturation, as well as apoptosis induction in 
several experimental models[21][36][47]. Based on this, it was hypothesized in this thesis that butyrate might 
also exert these functions in an HDM allergy experimental model. Therefore, the immunomodulatory effects 
of sodium butyrate were assessed in vitro using immune cells from HDM allergic donors, in terms of 
proliferation, apoptosis, cytokine secretion, and DC activation.  
Preliminary experiments, from the beginning of the project, showed that NaB inhibited cell proliferation, in 
a dose-dependent manner. As it was found that the number of viable cells in the NaB-treated cells was below 
that of the medium control, it was questioned whether this effect was due to an induction of apoptotic cell 
death by butyrate. Butyrate has in several studies been found to induce apoptosis in immune cells, and other 
authors have used a concentration of maximum 2 mM as a non-toxic dose to study the immunomodulatory 
effect of NaB[21][58]. In this thesis, apoptosis induction was examined through measurements of caspase-
activity and Annexin V binding. In agreement with Kurita-Ochiai et al. [44], who found that PBMC cell 
proliferation inhibition was due to apoptosis induction by butyrate and that the effector caspase-3 was 
involved, it could be observed that NaB leads to caspase-3/7 activation in a dose-dependent manner. It was 
found that particularly the high concentrations of NaB at 1 to 4 mM increased caspase activity. This result 
was in agreement with Bailón et al. [47] who found that butyrate at a concentration of 2 mM leads to a 
significant increase in caspase-3/7 activity. These findings indicate that NaB may trigger the activation of the 
caspase-cascade leading to apoptotic cell death. The induction of apoptotic cell death by NaB was further 
confirmed by Annexin V staining showing that NaB at concentrations of 1 to 4 mM lead to induction of 
apoptosis in both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells. Thus, the induction of apoptosis may be the mechanism via which 
higher concentrations of butyrate decrease PBMC proliferation. Since the concentrations below 1 mM 
showed no significant effect on apoptosis induction, concentrations of 0.25 and 0.75 mM were chosen for 
investigating the immunomodulatory effect of NaB on PBMCs from HDM allergic donors. In addition, these 
doses resemble the physiological concentration of 0.75 mM found in breast milk[59] and might therefore 
mimic the in vivo situation. 
==PAGE:58==
52 
 
In the first experiments, the effect of butyrate alone was tested on PBMCs without any detectable change in 
cytokine secretion (data was not shown). Those data are in line with several other studies, in which no 
apparent effect of butyrate on cytokine secretion was observed in immune cells stimulated in the absence 
of stimulus[21][35][57]. This could indicate that the effect of butyrate on immune cells is dependent on the 
activation status of the cells. Therefore, the immunomodulatory effect of NaB in terms of cell proliferation 
and secretion of IFN-γ, IL-5 and IL-10 were assessed on PBMCs from eight HDM allergic donors by applying 
NaB in combination with non-specific T cell- or allergen-specific stimuli.  
In these experiments, in addition to PHA, aCD3/aCD28, extracts of Der p- and Der f, the purified single 
allergens Der p 1 and Der p 2 were included in the stimulations. These allergens represent the major allergens 
within the Der p extract, which consists of body and faecal materials of the mites[74]. Thus, in these 
experiments the stimuli applied goes from stimuli that directly activates all T cells non-specifically 
(aCD3/aCD28 and PHA), to allergen-specific stimuli within the mite extracts and to single-allergen specific 
stimuli of Der p 1 and Der p 2. Thus, the allergen-specific effect of NaB can be elucidated in greater detail. As 
expected, stimulating PBMCs with the positive controls PHA and aCD3/aCD28 lead to a positive response, 
both in terms of proliferation and cytokine secretion. This was also found to be the case for stimulation with 
Der p- and Der f extract, indicating the presence of cells specific to proteins of the extracts. Furthermore, 
looking at the single extract components, Der p 1 and Der p 2, it was found that both of the allergens 
stimulated the PBMCs in the proliferation assay, suggesting that Der p 1 and Der p 2-specific immune cells 
were present. The cytokine measurements also showed that these allergens stimulated secretion of IL-5, IL-
10, and IFN-γ. However, the cytokine levels measured were found to be in a lower magnitude than for the 
other stimulants. This makes sense, as it was expected that a smaller proportion of single-allergen specific 
cells would be present.  
When stimulating PBMCs in the presence of NaB at a concentration of 0.75 mM, secretion of the Th1 cytokine 
IFN-γ in response to aCD3/aCD28- and Der f extract was found to be significantly downregulated. Whereas 
studies have obtained similar findings[21], this result contradicts the findings of Aitoro et al. [59], who found 
butyrate at 0.75 mM to significantly increase IFN-γ secretion from PBMCs stimulated with various food 
allergens in a food allergy model. This indicates that butyrate may employ both pro-and anti-inflammatory 
effects depending on the experimental model used. These opposing effects of butyrate have been highlighted 
in another recent study[75] in which butyrate was found to promote the differentiation of Tregs in vitro and 
in vivo under steady-state conditions, but to induce IFN-γ expression in an inflammatory milieu such as acute-
colitis. The authors of this study speculated that the anti-inflammatory effects of butyrate might be 
overridden during acute or chronic inflammation whereby butyrate-mediated pro-inflammatory effects 
==PAGE:59==
53 
 
might dominate[75]. More studies are required to understand these opposing effects of butyrate in greater 
detail, particularly in regards to ensure the safety and efficacy of applying butyrate in therapeutic settings.  
Secretion of the Th2 cytokine IL-5 was also found to be downregulated significantly by NaB at a concentration 
of 0.75 mM, but only for the non-specific T cell stimulations. Although NaB was not found to significantly 
downregulate the increase in IL-5 secretion in response to the allergen-stimulations, a clear tendency of 
downregulation was observed. Variation in response to each stimuli was observed amongst the donors, and 
for this experiment, particularly donor 3 varied in the response compared to the other donors. This variation 
can be expected as various factors such as age, gender, genetics, microbiota profile, as well as environmental 
influences for each individual can affect immunological responses[76]. Removing donor 3 from the dataset 
was found to lead to a statistically significant decrease in IL-5, but as there was no reason to remove this 
donor from the dataset, the donor was kept in the statistical analysis. Despite a statistically non-significant 
outcome, the observed trend that butyrate can decrease the secretion of the major Th2 cytokine IL-5 is an 
indicator that butyrate may have a suppressive function in HDM allergic responses. This tendency of butyrate 
would be interesting to study further with the inclusion of more donors in the experimental setup.  
However, as it was the higher concentration of 0.75 mM that was found to downregulate both IFN-γ and IL-
5 secretion, combined with the finding that NaB induces apoptosis dose-dependently, it could be speculated 
that the inhibition of cytokine secretion could be due to an induction of apoptosis in immune cells. Speaking 
against this theory is the finding that NaB significantly increased the expression of the regulatory cytokine IL-
10, indicating that butyrate regulates immune cell function through other mechanisms than simply an 
induction of apoptosis. The upregulation of IL-10 by NaB was found to be an allergen-specific effect, as this 
was only observed in response to Der p- and Der f extract, as well as Der p 2 and not in the non-specific T cell 
stimulations. However, as aCD3/aCD28 and PHA stimulate T cells directly, in an antigen-independent manner, 
immune cells are activated faster than when stimulated with Der p- or Der f extracts. This could lead to a 
timing issue with the non-specific stimulations, in which the IL-10 secretion may have been seen at an earlier 
time point than the allergen-specific stimulations. To exclude that this was the case, earlier time points 
should be tested as well. Furthermore, to further investigate whether the immunomodulatory effect of NaB 
is allergen-specific, studies should include non-allergic individuals to compare the immunological responses.  
From the finding that only allergen-specific stimulations led to an increase in IL-10 secretion, it could be 
speculated that it may be DCs (which are not directly stimulated by aCD3/aCD28 and PHA) that secrete IL-10 
in response to NaB in the allergen-specific stimulations. Several studies [35][36] have found that butyrate 
can lead to an increase in IL-10 production in DCs. Furthermore, a recent study has found butyrate to be a 
potent inducer of tolerogenic human DCs, capable of priming naïve CD4+ T cells to become IL-10-producing 
==PAGE:60==
54 
 
Tregs[77]. As Tregs play an essential role in maintaining immune tolerance to allergens, the association of 
butyrate to the induction of Tregs highlights butyrate as an interesting compound in the field of allergy 
therapy and prevention. However, more studies are required to understand this effect of butyrate in greater 
detail.  
To explore the immunomodulatory effect on cytokine secretion by NaB further, a FluoroSpot assay was 
conducted, allowing the analysis of cytokine secretion on a single-cell level. The first results from this assay 
were in line with the U-PLEX cytokine measurement, as a decrease in SFCs for IFN-γ and IL-5 was observed 
for most stimulations in response to NaB at 0.75 mM and as an increase in SFCs for IL-10 was observed in 
response to NaB in a Der p extract stimulation. However, these results were only preliminary, and further 
assay optimization is required to gain reliable data. For instance, the spot-oversaturated wells, found 
primarily in the non-specific T cell stimulations, could indicate that too many cells (5*105) were added to each 
well, or that 72h incubation time was too long. Lowering the number of cells added or the incubation time 
could lead to a more clear result, avoiding spot-oversaturation.  
Another mechanism by which butyrate could inhibit cell proliferation might be based on butyrate interfering 
with DC maturation, leading to a less-activated DC phenotype. A less-activated DC phenotype could hamper 
antigen presentation and subsequent T cell activation and proliferation. Using monocyte-derived DCs as a 
model for peripheral blood APCs, the effect of NaB on the surface expression of CD80, CD86, and MHC II, on 
human moDCs during co-stimulation with LPS, TNF-α, and extracts of Der f and Der p was analysed. In a first 
experiment, it was found that NaB did affect the expression of the investigated activation markers. For MHC 
II, an unexpected level of expression, equal to that found in the medium-cultured moDCs, was found in the 
stimuli-cultured moDCs. The expectation would have been that moDCs cultured in medium-only would 
express lower levels of MHC II on the cell surface, as they have not encountered strong maturation-inducing 
stimuli that would upregulate MHC II expression on the cell surface for the DCs to become efficient antigen-
presenting cells. For the MHC II expression analysis, it should be noted that this was only investigated in 
terms of the HLA-DR isotype. Although the isotypes of MCH II display approximately 70% similarity to each 
other[78], staining for other isotypes, such as HLA-DQ might lead to different findings. However, some 
downregulation of MHC II was seen in response to NaB addition, particularly in the Der p extract-stimulated 
cells. This result is in line with the findings of Millard et al.[38] who found that butyrate at a concentration of 
0.5 mM downregulated HLA-DR expression in human moDCs.  
The expression of the co-stimulatory signal CD80 was found to be decreased when applying a concentration 
of 0.75 mM of NaB. Conversely, the expression of CD86 was found to be increased by NaB; findings that are 
similar to the research of Liu et al. [36]. Both CD80 and CD86 have been shown to provide essential 
==PAGE:61==
55 
 
costimulatory signals through interactions with CD28 on T cells. However, some studies have shown that 
CD80 co-stimulation is superior to CD86 co-stimulation[79], indicating that high CD86 expression alone would 
not be sufficient to provide adequate co-stimulation. Therefore, low CD80 expression may be an effective 
mechanism to downregulate the T cell response. However, the data are preliminary and should be confirmed 
with the inclusion of more donors. Furthermore, to understand the effect of butyrate on DCs in greater detail, 
the T cell stimulatory activity of butyrate-stimulated moDCs could be examined.  
The findings of this master thesis project indicate that butyrate may play a relevant role in the modulation of 
HDM allergic responses, in terms of cell proliferation, cytokine secretion, apoptosis induction, and DC 
activation. The observation that NaB, in the presence of HDM allergens, downregulates the allergen-specific 
secretion of the Th2 cytokine IL-5, while upregulating the secretion of the regulatory cytokine IL-10, is a 
finding that highlights butyrate as a potential regulator of allergic responses. Thus, the effect of butyrate may 
be an important factor contributing to the allergy protective effect associated with consuming a high fiber 
diet. However, as the findings presented in this thesis represent the in vitro immunomodulatory effects of 
butyrate on circulating immune cells, it remains unknown whether these can be translated to immune cells 
involved in HDM allergy in vivo. In order to exert its immunomodulatory effect on HDM allergy in vivo, 
butyrate has to reach the mucosal surfaces of the airways on which the HDM allergens are encountered upon 
inhalation. Although it is known that butyrate reaches the systemic circulation from where it can be 
distributed to peripheral tissues, the amount of butyrate reaching and affecting these areas, including the 
airway epithelium, is not fully elucidated. Therefore, more studies are required to further investigate the 
level of butyrate needed for beneficial responses within those tissues. Besides defining the “right” level of 
butyrate needed, another aspect is then the challenge of delivering and maintaining the required butyrate 
concentration within the respective tissues, which is influenced by the individual variability in microbiota 
composition and metabolism. A diet low in dietary fiber may alter the microbiota composition away from 
butyrate-producing microbes, and by this, reduce the levels of butyrate in the colon and systemic circulation. 
However, other approaches to obtain adequate levels of butyrate, apart from a high fiber diet, include the 
use of a butyrate pro-drug or enema. This would enable the administration of a controlled dose and highlight 
the potential of butyrate to be translated into a preventive as well as therapeutic measure within a wide 
range of diseases, including allergy. 
In conclusion, the findings of this master thesis project indicate that butyrate regulates immune cell function 
through an inhibition of proliferation, induction of apoptosis, modulation of cytokine secretion and through 
an altered activation state of DCs. Furthermore, these findings support further the interest for butyrate as a 
modulator of allergic responses and the conducted experiments constitute a basis for more research of this 
==PAGE:62==
56 
 
SCFA. In particular, it would be interesting to study the upregulation of IL-10 secretion in greater detail, for 
example through measurement of IL-10 in butyrate-cultured DCs, specifically. From this, it could be 
elucidated whether it could be DCs that are responsible for the NaB-induced increase in IL-10 secretion 
observed in response to allergen-specific stimulations. A subsequent co-culture experiment with butyrate-
cultured DCs and T cells could further elucidate the T cell response, in terms of proliferation, cytokine 
secretion and potentially the expression of the transcription factor Foxp3, as an indicator of Treg induction 
by butyrate-cultured DCs.
