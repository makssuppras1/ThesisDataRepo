Alginate is a linear polysaccharide that originates from the brown seaweed cell wall consisting of 1,4 linked 𝛽-
d-mannuronic acid (M) and 𝛼-l-guluronic acid (G), either in homogenous blocks or heterogeneous MG 
stretches and are arranged in random orders. Humans lack alginate degrading enzymes; thus, some certain 
gut bacteria can ferment and utilize complex carbohydrates in vitro, resulting in the health-beneficial short-
chain fatty acids. 
This master thesis presents a preliminary biochemical characterization of a newly discovered 
polysaccharide lyase family 6 enzyme from the human gut microbiota Bacteroides ovatus (BoPL6). A 
biochemical characterization of the recombinant BoPL6 and BoPL6-NTD was performed to identify the optimal 
activity of BoPL6 when degrading alginate substrates. 
Based on the homology model, BoPL6 consists of two domains (BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD) and is 
monomeric in its structure. The two domains, BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD, could be separated and 
reconstituted. The BoPL6 was G-specific and exo-acting in its mode of action and released unsaturated 
monosaccharides when degrading polyguloronic acid. The BoPL6-NTD had an endo-acting mode of action, 
releasing oligosaccharides when degrading polyguloronic acid. The BoPL6 catalyzed the formation of 
unsaturated monosaccharides from polyguloronic acid with a Km 0.34623 ± 0.08788 mg/ml -1 and kcat 1.78182 
s-1. The results of this thesis can be used as the starting point for further in-depth experiments, characterization, 
and analysis of BoPL6.

The Enzyme and Protein Chemistry research group at the Department of Biotechnology and Biomedicine has 
identified an enzyme originating from an untyped human gut bacterium Bacteroides ovatus which will here be 
referred to as Bacteroides ovatus polysaccharide lyase family 6 (BoPL6) enzyme. Preliminary research has 
shown that the structural homology model of this newly discovered enzyme consists of two domains (named 
N-terminal and C-terminal due to their sequential location), where the N-terminal domain (BoPL6-NTD) is 
expected to be the catalytic domain, and the C-terminal domain (BoPL6-CTD) has an unknown function.  
There is limited knowledge about polysaccharide family 6 (PL6) in general and no knowledge of the newly 
discovered BoPL6 enzyme and its various physical and chemical properties. The initial steps involve 
characterizing the BoPL6 and the two terminal domains separately to determine their ability to degrade 
alginate. It is also relevant to understand the link and dependency between the two terminal domains and 
whether it is possible to separate them. The objective of this master thesis project was to conduct this 
characterization and relationship investigation.

The human body contains a complex mixture of microorganisms called microbiota that lives inside, e.g., the 
skin, blood circulation, intestinal lumen, and gut (Lange et al. 2014). The diversity of microbes on the skin is 
relatively low, whereas a great diversity of microbes can be found in the gut (Liang et al. 2018). The human 
gut microbiota (HGM) makes an essential contribution to health and disease in humans, such as developing 
and regulating host immunity, protecting from pathogen colonization, and supplying energy to the host through 
the generation of short-chain fatty acids (SCFA) (Ndeh and Gilbert 2018).  
The majority of the HGM is located in the colon (Kmezik et al. 2021). HGM is a microbial community 
consisting of many microbes, e.g., bacteria, protozoa, fungi, and viruses, which live inside the digestive tracts 
of humans (Liang et al. 2018; Ndeh and Gilbert 2018). Most HGM members are represented within two phyla, 
the Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes; however, the HGM also contains members of Actinobacteria, 
Verrucomicrobia, and Proteobacteria. The microbial community within the five phyla can vary significantly 
depending on diet, age, stress, and other physiological factors. Unhealthy HGM includes the progress and 
outcome of human diseases such as cancer, diabetes, and inflammatory bowel disease (Ndeh and Gilbert, 
2018). 
The primary nutrients available to the HGM are the dietary complex carbohydrates. However, 
overconsumption or lack of digestion can lead to most of the products degraded by the complex carbohydrates 
are not being fully absorbed in the upper part of the gastrointestinal tract, disabling enzymatic degradation in 
the digestive tracts of the human body (Chassard and Lacroix 2013; Kmezik et al. 2021).  
The SCFA is the main product of the anaerobic fermentation of indigestible complex carbohydrates such 
as dietary fiber and resistant starch. When certain gut bacteria produce SCFA, they are absorbed by 
colonocytes, which shape the gut microbiota and improve gut health. The effects range from maintaining the 
intestinal barrier integrity mucus production, protecting against inflammation, and reducing the risk of colorectal 
cancer (Rosa Pérez et al. 2020).  
Humans lack alginate degrading enzymes in their gut; thus, some certain gut bacteria, e.g., strains of the 
Bacteroides xylanisolvens, Bacteroides ovatus, and Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron, can ferment and utilize 
complex carbohydrates in vitro, resulting in the formation of the health beneficial SCFA (Stender et al. 2019). 
 
The dominant member of the gut microbiota community is bacterial species of the Gram-negative 
Bacteroidetes phylum. The Bacteroidetes bacteria can produce SCFA by fermenting the indigestible complex 
carbohydrates by human encoded genes, which then are utilized and absorbed by the epithelial cells of the 
gut (Grondin et al. 2017). The Bacteroides are skilled in degrading glycans and can degrade and identify many 
polysaccharides. 
The Bacteroides accomplish the degradation of polysaccharides by a gene cluster called polysaccharide 
utilization loci (PULs). PULs can be used for determining the occupation of the metabolic niche of the 
Bacteroides (Wexler and Goodman 2017). The starch utilization system (SUS) was the first described PUL 
and now serves as a general template (Kmezik et al. 2021). SUS contains eight genes SusC, SusD, SusE, 
SusF, SusG, SusA, SusB, and SusR (Martens et al. 2009).  
The outer membrane lipoproteins SusD, SusE, and SusF binds the extracellular polysaccharide and 
cleave the polysaccharide into smaller oligosaccharides by SusG-like glycoside hydrolases. Furthermore, the 
oligosaccharides are then transported into the periplasm in a TonB-dependent method by SusC channels. The 
oligosaccharides are further degraded into simple sugars (monosaccharides or disaccharides) by SusA and 
SusB, and then taken up into the cytoplasm through inner membrane permeases (Wexler and Goodman 2017). 
The activation of sus genes SusC, SusD, SusE, SusF, SusG, SusA, and SusB was reached by the regulator 
SusR, in the presence of starch (Martens et al. 2009).

The database Carbohydrate-Active enZymes (CAZy) (http://www.cazy.org/;(Drula et al. 2022)) is an online 
database that describes the families of enzymes that can modify, degrade, or create glycosidic bonds. The 
data of the CAZy database are accessible by either browsing the content of genomes in carbohydrate-active 
enzymes or browsing sequence-based families. The enzyme classes that are currently covered in the CAZy 
database and can catalyze the modification, biosynthesis, or breakdown of the carbohydrates and 
glycoconjugates are Glycoside Hydrolases (GHs), Glycosyl Transferases (GTs), Polysaccharide Lyases (PLs), 
Carbohydrate Esterases (CEs), and Auxiliary Activities (AAs). Furthermore, do the CAZy database contain the 
PULDB, a database of PULs in Bacteroidetes, where the PULDB database contains information on 
experimentally determined and predicted PULs for several Bacteroidetes genomes (Drula et al. 2022). 
 
Alginate is a polysaccharide that originates from the brown seaweed cell wall, where the alginate content is 
30–60% of the dry weight. Furthermore, alginate exists in red algae and can also be produced by bacteria 
such as Azotobacter and Pseudomonas (Cheng et al. 2020). Alginate is used in the pharmaceutical, food, and 
cosmetic industry as hydrocolloids, encapsulation of probiotics, or gelling agents. Furthermore, alginate 
oligosaccharides and alginates are applied in biomedicine and health sectors (Stender et al. 2019). The 
alginates are linear polysaccharides consisting of 1,4 linked β-d-mannuronic acid (M) and α-l-guluronic acid 
(G), either in homogenous blocks or heterogeneous MG stretches (Figure 1) and are arranged in random 
orders (Zhu and Yin 2015; Stender et al. 2019). 
 
Figure 1 – Illustration of linked 𝛽-d-mannuronic acid (M), and 𝛼-l-guluronic acid (G) molecules and polyG, 
polyM, and polyMG blocks 
The alginate structure determines the physicochemical properties of alginate, such as the molecular weight 
(Mw), M/G ratio, and arrangement of M and G residues. Furthermore, can these residues combine to form rich 
G-blocks (GGGGGG), MG-blocks (MGMGMGMGMG), and M-blocks (MMMMMM) (Fertah 2017). It is believed 
that only the G-blocks of alginate participate in intermolecular cross-linking with divalent cations (e.g., Ca2+), 
which form hydrogels (Lee and Mooney 2012). Alginates with higher GG content are stronger and stiffer 
compared to alginates with higher MM content. Furthermore, it has been proven that GG-rich alginates show 
better water solubility than M-rich alginates (Cheng et al. 2020).

Polysaccharides lyases (PLs), including alginate lyases (ALs), are a class of enzymes (EC.4.4.2-) that can 
cleave the 1,4-glycosidic linkages in polysaccharides via the β-elimination mechanism to generate unsaturated 
monosaccharides and a new reducing end (Linhardt, Galliher, and Cooney 1986; Drula et al. 2022). There are 
42 approved PLs families' pages in the CAZy database to date (1.03.2022). PLs are grouped into families and 
subfamilies within the Carbohydrate-Active enZyme (CAZy) framework, where the PLs are restricted to the β-
elimination mechanism (Drula et al. 2022). 
 
The CAZy database contains 22 characterized PL6 candidates (Drula et al. 2022), of which 20 have been 
shown to degrade alginate. The PL6 family can be divided into 3 subfamilies based on sequence similarity, 
and the 3 PL6 subfamilies all contain members who can catalyze the degradation of alginate. The substrate 
specificity of the 3 PL6 subfamilies differs, where the subfamily PL62 and PL63 have so far only showed 
specificity for polyMG, and subfamily PL61 has shown specificity for polyM, polyG, and polyMG. The proposed 
catalytic residues for PL6 are lysine and arginine (Huang et al. 1999). 
The first PL6 structure solved was the single domain enzyme chondroitinase B (Figure 2A) from 
Pedobacter heparinus DSM 2366 (PBD:1OFL), which is also shown to be calcium-dependent and to degrade 
dermatan (Huang et al. 1999). The first structure solved of alginate lyase was the two domains, homo-dimeric, 
exo-acting, and G-specific a PL6 AlyGC (Figure 2C) from Paraglaciecola chatamensis S18K6T (PDB: 5GKQ) 
that produce the unsaturated monosaccharide containing 4-deoxy-l-erythrohex-4-enopyranosyluronic acid (Xu 
et al. 2017). Furthermore, is the structure of the monomeric and single domain AlyF (Figure 2B) from Vibrio 
splendidus OU2 (PDB:5Z9T) also solved, where the AlyF releases unsaturated trisaccharide from alginates 
and guluronic acid (Lyu et al. 2019a). The first solved AL structure, which is M-specific, was the BcelPL6 from 
human gut Bacteroides cellulosilyticus (Stender et al. 2019).

structure of the monomeric and single domain AlyF from Vibrio splendidus OU2 (PDB:5Z9T) and the active

ALs can catalyze alginate degradation by β-elimination of the 1,4-glycosidic bonds with the formation of 
unsaturated alginate oligosaccharides (AO). ALs are either endo-acting or exo-acting (Figure 3). The endo-
==PAGE:16==
 
acting degradation by AL releases oligosaccharides which can undergo further degradation, typically to di- and 
trisaccharides, and the exo-acting degradation by AL releases unsaturated monosaccharides (Δ) (Stender et 
al. 2019). Unsaturated monosaccharides containing 4-deoxy-l-erythrohex-4-enopyranosyluronic acid (DEH, 
also denoted as Δ), can however, spontaneously open, which converts into 2-keto-3deoxyd-gluconate (KDG) 
(Figure 3). Most of the characterized AL are endo-acting, whereas few exo-acting AL is characterized; 
moreover, some AL has both degrading mechanisms, e.g., PL17 (Gao et al. 2021).  
AO can be used for many applications, and the significant physiological activities of AO such as 
antimicrobial, antioxidant, prebiotic, antihypertensive, antidiabetic, immunomodulatory, and antitumor activities 
have attracted much attention. AL is more specific and efficient when degrading alginate because of enzyme 
activity, action mode, and substrate specificity (Cheng et al. 2020).  
 
Figure 3 – Endo-acting and exo-acting degradation of alginate by AL and the product formation. Endo-acting 
AL produces alginate oligosaccharides, and exo-acting AL produces unsaturated monosaccharides (Cheng 
et al. 2020). 
 
The catalytic mechanism of AL cleaving 1,4-glycosidic bonds can be divided into 3 steps. The first step is 
neutralizing the negatively charged C6 carboxyl group by a divalent cation or water molecule, which allows the 
resonance stabilization of the enolate formed, which causes the reduction of the pKa α-proton on the C5 (Violot 
et al., 2021). the second step is the abstraction of the proton on C5, which is facilitated by Brøndsted acid-
base definition (Gao et al. 2021). The last step is when the β-elimination of the 1,4-glycosidic bond generates 
a double bond between C4 and C5, a transfer of electrons from the carboxyl group occurs, and elimination of 
the 1,4-glycosidic bond occurs by AL. When the AL has eliminated the 1,4-glycosidic bond, the 
oligosaccharides contain 4-deoxy-l-erythrohex-4-enopyranosyluronic acid as the unsaturated non-reducing 
terminal is the end product of this reaction and can be called unsaturated AO (Gao et al. 2021).  
The syn-elimination and anti-elimination are the general lyase degradation mechanisms dependent on 
the substrate. The syn-elimination (M/MG specific lyase) occurs when the C5 proton and glycoside oxygen of 
the bond is placed at the same side of the sugar ring (Figure 4A), whereas anti-elimination (G/MG specific 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
lyase) occurs when the C5 proton and glycoside oxygen of the bond is placed at the opposite side of the sugar 
ring (Figure 4B) (Violot et al., 2021).

The gene of interest was cloned into the vector and plasmid pET28a (+), with the cleaving sites BamHI and 
NdeI (provided by supervisors) (MilliporeSigma (Novagen) 2022) (Appendix 9.2) (provided by the 
supervisor). The pET28a (+) vector carries an N-terminal His-Tag/thrombin/T7-Tag configuration plus an 
optional C-terminal His-Tag sequence. Furthermore, is pET28a (+) a plasmid type used for bacterial 
expression and is bacterial resistant towards kanamycin (Novagen 2014). The cleaving site BamHI is a DNA 
restriction endonuclease that cleaves DNA at the recognition sequence 5′-G/GATCC-3′ to generate 5′-
cohesive termini (Sigma-Aldrich 2022a), whereas the cleaving site NdeI is a restriction endonuclease that 
cleaves DNA at the recognition site 5′-CA/TATG-3′, generating fragments with 5′-cohesive ends (Sigma-Aldrich 
2020). 
 
Competent cells and plasmids were thawed on ice, and 1 𝜇l of plasmid (containing genes of interest) were 
added to 50 𝜇l of BL21/ DH5𝛼 cells in a pre-chilled Eppendorf tube and gently mixed by pipetting. Negative 
control was included with double deionized water (ddH2O) instead of the plasmid. The cells were incubated for 
30 min on ice, and the two types of competent cells were handled differently. 
DH5𝛼 cells: After incubation on ice, the cells were heat-shocked at 42C for 30 s, followed by incubation 
on ice for 2 min. Afterward, was 500 𝜇l Lennox Broth (LB) medium added to the cells, followed by incubating 
for 1 h at 37C with shaking at 180 revolutions pr. min (RPM). The cells were centrifuged at 5000 RPM, the 
supernatant was discarded, and the cell pellet was resuspended in 100 𝜇l LB medium. 
BL21 cells: After incubation on the ice, 4 volumes of SOC medium (ThermoFisher 2022) were added, 
followed by incubating for 1 h at 37C with shaking. 
The cells containing plasmids and the negative control were plated out on LB agar plates, each containing 
50 𝜇g/ml of the antibiotic kanamycin (KAN). The plates were incubated at 37C overnight. After incubation, the 
negative control plates were visually inspected for growth. The overnight cultures were prepared from the BL21 
and DH5𝛼 LB agar plates. One single growing colony was selected from each plate and transferred to 10 ml 
LB medium with 50 𝜇g/ml KAN under sterile conditions and incubated at 37C for approx. 16 h. When needed, 
==PAGE:20==
 
plasmid DNA was purified using GeneJet Plasmid Miniprep Kit and following the manufacturer's protocol 
(Thermo Scientific 2022a). 
 
The BL21 overnight cultures were transferred into shaking flasks containing 800 ml LB medium, 50 𝜇g/ml KAN 
and 10 mM glucose. The cultures were incubated at 37C and 170 RPM until an optical density at 600 nm 
(OD600) 0.6-0.8 was reached. When OD600 0.6-0.8 was reached, isopropyl-β-d-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) 
was added to each culture to a concentration of 50 mM and incubated for approx. 18 h at 20C and 170 RPM. 
After incubation, the cells were harvested by centrifuging at 5000 xg at 4C for 20 min, and the cells were 
stored at -20C until further use.  
 
Proteins were purified by immobilized metal affinity chromatography (IMAC) using His-trap protein purification 
and gel-filtration by size-exclusion chromatography (SEC). To assess the protein purity and verify the 
theoretical protein size, sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) was performed after His-trap 
and SEC purification.  
 
The frozen pellets corresponding to 7-10 g of cell from 800 ml culture were resuspended in cold 20 ml 50 mM 
HEPES, 300 mM NaCl, pH 8 buffer, and 1 𝜇l Benzonase Nuclease (Merck 2022) was added. The resuspended 
pellet was sonicated for 12 min to disrupt the cells, and the sonication cycle used was with a frequency 20 
kHz, a sonication pulse rate of 10 s for each min, a sonicate amplitude of 40%, with 50 s pauses between the 
cycle.  
After sonicating, the lysed cells were centrifuged at 20,000 xg for 20 min at 4C. A gravity-flow column 
was prepared by adding 2 ml of HisPur Ni-NTA resin (Thermo Scientific 2022b) and equilibrating the resin with 
20 ml cold 50 mM HEPES, 300 mM NaCl, pH 8 buffer.  
The supernatant from the centrifuged lysed cells was added to the resin and mixed gently. The resin-
protein mixture was transferred to a 50 ml tube followed by 30 min incubation at 4C under gentle mixing. The 
mixture was transferred to the column, and the run-through was collected. The resin in the column was washed 
twice, first (W1) with 10 ml cold 50 mM HEPES, 300 mM NaCl, pH 8 buffer, and secondly (W2) with 10 ml cold 
50 mM HEPES, 300 mM NaCl, 20 mM imidazole pH 8, and the run-through was collected. To elute the protein, 
5 ml cold 50 mM HEPES, 300 mM NaCl, 300 mM imidazole, pH 8 was added to the column, and the eluate 
was collected and stored at 4C for maximum 24 h until further purification.  
 
The 5 ml eluate from the His-trap protein purification was loaded to a HiLoad Superdex 200 16 600 size 
exclusion column (Sigma-Aldrich 2022b). The column was running with a degassed 50 mM HEPES, 150 mM 
NaCl, pH 8 as the solvent, at a 0.8 ml/min flow rate and maximum pressure at 0.3 MPa (42 psi) at 4C. After

The enzyme activity was measured spectrophotometrically by the change in absorbance at 235 nm, where the 
absorbance at 235 nm measures the formation of the unsaturated derivative product of uronic acids, occurring 
by the 𝛽-elimination (Preiss and Ashwell 1962). To observe the enzyme activity of the purified enzyme, 180 𝜇l 
substrates (alginate, CS-PG, and CS-PM) and 30 𝜇l enzyme (BoPL6, BoPL6-NTD, and BoPL6-CTD) were 
separately added into wells of the UV-STAR Microplate (Greiner bio-one 2022) and incubated for 5 min at 
37C in the plate reader. After the incubation, 20 𝜇l enzymes were transferred to 180 𝜇l substrates and started 
the plate reader immediately. The measurement of the absorbance at 235 nm was obtained using the Software 
tool Gen5 Data Analysis. Negative control of each substrate was included. All the measurements were 
performed in triplicate.

A TLC was performed to estimate the product formation occurring from alginate degradation by BoPL6, BoPL6-
NTD, and BoPL6-CTD, as described in Section 3.4.4. The TLC showed that the BoPL6 releases 
monosaccharides (DP1) when degrading CS-PG (Figure 18A), indicating that the BoPL6 has an exo-acting 
degradation mechanism. In contrast, the BoPL6-NTD released oligosaccharides/di- and trisaccharides (DP3), 
which indicates that the BoPL6-NTD has an endo-acting degradation mechanism (Figure 18A). Also, a slightly 
transparent band at DP1 became apparent. The BoPL6-CTD showed bands between DP3 and DP6 at the 
same spots as the CS-PG (negative control). 
A TLC of timepoint product analysis was performed to estimate the product formation occurring from 
alginate degradation over time, as described in Section 3.4.4. The timepoint product analysis of BoPL6 showed 
slightly visible bands at DP1 from 0-2 min but showed clearly bands at DP1 from 5-60 min of BoPL6 degrading 
CS-PG (Figure 18B1). Furthermore, a band lower than DP1 became visible after 5 min and got clearer at 60 
min (Figure 18B1). The BoPL6-NTD clearly showed bands at DP3 from 0-60 min of BoPL6-NTD degrading 
CS-PG (Figure 18B2) and no bands at DP1. The mixture of BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD showed bands at 
DP3 from 0-60 min and showed a slightly visible band at DP1 at 5 min when the mixture of BoPL6-NTD and 
BoPL6-CTD degrades CS-PG (Figure 18B3).

The homology model of BoPL6 (Figure 7) was based on the structural template of AlyGC, where the BoPL6 
has been shown to have a monomeric structure consisting of two domains (Figure 7). The AlyGC has a 
homodimeric structure which also consists of two domains in its monomers (Xu et al. 2017). Another 
polysaccharide lyase BcAlyPL6 from the human gut microbe Bacteroides Clarus, which structure has been 
solved by Wang et al., 2021, consists of two domains and is a monomer in its structure, as the homology model 
of BoPL6. The homology model of BoPL6 was performed because there is no solved crystal structure of 
BoPL6. The CTD in AlyGC partly covers the open, active site on NTD, forming a cleft (Stender et al. 2019), 
which indicates to be the same for BoPL6. Moreover, it is unknown if the BoPL6 contains any metal ion, 
whereas both AlyGC and BcALyPL6 have Ca2+ metal ions located on the NTD (Xu et al. 2017; Wang et al. 
2021).  
 
The separation of the two domains of BoPL6 has been investigated to estimate if the two domains are 
dependent on each other and their relationship. The separation of the two domains was between the amino 
acids Histidine (His434) and Proline (Pro435) (Section 4.6), which were in the middle of the linker. Furthermore, 
the linker was not completely removed from both domains after separating the two domains due to the 
possibility of an interaction between the linker that belonged to each domain. 
The separation of the two domains showed that the BoPL6-NTD had lower activity than the BoPL6, 
indicating that BoPL6-NTD loses activity when separated from BoPL6-CTD (Figure 20A). Furthermore, BoPL6-
CTD did not show any activity compared to the BoPL6 and BoPL6-NTD (Figure 20A). It was necessary to use 
an enzyme concentration of BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD at 2 𝜇M to have detectable activity of the degradation 
of CS-PG (Figure 20B) compared to the enzyme concentration of BoPL6, which was 0.5 𝜇M (Figure 20A). 
Moreover, AlyGC and BcAlyPL6 lost activity when removing the CTD from NTD; even though the active 
site was located at the NTD, the CTD was still necessary to maintain the optimum activity (Wang et al. 2021; 
Xu et al. 2017). Moreover, Wang et al. did not identify any interaction between the linker of the NTD and CTD 
of BcAlyPL6. According to Xu et al., 2018, the active site is a long cleft localized on the surface of the NTD, 
which is surrounded by -strands and loops from the NTD and one loop from the CTD. Furthermore, one end 
of the long cleft is partly covered by the BoPL6-CTD, which leads to the exo-acting mode of action. This has 
been the case for AlyGC and BcAlyPL6 (Xu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2021), indicating that BoPL6 may have 
the same catalytic cleft structure as AlyGC and BcAlyPL6. 
According to Wang et al. 2021 the catalytic cleft on NTD of BcAlyPL6 becomes open when the CTD is 
removed and suggests that the removal of the CTD from the NTD has an impact on the endo-acting activity of 
NTD. Moreover, Xu et al., 2017 claims that the NTD of AlyGC belongs to the PL6 family. In contrast, the CTD 
of AlyGC has not shown any apparent similarity to already known protein sequences. Moreover, structural 
analysis of AlyGC has shown that a loop from the CTD stretches into the catalytic center (Xu et al. 2017).  
 
The BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD had been mixed after being separated and prepared separately to 
investigate if the two domains could reconstitute (Section 4.7). The screening of the activity of the mixture of 
BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD showed that BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD could reconstitute and had the 
highest activity with a higher concentration of the BoPL6-CTD (3 𝜇M) compared to BoPL6-NTD (1 𝜇M) (Figure 
27). Again, this indicates that the BoPL6-CTD assists the activity of the BoPL6-NTD. However, the activity and 
the product release of the BoPL6 were more efficient than the mixture of BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD (Figure 
29). The lowest activity of the mixture of BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD was shown by having a higher BoPL6-
NTD concentration (3 𝜇M) compared to the BoPL6-CTD concentration (1 𝜇M) (Figure 27). This indicates that 
even if the active site of BoPL6 is located at the BoPL6-NTD, the BoPL6-CTD is necessary to impose the 
BoPL6 mode of action.  
==PAGE:46==
 
 
The substrate specificity of BoPL6 (Section 4.5.1) showed the highest activity on the substrate CS-PG (Figure 
11), indicating that BoPL6 is G-specific. The substrate specificity of BoPL6 is similar to AlyGC, which is also 
G-specific (Xu et al. 2017). Furthermore, the BoPL6 showed to have the ability to degrade AlgMG (Figure 11), 
but the degradation of AlgMG was slower than the degradation of AlgG.  
Moreover, BoPL6 showed higher activity when degrading CS-PG (6-8 kDa, Table 3) compared to the 
degradation of AlgG (268.7 kDa, Table 2) (Figure 12). This led to the hypothesis, claiming that if the natural 
alginates (AlgG, AlgMG, and AlgM) were fragmented, the alginates would be more susceptible to BoPL6 
enzymatic degradation (Section 4.5.2) because of their shorter chains. The hypothesis was confirmed, as the 
BoPL6 showed higher activity on the fragmented alginate substrates (Section 4.5.2).  
However, the alginate substrates were extracted from natural sources and could have contained 
impurities such as heavy metals, endotoxins, proteins, and polyphenolic compounds (Lee and Mooney 2012b), 
compared to the CS-PG. The impurities might bind to the enzyme's active site, thus blocking the active site for 
the alginate substrate molecule, which inhibits the enzyme's activity. 
 
The rate reaction of the BoPL6 was investigated to estimate the enzyme kinetics of BoPL6, identifying the 
affinity and rate of converting the enzyme-substrate complex into a product. As mentioned, the substrate 
specificity of BoPL6 was performed by incubating BoPL6 with CS-PG, CS-PM, AlgG, AlgMG, and AlgM (1 
mg/ml) (Section 4.5.1). However, the substrates differed in MW (Section 3.1.1), which led to different molar 
concentrations in the reactions.  
The kinetic parameters of BoPL6 were obtained from Figure 19, Table 7, and the kinetic parameters of 
the BoPL6 degrading the CS-PG (R2=0.87) (Figure 19A) and AlgG (R2=0.95) (Figure 19B). The BoPL6 had a 
Km 0.34623 ± 0.08788 mg/ml -1 when degrading CS-PG, which showed a higher affinity than the degradation 
affinity AlgG, which was Km 1.24422 ± 0.42758 mg/ml-1 (Table 7). The higher the Km-value, the lower the 
enzyme affinity, which means the enzyme requires a high substrate concentration to be fully saturated and 
vice versa. Furthermore, the Km is dependent on enzyme concentration, substrate concentration, pH, 
temperature, and ionic strength. When the substrate concentration is low, it will lead to many available enzyme 
molecules. Increasing the substrate concentration leads to the substrate molecules competing for an enzyme 
molecule to pair with. Further increasing the substrate concentration will lead to all the enzyme molecules 
attaching to a substrate molecule leading to saturated enzymes(Berg et al. 2009). 
The BoPL6 had a kcat 1.78182 s-1 when degrading CS-PG and when degrading AlgG was kcat 0.75066   
s-1 (Table 7) which indicates that the degradation and product formation of CS-PG occurred faster than the 
AlgG, which is because of the impurities of the AlgG (64.6 % G) (Table 4) compared to CS-PG (82 % G) (Table 
3). Moreover, the BoPL6 has a kcat/Km 5.14629 mg/ml -1 s-1 when degrading CS-PG and a kcat/Km 0.60331 
mg/ml-1 s-1 when degrading AlyG (Table 7), where the BoPL6 had a more catalytic efficiency towards CS-PG. 
The kcat/Km is a rate constant measuring catalytic efficiency, taking both the kcat and Km into account (Berg et 
al. 2009). 
The Michaelis Menten fitting model was also performed with the substrates AlgMG, AlgM, and CS-PM, 
which showed that it was impossible to perform kinetics on these specific substrates, which can be caused by 
the BoPL6 having specificity towards G. 
 
The mode of action of BoPL6, BoPL6-NTD, BoPL6-CTD, and the mixture of BoPL6-NTD/BoPL6-CTD and 
what product each released when degrading CS-PG was investigated to estimate if these enzymes were endo-
acting or exo-acting. The mode of action was monitored by incubating BoPL6, BoPL6-NTD, BoPL6-CTD with 
CS-PG and then monitoring the absorbance at 235 nm over 1 h. The absorbance (235 nm) monitoring of the 
enzymes had the ability to indicate the mode of action, where the formation of the unsaturated product of 
uronic acids, occurring by the 𝛽-elimination, could absorb light at 235 nm (Preiss and Ashwell 1962).  
==PAGE:47==
 
 
 
The BoPL6 showed a rapid formation when degrading CS-PG, compared to BoPL6-NTD (Figure 20A), which 
indicates that the mode of action of BoPL6 is exo-acting and releasing unsaturated monosaccharides. In 
contrast, the BoPL6-NTD indicates an endo-acting mode of action releasing oligosaccharides (Figure 20B) 
(Preiss and Ashwell 1962). The mixture of the BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD also showed to be exo-acting and 
releasing unsaturated monosaccharides. (Figure 20A).  
The TLC analysis showed that BoPL6 releases DP1 (Figure 18A), which strengthens the estimation of 
the BoPL6 being exo-acting and G-specific. The timepoint analysis of BoPL6 started to show a band at DP1 
after 5 min of product formation (Figure 18B), which aligns with the monitored absorbance showing that the Δ 
absorbance starts to decrease after 5 min (Figure 20A), confirms indication of the mode of action and product 
release of BoPL6. A TLC analysis of AlyGC was performed by Xu et al., which also showed to release of 
unsaturated monosaccharides from CS-PG, strengthening the estimation of BoPL6 having an exo-acting 
mechanism. The timepoint analysis of the mixture of BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD (Figure 18B) also showed 
a slightly clear band at DP3 after 5 min of incubation. 
The TLC analysis of BoPL6-NTD showed to release DP3 (Figure 18A) as a clear band appeared, which 
indicates that the BoPL6-NTD has an endo-acting mode of action. The timepoint analysis of the BoPL6-NTD 
also showed to release DP3 when incubated with CS-PG, which strengthens the indication that the BoPL6-
NTD is endo-acting. Even though the activity measurement of the BoPL6-CTD (2 𝜇M) (Section 4.6.1) showed 
that the BoPL6-CTD did not have a detectable activity at absorbance 235 nm (Figure 20B), a TLC analysis of 
BoPL6-CTD was still performed to estimate if any product formation occurred of BoPL6-CTD with CS-PG. 
TLC analysis of BoPL6-CTD showed some bands at DP3 and higher (Figure 18A), which was 
hypothesized to be caused by CS-PG. The CS-PG was spotted on the TLC plate without any enzyme to 
observe any possible product formation from CS-PG. The TLC analysis of the CS-PG showed bands at DP3 
and a slightly clear band higher than DP3 (Figure 18A), which confirms the hypothesis that the product 
formation observed from BoPL6-CTD was originating from CS-PG.  
 
The activity and dependency on pH, NaCl, metal ion, and temperature of BoPL6 have been investigated 
(Section 3.4) to characterize BoPL6 and obtain knowledge on the optimal preferences of BoPL6. As the 
BoPL6-NTD showed a detectable activity, it was interesting to characterize the single domain BoPL6-NTD as 
some AL are single domains in their structure. It was relevant to obtain knowledge on the BoPL6-NTD from 
the BoPL6 and compare it with other single domain ALs, e.g., AlyF from the marine bacterium Vibrio splendidus 
OU2 and BcelPL6 from the HGM Bacteroides cellulosilyticus CRE21 (Violot et al. 2021). A BoPL6-CTD 
characterization was not performed because the BoPL6-CTD did not show any detectable activity at 
absorbance 235 nm.  
The difference in the activity of the enzyme, depending on pH, occurs because changing the pH can 
change the shape and structure of the enzyme and lead to the ionization of amino acids of the enzyme and 
the substrate. Moreover, a pH higher or lower than the optimal pH can lead to activity loss of the enzyme 
because of denaturation (Blanco and Blanco 2017).  
Most Als have optimal conditions close to a neutral pH (pH 7). The activity of BoPL6 was highly affected by 
the pH, where pH 3 completely inhibited the enzyme activity. As mentioned, most ALs has optimal conditions 
close to pH 7, which has been the same case for BoPL6 (Section 4.5.3). The optimal pH AlyGC was also pH 
7 (Xu et al. 2017).  
As mentioned, it is indicated that BoPL6 is G-specific and shown to have an optimal pH at 7 when 
degrading AlgG (Figure 13A). Furthermore, the optimal pH 7 is aligned with the pH of the human gut, which is 
also approx. pH 7 (Evans et al. 1988). The pH dependency was also investigated for BoPL6-NTD (Section 
4.6.2.1), which had an optimal pH around 7 when degrading alginate substrates. The optimal pH of AlyF (Lyu 
et al. 2019a) and BcelPL6 (Stender et al. 2019) are 7.5 and are similar to the optimal pH of BoPL6-NTD. 
The exposure of different NaCl concentrations to BoPL6 showed that the activity of BoPL6 differs (Section 
4.5.4). Generally, the activity of BoPL6 when degrading alginate substrates was inhibited as the NaCl 
concentration increased, except for the reaction that did not contain any NaCl (Figure 14). The BoPL6 and 
==PAGE:48==
 
BoPL6-NTD showed a low salt tolerance. This indicated that a low NaCl concentration in the range of 0.1-0.2 
M enhanced the activity of BoPL6 and BoPL6-NTD when degrading the alginate substrates compared to the 
BoPL6 and BoPL6-NTD without NaCl. However, the BoPL6-NTD showed the highest activity without NaCl 
when degrading AlgG. The AlyF had an optimal NaCl concentration of 0.2 M (Lyu et al. 2019b), and the 
BcelPL6 had optimal activity without NaCl (Stender et al. 2019). 
The metal ion for some enzymes is essential for the catalytic action, and it can supply the catalytic process 
through their capability of attracting or donating electrons. Furthermore, some metals bind the substrate by 
coordination links (Blanco, 2017). The metal ions showed to enhance or inhibit the activity of BoPL6 (Section 
4.5.5) and BoPL6-NTD (Section 4.6.2.3) when degrading alginate substrates. Furthermore, metal ions showed 
to be depending on the alginate substrate.  
The optimal metal ion of BoPL6 when degrading AlgG was Mg2+ (Figure 15A) and had the same activity 
as the recombinant BoPL6, indicating that the BoPL6 might contain Mg2+. The EDTA treated BoPL6 degrading 
AlgG showed to be approx. 50 % less active than the original BoPL6 and the Mg2+ incubated BoPL6 (Figure 
15A), which indicates that BoPL6 might contain a metal ion. The metal ion Ca2+ enhanced the activity of BoPL6 
(degrading AlgMG and AlgM) and BoPL6-NTD (degrading AlgG). Both AlyF and BcelPL6 had an optimal 
activity with the metal ion Ca2+ (Stender et al. 2019; Lyu et al. 2019b).  
The increase in temperature can increase the rate of degrading the substrate. Furthermore, when the 
enzyme activity increases as the temperature increases, a maximum temperature can be reached (Optimal 
temperature). Temperature above the optimal temperature decreases the enzyme's activity because of 
denaturation (Blanco and Blanco 2017). Generally, the activity of BoPL6 enhanced as the temperature 
increased. 
 As the BoPL6 and BoPL6-NTD is origin from a human gut microbiota, it would have been optimal if 
BoPL6 had the highest activity degrading alginate as the temperature as the human gut, which is approx. 37C. 
However, BoPL6 (Section 4.5.6) and BoPL6-NTD (Section 4.6.2.4) showed an increase in the activity as the 
temperature increased. The highest temperature where the highest activity of BoPL6 was at 50C, whereas 
for the BoPL6-NTD, the temperature was at 45C. The AlyGC had an optimal temperature at 30C when 
degrading alginate (Xu et al. 2018), and the AlyF and BcelPL6 had an optimum temperature at 30C. 
A high thermostability can result in the catalyst reaction proceeding at a higher temperature. Proceeding 
at a higher temperature can reduce the viscosity of the reaction mixture and improve enzyme activity, thus 
facilitating the transformation of the substrate. When applying alginate lyases, the most important factor is the 
thermostable or heat-stale property (Gao et al. 2021). Alginate lyases with high thermostability are very 
important for commercial and industrial purposes (Gao et al. 2021). 
The thermostability of BoPL6 was highly affected by the change of pH, and the thermostability of BoPL6 
depending on pH had a Tm range between 42-50C, with the highest stability at pH 5 with a Tm 50C and lowest 
stability at pH 3 at 42C (Section 4.5.7). The thermostability of BoPL6 with 0-1 M NaCl concentration was not 
affected and had a Tm range between 46-47C (Section 4.5.7). 
The pH had also shown to affect the thermostability of BoPL6-NTD and had a Tm range between 38-54C, with 
the highest stability at pH 6 with a Tm 54C and lowest stability at pH 3 at 38C (Section 4.6.2.5). The pH also 
showed to affect the thermostability of BoPL6-NTD and had a Tm range between 38-54C, with the highest 
stability at pH 6 with a Tm 54C and lowest stability at pH 3 at 38C (Section 4.6.2.5). The thermostability of 
BoPL6-CTD was also affected by the pH, but not as much as BoPL6 and BoPL6-NTD. The BoPL6-CTD (black) 
was not as stable as BoPL6-NTD (green) or the mixture of the two domains (red) (Appendix 9.7, Figure 37). 
BoPL6-CTD had a Tm range between 35-44C.  
The thermostability of the BoPL6 and the mixture of BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD did not have any 
significant differences, indicating that the thermostability of the mixture of BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD was 
as good as the BoPL6.

In this study, the structure of BoPL6 was based on a homology model, using the template of AlyGC, which is 
a dimeric structure, whereas the BoPL6 showed to be monomeric in its structure consisting of two domains. 
As the actual structure of the BoPL6 is unknown, is it essential to obtain a crystal structure BoPL6 and perform 
an X-ray diffraction crystallography to determine the atomic and molecular structure of the BoPL6 crystal, such 
as the catalytic site, the catalytic residues, and any possible metal ion or water molecules. 
Moreover, could it be interesting to perform a binding study of BoPL6, BoPL6-NTD, and BoPL6-CTD by 
Isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC), to obtain knowledge on the binding interactions that occur between the 
BoPL6, BoPL6-NTD, and BoPL6-CTD and substrate. 
 
This study indicates that the BoPL6 produces unsaturated monosaccharides in an exo-acting mode of action, 
whereas the BoPL6-NTD has been indicated to produce oligosaccharides in an endo-acting mode of action, 
both from the substrate CS-PG.  
To determine the mode of action and product formation of BoPL6 and BoPL6-NTD, a TLC analysis was 
performed. However, TLC analysis is not the most accurate analysis and only gives an estimation of the 
product released by BoPL6 and BoPL6-NTD. To quantify the end products from the degradation of the CS-PG 
by BoPL6, BoPL6-NTD, and BoPL6-CTD, it is essential to perform a liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry 
(LC-MS) analysis. Furthermore, the samples of the timepoint analysis were prepared to perform an LC-MS 
analysis, but due to the lack of time, this was not conducted.  
The LC-MS analysis of the timepoint analysis will identify the predominant end product and the progress 
of the products released at each timepoint from by BoPL6, BoPL6-NTD, and the mixture of BoPL6-NTD and 
BoPL6-CTD degrading CS-PG. However, could it be considered to conduct new timepoint samples for the 
mixture of the two domains, where the BoPL6-CTD have a higher concentration than the BoPL6-NTD in the 
mixture. The end products released from CS-PG degradation could also be identified by using SEC. 
Moreover, it could be interesting to incubate and perform a TLC analysis or a more accurate analysis LC-
MS of BoPL6 and BoPL6-NTD with the CS-PMG and CS-PM to identify the product released from these 
substrates and to see if the mode of action changes depending on the substrate.  
Also, this study indicated that the BoPL6 was G-specific, as a substrate specify experiment was 
conducted, whereas a substrate specificity experiment of BoPL6-NTD was not investigated because the 
BoPL6-NTD was a part of BoPL6. The substrate specificity of BoPL6-NTD would be interesting to investigate 
as the BoPL6-NTD is a single domain and has shown to be endo-acting on CS-PG. Furthermore, there was 
inconsistency in the measured absorbance and the apparent initial velocity of BoPL6-NTD when degrading 
AlgG was presented, but not when degrading AlgMG or AlgM. This could be an indication that BoPL6-NTD 
could be M-specific as BcelPL6.

The newly identified Polysaccharide lyase family 6 enzyme originates from an untyped human gut microbiota 
Bacteroides ovatus, referred to as BoPL6. The BoPL6 is from subfamily PL6, and based on the homology 
model of BoPL6 enzyme, which consists of two domains, a BoPL6-NTD (the catalytic site) and a BoPL6-CTD 
with an unknown function. This report presents the preliminary biochemical characterization of BoPL6, which 
can be used as the starting point for further in-depth experiments, characterization, and analysis.  
The separation of the two domains was possible; however, the activity of BoPL6-NTD significantly 
decreased when the BoPL6-CTD was removed. Furthermore, the BoPL6-CTD did not show any detectable 
activity when degrading alginate substrates by itself, and the BoPL6-CTD showed to assist the activity of the 
BoPL6-NTD. Moreover, it was possible to reconstitute the BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD, and the highest 
activity was when the BoPL6-CTD had a higher concentration than the BoPL6-NTD in the mixture of the 
reconstitution. However, the activity and product release was more efficient when the original BoPL6 degraded 
CS-PG than the mixture of the BoPL6-NTD and BoPL6-CTD. 
Moreover, the BoPL6 showed to be G-specific and have an exo-acting mode of action using the anti-
elimination mechanism releasing unsaturated monosaccharide from the degradation of CS-PG. The BoPL6-
NTD has an endo-acting mode of action, releasing oligosaccharides from CS-PG. Furthermore, BoPL6 and 
BoPL6-NTD prefer the CS-PG compared to the alginate substrate AlgG.  
While the optimal activity of BoPL6 depends on pH, NaCl concentration, metal ion, and temperature, it is 
also affected and dependent on the alginate substrate. Also, BoPL6 is G-specific and had a Km at 0.34623 ± 
0.08788 mg/ml -1 and kcat at 1.78182 s-1 when degrading CS-PG, and a Km at 1.24422 ± 0.42758 mg/ml -1 and 
kcat at 0.75066 s-1when degrading AlgG.

The fragmented alginate (AlgG, AlgMG, and AlgM) which was made by the supervisor Dr. Mikkel Madsen, 
were analyzed by Finn L. Achmann and Mina Gravdahl at the Norwegian University of Science and Technology 
(NTNU). The analysis was performed to determine the M୬ and M୵ of AlgG, AlgMG and AlgM, by using SEC-
MALS. The following description was obtained directly from the given description sent by Finn L. Achmann 
and Mina Gravdahl describing purpose, sample preparation method, and SEC-MALS use.  
Purpose: Determination of M୬ and M୵ using SEC-MALS  
Sample preparation: The sample was dissolved (1 mg/ml) in SEC-MALS buffer (0.15 M NaNO3 with 
0.01 M EDTA (pH = 6.0)) and left on stirring overnight. The sample was further filtered (0.22 μm) and injected 
into the S-MALS system.  
S-MALS: Size-exclusion chromatography (SEC) with online multi-angle static laser light scattering 
(MALLS) were performed at ambient temperature on an HPLC system consisting of a solvent reservoir, online 
degasser, HPLC isocratic pump, automatic sample injector, pre-column (LB guard) and two main columns 
(OHpak LB 803 and Ohpak LB 804). The column outlet was connected to a Dawn HELEOS-II multi-angle laser 
light scattering photometer (Wyatt, U.S.A.) (λ0 = 663.8 nm) followed by Shodex RI-501 refractive index 
detector. The eluent was 0.15 M NaNO3 with 0.01 M EDTA (pH = 6.0) and the flow rate was 0.5 
ml/min. Samples were dissolved in mobile phase overnight and filtered (pore size 0.22 μm) before injection. 
All samples were injected twice. Data were collected and processed (with dn/dc = 0.150 ml/g and A2 = 5.0 x 
10-3 ml mol g-2) using Astra software (Wyatt, USA).
