Abstract
The growth of hydraulic fracture depends on the in-situ stresses and the mechanical properties of the
rock. A layered reservoir with diﬀerent mechanical properties may lead to diﬀerent fracture propagation
geometry. In this study, a numerical model based on ﬁnite-element method is used to investigate the
propagation of a single hydraulic fracture in a three-layered model. The model simultaneously accounts
for ﬂuid ﬂow within the fracture, poroelastic deformation of the rock and the propagation of the fracture.
A softer, ﬁnite thickness layer is included in the model to investigate its eﬀect on the direction of the
hydraulic fracture. A sensitivity analysis is performed on the thickness and mechanical properties of this
soft, intermediate layer. The eﬀect of gravity is also considered by applying linearly increasing far-ﬁeld
stresses. The model is validated against several analytical solutions, including an analytical solution
for a plane-strain fracture under toughness-regime and for a radial fracture under the toughness- and
viscosity-dominated regimes. Simulations are done for both plane strain hydraulic fracture and radial
hydraulic fracture, and the results are compared. The results suggest that the mechanical properties
and the layer thickness control the fracture’s upward growth. When the gravity is included, the fractures
tend to grow upward as the gravitational gradient is reduced in this direction. The existence of a soft
layer at the top of the reservoir may prevent upward propagation of hydraulic fracture under speciﬁc
conditions.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Hydraulic fracturing is a process in which rock is fractured by a pressurised liquid. These processes can
happen both naturally or man-made by injecting a viscous ﬂuid into a selected section of a wellbore.
Dykes and mineral-ﬁlled veins are examples of natural hydraulic fractures (Philipp et al., 2013). In the
petroleum industry the technique is widely used to stimulate production from a reservoir. By fracturing
the reservoir, it creates high permeable paths where ﬂuid can ﬂow more easily, thus improving volu-
metric sweep and the recovery. Hydraulic fracturing is also known for its slang term “fracking”. The
combination of horizontal drilling and hydraulic fracturing made the American shale revolution, which
has changed the oil industry in the recent years, possible.
Hydraulic fracturing was ﬁrst introduced in the late 1940s in the U.S., but the methods can be traced
back to the 1860s when nitroglycerin was used to stimulate the well. Nowadays, the process of hydraulic
fracturing in the ﬁeld starts after the drilled hole is completed, casings are inserted and cemented in
place. Hydraulic fracturing happens in several stages and starts at the end of the wellbore (tow) and
is completed towards the heel. The ﬁrst step is to perforate the casing. This is done by a perforating
gun which is lowered into the position inside the well and shoots holes through the casing and into the
formation. This serves as the initiation point of the hydraulic fracture since the fracturing ﬂuid can enter
the formation and later allows for gas and oil to enter the wellbore. Step two is to inject the fracturing
ﬂuid into the formation at a rate high enough to break the formation. The high rate increases the liquid
pressure in the borehole so the tensile stress in the surrounding rock exceeds the tensile strength of the
rock and a fracture is induced. Continued ﬂuid injection increases the fracture surface in the direction
normal to the least resistance. The fracturing ﬂuid normally consists of water, some chemical additives
and solid particles called proppants. The chemical additives are normally some kind of acid to help
dissolve the rock and gel to thicken the ﬂuid, while the proppants are often just grains of sand to help
1
==PAGE:10==
keep the fractures open after the hydraulic pressure is removed from the well. This method of hydraulic
fracturing was such a success that it increased the production with an average of 75 % the ﬁrst year
(Montgomery et al., 2010). The hydraulic fracturing continues to play a signiﬁcantly role in order
to increase the well’s productivity, and thousands of treatments worldwide are successfully performed
each year. The technique is also used in other applications outside the oil and gas industry such as in
enhanced geothermal systems (EGS), groundwater remediation, water well production and rock stress
determination for constructions like tunnels. Due to its many applications, a robust, three-dimensional
hydraulic fracturing model is required to perform the simulations in a variety of diﬀerent properties in
rock, in-situ stresses, ﬂuids and proppants.
1.1
Hydraulic fracturing models
Subsurface formations are conﬁned and under stress which can be divided under three principal stresses.
The magnitude and the orientations of these stresses are important, since they control the size and
the direction of hydraulic fractures. Hydraulic fractures are tensile fractures (extension fractures) and
propagate in the direction perpendicular to the least stress. The minimum stress in a normal faulting
regime is in a horizontal direction making the fracture vertical. By knowing the in-situ stresses, the
properties of the rock and the fracturing ﬂuid, one can attempt to predict geometry of the induced
fracture. The ﬁrst models were analytical and the aim was to calculate the ﬂuid pressure, opening, and
the size of the fracture for simple geometries when injection rate and rock and ﬂuid properties were
given. Any hydraulic models needs to couple at least three processes. First, the mechanical deformation
induced by the ﬂuid pressure on the fracture surfaces. Second, the ﬂow of ﬂuid within the fracture,
and third, the fracture propagation.
1.1.1
Analytical solutions
The work of Khristianovic and Zheltov (1955) and Perkins et al. (1961) are often cited as the most
prominent models developed early on after the foundation was laid by Sneddon (Sneddon and Elliot,
1946; Sneddon, 1946). These are two dimensional models where the height is assumed ﬁxed. Khris-
tianovic and Zheltov and later Geertsma et al. (1969) based their model on plane strain assumption
in horizontal planes and is now called the KGD (Khristianovic-Geertsma and de Klerk) model. Fur-
thermore it assumes that the fracture width is constant along the vertical plane making it a vertical
rectangular cross section and suits for fractures where the length is much smaller than their height. The
model is applicable to hydraulic fractures that are created from either a vertical well, or a horizontal
well aligned with the maximum horizontal stress direction. In both cases, the induced fracture will be
2
==PAGE:11==
along the well, therefore, the height of the fracture can be assumed to be much longer than its length.
Perkins and Kern developed the PK model and later Nordgren et al. (1972) included the eﬀects of ﬂuid
loss in 1972 to make what is now known as the PKN model. The PKN model assumes a plane strain
in the vertical plane and the vertical cross-section has an elliptical form. This makes the model best
suited when the fracture length is large compared to the height.
The diﬀerence between KGD and PKN leads to two diﬀerent ways of solving the problem. The PKN
assumes that the width of the tip is zero, which implies that the ﬂuid pressure is also zero and ignoring
the eﬀect at the tip. The KDG on the other hand includes the fracture mechanics aspects of the fracture
tip with the use of Barenblatt’s tip condition which is a cusp-shaped tip where the tip closes smoothly
and the pressure is zero near the tip. Assuming zero pressure at the tip makes it impossible to deﬁne
the stress intensity factor (Adachi and Peirce, 2008). Geertsma and de Klerk argued that the toughness
of the rock was irrelevant since the tip was assumed a local singularity of the fracture, and would have
little eﬀect when the fracture is large enough. Abe et al. (1976) came to an opposite conclusion in
his paper about asymmetric penny-shaped model in 1976. If the well is along the minimum far-ﬁeld
stress, the induced fracture will propagate in a plane perpendicular to the well, which for homogeneous,
isotropic formation under uniform stress creates a penny-shape fracture also known as radial fracture.
Simplifying the material, loading, fracture geometry and the ﬂuid properties makes the modeling of the
hydraulic fracturing process easier. These assumptions include isotropic and linear elastic rock mass,
planar fractures, symmetric with respect to the wellbore, no leakoﬀand Newtonian ﬂuids to name a
few. Both the KGD and PKN models were derived on the basis of many assumptions. Daneshy et al.
(1973) argued that some of these assumptions, which aﬀected the computed results, were not essential.
They pointed out that some of the assumption could be eliminated without changing the results, while
eliminating other assumptions would change the results markedly. In their paper, they extended the
KGD model by introducing the power-law for ﬂuids which signiﬁcantly reduced the error for viscous
ﬂuids, and Spence and Sharp (1985) introduced fracture toughness into the KGD model.
Hydraulic fracturing takes place under a range of parameters such as ﬂuid viscosity, rock properties,
injection rate and far-ﬁeld stresses. The analytical models tend to take the form of asymptotic solutions
at the tip and over the last decades many solutions have been proposed. It has been found that the
asymptotic solution is determined by the relation between energy required to break the rock and the
energy dissipated by the viscous ﬂuid ﬂow at the crack tip. When the energy required to break the rock
is much larger than the energy dissipated by the ﬂuid, the regime is called toughness-dominated regime.
If it is the other way around, the regime is called viscosity-dominated regime (Savitski and Detournay,
2002; Detournay and Garagash, 2003). These solutions are accurate in these extreme cases, however in
3
==PAGE:12==
a real fracturing process the parameters are often intermediate. Nevertheless, these solutions provide
useful benchmark for numerical models.
In numerical modeling, operational parameters such as injection rate, pumping time and ﬂuid viscosity
can be chosen more satisfactorily. The oil industry were also keen to develop these models to estimate
how and where the fractures were growing. The prediction of growth behavior is essential to calculating
the performance and eﬃciency of the reservoir. Hence, hydraulic fracture simulation soon became an
integral part of the design and analysis of treatments both for stimulation and reservoir characteri-
zation (Lecampion et al., 2017). Variations of KGD, PKN and the radial model remain popular in
the petroleum industry as useful benchmarks for numerical simulations, but these simple models are
limited where perhaps the biggest limitation is the assumption of fracture shape. The classical models
assume a ﬁxed fracture height or assume that a radial fracture will develop. Additionally, the models
are not accurate in all parameter ranges and not applicable in layered reservoirs with diﬀerent conﬁning
stresses. Computers and numerical simulations have made it possible to model the fracture growth in
more complex fracture geometries.
1.1.2
Three-dimensional and pseudo-three-dimensional models
One of the most challenging problems in hydraulic fracture modeling is the prediction of fracture height.
Most hydraulic fracturing treatments are carried out in layered formations, where the layers have diﬀer-
ent mechanical properties. The properties of these layers alter the stress regime locally and hence aﬀect
the fracture growth. During a hydraulic fracturing treatment it is critical to control the fracture height
since an uncontrolled fracture height can lead to unwanted gas or water production if the fracture is not
contained inside the pay layer. Numerous of studies have shown the eﬀects of Young’s modulus, fracture
toughness and in-situ stress on hydraulic fracture height containment in layered formations. Simonson
et al. (1978) studied the stress intensity factor as the fracture approached the interface of a layer with
diﬀerent Young’s modulus. They found that the stress intensity factor increased to inﬁnity when a
fracture approached a low-modulus layer from a high-modulus layer, thus promoting growth toward the
low-modulus layer. When the fracture approached a high-modulus layer from a low-modulus layer, the
stress intensity factor decreased towards zero, thus making it harder for the fracture to grow towards the
high-modulus layer. They therefore stated that a layer with a higher Young’s modulus acts as a barrier
of fracture propagation from a lower modulus formation layer. Gu et al. (2006) found that both types of
modulus contrast, low-to-high and high-to-low, hinder fracture height growth, but caused by diﬀerent
mechanisms. A fracture approaching a high-modulus layer from a low-modulus layer is hindered due to
the decrease in the stress intensity factors, whereas a fracture approaching a lower-modulus layer from
4
==PAGE:13==
a high-modulus layer is hindered when the fracture is in it. This is because the fracture width is wider
in the lower-modulus, which then causes a lower net-pressure (the diﬀerence in ﬂuid pressure inside the
fracture minus the least principal stress). Similarly, Brenner and Gudmundsson (2004) concluded that
the aperture was greater in soft layers than in stiﬀlayers. Laboratory experiments by Daneshy et al.
(1978) and in-situ experiments carried out by Warpinski et al. (1982a) showed that contrast in elastic
moduli alone was not suﬃcient to stop the growth of a fracture across an interface, later Smith et al.
(2001) came to a similar conclusion.
Simonson et al. (1978) also argued that a diﬀerence in in-situ stress between two layers could serve as
a barrier to vertical extension of the fracture. In their paper, they considered sharp step variations of
in-situ stress between layers made from lateral tectonic stress. They showed that a layer with a greater
in-situ stress served as a barrier to vertical extension of the fracture due to the increase in required
fracturing pressure necessary to continue the propagation. They also wrote that if the barrier layer had
a lower in-situ stress, an extensive propagation into the barrier layer would be highly probable. Teufel
and Clark (1981) demonstrated that the vertical growth of a hydraulic fracture could be contained by a
weak shear strength along the interface of the layers and more importantly the increase in the minimum
horizontal in-situ stress in the bounding layer. They argued that the diﬀerences in elastic properties
of the layers aﬀected the hydraulic fracture growth indirectly since the variation of elastic properties
aﬀected the in-situ stresses state of individual layers. Laboratory experiments made by Warpinski et al.
(1982b) showed that the in-situ stress contrast was suﬃcient to control the fracture height contain-
ment. Although, the gravitational gradient of the minimal principal in-situ stress was not considered.
Because of these studies, it is generally recognized that the in-situ contrast is the dominant parameter
controlling fracture height growth and that Young’s modulus contrast is less important (Gu et al.,
2006). Daneshy et al. (2009) mentioned that blunting of the fracture tip or sliding at the interface also
were a likely mechanism for fracture containment.
The KGD and PKN-models are not applicable to layered reservoirs since they assume that the fracture
is contained by the interfaces. The development of Pseudo-3D (P3D) models therefore arose in the
1980s (Advani et al., 1982; Van Eekelen et al., 1982; Settari et al., 1986) to try to model the vertical
growth of the fracture across layers in layered formations. P3D models can handle the fracture height
growth while maintaining the lateral coupling for ﬂuid pressure, so the initial 3D problem is reduced
to a 2D elastic problem and a 1D ﬂuid problem, which reduces the computational time (Adachi et al.,
2010). The model builds on the work of Simonson et al. (1978) and is an extension of the PKN model,
thus applicable in cases where the height of the fracture remains small compared to its length. P3D
models are categorized in two groups: cell-based and lumped models. The cell-based model treats the
fracture as a series of discrete connected PKN-like cells in a plane strain, where each cell has its own
5
==PAGE:14==
computed height. Lumped models assume that the vertical fracture proﬁle consist of two half ellipse
joined at their centers in the fracture length direction. The fracture length is calculated at each time
step where the dimension changes according to the amount of ﬂuid in the fracture. P3D assume that
the reservoirs elastic properties are homogeneous and averaged over all layers containing the fracture
height. This of course is a simpliﬁcation of a real reservoir which can consist of multiple layers with
heterogeneous properties on all relevant scales. In the cases the P3D models are not applicable, such
as where the middle layer in a three-layered system is stiﬀer or when the layer conﬁning stresses vary
non-monotonically as a function of depth, a planar-3D model (PL3D) can be used (Adachi et al., 2007).
The numerical implementation of a planar-3D model were ﬁrst reported by Clifton et al. (1979). In
their paper, they coupled the fracture opening with a two-dimensional ﬂuid ﬂow, which previously was
only assumed in horizontal direction. Numerous numerical approaches to model these models have
been attempted since then, which is investigated in the next section. The PL3D models describe the
fracture geometry by a 2D mesh of cells, either on a moving triangular mesh or a ﬁxed rectangular
mesh. The ﬂuid ﬂow is accounted for in full 2D and coupled to the 3D elastic response of the rock.
The fracture propagation is represented in planar direction and oriented perpendicular to the far-ﬁeld
minimum in-situ stress, the PL3D is therefore not applicable to fracture growth with curvatures and
the interactions between multiple hydraulic fractures (Salimzadeh et al., 2017a).
Planar-3D models are more accurate than 2D- and pseudo-3D models, but can be computationally
expensive. 2D models are less expensive, but the results are often simplistic. The pseudo-3D models
provide a compromise and is why they are the most used model in the industry. A more extensive
review of the P3D- and PL3D models can be found in Mendelsohn (1984); Economides et al. (2000)
and Adachi et al. (2010).
1.1.3
Numerical approaches
It is now well understood that the hydraulic fracturing process involves several coupled phenomena
(Adachi and Detournay, 2008). The processes include: 1) Poroelastic deformation of the solid medium,
induced by the ﬂuid pressure acting on the fracture surface. 2) Flow of ﬂuid within the fracture. 3)
Propagation of the fracture. 4) Leak-oﬀof fracturing ﬂuid into the permeable medium, and 5) The ﬂow
through porous medium (Salimzadeh and Khalili, 2016). Adachi et al. (2007) write that the complexity
of this already challenging coupled problem can easily be extended by additional complications such
as, presence of layers of diﬀerent types of rock, changes in magnitude and/or orientation of the in situ
conﬁning stresses, the shear and temperature on the fracturing ﬂuid rheology etc. Often are one or more
of these mechanisms left out of the models, depending on the purpose of the model. The last decade
has seen numerous numerical approaches to solve the coupled system of equations and to model the
6
==PAGE:15==
hydraulic fracture problem. The major complication, of course, is that the governing equations depend
upon one another and needs to be solved simultaneously.
The mechanical deformation is usually modeled using the theory of linear elasticity. This express the
relationships between the fracture opening and the pressure due to the injected ﬂuid. The elastic problem
can be solved by any type of numerical methods but are essentially divided into two main choices: 1)
An integral equation formulation such as the displacement discontinuity method or 2) discretization of
the partial diﬀerential equations via the ﬁnite element or ﬁnite diﬀerence methods. The ﬂuid ﬂow is
usually modeled using the lubrication theory, which by a non-linear partial diﬀerential equation relates
the ﬂuid ﬂow in the the fracture to the fracture opening and the ﬂuid pressure gradient. To predict
the fracture propagation there are generally two main approaches: The ﬁrst approach explicitly tracks
the fracture front at each given time step based on the Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM).
This theory assumes that the material is isotropic and linear elastic and that the fracture propagates
when the stress intensity factor, which deﬁne the stress state at the tip of the crack, exceeds the rock
toughness. There are three modes of fracture opening at the crack tip: Mode I (KI) for opening due
to tensile loading, Mode II (KII) for in-plane shearing due to sliding, and Mode III (KIII) for out-of-
plane shearing due to tearing. In hydraulic fracturing processes, the solid medium normally experiences
tensile stress, thus, the mode I (opening) is dominant whereas mode II and mode III are negligible,
unless the shear components of the stress become considerable due to the interactions between the
hydraulic fracture and other fractures or layers (Salimzadeh et al., 2017a).
Figure 1.1: The three crack-tip deformation modes: (I) opening mode, (II) sliding mode, and
(III) tearing mode, in which top half of ﬁgure moves into page, bottom half moves out of page.
(Jaeger et al., 2009)
Another approach for predicting fracture propagation is based on the cohesive zone model (CZM)
(Barenblatt, 1962; Dugdale, 1960). The stress singularity that is present in the LEFM poses challenges
for numerical modeling (Chen et al., 2009). The CZM model assumes the existence of a fracture process
7
==PAGE:16==
zone, characterized by a traction-separation law. In this way, the singularity in the stress ﬁeld at the
fracture tip is vanished. Examples of models using the cohesive model are Chen et al. (2009); Carrier
and Granet (2012); Salimzadeh and Khalili (2015).
Peirce and Siebrits (2001) and Lecampion and Detournay (2007) based their model on the displacement
discontinuity method, which is an indirect boundary element technique. This application’s advantage is
in its high computational eﬃciency through discretizing only on the boundaries rather than the whole
domain. This makes it perhaps the most eﬃcient method for modeling in a homogeneous medium.
However, in a multi-layered medium it is necessary to discretize the layer interfaces and to bond the ma-
terials within the layers by imposing continuity conditions. The solution will therefore become complex,
require more computational eﬀort and the size of problem that can be solved accurately will be restricted.
Various ﬁnite element-based methods are often chosen when the body shape is irregular or when the
material properties are heterogeneous. For instance, Carrier and Granet (2012) used a zero-thickness
element to model the propagating fracture, and conventional bulk ﬁnite elements with a cohesive zone.
The use of this type of elements allow for explicit fracture representation and easy solution of reservoir
and fracture models on their respective computational domains (Chukwudozie, 2016). However, the
fracture path must be postulated a priori. In their paper, only two-dimensional results were presented,
but wrote that the method could be extended to 3D and to more complex geometries. Another ap-
proach is the use of adaptive re-meshing technique during the fracture growth. This means that the
ﬁnite element mesh always exactly ﬁts the fracture surfaces. This method is perhaps the most accurate
one and at the same time is computationally expensive. However, a method developed by Paluszny
and Zimmerman (2011) enables the three-dimensional propagation in a relatively eﬃcient and accurate
manner. They presented a discrete fracture model using the ﬁnite-element method with an adaptive
re-meshing. In their paper the geometry and mesh are diﬀerentiated, thus the fracture growth, which
is determined by an energy-based criterion based on the stress intensity factors around each tip node,
are performed directly on the geometry.
Recently, models using extended ﬁnite element methods (XFEM) have been developed (Lecampion,
2009; Taleghani, 2009; Mohammadnejad and Khoei, 2013; Salimzadeh and Khalili, 2015; Vahab et al.,
2018). This method allows fracture to propagate along an arbitrary path by locally enriching the ﬁnite-
element model. The XFEM solves the fracture discontinuity in the hydraulic fracture problem, since
it does not need the grids to be matching with the hydraulic fractures and can achieve high accuracy
without requiring highly reﬁned grids. However, application of XFEM in 3D problems can be a cum-
bersome task. In the phase ﬁeld method (Chukwudozie, 2016), the sharp displacement discontinuity
is approximated and often smeared out over a few elements. Other numerical approaches include the
8
==PAGE:17==
distinct element method (Marina et al., 2014), the lattice approach (Grassl et al., 2015) and the em-
bedded fracture model (Norbeck et al., 2016).
The recent developments in 3D hydraulic fracture modeling, have made it possible to investigate previ-
ously unknown 3D interaction mechanisms on fracture height growth in a multi-layered medium. Zhang
et al. (2010) developed a model using a ﬁnite element approach where they studied the eﬀect of in-situ
stress contrast, modulus contrast, tensile strength contrast and viscosity of the fracturing ﬂuid. Often it
is assumed that the layers in a layered medium are perfectly bonded together which enables the fracture
to penetrate the bedding directly without any deﬂection. Chang et al. (2015) investigated these inter-
faces and how it eﬀected the behavior of a propagating fracture. Li et al. (2016) used a model based on
the ﬁnite element method to capture the fracture growth in a layered rocks. Four types of interaction
between a fracture and an interface were found: penetration, arrest, T-shaped branching and oﬀset.
Khanna et al. (2016) studied the situation where a hydraulic fracture was required to be contained
in the pay zone, and how the fracturing ﬂuid was able to control the height. They also investigated
the interaction between multiple fractures and the fracture spacing. Huang et al. (2018) investigated
the height growth under the inﬂuence of stress barriers and natural fractures using the ﬁnite element
method for the mechanical response and ﬁnite volume method for the ﬂuid ﬂow. Other studies have
tried to create better and more accurate models, for instance, Zhang et al. (2017) presented a new
pseudo-3D model that aimed to include the eﬀect of elastic modulus contrast.
As seen from literature, a lot of research on fracture growth in layered rocks have been carried out. The
eﬀect of in-situ stress, material properties, modulus contrast, bedding interfaces, fracture spacing or
inﬂuence of natural fractures have all been investigated to some extent. However, the relation between
layer thickness and its corresponding stress jump has not received much attention. The term stress
jump refers to change in stress distribution between two layers when the stresses on both sides of the
interface are of diﬀerent value. A soft layer above the pay zone may prevent the upward extension of
a hydraulic fracture because of this stress jump or stress barrier it creates at the top of the pay zone.
Stress barriers normally occur at discontinuities between layers with diﬀerent Young’s moduli. In a
layered medium where the medium is subject to horizontal compression, the stiﬀlayers take a higher
share of the compressive stress resulting in an adverse stress gradient for fracture propagation towards
the softer layer (Gudmundsson and Brenner, 2001).
It makes propagation towards the softer layer
less likely. The maximum horizontal stress over the minimum horizontal stress is called the horizontal
diﬀerential stress. A fracture will generally be conﬁned if this diﬀerential stress is greater than the
net-pressure in the fracture (Nolte et al., 1981). If gravitational stresses are included in the model the
hydraulic fracture is expected to grow upward. Therefore, it is important include the gravity eﬀect in
the numerical models as it can have signiﬁcant eﬀect on the results.

In this paper, a numerical model developed by Salimzadeh et al. (2017a,b) based on the ﬁnite-element
method is used to investigate the propagation of a single hydraulic fracture in a three-layered model.
The model simultaneously accounts for ﬂuid ﬂow within the fracture, poroelastic deformation of the
rock and the propagation of the fracture. A softer, ﬁnite thickness layer is considered above the pay
zone in a three layered model to investigate whether the soft layer can prevent upward propagation of
the hydraulic fracture. A sensitivity analysis is performed on the thickness and mechanical properties
of this middle layer. The eﬀect of gravity is also considered by applying linearly increasing far-ﬁeld
stresses. The model is validated against several analytical solutions, under both viscosity-regime and
toughness-regime. Simulations are performed for two fracture geometries: a plane strain- and a radial
hydraulic fracture, to investigate the diﬀerence between the induced fracture geometries in two cases.

Chapter 2
Methodology
A fully coupled hydro-mechanical model is utilised to investigate the hydraulic fracturing process in
a layered media. The model consists of the mechanical deformation, ﬂow through fracture, and the
growth of the fractures. The mechanical model is elastic, the ﬂow through fracture is laminar, and the
growth is based on the LEFM approach. The exchange of the ﬂuid between fracture and rock matrix is
ignored. In this section, the governing equations, ﬁnite element approximation, the growth model and
the implementation of the model is presented.

Stress intensity factors and growth model
Within LEFM framework, the fracture growth is analysed in terms of stress intensity factors (SIF),
which deﬁne the stress state at the tip of a crack. This stress concentration increases as the rock
matrix deforms due to the injected ﬂuid, and determine the fracture failure at the tip which leads to
fracture propagation. In this study, the three stress intensity factors are at every timestep computed by
computing the energy-based interaction integral (Yau et al., 1980) over a set of virtual disk domains
distributed along the fracture tip (Nejati et al., 2015). When the equivalent stress intensity factor

topic are repeated for the sake of completeness.

Chapter 4
Results
In this section, the eﬀect of a soft, intermediate layer on the direction of a single propagating hydraulic
fracture is investigated. This is done by performing a sensitivity analysis on the thickness and the
mechanical properties of this middle layer. The model presented in the previous chapters are used for
the simulations where two geometries are considered: (1) KGD - plane strain fracture model and (2)
The radial fracture model. A three layered model is built for both geometries where the middle layer
is thinner and softer than the layer above and below. Section 4.1 presents the the distribution of the
minimum horizontal stresses of this model. Section 4.2 presents the sensitivity analysis done with the
KGD - plane strain fracture model and also introduces the gravity eﬀect to the simulations. Section
4.3 presents the simulation results from the radial fracture model and compare the results to the KGD
- plane strain hydraulic fracture model.
Simulations for the two fracture geometries are performed to see if there are any diﬀerence in the results.
The big diﬀerence between the two models are of course that the three-dimensional radial fracture will
grow both horizontally and vertically, while the two-dimensional plane strain only consider the vertical
growth. Three-dimensional modeling have the advantage to study the shape of the induced fracture
compared to a plane strain model, and also gives the opportunity to model non-planar fractures. Three-
dimensional modeling are more challenging than than plane-strain modeling. In this study, the focus is
on the vertical extension of a single hydraulic fracture in a layered medium, however it is interesting to
see if there is any signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the two geometries considered.

Stress distribution
In a layered medium, the stress distribution is not continuous across layer interfaces. The thickness
of the layers and the mechanical properties inﬂuence this stress distribution and consequently also the
hydraulic fracture growth direction. For this reason, it is important to know the distribution of the
minimum horizontal stresses prior to simulating the hydraulic fracture propagation in order to understand
the results. The distribution for three cases in which the stiﬀness of the middle layer varies between
15, 10 and 5 GPa, and its thickness also varies between 5, 10 and 20 m are plotted in Fig. 4.1, 4.2
and 4.3, respectively, where the far-ﬁeld stresses are set to 15 MPa in all direction and compression is
assumed positive. The ﬁgures show the stress dramatically changes due to variation of elastic property
across the interfaces.

It is evident that the stress jump due to the diﬀerent mechanical properties may cause restriction to
the vertical growth of the hydraulic fractures. In the previous simulations, the gravity eﬀects were not
included and the middle layer served as a barrier layer in most cases. In this section, the eﬀect of
gravity is investigated by applying a linearly increasing far-ﬁeld horizontal stress on the same model
described, see Fig. 4.6. The gravity eﬀect is result of the diﬀerence between the density of the rock and
the density of the ﬂuid (i.e water), thus per each meter increase in depth the eﬀect stress is increased
by (ρs −ρw)g, in which ρs is the density of the rock, ρw is the density of the water, and g is the
gravitational acceleration. In these simulations the vertical gradient of the stress is set to 15 kPa/m.
The linear increasing far-ﬁeld stress is arranged to keep the original 15 MPa at the injection point, thus
the applied horizontal stress will become larger at greater depth from the injection point, and smaller
when moving upwards from the injection point. Arranging it this way is done for an easier comparison
with the results without the gravity eﬀect included. The gravitational gradient should make a fracture
grow more upwards in a single layer as the gravitational stresses are reduced in this direction. In this
case however, the hydraulic fracture is facing a ”competition” between two stress gradient working
in opposite directions when approaching the softer layer. An increasing horizontal stress due to the
discontinuity between the layers and a decreasing stress due to the gravity eﬀect. Fig. 4.7, 4.8 and 4.9
present the results where the gravitational gradient is included, while all other parameters are kept the
same.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In this study, a numerical model based on the ﬁnite-element method is presented to investigate whether
a softer layer located above the pay layer can stop the upward growth of the hydraulically induced
fracture. Two fracture geometries are considered to model the hydraulic fracture growth, namely the
plain-strain (KGD) hydraulic fracture and the radial hydraulic fracture. The model is validated against
the analytical solutions for radial fracture in both the viscosity-dominated and toughness-dominated
regime. Also a validation for the plain-strain (KGD) hydraulic fracture in toughness-dominated regime
are performed. The validation shows a good match against the analytical solutions. The discontinuity
horizontal stress distribution due to the layered model with diﬀerent mechanical properties are presented
and included in the simulations. This stress jump, which depends on the thickness and the stiﬀness
contrast between the two layers, results in an adverse stress gradient for fracture propagation towards
the softer layer. The eﬀects of the soft, intermediate layer are done by performing numerous simulations
where diﬀerent thicknesses and Young’s moduli for the middle layer are investigated. The thicknesses
varies between 5, 10 or 15 meters, while the Young’s modulus varies between 5, 10 or 15 GPa. The two
stiﬀer layers above and below the intermediate layer has constant mechanical properties and a Young’s
modulus of 20 GPa. The eﬀect of the fracture toughness has been investigated. The simulations includ
both hydraulic fractures with and without a gravity eﬀect. The gravity eﬀect are simulated by applying
linearly increasing far-ﬁeld stresses.
Results show that the thickness and the stiﬀness of the soft layer has a signiﬁcantly eﬀect on the
vertical growth of the hydraulic fracture through creating stress concentration at the interface between
the pay layer and the soft layer. Higher thickness of the middle layer and lower Young’s Modulus result
in higher stress jump. A greater stress jump means that the fractures are pushed downwards, and
if the stress jump is large enough it can prevent the upward propagation in some speciﬁc conditions
39
==PAGE:48==
prevented upward propagation. However, if the stress jump is not large enough to prevent the upward
growth and the fracture hits the soft layer in majority of cases it continue to grow upward and having a
larger toughness does not guarantee the blocking of the fracture growth. The results for a plane strain
and penny shape show relatively similar results for the vertical growth, but the penny shape gives more
information about the fracture as it considers fracture in three dimensions.
This study has lead to several recommendations for future work. The eﬀect of a stiﬀer, intermediate
layer and multiple hydraulic fractures in a layered medium are some of the cases that can be investigated
using the presented model. The model can also investigate the eﬀect of local stress change due to
pressure and temperature variations on the growth of a hydraulic fracture.

As there are more than one tip per fracture, as soon as the Keq reaches the Kic at any tips the prop-

analysis on the minimum extension number Lmin is investigated.

Fig. A.1 present two cases, D5. 10vs20 and D10. 10vs20, where the Lmin is 0.3 and 0.7. The fractures

erroneous to some extent.
For the plane strain model, three cases were selected and simulated with a minimum extension number
of 0.9, with the gravity eﬀect included and compared to the original result with a Lmin = 0.7 . The
results in Fig. A.2 show that the fracture growth is similar, thus the growth is not sensitive to this
number in this case. This suggest that the radial fracture model is to a small degree more sensitive to
this number than the plane strain model. Care must therefore be taken when choosing the minimum
extension number for the radial fracture model.
