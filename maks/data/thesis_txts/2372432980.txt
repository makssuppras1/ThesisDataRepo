Abstract
In this thesis, the impact of power imbalances caused by wind power forecast errors
in the future Nordic power system with large scale wind power is studied.
After reviewing the frequency control theory and literature, the state-of-the-art fre-
quency control in the Nordic synchronous power system is described with special
attention to the requirements concerning frequency reserves and their characteris-
tics. Based on these, the simulation model (delta per-unit) is developed in MAT-
LAB/Simulink representing all automatic frequency reserves, namely, FCR-N (Fre-
quency Containment Reserve-Normal), FCR-D (Frequency Containment Reserve-
Disturbance) and a-FRR (Frequency Restoration Reserve). The state-of-the-art Nordic
synchronous power system uses manually activated FRR (m-FRR) and currently for
only a few hours in the day automatic FRR (a-FRR), whereas other systems, e.g. the
Continental European system, use LFCs to provide automatic FRR (a-FRR). a-FRR
is advantageous in terms of response times but is also more expensive than the m-
FRR solution.
Simulations are performed with four different types of input: (1) Step, (2) an es-
timated historical imbalance profile provided by ENERGINET, (3) Wind Forecast
Error (WFE) timeseries and (4) a combined input. Step responses are used for model
verifications whereas other inputs are used to foresee the frequency quality of the
future Nordic synchronous power system. WFE is calculated from real-time wind
power production, and hour-ahead wind power timeseries which are generated by
DTU Wind Energy’s CorWind software based on the available wind power develop-
ment scenarios. The project is estimated the duration of time in which the frequency
will be out of the normal band of 49.9 < f < 50.1 Hz in 2030. In addition, the effect of
automatic frequency reserve (a-FRR and FCR-N) capacities on this time duration is
studied. It is shown that allocation of an extra 200 MW automatic frequency reserve
capacity to a-FRR instead of FCR-N reduces the time duration outside the normal
band by approximately 5,108 [minutes/year], which is a remarkable reduction, high-
lighting the value of a-FRR. Furthermore, simulation results suggest that the current
200 MW capacity of a-FRR is not sufficient to limit the time in which the frequency
goes outside the normal band by maximum 10,000 [min./year] when WFE is added
on top of other power imbalances in 2030.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
A power system encompasses generating units, transmission and distribution grids,
substations and consumers. The focus of this thesis is on the utilization of conven-
tional generating units to balance the active power in a power system with high
penetration of stochastic wind power. Balancing the active power means making sure
the availability of enough generation to cover the consumption in the system at any
time. The power system frequency is strictly related to the rotational speed of the
synchronous electrical machines which are connected directly to the system. As a
result, the mechanical power used to drive the generators should be controlled such
that the electrical power generation matches the electrical power extracted from the
system by consumers.
The power frequency should be kept close to its nominal value, 50 Hz in Europe,
since the deviations in frequency can be harmful for generating units especially ther-
mal units and also for the consumers. Protection systems are in place to disconnect
the units if the frequency goes beyond the predefined bands. In case of low frequency
and thereby disconnecting of thermal generating units, the frequency decreases even
more since the imbalance between generation and consumption is increased causing
the eventual power system blackout. The purpose of frequency control in the power
system is to avoid such a situation [1].
In general, the active power balancing includes different tasks with different tem-
poral resolutions which are carried out separately. Balancing actions can be planned
in advance such as intra-day and seasonal planning or be spontaneous in response to
the real time conditions of the power system. The latter is in the scope of frequency
control.
Typically, the frequency control includes three different control actions namely, pri-
mary, secondary and tertiary control. The decentralized primary frequency control or
Frequency Containment Process (FCP) utilizes a droop control (proportional control)
on the governor of a generating unit. The governor has the frequency as an input
and adjusts the output power of the generating unit in proportion to the frequency
deviation. The primary control causes a steady state error in the frequency with
respect to the initial value given the characteristic of the proportional control. On
the other hand, the secondary control is a centralized frequency control managed by

TSO (Transmission System Operator). Different terminologies are used for the sec-
ondary control such as LFC (Load Frequency Control), AGC (Automatic Generation
Control) and FRR (Frequency restoration Reserve). The secondary control fulfills
two purposes, restoring the frequency to the nominal value as well as restoring the
power flow to the scheduled values on the links between two areas in the power sys-
tem. Nevertheless, in the Nordic synchronous power system LFC only controls the
frequency. The centralized tertiary frequency control or manual Frequency Restora-
tion Process (m-FRR) is managed manually by TSO and activates larger but slower
steps compared to LFC.
1.1
Motivation
The Nordic power system is developed over the past decades to some extent similar
to other power systems in the world. The major developments were from building
hydropower plants in 1950’s-1960’s, nuclear units in 1970’s-1980’s, introducing the
digital PI controllers instead of mechanical governors in 1980’s to a substantial or-
ganizational change in the 1990’s when the transmission of electricity was separated
from its production and the market was deregulated [1].
The frequency in the Nordic synchronous power system has been deteriorated since
market deregulation such that the frequency was outside the normal band of 49.9-
50.1 Hz in average 100 minutes per month in 1995 and was gradually increased to
1100 minutes in 2011 [2]. One of the reasons for this, is the electricity market design
with hourly blocks for selling and buying the electricity. After market clearing, the
production schedules follow the load changes with an hourly resolution. As a result,
e.g. in case of an increase in the load at the end of the hour, the production is too
low whereas in the starting point of the next hour is too high since the production
is increased to match the average load for the next hour. The FCR-N (Frequency
Containment Reserve for Normal operation) alone is not capable of handling these
large power imbalances [1].
Furthermore, the Nordic power system changes mainly due to the development of
more Renewable Energy Sources (RES), technology and establishment of the uniform
market, operation and planning framework in European countries. These changes will
be more noticeable by 2025. Likewise, operation and planning of the Nordic power
system will be challenged by changes such as increasing penetration of wind power
and shutting down of the thermal power plants. The wind power installed capacity is
expected to be tripled between 2010 and 2025. Furthermore, more than 50% increase
in capacity from interconnectors by 2025 is planned. The existing interconnectors in
Nordic power system is 9740 MW with further 3900 MW under construction by 2021
and 8000 MW is planned by 2025 [3].
The system frequency is a common parameter within the whole Nordic power sys-

1.2
Objectives and limitations
The objective of this project is to assess the impact of Wind Forecast Error (WFE)
caused by large scale wind power on the frequency control in the future Nordic syn-
chronous power system comparing a solution including load frequency control (LFC)
to the state-of-the-art without LFC. The power imbalances caused by inevitable wind
power forecast errors are simulated using DTU Wind Energy’s CorWind software to
generate time series of real time wind power production and consistent wind power
forecasts. The wind power time series are simulated using an available scenario for
wind power development in the Nordic power system by 2030. The project is then fo-
cused on development of the simulation model of the Nordic Synchronous Frequency
control in order to foresee the frequency quality i.e. the duration of time when the
frequency goes outside the normal frequency band of 49.90 < f < 50.1 Hz in 2030.
Remark that in this work the term a-FRR (Automatic Frequency Restoration Re-
serve) is used instead of LFC given that the Nordic LFC only controls the frequency
whereas the power flow of a tie-line between two control area is controlled manually
by operators (TSO).
Limitations
In this thesis the following limitations are existed:
• For Nordic Synchronous Frequency model, the cooper-plate approach is chosen.
In other words, the tie-lines between control areas are not considered.
• The market price for reserves are not taken into account.
• Power system inertia is considered constant and includes only the Hydro and
Thermal generation units.

theory
Satisfactory operation of a power system requires almost the constant frequency. The
frequency of a power system is highly dependent on the balance of active power. Any
change in active power at one point of the system is reflected all over the power system
by frequency change because the frequency is a common factor anywhere in the power
system. Due to supply of power into the system by many generators, some measures
must be taken in order to allocate the change in power demand to the generators [4],
[5].
Disturbances in the power system have different consequences e.g.
a momentary
imbalance between power generation and load caused by a short circuit on a trans-
mission line will be cleared without the need for specific adjustment of load and
generation. In contrast, an outage of a generating unit caused by the protection sys-
tem would cause a long term imbalance between generation and load in the system.
This imbalance will be initially compensated by the kinetic energy of the rotating
masses in system such as generators, motors and synchronous condensers resulting in
a change in the frequency. The dynamics of the change in frequency have different
phases which require different measures in order to control and stabilize the frequency
[6].

2.5
Spinning reserve
Given the practical limits on generation capacity of a turbine, if the turbine reaches its
maximum power output, assuming a linear droop does not hold anymore and thus the
generation will not increase in response to a drop in frequency. The reason is that, at
the maximum power, the droop characteristic is a vertical line. As a result, only units
which are not reached to their maximum power would be able to participate in the
equivalent droop characteristic of the power system. In other words, the equivalent
droop characteristic of the power system depends on the difference between total
nominal power of all generating units in the system and the total load demand. This
difference is the so called spinning reserve [6].

2.11
Tertiary frequency control
Unlike the automatic primary and secondary frequency control, tertiary frequency
control is manual.
The function of the tertiary control is different in a vertically
integrated power system and a liberalized one. In the former, operator defines the set
point of the individual generating units based on the economic dispatch or optimal
power flow studies. On the other hand, in the liberalized market (after the gate closure
of the hour-ahead market), operators adjust the bids from BRPs (Balance Responsible
Parties) in order to meet the system constraints such as congestion management on
the tie-lines. In addition, the task of the tertiary control may also include ensuring
the adequacy of the primary and secondary reserves, ramp rate control of the HVDC
lines and secondary control restoration. In the Nordic synchronous power system
the tertiary frequency control is called Manual Frequency Restoration Process since
it uses manual frequency restoration reserve m-FRR. Note that in this thesis, the
term ”process” which utilizes the related ”reserve” is often substituted by its reserve,
e.g. ”Manual Frequency Restoration Reserve” (m-FRR) is used when referring to the
”Manual Frequency Restoration Process”.

3.4
Automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve (a-FRR)
The frequency restoration reserve is required to bring the frequency back to the nomi-
nal value of 50.0 Hz and thereby to restore the activated FCR. Given the deteriorating
frequency quality in the Nordic sycnhronous power system, this reserve has been ac-
tive since 2013 on a trial basis where it is in the service only in some hours. Besides
faster response compared to m-FRR (manual frequency restoration reserve), a-FRR
can be beneficial due to the fact that it is capable of being based on a merit order and
taking into account the congestion in the grid. It should be noted that the a-FRR is
a companion to the existing m-FRR. Although, both FCR and a-FRR are automatic,
they encompass fundamental differences. FCR is locally controlled whereas the a-
FRR is controlled centrally . In addition, FCR is used to stabilized the frequency
(primary reserve) while the a-FRR as a secondary reserve, brings the frequency back
to the 50.0 Hz given that enough reserve is available [9].

3.5
Manual Frequency Restoration Reserve (m-FRR)
m-FRR alleviates both the power imbalance (consequently the frequency) and con-
gestion on the transmission lines. In the Nordic synchronous power system the main
source of frequency restoration reserve is the m-FRR. Given the limited value of the
a-FRR and frequent congestions on the transmission lines, m-FRR is crucial in the
Nordic power system and it will probably remain the main balancing reserve. In the
regulating power market, the m-FRR should be activated within 15 minutes. Since
different generation technologies are available in the system and some has faster re-
sponse such as hydro and some are slower e.g. thermal units, their activation time
(total time between receiving the activation command and delivering the full set
power) should be taken into account. Note that the faster a-FRR would help the
system to be restored to the normal frequency range in case of a disturbance within
15 minutes. m-FRR includes various forms such as voluntary bids by BRPs in the
RPM (Regulating Power Market), capacity reserves payed by the TSOs as well as the
peak load reserves. The purpose of capacity reserves is to secure the adequecy of the
reserve in case of a disturbance. Peak load reserves are another source for m-FRR.
All of these reserves are put in the NOIS (Nordic Operational Information System).
Furthermore, the m-FRR can be exchanged with the neighboring synchronous areas
outside the Nordic RPM [9].

3.6
Special regulation
TSOs might order regulations by utilizing m-FRR for reasons other than balancing
management purposes. These are called special regulation and in Nord pool scpe-
cific reasons are explained by codes [16]. Note that bids are not necessarily chosen
on a merit order given that e.g. availability of reserve for congestion management
on either side of a tie-line is the main determinants. Nevertheless, certain rules with
regards to price are applicable e.g. upward special regulation price must be equal to
or higher than the up-regulation price [9]. Counter trade in order to alleviate conges-
tion inside a bidding area or congestion among two bidding area because of decrease
in grid capacity, are two of the reasons for special regulation [16]. Based on [17] it
can be seen that the volumes of special regulation were smaller compared to m-FRR
and for shorter duration of time were activated.

4.5.1
Estimated historical imbalance 2010
This input connected to the port 1 of the Multiport Switch in Figure 4.1 is an esti-

This imbalance profile was generated in the RAR-project and documented in an
internal report [22] as quoted below 2:
”Methodology for converting a frequency deviation signal to an imbalance
signal
The Simulink model requires an ‘imbalance signal’ in MW as input. Imbalances be-
tween load and generation in the Nordic system are currently not measured directly
on a continuous basis. Indirectly, the frequency provides an indication of the imbal-
ance. The relation between imbalances and frequency is in principle the frequency
bias factor (FBF). However, the FBF is not the same for all frequencies. It is assumed
though that for slower frequency deviations, the FBF is constant and representative
for imbalances. For faster deviations, dynamic response of governors and machines
may lead to different responses of the frequency to imbalances. For this reason, the
imbalance signal has been created using two signals: a slow signal and a fast signal.
The slow signal is calculated by first filtering the input frequency (measured 5 s of
May, August and December 2010) with a low pass filter (1/300 s). After that, the
filtered frequency is divided by the frequency bias factor.
For the fast signal we assume that the imbalances are completely random. For this
reason, white noise has been used as an input. This white noise is integrated and
filtered with a high pass filter (1/300s). The power of the white noise have been tuned
in order to achieve a similar variance for the original and the simulated frequency.”

APPENDIX B
Balancing process [9]
Balancing in the Nordic power system should be performed such that the followings
are met [9]:
• Quality standards for frequency and time deviation (the difference in seconds
between the time shown by a clock synchronized with the grid frequency and
another clock which runs independently. Maximum acceptable time deviation
in the synchronized Nordic power system is ±30 sec. [27]).
• Requirements for frequency reserves
• The tie-lines capacity are not exceeded
In the Nordic power system Statnett and Svenska kraftnät are the Balance Opera-
tor such that they mutually make decisions and take necessary actions to maintain
the frequency and time deviation within the agreed limits. In Figure B.1 the overview
of the balancing process in Nordic power system is illustrated.
B.1
Before the gate closure of the Day-ahead market
The balancing process starts a couple of years in advance by contracting long term
capacity reserves with providers following by outage and capacity planning of tie-lines
(long and short term), finishing by power balance and dynamic stability forecasts
with various time spans (week, days to hours ahead). In the following, the process
concerning weeks ahead is described in detail.
B.2
Ensuring availability of m-FRR reserve
In Norway, there are seasonal and weekly capacity market in the winter where Stat-
nett calls obliged bidders for up-regulation in the RPM. In Sweden, Svenska kraftnät
ensures the availability of reserve for DI (dimensioning incident) by long term con-
tracts with gas turbines. Reduction of tie-line capacity can also be used in certain
circumstances such as tripping of the gas turbines. Fingrid uses its own reserve power
plants as well as contracted ones. Energinet.dk ensures the adequacy of m-FRR by
5-year up-regulation contracts in DK2 and daily up-regulation market in DK1.

B.4
After the gate closure of the Intra-day market
The gate closure of the Intra-day market (Elbas) is 45 minutes before every opera-
tional hour. Afterwards, Statnett and Svenska kraftnät as the Balance Operators use
updated information complied in the NOIS (see Appendix B.3) together with real-
time information for balancing planning. Following this, they estimate the balancing
volume for the coming hour in the synchronous Nordic power system. Energinet.dk
does the same for DK1. This estimation is made based on the current operational
situation and possible influence of the upcoming plans concerning consumption, pro-
duction and power flow of the tie-lines. After that, an optimal utilization of bids on
the RPM and new schedule for power flow on the tie-lines is agreed, normally 30-40
minutes before the upcoming operational hour. This is due the fact that ramping
on some of HVDC tie-lines starts 10-15 minutes prior to the hour shift. In normal
situation, the final decision for activation plan of RPM bids is made approximately
15 minutes ahead of the hour shift.
Bids activation is based on the priced merit order.
Nevertheless, there are some
distinctions concerning activation of voluntary bids in RPM and reserves with ca-
pacity payment. Voluntary bids are activated before reserves with capacity payment
by Fingrid and Svenska kraftnät given that the disturbance and peak load reserves
are subsidized. Peak load reserves have precedence compared to disturbance reserve
on the RPM. On the other hand, Statnett and Energinet.dk do not discriminate on
voluntary bids and reserves with capacity payment for m-FRR activation. If enough
m-FRR equivalent to the DI (N-1) in one bidding area is available on the NOIS, the
capacity payment reserves can be shared with other bidding areas providing opera-
tional security is not compromised. It is also possible to purchase additional power
outside the Nordic for balancing reasons in case there are not enough RPM bids in
specific areas in the Nordic power system.
While the expected imbalances for the upcoming operational hour are being eval-
uated, Statnett and Svenska kraftnät (Balance Operators) appraise if the planned
production will change in the entire Nordic power system. They also check whether
the power flow on the HVDC tie-lines at the hour shift is too large that affects the fre-
quency such that it cannot be contained using balancing regulation in a few minutes
before and after the hour shift. If this is the case, it is required to manipulate some
parts of the steps in the scheduled production. The production plan may change from
30 minutes prior to the hour shift. This concerns mostly the morning and evening
hours as well as the day shift. These possible big changes can be flattened out by

rescheduling the production. In case of congestion, Balance Operators should also
determine the order of production rescheduling. The decided volume after the hour
shift may be re-evaluated in case of happening an unscheduled event interfering with
the initial schedule.
B.5
During the operational hour
The Balance Operators observe and follow the course of the operational conditions
during the operational hour and constantly evaluate whether it is needed to tune the
balancing. This is similar to the process before the operational hour though consid-
ering a shorter time horizon for actions. Balance Operators shall react quickly to
alleviate the situation if an unforeseen event such as a production trip happens. The
decision on the appropriate response is made based on the plans and all available
real-time information. In these situation the priority may be given to bids with re-
sponse faster than 15 minutes as the Special Regulation instead of the merit order
price based. Those skipped bids might be activated later on if required. Special Reg-
ulation can also be replaced by balancing regulation providing bids from RPM are
not skipped any longer.
In case of congestion between two bidding areas in the Elspot during the operational
hour, respective TSOs decide in cooperation with each other whether it is not possi-
ble to regulate the affected areas jointly. The term congestion refers to the situation
when it is no longer possible to conduct balancing regulation without diverging from
the price order on the NOIS. High flow on the tie-line, technical constraints of the
tie-line or operational and trading rules are some of the reasons when it is not possible
to follow the price order. Note that balancing regulation concerning frequency and
congestion should be done at the same time and the appropriate measure is taken
iteratively based on the dominant need.
Normally, congestion has higher priority
compared to the frequency.

In case there is not any congestion on the tie-lines, balancing regulation is carried out
by initiating bids on the NOIS in price order.

Elspot day-ahead market
The main place for trading power is the day-ahead market. The Nord Pool is the
market operator making contracts between sellers and buyers for power delivery in
every hour of the next day. Balance Responsible Parties (BRPs) i.e. buyers and
sellers, submit bids including volume and price before the market closure deadline,
1200 CET. The price is determined using an advanced optimization algorithm as the
equilibrium point between aggregated demand and supply curves in a way that is
depicted in Figure C.2.

Aggregated supply and demand curves [29].
In addition to demand and supply, the transmission capacity plays an important
role in determining the uniform market price. The term uniform means that all ac-
cepted participants will have the same price regardless of their price offer. In case
a large amount of power is needed to be transmitted via transmission links between
biding areas (market areas) in order to meet the demand, bottlenecks could occur.
If a congestion happens, the price in that area increases so that the transmission
constraints can be attenuated. From an economic point of view, the increase in price
would lead to decrease in demand.
Furthermore, the power flows from area with
lower price to the area with higher price as illustrated in Figure C.3. This ensures
maximizing the benefit for the society.
