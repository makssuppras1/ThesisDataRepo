ABSTRACT 
  
In this project a sustainable energy community concept has been developed with 
focus on utilizing local resources and aiming at self-sufficient and sustainable energy 
production. After introduction of the local characteristics of Komló, Hungary, where 
the energy community concept is taking place, an overview of the current state of 
gravitational storage technologies, including companies Gravitricity and Energy 
Vault is displayed.  
After the definition of the energy community concept, the local electricity and heat 
demand is developed for the formulation of the proposed system, the amount of 
available biogas is determined and a scheduling for the CHP engines based on power 
market price is presented. The assessment on geothermal opportunities has led to the 
conclusion, to exclude this technology from the heat production concept. The amount 
of currently installed PV capacity is given by the research of official database and the 
capacity of the newly developed PV installations is set, to be able to provide the city’s 
total demand of 20 years with electricity produced by the local PV installations, of 37 
[MWp] altogether.   
The proposed final system for the community includes the latter amount of PV 
capacity besides considering the electricity output of two CHP engine groups, which 
will be supplemented by a conceptually designed gravitational storage facility using 
the local mineshafts, being balanced out by the national grid. The district heating 
network considers an 18 [MW] biomass boiler, which is supported by gas furnaces 
and a seasonal stone-water-pit storage concept. It will be proven, that the 
replacement of gas furnaces with the seasonal storage for summertime supply of the 
DH network is feasible in an economic and environmental perspective as well.  
A detailed formulation of energy balancing model is presented, followed by 
introduction of the financial modelling methodology used for evaluation. The report 
contains broad amount of information regarding European and Hungarian electricity 
grid, as well as technological data and assumptions on the included installations in 
the district heating system, as: electricity day-ahead market prices, electricity feed-in 
tariff for renewables, fuel costs, direct and indirect CO2 emissions from fossil fuels, 
EU ETS related expenses, inflation and discount rate, taxation. 
Throughout description of energy storage systems design and optimization is 
included with the results of opting for a 16 [MWh] gravitational storage facility and a 
9,600 [MWh] heat storage. The development of the local district heating network is 
also considered for the evaluation.  
Finally, the energy community concept is evaluated for financial feasibility as a 
whole, with the result of 13-15 years payback time achieved and a potential 120.000 
[tCO2] emissions avoided. The most financially relevant cases are considered as the 
object of sensitivity analysis on future electricity market price scenarios.

1. INTRODUCTION 
This report is presenting the project development and the work done as the final 
project for the MSc in Sustainable Energy curriculum at DTU.  
The city Komló, Hungary is based around a coal mine, which was closed in 2000, 
now the community needs new opportunities to avoid decay. Komló has a 
conventional high-temperature district heating system, supplied by natural gas and 
biomass facilities. Some smaller scale PV-plants are operational in the area, but the city 
is highly reliant on the national power grid. Properties of the location make it possible 
to introduce new, sustainable practices for energy production and eventually a self-
sufficient, sustainable energy cooperative, which would contribute to the future of the 
local community and serve as a pilot project and learning opportunity for similar 
endeavours in the region. 
The report will introduce the process of the formulation of the sustainable energy 
community concept, while presenting the local characteristics and further broad 
amount of information covered as part of the research. Then with the presentation of 
the final idea and description of examined cases the design and optimization of the 
proposed energy storage technologies as gravitational storage and seasonal heat 
storage are showcased. Introducing the formulation of the energy system balancing 
model in parallel with the used assumptions, before describing the financial model. 
Finally, the results of optimization exercise to find the best storage sizes to implement 
in the concept with the financial evaluation of proposed cases are presented followed 
by sensitivity analysis of the most relevant ones.  
Separate feasibility study has not been conducted, as economic, environmental, legal 
and social feasibility criteria was constantly considered throughout the project 
development, which considerations are noted in the report at the relevant sections. 
Summary regarding the project feasibility is presented in the Conclusion. 
The overall concept is worked out in cooperation with Júlia Kovács, Architecture 
MSc student of ’Moholy-Nagy University of Art and Design Budapest’ (MOME) in 
parallel with their MSc thesis on a subject of Architectural design in a sustainable 
energy community with storage technologies, and Márton Bene, Mechanical 
Engineering Msc student at Budapest University of Technology and Economics 
(BUTE).

Collaboration with Júlia Kovács MSc student of Architecture at ’Moholy-Nagy

have been started in winter of 2021. We have developed an interest for gravitational

energy of solid bodies of mass, for instance using an abandoned mine shaft, as per in 
the concept of Gravitricity Ltd.. [1] After research and consideration of several possible 
locations, it was discovered, that the city of Komló has several favourable properties 
to align with such concept and initiative.  
The city Komló is located in Baranya County, South-Western Hungary, in the close 
in proximity of Pécs. During the socialist era, the former village became a mining city, 
the second largest mining centre in Hungary, producing coal. There were 3 mine shafts 
in operation, out of which two are vertical with the total depth of 819.7 [m] and 756.7 
[m], suitable for gravitational storage technology similar to Gravitricity’s concept. 
Owing to the decreased national demand and the potentially cheaper supply of coal

settlements. The current population of approximately 22.500 inhabitants is struggling 
with the increasing demographic challenges of aging 
and emigration. [3] The development of the city has 
come to a halt since the beginning of the millennia, and 
the settlement needs a great new opportunity for 
advancement to avoid further decay.

2.2. On solid mass gravitational technologies 
Concepts which are based on the changing potential energy resulting from a shift in 
height of the mass are already well developed and play an important role energy 
systems regulation. Majority of these installations are coming in the form of water 
storage reservoirs located in areas of sufficient geographical level differences. These 
technologies are able to store huge amounts of energy, by pumping an easily 
accessible, environmentally friendly void (water), with high efficiency and basically 
unlimited charging cycles. The counterarguments include the issue of the occupied 
land area and the risk of a breach, but for the most important, rather few countries 
have sufficient level differences to develop such installation in economically feasible 
framework. 
Solid mass gravity energy storage systems are on the other hand are still in a 
development phase, despite being an appealingly simple technology concept. In plain 
terms, a solid mass gravity energy storage device uses an electric lifting system to raise 
one or more weights a vertical distance thereby transferring electrical energy to be 
stored as potential energy. These installations come with similar pros as pumped 
hydro, such as long operational lifetime, small O&M costs, high efficiency, while 
stored energy is basically independent of storage cycle time and environment 
temperature (opposingly to electrochemical technologies). [12] 
There are two considerable competitors in the field of solid mass gravitational 
energy storage technologies, Gravitricity [1] and Energy Vault [2]. As mentioned 
earlier this sector is still in development phase, currently neither of these companies 
have operating, utility scale installations, but they both have patented concepts and 
working small-scale demonstrator installations. [14] 
Gravitricity’s concept is mainly based on utilizing deep shafts, having only the 
winching system above ground level, similarly to an elevator. With its single-weight 
systems, Gravitricity plans to scale up to 500-tonnes weights, which would require 
substantially stronger foundations for the system. Their concept demonstrator is 
located in Edinburgh, U.K., which uses two 25-tonnes weights suspended by steel

Examining concepts of the two companies each have strengths and weaknesses

• Multi-weight systems are better scalable and do not require the utilization of

3. METHODOLOGY & ASSUMPTIONS 
This chapter will first present the thought process and the working method in the 
development of the energy community concept for Komló. Secondly it will introduce 
the formulation and the definition of cases which are going to be evaluated, thirdly, 
describe the calculation methods for the evaluation of the cases, with definition and 
presentation of the used assumptions, datasets, coefficients and factors influencing the 
evaluation. All calculations and used inputs are presented in the attached Microsoft 
Excel workbook called “Calculations and Results of Energy Community Concept” 
spreadsheet, with link included to access the final energy and finance models. 
3.1. Definition of energy community  
In Hungarian legislation energy community is only applicable in the electricity 
sector, owing to the fact that electricity grid falls under control of a nationwide 
operator, while heating systems are independent from each other. As it is defined in 
the applicable regulation (2007 LXXXVI. law on electricity [21]) an energy community 
is a legal entity operating in the form of a cooperative or a non-profit corporation 
whose primary purpose is not to obtain financial benefits but to provide 
environmental, economic and social benefits to its members or in the area of operation 
specified in the energy community's charter by generating, storing and consuming 
electricity.  
If provision of the Energy authority is granted, the energy community is allowed to 
carry out the following activities relevant for the case,  
• construction of a small power plant with a nominal capacity of 0.5 [MW] and 
more, up to 50 [MW], the production of electricity and the cessation of the 
production of electricity, 
• operation of electricity storage facilities with a rated output of 0.5 [MW] or 
more, 
but not allowed to conduct the distribution of electricity.  
This latter means, that there can be no control over the electricity provision to 
individuals, but only over the operation of the energy production and storage 
installations owned by the energy community.  
Owing to the latter regulation the scope of financial assessment will not include the 
individual consumers or consumer prices but will consider the city of Komló as a 
whole, the sum consumption and production of the local grid, both regarding 
electricity and heat. In the assessment it will be assumed that the current heating 
system operator (Komló Heating Power Plant Plc.) decides to set up an energy 
community for Komló, investing in the electricity producing facilities and heating 
facilities as well.  
In the electricity side, the whole consumption of the city will be considered, but on 
the heating side, residents with individual heating systems are left out of the scope as 
==PAGE:18==
11 
these stakeholders (excluding ones with electric heating) purchase fuels for heating (as 
natural gas or biomass) from players outside the energy community.  
Revenues from electricity sales can only be considered if it is sold to the national 
grid, as the residents are the customers of the regional electricity distributor company. 
Revenues from district heating service to customers is also not considered in the 
assessment, as the price of district heating - as the highest official price, the structure 
of the official price and the conditions of application shall be established by a decree 
of the Minister, taking into account the proposal of the Hungarian Energy and Utilities 
Regulatory Authority (as per the regulation of Act XVIII of 2005 law on District 
Heating Services [22]) The regulation states that district heating prices are set to cover 
the fix and variable costs of DH production increased with a given rate for profit. This 
means that costs of the facility should be covered by the paid bills, thus it would not 
make a difference in the final results but rather mean a distortion as the business case 
would be still profitable, independent from the quality of taken measures.  
On further regulatory requirements regarding energy communities, the operators 
need to adhere to the following points 
• A balance group is a clearing house composed of one or more members 
established to regulate the relevant liability for the causal identification and 
accounting of the use of balancing energy and the performance of related 
tasks; the Energy Community is obliged to form or join a balance group. The 
balance group is represented by the balance group manager in the direction of 
the transmission system operator. 
• The Energy Authority registers the energy community, which is obliged to 
provide sufficient information for the Authority to oversee its activity. 
• An individual who pursues his occupation or principal economic activity in 
the field of electricity or gas supply or has sole or majority ownership, a 
controlling influence or a related undertaking, shall not manage the Energy 
Community alone or, if the Energy Community is managed by a board, 
majority of board members cannot be such individuals. [21] 
3.2. Determination of electricity demand 
There was no available approximation or measured data for the electricity 
consumption of the city, thus it was determined through estimation process.  
As a starting point, appropriate energy demand timeseries had to be found. The Ten-
Year Network Development Plan (TYNDP) of the ENTSO-E provides hourly

3.4. The current system in Komló 
In the evaluation of possible development cases considered in the following the 
current state of the energy system in Komló, referred to as Case 0 or Base Case, will be 
acknowledged as avoided cost, appearing on the income side of the financial analysis.  
On the electricity side, population of Komló is supplied from the national grid, 
following the energy community definition this means that the city purchases 
electricity from the grid, this expense is considered at power market price. There is a 
great PV capacity already installed in the proximity of Komló, but these installations 
are operated by private companies, feeding to the grid, not influencing current state of 
the energy system directly. There are two CHP engines (2x1.94 [MWe]) in operation, 
which are part of a virtual power plant portfolio of a private company, their operation 
is not in local control. There is only available information regarding the total annual 
electricity production of the CHP engines, which was 3,200 [MWh] in 2020, and 7,600 
[MWh] in 2021. The other two CHP engines (2x0.7 [MWe]) are currently not 
operational, they require comprehensive renovation. [30] 
On the heating side, the heat from the operational CHP engines (2x1.95 [MWth]) is 
purchased by the local DH operator, but as its production is connected to electricity 
generation, it cannot be forecasted or controlled. The CHP engines (2x1 [MWth]) in 
property of DH operator are not operational. [30] The main source of DH is the 
biomass boiler, which has 18.6 [MWth] peak and 6 [MWth] minimal output. Heating 
demand over outside of this range is met by operation of gas furnaces. If demand is 
higher than biomass peak, gas furnaces produce only the additional demand with 
3x0.2 [MWth] smaller hot water boilers, if demand is under 6 [MWth], one 5.6 [MWth] 
hot water boiler is operated with the support of smaller boilers if needed.

3.5. The initial sustainable energy community concept 
At the beginning of the project development, the local energy system conception has 
been worked out, considering the researched and discovered possibilities of change in 
a more sustainable direction in Komló. The following period, after further 
understanding of local options led to changes in the concept, but it has to be described 
as for the first step of the problem formulation. This initial concept flowchart is 
pictured in Figure 10. The locations of installations and considered resources are 
presented in Appendix 2. 
The main constraint of the concept was, that the local system should produce the 
same amount of energy from renewable resources as much it consumes during the 
project lifetime of 20 years while staying compliant to national regulations. Further 
aims were to be economically feasible, utilize local resources and existing facilities as 
much as possible. 
On the electricity side of the initial concept two types of production is considered, 
PV and electric output of the CHP gas engine facility. Produced electricity is measured 
in the local system balancing facility. If local demand is higher than local production, 
local production is utilized to feed local demand. Further energy is obtained either 
from the local gravitational storage (depending on its charge level), or from the 
national electricity grid. If local production is higher than local demand, local demand 
is met by part of local production, surplus local production may charge gravitational 
storage (depending on its charge level), if gravitational storage is fully charged, 
surplus local production is fed to the electricity grid.

sustainable community concept. Newly installed solar heating would generate 
sufficient energy in the summer, but would also supply surplus heat in the heating 
season. If there was no heat storage, this could result in the overheating of the system 
due to slow regulation of biomass boiler. If there was a heat storage facility to take up 
surplus heat, it could not be utilized fully, since solar heat would meet great part of 
the summer demand.   
Individual heating solutions could be supported by addition of renewable fuels or

As per the current situation is described in chapter 3.4., the electricity output of 
operational CHP engines was 3,200 [MWh] in 2020, and 7,600 [MWh] in 2021. [30] 
There are several possible ways to operate these installations, as they may be regulated

daytime, the additional generated power would flow directly to the grid, but due to 
the fact that this period usually outside peak demand, the received price of production

system. In the current situation of the EU cutting ties with fuels from Russia, and 
natural gas supply chains are underdeveloped from other directions a general price 
increase of the fuel is inevitable, meaning at fix power price the CHP plant’s spread 
price (difference between price of unit of electricity produced and the cost of fuel 
needed to produce unit of electricity) would decrease. To make the CHP plant as 
profitable as possible, it needs to operate at the time of peak power price.

is due to the fact that increased gas prices are not yet affecting energy producers at 
they are paying a fixed price for the fuel, set in an annual contract, renewing at the 
beginning of each heating year (in October). [30] Thus, these producers might be 
operating at outstandingly high production levels, but it is only until the fixed fuel 
price will set their spread price so low, that they need to reduce production and aim 
at high power prices.

3.6.5. CONCEPT OF THE DH SYSTEM 
As other heating technologies have been ruled out, the biomass boiler, the CHP 
engines and the heat storage will be the equipment responsible for supply of DH 
network. Furthermore, it is to be noted, that the CHP engines continuously driven by 
the electricity market, thus the biomass and heat storage needs to fulfil the task of 
system balancing and the CHP will only feed the system with additional heat. It is 
assumed that the DH system is equipped with smaller scale heat tanks which decrease 
the stress on the system, deriving from the equipment of slower inertia, as the biomass 
boiler. Gas furnaces are only considered in cases without heat storage. 
Idea of the introduction of district cooling system was an optional element in the 
initial part already, but for the final system it had been discarded, as the concept has 
headed to a different direction and also the fact that local transmission system and the 
local characteristic household equipment is not suitable for such service without broad 
refurbishment. [30]  
3.6.6. CONCEPT OF ELECTRICITY BALANCING  
Local electricity production is done by the CHP engines, driven by the power prices, 
and the PV plants, which are constrained by the weather. Other parts of the system are 
the gravitational storage and the national grid connection. It is decided, that for energy 
security reasons and to use as much renewable energy locally, the energy storage will 
be automatically charged in case of oversupply, direct grid output may only happen 
if the storage is fully charged. In the case of the production is lower than the demand, 
the gravitational storage is the first to discharge electricity to the local grid, purchase 
of grid electricity may only happen if it is fully discharged, no power price constraint 
is considered.

3.7.1. GRAVITATIONAL ELECTRICITY STORAGE 
Based on the previously presented projects of companies in the fields of utility scale 
solid mass gravitational storage development, a new concept with mixed ideas from 
Energy Vault and Gravitricity and some new designs was developed in cooperation 
with Júlia Kovács, Architecture MSc student at Moholy-Nagy University of Art and 
Design Budapest’ (MOME) and Márton Bene, Mechanical Engineering Msc student at 
Budapest University of Technology and Economics (BUTE).  
The characteristics of the location in Komló include the two abandoned, vertical 
mine shafts with the total depth of H1= 819.7 [m] and H2 = 756.7 [m], suitable for the 
operation of underground gravity energy storage. The fact that there are two shafts 
available, creates the possibility of designing a system regulation, similar to Energy 
Vault’s, that the multiple hoists are to be moved alternatively, thus stabilizing system 
power consumption or -output, causing less disturbance in the grid frequency. 
For the better structural strength and simplicity on top of the possibility of using 
recycled materials, it was decided to design a multi-weight system with solid blocks. 
The diameter of the shafts is Ø6.6 [m]. Based on the sizing method described in [12], 
approximate base diameter should be 82% of shaft diameter, 5.412 [m] for the case of 
Zobák-shafts. A square-shaped base with sides of 3.8 [m], has the diameter of 5.374 
[m], and area of 14.44 [m2]. Considering concrete-like materials, with the ρcon=2,300 
[kg/m3] density and Lb = 3 [m] height of one block, results in the volume of 43.32 [m3], 
total mass of mb = 99,636 [kg/block]. 3 [m] of block height was set for the reached 
approximate mass of 100 [t] for one block considered easily scalable, while staying in 
size range which has developed means of mobilization. [40][41]

3.7.2. SEASONAL HEAT STORAGE 
The conceptualization of the seasonal heat storage is highly influenced by local 
characteristics. An andesite mine is located to the east from the inhabited area of 
Komló. The mine has two extraction sites, one of which has already been abandoned 
and is in the process of recultivation. The concept, formulated with Júlia Kovács is to 
install the heat storage into a hollow part of the abandoned mine site. Furthermore, 
the area surrounding the heat storage should be able to serve as a public space, for 
example as a venue for concerts, or similar events. For this, the top of the storage needs 
to have a solid structural grounding. In this concept, this structure is achieved by 
developing a stone-water-pit storage. Visual concept and current scenery of the mine 
is presented in Figure 17. 
Thanks to the location, there is potentially smaller need for excavation, in contrast 
with starting a heat storage development on a green-field. Similarly, the gravel which 
is already in proximity can be backfilled to the pit with economic advantage, since 
costs for disposal and purchase of gravel or soil are avoided. [51] 
 
 
 
As for the advantage of solid structure, and installation price efficiency, stone-water-
pit storage technology has downsides as well. Water by itself is a favourable material 
for heat storage, owing to its high heat capacity and low material price. The stone 
reduces the volume of water and at the same time contribute to the system with its 
own, lower heat capacity. As a result of the latter, based on operational examples of 
Stuttgart and Chemnitz, a water-pit storage with the same heat storage capacity 
requires 67-70% of total volume of gravel-water-pit storage. Other downside is, that 
the necessary complex thermal structure of the storage diminishes O&M performance 
of the stone-water-pit storage, as it is almost impossible to carry out maintenance 
inside the storage or repair leaks in the waterproofing elements. [51]

3.8. Final concept, examined cases 
The final concept of the energy community in Komló has been formulated through 
the thought process described in the latter section. The visual representation of energy 
flows in the proposed system is shown in Figure 19. In the forthcoming evaluation 
process five cases will be examined.  
3.8.1. CASE 1A 
In Case 1A the system does not contain energy storage technologies and the 
modernization and operation of CHP2 engine is not considered. Local electricity 
production and demand is constantly balanced with the grid resulting in great volume 
of traded energy. District heating is provided by the biomass boiler in the heating 
season and with the supply from the natural gas furnaces in the summer, resulting in 
a more CO2 intensive district heating system. The CHP1 engine is operating based on 
the peak prices of the power market, partially supplied with local biogas. 
3.8.2. CASE 1B 
Case 1B is examining the same system as Case 1A, with the difference, that the 
modernization and operation of CHP2 engine is considered.

3.8.3. CASE 2A  
Case 2A includes energy storage technologies, both gravitational electricity storage 
and heat storage. The gravitational storage serves as a puffer for the electricity flows, 
as in case of overflow of local power output, the gravitational storage starts charging, 
and the grid balancing only happens if it is totally charged. Also, if local production is 
not sufficient for meeting the local demand, gravitational storage starts discharging, 
and grid electricity is only used if there is no sufficient charge in the storage. Similarly 
to Case 1A, the modernization and operation of CHP2 engine is not considered.  
The district heating system is supplied with the biomass boiler, which will run on 
peak capacity, to fill the heat storage next to meeting the local demand, thus utilizing 
biomass at the highest possible efficiency. If the heat storage is filled, the boiler 
regulates its output based on the local demand, which is supported by the small heat 
tanks as puffers. When the heat demand is decreased below the minimal output of the 
boiler, the heat storage starts discharging to meet the local demand. If the storage 
would empty, the biomass facility would start operation at full capacity, to meet the 
demand and fill the heat storage simultaneously.  
The CHP1 engine is operating based on the peak prices of the power market, 
partially supplied with local biogas. 
3.8.4. CASE 2B 
Case 2B is examining the same system as Case 2A, with the difference, that the 
modernization and operation of CHP2 engine is considered. 
3.8.5. CASE 3 
Case 3 is considering the same system setup as Case 2A, but with the examination 
of the additional investment in the renewal of the district heating network, resulting 
in the decrease of the heat demand.

3.10. Financial modelling and used economic assumptions 
3.10.1. CAPEX AND O&M COSTS 
CAPEX (𝐶𝑠) and initial O&M costs (𝐶𝑂&𝑀𝑠,1) are calculated according to the 
descriptions for each installed system type (s) in Chapter 3.9. As the systems age, O&M 
costs might increase over the years. Therefore, a linear increase of the costs has been 
considered, so that the total final increase is of a given percentage at the end of the 
lifetime. This total increase is assumed to be 5%. 
3.10.2. ELECTRICITY DAY-AHEAD MARKET PRICES  
Another parameter of the economical investigations are the electricity prices. The 
electricity prices are considered both in the energy model (for CHP engine scheduling) 
and in the economic model and are significant for the formulation of the results. Two 
considerations were carried out in order to produce hourly timeseries forecast for the 
electricity market prices of the Hungarian grid.  
 For determining future prices, an analyst forecast for long-term evolution of 
average annual European electricity market prices, based on the “Sustainable 
Development Scenario” of the IEA’s World Energy Outlook 2021 [52] was considered. 
The analysis stated lower (-33%) and higher confidence (+20%) intervals on the 
forecasted case as well, marking the range for individual markets. These confidence 
scenarios will be later utilized to show market price sensitivity of the economic 
performance of the cases. [53] 
Market price projections are presented in Figure 22.   
Secondly, historical day ahead prices were collected for 2012-2022Q2 period in an 
hourly resolution and the annual average prices as well, from the Hungarian Power 
Exchange database. [54] Rate of each hourly price to the average annual price of the 
given year was then calculated. These rates were then have been averaged for the 
hours of the year, showing that how the value for a given hour of the year compares

National Energy and Climate Plan of Hungary, in which a 5% discount rate is used in 
the analysis of national energy investments. This value is assumed to be applicable for 
the case of energy community concept in Komló as well. [57]

4. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
 
4.1. Optimization of storage technologies 
 
In the development phase of the concept it has already turned out, that despite the 
connection of DH network and electricity grid, the heat and electric storage 
technologies would not influence each-others technical performance, due to the 
independently operated CHP facilities. Thus, the optimization can be separated for the 
two technologies. It is to be seen, as later described in more detail, that the economic 
performance indicators (NPV, IRR, PBT) will not be useful in finding the most suitable 
storage capacities, as the system setup without storage, will overcome the cases 
including storage in financial means, only owing to the proposed framework of the 
system. Figure 27 and 28 present the development of system NPV in relation to 
examined storage capacities.  
This characteristic is caused by the fact, that the only variable income generation (as 
cost abatement of base case is fixed) of the proposed framework is the revenues from 
selling electricity to the grid. Storage technologies do not produce any income, 
furthermore the gravitational storage reduces income, by decreasing the amount of the 
traded volumes by buffering the locally produced electricity. If the framework 
included the gravitational storage to serve the grid, then it’s relation to revenues 
would change. Heat storage could be examined for revenue generation, or at least cost 
reduction, if it would work besides the biomass and the gas furnaces as well, and 
would have an effect on costs, by influencing the ratio of heat generated from sources 
with different cost profiles.

4.2. Integration of energy storage technologies in the local network balancing 
4.2.1. DISTRICT HEATING 
Optimisation of the heat storage capacity has led to the desired cooperation and 
balancing scheduling of the biomass boiler, CHP and the storage, which this way 
avoids 2,900 [tCO2/year] emissions, adding up to 58,000 [tCO2] spared emissions on 
the examined project lifetime. the cooperation is presented on Figure 35 for a year of 
continued operation. It is visible that the biomass boiler serves the heating season 
demand efficiently, with a mixed operation in the transition period and the heat 
storage is providing input for the district heating network during the summer period, 
without having to reconnect the biomass boiler, serving possibility for maintenance.  
In the autumn season the boiler is on the most efficient full capacity, supplying the DH 
network demand and filling up the heat storage simultaneously. It assumed that 
storage sizing optimum has a definite connection with the amount of total summer 
period heat demand. In reality, in the transition periods in the beginning and end of 
the heating season it might be preferred to use a smaller scale boiler or to set the 
regulation levels, so that the biomass boiler would not be turned on and off in short 
amount of time, as it potentially does not have that inertia as it is assumed in this 
model. It is important to note, that the proposed stone-water-pit heat storage is 180,000 
[m3] in volume, that is generally unprecedented as stone-water-pits are rather smaller 
in size. If a water pit was considered a 120,000 [m3] would be sufficient, which on the 
other hand is a realistic size of a commercial plant, similar to the one in Gram, 
Denmark as an example. [68]

charge the gravitational storage at peak input capacity. Due to the sun setting soon, 
the storage starts discharging in the afternoon, but discharging time is elongated 
thanks to the second operation phase of the CHP. Grid trading is optimal in this day, 
as a surplus is fed to the grid around the midday peak, CHP and storage partially cover 
the evening peak period and purchase only occurs in the evening valley phase.

the peak of hourly demand, long-term loading to the network midday, as the storage 
is charged in the morning already. Charge is sustained until the evening when sun is 
setting, and finishes just before the evening peak demand. CHP production does not 
occur during this day.

4.4. Sensitivity of results on power market trends 
As the only variable revenue element of the financial model is the earnings from 
electricity trading on the grid, a sensitivity analysis has been conducted with 
considering potential range of the European market prices as described in Chapter 
3.10.2. In the assessment results of Case 1A and Case 2A have been considered, as these 
are the financially more reasonable scenarios and as Case 3 is the same as Case 2A 
regarding the electricity side.  
Figure 39 presents the sensitivity of Case 1A on power market price changes. It is 
visible, that lower price scenario has less effect on the financial performance of the 
system. Potentially, this phenomenon is due to the fact, that great ratio of the produced 
electricity from PVs is considered on a feed-in tariff, thus high rate of cashflow is still 
due, regardless of the power market pricing.

Figure presents the sensitivity of Case 2A on power market price changes. 
Opposingly to Case 1A, Case 2A shows to have a higher sensitivity regarding lower 
market prices. It is considered that as a general trend, at the time of evening peak 
power price, demand is partially covered by the storage, thus less purchases will be 
done. On the other hand, the midday peak prices are generally utilized by the PV 
production as when the storage is charged, the surplus is sold on the market. The 
market price changes influence the revenues greatly, as trading power to the grid is 
the only source of global earnings. On the other hand, the expenses are more 
diversified, several OPEX elements are not influenced by market price, thus the 
decreased expenses due to the storage have smaller effect. Overall, expenses are cut 
more significantly with the lower power price, thus the system would lose substantial 
cashflows, which would not be balanced by the change in expenses, thus higher losses 
would occur, compared to the case without storage.  Potentially this would mean, that 
in lower priced power market conditions, the Case 2A would lose proportionally more 
revenues compared to Case 1A, and could not generate better annual returns, 
becoming the less reasonable solution.

5. CONCLUSION 
 
In this project a sustainable energy community concept has been developed with 
focus on utilizing local resources and aiming at self-sufficient and sustainable 
production.  
As the most important outcome of the assessment regarding the economic feasibility 
of the proposed system, it can be stated that the energy community concept in the 
described form and framework has strong economic performance, despite the 
substantial initial investment. The main reason for this performance is that the system 
would be based around a great capacity of PV installations, which by themselves could 
potentially deliver a 7-8-year payback time, thus can carry the further installations to 
still become profitable. Furthermore, it was shown, that the system composition 
utilizing electricity and heat storage, in normal market conditions would have better 
annual returns than the one without these elements relying more on fossil-based 
solutions. It was also proven, that in the case of an investment of this scale, a further 
development of the DH network could also become a reasonable part of the project 
scope, enhancing returns and improving environmental performance of the system. 
The concept has been analysed from further feasibility areas as well, during the case 
formulation. From environmental perspective, the development certainly means a 
positive change in local and global scale as well, potentially avoiding 120,000 [tCO2] 
emissions during project lifetime in comparison with base case. From legal 
perspective, the concept was formulated with regards to comply all relevant 
legislation, both national and European scale, both as an energy community and a 
power and heat producer. The social side of the concept was not explained in detail, 
but in general, the energy community in principle would prioritize the social well-
being of the locals, from one side, by providing new jobs and helping the development 
of the city and the region, and from the other side, by creating an interconnected, 
sustainable community supplied by a modern energy system.  
As part of the project, a substantial amount of information regarding energy system 
future has been obtained and a well-founded and thorough energy balancing model 
has been developed for the electrical and for the district heating side of the system, 
supported by a comprehensive financial model for the evaluation of the proposed 
system set-ups. These efforts will potentially be utilized in the future as well, for one, 
that the model is easily modified with capacities and assumed input elements, for the 
other, that during the development a broad understanding has been obtained on the 
principles of grid balancing, role and control of energy storage and much more, which 
will certainly turn out useful in future projects.  
 
 
==PAGE:69==
62 
The next steps and opportunities regarding this project are broad, and intriguing. 
Further development of the concept and other modifications could be implemented, 
just to provide some examples:  
• assuming the gravitational storage system to be serving the national grid for 
system balancing purposes 
• assessment on small-scale heat production installation for transitional 
periods of the DH system 
• investigation on possible methods in solar heat production and its 
integration in DH system 
• implementing the concept for other communities, with different local 
characteristics and scales. 
Hopefully in the future energy communities of similar principles and aims will be 
able to take part in development of national energy systems and while generating 
social value and cooperation, contributing to the sustainable development of our 
planet.
