Abstract
A major challenge within the field of tissue engineering is to promote angiogenesis in vitro
and create vascularization that can sustain large tissues. One of the difficulties in developing
techniques that addresses that problem has been the lack of a powerful, non-destructive, and
minimally invasive oxygen sensing tool that can provide 3D spatial resolution. Phosphorescent
lifetime imaging (PLIM) is a promising candidate to fill that vacuum. PLIM measurements
come with the challenge that photon observations in general can be very low, thus appropriate
and robust data processing becomes essential to the efficiency and accuracy of the method.
This project aims to examine confocal TCSPC PLIM data and develop a methodology which
can be applied to future studies.
An evaluation of changing estimation uncertainties with
varying PLIM system settings, will be included as well as case studies using the developed
methodology to demonstrate oxygen gradients in both GelMA matrix and living tissue.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
More than 40 years ago, the term ’tissue engineering’ was introduced as an interdisciplinary
scientific field that applies the principles of biology and engineering to regenerate and create
tissue with support from biomaterials, biochemical- and physiochemical factors [1, 2]. Today,
tissue engineering is no longer a narrow scientific field but has been established as a vital pillar
and an alternative strategy in tackling current and future major public health challenges such
as solving organ shortages by replacing or repairing damaged organs. Also the private sector
comprised of both pharmaceutical and biomedical companies, have shown an ever increasing
interest in this field in anticipation for great leaps within development of personalized medicine
and robust drug tests [3].
One of the major challenges that tissue engineering faces today is the ability to create fully
vascularized 3D tissue. Multicellular organisms in the human body are capable of recruiting
new blood vessels by balancing pro- and anti-angiogenic factors so that cells are never located
further away from a capillary than the diffusion limit of oxygen (200 µm) [4]. That type of
vascularization seen in natural tissues is an extremely complex process that is yet to be fully
uncovered.
A lot of research is currently trying to determine the critical factors in creating angiogenesis
in vitro and is focused on the documentation of how to form blood vessels and ensure cell
survival. Less effort seems to be put into validation of whether the blood vessel network creates
an oxygen distribution that mimics the native tissue. Engineering correct oxygen gradients is
essential since the oxygen concentration has an impact on cell behaviour and cell phenotype [5].
Local oxygen variations has been shown to play a crucial role in embryonic development where
it contributes as a regulator for cell differentiation [6]. Tight monitoring of the oxygen levels
in both space and time is therefore necessary to report to create scientific progress within 3D
tissue engineering [7]. The lack of current oxygen level reporting can partly be assigned to a
lack of a powerful and easily accessible tool that allows for such monitoring.
The golden standard in determining oxygen levels in vitro has for a long time been the
Clark electrode that applies voltage and measures the electrochemical reduction of oxygen at
the probe surface [8]. The Clark electrode has several drawbacks: It consumes oxygen thus
affecting the local environment in the sample, it is incapable of measuring large areas in real
time, and it is invasive which induces needle-damage to the specimen. The method is simply
inadequate for most purposes of mapping of oxygen levels in 3D tissues. As an alternative to
the Clark electrode, radioisotope and magnetic resonance techniques have been able to show
spatial distribution of oxygen. It is however with a limited spatial resolution and it comes with
higher hardware costs and a high expertise level is required to properly handle the equipment
[8].
A third method of quantifying the oxygenation of in vitro 3D tissues is optical oxygen sens-
ing. Optical oxygen sensing has, in the void of low cost and versatile applications for oxygen
monitoring, emerged as a powerful tool for quantifying oxygen concentrations [8]. Especially
the use of fluorescent lifetime imaging microscopy (FLIM) and phosphorescent lifetime imag-
ing microscopy (PLIM) has gotten increased attention in the last decade [9]. The increased

popularity can be explained by numerous advantages such as being non-destructive, minimally
invasive, robust to changes in intensity, and have a high versatility in the sensor formats rang-
ing from planar foils and fiber-optic sensors to nanoparticles that allows sensing of oxygen in
both intra- and extracellular environments [8, 10]. The most common probe type is spheric
beads with a diameter in the micro- to nanometer range that is loaded with fluorescent or
phosphorescent dye complexes. The choice of indicator dye and the probe-type plays an es-

plication is to monitor oxygen levels in live cells and tissue, the autofluorescence originating
from biological substances like proteins, DNA etc., can contribute to the noise level [9]. While
the luminescence based oxygen sensing method provides a promising tool that could become
the new golden standard for oxygen mapping in 3D tissues, there is still a general interest in
clarifying the precision and robustness of the method.
1.1
Project aim
Today, the most well established method for PLIM is using confocal microscopy technique
together with time-correlated single photon counting (TCSPC) [11, 12]. The high spatial res-
olution the method offers from laser scanning microscopy together with a high photon count
efficiency creates opportunities to 3D map oxygen levels in living tissues even at low photon
counts. The technique is however still in a state of need for new and intelligent ways to obtain
faster and more accurate data acquisition at improved imaging depth, some of which can be
mediated with better data analysis procedures that maximizes the efficiency of all the recorded
photons. The lack of literature investigating how recording settings impact uncertainty of mea-
surements also motivates an exploration of the dependency between acquisition time, imaging
depth, and accuracy.
While several studies showcase the use and proficiency of PLIM in microfluidic applications
and tissue scaffolds, limited research have been published on the methods ability to depict accu-
rate and simple gradients in 3D. It is therefore of interest to approach the PLIM demonstration
with incremental increases in environment complexity and build causality from simple artifical
oxygen gradients to mapping of unknown tissue scaffolds.
This project will aim to address these concerns by:
• Exploring the current data analysis schemes used for luminescence lifetime imaging and
develop a methodology that is generally applicable, but that specifically improves future
studies conducted with the PLIM system available at site.
• Evaluate the uncertainty of the lifetime estimation for the developed methodology and
investigate the impact of imaging depth, data acquisition time, and grid mesh size on the
estimation error.
• Attempt to show an oxygen gradient with the developed methodology in a controlled
environment designed to be comparable with COMSOL simulations.
• Demonstrate the utility of the beads by attempting to show an oxygen gradient in live
tissue.

CHAPTER 2
Background
This chapter covers the background literature and theory that is applied in the project. It
includes an introduction to what optical oxygen sensing is and the physics behind emission
of fluorescent and phosphorescent signal. Following this will be a section that describes the
TCSPC method and lifetime imaging. An understanding of the physical processes behind the

is also useful to have an insight in the current data processing methods and statistical approach
to photon detection, thus a section addressing this is also included. The optical properties of
the phosphorescent beads will also be discussed since an essential part of 3D mapping is the
ability to accurately locate the spatial position.
2.1
Optical oxygen sensing
When a molecule is excited by the absorption of a photon and it transitions to a higher energy
state, it is energetically favourable to return to its ground state and reach the global system
minimum of stored energy. The relaxation of the molecule can occur through non-radiative
processes or by emission of a photon. The latter process is used in many scientific applications
including protein labelling, flow tracking, and cell staining [13, 14]. Fluorescence is observed
when a fluorophore transitions from its excited singlet state to its ground state. Phosphores-
cence, also known as long lived fluorescence, is on the other hand the emission of a photon
when the dye transitions from an excited triplet state back to the ground singlet state. In
order to observe phosphorescence, the dye molecule has to make an intersystem conversion
where the spin transitions from being paired in the singlet state into being parallel, which is
characteristic for the triplet state.
The dye complexes typically used for phosphorescent life time monitoring are Metal-centered
(MC) which also includes the PtTFP based dye used in this thesis. This type of MC dyes allow
a metal oxidation and a complimentary ligand reduction to occur henceforth creating a metal-
to-ligand charge-transfer (MLCT) state. MLCT state transitions are in general processes that
are manifested by intense absorption bands in the visible light spectrum range and is thus a
very efficient way to collect excitation light without harming cells [15]. Also, the MLCT excited
state is very efficient in producing bright luminescence making this type of dye complexes ideal
for PLIM applications [15]. The presence of heavy atoms in the center further increases the
probability of intersystem crossings and thus increases the efficiency of the phosphorescent dye
[9].
The different relaxation paths and state transitions are often visualized in a Jablonski
diagram to more easily obtain an overview of the multitude of processes that occur. Figure 2.1
shows the Jablonski diagram of the most common processes involved in oxygen quenching of
phosphorescent dye complexes.

2.1.1
State transition and quenching mechanism
Upon excitation, the dye molecule is brought from the ground singlet state (DS0) to the first
electronic excited singlet state (DS1) at rate ka as depicted in the Jablonski diagram. The
molecule will likely also transition into a vibrationally excited state in DS1. In that case, there
is a high probability that an internal conversion occurs which relaxes the molecule to the lowest
vibrational level within the electronic state [9]. Internal conversion is a non-radiative process.
From DS1 two major de-excitation processes can occur: The first one is emission of a photon
through fluorescence at rate kf and the second is a non-radiative intersystem crossing at rate
kisc bringing the DS1 to a isoenergetic vibrational level with unpaired spin called the first
excited triplet state (DT1). From DT1 a transition to DS0 is possible through another type
of photon emission named phosphorescence at rate kp. The transition from DS1 to DS0 is
more likely to occur than the phosphorescent de-excitation and is thus a short lived process
in magnitude of 10−9s. This is partly because the fluorecent process is spin-allowed whereas
the transition from Dt1 to DS0 is forbidden according to the selection rule as they are states
of different spin. The determining factor for good phosphorescent dyes are however the kisc
since the dye molecules are optimized for having a high quantum yield of observing triplet de-
excitation through emission . Phosphorescent lifetimes for dye molecules are typically around
10−6s [9].
Now considering that oxygen works as a quencher and is readily available in the local
environment.
The natural ground state of oxygen is the triplet state (OT0).
Deactivation
of the DT1 →DS0 may happen via a non-radiative process at rate kq, where the oxygen
molecule transitions into an excited singlet state (OS1). This behaviour creates a decrease in
the phosphorescent intensity and lifetime that can be recorded and analysed.
A multitude of different pathways besides those mentioned above are illustrated in Figure
2.1, and if all or any of these significantly affect the ideal system, then they should be considered
and incorporated in the design process of the experimental environment. Some of the larger
contributing factors that needs mentioning are: Triplet-triplet annihilation at rate ktt and
singlet oxygen accumulation at low kOisc.

Triplet-triplet annihilation
Triplet-triplet annihilation is an unwanted process that decreases the lifetime and intensity of
the phosphorescent signal as a function of the triplet dye concentration. It is a bi-molecular
radiationless quenching in which two excited dye complexes interact and one of the dye com-
plexes receives the excited state energy from the other. This results in the two excited triplet
molecules becomes one single deactivated dye molecule while the other dye complex converts
into an excited singlet state that relaxes through the regular pathway. The rate of triplet-triplet
annihilation is affected by the dye concentration thus high concentrations of dye molecules is
undesirable in regular PLIM measurements. Otherwise it is also possible to lower the photon-
flux i.e. the excitation light intensity to avoid building up a high DT1 population. [10, 16, 17]
Singlet oxygen consumption and accumulation
While the phosphorescent probes typically are praised as a sensor type that does not consume
oxygen, this might not be entirely true. The singlet oxygen (OS1) production that is a bi-
product of the collisional quenching is a highly reactive species that prior to deactivation may
react with the polymer matrix and cause photodegradation of the probe [18]. The degradation
will not only consume the oxygen but could potentially also cause chemical modifications to
the sensor and alter for instance oxygen permeability. A higher permeability would lead to
an increased influx of oxygen from the tissue to the probe and artificially read out a higher
observed oxygen concentration than the true oxygen level justifies [10]. Polystyrene (PS) has
been found to be among one of the most robust polymers to singlet oxygen oxidation [18].
The conversion of OT0 to OS1 in the PS matrix and the potential for an accumulation
of OS1 should also be considered. This is not something that is often reported or discussed
in the literature, however, Lehner et al. [16] has shown that the OS1 lifetime in PS ∼30
µs which is of same order of magnitude as the phosphorescent lifetime. Scenarios where dye
concentrations greatly exceeds the oxygen concentration might therefore have an impact on
readings where multiple subsequent measurements have been conducted. The same shift in
observed oxygen level might be induced by long excitation periods. This idea could also be
inferred by considering the diffusion problem of oxygen in the PS beads. Diffusional interchange
between bulk and sensor can in the timeframe of the excitation and recording period (µs) be
assumed negligible as diffusion in 50 µm PS beads is on the timescale of seconds.1
Other factors
Temperature has been shown to have a slight effect on the lifetime of the phosphorescent probes
[21, 22]. This behaviour might be explained by thermal quenching and an increased diffusional
activity at higher temperatures [10]. pH values outside the range of 4-8 have also been shown
to affect the measured lifetime for PS microbeads with PtPFPP dye complexes [21]. Lastly,
it is also worth mentioning that proteins such as albumin can have a strong passive binding
to the matrix material of the probe, which can irrevesibly impact the lifetime of the bead[21].
It is therefore often practice to pre-incubate the beads with bovine serum albumin prior to
seeding the beads into a sample.

2.1.2
Stern-Volmer quenching
The non-linear relation between luminescent lifetime and quencher concentration was first
discovered by O. Stern and M. Volmer and the Stern-Volmer equation can in its simplest
configuration be read as:
I0
I = τ0
τ = 1 + Ksv · [Q]
(2.1)
where I and I0 is the luminescence intensity of a sensor in the presence and absence of oxygen
respectively. τ and τ0 are likewise the lifetimes for the luminescence decay in presence and
absence of oxygen. KSV is the Stern-Volmer constant where KSV = kq τ0 and kq is the bi-
molecular quenching constant. [Q] is the concentration of the quencher, which for all purposes
in this thesis will be Oxygen and moving forward be written as so in the mathematical notation.
Figure 2.2: An illustration of how multiple dye populations can be perceived. (1) the classic Stern-
Volmer plot with linear dependency explains a luminescent probe with a single dye population. (2) the
equation is however often extended to more general appliances that better correspond with empirical
observations where the magnitude of the quenching mechanism is uneven in the probe bulk. (3) the
most common observation is that two dye populations sufficiently can explain the quenching behaviour.
The simple Stern-Volmer equation is however rarely depicting physical observations ac-
curately in its linear form. The simple equation assumes a homogeneous micro-environment
where the dye complexes are distributed in the whole probe matrix. In most circumstances,
it would seem that this criteria is not fulfilled and an extension to equation 2.1 that allows
non-linear Stern-Volmer fitting can be written as:
τ0
τ =
(
f1
1 + KSV1 · [O] + · · · +
fN
1 + KSVN · [O]
)−1
(2.2)
The equation now includes relative contributions (f1, . . . , fN) to the combined ratio of
lifetime for the luminescent probe where each fraction represents a dye population with its
own Stern-Volmer constant. The quenching constant (kq) is no longer only representing the
efficiency of the quencher but also the accessibility. The heterogeneities in solid optical oxy-
gen probes are not well characterized in literature, and an explanation for multiple different
Stern-Volmer constants could revolve around inhomogeneous dye distribution causing local de-
pletions of triplet oxygen or diffusion limited problems that obstructs effective quenching of
the lumniphore as discussed in Section 2.1 [8, 23]. Another suggested reason could be the as-
sumption of pure dynamic quenching is obsolete, and that indeed static quenching is present in
a microheterogeneous system[24]. The visual interpretation of one to several dye populations
is illustrated in Figure 2.2 where (1) is showing the assumption of a homogeneous distribution
of both oxygen, dye and quenching characteristics which is described by equation 2.1. (2) is
showing the case where multiple dye populations exist and showing that a heterogeneous distri-
bution of oxygen or dye complexes can infer sites where quenching is observed to lesser extend.

a fraction weighted solution. Plotting τ/τ0 vs. the oxygen concentration can give insight to
the behaviour of the quenching system, and the plot is called a Stern-Volmer plot. If a linear
relation is observed, it indicates that only a single class of fluorophores exist with complete
access to the quencher thus reducing equation 2.4 to 2.1. However, if the plot deviates from
linearity, this suggests that multiple dye populations exist. A negative curvature has therefore
commonly been observed in Stern-Volmer plots for luminescent solid beads [17, 22, 24, 28].
2.2
Phosphorescent Lifetime Imaging
The array of different imaging modalities for non-destructive and minimally invasive studying
of tissues, cells, cellular components etc. in 3D has over the last decade increased drastically
[29] Luminescent lifetime imaging, which in this thesis is used as a term that includes both
FLIM and PLIM, is one of those powerful optical methods that has emerged as a key technique
in imaging the local environment and interaction of specific probes in living cells and tissues
[30]. Compared to conventional fluorescence microscopy, FLIM is independent of probe con-
centration since it does not rely on intensity measurements. This eliminates the risk of induced
measuring error caused by a change in local fluorophore concentration due to e.g. diffusion,
photobleaching, or washing out the probes [31].
In Section 2.1 it was outlined that fluorescence and phosphorescence are fundamentally
two separate processes that both result in the emission of a photon. Since both processes are
explained by photon emission, FLIM and PLIM are very similar in ways of data acquisition and
typically systems can conduct both in parallel [30, 32]. The vastly different lifetimes, ∼10−9
s for fluorescence and ∼10−6 s for phosphorescence, separate the two signals in time which
makes recording both type of probes independent of one another.
Today, confocal microscopy technique together with time-correlated single photon counting
(TCSPC) is the most common approach to PLIM [11, 12]. The confocal scanning method
enables better reconstruction of 2D and 3D images compared to widefield methods by omitting
out-of-focus emitted light [33]. Utilizing confocal scanning comes however with the cost of
longer imaging times for the increased spatial resolution. The basic concept of confocal TCSPC
PLIM is to perform TCSPC while the laser scans the sample in (x,y,z).

2.3.1
Data origin and recording
To understand how and why the PLIM measurements look like they do, it is helpful to picture
an image of how light is captured and counted by a photon multiplier tube (PMT) or avalanche
photodiode (APD) as sketched in Figure 2.4. A low intensity light beam hits a photon counter
that propagates a number of voltage pulses to a digital counter that registers and integrates
the number of counts within pre-defined time intervals. The number of photons we expect to
observe in a given time interval is determined by the intensity of the beam, but the actual
observation will randomly fluctuate due to the intrinsic random nature of photon emission.
This fluctuation in the count rate has in the classic literature been assigned to the statistical
properties of the incoming stream photons [36]. It is however not self evident whether the
fluctuations in the light stream are solely caused by the photon statistics or whether they are
artefacts originating from the detection process. The origin of the fluctuations are however less
of a concern than the actual effects on the data recordings. The general assumption within
luminescent lifetime imaging is that classic light theory persists and that the photon emission
follows Poissonian statistics [31, 37, 38, 39].

2.3.2
Data analysis
Oxygen quenches the luminescense intensity of the fluorophore, but since the quenching mech-
anism is dynamic the excited state lifetime also decreases as the quantum yield decreases. This
opens up for two avenues of analysing the phosphorescent signal: Intensity-based and lifetime
based.
While intensity based oxygen sensing can be used in specific and highly controlled
environments, this is in many cases not a robust method to use for oxygen estimates. Intensity
readouts are highly susceptible to photobleaching of sensors, exterior lighting variations over
time, and inhomogeneities in the detector pixels (if a CCD camera is used). However, it has
still seen application within in vivo, in vitro and in aqueous conditions for use in 2D imaging
setups [40, 41, 42]. Particularly it has been attractive for microfluidic cell cultures because of
its simplicity and compatibility with standard fluorescent microscopy procedures [43].

Frequency domain
In the frequency domain the lifetime is estimated by analysing phase shifts between the phase
angle of the luminescent signal and a sinusoidally modulated excitation light. The shift for a
single exponential decay can be described by:
tan(ϕ) = 2πfτ
(2.6)
Where f is the modulation frequency of the excitation light and τ is the lifetime of the lumi-
nescent probe. When the modulation frequency is kept constant, the observed phase lag would
produce a lifetime and through a Stern-Volmer calibration yield a local oxygen concentration.
Selecting the modulation frequency is critical to the method’s sensitivity and precision, how-
ever not trivial to locate and does not always correspond to the theoretical value [44]. Phase
based lifetime estimation has a clear advantage to the time domain approach in analysing
specimens with multiple fluorophores with similar lifetimes.
It has however conventionally
suffered from more difficulties with interference caused by autofluorescence from the specimen.
Schmälzlin et al. [21] suggested a multifrequincy phase modulation that could mitigate the
noisy autofluorescence, but the method has seen a lack of general application [44].

2.3.3
Maximum Likelihood Estimation
Maximum likelihood estimation was originally identified by R. Fisher and is a statistical tool
that with the steeply increasing computational power and its basic concept of likelihood quickly
has become a cornerstone within statistical modelling and computational inference studies[50].
The versatility and ability of the method to objectively reason with observed data are among
the reasons why MLE has become so popular. MLE embodies some critical properties such as:
• Asymptotically efficient - The estimate approaches the lower bound on the variance for
unbiased estimators (the Cramer-Rao bound) as the sample size tends to infinity. This
also means that there is no other consistent estimator with a lower variance.
• Asymptotically unbiased - The bias of the estimator tends to zero when the sample size
tends to infinity.
• Consistent - The sampling distribution of the estimator increasingly concentrates around
the true value of the parameter being estimated as the sampling size increases. It is in
fact asymptotically normal to the estimate.

The method of using oxygen sensing probes for point estimates of oxygen concentration to 3D
map tissues relies on 3D reconstructions of where the luminescent signal originates from. This
brings about an interesting discussion concerning the spatial resolution of the PLIM measure-

2.4.1

An ideal optical system would image a point object as a point. By nature, this is not achievable
due to the diffraction of light, and the resolution of any optical system is thus diffraction limited
[51]. An image of a point object will blur no matter the effort and money spent on optical
equipment. This ’blur’ was first characterized by G. B. Airy in 1835 and turned out to be a
very well explained pattern consisting of a bright center known as the Airy disk with a series of
concentric rings around that form the Airy pattern. The size of the pattern was later described
by J. W. Strutt which resulted in the Rayleigh limit that states two point objects can be
resolved when the first diffraction minimum of a point object coincides with the maximum of
another. This occurs for wide field microscopes at distance:

Background characterization
The background signal can be divided into two separate categories, which needs to be treated
as such. The first contributor to the background signal is the inherent noise in the optical
detector. The other contributor is the potential phosphorescence originating from the GelMA
matrix, cells etc. The importance of the line drawn between the two background contributors is
that optical noise can be assumed to be affecting the bead signal, whereas the phosphorescence
from the matrix will not.
The first contributor is the optical noise, which can be split into three components: Dark
current noise, technical noise, and shot noise. Dark current noise (σdark) is originating from
small electrical currents that flows through the photon detector and cause random firing and
registration of photons without an actual incident. The currents are proportional to the temper-
ature. Decreasing the temperature will therefore decrease the dark current noise, which for low
signal strengths can be very beneficial. The HPM-100-40 detector has for a livingroom temper-
ature a dark count rate of 100-400 Hz which is poisson distributed. The origin of the technical
noise (σtech) is hard to specify and it is a term that embodies various non-idealities that often
are proportional to the quality and price of the system components. The inter-component
communication between analogue and digital equipment is also an origin of technical noise, as
well as interference from other equipment sharing the same room. For this system the technical
noise is unknown and in terms of analyses, it is assumed constant in time and of size, which
is negligible compared to its co-contributing factors. The last noise component related to the
photon detector is shot noise (σshot). Shot noise is another way to address the stochastic be-

4.3.2
Background over time
Measurement drift is a common phenomenon in optical and electrical instruments and can be
caused by many factors such as electric and magnetic fields, temperature changes, mechanical
vibrations, etc. Drifts can be observed when comparing a laser output before and after having
given time for it to warm up. Dark noise originating in the photon detector is another example
of a varying noise contributor that depends on the equipment temperature which will increase
(or decrease if actively cooled) during equipment on-time.
It can be difficult to assess and identify all possible contributors to measurement drifts, but
the total system drift can be assessed and allow comparisons of data that were obtained with
large temporal difference. Figure 4.5 shows the measured background level as a function of
time where t = 0 is where the equipment was turned on. It can be seen that the background
level is changing drastically in the first two hours before stabilizing after around four hours of
equipment on-time.
It was during experiments not always possible to turn on the equipment four hours prior
to the first measurement. Thus not a viable solution to implement general background levels,
but instead determine the background noise in each individual sample to allow comparison
between measurements with large temporal differences. A procedure like that would assume,
that the long term temporal effect observed in Figure 4.5 is a measurement drift, which does
not scale with the pixel intensity, but is a function only of time.

The next step is to determine which model fits the data best. Akaikes Information Criteria
(AIC) is an estimator that provides the relative performance of statistical models and is often
used in connection with MLE. AIC is defined as:
AIC = 2p −2log(L(θML))

The best candidate in a set of tested models is picked based on which model has the lowest AIC
score. The AIC deals with evaluating a functions capability to explain the data while punishing
models with too many parameters that does not provide sufficiently new information to describe
the data - effectively counteracting overfitting.

Where α is the significance level of the test. A p-value of observing the test-statistic in the chi-
square distribution can also be extracted from the test. The p-value is often reduced to binary
information by comparing it to the significance level and decide whether a specific observation
is significant or not. Looking at p-value distributions can however provide valuable information
to how well a function actually performs. It practically eliminates the impact of type I and II
errors since the hypothesis H0 is no longer a binary output. If in fact the H0 is true and all the
assumptions are met, then the distribution of p-values would be uniform [72]. The chi-square
distribution is a non-parametric tool that relies on the chi-square distribution to approximate
the underlying exact distribution. This approximation becomes better when the expected count
in each bin increases. On the other hand, this implies that low cell frequencies constitute a
very poor approximation and reliability of the test decreases. As a rule of thumb, 80 % of the
bins should contain at least 5 observations and no bins should have less than one observation
==PAGE:44==

38
[73]. The number of observations per bin in the tail of the phosphorescent signal is often is
less than five, ni < 5. A counter measure to mitigate the poor approximation was therefore to
re-bin the observations into bins of varying time intervals with the criteria that the number of
observations in the new bin had to be larger than five. The cost of increasing the power of the
non-parametric test is the cost of time resolution in the tails of the signal. Figure 4.10 shows
the p-value distribution for both a) the MLE fitted and b) NLLS fitted bi-exponential function.
There is a distinct difference in how the p-values distribute between the two histograms. While
both distributions seem to be skewed, the MLE fit performs significantly better than the NLLS
fit. The chi-square test rejects the hypothesis that the recorded data originates from the fitted
function 53 % of the times with a significance level at 0.05. Only 1 out of 19 data sets rejected
H0 for the MLE fit which is very close to the expected amount of type I errors. The skewness in
the p-values can however can suggest a three things: 1) The provided statistical model does not
fully explain the observed data. The model does however in Figure 4.9 visually looks to explain
the data quite well. 2) The assumptions of the chi-square test are not met. This could be a
discussion point as to how big an impact the reduced time resolution affects the chi-square
tests ability to accurately test the statistical model. 3) The effective number of degrees of
freedom is not accurately represented by the number of free parameters. It is well-known that
parameters in non-linear models under certain circumstances can have a non-integer number
of degrees of freedom [74, p.130]. This will affect the comparison between the test statistic and
the correct χ2 distribution and thus the p-value distribution.

Pooling of pixels
The presented fitting methodology relies on a set of assumptions that are critical to the validity
of the analysis:
• Each observation (ni) is iid. drawn from a Poisson distribution.
• The descriptive function (equation 4.25) that dictates the phosphorescent decay is con-
stant during the whole time span of recording a single frame.
These assumptions allow pooling of single pixel values into a single decay observation for
a whole bead. The reason why that is possible, is that the assumptions above state that all
observations belonging to an object pixel are drawn from the same distribution function, and
that the accumulated observations will belong to a superposition of this very distribution. This
can be formulated mathematically:
N1(i) ∼Pois(f(t))
N2(i) ∼Pois(f(t))
(4.35)
N(i) = N1(i) + N2(i) ∼Pois(f(t) + f(t)) = Pois(2f(t))
(4.36)

To summarize what has been achieved in this chapter, the first step was to simply inspect
the data. It was observed in the pixel histograms that no clear distinction between bead and
background exists and that a background signal is present. The decay curves confirmed that
high oxygen concentration gave less of a signal and seemingly another type of decay function,
which was expected after reading Section 2.1.2.
Next was the characterization of the background signal. It was deemed highly likely that the
background signal originates from inherent noise in the optical path rather than phosphorescent
signal in the bead embedding material. That observation would mean that the pixel with signal
originating from the beads would be subject to the same noise.
Background noise levels were then investigated a bit further to determine potential spatial
or temporal dependencies. It was found that optical aberrations in at the edges of the objective
affected the pixel readout, thus motivating a reduction of the FOV by using a digital zoom of
==PAGE:49==

2x moving forward. Measurement drift was also observed and caused a dependency of pixel
values to the system-on-time. The temporal effect can however be mitigated by measuring the
background level in each series of data acquisitions and use that information when comparing
measurements shifted in time.
Based on the data origin of single photon counting, the MLE methodology was adapted to
provide fitting estimates for 6 different pdfs. Three of these functions were based on a single
exponential function and the other three was based on a bi-exponential function. The three
variants for each set of exponentials were whether the background signal should be modelled
as a pre-determined constant, as a fitting parameter, or not included at all. With AIC, it was
determined that the bi-exponential term with an added constant background signal overall
across all oxygen concentrations performed best. The MLE fit was then evaluated by both
graphically comparing it to the most common NLLS fitting methods and by looking at the p-
value distribution of the performed Chisquare tests. In both tests, the MLE fit outperformed
all of the NLLS methods. To perform this evaluation, a grid search was conducted to evaluate
the impact of the initial parameter values and to locate the best values to use moving forward.
This investigation is located in the Appendix 7.
Finally, it was possible to show that pixel pooling is a valid approach to increase the total
data amount for the fitting procedure. In Appendix 7 is an outline of why some pixels with
low observation counts were still left out of the data pooling.

Discussion
Optical characteristics
The beads z-position is in the data processing determined by locating the most intense XY-
plane of pixels belonging to a bead. This method is relying on the fact, that the xy-plane
with the highest intensity is also the plane, where the actual bead diameter is the largest. As
we scan trough a bead in the z-direction, the accumulated intensity of the XY-plane would
be proportional to the number of pixels belonging to the bead and thus also the diameter of
the bead in that XY-plane. There is a concern to be raised, if the bead is effectively a super
absorbing sensor. The emission profile would behave differently, as the top part of the bead
would not recieve any excitation light due to the high absorption in the lower parts of the bead.
In this case, the XY-plane with the maximum intensity would be skewed towards the objective.
It is however clear from the light transmission images, that light does penetrate the whole
bead. However, there seems to be an offset difference of 68 µm in the z-direction for the PLIM
system compared to the Zeiss confocal microscope. The PLIM systems ability to resolve the
axial direction is also 28.7 µm larger on average, compared to the normal confocal microscope.
In theory, the Zeiss microscope should have a slightly worse FWHM since the laser wavelength
is 488 nm compared to the PLIM systems 473 nm.
Myun Kim et al. [76] has previously shown that PS beads with a diameter between 1 -
100 µm can act as a ball lens. The CPOx beads working as a ball lens could be a very likely
explanation for the high degree of light path distortion observed in the transmission images.
Influence of recording settings on estimation uncertainty
The perfect balance between lifetime estimation accuracy and data acquisition time is deter-
mined on a case to case approach. For long term steady state examinations, it can be favourable
to use the time consuming instrument settings to gather high quality data. If the system is
transient or dynamic, it would be more beneficial to balance out the data quality with lower
acquisition times and ensure that the signal is recorded. How the best balance is achieved
depends on the biological sample that is being analysed, but this project can provide intuition
and guidance to which imaging settings has the highest benefit to cost ratio.
Increasing the pixel dwell time beyond 5λ = 325 µs does not seem to provide any further
improvement that outweighs the cost of increased imaging time. There is also a big benefit
in operating at no less than the 5λ dwell time, if the system contains low oxygen segments.
Increasing the grid size from 128x128 to 256x256 comes with the benefit of decreasing the total
estimation error with close to a half of what the 128x128 grid is at low oxygen concentrations.
This observation is however confounded with the optical zoom of the system. The grid size
is effectively an expression for how many pixels are being recorded and that are belonging
to the same bead. Reducing the FOV four times would therefore most likely yield the same
improvement. Effectively, when the grid size is increased by a factor two, the number of pixels
belonging to the bead increases by four, which impacts the pooling of data greatly. The imaging
time is however also increased four times, thus the actual benefit is only half the reduction in
==PAGE:61==

55
error for four times the imaging time. The laser power does not come with any time cost, but
it increases the photo bleaching of the phosphorescent probes thus lowering data quality in the
long term. PtTFPP dye complexes are however very robust to bleaching, thus an increased
laser power for mid-length experiments might be viable [22]. Altering the laser power can
also state something about our ability to estimate beads deeper in the tissue. Increased laser
power resembles beads close to the objective, whereas the low laser power resemble beads lying
further away. The fitting error is therefore observed in Figure 5.4 to be quite affected by the
imaging depth. The increase in fitting error explains the majority of the increase in the total
error. A higher precision in deeper tissue can be achieved by increasing the number of frames
recorded at each pixel. Since data pooling of single pixel observations is possible, varying the
number of frames per pixel as a function of imaging depth might be an alternative solution to
increase the laser power.
An interesting overall observation is that changing the laser power (i.e. imaging depth)
affects mainly the fitting error.
Changing the pixel dwell time on the other hand is only
affecting the total error and thus the bead-to-bead error.

concentrations. It should however be noted, that the comparison between the measured and
the simulated oxygen concentrations should not be entirely one sided. The simulation is only
our best guess based on cell properties observed in 2D environments, thus not necessarily
representative for simulations in 3D tissues. The lower bead count for beads lying above 500
µm in height for high cell concentrations might be due to the increased attenuation of light
from the increase cell density. Increasing the laser power or the number of frames per pixel
could compensate for the diminishing signal.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion and Future
Work
This thesis aimed to develop a methodology for processing confocal TCSPC PLIM measure-
ments and subsequently asses performance and test application.
A thorough understanding of the underlying mechanisms that generate PLIM data was used
to develop intuition about how the data processing should be approached. It was established
that single photon counts follows Poissonian statistics and that this property especially is
important when treating data with low photon counts.
A single pixel analysis and a background characterization lay the footwork for developing
a MLE based statistical model. This model would benefit from both addressing the Poisson
statistics and utilize an estimator, which is asymptotically efficient, asymptotically unbiased,
and asymptotically normal. The model evaluation deemed the method to perform better than
all common NLLS methods.
The methodology was then applied to a series of experiments, one of which investigated
the effect of PLIM system settings on measurement accuracy.
Distinguishing between the
statistical fitting error and the bead-to-bead error opened up for new discussions to be had.
Future imaging routines could now focus on improving the bead-to-bead variation and not only
rely on smarter and better fitting methods.
The optical properties of the beads were also examined and gave interesting perspectives
to how the CPOx beads behaves when imaged with both a conventional confocal microscope
and a PLIM system.
Lastly, the application of the methodology together with the PLIM system in biologically
relevant scenarios were tested. A gradient platform was designed and used to showcase linear
oxygen gradients across three observations. The results could be compared with simulated
COMSOL models. The methodology was also used in living tissue and the observed differ-
ence between simulated results, and the estimated oxygen concentrations pose many questions
regarding 3D simulation studies based on cell properties determined from 2D cell cultures.
Future work Moving forward with this methodology would yield a more thorough investi-
gation of the method’s ability to resolve 3D oxygen gradients in both controlled settings like the
gradient platform and in living tissue. Also, further analyses of what determines the bead-to-
bead variation would be beneficial for improving the imaging environment and decrease error
estimates.

Intra bead intensity and lifetime assessment
An empirical observation that affected the quality of the fitting method was that some of the
pixel values with low intensity relatively to the highest intensity pixels contributed with an
increased uncertainty to the fitting result. While the decay lifetime theoretically is very robust
to the intensity profile, it can for the fitting function be difficult to accurately estimate the
lifetime for pixels where the signal is low and comparable with the background level. A cross
section of the pixel values in both x and y direction can be be seen in Figure 6 for a) 21 %
and c) 0% oxgygen. The pixel lifetime seems to reach a plateau quickly but the fluctuations
of the lifetime estimate can be quite severe as observed in c) for the cross sectional cut in
the x direction. The seemingly higher fluctuations are also supported by the higher standard
deviation of the fitted lifetime near the edges of the bead as it can be observed in b) and d).
From comparing the decreasing error and the pixel intensity as shown in b) and d), it was
decided to threshold and remove pixel values that were lower than 20 % of the maximum pixel
value.
