Abstract
As industries face increasing pressure to transition from linear to circular business models,
companies often lack the tools to translate circular economy principles into concrete strategies.
This thesis addresses that challenge by developing and testing a stakeholder­centered, decision
navigating support tailored to the needs of product manufacturers in the work textile industry. A
business­to­business focused industry producing textiles for use in healthcare, hospitality, and
cleaning services.
Using the Design Research Methodology, the research was structured into five phases, com­
bining literature review, industry collaboration, support conceptualisation, and empirical testing.
The primary output is the Circular Feasibility Navigator, a workshop­based support that guides
companies through four phases: understanding their stakeholders and value chain, identify­
ing circular strategies, assessing them through stakeholder­focused feasibility questions, and
prioritising the most valuable and implementable ideas.
The support was tested with two Danish companies, WHITEWEAR and EPOS, and evaluated
for relevance, usability, and strategic impact. Results show that the support aids internal align­
ment, stimulates critical reflection on circular readiness, and provides a structured pathway for
early­stage circular economy decision­making. By integrating stakeholder perspectives and
sector­specific considerations, the support helps bridge the gap between circular ambition and
actionable transition in a complex industrial context.
This thesis contributes to the growing body of research on practical circular economy imple­
mentation tools and offers actionable insights for companies seeking to evaluate and prioritise
circular strategies in their operations.

Introduction
The global transition toward a circular economy (CE) is no longer an ideal but a necessary re­
sponse to the accelerating challenges of resource depletion, environmental degradation, and
climate change. Linear economic models, defined by their “take­make­dispose” logic, have long
been dominant in the industrial sector, but the environmental consequences from greenhouse
gas emissions and environment pollution & waste generation, are increasingly difficult to ignore
(European Environment Agency, 2024a). In this context, CE offers an alternative that empha­
sises the continuous circulation of materials, products designed for durability and reuse, and
new business models focused on value creation beyond the point of sale (European Environ­
ment Agency, 2024a, 2024b).
The European Commission are actively advancing CE as a strategic pillar for achieving climate
goals and promoting sustainable growth (Directorate­General for Environment, 2023; European
Commission, n.d.­a). As these policy shifts intensify, so does the pressure on businesses to
reconfigure their operations, supply chains, and value propositions (European Commission,
n.d.­b; Planet Compliance, 2024). Despite growing political and academic consensus on the
need for circularity, practical implementation remains a complex challenge, as companies may
lack the resources, tools, and strategic clarity to translate CE principles into action (Dorrego­
Viera et al., 2025; European Environment Agency, 2024c).
This thesis addresses that gap by developing a structured support that enables companies,
particularly in the textile sector, to assess and navigate their transition towards CE. The work
was situated within the specific industrial context of the work textile industry, a segment that has
limited attention in CE literature but held potential for sustainable transformation.
To target this research effectively, a term, work textile industry, was introduced and used through­
out the thesis. The term referred to companies involved in the production and distribution of
durable textile products designed for use in institutional and service­oriented sectors such as
healthcare, hospitality, cleaning, catering, and pharmaceuticals. These textiles included, but
were not limited to, uniforms for healthcare workers, patient garments, chef jackets, tablecloths,
bed linens, and cleaning textiles.
Unlike the textile and clothing industry, which is often associated with fast fashion, the work
textile industry is not driven by seasonal trends or rapid turnover. Instead, it is characterised by
a focus on quality, hygiene, and product longevity. The textiles are typically used in Business­
to­Business (B2B) systems and are subject to frequent industrial laundering, requiring high
durability and performance over time.
This unique operational context presented both challenges and opportunities for circularity. For
example, many work textiles are owned not by the end­users but by intermediaries such as
laundries, who rents and maintains the textiles on behalf of clients, a model often described as
a Product­Service System (PSS). PSS refers to a business model that combines products and
services into a single integrated solution (Lingegård, 2020). Instead of just selling a physical
product, a PSS offers a combination of tangible products and intangible services. This opens
the door to circular strategies like reuse, repair, and controlled take­back schemes. At the same
time, the industry face obstacles related to sorting infrastructure, end­of­life (EoL) processing,
and the need for specialised textile properties that could limit the possibility of using recycled
material (EEA, 2024; Sandin & Peters, 2018).
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1
==PAGE:13==
By focusing on this segment of the textile sector, this thesis contributed to an understanding of
what CE meant in practice and how it could be tailored to specific industrial realities.
The overall aim of this thesis was to develop a support that help product manufacturers in the
work textile industry to strategically, tactically, and operationally plan their transition to CE.
The thesis has been conducted in collaboration with a Danish textile company, WHITEWEAR, as
case company. Through stakeholder participation, empirical testing, and iterative development,
the support was prototyped, evaluated, and refined. It was based on theoretical insights from
the CE literature and practical observations from industrial experts.
The thesis followed the Design Research Methodology (DRM), which provided a structured and
flexible framework for problem investigation and solution development (Blessing & Chakrabarti,
2009). DRM consisted of five key stages, Research Clarification (RC), Descriptive Study I (DS­
I), Prescriptive Study I (PS­I), Descriptive Study II (DS­II), and Initial Prescriptive Study II (Initial
PS­II) (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). Each stage had a specific purpose in understanding the
context, designing a relevant support, and validating its usability and feasibility in practice. A
thorough explanation of each stage is presented in section 2.
The remaining of this thesis is structured as follows:
Chapter 2 outlines the research methodology, including the DRM framework and its application
in this study.
Chapter 3 provides the research clarification, including a literature review on CE, readiness as­
sessment, an introduction to the case company and stakeholder context.
Chapter 4 details the findings from the systematic literature review (SLR) and the co­development
of a transition path with WHITEWEAR.
Chapter 5 presents the ideation and conceptualisation of the support, including development of
multiple support concepts, before deciding on the final one.
Chapter 6 discusses the empirical testing of the support and summarises the feedback and re­
sults.

Design Research Methodology
To investigate how work textile companies can integrate circular business model innovation and
value chain optimisation for a competitive and sustainable transition to CE, this thesis adopts the
Design Research Methodology (DRM) (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). DRM provides a struc­
tured and systematic approach that aligns well with the complexity of analysing and improving
business models and value chain processes in the work textile industry. The methodology is
relevant due to being iterative and flexible, because it is allowing a deeper exploration, empirical
validation, and a refinement of both theoretical and practical insights (Blessing & Chakrabarti,
2009).
DRM consists of four key stages: Research Clarification (RC), Descriptive Study I (DS­I), Pre­
scriptive Study (PS), and Descriptive Study II (DS­II), as presented in figure 2.1. These stages
facilitate an understanding of the existing challenges and opportunities, they enable the de­
velopment of targeted supports, and provide room to evaluate their effectiveness (Blessing &
Chakrabarti, 2009).
Adopting this methodology ensures a systematic approach to developing and validating solu­
tions. The iterative way of design research allows for continuous improvement, making it a good
method for addressing complex, practice­oriented problems while also discovering insights ap­
plicable beyond the immediate scope of the study.

Descriptive Study I
In the DS­I stage, a broad review of the literature is conducted to develop a more focused and

2.3.3
Prescriptive Study
In the PS stage, the focus shifts from analysis to synthesis. Building on the insights generated
in DS­I, this phase involves defining a desired future situation and developing targeted support
to help realise it. The goal is to conceptualise, design, and iteratively refine a support solution
that will be tested in the following phase (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009).
In this thesis, the PS was carried out through a structured ideation and conceptualisation pro­
cess. Brainstorm sessions were used to explore potential solutions based on previously iden­
tified support requirements. This was followed by the development concepts, which were then
iteratively refined through further brainstorming, feedback, and internal evaluation. The process
ultimately led to the creation of a detailed, stakeholder­centered support.
2.3.4
Descriptive Study II
In DS­II, the effectiveness of the developed support from the PS stage is evaluated to determine
whether it achieves the intended improvements. Empirical studies assess both the applicability
and usability of the support. The goal of this stage is to determine whether the developed sup­
port is promising and if it requires further refinement (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009).
In this thesis, DS­II was conducted through workshop­based testing with two product­manufacturing
companies: WHITEWEAR and EPOS. The support was evaluated against four key criteria: rele­
vance, usability, impact, and comparative. The workshops served as interactive testing environ­
ments, where participants engaged with the support and provided feedback through qualitative
discussions and a quantitative survey. These inputs formed the empirical basis for assessing
the supports performance and informing future improvements.
2.3.5
Initial Prescriptive Study II
In PS­II, the aim is to return to the prescriptive phase and integrate the insights gained from
DS­II. Given the iterative nature of the DRM, this phase mirrors the structure and objectives
of the first prescriptive study, but with a focus on refinement rather than original development
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009).

3.2
Circular Economy as a Concept
The concept of CE represents a shift away from the traditional linear economic model of ”take­
make­dispose” toward a more regenerative approach that prioritises resource efficiency, waste
minimisation, and sustainability. In contrast to the linear model, where resources are extracted,
used, and ultimately discarded, CE seeks to close material loops by designing products for
longevity, reusability, and recyclability. This transition is driven by the need to address envi­
ronmental challenges, resource scarcity, and economic inefficiencies while fostering innovation
and resilience. (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2015)
One of the key motivations for adopting a circular economy is its potential to decouple eco­
nomic growth from resource consumption. By extending product lifecycles through reuse, re­
furbishment, and remanufacturing, businesses and societies can reduce waste generation and
reliance on raw materials (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, n.d.). This contributes to lower carbon
emissions, reduced pollution, and enhanced economic opportunities. Moreover, circular strate­
gies can create new business models, such as product­as­a­service and industrial symbiosis,
where industries collaborate to repurpose waste streams as valuable inputs for other sectors.
In doing so, businesses not only reduce their environmental footprint but also gain economic
benefits by reducing costs associated with raw material procurement and waste disposal (The
Wall Street Journal, 2024).
A fundamental framework for understanding CE is the butterfly diagram, developed by the Ellen
MacArthur Foundation, see figure 3.1. This visual representation illustrates how biological and
technical materials can be effectively managed within a circular system (Ellen MacArthur Foun­
dation, 2021). As seen in figure 3.1, the left side of the diagram focuses on the biological cycle,
where biodegradable materials re­enter the environment safely through processes like com­
posting and anaerobic digestion. These materials can be returned to the soil to regenerate
natural systems, supporting biodiversity and sustainable agriculture (Ellen MacArthur Founda­
tion, 2022a). The right side represents the technical cycle, where durable materials such as
metals, plastics, and synthetic components are maintained within the economy through repair,
remanufacturing, and recycling (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2022b). By optimising both cy­
cles, CE ensures that materials retain their value for as long as possible, minimising waste and
environmental impact.

Implementing a CE requires an approach that involves redesigning products, rethinking supply
chains, and fostering collaboration among stakeholders. Businesses must shift towards sus­
tainable design principles, using materials that are easy to disassemble, repair, and recycle.
Governments play a crucial role by creating regulatory frameworks, offering incentives, and in­
vesting in infrastructure that supports circularity. Consumers also have a part to play by making
conscious choices, such as prioritising durable products, participating in sharing economies,
and properly sorting waste for recycling (Consilium, 2025; The Wall Street Journal, 2024).
The benefits of a CE extend beyond environmental sustainability. It has the potential to cre­
ate jobs, stimulate innovation, and enhance economic resilience by reducing dependence on
finite resources (Consilium, 2025; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, n.d.). As industries and soci­
eties increasingly recognise the limitations of the linear economy, the adoption of circular prac­
tices becomes a necessity rather than an option. Embracing this model can lead to long­term
sustainability, ensuring that future generations inherit a planet with abundant resources and a
stable climate (Consilium, 2025; Forest Stewardship Council International, 2024).
CE holds the potential to redefine value creation, drive systemic change, and contribute to
a more resilient and sustainable global economy. By leveraging innovation, collaboration, and
policy support, societies can move towards a system where economic activity is decoupled from
environmental degradation, which can help both people and the planet.
3.2.1
Circularity & Sustainability
While CE and sustainability share common goals, they are distinct concepts. Sustainability is
a broad framework that includes environmental, social, and economic dimensions, aiming to
ensure that resources are used in a way that meets present needs without compromising future
generations (Rosário et al., 2024). Circularity, on the other hand, specifically refers to the eco­
nomic and material systems designed to eliminate waste and extend the lifecycle of products
and materials (The Debrand Team, 2024).

==PAGE:20==
A key difference between CE and sustainability is their approach to resource use. CE actively
promotes the continual use of resources by keeping materials in circulation through various
strategies such as reuse, repair, remanufacturing, and recycling (Thomas, 2023). Sustainabil­
ity, while incorporating similar principles, extends beyond material flows to include aspects such
as ethical labour practices, climate change mitigation, and biodiversity conservation (Thomas,
2023).
Integrating CE principles into sustainability strategies can enhance efforts to build a more re­
generative and resilient economy (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017). However, it is important to ac­
knowledge that circular practices do not automatically lead to sustainable outcomes (Korhonen
et al., 2018). For example, some circular strategies may reduce material consumption but in­
advertently increase carbon emissions, energy use during the product’s life cycle, or negatively
impact biodiversity (Castro et al., 2022). Therefore, while CE can serve as a powerful strategy
for achieving sustainability, its implementation must be critically assessed to ensure that en­
vironmental, social, and economic impacts are aligned with broader sustainability goals (Kara
et al., 2022).
3.3
Strategies for Circular Economy
The transition to a CE requires coordinated efforts across multiple levels of society, from gov­
ernment regulations to business innovations and consumer behaviour shifts. This process is
complex, as it involves transforming long­established linear economic structures into more re­
generative systems. Due to the interplay between industries, regulatory frameworks, and so­
cietal norms, the transition is best understood through systemic approaches that account for
technological advancements, business models, and institutional dynamics (Geissdoerfer et al.,
2018).
To guide a transformation into CE, various frameworks have already been developed that high­
light the necessary steps for industries and businesses to adopt circular strategies. One tool
is the Circular Strategy Scanner, see figure 3.2, which helps organisations assess their current
circularity maturity and identify key areas for improvement. By evaluating resource flows, mate­
rial efficiency, and waste reduction strategies, companies can create actionable roadmaps for
integrating circular practices into their operations (Blomsma et al., 2019).

3.4
Sustainability & Circular Economy in the Textile & Clothing
Industry
Like many industries, the textile and clothing (T&C) industry has traditionally followed a linear
economy model, characterised by the ”take­make­dispose” principle. This linear approach has
led to the production of textiles that are optimised for their initial use, often without consideration
for sustainable practices such as eco­design, sustainable production, or end­of­life strategies
like the 5R framework (Refuse, Reduce, Reuse, Repurpose, Recycle) (Brad, 2023). A signifi­
cant driver of unsustainable practices in the T&C industry has been the rise of fast fashion. A
concept that emerged in the 1990s and gained widespread traction in the early 2000s. Fast
fashion emphasises the rapid transformation of the latest fashion trends into affordable con­
sumer products. However, this inherently contradicts principles of sustainability and circularity.
The fast production cycles, reliance on low­cost materials, and limited concern for sustainable
sourcing.
In 2019, the United Nations identified the T&C industry as the second most polluting industry
globally, responsible for approximately 10% of global greenhouse gas emissions (Brad, 2023;
Saha et al., 2021). Additionally, it contributes to significant environmental degradation, with tex­
tile dyeing alone accounting for 20% of global industrial water pollution(Brad, 2023; Saha et al.,
2021). As environmental concerns grow and new legislation, directives, and guidelines emerge,
companies in the T&C industry are increasingly seeking sustainable and circular alternatives.
Key focus areas include waste reduction, reuse, recycling of textile waste and wastewater, eco­
design, sustainable raw material sourcing, and the implementation of take­back systems.
To realise these ambitions, companies must rethink strategies across the entire value chain,
ranging from raw material extraction and manufacturing processes to supply chains, infrastruc­
ture, and consumer behaviour. While the circular transition presents both opportunities and
challenges, it is hindered by the industry’s deeply entrenched linear structures. Progress de­
pends on collective action, supportive policy, and targeted incentives.
Challenges exist throughout the value chain, from sourcing to end­of­life management. Achiev­
ing circularity requires sustainably sourced raw materials and, where appropriate, the integration
of recycled fibres. However, there is still limited knowledge on how best to combine new and
recycled fibres, the resulting material properties, and the associated costs of new production
technologies (Din et al., 2025).
Upstream in the value chain, significant barriers include insufficient awareness and a lack of in­
frastructure for collection and recycling. Current systems are inadequate for large­scale end­of­
life textile recovery, limiting the potential to create secondary markets for used textiles. Efficient
sorting and collection infrastructure, supported by public engagement and education (similar to
household waste sorting systems), are essential for enabling high­value recycling, upcycling, or

decomposition (Din et al., 2025). Meanwhile, the development of alternative textile materials is
gaining momentum. Innovations include wood­based fibres that can replace traditional materi­
als as cotton and synthetic, oil­derived fibres. Cotton production is highly water­intensive, while
synthetic fibres, which make up roughly 60% of all textiles, are estimated to consume 70 million

sector toward circularity.
3.4.1
The Work Textile Industry
The work textile industry, a term developed for the study specifically, shares many of the same
sustainability and circularity challenges as the broader T&C industry. However, the influence of
fast fashion is largely absent, as garments are typically designed for functionality and durability
rather than for following trends. Long­lasting, high­quality textiles are the norm, providing a
foundation for sustainable practices.
A major distinction in the work textile industry is its predominantly B2B value chain, as opposed
to the Business­to­consumer (B2C) model prevalent in mainstream fashion. This structural dif­
ference has important implications. In many cases, the end owner of the textiles is not the user,
but a third­party service provider, as a laundry service. These companies often operate within
a product­service system (PSS), offering rental and maintenance services, including collection,
laundering, and redistribution.
As in the broader T&C industry, there is currently no large­scale infrastructure for collecting
and sorting used work textiles, which hinders the development of circular solutions. Although
some firms are piloting initiatives in recycling, upcycling, and decomposition, these efforts are
hampered by limited cross­sector partnerships and a lack of knowledge about how to develop
materials that meet industry­specific performance criteria.
The requirements for work textiles differ significantly from those in the B2C sector. Textiles must
sustain industrial laundering processes involving high temperatures and specialised chemicals,
far beyond what is typical in household settings. Consequently, these textiles must be more
robust and durable. The diversity of products, from bed sheets and lab coats to chefs’ jackets
and hospital gowns, further complicates the development of standardised circular solutions. For
instance, surgical gowns must meet stringent protective standards, requiring complex material
compositions that are difficult to recycle or biodegrade.
Nevertheless, the emphasis on durability and quality in the work textile industry has led to early
signs of sustainable thinking. The adoption of design­for­longevity principles and made­to­order
manufacturing indicates a gradual shift toward circularity. These practices reduce overproduc­
tion, extend product life, and offer a platform for deeper integration of circular strategies moving
forward.

The ready2LOOP platform originated from the MATChE platform, which was developed as a
science­based approach to help manufacturing companies assess their readiness across eight
circularity dimensions. The goal was to enable companies to plan and implement a transition
process that enhanced their circularity while providing support through the selection of tools and
methods at key stages (ready2LOOP, n.d.­b). As the platform evolved and more diverse com­

shaped what is now the ready2LOOP platform (ready2LOOP, n.d.­b).
ready2LOOP aims to facilitate companies’ transition toward CE, ultimately contributing to in­

The Readiness Assessment
When a company signs up for a readiness assessment, they must follow a structured process
to ensure accurate results and maximise its benefits.
The first step involves defining the scope of the assessment by selecting the specific area to
be evaluated. This could include the company’s top­selling products, a particular department,

tributed across eight relevant dimensions for the selected area. These questions are answered
using a predefined readiness scale on ready2LOOP, which includes the following levels:

• Planning pilot implementation
• Piloting initiatives
• Planning scale­up
• Scaling up initiatives / Full scale implemented

Based on the responses, the platform generates an overall readiness assessment. The results
provide insights into three key aspects: overall readiness scores, assessment robustness, and
expertise coverage.
Expertise coverage measures the diversity of competencies among employees who participated
in the assessment. A higher coverage indicates a broad range of expertise, while lower cover­
age suggests a more uniform skill set.
Assessment robustness is determined by the number of employees who complete the assess­
ment. Ideally, the more participants, the more comprehensive and accurate the results will be.
By following this process, companies can gain a well­rounded understanding of their readiness
and take informed steps toward improvement.

chain. For example, governmental institutions and regulatory frameworks can substantially
shape the conditions for circular innovation (Sarbu et al., 2020). Advancing from a linear to
a circular economy requires reverse logistics and value chain transformation, both of which
rely heavily on strong collaborative structures to enable change and deliver stakeholder value

(Masoudipour et al., 2017).
Crucially, customers and consumers must also be integrated as active partners in the value

4.3.7
Models, Framework & Tools for Transitioning
The objective of the third search string was:
What organisational models, frameworks, and tools can manufacturing companies use to tran­
sition to and operate within a circular economy?”
The review of search string 3 approaching objective 3 resulted in 4 documents being included
in the study, as shown in figure 4.13
Figure 4.13: Result ­ Revised objective 3: Models, frameworks and tools for transitioning
The review identified three frameworks and one model that manufacturing companies can use
to transition to and operate within a circular economy.
1. Performance Evaluation Framework
The performance evaluation framework developed by Tong et al. (2024) is designed to op­
timise market mechanisms, enhance public oversight, and actively engage manufacturing
companies in the CE transition. It addresses both individual and collective responsibilities
within the value chain.
CHAPTER 4. DESCRIPTIVE STUDY I
37
==PAGE:49==
• Individual responsibility focuses on life cycle management and eco­design, driven by
data sharing.
• Collective responsibility highlights the broader social and environmental benefits of
circular transformation.
The framework promotes partnerships with both internal stakeholders in the value chain
and external actors such as public authorities and government bodies. Together, these
stakeholders collaboratively define circularity goals, using data sharing and extended pro­
ducer responsibility as key tools. Originally developed in China with a focus on electronics
manufacturers, the framework is applicable to a broader range of manufacturing sectors.
2. Circular Data Framework
The circular data framework proposed by Shevchenko and Danko (2022) builds on the
concept of a resource cycle, defined as “a set of transformations and spatial movements
of a substance or group of substances occurring at all stages of its use by humans.”
Shevchenko and Danko (2022, p.9) The framework divides the cycle into two phases:
(a) Transformation of minerals into materials
(b) Transformation of materials into products (where circularity is introduced)
Each process within the cycle produces both desirable and undesirable outputs. True
circularity is achieved when a reuse or repurposing pathway is created for the undesir­
able outputs. The framework enables companies to measure a products past and future
contributions to circularity, helping to map and monitor materials across the value chain
in order to slow and close resource loops.
3. Framework for Developing CE Implementation Strategies
Klenk et al. (2020) presents a framework consisting of two modules: a simulation module
and an optimisation module.
• The simulation module models customer behaviour, acknowledging the role of con­
sumers as critical actors in the CE transition. While individual data may be limited,
the model uses assumptions about customer types and behaviours, supported by
stochastic methods to handle uncertainty.
• The optimisation module focuses on business model decisions such as scaling, lo­
cation planning, and logistics.
The process begins by simulating the current state of the company, its production net­
work and stakeholder interactions, followed by forecasting the impact of key influencers.
If no CE elements are present, they are integrated during the optimisation phase. The
final stage applies discrete event simulation and a mixed­integer programming model to
determine optimal CE­aligned decisions.
4. Two­Stage Process Model of Ecosystem Transformation
The two­stage model described by Parida et al. (2019) guides the transformation of entire
ecosystems toward circularity. It positions manufacturers as ecosystem orchestrators due
to their pivotal role connecting both upstream and downstream value chain actors.
• In the first stage, orchestrators assess the external environment through three lenses:
environmental impact, business model structure, and ecosystem partner capabilities.
This forms a baseline understanding of the ecosystem’s current state.
• In the second stage, orchestrators use three mechanisms to drive the transformation:
CHAPTER 4. DESCRIPTIVE STUDY I
38
==PAGE:50==
– Standardisation mechanisms define shared CE goals and establish implemen­
tation paths.
– Nurturing mechanisms support partners in building the required resources and
capabilities.
– Negotiation mechanisms ensure continued collaboration and alignment toward
CE objectives.
Applied sequentially, these mechanisms have been shown to facilitate coordinated ecosystem­
wide progress toward a circular economy.

4.3.10
Research Gaps
The SLR revealed a notable lack of studies addressing the barriers and enablers of implement­
ing value chain collaboration, particularly in the context of establishing effective and valuable
partnerships. It revealed that only 3 of 6 documents approaching circularity in the manufactur­
ing sector approached barriers and enablers for value chain collaboration. This gap extends
beyond CE initiatives and reflects a broader deficiency in the manufacturing sector’s approach
to collaborative engagement.
Across the literature, data sharing was consistently outlined as a key enabler, while a lack of
awareness and expertise in enabling data sharing emerged as a critical barrier. However, the
majority of identified studies adopted an outside­in perspective, focusing on external drivers of
circularity, rather than an inside­out approach that examines how companies can proactively
engage stakeholders to co­create value. This highlights a gap in research exploring how busi­
nesses can initiate and maintain stakeholder collaboration as part of a broader strategy for
CHAPTER 4. DESCRIPTIVE STUDY I
41
==PAGE:53==
circular transformation for the entire value chain.
With data sharing outlined as a key enabler and the lack of knowledge as a barrier, a gap is
found in understanding which data to share and how the data can enable the transition while
benefitting multiple stakeholders.
The review further revealed that most frameworks, models, and tools designed to support the CE
transition focus predominantly on internal business processes. While stakeholders are acknowl­
edged as essential, there is limited insight into how they should be incentivised and engaged.
In particular, there is a lack of practical guidance on how shared value creation can act as a
catalyst for accelerating the transition toward CE. This is shown by the frameworks identified in
the literature review which centres the company and not the value chain and its stakeholders.
There is a lack of research into how an ecosystem perspective influence the transition to CE in
the textile industry.
None of the documents identified investigate how companies of different readiness levels can
enable transitioning into CE without being at the same readiness level highlighting another sig­
nificant gap. The consideration of organisational and industry readiness and how it impacts
partnerships and the value different readiness­levels can provide. Many proposed CE strate­
gies and tools assume a uniform ability to adopt circular practices, overlooking differences in
technological, organisational, and cultural readiness. As a result, the applicability and effective­
ness of these solutions may be limited in real­world scenarios.
CE is characterised by the involvement of stakeholder partnerships and collaboration, but the
results from the bibliometric analysis show a clear picture of a general lack of research in col­
laborative efforts, benefits and methods to transition into CE. Of the total of 287 documents
identified across the three search strings only 14 approached these topics, corresponding to
less than 5% of the documents, suggesting more research is needed to fully understand how to
collaborate, which enablers and barriers are present and how partnerships can help accelerate
the transition.
4.3.11
Discussion
The process of conducting the SLR was impacted by both methodological and practical lim­
itations. The first iteration of the review did not yield any relevant documents, resulting in a
compressed timeline for the second iteration. This time constraint affected the comprehensive­
ness of the search strategy and limited the scope of the inclusion criteria, which were applied
only to titles, abstracts, and keywords. Consequently, potentially relevant documents, particu­
larly those with insights buried in the main body of the text, may have been excluded from the
review.
An example of this limitation is seen in the secondary data analysis, which uncovered relevant
studies that had been excluded during the primary review due to the narrow application of the
inclusion criteria. This suggests that some important insights, especially those related to specific
enablers and barriers within frameworks, may not have been adequately captured.
Additionally, based on the author’s familiarity with CE literature from the empirical study, RC
and discussions with supervisors, it is evident that several well­established tools, such as those
found on the ready2LOOP platform, were not identified through the SLR. This raises concerns
about the effectiveness of the search strings and inclusion/exclusion criteria used, and whether
they were appropriately calibrated to capture the full spectrum of relevant literature. Though, a
plausible explanation for this is that many of these tools are primarily published in practitioner­
oriented databases or platforms rather than academic literature. As a result, they are not in­
dexed in the academic databases used for the SLR and thus fall outside the review’s scope.
CHAPTER 4. DESCRIPTIVE STUDY I
42
==PAGE:54==
This also highlights a broader limitation of the SLR methodology: certain perspectives, tools,
and practical approaches relevant to the research questions may exist outside the boundaries of
academic publications. These insights, while valuable, are not captured in traditional literature
reviews and may require alternative methods, such as database mining or expert interviews, to
be fully uncovered.
It is important to acknowledge that the design of the SLR was influenced by prior findings from
the RC and empirical study. The SLR was constructed to support and explore the gaps identified
in these phases, potentially introducing confirmation bias. Had the review been developed in­
dependently of these earlier processes, the research questions, search strategies, and findings
might have differed significantly. While this alignment ensures coherence across the research
phases, it also limits the openness of the SLR to broader or divergent perspectives.
The literature highlights value chain collaboration and data sharing as enablers of CE implemen­
tation. Data sharing improves transparency and enables coordination across stakeholders, par­
ticularly when combined with Industry 4.0 technologies. Moreover, shared data fosters strategic
alignment by enabling stakeholders to co­develop goals, track material flows, and evaluate cir­
cular performance. Despite its importance, a key gap remains in understanding the behavioural
and organisational drivers that motivate data sharing.
Partnerships across the value chain are vital. Collaboration is foundational as most compa­
nies could not implement CE without them. External actors as NGOs, universities, and public
institutions contribute knowledge while customer and consumer involvement is crucial for strate­
gies like product reuse and take­back systems. These partnerships are central to transforming
ecosystems, especially when led by manufacturers acting as orchestrators of change.
However, several barriers hinder these collaborative efforts. A widespread lack of CE aware­
ness and expertise limits the development of circular initiatives. The continued dominance of
linear models and absence of long­term collaboration mechanisms also reduce stakeholder
incentives to engage. Additionally, fragmented or superficial engagement can lead to misalign­
ment and wasted resources. Effective CE transition depends on deep, data­enabled collabo­
ration across the value chain. While enablers such as digital tools, partnerships, and strategic
frameworks are increasingly understood, addressing barriers like low awareness, misaligned
incentives, and limited stakeholder commitment is essential.

By combining findings from the SLR and the prioritisation of dimensions and aspects from

First highlighting the dimension ’Organisation’, specifically the aspects of ’Knowledge & Skills’

circular goals. This led to the following two key requirements: the need for strategic tools that
help link circular strategies to stakeholder value, and the need to strengthen stakeholder dia­
logue by making it easier to understand and communicate each other’s contributions and needs.
Next highlighting, the dimension ’Takeback & End­of­Life Strategies’, and the aspect ’Materials
Recycling’, pointed to challenges in collaboration across post­use loops. Based on both the
SLR findings and the empirical findings, there seemed to be a clear gap in communication and

role clarity between actors like WHITEWEAR and Company A. This supported the third require­
ment being the need for facilitation support to help stakeholders better understand the benefits

Lastly, in the ’Manufacturing & Value Chain’ dimension, where the chosen aspect was Supply

This chapter aimed to contextualise the challenges and opportunities associated with circular
transitions in the work textile industry. It began with a SLR that identified key enablers and

stakeholder needs and circularity challenges vary significantly across different value chain ac­
tors. Together, these inputs informed a set of eight support requirements, emphasising simplic­
ity, stakeholder engagement, and internal usability.

5.3.1
Concept 1: Journey Map
The Journey Map was envisioned as a structured support to guide companies through the early
stages of transitioning to CE. The idea was to create a simple, phase­based framework that
would help users move from understanding their current system to identifying, evaluating, and
implementing circular strategies. The concept aimed to combine considerations of feasibility
and stakeholder value in a format that was easy to use in workshops and did not require quan­
titative input or external facilitation.
Drawing inspiration from the Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010), the pro­
posed journey map would be divided into five phases, each accompanied by example supports
to guide users through key decisions and reflections:
• Phase 1: Understand ­ Map the current value chain, including actors, flows, and pain
points, to build a shared understanding of the system.
• Phase 2: Identift ­ Reflect on stakeholder needs and internal capabilities to generate po­
tential CE strategies.
• Phase 3: Assess ­ Evaluate these strategies on feasibility, technical readiness, economic
potential, stakeholder acceptance, and environmental benefit.
• Phase 4: Decide ­ Select a strategy, define KPIs, assess risks, and outline pilot scope
and stakeholder value.
• Phase 5: Employ ­ Plan implementation steps, including responsibilities, resources, and
partnerships.
5.3.2
Concept 2: Stakeholder Cards
The Stakeholder Cards were proposed as a simple, discussion­based support to explore how
circular strategies might affect various stakeholder groups. The idea was to support the devel­
opment of system­wide solutions by prompting reflection on stakeholder needs, barriers, and
value creation, without relying on quantitative data or tools.

Each card would represent a stakeholder in the value chain (e.g., customers, material suppliers,
logistics partners) and include guiding questions on feasibility and impact. The aim was to help
companies identify risks and opportunities from multiple perspectives and shape implementable
strategies.
5.3.3
Concept 3: Story Board
The Storyboard was imagined as a creative way to help companies explore the potential impact
of transitioning to circularity. The idea was to use visual storytelling to map how a company’s
business model might evolve from linear to circular, with a focus on stakeholder dynamics and
system­wide changes.
Participants would co­create a six­frame story, including elements like the current model, a trig­

and make abstract system changes more tangible.
Although less concrete than other concepts, the storyboard was seen as a promising way to
foster early­stage discussion and creative exploration of stakeholder impacts.
5.3.4
Concept 4: Decision Tree
The Decision Tree was imagined as support to help companies in selecting a suitable circular
strategy. The idea was to guide users through a structured sequence of Yes/No questions based
on qualitative input, gradually narrowing down options from the CE Strategy Scanner.
Each question would focus on stakeholder­related capabilities, constraints, or opportunities.
The branching logic was intended to prompt discussion and help teams make informed, context­
specific decisions without relying on quantitative data.

5.4.1
First Draft
The first draft of the support was shaped by the three selected concepts. The idea was to build
a journey map consisting of five phases:
• Phase 1: Understand
• Phase 2: Identify
• Phase 3: Assess
• Phase 4: Decide
• Phase 5: Employ
Phase 1 aimed to establish an understanding the company’s value chain. This step was meant
to ensure that, before discussing potential strategies, the company had a shared understanding
of its material flows and value creation. The Ecosystem Map from ready2LOOP by Finken et al.
(2013) was selected as the tool to be used as support in this phase.
In phase 2, the company were expected to explore which circular strategies were most relevant
to their context and select two to three strategies for further development, using the Circular
Strategy Scanner from ready2LOOP by Blomsma et al. (2019) as guidance.
Phase 3, focused on assessing the selected strategies from the previous phase using feasibility
cards developed during this study. These cards were organised into four focus areas considered
relevant for evaluating circular strategies:
• Technical Readiness: Evaluated whether the necessary technical conditions and capabil­
ities were in place for successful implementation.
• Stakeholder Acceptance: Considered how likely key stakeholders were to engage with or
adopt the strategy.
• Economic Potential: Assessed whether the strategy created or shared economic value

5.4.2
Analysis of First Draft
After developing the first draft of the final concept, it was important to revisit and evaluate how
well the support aligned with the initial requirements identified during the brainstorming phase.
This reflection revealed that the initial version of the support included too many elements and
steps, spanning across multiple phases of a transmission, which ultimately made it overly com­
plex. As a result, the supports overall purpose became unclear during use.
The evaluation of the first draft highlighted several problematic areas, prompting the need for
significant revisions before moving forward with testing. Despite these challenges, the core
idea of the support was considered unique. It successfully encouraged companies to engage
CHAPTER 5. PRESCRIPTIVE STUDY I
51
==PAGE:63==
in discussions from a stakeholder perspective, which was a key requirement identified in DS­I.
Moreover, it effectively aligned circular strategies with stakeholder value and supported commu­
nication of shared value across the value chain. The support was also well­suited to workshop
settings and designed for use during the early stages of a CE transition.
While the support addressed many of the initial requirements, one major issue stood out: the
structure was unclear, and the overall complexity made it difficult for users to understand and
apply. This became the primary focus for revision.
The following issues were identified in the first draft:
• The support was too complex and difficult to navigate.
• The user benefits of applying the support were not clearly communicated.
• Phases 3 and 4 were not clearly differentiated, making them hard to follow.
• The support attempted to cover too many stages of the transition instead of focusing on
the early stages.
To address these issues, the scoring system was replaced with a simpler approach: each ques­
tion would be answered with a ”yes” or ”no”. A ”no” response would identify a barrier, while a
”yes” would signal an opportunity. In addition, each card would include a suggested next step
on the back to guide the company in overcoming obstacles when the answer was ”no.” This
helped deepen the discussion and made the support more action­oriented.
A second major change involved merging phases 3 and 4. Instead of treating evaluation and
decision­making as separate steps, they were integrated into a single discussion phase. This
simplification made it easier to draw on the decision tree concept while adapting it into a more
intuitive and visual “decision pyramid.”
Based on these reflections, several key adjustments were made to improve the support:
• Phase 5 would be presented as a recommendation rather than an integrated part of the
support.
• Phases 3 and 4 would be combined into a single evaluation and decision phase.
• The scoring system on each card would be removed in favor of ”yes/no” answers.
• The decision tree would be replaced with a simplified ”decision pyramid.”
• The value of each prompt would be made explicitly clear to the user.
• The cards would offer guidance in case of ”no” answers to support progress and address
challenges.
• Each card would clearly indicate the value generated for both the company and relevant
stakeholders.
These revisions were incorporated into the final version of the support, and a high­level mock­
up was created to lay the foundation for testing and further refinement. This mock­up is shown

5.4.4
How to Use: Step­by­Step Guide
The following steps outline how the support was intended to be used in a company workshop
setting. It assumed that the company had already decided to explore CE opportunities and was
now looking for a structured way to assess and choose a suitable strategy.
Step 1: Map the Ecosystem and Identify Circular Strategies
Companies used an ecosystem mapping support by Finken et al. (2013) to visualise stake­
holders, material flows, and value creation opportunities. Then, using the Circular Economy
Business Model Pattern Cards by P. P. Pieroni et al. (2019), they identified 2­3 promising
strategies that could fit their context.
Step 2: Prioritise Evaluation Criteria
Participants ranked the four evaluation categories (Technical Readiness, Economic Potential,
Stakeholder Acceptance, Environmental Benefit) from 1 (most important) to 4 (least important)
based on their strategic priorities.
Step 3: Select Evaluation Questions
Based on the ranking, participants selected 10 feasibility cards:
• 1 card from priority 1
• 2 cards from priority 2
• 3 cards from priority 3
• 4 cards from priority 4
These cards were used to assess each selected strategy.
Step 4: Build the Decision Pyramid
Participants arranged the selected cards into a four­layer pyramid:
• Top layer: 1 card (priority 1)
• Second layer: 2 cards (priority 2)
• Third layer: 3 cards (priority 3)
• Bottom layer: 4 cards (priority 4)
They placed the cards front­up to clearly display the questions.
Step 5: Evaluate Strategies One by One
They went through each strategy using the pyramid. Starting at the top, they answered each
question with “yes” or “no”:
• If all cards in a layer were answered “no”, the strategy was considered unfeasible and
evaluation stopped.
• If at least one “yes” was given, they proceeded to the next layer.
• They turned over “no” cards to reveal the suggested next steps and a summary of the
value of a “yes” answer.
Step 6: Compare and Summarise the Results
Participants recorded the outcomes for each strategy:
CHAPTER 5. PRESCRIPTIVE STUDY I
64
==PAGE:76==
• Total number of “yes” answers
• Any layers with all “no” answers (indicating a major blocker)
• Which categories performed best/worst
• Specific barriers and proposed next steps
They used this scoring to place strategies into one of three categories, that helped them decide
on which strategies to move forward with:
• Go: 7­10 ”yes” answers, no full layer blocked
• Adjust: 4­6 ”yes” answers, manageable barriers
• Stop: 0­3 ”yes” answers or one fully blocked layer
Step 7: Decide and Move Forward
Participants selected the most promising strategy to move into implementation. They used the
roadmap or revisited the back of the “no” cards to refine strategies that needed adjustment.
This structured approach ensured that circular strategies were assessed not only for internal
business value but also for stakeholder alignment and environmental impact, increasing the
likelihood of successful implementation.

The relevance test confirmed that the support’s categories were well­targeted and capable

The support has undergone the first iteration based on insights gathered through testing and

In this chapter the updated version of the support was changed directly from observing how it
functioned in practice. The changes reflected a shift from a simplified decision­making model
to a more realistic, discussion­oriented process that acknowledged the complexity of circular
transitions. Rather than directing companies toward a single ‘right’ strategy, the support facil­
itated a more open­ended discussion, helping users uncover the strengths, uncertainties, and
potential barriers of several strategies. It also aligned more closely with how strategic decisions
were actually made in companies, often requiring C­level engagement, time to reflect, and room
for iteration. Visual clarity, flexibility in responses when going through phase 3, and a stronger
focus on stakeholder perspectives were key to making the support more usable and meaningful.
The adjustments did not change the core purpose of the support, but helped it better serve that
purpose in real settings. Though this was only one iteration, further testing would be needed to
see how well the revised version performed and where it might need to evolve to next.

cular business model innovation and value chain optimisation to achieve a competitive and
sustainable transition to CE. The research demonstrated that this integration is not a straightfor­
ward process, but rather a highly complex and context­dependent transformation. Although the
growing urgency to incorporate CE principles into business practices is widely acknowledged,
the transition is often constrained by fragmented value chains, competing stakeholder interests,
and limited internal capabilities. These challenges are evident in B2B product manufacturing
sectors, such as the work textile industry, where durability requirements and service­based
models introduce additional layers of complexity.
The study’s central contribution, the Circular Feasibility Navigator, was developed in response
to these challenges. However, the thesis also underscored the difficulty of encapsulating the
nature of CE into a single, simple decision support. CE is complex and cannot be fully captured
by static frameworks or assessments. The development process therefore had to balance be­
tween complexity and usability, ensuring the support remained accessible and usable for com­
panies without deep CE expertise. This finding confirmed that while simplification is necessary
for usability, it must be balanced with depth to ensure value.
The research process was structured using the Design Research Methodology (DRM), which
provided a clear framework across problem clarification, descriptive analysis, support ideation,
and prescriptive validation. DRM was particularly helpful in structuring the early phases of the
project and ensuring alignment between identified challenges, support requirements, and final
outcomes. Time constraints limited the number of iterations that could be completed, and future
work would benefit from more iterations of the different stages.
In the RC, the CE concept was defined and distinguished from broader sustainability concepts.
The ready2LOOP readiness assessment was used to evaluate WHITEWEAR’s maturity across
eight CE dimensions. The tool provided a structured overview of strengths and weaknesses,
with WHITEWEAR scoring 45% overall. This revealed organisational motivation but a lack of
knowledge, limited stakeholder engagement, and insufficient clarity in circular strategy planning.
These readiness results proved valuable in shaping the direction of the project. Specifically, the
readiness data led to the selection of three prioritised dimensions, Organisation, Manufacturing
& Value Chain, and Take­back & End­of­Life Strategies, which were used to structure both the
theoretical part and the subsequent design process.
The prioritisation of these dimensions directly informed the scope and structure of the SLR,
which was designed around three focused search strings corresponding to each of the selected
areas. This targeted approach ensured alignment between theoretical exploration and the case
company’s practical needs. The SLR revealed several important theoretical findings that fur­
ther shaped the project. First, it confirmed that many companies had similar barriers in early
CE transitions, such as low internal alignment, insufficient stakeholder engagement, and diffi­
culty prioritising among multiple circular options. Second, it highlighted that most existing CE
supports are focused on diagnosis or quantitative assessment (e.g., lifecycle analyses), with
very few offering structured support for qualitative strategy evaluation. Third, it exposed a gen­
eral lack of supports that integrate stakeholder perspectives into decision­making, despite the
recognised importance of stakeholder alignment in CE transitions.

Together, the empirical insights from WHITEWEAR’s readiness assessment and the theoretical
insights from the SLR provided a foundation for the support development phase. The transi­
tion path designed for WHITEWEAR, which mapped their readiness to specific support needs,
served as a practical framework for the ideation and conceptualisation that followed.
The next phase of the study was the ideation phase, which generated four conceptual sup­
ports: a journey map, stakeholder cards, a strategy storyboard, and a feasibility decision tree.
Each concept was evaluated against two sets of support requirements, the first based on com­
pany needs (e.g., accessibility, workshop usability, non­reliance on quantitative data), and the
second based on academic insights (e.g., support for prioritisation, stakeholder inclusion, and
iterative learning). A combination of several of the four initial concepts evolved into the Circular
Feasibility Navigator, that was developed due to its strong alignment with both sets of criteria.
This design process reflected the core insight that companies need support not in generating
ideas, but in evaluating and prioritising them based on feasibility across multiple dimensions.
Support development was guided by structured brainstorming and grounded in the SLR and
case data. The prompt questions included in the feasibility cards were created through internal
discussions and grounded in themes identified in the SLR, specifically the need for an eval­
uation that includes technical, economic, stakeholder, and environmental perspectives. Each
question was designed to surface potential risks or barriers and was accompanied by guidance
on stakeholder value and potential next steps. This structure aimed to ensure that evaluation
was not only diagnostic, but also solution­oriented. While the process of developing the prompt
questions allowed for focused development, it also highlighted the limitation that each prompt
question in the focus areas, may benefit from dedicated evidence bases and validation. A more
rigorous theoretical foundation for each focus area, possibly supported by its own SLR, could
strengthen the overall robustness of the support.
The support was tested with two companies, WHITEWEAR and EPOS, in workshop settings.
Participants came from different roles, including sustainability, product development, and busi­
ness strategy. The testing confirmed the support’s ability to facilitate strategic discussion, iden­
tify barriers, and structure the evaluation of circular initiatives in a collaborative setting. Par­
ticipants emphasised the intuitive design of the pyramid, the relevance of the questions, and
the value of involving diverse perspectives. However, the limited number of tests and only
one iteration per company constrained the opportunity for extended validation. Additionally, the
number of stakeholders involved beyond the case company was limited. Although an interview
with Company A offered external perspective, broader stakeholder engagement would have
improved alignment with real­world value chain complexity.
The application of the ready2LOOP platform provided a structured diagnostic tool that helped
initiate the CE conversation and identify organisational priorities. However, its high­level results
were not directly actionable without additional supports and guidance. This thesis demonstrated
that combining readiness assessment with a prioritisation framework and a tailored transition
path significantly increased the value of the diagnosis, making it more strategically useful for
the organisation.
In this context, the Circular Feasibility Navigator contributes to both theoretical and empirical
gaps in current CE transition research. Theoretically, it addresses the lack of tools that support
early­stage decision­making while also considering stakeholder perspectives. Many existing
frameworks tend to focus on the company itself and their internal opportunities and goals. In
contrast, this support encourages reflection on other perspectives like the potential value for
stakeholders when transitioning to circular strategies. In doing so, it adds a more nuanced and
practical layer to the growing portfolio of CE implementation supports.

Empirically, the support offers practical value to companies like WHITEWEAR by translating
complex strategic ambitions into actionable discussions. As a micro­sized firm the structured
and accessible format allowed WHITEWEAR to work through complex circular strategies in a
concrete and collaborative way. During testing, the workshop format created space for discus­
sion, alignment, and new insights, and the ’next­step’ suggestions helped to ensure that ‘no’
answers became starting points for further action, rather than dead ends.
Furthermore, the project shows how the DRM can be used to develop CE transition supports
that are both grounded in theory and directly applicable in practice. The structured process
made it possible to move from initial problem framing to a tested, real­world support, while still
leaving room to adapt and improve based on company feedback. It also became clear that
supports like this need to be flexible enough to suit different company sizes, maturity levels,
and value chain positions, something that was only achievable through close collaboration and
iterative development.
In summary, the thesis demonstrated that facilitating CE transitions in the work textile indus­
try requires support that not only structure feasibility assessments, but also support cross­
functional reflection, prioritisation, and stakeholder awareness. The developed Circular Feasi­
bility Navigator contributed to filling a gap in early­stage circular strategy planning and showed
that even in a complex domain such as CE, structured and practical supports can offer meaning­
ful support. Though, future work is needed to scale and validate the support in broader contexts
and through repeated applications.
8.1.1
Limitations & Future Work
While this thesis produced a validated and context­specific support for evaluating circular strate­
gies, its limitations should be acknowledged in relation to both the research process and the
scope of outcomes. These limitations also highlight opportunities for future research and sup­
port development.
The SLR encountered limitations, particularly in identifying comprehensive CE supports through
academic databases. Many relevant supports and frameworks were found in external, non­
academic sources such as industry reports and platforms. This highlights a gap in the academic
dissemination of practical CE supports and suggests the need for future research to map and
evaluate existing resources more systematically, possibly through integrative databases that
combine academic and industry knowledge.
Furthermore, the number of stakeholders engaged during the study was limited, both in terms
of breadth and diversity. Although the support itself emphasises stakeholder value and accep­
tance, external stakeholder involvement, such as end­users, laundries, or recyclers, was not
included in the workshops. While some stakeholder input was gathered through preliminary
interviews (e.g., with Company A), the overall number of interviews conducted was relatively
low. Expanding stakeholder engagement would potentially strengthen the support content.
Another limitation lies in the geographic and industrial scope of the support testing. The support
was tested with only two companies, both operating in Denmark. While this ensured depth of
insight, it limits the generalisability of the findings. Future work should include testing the tool
across a broader range of industries and cultural contexts, to assess its relevance, flexibility,
and impact in diverse circular transition settings.
Additionally, time constraints also significantly shaped the design and execution of the study.
The support development and testing were conducted within a limited project timeline, which
restricted the number of iterations and the extent to which feedback could be incorporated into
multiple design cycles. The support was tested in only one full iteration per company, which,

while sufficient for initial validation, did not allow for longer assessment or ongoing develop­
ment with users. Additional iterations could improve the supports robustness and adaptability
to varied organisational contexts.
To address these limitations and extend the work initiated in this thesis, the following areas are
proposed for future research and development:
• Perform a more elaborate analysis of the content to the prompt questions to ensure rele­
vance.
• Engage a wider range of internal and external stakeholders to validate the supports col­
laborative potential.
• Conduct additional design iterations and testing of the support within organisations.
These efforts would enhance the supports utility, broaden its applicability, and contribute to a
more comprehensive understanding of how decision support can accelerate circular transitions
across varied industrial contexts.
8.2
Conclusion
This thesis addressed the central research question: How can work textile companies integrate
circular business model innovation and value chain optimisation to achieve a competitive and
sustainable transition to Circular Economy? Through the application of DRM, the study devel­
oped and validated a structured evaluation support aimed at supporting early­stage decision­
making for circular strategies in product manufacturing contexts.
The research began by clarifying the core principles of circular economy and distinguishing them
from broader sustainability goals. A readiness assessment using the ready2LOOP platform
identified key barriers and maturity gaps within the case company WHITEWEAR, including lim­
ited internal alignment and unclear prioritisation of circular initiatives. These findings informed
the design of a transition path tailored to the company’s needs and served as a foundation for
ideating support.
Based on a SLR and empirical input from stakeholders, four conceptual supports were devel­
oped. These were combined and further developed into the Circular Feasibility Navigator that
had the capacity to structure strategic reflection and support feasibility assessment. The support
was tested in workshops with two companies and evaluated across dimensions of relevance,
usability, impact, comparison.
The outcomes of the testing confirmed the supports potential to address a critical gap in early­
stage CE transition planning, specifically, the ability to evaluate and prioritise circular strategies
from a stakeholder­informed perspective. The support contributed to increased clarity, internal
alignment, and structured dialogue, enabling companies to reflect on not only technical and
economic feasibility but also stakeholder acceptance and environmental value.
While the scope of the testing was limited, the results indicate that the support is a viable support
mechanism for companies operating in B2B manufacturing sectors. It offers an accessibility
and structure, making it relevant for organisations that are ready to act but require structured
guidance on where and how to begin.
This thesis contributes to the field of circular economy transitions by delivering a validated
support grounded in both academic literature and practical application. It demonstrates that
stakeholder­centered design and feasibility focused evaluation can enhance decision­making
processes during CE transitions, particularly in under explored industrial contexts such as the
work textile sector.

1. Define and describe Circular Economy’s core principles and how they relate to organ­
isational transitions, using the ready2LOOP framework and tools as points of departure.
This objective is fulfilled in chapter 3, where the CE concept is defined in contrast to sustain­
ability, and the butterfly diagram is introduced to explain technical and biological cycles. The
ready2LOOP framework is clearly explained, including its origin in MATChE and its eight­layer
structure. The thesis explores how CE principles apply to product manufacturers and organisa­
tional transitions through assessments and tailored support.
2. Identify and explain key stakeholder needs and expectations during Circular Economy
transitions, focusing on the case company’s industry sector.
Stakeholder needs are explored in chapters 3, 4 and 5, especially through interviews with
WHITEWEAR and Company A. The final support was designed specifically to evaluate circular
strategies from a stakeholder value perspective. The empirical data highlights the importance
of stakeholder alignment in the B2B laundry­textile context.
3. Facilitate the application of a circular readiness assessment, via the ready2LOOP tool
and evaluate the case company’s preparedness for CE adoption, ensuring accurate and
actionable results.
A full readiness assessment using the ready2LOOP platform is conducted with WHITEWEAR
and reported in chapter 3. The process is described step by step, and the results (45% readi­
ness) are visualised and analysed, including benchmarks against similar companies. The anal­
ysis leads to identifying key opportunities for development.
4. Analyse the data gathered from readiness assessments to determine the organisa­
tion’s strengths, weaknesses, and potential areas for improvement in transitioning to
Circular Economy.
The thesis analyse WHITEWEAR’s assessment data, highlighting organisational gaps (e.g.,
in internal processes and CE knowledge) and strengths (e.g., alignment across employees).
These insights are used to prioritise dimensions and create a tailored transition path in chapter
4.

holder input.

gies, and the development of the support. The workshops were structured to promote internal

As industries face increasing pressure to transition from linear to circular business models, compa­
nies often lack the tools to translate circular economy principles into concrete strategies. This thesis
addresses that challenge by developing and testing a stakeholder­centered, decision navigating
support tailored to the needs of product manufacturers in the work textile industry. A business­to­
business focused industry producing textiles for use in healthcare, hospitality, and cleaning services.
Using the Design Research Methodology, the research was structured into five phases, combining
literature review, industry collaboration, support conceptualisation, and empirical testing. The pri­
mary output is the Circular Feasibility Navigator, a workshop­based support that guides companies
through four phases: understanding their stakeholders and value chain, identifying circular strate­
gies, assessing them through stakeholder­focused feasibility questions, and prioritising the most
valuable and implementable ideas.
The support was tested with two Danish companies, WHITEWEAR and EPOS, and evaluated for
relevance, usability, and strategic impact. Results show that the support aids internal alignment,
stimulates critical reflection on circular readiness, and provides a structured pathway for early­stage
circular economy decision­making. By integrating stakeholder perspectives and sector­specific con­
siderations, the support helps bridge the gap between circular ambition and actionable transition in
a complex industrial context.
This thesis contributes to the growing body of research on practical circular economy implemen­
tation tools and offers actionable insights for companies seeking to evaluate and prioritise circular
strategies in their operations.
