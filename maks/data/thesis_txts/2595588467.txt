Abstract
Dysfunctional synovial joints can cause severe and long-lasting medical conditions causing
pain and limiting joint movement. These conditions are often caused by the degradation of
articular cartilage, which has a limited ability to regenerate. Understanding and being able
to model synovial joints is crucial in maintaining functional joints and developing implants to
recuperate dysfunctional joints. To contribute to the understanding of synovial joints and their
modelling this thesis aims to derive a model that combines Ô¨āuid Ô¨Ālm lubrication, as it is present
between non-contacting cartilage surfaces, with the porous Ô¨āuid Ô¨āow and elastic deformations
of articular cartilage. A 2D Ô¨Ānite element model based on Newtonian Ô¨āuid Ô¨Ālm lubrication, a
linear-elastic biphasic porous medium, and a condition for surfaces in contact is derived and
validated. The model can be used to study synovial joint lubrication and the remarkably low
friction within them. It is applied to the lateral condyle of a human knee for conditions during
walking. Results indicate elastohydrodynamic lubrication enforced by joint movement to be an
important factor in reducing friction and wear and bringing nutrients, which contribute to the
regeneration of cartilage, to areas of articular cartilage susceptible to wear.

1
Motivation
The bones in load carrying joints such as knees and hips, are covered by a layer of articular
cartilage. Millions of people worldwide, over 30 million in the USA alone, are aÔ¨Äected by the
disease known as osteoarthritis, which is caused by the degradation of the articular cartilage.
The degraded cartilage increases friction in the joint and causes mild to severe pain. Many
factors aÔ¨Äect the development of osteoarthritis, this includes age, gender, genetics, working
conditions, and sports injuries. However, the prevention and treatment is limited due to the
limited capability of cartilage to regenerate and the lack of understanding of the causes of os-
teoarthritis. The causes of osteoarthritis are often studied by experiments and trials. However,
some aspects in the study of cartilage are not feasible in a physical environment, as the mea-
surement conditions themselves alter the behaviour of the cartilage and thus the results. This
is where numerical modelling comes in as a very powerful tool. It can be used to model con-
ditions which cannot be produced in physical experiments or to test hypotheses based on well
understood physical phenomena which would have been altered in physical experiments. The
beneÔ¨Āts to numerical modelling are numerous, and in combination with physical experiments
may prove to be a vital tool in furthering the understanding of cartilage and osteoarthritis.
When a person suÔ¨Äers from a severe joint injury, or severe osteoarthritis, a partial or full
joint replacement by the means of an implant is a common remedy to recover joint functioning.
The friction in human joints is remarkably low, and for the proper functioning of the joint it
is desirable for implants to mimic this behaviour. This is where numerical modelling becomes
a valuable tool as it can contribute to the development of well-functioning low friction joints.
It also oÔ¨Äers the possibility of furthering the understanding of the lubrication mechanisms in
load carrying joints and possibly applying this knowledge to other areas.

This section provides a description of the type of joint to be modelled as well as the challenges
in modelling it.
2.1
Nature of human synovial joints
Joints are the connection between bones which allows for relative movement.
The ends of
bones which meet in the joint are covered by a layer of articular cartilage, which is diÔ¨Äerent
from structural cartilage, such as found in the nose or ear. The cartilage varies in thickness,
typically in the range of 1 to 4 mm in the hip or knee [1]. In load bearing joints, such as the
hip or knee, a Ô¨āuid is present between the cartilage surfaces, this Ô¨āuid is known as synovial
Ô¨āuid and it lubricates the joint and carries nutrients to the cartilage. The load carrying joints
containing synovial Ô¨āuid are known as synovial joints, such a joint is illustrated in Ô¨Āgure 1.
Cartilage is an avascular tissue, meaning that there are no blood vessels within the cartilage
or reaching the cartilage. Due to its avascular nature the cartilage depends entirely on the
synovial Ô¨āuid for nutrients which it needs to grow, regenerate, and heal if damaged.
The
nutrient transport based on synovial Ô¨āuid is much less eÔ¨Äective than nutrient transport by
blood. However, due to high contact pressures in the synovial joints blood vessels would burst if
they were present in the cartilage. This reliance on synovial Ô¨āuid for nutrient transport severely
limits the ability of cartilage to regenerate when damaged. Thus it is of utmost importance to
keep the cartilage intact under all circumstances.
Cartilage has a complex structure which can be divided into three main zones (see Ô¨Āgure 1):
1) the superÔ¨Ācial zone which plays an important role in boundary lubrication of the cartilage, 2)

that shear stress is one of multiple factors which stimulate the chondrocytes to produce these
enzymes [3]. On a macro-scale this would cause a self-enforcing degeneration of cartilage as
damaged cartilage experiences higher friction and larger shear stresses [1]. From this perspective
a minor damage can rapidly lead to a severe degradation of cartilage. However, on the micro-
scale the shear stress degradation can give the cartilage surface a self-smoothing property, i.e.
the shear stress will be larger at surface perturbation than in the bulk cartilage, thus the
perturbations will degrade faster than the bulk, leaving a smooth surface.

face roughness. This limiting assumption was alleviated when Grubin in 1949 included elastic

surfaces apart and increases squeeze Ô¨Ālm times. The boosted lubrication mechanism has been
illustrated in Ô¨Āgure 2. It should be noted that eÔ¨Äusion and boosted lubrication are not mutually
exclusive, rather they are two diÔ¨Äerent phenomena active under diÔ¨Äerent conditions.

Disagreement about the nature of the lubricating mechanism in joints has led to hydrody-
namic and elastohydrodynamic models being developed in parallel with boundary lubrication
models. Whereas there are those who strictly believe one lubrication mechanism to be dom-
inant in synovial joints, the more general consensus is that lubrication in synovial joints is
a combination of hydrodynamic, elastohydrodynamic, and boundary lubrication mechanisms.
The most signiÔ¨Ācant developments in the theory of synovial joint lubrication are given in a
timeline in Ô¨Āgure 4.

2.3.2
Cartilage
A property of cartilage which has not been mentioned in the previous sections is its inherent
osmotic pressure. The phenomenon is well known from cell biology; an imbalance of solute
concentrations (often of electrolytes) across a selectively permeable cell boundary, which for
the case of a cell is permeable for the solvent (water) but not for the solute.
In the case
of cartilage the osmotic pressure does not originate from a selectively permeable membrane,
rather it originates from large polar molecules being locked and unable to move through the
matrix of collagen Ô¨Ābres. This means that balance in concentrations cannot be achieved by
polar molecules moving through the cartilage, rather the interstitial Ô¨āuid has to move towards
the polar molecules. The osmotic pressure in cartilage has been measured to reach values up to
0.35 MPa [17, Chapter 14]. For reference, the pressure in a synovial joint reaches values around
18 MPa [13]. The contribution of osmotic pressure to the load carrying capacity of cartilage may
be little, but in unloaded areas the osmotic pressure will inÔ¨āuence reabsorption and distribution
of interstitial Ô¨āuid, thus being important to the transport of nutrients. DiÔ¨Äerent multiphase
models have been developed to include the eÔ¨Äects of osmotic pressure when modelling articular
cartilage. However, according to Klika et al. [18] results obtained by adding an electrolyte
phase to a cartilage model with isotropic and linear elastic solid phases in order to include
the osmotic pressure can be directly related to results obtained without regarding the osmotic
pressure, thus deeming the contribution of the osmotic pressure insigniÔ¨Ācant.
In addition to multiphase models much research has been focused on characterising the
anisotropic properties of cartilage; this includes the permeability [19]. A major factor con-
tributing to the anisotropic characteristics is the orientation and concentration of collagen Ô¨Ābres

2.3.3
Synovial Ô¨āuid
The historical perspective revealed lubricin and HA as important constituents of synovial Ô¨āuid.
There has been much disagreement regarding which of these constituents is responsible for the
low friction in the boundary lubrication regime. Schimidt et al. [21] showed that lubricin and
HA have an independent as well as a combined eÔ¨Äect on lowering the friction in cartilage-
cartilage contacts. They found the eÔ¨Äect of phospholipids to be insigniÔ¨Ācant, which contradicts
the results from other studies. The diÔ¨Äerence in results may be caused by the diÔ¨Äerent con-
stituents of the synovial Ô¨āuid acting as boundary lubricants under diÔ¨Äerent conditions. Today
there is a general agreement that the low friction behaviour in the boundary lubrication regime
is only achieved by the combination of lubricin and HA as well as the phospholipids discussed in
section 2.2. However, the nature of the interaction of the constituents and how this contributes
to the remarkably low friction in synovial joints has remained unknown [13].
One characteristic of synovial Ô¨āuid which is well-described and enjoys little disagreement
is its shear thinning property, i.e. the viscosity of the synovial Ô¨āuid decreases as the shear
rate increases.
The shear thinning behaviour of synovial Ô¨āuid is often described as being
pseudoplastic, see Ô¨Āgure 7. The eÔ¨Äect of the shear thinning behaviour of the synovial Ô¨āuid was
described in section 2.3.1.
2.4
Summary of cartilage lubrication and synovial joints
From the initial discussion in section 2.3.1 on the lubrication in synovial joints it has become
clear that three diÔ¨Äerent lubrication regimes contribute to the lubrication of articular cartilage,
from the Stribeck curve in Ô¨Āgure 6 these are: hydrodynamic, elastohydrodynamic, and boundary
lubrication.
In addition to distinct lubrication regimes the synovial Ô¨āuid and the cartilage
possess special properties which contribute to the remarkably low friction in synovial joints. The
8
==PAGE:15==
synovial Ô¨āuid itself is shear thinning, meaning the viscosity decreases as the shear rate increases,
which in turn reduces the hydrodynamic contribution to friction. Additionally the synovial
Ô¨āuid contains diÔ¨Äerent molecules which can hydrate, form protective surface monolayers, and
form lamellar oligolayers acting as boundary lubricants. Likewise, the porosity of cartilage
enables eÔ¨Äusion and ultraÔ¨Āltration which are mechanisms enhancing the lubrication properties
of the synovial joint. Within the porous cartilage there is an intrinsic osmotic pressure which
is low compared to contact pressures, yet signiÔ¨Ācant in unloaded areas. It is well known that
cartilage is an anisotropic material, this has been linked to biological and mechanical properties
of cartilage, this includes the orientation and concentration of collagen Ô¨Ābres, concentration of
chondrocytes, the cells which regenerate cartilage, and the permeability and porosity of the
cartilage.
Despite all advancements in modelling synovial joints and their lubrication the challenge of
posing a model which is accurate under various realistic loading conditions remains. Modelling
eÔ¨Äorts are further hindered by the diÔ¨Éculties which lie in measuring the properties of cartilage
and synovial Ô¨āuid under the various loading conditions of synovial joints.
2.5
Modelling objective
With the nature of synovial joints, the eÔ¨Äorts in their modelling, and motivation for their study
outlined in the previous sections, the objectives of the modelling eÔ¨Äort in the present work
can be outlined. The objective is to develop and validate, conceptually and with analytical
results, a program, which can solve general joint lubrication problems involving a combination
of hydrodynamic, elastohydrodynamic, and boundary lubrication. The aim is to contribute to
the current understanding of synovial joints by providing a model which combines the diÔ¨Äerent
lubrication regimes with the mechanical properties of cartilage. It should be noted that most
literature focuses only on one aspect of the lubrication in synovial joints, commonly it is either
boundary lubrication or EHL. Seemingly, there is a lack in the literature of a model combining
all three lubrication regimes with a representative description of cartilage as a porous medium;
and this is exactly what the model in the present work will do. The next section outlines how
the model is build.

Variational principle
The variational principle is the principle of Ô¨Ānding functions which yield the extremes of quanti-
ties which depend on these functions. Wikipedia [26] provides a good example of the principle:
consider a chain suspended at both ends, what is the shape of the chain? According to the
variational principle the shape of the chain (a function) must minimise (an extreme) the grav-

a FE formulation of the problem. Alternatively, a Ô¨Ānite diÔ¨Äerence or Ô¨Ānite volume approach
could have been used to solve Reynolds equation numerically. The FE formulation has been
chosen since it eases the coupling of Reynolds equation to the equations governing the cartilage,
which is also modelled using the FE method.

method. The FE formulation which is based on the variational method relies on a functional,
which in general terms is a function which takes functions as an input and returns a real

7.1
Biphasic model
The biphasic model presented in this section is based on Biot‚Äôs [32] model for a Representative
Volume Element (RVE) of a porous medium consisting of a solid phase, commonly referred to
as matrix or skeleton, and a Ô¨āuid phase. Biot‚Äôs model assumes that the RVE is large compared
to the pore size, so that it can reasonably be characterised as homogenous, yet small compared
to the scale of macroscopic phenomena, so that it can be treated as being inÔ¨Ānitesimal.
In addition to material properties the biphasic material is characterised by volume fractions
of the two phases. The sum of the volume fractions must equal 1:
ŌÜ + ns = 1
(7.1)
Where ns is the solid phase volume fraction and ŌÜ is the Ô¨āuid phase volume fraction. For a
porous medium the Ô¨āuid phase volume fraction is often referred to as the porosity. By using
equation (7.1) a biphasic porous medium can be described using only one of the two volume
fractions, in this case the porosity ŌÜ.
7.1.1
Constitutive relation
Two states of the porous medium are introduced: the drained and the undrained state. When
a porous medium is drained it has an open boundary where the Ô¨āuid from the pores can freely
Ô¨āow through.
Moreover, the pressure in the pores is neutral (p = 0) so that any load is
carried only by the skeleton of the porous medium. In contrast, an undrained porous medium
has closed boundaries where no Ô¨āuid can pass through, in this case the pore Ô¨āuid pressure
contributes to the load carrying capacity of the porous medium. The drained and undrained
properties of the porous medium are interrelated. Thus, either one or a combination of the
two, can be chosen as a basis for the constitutive relations. The most common approach in
the literature is to choose the properties of the drained porous medium for the constitutive
relation; for consistency this approach has also been adopted here. The undrained properties
and their relation to the drained properties are included in a later section when the FE model
is validated by comparing results with an analytical solution.
For an RVE there are two contributions to the deformation, these contributions will be
referred to as external and internal. The external contribution is the averaged total stress
acting on the RVE, for a homogenous solid this is simply equivalent to the stress state in
the material. The internal contribution comes from the the pressure in the pores within the
RVE. Assuming that the porous medium is linear elastic the total strain can be determined
by superposition of the external and internal strain contributions, the concept is illustrated
with stresses for an incompressible matrix material in Ô¨Āgure 15. The internal contribution from
the pore pressure is elaborated by Ô¨Āgure 16. Note that this contribution is independent of the
porous structure being open or closed as long as the pressures within the pores of the RVE are

The traction at the interface can be split into two contributions: shear and normal load. The
the normal load is equivalent to the corresponding case covered for the EHL model in section

remarkably low, which is a good argument for neglecting a shear coupling term. Further, the
shear coupling term would require special treatment as the Ô¨āuid Ô¨Ālm becomes very thin and the
surfaces engage in contact. It should also be noted that the shear stress cannot be determined
as for the EHL model, since the no-slip condition is not adequate at a porous boundary [34, 35].
Thus the shear coupling term is neglected.

cartilage being homogeneous and isotropic, depend on the loading and measurement techniques
used to determine them. In the literature the most common approach of determining elastic
parameters of cartilage is through compression tests. These tests are often carried out with
small deformations where the cartilage can be assumed to behave as a linear elastic material,
however, under realistic loading conditions deformations are no longer small. Ideally, the model
would be able to take this non-linear behaviour of the cartilage into account, but in its current
form it cannot. A diÔ¨Äerent and less common approach to measuring the elastic properties of
cartilage is through a shear test. However, by the imaging technique applied by Wong et al. [40]
the variation in shear modulus across the thickness of the cartilage can be obtained. Results by
Wong et al. show that the shear modulus varies from 0.1 MPa in the superÔ¨Ācial layer to about

1 MPa is a rough average of the shear modulus. This yields a Young‚Äôs modulus of 2.5 MPa for a

also apply small shear deformations to satisfy the assumption of small strains. For realistic

seen in Maier et al. [44] for osteoarthritic cartilage. The model is not designed to include
strain dependent elastic properties, thus a Ô¨Āxed value has to be chosen. A shear modulus of

11.4.2.2
Transport of nutrients and the velocity with the cartilage
Tracing particles in the cartilage to study the transport of nutrients in the cartilage is out of
scope since only one gait cycle has been modelled. Velocities in the cartilage are extremely low
due its low permeability. The maximum velocity reached within the cartilage during the gait
cycle of Ô¨Āgure 30 is 0.14 mm/s. For a particle to travel a distance equivalent to the thickness
of the cartilage by the maximum velocity would thus require 17 gait cycles. It should be noted
that the high velocities occur close to the contact surface, within the bulk of the cartilage
the velocities are much lower. Surface wear will also be greatest in these areas. Fortunately,
synovial Ô¨āuid is forced from the synovial cavity into the cartilage in these areas due to the
condyle movement drawing Ô¨āuid into the contacting areas. This means that the Ô¨Ārst place where
synovial Ô¨āuid enters the cartilage is in the contact area where the highest wear is expected.
Nutrients, which are needed by the cartilage, are present in the synovial which is produced in
a membrane encapsulating the synovial joint. The synovial Ô¨āuid entering the cartilage in areas
susceptible to wear (the contact areas), due to the movement of the condyle drawing in the
synovial Ô¨āuid, may thus play a key role in maintaining cartilage.

Discussion
Numerous small discussions have been included in the previous sections. Rather than sum-
marising these, this discussion focusses on the model as a whole, the assumptions made to
obtain it, and the results which it has produced.
Numerous assumptions have been made, some better than other, in the modelling process
due to the complex physical nature of synovial joints. The six most signiÔ¨Ācant assumptions
are:
1. Small strains
2. Linear elasticity
3. Isotropy
4. Homogeneity
5. Newtonian synovial Ô¨āuid
6. Negligible cartilage-Ô¨āuid Ô¨Ālm shear forces interaction
Considering the results from the lateral knee condyle the bulk compression of the cartilage
was at the order of 10 %, which arguably leads to strains which can no longer be considered
small enough to apply a constitutive model assuming inÔ¨Ānitesimal strains. The foundation
for linear elasticity are the assumptions of: 1) small strains and 2) a linear relation between
stresses and strains. As stated, the Ô¨Ārst is not the case for cartilage under realistic loading
conditions, and the second is not the case for most biological tissues, especially not under large
strains [44]. However, the assumption of linear elasticity seems to be a widespread practice
in the literature; stretching the challenge of assessing and modelling the non-linear elastic
behaviour of cartilage.
Assessing the non-linear behaviour is especially diÔ¨Écult due to the
inhomogeneous and anisotropic nature of cartilage, which is in direct contrast to the third
and fourth assumption. However, models taking anisotropy and inhomogeneity of cartilage
into account are numerous whereas models dealing with large strains and non-linear elastic
behaviour of cartilage are more scarce, one very recent model may be found in [20].
In section 2 the non-Newtonian behaviour of synovial Ô¨āuid was outlined, this directly opposes
the assumption of a Newtonian behaviour [29]. As synovial Ô¨āuid is shear thinning, implementing
a non-Newtonian viscosity may impact the EHL observed for the lateral knee condyle. Since
the shear rate increases as the Ô¨Ālm thickness decreases, contact may be established and retained
in a higher degree than indicated by the results for the lateral knee condyle, thus reducing EHL
and increasing boundary lubrication. However, in EHL the Ô¨Ālm thickness is very small, and
the contact Ô¨Ālm thickness was chosen to be at the magnitude of the thin Ô¨Ālm of ultraÔ¨Āltrate
which is generated upon absorption of synovial Ô¨āuid at the cartilage surface. This ultraÔ¨Āltrate
has a higher viscosity than unaltered synovial Ô¨āuid, thus reducing the degree of contact which
would be expected. Both eÔ¨Äects, ultraÔ¨Āltration and shear thinning, are not included the model.
However, for EHL at very small Ô¨Ālm thickness values, the eÔ¨Äect of ultraÔ¨Āltration may be
dominant, thus supporting the theory of EHL reducing contact in the joint. It should be noted
that boundary slip, which may reduce the eÔ¨Äect of condyle movement, is not included in the
model, and may play a role at very small Ô¨Ālm thickness values. This is related to the sixth and
last assumption above, that of negligible shear forces in the contact and Ô¨āuid Ô¨Ālm. It is not
as poor an assumption as the others since it can be backed up by a physical behaviour of the
synovial joint; a remarkably low friction. With Ô¨Āve out of six assumptions clearly being poor
in regard to realistic conditions found in synovial joints, results obtained using the model only
serves to give an indication of the actual lubrication behaviour in synovial joints.

Regarding the numerics, the model is open for many improvements. The results in the
previous section were obtained with a very coarse mesh due to many Newton-Raphson iterations
needed for convergence and small time steps required for stability. Having to apply a coarse
mesh to model a gait cycle further reduces the quality of results. Additionally it limits the
application of the model itself. This limiting behaviour should addressed before alleviating any
of the assumptions used to obtain the model, so that the model becomes more tractable. One
advantage of the current implementation is that a stabilisation scheme was already available,
this needs to be taken into account if the elements or temporal discretisation are changed.
In summary, the model is inadequate for any realistic application, however, the physical
couplings and principles which it is build on provide a good foundation for more advanced
models. Results obtained by the model may also be indicative of important eÔ¨Äects which need
to be considered when modelling a synovial joint.
13
Conclusion
From current knowledge of synovial joint lubrication and the results obtained using the model
derived in previous sections, the following conclusions can be made:
‚ÄĘ Large strains, non-linear constitutive relations, anisotropy, inhomogeneity, and the non-
Newtonian properties of synovial Ô¨āuid are important factors which need to be taken into
account when modelling a synovial joint under realistic loading conditions.
‚ÄĘ There is a seeming lack of models applying non-linear constitutive relations to model
cartilage.
‚ÄĘ The permeability of cartilage is low, which causes very low Ô¨āuid velocities within the
cartilage, yet suÔ¨Éciently large to allow for boundary contact to be engaged as well as
disengaged.
‚ÄĘ Results obtained for the gait cycle with a rigid implant in the lateral knee condyle show:
‚Äď the load carried by the solid phase of the cartilage to be reduced by the condyle
movement, which may reduce wear.
‚Äď elastohydrodynamic lubrication to be a key factor in reducing both friction and wear
during walking.
‚Äď elastohydrodynamic lubrication enforced by the movement of the condyle to be an
important factor in bringing nutrients to areas at risk of wear.
‚Äď little eÔ¨Äect of condyle movement on the pressure distribution within the cartilage.
14
Future work
In its current form the model is not capable of modelling a synovial joint under realistic con-
ditions. As pointed out in the discussion, in order to model a synovial joint under realistic
conditions the model needs to take non-Newtonian Ô¨āuid properties, inhomogeneity, anisotropy,
and large strains with non-linear elasticity into account. However, this might not be suÔ¨Écient
to obtain an accurate description. Other conditions such as boundary slip, ultraÔ¨Āltration, shear
forces on the cartilage, osmotic pressure, and boundary lubrication mechanisms are minor fac-
tors compared to those listed above, but may be signiÔ¨Ācant in obtaining an accurate model.
Even if all these properties are taken into account, obtaining the required physical parameters
may prove to be a limiting factor due to the inherent diÔ¨Éculty in their measurement.

Implementing the above is an ambitious task. However, as a starting point, the model could
be extended to include shear thinning synovial Ô¨āuid. This would contribute to gaining further
insights in the eÔ¨Äects of EHL in the friction, wear, and transport of nutrients in cartilage.
