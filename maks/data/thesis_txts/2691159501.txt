Abstract
Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) is a promising non toxic earth abundant alternative photo absorbing
material used in thin ﬁlm solar cells. However, with the current architecture, the n-type buﬀer
layer consists of the toxic material cadmium sulﬁde (CdS). It would therefore by advantageous
to ﬁnd a good substitute for CdS, both because of the toxicity, but also because it has been
shown that the conduction band alignment between CdS and CZTS is non ideal, leading to a
decrease in Voc and ﬁll factor.
In this thesis, a potential substitute using the alloy (Zn1−x, Mgx) O (ZMO) is tested. By
using an alloy, the band gap can be tuned and since the primary change is because of movement
of the conduction band, this tuning can be used to get the optimal oﬀset where the conduction
band of ZMO is between 0 and 0.4 eV above that of CZTS.
To test this, the existing CdS layer was ﬁrst optimised. First by testing the eﬀect of depo-
sition time and then by testing the eﬀect of deposition temperature in a range from 60 °C to
80 °C. It was found that an improvement of 28 % could be achieved by increasing the depo-
sition temperature from 70 °C to 75 °C while lowering the deposition time from 4:30 to 2:00 min.
With this optimised CdS to compare to, ZMO was tested. Since ZMO was deposited using
a single cathode sputtering system, the eﬀect of Al2O3 as sputter protection was tested, but it
was found to have a detrimental eﬀect on the cells. Furthermore, was it found that ZMO with
10 % Mg concentration yielded the best results with an improvement from the reference CdS
cell of 86 %. It is further postulated that 10 % Mg concentration is close to the upper level of
0.4 eV in conduction band alignment, since a diﬀerent CZTS showed a 50 % deterioration of the
eﬃciency and because 15 % Mg concentration showed signiﬁcant deterioration of the Jsc and ﬁll
factor in line with the theory of a conduction band oﬀset above 0.4 eV.
It’s concluded that ZMO is an interesting material for a potential substitution of CdS but that
more testing is needed to fully understand how thickness, composition of ZMO and composition
of CZTS interact with each other and aﬀect the resulting eﬃciency of the solar cell.

1.1
Is there a need for alternatives to fossil fuel?
That the increase in global mean temperature is strongly correlated with the increased emission
of greenhouse gasses is a proven fact. Although the temperature anomaly experienced now, is by
no means unique in the Earth’s history in terms of size, it is the rate of change that are much more
rapid than previously observed. Where temperature increases 4-7 degree Celsius after an ice age is
relatively common, they also happen over a time frame of around 5000 years. [1]
This is in concerning contrast to the 1.18 degree increase in temperature since 1880 where the global
temperature has been recorded. Making the rate of change nearly 10 times higher than previous
estimated maximum. Looking only at the time period from 1880 and forward, it is clear that the
temperature anomalies tend to be positive in the last half of this period, the last 20 years has
yielded signiﬁcantly higher temperatures, compared to the rest of the period, with 19 of the highest
recorded values [2].
But, although the increase observed now is signiﬁcantly higher than what has previously been
observed, is it necessarily because of our actions?
Increase in the Earth’s mean temperature can be caused by a number of diﬀerent phenomena,
including the suns natural changes in power output. When looking at the data, a running average of
solar power and earth temperature is normally used to visualise the underlying trend and reduce the
eﬀect of yearly ﬂuctuations, and although the average of solar power output and Earth temperature
seems to correlate in the ﬁrst half of the recorded history from 1880 up to 1960, the last 60 years,
the temperature has not been following the overall descending slope of the suns power output [3].
Therefore, something else is needed to explain the increase in temperature, and here the histor-
ical data of CO2 concentration in the atmosphere together with the Earth’s change in temperature
from present values gives insight in the link between the two. As seen in ﬁgure 1 there seem to be
a clear correlation between the two. However, it remains diﬃcult to explain what the cause is and
what the eﬀect between the two is.

1.3
Why not keep using silicon?
Although silicon based technologies are dominating, there are some inherent advantages that
thin ﬁlm technologies can utilize to compete with the standard mono or poly crystalline silicon cells.
As the name suggests, thin ﬁlm technologies are signiﬁcantly thinner than conventional silicon
cells, and therefore use less material. This in itself is not a problem for silicon to use more material,
since silicon is the second most earth abundant element in the earth crust [11] so it is not a problem
that the cells are thicker. However, with the use of more material a signiﬁcant higher energy cost
is attributed, leading to a energy payback time for the panels that are nearly double that of thin
ﬁlm technologies [12].
Another potential advantage is that most thin ﬁlm material have a lower temperature coeﬃcient
for their power output, making them more ideal for deployment in warmer climates than the con-
ventional silicon solar cell. [13]
A ﬁnal point for thin ﬁlm technologies is the possibility for lightweight and ﬂexible cells, opening up
new markets, such as mobile applications and deposition directly on top of excising surfaces such
as curved roof tiles. [14] Thereby ensuring that thin ﬁlm solar cells does not need to be in direct
competition with the crystalline silicon industry.
Within the types of thin ﬁlm solar cells, a number of diﬀerent types already exists. As shown
before CdTe-based solar cells already have around 6 % of the market and amorphous silicon and
CIGS (Cu(In, Ga)S2)-based solar cells are also on the market. Together with this, Gallium Arsenic
(GaAs) cells also have commercial applications. [15]

1.4
The CZTS solar cell
The rare element problem can be solved by replacing In and Ga with Sn and Zn in the CIGS
solar cell, thereby, creating the p-type photo absorber Cu2ZnSn(S, Se)4 (CZTS). CZTS was ﬁrst
proven to have photovoltaic eﬀect in 1988 [17]. Since then, other researchers have increased the
eﬃciency from 0.66 % in 1996 to 12.7 %, the current eﬃciency record. This relatively low eﬃciency
gives potential for further improvement of the technology, where both work in light management and
carrier management needs to be carried out to be compatible with existing commercial technologies
[18].
Since CZTS is a quaternary material, the control of the composition is of vital importance for
the performance. Especially the ratio between the three metals Cu, Zn, Sn is vital. As shown in
ﬁgure 5, Cu/Sn should be closely around 1.9 and with steep drop as soon as the ratio get outside of
a narrow range from 1.7 to 2.0. The same is true for the Zn/Sn ratio around 1.2. This means that
although the chemical formula is Cu2ZnSn(S, Se)4, the ideal composition should be Zn rich and Cu
poor compared to the given formula [19]. For this work the precursor composition is therefore, tried
to be kept as much the same as possibly between diﬀerent pre cursors.

Theory
3.1
The hetero junction
With the proposed structure, the P-N junction is consisting of two diﬀerent semiconductor
materials, either CZTS/CdS or CZTS/ZMO. Therefore the structure is a hetero junction, instead
of the homo junction known from crystalline silicon solar cells.
When working with hetero junctions, there is two diﬀerent types. Either isotype or anisotype junc-
tion depending on the doping. In the isotype junction the doping of both sides are the same, either n
or p type and for the anisotype junction the doping are diﬀerent in the two semiconductors making
up the junction. Because diﬀerent materials are used, the variation in band gap, reﬂective index
and electronic properties increases the ﬂexibility in designing semiconductor systems, including the
design and tuning of electrical and optical properties of solar cells. [26]
For usage in solar cells, it is the anisotype junction there is of interest, since it will exhibit current
rectiﬁcation and non linear voltage depending capacitance, like the p-n homo junctions.
Apart from the doping classiﬁcation, hetero junctions are also classiﬁed based on the alignment of
the conduction and valance band between the two semiconductors. Since diﬀerent materials are
used, the band gap of the two semiconductors are often diﬀerent, meaning that the alignment of
the conduction band minimum Ec and valance band maximum Ev is not at the same energy levels
giving raise to the band oﬀsets often exhibited in hetero junction systems. Here ∆Ec and ∆Ev
denotes the oﬀset in the conduction band and valance band respectively. From this, the diﬀerence
in band gap can be calculated as∆Ec + ∆Ev = ∆Eg.
Hetero junctions are classiﬁed based on the sign and magnitude of the band oﬀsets. The sign con-
vention is based on type I hetero junction shown in ﬁgure 7a that are called a nested hetero junction.
Here the entire band gap of one of the semiconductors are inside the band gap of the second. With
this structure both ∆Ec and ∆Ev are deﬁned as positive. For this structure, both electrons and
holes might be trapped inside the material with the narrow band gap material.

For the use to single junction solar cells, only type I and type II is of interest, since here the

In order to calculate this band oﬀsets, traditional theory is generally not enough, since these
calculations tends to be based on inﬁnite crystals, and therefore do not take the interface between
the diﬀerent semiconductors into account. In order to actually calculate band oﬀsets, the most
accurate methods utilize computational intensive ab initio density-function calculations, to analyse
a super lattice consisting of alternating slaps of each material.

3.1.4
The optimal band alignment for hetero junction
With the equations for the current, bias voltage and depletion width in the hetero junction, the
question remains: what should the alignment of the hetero junction be, for optimal performance in
a solar cell?
Minemoto has done a theoretical analysis of the eﬀect of conduction band oﬀset on the perfor-
mance of a CIGS solar cell [30].
In the analysis, the system consists of a n-type high band gap window layer, and thin (20
nm) n-type ordered vacancy compound (OVC) and ﬁnally the p-type CIGS layer, the p-n junction
studied is therefore formed between the n-type window layer and the p-type CIGS. The alignment
between the conduction band of the CIGS and window layer are changed by changing the electron
aﬃnity of the window layer. The analysed system is shown in ﬁgure 10. Here it shows that a
positive conduction band oﬀset will make the solar cell into a type I hetero junction, where the
photo absorber (CIGS) is nested inside the window layer. A small negative conduction band oﬀset
makes it into a type II hetero junction.

4.1.2.1
Chemical deposition of CdS
In the chemical bath deposition of CdS, solid matter is deposited, meaning that the solution
must be thermodynamicaly unstable, such that material precipitate out of the liquid. This can
generally happen in one of two ways: either homogeneous nucleation where particles are formed in
the liquid, or heterogeneous nucleation, where the solid phase is deposited on all surfaces. For this
application, the heterogeneous precipitation is relevant since the goal is to form a thin ﬁlm on the
entire surface of the samples.
Both homogeneous and heterogeneous nucleation will play a roll in the deposition of thin ﬁlms.
The heterogeneous nucleation gives an ion by ion deposition where the S2−and Cd2+ ions condense
on the surface to grow the ﬁlm. Another path for the ﬁlm formation is with homogeneous nucle-
ation, where agglomeration of small particles is formed in the solution and then absorbed on the
surface.
Both reactions is happening and which one is dominating is depending on the exact conditions
for homogeneous versus heterogeneous nucleation. Either way, the creation of cadmium hydroxide
species is fundamental for the growth of adherent ﬁlms. [32].

4.1.3.1
RF magnetron sputtering
For this work, ZMO will be sputtered using a single cathode RF magnetron sputtering system.
Sputtering is a technique widely used in the semiconductor industry because of it versatility and
ability to deposit most materials. It can be done in a number of diﬀerent ways depending on what
type of material is needed and how thick a layer is desirable.
Fundamentally, all sputtering systems works in the same way: the substrate or sample is placed
in a vacuum chamber. For the single cathode system, the cathode and substrates are usually placed
parallel to each other. The target is placed on the cathode, containing the desired material to
deposit. When the chamber is pumped down to an appropriate vacuum level, an inert gas, often
Argon, is introduced to the chamber. With the Argon in the chamber, a negative charge is applied to
the cathode, this forces free electrons to ﬂow from the cathode and collide with the outer electronic
shell of the Argon case. Knocking these outer electrons out, the gas is ionising and the plasma is
ignited. The positively charged ions will then be attracted to the negatively charged cathode and,
at high speed, bombard the surface of the target, where they knock oﬀatomic sized particles due to
the momentum of the collisions. The atomic sized target particles then travel across the chamber
and are deposited as a thin ﬁlm on the surface of the substrate. In ﬁgure 21, a schematic of how
the sputtering system works is shown in ﬁgure 21.

For this application, the system utilizes a magnet behind the cathode. This aﬀect the charged

In a magnetron sputtering system, electrons
are trapped over the negatively charged cathode ensuring that they are not free to bombard the
substrate, ensuring a faster deposition rate. Since very thin ﬁlm is not of interest, the increased
deposition rate is preferred. [42]
On top of the magnetron sputtering system, radio frequency (RF) sputtering is here used. This
is done because ZMO is not a good conductor and therefore a DC system would lead to charge
build up on the surface, limiting the sputtering rate signiﬁcantly. This does not happen when using
RF systems. The reason for this, is that with the high frequency change of current, the build up of
electrons on the surface which happens in the positive half cycle of the wave is counteracted in the
negative half cycle, thereby leading to a net charge build up of zero.
Since the ionized gas is so much heavier than the electrons, the eﬀect of the high frequency
change in potential does not eﬀect the gas as much and they will therefore still bombard the target,
leading to the depositions [43].

4.1.3.3
Sputtering of ZMO
One potential problem with using sputtering as the deposition technique for creating the ZMO
layer, is surface damage to CZTS known as sputter damage. Sputter damage is when highly ener-
getic particles bombard the surface of the substrate, potentially destroying the surface passivisation
[49] applied through etching. This has been reported as an issue in [44] where a thin 10 nm layer
of CdS was introduced as sputter protection. However, since the goal is to not use CdS at all, this
method of sputter protection is not applied.
Instead Al2O3 is tried as sputter protection, with diﬀerent thicknesses, to see if it has a positive
eﬀect. Furthermore, there will be used generally milder conditions than in [44] for the sputtering.
The general procedure for sputtering of ZMO used for this work is detailed below
1. Pre-sputter for 10 min, at 20 mtorr, 20 Watt with a ramp time of 100 s.
2. Close down the plasma
3. Sputter at 20 mtorr, 20 Watt with a ramp time of 150 s.
4. The shutter is removed as soon as the plasma ignites and pressure is regulated.
5. Sputter for the desired time.
6. Move the shutter in front of substrate and close down plasma.
The pre-sputtering is done in order to clean the surface of the target. This ensures that there
should not be any contamination in the actual sputtering on the substrate.

The plasma is closed down and re-ignited in order to start the deposition at as low a power as

simulator is used to simulate the AM1.5G spectrum at STC. A Keithley 2400 is used to measure
the voltage and current at diﬀerent loads. Before measurements on the CZTS cells are carried out,
a calibration using a reference Si solar cell is done for each new series of measurements. In order to
calibrate the measurements with respect to area afterwards, the measurements are always carried
out in the same order with respect to the indium front contact that are soldered on in one side of
the piece. The area of each cell is done by taking pictures of the cells and using ImageJ software
to ﬁnd the area. Since each pieces yields up to 15 measuring points for the IV measurements, the
results are plotted in a box plot which is presented later.

5.1.1.3
Discussion of thickness study
Looking at the results from ﬁgure 36, the found optimum of a deposition time of 4:15 min
equivalent to a thickness of 84 ± 13 nm is a bit higher than what was found in the literature [35]
where a optimum around 60-70 nm was found. The similar optimum is mostly due to the same
factors with optimum FF and Voc on our cells around 4:15, but where [35] also found a Jsc optimum
around 70 nm, there is no clear optimum in the over all data from this investigation for the Jsc. The
tendency for Jsc seem to be that the thinner samples exhibit a slightly higher Jsc, although this is
not the case for every batch and only shows in the combined results. However, an increase in Jsc is
found by the data from the EQE where the thinnest samples also showed the highest Jsc, even for a
series where this is not the case from the JV measurements. Furthermore, the Jsc from the EQE is
generally lower than that from the JV measurements. This mismatch was also found in [60] where
they found a consistent higher Jsc from JV measurements than from EQE for perovskite solar cells.
Looking at the optical properties of CdS, [36] found that the band gap would decrease with
increased thickness up to 700 nm. This was also found for the absorption measurements, that the
band gap decreased with thickness as shown on ﬁgure 32. However the found band gaps from EQE
Page 44 of 74
==PAGE:48==

CdS
as shown in table 1, did not show this behavior and was also found to be on average 0.11 eV higher
from the EQE estimates than the absorption estimated. One explanation for this, could be the
heating that is used to deposit AZO that recrystallise the CdS, but [36] found that post annealing
decreased the band gap and own investigations with post annealing have also shown a decreased
band gap energy after annealing in air. However [61] found that annealing increases the band gap of
magnetron sputtered CdS. Another explanation could then be that the growth of CdS on SLG glass
and CZTS is diﬀerent and that this diﬀerence is aﬀecting the band gap of the material. Others have
reported that the substrate can inﬂuence the band gap of CdS with up to 2 % [61]. However the
diﬀerences found here are between 2 and 7 %. [61] did not deposit using CBD but with magnetron
sputtering and it is therefore not known if the same diﬀerence would be seen in CdS deposited using
CBD. This work indicates that the band gap is higher for CdS when deposited on CZTS than on
SLG, but if the increase is because of diﬀerent growth mechanisms leading to increased band gap
on CZTS or if it is a result of the subsequent processes is not known.

Discussion of temperature study
Based on the results of the temperature study, it is found that temperatures, above 70 °C show
an improved eﬃciency as compared to the lower temperatures. Especially 75 °C for 2:00 min showed
an signiﬁcant improvement of 28% compared to the 70 °C reference. Furthermore increasing the
temperature to 80 °C showed similar results to 75 °C but with a wider range of depositions times
from 1:20 to 2:00 min, yielding comparable results. However, other series show that at 80 °C the
optimal time was much more narrow. This variation is largely attributed to the controllability of
the process at high temperatures, where the temperature of the chemicals have a higher impact on
the deposition temperature, and combined with the shorter depositions time. The actual deposition
temperature is not stable and therefore harder to control. This is also the problem at 75 °C but not
to the same extend.
The problems with controlling the process at 80 °C is also the reason why higher temperatures
have not been tested, since it is expected that control will only become harder to maintain as the
temperature is increased.
Since no comprehensive study of the eﬀect of the deposition temperature has been found, com-
parison with literature is not possible. However both temperature series presented here, shows the
same trend and it is largely do to the increase in Voc and Jsc as documented from the JV plots
that the eﬃciency at higher temperatures is improved. However, the improved Jsc seen in the JV
measurements, is not as clearly shown from the EQE where 75 °C deposition has the highest Jsc for
both series.
Especially the drop in Jsc between 75 °C and 80 °C is signiﬁcant. This can be explained from
two diﬀerent things. When looking at the highest Jsc in ﬁgure 41 the highest value is found at 80
°C with a deposition time of 1:40 min. This is however not the deposition time with the highest
eﬃciency and therefore not the cell used for the EQE comparison. The cell actual used does have a
decreased Jsc compared to the best cells at 75 °C. Furthermore, where the Jsc from the JV curves
are area corrected, it is assumed in the EQE that the entirety of the laser light is hitting the surface
of the cell. This is also the case for most cells, but not necessarily always. If the entirety of the
laser light does not ﬁt on the surface of the cell in the EQE the values will be decreased across all
wavelengths, leading to a signiﬁcant decrease in the calculated Jsc.
That Jsc improves with temperature, can at least partially be understood when looking at the
absorption spectrum where the absorption across all wavelengths are signiﬁcantly reduced for 75 °C
and 80 °C. Which is also evident from the EQE, where the parasitic absorption at low wavelengths
is greatly reduced, which is seen as an improvement in the EQE.
Another explanation for the improved η and Jsc could be an increase in band gap of CdS when
the deposition temperature increases, as was reported in both [39, 40]. The results from the ab-

thickness increases the band gap both eﬀects could potentially play a roll. Unfortunately few data
points with the same thickness have been obtained, and those that are showing similar band gap
energies for the most part.

depositions is started at 2-3 W instead of starting out at the full 20 W that the majority of the
deposition is carried out under. These conditions are also milder than the conditions used in [49]
where sputter damage was reported.

From the thickness study it seems like a deposition time around 60 min is preferable and will
therefore be used for the last 5% target tested.

preliminary result because if 10 % Mg concentration would give a near perfect 0 eV conduction

give good results, which is not the case. Looking at the results for 15 % it looks like especially
the FF and the Jsc is limited, which could indicate that it is close to the 0.4 eV conduction band
oﬀset that would decrease both of these values quickly [30]. However, if it is the case that 15 %
is close to a positive oﬀset of 0.4 eV, and assuming that the small decrease in band gap which is
found in the 10 % Mg concentration is enough to be on the safe side, then 5 % Mg concentration
should also perform to at least the same level as 10 % Mg concentration. However this is also not
the case. However, a single repeat of the experiment for the 10 % Mg concentration was performed

by ZMO, since a thicker layer increases the eﬃciency. However looking at the SEM cross section,
the surface of CZTS does not seem to be signiﬁcantly rougher on the 0423c compared to the 0423d
sample. Therefore another explanation is needed.

6
Conclusion
In this thesis, the goal was to test if (Zn,Mg)O alloy could substitute the toxic CdS as the
n-type buﬀer layer in CZTS based solar cells. This was done by ﬁrst optimizing the chemical bath
deposition of CdS already done in the group in two stages. First the deposition time and thereby
thickness was optimised and it was found that by decreasing the deposition time from 4:30 to 4:15
min, the peak eﬃciency increased by 6.2 % whereas the median increased by 4.7 %. To further
optimize the deposition of CdS, diﬀerent deposition temperatures was tried in a range from 60 °C to
80 °C. For this test it was found that increasing the deposition temperature from 70 °C to 75 °C and
changing the deposition time from 4:15 min to 2:00 min, the eﬃciency could be increased with 28
%. It is concluded that this improvement is especially due to an improved current collection, where
the parasitic absorption of CdS is signiﬁcantly reduced together with a slight increase in band gap
that overall increase the Jsc of the 28 %, the same as the eﬃciency. Further raising the temperature
showed similar results, but the process also became progressively more unstable and therefore these
results were harder to replicate.
With this improvement of the CdS layer the sputtering of (Zn,Mg)O (ZMO) was tested. To do
this, four diﬀerent tests were carried out; sputter protection, ZMO thickness, ZMO composition and
CZTS composition. The sputtering was done under relatively mild conditions of 20 W, 20 mtorr
with a long ramptime of 150 s.
To test the sputter protection three diﬀerent thicknesses of Al2O3 was deposited using ALD
and a reference without any protection, where a sputtering time of 26 min was used. However
it was found that Al2O3 as sputter protection was detrimental for the performance of the cells.
However, this test also showed that ZMO had potential to substitute CdS. The cells without any
sputter protection showed and incredible improvement of 86 % compared to the CdS reference. The
improvement was found to be across all essential parameters, Jsc, Voc and FF, with improvements
of 30 %, 13% and 27.7 % respectively.
From these results the eﬀect of thickness was also tested on two diﬀerent precursors, with 10 %
Mg and 15 % Mg respectively. The results showed that deposition sputtering times of up to 68 min
was advantageous, however non of the tested samples improved the eﬃciency compared to that of
the CdS reference. Even the best cells had eﬃciencies that was around 50 % decreased compared
to the reference CdS.
The use of two diﬀerent precursors of CZTS for the thickness study also made it possible to
see if the CZTS composition had an eﬀect. Although the composition of one of the precursors is
unknown, the sputter conditions used is and there is a slight diﬀerence. This diﬀerence was found
to eﬀect the performance of the cells drastically, to such an extend that despite the precursor 0423c
being nearly 100 % better on the CdS reference than 0423d, the ZMO had the exact opposite rela-
tion, where the 0423d was nearly 100 % better than 0423c. The reason for this signiﬁcant variation
between the two precursors is not known, it is postulated that a small change in the band gap en-
ergy of CZTS could lead to a conduction band oﬀset that is outside the desired range oﬀ0 to 0.4 eV.
The ﬁnal test carried out focus on the composition of ZMO. Here it was found that the com-
position of 10 % Mg showed superior performance on all parameters compared to 5 % Mg and 15

% Mg. The JV results indicates that 15 % might shift the conduction band oﬀset above 0.4 eV due
to the greatly reduced FF and Jsc that is observed. Furthermore the expected linear increase of the
band gap documented in literature was not found between these three measurement points.
Although the results of ZMO as a substitution to CdS presented in this thesis is promising,
it is collected on a very narrow basis, with few measurement points and it can therefore only be
concluded that ZMO works as a n-type material for CZTS based solar cells and it shows potential
to be at least as good as CdS if not better, but more work is needed to understand why it works
signiﬁcantly better on one precursor of CZTS and signiﬁcantly worse on another and to determine
what the optimal thickness and composition of ZMO is.
Except the need for more research using ZMO as n-type buﬀer layer. Further research with
CdS deposition at higher temperatures could also lead to further improvement. For this, it is neces-
sary to stabilize the process at higher temperatures. For this, it is especially the temperature drop
when adding Ch4N2S and NH3OH that should be counteracted. For this, four possibly solutions is
proposed:
1. Increase the volume of the water bath around the reactor to ensure more thermal energy to
counteract the temperature drop quicker.
2. Use a more powerful heating source, again to quicker raise the temperature to the desired
level after adding the chemicals.
3. Heating the chemicals before adding them to the reactor, to decrease the temperature drop.
4. Adding less of the chemicals to minimize the temperature drop.
The last of these would also lower the concentrations and therefore the reaction rate of the
depositions and would therefore require more extensive testing to implement.
