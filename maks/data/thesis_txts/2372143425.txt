Abstract
Abstract
This thesis develops a real-time application based performance indicator system in technical
project management. It is carried out in collaboration with the Chassis Systems Control
- Driver Assistance - Ultrasonic Engineering Project Management of automotive supplier
Robert Bosch GmbH.
The main purpose of the thesis is to enhance project management and organizational
maturity through the development of a performance indicator system.
The system is
applied in project management for embedded software development, but its concept can
be transferred to other applications, too.
The developed performance indicator system covers the process from problem assessment,
indicator deﬁnition and identiﬁcation towards implementation. It contains methods for
each of the process steps. The assessment follows a holistic objective catalogue, which
deﬁnes requirements on indicators. Mandatory objectives regard the use case of a metric,
data accessibility, general applicability, visualization, automation, and prototyping.
As part of the thesis, the developed performance indicator system is tested and applied
inside the organization. Through a situation analysis issues in project management and
white spots of metrics are identiﬁed. With the identiﬁed methods, a set of alternative
indicator families is developed. Through User Experience (UX) two indicator clusters are
selected for a pilot implementation. The pilot implements prior developed risk management
and modiﬁed Earned Value Management (EVM) indicators for agile software development
on two separate projects.
Executives, project managers and project members inside the organization highly appre-
ciate the holistic performance indicator system and the pilot implementation of metrics.
Both clusters oﬀer valuable insight and are transferred in a standard process. The execution
of the pilot under the new performance indicator system ensures an immediate integration
into an existing advanced software application. This allows automated computation and
visualization of indicators in real-time.
Besides the transfer of piloted performance indicators in a standard process, the thesis
elaborates on practices for an eﬀective change management process towards the successful
deployment of the performance indicators system inside the organization.

The following chapter will set the framework the thesis is carried out in. Initially, in Section
1.1 the motivation and context of the thesis is presented. In the following Section 1.2, the
overall scope is deﬁned, at which in Section 1.3 the four research questions of the thesis
are formulated. Finally, in Section 1.4 the outline of the thesis is presented.
1.1 Motivation and Context
"If we could ﬁrst know where we are, and whither we are tending, we could then better
judge what to do, and how to do it." Lincoln (1858)
The quote above by Abraham Lincoln shows the desire of humankind for making good and
informed decisions. Every leader, manager or worker has to make decisions. While these
have a great impact on the prosperity of an organization, the available information is one
of the limiting constraints towards doing the right things and doing things right.
However, one could argue that in today’s information society almost every information is
available. Information can be represented through data, which is "information in numer-
ical form that can be digitally transmitted or processed" (Merriam-Webster, nda). Thus,
although extensive information is [through data] available, a decision-maker may have
no access to it. It requires an observer, who converts the information into knowledge.
Only knowledge, which is the actual "fact or condition of having information" (Merriam-
Webster, ndb) can in turn lead towards action for eﬀective and eﬃcient decision-making.
The challenge lies in identifying the required data, selecting the right information and
combine it towards a valuable set of knowledge. The observer needs to access data resp.
information sources, balance out the minimum required information against an information
overﬂow, and retrieve the essential knowledge for an action. While too little knowledge
does not allow decision-making, too much information in knowledge sets neither does. It
might be confusing and one will likely loose focus on the things that matter.
In this context the thesis is carried out. The director of Driver Assistance (DA) ultrasonic
engineering project management of worldwide automotive supplier Robert Bosch GmbH
wants to enhance decision making and communication processes by setting up a holistic
performance indicator system.
It should enable a Technical Project Manager (TPM),
project members and managers to make informed decisions. Moreover, the tool shall be
used to enhance stakeholder communication through relevant performance indicators.
The department within the thesis is carried out is responsible for the technical project

1.2 Scope of the Thesis
Based on the motivation and context of the thesis, the initial scope was deﬁned. Within
the ultrasonic product area, recently the tool suite IBM Application Lifecycle Management
(ALM) was introduced. This software is used for topics covering project, task, change and
defect management.
The application, therefore, contains key data of the project and
also allows to gather data from other systems used in the development cycle. Regarding
indicators, ALM is capable of gathering data in real-time and providing a visualization of
indicators on dashboards.
But currently, the usage of ALM towards its full potential is constrained by several issues.
Firstly, stakeholders and stakeholder requirements are not clearly identiﬁed.
Secondly,
current performance indicators do not provide a clear image of project issues and do not
allow to derive corrective actions on time. Thirdly, the full potential of ALM remains
unclear as no holistic assessment of its functions and capabilities was made.
Based on that, the initial scope was to develop a real-time application based performance
indicator system. It should allow technical project management to measure the perfor-
mance of a project for deriving corrective actions on time and communicating relevant is-
sues towards stakeholders in a structured process. Therefore, stakeholders and stakeholder
requirements need to be identiﬁed, prioritized and translated into appropriate performance
indicators. These performance indicators should then be subsequently implemented in an
ALM real-time dashboard report. As a result, a structured and automatized reporting tool
is obtained that can be used within the technical project management to derive corrective
actions on time and communicating relevant issues to aﬀected stakeholders.
However, through the ﬁndings of the situation analysis in Section 4.4 the initial scope
had to be slightly readjusted. Firstly, current indicators were deﬁned by various stake-
holders and no holistic performance indicator management system existed. Therefore, not
only a deﬁnition of suitable indicators was required, also an overall framework for perfor-
mance measurement from indicator identiﬁcation and deﬁnition towards implementation
and management needed to be developed. Secondly, ALM was only used within the ultra-

2.2 Systems Engineering
The framework of SE is used as a central research method, as it allows to solve complex
problems and its approach is to design successful systems. Walden et al. (2015) present
diﬀerent deﬁnitions for SE.
Although they are slightly diﬀerent, they share the basic
concept and idea of SE. It allows successful system design by decomposing the system
into subsystems and showing interaction with other system elements of the environment.
Regarding its methodology, SE is "an iterative process of top-down synthesis, development,
and operation of a real-world system" (Walden et al., 2015, p. 11), that fulﬁlls business
and technical needs of customers based on deﬁned requirements.
One model of the general process for SE is presented by Bahill and Gissing (1998) and
adopted by the International Council on Systems Engineering (INCOSE). The so-called
SIMILAR Process is an acronym of the seven tasks State the Problem, Investigate al-
==PAGE:23==
2.2 Systems Engineering
9
ternatives, Model the system, Integrate, Launch the system, Assess performance, and
Re-evaluate.
In the ﬁrst task, the problem statement, top-level customer requirements are derived from
the problem statement. Also, each requirement should be linked and therefore traceable to
the initial problem statement. Requirements should be ranked according to their priority,
as the ﬁnal system must fulﬁll at least all mandatory requirements. Input for requirements
or the problem statement can come from various stakeholders such as end users, operators,
suppliers, customers, government agencies, sponsors and others. Critical for the problem
statement is that it focuses on what must be done but not on how to do it.
Based on the requirements, alternative designs are created and evaluated. As no design
usually ﬁts best all objectives, multicriteria decision aiding techniques can be helpful. As
results might change when more data is available, the analysis should be carried out in
an iterative way. Also prototyping diﬀerent designs and running tests on the real system
should be done to ﬁnd the most suitable design.
Modeling the system is done for most alternative designs. As the SE process is not sequen-
tial but parallel and iterative, models must be created before alternatives can be assessed.
Models can be various, ranging from physical analogs to state machines, functional ﬂow
diagrams, or computer simulations. SE covers both, creating a product and also a process
for producing it.
The integration step deﬁnes interfaces between subsystems, combines subsystems towards
systems and integrates them with businesses and people so that they can interact with one
another. Critical is that subsystems are well-designed and information exchange between
these is minimized. This follows a modular approach, as "feedback loops around individual
subsystems are easier to manage than feedback loops around interconnected subsystems"
(Bahill and Gissing, 1998, p. 517).
After the integration, the system is launched. This is where the entire system runs the ﬁrst
time and produces outputs. The selected alternative is designed in detail, and by deriving
lessons learned from developing and building the system, improvements in earlier steps can
be made. Also, the formulated requirements are validated and veriﬁed as well as system
functions are linked to physical components.
Performance assessment is crucial to evaluate the system’s performance and improve it
further. Therefore, critical success factors are deﬁned and tracked to integrate them into
a feedback loop.
Re-evaluate is seen as the most important task. It should be considered as a continual
process with many parallel loops.
The feedback from observing outputs supports sys-
tem control and allows to improve performance by modifying the system, the inputs, the
product or the process. Figure 2.2 shows the summarized SIMILAR process.
Züst and Troxler (2006) propose a slightly diﬀerent model for SE. Here, the interaction

The life cycle model consists of the four major phases development, realization/ implemen-
tation, utilization, and disposal. The development phase starts with a proposal for system
design which covers the time span between the detection of a problem and the decision
to develop a solution for it. After that, the preliminary study is carried out with the pur-
pose of understanding the problem and derive appropriate goals. Also, ﬁrst solutions must
be developed for evaluation so that a conceptual framework for a solution can form the
foundation for the main study. In the main study information regarding aﬀected systems
and interfaces is collected, so that the overall system can be designed. This also includes
the development and evaluation of alternative system designs towards a preferred concept.
This concept provides the framework for detailed design. The detailed studies focus on
subsystems of the overall system. By evaluating subsystems and their usefulness towards
a ﬁnal solution, performance can be increased, and overall system design is enhanced.
The second major phase is the realization phase. In a ﬁrst step, the system realization is
carried out. It includes the supply of individual components (subsystems), its assembly
and preparation for an introduction. In a second step, the system installation, the ready-
for-use system is handed over to the user and put into operation. This step also may
include phasing out old systems.
In the utilization phase, the system is operated and administrated by the user (system uti-
lization). Through feedback loops and monitoring systems, misalignments or malfunctions
of the system can be collected and used for proposals for new design, redesign or disposal.
This can either be a complete redesign with a new preliminary study or the modiﬁcation
of single subsystems to achieve the intended behavior.
In the fourth stage, the disposal phase, the system is disposed and will no longer exist. As
it can be very challenging for physical systems, this process should already be considered
during the development phase. Also, a re-use concept of single subsystems can be beneﬁcial
regarding life cycle thinking.
Inside each phase of the life cycle model, the problem-solving cycle is carried out as a micro
strategy. It is composed of three major and six minor steps. Firstly, the search for goals
with the two minor steps situation analysis and goal deﬁnition is carried out. This process
step aims to deﬁne the speciﬁc requirements that need to be fulﬁlled. In a second step, the
search for solutions with concept synthesis and concept analysis is carried out. Developing

alternative solutions allow a holistic view on the problem, as projected performance of
individual solutions can be compared and the fulﬁllment of requirements can be assessed.
After that, the third step selection with evaluation and decision can be performed. Based
on the assessment criteria, methods for decision support can be applied, and an optimal
solution among the developed alternatives can be chosen. Particular attention is given to
the point that problem solving follows a cyclic and not linear development. As it relies
on alternative scenarios and various possible decisions in later steps, re-evaluation and

While the SIMILAR process oﬀers a compact view on the application of SE with outlining
the importance of re-evaluation and feedback loops, Züst and Troxler present a holistic

2.3 User Experience
According to International Organization for Standardization (ISO) (2010), UX is deﬁned
as "a person’s perceptions and responses that result from the use or anticipated use of a
product, system or service". Kraft (2012) describes UX in a more informal way "as the
feelings that the user gets when using a product". These feelings can be anything from hate
to love, anger to happiness and so forth.
To create positive feelings for the end user, products or services must be designed according
to UX principles. For successful UX innovation, Kraft (2012) presents ﬁve core elements.
Relevance means that the innovation covers a need of the user and is solving an actual
problem. Positive feelings should be triggered by making the user happy and feel satisﬁed
with the product or service deployed. Negative feelings should be eliminated and avoided in
any case. Uniqueness or novelty is not about the real situation, but to create the perception
of it. Visibility builds on the assumption that innovation needs to be visible and to be
placed where the majority of target users is. Otherwise, it will neither be noticed nor seen
as an innovation. Marketability principle says that there is no better way to promote a
product than through successful UX innovation, as the product or service markets itself.
However, the principles above only present the requirements for the innovative product or
service but do not state how the user is integrated into the innovation process. Central to
UX is that it considers the user besides business and technology as third innovation source.
It uses the user as starting point of an iterative process with asking "what is desirable?",
while moving on to the business ("what is viable?") and technology ("what is feasible?")

dimension (Robert Bosch GmbH, 2015).
Traditional methods of innovation tried to ﬁnd user needs and then generate innovative
products. In order to actively involve users into the innovation process, it is not suﬃcient
to ask them for problems, speciﬁc needs or functions. Kraft proposes to ﬁnd so called lead
users that are characterized by creatively using products. This can be either by designing
their own applications, creating workarounds for their needs or improve the product in any
other way. These users are particularly valuable for gathering requirements and providing
feedback on prototypes.

2.5 Field Research with Qualitative Methods
Every real-life problem requires to some extend ﬁeld research. On the one hand mismatches
and misalignments from theoretical models can be identiﬁed, on the other hand, valuable
input from stakeholders during every life cycle phase can be obtained for developing a
solution based on an initially formulated problem statement.
One widely used qualitative method are interviews. With the support of semi-structured
questionnaires, various topics can be addressed, and perceptions of individuals can be
obtained. As the outcome depends on the interview and the formulated questions, special
attention should be given to these points. Züst and Troxler (2006) provide both, guidelines
for interviewing and formulating questions. These can be very helpful when the results are
used for a new system design.
Another powerful method is observation.
Through the observation of user groups and
stakeholders, these can be monitored in their natural environment. Inside organizations,
especially meetings with e.g. project members, customers, executives or reporting routines
can give valuable insight. Often symptoms of underlying problems become present in such
meetings, which can be identiﬁed by an RCA. Furthermore, these routines are perceived as

2.6 Iterative Development
Iterative development as a life cycle model divides the project into several iterations. These
iterations can be seen as a mini-project that also go through the typical life cycle phases.
Iterations can be performed in parallel or sequential order before they get integrated to-
wards a ﬁnal product. The great advantage of iterative development is the deployment of
single subsystems, where the user can get a feel for the functionality before its completion.
This allows cost and time eﬀective feedback loops and adaptations of subsystems towards
an increased overall system performance. Through the early deployment of subsystems in
iterations user participation is also enhanced, which leads towards a higher likelihood of
producing an acceptable system at the end (Foster, 2014, Hvam et al., 2008).
Although iterative development is associated with some disadvantages such as an increased
likelihood for poor documentation, poor integration of the entire system and increased
eﬀort for maintenance, these can be mitigated by precaution and following the life cycle
stages in each iteration (Foster, 2014).
Especially projects dealing with high uncertainty require methods such as iterative devel-
opment. Total knowledge is not obtained at the beginning of the project, and life cycle
phases cannot be signed oﬀonce they are completed the ﬁrst time. Through active user
feedback, prototyping and changing perceptions diﬀerent concepts may emerge and provide
an alternative view on the optimal solution.
However, regarding project management, this approach might be challenging as constant
changes towards an optimal solution can put high pressure on schedule and project mem-
bers. That is why also iterative development needs to respect the fact that at some point
subsystems only allow minor changes or can only be optimized in a future study.

3.2 Software Engineering and Development in Automotive Industry
Software engineering and development follows in principle general practices, with some
additional requirements. Foster (2014) examines several life cycle models for software de-
velopment. These life cycle models provide guidance in which order a project should carry
out its major tasks of problem analysis, requirements speciﬁcation, draft and detailed de-
sign, programming and test, implementation, maintenance and user support. The success
of a software development project is highly dependent on pursuing the various development
and evolution phases in the right order (Boehm, 1988).
Particular relevant for the automotive industry and further examined are the waterfall
model and the closely related v-model (see Subsection 3.2.1) as well as agile development
(see Subsection 3.2.2). Besides the life cycle models for software development, the ISO
Standard 15504 with its speciﬁc domain for the automotive industry, so-called Automotive
Software Process Improvement and Capability Determination (ASPICE), is described.
ASPICE is a process assessment model and focuses on the development of embedded
software, which means that software is embedded in a technical system with hardware
components. Through the software, the vast majority of functions of the overall system
is realized (Höhn et al., 2009). These systems are very common in the automotive indus-
try. As the primary life cycle processes of ASPICE follow a v-model, its fundamentals are
described in the course of Subsection 3.2.1.
3.2.1 Waterfall Development, V-Model, ISO 15504 and ASPICE
The waterfall development life cycle is the most traditional approach and is widely used
since the 1970s as an evolution of the stagewise model (Boehm, 1988), although others
perceive the waterfall model as the ﬁrst one, which describes how projects proceed (Hvam
et al., 2008).
The three key elements of the waterfall model are ﬁrst that all system
requirements are known and well deﬁned before starting the development phase. This also
requires, in theory, no changes of requirements throughout the project. Secondly, each life
cycle phase needs to be approved by the project’s stakeholders (i. e. users). Without the
ﬁnal approval, no advancement towards the next phase is possible. Thirdly, the waterfall
model does not allow iterations. This means, that until the ﬁnal system is deployed no
process can be modiﬁed. This is only possible by running through the entire model again.
==PAGE:32==
18
3 Fundamental Concepts in Current Literature
Its key advantages are a well-integrated system, user participation through sign-oﬀs, a
high likelihood of proper documentation and lower cost through eﬃcient programming
by well-deﬁned requirements. However, the waterfall model is also associated with some
disadvantages. System development may take a long time, which makes users impatient
and might require ineﬃcient workarounds in the meantime. Also, requirements are likely to
change throughout the development phase. Indeed the model leads to a well designed and
documented, but maybe antiquated system at its completion (Foster, 2014, Hvam et al.,
2008).
Like the waterfall model, the v-model has a sequential approach and shares the same
advantages and disadvantages. It was introduced by Forsberg and Mooz (1991) to provide
a visualized method for highlighting key SE areas, emphasize on continuous validation with
stakeholders, the relevance of risk management and the importance of deﬁning test and
veriﬁcation plans already during requirements development (Walden et al., 2015).
Figure 3.1 shows a version of the developed v-model by Forsberg et al. (2005). Time and
system maturity evolve from left to right. The process starts with system development and
system requirements deﬁnition. Based on the deﬁned system requirements, test cases and a
veriﬁcation strategy are already derived (indicated trough the horizontal arrow to the upper
right v). After the system requirements, system architecture with system element design
is performed. Again, test cases and veriﬁcation strategies are deﬁned for the opposing
side. In the lowest part, detailed software development (resp. subsystem development)
by programming single code is made. With the direct link towards the formerly deﬁned
integration, validation, and veriﬁcation strategies, ﬁst small system elements are tested and
integrated towards subsystems, and then are further combined, connected and integrated
towards an entire system. This point marks the solution/ system realization with fulﬁlling
the initially deﬁned system requirements.
Car manufacturers have high requirements for the successful delivery of software. It is
estimated that more than 85% of today’s functionality in a car is controlled through soft-
ware. Moreover, the vast majority of this software is developed and provided by external
suppliers. As the quality and functionality of a car is therefore highly dependent on the
successful integration of each software subsystem, car manufacturers were seeking out not
only to monitor ﬁnished hardware and software products, but also a suppliers’ development
processes (Höhn et al., 2009).
Sophisticated (software) development processes in automotive industry must fulﬁll certain
requirements. Cars are perceived as mature products of high quality, with a long lifetime
and guarantee. For legal reasons, a high degree of product documentation is required that
needs to be taken care of in the development process. Moreover, a car consists of various
subsystems, supplied by hundreds of companies, and developed by thousands of engineers.
This requires interoperability between subsystems and a model to successfully integrate
these subsystems towards an overall system (Mueller, 2011).

3.2.2 Agile Development
Although the sequential v-model fulﬁlls the requirements of the automotive industry to a
large extent, its disadvantages lead towards searching for alternative development methods.
According to Mueller (2011) frequently changing customer requirements during the de-

cycles that are not able to deliver innovative results within a few weeks. In a competitive
environment with ever decreasing development times and shorter time-to-market, these
needs cannot be neglected. From a supplier point of view, current development processes
include a series of non-value-adding process steps and work products. These lead to a slow,

Also outside the automotive industry people were looking for software development pro-

implement lightweight, agile methodologies. Common ground was established through the

The twelve uniting principles of agile methodologies do not describe a single method but
set the framework towards which objectives software development methods should be used.

1. Satisfy the customer through an early and continual delivery of valuable software.
2. Welcome changing requirements, as they can increase customers’ competitive advan-

3. Deliver working software frequently with a preference for short time cycles.
4. Daily and close collaboration between business people and developers.
5. Build projects around motivated individuals and empower them to get the job done.
6. Face-to-face communication is the most eﬀective and eﬃcient method of conveying
information.
7. Working software is the primary measure of progress.
8. Agile processes promote sustainable development.
9. Focus towards technical excellence, and good design enhances agility.
10. Simplicity by doing ﬁrst things ﬁrst and second things not at all is essential.
11. The best software emerges from self-organizing teams.
12. Retrospectives allow the team to reﬂect on how to become more eﬀective and adjust
its behavior accordingly.

3.3.2 The Project Manager Role
As initially stated, the project manager is the person assigned by the organization to
achieve the project’s objectives. Project management is perceived as a critical strategic
discipline, as the well-being of the organization depends on the successful execution of
projects. The project manager serves as the link between the strategy and the team. His
general responsibility is to satisfy needs. These can be tasks, team needs or individual
needs. For meeting these requirements, the project manager should not only apply good
practices of project management but also should be equipped with three key competencies.
Firstly, knowledge about project management and area speciﬁc skills. Secondly, applying
knowledge of project management and domain-speciﬁc skills. Thirdly, personal skills re-
garding the behavior of the project manager. This includes leadership, attitudes and core
personality characteristics. These soft skills are required for leading the project team and
guide the project members. Typical examples of these interpersonal skills are motivation,
communication, political and cultural awareness, trust building, conﬂict management and
coaching (Project Management Institute, 2013).
Based on the situation analysis carried out in Chapter 4 it was found out that at Robert
Bosch DA, the role of the project manager, is split between two, the Project Manager (PjM)
and the TPM. Both work closely together with the Product Component Manager (PCM)
who also needs some skills of a project manager to accomplish the needs emerging from
the project eﬀectively. To understand the organizational speciﬁc deﬁnitions of the three
roles that are closely linked to the project managers general description above, they will
be presented in the following based on Robert Bosch GmbH (2017).
The task of the PjM is to achieve the project’s overall targets, deﬁned in the project
charter. The PjM must take the necessary decisions for the project organization and is
authorized to issue project related instructions to the project team members. He is part
of the sales organization and should be the ﬁrst representative of the project towards the
customer. The PjM also maintains all necessary project and client related documentation
from start to end of the project. Regarding project management, he is accountable for
project planning, monitoring, and control. He is also in charge of project budgeting and
monitoring of development expenses. With the support of the TPM, the PjM coordinates
the PCMs within the departments.
The TPM is the technical responsible of the project, ensuring the performance meets
system and safety requirements. He is the interface towards the engineering line organi-
zation, handles technical requests and is in charge of the technical coordination between
the components of a project. With this task, the TPM also informs the project members
continuously about technical modiﬁcations and requirement changes. Regarding project
management, the TPM tracks the SE milestones in the system schedule and coordinates

3.3.3 Core Principles of Project Governance
Eﬀective project management requires a framework for structure and rules where it is
executed.
Garland (2009) presents four core principles of project governance that are
essential for eﬀectively manage projects towards the desired outcome.
The ﬁrst principle is that only one role can be accountable for the success of the project.
This single point of accountability, which means that there is always only one who is
ultimately responsible for the outcome of a decision. This provides clear leadership and
timeliness in decision making. It also sets clear boundaries for decision makers where a
decision is required and in which stage one can only deliver input towards the person in
charge.
The second principle states that service ownership determines project ownership. Garland
(2009) argues that one of the projects core intentions is to move from a current towards a
future, desired state. This desired state or outcome can be perceived as a service delivered
through the project to the customer. There might be assets (or hardware) contributing
to the service, but only the fully integrated system that provides the functionality is the
service, the customer desires and is willing to pay for. For a successful project outcome,
the service owner inside the organization also has to be accountable for the project and
therefore own the project from an organizational point of view.
The third principle requests a separation of stakeholder management and project decision-
making activities. Without any doubt, stakeholder management is a very critical task in
project management, but it should be separated from project decision-making. Project
performance will decrease if decision-making boards are ﬁlled up with stakeholders not
required for the decision-making process. These stakeholders only seek for information and
==PAGE:41==
3.3 Project Management and Project Governance
27
hinder the process of eﬀective decision making by asking questions about what is happening
in the project. This can cause severe delays in the project, as important decisions are not
made on time.
The fourth principle states a separation of project governance and organizational gov-
ernance structures. The need for this principle mainly becomes eﬀective when making
decisions. A project needs its own governance model as the one of the organization cannot
serve for both. The steering committee of a project needs to be empowered by the project
governance framework to make decisions in an eﬀective and timely manner. This reduces
decision layers as it does not need to follow the organizational line of command. Other-
wise, project progress is slowed down, and a bottleneck in the decision-making process is
created.

3.4.1 Distinction and Deﬁnition of Terms
The terms metrics, measures, measurement, indexes, and indicators are often used as
synonyms in the literature (Mascarenhas Hornos da Costa et al., 2014) and (Parmenter,
2015). However, there exist diﬀerences in the deﬁnition of these terms.
The term measure describes the result of a measurement process; this can be (technical)
artifacts or outcomes of a process. It only reﬂects the primary data output of a process and
does not involve further computation. By that deﬁnition measures form the basis for an
indicator system, as they provide the data for any further calculations and combinations
(Mascarenhas Hornos da Costa et al., 2014).
A metric can be deﬁned as "a variable that indicates one or more aspects of the eﬀectiveness
or eﬃciency of a process" (Mascarenhas Hornos da Costa et al., 2014, p. 371). It is equally
used with the term indicator, and it involves a processing or computation of input data.
Thus, indicators and metrics can be seen as one level above measures in the performance
measurement system. But also indicators allow a further diﬀerentiation in their hierarchy.
They can be grouped in contributing and key indicators (Parmenter, 2015). In this thesis
contributing indicators are used for computing key indicators.
Key indicators are per
deﬁnition all indicators that are visually represented in a graph or diagram and stand on
the top of the performance measurement system.
Indexes, however, are a special case of an indicator. While an indicator or metric can
consist only of one measure, an index is always computed from at least two input values
(Mascarenhas Hornos da Costa et al., 2014).
Besides the classiﬁcation in a hierarchy, indicators can be grouped into lagging and leading
indicators. A lagging indicator describes past performance of a system, whereas a lead-
ing indicator makes a predictive statement about the performance of a system. Leading
indicators often build on the same data or metrics like lagging indicators. The main diﬀer-
ence is how the information is gathered, processed, evaluated and interpreted to transform
it into a forward-looking perspective.
If a metric is deﬁned as lagging and leading, it
somehow reﬂects both perspectives, as based on past performance also predictive analysis
can be derived (Mascarenhas Hornos da Costa et al., 2014) and (Roedler et al., 2010).
A performance measurement system should include both, leading and lagging indicators.
This allows assessing past performance as well as making predictive analysis and derive
appropriate actions on time.
The deﬁnition of terms establishes in the further course of the thesis a common ground
on terminology, allows a better understanding of the used terms and contributes towards
standards in the metric management system.

3.4.2 Quantitative and Qualitative Measurement Methods
Measurement methods can be both, deliver the required input data for a metric but also put
a key indicator on a scale and measure the output obtained from the performance indicator
system. As there exist numerous deﬁnitions (see Pedhazur and Schmelkin (1991) for a
comprehensive evaluation of diﬀerent deﬁnitions), here the one developed by Pedhazur and
Schmelkin (1991) is used: "Measurement is the assignment of a number to a characteristic
of an object or event, which can be compared with other objects or events".
Based on this deﬁnition it can already be seen that a measurements output is always
qualitative (a number), while the input can be either qualitative or quantitative. This is
an important aspect towards the development and selection of metrics, as it, for instance,
allows to include people’s feelings and perceptions in the measurement process. So the
deﬁnition makes no statement about the input data, the only requirement is that at the
end a number is obtained. While this leads to the fact that one can measure anything, it
also signiﬁes that measurements rely heavily on their qualitative or quantitative input.
In the context of performance measurement, Hubbard (2010, p. 23) provides an alternative
deﬁnition that measurements are "observations that quantitatively reduce uncertainty".
Although this might be true and users perceive that measurements do so, one should
foresee possible limitations that come along with the collected data and not eliminate
common sense when making decisions based on performance measurements.
Quantifying measurements can be done by applying nominal, ordinal, interval or ratio
scales. Nominal scales do not match the deﬁnition presented by Pedhazur and Schmelkin
(1991), as they classify objects in a set. For example, the impact of a risk can be considered
as high, medium or low. However, based on this classiﬁcation, each set can be assigned
a number. That is why nominal scales are considered as the primary measurement scale.
An ordinal scale assigns numbers to the measured object so that they reﬂect their rank
on an attribute. The scale only makes a statement about the rank (greater than, equal,
smaller than), but does not say how much an object exceeds another. Such scales are called
asymmetric. Interval scales are ordinal scales with constant units of measurement. This
allows a comparability of objects, as diﬀerences and ratios can be computed. However, one
has to be careful as the zero point on an interval scale is arbitrary. This means that only
the diﬀerences in scores can be scaled as a ratio, but not the scores themselves. This is
the diﬀerence to a ratio scale, where also an actual, absolute zero point can be determined.
This allows the computation of ratios of scores, and even changing them to another unit
will not change the ratio, e.g. expressing monetary values either in euro or dollar (Pedhazur
and Schmelkin, 1991).
Depending on the measurement and the scale, one has to be cautious which conclusion can
==PAGE:46==

be drawn from the measurement. In a performance indicator system, it can have a great
impact, as measurement processes are applied as input (for obtaining data and metrics)
and also for processing output when measuring key indicators and putting them on a scale
for visualization.
The measurement methods contribute towards the further development of metrics and the
deﬁnition of new metrics in Section 6.2. The theory on measurement and the investigation
of scales allow a better development of metrics and help to identify possible limitations in

3.4.3 Methods for Metric Deﬁnition and Identiﬁcation
The deﬁnition and identiﬁcation of suitable indicators for an organization is a challenging
task. But, no matter if it is on a strategical or operational level, or if the metrics shall be
used in a project environment; ﬁnding suitable metrics and derive appropriate actions can
be very beneﬁcial to a company’s overall performance.
From a strategic point of view, Ansoﬀ(1975) was one of the ﬁrst who pointed out the
importance of this task. In his work, he describes the two variables that are critical for a
company to respond on time to strategic surprises. First, the speed in which a threat or op-
portunity aﬀects the business and second, the amount of time required to act thoughtfully
on the situation. While the pace of change in the business environment increased, a ﬁrm’s
response has become slower through growing in size, complexity and geographic locations.
The only way to exit this vicious circle is to change the way strategic information is used.
Following Ansoﬀ(1975, p. 23) "a ﬁrm should determine what planning and action are
feasible as strategic information becomes available in the course of the threat/opportunity".
Based on that, several actions can be planned to increase the strategic ﬂexibility of the
company. By the time, the level of uncertainty decreases and a suitable response can be
executed. This is called "graduated response through ampliﬁcation and response to weak
signals" (Ansoﬀ, 1975, p. 23). Although the model focused on strategic issues that mainly
would aﬀect the company’s ﬁnancial results, its principle can also be applied on a more
operational level and in project management. Instead of searching for the most desirable
information and make no decision at all, one should focus on the information available and
use this towards an eﬀective and eﬃcient decision-making process.
With their work on the Balanced Scorecard (BSC), Kaplan and Norton (1992) suggested
four sets of parameters to track both, traditional measures of ﬁnancial performance and
newly emerging operational measures. The four parameters with the key questions were:
• Financial Perspective - How do we look to shareholders?
• Customer Perspective - How do customers see us?
• Internal Business Perspective - What must we excel at?
• Innovation and Learning Perspective - Can we continue to improve and create value?

On a project level, Project Management Institute (2013) propose various tools and tech-
niques to, for example, control schedule and cost performance. Regarding schedule perfor-
mance common tools are:
• Trend analysis - Assesses project performance over time to analyze whether perfor-

of performance to date and their impact on future completion dates.
• Critical path method - Evaluating the process along the critical path can help assess-

the overall schedule and project end date. Also analyzing the status of tasks near
critical paths can help to identify potential schedule risk.
• Critical chain method - Based on the theory of constraints by Goldratt and used when
executing a project according to Critical Chain Project Management (CCPM), com-
paring the buﬀer remaining versus the buﬀer required can help determine schedule
status. The gap between the buﬀer required and the buﬀer remaining can indicate
whether corrective action needs to be taken.

releases (see Section 3.2), where content and work estimates are well deﬁned as it covers
only a time span of around two to three months (some might be up to 12 months, although
this is an exceptional case (Kugler Maag Cie, 2015)). This enables the application of EVM,
as detailed planning of a release contains all relevant information for computing metrics
and indicators. In the following the three approaches of Bower (2006), Crowder and Friess
(2015) and Sulaiman et al. (2006) are discussed towards their potential applicability.
Bower (2006) does not follow the approach of an AgileEVM but proposes instead a so-called
phase earned value analysis. In theory, this splits the project into diﬀerent phases and
reduces thereby the complexity of traditional EVM. Furthermore, the integrated approach
of a common baseline of cost and schedule is split, and a series of new terms and calculations
is introduced. Although the concept itself might be possible to implement, some of the key

elements of EVM are taken away. In this thesis, the concept is not further investigated, as
the simplicity of EVM outputs is removed, and the concept is prone to misunderstandings
and false assumptions. Particularly in a large organization, it would take a considerable
amount of time to spread the information regarding this unusual concept and inform all
relevant stakeholders and decision-makers about the meanings of the newly created output
performance indicators.
Crowder and Friess (2015) introduces a concept of AgileEVM, which builds upon the above-
described release view. Inside the releases, measurement intervals are speciﬁed through
sprints. Sprints are short time periods of around two to four weeks, where a deﬁned amount
of work is accomplished (Kugler Maag Cie, 2015). The agile methodology for software de-
velopment used in this context is Scrum (for further information consult e.g. Schwaber
and Beedle (2002)). The main diﬀerence between the classical EVM and the concept in-
troduced by Crowder and Friess (2015) is how key indicators, such as EAC are computed.
In the conventional theory, EAC is calculated based on current performance and the pre-
deﬁned work breakdown structure that still has to be accomplished. In this AgileEVM
concept, key indicators are calculated through technical measures of software development
and entropy measures of team and software volatility. The key technical measure is the
sprint complexity factor, which is a product of computational complexity (time to write
and test software), functional complexity (the eﬀort to achieve functional requirements of
integrated software) and representational complexity (graphical and textual notations from
systems architecture). The sprint complexity factor is used with cost variance and schedule
variance to compute the agile eﬀectiveness, a measure of eﬀectiveness in a given sprint.
This indicator can be summed up across multiple sprints to a cumulative agile earned
value. This indicator inherits all technical measures of the developed concept. In a second
step, entropy measures are calculated. These are computed on volatility in sprint team
membership and volatility of sprint software defects. An increasing turnover rate would
destabilize the team, and decreasing volatility in errors is expected throughout the release
from gained experience. Based on further assumptions, a cumulative density function for
agile team volatility in dependency of team size is constructed. This feature reduces the
cumulative agile earned value by a factor depending on the function. Regarding the entropy
of software defects, a software defect factor is introduced that is a product of the number
of defects and a normalized complexity factor that decreases with the number of sprints.
This factor then again is used to adjust the cumulative agile earned value. Although the
concept seems to build on a few good thoughts, it will not be further considered for the
thesis. The main reasons are its immense complexity for measuring technical performance
based on detailed technical artifacts of software development, various assumptions that
are not proved and can doom the concept when deployed in an organization and wrong
conclusions from entropy. For instance, does the entropy of teams assume that the number
of people working on all projects is always constant. That is in a real world environment
not the case. Projects can be assigned additional resources from contractors or the line
organization without compromising another project’s resources. Also, the defect entropy is
prone to misleading conclusions. The metric lacks a learning curve eﬀect of the test team.

3.4.5 Deﬁnition of Threshold Limits
Besides the deﬁnition and identiﬁcation of suitable metrics, eﬀective measurement requires
threshold limits. These limits specify in which range a metric or an indicator is considered
as acceptable and in which state corrective action is required. Although threshold limits
depend to some extent on the chosen indicator, well-deﬁned threshold limits are at least
suﬃciency for the eﬀective deployment of an indicator.
The indicators presented by Roedler et al. (2010) are good examples of how threshold
limits can contribute towards the quality of indicators. By deﬁning meaningful limits, a
lot of indicators switch their perspective from a lagging to a leading view and become much
more valuable through predictive analysis.
Threshold limits can be deﬁned through four ways (Roedler et al., 2010). Firstly, well-
known deﬁned limits based on set goals, such as a project plan, customer expectations
or a ﬁnancial budget. In this case, the limit is a (pre)-deﬁned scalar that should not be
exceeded. A second way of how to deﬁne a threshold limit can be deviation or change over
time. This sets objectives towards the change of an indicator value and can be beneﬁcial
when tracking progress. If for example, a status of an open task does not change over
a certain period, an indicator could inform about insuﬃcient action. A third example is
volatility, the rate of change over time. This is somewhat connected to the prior example
and can be useful in the context of requirements analysis. A high volatility of requirements
requires an eﬀective change management process and might be a threat towards higher total
cost of the deployed system. The fourth and most advanced method to deﬁne threshold
limits is based on mathematical analysis of historical data sets. Especially with large data
sets threshold limits such as defect discovery rates in diﬀerent life cycle stages of a project
can be deﬁned. Any deviation towards the upper or lower limit might indicate a risk of
low system maturity or overseen defects in previous quality assessments.
The discussed literature shows that metrics alone are not suﬃcient, but threshold limits
are an important element and can even transform a lagging into a leading indicator. In
the further course of the thesis current metrics, and their threshold limits are assessed in
Section 4.3. Moreover, the consideration of threshold limits for newly developed metrics
in Section 6.2 is an important element for a set of meaningful metrics.
3.4.6 Visualization of Metrics
Besides the identiﬁcation of metrics and deﬁnition of threshold limits, performance indica-
tors require visualization. A collection of data sets alone might not contribute towards the
indented beneﬁts of building up a performance indicator system. Visualization is essential
to access information and convey the intended message. From the early beginnings of data
visualization in business by Brinton (1914) until today the main objective remained the
same.
==PAGE:56==
42

To create value with a chart, it is not suﬃcient to just make it good. Picking the right
chart type, good colors or correctly labeled axes are important but do not necessarily
create value. The most critical aspects of data visualization are who is addressed?, what
is their need?, what do they want?, what idea or message should be conveyed?, what could
be shown, what should be shown and ﬁnally how will it be shown?. Putting these questions
in the center of visual management will allow creating good charts with value. In this
context, a perfectly executed, visually appealing chart with wrong data or communication
of a misleading message is not the objective. A good chart following principles of visual
management is both, design execution and contextual awareness (Berinato, 2016).
Between data-driven vs. conceptual and declarative vs. exploratory, data visualization
can be grouped into four categories. Depending on the category, Berinato (2016) proposes
diﬀerent parameters to obtain a good chart. The categories and parameters for data-driven
visualizations are shown in Figure 3.9.
Figure 3.9: Four types of visualizations with parameters (adapted from Berinato (2016)).
Declarative and data-driven visualizations are grouped towards everyday visualizations.
Key of these charts is simplicity. Build on small data sets, only a few indicators are shown
in the chart. Charts can be plotted in Excel or similar applications and then further used
for presentations or print-outs. Besides simplicity, such charts should be designed with
great clarity and consistency. Through their relatively simple content, these charts should
be self-explanatory and not require much additional explanation.
The second highlighted group are visual discovery charts.
These are data-driven, but
exploratory visualizations.
They build on large data sources and require sophisticated
software, programming and business intelligence skills to be visualized in a chart. The
insights gained from discovery charts can often not obtained in another way. Through the
visualization of complex data and possibilities of scenario analysis entire systems and their
interaction can be modeled. Although building up such models requires high eﬀort, the
insights gained can be worth it.
The thesis will focus on the ﬁrst group, called everyday visualizations, as the primary scope
is to provide project managers and key personnel visualized data sets that support eﬀec-

tive and eﬃcient project management. However, in the context of a holistic performance
indicator system, the type of visual discovery charts should be considered as a desirable
future model. This, however, might need a certain level of organizational maturity and
dedicated data analysts and programmers.
A key success factor for the development of expressive charts is sketching and prototyping.
Sketching should be seen as a generative process together with stakeholders that bring up
ideas. These sketches can be further evaluated, and promising ones can move towards a
prototype state. Prototyping incorporates then the use of real data, and in an iterative
process, the sketched ideas are optimized and prepared for implementation.
For both, sketching and prototyping a so called chart guide can be very useful. A com-
prehensive guide is provided by Abela (2013). It provides guidance which chart type to
select based on the data at hand and might be especially applicable for sketching everyday
visualizations.

3.5 Summary and Discussion of Literature
The main purpose of the chapter was to answer the ﬁrst research question "how is software
development in the automotive industry performed and which frameworks for performance
measurement in such projects exist?".
Regarding software development, automotive industry follows widely the v-model devel-
oped by Forsberg and Mooz (1991). The model is an evolution of the sequential waterfall
model, the ﬁrst life cycle model for software development. The great beneﬁt of the v-model
lies within its connection from requirements speciﬁcation and software design towards soft-
ware testing and system integration. This makes it especially for the automotive industry
with its commonly used embedded software systems very useful (Höhn et al., 2009). An
important requirement is an alignment with the ASPICE process reference model. This
model is used by OEMs to verify the capability of suppliers delivering software systems.
However, the v-model has a considerable weakness.
Through its sequential approach,
changing requirements and late requirements speciﬁcation by customers cannot be eﬀec-
tively handled. That is why agile methods such as Scrum and Kanban are more and more
used in automotive software development. These promise to be capable of dealing with ever
changing requirements and focus on delivering working software in short intervals (Beck
et al., 2001). But, as documentation is in the agile world perceived as non-value adding,
==PAGE:58==
44
3 Fundamental Concepts in Current Literature
companies only do a cherry picking of agile methods and still maintain the v-model as the
primary life cycle model for software development (Kugler Maag Cie, 2015). Some authors
like Lappo and Andrew (2004) even argue that using agile methods do not necessarily
mean being agile. This requires a process assessment with quantiﬁable objectives and a
measurement system. The technical background knowledge of life cycle models used for
software development in automotive is considered as very useful for the overall objective
of developing a performance indicator system. Every project of the investigated organiza-
tion goes through these life cycle stages and might apply methods and techniques of the
v-model and agile principles.
The software development tasks are carried out in projects. It was, therefore, essential to
investigate the role of projects inside the organization and how they contribute towards
innovation and further prospering of the company. This includes the organizational setup
and the role of the project manager in the literature (Project Management Institute (2013))
and at Robert Bosch (Robert Bosch GmbH (2017)), as he is the key user for the to be
deployed performance indicator system. For an eﬀective and eﬃcient performance indi-
cator system, project governance is the key interface for a successful integration into the
entire organization. With its framework of how projects are executed within the organiza-
tion, it deﬁnes key principles of project management, including decision-making processes
(Garland, 2009). Project governance can also help to analyze where core principles are ne-
glected, and problems arise. This can be a starting point for deﬁning suitable performance
indicators.
Regarding performance measurement, ﬁrstly a deﬁnition of terms was conducted based on
Mascarenhas Hornos da Costa et al. (2014). This common baseline helps whenever the
terms metrics, indicators, key indicators or others are used. Quantitative and qualitative
measurement methods provide input data for metrics, whereas only quantitative output
data from indicators is obtained.
Measurements always assign a number to an entity
(Pedhazur and Schmelkin, 1991), whereas some authors believe that measurements always
reduce uncertainty (Hubbard, 2010).
That a number is a good starting point but not
suﬃcient for reducing uncertainty was shown in the subsequent sections of the literature
review. It also requires at least the right combination of data towards meaningful metrics,
intelligent threshold limits, and visualization. But, metrics can reduce uncertainty and
provide useful information to be better prepared for future challenges (Ansoﬀ, 1975). To
select the right ones, the BSC of Kaplan and Norton (1992) combines various important
topics when designing a performance measurement system. The holistic view, including
ﬁnancial and operational measures, improve decision-making by considering various per-
spectives. Deriving measures based on the most important goals of each parameter is an
eﬀective and eﬃcient way of assessing if a company’s objectives are fulﬁlled. It also con-
siders the importance and feedback of senior managers when implementing it and creates
an environment of action, but not control for employees. The perspectives and goals of
the BSC can be further used to identify suitable metrics. Bauer (2005) uses the six-sigma
method of an Ishikawa diagram to build on the BSC perspectives and derive meaning-
==PAGE:59==
3.5 Summary and Discussion of Literature
45
ful metrics that contribute towards the success of the entire organization. Moreover, this
method is not limited to BSC perspectives; it can be applied in any case the cause and
eﬀect relationships between indicators and goals are analyzed.
Analyzing already deﬁned metrics, a comprehensive collection of process and technical
project leading indicators is provided by Roedler et al. (2010).
Their broad catalogue
of indicators with detailed descriptions can be very useful when building up a holistic
performance indicator system. On a project management level, various indicators can be
used, one of the most popular is the concept of EVM (Project Management Institute, 2013).
Although diﬃcult to implement though its high requirements on a detailed work breakdown
structure, there are ways of applying the technique in the context of software development
projects on a release level, called AgileEVM. Diﬀerent concepts were analyzed towards
their suitability, and the one of Sulaiman et al. (2006) is a well-tailored approach that
deﬁnitely should be considered when EVM is a valid option for a suitable metric in software
development projects. The early warning signs of IT project failure by Kappelman et al.
(2006) and the process of transforming them into a metric by Nikander and Eloranta (2001)
is an interesting model for a mature organization and provide an outlook how proactive
project management can be supported. But not all metrics are useful. Especially in a
transformation towards agile processes, measuring technical artifacts proposed by Höhn
et al. (2009) are outdated. These metrics barely help to achieve a deﬁned objective, as
some of their measurements are simply misleading. A typical example is a non-existing
correlation between lines of code and code complexity (Lappo and Andrew, 2004). A more
useful set in the context of agile software project management is provided by Rossberg
(2016). Here, agile metrics such as burndown charts, velocity reports or unplanned work
reports are described. These metrics can oﬀer a good and quick overview of the performance
of a project.
But metrics alone are not suﬃcient. Firstly, it requires threshold limits. Among the four
groups of threshold limits, the ones based on historical data sets are the most advanced
and can turn a lagging into a leading indicator. Other threshold limits just set a deﬁned
limit based on a static objective, volatility or a derivative of change over time. The second
essential part towards the eﬀective and eﬃcient use of metrics is visualization. The key is
to address the needs of the end user and build the chart around these needs, the principle of
UX describes this in a nutshell. For small data sets in an operational environment, simple
charts based on applications such as Excel can be used. Key for those charts is simplicity.
Advanced data sets that require deep data analytics are far more complex but can lead
towards visual discovery (Berinato, 2016). This might be a strategic mid-to-long-term goal
of an organization when a solid foundation of simple charts with manageable data sets is
build up in a performance indicator system.
The very broad and extensive theory research is the foundation for an eﬀective situation
analysis and a subsequent gap analysis. The theory also allows drawing a future state while
considering all relevant aspects towards the design and implementation of a performance
indicator system in technical project management.

5.1 Challenges for Designing a Metric Management System
Emerging from the validated conclusions of the situation analysis, three major challenges
were identiﬁed. These challenges build the foundation for the goal deﬁnition process.
The ﬁst challenge is how metrics can enhance project management and software develop-
ment. For addressing this challenge three key questions are formulated:
• How can metrics support (technical) project management?
• How can metrics support the software development process?
• How can metrics enhance agile software development?
Although the formulated questions seem to be simple, they are of vital importance for the
goal deﬁnition process. In a future process metrics only have a purpose if they can enhance
project management or software development. Otherwise, they consume resources but do
not add any value to the organization.
The second challenge addresses metric deﬁnition and continuous improvement.
Again,
three key questions are formulated:

• Which metrics are value adding and which ones are non-value adding?
• How can the usability of metrics be enhanced?
• How can new trends/requirements in project management or software development
be integrated?
A metric has to provide value through its measurement. Also usability is a major challenge,
as it is directly connected to the value a metric can add and how metrics are perceived in
the organization. A tool with great usability and value is likely to be adopted. The third
question addresses maintenance and further development of a metric set. As seen with
agile software development methods, new tools and techniques emerge that require other
metrics.
The third challenge is how process experts can be integrated towards a process of co-

• How can users and process experts included in metric development?
• Do the deﬁned metrics still reﬂect the actual practices in project management and

5.2 Clustering of Metrics and Use Cases
A clustering of metrics in diﬀerent categories is a major step in managing the metric
portfolio. These clusters are developed based on the literature review in Section 3.4 and
the situation analysis in Chapter 4. Grouping metrics together in deﬁned clusters can
reveal possible gaps in the metric portfolio, assess their current performance and identify
actions for improvement.
The distinction in key and contributing indicators is made based on the deﬁnition and
distinction of terms in Subsection 3.4.1. A key indicator is visualized in a chart or diagram
and consists of one or more contributing indicators (or metrics). A contributing indicator
computes input data in an intermediate process towards a key indicator for visualization.
Through the diﬀerentiation the relationship between input data, contributing indicators
and key indicators can be shown. It oﬀers the possibility to track down each element of an
indicator and determine the inﬂuence of input data and contributing indicators towards key
indicators. A ﬂow chart diagram is a very good tool for visualizing these interdependencies.
Also based on the literature review in Subsection 3.4.1, a metric can be classiﬁed as a
lagging or leading indicator. While lagging indicators only look backwards and allow to

assess past performance, leading indicators can make predictive statements. In general,
leading indicators are more desirable and useful for an organization than lagging indica-
tors. Therefore, the objective should be to have as many leading indicators as possible.
As discussed in Subsection 3.4.5, a lagging indicator may already become a leading indi-
cator through the eﬀective deﬁnition of threshold limits. As a result, a huge improvement
may not even require the deﬁnition of new metrics. Knowing existing lagging indicators
and assessing their potential towards a transformation in leading indicators through the
deﬁnition of threshold limits can enhance organizational and project maturity signiﬁcantly.
Placing indicators in a projects life-cycle can also help in the transition from a lagging to
a leading indicator. Moreover, projects within a program are more easily comparable, as
they are often not share the same start or end date. Also, it is easier to assess threshold
limits based on historical data, as placing a metric in the context of a life-cycle phase
provides a common reference point.

ASPICE process groups. Metrics can be grouped towards the process groups of SYS, SWE,

feedback for readjusting the metric set towards a balanced portfolio.
In order to regard current trends in software development, such as agile methods, metrics
can be grouped in agile and non-agile metrics. An agile metric should support the process
of not only applying agile methods, but also to become agile. Metrics can support this
transition. A introduction of agile metrics is given in Subsection 3.4.4, additional indicators
are provided by Rossberg (2016).
Based on the situation analysis it was found out that current metrics measure almost en-
tirely on a project level. Creating a level cluster reﬂecting the hierarchy of projects and
assigning metrics towards a release, project, program or portfolio level is an important as-
pect in a holistic metric management system. Putting metrics in the context of a level
enhances usability, as user groups know which metrics are relevant. A department head
might focus on program and portfolio metrics, whereas a software developer is more inter-
ested in current release metrics. Such a cluster also reveals gaps in the current metric set,
as it calls for additional metrics on a program or portfolio level. Moreover, it can ensure
that metrics are applied in the right context and information is neither too speciﬁc nor too
general.
However, the most important cluster is the use case. The use case deﬁnes the main objective
for collecting a metric. Any metric should be assigned towards an use case, as the use case
reﬂects the purpose and objective of the measurement process. Without the context of an
use case, there is a high risk of deﬁning metrics solely for the purpose of measuring and
providing a sense of security through quantitative data. It is a cornerstone for tackling
the challenge of deﬁning value adding and meaningful metrics for project management

The design and visualization of metrics however, can be considered as an own discipline.
It requires high eﬀort and willingness of the organization to take this step. Especially
exploratory charts (see Subsection 3.4.6) can oﬀer great beneﬁts, but require dedicated
competencies and tools beyond Excel or PowerPoint.
In the context of this thesis and the framework of the metric management system, the focus
is on highlighting the importance of visualization and place it as an equally important
objective towards data collection and metric design. Visualization should be executed as
a parallel process to metric development with a strong emphasis on iterative development.
5.4 Metrics as a Part of Project Governance
The integration of the metric management system in the project governance framework
must be ensured to embed the newly developed system in the organization. Metric manage-
ment must become an integral part of the organizations procedures and be a key element
in project management (see Section 3.3.3. Therefore, two major objectives have to be
fulﬁlled.
Firstly, the metric management system must be applicable to the entire organization and
metrics deﬁned inside the system should ﬁt the vast majority of customer projects. The
more users the system has, the easier it can spread out across the organization and en-
rich project management. Also more feedback is available, which can be incorporated in

iterative development cycles. This will lead to an even better system and increase overall
performance.
Secondly, the metric management system must be documented in the company’s process

entire concept and become a co-creator for further development.

therefore an integral part of the metric management system. Spreading out key project

decision-making forums exclusively open to decision-makers.

categories and can track subsystems within the project. For instance, an indicator family

6.2.3 Risk Management
Currently risk management only consists of one metric. Each risk is a measure assigned
and based on its initial and target probability and impact, a risk level is computed. In a
butterﬂy chart the risks before and after measure are tracked (see Subsection 4.3.1). The
current metric is limited in its signiﬁcance when it comes to track the execution of the
measure, quantify the possible ﬁnancial impact on the project or organization, compare
risk levels of projects or portfolios and focus on the risks that really matter.
The key element of the proposed risk management metrics is a risk burndown chart, which is
divided in three key indicators. Actual, forecasted and planned burndown of risks over time.
Common base line is the cumulative ﬁnancial impact of risks. As each risk has a measure
with a due date assigned, the risk should be mitigated at a certain point or pass completely
away. This initial plan is plotted in the planned burndown. In the actual burndown, the
current cumulative ﬁnancial impact of risks is visualized. Deviations from the planned
burndown can happen through identiﬁcation of new risks, reevaluation of ﬁnancial impact
or delayed execution of measures. A forecast based on the actual performance and planning
is plotted in the forecasted risk burndown.
The objective of this chart is to oﬀer an overview of the ﬁnancial impact of project risks
at a given point of time. It also shows how risks evolve during the project, if ﬁnancial
impact is reduced through the timely handling of measures and how well risks are already
identiﬁed at the beginning of the project. The burndown chart can easily be aggregated
on a program or portfolio level to show the possible ﬁnancial impact on the organization
in an aggregated view. In Figure 6.4 the metric build up with input data, contributing
and key indicators is shown. Also a visual prototype of each chart is provided.

severity and open risk actions by state oﬀer a more detailed view of risk and can be used to

6.2.4 AgileEVM
EVM is one of the most traditional metrics in project management and its integration of
scope, time and cost in one metric set makes it very popular. In the investigated organi-
zation, EVM was also one of the four use cases in the requirements deﬁnition particularly
relevant (see Section 5.2). As discussed in Subsection 3.4.4 the AgileEVM concept of Su-
laiman et al. (2006) can be applied in software development projects on a release level,
without the typical constraint of detailed upfront sequential project planning.
As measurement is done on a release level, the typical time interval for the metrics are
sprints. Although each sprint is a date assigned, the sprint interval provides better compa-
rability. The ﬁrst key indicator is velocity and mean velocity. Velocity measures the task
points completed during a sprint, mean velocity is the average until the actual sprint. In
this case velocity is both, a contributing and a key indicator.
Based on the mean velocity and other indicators, the EAC can be computed. This indicator
plots an indication of the ﬁnish date for the current release and compares it to the initial
==PAGE:93==

plan.
There are diﬀerent ways of how to compute the EAC (see Project Management
Institute (2013)), the three diﬀerent calculations applied here use mean velocity, actual
cost with earned value, and actual cost with CPI and SPI. Which one works best has to
be determined in the speciﬁc project environment. Usually, in a later stage of the release
they are almost identical.
One of the most commonly used agile indicators is the burndown chart. It combines three
key indicators, planned trend, ideal trend and actual work completed. Either the indicators
are computed on workload hours or task points. The ideal trend reﬂects the initial baseline,
work completed (i.e. work burned down) the accomplished work until the current date and
the actual trend an estimate based on the current burndown.
In the AgileEVM model the key chart is the combination of burndown indicators with the
EVM metrics of SPI and CPI. This integrates task, schedule and cost performance in one
chart. Instead of burndown metrics, CPI and SPI are index values. A value above one
means that the current performance is ahead of plan, a value below one indicates a worse
performance compared to initial planning.

The metric cluster consists of one main chart with four key indicators.
The ﬁrst key
indicator represents the total work hours by project members over time according to their
activity. Activities are clustered in for example, requirements engineering, architecture
and design, technical management or integration and validation. The indicator tracks past
performance as well as projected resource requirements. This shows which resources are
needed in the respective life cycle stage.
The second key indicator is the accumulated total planned eﬀort. If the actual workload
is less than planned workload, this may indicate a backlog in tasks accomplished or a
planning with too much buﬀer. On the opposite, if actual workload exceeds planning, this
might impose a risk for cost overrun. In both cases the indicator should trigger further
investigation towards the root cause of the deviance.

actual skills staﬀed and accumulated planned skills required for a time frame or sprint

and actual skills available. In combination with the SPI performance of task closure can

6.3 Prioritization of Clusters by User Experience
The prioritization of deﬁned metric clusters in Section 6.2 follows the principle of UX

viability for the organization reﬂected trough enhancing project management and organiza-

in accordance with the metric management system objectives.
Gathering stakeholder feedback is the ﬁrst step in UX. Therefore, the metric build up
for each cluster with the prototype visualizations of key indicators were shown to key
stakeholders in project management. Each of the persons were asked for their feedback

group was selected in accordance with the stakeholder analysis in Section 4.1 and consisted
of one director for engineering project management in ultrasonic, one group leader in
general project management for acquisition and series, one team leader of a customer
team, three TPMs, one software PCM, a member of the PMO and the author of the
thesis. All votes counted equally regardless of the position and only one vote could be
assigned to one cluster. This means that a ﬁst choice vote must be assigned to a diﬀerent
cluster than the second choice vote.

7.4 Transfer into an Integrated Metric Management System
The hand-over towards the PMO and the CPC Cockpit Chart build the foundation for
a further development of the metric cluster and a transfer in an integrated metric man-
agement system. Here, one major objective is to enhance usability by automated data
collection and visualization of metrics.
The infrastructure of the CPC Cockpit Chart with data agents collecting input data from
Excel sheets, databases and customizable scripts for metric computation and visualization
provide the necessary tool set for fulﬁlling this objective.
Currently the risk management template the metrics rely on is only used in new projects.
The goal should be that all projects within radar, video and ultrasonic mitigate to the new
template and track the required input information. By showing the beneﬁts of the new
risk metric cluster, willingness for a transfer can be increased and the extra workload is
likely to be compensated by the added value of the metrics.
However, the Excel template should not be seen as the ﬁnal solution. Already in the pilot it
was found out that projects customize the template and modify for example the options for
risk status. For an automated solution this is very counterproductive, as data agents would
not be able to extract the required data and metric computation would fail. Therefore,
the process owner need to create awareness among project leaders that any change in the
template will cause a non-working metric visualization. If there is an urgent need for a
change, this can only be done centrally through the process owner (PMO). But this would
still require that all other projects mitigate towards this updated solution. Thus, using an
Excel template for tracking of risks is prone to individual customization, lacks the ability
to improve or update the template and therefore makes it very diﬃcult to improve metrics
if they require other input data than already provided.
On a mid-term perspective, the organization should abandon the Excel template and switch

In Subsection 4.3.2 it was shown that current metrics for the velocity report and burndown
use workload estimates to calculate planned vs. actual progress. The piloted AgileEVM
metric set instead uses task points to calculate work progress and work hours as cost.
Using task points requires that each task is split to equal workload estimates. Otherwise
a closure of few tasks could mean a huge progress, which is not visible in metrics and vice

9 Lessons Learned from Pilot Implementation
9 Lessons Learned from Pilot Implementation
Based on the insight gained from the pilot implementation of the risk management and
AgileEVM cluster in Chapters 7 and 8, lessons learned for a successful deployment in a
standard process are derived. Firstly, the challenges faced during the pilot implementation
will be highlighted in Section 9.1. As part of the discussion, solutions for the pilot imple-
mentation and the impact with possible mitigation strategies for a standard process will
be presented. Of special importance in this context is the successful execution of a change
management process presented in Section 9.2. The implementation of a holistic metric
management system means a considerable change in current organizational practices. Al-
though the beneﬁts of the alternative system and a validation of the concept through a
pilot implementation were demonstrated, some parties inside the organization might be
resistant to a deployment of the new system. An eﬀective transition therefore needs to
identify resistant parties, understand their concerns, develop a strategy for considering
their inputs and turn resistance into support. In the light of change management, the
subsequent Section 9.3 examines the transfer of ownership. The challenge here is how a
collaboration model between the project organization and staﬀdepartments (like PMO)
can be designed.
Although one player needs to take over overall responsibility for the
metric management system, only the involvement of both, projects on the one hand and
staﬀdepartments on the other, will guarantee an eﬃcient and eﬀective metric management
process. Finally, in Section 9.4 a summary and discussion of the aforementioned points is
made.
9.1 Challenges Identiﬁed during Pilot Execution
The challenges identiﬁed during the pilot execution regard input data, interpretation of
metrics, process ownership and application of the metric management system.
Although input data for both metric clusters were available, the data quality of the risk
management template was much worse. Here, the data was collected manually in an Excel
ﬁle by the project managers. This caused mainly two problems. Firstly, important data
was missing and for the pilot dummy data had to be created. This issue might be reduced
if projects see the beneﬁts of metrics and realize the importance of high data quality. But
second and more important, only after a few months of deploying a new risk management
tracking template by the PMO, the project with the pilot was carried out customized the
template and changed it slightly. For the computation of metrics any individual modiﬁca-
tion by projects is harmful. Automated scripts for data collection and metric computation
might not work anymore and aggregation from project to program or portfolio level is
==PAGE:119==
9.2 Change Management for a Successful Roll-out
105
barley possible as no common standard is used. The AgileEVM metric cluster instead
builds upon data collected in ALM, which cannot as easily modiﬁed as an Excel sheet.
Issues regarding data were only encountered in a high deviation of workload estimates for
tasks. Thus, whenever possible Excel templates as used within risk management should
be transferred in a proper task respectively risk management software such as ALM, Jira
or others.
Incorporating feedback loops and understanding output of metrics is another key challenge
identiﬁed during the pilots. Although a ﬁrst prototype can already give an indication of
what the metric is going to measure and the output can be used for, revalidation is essential.
Together with the key stakeholders of the project the output of metrics has to be evaluated.
By identifying the cause of an indicator’s movement together with other metrics in the same
set, conclusions and general rules can be derived. In the AgileEVM cluster for example,
the metric set of burndown indicators with CPI and SPI provides various information.
Key insight can only be gained if resources are invested in understanding output and
establishing relationships between indicators and incidents.
The third major challenge faced in the pilot implementation is a lack of overall responsibil-
ity regarding metrics. As current metrics were deﬁned by various parties, ownership and
responsibility is spread among them. Especially for metrics not deﬁned by the PMO this
is a problem. Although they are perceived as very useful, such as burndown charts and
velocity reports, no standard across the organization is established. Regarding the pilot
implementation these diﬀerences became present. While the PMO is actively involved in
risk management metrics and had a huge interest in taking over responsibility for the newly
developed metric cluster, metrics of AgileEVM are taken over by the project organization
to improve their current metric set of burndown indicators and velocity reports. How-
ever, this hinders application across the organization and further improvement of metrics.
Aspects of a possible collaboration scenario are further discussed in Section 9.3.
The actual application of the metric management system with its objective catalogue is
the fourth key challenge. While the deﬁnition of suitable metrics and prototyping them
is important, the reassessment of metric objectives during and after the pilot is at least
equally necessary. The pilot implementation of the AgileEVM cluster has shown that most
of the metrics oﬀer additional insight. Unfortunately this was not the case for the release
date projection indicators. As a mandatory objective was not fulﬁlled, the metric must
not be implemented in a standard process. Also this is a valuable and desirable output of
a pilot process. A pilot is not only about prooﬁng a concept, its potential outcome should
be open and unbiased.
9.2 Change Management for a Successful Roll-out
The introduction of a new process or system can always be understood as a change of the
status quo. The deployment of the metric management system is such a change that needs

to be managed accordingly.
Although some are aware of the need to change, there is still considerable resistance in the
organization to move towards the new metric management system. This resistance became
present in the staﬀ, project and line organization throughout the development and pilot
phase of the metric management system.
Staﬀdepartments of PMO and EPG are currently focusing on increasing eﬃciency of their
deﬁned metrics by implementing an automated solution. Although an automated solution
increases eﬃciency, it is not guaranteed that the right metrics are used. The deployment of
the metric management system would reassess current indicators of PMO, EPG and QSW
with the result that some might be excluded from the metric set. This is a considerable
change, as currently no evaluation of metrics is made.
Also in the project organization resistance towards a new process exists. Project man-
agers tend to be very busy with their projects, are blocked in task forces and barely have
time for working on a holistic concept for metric management in projects. Thus, metrics
emerging from the project organization are often useful, but are not further developed and
maintained. Knowledge is shared on an informal basis and a transfer across departments
outside ultrasonic is even more diﬃcult. But still project managers are willing to give feed-
back on which indicators they perceive as useful. Yet, this feedback is not incorporated by
staﬀdepartments.
Staﬀin the line organization are also skeptical about metrics. Some of them argue that a
project manager does not require metrics, instead communication with project members
is the key success factor. Moreover, it is thought that most of the metrics are not useful
and only require extensive documentation. As these people are often responsible for the
documentation of various data also needed for metric computation, their views have to be
included in the change management process. For a successful deployment of the metric
management system, the beneﬁts and gains for them must be visible.
There is a considerable amount of theory of how to execute a change management process
and which steps have to be taken towards the deployment of a new system (see for example
Kotter (2007) and Heath and Heath (2010)).
These models give a thorough advice of
how to perform a change management process. Although this thesis does not include a
full implementation towards a standard process, in the following two major aspects for
managing change are highlighted.
The ﬁrst aspect is communication and feedback. Especially in a large organization with
various stakeholders it is important to communicate and explain thoroughly your plan.
Communication should not be one directional, instead feedback can contribute to further
improvements. However, one should be aware of not losing track of the formulated vision
and focus on the big picture.
For example was the PMO at the beginning concerned
that a parallel solution to the CPC Cockpit Chart would be the objective of the thesis.
Through communication and collaboration this fear was taken oﬀand instead valuable

input, especially for the risk management pilot was provided.
The second aspect is to show results. The metric management system is the major concept
the piloted metrics build on. But, to convince people of the beneﬁts and potential of such
a system the executed pilots were essential. The concept is only perceived as theory that
might work, whereas the metric pilots are tangible and can directly related to projects.
Thus, the acceptance rate and willingness to adapt the metric management system can
be considerably increased. Also the selection of the two implemented clusters was from
a strategical point of view reasonable. Risk management is a metric owned by the PMO
and usually project members are not largely aﬀected by it. However, the pilot created
an instant beneﬁt for the PMO, as the proposed solution builds up on an existing risk

as useful among project managers, which increases willingness to adapt to the new risk

perceived documentation of input data as a non value adding task, will likely acknowledge
the beneﬁts.
To sum up, change management needs to overcome resistance. The metric management
system as a holistic concept might not be perceived as useful in the ﬁrst place. It needs to
create tangible output that is seen as valuable among the diﬀerent stakeholder groups.In
this context the pilot implementation of deﬁned metric clusters is considered as the best
method to convince people from the beneﬁts of a holistic metric management system.
9.3 Transfer of Ownership
Transfer of ownership addresses the question of who will be responsible for the metric
management process. The key aspect is of how responsibility can be consolidated to deﬁne
standards, share knowledge across projects and increase overall organizational maturity
without blocking innovation.
As the PMO established with the CPC Cockpit Chart a software independent solution
that allows a consolidation and visualization of all metrics in one place for radar, video
and ultrasonic projects, overall responsibility should be within this department. The major
challenge will be not losing contact to the process experts in project teams.
Therefore, a collaboration model between PMO and projects should be established. Con-
sidering and actively listen to the feedback of process experts is of vital importance, as
processes or routines implemented against experts advice are usually doomed to fail. These
are perceived as non value adding and other tasks will be over prioritized in daily project
work. To enhance the collaboration model further, one could also think about promoting
job rotation between staﬀdepartments and the project organization.

9.4 Summary and Discussion
Lessons learned from the pilot implementation can be summarized as followed. First and
foremost, the successful execution of two metric clusters proofed the holistic concept of the
metric management system. But its deployment across the organization is considered as
a change process aﬀecting various stakeholders. In order to overcome possible resistance,
communication and producing tangible beneﬁts is key. These tangible beneﬁts are metric
clusters such as risk management or AgileEVM that enhance project management and
provide an instant value to stakeholders inside and outside the project.
In regard to
the process roll out, also a transfer of ownership of the metric management system has
to be considered. Based on the ﬁndings in the thesis, the PMO should take over overall
responsibility, but establish a collaboration model towards the project organization. Active
listening to feedback from process experts prevents the development of doomed to fail
solutions and promoting job rotation between projects and staﬀdepartments can further
enhance close collaboration.

10 Discussion, Limits and Next Steps
The following Chapter provides a discussion of results, points out limits of the developed
solution and addresses future topics. In Section 10.1 a discussion of the developed solution
and its limits based on the initially deﬁned research questions is carried out. Finally, in
Section 10.2 an outlook on possible future actions based on the developed solution and
beyond is given.
10.1 Discussion of Results and Limits of the Developed Solution
The initial objective of the thesis was to develop a real-time application based performance
indicator system in technical project management. This was divided into four research
questions, addressing the topics of state of the art practices in literature, a situation analysis
of the organization the thesis is carried out, a development of a holistic metric respectively
performance indicator system and a proof of concept through two pilot implementations.
RQ1 asked for "how is software development in the automotive industry performed and
which framework for performance measurement in such projects exist?". To answer this
question methods for software development were assessed towards their relevance for the
automotive industry. Moreover, a detailed view on project management, projects as part
of the organization and especially performance measurement in projects was taken. Knowl-
edge about concepts of software development and project management were the foundation
for an extensive situation and gap analysis, for identifying weak spots and ﬁelds of action.
Regarding performance measurement, three major aspects were investigated. Firstly, met-
ric deﬁnition and selection. The identiﬁed concepts provide an eﬀective approach to deﬁne
and identify suitable metrics for enhancing project management. Secondly, a variety of
metrics in SE and software development were assessed towards their potential applicabil-
ity. This provided a basic set of indicators, which later might be applied in the study.
Thirdly, to make metrics meaningful deﬁning threshold limits and visualization of indica-
tors is key. However, the aspect of automated data collection and metric computation was
found to be underrepresented in literature. Also, its relevance for enhancing usability was
not mentioned. Further investigation is needed to identify a possible research gap in this
ﬁeld.
RQ2 addressed the issue of "how is performance measurement at Robert Bosch Driver As-
sistance ultrasonic customer projects applied and which problems may arise?". Through an
analysis of key stakeholders, relevant parties in customer projects were identiﬁed, and their
importance was assessed. Through semi-structured interviews, observation of routines and
==PAGE:124==
110

process investigation a comprehensive situation analysis was carried out. It allowed not
only a mapping of the current metric deﬁnition process and an assessment of actual perfor-
mance indicators, more importantly, a gap and RCA on metrics and project management
was carried out. Although there exist some good metrics in projects and there are steps
taken towards a more eﬃcient metric process, the lack of a holistic metric management
system was obvious. The to be developed metric management system aims to combine
eﬀective metrics with eﬃcient tools to enhance project management and organizational
maturity.
RQ3 deﬁned the requirements for a metric management system by asking "how can a metric
management system be developed and implemented in the framework of project governance
at Robert Bosch Driver Assistance ultrasonic?".
Through a synthesis of the situation
analysis and the application of concepts in literature, an objective catalogue in a metric
management system is developed. Every objective deﬁnes a requirement in the metric
process. This ensures that only elements contributing value become a part of the metric
management system. One of the most important elements are the deﬁned use cases that
every metric must match. These reﬂect possible application scenarios for metrics. If a
metric cannot be matched to one of the four use cases, it will likely not be useful at
all. In total, the goals are clustered in six categories with a prioritization in mandatory,
supplementary and gold plating objectives. However, being not compliant with an objective
does not require abandoning the metric. The system follows an iterative approach that
helps to identify weaknesses before an implementation and develops the optimal solution
ready for deployment. Through this process, it is ensured that suitable metrics are selected,
and an assessment with continuous improvement loops towards a successful implementation
is made.
RQ4 dealt with the challenge of "how is an exemplary process from metric identiﬁcation to-
wards pilot implementation executed"?. Throughout the process of answering the question,
a concept validation of the metric management system should be performed and lessons
learned towards a deployment in a standard process shall be derived. Therefore, based on
the identiﬁed gaps in the current metric set and the deﬁned objective catalogue, ﬁve alter-
native metric clusters were developed. By principles of UX two of them, risk management
and AgileEVM were selected for implementation. After a roll-out preparation with the
identiﬁcation of suitable projects, the pilot was executed. The important aspect here was
that the metrics were integrated into the existing application environment to allow later
a fully automated data collection, metric computation, and visualization. Both metric
clusters oﬀered a completely new perspective and all proposed metrics, except one of the
AgileEVM, will be rolled-out in a standard process. The challenge for a successful long-
term deployment will be to overcome resistance and manage the change process accordingly.
The achievements of the pilot are an important building block for communicating the huge
beneﬁts and opportunities of a holistic metric management system. On the way, one im-
portant milestone is the consolidation of process ownership with the establishment of a
collaboration model between staﬀdepartments and the project organization.

Although the pilot proofed the success of the metric management system, it has to be
said that there are some limitations and diﬃcult steps ahead. The long-term success of
the metrics is closely connected to the holistic metric management system and its iter-
ative development approach. The eﬀort for the change management process towards its
deployment should not be underestimated. There need to be people in leading positions
of staﬀdepartments, the project and line organization convinced of the system and willing
to drive its deployment across the organization.
Moreover, the system can be prone to misuse and can create disbeneﬁts if not fully applied.
There is a risk of using the system for deﬁning and implementing new metrics, but skip
the reassessment and check if the metrics have the indented behavior. The same is likely
to happen with already deﬁned metrics, although they might not ﬁt the requirements in
the projects anymore. If the metric set is only inﬂated, additional non-value adding work
is created, and one cannot focus anymore on the metrics that matter.
Regarding the further implementation of the metrics, ﬁnancial resources have to be invested
for programming of automated software. Although the pilot included a detailed preparation
plan for the metrics and the ﬁnal application was already identiﬁed, the transfer requires
some resources. If the resources for an automated solution are not provided, the metric
cluster is doomed to fail. A manual process should only be applied during a pilot phase;
otherwise, long-term costs can outweigh the beneﬁts.
Also one may consider doing a
quantiﬁed cost-beneﬁt analysis before an implementation. Such a quantiﬁed calculation
was not part of the thesis, as it is believed that especially quantifying beneﬁts of a metric
can only be vague guesses.
Instead, costs mainly arise for programming tasks, which
might sum up to a few man days. It is assumed that through the enhancement of project
management and organizational maturity costs are compensated.
The positive evaluation of the performance indicators already leads towards a decision for
implementation of both metric clusters. The risk management metric cluster can directly
be implemented, and executives were delighted about the concept, its great applicability,
and the advancements it provides to the entire risk management. The PMO of DA took
over responsibility for implementation and presented the concept recently at an internal
conference with PMO participants from other business units.
Most likely, the metric
cluster will not only be deployed inside DA but also across further business units within
the company. The same holds true for AgileEVM metrics. Decision-makers and project
managers appreciate the high usability of the concept, its easy transfer in an automated
solution and the additional knowledge it contains.
The great advancement is seen in
the integration of schedule and cost (resp. workload) performance. There was no such
information available until now. The metric cluster will be deployed across various projects
within the ultrasonic product area, and it is likely that others will follow. Although no
ﬁnal decision regarding the full deployment of the metric management system is made, it is
assumed that the beneﬁts of the implemented clusters will shift focus again on the holistic
system. This will enhance chances towards a roll-out and continuous usage of the system.

10.2 Outlook
After the development of the metric management system and the proof of concept through
two successful pilot implementations, still steps have to be taken for long-term prosperity.
Figure 10.1 shows the major milestones and steps ahead on a short, mid and long-term
perspective for making the holistic performance indicator system a success.

As described earlier, on a short term basis of up to three months a responsible person
should be assigned for consolidating metric topics inside the organization. The ﬁrst task
would be to implement the piloted metric clusters in an automated system and develop
them in an iterative approach through feedback loops with process experts further. The
deployment of the two metric clusters in a standard process should be the start of the full
deployment of the metric management system.
Within the next three to twelve months a coalition among diﬀerent levels of staﬀdepart-
ments, project and line organization should be established to overcome possible resistance
inside the organization when deploying the metric management system. Managing change
and creating short-term wins by applying the metric management system are essential for
its roll-out. It will allow to design additional metric clusters and manage the metric port-
folio eﬀectively and eﬃciently. The further developed metric clusters should also address
white posts of metrics in program and portfolio management. As a ﬁrst step, selected met-
rics of project management, like the risk burndown, can be aggregated to this higher level.
The application on a portfolio level also includes the roll-out towards platform projects
and projects beyond ultrasonic or even driver assistance.
On a long-term perspective with a well established metric management system, the or-
ganization can develop capabilities regarding visual management and build up know-how
beyond declarative data visualizations. With the assessment of big data, steps towards
exploratory data analysis can be taken. This might provide insights which cannot be ob-
tained in any other way. Moreover, a metric strategy with overall objectives should be
formulated that guide the organization towards further development of the performance
indicator system.
