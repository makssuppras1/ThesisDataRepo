A B S T R A C T
In this study, the time course for primary biodegradation of a mixture
of 9 petroleum hydrocarbons (PHC) was measured at environmentally
relevant concentrations. Three surface freshwater samples were used as
inocula. The aim of the study was to determine degradation rate constants
and lag periods for the test substances, and to examine variations in
their biodegradability in the three inocula. The test substances were:
1,3,5-Trimethylcyclohexane, 2,3-Dimethylheptane, 1,2,4-Trimethylbenzene,
n-Decane, Decalin, Tetralin, Naphtalene, Biphenyl, and Bicyclohexyl. The
freshwater samples used as inocula were taken from three Danish streams
with different histories of exposure to PHCs; 1) Fønstrup Brook: a small,
pristine stream, regarded as not preexposed to PHCs, 2) Gadevangsren-
den: recipient of discharge from a wastewater treatment plant (WWTP),
regarded as partly preexposed as all test substances except the aromatics
are predicted to be removed in a WWTP, and 3) Harrestrup Stream: recip-
ient of surface run-off from two highways, regarded as preexposed to PHCs.
The performed biodegradation tests were shake ﬂask batch tests based on
OECD 309 Aerobic Mineralisation in Surface Water - Simulation Biodegradation
Test. The test was modiﬁed to handle volatile substances. Incubation was
done at 20 oC for up to 56 days. Partitioning based methods was employed
in several steps of the experimental procedure, which allowed for biodegra-
dation testing at environmentally relevant concentrations. The extent of
biodegradation was determined relative to parallel abiotic controls. By
ﬁtting a model to this normalised experimental data, the degradation rate
constant and lag period were determined for each substance in all inocula.
A general order of degradation was observed for the test substances: n-
Decane > Bicyclohexyl > 1,2,4-Trimethylbenzene ∼Biphenyl ∼Naphtalene
∼Tetralin > 2,4-Dimethylheptane > Decalin > 1,3,5-Trimethylcyclohexane.
This order of degradation between substances was maintained in the
three inocula. The lag periods were generally observed to be shorter in
Harrestrup inoculum than in Fønstrup and Gadevangsrenden inocula. Lag
periods were shorter in Gadevangsrenden inoculum than Fønstrup inocu-
lum for 4 substances, 3 of them aromatics. This indicates that preexposure
of a water sample to PHCs will increase the immediate potential of the
in-situ microbial population to utilise these. Degradation rate constants for
individual substances were within one order of magnitude in the three
inocula. The rate constants were generally larger in Harrestrup inoculum
than in Fønstrup and Gadevang inoculum. However, the difference in rate
constants had less impact on the overall lifespan of a substance in different
inocula than differences in length of the lag period.

I N T R O D U C T I O N
Petroleum hydrocarbons (PHC) are hydrophobic organic substances widely
found in the environment, released from both natural and anthropogenic
sources (Head et al., 2006). PHCs are undesirable in the environment
as they can be carcinogenic, mutagenic, and potentially bioaccumulative
(Sikkema et al., 1995; Bouchez et al., 1995; Haritash and Kaushik, 2009).
Biodegradation is the main process for transformation of hydrocarbons in
the environment (Röling et al., 2002; Head et al., 2006).
Biodegradation of hydrocarbons was investigated as early as in 1913
(Head et al., 2006). In the 1970s and 1980s a new wave of research occurred,
which is summarised in several reviews (see e.g. Atlas (1981) and Leahy and
Colwell (1990)). Since then, biodegradation of PHCs has been an ongoing
research topic with the main focus on diversity of microbial populations,
community dynamics, and identiﬁcation of bacterial strains responsible
for PHC degradation (Macnaughton et al., 1999; Röling et al., 2002; Van
Hamme et al., 2003; Head et al., 2006; Kostka et al., 2011). Yet, the response
of indigenous bacterial communities to PHCs under in-situ conditions is
still poorly understood (Kostka et al., 2011).
The persistence of PHCs is related to the presence of speciﬁc hydro-
carbon degraders, which exist in most environments. The density of speciﬁc
degraders within a given environment will increase when exposed to
PHCs (Caparello and Larock, 1975; Leahy and Colwell, 1990; Macnaughton
et al., 1999; Kostka et al., 2011). This is due to several mechanisms such
as the activation or increase of enzyme production, interchange of genes
between microorganisms, and population mutations. The overall effect
is an improved capability of that microbial population to immediately
utilise PHCs (Van Hamme et al., 2003; Khomenkov et al., 2008). This might
for instance manifest itself in a shortening of the lag period for PHCs in
environments that were previously exposed to hydrocarbons (Caparello
and Larock, 1975; Toräng and Nyholm, 2005). Various environmental factors
such as temperature, nutrient availability, pH and oxygen concentration
are of importance for the welfare and functionality of the microorganisms
(Atlas, 1981; Van Hamme et al., 2003; Head et al., 2006).
In accordance with the current European regulation of chemicals, Regis-
tration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Resctriction of Chemicals (REACH),
environmental assessment of chemicals is based on single substances
(ECHA, 2009). However, PHCs rarely appear as single substances but
rather in mixtures. The degradation of these mixtures involves many
complex processes. Some PHCs will compete for enzymatic sites, while
some are only degraded either in the presence of or simultaneously with
other PHCs (Horvath, 1972; Bouchez et al., 1995; Van Hamme et al., 2003;
Rojo, 2009). These mechanisms among others give rise to certain patterns
in biodegradation of PHC mixtures. Several studies have observed large

variations in susceptibility of different PHC groups towards biodegradation.
This is expressed in a recurring (but not universal) order in degradation of
PHC groups (Van Hamme et al., 2003).
Previously, much attention has been given to biodegradation of PHCs
in marine and terrestrial environments. However, substantial amounts
of PHCs are released into freshwater systems, e.g. through wastewater
discharges, surface runoff and atmospheric deposition (Latimer et al., 1990;
Rocher et al., 2004; Azimi et al., 2005). Biodegradation studies of these
dissolved concentrations are important, as some hydrocarbons might only
be available for biotransformation when dissolved in the aqueous phase
(Sikkema et al., 1995). One of the challenges of studying biodegradation of
PHCs in freshwater systems is that they appear only at very low concentra-
tion due to their hydrophobicity. In the past few decades, new laboratory
methods have emerged that provide new possibilities for studying biodegra-
dation of PHCs at environmentally relevant concentrations. Employing
these methods in simulation tests with environmental water samples
as inocula might offer insights into the ability of different indigenous
microbial populations to utilize hydrocarbons, while maintaining many of
the physical and chemical characteristics of the natural environment.
1.1
aim and working hypotheses
The ﬁrst purpose of this study is to determine lag periods and biodegrada-
tion rate constants for a mixture composed of 9 petroleum hydrocarbons
freely dissolved in three inocula. The inocula are freshwater samples
taken from three Danish watercourses with different histories of PHC
exposure. Partitioning based methods are employed in several steps of
the experimental procedure, which allows for biodegradation testing of
the PHCs at environmentally relevant concentrations. Another aim of the
study is to get an indication of possible variations in biodegradation in
the three inocula caused by differences in locational origin and previous
PHC exposure. Thus it will be examined if the biodegradation kinetics for
the test substances, i.e. the degradation rate constant and length of the lag
period, varies in the different inocula. It is also examined whether the order
of degradation of the test substances varies between inocula.
The work is based on the following hypotheses:
• The order of degradation between substances is consistent in the three
inocula in spite of different preexposure to petroleum hydrocarbons
• Lag periods are shorter in inocula that have previously been exposed
to petroleum hydrocarbons compared to inocula with no recent history
of PHC contamination
• Biodegradation rate constants for single substances are similar (within
1 order of magnitude) in the three inocula in spite of different preex-
posure to petroleum hydrocarbons.

2.1
regulation and testing of chemicals
Chemicals are used to a large extent in the modern society, many of which
end up in the environment where they have undesirable effects. As a
consequence regulation of chemicals is necessary. The current European
legislation on chemicals is Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and
Restriction of Chemicals (REACH), which became effective in 2007 (Euro-
pean Comission, 2015).
REACH builds on a "no data no market" concept, which means that
the responsibility for collection of data and documentation of possible
environmental impacts lies with the industry (European Comission, 2015).
This implies that a Chemical Safety Assessment (CSA) is required for
chemical substances manufactured or imported above a certain tonnage
(ECHA, 2009). A CSA begins with a collection and evaluation of all relevant
data and a hazard assessment. If a substance is classiﬁed as dangerous
or persistent/bioaccumulative/toxic, an exposure assessment has to be be
conducted and combined with the hazard assessment to form an overall
risk characterization (ECHA, 2009).
A hazard assessment is based on predictions of the toxicity, behaviour,
and fate of a substance in the environment. While partitioning behaviour
of a substance can be estimated based on various intrinsic physical
chemical properties, degradation is a key parameter for predicting loss
and transformation, and thus eventual fate, of that substance (ECHA,
2014). Degradation can be divided into abiotic degradation (e.g. hydrolysis
and photolysis) and biodegradation. Degradation data is obtained by
standardised OECD screening or higher tiered tests. For biodegradation
these tests can be divided into tests for ready biodegradability, tests for
inherent biodegradability and simulation tests (ECHA, 2014; Comber and
Holt, 2010). Tests for ready biodegradability are stringent screening tests
using unadapted microorganisms. OECD 301 Ready Biodegradability offers
six methods for ready biodegradability testing with a pass level of 70% re-
moval of dissolved organic carbon (DOC) or 60% mineralisation in a 10-day
window within a 28-day period (OECD, 1992). Inherent biodegradability
test are used to determine whether a substance is inherently biodegradable
and not persistent. Tests for inherent biodegradability includes OECD 302
Inherent Biodegradability (Comber and Holt, 2010; ECHA, 2014). Simulation
tests are used to obtain information about biodegradation in speciﬁc
environments, and include OECD 303 Aerobic Sewage Treatment, OECD 306
Biodegradability in Seawater and OECD 309 Aerobic Mineralisation in Surface
waters - Simulation Biodegradation tests (ECHA, 2014).

2.2
petroleum hydrocarbons
Petroleum contains a vast amount of substances, the major part of these
being hydrocarbons. Petroleum hydrocarbons can generally be divided
into the following subgroups: linear alkanes (n-alkanes), branched alkanes
(isoalkanes), cyclic alkanes (cyclo-alkanes), aromatic hydrocarbons, and
asphaltenes. The hydrocarbon composition of these subgroups can vary
highly between different types of petroleum and petroleum products
(Marchal et al., 2003; Head et al., 2006; Atlas, 1981).
Petroleum hydrocarbons enter into the environment from various sources.
Media attention is often on large marine oil spills, however small point
and nonpoint (diffuse) sources contribute substantial amounts of HCs to
both marine and freshwater ecosystems (Latimer et al., 1990). Some of
the signiﬁcant exposure pathways are particles in surface runoff (Latimer
et al., 1990; Rocher et al., 2004), atmospheric deposition (Latimer et al., 1990;
Azimi et al., 2005), and wastewater discharges (Latimer et al., 1990; Rocher
et al., 2004). While particles can act as carrier for PHCs, they are also a
major sink for PHCs in the aquatic environment; where the more volatile
PHCs tend to evaporate (Jaffé, 1991), the less volatile PHCs will sorb to
sediments (Latimer et al., 1990; Rocher et al., 2004; Hilscherova et al., 2007)
thereby decreasing the bioavailability and increasing the persistence of
the PHCs in the environment. However, the presence of dissolved organic
matter and some anthropogenic surfactants can increase the water solubility
of the PHCs (Jaffé, 1991) and thus increase their bioavailability. The most
common fate of PHCs in the aquatic environment is biodegradation (Head
et al., 2006; Marchal et al., 2003; Röling et al., 2002). PHCs can also undergo
photolysis (Yang et al., 2015) while they are insusceptible to hydrolysis
(Howard et al., 2005).
2.2.1
Test substances
Nine test substances are selected for the biodegradation test. 1-octanol is
used as reference control to conﬁrm that the inoculum is biologically active.
All test substances have 8 to 12 carbons atoms but very different molecu-
lar structures. They represent most classes of petroleum hydrocarbon sub-
groups; linear alkanes (Decane), branched alkanes (2,3-dimethylheptane),
cyclic alkanes (1,2,5-Trimethylcyclohexane, Decalin, Bicyclohexyl), and aro-
matic hydrocarbons (1,2,4-Trimethylbenzene, Tetralin, Naphtalene, and
Biphenyl). The test substances are listed in Table 1 with molecular struc-
tures and formulas, in Table 2 with selected physico-chemical data, and in

Biodegradation is a complex process composed of many subprocesses
that all contribute to the overall biodegradation rate. For example, the
bioavailability of a substance or rate of uptake by microorganisms can
be limited due to the nature of a substance, e.g. if it is very volatile
or hydrophobic, but also by the uptake mechanisms and enzymes that
microorganisms possess to address that speciﬁc substance (Leahy and
Colwell, 1990). Whereas the intrinsic properties of a substance cannot be
changed, microorganisms can adapt (cf. section 2.3.1). External factors, such
as the availability of oxygen or nutrients, can also affect the biodegradation
rate (cf. section 2.4.1).

2.3.1
Microorganisms and petroleum hydrocarbons
Microorganisms play the lead role in biodegradation when they use the
substances in question as substrates. The microbial physiological responses
to hydrocarbons and related population changes are in no way trivial; full
reviews and several studies have been dedicated to this (e.g. Van Hamme
et al. (2003), Head et al. (2006), Wentzel et al. (2007), Kostka et al. (2011), and
Macnaughton et al. (1999)). Understanding these mechanisms is beyond
the scope of this study, and thus only a brief overview of some important
mechanisms are given in the following paragraphs.
When encountering a chemical substance, a single microorganism might re-
act with an increase in amount of enzyme, either by reactivating "sleeping"
genes (induction) or by activating and increasing the amount of existing
enzymes. The enzymes can already be in place in the microorganism or they
can emerge as result of mutations (Van Hamme et al., 2003; Khomenkov
et al., 2008). Within a population microorganisms can also exchange e.g.
genes and biosurfactants useful to degrade certain substances (Leahy and
Colwell, 1990; Head et al., 2006; Khomenkov et al., 2008; Kostka et al.,
2011). Microbial strains can cooperate in the degradation of hydrocarbons
(Marchal et al., 2003).
On a populational level, exposure to PHCs trigger an increase in speciﬁc
hydrocarbon degraders, potentially at the expense of other microorganisms.
This can result in a change of population composition, and potentially
also in an overall increase in microbial density. The microbial density can
be orders of magnitude higher in environmental samples preexposed to
petroleum hydrocarbons than in comparably unpolluted samples (Leahy
and Colwell, 1990; Van Hamme et al., 2003; Kostka et al., 2011; Mac-
naughton et al., 1999; Caparello and Larock, 1975).
Many hydrocarbons are toxic to cells and can only be biodegraded at
concentrations substantially below their saturation points. At higher
concentrations the substance might simply inhibit and deactivate the cell
(Sikkema et al., 1995; Bouchez et al., 1995). Degradation pathways can also
be inhibited if the concentration of degradation products exceed certain
levels (product repression) (Rojo, 2009). Not all microorganisms are capable
of adapting to hydrocarbons (Sikkema et al., 1995). Sometimes presence or
absence of single bacterial strain can determine whether a certain metabolic
pathway is possible or not (Bouchez et al., 1995).
biodegradation of mixtures
Many complex mechanisms occur
during biodegradation of hydrocarbon mixtures. A widely observed
phenomena is co-metabolism where a less biodegradable hydrocarbon
is degraded simultaneously with another hydrocarbon used as substrate
(Horvath, 1972; Bouchez et al., 1995; Van Hamme et al., 2003). Most
bacterial strains can perform co-metabolism, which widens the range of
hydrocarbons that can be attacked by that strain (Bouchez et al., 1995).
Hydrocarbons can also compete for the same enzymatic sites (competitive
inhibition). Thus degradation of a hydrocarbon might be inhibited by
the presence of a less hydrophobic hydrocarbon, or a hydrocarbon more
==PAGE:20==
8
background
favoured as substrate (Rojo, 2009; Bouchez et al., 1995; Van Hamme et al.,
2003). This inhibition can occur whether co-metabolism takes place or not
(Bouchez et al., 1995).
While degradation of some hydrocarbons might be accelerated or lagged by
the presence of other hydrocarbons, this does not seem to have a substantial
effect on the kinetics of degradation in the exponential degradation phase
(Yerushalmi and Guiot, 1998; Solano-Serena et al., 1999). However, there
seem to be a lack of research in this ﬁeld.
Microorganisms exist that can metabolise several types of hydrocar-
bons, however a mixed microbial population is still essential to degrade
more complex mixtures of PHCs (Leahy and Colwell, 1990).
2.3.2
Model
Mathematically, biodegradation rate constants and degradation half lives
can be determined by ﬁtting a nonlinear model to an experimental data
set. As mentioned, several factors affect the rate of biodegradation. If
the bioavailability of the substance does not limit the biodegradation,
the enzyme activity and the rate of microbial growth will determine the
biodegradation rate (Schwarzenbach et al., 2003). However, for simplicity,
the biodegradation is in this project looked at as a black box where only
the removal of "mother compound" is considered. At low concentrations
(≤100µg/L) the biodegradation process can thus be approximated by ﬁrst-
order kinetics (OECD, 2004; Schwarzenbach et al., 2003):
dmHC

= −k · mHC
(2.1)
where mHC is the mass of a hydrocarbon in the test system, t is the incu-

degradation rate in the test system. By separation of variables and integra-
tion from t0 = 0 to t and from m0 to m, equation 2.2 is obtained:
mHC(t) = mHC,0 · e−k·t
(2.2)
This equation describes the exponential degradation phase of biodegrada-

apparent degradation, a lag period, before the exponential degradation be-

section 2.3.3.
some remarks
"True" ﬁrst-order kinetics as described above are rather
idealized, as complete independence of the degradation rate constant in
relation to time and concentration is required. Deviation from "true" ﬁrst
order kinetics is seen for example if some processes such as diffusion or
sorption/desorption affect the bioavailability and becomes the rate limiting
step (Van Hamme et al., 2003; OECD, 2004; Jaffé, 1991). However, OECD
(2004) states that pseudo ﬁrst-order kinetics with a concentration dependent
degradation rate constant is broadly applicable.

The degradation rate constant, k, in equations 2.1-2.2 represent the
overall degradation rate in the system, i.e. the distribution of test sub-
stances in the test system is not taken into account. For semi-volatile and
volatile substances, measures must be taken to assure that transfer over the
water/air boundary does not become limiting (Solano-Serena et al., 1999).
Toräng and Nyholm (2005) observed a shift in biodegradation kinetics
at a certain concentration threshold, arguing that below this threshold a
lack of microbial growth caused a much slower degradation than otherwise
observed. Degradation above the threshold adhered to the above described
kinetics. For four analysed hydrocarbons the observed adaption thresholds
were in the concentration range 0.5-2 µg/L. This potential effect should be
kept in mind when designing an experimental set-up. However, if concen-
tration are above a certain level, toxic inhibition might occur (Bouchez et al.,
1995; Sikkema et al., 1995).
In some cases degradation can occur at a slow, non-adapted rate be-
fore onset of the exponential degradation phase (Toräng and Nyholm, 2005).
In the chosen model no distinction is made between this phase and the lag
period.
2.3.3
Preexposure: effect on biodegradation kinetics
In accordance with the adaption mechanisms of microorganisms mentioned
in Section 2.3.1, several studies observe that microbial populations in
natural environments (water or sediment) preexposed to PHCs exhibit
an immediate higher potential to oxidize these compared to populations
in comparably unpolluted environments (Caparello and Larock, 1975;
Atlas, 1981; Marchal et al., 2003; Van Hamme et al., 2003; Powell et al.,
2007). However it is often not speciﬁed whether observed increases in
degradation rates (i.e. % removal within a certain amount of time) is due
to larger degradation rate constants or merely caused by shorter lag periods.
Some studies have observed a shortening of the lag period as result
of pre-adaption to PHCs, but without any substantial change of the degra-
dation rate constant (Prince et al., 2007; Caparello and Larock, 1975). The
decrease in length of lag period can be ascribed to an increase in number of
speciﬁc degraders and other mechanisms such as induction and increased
amounts of enzymes as described in Section 2.3.1 (Caparello and Larock,
1975; Leahy and Colwell, 1990; Wood et al., 1995). While preexposure
to hydrocarbons might increase the microbial density, an increase in
microbial density is not alone comparable to an increased capability to
degrade hydrocarbons. For example, examination of water downstream
of a wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) showed a substantially higher
microbial density compared to an unpolluted sample, but no changes were
observed in size of the carbon utilizing population (Caparello and Larock,
1975).

2.3.4
Batch test considerations
Some possible issues have been observed for biodegradation batch tests.
For example, the oxygen content should be high enough for the test en-
vironment to stay aerobic throughout the experiment, and nutrient contents
should be of a size where it does not become limiting for the microbial
growth (cf. section 2.4.1) (Toräng and Nyholm, 2005; OECD, 2004). Another
potential problem with aquatic batch tests is that the lag period is highly
variable and irreproducible due to a various factors such as the nutrient
content, temperature and a too small test volume, and that sometimes the
microorganisms even fail to adapt (Toräng and Nyholm, 2005).
2.3.5
Passive dosing
The hydrophobicity of petroleum hydrocarbons poses a challenge in
aqueous biodegradation testing when the test substances have to be
dissolved in the water phase (Smith et al., 2010). A common approach is
to use solvents (OECD, 2004), however, a relatively novel method, passive
dosing, enables preparation of a stock solution by use of partitioning based
methods. The principle behind passive dosing is to utilize the distribution
of a substance between a ﬂuid and a passive dosing phase. The passive
dosing phase is loaded with test substances and brought into contact with
the ﬂuid whereby a part of the test substances sorbed in the passive dosing

that it is solvent-free, simple and low-cost, and that the freely dissolved
concentration is easily controlled by changing the concentration in the
passive dosing phase (Gouliarmou et al., 2012; Smith et al., 2010).
If the passive dosing phase is a silicone and the ﬂuid is water, the
equilibrium partitioning can be described by a linear sorption isotherm

2.4
inoculum
An environmental sample used as inocolum will contain an indigenous mi-
crobial population. In addition to this the inoculum will have some physical
and chemical characteristics that might enhance or limit the biodegradation
process. This is examined in the following paragraphs.
2.4.1
Physical and chemical characteristics
Various physical and chemical characteristics of the inoculum can affect the
biodegradation of hydrocarbons (Tyagi et al., 2011; Leahy and Colwell, 1990).
The most important of these are brieﬂy discussed in the following para-
graphs.
temperature
Temperature affects the physical behaviour of HCs and
thus potentially the bioavailability and uptake rate (Leahy and Colwell,
1990). It also affects the welfare of a microbial population and thus their
ability to grow and to utilise substrates (Tyagi et al., 2011). Hence, a de-
crease in incubation temperature can lead to a decrease of the degradation
rate constant and longer lag periods (Leahy and Colwell, 1990; Bagi et al.,
2014). However, the season of sampling, and thus the sampling temperature,
does not affect the degradation rate constant for a substance at a certain in-
cubation temperature (Prince et al., 2007). The lag period on the other hand
might highly depend on the season of sampling (Toräng and Nyholm, 2005).
nutrients
Nutrients, mainly nitrogen and phosphorous, can be limit-
ing for biodegradation of petroleum hydrocarbons (Head et al., 2006; Leahy
and Colwell, 1990; Van Hamme et al., 2003; Röling et al., 2002), however this
is mainly when PHCs occur in high amounts in oligotrophic environments
such as some marine environments and some freshwater lakes. At low con-
centrations of HCs, nutrient addition above a certain point will not enhance
the degradation further (Röling et al., 2002). Though addition of nutrients
have been shown to speed up the degradation process, the overall "extent"
of degradation might not be affected in the long run (Head et al., 2006).
Some nutrient concentrations might facilitate the growth of certain speciﬁc
degraders. (Head et al., 2006)
suspended solids
Hydrocarbons are hydrophobic substances with a
high tendency to sorb to particulate matter. A high level of suspended solids
in an inoculum will thus affect the bioavailability of the hydrocarbons (La-
timer et al., 1990; Rocher et al., 2004).
ph
As most microorganisms in natural environments are adapted to neu-
tral pH, a large deviation from this will affect their functionality and viabil-
ity, thus affecting their ability to degrade hydrocarbons (Leahy and Colwell,
1990; Tyagi et al., 2011).

2.5.1
Gas Chromatography - Mass Spectrometry
A gas chromatograph - mass spectrometer (GC-MS) is an instrument for
chemical analysis that enables separation and analysis of substances in a
complex mixture. It consists of the following important steps and compo-
nents (Harris, 2007):
• sample preparation and injection
• column and column oven
• detector: mass spectrometer
sample preparation and injection
Sample preparation is essen-
tial for succesful chromatography, and there are several possible methods
(Harris, 2007). Solid-phase Micro Extraction (SPME) is a superior choise
when dealing with low concentrations of analytes as it combines extraction,
concentration and puriﬁcation of the analytes (Salquèbre et al., 2012; Ulrich,
2000). Using an autosampler for the injection will improve the injection
==PAGE:26==
14
background
precision with a factor 3-10 compared to manual injection (Harris, 2007).
With SPME, analytes can be extracted without the use of solvents; a
syringe needle with a polymer-coated ﬁber inside is used to penetrate
the septum of a test vial. When inside the test vial the ﬁber is moved
into position in the aqueous sample or in the headspace of the vial, and
the analytes are sorbed onto the ﬁber. The extraction is done at a ﬁxed
temperature for a ﬁxed time under continuous agitation, and the mass of
analytes sorbed on the ﬁber is proportional to the concentration of these
analytes in the sample. The ﬁber is retracted into the syringe needle before
the needle is extracted from the vial and moved to the injector port. Here
the ﬁber is exposed to a carrier gas for a speciﬁed amount of time for the
analytes to desorb. After desorption the ﬁber is retracted into the needle
and removed from the injector port (Ulrich, 2000; Harris, 2007).
For an optimal result, the time required for the ﬁber to become equi-
librated with analytes should be determined experimentally. Also, the
stationary phase (ﬁber) should be chosen based on type of analytes
("like-dissolves-like" principle), and if using head space SPME (HS-SPME)
the liquid sample should occupy approximately 2/3 of the vial to optimize
extraction efﬁciency (Harris, 2007).
column and column oven
When the mixture of analytes are intro-
duced in the column they are carried by a mobile phase, i.e. an inert or non-
reactive carrier gas, through the column. In the column there is a stationary
phase to which the analytes are sorbed, thus retarding the movement of the
analytes. For a non-polar stationary phase the retention time is governed by
the volatility of the analytes and they will more or less leave the column in
order of increasing boiling point. Retention times of the most retarded an-
alytes can be reduced by a stepwise increase of temperature in the column
during the run. For a ﬁxed column length and gas ﬂow rate, the analytes
will each have a characteristic retention time, which can be used to identify
the analyte. (Harris, 2007)
detector
GC can be coupled with various detectors depending on the
scope of the analysis. In this case focus is on the mass spectrometer (MS).
A mass spectrometer identiﬁes the spectrum of a chromatographic peak
from a library of spectra (Harris, 2007). These spectra are the product of
ionized substances being accelerated through an electrical ﬁeld and divided
based on their mass-to-charge ratio, m/z, which yields a plot of detector
response vs. m/z. In this plot, the area of each peak is proportional to
abundance of a speciﬁc isotope. The integrated values can be transformed
into computer-generated bar-graphs showing relative abundance vs. m/z,
and these are the spectra used to identify certain analytes (Harris, 2007).
Combining the characteristic retention time of the GC with the characteristic
mass spectrum of the MS allows for very precise substance identiﬁcation,
even in mixtures with many components. (Harris, 2007) The response of
the analytes measured at the detector is stored in a computer.

3.5
chemical analysis
The chemical analysis was carried out on a commercial GC-MS (Agilent
Technologies 7890B/5877A GC/MSD) with a PAL RSI 85 auto sampler as
shown in Figure 2d. Sample extraction and injection was done with SPME
and fully automated. For this, the auto sampler needle was installed with
an 100µm PDMS ﬁbre. Helium gas was used as mobile phase with a ﬂow of
1.2mL/min.
The set of vials to be analysed was placed on the sample tray. The
auto sampler "arm" would place the ﬁrst vial to be analysed in an agitator
station for 20 minutes for it to get a temperature of 35oC. When the samples
were acclimatized, the septum was pierced by the syringe and the ﬁbre was
introduced in the head space for 10 minutes under gentle agitation. After
completed extraction the needle was transferred to the injector port and the
ﬁbre exposed to the carrier gas for 5 minutes at a temperature of 250oC.
The temperature in the column was kept at 35oC for 6 minutes and
then raised to 300oC at a rate of 15oC/min. The carrier gas stream was split,
and one part was led to a ﬂame ionization detector (FID) and the other part
to a MS detector with selected ion monitoring. The detected signals were
stored in an attached computer.
quality assurance
Due to the volatile nature of the test substances no
internal standard was added to the vials before chemical analysis. Instead,
in order to identify possible variations in the instrument response, vials con-
taining silicone oils with test substances in known amounts were analysed
each time an analysis was conducted. These silicone oils were used to cor-
rect response measured in test vials and abiotic controls, thereby correcting
for small ﬂuctuations in e.g. detector sensibility or carrier gas ﬂow.

D I S C U S S I O N
5.1
sequence of degradation
The ﬁrst working hypothesis was that the order of degradation between
substances is consistent in the three inocula in spite of differences in
petroleum hydrocarbon preexposure. The order of degradation between
substances can be interpreted in different ways. If it is construed as the
relation between rate constants for a range of substances in an inoculum,
Figure 5 and 6 clearly illustrate that the sequence of degradation between
test substances is consistent in the three inocula. With this deﬁnition,
the following sequence of degradation rate constants is observed for the
9 test substances: n-Decane > Naphtalene ∼1,2,4-Trimethylbenzene ∼
Biphenyl > 2,3-Dimethylheptane ∼Bicyclohexyl ∼Tetralin > Decalin >
1,3,5-Trimethylcyclohexyl.
If sequence of degradation between substances is interpreted as the
relation between the length of lag period for a group of substances, or
the time at which each substance is removed by a certain amount, the
observed sequence is slightly different. Based on length of the lag pe-
riod, the observed sequence of degradation is: n-Decane > Bicyclohexyl
> 1,2,4-Trimethylbenzene ∼Biphenyl ∼Naphtalene ∼Tetralin > 2,4-
Dimethylheptane > Decalin > 1,3,5-Trimethylcyclohexane. Though slightly
different than the sequence of degradation based on the rate constants, this
sequence is also more or less consistent in the three inocula.
With the above two observations in mind, the sequence of degrada-
tion between substances seems to be generally consistent in the three
inocula in spite of differences in their previous exposure to petroleum
hydrocarbons. This indicates that mechanisms taking place during degra-
dation of hydrocarbon mixtures, such as co-metabolism, induction, and
inhibition, are more determining for the observed sequence of degradation
than the presence or absence of certain microbial strains.
As described in Section 2.3, an often observed sequence of hydrocar-
bon biodegradation is n-alkanes > isoalkanes > low-molecular-weight
aromatics > monoaromatics > cyclic alkanes > polycyclic aromatics (Van
Hamme et al., 2003). Independent of whether the order of degradation
between the test susbtances is based on the lag period or the degradation
rate constants, it roughly adhere to this overall sequence. There is a
small deviation as the aromatics generally seem to be degraded before
the isoalkane (2,3-Dimethylheptane), even Naphtalene which is a simple
polycyclic aromatic. Two out of the three cyclic alkanes, namely Decalin and
1,3,5-Trimethylcyclohexane, are degraded last in all inocula. The third cyclic
alkane, Bicyclohexyl, is more or less degraded simultaneously with the
aromatics. Some of this observed difference might be related to the different

phase partitioning of aromatic vs aliphatic compounds. However, these
small nonconformities do not challenge the generally observed sequence, as
this study only covers a small number of test substances. That cycloalkanes
are generally degraded last might be ascribed to their high potential for
toxic inhibition (Sikkema et al., 1995).
5.2
lag phase
CFU is not a direct measure of the overall microbial population, however it
is a good indicator of the microbial activity. As described in Section 2.3.3,
the initial microbial density in a sample might directly relate to the length
of the lag period with a higher density resulting in a shorter lag period
and vice versa (Caparello and Larock, 1975). This seems to be consistent
with all test substances, except n-Decane and Bicyclohexyl, having shorter
lag periods in Harrestrup inoculum, which had an initial CFU count more
than 5 times the number observed for Fønstrup and Gadevang inocula.
For n-Decane and Bicyclohexyl the lag period was 0 days or close to 0
days in all inocula. The higher number of CFUs in Harrestrup inoculum
can most likely be attributed to an overall high degree of pollution, with
hydrocarbons and other substances as well.
In cases where the overall microbial density is not changed by preex-
posure to hydrocarbons, the fraction of speciﬁc degraders within that
population might still be increased, as described in Section 2.3.3(Van
Hamme et al., 2003; Caparello and Larock, 1975; Kostka et al., 2011).
In relation to this it is noteworthy that three of the four substances for
which the lag period is visibly shorter in Gadevang than in Fønstrup are
aromatics, as EPI BIOWIN predictions stated in National Food Institute
(DTU Food) (2015) suggest that all test substances except the aromatics will
be extensively removed in a WWTP. If the intial bacterial density alone
would determine the length of the lag period for a certain substance, no
or only slight differences should be observed between lag periods for test
substances in Fønstrup and Gadevang inocula, and the difference should
be consistent. However, the lag period appears to be different in the two
inocula mainly for substances that belong to a group of hydrocarbons which
might not be removed in a WWTP and thus to which Gadevangsrenden
inoculum is likely to be preexposed (if not to the exact substances then to
other aromatics). This indicates that although the initial number of CFUs in
Gadevangsrenden inoculum was just 25% higher than in the comparatively
unpolluted sample, Fønstrup Brook, Gadevangsrenden inoculum contained
speciﬁc degraders capable of degrading aromatics. This is the opposite as
was reported by Caparello and Larock (1975), where a higher microbial
density was observed downstream of a WWTP, but with no increased
potential do degrade petroleum hydrocarbons.
To sum up, on the assumption that Harrestrup incoulum is pre-exposed to
the test substances, Fønstrup is not pre-exposed, and Gadevang inoculum is
partly pre-exposed, the observed interrelationship between lag periods for
the test substances in the three inocula support the working hypothesis that
lag periods are shorter in inocula preexposed to petroleum hydrocarbons
compared to inocula with no recent history of exposure.

5.3 degradation rate constants

5.3
degradation rate constants
The difference in degradation rate constants between substances is percep-
tible; from the slowest degraded test substance, 1,3,5-Trimethylcyclohexane,
to the fastest degraded test substance, n-Decane, the span of degradation
rate constants is about 3 orders of magnitude.
The difference in degradation rate constant for the single substances
between incoula is also apparent from Figure 5, and the observed difference
was substantiated by a one-way ANOVA with repeated measures showing
the difference to be statistical signiﬁcant. Nonetheless the difference for
individual substances is still within one order order of magnitude as
illustrated in Figure 5 and 6.
More than half of the test substances (n-Decane, Naphtalene, Biphenyl,
1,2,4-Trimethylbenzene, and 2,3-Dimethylheptane) have rate constants
equalling half lives of about 1 day or less, while Tetralin, Bicyclohexyl
and Decalin have half lives ranging from 1 to 4 days in the three inocula.
For these substances, the effect of the difference in rate constant between
inocula, in terms of actual impact on the lifespan of the substance, might
not be very substantial compared to the effect of the lag period. However
for 1,3,5-Trimethylcyclohexane, which has a rate constant equalling to a half
life of 8-9 days in Harrestrup inoculum compared to 20 days in Gadevang
inoculum, a difference in rate constant of one order of magnitude might
actually mean a substantial difference in lifespan of that substance in
different inocula.
5.4
predicted vs. observed degradation
As described in the previous paragraph, all test substances except 1,3,5-
Trimethylcyclohexane have degradation rate constants equalling to half
lives of less than 4 days in the three inocula. This is not in good accord
with the EPI BIOWIN predictions listed in Table 3; for Tetralin and
1,2,4-Trimethylbenzene the observed and predicted half lives are more or
less consistent, and for 1,3,5-Trimethylcyclohexane the half life observed
in Harrestrup inoculum is consistent with the predicted half life whereas
the half life observed in Gadevang inoculum is higher than the predicted.
However, for all other substances the observed half life is 1-2 orders
of magnitude lower than predicted. This discrepancy can potentially be
coupled to mechanisms observed to become effective in biodegradation of
hydrocarbon mixtures, such as co-oxidation and induction, see also Section
2.3.3) (Horvath, 1972; Van Hamme et al., 2003; Khomenkov et al., 2008).
Needless to say, these mechanisms do not occur when testing substances
individually, which might be the reason for the predicted half lives for
the single substances being higher than the ones observed for the test
substances degraded in a mixture. Another possible explanation is that
the concentration ranges used in this study are lower than for the ones
used to generate the EPI BIOWIN model, thereby lowering the risk of toxic
inhibition, or it can be due to concentration dependency of the rate constant
(Yerushalmi and Guiot, 1998). It is also possible that the model simply gives
==PAGE:50==
38
discussion
conservative estimates.
As shown in Table 3, all test substances except n-Decane are predicted to be
not readily biodegradable. As tests for ready biodegradability are stringent
test, this statements cannot be challenged with the data in hand. However, it
is worth noticing that all test substances except 1,3,5-Trimethylcyclohexane
and Decalin were degraded within 15 days of the test onset and within 10
days after end of the lag period in all inocula. Again, this might be ascribed
to mechanisms occurring in the degradation of hydrocarbon mixtures. It
should also be kept in mind, that there is a distinct difference between
primary biodegradation and complete mineralization as is the requirement
in tests for ready biodegradability.
Solano-Serena et al. (1999) observed a tendency of increasing degrada-
tion rates for n-alkanes and iso-alkanes with increasing carbon chain length,
which was coupled with decreasing vapour pressure, thus suggesting that
transfer over the water-air boundary might be a limiting step. As only one
n-alkane (n-Decane) and one iso-alkane (2,3-Dimethylheptane), both of
similar chain length, were employed in this study, nothing can be concluded
regarding the impact of the chain length. However, concerning volatility
no link between Henry’s law constants and degradation rate is observed in
this study. n-Decane, the test substance with the highest (known) KH was
degraded ﬁrst, while Decalin, the test substance with the second highest
(known) KH was degraded second to last. This indicates that the test system
is well ﬁt for handling volatile substances.
5.5
further work
As stated by Kostka et al. (2011), understanding the role of in-situ bacterial
populations under in-situ conditions in hydrocarbon degradation is a rela-
tively novel ﬁeld of research. The data obtained in this project can indicate
how the degradation varies between different environmental freshwater
recipients with different indigenous microbial populations. However, there
is still a long way to go. Further testing is needed that employs a higher
number of different, and maybe more diverse, inocula. The performance
of indigenous microbial populations should also be tested with even more
natural environmental conditions maintained, such as in-situ temperatures.
Tests comprising a higher number of test substances is also relevant
to investigate whether the observed difference in degradation rate constant
for a substance of up to one order of magnitude between pre-exposed and
not pre-exposed inocula is actually of importance. As a key parameter
of hazard assessment, it is important to consider how degradation rate
constants are obtained. If even slight differences in rate constants might
be equivalent to a substantial longer life time in the environment for more
slowly degradable substances, it is important to consider the inoculum
used to obtain degradation rate constants used in fate modelling.
Hazard assessments are done based on single substances, however lit-
erature repeatedly shows how degradation of a certain hydrocarbon is
induced or inhibited by the presence of other hydrocarbons (cf. Section
==PAGE:51==
5.5 further work
39
2.3) (Van Hamme et al., 2003; Bouchez et al., 1995; Horvath, 1972). This is
of importance as hydrocarbons seldom appear as single substances in the
environment but rather in mixtures. However there is still much to learn
about the behaviour of substances singly compared to in a mixture. It could
for example be tested whether the sequence of degradation observed for
a mixture of hydrocarbons is maintained if the substances of the mixture
are tested individually. It could also be investigated systematic whether the
degradation rate constant is maintained for a single substance tested singly
and in different hydrocarbon mixtures.

6
C O N C L U S I O N S
Three biodegradation experiments were conducted to determine lag pe-
riods and rate constants of the primary biodegradation of a petroleum
hydrocarbon mixture in three different inocula. The mixture consisted of 9
hydrocarbons with 8-12 carbons atoms that reﬂect the major petroleum hy-
drocarbon groups, i.e. n-alkanes, iso-alkanes, cyclic alkanes and aromatics.
As inocula were used freshwater samples from three small Danish streams
with different histories of petroleum hydrocarbon exposure. Biodegradation
rate constants were determined fairly well by ﬁtting a nonlinear model
employing a lag period followed by an exponential degradation phase
to experimental data. In some cases a better ﬁt was obtained with linear
regression to experimental data in a semi-logarithmic coordinate system.
The observed span of degradation rate constants between test substances is
about 3 orders of magnitude in all inocula.
The project yields the following ﬁndings:
• The maintained sequence of degradation between substances is ob-
served in the three different inocula regardless of previous exposure
to petroleum hydrocarbons. The observed sequence of degradation be-
tween substances, with decreasing rate constant, is: n-Decane > Naph-
talene ∼1,2,4-Trimethylbenzene ∼Biphenyl > 2,3-Dimethylheptane ∼
Bicyclohexyl ∼Tetralin > Decalin > 1,3,5-Trimethylcyclohexyl.
• Observed lag periods for the 9 test substances are generally shorter in
an inoculum previously exposed to petroleum hydrocarbons than in
an inoculum with no recent exposure to petrolelum hydrocarbons.
• The observed biodegradation rate constants for individual substances
in the different inocula are within one order of magnitude. Lower rate
constants are observed in the inocula not exposed or partly exposed to
petroleum hydrocarbons compared to the preexposed inoculum. For
substances with rate constants equalling to a half life of 1 day or less
this difference is not of importance for their expected lifespan. For
substances with longer half lives, a difference of 1 order of magni-
tude might imply a substantial increase of the expected life time in the
environment. Thus for these substances, the inoculum used to gener-
ate degradation rate constants for fate predictions are of importance.
However, further tests are necessary to support this observation.
• Biodegradation half lives for test substances in the three inocula are
generally lower than EPI BIOWIN predictions. This might be ascribed
to dynamics occurring in degradation of hydrocarbon mixtures, the
concentration range used in the experiment, or the model giving con-
servative estimates.
