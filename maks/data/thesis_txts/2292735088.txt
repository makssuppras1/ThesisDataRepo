Abstract 
 
To investigate the influence of pore fluid on the rate dependent deformation of chalk, 
samples from Stevns Klint were tested. The samples were saturated with distilled water, 
isopar-L, hydraulic oil, and mixtures of distilled water and glycerin to reproduce the 
relationship from Biot’s critical frequency of viscosity over density for isopar-L and 
hydraulic oil. Dry samples were also tested as a reference measure.  
 
The Biot critical frequency is a function of the fluid properties viscosity and density, and 
the material properties porosity and permeability; it is for this thesis understood as a 
measure of the solid fluid friction for the rate dependent tests carried out. The critical 
frequency is therefore anticipated as a measure of the strength of chalk. 
 
The main tests were carried out in a triaxial cell under Oedometer circumstances 
without pore pressure to see the direct fluid influence on the 54mm chalk samples. 
From de Wall the rate dependent b-factor was found for the different fluids:

Introduction 
This chapter describes the purpose, the motive and the background knowledge for this 
thesis. 
One of the top priorities worldwide today is the enormous demand for resources. This is 
a matter of both politics and economies, both because of the increase in population 
worldwide and because of the rise in standard of living. Last but not the least the 
environmental issues have to be taken into account. 
Hydrocarbons are still today the largest and an indispensable source of energy. This 
fossil fuel has been buried deep beneath the ground for millions of years. It originates 
from microorganisms, mainly algae and other small organisms; accumulated on the 
bottom of the seafloor. During time being buried under various layers of earth has 
resulted in a rising pressure and temperature, which has transformed the microscopic 
organisms into oil.     
Hydrocarbons being a natural resource challenges research and the ability to sustain the 
development of energy technology of the constant rising demand. This disequilibrium 
creates problems of different kinds. This is not a limitless recourse and therefore 
production wise making it more challenging to produce, and sometimes what is done to 
the reservoir has side effects.  
To perform empirical laboratory experimental tests with axial stress; confining pressure 
is provided and controlled by a Hoek- cell to investigate ”the influence of pore fluid on 
the rate dependent deformation of chalk”. For this master thesis it has deliberately 
been chosen to disregard the pore pressure, in spite of the important impact it has on 
reservoir behavior. This was chosen to focus on the direct influence of how the 
sedimentary rock reacts with different fluids.  
The theoretical scientific method of investigation for this thesis will overall be done by 
empirical positivistic induction (Thurén, 1998):  
 To obtain empirical data from the samples. 
 To investigate the influence of pore fluid, the moduli and b-factor are calculated 
and interpreted through relevant scientific literature 
 To discuss and assess whether the relationship of viscosity over density, from 
Biot’s critical frequency, is relevant as a measurement of soil liquid friction.   
 In one section of the project a deductive approach is used to simplify and let 
ends meet between theory and empirical work. 
Below a field case from the Ekofisk is shown, because of its relevancy. It will briefly be 
described and discussed to show relevance of the upcoming studies on chalk.

Ekofisk Reservoir 
 
Ekofisk is an oil field situated in the North Sea. It is a very important reservoir due to 
size, and it was discovered by Philips Petroleum Company in 1969. The area is 
approximately 49 km2, 6,7 km wide, 9,4 km long and the pay zone is around 300 m thick 
(Johnson, 1989).The estimated amount of oil in the field is 6,4 billion barrels. The field 
consists mostly of chalk of Maastrichtian, Late Cretaceous through Upper Danien Age. 
The Ekofisk oil field became known too all in November 1984, when it was identified 
that the seabed subsided.   
 
In the beginning the reservoir was strongly overpressured of about 48,3 MPa; 17 years 
later the formation pressure only amounted to 27,6 MPa. This left the effective 
overburden pressure to rise from 13,8 MPa to 34,5 MPa  (Johnson, 1989).  
The chalk in the reservoir is highly porous limestone with a porosity range of 35-50 % 
and permeability on the level of 1-10 mD, but the entire field is profoundly fractured. 
(Rhett, 1998). This discloses the high production rates rather than the low matrix 
permeability. In this thesis the chalk specimens used are consistent to the chalk in the 
Ekofisk field, both regarding the time age and the specific values.  
The Ekofisk reservoir production facilities are a huge combination of platforms and 
structures, making a transportation hub. Ekofisk consists of 7 platforms, along with the 
platforms located in surrounding fields, the whole complex amounts to 29 platforms 
(Statoil, 2015).  
The platforms are placed in a water depth of about 73m (Sulak, 1988). While situated in 
the North Sea it can be a rather hostile environment weather wise, both in the fall and 
in the winter. The production began in 1971 and had a peak production in 1976 with a

capacity of 350.000 bbl. of oil per day. These days (2013) the production consists of 
127.000 bbl. per day. In the mid 1980’s Ekofisk began water flooding, and in the early 
1990’s the whole field was injected with water (brine) (Rhett, 1998), because of the 
water injection the ultimate predicted recovery factor of production is 44% of oil in the 
reservoir (ekofisk, 2015). The field will be economically safe until 2028 according to the 
newer studies, depending on oil prices and technology advances (petroleum-economist, 
2015).  
As mentioned an interesting but serious problem was detected in 1984, it was found 
that the whole Ekofisk field with platforms was suffering from an apparent degree of 
subsidence, which precipitates from production. According to (Rhett, 1998) seafloor 
subsidence has occurred at an average rate of 38 cm per year since 1984 at least until 
1998; since 1987 water flooding has been used to maintain the pressure in the reservoir 
while depletion, and should therefore have choked the subsidence, but still almost 10 
meters of subsidence have been the result. The pore pressure stabilized in 1994 and 
then slowly started to increase. In 1998, the subsidence had declined to 0.1 m/year. The 
Seawater injections in the reservoir can be seen as a success as it increases the 
production of oil. Still it remains that the subsidence is ongoing after the pore pressure 
has started to increase. As suggested in (Omdal, 2010) the chalk formation experiences 
substantial time dependent strains. 
When subsidence occurs; the layers of earth, the mantle, is compressing against a lower 
pressure layer, this is called isostasy. When depletion is imposed on a reservoir, due to 
the high compressibility of the chalk and the sudden decrease in pressure, there will be 
isostasy; to avoid or minimize this, substitution of the sediment fluid with brines can be 
useful as to maintain the equilibrium of pressure within this void.  
Many long term laboratory studies have been made to understand the effects of water 
flooding on chalk strengths and to predict reservoir compaction and seafloor 
subsidence. They found that the water injections had little or no effect on the 
compaction. Recent laboratory analyses have led to a new model of Ekofisk rock mass 
behavior. Within this model two graphs are described, the rock in the reservoir prior to 
water flooding elastic, but with low compressibility, and the other, when the chalk is 
fully water saturated, fully weakened, is described. It is when fully saturated it is highly 
compressible and has high strain rates (Rhett, 1998). 
Prior conclusions have almost displayed misguiding results like (Johnson, 1989), where 
water injections, only helped by maintaining the pressure, disregarding the during time 
removal of the coating on the pore surfaces (Rhett, 1998). It is when this protective 
organic compound coating is gone; there will be sufficient contact between the chalk 
and the water to see the capillary effects, and an increase in the compaction of the

reservoir (Andreassen et al, 2010).  The chemical composition of seawater also needs to 
be taken into account of the continuous flooding. 
 
The case of Ekofisk has briefly been described and discussed in this introduction to show 
relevance of the upcoming studies on chalk. When studying literature and lab work 
made upon the Ekofisk reservoir through time, it displays the ongoing need to 
understand and learn about the ground beneath us, both within the oil industry as well 
as for this thesis. Ekofisk is a good present example on the ongoing advancements of 
the knowledge of subsidence, time-dependency and reservoir depletion. This case was 
found and still is interesting, because while producing 3 km below the seafloor 
(Conocophillips, 2015), the seafloor suddenly subsides. While trying to fix the reservoir 
and improve the production, the effects are not exactly as anticipated in relation to 
subsidence; this makes room for new ways of thinking, and new knowledge.

Permeability 
 
Permeability is a geometric parameter that defines a rock’s ability to transmit fluid 
through a porous medium and it is measured in m2. If a rock transmits fluid, it is 
referred to as having a permeable formation and if it happens easily it is having a high 
permeability. If the rock has less interconnected pores and the conductivity is non 
excitant, it is impermeable. Permeability is affected by the pressure in the rock and can 
have different values for the same porous medium depending on the type of fluid 
flowing through the sample.

The effective stress relation of a porous specimen is a complex physical scenario, where 
the force in the fluid is acting as hydraulic pressure. Forces in a porous rock can be 
described as a cross between the solid matrix and the pore fluid, if the matrix was dry, 
the only thing carrying the load would be the matrix itself. Terzhagi’s concept of 
effective stress describes that the balance between the total stress (external applied 
load) and the pore pressure for a water saturated soil scenario, where the effective 
stress σ’ is simply defined as the total stress σ minus the pore pressure PP. (Fjær, 2008) 
Later the Biot’s factor, known as the effective stress coefficient or poroelastic 
coefficient, was added. This correction factor shall be understood as if two particles are 
having intergranular contact, the area that is occupied by the contact divided by the 
total area of the grain. This gives the ratio of the grain surface not exposed to the pore 
pressure. This could be linked directly to the cementation and packing of the grains in 
the material (Omdal, 2010) (Fabricius, 2010).

Time-Dependent Behavior  
 
It is often assumed that a change in the applied stress on a material is followed by a 
corresponding deformation of the material. Nevertheless, the strain or the deformation 
of sedimentary matrix is dependent on the load rate, and the strain can even be 
continued for a time period after the load is held constant. This indicates, a time 
constant needs to be taken into account (Mesri and Godlewski, 1977). 
According to the de Waal model, the reservoir rocks deformation is dependent on a 
time component. This means that different deformation results can be obtained by 
different loading rates.  
 
Figure 4 illustrates a slow virgin compaction curve, which is the red curve, and a blue 
curve for a fast virgin compaction, the green line is where there is a change in the load 
rate, first from the fast to the slow, later back again. Along the x-axis, the loading rate of 
the green curve is exemplified.   
When observing the figure it is apparent that after a period of time the loading rate of 
the green curve is decreased; now the material is more compressible than it was under 
the faster loading rate, and it approaches the slower rate virgin curve, steadily. When 
the loading rate increases, the material pressed upon will still have some resistance to 
the load rate applied and remain stiff, until the stress level reaches the faster virgin 
curve.  
A reservoir is held in a structural equilibrium, from the layers above the reservoir 
weighing down on it, being held partly by the matrix and partly by the pore fluid. Once a

hydrocarbon reservoir is in its depletion phase, the loading rate on the reservoir matrix 
is all of a sudden increased, from that carried out by the sedimentation process, over a

the depletion. In addition, subsidence continues after the pressure is reconstructed 
(consolidation effect). This situation is also described in the introduction about the 
Ekofisk reservoir.  
The time-dependent effects can be separated into two different groups: creep and 
consolidation, although the two can be hard to differentiate between (Fjær, 2008). 
Creep is related to visco-elastic behavior of the solid framework, therefore a rock 
parameter. Creep may occur in both dry and saturated rocks. Consolidation is due to a 
change in pore pressure, brought on by a change in the stress, and thus it will take time 
to reestablish a pore pressure balance. Therefore the process will involve a flow of fluid 
in the pore space. The experiments done in this thesis are not done with any pore 
pressure, and hence the time-dependency effect will be in the creep category. On the 
other hand it is hard to prove that within a fast loading rate, there will not be 
established local pore pressure pockets in any areas of the rock, since the material used 
here is fairly permeable, it can be assumed that the effect from the consolidation will be 
negligible. This will however be discussed later.

Biot Critical Frequency  
 
The subject of this thesis is the influence of the pore fluid on the rate dependent 
deformation of chalk; it is plausible that the fluid factor in Biot’s critical frequency can 
be associated with the strength of chalk, and is therefore used for testing.   
Sound is acoustic waves. Ultrasonic waves are sound waves with frequencies higher 
than the audible limit of human hearing. Ultrasonic waves applied on a rock sample are 
elastic waves; elastic waves are mechanical disturbances that propagate through a 
material. These waves can travel over long distances and bring back information about 
the elements it traveled trough. Earthquakes are an example of naturally generated 
waves, by sudden failure processes in the mantle, and propagate as elastic waves. The 
elastic waves are mechanical disturbances with a velocity that is given by the elastic 
stiffness, and the density of the rock. The elastic stiffness depend on parameters such as 
porosity and fluid, this means that there is a fundamental connection between rock 
acoustics and rock mechanics (Fjær, 2008).  
M. A. Biot developed a theory for the propagation of stress waves in a porous elastic 
solid containing compressible viscous fluid. The theory is divided into two parts, one 
above, and one below a critical frequency. The critical frequency is where the 
assumption of Poiseuille flow will break down, because there will no longer be laminar 
flow in the pores of the rock. The regime will be dominated from inertial forces, which 
will dominate the fluid motion (Biot_B, 1956). Under the critical frequency it is assumed 
that the fluid will flow relative to the solid, causing friction to arise. The critical 
frequency denoted fc depends on the viscosity and the density of the fluid, and the 
porosity and the permeability of the rock (Biot_A, 1956). 
In establishing the theory for the critical frequency, the assumption of the situation is 
that the elastic waves are in a system composed of porous elastic solid containing fluid, 
which is laminar flowing. The fluid in the pores will be a Newtonian fluid, where the 
viscosity does not depend on the speed. Since it is in the low frequency regime it is 
assumed that the fluid is compressible and has the ability to flow relative to the solid 
material, causing friction (Biot_A, 1956).

Water Weakening Effect  
 
The water weakening effect has been a topic for researchers for many years and still 
new findings uncover the details of this complex phenomenon.  (Omdal, 2010) The 
weakening effect can be divided into several different groups, in the following, the two 
primary are described.  
The physical effects are where the phenomenon is described by the capillary pressure 
effects. These effects refers to having a wetting and a non-wetting fluid, which are 
immiscible and present in the pores at the same time, and a capillary pressure in the 
pores of soils is an important aspect of the mechanical behavior of soils (Fjær, 2008). 
However this is not fully relevant for these tests, even though they are fully saturated, 
there is as earlier described a lack of pore pressure. Newer studies have more and more 
been going in another direction. (Madland, 2009)   
The chemical effects are when chalk is subjected to water, and this is weakened due to 
a substitution process, where calcium ions from the calcite grains are substituted by 
magnesium ions from the water. Studies have shown that seawater contains ions, as 
calcium, magnesium and sulfate divalent ions which are potential determining ions 
towards the chalk surface (Andreassen, 2011). It was furthermore suggested that the 
substitution is required to occur at the intergranular contacts, where the load is 
transmitted in order to affect the mechanical strength. It was additionally suggested 
that it has to happen at the intergranular contact, where the load is afflicting the 
mechanical strength for the substitution to have an effect. (Omdal, 2010). 
The weakening effect is not the primary focus of the report, additional theory for this 
area can be found in the referenced literature.

The Origin 
 
The Samples, which were tested in this master thesis, are made of chalk from the 
Danish quarry located at Stevns in Zealand. It is a costal cliff area of approximately 15 
km. Stevns Klint raises 41 meters above the sea level at its highest point.  The cliff can 
be divided into a series of more specific lithostratigraphic units, which gives us the 
evolution from the uppermost Maastrichtian, then the Cretaceous- Tertiary boundary 
and into the lower Danien lithostratigraphic era (Surlyk, 2006). For this report it should 
be mentioned, that the choices of the specific chalk samples are very dependent on the 
possibility of getting them from the company, now owning the quarry. The samples 
taken from the quarry are from the uppermost Maastrichtian, lower Danian, the 
Cretaceous-Tertiary boundary era; the samples are drilled out of larger, rougher pieces 
of chalk from the same place in the quarry. This was done with two different machines, 
because two different sizes of samples were required.  
Chalk contains several minerals, the predominant being fine crystalline calcite along 
with silica and clay minerals. The chalk originates from skeletons of algae, called 
coccospheres, which are crushed and both particles and the pore size of present chalk

of 2500-3500m, with a permeability of 0, 1-10 mD. Although the chalk has a high

Mounting of Equipment  
 
The equipment used in this thesis to perform the mechanical testing consists of several 
individual components. They either apply load and or do data logging. 
 
The Load Frame  
 
To test the b-factor seen in equation 23 and the unconfined compression strength, an 
electro mechanical press has been used. In Figure 22 a sketch of the load frame can be 
seen, an INSTRON 5500R. The load capacity of the frame is 100 KN; this was high 
enough to simulate relevant stress states at these tests of sedimentary rock.  The main 
principle was that the test specimen was mounted under the beam, the machine was 
designed to apply the load on the specimen by moving crosshead up or down, and the 
applied load was measured by a load cell, which was mounted in the top frame of the 
machine.  
This machine is used for the UCS tests and for the b-factor. The sample was put in a 
Hoek-cell, which then was placed in the loading frame.  
 
Calibration of the Load Frame 
 
The calibration of the loading frames movement was checked. Due to the complex and 
heavy setup it was decided to check if the machines’ measurement of the moving beam 
was correct, instead of having the extra measuring equipment attached for every test. 
This was done only for the first test.  
Figure 22 Sketch of the load frame 
==PAGE:41==
40 
 
 
The calibration was done by attaching a second measuring device, a movable piston, 
where the resistance was measured. The device was connected to a power supply and a 
computer. The device measured the current sent, and then what it received back, which 
would be altered by the resistance in the piston as it moved. The reason for measuring 
the sent current was that there would be a little difference in the current sent, and this 
would then be subtracted from the measurement. As the piston moved, the resistance 
would change, and this was measured.  
𝜀𝑡𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙= 𝜀𝑠𝑎𝑚𝑝𝑙𝑒+ 𝜀𝑙𝑜𝑎𝑑𝑓𝑟𝑎𝑚𝑒= 𝜀𝑠𝑎𝑚𝑝𝑙𝑒 
It was found that the loading frame was stiff enough 𝜀𝑙𝑜𝑎𝑑𝑓𝑟𝑎𝑚𝑒= 0 and had no 
complications measuring accurately in the used area.  
However first the load frame’s measuring device had to have calibrated the dimming 
frequency, especially for the slow loading part of the test. This had to be done by the 
trial and error method, until the machine was able to produce a smooth line suitable for 
calculations. If the frequency was not correct there were two possible outcomes. The 
machine either gave less usable results or the machine started overcorrecting due to 
false readings, this resulted in shooting the specimen with attached equipment out of 
the load frame, this was the least desired situation, when the specimen was in the 
Hoek-cell. The range of the frequency amplification for the measuring device on the 
load frame was [-50; 50dB], for this material at this speed, the best results came when 
the dimming effect was -27,84dB.  
 
Hoek-cell 
 
The triaxle cell used in this thesis is the Hoek-cell to simulate Oedometer circumstances, 
since there was no oedometer cell in this size on DTU. Figure 23 shows the outer edge 
of the Hoek-cell, which consists of a thick metal casing, with the opportunity of opening 
the ends for changing the rubber sleeve. (Fjær, 2008) 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:42==
41 
 
 
The loading pistons are divided in two parts; this is done to avoid bending of the 
samples, if the ends of the samples are not totally parallel. This is done by the spherical 
seats in the pistons, which also compensate for the initial misalignment of the specimen 
ends (Hoek, 1968). It also works as a safety mechanism, if the cell is not placed in 
alignment with the loading frame; so the axial stress inside the cell will still be aligned 
with the cell, and thereby applying the axial stress correctly and not get damaged or 
damage the frame. The inner part of the piston has fluid ports, these ports were for this 
test only used for allowing the liquid free mobility, and so fluid pressure did not have to 
be taken under consideration. The ports could also be used to control the fluid pressure 
in the sample, or to add a fluid to the sample. Inside the Hoek-cell, the filters are visible; 
these have two functions, fluid distribution, if fluid is pushed in, and as a filter in our 
case, so the channels would not get stuffed and thereby create pore pressure.  
The rubber sleeve exists to make a barrier between the sample and the fluid making the 
chamber pressure. The sleeve is so soft, that it will not provide significant support to the 
core, but soft enough to fit along the side of the core. The fluid comes from the port in 
the side of the chamber, is supplied by a worm press (Hoek, 1968). Once the pressure is 
raised in the chamber, the inner pistons are locked into position, by the radial pressure. 
(Instruments, 2014) 
The axial vertical strain is measured directly on the sample by the load frame; the radial 
strain would be possible to measure in the 2 plane directions by attaching strain gauges 
to the sample and let them follow channels along the side of the piston and out of the 
Hoek-cell. However the strain gauges were not used for the tests done in this thesis.

UCS 
 
The unconfined compression strength test (UCS) is intended to measure the stress 
strain relationship in a specimen, which was exposed to the increasing axial load with 
non-confining pressures 𝜎𝑦= 𝜎𝑧= 0 for this case. The stress strain relationships are 
plotted from the compressive stress, where the strength was calculated by dividing the 
maximum load the specimen could withstand, with the area of the specimen as seen in 
equation 7. The axial strain ɛ, is shown in the calculations. 
The apparatus used, must be suitable for applying load to the specimen, the same with 
the logging equipment (Z.T. Bieniawski, 1978) which for this case was the ISTRON 
console software, which in this case logged data every 1/10 sec. The load frame must be 
applied spherical seat plates, to ensure pressure on the whole specimen.  
The method was fairly straight forward: Specimens had to be flat with parallel ends, 
perpendicular to their x-axis as they should for the Hoek-cell, the specimens were 
centered in the machine and the load was applied to the specimen continuously, where 
failure had to occur within 5-10 min. Therefore the speed of the test was relatively high, 
0.0013 mm/sec so ½ % deformation would occur at 7 minutes, with the average length 
10,9 cm samples. The max load was recorded in Newton and then recalculated into 
MPa. The number of specimens should be above 5, for getting a comparable result, whit 
certainty. (Z.T. Bieniawski, 1978)

ability to get into the sample and therefore was not measured, but this is normally not 
the case for chalk. For this thesis it has been calculated using 2,71 g/cm3 for chalk and

When looking at the combined picture of all the samples Figure 41 with the different 
fluids; Figure 41 indicates that there is a general tendency; the higher the porosity, the 
higher the b-factor, as shown in Figure 9, except for the water, which will be discussed

Figure 41 shows that the samples saturated in isopar have the highest resistance to the 
rate dependent effect; even though Table 1 combined with Figure 9 implicates that, 
hydraulic oil should be the most resistant. One Plausible reason for this could be that 
while most Newtonian fluids are almost incompressible at low-medium pressures [0,1 
;30] MPa, where the change in viscosity will be on the same scale as a temperature 
difference of 1°C, even seen on a larger scale [0,1 ;200] MPa, the viscosity change will 
be of a maximum factor 3. For mineral oils with higher viscosity this factor can be up to 
2·104 (Mezger, 2015). However for synthetic oils (isopar-l), the change in pressure 
applied on the fluid can even result in a viscosity increase by a factor of up to 8·106 
(Mezger, 2015). A little fluid was visible in the end of the tube from the Hoek-cell under 
the water and fake isopar saturated samples; this was not seen for the isopar, which has 
a viscosity in the same region as water. Isopar also had the highest E and M modulus. 
This can only be the explanation if the fluid did not have the ability to escape due to the 
initial high load rate, and the small possibility of escape in the Hoek-cell. This could also 
explain the lack of difference between the fluids seen in the UCS test in Figure 31, and 
the rate-dependent test in Figure 41.

Liabilities 
 
Despite the experiments have been carefully carried out and not being biased with 
respect to the calculated outcome, it cannot be eliminated that there may be potential 
errors. Whether these have had a direct influence on the results is not known. The 
believed possible sources of error are described in the following. 
Numbers 
The number of test samples can never be too high for each different test. It is stated in 
many of the test standards; the minimum number of samples is 5.  The UCS test was not 
done with fresh samples, and not five tests were done for each fluid. The tests can due 
to this not be used to conclude anything with a realistic certainty.      
Sample Swap 
The first batch of samples [1-27] was drilled out of three large rocks. When the samples 
were drilled they were marked; then they were sent to cutting and grinding; at some 
point in this process the marks were removed by accident, and they were mixed. Due to 
this there was no record of which samples came from what stone. The stones came 
from the same quarry, but were not all marked with directions. It is assumed that the 
chalk is isotropic for simplicity in the calculation, but from (Shamsolhodaei, 2015) we 
know that this is not the case for Stevns chalk. This could therefore explain why the 
measurements for water got the opposite relationship with regards to the b-factor and 
the porosity. At the same time it is known, that samples also from the second batch 
were used, and they alone gave an indication of a non-existing relationship with the 
porosity (straight line).   
Liquid Evaporation 
Many fluids can evaporate at room temperature; this is the case for water. The fake 
hydraulics viscosity was very sensitive to the quantity of water. 5 liters were mixed and 
the viscosity was lowered in the last part from 82 to 80 [mPa*s] by adding 5 ml of 
water. For the saturation with this fluid the bottle with fluid was sealed tight around the 
tube and connected to the saturation chamber, for the purpose of avoiding 
evaporation.  After the saturation, the samples were kept in a container, where the lid 
only was removed to take out samples.  
Saturation 
The residue seen in Figure 16, should have been dried, and weighed. The average value 
could then have been subtracted from the dry weight of each specimen.

The Load Frame 
The load frame gave correct results, but it did not always want to do the whole test, 
therefore two b-factor values were not found for all specimens. This can be seen in the 
appendix.  
Pore collapse was only observed for the water saturated samples as the load frame did 
not accept slow loading rate over a certain point, or after a certain period of time, even 
after these unknown limits were found, the load frame only worked perfectly for half 
the test.   
A switch to a hydraulic load frame was tried, but the vibrations from the pump in this 
machine were too large for the tests wanted. 
The hassle with this machine took 2,5 months with testing every day, with great support 
from the technicians in building 109; Instron service team, whom split the whole 
machine apart, found many things that were broken, and they fixed them; 
unfortunately this did not help my situation. At last all tests were “done”. 
Gyro Bent Samples for the UCS Test 
The Gyro in the Hoek-cell was there to avoid bending of the samples, but when the 
samples were put into the load frame a second time for the UCS test; it could be 
observed that some of the samples no longer had parallel ends, this was not easy to 
correct with the ad hand gyro, as some samples were very soft. Here it must be 
mentioned that the UCS test was not initially a part of this thesis, but after the troubles 
with the machine, it was decided to do these tests while the machine did not have any 
problems, as it was controlled by distance (fast) instead of load rate. Therefore it is a 
very troubling thought if the bending had any effect on the rate dependent tests. 
Impure Samples 
As observed in Figure 10 some samples were not clean chalk, some had flint in them 
others clay as seen in the picture; this can obviously also be the case for some 
reservoirs, where there will be areas which are not clean, but for test samples it brings 
an uncertainty.

Conclusion 
 
The purpose of this master thesis was to explore and investigate the influence of pore 
fluid on the rate dependent deformation of chalk. Tests with confining pressure from a 
Hoek-cell and axial load from a load frame were performed to simulate reservoir rate 
dependent deformation. The samples were saturated with hydraulic oil, isopar-l and 
made to compare with a mixture of water and glycerin, to simulate their relationship of 
viscosity and density from the Biot critical frequency formula. Water and dry samples 
were also tested as reference samples.   
From the saturation process carried out for this thesis, it can be seen that all the fluids 
were fully saturable, even though the high viscous fluid took longer time. Also water 
dissolved some of the bindings in the material. It is shown that there is a direct link 
between the permeability and the porosity for Stevns chalk. The specific surface was 
found bigger than the one calculated; the calculated one from the permeability 
measurement was used due to the quantity difference.   
It has been proven that the b-factor from de Waal is dependent on the pore fluid for 
chalk. However with respect to Biot only one test showed that the relationship of 
viscosity over density was true, and that was hydraulic oil and the mixture to simulate 
this.  
Due to the deductive approach, where the different saturations from the experiments 
were adjusted to the order of Biot’s critical frequencies, the validity is uncertain. The 
corrections to the empirical data seems plausible, but the frequency cannot for certain 
be used as a measure of friction for these rate dependent tests; the tests indicate the 
order of their critical frequencies, more experiments need to be made.  
Referring to the real scenario of the Ekofisk reservoir in the beginning of the thesis the 
newer studies found that even though the water injections maintained the pressure in 
the reservoir, the water had a side effect, which was the influence of change in pore 
fluid. This led to a new additional compaction. This is consistent with the findings of this 
thesis.

rock would behave differently with the same fluids 
 
 The water substituted with another non-reacting fluid; for a better picture of the 
Biot critical frequency could be used as a friction factor in fully saturated chalk. 
Also the critical frequency correctly estimates the decrease in the strength 
properties.  
 
 The same tests done with pore pressure.  
 
 A consequence analysis for reservoir modelling, incorporating changes in pore 
fluids, due to water.  
 
 An Oedometer-cell for 54mm samples, so the huge and heavy setup of the Hoek-
cell can be avoided. 
 
 Better safety mechanisms for the Hoek-cell. 
 
 To have different liquids flushing trough the specimen, while slow loading rate is 
applied. 
 
 Long-term effects of subsidence on the environment, do cracks occur in the gap 
on the reservoir, and therefore possible contamination.  
 
 Coating the samples before tests are done to see the effect of this.

 Adding a safety string to the Hoek-cell as it can be seen in the appendix, to avoid 
dropping the equipment.  
 Adding an inverter to the power supply for drilling the large samples to get 
usable ones.
