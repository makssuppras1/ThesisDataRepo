Abstract
The detection of microbial species in metagenomic data has been a crucial
part of metagenomics since the ﬁeld was developed. Some studies suggest
that the composition of the gut microbial population plays a relevant role in
protecting the host against pathogenic microbes and in regulating metabolic
processes, among others. Therefore, a successful detection of all diﬀerent
bacteria in a sample could shed light on the ﬁnding of new strategies to un-
derstand the human body. In this project, we pursue the development of a
method that is capable of identifying microbial species in a given sample.
To that end, we have introduced a deep learning method using variational
autoencoder which is implemented and tested to detect diﬀerent microbial
species in metagenomic data combining an abundance matrix and tetranu-
cleotide frequencies. First, we used a variational autoencoder with one hidden
layer followed by one with two hidden layers. Overall, the results show that
the method is able to detect some of the species with signiﬁcant accuracy,
which could result in a signiﬁcant shift in the current paradigm of proper
identiﬁcation of microbial species present in a speciﬁc sample.

1
Introduction
During the last decades, new technologies and methods to improve the area
of metagenomics have been developed. Metagenomics is a discipline in the
ﬁeld of Environmental Genomics which extracts genetic material from the
natural habitat and analyses it. This ﬁeld of study has opened a new way of
assessing microbial communities leading to the understanding of phylogenetic
traits or a deep knowledge of metabolic pathways. It has deﬁnitely brought
many beneﬁts such as studying microbial species in their own habitat, since
many species cannot survive outside it, besides there is a big loss of diversity
in cultured microbial species. Along with all the positive aspects, there are
some issues still unsolved. The most relevant one is the high computational
power needed in order to analyze all the data generated, together with the
fact that many of the methods that are currently available for analyzing
metagenomic data rely on comparisons with reference genomes. The last
issue is in which this project is going to focus on.
Reference-based methods are not the ideal solution since the microbial di-
versity is not covered by those genomes in the database. Therefore, there
are other approaches like, for example, de novo assembly, which does not use
a reference genome to assemble genomes. However, this diﬀerent approach
is not totally capable of identifying speciﬁc biological entities from complex
metagenomic data.
During the last years, new methods have been developed allowing a bet-
ter identiﬁcation of diﬀerent microbial species. One of them was described in
2014 by H.B. Nielsen and M. Almeida [1], in which they presented a method
based on binning co-abundant genes across a series of metagenomic samples
that, since it does not use any reference genome, can also discover new mi-
crobial organisms, viruses and co-inherited genetic entities. Another recent
study proposed MetaBAT, a software tool that combines tetranucleotide fre-
quency and contig abundance probabilities [2].
In this project, an artiﬁcial neural network using a variational autoencoder is
developed in order to ﬁnd a way to improve the detection of microbial species
without using reference-based methods. By using an abundance matrix of
contigs and z-scores of the tetranucleotide frequencies it attempts to ﬁnd a
relationship between a latent representation and microbial species.

Theoretical Background
2.1
Metagenomics
Metagenomics is a relatively young discipline that consists of the study of
genetic material taken from environmental samples. That implies studying
communities of microbial organisms in their natural environment with no
need of isolation and lab cultivation [3].
The study of microbial species has changed dramatically in the last years.
Originally, in order to study the organisms, they had to be isolated and cul-
tured in solid nutrients like potato slices or gelatin [4]. After that, the use
of the microscope started to expand, becoming the most important tool for
the assessment of microbial species in the ﬁeld. Thus, a number of new tech-
niques optimized the use of the microscope such as Grahm or Ziehl-Neelsen
staining that allowed to distinguish diﬀerences in the microbial population
analyzed. It was at that moment when scientists realize that the number of
observed microorganisms in the microscope did not correspond to the number
of microorganisms observed in culture plates, which gave birth to microbial
ecology.
For the next centuries, the ﬁeld continued its discrete development until
the late 1970sC, when Carl Woese decided to start a life classiﬁcation us-
ing ribosomal RNA (rRNA) genes as molecular markers. His idea and the
development of Sanger(1977) sequencing improved the classiﬁcation of mi-
croorganisms [4].
Furthermore, years later, new techniques including the polymerase chain
reaction(PCR), rRNA genes cloning and sequencing, gave rise to the open-
ing of the uncultured world of microbial communities. Scientists started to
study the distinct functions of the diverse genes sequenced and the proteins
they codify for, which implied the development of new techniques for gene
expression. Moreover, new genes functions were discovered and collected,
leading to a new ﬁeld of research known as metagenomics analysis.

• Sequence-based metagenomics involves sequencing and analysis of

applications such as assemble genomes, identify genes, ﬁnd complete
metabolic pathways and compare organisms of diﬀerent communities.
Although genome assembly requires a really high computer power given
the tremendous amount of info needed, it can lead to a better under-
standing of how certain genes help organisms survive in a particular

In conclusion, metagenomics is a key research tool since a high percentage of
the microbial species are not able to survive out of its own habitat besides
that the cultured microbial species miss a big amount of microbial biodiver-
sity [5]. Moreover, this discipline at the same time provides knowledge of
phylogenetic and evolutionary traits. These big advantages result into a big
amount of data generated every day, which demands a high computational
power. Indeed, this is one of the main complications of metagenomics since it
determines the amount of time needed to analyze the data , if it is even pos-
sible. Together with the computational power needed, and since the samples
consist of a lot of small fragments of DNA from unknown bacteria, detecting
the microbial species located in a metagenomic sample is still challenging.
Therefore, the eﬀort and work put in metagenomics are in developing new
and better methods for detecting microbial species in the samples.
2.1.1
Binning
Metagenomic binding is deﬁned as the study and processing of reads and
contigs, grouping and assigning them to an operational taxonomic unit. In
order to clarify, a contig is deﬁned as a set of overlapping DNA segments that
together represent a consensus region of DNA. As mentioned before, metage-
nomic data contain big amounts of DNA fragments of diﬀerent organisms,
and metagenomic studies try to assemble these DNA fragments to bigger nu-
cleotide sequences. Usually, many samples are not complete and most likely
they will not contain complete genomes or even genes. Therefore, binning
techniques are a way to overcome this deﬁciency.
We will focus on the binning algorithm that targets the diﬀerent tetranu-
cleotides of the sequences. This algorithm is based on that every tetranu-
cleotide and their frequencies carry an innate phylogenetic signal [6]. There
are 256 diﬀerent tetranucleotides, the algorithm works by counting all the
nucleotides both in the sequence and in the reverse sequence followed by
the calculation of the frequency. From these frequencies, z-scores are then
computed in order to indicate how is the tetranucleotide represented in the
sequence.

Supervised Learning
Supervised learning is the part of machine learning that infers from labeled
training data, also called training examples. Supervised algorithms consist,
therefore, on the training data as input and the desired output value, it
analyzes the input and infers a function that maps the speciﬁc output. The
ideal result of an algorithm is to perfectly match and label an unseen instance.
The workﬂow of a supervised learning algorithm can be seen in the Figure 2.
Figure 2: Workﬂow of supervised learning algorithms. The upper
image represents the training procedure. On the other hand, the image below

Supervised learning methods have also several issues that have to be taken
into account:
• The bias-variance trade-oﬀof an algorithm is a relevant issue. If a
dataset ﬁts too well an input but does not ﬁt well another input, it is
likely that the algorithm has a high variance and it is overﬁtting. On
the other hand, the algorithm could be ”too ﬂexible” and systemati-
cally produce incorrect outputs. Therefore, there has to be a balance
between bias and variance.
• Complexity of the algorithm and amount of data. Depending
on how complex is the underlying function, diﬀerent amount of data is
going to be needed in order to detect the whole complexity.
• Dimensionality of the input. The igh dimensionality of the train-
ing data could lead to error in the algorithm since it is common that

the underlying function only depends on a small number of features.
Therefore some algorithms try to identify the relevant features known
as feature selection algorithms.
Supervised learning methods can be implemented in many diﬀerent ways
depending on the input data and the aim. In this project, an artiﬁcial neural
network is implemented using a variational autoencoder.

Autoencoder
In this project we will focus on Artiﬁcial Neural Networks with autoencoder,
therefore in this section, we proceed to explain how the networks work.
An autoencoder is an artiﬁcial neural network that aims to discover a rep-
resentation of the given data as input. The simplest structure consists of an
input and output layer with one or more hidden layer connecting them, it
can be observed in Figure 4.

layer which reduces the dimensionality of the input

The output layer has the same amount of nodes as the input layer since the
aim is to reconstruct the own inputs. For that purpose, the hidden layer has
fewer nodes than the outer layers, and they are divided in two parts: encoder,
deﬁned as Φ, and decoder, deﬁned as Ψ. The ﬁnal aim of an autoencoder is
:
Φ,Ψ = argmin∥X −(Ψ ◦Φ)X∥2
(2.5)
The ﬁrst part corresponds to the encoder:
Φ : X →F
(2.6)
the function of the encoder is to reduce the dimension and map into the
latent space:
z = σ(Wx + b)
(2.7)
where σ is the activation function, W is the weight matrix and the b is the
bias.
The latent space tries to ﬁnd a representation in order to reconstruct the
data with the same shape as the training data. This process of reconstruc-
tion is done helped by the decoder:
Ψ : F →X
(2.8)
and the reconstruction is the inverse of the map of x to z:
x′ = σ′(W ′z + b)
(2.9)
where x′, W ′ and b′ can be diﬀerent from the ones of the autoencoder.
In order to train the autoencoder, the objective is to minimize the error
between input and output following the next equation:
L(x,x′) = ∥x −x′∥2 = ∥x −σ′(W ′(σ(Wx + b)) + b′)∥2
(2.10)
Since there is a possibility that the autoencoder learns the identity function
there are diﬀerent variations of autoencoders that also improve the capacity
of capturing the information.
In this project, the autoencoder used is a
variational autoencoder.
2.2.3
Variational Autoencoder(VAE)
The variational auto-encoder was introduced by Kingma and Welling in 2013
[8]. It diﬀers from a normal autoencoder in the sense that the encoding net-
work is forced to generate latent space that follow a gaussian distribution,

This constraint avoids the autoencoder to overﬁt.

the latent variables match the gaussian distribution. Therefore, both the loss
and the latent function are combined forming a new objective function in the
autoencoder:

K-Medoid Clustering
K-mediod is a clustering algorithm, which is associated to the k-means al-
gorithm. It is a partitional algorithm that breaks a dataset into groups in

forehand. This algorithm chooses datapoints as centers and works normally
with the Manhattan Norm to determine the distance between diﬀerent data
points.
Partitioning Around Medoids(PAM) algorithm is the most common k-mediod
algorithm. The way it works can be divided into three steps:
1. Initialize variables k and n, and chose n datapoints to be the medoids.
2. Relate all the datapoints to their closest medoid.
3. While the distance is decreasing.
(a) For each medoid and for each of the rest of the data points(non-
medoids).
i. Change the medoid for the non-medoid and recalculate the
distance.
ii. If the distance incremented annul the change.

3
Methods
3.1
Data
The data used in this project is taken from the MetaHIT consortium. As de-
scribed in [10] the data consists of collected fecal specimens from Spain and
Denmark, from 124 individuals including healthy, over-weighted and obese
subjects.
The data was sequenced and processed in diﬀerent manners in
order to establish a catalogue of non-redundant human intestinal microbial
genes.
Dongwan D. Kang and collaborators described in their study published in
2015 [2] 290 known genomes from NCBI that are contained in the catalogue
we mentioned before. These genomes are chosen with a mean coverage of
> 5x. In order to simulate a real metagenomic pool of samples all the 290
genomes are afterwards shredded into contigs of random sizes of more than
2.5 kb following an exponential distribution, in which the abundance is also
collected from real data.
In this project, we only select 264 species that we process using a matrix
where the rows are all the distinct contigs found in all the species and the
columns are the diﬀerent samples. Inside the matrix, each value represents
the normalized count of the contig of that row in that sample. This ma-
trix together with the sequences of all the contigs and the z-scores of the
respective tetranucleotide frequencies are the data used for the training of
the variational autoencoder implemented in this project.
3.2
Implementation
In this project, we use an Artiﬁcial Neural Network to identify microbial
species in a metagenomic pool of samples.
The artiﬁcial neural network
implemented is a variational autoencoder, which is structured in diﬀerent
ways in order to ﬁnd the most eﬃcient approach that satisﬁes the objective
of the project. In this section, the implementation and the diﬀerent variations
are explained.
3.2.1
Main Model
The main program is at ﬁrst structured following a basic autoencoder with
only one hidden layer between input and the latent layer. It has a starting
layer in which the input is inserted followed by the hidden layer, that would
11
==PAGE:18==
represent the encoder, in which the dimensionality of the input is reduced in
order to ﬁnd the latent representation of it. Consecutively, the most mean-
ingful part of the model is reached, the latent space. This layer reduces again
the dimensionality of the previous hidden layer and it is formed by a mean
and a standard deviation that are forced to have values that follow, as said
before, a Gaussian distribution N(0,I). It is followed by another hidden layer
with the same size as the ﬁrst one and lastly, the output is reconstructed in
the next step of the structure.
For a better performance of the autoencoder, there are two control con-
straints, cross-entropy for control that the output is as close as possible to
the input in terms of loss, and Kullback-Leibler divergence that is important
to control that the latent space is still following the correct distribution, in
this case, a Gaussian distribution. Furthermore, the latent space needs an-
other control constraint called ”warm-up”, it allows more units in the latent
space [9] to be active, reducing the inﬂuence of Kullback-Leibler divergence
at the beginning of the training and gradually giving it more relevance in the
model.
For a correct function and a optimized ﬂexibility of the what we aim for,
the implementation includes input options that permit the user to change
several aspects of the training.
• Size of validation test. It regulates the size of the validation test in
the model. By default, if it is not speciﬁed, the validation size will be
automatically 10% of the data.
• Batches. This option enables to chose the size of the batches of data
running at the same time while the program is training the autoencoder.
• Hidden Layer Size.
It determines the number of neurons in the
hidden layer.
• Latent Space Size. It determines the number of neurons in the latent
layer.
• Number of Epochs. This option allows you to decide how many
times the program has to run the data.
• Warm up. Lastly, you can chose how many epochs it takes for the
warm-up option.
12
==PAGE:19==
Summarizing, the program collects as input the matrix of normalized counts
of contigs described previously. Afterwards, it divides the contigs into train-
ing and test data and initializes the structure of the autoencoder and the loss
functions. Subsequently, the training starts and runs the training the num-
ber of epochs determined by the user. In the training, the training data is
divided into a number of arrays corresponding to the number of steps needed
to cover all the data with the batches chosen.
In each routine of the training, the program calculates the cross-entropy
and the Kullback-Leibler divergence for adjusting the weights of the neurons
in order to minimize them next epoch. In addition, all the data generated
each cycle is saved in order to make further analysis.

5
Discussion
This study shows that this method successfully facilitate the detection of
microbial species in the given data avoiding the use of any reference-based
method and in reasonable duration time.
5.1
Interpretation of the results
Several variations with diﬀerent inputs were analyzed, with distinct results.
Within all of them, it is observed that the VAE model with the abundance
matrix as input presented the best results. The 2-hidden layer VAE with a
hidden layer of 400 and 200 neurons and a latent layer of 128 neurons, was
the best model in terms of loss and by far the best model in terms of distinct
microbial species detected with 110 valid clusters. The model was, therefore,
the one which could extract and learn information from the data.
The outcome of the 2-hidden layer VAE can be compared with the MetaBAT
tool [2] since they use the same data. The results obtained with these two
methods are similar. While with the 2-hidden layer VAE 110 clusters were
formed, MetaBAT was able to form 111, using the same precision and recall
parameters.
Inspecting what characteristics make a VAE a better method than the other,
it is pretty clear that when all the variations of both 1-hidden layer VAE
and 2-hidden layer VAE are shown, the largest 2-hidden layer VAE and the
1-hidden layer VAE with the smallest batch and largest hidden and latent
layer are in the three cases the most eﬃcient models in terms of loss. It
is probably because the most relevant characteristic of an autoencoder for
the reassembly of data is the size of the latent layer. Thus, a larger latent
layer gives the VAE the possibility to store more variables and to reconstruct
better the data.
Taking into account what inputs the VAE runs with, it is observed that when
using an abundance matrix, the training and the clustering are working in
a more eﬃcient way. In fact, the model that was found more eﬀective dur-
ing this thesis was the largest 2-hidden layer VAE that used the abundance
matrix as input. It is surprising that the z-scores from the tetranucleotide
frequencies do not work as optimal as expected in the VAE. It is possibly one
of the reasons why the combination of inputs do not improve signiﬁcantly
the 2-hidden layer VAE. A future investigation could be to understand why
the z-scores does not work well in this model.
37
==PAGE:44==
Furthermore, the ﬁnal results with the combined model show an improve-
ment in the 1- hidden layer VAE, in which all of them have an improvement
in the cluster formation, but have a signiﬁcant worsened loss performance
which means a worse reconstruction of data.
On the other hand, the 2-
hidden layer VAE performance is relatively worse both in terms of error in
the reconstruction and in cluster formation. It is surprising taking into ac-
count that the combined input is using more information than when only the
abundance matrix is introduced as input.
Moreover, one would have thought that introducing more data in the in-
put may imply that more layers would be needed in order to ﬁnd the best
latent representation. However, in this case, more layers led to a worse rep-
resentation of the data since fewer clusters with more than 90% of precision
and 30% of recall were formed.
5.2
Further Work
Several facts from this project could be selected for further investigation in
order to improve and optimize the method and its performance. First of all,
the data used is ”error-free” data. Indeed, it is a dataset that is ﬁxed and
exclusively built for testing methods. There is a possibility that the VAE
does not work as eﬃciently with ”real” data.
Another aspect which could be taken into account for future work is the
improvement of the VAE in order to build it more eﬃcient for the z-scores.
Deﬁnitely, the results in this case are not satisfactory, and it is potentially the
reason why the VAE with the joined inputs did not accomplish completely
the results expected.
The structure of the VAE could as well change, with the aim of develop-
ing the function of the latent space, a ladder variational autoencoder [9]
could be implemented. The structure would consist on several latent layers
separated by diﬀerent hidden layers where all of the latent layers retain some
information during training. Therefore, it is possible to improve this VAE
for the detection of bacteria in a given sample.

6
Conclusion
The human body has 10 times more microbial organisms than human cells,
therefore, the proper identiﬁcation of these microbial species and its frequen-
cies may lead to a step forward in the understanding of many metabolic
mechanisms that take place and its impossible to reproduce in vitro.
Thus, this study aimed for designing a VAE capable of identifying as accu-
rate as possible the diﬀerent microorganisms found in a given metagenomic
sample.
We can conclude that between the two inputs separately, the abundance
matrix input is the one that makes the method more eﬀective. Along the
variations of the VAE, the 2-hidden layer VAE with largest hidden and la-
tent layer is the one which gives better results forming a total of 110 clusters.
Looking at the results with z-scores, the best model is as well the largest 2-
hidden layer VAE, nonetheless, the results compared to the other VAE are
poorer than predicted. Lastly, in the case of the combination of inputs, the
most eﬃcient model is the 1-hidden layer VAE that takes batches of 100 and
has a size of the hidden layer of 200 and a latent layer size of 128.
Even though there are some results that are not as optimal as we would
have expected, the results described in this thesis open the possibility of
developing new variational autoencoders that improve in their learning and
will be able to develop a proper identiﬁcation of microbial species present
in a speciﬁc sample. This would allow to overcome the current diﬃculties
regarding the identiﬁcation of microbial species,which is one of the key issues
in the metagenomic ﬁeld.
