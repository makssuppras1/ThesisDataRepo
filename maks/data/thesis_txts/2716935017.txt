Abstract 
 
The Danish fishery for Nephrops (Nephrops norvegicus) in Kattegat and Skagerrak is a mixed demersal 
trawl fishery and generates large parts of its revenue from wanted bycatches of flat- and roundfish. 
These fish species are subject to the EU Landing Obligation and may, therefore, not be discarded. Cod 
(Gadus morhua) stocks in Kattegat and Skagerrak are outside safe biological limits and catch quotas 
for cod are low. Because all cod catches must be landed and counted against quota, Nephrops fishers 
are tasked to reduce the capture of cod, as they would have to cease fishing once quotas are 
exhausted and if cod cannot be avoided during further fishing activity. The requirement for a better 
species selectivity in the fishery has resulted in the development and introduction of bycatch 
reduction devices (BRDs) that aim to facilitate the escape of cod from the trawls. The use of either one 
of two BRD concepts is currently mandatory to fishers: A sorting grid that eliminates virtually all 
commercial fish catches and results in a single-species Nephrops fishery, or an escape panel that leads 
to large reductions of commercial catches of many other roundfish species along with cod. These 
distinct profiles of species selectivity often do not match the quota availability of individual vessels, 
and the fishers, therefore, require a variety of available gears that allow them to target the desired 
catch compositions. In particular, at present, there is no gear option available that facilitates the 
escape of cod while maintaining a high retention of other important commercial roundfish species 
such as haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus) and whiting (Merlangius merlangus). 
This thesis describes the gear development and trial of a BRD aimed at the species-specific reduction 
of cod catches in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak. The BRD consists of an escape 
window that is located under an inclined panel in the trawl extension and can be accessed by fish 
swimming forward from the codend. This configuration aims to exploit the species-specific behavioral 
traits of cod, which have a strong swimming capacity and are more closely associated with the bottom 
of a trawl extension than other roundfish species. In a gear trial, the developed BRD facilitated the 
escape of 67 % of the encountered cod while retaining large parts of the catch of Nephrops, flatfish, 
and many other roundfish species. Such species selectivity has never before been demonstrated in a 
Nephrops fishery and a similar BRD could be a valuable addition to the toolbox of available gears for 
fishers. Further, the concept has great potential for application to a number of fisheries across the 
North Atlantic Ocean that are limited by catches of cod.

1 Introduction 
Mixed fisheries are often primarily directed at a small number of target species, however, wanted and 
unwanted bycatch can compose a large part of the catch. Wanted bycatch consists of individuals of 
commercial species that are marketable, for example because they are of legal size and because the 
vessel has a quota for them (Crowder & Murawski, 1998; Graham, 2010). Despite not being the 
primary target of the fishery, the wanted bycatch can account for large parts of the revenue in mixed 
fisheries (Crowder & Murawski, 1998; Krag et al., 2008). Unwanted bycatch is the fraction of the catch 
that is undesired by the fishers for various reasons, for example it includes species that are not 
marketable, undersized individuals of commercial species, protected species, and commercial species 
for which the vessel has no quota (Clucas, 1997). Unwanted bycatch is often discarded overboard with 
many individuals dead or injured (Graham, 2010; Hall et al., 2000). Discarding has for a long time been 
criticized for being unethical as well as for being a waste of ecological and economic resources (Bellido 
et al., 2011; Crowder & Murawski, 1998). To prevent this waste, fisheries management increasingly 
relies on so called discard bans that obligate fishers to land all individuals of regulated species and 
count them against their respective quotas (Diamond & Beukers-Stewart, 2011; European Union, 
2013; Hall et al., 2000).  
Vessels engaged in mixed fisheries usually have individual quotas for the different species 
encountered in the fishery, however, the species composition among their catch often does not reflect 
the availability of quotas. Despite the fishers’ ability to influence their catch composition through the 
consideration of spatial and seasonal fish assemblies (Poos et al., 2010), quota uptake among the 
different species often remains unbalanced (Hornborg et al., 2017). Under a discard ban, this can lead 
to the quota of a so called choke species to be exhausted before the full harvest potential for the other 
species in the fishery is reached (Hatcher, 2014). If further catches of the choke species cannot be 
avoided, the vessel must stop fishing and the fishery is choked. This scenario can be mitigated by 
individual transferable quotas (Squires et al., 1998), however additional quota under a choke species 
scenario can be expensive and approach the price of fines for over-quota landings (Hatcher, 2014). 
Regardless of whether the fishing activity is stopped, additional quota is bought, or fines are paid, 
reaching the quota limit of a choke species negatively affects the economic viability of a fishing 
operation. 
This has sparked intensive research into bycatch reduction devices (BRDs) that aim to reduce the 
fraction of unwanted species and undersized individuals in the catch (Hall et al., 2000; Kennelly & 
Broadhurst, 2021). BRDs use morphological and behavioral differences among species and sizes to 
==PAGE:6==
 
 
 
 
5 
facilitate the escape of unwanted individuals (Valdemarsen & Suuronen, 2003; Winger et al., 2010). 
The ability to exploit morphological differences between species is limited when they are similar in 
size and shape (Beutel et al., 2008), and many BRDs can only partially isolate a single unwanted species 
based on its behavioral properties (Krag et al., 2009; Thomsen, 1993). In mixed fisheries the avoidance 
of unwanted species in the catch is, therefore, often only achieved in combination with partial losses 
of the wanted catch and results in lost revenue (Catchpole & Revill, 2008; Graham, 2010; Griffin & 
Oliver, 1991). Many mixed fisheries would, therefore, greatly profit from gear modifications that allow 
to specifically reduce the retention of unwanted choke-species while retaining large fractions of the 
commercial catch. The ability of a fishing gear to capture certain fractions of the encountered fish 
population in terms of species, age, size, or behavior, and to release other fractions is called selectivity 
(Thomson & Ben-Yami, 1984). 
This thesis consists of a synopsis and a scientific manuscript. The synopsis aims to characterize the 
Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak and the challenges it faces. It discusses the 
behavioral and morphological factors that determine the catchability of different species and how 
previously trialed gear modifications aimed to exploit them to improve selectivity. The synopsis 
further describes the conceptualization and development of a BRD and identifies methods to describe 
and determine its selectivity. Addressing the lack of available gear options to selectively facilitate the 
escape of only the choke species, cod, a standalone scientific manuscript aims to describe the design 
and trial of a cod-specific BRD (manuscript). The results are put into management context, highlighting 
the novelty of the study and the significance of species selective avoidance of choke species. 
 
2 The Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak 
2.1 Fleet and profile 
The Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak has economic and cultural significance for the region, 
creating employment, and continuing Denmark’s tradition as a fishing nation (Semrau & Ortega Gras, 
2013; Skov et al., 2020). The Danish part of the fishery is exclusively conducted with demersal trawls, 
many of which are designed to catch various round- and flatfish species along with Nephrops, making 
it a mixed demersal trawl fishery (Frandsen, 2011). These wanted bycatch species account for a large 
part of the fishery’s economic returns (Krag et al., 2008). The Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and 
Skagerrak is shared with Sweden, where two fleet segments use trawls and pots, respectively (ICES, 
2021a). In 2021, Nephrops landings by Danish vessels amounted to 2658 tons in Skagerrak and 1805 
tons in Kattegat, generating 147.2 and 108.6 million DKK (19.8 and 14.6 million EUR), respectively 
==PAGE:7==
 
 
 
 
6 
(Danmarks Statistik, 2022). Among the many commercial fish species that are caught in the fishery, 
plaice (Pleuronectes platessa) and cod are the most important (Madsen et al., 2010). In 2021, Danish 
landings of plaice and cod from Skagerrak and Kattegat amounted to 4188 and 1386 tons for the 
respective species, generating 87.1 and 45.6 million DKK (11.7 and 6.13 million EUR), respectively 
(Danmarks Statistik, 2022). However, not all landings of demersal fish in the area are caught in the 
Nephrops-directed fishery, as other gillnet-, demersal seine- and trawl fisheries exist (ICES, 2022a). 
Landings of demersal fish from Kattegat are much lower than those from Skagerrak (Danmarks 
Statistik, 2022). The Danish Nephrops fleet is mainly comprised of vessels between 10 and 18 meters 
(Eliasen, 2014; Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2020). These can be operated by 1 to 4 persons and 
usually conduct day trips out of the many harbors along the Danish coast (Eliasen, 2014). Fishing 
activity is often focused on the early morning hours and generally tries to match the burrow-
emergence behavior of Nephrops. The fishing effort is focused on areas with muddy substrates that 
harbor colonies of Nephrops, the seabed in these areas is often swept by demersal trawls multiple 
times a year (Eigaard et al., 2017). 
 
2.2 Kattegat and Skagerrak 
Kattegat and Skagerrak are classified as FAO division 3a of the North-East Atlantic and considered part 
of the greater North Sea ecoregion (ICES, 2021b). The geographical divide between the two regions is 
described by the International Hydrographic Organization (1953) as a line connecting the 
northernmost point of Denmark’s Jutland peninsula with Paternoster Skær on the Swedish west coast 
(Figure 1). Skagerrak borders the North Sea in the West, Kattegat borders the Danish Straits of 
Øresund and the Great Belt in the South. Kattegat and Skagerrak are, therefore, marked as the straits 
that connect the Baltic- to the North Sea. 
 
==PAGE:8==
 
 
 
 
7 
 
Figure 1: Bathymetric map of Kattegat and Skagerrak. Data from ncei.noaa.gov 
Kattegat is a shallow, semi-enclosed sea with a strong halocline due to a large inflow of brackish water 
from the Baltic Sea (Ærtebjerg et al., 2003). Its bathymetry is dominated by a large coastal shelf with 
shallow waters < 20 m in the West, deeper areas of between 50 - 100 m are only located in a trench 
in the Eastern part, close to the Swedish coast (Carstensen et al., 2002; Gustafsson, 2000). The seabed 
is dominated by sand in the western and mud in the eastern part (Leth, 2008). Demersal fishing effort 
in Kattegat is inhomogeneously distributed with most effort allocated to muddy sediments at more 
than 22 m depth, resulting in heavily trawled areas in the Eastern and Northern parts (Pommer et al., 
2016). Skagerrak is an open shelf sea (Ærtebjerg et al., 2003) with an average depth of 210 m 
(Fonselius, 1990). The deepest areas are found in the Norwegian trench in the North, where depths 
reach roughly 700 m (Fonselius, 1990). The substrates found in Skagerrak are mostly sandy along the 
Danish coast and muddy towards the east and in the Norwegian trench, hard substrates are only found 
along the Swedish and Norwegian shores (Populus et al., 2017). Demersal trawling in Skagerrak is 
aggregated in the central and Eastern parts, the deepest areas of the Norwegian trench experience 
comparatively little demersal fishing (Eigaard et al., 2017). The area is exposed to a lot of human 
influence, including shipping (Grimvall & Larsson, 2014) and offshore wind energy generation 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
 
 
8 
(Bergström et al., 2014). Kattegat and Skagerrak are among the most intensely trawled areas in Europe 
(Eigaard et al., 2017).  
 
2.3 Commercial catch species 
2.3.1 Nephrops 
Despite catching a mix of demersal species, the fishery is mainly directed at Nephrops (Nielsen et al., 
2013), a marine astacid crustacean that lives in burrowed tunnels in the seabed (Tshudy, 2013) (Figure 
2). The species is widely distributed across the North-East Atlantic continental shelf and -slope, as well 
as parts of the Mediterranean Sea (Johnson et al., 2013). It occupies areas between 4 and 750 m depth 
with water temperatures between 6.4 and 17.3 °C (Johnson et al., 2013). Habitat suitable for 
colonization by Nephrops is characterized by large fraction of silt and clay among the substrate. This 
type of muddy sediment best allows Nephrops to build the burrows that they dwell in (Johnson et al., 
2013). Nephrops are opportunistic feeders and their diet includes crustaceans, molluscs, echinoderms 
and polychaetes (Parslow-Williams et al., 2002) as well as fish (Cristo, 1998). Diet composition is 
largely based on availability and food is acquired through active predation and scavenging (Cristo, 
1998; Parslow-Williams et al., 2002), but Nephrops are also capable of suspension feeding (Loo et al., 
1993). Most feeding activity occurs after dark and at dawn (Parslow-Williams et al., 2002), severely 
affecting catchability as Nephrops often leave their burrows to feed.

Nephrops eggs are fertilized internally upon egg-laying and subsequently attached to the pleopods 
under the abdominal section of the female (Farmer, 1974). Berried female Nephrops incubate the eggs 
for 6 to 10 months, depending on the water temperature (Farmer, 1974). During incubation, females 
are more closely associated with their burrows and may display altered behavior, negatively affecting 
their catchability and overall growth (Aguzzi et al., 2007; Johnson et al., 2013). Larvae hatch into the 
water where they undergo three pelagic larvae stages (Farmer, 1974). This pelagic phase can last up 
to 50 days, advection during this time leads to regional genetic interconnectivity between stocks and 
recruitment success is, therefore, only partially dependent on local stock status (Johnson et al., 2013). 
The mean weights of the species often lay between 18 to 40 g (Johnson et al., 2013). Maximum length 
L∞ (Bertalanffy, 1938) for the carapace of the larger, male Nephrops commonly ranges between 60 
and 80 mm (Johnson et al., 2013). Both mean weight and L∞ correlate negatively with the density of 
Nephrops in an area (Johnson et al., 2013; Ulmestrand & Eggert, 2001). Nephrops in Skagerrak and 
Kattegat are considered a large-growing stock (Ulmestrand & Eggert, 2001). Nephrops are a valuable 
species for human consumption, the average wholesale price at Denmarks largest auction for 
Nephrops in Strandby in 2021 was 72.74 DKK (9.78 EUR) per kg for the most common size class (2) 
(www.fiskeauktion.dk, 24-04-2022).  
 
2.3.2 Commercial bycatch 
While Nephrops are the main target of the mixed demersal fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak, a large 
part of the landings and revenue is generated from commercial bycatch species (Krag et al., 2008). 
These include mainly gadoids and flatfish, with cod and plaice being the most important (Madsen et 
al., 2010).  
 
Cod 
Atlantic cod is a benthopelagic predatory roundfish species that occupies various habitats across the 
North Atlantic and has great ecological and commercial value (Barth et al., 2017). Cod feed mainly on 
crustaceans and fish, but also consume mollusks and worms (Daan, 1973). With increasing size, the 
domination of food intake shifts from crustaceans towards fish (Daan, 1973).  Spawning mainly takes 
place between January and April (Brander, 2005), when cod aggregate in specific spawning sites where 
the populations are vulnerable to overfishing (Jonsson et al., 2016; Zemeckis et al., 2014). While 
Skagerrak does not have any major spawning areas and draws recruitment from the North Sea stock 
(André et al., 2016), cod in Kattegat mainly spawn in an area in the South East, further areas in the 
North and South-West have been frequented historically when the stock was healthy (Jonsson et al., 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
 
 
10 
2016). Egg and larval dispersal may last 60 to 90 days, causing geographical mixing of juveniles of 
different stocks (Brander, 2005; Jonsson et al., 2016). At present, cod encountered in Kattegat only 
include few individuals of the collapsed local stock, with recruits from both the North Sea and the 
Danish straits settling in the area for their juvenile life stages (André et al., 2016; Bryhn et al., 2022; 
Jonsson et al., 2016).  
The majority of cod landed in Denmark weigh between 1 and 4 kg, with the most common size class 
of 1-2 kg generating an average of 29.61 DKK (3.98 EUR) per kg at Denmark’s biggest auction for cod 
in Hirtshals in 2021 (www.fiskeauktion.dk, 28-04-2022). 
 
Plaice 
The European plaice is a right-eyed flounder distributed along the European coasts between Portugal 
and Iceland (Nielsen, 1986). The species is abundant in both Kattegat and Skagerrak (ICES, 2022b, 
2022c). As a flatfish, it is closely seabed-associated where it lays on- or digs into the sediment. Pelagic 
larvae settle into the species’ nursery habitat in shallow sandy areas, with increasing size the juveniles 
move to deeper water (Heessen et al., 2006). Plaice are opportunistic feeders (Wolff et al., 1981) and 
mainly consume polychaetes and mollusks (Beare et al., 2013). 
Plaice is a common species for human consumption and is frequently caught in mixed trawl fisheries 
as well as flatfish-directed fisheries using demersal seines (Noack et al., 2017). The majority of Danish 
landings of plaice is in the size class between 0.15 and 0.3 kg, generating an average of 16.6 DKK/kg at 
Denmark’s largest auction for plaice in Hanstholm in 2021 (www.fiskeauktion.dk, 03-05-2022). 
 
Other Species 
The fishery further catches a number of different commercial species of flat- and roundfish. Among 
the commonly caught roundfish are haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus), hake (Merluccius 
merluccius), whiting (Merlangius merlangus) and saithe (Pollachius virens). Witch flounder 
(Glyptocephalus cynoglossus) and lemon sole (Microstomus kitt) are the most important flatfish 
species apart from plaice. 
Haddock is a demersal roundfish species from the Gadidae family that feeds on small to medium sized 
endobenthos and, with increasing size, also on fish (Heessen et al., 2006; Mattson, 2011). The 
minimum size of 27 cm in Kattegat and Skagerrak is typically reached between ages 2 and 3 (Heessen 
et al., 2006). Haddock landed in Denmark most commonly weigh between 0.4 and 1 kg 
(fiskeauktion.dk; dfa.dk, 19-10-2022).  Hake is a long-bodied, fast-growing predatory roundfish (Mahe 
et al., 2007; Murua, 2010). The species is widely distributed in the Eastern Atlantic and the 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
 
 
11 
Mediterranean Sea and is most commonly found in depths between 70 and 370 m (Murua, 2010). In 
Denmark, hake are most commonly landed in sizes between 1.2 and 2.5 kg, but there are significant 
landings of hake of over 4 kg (fiskeauktion.dk; dfa.dk, 19-10-2022). Whiting is the smallest commercial 
gadoid species caught in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak. Whiting occur in large 
numbers and mainly feed on small fish and crustaceans (Hislop et al., 1991). The most common landing 
size of whiting in Denmark is between 0.35 and 0.5 kg, however, the often much smaller fish caught 
in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak only generate very low prices at auction 
(fiskeauktion.dk; dfa.dk, 19-10-2022). Saithe is a semi-pelagic gadoid that mainly feeds on fish and 
crustaceans (du Buit, 1991). In the fishery, saithe are most frequently encountered along the 
Norwegian trench in Skagerrak (Nielsen et al., 2013). The weight of saithe landed in Denmark most 
commonly ranges between 0.30 and 3 kg, but fish of over 5 kg are also landed frequently. Saithe have 
darker meat than other gadoids and are considered to be of less quality for eating, reflected by the 
low value compared to, for example, cod of equal size (fiskeauktion.dk; dfa.dk, 19-10-2022). 
Witch flounder is a right-eyed flatfish with a long, oval body and is often caught in the fishery, as it 
lives on fine sediments such as mud, silt and muddy sand, similar to those that Nephrops colonies are 
often found in (Cargnelli, 1999). The species prefers deep, cold water and is slow growing, reaching 
sizes up to 60 cm, but it is more commonly encountered in sizes up to 40 cm (FAO, 2022). Lemon sole 
is a right-eyed flatfish with a small head and a fleshy body, common size ranges between 20 and 30 
cm (Picton & Morrow, 2022). The species generally prefers hard sediments and feeds mainly on small 
invertebrates (Picton & Morrow, 2022). Further, there are catches of many other species that add to 
the generated revenue, including common sole (Solea solea), monkfish (Lophius piscatorius), ling 
(Molva molva), wolf fish (Anarhichas lupus) and turbot (Scophthalmus maximus). 
 
2.4 Demersal trawls for targeting Nephrops 
2.4.1 Design and operation 
Demersal trawls are, in the widest sense, conically tapered nets that are dragged over the sea bottom 
to capture marine animals (Figure 3). The mouth of the trawl is spread horizontally by trawl doors, 
also called otter boards, which are in contact with the seabed and attached to the towing vessel by 
long warps (Winger et al., 2010). The trawl is attached to the doors through its wings, bridles and 
sweeps. The vertical opening of the trawl mouth is achieved with floats on the headline and a weighted 
footrope. To prevent abrasion on uneven bottom, the footropes of demersal trawls are often fitted 
with chains or rubber discs that are meant to roll over the seabed (Pol & Carr, 2000). Depending on 
the bottom substrate and target species, different sizes and configurations of rubber discs on 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
 
12 
footropes are used, for example small, tightly spaced discs on cookie-sweep-footropes or large, 
broadly spaced discs on rockhopper gears (Pol & Carr, 2000). The body of a trawl is marked by a 
gradual reduction of the cross section that leads to the codend, the aft end of the trawl, where the 
catch is collected. Trawls are active gears and have to be towed through the water to catch fish and 
crustaceans. During deployment, a trawl is typically unraveled into the water from a drum and the 
trawl doors are attached. Then, depending on the water depth, several hundred meters of warps are 
released to lower the gear to the seabed behind the vessel. Towing progresses at speeds between 2 
and 3 knots (Frandsen, 2011) and typically lasts for around 4 hours in the Danish Nephrops fishery in 
Kattegat and Skagerrak. The process of bringing the gear and the catch back to the vessel is called 
haul-back. The warps are wound up by winches and the vessel reduces or stops steaming for this 
process (Madsen et al., 2008). Finally, the codend with the collected catch is heaved onboard the 
vessel (Madsen et al., 2008). 
Many of the trawls used in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak are designed to 
optimize total catch value by catching both Nephrops and fish, and, therefore, referred to as Combi-
trawls (Frandsen, 2011). In comparison to standard crustacean trawls, these are characterized by a 
higher vertical opening that reduces fish escape over the headline and they are often fished on longer 
sweeps that increase the catch of fish that are not in the direct path of the trawl (Melli, 2019; Winger 
et al., 2010). Especially the capture of Nephrops requires close bottom contact of the gear. Therefore, 
the footrope is often fitted with weighted grass-rope or rubber bobbins (seafish.org, 19.10.2022) that 
leave little space for escape below the fishing line. Commonly, two or more trawls are towed by the 
same vessel simultaneously using twin- or multi-rig trawl systems, where a central metal clump 
supplements the trawl doors as an attachment point (Feekings et al., 2016; Frandsen, 2011) (Figure 
3). The use of multiple trawls at once decreases the total amount of netting required, reducing the 
drag of the gear and thus increasing achievable horizontal spread by 20 to 30 % compared to a single 
trawl at a maintained vessel power (Graham & Ferro, 2004). The improved trawl spread increases the 
total catches of both Nephrops and commercial bycatch species (Sangster & Breen, 1998). The fraction 
of Nephrops among the catch is greater when fishing with twin- and multi-rigs because the defining 
parameter for their capture is the seabed swept by the trawls, whereas it is the distance between the 
trawl doors for many fish species (Feekings et al., 2016). 
 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
 
13 
 
Figure 3: Components of a twin-rig trawl setup for a mixed Nephrops fishery. (Adapted from seafish.org) 
The Combi-trawls are typically made of standard diamond-shaped polyethylene netting with a mesh 
size of 80 mm in the trawl body and 90 mm in the codend. Sections of the trawl are subject to 
modifications of this standard netting, these are described in section 3.1.3. Often, the trawls have 
around 400 meshes circumference in the trawl mouth with a footrope length of roughly 40 m and a 
headline height of 1.5 - 2 m. Both door spread and sweep length for a setup as described can be around 
100 m (Frandsen et al., 2009).  
 
2.4.2 Behavior of Nephrops and fish and its role in catchability 
The probability for an individual to be captured by a trawl depends on its morphology and behavior. 
While the body cross section ultimately determines whether escape through a mesh opening is 
possible, behavior plays a large role in determining whether an individual will enter the trawl and 
whether it can facilitate successful escape. Different species react to stimulation by the vessel, warps, 
trawl doors, sweeps, and components of the trawl in different ways, directly influencing their 
catchability. Among the species caught in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak, the 
behavioral differences are most pronounced between Nephrops, flatfish and roundfish, but there are 
also differences within these groups, particularly among the roundfish species. 
 
==PAGE:15==

Pre- trawl determinants of catchability 
To be caught by a demersal trawl, fish and crustaceans must be directly in the path of the trawl where 
they can be overtaken by the trawl mouth (Winger et al., 2010). Fish that are not in the direct path of 
the trawl in the first place are often herded there as an effect of active avoidance of threatening stimuli 
(Winger et al., 2010). These include the noise of the vessel and the vibrations of the warps that may 
cause fish in the water column to seek bottom proximity (Handegard & Tjøstheim, 2005; Winger et 
al., 2010), as well as sediment kicked up by the doors and sweeps which triggers mainly avoidance 
movement in the horizontal plane (Catchpole & Revill, 2008; Winger et al., 2010). Roundfish often 
actively navigate around an approaching trawl door and, depending on flight direction, frequently 
swim directly towards the trawl mouth and into the trawl path (Hall et al., 1986; Winger et al., 2010). 
Flatfish and monkfish generally keep a closer reaction distance (typically < 1 m) to the doors and 
sweeps, moving away from the stimulus at a 90 degree angle, gradually getting herded into the trawl 
path (Ryer, 2008; Walsh & Hickey, 1993; Winger et al., 2010).  
When the mouth of the trawl arrives within visual distance, many fish swim along in towing direction 
ahead of the trawl for up to several minutes and assume different heights above the seabed (Winger 
et al., 2010). This ordered response is most prominent in roundfish and the vertical position is often 
species dependent. Cod display this cruising behavior close to the bottom, whereas haddock and 
whiting stay higher in the water column (Main & Sangster, 1981; Walsh & Hickey, 1993). Low 
swimming species such as cod and flatfish can seek escape under the footrope between rock hoppers 
or when bottom contact is briefly lost (Dahm, 1997), but escape below the footrope may also occur 
as a result of accidental collision (Winger et al., 2010). Likewise, early rising species such as haddock 
can sometimes escape over the headline (Krag et al., 2015). Nephrops respond to a trawl’s ground 
gear late and are often touched before they try to flee by tail flicking (Newland & Chapman, 1989), 
lifting them up to 50 cm above the seabed (Newland et al., 1988). The directional coordination and 
continuity of these swimming bursts are limited, which means that there is very little herding effect 
for Nephrops by the trawl doors and sweeps (Graham & Ferro, 2004; Newland & Chapman, 1989). The 
catch efficiency regarding this species is, thus, largely dictated by the maintained close bottom contact 
of the footrope and the width of the trawl path (Graham & Ferro, 2004; Sangster & Breen, 1998). Both, 
herding and escape behavior as reactions to visual stimuli, are strongly light dependent and greatly 
reduced at night (Walsh & Hickey, 1993). This may influence fish behavior and the resulting selectivity 
in Nephrops trawls, as the disturbance of the soft sediments that Nephrops live in likely reduces the 
light level around the trawl (Catchpole & Revill, 2008). 
 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
 
15 
In-trawl behavior and escape from the trawl 
After cruise-swimming ahead of the trawl mouth for some time, fish tire and drop back into the body 
of the trawl (Walsh & Hickey, 1993). Many fish get overtaken by the footrope and headline, but some 
species such as haddock are also known to actively turn and swim into the trawl (Walsh & Hickey, 
1993). Haddock and saithe enter the trawl high, whereas cod and flatfish generally enter the trawl at 
a lower level (Catchpole & Revill, 2008; Ferro et al., 2007; Main & Sangster, 1985). Fish behavior after 
entering the trawl depends on a range of factors, including lighting, water temperature, species, size, 
towing speed, fish density in the trawl, and more (Jones et al., 2008; Winger et al., 2010). Knowledge 
of cod behavior in trawls is still very limited (Pol & Eayrs, 2021). However, it is agreed that cod 
generally swim in tow direction while slowly falling back within the trawl (Catchpole & Revill, 2008). 
This process is suspended by periods where cod hold a certain position in the trawl for some time and 
even drift back and forth (Briggs, 1992). The ability to hold station and to swim along at trawl speed 
for prolonged periods is related to the high swimming endurance of cod (Beamish, 1966). Despite 
usually entering the trawl low, cod often display some rising behavior in the tapered middle section 
of the trawl (Fryer et al., 2017; Thomsen, 1993), however, they are much closer associated with the 
bottom of trawls than most other roundfish species (Ferro et al., 2007; Fraser & Angus, 2019; Fryer et 
al., 2017; Krag et al., 2009). Cod rarely undertake active escape attempts (Briggs, 1992; Grimaldo et 
al., 2007). This is in contrast to the behavior of many other roundfish such as whiting and saithe, which 
are strongly associated with the top of the trawl where they contact mesh openings (Briggs, 1992; 
Catchpole & Revill, 2008). Saithe further display fast, forward swimming bursts within the trawl 
(Briggs, 1992). Haddock often swim down the trawl towards the codend, likely because this is the only 
direction where no netting is seen (Winger et al., 2010). Haddock swimming against tow direction will 
turn around once they reach a narrowing or obstruction in their path and often proceed to actively 
contact the mesh (He et al., 2008). In contrast to most roundfish species, flatfish usually stay very close 
to the lower sheet of the net, but can also rise up in some cases (Briggs, 1992; Fryer et al., 2017; Ryer, 
2008). Occasionally, flatfish attempt to swim forward or turn to swim deeper into the net (Ryer, 2008). 
Nephrops drift along the lower sheet of the trawl and their tail flicking rarely results in coordinated 
movements (Briggs, 1992; Catchpole & Revill, 2008; Fryer et al., 2017). 
As fish fall back in the trawl, roundfish such as haddock and cod often hold their position ahead of the 
narrow codend (Winger et al., 2010). When tiring, they fall back into the codend, where they either 
become part of the buildup of exhausted and dying fish pressed to the back of the codend, or maintain 
their position ahead of this buildup for some time (Winger et al., 2010). This area of the trawl is 
characterized by a lot of crowding, which often causes the orderly behavior to cease and panicked 
burst swimming can cause fish to contact the netting, often facilitating the escape of smaller fish 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
 
16 
(Winger et al., 2010). Further, the catch buildup in the back of the codend causes the current to flow 
out laterally through the mesh on the circumference of the codend, carrying with it smaller fish and 
opening the mesh for bigger roundfish to fit through (Grimaldo et al., 2007). Fish, that cannot escape 
due to their morphology, their lack of energy or their inability to contact the mesh at an optimum 
angle, exhaust and become part of the catch accumulation in the back of the codend.  
A significant portion of the escape from a codend happens during haul-back (Grimaldo et al., 2009; 
Herrmann et al., 2013a; Madsen et al., 2012a). Madsen et al. (2008) observed the escape rate of 
Nephrops during haul-back and at the surface to be 7 times higher than during towing, for haddock it 
was 2.7 higher during haul back and 1.7 higher at the surface. The improved escape during haul-back 
is likely the result of the slower speed, slack netting, and pulsing movements that together may 
promote active escape as well as sieving the catch (Madsen et al., 2008). 
 
3 Challenges to the fishery and attempts to address them 
3.1 Management  
3.1.1 Stock distinction 
The fishing opportunities for many of the commercial species caught in the Danish Nephrops fishery 
in Kattegat and Skagerrak are set by the EU Fisheries Council in joint management with Norway and 
the UK (oceans-and-fisheries.ec.europa.eu, 04.10.2022). The decisions for TACs are made on the base 
of proposals by the EU Commission, which receives advice from the International Council for the 
Exploration of the Sea (ICES). The advice is given in the form of catch recommendations based on stock 
assessments for the individual fish stocks under the current management goals. The individual fish 
populations in the Kattegat-Skagerrak area are often assessed as parts of larger interregional stocks 
in the greater North Sea ecoregion (e.g. together with the North Sea and the English Channel) and in 
the North East Atlantic (e.g. West of Scotland, Rockall, Celtic Seas, Bay of Biscay). Catch advice and 
TACs, however, are in the cases of cod, haddock and plaice given for each of the subareas (ICES, 2022d, 
2022e). Catch advice for hake, saithe, lemon sole and witch flounder is given as a combination for 
Kattegat, Skagerrak and additional regions (ICES, 2022f, 2022g, 2022h, 2022i). Cod in Kattegat are 
managed as an independent stock (ICES, 2022j). Plaice in Kattegat are managed together with 
populations in the belts and sound of the North-Western Baltic Sea, but are assigned an individual TAC 
(ICES, 2022c). Of the species frequently caught in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
 
17 
Skagerrak, only whiting and Nephrops are treated as stocks exclusively covering both Skagerrak and 
Kattegat, advice for these species is given for both regions combined (ICES, 2021a, 2022k).  
Many of the stocks that are fished on by the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak are 
managed as part of the EU multiannual management plan for demersal stocks in the North Sea 
(European Union, 2018; ICES, 2022a). This management plan aims to restore and maintain all fish 
stocks above a biomass level that allows to harvest the highest yield that can be sustained over time 
(European Union, 2018). The multiannual approach also allows better predictability for fishers. The 
common management of multiple species under one plan allows to account for mixed fisheries 
interactions, as most stocks are caught in a range of mixed fisheries (ICES, 2022a). Further, it takes 
into account biological interactions within and between fish stocks (European Union, 2018).  
 
3.1.2 Stock status 
The Kattegat cod stock has declined drastically since the 1980s and the spawning stock biomass (SSB) 
has been the lowest on record in recent years (ICES, 2022j; Svedäng & Bardon, 2003). ICES has 
renewed its advice of zero catch yearly since 2020 and with no targeted cod fishery, cod is mainly 
taken as bycatch in the fishery for Nephrops (ICES, 2022j). The SSB of the greater North Sea cod stock 
is outside safe biological limits and below the biomass reference point Blim, at which the population is 
considered to have reduced reproductive potential (ICES, 2022e). Accordingly, ICES recommendations 
for fishing pressure and catches are low, and the TAC of 1893 tons for Skagerrak in 2022 marks an all-
time low (ICES, 2022e).  
In contrast to cod, the stocks of many of the other commercial fish species caught in the Danish 
Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skaggerak are healthy and offer better fishing opportunities. For 
example, plaice are abundant and catches in both Skagerrak and Kattegat could be increased to reach 
maximum sustainable yield (MSY) (ICES, 2022c, 2022b). Likewise, Nephrops in Kattegat and Skagerrak 
are underexploited, with catches staying below the TACs in recent years (ICES, 2021a). There are no 
signs of overexploitation for whiting and lemon sole (ICES, 2022k, 2022h). The stock of hake that 
includes Kattegat and Skagerrak is fished sustainably (ICES, 2022f). Haddock SSB in the assessment 
that includes Skagerrak has increased greatly as the result of high recruitment of the 2019 and 2020 
year classes, however, large increases of TAC have only been agreed on for the West of Scotland (ICES, 
2022d). The TAC for haddock in Skagerrak is 2761 tons in 2022 (ICES, 2022d). Catch advice for saithe 
in the North Sea and Skagerrak is set at a comparatively low level (58 912 tons combined for 2023), as 
there is no longer 95 % confidence that the SSB is above Blim. Likewise, the SSB for witch flounder may 
be outside safe biological limits, however, the species has seen great recruitment classes in recent 
years (ICES, 2022i).

18 
Under a single species approach, catches of a number of the species in the Danish Nephrops fishery in 
Kattegat and Skagerrak could be improved and maintained over time without compromising 
sustainably. Given its nature as a mixed demersal fishery, however, the Danish fishery for Nephrops in 
Kattegat and Skagerrak is, like many other fisheries in the greater North Sea ecoregion, limited by the 
low quotas for cod that are maintained to rebuild the stock (Mackinson et al., 2018; Mortensen et al., 
2018; Nielsen et al., 2013).  
 
3.1.3 Management tools 
A number of management measures are in place to regulate the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat 
and Skagerrak. Among them are gear restrictions, the EU Landing Obligation, as well as spatial and 
temporal closures. Often, multiple management tools are combined. 
Nephrops trawling in both Kattegat and Skagerrak is allowed using two different concepts of codend 
configurations: a 35 mm bar-space grid with a 70 mm square mesh codend (SMC) or a 90 mm diamond 
mesh codend with a SELTRA top-escape panel as the top sheet of a 4-panel sorting box of at least 3 m 
length located no more than 4 m from the codline (Ministeriet for Fødevarer Landbrug og Fiskeri, 
2022b). The SELTRA panel must be constructed of square- or diamond mesh. When using square mesh, 
a mesh size of at least 300 mm is required in Kattegat, in Skagerrak square mesh SELTRA panels must 
have at least 140 mm mesh size (Ministeriet for Fødevarer Landbrug og Fiskeri, 2022b). Diamond mesh 
SELTRA panels must have at least 270 mm mesh size and joining ratios to the 90 mm mesh are 1:3 and 
1:4 in Skagerrak and Kattegat, respectively (Ministeriet for Fødevarer Landbrug og Fiskeri, 2022b). 
Both, the SELTRA panels and the 35 mm grid-70 mm SMC combination, aim to reduce bycatches. While 
the grid excludes catches of all commercial species other than Nephrops, the SELTRA panels mainly 
reduce the catches of roundfish including cod, depending on mesh size also of commercial sizes 
(Madsen et al., 2012b; Valentinsson & Ulmestrand, 2008).  
The Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak falls under the EU Landing Obligation that has 
gradually been introduced to the fishery between 2016 and 2019. The Landing Obligation allows 
exceptions for species and fisheries where discard survival has proven to be high and de minimis 
exceptions where selectivity is hard to increase or the handling of unwanted catches generates 
disproportionate costs (European Union, 2013). Based on survivability, Nephrops are exempt from the 
Landing Obligation in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak (European Union, 2020; 
Fox et al., 2020). A de minimis discard exemption at a level of 4 % of the combined annual total catch 
by weight of Nephrops, common sole, haddock, whiting, Northern prawn (Pandalus borealis), cod, 
saithe and hake is in place for undersized individuals of common sole, haddock, whiting, cod, saithe 
and hake caught in the 35 mm grid - 70 mm SMC combination (European Union, 2020). Fishers using 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
 
19 
90 mm mesh in combination with a SELTRA panel are allowed to discard undersized whiting of up to 
2 % in weight of their total annual catch of Nephrops, cod, haddock, whiting, saithe, common sole, 
plaice and hake (European Union, 2020). Discards under the exemptions from the Landing Obligation 
do not need to be counted against quota, however, they must be documented (European Union, 
2013). The inclusion of cod in the Landing Obligation makes it a choke species in many fisheries in the 
greater North Sea (Mackinson et al., 2018; Mortensen et al., 2018; Nielsen et al., 2013). 
To ensure that vessels do not discard cod despite the Landing Obligation, Danish bottom trawlers in 
Kattegat must monitor their fishing activities electronically using cameras since 2022 (Ministeriet for 
Fødevarer Landbrug og Fiskeri, 2022a). This addresses one of the major perceived weak points of the 
Landing Obligation, the lack of surveillance and control to detect and deter non-compliance (Plet-
Hansen et al., 2017). 
The Danish fisheries are managed through Individual Transferable Quotas (ITQs) (Savina et al., 2022). 
Under an ITQ system, the quota shares for each vessel and species can be traded. This management 
tool can help to make the fishery more efficient by reducing overcapacity and favoring more efficient 
fishers (Hoff et al., 2021). An applied example of this could be a vessel that successfully avoids catches 
of the choke species cod during its fishing operations for other species. The fisher could then trade 
their remaining cod quota for additional quota of other species or sell it, rewarding their efficiency 
with increased economic returns. Likewise, the choke risk for an individual vessel can be reduced when 
it has the option to buy or trade for additional quota. This allows to use capacities more efficiently up 
to the point where the choke species quota availability becomes restrictive at the fleet level (Frost & 
Hoff, 2017). 
To protect juveniles of cod, haddock, saithe and whiting, real-time closures for demersal fishing can 
be declared for areas in Skagerrak (European Union, 2010). This measure takes effect when more than 
10 % of the combined weight of the four species in a haul is made up of juveniles of the four species, 
or when 7.5 % of it is made up of juvenile cod (European Union, 2010). In Swedish Kattegat, the 
avoidance of finfish bycatch, especially cod, was encouraged by allowing access to near-coastal 
Nephrops grounds only for such trawlers that use a grid (Hornborg et al., 2017). Further, quota 
allocations and effort restrictions based on selective gear choice have been used to pursue the same 
goal (Hornborg et al., 2017). 
 
3.2 Improving selectivity in the fishery 
The problem of unwanted bycatch in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak has led 
to intensive research into the development of more selective gears. These often aim to facilitate the 
release of undersized individuals of different species, but in many cases intend to alter the species 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
 
20 
composition of the catch, primarily by reducing the retention of cod. Quota availability for different 
commercial species can vary greatly between vessels, and quota uptake depends on several factors 
related to the fishing operation and the choice of gear. The fishers, therefore, require a toolbox of 
fishing gears that allows them to selectively catch compositions in dependence on quota availability 
(Feekings et al., 2019). 
 
3.2.1 Codend mesh 
The most straight-forward option to influence the selectivity of a codend is the adjustment of the size, 
shape and opening angle of the codend mesh (MacLennan, 1992). An increase in mesh size allows a 
larger fraction of the unwanted undersized part of the catch to escape, but eventually leads to the 
escape of commercially sized individuals (Briggs, 1986). When altering the shape and opening angle 
of the mesh, different species experience increased or decreased escape, depending on how their 
body cross section matches the opening of the mesh (Herrmann et al., 2009). 
 
 
Figure 4: Depending on orientation, the same sheet of netting can be used as diamond mesh (left), square mesh (T45) (middle), and T90 mesh 
(right) to generate different mesh shapes and selection properties. (Browne et al., 2016). 
Nephrops have a shallow selection curve because they often do not contact the mesh with their 
smallest cross section and get caught with their many appendages (Briggs, 1986; Frandsen et al., 
2010a). In choosing a mesh size for appropriate size selectivity for Nephrops, fisheries management 
must, therefore, compromise between high retention of undersized Nephrops at small mesh sizes and 
losses of commercially sized individuals at greater mesh sizes. 90 mm diamond mesh, as used in the 
Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak, is considered to be the largest mesh size where 
no large escape of commercially sized Nephrops must be expected (Catchpole & Revill, 2008). This 
mesh size is bigger than that used in many other Nephrops fisheries that aim for total retention of 
commercially sized Nephrops, often with minimum sizes smaller than the 32 mm carapace length 
currently mandated in Kattegat and Skagerrak (Briggs, 1986; Campos et al., 2002; Stergiou et al., 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
 
21 
1997). Despite being wider than the mesh used in many other Nephrops-directed fisheries, the 90 mm 
diamond mesh used in Skagerrak and Kattegat retains large numbers of undersized individuals of 
many fish species caught in the mixed fishery (Krag et al., 2008). An increase of mesh size to 120 mm, 
as used to facilitate appropriate selectivity in many white- and flatfish fisheries, would lose large parts 
of the commercial Nephrops catch (Krag et al., 2008).  
Diamond mesh codends are under increased stress when catch is accumulated, closing the mesh 
(Herrmann et al., 2007). At the same time, the catch accumulation causes the water to flow out of the 
codend laterally, causing a bulb that opens the mesh just ahead of the catch accumulation (Robertson 
& Stewart, 1988). This effect is reduced with larger codend circumferences and the Danish Nephrops 
fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak allows no more than 100 meshes around the codend to facilitate 
sufficient mesh openings to release part of the undersized fish catch (Herrmann et al., 2007; 
MacLennan, 1992). Because most fish escape from the more widely opened diamond meshes just 
before the catch accumulation, many studies have explored the possibility to intentionally increase 
the opening angle of the codend mesh to influence selectivity. At a given mesh size, smaller opening 
angles will generally have little or positive effects on the escape of flatfish (He, 2007; Sala et al., 2016), 
whereas larger opening angles will increase the lengths at which many roundfish species can escape 
(Herrmann et al., 2009).  
A common approach to widen opening angles is the use of so called T90 mesh, diamond mesh turned 
90 degrees. Given the nature of the knots used to fabricate diamond mesh, turning it by 90 degrees 
achieves much greater opening angles (Graham, 2010; Herrmann et al., 2007). Madsen et al. (2012a) 
compared a T90 90 mm codend to a standard 90 mm diamond mesh (DM) codend in the Kattegat-
Skagerrak Nephrops fishery. They found the T90 codend to pass significantly more Nephrops above 
and below the 40 mm carapace length (CL) minimum landing size that was in place at the time of the 
study. The T90 codend retained significantly more undersized plaice while indicating an escape of cod 
at greater sizes than in the standard codend (Madsen et al., 2012a). 
Many studies have investigated the use of square mesh, either by using specially made knotless square 
mesh or by simply turning standard diamond netting by 45 degrees (T45). Square mesh has the 
advantage of maintaining its shape during all phases of the haul and thus staying open independent 
of catch weights and towing speed (Robertson & Stewart, 1988). This can be particularly important 
when aiming to improve size selectivity for Nephrops, as they tend to roll along the bottom sheet and 
in the process contact more open meshes in square mesh (SM) codends, increasing the chance of 
lining up at an angle favoring escape (Briggs, 1992; Frandsen et al., 2010b). At a given mesh size, the 
wide open mesh in SM codends facilitates escape for larger roundfish such as cod, haddock and 
whiting at a given mesh size, improving the selectivity for these species (Halliday et al., 1999; 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
 
22 
Robertson & Stewart, 1988). The opposite effect applies to flatfish, where square mesh tends to have 
a worse selectivity than diamond mesh (He, 2007; Walsh et al., 1992). Further, square mesh 
significantly increases the water flow through the codend, tiring fish out more quickly and potentially 
reducing the number and efficiency of escape attempts (Main & Sangster, 1991). Frandsen et al. 
(2010b) compared a 70 mm SMC to a 90 mm diamond mesh codend (DMC) in the Kattegat-Skagerrak 
Nephrops fishery and found sizes at which haddock, whiting, cod, and Nephrops could escape to be 
higher in the SMC despite the smaller mesh size. Plaice experienced a higher retention of undersized 
individuals in the SMC than in the DMC (Frandsen et al., 2010b). 
Further effects on the selectivity of the mesh in a codend are induced by the material and thickness 
of the twine, as well as the number of twines used (single- or double twine) (Herrmann et al., 2013b). 
An increase of rigidity by thicker or double twine or a less flexible material will generally reduce the 
selectivity, as fish will be less successful in pushing open a mesh to fit their body through (Herrmann 
et al., 2013b). Further, the twine properties also play a role in flow dynamics and affect the behavior 
of the fish, further influencing the selectivity (Ferro & O’Neill, 1994). 
The species caught in the Danish mixed demersal fishery require different mesh sizes and shapes to 
facilitate appropriate size selection. To date, no one-fits-all solution for codend mesh has been found 
that can retain large fractions of commercially sized individuals while facilitating the escape of 
undersized individuals across the different species of flat- and roundfish as well as Nephrops. The 
codend mesh used in the fishery is, therefore, a compromise in terms of size selectivity. Species 
selectivity, e.g. the reduction of choke species catches, can only be addressed by mesh choice to a 
small degree, as large individuals will always be retained. 
 
3.2.2 Escape panels 
Selectivity for the fish species caught in Nephrops fisheries can often be improved by the use of escape 
panels. These typically use mesh sizes and shapes that enable fish, particularly of many roundfish 
species, to leave the trawl. In Nephrops fisheries, escape panels are usually placed in the top section 
of the trawl, where the lowest contact likelihood for Nephrops is expected (Drewery et al., 2010; Krag 
et al., 2009). This position also favors the escape of many roundfish such as haddock, whiting and 
saithe that are known to display strong rising behavior in the trawl and stay close to the top sheet 
while gradually falling back in the trawl, making them likely to contact a top panel (Catchpole & Revill, 
2008; Krag et al., 2009). The reduction of cod catches through the use of escape panels is highly 
dependent on panel position (Herrmann et al., 2015). The panels are often placed in the codend 
extension where crowding occurs as fish hold position in the narrowing trawl, making them more likely 
to contact the mesh and escape (Catchpole & Revill, 2008; Herrmann et al., 2015). Therefore, 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
 
 
23 
positioning can be important in the design of escape panels (Herrmann et al., 2015; Winger et al., 
2010). If the panel is placed too far forward, fish will likely undertake few if any escape attempts when 
passing it while falling back to the codend and the distance can be too great for them to swim forward 
from the codend and escape later (Herrmann et al., 2015; Krag et al., 2008; Winger et al., 2010). If the 
panel is located too far towards the codend, accumulated catch including Nephrops may be washed 
out (Krag et al., 2008; Winger et al., 2010). 
The escape panels used in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak are called SELTRA 
panels and are installed as the top sheet in a four sheet codend extension (see section 3.1.3). 
Compared to panel placement 3 m from the codline, placement of a SELTRA panel with 300 mm SM 4 
m ahead of the codline has been found to retain significantly more plaice between 25 and 32 cm and 
significantly more large individuals of cod and Nephrops (Feekings et al., 2020). The maximum legal 
placement at 4 m from the codline must, therefore, be understood as a compromise that allows the 
retention of large fractions of plaice and Nephrops catch while reducing the retention of cod, though 
to a lesser extent than possible with placement at 3 m (Feekings et al., 2020; Ministeriet for Fødevarer 
Landbrug og Fiskeri, 2022b). The SELTRA panels used in Kattegat (300 mm SM or 270 mm DM with a 
joining ratio of 1:4) aim to facilitate a high escape of cod, including large individuals. In Skagerrak, 
trawlers have more flexibility. Besides a 270 mm DM top panel with a joining ration of 1:3 and, 
therefore, a more closed mesh than that in Kattegat, they are also allowed to use a 140 mm SM panel 
as part of either the SELTRA sorting box or a two panel design (Ministeriet for Fødevarer Landbrug og 
Fiskeri, 2022b). This is because there is a TAC for cod in Skagerrak and thus the possibility to target 
roundfish including cod of commercial size along with Nephrops, should a fisher with available cod 
quota choose to do so (ICES, 2022e).

Several studies have explored the potential to increase the contact likelihood of fish with top escape 
panels by using obstructions in the trawl path to trigger an escape response. Herrmann et al. (2015) 
found that placing float ropes under a top escape panel greatly stimulated cod escape behavior and, 
therefore, reduced the retention. A design based on similar obstructions was tested in the Danish 
Nephrops fishery in Skagerrak and the North Sea by Krag et al. (2016) and achieved a small but 
significant increase in the escape probability of cod between 20 and 40 cm without losses of Nephrops. 
Savina et al. (2022) tested a design with scaring floats on ropes under a large diamond-shaped opening 
in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak and achieved catch reductions of 71 % for 
cod, 94 % for saithe and 22 % for plaice compared to a standard SELTRA without the added device. 
The design is intended for use by fishers who aim to minimize catches of all fish without the loss of 
Nephrops and a similar design using scaring floats under a SELTRA panel is legal to use in Kattegat 
(Ministeriet for Fødevarer Landbrug og Fiskeri, 2022b; Savina et al., 2019). The use of a large diamond 
opening instead of a SELTRA mesh panel has also been investigated by Feekings et al. (2020). They 
found a high retention of Nephrops and plaice with a low retention of cod (Feekings et al., 2020). 
Escape panels can be effective in the reduction of cod and the escape effectivity can be further 
improved by the use of scaring floats and large diamond openings instead of mesh panels. However, 
both panels and possible adaptations often increase the escape of other fish species along with cod. 
In a mixed fishery where these make up a large part of the revenue, the SELTRA panels can, therefore, 
not fully provide the desired species selectivity.  
 
3.2.3 Grids 
A common way to reduce fish bycatch in crustacean fisheries is the use of sorting grids (Broadhurst, 
2000). There, the selection is mainly facilitated through size, as the bar spacing is chosen to be wide 
enough to pass the wanted crustacean catch into the codend while physically sorting out larger fish 
and other bycatch species (Broadhurst, 2000). Often, grids are aimed at total fish exclusion and their 
use results in a single-species fishery (Catchpole et al., 2006; Valentinsson & Ulmestrand, 2008). 
The grid design used in the Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak has vertical bars with a bar 
spacing of 35 mm. It is referred to as the “Swedish grid” because it was first introduced to the Swedish 
Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak in 2004 to prevent the catch of cod (Valentinsson & 
Ulmestrand, 2008). The grid is angled back, leading the sorted-out individuals to an escape opening in 
the top panel of the codend extension. The Danish law allows its use in combination with an 8 m long 
70 mm SM codend that facilitates escape for many fish that are small enough to pass the grid and also 
facilitates size section for Nephrops (Ministeriet for Fødevarer Landbrug og Fiskeri, 2022b; 
Valentinsson & Ulmestrand, 2008).

Guiding panels can be used to funnel all individuals towards the bottom of the grid (Madsen et al., 
2017). This improves the likelihood of contact with the grid, as some individuals may perceive the grid 
as an obstacle and escape without being subjected to its size selective properties or incidentally get 
swept out of the area close to the escape opening (Brčić et al., 2015; Madsen et al., 2017). The use of 
grids is tied to several challenges including the risk of clogging (Polet, 2002), hydrodynamic influences 
that can lead to catch reductions (Grimaldo & Larsen, 2005), as well as complications with onboard 
handling given their rigidity and weight (Grimaldo & Larsen, 2005). 
In mixed Nephrops fisheries, the use of grids results in large losses of revenue through the lack of 
commercial fish bycatch (Catchpole et al., 2006). Further, the 35 mm bar-spaced Swedish grid is known 
to also lose part of the catch of large Nephrops (Drewery et al., 2010; Frandsen et al., 2009). In the 
Danish fishery, Swedish grids are, therefore, mainly employed by fishers who do not hold quota for 
the fish bycatch species or have exhausted it.  
 
3.2.4 Other approaches aiming to improve selectivity in Nephrops fisheries 
Some studies have explored the potential to further exploit species-specific behavior to facilitate 
selection. For example, the use of trawls with shifted back headlines (i.e. “topless” trawls) allows rising 
fish to escape before entering the trawl. This method favors the escape of species with strong rising 
behavior such as haddock and whiting while retaining the Nephrops catch (Krag et al., 2015; Revill et 
al., 2006). The design can also reduce catches of cod when used in small trawls with a low headline 
(Krag et al., 2015). Another approach aiming to reduce unwanted catches before they enter the trawl 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
 
 
26 
is the use of anterior gear modifications. For example, herding of fish towards the trawl mouth can be 
reduced by the addition of scaring lines and thus reduce the fraction of fish catch in crustacean-
directed fisheries (Melli et al., 2017). Likewise, adjustments of sweep angles and lengths can influence 
herding efficiency for both flat- and roundfish (Sangster & Breen, 1998; Winger et al., 2010). 
Literature describes various designs that aim to exploit vertical species distribution within the trawl to 
facilitate the separation of fish from Nephrops (Krag et al., 2009; Rihan & McDonnell, 2003; Santos et 
al., 2018). This approach can be used to lead fish that are higher up in the trawl out (Rihan & 
McDonnell, 2003) or into a separate codend (Cosgrove et al., 2019; Santos et al., 2018). The installation 
of panels or frames that span the trawl horizontally achieves limited separation from Nephrops for 
fish such as cod and flatfish that are less likely to enter compartments higher up in the trawl (Fryer et 
al., 2017; Krag et al., 2009). Therefore, such designs often further use inclined mesh panels to guide 
or sieve fish up (Rihan & McDonnell, 2003; Santos et al., 2018). Vertical separation, especially with the 
use of inclined mesh panels, can separate fish catch from crustaceans, but does not differentiate 
between wanted and unwanted fish species. 
Many fish species display responses to light, and multiple studies have tried to use this to facilitate 
species selectivity (Hannah et al., 2015; Melli et al., 2018; Southworth et al., 2020). For example, Melli 
et al. (2018) examined the potential to use movement towards or away from green LED lights to 
improve vertical species separation in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Skagerrak. While they did 
observe fish responses to the lights, they could not identify any clear species specific phototaxis (Melli 
et al., 2018). Karlsen et al. (2021) inserted sheets of luminous netting into the tapered trawl section 
ahead of a vertically divided codend and found it to increase the presence of gadoids and large 
Nephrops in the lower compartment. The use of light in Nephrops trawls has, therefore, not been 
shown to improve vertical separation of fish from Nephrops, however, the observed behavioral 
responses may be of relevance in future experiments that aim to facilitate selection with the help of 
lights (Karlsen et al., 2021; Melli et al., 2018). 
 
4 Aims 
With grids and SELTRA panel as only options, the gear choices available to the Danish Nephrops fishery 
in Kattegat and Skagerrak offer two distinct profiles of species composition among the catch. The 
fishers, however, require a range of options to better match their catch compositions to the desired 
catch in dependence on quota availability, seasons, market prices and other factors (Feekings et al., 
2019). The present study aims to develop a BRD that facilitates a high escape of cod with concomitant 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
 
27 
high retentions of the other commercial bycatch species, particularly roundfish that often escape 
along with cod in the available gears.

as methods to test them and to determine their selectivity. Here, considerations and choices regarding

5 Gear development and determination of selectivity 
5.1 Gear development process 
5.1.1 Conceptualization 
Often, concepts that have found use in one fishery can be adapted to-, trialed in- and implemented in 
another. For example, the uptake of twin-rig trawl systems in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat 
and Skagerrak in the 1980s relied on a method that had been used by shrimp fishers in the Gulf of 
Mexico before (Ungfors et al., 2013). Likewise, the Swedish grid, which only started trial in the Swedish 
Nephrops fishery in 2002, relies on the principles of the Nordmøre grid, which was originally used to 
reduce catches of jellyfish in a Norwegian shrimp fishery (Graham, 2003). In some cases, however, 
solutions that work in one fishery are unlikely to be applicable in another. For example, Krag et al. 
(2010) developed a trawl design that can successfully separate cod from haddock based on the low 
position in the water column that cod occupy ahead of the trawl mouth. While such a design can be a 
successful method to reduce cod catches in a whitefish fishery, it is unlikely to find application in a 
Nephrops fishery because the raised fishing line used in the experiment would pass a large fraction of 
the encountered Nephrops and flatfish together with cod (Catchpole & Revill, 2008; Krag et al., 2010). 
The ideas for new BRD concepts are often the result of an improved understanding of fish behavior 
and the resulting ability to more specifically address it to facilitate selection. Knowledge about fish 
behavior in trawl can be derived from try-and-error type of experiments that seek to facilitate the 
capture or release of different species in different parts of the trawl, but behavioral analysis of fish in 
trawls is increasingly supported by the use of cameras (Pol & Eayrs, 2021). Further, many of the 
suggestions for gear modifications come from the industry and are often based on fishers’ experience 
and observations (Feekings et al., 2019). 
In the present study (manuscript), the tested BRD concept was based on a design described by Fraser 
& Angus (2019) (Figure 7). Fraser & Angus (2019) were able to reduce catches of cod in a Shetland 
mixed demersal whitefish fishery without losses of other commercial species of both flat- and 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
 
 
28 
roundfish. The design relies on two key behavioral traits of cod: their closer association with the 
bottom of a trawl extension compared to many other roundfish species, and the fact that they are 
strong swimmers. In the design described by Fraser & Angus (2019), the catch was guided over an 
inclined- and horizontal mesh panel towards the codend. Cod could then swim forward from the catch 
accumulation and would enter the compartment below the horizontal panel where they could escape 
through a bottom escape panel made of 300 mm square mesh. A similar concept could, in principle, 
also work in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak, as it catches many of the same 
species and because Nephrops are unlikely to swim forward and escape. 
 
 
Figure 7: BRD design used by Fraser & Angus (2019) 
 
5.1.2 Gear development  
Gear adaptations taken from other fisheries and new ideas alike often require multiple changes and 
versions until a final design for both testing, and later possibly the implementation to the fishery, is 
generated. To keep this process as short and economic as possible, it is important to consider any 
complications that could arise from the use in advance and to address them accordingly. Further, it 
can be advantageous to test a design before a gear trial to confirm that the geometry is as desired, 
allowing to make the necessary changes before the data are collected.  
The adaptation of the concept described by Fraser & Angus (2019) to the Danish Nephrops fishery 
Kattegat and Skagerrak required to account for a number of considerations. Among them was the 
smaller mesh size of the inclined panel that would be required to pass all Nephrops catch above the 
bottom compartment, which could be prone to clogging and mask the escape window below it. The 
stability of the geometry of the gear was an issue that needed to be addressed, particularly with 
respect to the inclusion of an inclined mesh panel in the codend extension. Further, small cod are 
weaker swimmers than large cod and could, therefore, potentially be less favored in their escape by 
the design. This was important because the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak mainly 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
 
 
29 
catches small cod as a result of the small mesh size (90 mm) and the general size structure in the 
population.  
During the drafting process, the consultation of stakeholders played an important role. A series of 
video calls with one of the authors of the original concept allowed insights into ideas and pitfalls that 
were part of the development and trial of the initial- and further, adapted designs. For example, the 
original authors had initially observed some indications of unstable gear geometry, an issue that was 
addressed by the use of floats and leadline in our trial (see manuscript for details on construction). 
Further, the consulted author suggested the use of a V-shaped incision at the trailing edge of the 
horizontal panel that would reduce slack netting and allow even tension across the panel. The initial 
idea for the design tested in the present study was the implementation of the BRD in a four-panel 
codend extension, similar to that used with SELTRA-panels. However, the potential hydrodynamic 
effects of the BRD were discussed in two meetings with a net maker. The net maker suspected that a 
strong force pushing down on the inclined panel during towing could pull the sides of the codend in, 
potentially resulting in an unstable “figure 8” cross section. He, therefore, suggested a 6-panel design 
that he expected to maintain a more reliable cross section. Further, the selvedges on the sides could 
be used for the attachment of the horizontal panel, and the false selvedges on the top and bottom 
sheets could be used for the attachment of floats and leadline. In anticipation of hydrodynamic forces 
pushing on the inclined panel, potentially masking the escape window and leading to sagging or 
debris-accumulation, the net maker ordered the construction of two pairs of kites that could be used 
to add additional outwards pulling forces if the desired hexagonal cross section could not be achieved 
without. The net maker further suggested the addition of floats to the side selvedges that could help 
to maintain horizontal spread of the codend.  
Experiences from past trials in the fishery indicated that the use of a large diamond-shaped opening 
has a great potential to facilitate better escape of roundfish than large mesh panels (Feekings et al., 
2020). Therefore, such a design was chosen for the escape window. The use of an upwards directed 
grid instead of the inclined panel was considered, however, there was concern about potential 
complications with the flow in the codend and additional difficulties with handling the grid onboard 
the vessel, and the use of an inclined mesh panel was, therefore, preferred. The location of the escape 
window was closer to the codline compared to Fraser & Angus (2019), as this was hoped to better 
facilitate the escape of smaller cod that are less powerful swimmers (He, 1993). The test codend was 
constructed according to the specifications agreed on with the net maker. It was then tested in the 
Hirtshals flume tank, were its gear geometry under towing could be estimated. The availability of such 
an option to display an experimental fishing gear in the water is a valuable asset, particularly when 
testing new design parameters for which the performance is still uncertain. While the flume tank was 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
 
30 
sufficiently large to test the full scale codend developed for the present study, designs for entire trawls 
are often tested as downscaled models to be able to fit into a flume tank (Fiorentini et al., 2004; Krag 
et al., 2010). 
 
 
Figure 8: Flume tank testing of an initial design of the BRD. Kites were needed to achieve a hexagonal cross section and to

During an initial test in the flume tank, the codend did not take on the desired hexagonal cross section 
that would prevent sagging in the inclined panel, and the kites were, therefore, attached to the side 
selvedges. Further, buoys on the side selvedges and heavy leadline (1 kg/m) on the bottom selvedges 
vertically stretched the bottom side panels of the codend, resulting in an uneven height distribution 
between the compartments above and below the horizontal panel. After the flume tank test, the 
buoys on the side selvedges were, therefore, removed and the leadline on the bottom selvedges was 
downscaled accordingly to reflect the altered buoyancy. Further, the kites were fixed to positions just 
ahead and just behind the horizontal panel. A positioning further forward was deemed unnecessary 
as the flow coming from the tapered section of the trawl would likely exert spreading forces, there.  
The first four hauls of the sea trial one June 16th, 2022 were used to monitor the gear geometry of the 
codend during towing using cameras (manuscript). The use of cameras to observe and adjust gear 
geometry before collecting data is common in the development of fishing gears. Given the towing 
speeds that were higher than those that could be simulated in the flume tank, the inclined panel was 
observed to be pressed down in its middle. Therefore, the inclined panel was tapered toward the 
bottom, resulting in a tighter fit and diminished caving to the force pressing down on it, however, the 
inclined and horizontal panels were still marked by a concave shape and an incline throughout. The 
codend was confirmed to take on a hexagonal shape during towing and the two compartments had

the present study was concluded (manuscript).  
 
5.2 Data collection 
Once a fishing gear has been developed, its performance must be studied. Therefore, data must be 
collected in the field which can be used to compare the retention of individuals of different species 
and sizes in the gear to either the encountered population or to another type of gear. Selectivity 
experiments can be conducted with multiple methods. Because each method collects data to address 
specific questions about selectivity, they require different experimental setups. It is important to keep 
in mind the limitations and possibilities of the respective methods to collect appropriate data to 
address the research question. 
 
5.2.1 Cover experiments 
In cover experiments, the area of the trawl where most of the selection happens (i.e., the codend and 
location of a BRD) is covered with a small mesh outside bag (cover) (Wileman et al., 1996). This small 
mesh cover retains those individuals that escape from the covered part of the trawl. Therefore, the 
analysis is based on the assumption that each individual is retained either in the codend mesh or in 
the cover. The length dependent retention in the trawl as a fraction of the total encountered 
population can then be calculated as 
𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑛𝑑,𝑙
𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑛𝑑,𝑙 + 𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑣𝑒𝑟,𝑙 with 𝑛 being the number of individuals retained 
in the respective compartment for a length class 𝑙 (Brooks et al., 2022). The fraction of individuals 
retained in the codend can assume values between 0 and 1, and the covered codend method 
therefore, determines the absolute selectivity of a gear. The method can be further refined to 
determine the selectivity for individual components of a trawl, for example by covering an escape 
opening and the codend with separate covers (Sistiaga et al., 2010) or by using separate covers for 
split codends with different mesh sizes (Santos et al., 2018).  
While the use of covers is a very straight-forward method, the measured selectivity is often influenced 
by the cover itself and does not fully reflect the actual selectivity of the same gear, as it would perform 
if it were fished without the cover (Madsen & Holst, 2002). The altered selectivity can be the result of 
masking, where the cover lays directly on the codend mesh or an escape opening, physically hindering 
the escape (Beverton, 1963). To avoid masking effects, covers are often rigged with kites, weights or 
hoops that aim to hold the cover clear of the codend (Madsen et al., 2001; O’Neill & Kynoch, 1996). 
However, there can still be further effects on selectivity by the cover. For example, the twine color 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
 
 
32 
can influence the visual contrast of the cover and fish might not see a clear path out of the trawl, 
making them less likely to escape (Frandsen, 2011). Because the accumulating catch in the cover can 
bear the risk of tearing it, hauls must be kept shorter than under commercial conditions, a factor 
known to influence selectivity (O’Neill & Kynoch, 1996).  
 
5.2.2 Paired gears experiments 
Paired gears experiments rely on the principle of two gears encountering similar populations of fish. 
This can be achieved using the alternate haul-, parallel haul-, twin trawl- and trouser trawl methods 
(Wileman et al., 1996). In paired gears experiments, the experimental gear is paired with a control 
gear that is fished with a small mesh codend to collect a catch that represents the encountered 
population (Brooks et al., 2022). While avoiding the bias that is introduced through the use of a cover, 
paired gears experiments usually require more data and can thus be more costly (Wileman et al., 
1996).  
In the alternate haul method, the two gears are fished successively and switched between hauls. The 
hauls are then matched into pairs based on similarities of encountered conditions, for example in 
terms of trawl track, towing duration and speed, time of day etc. (Browne et al., 2021). This allows the 
generation of a set of haul pairs that are combined into a dataset that can be used to determine the 
selectivity of the test gear. Similar to the alternate haul method, the parallel haul method uses two 
gears. However, these are towed in parallel by two vessels. While this method allows more accurate 
haul-pair matching of time and area and thus in terms of the encountered fish population, there is 
space for bias introduced by different vessel configurations and fishing practices by different skippers 
(Wileman et al., 1996).  
This variation can be reduced by using the trouser trawl and twin trawl methods. The trouser trawl 
method makes use of a single, vertically split trawl where each of the sides tapers down into an 
individual codend (Wileman et al., 1996). Near similar fractions of the encountered population are 
expected to enter each side and the selectivity, for example of a codend or BRD, can be determined. 
In the twin trawl method, two identical trawl bodies are towed by the same vessel simultaneously 
using a central clump to supplement the trawl doors as an attachment point. One of these trawls has 
a non-selective codend and acts as the control, whereas the other trawl is the test trawl that includes 
the codend mesh or BRD to be tested. The analysis follows the assumption that similar parts of the 
encountered fish population enter each trawl and reduced catches in the test codend can be 
attributed to its selectivity. The experimental setup of the twin trawl method closely resembles the 
twin-rig trawl systems commonly used by the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak. 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
 
 
33 
The number of required hauls in paired gears experiments is greater than that in cover experiments 
because in reality, a school of fish will rarely enter two trawls or the two sides of a trouser trawl in 
equal fractions (Millar, 2010). To detect and account for this effect along with potential differences in 
fishing power between trawls or compartments, modern statistical methods for paired gears 
experiments estimate the split parameter that describes the distribution of the population between 
units (Brooks et al., 2022; Sistiaga et al., 2009). 
When determining selectivity through paired gears experiments, catches in each codend are counted. 
The fraction of the total catch caught in the test codend can be calculated as  
𝑛𝑡𝑒𝑠𝑡,𝑙
𝑛𝑡𝑒𝑠𝑡,𝑙 + 𝑛𝑐𝑜𝑛𝑡𝑟𝑜𝑙,𝑙  (Brooks 
et al., 2022). This is referred to as the catch sharing rate. At values < 0.5, the test gear retained fewer 
individuals than the control as a result of its selectivity. The catch sharing rate is not expected to take 
on values >> 0.5. To determine the absolute selectivity, the retention is calculated from the catch 
sharing rate and can range from 0 to 1.  
The data collection for the present study was conducted with the twin trawl method (manuscript). 
Because the method determines absolute selectivity, the selectivity determined in the present study 
can be compared to other gear options that are used in the fishery and to the performance of future 
versions of the developed BRD. Among the methods to determine absolute selectivity, the twin trawl 
method was chosen because it avoids the bias introduced by the use of a cover and because it can be 
conducted with a single vessel fishing both gears simultaneously, most efficiently using the available 
resources.  
 
 
5.2.3 Catch comparison experiments and relative selectivity 
Catch comparison experiments use the same setups as paired gears experiments. However, they use 
two selective gears to compare the catches between them. For example, they can be used to compare 
the performance of different codend mesh types or different versions of a BRD against each other. 
Similar to paired gears experiments, the catch comparison rate is calculated as 
𝑛𝑔𝑒𝑎𝑟1,𝑙
𝑛𝑔𝑒𝑎𝑟1,𝑙 + 𝑛𝑔𝑒𝑎𝑟2,𝑙. It can 
take on values between 0 and 1. Catch comparison curves are usually attained using flexible curve 
fitting models such as polynomials or splines (Brooks et al., 2022). Catch comparison experiments can 
only be used to compare the relative selectivity of two gears against each other, but cannot determine 
the absolute selectivity of these gears, as there is no indication of the encountered population.

5.3 Describing selectivity 
5.3.1 Definition and codend selectivity 
The absolute selectivity for a given species in fishing gears is described as the retention probability of 
an individual in dependence on length 𝑟(𝑙) (Melli et al., 2020). For all individuals that enter a standard 
trawl and do not swim out of the trawl mouth again, the selection is mainly facilitated through the 
mesh of the codend (Herrmann et al., 2009).  Here, small individuals are expected to pass through the 
mesh and escape, whereas large individuals are retained. At intermediate sizes, some individuals may 
escape while others are retained, and the retention probability increases with size. The selectivity of 
a trawl for a given species can be visualized by graphing 𝑟(𝑙) in a selection curve (Figure 9).    
 
 
Figure 9: Example of codend selection curves. Both curves have the same 𝐿50, however the dashed curve has a much smaller 
selection range. (Graham, 2010)  
The absolute selectivity of a gear is often reported through 𝐿50 and 𝑆𝑅 values. 𝐿50 is the length at 
which an individual has a 50 % probability to be retained in the net (MacLennan, 1992). It is used to 
report the size of fish at which the gear is effective. The 𝑆𝑅 is calculated as the difference between 
the lengths of 25 and 75 % retention probability (𝑆𝑅= 𝐿75 −𝐿25) (MacLennan, 1992). It is used as 
an indication of the slope of the selection curve. A narrow 𝑆𝑅, therefore, describes a steep selection 
curve. In an applied setting, a narrow 𝑆𝑅 means that catches of undersized individuals and losses of 
commercially sized individuals can be avoided more effectively, assuming that the minimum size is 
located within the steep section of the selection curve. The sigmoid curves of selection processes 
facilitated by netting are often described by a simple logistic function (Herrmann et al., 2012; Millar 
et al., 2004). The selectivity for different species in the same netting can vary greatly (Frandsen et al., 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
 
 
35 
2010b). This is mainly the result of species-specific morphologies, as it is body cross section, rather 
than length, that ultimately determines whether an individual can fit through the meshes (Herrmann 
et al., 2009). Further, species behavior plays a large role, as active contact with an open mesh, i.e., 
swimming into the opening head first, lines up the smallest cross section with the opening most 
efficiently for most species. 
 
5.3.2 Combined selection processes 
Despite most of the selection in a standard trawl taking place in the codend, the retention probability 
for the whole gear 𝑟(𝑙) is the product of several selection processes that happen in different areas of 
the gear (Briggs, 1986; Krag et al., 2014; Melli et al., 2020). To be caught, an individual must be retained 
during the herding process, in the body of the trawl, in the trawl extension, and in the codend (Melli 
et al., 2020). This is particularly important for fisheries management when aiming at the reduction of 
catches of certain species or sizes. Often, the selectivity of a trawl gear can be influenced by including 
bycatch reduction devices (BRDs) in one or more sections of the gear (Catchpole & Revill, 2008). BRDs 
use the differences in morphology or behavior among target- and non-target individuals and -species 
to facilitate a better selectivity (Kennelly & Broadhurst, 2021).  
While codend retention increases with length, BRDs can often select against larger individuals or 
achieve escape at intermediate size ranges (Lövgren et al., 2016; Madsen et al., 2002; Santos et al., 
2016). Therefore, modelling combined selection processes, for example of a BRD and a codend, often 
requires more flexible options than the S-shaped curves used to model codend selectivity. In 
combined selection processes, selection curves may take on bell-shaped or more complex forms 
(Lövgren et al., 2016). To model such processes, there are two main available options: polynomials 
and structural models. Polynomials are highly flexible in describing the data, therefore, requiring no 
assumptions on what the curve should look like. However, polynomials use a purely statistical curve 
fitting procedure and cannot quantify the selective effects of the individual selective processes in a 
gear (Lövgren et al., 2016). Further, their predictive power outside the length range, for which data is 
available, can be low (Fryer & Shepherd, 1996). When using a structural approach, multiple models 
are selected that make assumptions about the underlying physical, biological, and behavioral 
mechanisms driving the individual selection processes in a gear, e.g. those happening in the codend 
and BRD, respectively (Fryer & Shepherd, 1996; O’Neill & Herrmann, 2007). The models are then 
compared with each other based on their fit, identifying the best model. The assumptions associated 
with the best fitting model can offer insight into the drivers of the individual selection processes. 
Structural models can in many cases also define the individual selection processes’ contribution to the 
overall selectivity by defining, for example, an 𝐿50 and 𝑆𝑅 for each, a BRD and the codend (O’Neill & 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
 
 
36 
Herrmann, 2007). The selection facilitated by a BRD only applies to those individuals that make contact 
with it (Brčić et al., 2015; Herrmann et al., 2015). The probability of an individual making contact with 
a selective device can be length dependent and can be accounted for in structural models (Melli et al., 
under review; Krag et al., 2014). Because trawl selectivity varies between hauls, it is determined in a 
haul-by-haul approach and then combined through maximum likelihood estimation (Brooks et al., 
2022; Fryer, 1991; Fryer & Shepherd, 1996; Millar et al., 2004). In the present study, a structural 
approach was chosen because the drivers of escape likelihood for the different species were of 
interest (manuscript). 
 
5.3.3 Performance indicators 
The retention probability for a given species in a gear is typically described as a function of length, 
however, catches, quotas, and sales are often measured in weight. Therefore, stakeholders are often 
interested in quantifications of gear performance in weight, e.g. to estimate the retained fraction of 
commercially sized- and undersized individuals in a gear. To be able to quantify gear performance in 
terms of retained catch weights, lengths must be associated with weights and this can be done using 
the length-weight relationship formula  𝑤(𝑙) = 𝑎× 𝑙𝑏, where 𝑤 is the weight in grams, 𝑙 is the length 
in cm and 𝑎 and 𝑏 are coefficients that are specific to a species, but can vary with different factors 
such as seasons, regions and sex (le Cren, 1951). By multiplying 𝑤(𝑙) with a population 𝑛𝑃𝑜𝑝𝑙 and the 
length dependent retention 𝑟(𝑙), the retained catch weight for the length 𝑙 can be calculated. Dividing 
this number by the product of 𝑛𝑃𝑜𝑝𝑙 and 𝑤(𝑙) results in the fraction of retained weight 𝑤𝐹𝑙 for the 
length class. To estimate the fraction of retained weight for a length range of interest, e.g. individuals 
below or above minimum size, these steps can be done in summation over the respective length 
ranges (Melli et al., 2020). If the selectivity of a gear 𝑟(𝑙) is known, it can be applied to any population 
𝑛𝑃𝑜𝑝𝑙 without the need for additional trials. When presenting performance indicators summed over 
length ranges, it is important to stress that they are specific to the population used in the calculation, 
as diverting size distributions may result in different retained fractions.  
 
6 Results and conclusions 
The BRD developed and tested in this study resulted in a low retention of cod with simultaneously 
high retentions of many other species such as Nephrops, haddock, whiting, plaice, lemon sole and 
witch flounder. Such species selectivity has never before been achieved in a Nephrops fishery. The in-
==PAGE:38==
 
 
 
 
37 
trawl separation of cod from other species, particularly other roundfish, offers a new selective tool 
that has great potential for application in many other trawl fisheries that are limited by cod quotas. 
The BRD makes use of the species specific behavior of cod that allows them to access an escape 
window. The location of the escape window under an inclined panel and the necessity for fish to swim 
forward at the bottom of the codend extension makes it less likely to be contacted by other roundfish, 
that are more closely associated with the top of the trawl than cod, and by species with lower 
swimming capacities such as Nephrops and flatfish.  
With quota availability for different species varying between vessels, fishers require a range of options 
to achieve the desired catch compositions (Feekings et al., 2019). The existing gear options in the 
Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak offer the choice between a single-species Nephrops 
fishery using the Swedish grid and the reduction of catches of many other roundfish species along with 
cod using SELTRA panels. Therefore, the escape window under an inclined panel could be a valuable 
addition to existing gear options for Nephrops fishers who wish to fish for the other commercial 
bycatch species while retaining only a small part of the cod population. The present study proves that 
the developed gear concept can achieve its goal in the Danish Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and 
Skagerrak. The implementation of a gear design based on the developed BRD into the fishery, 
however, should be preceded by additional research and potentially by adaptations of the BRD 
geometry.  
In the case study, kites and a hexagonal six-panel codend configuration were used to maintain a stable 
gear geometry. A design achieving similar or better results with a rectangular cross section, a 100 
mesh circumference similar to the codends currently used by the fishers, and without the use of kites 
could simplify the gear significantly and allow fishers to employ it more flexibly. Future development 
should also aim to further exploit cod-specific behavior to improve selectivity (Pol & Eayrs, 2021). 
Fraser & Angus (2019) assume that their design was successful because of the strong swimming ability 
of cod and their association with the bottom of the trawl extension. The present study found strong 
evidence in support of these assumptions (manuscript). Cod escape likelihood increased with size, 
indicating a link between the ability to swim forward to access the escape window and stronger, more 
enduring swimming capacities. Both, the low retention of cod and the camera observations, confirmed 
that cod occupied positions in the lower half of the codend extension, from where they were able to 
access the compartment under the horizontal panel. Adding to the assumptions made by Fraser & 
Angus (2019), it is possible that cod presence in the bottom half of the codend extension was partially 
the result of sheltering behavior in an area with reduced flow. Cod are able to exploit turbulent flow 
caused by substrate ripples to hold position in currents (Gerstner, 1998), and what could be similar 
sheltering behavior has been observed near the sheets of trawls before (Fraser and Melli, personal 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
 
 
38 
observations). While no measurements of the flow were taken, the small-mesh inclined panel likely 
blocked or diverted some of the flow away from the lower half of the codend, allowing cod to occupy 
this area while conserving energy. The tendency of finfish to seek out low-flow areas in trawls has 
been used to guide them towards escape panels in crustacean trawls before (Courtney et al., 2006; 
Engaas et al., 1999; Watson et al., 1986). Because the high escape of cod was likely the result of a 
combination of multiple behavioral factors, a better understanding of these could be used to identify 
and adjust the decisive parameters in the design, for example, the window’s distance from the codline, 
compartment heights, flow speeds, and codend circumference. Further, the role of escape during 
towing and haul-back on total escape should be investigated. A large fraction of escape during haul-
back could have implications for BRD effectiveness during the long hauls used under commercial 
conditions and may reduce escapee survival.  
Further development of the BRD trialed in this study will help to achieve a simplified, more flexibly 
employable design that allows to approach the species selective exclusion of cod from the catches of 
mixed demersal trawl fisheries. An implementation of such a gear into the Danish Nephrops fishery in 
Kattegat and Skagerrak would add a valuable option to the fishers’ toolbox, allowing them to both 
maintain the efficiency of the mixed-species fishery and to conserve the choke species, cod.

Approaching single-species exclusion in 
mixed demersal trawl fisheries 
 
 
O. Jonas Palder1, Jordan P. Feekings1, Shaun Fraser2, Valentina Melli1* 
 
 
1Technical University of Denmark, National Institute of Aquatic Resources (DTU Aqua),  
1DK-9850 Hirtshals, Denmark. 
2NAFC Marine Centre, University of the Highlands and Islands, Port Arthur, Scalloway, Shetland, 
2ZE1 0UN, UK 
 
*Corresponding author: Valentina Melli, Technical University of Denmark, National Institute of 
Aquatic Resources (DTU Aqua), DK-9850 Hirtshals, Denmark. Email: vmel@aqua.dtu.dk  
 
Abstract 
Under a discard ban, mixed fisheries must often reduce catches of low-quota species to allow the 
continuation of fishing activities. This has led to the development of a range of bycatch reduction 
devices (BRDs) that aim to exploit morphological and behavioral differences among species to 
facilitate escape of unwanted catch from the fishing gear. However, the exclusion of unwanted low-
quota species from the catch is often only possible with concomitant losses of other species’ 
commercial catches. The advancement of species selectivity in fishing gears, therefore, has great 
potential to improve the efficiency of many mixed fisheries. In this study, we developed and tested a 
BRD design aimed at exclusively reducing cod (Gadus morhua) catches in a mixed species trawl fishery 
targeting crustaceans. The design exploits behavioral traits that set cod apart from other species of 
crustaceans, roundfish, and flatfish to allow them to swim forward from the catch accumulation zone 
and access an escape window in the bottom sheet of a trawl codend. The low retention of cod (33 %) 
in combination with high retentions of commercial catches of Nephrops (Nephrops norvegicus) (89 %), 
haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus) (76 %) and plaice (Pleuronectes platessa) (100 %) demonstrates 
a selectivity never before achieved in mixed species fisheries, that may find application in a number 
of cod quota-limited fisheries.  
 
Keywords:  
bottom escape panel; escape window; inclined panel; bycatch reduction; Nephrops fishery

Introduction 
Mixed fisheries often have high levels of bycatch that is caught along with the main target species. A 
fraction of the bycatch is typically considered wanted bycatch, which can be marketed and contributes 
to the generated revenue. The unwanted part of the bycatch includes individuals that are undersized, 
caught in excess of the available quota, or belong to non-commercial species. This fraction is often 
discarded, a custom that is a waste of both ecological and economic resources (Bellido et al., 2011). 
To prevent this wasteful practice, fisheries’ management increasingly relies on discard bans, 
challenging mixed fisheries to avoid undersized fish and low-quota species (Borges et al., 2016; Condie 
et al., 2014; European Union, 2013). Under a discard ban, all catches of regulated species must be 
landed; thus, quota exhaustion for a single species may result in choking a mixed fishery if additional 
catches of this species cannot be avoided during further fishing activities (Hatcher, 2014; Mortensen 
et al., 2018). Therefore, research often focuses on the exclusion of choke species from the fishing gear 
to allow a fishery to exhaust remaining harvest potential for other species and remain profitable 
(Kennelly & Broadhurst, 2021; Krag et al., 2010). 
Selectively excluding individual species from the catch is challenging when target- and non-target 
species are similar in morphology (Beutel et al., 2008), and behavioral differences can often facilitate 
only partial species separation (Krag et al., 2009; Thomsen, 1993; Winger et al., 2010). The successful 
exclusion of a single unwanted bycatch species is, therefore, often linked to partial losses of other 
species that are part of the desired catch in mixed fisheries (Catchpole & Revill, 2008; Graham, 2010). 
Sacrificing parts of the commercial catch to achieve the exclusion of only a few unwanted species 
reduces a fishery’s efficiency and results in lost revenue (Graham, 2010; Krag et al., 2010). 
Furthermore, the acceptance of gear adaptations can be low when fishers fear losing commercial 
catch (Eayrs & Pol, 2019; Fonseca et al., 2005; Suuronen, 2022). Therefore, the development of 
selective gears should aim to facilitate the exclusion of choke species as specifically as possible, 
allowing to maintain high retentions of other commercial species.  
Across the North Atlantic Ocean, low stocks of cod (Gadus morhua) often make it a choke species and 
various trawl fisheries aim to reduce its catch through gear modifications (Pol & Eayrs, 2021). Among 
these are crustacean-directed mixed trawl fisheries that often have high rates of bycatch of both non-
commercial and commercial fish, the latter making up a significant part of the generated revenue 
(Catchpole et al., 2006; Krag et al., 2008). Mixed crustacean fisheries often lose revenue when aiming 
to exclude cod from their catches, as they commonly rely on either a grid that eliminates all 
commercial bycatch of flatfish and roundfish (Catchpole et al., 2006; Hornborg et al., 2017; 
Valentinsson & Ulmestrand, 2008), or a top escape panel, which loses large amounts of other 
==PAGE:61==
 
 
 
 
60 
commercial roundfish such as haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus) and whiting (Merlangius 
merlangus) along with cod (Catchpole & Revill, 2008; Krag 2016). To date, no gear option for mixed 
crustacean fisheries allows to specifically exclude cod without losses of commercial bycatch, 
particularly other roundfish species. 
The reduction of cod catches without large losses of other roundfish species has, however, been 
achieved with two different gear modifications in mixed whitefish fisheries in the past: First, Krag et 
al. (2010) developed a trawl with a raised fishing line that passes low-swimming cod under the trawl 
mouth while catching higher swimming species such as haddock, whiting and saithe (Pollachius 
virens); and second, Fraser & Angus (2019) used a bottom escape panel located under an inclined net 
panel in the trawl extension, which exploits two characteristics of cod behavior that distinguish them 
from many other fish species: their strong swimming capacity (Beamish, 1966) and their closer 
association with the bottom of the trawl compared to other roundfish species (Krag et al., 2009). While 
both designs can facilitate the selective reduction of cod catches in whitefish-directed trawl fisheries, 
a raised fishing line as described by Krag et al. (2010) is not applicable for mixed crustacean-directed 
fisheries, where both the target species and commercial flatfish species are strongly bottom-
associated and would be passed below the trawl along with cod (Catchpole & Revill, 2008; Ryer, 2008). 
The design described by Fraser & Angus (2019), however, requires cod to swim forward against the 
flow at the bottom of the trawl to access the escape panel. Given the limited swimming capacities of 
many crustacean- and flatfish species, a concept similar to that described in Fraser & Angus (2019) 
could, in principle, improve species selectivity in mixed crustacean trawl fisheries that are limited by 
cod quota availability.  
A design similar to that used by Fraser & Angus (2019) has a unique potential to separate cod from 
many other species of roundfish, flatfish and crustaceans, however, an adaptation of such a concept 
to a crustacean-directed fishery would entail significant engineering challenges. First, the small mesh 
sizes required to retain crustaceans may lead to debris accumulation and masking of the escape area, 
resulting in diminished escape window accessibility. Second, cod escape behavior may be inhibited by 
factors such as sediment clouds in the lower part of the trawl and by the close proximity of the escape 
window to the seabed (Main & Sangster, 1981). And third, cod caught in crustacean fisheries are often 
of smaller size than those encountered in whitefish fisheries (e.g. 30-40 cm as opposed to 60-90 cm), 
which could have implications on escape behavior and swimming capacity. This study aimed to 
investigate the applicability of a cod-specific bottom escape window located under an inclined panel 
that can be accessed by fish swimming forward from the catch accumulation zone to a Nephrops 
(Nephrops norvegicus)-directed mixed demersal trawl fishery.

Materials and Methods 
Gear design 
The codend tested in this study was made of two sheets of standard 90 mm (nominal) diamond mesh 
(DM) polyethylene (PE) 4 mm double twine (D4) netting. Both, the top- and bottom sheets, were 
subdivided into 3 evenly sized, 20 mesh-wide panels by tying two false selvedges of 2 meshes each 
along the length of the sheet, resulting in a 6-panel configuration with a circumference of 120 open 
meshes (Figure 1). This configuration does not correspond to the codends used in the fishery, but was 
chosen for the experiment as it was expected to best maintain a stable gear geometry. To determine 
the actual mesh size, two rows of 10 consecutive meshes in longitudinal direction were measured for 
each of the six panels on the wet codend after the last haul of the trial using an OMEGA gauge 
(Fonteyne et al., 2007). 
An inclined net panel and a horizontal panel were used to separate the codend into two compartments 
(Figure 1). Both panels were constructed from 40 mm (nominal) DM PE netting to ensure that no 
Nephrops could pass through to the bottom compartment and that all individuals would first be 
directed towards the end of the codend. The leading edge of the 40 mm sheet was fixed to the bottom 
panel and its sides were attached to the bottom side panels of the codend, following the twine of the 
90 mm netting up to the side selvedges. The inclined section measured 1.9 m and the inclined panel 
was tapered from 49 meshes at the bottom to 57 meshes at the top. Once reaching the side selvedges, 
the 40 mm panel was continued horizontally for another 1.9 m. The trailing edge of the horizontal 
panel was V-shaped with the incision 0.9 m deep towards the front of the codend. The V-shape was 
chosen to avoid slack netting in this area, which may have blocked access to the bottom compartment. 
The back end of the V was 0.7 m ahead of the lifting strap and 2.5 m up from the codline. Under the 
inclined panel, a diamond-shaped opening was cut into the bottom panel over a length of 1.9 m (20 
meshes long and 20 meshes wide). The back edge of the escape window was located 4.4 m ahead of 
the codline, 2.6 m ahead of the lifting strap, and 1 m ahead of the tip of the V (Figure 1). This 
configuration was expected to prevent losses of Nephrops and many other commercial catch species 
due to flow dynamics in the codend, while facilitating escape for those fish that swim forward into the 
lower compartment from the codend. 
To improve gear stability, the two false tied-mesh selvedges of the bottom sheet were weighted with 
0.5 kg/m leadline, and 20 floats with a buoyancy corresponding to 130 g each were attached to each 
of the two false tied-mesh selvedges of the top sheet. The weight and floatation were evenly 
distributed over the distance from the beginning of the codend to the lifting strap (5.5 m). Two pairs 
of kites (5 pockets) were attached to the selvedges running along the lateral sides of the codend at 
==PAGE:63==
 
 
 
 
62 
the beginning and the end of the horizontal panel. The six panel design in combination with the kites 
was chosen to reduce slack in the inclined and horizontal panels by applying outward pulling forces.   
Before the sea trial, the codend was tested in a flume tank (SINTEF, Hirtshals 9850, DK). Observations 
during this test resulted in adjustments leading to the final design, including the distribution of floats 
and leadline as well as the tapering of the inclined panel.

Data Collection 
The gear trial was conducted on R/V Havfisken (17m, 373 kW) that was rigged for three-wire twin 
trawling using Type 2 Thyborøn doors (1.78 m2, 197 kg) and a 400 kg triangular central clump. The 
trawls were Combi-trawls that are designed to catch both fish and Nephrops and are commonly used 
in the fishery. The trawls used 40 m footropes and had a circumference of 420 meshes of 80 mm 
netting at the trawl mouth. The test codend and a control codend with 100 meshes circumference and 
a 40 mm (nominal) inliner were attached to the trawls and fished in parallel. This experimental setup 
allows to estimate the catch retention in the test codend compared to the encountered population, 
as retained in the non-selective control codend. To attach the test codend, a section of the respective 
trawl’s tapered section was cut off, both codends could therefore be joined to the trawls with the 
same joining ratio.   
==PAGE:64==
 
 
 
 
63 
Data were collected between June 16th and June 24th 2022 in Kattegat and Skagerrak (FAO Division 
IIIa) (Figure 2). Fishing was conducted to match commercial conditions in terms of towing speed and 
fishing grounds, however tow durations were shorter than commonly observed in the Nephrops 
fishery to prevent over-filling the small-mesh control codend. During towing, the geometry of the gear 
was tracked using acoustic double-spread and headline height sensors (SIMRAD PX, 
www.simrad.com). Headline heights and distances between the trawl doors and the clump were 
noted by the skipper at the beginning, middle and end of each haul. The values were then averaged 
for each trawl and haul. A total of 26 hauls were conducted. The first four of these were not included 
in the analysis because they were used to observe the gear geometry and therefore held very short 
and conducted in shallow water. Further, one haul with cameras and lights attached to different parts 
of the codend was conducted during the data collection phase to provide an indication of fish escape 
behavior. This haul was exempt from all analyses, as lights may influence fish behavior (Melli et al., 
2018). Therefore, data included in the selectivity analysis stems from 21 hauls. Codends were shifted 
between trawls after the first 8 valid hauls to account for potential systematic differences of capture 
efficiency between the trawls.

At the end of each tow, the catch in the test codend was lifted onboard and processed first to prevent 
it from washing out at the surface, the catch in the control codend followed second. For each codend, 
the weight of the total catch was taken. Carapace length was measured to the nearest mm below for

Nephrops using digital calipers, and total length was measured to the nearest cm below for cod, 
haddock, hake, lemon sole, Nephrops, plaice, saithe, whiting and witch flounder. Due to a particularly 
large catch, subsampling was required for lemon sole in the control codend for a single haul, where 
the weights of a length-measured sample and the total species catch were taken to calculate the 
sampling ratio. Length-based analyses were conducted for only those species where sufficient data 
were collected. Catch weight of skates was compared between the test and control codends using a 
Wilcoxon signed-rank test, as no influence of the different mesh sizes on the selection for these 
species was expected. 
 
Video observations 
To observe the gear geometry and potential points of slack, cameras (Paralenz DC+) were fixed to 
various positons in the test codend during the first four hauls. The hauls were kept short, conducted 
in shallow water (i.e. < 50 m) to allow sufficient lighting, and sandy bottom was chosen to minimize 
sediment clouds inside the trawl.  
In addition, haul 15 was dedicated to gaining insight into potential debris accumulations during actual 
fishing and to better understand the underlying behavioral mechanisms that lead to the escape and 
retention of fish in the test codend. Therefore, cameras were fixed to three positions of interest during 
haul 15 of the data collection phase: on the back of the escape window looking forward; on the back 
edge of the horizontal panel, in the middle of the V-shaped incision, looking backwards; and on the 
top sheet facing down and forwards, filming the back edge of the horizontal panel. Because of the 
depth (125 m), artificial light (Inon LF 3100-EW, white) was added and, therefore, catch data were 
excluded from analyses to avoid including potential effects of the lights on fish behavior (Melli et al., 
2018).

Among the models 1 to 7, the best model was identified based on the lowest AIC value (Akaike, 1998). 
Candidate models were identified as those that deviated less than 6 AIC points from the best model 
(Wagenmakers & Farrell 2004). The aptness of the model fit for the best model for each species was 
assessed by the p-value and by confirming that deviance and degrees of freedom were in the same 
order of magnitude (Wileman et al., 1996). A significant p-value would indicate that the variance in 
the residuals was unlikely to be a coincidence, suggesting potential issues in the model fit to the data. 
In case of a significant p-value (p < 0.05), the predicted curve was, therefore, plotted against the data 
and the residuals were examined for patterns. When no clear patterns could be identified, it was 
assumed that the poor fit statistics were a result of overdispersion in the data, rather than the model’s 
inability to describe the experimental data (Melli et al., under review; Wileman, 1996). Between- and 
within-haul variation was accounted for by using the double bootstrapping method with 1000 
iterations to generate 95 % Efron confidence intervals (Efron, 1982; Millar, 1993). In the 
bootstrapping, the single case of subsampling was accounted for by resampling before the data were 
raised by the subsampling fraction (Melli et al., under review).

Camera observations 
The camera footage collected during the first four hauls allowed to observe the gear geometry during 
both towing and haul-back. During towing, the test codend had a hexagonal cross section with the 
kites pulling outward. Given the water flow pressing on the inclined panel during towing, the inclined- 
and horizontal panels took on a concave shape and were marked by a gradual incline throughout the 
full length, rather than clear division between inclined and horizontal sections. The gear geometry was 
stable and no slack or fluttering was observed in the inclined and horizontal panels. No clogging of the 
inclined panel that resulted in masking of the escape window was observed. When haul-back was 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
 
 
70 
interrupted, the lack of outward pulling forces allowed the sides of the codend to move inwards, 
resulting in a more rectangular cross section and a greater compartment height both above and below 
the horizontal panel than during towing. 
In haul 15, all three cameras recorded during towing and haul-back. No observations could be made 
during towing, as suspended sediments blocked the view. Further, the camera surveying the escape 
window became clogged with organic matter. The footage generated by the remaining two cameras 
during haul-back allowed limited insight into the distribution and behavior of fish in the codend, 
insufficient to conduct quantified analysis on. The observations made can therefore only be 
interpreted as indications of the processes facilitating selection in the experimental codend. Cod and 
haddock were observed swimming ahead of the catch accumulation and behind the back edge of the 
horizontal panel during haul back. Here, cod often assumed positions near to the bottom sheet, 
whereas more haddock were observed in the top half of the codend (Figure 3). When the haul-back 
was interrupted, e.g. when the doors and clump were brought onboard, the reduction in trawl speed 
resulted in surges of fish moving forward in the codend extension. During these surges, fish located 
near the bottom were likely to move forward under the horizontal panel, approaching the location of 
the escape window.

Discussion 
In this study, we developed and tested a BRD that successfully reduced catches of cod in a Nephrops-
directed mixed demersal trawl fishery while retaining most of the wanted bycatch of other 
commercially important round- and flatfish. Such species-specific exclusion has never before been 
achieved in the mixed Nephrops-directed trawl fishery, one of the most studied fisheries in the world 
in terms of bycatch reduction and gear innovation. The BRD caused no issues during use and was not 
prone to clogging by debris or fish, one of the major concerns often tied to the use of guiding and 
sieve panels (Fraser & Angus, 2019; Larsen et al., 2018). 
In our trial we retained 33.05 % (25.96–41.47) of cod total catch weight. Cod retention was low across 
lengths and the data showed an increase of window contact with length. This supports the assumption 
that strong swimming fish are more likely to swim forward from the codend and access the BRD, as 
cod endurance and swimming speed have been described to increase with size (He, 1993; Main & 
Sangster, 1981). Bycatch reduction through designs allowing strong swimming fish to swim forward to 
==PAGE:78==
 
 
 
 
77 
escape have been successfully used in the past in both, fish- and crustacean fisheries (Lomeli & 
Wakefield, 2012; Watson et al., 1993). Our BRD design favors the escape of fish that stay low in the 
codend and facilitated a high catch reduction of cod, which are generally regarded to display some 
rising behavior (Eayrs et al., 2017; Krag et al., 2015), but to maintain a closer association with the 
bottom of a trawl than many other roundfish species (Ferro et al., 2007; Krag et al., 2009). The 
presence of cod in the lower half of the codend was further confirmed by the limited camera footage 
from the haul-back process, matching the observations made by Fraser & Angus (2019) who describe 
a consistent tendency of cod to occupy the lower half of a trawl extension. However, lights were used 
to generate the footage in both studies, and altered species distribution as a response to the lights 
cannot be ruled out (Melli et al., 2018). The low retention of undersized cod (38.95 % (29.27–51.26) 
in weight) in our experiment was facilitated by both the codend selection and escape through the 
window. Our codend 𝐿50 (21.59 cm (18.91–24.40)) is similar to that found by Krag et al. (2016) (21.43 
(7.85–40.00)) in a 95.1 mm D4 codend with 96 open meshes around the circumference. 
Nephrops selection was considered to be equally well described by six models that assumed widely 
different scenarios spanning increasing- and decreasing escape window contact likelihoods with 
length as well as no contact at all. This uncertainty is likely the result of very limited contact with the 
escape window that resulted in no significant difference between the window contact models. We 
can therefore conclude that the presence of the bottom escape window did not reduce the catch of 
Nephrops. A high retention of Nephrops is an important factor when developing a BRD for the fishery, 
as fishers are unlikely to accept large losses of the commercially most valuable part of the catch 
(Milliken 2004). The small observed losses of commercially sized Nephrops (89.31 % retention (77.20–
99.19)) were likely the result of codend selection, as the 𝐿50 (25.21 cm (-1.46–29.82)) and the 𝑆𝑅 
(22.22 cm (15.25–64.25)) of the 92.5 mm codend indicate that individuals above the 32 mm MCRS 
were released through the mesh. The 𝐿50 in our codend was similar to that found by Melli et al. (under 
review) for a 96.1 mm codend with 92 open meshes in the circumference (24.33 cm (21.68–30.05)). 
However, given the very low or absent window contact, the two selection processes identified by the 
model may have been consequentially facilitated by the codend, e.g. during towing and haul-back. 
The indicated codend selectivity parameters may, therefore, only describe one of these processes with 
the selectivity parameters that are listed for the escape window describing the other. 
All three flatfish species had very high retention of commercially sized individuals and the models 
predicted decreasing window contact at length with the assumption that fractions 1 – 𝐴𝑤𝑖𝑛𝑑𝑜𝑤 of the 
respective populations, independent of length, would not contact the window. Further, there were 
no other candidate models indicating increased escape at length for plaice and lemon sole. For witch 
flounder, however, 4 different models of contact likelihood were within 6 AIC points, including one 
==PAGE:79==
 
 
 
 
78 
assuming no contact with the escape window. It is likely that the two selection processes, which were 
identified by the models for each the three flatfish species, both occurred in the codend, rather than 
in the codend and in the escape window. This is supported by our identified codend 𝐿50 for plaice 
(17.11 cm (14.32–20.38)), that was lower than plaice 𝐿50s reported from other 90 mm nominal mesh 
codends with 96 meshes in circumference (22.97 cm (0.10–24.43)) (Krag et al., 2016) and 92 meshes 
in circumference (21.41 cm (20.79–22.46)) (Melli et al., under review). The combined L50 of both 
selection processes for in our experiment was 20.61 cm (20.14–21.12) and the two selection processes 
underlying it could have been consequentially facilitated by the codend, e.g. during towing and during 
haul-back. The high retention of flatfish in our trial matches the results obtained by Fraser & Angus 
(2019), who found no indication of reduced plaice catches in their catch comparison analysis. Despite 
their strong association with the bottom of trawls, we did not find evidence that flatfish were able to 
swim forward and escape through the window. This is likely due to their low swimming endurance 
(Ryer, 2008). Catches of flatfish are an important contributor to the fishery’s revenue and their 
retention is, therefore, an important factor when assessing the efficiency of a BRD.  
The best model for haddock assumed an increasing window escape likelihood with size, similar to the 
assumptions made by the best model for cod escape, a length-independent contact scenario also 
fitted the data relatively well. The 𝐿50𝑤𝑖𝑛𝑑𝑜𝑤 for haddock was 65.41 cm (52.73–89.39), indicating a 
relatively high retention for large haddock despite the increased escape at length. While relying on 
the same premise of increased escape at length as cod, the retention of commercially sized haddock 
(75.84 % (58.69–96.79)) was much greater than that of cod (26.54 % (18.73–36.07)), which is favorable 
to the fishery where haddock, as opposed to the choke species cod, are often part of the wanted 
bycatch. The results are consistent with those obtained by Fraser & Angus (2019) and are attributed 
to haddock’s preference to occupy positions high in the trawl extension (Ferro et al., 2007; Krag et al., 
2009), making them less likely to move down and forward in the bottom half. The partial loss of 
haddock in our experiment could have been influenced by the observed accumulation of haddock and 
cod shortly behind the back edge of the horizontal panel, where crowding may have led some haddock 
to enter the area under the horizontal panel. The increased window contact likelihood with length is 
likely the result of the higher swimming endurance of larger haddock that could have allowed them 
to swim forward to contact the window (Breen et al., 2004). Our codend 𝐿50 for haddock of 23.90 cm 
(16.78–28.18) is similar to those reported in literature, for example 21.83 cm (7.26–32.70) reported 
for a 95.1 mm codend with a 96 mesh circumference by Krag et al. (2016) and 23.3 cm reported for 
an 87.1 mm double twine 120 mesh circumference codend by Robertson (1983). 
Whiting was encountered in high numbers in the trial. The best model for whiting predicted a 
decreasing escape likelihood with length, and there were no candidate models suggesting other 
==PAGE:80==
 
 
 
 
79 
drivers of contact likelihood. Like haddock, whiting are associated with the top of the trawl extension 
(Ferro et al., 2007; Krag et al., 2009) and likely did not swim forward under the horizontal panel in 
large numbers. The retention of 62.22 % (41.77–81.48) of commercially sized whiting indicates that 
there were losses, however these were likely the result of codend selection, as the species has a low 
MCRS of 23 cm in comparison to its 𝐿50 in the codend mesh (22.90 cm (12.85–27.53)). While large 
whiting are considered wanted bycatch in the fishery, smaller individuals often generate low prices 
despite being of commercial size. A codend selection that reduces the fraction of small individuals 
among the commercially sized whiting catch is, therefore, generally embraced by the fishers, as less 
onboard capacities have to be used to handle and store this often large part of the catch. The codend 
𝐿50 in our experiment was similar to the 24.5 cm 𝐿50 reported by Robertson (1983) for whiting in an 
86.5 mm (90 mm nominal) double twine 120 mesh circumference codend.  
The best model for saithe predicted a decrease in window contact with size, but a length-independent 
contact scenario and scenario of increasing contact with length also represented the experimental 
data well. The drivers of contact likelihood for the escape window are therefore unclear for this 
species and escape of commercially sized individuals was present (55.18 % retention (25.57–92.04)). 
Fraser & Angus (2019) did not find a significant difference between saithe retention in their codends, 
but this was likely the result of very low catches of the species (24.8 kg in the 120 mm control and 3.3 
kg in the test codend). In the Kattegat-Skagerrak Nephrops fishery reductions of catches of saithe may 
be a bearable loss to fishers as they have a low commercial value and are thought to damage Nephrops 
when thrashing in the codend (Savina et al., 2022). 
Multiple species of skates are protected in the North Sea and their accidental capture needs to be 
avoided. Unfortunately, no difference for catches of skates between codends was found in our 
experiment, indicating that this type of BRD cannot be used to avoid their capture. 
Comparing the catch performance of our test codend to that of a SELTRA 270, both designs retained 
similar high fractions of Nephrops. While the retention of total cod catch was similar between the 
designs, the codend with the bottom escape window had a higher retention of undersized individuals 
while the SELTRA 270 retained a larger fraction of the commercially sized individuals. For the fishery, 
this means that a greater fraction of the available cod quota would be taken up by undersized 
individuals that may not be marketed for human consumption and generate lower market prizes 
(European Union, 2013). However, both the retention of commercially sized plaice and that of 
haddock, were higher in the test codend compared to the SELTRA 270.  
Differences in catch goals across vessels result in a wide variety of needs towards gear selectivity, 
requiring a broad portfolio of gears that can provide the desired catch compositions (Feekings et al., 
2019). At present, Nephrops fisheries that use top escape panels lose large fractions of the commercial 
==PAGE:81==
 
 
 
 
80 
roundfish bycatch along with cod. Likewise, sorting grids employed in Nephrops fisheries eliminate 
almost all commercial fish catch (Valentinsson & Ulmestrand, 2008), causing high levels of lost revenue 
if their use is only directed at the avoidance of cod. Therefore, the protection of a single choke species, 
cod, has resulted in losses of many wanted bycatch species in the past, reducing fisheries’ efficiency 
(Krag et al., 2010). Our BRD offers a wholly new combination of species selectivity that could provide 
a valuable alternative for fishers that aim to maximize their bycatch of commercial fish species while 
avoiding cod.  
Additional knowledge on the decisive escape-facilitating behavioral traits of cod and of factors 
influencing the selectivity of our BRD design (e.g. distance from codline, compartment height, flow 
speeds, light levels, codend circumference etc.) could help to further improve selectivity, possibly 
retaining even lower amounts of cod and maintaining full retention for haddock (Pol & Eayrs, 2021). 
Further, it could be important to understand the role that escape during towing and haul-back plays 
in the reduction of cod catches. Elevated fish escape through BRDs during haul-back have been 
observed in other fisheries (Engaas et al., 1999; Yochum et al., 2021) and a high level of escape during 
haul-back would imply a dependence of BRD effectiveness on haul duration, rendering it less effective 
during longer hauls, as often conducted in the case study fishery (i.e. around 4 hours). Further, fish 
that escape during haul-back may have lower chances of survival than those escaping during towing 
because they have been exhausted for longer and have experienced increased stress (Madsen et al., 
2008; Wood et al., 1983). Our camera footage revealed cod surging forward under the horizontal panel 
towards the position of the escape window during interruptions of haul-back, but we could not 
monitor for escape during towing because of low visibility caused by the suspension of fine sediments 
on the Nephrops fishing grounds. However, Fraser & Angus (2019) observed escape against the flow 
during towing in their design at similar towing speeds as used in this study. A better understanding of 
the role that towing and haul-back play in the effectiveness of our BRD could, for example, be achieved 
by the use of multiple covers for the respective periods of the haul (Madsen et al., 2008). While the 
design used in the present study was chosen to demonstrate the applicability of the concept to a 
crustacean fishery, future development should aim for a simplified design, for example by 
implementing the BRD in a codend with a smaller circumference. This would make the codend more 
similar to the codends currently used in the fishery, making an uptake of the new technology easier 
for fishers (Milliken & DeAlteris, 2004). At the same time, a smaller codend circumference would likely 
facilitate a better selectivity for undersized individuals of many species through more widely open 
meshes (Herrmann et al., 2007). 
The unique ability of the tested design to specifically exclude cod from a trawl achieves a species 
selectivity that has never before been demonstrated in a crustacean fishery. A further development 
==PAGE:82==
 
 
 
 
81 
of the BRD and its uptake by legislation could provide fishers with a valuable tool allowing to retain a 
range of bycatch species in mixed fisheries that are limited by low quotas of cod.
