Surfactant treated organic field-effect transistors: Simulation, 
preparation and structural and electrical characterization
Andreas Ejdrup Lauritzen
December 2017
Master’s Thesis
==PAGE:2==
Abstract
This thesis concerns organic ﬁeld-effect transistors (OFETs) consisting of a bottom-contact
OFET substrate, an octadecyltrichlorosilane (OTS) self-assembled monolayer (SAM) and a con-
ducting 5,5'-bis(naphth-2-yl)- 2,2'-bithiophene (NaT2) layer. The thesis investigates how OTS
monolayers can be formed on the OFET substrate and how this inﬂuence on the structural and
electrical properties of NaT2 OFETs. This task of identifying in which conditions monolayer
growth resulted in high performing NaT2 ﬁlms was carried out by the combination of electrical
characterization in terms of charge carrier mobilities and surface and structural characteriza-
tion in terms of AFM and grazing-incidence X-ray diffraction (GIXRD) in operando.
Complementary to the experimental work, the atomic conﬁgurations of two different bulk
polymorphs, with either ﬂipped or same direction thiophene rings, were investigated using
density functional theory.
This was an exploratory project and no prior data existed.
The results are as follows:
The two simulated NaT2 polymorphs differ from the experimentally observed results, but the
ﬂipped conﬁguration is found to be slightly more probable due to retaining a monoclinic motif.
NaT2 ﬁlms formed without OTS grow continuously without distinctive islands. The lateral
crystallite size is 30 nm. Anhydrous OTS deposition leads to layer-by-layer formation of NaT2
as pyramidic islands with interlayer spacings corresponding to upright standing rodlike NaT2
molecules. The crystallite size is large, up to 100 nm in the lateral direction. Humid OTS depo-
sition also promotes NaT2 layer-by-layer growth, but the grain size is comparatively smaller.
NaT2 ﬁlms conduct charge carriers reliably when ﬁlms are thicker than approximately 8 nm,
both with and without an OTS layer. This corresponds to four molecular layers. At this point
unevenly grown thin ﬁlms cover macroscopic areas.
25 nm ﬁlms without OTS exhibited mobilities of 2.9 × 10−4 cm2/(Vs). Despite having larger
crystal grains and ordered layers, NaT2 ﬁlms deposited with OTS manifest lower mobilities.
i
==PAGE:3==
The structure determined in OFETs differs slightly from the one in bulk and is monoclinic with
approximate unit cell parameters a = 20.3 Å b = 5.9 Å c = 8.1 Å β = 96.9 ◦. The unit cell volume
is smallest for the thinnest ﬁlms, approaching that of bulk as the ﬁlm thickness increases up to
25 nm.
An applied voltage does not inﬂuence the unit cell parameters of the ﬁlms when probed in
operando.
This study enables qualitative understanding of NaT2 growth and how its physical properties
are affected by the presence of an OTS monolayer in OFETs. While the increased ordering due
to an OTS monolayer would be expected to result in higher charge mobilities, the highly struc-
tured morphological grains likely cause poor transport across grain boundaries. Possible rea-
sons for this are discussed. It is possible that thicker NaT2 ﬁlms on OTS monolayers would
overcome this problem and achieve better transport than ﬁlms deposited directly on the di-
electric.
Further understanding of the system could be gained by using molecular dynamics simulation
(MDS) to make comparisons to both DFT simulations and experimentally derived diffraction
data.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Organic ﬁeld effect transistors
Organic electronics have been the subject of much interest as active components in cost-effective
and ﬂexible devices well-suited for applications such as printed electronics or ﬂexible displays
[1, 2]. As for any kind of electronics, well understood transistor behaviour is of critical impor-
tance.
Figure 1.1 shows the outline of a bottom-gate ﬁeld effect transistor (FET), which consists of
the following components: a semiconducting layer or ﬁlm which is isolated from the gate elec-
trode by an insulating gate dielectric. The source and drain electrodes are in contact with the
semiconducting layer through which charge transport is possible [1]. The source electrode is
the charge injecting electrode and is kept as ground (Vs=0 V). Voltage is then applied to the gate
electrode, known as the gate voltage Vg, and to the drain Vd. The voltage drop between drain
and source is called Vds - the drain-source voltage. Doped Si is a standard choice for the gate
electrode as it can also serve as a substrate. If the Vds is negative, holes are injected into the
transistor. This is known a p-type transistor. If the Vds is positive, electrons are injected into
the transistor. This is known a n-type transistor. When a gate voltage is applied charge accumu-
late at the semiconductor-dielectric interface. By applying a voltage drop between the source
and gate electrodes, charge carriers can be made to ﬂow across the semiconducting layer. This
current can then be modulated by varying the gate voltage.
The organic in organic ﬁeld effect transistor (OFET) comes from the organic semiconducting
layer. For OFETs the dielectric is typically a thin SiO2 layer. The most important material-
related parameter of the OFET is the mobility µ of the semiconducting layer. This characterizes
the mean drift velocity of the charges carriers when Vds is high enough to drive a current. The
current I is proportional to the mobility and a high mobility is thus desirable [1, 2]. Another
important parameter is the threshold voltage Vth. When an applied Vg induce charge carriers
at the dielectric/semi-conductor interface the initial charges do not take part in transport, but
are instead bound by deep "traps", corresponding to localized states. The gate voltage must
thus exceed the threshold voltage before mobile charge carriers and the number of induced
charge carriers is thus proportional to the effective gate voltage Vg −Vth.

When a small source-drain voltage is applied Vds << Vg −Vth, a linear voltage drop will form
across the transistor channel and the current I will be proportional to Vds. This is known as the
linear regime. When Vds is increased to the point Vds = Vg −Vth, the drain-source current starts
to exceed the effective gate voltage and the channel saturates. This is known as the saturation
regime, where the current is given by
Isat = W
2L µsatCi(Vg −Vth)2,
(1.1)
where W is the channel width, L the channel length and Ci the capacitance per unit area of the
dielectric layer. Much effort has been made to understand which material and structural prop-
erties lead to high performing ﬁlms and how to consistently create OFETs displaying these qual-
ities. However, it is difﬁcult to probe these qualities directly as they often depend on nanoscale
changes to the molecular packing or longer range morphological irregularities due to the man-
ufacturing process. Furthermore, as the charge carrier transport primarily occurs close to the
semi-conductor/dielectric interface, where charges are induced, the OFET structure must be
understood across the whole ﬁlm. A wide array of methods is thus needed to study OFET ma-
terials.

1.2
Organic semiconducting ﬁlms
How well a semiconducting ﬁlm conducts charge carriers depends on several factors, such as
choice of molecule, molecular packing [3], crystallinity, grain size of crystallites and their in-
terconnectivity [4] as well as defects. The latter factors depend on the deposition method for
the ﬁlm as well as other environmental conditions. As such, any evaluation of a semiconductor
material must take into account the interplay between material, deposition method, choice of
substrate and the environment in which the OFET will operate.

1.2. ORGANIC SEMICONDUCTING FILMS
For disordered materials charge transport happens primarily through thermally enabled hop-
ping [1]. In ordered materials charge transport arises primarily from delocalized π-orbitals
overlapping. When charge carriers are injected by an electrode the overlapping orbitals allow
them to move throughout ordered areas of the ﬁlm [2]. Good charge transport, corresponding
to a high mobility, is thus dependent on short range molecular conﬁgurations that enable high
orbital overlap. A good way to achieve consistent π−π-bonding is by choosing a material with
a backbone of aromatic rings. Figure 1.2a and Figure 1.2b and , show simple thiophene and
benzene which are two examples of the rings that comprise molecules used in OFETs.

The rings are merged to form conjugated systems, where the electrons are shared across the
rings and, when embedded in a crystalline system, across the whole ﬁlm [5]. Well-known ex-
amples of such material are sexithiophene and pentacene. Figure 1.3b and Figure 1.3a show
the chemical structures of sexithiophene and pentacene.
Pentacene is a popular choice because pentacene ﬁlms have exhibited mobilities exceeding
those of amorphous silicon, a common benchmark target [3, 4]. While other organic molecule
systems may not reach the mobilities of pentacene, there is a large ﬁeld of similar rodlike
molecules the performance of which can improved by the use of functional groups. Oligothio-
phenes, with a backbone consisting of thiophene rings, Fig. 1.2a, is the second major material
class used in OFETs due to good molecular packing, favourable intra- and intermolecular in-
teractions due to the high polarizability of the sulfur atoms leading to high mobilities and that
these molecules can be easily modiﬁed by end-capping [6, 7]. Intermolecular S-S interactions
may also contribute to charge carrier transport [8]. End-capping hinders polymerization or
oxidation and can also improve self-organization leading to better molecular packing, as un-
capped molecules can be liable to twist, reducing their orbital overlap [8].
The self-organization process of organic molecular crystals is guided by a combination of close
packing consideration as well as non-covalent inter- and intramolecular bonds such π −π
bonding and van der Waals interactions. Most of the charge carrier transport happens in the
molecular layers close to the interface where the charges are induced, the effect of the dielectric
substrate must be taken into consideration [9]. Conjugated oligomers are often polycrystalline
and surface interactions can change which crystalline polymorph manifests, as they are often
within less than 2 kJ per mole [10]. A crystal polymorph induced by the substrate does not nec-
essarily propagate through the molecular layers. Substrate-induced phases (SIPs) are phases

that exists at the ﬁrst few molecular layers closest to a solid interface, before tapering into the
bulk phase [9]. Dimitrakopoulos et al. were the ﬁrst to identify the presence of both a bulk
phase with a molecular tilt of 21◦as well as a SIP with a molecular tilt of 3◦for sufﬁcently
thick pentacene ﬁlms on SiOx [11]. This mixed phase ﬁlm showed worse transport properties
than thinner ﬁlms which contained only the SIP, underlining the need to pay attention to the
surface. Mannebach et al. later observed similar behaviour for sexithiophene [12].
Organic ﬁlms cannot be treated as a single perfect crystal for practical purposes. The Vth is
inﬂuenced by traps at the interface which may occur due to impurities or interface states [1]
and the roughness of the substrate may reduce mobility [13]. It is thus desirable to tune the
dielectric surface to match the semiconductor. Grain boundaries represent charge trapping
sites and mobility can be lower by a factor of 10 across polycrystalline ﬁlms compared to single
crystals [13]. How grain size correlates with mobility is not exactly clear. Matsubara [13] et al.
ﬁnd mobility to be proportional to grain size , whereas Di Carlo et al. [14] ﬁnd the mobility to
depend on grain size in a non-linear fashion, being dependent on inter-grain mobility at low
grain sizes and intra-grain mobility once a size threshold is crossed. In both cases, a larger grain
size corresponds to a higher mobility.
1.3
Surface smoothing surfactants such as octadecyltrichlorosilane
A common way to inﬂuence the dielectric (typically SiO2) is by applying a self-assembled mono-
layer (SAM) of surfactants such as octadecylsilanes, for example octadecyltrichlorosilane (OTS).
The OTS SAM inﬂuences the dielectric surface by reducing dielectric surface traps, modifying
the surface energy, planarizing and reducing the roughness of the surface [15, 16]. OTS also
forms a hydrophobic surface (unlike SiO2, which is hydrophilic) and is thought to passivate the
SiO2.
When crystalline ﬁlms form on a substrate, their growth mode is inﬂuenced by the physical
properties of the surface. How strongly the molecules bind to the surface will inﬂuence whether
the initial ﬁlm growth occurs as isolated islands or as layers that fully cover the surface. The
mobility of the molecules on the surface will inﬂuence how initial molecule clusters grow into
a continuous ﬁlm and thus affect the granular nature of the ﬁlm [17]. A SAM OTS is assumed to
induce better ordered crystal structure with larger grains.
The preferred crystal structure for high mobility is 2D layer-by-layer growth, which is superior
to 3D or island growth [15]. Well-ordered crystals facilitate electrical behaviour, and this way
a well-ordered SAM may facilitate good crystal growth. Walter et al. [18] investigated solution
based methods to deposit OTS SAMs at different humidity levels . Samples were prepared in
both high humidity, corresponding to between 4% and 85% relative humidity, using a hexane-
OTS solution as well as in anhydrous conditions (0% relative humidity) using a toluene-OTS
solution. They ﬁnd, that while different OTS deposition methods do not result in different lat-
tice spacings of the semiconductor, the SAMs prepared at high humidity show higher mobilities
and larger grain sizes.

NaT2 is found to form a monoclinic lattice with the longitudinal direction standing nearly per-

The open questions are: How can SAMs be formed at the NaT2-SiO2 interface? Can a SAM
improve the NaT2 structure, grain morphology and mobility? How is the structure and proper-

Objectives of the study
This project is an exploratory study of how SAMs may affect the structural, morphological and
electrical properties of napthyl end-capped NaT2 thin ﬁlms in OFETs. Films will be deposited
by vacuum sublimation as they typically exhibit higher mobilities compared to ﬁlms deposited
by solution based methods [1], due to superior packing, which makes the deposition method
well suited for the goals of the project. The scientiﬁc goals are as follows:
Investigate different ways to deposit a smoothing surfactant layer i.e. SAM and identify how
this affects the growth, structure, grain size and charge carrier properties of NaT2 ﬁlms.
Investigate the lower ﬁlm thickness limit at which a semiconducting layer is able to provide
a continuous pathway for charge carriers with or without a SAM.

2.1.1
DFT Implementation
All DFT computations were carried out using GPAW, which is a density functional theory Python
front-end code based on the projector-augmented wave (PAW) method and the atomic simu-
lation environment (ASE).
A key goal of DFT computations of organic molecular crystalline systems is to be able to suc-
cessfully discern between different polymorphs and identify those of lowest energy. This re-
quires a high degree of accuracy as polymorphs of organic molecular crystals are often very
close in energy [2]. This of course has to be balanced against the speed of computation, es-
pecially due to the large size of molecular crystals. Standard DFT xc-functionals, such as the
Generalized Gradient Approximation (GGA) xc-functionals, model intra-molecular covalent
bonds well [23]. However, organic molecular crystals are often bound by long-range van der
Waals interactions, chieﬂy the attractive London dispersion interaction [2, 23]. These are de-
scribed poorly by GGA functionals, but it is possible to remedy this by using long-range correc-
tions. Brandenburg and Grimme used the DFT-D3 correction - Density Functional Theory with
atom-pairwise and three-body dispersion correction - along with the Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof
(PBE) GGA potential dispersion correction to obtain results for organic molecular crystals that
matched well with experimental data. The DFT-D3 correction acts as an approximate long
range correction and is very fast compared to self-consistent DFT calculations [23].

The initial attempt at treating the NaT2 system was with one such DFT-D3 corrected PBE func-

cutable, which did not function properly on the High Perfomance Computing (HPC) readily
available for students. The treatment of the system was thus moved to Niﬂheim, the DTU

this conﬁguration change did not fully remedy the issues as the system was still prone to crashes.
While some of these issues, such as the computation losing connection to the Niﬂheim server,
were likely one time issues, the computations started repeatedly crashing after a certain amount
of iterations. Attempts to "backtrack" the computation to earlier iterations and thus possi-
bly avoid the cause of the process terminating were attempted, but with no success. This
approach was subsequently abandoned and the system instead treated with the mBEEF-vdW
(meta Bayesian Error Estimation - van der Waals) exchange functional.
The BEEF (Bayesian Error Estimation Functional) class of functionals rely on Bayesian statis-
tics to create functionals based on empirical data sets of material data [24]. The correlation part
of the mBEEF-vdW functional has been ﬁtted as a mixture of the local density approximation
(LDA), where the xc-functional only uses the local electronic density (Vxc(n(r)9, the semilocal
GGA which also takes the density gradient into account Vxc(n(r),∇n(r)), and nonlocal corre-
lation of the Rutgers-Chalmers approximation for van der Waals forces, which takes takes the
electronic density of the entire system into account [25]. It is this non-local part, which is of
critical importance for the given system. The exchange part of the xc-functional has been ﬁtted
using only a GGA for computational speed reasons. The mBEEF-vdW xc-functional has been
shown to have a high degree of accuracy when it comes to the estimation of lattice constants,
outperforming all other functionals it was tested against [25]. This makes the functional well
suited for unit cell optimization.

ments.
Anhydrous layer formation is reported to be slow so the substrates were left in the solution
for at least 15 hours. The beaker was capped during deposition to prevent evaporation. After
removing the substrates from the solution, they were cleaned in acetone, isopropanol and lastly
deionized water under sonication for 3 min. per step, then dried in a N2 stream. The substrates
were then stored under inert gas
For high humidity SAM deposition the OTS-Hexane solution was mixed a fume hood with 48%
relative humidity. Before substrate immersion the beaker containing the solution was left for
at least an hour in order to let a water equilibrium form between the air and the solution to
ensure it reached a high water density state. The ﬁrst two substrates were immersed in the
OTS-Hexane solution for 4 hrs. After removal, they were cleaned in the same way as the sub-
strates prepared in anhydrous conditions. Upon visual inspection by eye, it became clear that
these substrates were damaged as the gold contact electrodes were seen to be either missing or
frayed. It was deemed that this was due to prolonged exposure to the Hexane and another two
substrates were made with a shorter deposition time of around 1 hr, followed by rinsing. Four
more high humidity samples were prepared in OTS-Hexane as well as a blank Si/SiO2 substrate
for contact angle measurements and rinsed afterwards. Further analysis of the damaged sam-
ples can be found in the section 8.1.

3.6. STRUCTURAL CHARACTERIZATION THROUGH GIXRD
3.6
Structural characterization through GIXRD
GIXRD measurements of the ﬁlm were done at the UK CRG beamline, XMaS (BM28), at the
European Synchrotron Radiation Facility (ESRF) in France. The samples were measured in
nylon/metal-printed sample chamber continously ﬂushed with helium in order to reduce ab-
sorption and scattering due to air [28]. An X-ray energy of 10 keV was used and the beam was
deﬁned by slits giving the beam a dimension of 50 µm × 1 mm (height × width). The incidence
angle was optimized for each sample to maximize the intensity of the diffraction and was gen-
erally kept between the critical angle of NaT2 (0.141◦) and the critical angle of SiO2 (0.173◦).
The electrode length was around 14 mm at the X-ray beam position and the X-ray footprint
11.5 mm for an incidence angle of 0.5◦. The X-ray intensity images were recorded with an MAR
165 charge-coupled device (CCD) detector. Samples were mounted onto a PCB slot, see green
object in Fig. 3.3, to allow electrical connections to the ZIF-socket.
In operando electrical measurements were carried out by biasing the device with programmable
voltage supplies (Kepco and HP) and recording the resulting current with a Keithley picoamme-
ter. As the beamstop in sample chamber did not fully stop the beam, the direct beam point was
visible in the x-ray images. While a calibration of the sample-to-detector was done by a mea-
surement of silver behenate, the distance found from this calibration lead to derived lattice
parameters far from those reported by Tian et al. [6]. However, high angle gixrd measurements
of a 25 nm no-OTS sample was also carried out at the ESRF.
Figure 3.5 shows a high and a low angle diffraction pattern interlaced, covering a range of
Qxy < 3.3 Å−1. The two continous rings at high angle corresponds to the powder scattering
rings from the (111) and (200) reﬂections of the gold electrodes. Using these reﬂections as cal-
ibration measurements, a sample-to-detector distance was derived for the angle set-up and
lattice values closely matching those of Tian were found. The sample-to-detector distance for
the regular measurements was then chosen in order to match the a lattice parameter found
from the high angle measurements.
In operando measurements were performed by applying a ﬁxed Vds, starting at Vds=0 V, to the
sample while the Vg was swept from 0 V to -30 V and back to zero volt. A 90 s GIXRD measurent
was then recorded. The Vds was then decreased by 10 V and another scan taken. This was re-
peated for Vds = 0,−10,−20,−30 V. This was done to further investigate the effect of applied
gate voltage on lattice parameters.

4.1
DFT analysis
Figure 4.1a and Figure 4.1b show conﬁguration A before and after optimization. Figure 4.2a
and Figure 4.2b show conﬁguration B before and after optimization. Table 4.1 shows the en-
ergy, unit cell stress and lattice parameters.
After optimization conﬁgurations A and B have moved towards more obtuse angles. This shift
is by 4.8◦for conﬁguration A and 6.6◦for conﬁguration B. We note that the initial lattice pa-
rameters are derived from room temperature measurements, whereas the DFT optimization
occurs at the 0 K groundstate. Additionally, simulated results may not be expected to match
completely with experimental data. However, a large discrepancy of the monoclinic angle for
both conﬁgurations means caution must be taken in choosing one conﬁguration as the more
likely polymorph of NaT2. A more likely conﬁguration will be chosen based on a comparison
of all results derived by DFT.
Both conﬁgurations have higher energies compared to the 700 eV locked unit cell optimiza-
tion carried out during the convergence test. Both also have lower total unit cell stress. This is
not unexpected, as the DFT process will attempt to minimize the stress on the cell and forces
on the atoms simultaneously, leading to compromises. Conﬁguration A has a higher stress than
conﬁguration B, but a lower energy of about 2.6 meV per atom. While differences in energies
between different molecular crystal polymorphs can be less than 1 kcal/mol [2], this only cor-
responds to 0.06 kcal/mol.
Keeping in mind that the energy is likely not converged at the given plane wave cut-off, the
energies are practically identical. For both conﬁgurations, lattice parameters a and b expand
while c shrinks. This is effect is stronger for the B conﬁguration, which also becomes triclinic
(α and γ for conﬁguration A has their ﬁrst non-9 digit at the 5th decimal). Conﬁguration A thus
seems slightly more favourable based on the DFT optimization due its closer resemblance to
experimentally observed results.

Chapter 5
Discussion
We will ﬁnally discuss possible physical explanations of the described experimental observa-
tions. When OTS coated substrates are concerned, here measured contact angles (102-107◦are
similar to those reported for high humidity OTS by Walther et al. [18] (102−104◦) for oligothio-
phenes. However, Walter et al. report that anhydrous deposition results in a signiﬁcantly lower
contact angles than high humidity depositions. This points to our anhydrous OTS SAMs having
different microstructural characteristics than those of Walter et al..
The surface analysis shows that the surface of the OTS surfactants on SiO2 is smoother than
the raw SiO2. No clear differences can be discerned between an OTS SAM deposited via hex-
ane or toluene. However, it also leads to the formation of 2-8 nm tall clusters, possibly formed
from excess OTS or impurities. While areas smoothed by OTS would likely induce a more or-
dered ﬁlm growth, it is unclear to what degree the presence of tall clusters impedes ordered
ﬁlm growth.
During initial ﬁlm growth, as characterized by the 8 nm ﬁlms, the NaT2 does not grow in an
even manner, instead forming rapidly rising islands with ﬂat terrace tops. This occurs both
with or without an OTS SAM. This means NaT2 prefers stacking on itself despite the OTS low-
ering the surface energy of the substrate
The effect of the OTS SAM becomes apparent as the average ﬁlm thickness reaches 11 nm.
Both the anhydrous T11 and the high humidity H11 conﬁgurations with OTS show layer-by-
layer growth forming terraces, whereas NaT2 in the N11 conﬁguration without OTS manifests
a surface that is primarily smoothly varying. This indicates that OTS SAMs lower the surface
energy or otherwise passivates the surface of SiO2, allowing NaT2 to self-arrange in the gaps
between the ﬁrst sharply rising levels in an ordered fashion that propagates throughout the
layers. OTS SAMs thus favours layer-by-layer growth in NaT2 ﬁlms.
48
==PAGE:60==
Anhydrous and high humidity deposition of OTS SAMs do not result in NaT2 ﬁlms showing the
same physical properties for "bulk" 25 nm ﬁlms. While the anhydrous T25 conﬁguration fully
retains its layer-by-layer growth, the surface of the high humidity H25 conﬁguration shows both
terraces and less ordered areas. This suggests OTS deposited in anhydrous conditions is ulti-
mately more ordered than OTS deposited in high humidity conditions and thus preferred for
inducing layer-by-layer growth. It is unclear how the order of the OTS deposited in anhydrous
conditions propagate into the bulk 25 nm ﬁlms, as AFM scans of both OTS depositions appear
similar.
Some AFM scans revealed the presence up to 100 nm tall spike or wall-like structures, appear-
ing throughout the ﬁlm, see Fig. 4.21. Balzer et al. [19] have previously observed the presence
similar "nanoﬁbres" in NaT2 ﬁlms and found them to correspond to molecules ’lying’ at a small
angle to the surface. As oligothiphenes transport charge perpendicular to their longitudinal di-
rection, the ﬁbres may actually suppress electronic transport. Furthermore, they "consume"
NaT2 which could lead to valleys around them, reducing inter-grain contact. A possible exam-
ple of such behaviour can be seen in the lower right corner of Fig. 4.19a. It is possible that
the formation of these nanoﬁbres, in addition to poor inter-grain connection due to the ﬁlm
consisting of plate-like islands, are the reasons why the H25 conﬁguration to has the lowest
mobility among the 25 nm ﬁlms.
Considering the X-ray analysis, the NaT2 ﬁlms generally had smaller unit cell volumes the thin-
ner they were. A smaller unit cell volume corresponds to a closer packing of molecules, which
could indicate the molecules are able to pack better in thinner ﬁlms, transforming towards bulk
packing as the average ﬁlm thickness increases. This is attributed to interface driven frustration
of what is essentially the same unit cell as in bulk. However, no real substrate induced phases
i.e. other polymorphs were observed. It is possible that T25 (which remains highly ordered
based on AFM scans, see Fig. 4.18a) is able to maintain a closer packing and thus smaller unit
cell even as its thickness approaches bulk.
The unit cell was found to remain stable under an applied voltage. This can be contrasted with
the work of Liscio et al. [21], who reported that pentacene will tilt towards the applied ﬁeld.
In previous studies by Huss-Hansen [20] NaT2 was observed to stay stable under applied volt-
age possibly due to its higher layer-to-layer molecular ordering. The studied ﬁlms were 50 nm
and thus still considerably thicker. This study conﬁrms that the ﬁlms retain structural stability
down to a thickness of 8 nm and that a surfactant layer does not cause structural instability in
operating OFETs.
The OTS based ﬁlms show larger directional crystalline grain sizes compared to the no-OTS
ﬁlms, with the anhydrous SAM ﬁlms showing even larger grain size of the high humidity SAM
ﬁlms. This indicates close correspondence between morphological grains, as shown by the
AFM scans, and crystalline grain size as measured by X-rays. This further indicates that an an-
hydrously deposited OTS SAM promotes ordered layer-by-layer growth of NaT2.
49
==PAGE:61==
The crystalline grain size perpendicular to the surface generally corresponds to around 80-
100% of the average ﬁlm thickness for the 11 and 25 nm , indicating the ﬁlms consists of a
mosaic of single crystals. For the 8 nm ﬁlms the thickness is found to be higher than the aver-
age thickness by around 50-100 %. If this is not an artefact of the grain size derivation method,
it is possible this reﬂects the grain size of the initial steep pyramids, which were around 10-16
nm tall.
Electrical analysis show 11 nm to be the minimum ﬁlm thickness for reliable charge carrier
transport throughout the ﬁlm. As the 8 nm ﬁlms do not fully cover the surface and show sharply
rising morphological domains charge carries will have to percolate through narrow pathways
to travel between the source and the drain electrodes. Such "choke points" are a likely source
of poor and unreliable transport.
The ﬁlm mobilities increase with thickness and this is most obvious for the no-OTS ﬁlms. This
observation is likely due to the additional NaT2 causing full coverage of the surface as well
better contact between the bottommost molecular layers. The mobilities of the no-OTS are
generally higher than those of the OTS SAM based ﬁlms of similar thickness.
We will ﬁnally discuss the possible physical origins of this surprising result, which does not
seem to be consistent with the previous literature. Depositing OTS in anhydrous conditions
lead to a well ordered SAM, which in turn promotes well ordered 2D layer-by-layer growth and
large grain sizes. However, even for the thickest ﬁlms, OTS based ﬁlms show lower mobilities
than untreated ﬁlms. This goes against the general tendency, where increasing grain size and
molecular order correlates strongly with higher mobility. Three possible explanations spring to
mind: Interactions at the interface, interactions at the grain boundaries and surfactant induced
contact resistance at electrode-NaT2 interface .
Previous work by Shouhu Liu [32] at University of Southern Denmark investigating the per-
formance of NaT2 based OFETs found the contact resistance between the electrodes and NaT2
to be negligible for channel lengths above 20 µm. While the presence of a SAM will affect the
contact resistance at the electrode interface, it is unlikely to increase the resistance signiﬁcantly
compared that of the 500 µ channel.
Unfavourable interactions at the OTS-NaT2 interface is a less likely scenario. The OTS molecules
serve to smooth out the jagged SiO2 surface, lower the surface energy and are more likely to in-
teract positively with the organic oligothiophenes as both upward poiting OTS tails and NaT2
are hydrophobic in nature. This is rather the reason surfactants have been found to improve to
improve mobilities in organic ﬁlms, see Walter et al. [18]. However, the ﬁlms reported in the
literature often had a thickness of 50 nm or more, whereas the thickest ﬁlm investigated in this
study is only 25 nm thick.
50
==PAGE:62==
Anhydrously deposited SAMs cause NaT2 to grow as tiered pyramids. As a result, a larger frac-
tion of the deposited semiconductor molecules are further away from the interface compared
to ﬁlms grown without a SAM, which displays more continuous growth. A possible explana-
tion for the lower mobility of the highly terraced ﬁlm is thus that contact between morpho-
logical grains is limited to the very bottommost layers as it is only here where the layer level
is fully spanned by molecules or that the high ordering of the layers cause poorer alignment
of molecules at the grain boundaries. It is however hard to verify this based on the electrical
characterization of the samples, see Table 4.5, where the threshold voltage, which would be ex-
pected to correlate with poor transport across grains, varies across thickness and conﬁguration
type.
This observation contrasts with similar studies, which showed high humidity deposition of
OTS SAMs to lead to higher mobility for oliothiphenes [18]. Based on AFM imaging, it is not
clear why anhydrous OTS facilitates more ordered growth than high humidity OTS and indeed,
Walter et al.[18] observe the opposite. The hexane-OTS based ﬁlms generally display the lowest
mobility values. This is true even for the 25 nm ﬁlms, despite the hexane-OTS based ﬁlm be-
ing the only conﬁguration measured in helium, which should improve its electrical transport
compared to the other 25 nm conﬁgurations measured in air. It is possible that this is a due
to their having both poor inter-grain mobility, similar to toluene-OTS based ﬁlms, and smaller
crystalline grains, leading to lower intra-grain mobilities.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The main goal was prepare OTS SAMs and study their effects on the structural and electrical
properties of NaT2 in OFETs. This was an exploratory study that sought to broadly characterize
the different aspects of semiconductor optimization for OFETs using AFM scans of the surface,
GIXRD to probe the crystal structure and electrical tests to derive perfomance parameters.
As a prelude for the experimental works, two different NaT2 bulk polymorphs were optimized
and compared using density functional theory. Both ground state conﬁgurations were found
to differ from room temperature structures observed in bulk as well as in thin ﬁlms. As both
conﬁgurations were close in energy, conﬁguration A was chosen as more likely due to retaining
a monoclinic stacking pattern.
OTS SAMs were deposited in both anhydrous and high humidity conditions. The anhydrous
deposition was found to be preferred, leading to layer-by-layer terrace growth and large do-
mains across all thickness conﬁgurations. For high humidity depositions, the ordering induced
by the SAM is profound and tapered off as the ﬁlm thickness was increased. This stands in con-
trast to prior studies, such as Walter et al. [18],who found humid SAMs to induce a higher
degree of structural order than anhydrous SAMs.
While both OTS conﬁgurations displayed larger crystallite grain sizes and are shielded from
interfacial defects, they generally display lower mobilities than the untreated ﬁlms once the
ﬁlm can reliably conduct charge. This is presumably due to poorer inter-grain charge trans-
port.
The NaT2 unit cell consists of two rigid molecules forming a monoclinic unit cell with approx-
imate unit cell parameters a = 20.3 Å, b = 5.9 Å ,c = 8.1 Å and β = 96.9◦. The rigid molecules
stand upwards almost perpendicular to the surface.
The presence of an OTS SAM was found to neither alter the unit cell structure of ﬁlms nor lead
to a substrate induced phase. However, 1-4% smaller unit cells, corresponding to closer molec-
ular packing were observed for thinner ﬁlms trending towards bulk values as the ﬁlm thickness
increases up to 25 nm.
52
==PAGE:64==
A lower thickness limit for NaT2 to reliably conduct charge was found to be 11 nm, correspond-
ing to an average of 4-5 unit cells. This should be understood as an average thickness. Below
this limit, the NaT2 ﬁlms consist of terraced islands rising 10-16 nm from the substrate sur-
face. This behaviour occurs for ﬁlms with or with surfactants and likely leads to poor charge
transport due to narrow pathways over longer distances and empty areas in between in the is-
lands. Films down to 8 nm, with or without an OTS SAM, were found to not undergo crystalline
changes due to applied voltages in operando due to sufﬁcent structural stability.
This study identiﬁes anhydrous deposition of OTS SAMs as a facile way to achieve well ordered
layer by layer growth of NaT2 with large grains. However, steps must be taken to identify the
cause of sub-par mobility across the ﬁlm.

Chapter 7
Outlook
As organic molecular crystals are complex and sensitive to minute changes it is desirable to
investigate them from as many angles as possible. One additional angle not employed, but al-
ready planned by us, in this thesis is MDS.
Structures optimized via MDS can be used to generate simulated diffraction patterns which
can be compared against experimental GIXRD images. The behaviour of SAMs have already
been studied using MDS for example by Roscioni et al. [33] and by expanding the simulation
to include small conducting molecules a realistic system could be treated. Any derived struc-
tures could then simultaneously be compared to results derived from DFT, which offers greater
atomic level accuracy, but which cannot be used to evaluate larger systems directly.
