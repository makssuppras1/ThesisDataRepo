Abstract
Intact chalk, when found in-situ, has the properties of a competent soft rock, whose
natural moisture saturation frequently lies close to or even slightly above the liquid
limit. Various activities (e.g., pile driving, tunneling, weathering, etc.) can alternate
chalk’s structure, creating a viscous, silt/clay-sized, low strength material, which is
generally identified as remolded chalk. Despite almost all cases of remolded chalk
being troublesome for geotechnical & geomechanical projects, little is yet understood
of the mechanical properties of chalk in this remolded state. Recent in-situ investi-
gations and laboratory studies have shown that, if given appropriate time, remolded
chalk will redevelop a percentage of strength via a process known as ageing.
To test this phenomenon, a laboratory test program was run on chalk specimens
obtained from Sigerslev kridtbrud, close to Stevns Klint. The specimens were first
characterized in the Civil Engineering Department’s Petrophysical laboratory for pore
throat distribution, specific surface, porosity & permeability, and subsequently, a 2-
step testing program was followed in the Rock Mechanics Laboratory at Geo. The
first step was to test the intact chalk specimens, for uniaxial compressive strength
and undrained shear strength, as a point of reference. Afterwards, two batches of
remolded specimens were created with a particle size of < 1mm and moisture content
of 31% in an identical manner, but stored in different conditions. The first batch was
stored at 25◦C, where evaporation of the moisture content was allowed, whereas the
second batch was stored at 7◦C where no moisture evaporation would occur. Multiple
remolded specimens were tested in different time stages for an ageing span of up to
two months in order to estimate strength development over time. Further comparison
to previous in-situ and laboratory investigations was conducted to the extent possible.
The experimental process showed moisture content to be a governing factor in
strength redevelopment, while monitoring the specimens bulk volume, suggest thixo-
tropic hardening to be the most reasonable explanation for large increases in strength.
More specifically, specimens stored at 25◦C and tested via fall cone penetrometer
exhibited undrained shear strength from 10kPa on preparation day to 1800kPa after
61 days for moisture content loss of 13%, and specimens tested via tor vane, from
10kPa on preparation day, to 1600kPa after 62 days for moisture content loss of 7%.
Contrariwise specimens stored at 7◦C, where no moisture content loss was observed,
tested via the same processes, exhibited constant low strength in the range of 5 −
10kPa for the entire ageing period.
In addition, limitations of specific test methods with such low strength material

are highlighted. Specimens stored at 25◦C and tested under uniaxial compression
exhibited minimal increases in uniaxial compressive strength from 10 on preparation
day to 20kPa after 62 days for moisture content loss of 5%, comparable to strength
ranges obtained from identical specimens stored at 7◦C. Finally, specimens tested
under unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression showed fluctuations in strength
over an ageing period of 60 days, from 10kPa to 25kPa, while no significant moisture
content was observed.
Overall this study highlights the importance of moisture content in the strength
redevelopment of remolded chalk and establishes an effective and repeatable process
for future research. Given the abundance of chalk in Denmark and the North Sea, it
is safe to assume that a more thorough investigation regarding the controlling factors
of remolded chalk behaviour could provide a clearer picture as to how this specific
carbonate rock performs mechanically when damaged, and identify further physical
mechanisms controlling strength redevelopment.

Introduction
Problem framing
Numerous occurrences of remolded chalk are reported as being problematic for the
successful completion of geotechnical, geomechanical, and civil engineering projects
(Clayton & Serratrice 1993 [1], Lord et al. 2002 [2]), especially regarding pile foun-
dations (Vijayvergiya et al. 1977 [3], Burnland et al. 1983 [4], Muir Wood et al.
2015 [5]). When intact chalk is reworked, particle bonds are broken, cementation
and cohesion are lost, whereas chalk grains and lumps are floating on intact chalk’s
natural moisture saturation.
This remolded material exhibit low strength in the short-term but is reported
to build up strength with time, which in laboratory conditions is observed as devel-
opment in properties such as undrained shear strength (Doughty et al. 2018 [6]),
cohesion ( Clayton 1977 [7]), small strain stiffness (Bialowas & Diambra 2018 [8]),
etc., while in-situ as improvement of the stability of structures build on chalk, such
as, gain in the shaft capacity of driven piles in chalk (Ciavaglia et al. 2017 [9], Buckley
et al. [10], Nowak & Thijssen 2018 [11]).
Stimuli & motivation
Considering the increased activity in onshore and offshore infrastructure construction
and the abundance of chalk in Denmark and the North Sea, a better understanding of
remolded chalk’s mechanical behaviour can be proven valuable for future geotechnical
and civil engineering projects regarding foundation installations. Hence, this project’s
motivation is to help provide an understanding of the physical processes occurring in
remolded chalk with regard to pile foundation design.
Project objectives
This project aims to create a reproducible and meaningful test procedure for studying
remolded chalk’s strength development. The remolded specimens were produced from
intact chalk blocks obtained from Sigerslev kridtbrud in Stevns Klint. In order to
have a thorough understanding of the parent material, a series of tests were run on
==PAGE:18==

the intact specimens to obtain pore throat distribution, specific surface, porosity &
permeability, elastic moduli, undrained shear & uniaxial compressive strength, and
liquid limit.
Subsequently, the specimens were crushed and reconstituted under controlled

The produced remolded specimens were tested in several time
stages over two months to determine the undrained shear & uniaxial compressive

Two batches of specimens were produced with the same

This project thesis constitutes of three parts. The Theoretical concepts, the Experi-
mental process and the Concluding remarks.
• Theoretical concepts
In the first chapter, Chalk, the material, chalk is presented both in intact and re-

1.1.1
Chalk description
Chalk, in broad terms, is a soft granular sedimentary rock, consisting of more than
98% calcium carbonate (CaCO3). Sparse amounts of clay and silica minerals (Bell
et al. 1999) [12], (Clayton et al. 2003) [13], formed from the gradual deposition of
marine micro-organisms on the seabed. The dominant part (calcium carbonate) con-
sists of skeletons of algae & nannoplankton and fossil fragments known as coccoliths
and foraminifera. In Danish carbonate rocks, benthic organisms are found, such as
bryozoans, brachiopods, and echinoderms (Thomsen 1995) [14].
Coccoliths are circular arrangements ranging from 5 −20µm in diameter of fos-
silised CaCO3 platelets (Hancock 1975) [15], (Clayton 1983) [16], (Clayton et al.
2003) [13]. Foraminifera are single-celled marine organisms ranging from less than
1mm −20cm in diameter which produce a shell made of CaCO3 made of different
structures of calcite (Clayton 1983) [16] (Mortimore 1990) [17], (Marcelle 2018) [18].
Bryozoans are aquatic animals ranging from 5−23mm in diameter. Their exoskeleton
has the shape of a hollow shpere and consists of either organic material or CaCO3
(Thomsen 1995) [14].
The minor part, as mentioned, consists of clay minerals, such as kaolinite
(Al2Si2O5(OH)4), montmorillonite ((Na, Ca)(Al, Mg)6(Si4O10)3(OH)6−nH2O) etc.
which are attributed to the erosion of continental shelves (i.e. due to glacier scrapping
of the seabed), and silica minerals such as quartz (SiO2), feldspar ((K, Na)AlSi3O8−
CaAl2Si2O8) etc, which can be transported via wind, rivers and turbidites from delta-
slopes, or be a consequence of volcanic ash (Kroenke et al. 1991) [22].
The most common form of silicate minerals in chalk is flint, which occurs as
nodules and masses, due to the precipitation of silica from organic sediments, such as
siliceous sponges, which fill the cavities formed from crustacean arthropods in newly
deposited chalk oozes (Maliva & Dickson 1992) [23].

1.1.4
Physical & mechanical properties of intact chalk
Most properties of chalk such as porosity, permeability, capillary entry pressure, and
elastic moduli are the outcome of two mechanisms: primary sediment composition
and subsequent diagenetic history caused by microbial action, burial stress, tempera-
ture, and pore pressure (Fabricius 2007) [37], with porosity being the main governing
property.
At the sea bed, the porosity of freshly deposited calcareous oozes, which is inter-
particle porosity, between the sediment grains and within the shells of micro-organisms,
is around 70%, thus making water the main component. As new sediments are de-
posited and burial occurs, porosity is reduced due to two mechanisms: mechanical
compaction from overburden formations and pore-filling cementation due to pressure
dissolution at stylolites, causing chalk to stiffen.
For the North Sea basin, where the overburden stress is the maximum stress, the
governing stress in mechanical compaction is the highest effective stress (σmax) which
is defined by Terzaghi (1923) [38] as:
σmax = sov −Pp
(1.1)
==PAGE:26==
16
1 Chalk, the material
where, sov is the total stress of the overburden and Pp is the pore pressure. Me-
chanical compaction can then be described as the product of stress dependant con-
solidation and time-dependent creep (Krogsbøll & Foged 2003) [39].
Cementation can be described as the recrystallization of calcite due to sufficient
burial depth and diagenesis progression. With increasing depth, initially contact ce-
ment is formed, thus stiffening chalk, while with sufficient effective stress, the pressure
solution of stylolites will add Calcium and carbonate ions to the saturating pore wa-
ter, and pore-filling cementation may occur as well. Hence, with increasing contact
and pore-filling cementation, overall porosity decreases (Fabricius 2007) [37].
Permeability describes the ability a porous medium has to allow fluid conduc-
tion. Porosity and permeability are related but not entirely dependant on each other.
Instead, permeability is mostly correlated with the effective porosity, meaning that
the pores’ cross-sectional area (pore throats) and the specific surface of the pore
throats are the determining factors of permeability. Naturally, as porosity reduces
with depth, permeability will follow the same trend, although there are cases in which
the two trends will deviate. Fabricius (2007) [37], investigated specimens of varying
carbonate content from < 50% −100% from the North Sea, and showed that, for ef-
fective stress up to 10MPa, while the porosity was steadily decreasing, permeability
hardly decreased, and even showed a tendency to increase. This trend was attributed
to the decrease of the specific area due to suitable stress conditions, which allow
re-crystallization of calcite.
Specific surface, which describes the surface of the pores and the capillaries con-
necting them, as mentioned, is closely related to porosity, which describes pore vol-
ume and combined they best describe pore radius. Since specific surface is measured
on dry material via nitrogen adsorption (see section 3.1.2), a reproducible value for
dried chalk can be obtained (Clausen & Fabricius 2000) [40].
Specific surface is
dependant on the particle size distribution. As mechanical compaction progresses,
re-crystallization of calcite occurs depending not only on the overburden stress but
also on time and temperature (Baker et al. 1982) [41], (Borre & Fabricius 1998) [42].
With increasing depth, calcite re-crystallizes due to the increase in stress, and spe-
cific surface drops. However, non-calcite surfaces affect the total value as well and
should be taken into account. Before diagenesis progresses and re-crystallization of
the chalk advances, the specific surface’s determining component is calcite. On the
contrary, as diagenesis and re-crystallization progress, the determining component
become fine-grained silica and clay content.
Diagenetic components in chalk can be a consequence of bacterial action when the
calcareous sediments are rich in organic content, or otherwise, chemical equilibrium
of pore water and minerals upon burial or a combination of both processes (D’Hondt
et al. 2003) [43]. Diagenesis is a crucial process in chalk that should be taken into
account for appropriate identification of chalk properties, but further elaboration in
the diagenetic mechanisms is beyond this project’s scope.

1.1.5
Stevns Klint geology
This section provides a general overview of the area where the material used for this
project was obtained.
Stevns Klint is of exceptional geological interest, and in 2014 was inscribed by
UNESCO on the World Heritage list since it provides visible traces of the meteor
impact, which occurred 66 million years ago and lead to the extinction of the majority
of flora and fauna on Earth, including the dinosaurs.
The 14.5km long cliff is an exceptional example for studying the succession across
the Cretaceous-Tertiary boundary, from the upper Maastrichtian to the lower Danian
formation, with relatively uniform stratigraphical development along the cliff.
The lower part of the succession consists of about 25m upper Maastrichtian chalk
of the new Sigerslev Member. The benthos-rich mound-bedded chalk gradually shifts
upwards, from gently wavy-bedded, to horizontally bedded benthos poor chalk. The
member is overlain by a double hardground layer which encloses a marker band of
nodular flint and can be traced alongside the cliff.
On top of the double hardground layers is the Højerup member, consisting of
bryozoan-rich chalk, deposited in asymmetrical mounds. The bryozoan chalk wacke-
stone is 4 −5m thick at the south part of the cliff and gradually gets thinner to the
north.
The top of this member coincides with the Cretaceous-Tertiary boundary, marked
by the base of the overlying lowermost Danian Fiskeler Member, which is the reason
for Stevns Klint’s uniqueness distinctly marking the meteor impact incident.
The 5−10cm thick Fiskeler Member passes gradually upwards into the Cerithium
Limestone Member, which is up to 60cm thick. The two members are grouped into
the new Rødvig Formation, which was deposited in shallow basins between the crests
of the underlying mounds of the Højerup Member.
The Cerithium Limestone and the mound crests of the intervening Højerup mem-
ber are truncated by an erosional hardground, forming the basis for the overlying
impressive bryozoan limestone mounds of the 20m thick packstone-rudstone Danian
Korsnæb Member.
Although a more detailed description of Stevns Klint geological conditions falls
outside the scope of this project, it is important to note that the area experienced
uplift close to 600m by combined Late Cretaceous and Cenozoic basin inversion and
regional Neogene uplift (Liboriussen et al. 1987 [44], Japsen et al. 2002 [45], Vejbæk
& Andersen 2002 [46]), while subsequently, the glacier movement eroded the Ceno-
zoic deposits, thus exposing the top chalk group which was originally buried beneath
them. The reader can find more information for Stevns Klint in the excellent paper
published by Surlyk, Damholt, and Bjerager in 2006: Stevns Klint, Denmark: Upper-
most Maastrichtian chalk, Cretaceous-Tertiary boundary, and lower Danian bryozoan
mound complex. [47].

1.1.6
Engineering significance of chalk
Chalk is of broad engineering interest. It is used as a primary component of cement,
an additive in soils in order to control pH and lower the acidity, filler in paper and
plastic manufacturing, and even as building material when its properties are ade-
quate. Moreover, construction of infrastructure on chalk formations has been widely
performed for the foundations of onshore and offshore structures. Lastly, due to its
typically high porosity, chalk hydrocarbon reservoirs are of high economic interest.
Regarding the scope of this project, it defines the starting point and largely the state
of the remoulded material.

1.2.1
Remolded chalk description
There are three different terms in the literature, describing the product of granulated,
mixed with water, chalk.
All of the terms describe a state of chalk, which fulfill
the main criteria: loss of cement bonding between the grains of the parent
material. The different terms used are presented below as stated by Lord (et al.
1993) [29] :
• Reconstituted chalk: as a material completely reworked, to which water is
added, forming a slurry, before being compressed.
==PAGE:29==
1.2 Remolded chalk
19
• Chalk slurry: as completely reworked chalk with a moisture content exceeding
its liquid limit.
• Remolded chalk: as reworked chalk in its natural moisture content, without
any addition or loss of the moisture.
Regarding this project, the term remolded chalk is used to describe the material
in hand since the remolding process was performed at the natural moisture content
of the intact chalk as presented in section 3.2.2.
1.2.2
Remolded chalk occurrence
Intact chalk strength is derived from three main attributes, inter-granular molecular
bonding, inter-granular friction, and grain cementation. When sufficient stress is ex-
erted, chalk disintegrates in finer segments, and the existing cementation is lost. Lake
(1975) [48] states that the natural moisture content of chalk generally lies between
the plastic and the liquid limit, as indicated by the Atterberg Limit test [49], thus if
remolded, it will reduce to a viscous, plastic state.
Several processes, both natural occurring & human-induced, account for the dis-
mantling of chalk’s internal structure and the production of remolded chalk. In the
natural processes, freezing, weathering, and tectonic movement are the main factors,
whereas, in human-induced activities, civil engineering projects such as tunneling, cut
& fill earthworks, and foundation installation via pile driving are the most common
processes to generate the remolded material. A brief overview of how the aforemen-
tioned processes generate remolded chalk is presented below.
• Freezing.
In sub-zero temperatures and permafrost conditions, water flowing freely through
cracks and fissures in chalk freezes, while pore water remains liquid due to the
immense pressure applied to it from the rock frame, causing it to have a lower
freezing point. Capillary forces will eventually push the pore water to the ice
veins, thus allowing the ice volume growth. This enlargement of the ice can
cause visible disturbances in chalk, such as expansion of the volume (Lewis &
Croney 1996) [50], shattering, and disaggregation. With a plethora of freez-
ing/melting circles, intact chalk will reduce to lumps floating in a finer-grained
matrix (Lake 1975) [48].
• Weathering.
As with all naturally occurring deposits, chalk is subject to weathering, erosion,
transportation, and redeposition. Especially the shallow layers are more prone
to weathering since fracturing can be very severe due to frost and flora roots.
Due to the movement of their massive body, glaciers heavily impact the top
bed of the chalk layer, scrapping off material as they move. The majority of
naturally occurring chalk soils can be correlated to the glacial and periglacial
==PAGE:30==
20
1 Chalk, the material
environment of the Pleistocene period (2.580.000 to 11.700 years ago) (Lake
1975) [48].
• Tectonic movement.
Fabric changes can be induced in chalk from the movement of the tectonic plates.
Intact chalk is mainly found as severely jointed in situ (Lake 1990) [51], a state
attributed to weathering and tectonic processes such as faulting, folding, and
internal shearing (Mortimore et al. 1990) [17],(Mortimore & Pomerol 1997) [52].
It is at these discontinuities that remolded chalk is found, as Mortimore showed
for the case of Seaford Chalk where veins of grey chalky material were observed
(Mortimore 1979) [53].
• Pile driving.
Pile driving in chalk is generally known to generate a zone of remolded material
in the annulus of the pile (Lord et al.
2002) [2], (Hobbs et al.
2002) [54],
(Clayton et al. 1993) [1], affecting the pile bearing capacity with time, due to
the change in the properties of the material (Buckley et al. 2018) [10].
When conceptualizing the driven pile in chalk, the most simplistic conception is
to consider a cavity expansion which is followed by consolidation (Carter et al.
1980) [55], thus increasing the pile’s shaft capacity with time (Axelsson 2000)
[56], (Vijayvergiya et al. 1977) [3], due to pore water dissipation (Bhattacharya
et al. 2009) [57], remolded chalk-pile bonding (Lord et al. 2002) [2], structural
rearrangement and recementation.
Proof of this remolded zone can be seen both in situ and in laboratory conditions.
Muir Wood (et al. 2015) [5] describes the formation of an annular zone following
the driving of a 762mm pile in chalk, which spanned 80 −160% of the pile’s
diameter, and Alvarez-Borges (et al. 2018) [59], showed with X-ray computed
tomography, an apparent increase in the density of chalk, around the driven
pile replica, as seen in figure 1.8 (on the right).
After installing the pile, all semblance of the intact chalk is lost, whereas chalk
grains are most likely in suspension in the pre-existing saturating fluid, resulting
in extremely low radial effective stresses. This process causes stability problems
to foundation design since the pile’s shaft capacity may be significantly reduced.

capacity of the pile and the shaft (or skin) friction.

1.2.3
Remolded chalk classification
Remolded chalk is classified using the classification schemes presented in section 1.1.3.
The Mundford system classifies remolded chalk as Grade V material: structureless
remoulded chalk containing lumps of intact chalk, while the CIRIA system classifies it
as Grade D material, which is subdivided into grades Dm and Dc. Dm grade describes
a fine soil where the matrix dominates (chalk matrix > 35% and coarser fragments <
65%), whereas Dc grade describes a coarse soil dominated by clasts (chalk matrix <
35% and coarser fragments > 65%).
Razoaki (2000) [62] highlights the importance of distinguishing materials which
contain fine fragments > 15% in Dc graded chalks, since it is considered to be the
critical percentage of fines controlling the behaviour of the whole material (Puig
1973) [63], (Rat & Schaeffner 1990) [64].
1.2.4
Physical & mechanical properties of remolded chalk
As it can be intuitively understood, the physical & mechanical properties of remolded
chalk heavily depend on the parent material properties. Moreover their range, is a
consequence of different stress history and diagenetic processes preceded in the intact
formation (Clayton 1983) [16], (Clayton 1990) [65],(Clayton et al. 2003) [13].
The composition & mineralogy of remolded chalk mostly depend on the intact
chalk characteristics. Particularly for low-medium density chalks, which are mainly
composed of coccoliths and coccolith fragments, and the presence of stronger authi-
genic calcite grains is scarce (Clayton 1983) [16], (Clayton et al. 2003) [13] , grain
size, shape, and mineralogy almost exclusively depend on the intact chalk characteris-
tics. Instead, for higher density chalks where the grain cementation is more extended,
the crushing process can heavily impact the particle size distribution & density of
the produced material. This is because the more cemented the grains are, the more
likely grain breakage is to occur than grain un-bonding during the crushing process
(Katsaros 2008) [66]. As it can be comprehended, porosity will also follow the same
trend since it is closely related to density.

The state of intact chalk is not the only factor governing the properties of remolded
chalk. The saturating pore fluid & the interaction with the chalk grains also affect
the post remolded state of the material since varying saturation degrees will generate
different compositions of remolded material.
Lord (et al.
2002) [2] provides the
plasticity index range at 4 −30% and the liquid limit range at 18 −53% for different
chalk specimens in the UK. With such range span, it is safe to assume that the natural
saturation degree can have quite an impact, especially when taking into account that
remolded chalk can swiftly change from a plastic to a liquid state with minimal
addition of water (Bundy 2013) [67].
Moreover, any mechanical processes taking place (i.e., consolidation from overbur-
den &/or radial stresses), temperature fluctuations & chemical processes occurring
can impact the state of the remolded chalk as well. Lastly, time & the conditions in
which ageing takes place (drained, undrained) are important since there is evidence
that remolded chalk properties differ as time progresses under different conditions. A
more elaborate presentation of laboratory and in-situ studies on remolded chalk prop-
erties follows in the next chapter, where the current knowledge of this phenomenon
is presented.
1.2.5
Engineering significance of remolded chalk
Remolded chalk is a factor of uncertainty for a series of foundation and infrastructure
designs due to the material’s unpredictable mechanical behavior. This unpredictable
performance can be troublesome for various engineering projects, affecting the stabil-
ity of foundations (Clayton 1978) [68], (Razoaki 2000) [62], resulting in unforeseen
formation strain in hydrocarbon reservoirs (Addis 1987) [69], (Leddra 1989) [70], pad
footings (Burland et al. 1983) [4], (Matthews 1993) [71], and tunnelling works (Millar
2000) [72]. Regarding the scope of this project, it should be noted that similar work
has not been performed for Danish chalk formations, and this project can serve as a
basis for future elaboration on the topic.

Literature review
In this section, the current knowledge obtained from past studies regarding the
strength redevelopment of remolded chalk with time is presented. Laboratory studies
conducted on remolded chalk can help understand previous authors and researchers
focus areas and what factors they concluded affect chalk strength redevelopment.
Notwithstanding the limited laboratory studies performed, the most relevant conclu-
sions of available literature are highlighted. In addition, field investigations in chalk
formations can shed light on the behaviour of remolded chalk in-situ. Due to the
plethora of field studies regarding pile driving in chalk, and the shortage of studies
in other occurrences of remolded chalk, the focus, is on the changes taking place in
the remolded chalk annulus generated by pile driving.
2.1
Laboratory studies
The effect of remolded chalk restrengthening with time has been discussed at least
from 1977 when Clayton [7] argued that the main strength/time dependency for the
material is the increase in effective cohesion with excess pore water dissipation when
there is no change in the load applied on the material.
Clayton (1990) [65] performed a series of triaxial tests under drained conditions,
for various remolded chalk specimens, in different time stages post preparation, while
the moisture loss was reported to be minimal. The material had moisture content of
25%, CaCO3 content of 98.1%, liquid limit of 26% and plastic limit of 21%. Clayton
assumed a constant effective angle of friction of 35.8◦, during the testing period,
but it should be noted that this assumption should be questioned since the effective
forces between the individual grains tend to change with time due to changes in the
pore pressure, the fabric, and the re-cementation degree (Bundy 2013) [67]. Clayton
observed a three-fold gain in effective cohesion around the 100th day from 3kPa to
10kPa (figure 2.1 on the left), and Lord (et al. 2002) [2] attributed that gain to the
re-cementation of chalk grains due to change of authigenic aragonite to calcite.
In addition, Clayton (1977) [7] studied the behaviour of remolded chalk with de-
creasing moisture content in order to test the hypothesis that pore water dissipation is
associated with the strength redevelopment of the material. To this end, he conducted
a series of undrained triaxial tests at varying cell pressures from 50kPa−200kPa and
varying moisture content from 0 −30%, in chalk specimens of the Upper Cretaceous

Clayton observed a consistent trend, where the undrained shear strength of the spec-

the liquid limit, at which point the interparticle voids become saturated, and all
cohesion of the material is lost (figure 2.1 on the right).

2.2
In-situ investigations
When piles are driven in chalk, the calcium carbonate particles are crushed beneath
the advancing pile tip, and a low-strength remolded material is produced, which
spreads along the pile and limits drastically the effective radial stresses and shear
resistances that can act on the shaft (Hobbs & Atkinson 1993) [54], (Lord et al.
2002) [2].
That can be problematic to the stability of the pile since lower radial
effective stresses acting on the shaft of the pile could lead to less friction between the
shaft of the pile and the surrounding formation, ultimately reducing shaft capacity,
making the pile more prone to fail under the applied load or dislocate from the
indicated position.
There have been conducted numerous in-situ studies on piles driven in chalk,
which have shown an increase in the shaft capacity with time (e.g. Ciavaglia (et al.
2017) [9], Buckley (et al. 2018) [10], Jardine (et al. 2018) [75], Nowak & Thijssen
(2018) [11], to mention a few), which are presented in figure 2.3 by Alvarez-Borges
(2019) [76], whereas the observed increase c an be attributed to:
1. Elastic rebound of the surrounding chalk formation, due to the tendency to
reach equilibrium again, after being disturbed from the driving of the pile, thus
pushing the remolded chalk on the pile’s shaft and generating higher effective
stresses. Buckley (et al. 2018) [10] suggests that lateral stresses of structured
chalk require some time to act on the remolded annulus since they become
”locked-in” by an arching mechanism as orthoradial stresses through the struc-
tured chalk. Progressively, the observed increase in radial stress on the shaft
is due to creep relaxation, which allows for the transfer of the stresses to the
annulus and producing densification of the remolded chalk.

Piezoelectric elements produce ultrasonic waves when voltage is applied. The ultra-

receiving element, and the required time lapse is measured. The deformation caused

receiver element. Three piezoelectric elements are used, which account for three dif-

3.2.1
Specimen preparation
There is no standard specimen preparation method in laboratory studies, in order
to study the mechanical behaviour of remolded chalk, mostly due to an absence of
an agreed-upon method, the limited past experience with such material, and the
extensive range of properties of intact chalk from different locations.
Clayton (1978) [68] used the Tema mill to crush dry blocks to a gravel size, which
then sieved and utilized the material < 425µm, whereas, the same author (Clayton
1983) [16] used a motorized mortar & pestle to crush material to the same size,
but used a 2mm sieve for grading, while for a different batch of specimens, allowed
unrestricted grading.
Razoaki (2000) [62] crushed and air-dried tunneling remolded chalk products and
sieved with a 425µm mesh. Alvarez-Borges (2014) [102] utilized the proctor com-
pactor and mortar & pestle for crushing, subsequently pressing the material with a
==PAGE:62==
52
3 Materials and methods
knife against a glass plate, to reduce the moisture content and sieved with a 425µm
mesh. Bundy (2013) [67] followed the same process as Clayton (1978) for crushing
the material but utilized unrestricted grading.
Doughty (2016) [103] remarked that the aforementioned methods do not represent
the state of the material in-situ (e.g., in the remolded annulus of driven piles) and
suggested a specific number of blows with a proctor compactor under constant initial
moisture content while utilizing all the material produced.
Bialowas (2017) [104],
advised that in order to ensure repeatability of the tests, similar specimens need
to be produced, thus emphasized the importance of dry crushing. He used a disc
grinder & a jaw crusher to crush cobble-sized dry blocks of chalk without grading the
produced material.
Alvarez-Borges (2019) [76] used two methods to prepare the remolded specimens.
In the first method, he utilized a food grader to achieve particles < 100mm, and
afterwards dry sieved with a 425µm mesh, while the desired moisture content was
achieved by adding deionized water. In the second method, he used a hammer to
crush 100mm saturated chalk blocks to < 25mm diameter, which he later mixed
again with the gravel-sized blocks. Subsequently, the mix was crushed with a food
mincer while incrementally adding deionized water to form the slurry, ultimately wet
sieving the material through a 425µm mesh.
For the current project, the intact chalk blocks were initially hammered to a
diameter < 2cm, dried in the oven, and subsequently crushed using a mill and dry
sieved, using a 1mm mesh.
The crushed material was mixed with 31% moisture
content of chalk-infused tap water using an industrial mixer. The reason for selecting
such moisture content is described in the next section (section 3.2.2). The produced
mix was vacuumed for 15 minutes and subsequently sealed in the specimen mold
and submerged in an ultrasonic bath to enhance trapped air removal. The relevant
equipment used can be seen in figure 3.12.

3.2.7
Liquid limit
The liquid limit is defined as the moisture content where the soil behaviour shifts
from the viscous liquid state to the plastic state (Atterberg 1911) [109]. It provides
valuable information regarding the consistency state of soil, and it can be used to
predict the consolidation properties of soil while calculating allowable bearing capacity
& settlement of installations. In order to yield the liquid limit of remolded chalk,
the fall cone penetrometer method was deployed as presented in DS/EN ISO 17892-
12:2018 [49].
The process followed consists of 4 consecutive test points, corresponding to 4
different values of moisture content. The process started with a moisture content
of ≈35% and the sample was consecutively step-wise dried until ≈30% moisture
content. In each step, an average of 2 readings is taken to minimize the measurement’s
error range.
Ultimately the liquid limit is determined as the moisture content at
a penetration depth of 10mm of the 60g/60◦cone.
It should be noted that, in
order to get a precise measurement of the liquid limit, this procedure holds two
restrictions. The two consecutive measurements should differ no more than 0.4mm
while the acceptable range of the cone penetration should be 7 −15mm while using
the 60g/60◦cone.

Statistical tools & methods
This section briefly describes the statistical tools & methods utilized in the context
of this thesis in order to handle and present the data obtained from the experiments
conducted.
3.4.1
Regression analysis
Linear and polynomial regression trendlines were fitted to the various strength-time-
moisture content datasets. The regression algorithm is based on the least-squares
method meaning that the trendline is drawn throughout the data, following the se-
lected polynomial order, which ensures the minimum error (SSE) from the measured
data points (i.e., the minimum summation of the squared distances, between the
drawn trendline and the data points). Different polynomial regression orders were
fitted to explain the highest variance possible (highest coefficient of determination
R2) while simultaneously maintaining a curve reasonable for the phenomenon inves-
tigated (e.g., regression models showing negative values for strength or meaningless
fluctuations trends were rejected).
==PAGE:71==
3.4 Statistical tools & methods
61
3.4.2
Confidence intervals
Confidence interval is a range of plausible values for an unknown parameter (e.g.,
Undrained shear strength, Uniaxial compressive strength, etc.)
derived from the
observed data. The interval has an associated confidence level (e.g., 95%) that the true
parameter lies within, which is manually decided. The higher the level is, the wider
the range of values will be. Confidence intervals which can be seen in the relevant
plot as the translucent areas were used in this thesis due to the shortage of material
in hand, equipment limitations & human-induced errors during testing, whereas the
process of calculating them is described in Appendix B: Confidence intervals.
3.4.3
Boxplots
Boxplot is a statistical tool used to graphically depict data through the median and
the quartiles. In a sorted and ascending dataset, the median (Q2 or Q50%) is the value
where 50% of the data lies above, and 50% lies below, whereas the quartiles (Q1, Q3)
are statistical points, dividing the dataset in different ranges. Q1 (or Q25%) is defined
as the middle value between the minimum value and the median depicting the lower
25% of the dataset and Q3 (or Q75%) is defined as the middle value between the
maximum value and the median depicting the higher 25% of the dataset. Extending
from the box, whiskers are displayed, which indicate the variability of the data outside
from the upper and lower quartiles, whereas the spacings between the different parts
of the box show how dispersed and skewed the dataset is. The interquartile range
(IQR) is used in order to draw the whiskers, which help identify possible outliers,
and is calculated by simply subtracting Q1 from Q3:
IQR = Q3 −Q1
Then the fence of the lower (F1) and higher whisker (F2) are calculated as:
F1 = Q1 −1.5IQR
F2 = Q3 + 1.5IQR
Outliers can then be distinguished as individual points which fall outside of the
whisker fences, meaning that they significantly diverge from the rest of the values.
Although boxplots are widely used to identify outliers in datasets, care should be taken
when utilized due to the limitations the methods exhibit since it operates without
considering a statistical distribution, trends, or correlations inside the dataset. Thus,
it should be used merely to help visualize outliers, while ultimately, the user has to
decide, based on further input, whether the points proposed are outliers or not.

3.4.4
ANOVA
ANOVA (Analysis of variance) is a statistical tool used in order to find correlations
between two or more variables. For example, if the undrained shear strength (depen-
dant variable) is correlated to time or moisture content (independent variable). The
method functions, assuming that the variables are independent (Null Hypothesis or
H0) while a simple linear regression is run throughout the data via the least mean
squared error process, as discussed previously. The method then calculates with a
predefined significance level α (i.e., the probability of rejecting the H0 when it is
true, for example, a significance level of 0.1 indicates a 10% risk of falsely claiming
dependence between the variables when in fact they are independent), the correlation
between the variables, using:
• The coefficient of determination (R2), which shows how much of the variance
of the dependant variable is described by the independent variable.
• The variables coefficients from the fitted regression trendline (Y-intercept and
slope), which indicate the sign and magnitude of the relation of the two vari-
ables.
• The probability value (P −value), which shows how close to zero the slope
is (i.e., zero slope indicates complete independence between the two variables).
Depending on the P −value, H0 is either rejected or not. If the P −value is
less than 1 −α, then the two variables are not independent and H0 is rejected,
otherwise if the P −value is more than 1−α, then the two variables are indeed
independent and H0 cannot be rejected. The process of obtaining P −value is
described in Appendix C: P-value for ANOVA.

Taking into consideration the particle size of the remolded chalk, which was arti-
ficially created < 1mm, and the majority of the pore throats, which revolved around
1µm, it is safe to assume that the material was not crushed to single grain consistency.
The values of dry (1.45 −1.51g/cc) & bulk (1.88 −1.93g/cc) density indicate
low-medium density chalk, whereas the high porosity of 45% suggest low degree of
compaction, since the overburden layers of the top chalk formation at Stevns Klint,
==PAGE:96==
86

were eroded by glacier movement after experiencing uplift, thus limiting the time, the
formation was under considerable compaction.
The similar horizontal (3.95 −5.02mD) & vertical (3.71 −5.53mD) permeabil-
ity values indicate isotropic material, allowing restricted fluid flow inside the rock.
This can be further understood from the high specific surface area of the material
(2.64m2/g), which is a typical value for chalk (Fabricius et al. 2007) [110], but still
rather significant. The magnitude of the specific surface can be a driving factor for
the rate of pore pressure dissipation in remolded chalk, since the higher the area water
can cling on, the slower it will move due to surface tension between the liquid and
the grains.
The ultrasonic pulse velocity tests produced similar results for the elastic moduli
of dry & saturated specimens. P waves travel faster in denser mediums, thus the
higher bulk density should indicate higher velocities of propagation, although oppo-
site results were obtained. The lower V P though can be explained from the water
weakening effect. A phenomenon that has been studied thoroughly the last decades
from various aspects and several mechanisms are believed to be involved. From chemi-
cal dissolution (Madland et. al 2011) [111], and higher surface energy of dry calcite as
compared to water-saturated calcite (Røyne et al. 2011 [112], Voake et al. 2019 [113]),
to capillary effects (Schroeder et al. 1998) [114], and electrical repulsion of calcite
particles (Meireles et al. 2020) [115]. Most likely, the reactivation of fractures (Chris-
tensen et al. 2003) [116], is the key mechanism explaining lower V P, since water
interacts with the chalk grains at the pore level, reducing the inter-particle cohesion
and promoting the growth of pre-existing cracks and fractures, where the P wave is
refracted and decelerated. This phenomenon, which is also called stress corrosion,
occurs because the hydrogen bonds link with the mineral lattice on the crack tips,
ultimately weakening the lattice (Atkinson 1982) [117]. Since liquids cannot transfer
shear impulses, S waves cannot propagate through water. For this reason, increasing
pore moisture content decreases V S. The reduction observed in V S1&V S2 though,
was compensated in the moduli values from the increase in the bulk density due to
the saturation of the specimens.
The UCS & UU tests performed on saturated intact specimens produced values
for Cu of 1.92 −4.22MPa, and Su of 2.05 −2.86MPa. The two dominant failure
mechanisms observed to be, α) clear shear failure plane, β) combined failure mech-
anism of multiple shear planes suggest typical behaviour of low-medium density &
high porosity chalk, under increasing axial stress.
Finally, the liquid limit of 32.4%, being in the range of the theoretical moisture
content (28.30−32.46%), allows for safely assuming that suitable conditions exist for
a viscous remolded material to be formed once the intact material is crushed.

5.3 Limitations of the study
89
5.3
Limitations of the study
The increase in strength observed from the fall cone & tor vane specimens is largely
explained by thixotropic hardening (following Bundy 2013 [67]), as a strength gain
over time, due to moisture content loss, over constant volume. Atkinson (1993) [118],
Clayton (1977) [7] and Razoaki (2000) [62] argued that the principal processes of
soil strength development in-situ, are compaction, creep, cementation and pore wa-
ter dissipation. If assumed that water evaporation can be correlated to pore water
dissipation in-situ (e.g., in the remolded annulus of a driven pile), such that drying
can be equal to a pressure difference inside the annulus, then the increase in strength
observed in this study could be correlated to excess water draining, which causes re-
strengthening of the material. However, it is important to highlight that even though
thixotropic hardening could partially explain the strength redevelopment, is nonethe-
less rather limited in precisely resembling in-situ conditions since air drying is highly
unlikely to occur in the remolded annulus. Moreover, changes in specific surface &
permeability were not monitored, thus consistency changes of the material could not
be identified. The particle size distribution used was artificially created to < 1mm
when in reality, lumps of chalk would be floating in chalk grains. Although Razoaki
(2000) [62] and Clayton (1977) [7] considered re-recemention to be possible, Lord (et
al. 2002) [2] argued that chalk is unlikely to recement in fully saturated conditions,
while the statement itself, that remolded chalk will significantly re-cement in a span of
a few months is rather contradictional, given the millions of years required to produce
the intact rock. In order to properly assess the ageing process in-situ, effective stress
regime changes need to be accounted, while possible redox phenomena as mentioned
by Buckley (et al. 2018) [10], Jardine (et al. 2018) [75], and Lehane (et al. 2012) [78]
occurring between the pile and the remolded annulus, can have an impact as well.
Lastly, the results produced concern solely the chalk acquired from Sigerslev quarry,
and in order to verify and extend them, material from more sites must be tested.
5.4
Future work
This project was, to the author’s knowledge, the first attempt to provide a laboratory
framework in order to study strength redevelopment of remolded chalk from a Danish
site, thus it can hopefully serve as a basis for future work, for a deeper understanding
of the material’s behaviour under varying conditions. In that sense and considering
the results and the limitations of this project, future studies could investigate:
• The effect mechanical compaction & creep have on strength development, which
could be performed via oedometer tests under varying load.
• The effect stress regime acting on the remolded material has, which could be
achieved by advanced triaxial compression tests with varying confining stresses.
==PAGE:100==
90
5 Discussion
• Changes in the consistency, through real-time monitoring of the remolded ma-
terial’s density and saturation degree, via a continuous ultrasonic pulse velocity
test.
• A small-scale replication in laboratory conditions, of in-situ pile driving. The
process could be monitored via computed tomography scanner on a replica of
a pile driven in a chalk block, and changes occurring in the remolded annulus
could potentially be identified in real-time.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
The scope of this thesis was to investigate which mechanisms govern the strength
development of remolded chalk over time. The importance of moisture content as
an indicator of strength development and limitations occurring from the soft nature
of the material with specific test methods are highlighted. An extensive laboratory
program was run on chalk specimens from the Sigerslev quarry in Stevns Klint which
consisted of two stages.
First, intact specimens were tested, and the properties, pore throat distribution,
specific surface, porosity, permeability, dynamic elastic moduli, uniaxial compres-
sive strength, undrained shear strength, and liquid limit were obtained to provide a
clear understanding of the parent material. Subsequently, the intact material was dry
crushed to particle size < 1mm and mixed with chalk-infused tap water at the theoret-
ical moisture content (31%). Two remolded specimen batches created in an identical
manner were stored in different temperature conditions, at 25◦C where moisture con-
tent was allowed to evaporate, and 7◦C where moisture content remained constant,
for an ageing period of up to two months. Uniaxial compressive strength was ob-
tained from standard uniaxial compression test, while undrained shear strength was
obtained from fall cone penetrometer, tor vane, and undrained unconsolidated triaxial
test in various time stages.
Specimens stored at 25◦C and tested via fall cone penetrometer and tor vane
exhibited a significant increase in strength due to severe moisture content loss, rang-
ing from 10kPa on preparation day to values up to 1800kPa after 61 days, for 13%
moisture content loss, and 1600kPa after 62 days for 7% moisture evaporation, re-
spectively. Contrariwise specimens stored at 7◦C, and tested in the same manner,
exhibited constant strength values from 5kPa−10kPa, throughout the ageing period,
under constant moisture content. Specimens stored at 25◦C and tested via uniaxial
compressive load exhibited a slight increase in strength, from 10kPa on preparation
day to 25kPa 62 days later, due to limited moisture content loss, of 4%, while speci-
mens stored at 7◦C and tested in the same manner, showed constant strength values
of 7 −14kPa under constant moisture content. Finally, specimens stored at 25◦C
and tested via undrained unconsolidated triaxial compression exhibited fluctuations
in strength from 12 −25kPa with time, while minimal moisture content loss was
recorded.

Appendix C: P-value
for ANOVA
Calculating the P-value for simple linear regression.
1. Define a simple linear regression function
The least squares method is used using two variables. For example:
y = β0x + β1
where, x is the independent and y is the dependent variable (i.e. how x affects
y).
2. Define the Null hypothesis (H0)
H0 states that the two variables are independent, (i.e. x does not affect y). In
order to determine if that is statistically true or not, (i.e. assess the confidence
in rejecting H0 or not), one statistical tool which is widely used is the P −value
(probability value), which is an indicator of evidence against a Null hypothesis.
Generally, the smaller the P −value is, the more reason there is to reject H0.
3. Determine the t −statistic
P −value in simple linear regression is determined using the t −statistic from
t-distribution, with n −2 degrees of freedom (tn−2), where n is the number of
observation in the dataset and 2 is the amount of variables in hand. Graphically
it is represented as the area past the t −statistic, under a t-distribution curve
(figure 1).
==PAGE:126==
116
Appendix Appendix C: P-value for ANOVA
Figure 1. An example of a t-distribution curve, depicting with red the area where P −value
falls under.
In order to find the t −statistic, two parameters are needed. The coefficient
(β) and the standard error (se) of the investigated variable, as obtained by the
linear regression function. The t −statistic, can then be simply calculated as:
t −statistic = β
se
where, se is calculated as shown in Appendix B: Confidence intervals, and β is
obtained by the regression function for each variable.
4. Determine the significance level (α)
In order to argue, if there is enough confidence to reject H0 or not, a user
inserted significance threshold (α) is used. The higher the precision needed, the
smaller α will be. Generally α = 0.05 is used, where it corresponds to 95%
significance in the statistical study conducted.
5. Obtain the P −value
When α and t −statistic are known, then the P −value can be determined, by
the corresponding t-distribution tables as defined by Gosset (1908) [119].
6. Decide to reject H0 or not
In order to decide if H0 will be rejected or not, P −value is compared to α.
• If the P −value < 1 - α there is not enough statistical evidence to argue
that the two variables are independent, thus H0 is rejected.
• If the P −value >1 - α, there is enough statistical evidence to argue that
the two variables are indeed independent, thus H0 cannot be rejected.
