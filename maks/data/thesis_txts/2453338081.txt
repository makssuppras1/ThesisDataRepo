Abstract  
 
Interleukin(IL)-17-producing RORγt+ γδ T cells (γδT17) are one of the main subsets involved in type 3 
responses in peripheral tissues. In this project, we aimed to study two different features regarding 
γδT17 function. First, we analyzed inhibitory mechanisms that regulate their function. Second, we 
characterized the recently discovered T-bet+ γδT17 cells in the gut at steady state. 
Besides having a protective role, exaggerated IL-17 production by γδT17 cells is involved in 
autoimmune models such as psoriasis. Immune checkpoint receptors (ICRs) are one of the 
mechanisms that provide cellular inactivation after inflammation. However, which inhibitory 
receptors are expressed in γδT17 cells and how this receptors signal is still unclear. Moreover, in vitro 
assays implicated SHP-1 and SHP-2 as transducers of inhibitory receptors signal. To address this, we 
investigated inhibitory receptor expression in vitro and during IMQ induced psoriasis mice model. 
Furthermore, we used specific SHP-1 and SHP-2 deficient mice in RORγt+ cells to asses inhibitory 
signaling during inflammation.  Herein, we show that γδT17 cells express PD-1, BTLA, CTLA-4 and 2B4 
upon cytokine stimulation and that PD-1 seems the most abundant receptor during inflammation in 
mice treated with IMQ. Moreover, we show that SHP-1 and SHP-2 seem dispensable for γδΤ17 cells 
development and function. These results reveal new ICRs expressed in γδT17 cells and that their 
downstream signaling is SHP-1 and SHP-2 independent. 
On the other hand, newly described intestinal T-bet+ γδT17 cells from small intestine and colon lamina 
propia indicate a tissue specific maturation of γδT17 cells. However, the differences between T-bet+ 
γδ T cells and T-bet+ RORγt+ γδ T cells in the gut still remain unknown. To asses this, RNA bulk 
sequencing was performed. Collectively, our results suggests that T-bet+ RORγt+ γδ T cells seem to 
differ from T-bet+ γδ T cells and that T-bet+ RORγt+  γδ T cells are very similar regardless their intestine 
location.

Introduction  
 
The immune system can be largely divided into innate and adaptive. The innate immune system 
recognizes conserved patterns that are found in pathogens and responds rapidly. On the other hand, 
adaptive immunity is slower but highly antigen specific. Moreover, adaptive cells have the ability to 
develop memory, providing long-lasting immunity 1.  
Four different immune responses have been characterized according to the cytokine profiles that both 
innate and adaptive cells produce: type 1-4 2. Moreover, specific master transcription factors provide 
molecular regulation between different types of immunities 2. Type 1 immunity is directed against 
intracellular bacteria, viruses and tumors. Type 2 is activated in response to large extracellular threats 
such as helminths. Furthermore, type 3 response is triggered by extracellular bacteria and fungi, 
whereas type 4 focuses on pathogens before they reach the tissue. All these 4 types of immunities are 
mutually inhibitory in order to provide an immune equilibrium 3.  
Type 1 immunity is initiated by the secretion of interleukin(IL)-12 by dendritic cells (DCs) and 
macrophages at the site of inflammation. IL-12 induces interferon(IFN)γ secretion in T-helper(Th)1, 
innate lymphoid cells group 1 (ILC1) and IFNγ-producing innate-like γδ T cells. Furthermore, all type 1 
cells are characterized by the expression of the transcription factor T-bet. Type 2 immunity starts with 
the production of IL-25, IL-33 and thymic stromal lymphopotein (TSLP) by non-hematopoietic cells. 
These cytokines induce the secretion of IL-4, IL-5 and IL-13 by Th2 and ILC2, both of which express the 
transcription factor GATA3. Type 3 immunity is initiated by the secretion of IL-1β and IL-23 by DCs and 
macrophages. IL-1β and ΙL-23 enhance the production of IL-17 and IL-22 by Th17, ILC3 and IL-17- 
producing γδT cells (γδT17). IL-17 recruits neutrophils in the mucosa and promotes inflammation 4. 
On the other hand, IL-22 helps in to maintain the epithelial barrier 5. All type 3 cells are characterized 
by the expression of the transcription factor Retinoic-related orphan γt (RORγt). Finally, type 4 
immunity is responsible for microorganism exclusion in mucosal surfaces. This process is mainly driven 
by IgA secretion in tissues such as the intestine or the eye 3.  
There is a resemblance between innate and adaptive cells effector function seen in some subsets such 
as Th1-ILC1 and Th2-ILC2 and Th17-ILC3 2. ILCs have the ability to rapidly secrete cytokines and 
simulate T-helper functions 6. Moreover, γδ T cells are also considered T-helper inducers. Besides 
enhancing adaptive responses, γδ T cells share characteristics from both innate and adaptive cells 7. 
For instance, γδ T cells have the ability to recognize antigens through their T-cell receptor (TCR), as 
well as to recognize patterns through Toll-like receptors (TLR) 8. After pathogen recognition, γδ T cells 
rapidly secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines in surface barriers. Furthermore, it has also been shown

that γδ Τ cells are involved in some adaptive mechanisms. For example, γδ T cell memory-like 
responses have been described 9 such as IL-17-producing γδ Τ memory in psoriasis-like skin 
inflammation 10. Therefore, γδ T cells are critical in early stages of inflammation but also have the 
ability to develop long-lasting immunity. Hence, γδ T cells are an unique subset entailing both innate 
and adaptive features. There are several γδ T cell subsets classified regarding anatomical location in 
mice.  
 
Murine γδ T cell subsets 
Animal models, especially murine models, have been indispensable tools to define and classify the 
different γδ T cell subsets and to identify key features of their development (Table 1). Furthermore, 
murine models have provided a first guidance to define γδ T cell subsets in humans, which some 
correlate with the different murine subsets 11. 
Murine γδ T cells correspond to a small proportion of the circulating T cells (2-10%) 12. However, γδ T 
cells are strongly present in peripheral tissues such as skin, gut mucosa, lung, vagina, uterus, gingival 
and cornea 13. This unique location, together with their innate kinetics, makes them one of the first 
lines of defense against pathogens, especially at barrier surfaces such as the skin and intestine. 
Murine γδ T cells are classified in 4 subsets: dendritic epidermal γδ T cells (DETCs), γδ intraepithelial 
lymphocytes (IELs), innate IL-17 producing γδ T cells (γδT17) and innate IFNγ-producing γδ T cells 
(Table 1).

DETCs 
In murine epidermis, most of T cells correspond to the dendritic epidermal γδ T cell (DETC) subset, 
which is characterized by the surface expression of Vγ5 (Vγ nomenclature according to Heilig & 
Tonegawa 14), CD44 and CD122 and a lack of CD25 16. DETCs are a highly specialized subset that creates 
a cellular network within the tissue thanks to its dendritic shape. These cells recognize stress and 
damage signals from skin keratinocytes and secrete cytokines, such as IFNγ and IL-2 to recruit other 
leukocytes in the local tissue 17. Active DETCs also secrete specific growth factors such insulin like 
growth factor 1 (IGF1) that induce cellular proliferation and have wound healing properties 18,19. γδ T 
cells expressing Vγ5 chain are the first ones to develop within the murine thymus, between embryonic 
day 13 (E13) and E18. Then, these cells migrate from the thymus to the epidermis, where they remain 
in the tissue as self-renewal cells 19. 
γδ IELs 
 
IELs are located between epithelial cells of the gastrointestinal tract and around 50-60% of these cells 
express γδ TCR 20. γδ IELs are identified as Vγ7+, CD27+, CCR9+ and in the small intestine are mostly 
CD8αα+ 15. These cells have a critical role in epithelial homeostasis due to the expression of KGF-1, 
which enhances cell proliferation and regeneration, especially in the intestinal crypt 19. Furthermore, 
recently it has been shown that γδ IELs location within the single-cell layer depends on microbiota 
stimulation maintaining the gastrointestinal barrier 21. Mice without γδ T cells have an increased 
intestinal permeability that results in a higher susceptibility to develop spontaneous colitis 19.  
γδ IELs develop in the embryonic thymus and egress towards the gut 3 weeks after birth 22. Once in 
the intestine, these cells undergo an extrathymic maturation process driven by intestinal enterocytes. 
Differentiated enterocytes in the gut express butyrophilin-like 1 (Btnl1), which shapes and regulate 
local murine Vγ7+ IELs, completing their maturation and expansion process 22.  
Innate IFNγ γδ T cells 
 
Based on their cytokine secretion, γδ T cells are divided in two innate-like subsets: IFNγ-producing 
cells and IL-17-producing cells. 
IFNγ-producing γδ T cells are one of the main innate subsets in type 1 responses. These cells express 
T-bet, CD27 and around 80% of them are NK1.1+ 23. There is still a lot to understand regarding the role 
of this subset in certain processes, however, it has been found that IFNγ-producing γδ T cells are 
important in the immunogenicity of liver-derived cerebral malaria 24 and blood based malaria 
infections 25. IFNγ-producing γδ T cells develop in the thymus, expand around E15 and E18 23 and 
require a strong TCR signal during their development 15.

Innate γδT17 cells 
 
 γδT17 cells are one of the main lymphocytes involved in type 3 innate responses in mice. They are 
located in the dermis, secondary lymphoid tissues and mucosal surfaces such as the intestine and 
lungs. They have the ability to be rapidly activated upon cytokine recognition making them critical in 
early stages of bacterial infections 26.  IL-1β and IL-23 activate γδT17 cells without TCR engagement 
and induce IL-17 and IL-22 secretion shortly after pathogen recognition 27. 
Given their peripheral localization and their innate-like kinetics, γδT17 cells are the principal source of 
IL-17 in early innate immune responses against bacterial and fungal infections 28,29. IL-17 is a pro-
inflammatory cytokine, which induces the expression of chemokines and enhances neutrophil 
recruitment in the peripheral tissues 4. Moreover, IL-17 is also important for the maintenance of the 
intestinal homeostasis, preventing inflammatory bowel diseases 30.   
Similar to other type 3 immune cells, γδT17 are characterized by the expression of RORγt, while 
phenotypically they can be identified as CD44+ and CD27− in the LNs and CD45+, CD3LOW, Vγ5− and 
CCR6+ in the skin 23,31. Furthermore, they can express either Vγ4 or Vγ6 32. It is known that these two 
subsets are activated upon different stimuli. For instance, skin Vγ4+ subset is dominant in response to 
Corynebacterium, whereas Vγ4- subset governs the response to Straphiloccocus 33.  
γδΤ17 cells develop in the embryonic thymus where they acquire their effector function 15. Although 
TCR signaling seems not important for IL-17-producing linage specification in the thymus 34, it is critical 
for γδT17 cells development 35. Moreover, it has been shown that γδT17 only require a weak TCR 
signal to be fully developed 36,37. γδT17 cells leave the thymus around E18 and during neonatal life 38,39 
to populate lymphoid tissues, lung, gut, dermis, kidney and reproductive tract 40. In previous studies, 
it was found that many RORγt+ γδ T cells present in the gut also co-express T-bet at steady state 39. 
This study shows that there is an intestinal γδ T cell population that is able to secrete IL-17A, IL-22 and 
IFNγ. These findings indicate a post-thymus plasticity between IL-17 and IFNγ- producing γδ T cells 39. 
 
Importance of γδT17 cells in disease 
 
Although the primary role of γδT17 cells is to protect from bacterial and fungal infections, they have 
been shown to be highly pathogenic in a number of inflammatory and autoimmune diseases 26,41,42. 
Mice deficient in IL-17 show reduced severity in collagen-induced arthritis model (CIA) 42, experimental 
autoimmune encephalomyelitis (EAE) 43 and imiquimod (IMQ) induced skin inflammation 41. 
Furthermore, γδT17 cells are required for the development of EAE and IMQ induced psoriasis 26,44. 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
Although a γδT17 cells subset in humans has not been defined yet, there are studies that relate IL-17-
producing γδ T cells to some diseases, such as psoriatic inflammation 45 and cancer 46. 
The CIA model is very similar to rheumatoid arthritis (RA), which leads to the destruction of cartilage 
and bone in the bone joints. The CIA model shows that early sources of IL-17 come from γδT17 cells, 
allowing Th17 differentiation and maintenance in later stages of the disease 42. Furthermore, it was 
shown that Il-17-/- mice showed reduced severity of the pathology 47.  
Conversely, EAE is a murine model of human multiple sclerosis, which induces inflammation, 
demyelination and axonal loss in the central nervous system (CNS) 48. The main driver of EAE is the 
Th17 subset and pro-inflammatory cytokine IL-17. However, the EAE model has shown that Vγ4+ γδ 
are highly present in the CNS during inflammation and are responsible for pro-inflammatory cytokine 
secretion and Th17 activation 49. For instance, Tcrδ−/− and γδ T cell-depleted mice display a reduction 
in disease severity 49. Therefore, both Th17 and γδT17 cells are important for EAE development and 
progression.  
IMQ-induced psoriasis depends on IL-23- and IL-1β-driven activation of γδT17 cells, which will secrete 
IL-17 and IL-22 promoting neutrophil recruitment and keratinocyte hyperproliferation 41. Dermal DCs 
and macrophages are the main producers of IL-23 in IMQ-induced model. IL-23 activates dermal γδT17 
cells and enhances their IL-17 secretion. Dermal γδT17 cells are known to be the major IL-17 source in 
the skin upon IL-23 stimulation 41. Moreover, conditional depletion of γδ T cells showed reduced IMQ-
induced pathology 50. After ΙΜQ, γδT17 cells expand in the lymph nodes (LNs) and migrate to the 
dermis in CCR2 and fingolmoid dependent manner 10. Furthermore, Vγ4+ γδT17 memory-like cells have 
the ability to develop a more efficient response after a second IMQ challenge 10.  
Additionally, it has been shown that γδT17 cells promote tumor progression in cancer mice models 51. 
IL-17 from murine γδ T cells is known to recruit pro-tumor macrophages in ovarian cancer models 52. 
Furthermore, it has also been described that γδT17 cells also recruit pro-tumor neutrophils that 
promote breast cancer metastasis 53.  
 
Overall, γδT17 cells have an important role in early stages of bacterial infections. Nevertheless, a 
dysregulation of these cells can lead to inflammation or autoimmunity disorders. Therefore, there is 
a need to define which signals initiate and terminate γδT17 effector functions. One of the inhibitory 
mechanisms described in γδT17 cells to attenuate their activity is the expression of Immune 
checkpoint receptors (ICRs).

Immune Checkpoint Receptors (ICRs) 
Co-stimulatory and inhibitory immune receptors guarantee balanced immune responses. These 
receptors control the initiation and termination of immune cells functions  54. Inhibitory receptors, 
also called immune checkpoint receptors (ICRs), are expressed upon leukocyte activation. 
Dysfunctional inhibitory signals often lead to an uncontrolled immune response 55. In contrast, a 
chronic ICRs overexpression results in excessive inhibitory signals, which enhances a loss of T cell 
function. This phenomenon is called cellular exhaustion 56. 
Hence, ICRs are an attractive target to modulate the immune response against tumors, infection or 
autoimmune diseases. The most studied ICRs are PD-1, CTLA-4 and BTLA, but there are other ICRs 
expressed in immune cells, such as KLRG-1, 2B4, Lag-3 and CD31 (Table 2). In addition, PD-1 and CTLA-
4 antibody blockade in T cells has had a huge impact in the cancer immunotherapy field 57–60.  
ICRs are expressed upon cellular activation but some are constitutively expressed such as BTLA 44,61. 
ICRs on T cells membrane interact with their ligands, usually present on the surface of antigen 
presenting cells and epithelial cells 59.

different types of inhibitory motifs, for example immunoreceptor tyrosine-based inhibitory motif 
(ITIM), immunoreceptor tyrosine based switched motifs (ITSM) and the amino acid sequences YxxM 
and KIEELE 55. 
Most ICRs use ITIM or ITSM inhibitory motifs to transduce their signal. Once the ICR binds to its ligand, 
inhibitory motifs are phosphorylated. Phosphorylation of ITIM causes the recruitment of cytosolic 
enzymes that contain a Src homology domain, such as Src homology region 2 domain-containing 
phosphatase (SHP)-1, SHP-2 and SHIP-1. Once these phosphatases are recruited, they 
dephosphorylate other signaling molecules involved in cell activation, such as downstream molecules 
required for TCR and BCR signal 55. Moreover, recent studies indicate that SHP-1 and SHP-2 
dephosphorylate costimulatory receptors, such as CD28 64. Furthermore, it has been shown that 
depending on the cell type, ICRs recruit either SHP-1, SHP-2 or both phosphatases 64. However, recent 
studies found a redundant role between SHP-1 and SHP-2 in ICRs signaling pathway 65. Consequently, 
the role of SHP-1 and SHP-2 in ICR signaling remains unclear.  
On the other hand, CTLA-4 contains a YxxM motif that once phosphorylated, recruits SHP-2 in an 
indirect manner 55. However, it has also been shown that CTLA-4 signals through PI3K indicating that 
CTLA-4 has the ability to use other effector molecules 66. Lag-3 transduces its signal though a KIEEELE 
motif although little is known about molecules involved with this motif 64. 
Besides the inhibitory motifs, ICRs have two more mechanisms that attenuate immune cell functions. 
For example, some inhibitory and co-stimulatory receptors bind to the same ligands such as CTLA-4, 
which competes with the costimulatory receptor CD28 for CD80 and CD86. Additionally, ICRs are 
known to induce upregulation of genes involved in T cell loss of functions 55. For instance, it has been 
shown that PD-1 is responsible for BATF activation that leads to a decrease of IL-2 secretion and cell 
proliferation 67. 
ICRs have a critical role in chronic infections like HIV in humans or LCMV in mice. During chronic 
infection, the antigen is persistent and induces overexpression of ICRs 55. Similarly, responses against 
tumors also result in a cellular exhaustion. In murine tumor models, PD-1 blockade improves immunity 
and survival. Moreover, ICR blockade therapies are suggested against chronic viral infections and 
tumors to boost the immune system. Currently, there are several ICR blockade therapies for cancer 
treatments such as anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 blockade targeting CD8+ and CD4+ T cells 59,68. 
Nevertheless, ICR immunotherapy has some drawbacks, such as the appearance of adverse effects 69. 
For instance, there are several cases of psoriasis induced by ICR immunotherapy 70,71.

The study of ICRs function in other lymphocyte subsets may lead to the design of new ICRs targeting 
therapies. For instance, γδT17 ICRs characterization can provide new therapeutic targets for psoriasis 
treatment. Unfortunately, there is not enough information about the expression of ICRs on γδ T cells 
in homeostasis or inflammation. However, it has been shown that γδ T cells have the ability to express 
PD-1, BTLA and occasionally CTLA-4 72. Furthermore, it has been shown that γδT17 function is 
regulated by PD-1 73 and BTLA 44. Due to the lack of comprehensive understanding regarding the role 
of ICRs in γδT17 biology, it is important to define whether ICRs signaling can regulate γδT17 function 
and under which context ICRs expression can be induced.

Chapter 1 
 
Specific aim 
The aim of this chapter is to investigate the role ICRs signaling in γδ T cells in homeostasis and 
inflammation. 
 
Studying immune cell regulation in homeostasis and inflammation is critical to understand cell 
behavior and dysfunction in immune pathologies 59,60,68. It is already known that BTLA and PD-1 
regulate γδ T cell function 44,73. However, there is a need to further define ICRs role in γδT17 cells. 
Therefore, it is important to first characterize which ICRs are required for γδT17 function. 
Furthermore, to better understand ICRs signaling may lead to a better design of ICRs blockade 
therapies.  
In this project, there are two aims. First, to characterize ICRs expression in γδT17 cells and second to 
define the role of SHP-1, SHP-2 in ICRs signaling in γδT17 cells. As ICRs are expressed upon cellular 
activation, an inducible IMQ psoriasis-like model, which is known to be γδ T cell dependent was used 
to promote skin inflammation 41. Moreover, this IMQ induced psoriasis was induced in mice that had 
SHP-1 and SHP-2 KO specific for RORγt+ cells in order to study their role in ICRs signaling pathway.

Results 
 
Differential ICR expression on γδΤ17 by IL-7, IL-1β and IL-23 in vitro 
 
In order to test the expression of ICRs on γδT17 cells, LN cells from WT mice were cultured during 48h 
with IL-7, IL-1β, IL-23 and IL-1β + IL-23. Next, samples were stained to detect several ICRs in γδT17 
cells: PD-1, BTLA, CTLA-4, Lag-3, 2B4, KLRG-1 and CD31. To measure ICRs expression a flow cytometry 
analysis was done and mean fluorescence intensity (MFI) was measured. MFI measure was used 
instead of positive cell percentages given that positive and negative populations were not distinctive 
from each other.  Compared to unstimulated cells, only BTLA, PD-1, CTLA-4 and 2B4 were measurable 
on stimulated γδT17 cells. Furthermore, BTLA and PD-1 expression was highly induced by IL-1β and 
expression of CTLA-4 and 2B4 was induced with IL-1β + IL-23 (Figure 1A-D). 
This results indicate that PD-1, BTLA, CTLA-4 and 2B4 seem to be the most important ICRs in γδΤ17 in 
vitro activation, given that the rest of the ICRs analyzed were not expressed (Figure S2A). Expression 
of BTLA was increased in all cytokine conditions, especially under IL-1β stimulation. (Figure 1A). 
Similarly, expression of PD-1 was high in IL-7, IL-1β and IL-23; however, cells cultured with IL-1β + IL-
23 had remarkably lower expression of PD-1 compared to the other cytokines (Figure 1B). On the other 
hand, expression of CTLA-4 was only higher in IL-23 + IL-1β culture (Figure 1C). Similarly, the same 
tendency was observed in 2B4 expression (Figure 1D). Expression of ICRs was very similar between 
γδT17 Vγ4+ and Vγ4- subsets. However, BTLA expression was lower in Vγ4- subset compared to total 
γδT17 and Vγ4+ subset (Figure S2B, S2C). 
Overall, this results show that ICRs expression in γδT17 cells can be differentially induced by cytokines.

Expression of ICRs in LN and skin γδT17 cells during inflammation  
 
Next, to assess whether ICRs could be induced in γδT17 cells in vivo during inflammation, a 
imiquimod(IMQ)-induced psoriasis model was used. This model requires γδT17 cells for pathology and 
the cytokines IL-1β, IL-23 and IL-7 41.   
In this experiment, expression of ICRs was measured during IMQ induced inflammation, after 14 days 
of the first inflammation and during a secondary IMQ induced inflammation. Therefore, 3 time point 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
were measured. First, mice ears were treated with IMQ cream for 7 days to induce inflammation. 
Second, after 14 days from the last day of treatment to measure if the inflammation persisted. Third, 
mice ears were treated a second time with IMQ cream during 7 days to induce secondary 
inflammation. The design of this experiment provides data from primary response (d7), after 14 days 
of the primary response (d21) and secondary response (d28). Non-treated mice were used as controls 
in very time point. In all time points, MFI from PD-1, BTLA, Lag-3, KLRG-1, 2B4, CTLA-4 and CD31 was 
measured from skin, draining auricular and cervical LN. CTLA-4, PD-1 and CD31 were found to be the 
only ICRs measurable during murine psoriasis induced disease (Figure 2). 
In the draining LNs, only expression of CTLA-4 was detected, although this was very low. (Figure 2A). 
When IMQ treatment was paused for 14 days, CTLA-4 expression in LN γδT17 cells returned to control 
levels. After re-challenge with IMQ, CTLA-4 could not be detected in LN γδT17 cells (Figure 2A). Skin 
γδΤ17 cells only expressed PD-1 and CD31. At d7, PD-1 expression had 3 fold increase compared to 
the controls. Furthermore, at d21 expression of PD-1 in skin γδT17 cells retuned to control levels and 
at d28 expression of PD-1 increased reaching similar expression to the primary inflammation (Figure 
2B). Surprisingly, expression of CD31 in skin γδT17 cells was induced after the IMQ re-challenge (Figure 
2C). 
Collectively, these results demonstrate that expression of ICRs differs between LN and skin γδT17 cells 
and that PD-1 appears to be the most abundant ICR during psoriasis-like disease.

ICRs signaling in γδT17 cell homeostasis is independent of SHP-1 and SHP-2 
In order to understand better how the ICRs signaling functions, SHP-1 and SHP-2 were studied. 
Therefore, mice that expressed the Cre recombinase under RORγt promoter with a mice floxed for 
SHP-1 or SHP-2 were crossed to generate the lines RORγtCRE-Shp1 and RORγtCRE- Shp2. Cre+ mice from 
these strains have SHP-1 or SHP-2 KO specific for RORγt+ cells. Then, these mouse lines were 
intercrossed to generate the double deficient RORγtCRE- Shp1Shp2 mice. 
First, the function of LN γδT17 cells at steady state was analyzed in RORγtCRE- Shp1 and RORγtCRE-Shp2 
mice 44. However, there was no significant difference between Cre+ and Cre− mice regarding γδT17 cell 
numbers or IL-17 production by γδT17 cells in either RORγtCRE- Shp1 or RORγtCRE-Shp2 (Figure 3A-B). 
As there was the possibility that SHP-1 and SHP-2 had redundant functions, the double KO strain 
RORγtCRE- Shp1Shp2 mice was generated. γδT17 cell numbers and their cytokine production in the LN 
was analyzed. Although no significant difference was found, Cre+ RORγtCRE- Shp1Shp2 mice had a 
tendency to have increased γδT17 cell numbers and IL-17 production. However, none of the results 
was statistically significant. (Figure 3C). 
Collectively, this data showed that either SHP-1 or SHP-2 have any important impact on γδT17 cell 
development or homeostasis.

ICRs signaling in γδT17 cells during inflammation is SHP-1 and SHP-2 independent 
Since in the steady state experiments no difference was detected between Cre− and Cre+ mice in any 
of the strains, the role of SHP-1 and SHP-2 was investigated during inflammation. Therefore, double 
KO RORγtCRE-Shp1Shp2 mice were used and IMQ cream was applied to induce inflammation. In this 
experiment, the same experimental design as in the expression of ICRs in vivo  was used. Therefore, 
results from primary inflammation (d7), after inflammation (d21) and secondary inflammation (d28) 
were obtained. Flow cytometry was used to investigate activation of γδT17 cells in the skin and 
draining cervical and auricular LN. Histology staining of mice ears was performed to assess the degree 
of inflammation. The histology results showed an increase in epidermal thickening after 7 days of 
cream application in both, Cre− and Cre+ mice. At time point d21, the inflammation returned to the 
steady state given that the epidermal thickening was similar to control mice. Finally, at d28 the 
inflammation and thickening of epidermis was higher compared to d7. Comparison between Cre− and 
Cre+ mice did not showed any significant difference at d7 or d21. However, at d28 Cre+ mice had 
reduced inflammation (Figure 4A). 
Next, LN and skin γδT17 cell numbers and IL-17 production were analyzed during different time points. 
LN and skin γδT17 cells expanded during inflammation at d7 and returned to control levels at day 21, 
which corresponded 14 days after the IMQ treatment (Figure 4C-D). After re-challenge with IMQ, 
γδT17 cell numbers increased in both LN and skin compared to control and d7 numbers (Figure 4C-D). 
Moreover, IL-17 production was induced during inflammation at day 7. During re-challenge with IMQ, 
IL-17 production by γδT17 cells was higher compared to control levels. (Figure 4D). No difference was 
detected between Cre− and Cre+ γδT17 numbers and IL-17 production during the different time points 
(Figure 4C-D). Collectively, we showed that SHP-1 and SHP-2 are not required for γδT17 cell function 
during inflammation.

Discussion  
This project aimed to investigate which ICRs are induced in γδT17 cells and whether ICRs signaling is 
SHP-1 and SHP-2 dependent in γδT17 cells. The expression of ICRs was studied following in vitro 
cytokine stimulation or in vivo psoriasis induced inflammation. Furthermore, the role of SHP-1 and 
SHP-2 in ICRs signaling was studied  in mice with specific SHP-1 and SHP-2 deficiency in γδΤ17 cells. 
Τhe present study showed that in vitro cytokine stimulation enhanced ICRs in γδΤ17 cells. In addition, 
the ICR profile in γδT17 cells was dependent on the cytokine environment. Moreover, ICRs were 
induced upon γδT17 cell activation during IMQ psoriasis-like inflammation in vivo. Furthermore, 
results indicated that PD-1 was the most abundant ICR in activated γδT17 cells. However, the 
expression of BTLA, CTLA-4 and 2B4 was also increased upon in vitro cytokine stimulation. Likewise, 
CD31 was enhanced during re-challenge response to IMQ induced inflammation. Furthermore, ICRs 
signaling through SHP-1 and SHP-2 was not critical for γδΤ17 development or function during 
homeostasis or inflammation.  
This data provides a clearer profile of ICRs in γδT17 cells. In this regard, results indicated that ICRs in 
γδT17 were expressed upon cytokine activation without TCR stimulation. In CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, 
cytokine stimulation also promotes the expression of ICRs 75. This same study showed that both 
cytokine and TCR stimulation enhanced similar levels of ICRs in CD4+ and CD8+ T cells; however, the 
induction of ICRs following cytokine stimulation was slower than following TCR stimulation. Moreover, 
during chronic antigen exposure there is an overexpression of ICRs in T cells due to continuous TCR 
stimulation 76.  
However, the data herein suggested that cytokine activation was enough to significantly induce the 
expression of ICRs in γδT17 cells. This is consistent with previous studies that found more BTLA 
abundance during IL-7 or IL-2 in vitro stimulation of γδ RORγt+ cells 44.  Nevertheless, future studies 
could investigate whether TCR signaling induces higher ICR expression in γδ Τ cells or not. 
The expression profile of ICRs in γδT17 was different depending on the cytokine milieu. In vitro 
stimulation assays showed IL-23 and IL-1β as the main ICRs inducers. However, other studies have 
shown that IL-23 and IL-1β do not induce significant ICRs expression in γδ T cells 44. It would be 
interesting to study whether cytokine receptors signaling directly induces expression of ICRs. For 
instance, as IL-23R signals through STAT3, RORγtCRE-STAT3 mice could be used to study PD-1 and BTLA 
expression. This experiment would show if their expression is STAT3 dependent in γδT17 cells. 
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
Similarly, MyD88 KO mice with inhibited NFκB signaling  could be used to determinate IL-1β dependent 
ICRs. These studies could help to provide a better definition of the required signals for ICRs expression 
in γδT17 cells.  
The studies regarding ICRs in vivo expression during psoriasis inflammation contribute to a better 
understanding of the murine model and the role in ICRs in γδT17 cells during inflammation. In addition, 
PD-1 was the most abundant ICR in skin γδT17 during IMQ induced psoriasis. However, PD-1 was not 
expressed in LN γδT17 cells. Previous studies also found an upregulation of PD-1 in skin γδ T cells but 
not in the LN γδ T cells during IMQ induced psoriasis 73. Moreover, studies using PD-1 KO mice had 
increased skin inflammation due to an increase of IL-17 production by γδ T cells 73. However, all these 
studies used full PD-1 KO mice. Therefore, it would be interesting to study IMQ induced psoriasis in 
mice with γδT17 specific PD-1 KO. These experiments would help to determinate whether ICRs have 
a critical inhibitory role in γδT17 cells or not. In addition, other autoimmune models such as EAE and 
CIA could be used to characterize the expression of ICRs in γδT17 cells during inflammation. 
During secondary inflammation γδT17 cells from skin expressed CD31. As γδT17 did not expressed 
CD31 during primary response, these findings indicate a differential expression of ICRs between 
primary and secondary response to IMQ induced psoriasis. CD31 is expressed by a variety of immune 
cells such as DCs, platelets, macrophages, B and T cells 77. Furthermore, it has also been described that 
CD31 is involved in transendotelial migration of memory T cells. .Therefore, the results obtained 
correlate with previous studies that identified CD31 in other memory lymphocytes. However, there a 
need to investigate CD31 role in γδT17 cells. 
On the other hand, neither SHP-1 nor SHP-2 were required for γδT17 cell development or function. 
Studies that used conditional CD4CRE-Shp-1f/f, indicated that SHP-1 was not required to inhibit TCR 
signaling 78. Moreover, experiments using Shp-2 KO mice indicated that SHP-2 was irrelevant for T cell 
exhaustion and for PD-1 signaling in CD8+ T cells 79. Likewise, in vitro assays using T cells with a 
dominant negative mutant of SHP-2 still responded to PD-1 ligation 80.Furthermore,  a recent study 
showed that SHP-2 had a redundant role of SHP-1 in PD-1 signaling 65. Results presented in this project 
using single SHP-1 and SHP-2 conditional KO correlate with previous studies. However, in vitro studies 
provided evidence that both SHP-1 and SHP-2 were bound to the inhibitory motifs of ICRs 64. As no 
difference in γδT17 cell function was detected in RORγtCRE-Shp1Shp2, it suggests that SHP-1 and SHP-
2 are nor critical for ICRs signaling. Thus, it is possible that another molecule has the ability to be 
recruited and transduce the inhibitory signal when SHP-1 and SHP-2 are not present.  
Besides SHP-1 and SHP-2 recruitment, ICRs have two more inhibitory mechanisms: ligand competition 
with costimulatory receptor and the induction of genes related with cell inhibition, inhibiting IL-2 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
production and cell proliferation 55. Therefore, it is possible that SHP-1 and SHP-2 KO cells compensate 
this deficiency by enhancing SHP-1 and SHP-2 independent inhibitory mechanisms. For instance, ICRs 
signal is able to upregulate genes that enhanced loss of proliferation and activity, such as BATF 
increased levels in HIV-specific CD8+ T cell 67. However, the signaling pathway that governs this 
mechanism is still unknown. Therefore, ir could be perform a  RNA sequencing analysis of γδT17 cells 
from RORγtCRE-Shp1Shp2 to investigate if ICRs induces the expression of inhibitory genes. Given the 
obtained results, there is still a need to study ICRs expression and signaling in γδT17 and to identify 
new molecules that could be involved in ICRs signaling. 
Several disease models showed that ICRs antagonistic or blockade treatment can modulate the 
immune response and decrease pathology. Therefore, this project provides a better understanding on 
how these ICRs are induced in γδT17 cells in homeostasis and during inflammation of LN and skin. 
Furthermore, there was a differential expression of ICRs depending on the inflammatory environment 
or cytokine milieu. Moreover, SHP-1 and SHP-2 were dispensable for a proper γδT17 development and 
function. Nevertheless, there are still some questions regarding the mechanism of ICRs induction and 
the molecules used to transduce their signal to inhibit γδT17 cell function. Collectively, γδΤ17 cells 
express PD-1, BTLA, 2B4, CTLA-4 and CD31 under specific conditions and ICRs signaling through SHP-
1 and SHP-2 is not critical for the function of γδT17 cells in homeostasis or inflammation.

Chapter 2  
 
Specific aim 
The aims of this chapter is to investigate the differential gene expression of type 1 and type 3 γδ T 
cells in the small intestine, colon and LN. 
 
Recent studies performed in our research group found a γδ T cell population that co-express T-bet and 
RORγt in murine intestinal lamina propia at steady state. As this population appeared during neonatal 
life, suggests that these cells show tissue specificity 81 .  
To define further this population and detect other genes that could be differentially expressed, RNA 
sequencing was performed. To asses this, a double reporter T-betAmcyan-RORγtGFP mice was used to sort 
the cells. This project aimed to sort for six different populations to study whether this recently 
described cells display tissue specificity or not. Besides double positive (T-bet+RORγt+) populations 
from small intestine and colon lamina propia, other populations were also analyzed, such as small 
intestine and colon lamina propia T-bet+ single positive γδ T cells. Moreover T-bet+ single positive and 
RORγt+ single positive γδ T cell population from LN were also used in this project.

RNA sequencing analysis was first quality checked with FastQC. FastQC provides quality report for 
each file describing the quality of the reads. Next, kallisto alignment tool was used to perform the 
alignment. Most of alignment tools map to a reference genome. However, kallisto maps to a reference 
transcriptome. Therefore, kallisto analysis is suitable for RNA sequencing files with the purpose of 
analyzing differentially expressed genes. The main advantage of using kallisto is that it does not 
require a lot of computational memory compared to other alignment tools. Furthermore, as kallisto is 
mapping against the transcriptome instead of the entire genome this method is two order of 
magnitude faster than other approaches 82. Moreover, this tool have the same quality than other 
standard RNA sequencing aligners. Overall, kallisto is specially designed to align RNA sequencing files 
to a reference transcriptome and perform a differential gene expression analysis 82. To analyze the 
aligned files sleuth R package was used 83. 
In order to asses overall similarity between samples, principal component analysis (PCA) was used. 
PCA allows to visualize a high dimensional dataset into a two-dimensional graph display (Figure 7). In 
RNA sequencing files, each expressed gene corresponds to one dimension. Therefore, PCA analysis is 
useful for to pre-analyze RNA sequencing datasets, given their size. As there are some genes that have 
the same abundance in all samples, PCA analysis ignores genes equally expressed to decrease the 
number of dimensions. PCA uses the most differentially expressed genes to represent most different 
samples. Therefore, the distance between two dots in the PCA plot correlates with the variance 
between these two samples 84. In the PCA analysis, similar samples cluster together and different 
samples are separated. Likewise, the PCA plot is also used to determinate whether experimental 
replicates are similar.

In this case, all replicates cluster in similar locations (Figure 7). Moreover, PCA also indicates that this

Discussion 
This project aimed to perform a differential gene expression of the recently described intestinal subset 
T-bet-expressing γδT17 cells. However, only PCA analysis was presented. 
The results from the PCA analysis indicate that the three replicates are very similar to each other, 
supporting the quality of the samples. Moreover, LN population cluster very close together, which 
suggest that in the LNs few genes are different from T-bet+ γδ T cells and RORγt+ γδ T cells. However, 
T-bet+ γδ T cells and RORγt+ γδ T cells originate from different lineages 23,40. Although in the LNs all 
subsets cluster following location, this is not shown in the intestine samples. Small and colon samples 
are not similar to each other, in fact they seem very different. Furthermore, double positive subset 
cluster together regardless of tissue location. However, a deeper analysis needs to be performed. For 
example, a differentially expressed genes analysis represented in a heatmap. This analysis would 
provide information about which are the genes that are more differentially expressed between the 
different populations. Furthermore, these differentially expressed genes can be related to an effector 
function or a maturation stage. Collectively, these results show that the replicates and the samples 
seem reliable. Furthermore, it can be concluded that T-bet+ RORγt+ γδ T cells are not organ dependent 
in the intestine.

Materials and Methods 
Mice 
All animal breeding were performed in house at DTU and only after approval from Danish Animal 
Experiments Inspectorate. RORγtCRE and RORγtGFP mice 59 were provided by Professor Gerard Eberl 
and subsequently bred in house. Frozen sperm samples from Tbet-AmCyan mice were provided by 
Professor Jinfang Zhu (NIH/NIAID, MD) and after re-derivation mice bred in house.  
For Shp1 and Shp2 conditional KO we crossed mice that expressed the Cre recombinase under RORγt 
promoter with a mice floxed for SHP-1 or SHP-2 to generate the lines RORγtCRE-Shp1 and RORγtCRE- 
Shp2. Then, we intercrossed these mouse lines to generate the double deficient RORγtCRE- Shp1Shp2 
mice. 
Cell culture media and buffers 
 
All cell culture and single cell suspensions were prepared using RPMI 1640 (Invitrogen) supplemented 
with 10% heat inactivated fetal bovine serum (FBS)(Gibco), 20mM Hepes pH 7.4 (Gibco), 50 µM 2-
mercaptoethanol, 2 mM L-glutamine (Gibco) and 10,000 U/ mL penicillin-streptomycin (Gibco). 10x 
Hank’s balanced salt solution (HBSS)(Gibco) was diluted to 1x with sterile nuclease-free water and 
supplemented with 15 mM HEPES pH 7.4 (Gibco) to prepare HBSS-HEPES while it was supplemented 
with 2mM EDTA, 15mM HEPES, 50μg/mL Gentamycin and 2% FBS to prepare HBSS-EDTA. Isotonic 
Percoll was prepared by mixing 90%v/v of Percoll (GE healthcare) with 9%v/v 10x HBSS and 1%v/v 1M 
HEPES pH 7.4. Isotonic Percoll was diluted with HBSS-EDTA to the desired concentration. FACS buffer 
was prepared by mixing 3% heat inactivated FBS with DPBS (Gibco). 
IMQ-induced psoriasis and histology  
To induce psoriasis, mice were anesthetized using isoflurane and 10mg of 5% Aldara™ (MEDA) was 
applied daily for 7 days at the dorsal side of both ears. For histology, ear tissue was fixed in 10% 
formalin overnight and paraffin embedded sections were H&E stained. For first inflammation data, 
mice were treated during 7 days with Aldara cream. For analysis of inflammation persistence, mice 
were treated during 7 days with Aldara cream and after 14 days of the last application, mice were 
sacrificed. For the re-challenge assays mice were treated during 7 days with Aldara cream, then no 
treatment for 14 days and after they were treated again during 7 days. 
Isolation of lymphocytes from lymph nodes (LNs), skin, small intestine and colon 
LNs were dissected, cleared off fat and crushed against a 70μm cell strainer to prepare single cell 
suspensions. Cell suspensions were then washed and filtered through a 40μm cell strainer. Cells were 
counted and 2.5x106 cells were used for staining of surface antigens and flow cytometry analysis. 
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
Skin lymphocytes were prepared from ears as follows: first, the dorsal and ventral sides of the ears 
were mechanically separated, they were subsequently  cut into small pieces followed by enzymatic 
digestion with 0.25mg/ml collagenase IV, 0.166mg/ml hyaluronidase and 0.1mg/ml DNase I (all 
enzymes from Sigma-Aldrich) in supplemented RPMI for 1 hour at 37°C with constant stirring at 700 
rpm. The remain tissue that was not digested was crushed against a 70μm cell strainer to prepare a 
single cell suspension. After washing, the cell pellet was re-suspended and filtered through a 40μm 
cell strainer to remove tissue debris and used for flow cytometry staining. 
Small intestines and colons were dissected from adult mice and HBSS-HEPES was used to remove 
intestinal contents. Fat and Peyer’s patches were removed and then the tissues were open 
longitudinally  and cut into small pieces of approximately 2-3 cm. Chopped tissue was washed 4 times 
(2 alternate cycles of 10 and 15 min each ) using 15 mL of HBSS-EDTA buffer at 37°C in a shaking 
incubator. Tissue pieces were then digested using 0.3 mg Liberase TM (Roche) and 0.15 mg of DNase 
(Sigma Aldrich) per preparation in 5mL supplemented RPMI for 40 minutes on the magnetic stirrer at 
37°C. The resulting cell suspensions were filtered through 70μm cell strainers, collected in complete 
RPMI and subsequently pelleted by centrifugation.  The cell pellets were then re-suspended in 5 mL 
40% Percoll, layered on 4 mL of 70% Percoll, and centrifuged at 20 °C and 800 × g for 20 min with 
deceleration set to 0. Cells from the interphase were collected, washed once and then re-suspended 
in supplemented RPMI.  
In vitro culture 
 
Lymphocytes obtained from mice organs were transferred to culture plates in a concentration around 
105 cells/mL the control samples and 107 cells/mL the samples with cytokines cultures. Then, 20ng/mL 
of each cytokine was added to the culture IL-7, IL-1β and IL-23. In the control sample, no cytokine was 
added. Cultures were incubated during 48h at 37°C and then stained using Flow cytometry protocol. 
Flow Cytometry 
 
Cells were harvested by centrifugation at 400 g for 5 minutes at 4°C followed by staining with fixable 
viability stain (BD Horizon FVS700) for 10 minutes on ice in PBS. Subsequently, surface antigens were 
stained in FACS buffer for 30 minutes on ice. For CTLA-4 staining the 30 minutes on ice were followed 
by 30 minutes incubation at 37°C.  For cytokine staining, cells were then fixed and permeabilized by 
incubation in BD Fix/Perm solution for 15 minutes at room temperature followed by washing once in 
BD Perm/Wash solution. Intracellular cytokines were stained in BD Perm/Wash for 15 minutes at room 
temperature. 
Cells were stained in U-bottom 96-well plates in 75μl PBS containing 3% FBS with combinations of the 
following antibodies: CD4-FITC (RM4-4), CD19-FITC (6D5), CD8-FITC (53-6.7), BTLA-PE(6F7), PD-1-

Data Analysis 
 
Flow cytometry data was analyzed using Flow Jo v9.8.3 or v10. H&E sections were photographed on a 
ZEISS Axo10 Observer microscope at 10X magnification with camera Axiocam 702. Epidermal 
thickening was measured using blue Zen lite Version 3.0 software form ZEISS microscopy. All graphs 
were generated and statistical analyses were performed using Prism v8. We used unpaired non-
parametric Mann-Whitney U-test when n>3 and Unpaired t test when n≤3 for in vivo assays. We used 
Paired t test for in vitro assays. 
Purification of γδ T cells using MACS 
Magnetic depletion of B cells, CD4 and CD8 T cells was used to purify γδ T cells from cell suspension. 
Cells were resuspended in MACS buffer to achieve 108 cells/mL and rat serum (Normal Rat Serum, 
STEM CELLTM) and biotin-linked antibodies were added. We used CD19-biotin (6D5), CD4-biotin 
(GK1.5), CD8-biotin (53-6.7) antibodies for 10 minutes incubation at room temperature. Subsequently, 
beads (BDiMagTM- Streptavidin Particles Plus DM- BD Biociences) were added (75μL/mL) and 
incubated during 2.30 min and we added MACS buffer to reach 5mL. For magnetic depletion,the 
magnet (STEM CELLTM EasyStepTMMagnet) during 2.30 min was used. We transferred the liquid into a 
new tube and resuspend the remaining cells in RPMI buffer.  
Cell sorting  
Stained cells from LNs, small intestine and colon were sorted using BD ARIA FUSION™ and BD FACSDiva 
software v8.0.2. Targeted populations were sorted directly in RNA protect buffer (RNAprotect Cell 
Reagent – Quiagen)  and stored at 4°C. Samples were sended to Genomics Core Facility “KFB - Center 
of Excellence for Fluorescent Bioanalytics” (University of Regensburg, Regensburg, Germany; 
www.kfb-regensburg.de). 
Total RNA extraction 
Total RNA was extracted from FACS sorted cells according to the “Purification of total RNA from animal 
and human cells” protocol of the RNeasy Plus Micro Kit (QIAGEN, Hilden, Germany). 
==PAGE:40==
39 
 
In brief, cells were stored and shipped in buffer RNAlater at 2-8 °C. After pelleting by centrifugation 
for 5 minutes at 5,000 x g, the RNAlater was replaced by 350 µl buffer RLT Plus and the samples were 
homogenized by vortexing for 30 sec. Genomic DNA contamination was removed using gDNA 
Eliminator spin columns. Next one volume of 70 % ethanol was added and the samples were applied 
to RNeasy MinElute spin columns followed by several wash steps. Finally total RNA was eluted in 12 
μl of nuclease free water. Purity and integrity of the RNA was assessed on the Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer 
with the RNA 6000 Pico LabChip reagent set (Agilent, Palo Alto, CA, USA). 
RNA sequencing 
The SMARTer Ultra Low Input RNA Kit for Sequencing v4 (Clontech Laboratories, Inc., Mountain View, 
CA, USA) was used to generate first strand cDNA from 500 pg total-RNA. Double stranded cDNA was 
amplified by LD PCR (12 cycles) and purified via magnetic bead clean-up. Library preparation was 
carried out as described in the Illumina Nextera XT Sample Preparation Guide (Illumina, Inc., San 
Diego, CA, USA). 150 pg of input cDNA were tagmented (tagged and fragmented) by the Nextera XT 
transposome. The products were purified and amplified via a limited-cycle PCR program to generate 
multiplexed sequencing libraries. For the PCR step 1:5 dilutions of index 1 (i7) and index 2 (i5) primers 
were used. The libraries were quantified using the KAPA SYBR FAST ABI Prism Library Quantification 
Kit (Kapa Biosystems, Inc., Woburn, MA, USA). Equimolar amounts of each library were sequenced on 
a NextSeq 500 instrument controlled by the NextSeq Control Software (NCS) v2.2.0, using a 75 Cycles 
High Output Kit with the dual index, single-read (SR) run parameters. Image analysis and base calling 
were done by the Real Time Analysis Software (RTA) v2.4.11. The resulting .bcl files were converted 
into .fastq files with the CASAVA Software v1.8.2. 
RNA extraction, library preparation and RNAseq were performed at the Genomics Core Facility “KFB - 
Center of Excellence for Fluorescent Bioanalytics” (University of Regensburg, Regensburg, Germany; 
www.kfb-regensburg.de). 
Pre-processing analysis and graphic display of RNA sequencing 
Raw reads were preprocessed with FastQC tools to generate quality control files for high throughput 
sequencing and Trimmomatic tool was used to trim the reads. Alignment was done using Kallisto 
pseudoaligment tool to quantify transcript abundances, mapping to the reference genome mm10 Mus 
musculus.  Transcripts were matched to gene id, generating gene abundance analysis. 
For the graphics display R statistical program version 3.6.1 was used. Kallisto output files were 
imported and processed using sleuth R package. Sleuth package generated PCA analysis and gene 
differential analysis displaying a heatmap of most differential expressed genes.

*p< 0.05, **p<0.01, ***p<0.001 using Unpaired t test for groups with n≤3. Mice were treated with 
IMQ cream once a day during 7 days, mice were sacrificed to obtain data from time point day 7 (d7) 
representing first inflammation response. After 14 days without treatment, mice were sacrificed to 
obtain data from time point 21 (d21). Mice were treated again with IMQ cream for 7 days and then 
sacrificed to obtain data from time point day 28 (d28) representing response to secondary 
inflammation. (A) MFI measure of PD-1, BTLA, Lag-3, KLRG-1, CD31 and 2B4 in LN γδT17 cells during 
d7, d21 and d28 time points comparing non-treated mice with IMQ treated mice. (B) MFI measure of 
BTLA, Lag-3, KLRG-1 and 2B4 in skin γδT17 cells during d7, d21 and d28 time points comparing non-
treated mice with IMQ treated mice.
