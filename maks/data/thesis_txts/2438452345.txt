ABSTRACT 
 
In response to the growing divide between inhabitants of ever-expanding cities and the natural 
environment, green facade systems are one of the solutions put forth. Such installations can 
have a very positive impact on the thermal performances of a building as well as its local 
environment in terms of heat island effect, water management, air quality, noise pollution, 
visual comfort or biodiversity. In recent years, green facade systems became increasingly 
developed, diverse, and sophisticated. However, their implementation is still scarce, and all the 
solutions are not equal. Most of the existing systems present several drawbacks in terms of 
installation and maintenance costs, low level of integration with the structure, extra loads or 
limitations in flexibility over time. 
The current project is looking into a new type of green facade based on the growth of lichens 
on a concrete substrate. Lichens are a unique life form characterized by a symbiotic relationship 
between a fungus and an alga, drawing all their nutrients from the air. The aim is to investigate 
the use of concrete and supplementary cementitious materials to tailor a facade element that 
sustains the growth of lichens. 
The strategy is to use supplementary cementitious materials in the mix design, namely 
limestone and calcined clay. The replacement of 35% of the cement with a combination of the 
clay and limestone reduces the level of CO2 emissions significantly compared to ordinary 
concrete with comparable mechanical performance(K. Scrivener, 2018). Furthermore, being 
abundant worldwide, it constitutes a technical solution than can be implemented globally. The 
use of these binders presents an additional advantage for this context, by allowing a faster 
carbonation which reduces the pH of the material and therefore facilitates the colonization and 
growth of lichens. 
A series of concrete tiles were cast using different mix designs and surface treatments to assess 
their efficiency as a substrate for lichen growth while their hygrometric properties were tested. 
The growth is initiated with three different methods of seeding within a controlled environment 
in a climate room. The growth environment in the climate room is controlled by scheduled 
artificial lighting and constant high humidity for an optimised growth rate. The results of the 
growth tests are monitored while each concrete mix is analysed in terms of porosity and 
hygrometric environment of its surface.  
The experiments showed that the limestone and calcined clay concrete provided a more suitable 
substrate for the seeding and the transplantation of the lichen Xanthoria parietina compared to 
a reference ordinary Portland cement concrete. However, it showed a higher permeability 
which can impair the micro-climatic environment on the surface.

1     INTRODUCTION 
 
1.1  Context 
 
In the context of the Nordic Built Sustainable Transformation and Environmental Design 
(STED) project, a broad collaboration between the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) 
and various professional parties, the current project was proposed for master’s level students 
of the Civil Engineering department. 
In 2015, the initial input for the project came from the Icelandic architecture office Studio 
Granda with the idea of creating a building material that would naturally get covered with 
lichens and create colourful self-sustaining vegetal facades. The project was started in 2015 
with a master’s thesis written by Bradanini (2016) and is investigated again through the current 
work. 
 
1.2  Scope of research 
Over the past few decades, an increasing focus has been placed on bringing nature back into 
the city. Recently, a symbolic threshold has been reached: more than half of the population of 
the planet are living in cities, and according to estimations by the United Nations (2014) this 
ratio is set to increase exponentially to reach 66% in 2050. The global population living in 
medium-sized cities nearly doubled between 1990 and 2014 (United Nations, 2014). 
To cope with this growth, cities across the globe are sprawling rapidly and new cities are rising. 
This phenomenon threatens not only the natural habitats and green areas overtaken by urban 
development but also opportunities for people to interact with the natural environment. The 
issue of integrating nature and green spaces into the urban environment is therefore evermore 
important in the wake of this fast urbanization 
Having green spaces in cities is becoming unanimously recognised as a valuable good that can 
have a great number of positive qualities. This is supported by many studies  and policies such 
as a European recommendation of square meter of green area per inhabitant, or, more locally, 
Copenhagen’s 1947 ‘finger plan’; promising five green perimeters to be spared from the city’s 
expansion (Bradanini, 2016). However, those requirements are rarely met. A high number of 
European cities are below the 10m² of green space per capita recommended by the World 
Health Organisation (Manso, 2014), and, after an excessive densification, it is difficult to act 
retroactively to provide most urban inhabitants with a desirable level of access to green spaces.  
There are several ways to integrate nature into the built environment. While green areas and 
parks are the most radical way to include nature in the city, the urbanization process and the 
need for density often restricts its implementation. Various technologies in which vegetation is 
an integral part of the built environment were thus developed, such as green roofs and green 
facades. Although many examples of integration of vegetation within the built environment 
can be found throughout history, their development, diversity, and degree of sophistication is 
rapidly increasing within current urban and ecological contexts. 
==PAGE:16==

These technologies, often in an early stage of development, present a number of drawbacks and 
disadvantages related to the maintenance costs, the lifespan, the level of integration with the 
building components or the flexibility over time (Manso, 2014). 
 
The current project is looking into an innovative way to integrate nature into the built 
environment by investigating the possibility of using concrete building facades as a direct 
substrate for living organisms and tap into the possibilities of this material to create a solution 
that fulfils its dual purpose in the most sustainable way.  
 
1.3  Objectives 
The general objective of this work is to investigate the use of cementitious materials as a 
substrate for lichen growth. The specific objectives are the following: 
 
• Identify the factors influencing the bioreceptivity of a concrete substrate. 
• Identify the factors influencing the colonization and growth of lichens.  
• Create a set of concrete samples to test the different bioreceptivity factors identified. 
• Develop appropriate seeding methods and a growth environment for lichen colonization 
on concrete. 
• Investigate and characterize the water transport and the porosity of the concrete samples 
created. 
• Monitor and compare the behaviour of lichens on different concrete samples.  
 
To gradually complete these objectives, the work is divided into the following chapters. 
The first and current chapter gives an introduction to the project through its context, scope, 
objectives and methodology. The second chapter is a review of the most relevant research 
leading up to the current work. Chapter 3 reviews the theoretical literature required to develop 
the experimentation strategy and direct discussion with regards to both the lichen and concrete 
aspects. Chapter 4 closely outlines the materials and methods used in the different steps of the 
experiments and tests. Chapter 5 presents the results of the different experiments and tests and 
discusses them. Chapter 6 then states the conclusions drawn from the discussion and gives 
recommendations for further research.  
 
1.4  Methodology 
The experimentation strategy is to create a series of concrete tiles using different mix designs 
and surface treatments to assess their efficiency as a substrate for lichen growth. The growth 
will be initiated with three different methods of seeding in the controlled environment of a 
climate room. This is controlled by scheduled artificial lighting and constant high humidity for 
an optimised growth rate. The climate room is a crucial element in accelerating the process and 
tackling the timing challenge presented by the project. 
 
Regarding the mix designs, it was decided that supplementary cementitious materials be used. 
More precisely limestone and calcined clay are used in the mix design. The resulting binders 
contain less calcium and the carbonation process is therefore accelerated (Khan, Nguyen, & 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
INTRODUCTION 
17 
 
Castel, 2018). The environmental impact of the mix is also lowered, which is a significant asset 
from the perspective of developing a sustainable ‘green’ facade. The two main mix designs are 
thus based on conventional Portland cement for reference and a mix in which 35% of the 
cement is replaced by limestone and calcined clay. Variations of the water-cement ratio 
influence the porosity of the binder and therefore its properties of water transport and water 
retention. These properties are crucial in the creation of a suitable micro-climatic environment 
for lichen growth. The durability of the material is also a significant property influenced by the 
porosity. The porosity of the investigated substrates is characterized for each concrete mix 
design with a capillarity ‘suction’ test and the monitoring of vapor sorption/desorption in a 
climate chamber to investigate the water transport and retention respectively. 
 
Hydrated cement creates a high pH environment that is harmful for the growth of lichens. Over 
time, the pH will decrease through the carbonation reaction of the concrete, but it can take years 
to reach a level desirable for the colonization of organisms. The carbonation reaction is 
therefore accelerated to lower the pH value of the concrete tiles and to produce a more suitable 
substrate for organisms to settle on. This is achieved by submerging the tiles in a sodium 
bicarbonate solution for 25 days.  
With each of these mix design variations, different surface treatments were applied to assess 
the impact of the concrete surface texture on colonization and growth of lichens. Six types of 
surface treatments were tested. 
Since lichens have a specific way of reproducing and of colonizing a surface, different seeding 
methods are used. The first one is to crush the lichen specimens in flakes and spread them on 
the substrate through a suspension media made of a casein peptone solution. The second one 
seeds the lichens the same way, but with a previous alga seeding that could possibly accelerate 
the lichen colonization. The last one is an attempt of full lichen thalli transplantation. These 
strategies were derived from an analysis of the different growth and colonization properties of 
lichens. 
Through these broad variations of growth attempts, the objective is to monitor the growth of 
lichen in a climate room for as long as possible within the project’s duration. The humidity 
level was chosen to accelerate the lichen growth but simultaneously minimize the probability 
of mould growth. To ensure a controlled lighting and extend the growth period of the lichen, 
LED growth lamps were used and scheduled in a day and night cycle that provides more light 
than naturally available outdoors in Denmark at the time of the project. The growth itself is 
monitored through visual and photographic analysis and spectroscopic measurements.

2     STATE OF THE ART 
 
2.1  Introduction 
The last decade has seen a drive in research focused on ways to integrate nature into the urban 
environment in the form of green roofs and green facades. The possible solutions are more and 
more developed, diverse, and sophisticated (Pérez-Urrestarazu, Fernández-Cañero, Franco-
Salas, & Egea, 2015; Perini & Ottelé, 2014). The complexity of these solutions is directly 
related to the numerous functions and qualities that these structures need to fulfil such as 
energy, water management, pollution or noise reduction, biodiversity or visual comfort (Perini 
& Ottelé, 2014; Van Renterghem, Hornikx, Forssen, & Botteldooren, 2013).  
However, this is nothing new. The use of vegetation on building surfaces has been traced back 
2000 years, for example in the Mediterranean region with climbing plants used to provide 
shade, or in the famous example of the Hanging Gardens of Babylon (Perini & Ottelé, 2014). 
In northern Europe, examples of other types are found, such as green envelopes used for their 
insulation properties in traditional Icelandic construction methods (Perini & Ottelé, 2014). Yet, 
it is in the last decades that most research and developments have emerged in the field, due to 
environmental concerns (Sheweka & Mohamed, 2012).  
 
 
 
Figure 1: Turf house in Thverá, traditional Icelandic green envelope. (Milek, 2012) 
 
The principal distinction in the field is to be made between green roofs and green facades, 
which are relying on specific principles and resulting in distinct outcomes. In the context of the 
current project, the following section will focus exclusively on the different forms of green 
facades or ‘vertical green’.

2.2  Green facades 
 
Figure 2: Green façade on the campus of the Technical University of Denmark 
 
The term  ‘green façade’ comprises a broad spectrum of practices: from vertical gardens to 
hanging gardens, planter boxes to wall planters, double skins to climbing plants (or climbers) 
on bare facades (Rakhshandehroo, Mohd Yusof, & Arabi, 2015). They are generally adopted 
for their numerous qualities and the solutions they offer to urban problems. Vertical green can 
have an important impact on the thermal performances of a building both in summer and winter 
by buffering solar radiations, insulating or hampering the surface cooling effect of direct wind 
(Perini & Ottelé, 2014). The problems of Urban Heat Islands (UHI) occurring due to an 
excessive mineralisation of the urban areas can be mitigated by surface greening; allowing the 
plants to consume the excess heat through evapo-transpiration (Sheweka & Mohamed, 2012). 
Numerous other factors of the local urban environment can be improved by the presence of 
green facades, namely the air quality through CO2 absorption, the level of noise pollution and 
visual comfort (Van Renterghem et al., 2013). 
Perini and Ottelé (2014) are separating green facades into two types: facade greening and 
Living Wall Systems (LWS). The first concerns the more basic methods with ground-rooted 
climbers directly attached to the facade or with a basic supporting structure (cables or metal 
meshes). The LWS concern more sophisticated systems where the plants can be rooted in 
multiple locations with planter boxes, wall planters or substrate panels of various materials. All 
these solutions have a different financial impact but are also very different in terms of global 
environmental footprint (Altan, John, Yoshimi, Ilyas, & Galadari, 2017). The price per m² can 
range from 30-45€ for climbers on the bare facade to 750-1200€ for a LWS based on foam 
substrate (Perini & Ottelé, 2014). 
 
==PAGE:21==

Figure 3: Green facades consisting of (a) climbers attached directly to the building surface, (b) climbers supported by

The lifespan of the system, its embodied energy, maintenance and operating costs therefore 
need to be taken into consideration.  
Instead of going towards more sophisticated systems which imply more embodied energy, 
another possible solution is to minimise the use of materials and aim for simplicity. 
 
Several researches worked in that direction (Bradanini, 2016; Christer, 2018; Manso, 2014; 
Richard Beckett, Marcos Cruz, Chris Leung, Sandra Manso-Blanco, 2016) with the goal of 
developing more integrated systems in which the building element of a facade itself can fulfil 
the role of a substrate for plants. This objective can be achieved by using epiphytes, such as 
mosses, ferns or lichens. Epiphytes are organisms that grow on the surface of other plants or 
objects (called lithophytic in the particular case of rocky substrates (Pérez-Urrestarazu et al., 
2015)) and draw their nutrients exclusively from the air or rain. The connection to their 
substrate only serves for physical support, and they are not parasitic (Petruzello, 2017). By 
using epiphytes, a building facade can play the role of substrate without needing to provide 
nutrients, simplifying its task. There is therefore no need for roots in the ground, planter boxes 
or hydroponics. However, the relationship between the substrate and the plant is still very 
complex and interactions occur.  
Evidently, examples of unwanted natural colonization of man-made surfaces are common and 
usually seen as problematic. The study of the natural colonization of building materials is 
commonly conducted from a biodeterioration and biodegradation point of view (Manso, 2014). 
Nevertheless, researchers have recently started studying such phenomenon to learn how to 
control and induce them voluntarily, with the goal of creating self-sustaining epiphytic green 
facades.  
 
These organisms can grow on mineral surfaces without the need for roots in a soil substrate. In 
a typical ivy green facade, the interaction between the facade and the plant consists in rootlets 
punctually anchored to the wall, while the actual substrate is the soil at its base or an 
intermediate planter box. In the context of epiphytic green walls, the interactions between the 
mineral wall facade and the organism are stronger and more complex than with ivy rootlets 
that simply provide punctual anchor points. The factors of porosity, pH value or surface 
geometry play a crucial role in these interactions and must be further investigated. These factors 
are key in the concept of bioreceptivity, which is an important notion for this project. 
Bioreceptivity is defined by the aptitude of a material to be colonized by one or several groups 
of living organisms without necessarily undergoing any biodeterioration (Guillite, 1995). This 
notion will be further discussed in section 3.2. With bioreceptivity as a framework, numerous 
==PAGE:22==

researches have been conducted and some realisations have already been completed with the 
aim of transforming regular building material into biological substrate. 
 
One of the relevant examples of epiphytic green facades is the Reykjavík City Hall’s ‘moss 
wall’ designed by the Icelandic architecture firm Studio Granda. Although the firm had already 
experimented with green roofs or green facades, in line with the traditional Icelandic 
construction methods, they were here innovating with a concept of cementitious wall as direct 
substrate for the growth of mosses. The facade of the wall consists of basalt stones cast in a 
concrete bed onto which mosses of all kinds have taken hold. A key aspect is the constant feed 
of water to the mosses. A small pump is distributing water over the whole surface of the wall 
that then drips into a large pond from which the water was pumped. The current project has a 
filiation to this ‘moss wall’ since it is Steve Christer from Studio Granda who came up with 
the idea of a lichen covered facade after working on the ‘moss wall’ of Reykjavík. Christer 
(2018) stated that he would have preferred a totally self-sustaining system rather than use a 
pump, hence the idea of the lichen wall, more resistant to desiccation. 
 
 
Figure 4: Reykjavík City Hall’s ‘moss wall’. (Alter, 2012) 
 
The Reykjavík experiment shows the possibility of direct colonization of a building element 
by epiphytic organisms. These organisms, in this case mostly mosses, are taking most of their 
nutrients from the air, and don’t need to be rooted in the ground (Petruzello, 2017). 
 
Sandra Manso (2014) studied the bioreceptivity of various mineral materials to assess the 
possibility of natural colonization of building surfaces by epiphytic organisms. Her approach 
was, on one hand, to work on the modification of the chemical and physical properties of 
mineral materials, and, on the other hand, evaluate their bioreceptivity in both laboratory and 
environmental conditions. The experiment was focused on low-pH cementitious materials 
using Magnesium Phosphate Cement (MPC) and Ordinary Portland Cement (OPC) with 
accelerated carbonation. Her research combines both theoretical and experimental components 
(Manso, 2014). After positive results in terms of bioreceptivity for the MPC samples in this 
experiment, Manso designed a solution of a multi-layered panel using MPCs. The research led 
to an international patent for this multi-layer solution.

Larger scale experimentations were pursued within the Bartlett School of Architecture in the 
BiotA lab, with a stronger focus on surface geometry, with which Manso was involved. The 
project consisted of monitoring the biofouling of facade elements. Large facade panels were 
designed with different computer-generated surface geometries and underwent an accelerated 
carbonation. Both MPC and OPC were used as well as an internal seeding for biological 
growth. The panels were set in an outdoor environment for 12 months while being monitored 
for natural colonization (Richard Beckett, Marcos Cruz, Chris Leung, Sandra Manso-Blanco, 
2016). The project, however, did not provide any substantial results, and no remarkable 
advantage for MPCs were observed (Beckett, 2017). Beckett (2017) stated that the possible 
reasons for the lack of observable results in the project were the limited timespan of the project 
and the new scale factor of the panels compared to the proposition by Manso (2014).

Within the realm of possible epiphytic organisms colonizing a mineral substrate, the current 
project focuses on lichens.  Some research has already been carried out in this specific area. 
The idea of using lichens to colonize building facades was explored in 2011 by the artist 
Elisabeth Demaray in her Lichen for Skyscrapers Project. She developed a crushed lichen 
slurry with biologist Natalie Howe that she applied on several New York City landmarks as a 
way to connect New Yorkers with the natural world and exhibit the stunning variety of colours 
and shapes of this little known organism (Eveleth, 2011).  
Bradanini (2016) specifically discusses the potential of lichens in a research on an integrated 
green facade with building material as direct substrate. The project was a precursor to the 
current research and was part of the Nordic Built (STED) project too. Bradanini (2016) 
experimented on 15 different rock and cementitious samples, including variations of MPC 
mixes. The samples were tested for lichen growth in an outdoor environment. The species of 
lichen used were Xanthoria parietina and Physcia tenalla collected from both the local Danish 
environment and from Iceland. In addition, five sets of samples were produced to be tested 
with different orientations, angles and sheltering conditions. 
Parallel to this, a second experiment in an indoor environment was conducted to assess the 
influence of pore size on the bioreceptivity of the samples. Even though the growth period of 
the lichens was very limited (37 days), several conclusions were drawn. 
It was observed that, as expected, the local lichens were found in higher quantities on most of 
the samples’ surfaces than the Icelandic ones, suggesting that lichens prefer the climatic 
==PAGE:24==

conditions of the location from which they were collected. From the set of samples, the basalt 
samples showed the highest bioreceptivity for both Icelandic and Danish lichens. Also, 
sheltering the lichens from heavy rain proved to be beneficial for growth. Finally, similarly to 
the BiotA lab project, the cementitious materials tested (MPC and OPC) showed no advantage 
and a lower degree of bioreceptivity when compared with the other mineral samples. 
Regarding the second experiment, it was shown that the pore size and bioreceptivity are di- 
rectly correlated, and that specimens with higher pore sizes were colonized faster and more 
intensively. However, this was not proven for lichens specifically as other organisms colonised 
the samples. (Bradanini, 2016) 
The examples above provide inspiration for the current project and give insight into what has 
been achieved so far.

3     THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
3.1  Lichen studies 
 
Lichens are unique life forms characterized by a symbiotic relationship between a fungus and 
a photosynthetic organism such as an alga (Lutzoni, François and Miadlikowska, 2009). The 
fungus is referred as the mycobiont, which provides protection, minerals and water to the 
symbiotic partner. The algae or cyanobacteria is referred to as the photobiont, providing 
organic nutrients through photosynthesis (Bradanini, 2016). This unique mutual relationship 
allows the lichen to colonize inorganic materials. Because of the complexity of this cooperative 
inter-species relationship, lichens are considered to be a challenging topic for field and 
laboratory research (W. L. Culberson, 1985), and as a consequence relatively few scientific 
articles have been published in this area. More research has been made to prevent their 
spreading on building materials than to discover the factors that make them thrive. Also, lichens 
are some of the slowest growing living organisms, which make their study difficult and tedious. 
Experimental aspects of lichenology are then lagging far behind the descriptive studies due to 
this difficulty in culturing lichens (Verma, 2011). 
 
The most remarkable traits of lichens are their very slow growth, longevity, tolerance to 
extreme environments and sensitivity to air pollution (Lutzoni, François and Miadlikowska, 
2009). 
In most lichens, it is the fungus that builds the structural tissues of the thallus (body) (Sanders, 
2001) in the upper and lower cortex while the algae are sitting in an in-between layer. The 
lower cortex attaches to the substrate by means of small supports called rhizines. 
 
  
Figure 6: Foliose lichen cross section. (E.M. Collins, 2000) 
 
Lichens are typically found on tree bark or mineral surfaces and can be spotted in every region 
of the world ranging from deserts to polar areas (Kim, M. K., Park, H., & Oh, 2013). 
The major factors influencing the growth of lichens are the substrate, humidity and lighting 
conditions and the temperature. 
The reproduction process of lichens is defined by the two types of reproduction of the 
mycobiont. The mycobiont can either reproduce asexually or sexually. If it reproduces 
asexually, the thalli ejects vegetative spores called soredia that contain both mycobiont and 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
 
THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
26 
 
photobiont partners and can therefore create a new thallus without the need for external input 
(Ott, 1987).  
 
If it reproduces sexually, fungal spores are dispersed from the thalli to germinate on the 
substrate and will need to find an external compatible photobiont to resynthesize into new thalli 
and achieve symbiosis; a process called re-lichenization (Lutzoni, François and Miadlikowska, 
2009).  
There are several ways for a spore to find a compatible photobiont. Firstly, the spore can find 
a free-living compatible photobiont in the air and assimilate it to start a symbiosis (Ott, 1987). 
However, a compatible free-living photobiont is not always easy to find and, in some cases, 
even very rare. Adaptive methods have been observed, such as obtaining it from the soredia of 
a foreign lichen or directly from an adjacent thallus. Also, the extraordinary resistance to 
desiccation of the photobiont often allows it to live long enough for a spore to find it and 
overcome the issue of scarcity (Lutzoni, François and Miadlikowska, 2009).  
 
 
3.1.1 Xanthoria parietina 
 
The selection of the lichen species has an important impact on the choice of seeding method 
and growth environment for experiments. The species selected for the current project is the 
foliose macrolichen Xanthoria parietina (X. parietina). The most striking criteria is its aesthetic 
aspect. X. parietina has a bright yellow to orange coloration. The species is common and 
widespread all around the world, has a high resistance to pollution and has a preference for 
relatively high pH rock or bark substrates (Armstrong, 1990). The species is also one of the 
most researched lichens (Honegger, 1996). These aspects made X. parietina the most 
appropriate choice of species to experiment on in the present project.  
X. parietina’s properties can be seen through its different classifications. It belongs to the 
foliose type, meaning that it grows radially in flattened leafy thalli (Chowdhury, 2015), it is a 
chlorolichen, implying that the photobiont is green alga (Honegger & Mary, 1996) and is 
nitrophytic, which refers to its preference for nitrogen-rich environments (Munzi, Branquinho, 
Cruz, & Loppi, 2013). X. parietina thalli have been observed to grow radially around 6mm per 
year in a favourable environment (Honegger, Conconi, & Kutasi, 1996). 
X. parietina reproduces only sexually, meaning that an external compatible alga has to meet 
the mycobiont spore for a new generation to start. However, as it is often the case, Trebouxia 
or Pseudotrebouxia, the compatible algae for X. parietina is rarely found free-living. Although 
Bubrick, Galun, & Frensdorf (1984) have proved the existence of free-living Trebouxia, X. 
parietina has developed the alternative method of capturing Trebouxia or Pseudotrebouxia 
from the soredia of foreign lichen species that have this same photobiont partner (Ott, 1987). 
This phenomenon called ‘cleptobiosis’ is typical with crustose lichens but seems to be less 
common with microlichens. However, this ability of X. parietina allows it to prosper by taking 
hold of compatible photobionts found in Physcia spp. such as Physcia tanella, also widespread 
in Europe (Honegger et al., 1996). 
 
Even though the creation of lichen facades is a very recent objective, several experiments have 
been carried out to resynthesize or transplant lichens to a new substrate. No specific example 
using concrete as substrate has been found other than the study of Bradanini (2016), however, 
several successful attempts on the re-synthesis of lichens from their isolated symbionts were 
conducted both in indoor and outdoor environments (Booth, 1971; Bubrick & Galun, 1986; 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
 
THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
27 
 
Culberson & Ahmadjian, 1980; Fahselt, 2003; Honegger, 1996). The transplantation of a 
thallus under natural conditions has been completed successfully (e.g. Armstrong (1977), 
Honegger (1996)), however, it has been proven very difficult to maintain lichens in a healthy 
condition or get significant growth in thalli transferred to the laboratory (Booth, 1971). 
 
 
3.2  Bioreceptivity and other properties of 
concrete 
 
As stated earlier, the properties included in the term bioreceptivity are both physical and 
chemical properties, including the surface roughness, the porosity, the permeability, the 
chemical composition and the pH value (Guillite, 1995; Manso, 2014). To investigate the use 
of concrete as a substrate for lichen growth, those properties were adapted and reformulated to 
become the main parameters on which to base the experimental strategy. The chosen 
parameters are composition, which is defined by the production of different mix designs, 
porosity, pH value and surface geometry. These factors influence the bioreceptivity and thus 
help to determine the experimentation strategy.  
The mix design is the selection of ingredients composing the concrete and their proportions. It 
is the most crucial factor in defining the properties of a concrete. An appropriate mix design 
will define a series of aspects of the fresh and hardened concrete, such as the consistency, the 
rate of stiffening, the strength, the durability etc. In a large production scale, the selection of 
materials is constrained by several factors such as the availability and cost, the conformity with 
standards or the conformity with the specifications of a contract, e.g. the maximum aggregate 
size (Dewar, 2003). 
While the basic ingredients of concrete are cement, water, sand and gravel, the relationship 
between the cement and water is the most critical. In the presence of water, the silicates and 
aluminates contained in the cement will form products of hydration which will result in a hard 
mass called the hydrated cement paste (Neville, 1995c). This key reaction transforming the 
cement into a bonding agent is the cement hydration. The cement hydration is a complex 
reaction the rate of which decreases over time but still occurs long after the concrete is put in 
use. 
One of its defining factors is the ratio of weight of water to cement (w/c), which is generally 
within 0.3 and 0.6 (Taylor, 1990). A lower w/c will typically increase the strength and the 
durability of the mix but will decrease its workability (Neville, 1995d). However, the 
workability is also influenced by the aggregate/cement ratio and the total water content 
(Neville, 1995b) and can be modified by the use of admixtures such as plasticizers.  
In terms of porosity, the w/c also plays an important role. Higher w/c will, for example, result 
in higher total porosity and higher air permeability (Kim, Lee, Bang, & Kwon, 2014). 
 
One of the main deterioration mechanisms of concrete is the carbonation reaction. Carbonation 
of concrete can be a negative factor, for example, in the case of the oxidation of steel 
reinforcement (Neville, 1995a), but constitutes a beneficial factor in the attempt to create a 
bioreceptive material. The carbonation reaction designates the reaction between the cement 
paste and atmospheric CO2 in the presence of moisture. The reaction occurs progressively from 
the outer surface of the concrete to the core at a decreasing rate while the CO2 diffuses through 
the pore system of the concrete (Neville, 1995a). It is indeed an important aspect for the current 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
28 
 
research because of the effect that carbonation has on the pH value of concrete. Fresh Portland 
cement concrete is a very alkaline material, and therefore not suitable for biological growth. 
However, the reaction of carbonation can reduce the pH of Portland cement paste from 12.6-
13.5 to about 9, and even as low as 8.3 after a complete carbonation (Neville, 1995a).  
 
Carbonation of Portland cement concrete is relatively slow, considering that in normal open-
air conditions, the surface only reaches acceptable pH for biological colonization after several 
years. It then constitutes a significant challenge for the current work. 
A potential work around to this problem can be to accelerate the carbonation process and 
therefore reach a suitable pH in a shorter term. This can be achieved by altering the parameters 
responsible for carbonation: moisture and the presence of CO2. The carbonation will, for 
example, occur faster underwater (Neville, 1995a) but it can also be done by increasing the 
CO2 content in the surrounding air or water (Beckett, 2017; Christer, 2018; Manso, 2014). 
 
 
3.2.1 Supplementary cementitious materials 
 
After identifying some important aspects of concrete, it is possible to investigate methods of 
modifying the mix design to positively influence the properties of the concrete. It was decided 
that Supplementary Cementitious Materials (SCM) be used in the mix, more precisely 
limestone and calcined clay. This choice of materials was motivated both for their potential 
effect on bioreceptivity and embodied environmental qualities. 
 
SCMs are inorganic materials contributing to the hydraulic or pozzolanic activity in a 
cementitious mixture (ASTM, 2015). For example, blast-furnace slags, fly ashes or silica fumes 
can partially substitute Portland cement in a concrete mix and influence its properties 
(Lothenbach, Scrivener, & Hooton, 2011). 
The clinkering process, during which the cement clinker is produced at a calcination 
temperature of ±1450 °C, requires large amounts of energy and is responsible for most of the 
CO2 emissions of the concrete production (K. Scrivener, 2018). By partially replacing the 
cement with SCMs, and therefore reducing the amounts of clinker produced, there is significant 
potential for reducing carbon emissions. Additionally, a number of the SCMs used are by-
products from industrials processes, allowing a diminution of virgin resource consumption in 
the concrete production (K. Scrivener, 2018). 
 
 
3.2.1.1 
Limestone and calcined clay 
 
For the specific purposes of the project, the objectives pursued using SCMs were as follows, 
an acceleration of the carbonation process, a more suitable chemical composition, and a lower 
environmental impact. To reach these goals, the decision was made to use of a blend of 
limestone and calcined clay to partly substitute Portland cement. 
In the implementation of SCMs in the concrete industry, one of the pressing issues is the 
availability of the most widely used SCMs, which might be put at risk considering predictions 
for overall concrete demand, especially in developing countries. Recent innovations (K. 
Scrivener, 2018; Karen Scrivener, Vanderley M, & Gartner, 2016) have permitted a significant 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
 
THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
29 
 
substitution of the Portland cement with a certain ratio of calcined clay and limestone while 
maintaining similar mechanical performances. Both these materials are available worldwide 
and in great quantity. The calcination of clay requires much lower temperatures than clinker, 
around 700-850 °C, and therefore does not rely on sophisticated equipment to be produced. 
This solution therefore potentially lowers the costs, availability constraints and the 
environmental impact of the concrete production. With a replacement of 50% of the clinker in 
the mix, the CO2 emissions savings have been reported  to reach 30% (K. Scrivener, 2018). 
Besides its sustainability, the limestone and calcined clay blend solution offers interesting 
perspectives for the current project. As stated earlier, the main advantage regarding the project 
is its faster carbonation rate. Containing less calcium, the blend has less capacity to bind CO2 
during carbonation (K. Scrivener, 2018), lowering the buffering capacity against CO2 diffusion. 
The rate of carbonation is then increased with the degree of substitution (Lagerblad, 2005). 
One additional characteristic of the use of SCMs is its impact on workability. The high fineness 
of the calcined clay reduces the workability of the mix and may require additional use of 
plasticizers. 
 
 
3.2.1.2 
Sewage sludge ash concrete 
 
In the course of the project, the opportunity was presented to use additional mortar samples 
produced as part of a previous project focusing on the use of Sewage Sludge Ashes (SSA). It 
was decided to include them in the set of samples of the project because of their more advanced 
stage of carbonation, due to their age, and because of the time saving opportunity to test on 
additional readymade samples. Different texture patterns on the surface were also a point of 
interest in these samples. 
These samples were developed with the aim of upcycling SSA, a residue of the thermal 
treatment of sewage sludge destined for landfill, into a mortar mix while extracting its 
phosphorous content, an essential resource in agriculture that is alarmingly in depletion 
(Kappel, Viader, Kowalski, Kirkelund, & Ottosen, 2018). In doing so, the project has a dual 
objective of acting on both the issues of waste and phosphorous depletion with a concrete that 
only suffers a minimal strength loss. 
 
 
3.2.2 Surface geometry 
 
One of the critical factors in the creation of a suitable microclimate for lichen growth is the 
surface geometry of the substrate. Much more so than the porosity and the microstructure of 
the concrete, the surface geometry can strongly influence the moisture level and the drying 
time of the concrete. Depending on the roughness, the regularity or the presence of cavities on 
the surface, the resulting hygrometric environment can be drastically different. It should also 
be considered that these factors play a role in the lichens’ ability to attach to the substrate. 
In order to test the influence of the surface geometry, different surface treatments have been 
selected. A total of six variations have been designed and applied to each concrete mix.  
The first pair of treatments are creating two flat surfaces with different degrees of roughness.  
The second pair of treatments is a tree branch texture imitation. It has been noticed that the 
bark of a tree and its branches are generally a very good substrate for the growth of X. parietina 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
 
THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
30 
 
and other lichens. To take advantage of that observation, a set of samples was designed to 
recreate the qualities of the irregular texture of a tree branch on the concrete. 
The last two treatments involve the incorporation of basalt stones. It is concluded in the work 
of Bradanini (2016) that the basalt samples were the most prone to lichen colonization. The use 
of basalt in his research was suggested by Steve Christer from Studio Granda, who himself 
used basalt stones in the crafting of the Reykjavík City Hall’s ‘moss wall’. The use of basalt 
stones in the ‘moss wall’ was very successful in enabling a thick layer of moss to grow. For 
these reasons, it was decided to create a set of samples of which the surfaces contain basalt 
stones, by imitating the construction process of Studio Granda’s ‘moss wall’ on a smaller scale. 
 
 
3.2.3 Water transport and porosity 
 
Within the context of this project, it is important to understand the properties of the materials 
we are working with in terms of water transport and porosity. Therefore, these terms must be 
clearly defined. In our context, ‘water transport’ groups two distinct aspects: permeability and 
sorption. Neville (1995a) defines the porosity of concrete as a measure of the proportion of the 
total volume occupied by pores, while permeability is defined as a flow through a porous 
medium. The correlation between both notions is only partial: only the open porosity is 
contributing to the permeability through interconnected pores (Neville, 1995a). The closed 
pores do not influence the permeability since no flow can occur. Sorption is defined as the 
result of capillary movement in the concrete pores which are open to an ambient medium, and 
can only happen in partially dry concrete (Neville, 1995a). 
 
To obtain information about the water transport and porosity properties of the concrete samples 
used in the project, a wide range of tests are available. Four different tests were carried out: 
 
• A drying test, consisting of monitoring the weight changes of concrete samples subject 
to a decreasing relative humidity in a climate chamber. 
• 
A test of determination of real and apparent density, total and open porosity, according 
to the standard SIA 262-1. 
• 
A Karsten tube test, consisting of a non-destructive test designed to measure water 
penetration on a mineral surface imitating the effect of driving rain on a building’s 
surface. 
• 
The observation of a thin section of samples under a microscope.

4.1.2 Sample casting and curing 
 
All the casting and concrete production was done in the mortar room in the concrete 
laboratory of the Department of Civil Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark.  
To begin the casting process cement, aggregates and eventual limestone and calcined clay were 
poured into a 5l mixing bowl, water was then added. The concrete was then mixed in a 
HOBART planetary mixer at low speed for 1 minute and 30 seconds. The concrete rested for 
five minutes while the edges of the bowl were scraped, and the eventual admixtures were 
added. The concrete was then mixed for 1 minute at high speed before being poured into 14 x 
14 x 2.5 cm oiled wooden moulds. The mould was then placed on a vibrating table for five 
minutes then sat for 24 ± 2 hours at 20 °C. After being unmoulded, the samples cured for 7 
days in deionised water at 20 °C. The SSA samples, with dimensions of 8 x 7 x 2.5 cm had 
been cured at 20 °C for 28 days (40 days for the SSA ED samples) (Kappel, 2018). 
 
 
4.1.3 Carbonation process 
 
After all the samples had been cured in water for 7 days, they sat for a further 25 days in a 
sodium bicarbonate solution for accelerated carbonation in a large insulated bath. The solution 
was composed of 30g/l of sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3) in deionised water and was 
maintained at 20 ± 1 °C. The SSA samples underwent no specific accelerated carbonation 
process.

4.1.4.2 
Basalt stones 
The methodology to produce the basalt stone samples was fine-tuned through a series of trials 
using different quantities of retarder and different setting times. The ideal solution was obtained 
by pouring SIKA Retarder over the bottom surface of the mould until an even film was formed. 
The basalt stones were then laid evenly on that surface up to half of the depth of the mould. 
The mould was then filled with concrete and vibrated for five minutes. The sample was 
demoulded after 24 hours and the bottom surface placed under running tap water to wash off 
the concrete that was in contact with the retarder and was therefore still fresh. At this point the

4.2.2 Drying test  
 
 
  
 
 
Figure 11 : Climate chamber for the drying test (left) and scheme of the testing sample (right) 
 
The sections of the concrete prisms used in this test were both ends of each prism cut at a length 
of 0.71cm. The volume of the cut sample was then 4 x 4 x 0.71 cm, which represents a scaled 
version of the concrete samples used in the growth experiment with an identical surface area 
to volume ratio. The sample was then placed in a Petri dish, lying on its cut surface and tested 
in the climate chamber. In doing so, most of the moisture exchange was made through the 
external surfaces of the original sample. The test is therefore as representative as possible of 
the drying behaviour of the concrete tiles in the growth experiment. 
In addition, each sample tested was made twice in order to assess and compare the influence 
of the peptone solution on the drying process of one of them. To do so, the solution was coated 
on the top of its surface with a brush. 
 
Three sets of samples were tested. The first set was tested after the samples cured in water for 
7 days. The two other sets were tested on day 33 after having either cured in water or in sodium 
bicarbonate solution for accelerated carbonation and therefore having followed the same 
treatment as the concrete tiles. 
 
The samples were vacuum-saturated in water before sitting in the climate chamber at an initial 
relative humidity of 97% and was gradually decreased to 90, 80 and 70 % every 72 ± 2 hours. 
 
 
4.2.3 Determination of real and apparent density, total 
and open porosity 
 
This test has been executed according to the Swiss standard SIA 262-1. The goal of the test is 
to obtain information on the density of the mix designs created, both real and apparent, as well 
as the porosity, both total and open.  
8 types of samples were tested, consisting of samples of each of the 4 mix designs that were 
either carbonated or not. Each sample was made twice in order to obtain a mean value, resulting 
in a total of 16 samples tested.

4.2.4 Karsten tube test 
 
The Karsten tube test was performed with a MOHREN Karsten tube for horizontal surfaces 
sealed on the surface of the sample with a blue putty, in a room at 45 ± 5% RH and 21 ± 2°C. 
The tube was filled with deionised water and the water level was monitored over 48 hours. 
The tube was topped up whenever the level dropped by more than 2 ml to keep a constant 
pressure above the sample. To avoid any evaporation from the tube, it was capped with a thin 
plastic foil (Hendrickx, 2013). 
The test was carried out on carbonated and non-carbonated 4 x 4 x 6.5 cm samples of each mix 
design after being dried for 24 hours in an oven at 50°C and cooled in a desiccator. 
Leaking problems made it necessary to take the test several times after spending an extra 24 
hours drying in the oven at 50°C.

4.3  Lichen seeding 
4.3.1 Lichen collection 
The specimens of X. parietina used in the growth experiment were collected on both oak tree

4.3.2 Seeding methods 
4.3.2.1 
Growth media 
 
The peptone suspension used for the inoculation of the lichens seemed to favour the adhesion 
of the lichens thalli to the surfaces and to accelerate the colonization process (Bradanini, 2016).  
The growth media is a solution of peptone from casein. The peptone powder from casein 
(pancreatic digest for microbiology, SIGMA-ALDRICH) was dissolved in water in a ratio of 
1:2. The crushed lichen flakes were added to the solution in a ratio of 1:6 obtaining a dense and 
thick solution. 
4.3.2.2 
Regular application 
 
The first method of lichen seeding involved spreading the crushed lichen solution over the 
sample’s surface with a brush in a 1-2 mm layer. 
4.3.2.3 
Prior algae covering 
 
Blue-green alga (or cyanobacteria) was collected from several concrete curing baths of the 
concrete laboratory of the Technical University of Denmark. The alga had developed in the 
standing water and settled on the inner walls of the baths, from which it was scraped and stored. 
The alga was left to dry at room temperature for 72 hours then grinded into a fine powder. The 
alga was then mixed with water in a 1:6 ratio and applied on the samples with a small brush. 
Three layers of alga were applied with a 5 days interval between the coating of each layer. The 
lichen flake solution was then applied 24 hours after the final alga layer.

Transplantation 
 
Whole and intact thalli were necessary for the transplantation method. They were collected on

4.4  Climate room setup 
 
The climate room is a 3 x 6.5 m sealed and insulated room located in building 119 of DTU. 
Two insulated doors separated by an airlock insure a minimal air exchange with the exterior. 
A structure of two supporting tables was assembled to support the 44 concrete samples and the 
lighting fixtures.  
 
 
 
Figure 16 : Scheme of the climate room setup 
To control the relative humidity in the room, a system composed of an electric humidifier 
(Axair AG 8808), a water tank and a BOSCH fan has been put together. The system is regulated 
by a humidity controller (Sauter HSC 120) that is insuring a constant relative humidity of 90 ± 
5 % in the room.  
Rainwater was collected from the run-off of a lichen colonized roof in the immediate vicinity 
of where the lichen was sampled. This rainwater was manually sprayed daily on each sample 
to provide nutrients from environments in which lichens grow. 
 
To insure ideal lighting conditions, 10 LED growing light bulbs (3.5V lm Grow Lights AC 85-
265V) were installed on the set-up. Each lamp is positioned at a distance of 25 cm from the 
table and distributes its light on four samples. The intensity of the light in the centre of the 
samples has a mean value of 417 lx and the extreme values are 171 lx at the outer corners and 
520 lx immediately below the lamp. The lamp’s light spectrum fulfils the needs of the algae as 
the major wavelengths required for photosynthesis are present. A ‘day and night’ schedule is 
set up to simulate a daily light cycle. The lights are turned on for 16 hours and turned off for 
the remaining 8 hours of every day.  
The temperature in the room remained at a constant 23 ±1 °C through the whole experiment. 
The climate room experiment lasted 11 weeks, after which the samples were moved and placed 
outside in a shaded area for 3 weeks to assess their re-hydration capacity. The 44 samples were 
placed on the lower table of the supporting structure, supported from the rain by the top table. 
Being conducted across June and July, the outdoor experiment offered lighting and temperature 
conditions that were not available at the start of the project.

5.1  Carbonation 
 
The main goal of accelerating the carbonation process of the samples was to lower the pH 
making it suitable for biological growth. The accelerated carbonation was carried out in a 
NaHCO3 solution for 25 days. 
The pH of the NaHCO3 solution was tested during the carbonation process with ColorpHast pH 
indicator strips. The pH of the solution was around 8.8 when the samples were added to the 
bath and gradually rose to around 9.5 after 25 days. 
The pH of the samples was tested by means of a phenolphthalein test. Phenolphthalein is a 
colourless acid-base indicator that turns purple above a pH of 8.3 (Petruševski & Risteska, 
2007). When the chemical is poured on a freshly cut concrete surface, it is then possible to 
evaluate the carbonation depth of a sample through the resulting colour change. 
The test was carried out on the 4 mix designs after the 25 days carbonation process as well as 
on the 2.5 year old SSA samples. 
 
As shown in figure 17, the 4 mix designs showed a visible carbonation depth, implying that the 
pH on the surface and down to a certain depth was below 8.3, being therefore suitable for the 
growth requirements of X. parietina, which doesn’t have any roots or interactions below the 
surface of the samples. 
As expected, the LC samples showed the deepest carbonation. Additionally, LC 5, with its 
higher w/c, had the deepest carbonation of either LC samples. The AER sample showed a less 
consistent depth, however overall deeper than PC, presumably as a result of the presence of 
additional air-filled pores in the microstructure. 
 
 
Figure 17 : Carbonation depth of the four mix designs after 25 days in a NaHCO3 solution 
 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
 
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
44 
 
The lichen X. parietina generally grows on slightly alkaline surfaces such as limestone or 
asbestos (Armstrong, 1990; Bradanini, 2016; Welch, Gillman, & John, 2006). The carbonation 
observation therefore suggests that the pH of each sample is suitable for the growth of 
Xanthoria parietina, with a higher degree of suitability for the LC samples, which present a 
consistent and substantial carbonation depth. 
No visible carbonation was observed when the same test was carried out with 33 days old 
samples which had stayed continuously in water. This observation confirms the efficiency of 
the NaHCO3 treatment.  
Finally, phenolphthalein was also applied on the 2.5 years old SSA samples revealing different 
degrees of carbonation. The SSA ED and SSAacid, from which the phosphorous was extracted, 
did not react to the chemical, suggesting a complete carbonation. The SSA 1 0s and 10m had a 
carbonation depth of 7mm and 10mm respectively, while the Portland cement (ref) sample had 
5mm. These differences relate to the same principle of cement substitution. 
 
5.2  Water transport and porosity study 
5.2.1 Drying test 
 
The drying test provided a large amount of data for both the sorption and the desorption phase. 
As the RH steps of the test correspond to the range of monthly mean value of outdoor relative 
humidity found in Copenhagen (70% to 90%) (DMI, 1999), we can assume that the data 
obtained in the test can be compared to outdoor behaviour in the Danish climate.  
 
The most indicative stages of the experiment are the high RH steps as well as the first 
measurement after a new RH regime, showing the most drastic fluctuations. The full set of data 
collected in the test were organised into different graphs.  
 
The full graph in figure 18a shows these pronounced fluctuations in the high humidity ranges. 
It is clearly shown for example in the 97-90% or 80-90% RH steps, in which the curves of the 
carbonated samples are steeper, illustrating a quicker water loss. The AER samples lost a large 
amount of water early in the desorption phase which they did not recover at the end of the test. 
In figure 18b, a partial desorption hysteresis curve is drawn. The curves displays the weight 
changes of the samples after 3 days at a given RH step. Such a curve displays the extent to 
which a sample’s water content is dependent on its history; a phenomenon called hysteresis. A 
high hysteresis translates into a large weight gap between the desorption and sorption phases 
at the same RH. 
The profile of all the uncarbonated curves is very similar, with a linear desorption phase and a 
slightly exponential sorption phase. The highest hysteresis is seen on the uncarbonated LC 
samples. Schiller et al. (2016) suggests that a pronounced hysteresis in similar mix designs can 
be explained by a higher density of micropores and small pores. However, the carbonated LC 
samples (called LCC in this chapter, where the additional C indicates the carbonated samples) 
show a different profile with a less pronounced hysteresis and a higher total weight change, 
suggesting that the carbonation process modified the microstructure of the pores towards an 
overall higher permeability. These observations are consistent with both w/c but more 
pronounced in LC 5C. PC and AER samples present very similar curves except for an important 
water loss in the first RH step for the AER samples.

5.2.5 Conclusion 
High humidity is a crucial factor for the lichens photosynthetic activity (Gauslaa, 2014); it is 
thus essential to study the water transport and porosity properties of the substrates to maintain 
good growing conditions. Both the drying test and the Karsten tube test show that the 
accelerated carbonation of the LC 45C and LC 5C samples has the effect of increasing the 
infiltration through the surface and that the speed of water exchange is high for relative 
humidity changes.  
The Karsten tube test did not show any difference between both PC samples. However, it must 
be mentioned that the dimension of the samples used in the drying test (4x4x0,71cm) are 
increasing the influence of the carbonation on the results. Because of the scale factor, it can be 
assumed that this influence is of lesser importance in the actual size of the samples (14x14x2,5 
cm). It also explains why the carbonation doesn’t show a consistent influence on the results of 
the density and porosity test, where the samples dimensions where 4x4x4,4 cm. 
==PAGE:49==
 
 
 
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
49 
 
From all the tests conducted, it is shown that the accelerated carbonation process has an effect 
on the water transport and porosity of the samples, especially for both LC mix designs and that 
this effect is inversely proportional to the volume of the samples. It is shown also that the LC 
45C and LC 5C samples are the most permeable, and are therefore likely to dry more quickly, 
which constitutes a disadvantage to sustain a high humidity at the surface of the concrete for 
the growth of lichens.

5.3.1 Visual and photographic observations 
The growth experiment was conducted over 11 weeks in an indoor environment in a climate 
room then moved outdoors for 3 weeks. 
Generally, the lichens applied both in the peptone solution and transplanted showed signs of 
degradation throughout the growth experiments. The most evident observation is the gradual 
change of colour. From a homogeneous green colour, the lichen darkened and turned 
brown/orange (figure 23, 24). From the beginning, the peptone solution formed a white layer 
over the lichens, which slowly washed off during the first weeks of the experiment with the 
manual spraying (figure 25). Some of the crushed lichen-flakes also washed off while part of 
it attached to the substrate. Some of the lichens seemed to slightly shrink and lose volume, and 
the substrate became visible through the bulk of some thalli that previously had a full coverage.

To assess the health conditions of the lichens after the climate room experiment, the 
observations were divided in two distinct types of deteriorations. The first type is the colour 
change, which indicates biological factors such as the synthesis of screening pigments  
(Gauslaa, 2014; Gauslaa & Ustvedt, 2003; Knut Asbjørn Solhaug, Gauslaa, Nybakken, & 
Bilger, 2003) or the concentration of photosynthesising green algae (Honegger, 2003). The 
second type of deterioration is the drying damage, appearing as a structural change in the thalli 
linked to water depletion. After visual observations, scores were given to each sample based 
on the colour change and the drying damages. To account for the complexity of the structural 
changes due to drying damages, the score was obtained as mean value of specific factors: 
washing off, detachment from the substrate, volume loss and loss of cover. 
 
Consistent differences between both LC mix designs and the rest were observed. Figure 26 
shows the mean score values of the samples grouped by mix design for darkening and drying 
damages. While LC 45 and LC 5 samples suffered more from drying damages, their colour 
remained brighter.  
 
The SSA samples were only tested with normal lichen solution seeding and did not show any 
sign of resynthesis. The colour change seemed similar to the LC 45 samples, but no conclusion 
should be drawn considering the reduced size and number of each sample.

and a colour shift towards brighter shades. However, it was observed that the LC 45 and LC 5 
samples, which had suffered more from drying damages, had overall a better re-hydration

5.3.1.2 
Seeding method comparison 
 
Another important parameter investigated in the experiment is the seeding method. From the 
three seeding methods (solution, prior algae cover and transplantation), only the transplantation 
seemed to work out as expected. No resynthesized thallus was observed growing from the 
crushed lichen solution, but it seems that the lichens were still active and behaving as individual 
fragments of thalli.  
The blue-green algae scraped from the concrete baths that were used for the algae cover were 
selected for their alleged capacity to serve as temporary photobiont for X. parietina. Ott (1987) 
states that foreign algae of the Pleurococcus-type were found coupled with X. parietina before 
starting a true symbiosis with its adequate algal partner. Bessey (1914) characterized blue-
green algae as part of the Pleurococcus-type. However, the prior algae cover did ultimately not 
seem to help the resynthesis.  
The transplantation showed better results with clear signs of attachment to the substrate, despite 
the colour change endured by the thalli.  
5.3.2 Spectroscopic measurements 
 
After defining the lichen covered area through NDVI measurements on each sample submitted 
to DFM, the chlorophyll response was measured through spectroscopy (figure 30). The 
chlorophyll, green pigment of the algae essential to photosynthesis (Yilmaz & Gökmen, 2015), 
gives indications on the health conditions of the lichens observed. Due to the lack of reference 
value or reviewing by a lichen health specialist, the results of chlorophyll response can only 
give comparative information between the different samples. Two comparisons were plotted 
namely between the mix designs and the seeding methods. 
Figure 31 shows the mean chlorophyll response index of the samples averaged per mix design.

LC 5 samples. PC and AER samples have no significant difference. This observation comforts

these results inversely correlates with the visual observations of darkness. It is therefore 
suggested that the limestone and calcined clay samples kept the lichens in better health 
conditions than PC and AER throughout the experiment.

5.3.3 Conclusion of the health assessment  
 
After the growth experiments and the outdoor recovering period, the main observation showed 
that the lichens growing on LC samples, and especially LC 5 samples, were suffering of a 
higher water depletion leading to more structural damages, but that they kept a brighter colour 
and reacted better to re-hydration. This is surprising as high humidity during indoor storage 
was chosen to prevent drying but may indicate a certain inadequacy of the environmental 
conditions or other unforeseen factors. This issue will be discussed in the next section. 
The spectroscopic measurements permitted to confirm the hypothesis that the darkening of the 
lichens is directly linked to their health conditions and comforts the previous observations. 
X. parietina, and especially its photobiont Trebouxia has a very high tolerance to desiccation 
and can survive up to three years without moisture (Honegger, 2003; Lutzoni, François and 
Miadlikowska, 2009). Higher water depletion, while reducing or stopping the photosynthetic 
activity, does not harm the possibility of re-hydration. On the other hand, the darkening of the 
lichen indicates more permanent damages such as the loss of significant portions of the 
photobionts. Even though no net growth was noticed, the observations of the present 
experiment indicate that the lichen seeded or transplanted on the LC samples presented a better 
long-term health condition while the water transport and porosity study showed that the higher 
permeability of LC is problematic for the micro-climatic environment on the surface of the 
sample and may need to be optimized. 
 
5.4  Review of the growth factors  
5.4.1 Introduction 
 
Even though the experiments showed the advantage of the LC samples as a substrate for lichen 
growth compared to the ordinary Portland Cement samples, the cultured lichens underwent 
several degradations over time. 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
 
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
55 
 
It is therefore important to investigate the factors that caused the observed colour change in the 
lichens and prevented any substantial growth since it indicates a certain inadequacy of the 
growing environment. The following sections examine separately the factors influencing the 
climate room experiment to identify which ones might bear a responsibility on the damages 
suffered by the lichens. 
 
5.4.2 Lichen collection and seeding 
 
Successful collection is important to assess the conditions of the lichens at the beginning of the 
experiment. Good health of the lichens on the first day of the experiment can be assumed since 
the collection of all lichens happened within one week prior to seeding (Verma, 2011). 
The lichens were collected from both bark and rock substrate on the campus of the Technical 
University of Denmark. Ott (1987) states that the lichen must be collected in the immediate 
vicinity of the place where an outdoor growth experiment takes place to insure a consistent 
growth environment. This applies for the nearby harvested rainwater sprayed on the lichens 
and the possible useful organic compounds that it carries, but not to the remaining 
environmental factors of the growth experiment which are not natural.  
Additionally, as it was mentioned in section 4.3.1, the lichen Physcia tenella, found 
sporadically growing in the X. parietina thalli agglomerations on tree barks, were kept within 
the lichen harvest. Ott (1987) states that X. parietina, as part of an adaptative reproduction 
mechanism, can use pseudotrebouxia from soredia of other lichens and assimilate it, e.g. 
Physcia tenella. Therefore, the presence of Physcia tenella can be beneficial for the resynthesis 
of the lichens by means of this cleptobiosis principle described in section 3.1.1.  
The growth media solution containing peptone from casein was used following the method 
found in Bradanini (2016). An indication of its efficiency as a growth media can be seen in the 
fact that a test PC sample with 0.5 w/c covered with the lichen flakes and peptone solution that 
was placed in the climate room, but which was not manually sprayed, developed an extensive 
colonization of moulds (figure 33). This colonization has been prevented on the 
experimentation samples with the manual spraying of rainwater. Based on the harvesting and 
seeding conditions, it is assumed that those factors are not to blame for the impaired health and 
the darkening of the lichens.

5.4.3 Climate room factors 
 
By using an indoor environment to conduct the experiment, several problems were avoided 
such as frost, snow falls or the washing off of the lichens due to heavy rain or strong winds. At 
the same time, it was an opportunity to recreate optimal and controlled environmental 
conditions to boost growth.  
However, according to the visual and photographic observations, a certain inadequacy occurred 
in the climate room factors. It is therefore important to investigate in what way the humidity, 
the lighting or the temperature was inadequate. 
 
In the effort of trying to accelerate the growth of lichens in this experiment, it is crucial to 
understand that the growth rate, which represents the speed at which carbon is captured from 
the air through photosynthesis, is constant and cannot be accelerated. It is only possible to 
influence the duration of the growth, which is the amount of time in which the growth can 
happen, excluding every moment when the conditions of moisture, lighting or temperature are 
not met. By meeting all these conditions during the ‘day time’ light cycle, the growth time was 
extended as long as possible, resulting in a maximum growth rate.  
 
 
5.4.3.1 
Humidity 
 
Humidity is an essential factor in lichen growth, a condition which limits where they may be 
found: lichen will grow poorly in direct sunlight and if the lichen is not hydrated, no carbon 
gain is possible (Green, Sancho, & Pintado, 2011). Gauslaa (2014) sets a minimum RH for 
proper growth of chlorolichens such as X. parietina at 75-80 %. The RH in the climate room 
has then been set to 90 % to ensure sufficient humidity.  
The determination of moisture and direct water needs of lichens for a maximum net 
photosynthesis is complex and varies between the species. Many chlorolichens and 
cephalolichens can initiate photosynthetic activity in the absence of liquid water from dew or 
rain, although it often occurs at slower rates (Gauslaa, 2014). However, cyanolichens need 
liquid water to initiate any photosynthesis (Gauslaa, 2014). The case of X. parietina, a 
chlorolichen, was further studied by Sousa-Egipsy et al. (2000) where it was observed growing 
in places where the main source of water was atmospheric, defining it as an aero-hygrophytic 
lichen.  
Considering that the lichens had at all times sufficient atmospheric water and regularly direct 
water during the experiment, it is assumed that the humidity conditions were met for a proper 
photosynthesis and respiration activity.  
Additionally, the nitrogen content of the rainwater manually sprayed should be beneficial for 
the growth since as stated in section 3.1.1, X. parietina prefers nitrogen-rich environments 
(Munzi et al., 2013). Indeed, the nitrogen content of precipitations in Denmark is high 
compared to the rest of Europe. The annual rainfall-weighted average concentrations of 
nitrogen for the Copenhagen area is 0,8-1 [mg N l-1] (Cape et al., 2012). 
 
However, some reserves can be emitted as to the importance of the drying and wetting cycles 
of the lichen (Green et al., 2011) and the potential harm of excessive direct water (Souza-
Egipsy, Valladares, & Ascaso, 2000). Armstrong (1977) determines that the frequency of

wetting and drying cycles influences the growth, but could not define it accurately enough to 
provide predications applicable on an artificial environment created for indoor growth. 
This concern is supported by the observations from the 3 weeks outdoor experiment, where the 
lichen gradually restored his re-hydrating capacity. These observations suggest that, even 
though it might often stop the growth, exposure to lower relative humidity may be necessary 
to maintain the health of the lichens.  
 
 
5.4.3.2 
Lighting 
 
The lighting conditions, consisting in a 16 hours/day illumination with LED growing light 
bulbs, were chosen to offer an elongated growth period and the required wavelengths for 
photosynthetic activity. The spectrum of regular blue and red LED growth lights is shown on 
figure 34. Such a spectrum contains only the necessary wavelengths for photosynthesis and 
therefore no UV were casted onto the lichens.  
In the investigation on the darkening of the lichens, UVs are an important aspect because of 
their influence on the colour change on X. parietina. The yellow/orange colour characteristic 
of X. parietina is caused by the presence of the photoprotective pigment parietin, that is 
synthesised under certain stresses (Gauslaa & Ustvedt, 2003; Knut Asbjørn Solhaug et al., 
2003). Figure 35 shows the colour difference of a thallus after the extraction of its parietin 
pigments. Gauslaa & Ustvedt (2003) showed that the synthesis of parietin was only due to UV 
stresses, especially around UV-B wavelengths (280-320 nm). Therefore, the colour change 
witnessed during the indoor growth experiment cannot be attributed to light stresses since they 
received no UVs.  
However, it is not clear what are the necessary wavelengths to provide to the lichens in addition 
to photosynthetically active radiations (PAR) and if the blue and red LED lights are entirely 
sufficient.

5.4.3.3 
Temperature 
 
The temperature in the climate room was at a constant 23 ± 1 °C. This temperature is higher 
than what the lichens usually experience in the Danish environment and do not reflect the day 
and night temperature cycle.  
Lichens need an unproductive period in the day to maintain their photosynthetic activity, which 
can be induced by a temperature change, but also by a period of lower illuminance. Korhonen 
& Kallio (1987) showed in an indoor experiment that the photosynthetic activity can be kept 
at a healthy level with a constant 20 °C and only 4 hours of dark (with at least 75 % less light 
than during the day). Concurrently, Huss-danell (1979) showed that 20-30 °C was the optimum 
range of incubation temperature for the fixation of nitrogen in lichens. 
Therefore, it is assumed that the temperature factor was adequate for the lichens considering 
the day and night schedule of the lamps. 
  
5.4.4 Conclusion 
 
After evaluating the growth parameter, it is not clear what are the precise factors that caused 
the observed colour change in the lichens and prevented any substantial growth. 
Booth (1971) states that it has been proven very difficult to maintain lichens in a healthy 
condition or get significant growth in thalli transferred to the laboratory. Indeed, the complexity 
of the interactions between the growth factors and the slowness of lichen growths makes it very 
difficult to create an adequate environment and study its effect on the lichens.  
If the darkening of the lichens is not a sign of the production of parietin, it can be a sign of 
depletion of photobionts. It was observed by Honegger (2003) that X. parietina could lose its 
green coloration and turn brownish after a long-term storage in the dark, signifying the decay 
of parts of the algal partner.  
Although all the lichens have suffered a relative colour change, the visual and photographic 
observations showed a clear difference in darkness between the LC samples and the PC and 
AER samples. Additionally, the spectroscopic measurements showed that this darkness was 
linked to a lower chlorophyll content. The hypothesis that the substrate, and particularly its pH 
value, bears a responsibility has then to be considered.

6     CONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK 
 
The idea of using lichens to create an epiphytic green façade responds to the drive towards new 
ways of integrating nature to the built environment in the simplest way, relying only on the 
building material itself as substrate and without the need for feeding, ground roots or additional 
maintenance.  
The main hypothesis was the use of a blend of limestone and calcined clay replacing 35% of 
the cement which, aside from its lower embodied CO2, has promising properties potentially 
enhancing the growth of lichens on its surface.  
After 11 weeks of indoor experiment and 3 weeks of outdoor experiment, important 
conclusions can be drawn. While no net growth of the lichens was observed in this time frame, 
as radial expansion for the transplanted thalli or as resynthesis for the lichen solution, the 
limestone and calcined clay concrete was proven to keep the lichen in better health than regular 
Portland cement concrete. However, its water transport and porosity properties constitute a 
disadvantage for the local microclimate. 
 
Regarding the specific objectives of this thesis, the most relevant conclusions are listed below: 
 
• The experiments show that a concrete substrate utilizing a concrete mix where 35% of 
the cement was replaced by a blend of limestone and calcined clay, and thus reducing 
significantly the embodied CO2 of the material, provided a more suitable substrate for 
the seeding and the transplantation of the lichen Xanthoria parietina than a reference 
ordinary Portland cement concrete. Between both water/cement ratios, the mix with 0,5 
w/c showed the best results. 
• The limestone and calcined clay concrete after accelerated carbonation showed a larger 
permeability than the carbonated Portland cement concrete which constitutes a 
disadvantage to sustain a high humidity at the surface of the concrete for the growth of 
lichens. The permeability was largest in the 0,5 w/c limestone and calcined clay mix 
design. 
• The presence of air entrainer in the ordinary Portland cement mix design increased the 
porosity and slightly increased the carbonation speed but did not significantly influence 
the growth of lichens  
• The two flat surfaces were the surface treatments that showed the best attachment of 
lichen to their surface. No significant difference was found between the two scales of 
branch samples and of basalt stones.  
• The transplanted thalli showed better health conditions than the crushed lichens spread 
on the surface which did not succeed to resynthesize. Moreover, he prior algae cover 
did not influence the evolution of the lichen cover. 
• The peptone solution seemed to be beneficial both for the attachment to the substrate 
and for the biological growth. However, it has not been proven that it helps the growth 
of lichens specifically. It was showed that the peptone coating had the effect of reducing 
the speed of water transport through the concrete samples.  
==PAGE:62==
 
 
 
CONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK 
62 
 
• The accelerated carbonation process using a sodium bicarbonate solution succeeded in 
providing a substantial carbonation depth to the limestone and calcined clay concrete 
and only a 0,1 mm depth for the ordinary Portland cement concrete.  
• The air entrainer mix design worked properly in trapping closed air-filled pores 
homogeneously in the sample. 
 
 
The results of this thesis contain encouraging perspectives for further development of a more 
bioreceptive concrete. The use of limestone and calcined clay has been proven advantageous, 
but its water transport and porosity properties remain a substantial issue.  
In further research, these properties would represent a major challenge, the key of which lies 
in dissociating high permeability and fast carbonation. The choice of the environmental factors 
of further growth experiments would represent another challenge to handle carefully.

8.2  Project plan 
 
The project lies at the intersection of the fields of material science and biology. The overarching 
idea is to find ways to make organic and mineral material meet and work together, namely 
concrete and lichens. The task at hand lies in the search of the best way to use concrete as a 
substrate for the growth of lichens. The combination of both types of materials and their 
respective properties offers numerous and stimulating engineering challenges. 
Throughout the project, it will be necessary to reflect and identify on technical and scientific 
issues within the field of both material science and biology. Such a scope implies the 
mobilisation of scientific knowledge due to the requirements of different methodologies, 
theories and tools. The project requires the development of innovative approaches and 
strategies to solve problems based on the combined analysis of both concrete properties and 
the lichens growth cycle.  
The main obstacles that have been preventing a substantial breakthrough in the field so far are 
the factor of time and the lack of clear understanding of the growth conditions of lichens. Being 
one of the slowest growing living organism in the world, lichens are a challenging organism to 
work with, especially in the context of a scientific research project with a limited time span. 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
 
APPENDICES 
68 
 
Also, because of its unpredictable colonizing behaviour. More research has been made to 
prevent its spreading on building materials than to discover the factors that make it thrive. 
Those elements will therefore be part of the challenges to face in this project. 
To come up with an experimentation strategy, the different aspects of the issue must be 
delimited, both on the concrete and on the lichen side. Concerning the concrete, the major 
aspects are the composition, that is controlled by the production of different mix designs, the 
porosity, the pH value and the surface geometry. On the lichen side, the aspects identified are 
the seeding method, the relative humidity of the environment and the lighting. 
The experimentation strategy is further described in the following plan: 
Based on the above-mentioned aspects, the experimentation strategy is to create a series of 
concrete tiles using different mix designs and surface treatments to assess their efficiency as a 
substrate for lichen growth. The growth will be initiated with three different methods of seeding 
in a controlled environment in a climate room. The growth environment in the climate room is 
controlled by a scheduled artificial lighting and a constant high humidity for an optimised 
growth rate. The climate room is a crucial element to accelerate the process and tackle the 
timing challenge faced in the project. 
Regarding the mix designs, it was decided to utilise supplementary cementitious materials. The 
focus is to utilize limestone and calcined clay in a mix design. These binders contain less 
calcium and the carbonation process is therefore accelerated. The environmental impact of the 
mix is also lowered, which is a significant asset in the perspective of developing a sustainable 
“green” facade. The two main mix designs are thus based on conventional Portland cement for 
reference and one containing Portland cement, limestone and calcined clay. Within those two 
categories, variations are made with the water-cement ratio and different admixtures.  
Variations of the water-cement ratio influence the porosity of the binder and therefore its 
properties of water transport and water retention. These properties are crucial in the creation of 
suitable micro-climatic environment for lichen growth. The durability of the material is also a 
significant property influenced by the porosity. A study is conducted on each concrete mix 
design to get information about the porosity of the tiles both with a capillarity suction time test 
and a dynamic vapor sorption test investigating the water transport and retention respectively.  
Hydrated cement produces a high pH environment that is harmful for the growth of lichens. 
Over time, the pH will decrease through the carbonation reaction of the concrete, but it can 
take years to reach a desirable level. The carbonation reaction must be accelerated to lower the 
pH value of the concrete tiles within the time frame of the project and therefore make them 
more suitable for organic material to settle. This is achieved by submerging the tiles in a sodium 
bicarbonate solution.  
With each of these mix design variations, different surface treatments are executed to assess 
their impact on the performance of the tile as a substrate for lichen growth. Six types of surfaces 
were designed.  
Since lichens have a specific way of reproducing and of colonizing a surface, different seeding 
methods will be used. The first one is to crush the lichen specimens in flakes and seed them on 
the substrate through a suspension media made of a casein peptone solution. The second one 
seeds the lichens the same way, but with a previous algae seeding that could possibly accelerate 
the lichen colonization. The last one is an attempt of full lichen thalli transplantation. These 
strategies were derived from an analysis of the different growth and colonization properties of 
lichens. 
Through these broad variations of growth attempts, the objective is to monitor the growth of 
lichen in a climate room for as long as possible within the projects duration. Over time, the 
humidity is optimized to accelerate the lichen growth but simultaneously prevent the growth 
of mould. An optimal lighting input is insured by LED growth lamps scheduled to extend the 
growth period of the lichen. The growth itself is monitored through visual and photographic 
==PAGE:69==
 
 
 
APPENDICES 
69 
 
analysis. Finally, with the monitoring results, conclusions can be drawn on the interactions of 
lichens with concrete as substrate material.

8.7  Water transport and porosity study 
8.7.1 Initial drying test 
 
 
Figure 41 : Drying test on 8 days old samples cured in water 
Figure 41 shows an initial drying test that was carried out on 8 day old samples of the four mix 
designs and a peptone coated copy of each. The samples were sitting in the climate chamber at 
an initial RH of 97%. The RH was gradually decreased by steps of 5% every 48 ± 2 hours down 
to 70%. Then, the RH brought back up by steps of 10% every 72 ± 2h to 97%. The test was 
useful to assess the effect of different RH steps and time steps. It was concluded after the test 
that a 48 hours test period was too short and that the RH steps should also be increased to obtain 
clear results.
