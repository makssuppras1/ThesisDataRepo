Abstract
This project is an exploratory study of Capillary Perfusion Maps captured by the
Retinal Function Imager. We have examined whether image analysis techniques
can be used to extract information from the Capillary Perfusion Maps. To do
this we have segmented the images. We have done this using a thresholding
method and a region-growing method. In general we ﬁnd that the quality of
the segmentation is highly dependent on the quality of the Capillary Perfusion
Map. For 12 high-quality Capillary Perfusion Maps the vessel diameter has been
quantiﬁed. From this quantiﬁcation we ﬁnd that the diameter distribution of
vessels appears to be unique to the individual, but due to the size of the dataset
the interpretation of this ﬁnding is questionable. By computing the isophote
curvature along the vessel boundary we are able to quantify the curvature of
vessels; however, the complex nature of blood vessels in the retina suggest that
this is not an ideal expression of vessel curvature. Further analysis on a larger
dataset should be carried out to determine whether the methods tested in this
project are useful in practice, but overall our work motivates the feasibility
of extracting information from Capillary Perfusion Maps using image analysis
techniques.

Chapter 1
Introduction
All animals depend on their senses to survive and any sensory disruption is
therefore critical. Humankind has developed ﬁve primary senses: sight, hearing,
taste, smell and touch, and as our life expectancy grows, unfortunately, so does
the occurrence of diseases aﬀecting our senses. Medical specialists, engineers and
other scientists alike work hard to improve the diagnostic process and treatment
of these diseases.
The eye is an organ in the visual system which is responsible for the sense of
sight. One of the best known diseases aﬀecting the eye is diabetic retinopathy.
It is a complication of diabetes that damages the blood vessels in the retina (a
light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye), which may result in a loss of vision.
The known number of diabetes cases in Denmark alone is close to 300,000 [1],
and that ﬁgure has doubled within the last 20 years.
Knowledge regarding
blood vessels in the retina is therefore of great interest, as it can help diagnose,
monitor and treat diseases such as diabetic retinopathy.
This project is an exploratory study of Capillary Perfusion Maps created by a
Retinal Function Imager. A Capillary Perfusion Map is an image that visual-
izes the microvasculature around the fovea, which is an area in the retina. A
trained, medical professional can use such an image to detect valuable informa-
tion regarding the microvasculature, but it is a time consuming process. We
want to investigate whether there is a feasible way of applying image analysis
==PAGE:9==
1.1 Related work
2
techniques to the Capillary Perfusion Maps, such that useful information can
be extracted. Hopefully this will make the manual process less time consuming.
We have formulated the following questions which we would like to answer:
• Can we segment the Capillary Perfusion Maps into vessel and non-vessel
pixels?
• Can we quantify the diameter of vessels in the Capillary Perfusion Map?
• Can we ﬁnd a meaningful expression for the curvature of vessels in the
Capillary Perfusion Map?
Segmenting the images into vessel and non-vessel pixels can be useful in itself, for
instance when formulating a measure of vessel density, but it is also fundamental
for any further analysis. We will apply two segmentation methods, one is based
on thresholding the images and the other is based on a region-growing algorithm.
Being able to quantify the vessel diameter could help detect changes through
time, and whether these are random, related to disease or perhaps simply a result
of aging. We want to see if we can quantify the diameter distribution, but also,
we wish to create a mapping from the Capillary Perfusion Map to a Diameter
Map. In the same way that a trained professional can assess the information in
the Capillary Perfusion Map they can then also assess the information in the
Diameter Map.
Commonly in diabetic retinopathy patients, as vessels in the retina deteriorate,
new and increasingly curved vessels develop. Creating an expression for the
vessel curvature may therefore be a useful tool for assessing the stage of the
disease. We will attempt to create such an expression based on the isophote
curvature of vessels in the Capillary Perfusion Map.
By answering these questions we hope to shed light on new potential uses of the
Capillary Perfusion Maps.
1.1
Related work
As previously explained, knowledge regarding the microvasculature in the retina
is crucial for improving medical treatment of diseases to the eye. Therefore there
is a vast array of research in the area. In this section we go through some of the
work that is most relevant for our purposes.
==PAGE:10==
1.1 Related work
3
Niemeijer et al. [2] published a study that compared several retinal vessel seg-
mentation methods. These were, among others, based on a scale space analysis,
a region growing approach and a curvature estimation. Although all methods
produced acceptable segmentation results Niemeijer et al. concluded that for a
task as complicated as vessel segmentation it is hard to ﬁnd a general method.
In particular, segmenting wide and narrow vessels simultaneously proved chal-
lenging.
There are numerous studies that focus on diagnosing retinal diseases based on
data from the Retinal Function Imager. However, there has not been performed
much quantitative analysis of the Capillary Perfusion Maps. In 2013 Jiang et al.
[3] assessed this problem. Their work focused on an unsupervised segmentation
algorithm which successfully detected vessels of all shapes and sizes.
Research of retinal imaging in general as well as the Retinal Function Imager
modality indicate that knowledge in the area is far from exhausted, and hope-
fully our work in this thesis will help further the developments that are being
made.

2.1
The Human Eye
The eye is an organ in the visual system.
It works by detecting light and
converting it to electrical signals. These signals are transmitted via the optic
nerve, through the optic canal, to the visual cortex in the brain. Figure 2.1
shows a diagram of the eye. Here we see that the optic nerve is placed at the
very back of the eye. The back of the eye is also referred to as the fundus.
The internal part of the eye is surround by a light sensitive tissue known as
the retina (indicated in Figure 2.1).
The retina consists of several layers of
neurons known as photoreceptor cells. There are two types of photoreceptor
cells known as rods and cones. The rods are distributed across the entire retina,
while the cones, which are responsible for color vision, are found in an area of
the eye known as the macula. The macula, like the optic nerve, is placed at the
back of the eye, and it is an oval-shaped, pigmented area. In adult humans the
diameter of the macula is approximately 5.5mm. At the center of the macula,
with a diameter of approximately 1.5mm, is a pit known as the fovea. The fovea
is a completely rod-free, avascular zone containing cone photoreceptor cells. The
fovea is responsible for sharp vision. It appears as a dark spot in fundus images
as seen in the right hand side of Figure 2.1.

2.2
Retinal Function Imager
The Retinal Function Imager (RFI) is a fundus photography modality devel-
oped by Optical Imaging Ltd. (Rehovot Israel, Israel). Through an integrated
hardware and software system the RFI captures retinal images that can oﬀer
insight into the blood ﬂow, microvasculature, oximetry and metabolic function
of the eye. This thesis is based on images of the microvasculature around the
fovea of the eye, and this section brieﬂy describes how the RFI creates this data,
as explained in [6], [7] and [8].
The RFI hardware is a modiﬁcation of a standard fundus camera where, amongst
other things, a stroboscopic ﬂash system has been added. This allows up to eight
sequential ﬂashes that can illuminate the retina with light at green wavelengths.
==PAGE:13==

In this way eight images can be recorded in a rapid sequence which minimizes
inter-frame retinal movement while capturing the movement of red blood cells
through the small vessels in the eye.
To ensure that the back of the eye is
properly illuminated the pupils must be dilated before the photographs are
taken.

The raw images can be processed by
the RFI software to create what is

Afterwards

an enhanced visualization of the mi-
crovasculature several Series nCPMs

dust mask is created, the series are aligned and the Combined nCPM is created
by averaging over all non-dust pixel.
The process is called noninvasive since, unlike methods such as Fluorescein An-
giography which requires intravenous injection of dye to visualize the microvas-
culature, the RFI relies solely on tracking the movement of red blood cells by
illuminating the retina with green light.
The quality of the nCPM is highly dependent on the skills of the person op-
erating the RFI, as they have to manually adjust the focus before taking the
photographs. The quality also relies on the subject who is being photographed,
as acquiring sharp images requires the subject to keep their eyes very still.
This makes some subjects more ideal for this method than others, and typically
younger subjects will produce sharper results than older subjects.

3.1.1
Adaptive Histogram Equalization
A common modiﬁcation of histogram equalization is Adaptive Histogram Equal-
ization (AHE). Essentially, AHE splits the image into a grid of smaller regions
and performs histogram equalization locally on these regions. This makes the
method more sensitive to small changes in image contrast that would otherwise
be suppressed by histogram equalization. Unfortunately this will oftentimes also
produce an undesirable increase in the contrast of background noise. Therefore,
as described in [11], a slight modiﬁcation of AHE known as Contrasted Limited
Adaptive Histogram Equaliztion (CLAHE) can be used instead. A homogenous
area, such as a noisy background, will produce a peak in the local histogram.
To combat the increased contrast in these areas, CLAHE allows only a maxi-
mum number of pixels in each histogram bin, and redistributes the remaining
pixels evenly across the entire histogram. An example of applying CLAHE to
an image is given in Figure 3.1.

3.3.3
Watershed Segmentation
The watershed algorithm is an image segmentation method described in [17,
Chapter 12].
Consider an image, I, as a topographic surface.
We refer to
minima in I as mi(I) for i = 1, 2, ..., n, where n is the total number of minima.
Conceptually, the watershed algorithm segments I by ﬂooding the image from
all minima simultaneously, and at constant speed, forming n catchment basins.
At some point in time, water from two basins intersect. This intersection is
known as a ridge, or a watershed line. Once the entire image is ﬂooded we have
found watershed lines separating all catchment basins, and each catchment basin
will contain exactly one minima. Each catchment basin is then a segment in the
segmented image.
Watershed lines can be determined using Meyer’s ﬂooding algorithm.
The
method is initialized by assigning a label to all minima, mi(I). A priority queue
is created, in which all gray levels in I are assigned a priority. All neighbors
of a minima are assigned the same label as the minima and are added to the
priority queue. For the pixel, p = (x, y), with the highest priority in the queue
we do the following: Find all neighbors of p that have not yet been assigned
a label, assign them the same label as p, add them to the priority queue and
remove p from the queue. If no new pixels were assigned, and the queue is
empty, we stop. Otherwise we repeat the process for the pixel that is now of
highest priority in the queue. In practice the algorithm is often applied to the
gradient image instead of I.
One of the main pitfalls of the watershed algorithm is over-segmentation. This
issue is especially prominent in noisy images, and occurs because images often
contain numerous minima within a single region. This problem can be countered
in many ways, for instance by merging regions, manually seeding the algorithm,
or suppressing certain minima prior to performing the segmentation.

Chapter 7
Discussion
The main goal for this project was to explore the feasibility of applying im-
age analysis techniques to nCPMs in a way that would ease the interpretation
process for a medical professional, and perhaps uncover new potential uses. In
particular we wanted to determine if we could segment the nCPMs into ves-
sel and non-vessel pixels, quantify the vessel diameter and express the vessel
curvature.
7.1
Segmentation
We have tested two segmentation methods. One method uses thresholding to
segment the nCPMs, while the other uses a region-growing algorithm. As seen
in Figures 6.1 - 6.3, the two segmentation methods produce comparable results
for high quality nCPMs. Both methods segment large vessels well while cap-
turing overall tendencies of the capillaries. The most prominent diﬀerence is
that the region-growing method, unlike the thresholding method, assumes a
connected foreground. This diﬀerence is especially visible in Figure 6.3. Figure
6.3c shows how the region-growing method marks some capillary areas as back-
ground whereas the thresholding method, seen in Figure 6.3b, marks capillaries
in these same areas as scattered specks. In this case neither method is really
==PAGE:53==
7.1 Segmentation
46
ideal, while in areas such as the fovea, which is a known avascular zone, the
connectivity assumption works in favor of the region-growing method.
For a more detailed comparison of the two methods we consider Figure 6.4. In
areas of high capillary detail, such as regions A and B, the methods are hardly
distinguishable.
Region C shows a close-up of one of the more problematic
capillary areas. This region clearly illustrates the previously explained diﬀerence
between the two methods. However, referring to the nCPM in region C we see,
that this is also a region with low capillary detail. The exact boundaries of
the capillaries are hardly visible, which makes it very hard to favor one method
over the other in this case. Regions D and E also show areas with less than
ideal capillary detail, however both methods capture the presence of capillaries
in these regions.
One of the biggest obstacles when developing a segmentation method for this
dataset, was that there was no available ground truth segmentation. In areas of
high detail the accuracy of the results can be assessed without medical expertise,
but in areas with little detail, such as regions C and E in Figure 6.4, we simply
do not know what we are looking for. Another obstacle, as explained in Section
4.2, is that even high quality nCPMs contain numerous artifacts. In the top
left corner of the nCPM in Figure 6.3a we see prominent dust artifacts. These
artifacts are clearly visible in the segmentation results, and if these results are
used as a basis for further vessel analysis, as is the case in our work, this will pose
questions regarding the validity said analysis. We typically also see alignment
artifacts along the edges of nCPMs, which also carry through to the segmented
results. To ensure the validity of further analysis one could consider cropping
the images to remove alignment artifacts and perhaps masking out areas of low
capillary detail.
Something that works in favor of both methods is that there was no manual pa-
rameter tuning when applying the methods to diﬀerent nCPMs. This indicates
that the segmentation process could easily be automated, however, this requires
that there is a way to quantitatively asses the accuracy of each method. Unfor-
tunately this leads back to the fact that there is no ground truth segmentation,
which makes it very diﬃcult to measure accuracy. Furthermore it would require
a larger dataset to determine whether these methods work in general.
Figure 6.5 shows segmentation results for a low quality nCPM. The two methods
still produce comparable results, but as explained in Section 4.1 a low quality
nCPM is an image with poor capillary detail. For reasons previously explained,
this makes us unable to validate the segmentation results in capillary regions
of low quality nCPMs. Instead of the previously proposed solution of masking
out areas of low capillary detail, one could also consider creating a modiﬁed
segmentation algorithm tailored towards segmenting only larger vessels, as these
==PAGE:54==
7.2 Diameter Estimation
47
are visible regardless of capillary detail.
Figure 6.6 shows the Dice Similarity Coeﬃcient between the two methods for
all nCPMs. The average Dice Coeﬃcient is above 0.91 which motivates that the
methods really are comparable. The thresholding method is incredibly simple,
and a question to consider is whether it is necessary to use a more complicated
algorithm. In general this would have to depend on the further application.
When we, for instance, estimate the diameter of vessels, the connectivity as-
sumption of the region-growing method is a desirable property, but perhaps
post-processing the thresholding results would be a simpler way of achieving
this to a suﬃcient degree.
7.2
Diameter Estimation
As previously explained, this project was an exploratory study. We wanted to
determine what, if any, information is available in an nCPM. One of the question
we wanted to answer was whether we could extract a diameter estimate of vessels
in the nCPM.
We see in Figure 6.7 that the diameter estimation method works as expected
and produces a reasonable estimate. Of course, the complexity of the vessels
inﬂuences the quality of the result. The microvasculature in the retina is one
big network of vessels that branch out from each other. This property is passed
on to the vessel skeleton, as shown in Figure 6.8A, and it is one of the biggest
disadvantages of the method. We do not want a diameter estimate along the
skeleton from a branching point to the point where two vessels intersect. For a
more accurate estimate we want something more like Figure 6.8B. This is the
result for the same region, but we have manually erased some diameter estimates
for illustrative purposes. Whether a modiﬁcation of the method to counter this
problem is necessary would, of course, have to depend on the application.
When evaluating these results we have to remember that we do not know the true
vessel diameter. Therefore, a success criterion for a method like this is not so
much if it is correct, but if it is useful. Therefore, we have compared the diameter
distribution of all 12 high quality nCPMs as seen in Figure 6.9. Although the
distributions appear similar, a χ2 test revealed that the distribution of any
subject is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from all others at a 5% signiﬁcance level. This
indicates, that the diameter distribution of vessels in the retina are unique to
each individual.
It would be interesting to investigate a larger dataset, for
instance a dataset containing multiple nCPMs from the same subject, to see if
the vessel diameter changes over time.
==PAGE:55==
7.2 Diameter Estimation
48
Figure 6.10 shows a close-up of the diameter distribution for small and large
vessels separately. We see that subject 205 generally has more small vessels
than the other subjects and fewer large vessels. Conversely, subject 608 has
fewer small vessels than the other subjects but more large vessels. This raises
some questions regarding the relationship between vessel diameter and vessel
density.
Of course we need to stress the point, that the diameter estimates are based on
segmented nCPMs. Therefore, one should be careful when drawing conclusions
based on diameter estimates without thoroughly validating the segmentation
ﬁrst. For applications such as classifying subjects based on e.g. age this is not
as crucial; however, our work does not seem to indicate that such a classiﬁcation
is possible.
7.2.1
Diameter Map
The Diameter Map was meant to serve two purposes. Mainly it was intended
as a quick way to visually assess the vessel diameters, but a good diameter map
could potentially also be used to segment vessels in an nCPM based on diameter.
Figure 6.11 shows the diameter maps of an nCPM created using the four diﬀerent
methods. Overall these methods all look like good contenders, but a striking
observation is that small diameters are much more frequent than large ones.
We already know this from Figure 6.9, but the problem with this in terms
of the diameter maps, is that the diﬀerences in small diameters are almost
completely lost with a linear colormap. If the diameter maps are to be used as
a visual tool these diﬀerences need to be more prominent. For this reason we
stretched the colormap, as seen in Figures 6.12 and 6.13. These maps give a
much easier visual interpretation of the diameters, but also highlight some of
the main diﬀerences between the four methods. In general we see that the SDM
and watershed method produce similar results, and tend to overestimate vessel
diameters. This happens because these methods group pixels together, and it
results in a very blocky looking diameter map. The nearest neighbor and the
RDM diameter maps look very similar and, unlike in the other maps, changes in
vessel diameter appears gradual. However, these methods tend to underestimate
the diameter, especially along the edge of vessels. This has the unfortunate eﬀect
that the diameter estimate is not even across the entire surface of a vessel.
To further investigate the diﬀerences between the methods we refer to Figure
6.14. For an isolated vessel, such as the one in region A, all methods produce
slightly varying but good results. Region B demonstrates an issue that is com-
mon across all methods, which is areas where vessels branch.
This is quite
==PAGE:56==
7.3 Vessel Curvature
49
problematic, as all vessels generally branch out from each other.
While the
nearest neighbor method and the RDM produce a gradual overlap between two
intersecting vessels, the SDM and watershed method tend to let the diameter of
the larger vessel extend into the smaller vessel. The SDM method in particular
tends to assign the same diameter to very big blocks of pixels. Furthermore,
this method appears to be very sensitive to the choice of structuring element.
This is clear in region B as well as in region C, where we see that pixels are
grouped together in square areas. As mentioned, the watershed method also
creates blocky results, but it is more sensitive to the characteristics of the vessel
in the particular area. Region D shows a capillary area in which the methods
produce varying results, but it is diﬃcult to determine if one solution is better
than the other. In this case it would have to depend on the application.
In general we ﬁnd that all methods are suboptimal and have problems that
should be addressed if these methods are to be used. We consider the SDM to
be a bad method, as we simply cannot trust the assigned diameter in this map.
Whether any of the other three methods are useful as a visual tool would have
to be determined by a medical professional.
In terms of using the methods to segment the nCPM based on vessel diameter,
none of these methods are complete. Branching vessels would have to be handled
better, and the image should be smoothed in such a way that, for the watershed
method, the map is less blocky, and for the nearest neighbor method and the
SDM, such that the diameter is even across the entire vessel.
7.3
Vessel Curvature
As previously explained, diseases such a diabetic retinopathy may lead to the
development of increasingly curved blood vessels in the retina. Therefore, we
have tried to develop a method that measures the vessel curvature.
Figure 6.16 visualizes the curvature in a region of the nCPM for subject 205.
For σ2 = 1, seen in Figure 6.16a, the method seems to capture the curvature
rather well, with more curved areas being assigned a higher curvature estimate
than less curved areas. When increasing σ2, seen in Figure 6.16b - 6.16d, the
curvature estimate becomes harder to interpret. In some cases, almost straight
line pieces are assigned a high curvature estimate relative to curved line pieces
that are assigned a lower curvature estimate. In other areas, the curvature is still
captured well when increasing σ2. This result indicates that the method quickly
becomes unstable. The main reason for this is presumably the complexity of
the images. When increasing σ2 we also increase the size of the gaussian kernel.
==PAGE:57==
7.3 Vessel Curvature
50
The eﬀect of this is that the curvature along one vessel piece is aﬀected by the
curvature along a diﬀerent vessel piece. This point is emphasized in Figure 6.17,
where a variance of σ2 = 225 is used. It is clear that a scale of this size does not
capture the vessel curvature at all. To combat this instability we have tried to
average the curvature over diﬀerent scales, but as seen in Figure 6.18, this does
not seem to produce a better estimate than simply choosing σ2 = 1.
Figure 6.19 compares the curvature distribution of all high quality nCPMs. As
with the diameter estimate, we investigate whether any two subjects follow the
same curvature distributions at a 5% signiﬁcance level. In some cases we accept
the null-hypothesis while in other cases we reject it. We expected, perhaps, to
see that people that were close to each other in age would also be close in terms
of their curvature distribution, but this does not seem to be the case. However,
this investigation is hardly a solid foundation to draw conclusions upon, as the
method is diﬃcult to validate and unstable. As with the diameter estimates
we also need to remember that these results assume that the segmentation
algorithm is accurate.
A general observation regarding the nature of vessels in the nCPMs is that the
curvature in the intersection between two vessels is high. This, of course, trans-
lates into the curvature estimate. Therefore, it would be interesting to remove
the estimate in these intersections to see how this would aﬀect the overall cur-
vature, as it could be argued that this would be a more accurate representation
of the vessel curvature. It would also be interesting to investigate the curva-
ture of vessels in diﬀerent diameter intervals. A good diameter map would have
made this analysis simple, but unfortunately the diameter maps produced in
this project were not suﬃcient in this regard.
The usefulness of this method would have to be assessed by applying it to a
larger dataset, but overall we ﬁnd that using the isophote curvature to express
vessel curvature is not the most obvious choice.
==PAGE:58==
Chapter 8
Conclusion
Technological developments are essential for ensuring the quality of life in todays
society. Many resources are devoted towards developing diagnostic tools and
treatments for diseases that threaten a happy and healthy life. In this project
we explored the feasibility of applying image analysis techniques to Capillary
Perfusion Maps captured by the Retinal Function Imager, as this would increase
the usability of the modality. In particular we have tried to segment the images,
quantify the diameter and express the curvature of vessels.
An accurate segmentation of any vessel image is a diﬃcult task. We propose
two methods for doing so, but more importantly we show how a segmented
Capillary Perfusion Map can be used to quantify vessel information. In our
work we have quantiﬁed both the vessel diameter and curvature, and proposed
a visual representation of the vessel diameter by creating a Diameter Map.
Whether the methods used in this project are useful in practice would require
further analysis on a larger dataset, but regardless, our work motivates the
feasibility of using image analysis techniques on capillary perfusion maps. In
general we ﬁnd that the Capillary Perfusion Maps are packed with information
waiting to be uncovered, and our work is simply scratching the surface.
