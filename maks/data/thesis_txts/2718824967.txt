Abstract
Marine ecosystems are facing challenges from climate change, commercial fishing, and pollution, and
they could require human intervention to recover.
This project aims to investigate active ways of promoting marine ecosystems by designing artificial
concrete reef units tailored to boost the growth of perennial seaweeds. Specifically the edible sugar
kelp species Saccharina latissima, is attempted propagated on concretes in the laboratory destined for
transplantation.
The research will include a stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart analysis of concrete reefs and related articles, which will inôÙ
form the design and potential applications of the artificial reef units. The analysis will focus on identifying
the potential ecological and environmental benefits of concrete reefs, such as serving as a foundation
for marine ecosystems, protecting coastlines against storms and erosion, and increasing the positive
effects of offshore wind turbine scour protection. Additionally, the research will explore the potential of
incorporating ecological value into the concrete itself, which could lead to increased durability, such as
bacterial bioôÙfilm.
A hypothesis of porosity being an important trait is being tested through two experiments. The experiôÙ
ments are partially completed and described in this report, with plans to complete them and combine
them into a third with a new hypothesis on the effectiveness of preôÙseeding.
To test the potential of using porous concretes as a cheaper and more environmentally friendly alterôÙ
native to traditional materials in artificial reef construction, two different porous concretes, ACC and
LECA, were chosen for examination and compared to control materials of construction concrete and
granite. The results so far have not yet reached a decisive conclusion on the ecological value of the
materials, but the ACC has shown slightly better performance in terms of early colonization and reôÙ
tention. However, the hypothesis that high porosity would be a decisive advantage for the porous
concretes was not supported by the results. Further testing and numerical data is necessary to fully
determine the best candidate material.
To test the ecological value of the concretes, laboratory and field tests are used to determine the
colonization of the concretes by seaweed. In the lab tests, the concretes will be subject to a model
species of seaweed with the objective of maximizing retention. Due to difficulties in validating early
species, a more general algal growth is considered until the model species matures. In the field test, the
same materials are tested by being put near an active seaweed farm and are monitored and evaluated
based on the total algal growth seen at inspections. Opportunistic species are likely to be the first to
colonize and dominate this test.
The third future test aims to combine the labs and field into a single field test, to evaluate the effectiveôÙ
ness of preôÙseeding reef elements, both directly and indirectly with sporeôÙlines akin to how the seaweed
industry grows on buoys. The ultimate goal of this project is to design artificial concrete reef units that
are tailored to boost the growth of perennial seaweeds and justify the additional resources used in
concrete production.

0.1
Motivation
Optimization is a process of improving something along a specific axis or dimension. Given limited
resources and flexibility of all things, a value hierarchy must be made to take steps in any direction.
Defining objective motivation complicates this process, as the value hierarchy greatly differs between
individuals. In banding together humans gave up a bit of autonomy in place of more security. In doing
so we created new entities in the groups we directly took part in, like cities, traditions or book clubs.
Nested communities developed from these, that we are more abstractly related to, like countries or
religions. Then again with further complexity for the EU and UN. These two supranational entities, just
like individuals, make value hierarchies. It is therefore reasonable to assume these as representative
of the individual goals of many single persons. The sustainable development goals (SDGs) are a set
of targets that have been established by the United Nations as a way to represent the priorities and
values of many individuals and communities around the world. These goals aim to address a range
of global challenges and promote sustainable development for all. Overall, the process of optimizaôÙ
tion is complex and multifaceted, requiring consideration of individual and collective values, resource
constraints, and the dynamic nature of human society.

Why concrete as a starting point
concrete}
Why use resources to build artificial reefs (AR), when human interventions seem the primary factor
for the marine decline? This first question builds upon another, on the relevance of reefs in general
and human influence on them. In the following this relevance will be explored in brief, reasoning that
it would be beneficial to have more reefs.
Hard substrate habitat in the worldãs oceans is a relatively rare occurrence compared to the total availôÙ
able shallow water area, but of vital importance to many species not limited to seaweeds but also
species of the animal kingdom [3]. Demand for seafood and building materials has historically led to
bottomôÙtrawling and stoneôÙfishing [4], [5], resulting in a significant decline hard bottom substrate habiôÙ
tat [6]. Many stones have simply been relocated from the continental shelf to the coast as erosion
barriers. This activity has not established new ecosystems, replacing the lost for previous stone reefs.
Similarly, while many new surfaces in the form of harbor walls or rock mounds create the possibility
for new habitats, there has not been colonization to their potential as it has not previously been a part
of the design philosophy [7]. More new attempts have been made to rectify this decrease of life in the
oceans by giving back better hard substrates for colonization [8], [9]. Generally, this inclusion of the
environment to either coexist with or benefit from the structures we make has been termed Ecological
engineering, and as such seeks to optimize for ecological value alongside existing structural goals.
Many reports and articles begin with a preface on the increasing challenges arising from climate
change, population growth and larger middle class [10]. These phenomena are linked even tighter
in the coastal environment [11]. As seen by the UN creating an entire SGD sorely for tackling the
problems of overfishing, habitat loss, invasive species, nutrientôÙ, plastic pollution, and ocean acidifiôÙ
cation. As the world population is growing there will be an increasing demand for housing, food, and
energy, the first of which historically has been concentrated around coastlines [12] which is most likely
to continue [13] especially around Africa and China [14]. This demand for coastalôÙcity housing could
in turn lead to more artificial islands as seen locally in Denmark and globally in China. The need for
food, in many instances provided primarily for by the oceans and reefs [15], is currently seeing a rise
in seafood and healthy proteins as the middle and upper classes grow. The trend towards more and
greener electricity should see significantly more offshore wind turbines (OWT) built, perhaps with acôÙ
companying powerôÙtoôÙX islands [16].
Itãs hard to imagine that the reestablishment of the reefs and the future coastal constructions, the soôÙ
called ocean sprawl [17], being built without large quantities of concrete [18], which already is the
dominating material for waterôÙbased structures, both by volume and weight [19]. This future concrete
should ideally aim to be cheaper, less emissionôÙheavy to manufacture, durable with minimal mainteôÙ
nance during its lifetime, and without leeching heavy metals into the precious ocean environment.

While many independent optimizations are underway, especially for ãgreenerã concrete [20], significant

the fiveôÙyear DTU BARREEF project. Success here would require deep integration of hydrodynamics

In total, a slightly more bioôÙfriendly marine concrete could be a viable product on its own. A solution
that solved more of the existing problems, perhaps better or cheaper, could facilitate the largeôÙscale
adoption for the purely ecological aspects to materialize in a meaning full way.

The field in brief
Artificial reefs are manôÙmade structures that are submerged in a body of water to enhance marine
habitat and biodiversity. Concrete is one of the most commonly used materials for constructing artificial
reefs, as it is strong, durable, and relatively inexpensive. As illustrated in fig. 1.1 concrete is also the
most commonly used material in studies of AR and OWFs, with various purposes, including fish habitat
enhancement, erosion control, recreational diving, and coastal protection.

In recent years, there has been an increasing focus on the development of sustainable and enviôÙ
ronmentally friendly AR. One promising approach is the use of new concrete mixes, which can be
formulated with a variety of additives that enhance their performance and reduce their environmental
impact. These additives commonly includes fly ash or slag, which reduces the cement required in the
mix. Alternatively new mixes of aggregate composition or inclusion of upcoming fibers like hemp. This
could help to improve the habitat value of the reef for marine life, and reduce the carbon footprint of
the construction.
Other innovative approaches to AR include the use of 3D printing technologies, which can be used to
create complex and customizable reef structures with minimal waste and at lower costs. There are also
ongoing efforts to incorporate recycled materials into artificial reefs, such as tires and plastic waste, to
help address issues of marine litter.
Overall, the field of AR is an active area of research and development, with a growing focus on sustainôÙ
ability and environmental responsibility. While there are still many challenges to be addressed, the use

Reef performance scale
A performance score to evaluate reefs are developed in [21]. The soôÙcalled ãReef performance scaleã
(RPC) is a single integer value in the range of ôÙ3 to +3. The RPC score intends to quatify the value in
terms of how well a project met its objectives, how it impacted the local environment, and how useful
the data gathered was. The author of the article suggests that a project is given the score ã0ã if the
following definition is met:
ãThe reefãs performance in terms of its objectives is inconclusive. Both negative and positive aspects
of its creation are identifiable but the overall success of the reef is indeterminable. Management and/or
design of the reef are flawed. Published material is unclear and/or confusing. Research reefs providing
inconclusive data.ã
Of the 249 abstracts examined in the review, 30 were chosen as case studies for evaluation. The most
interesting studies where the two case studies, which received a RPC score of +3, [25], [26]. These
studies utilized concrete reef units to target a specific species.
In the first case, artificial reefs were installed at the marine reserve of Tabarca, Spain, to dissuade
fishermen from illegal trawling, hereby destroying meadows of the important Posidonia oceanica. The
reefs consisted of sunk boats at first, but due to durability issues were followed up by concrete units.
These concrete units were cubic modules with a side length of 1.5môÙ2m and a thickness of around
20cm. They had multiple large openings for water and fish, with ãdiverse blocks of concrete vaults
and tubesã inside each. Lines of modules were installed strategically to protect the bottom. The study
indicates that illegal trawling is a major factor for the deterioration of Posidonia oceanica meadows, but
it can be protected by beneficial AR.
The objective in the other study was to protect the spiny lobster Palinuridae. In Biscayne Bay, Florida,
an array of small concrete block structures was placed to protect the population precious to commerôÙ
cial and recreational fisheries. Four configurations of concrete blocks and plastic pipes were tested for
lobster occupancy and stability in the Elliott Keyinrina. A nineôÙblock pyramid design was determined to
be the most effective and was chosen for the study. An array of these pyramids was then constructed
in the bay near the marina at a depth of 1.5ôÙ2m. The concrete block structures effectively reduced the
impact of marina construction on the local lobster population, but postôÙlarval recruitment was a limiting
factor for an increased population.
Both studies use structures to protect the targeted species from human threats. Additionally an extenôÙ
sion to natural habitat. Species targeting likely helps in shaping more relevant metrics, which addresses
documentation issues of subôÙnautical studies. This is backed up by a comment in [21], citing that many
of the +1/+2 cases would have benefited from improved review and management and the score of 0
was given based on a lack of adequate monitoring. This cements the difficulties of attempting conôÙ
trolled experiments in wild environments.

Indexes for biological evaluation
A new paper [27] describes different indicators used to evaluate the environmental performance of
many different artificial structures (AS) not limited to reefs. It gathers applicability by project circumôÙ
stances and makes recommendations for multiôÙmetric indexes, but highlights the lack of one developed
for ecoôÙengineering. Here ecoôÙengineering is structures that support biodiversity without compromising
on structural purposes. A hindrance to assessment, is the lack of good reference conditions, espeôÙ
cially in the evaluation of epibenthic growth in soft substrates. In areas dominated by soft substrates,
a multiôÙmetric index could be developed to compare epibenthic communities across different types of
substrates, such as natural hard substrates or submerged blocks. This would better allow for the evalôÙ
uation of these communities without the need for a reference condition. The paper further identifies
some important indicators, which should optimally be observed.
The first indicator is the abundance ratio of opportunistic to characteristic species, which asses the
ecological succession stage. The other indicator is assessing vulnerability to nonôÙindigenous species
(NIS), by use of the ALien biotic indEX (ALEX). In the paper [28], itãs concluded that ALEX correlates
to humanôÙmade changes, especially canals and harbors. This is unsurprising given ballast water from
container ships is carried and emptied here. It does not mention the effects of AR but does discuss the
distinctions made between invasive and opportunistic species. As newôÙcoming species would need to
carve out a piece of the existing ecosystem or find new opportunities. Herein lies the potential issue
of providing new empty habitat, as a reservoir for opportunistic and potential invasive species, which
would otherwise fail to establish.
In the paper [29], two ecological state groups (ESG I & II) are identified, relating to the abundance ratio.
ESG I categorizes seagrasses and other slowôÙgrowth longôÙlife perennials. ESG II describes the opporôÙ
tunistic species with short life cycles or annuals. While the authors acknowledge species existing on
a spectrum between ESG, species belonging to ESG I should be the majority for pristine reef ecosysôÙ
tems. This reassembles the same phenomenon seen on land, whereas an example permaculture can
suppress new weed growth or vice versa. A new and bare environment suddenly introduced, should
favor the opportunistic species, but measures could be taken to not keep that status quo.
Trait assesment
In the review [30] is examined how to improve the habitat and biodiversity of artificial coastal structures

The first type is aerated concrete, where a mixing agent or foaming equipment introduces air bubbles

2.2.1
Cement production as a source of emissions
While strength and shaping could still be taken into consideration, as to be applied in another case, the
simplest approach is to disregard it. When the concrete is not required to yield any strength in tension
the reinforcement can be excluded. This significantly enlarges the possibility of space for mix designs.
Since there is no risk of oxidization expansion, the pH can be lower than if to protect against corrosion.
In place of the structural requirement comes the biological one, since the purpose of these reefs is not
sediment or wave protection but giving back habitat. Therefore concrete needs the ability to harbor
life, a feat rarely focused intensely on.
These two can be seen as the two largest pointers taking us away from traditional concretes, but softer
goals are also listed, namely price and emissions. The unit price or the volume price is very important
since this concrete will not exist in a vacuum, but compete against other materials such as natural rock
for potential projects.
Emissions are very relevant since we are facing an environmental crisis, worth mentioning that is also
an energy crisis, which intensifies the cost of calcination. Since concrete is chosen from the beginning

price and emissions is simply by reducing the quantity of cement used. Generally, highôÙperformance

2.4

2.4.1
Performance metrics
The objective of this study is to evaluate the suitability of highly porous concretes as candidates for
use in artificial reef construction. Four performance metrics have been identified as relevant for this
evaluation: ecology, emissions, affordability, and miscellaneous.
Ecology ôÙ Primary: The primary focus of the evaluation will be on the ecology of the concrete, as it is
essential to support the settlement and growth of marine organisms to function as a reef. This report
will likely focus on settling, to fit within the timeframe of the thesis, however, longôÙterm growth should
ideally be assessed and evaluated as well. While a hard metric to accurately quantify, it should also
be made comparable, for example through normalization of unit volume or the two secondary metrics
below.
Emissions ã Secondary: As for most constructions currently, the greenhouse gas emissions must
be considered if not only to preôÙemptively counteract incoming emissions requirements. This metric
could be viewed as the secondary objective, vital if the public image of concrete is to change. This last
point could prove even more important if AR is to become truly widespread, as public approval often
is needed.
Affordability ôÙ Secondary: To increase the likelihood that a given party will choose to initialize a reef
project, or to increase the span for a fixed budget, this would also be a secondary objective. Measuring
and judging this metric should emphasize important threshold values for competing solutions such as
granite, noting that concrete production could benefit hugely from industrialization if designed optimally.
Miscellaneous ôÙ Tertiary: If the concrete has additional features, such as the strength to be considered

2.4.2
Possible test setups for evaluation of ecology
Laboratory test: A laboratory (lab) test where samples in a controlled environment are spored by a
single species of seaweed, a model species. The samples are then regularly checked for the number
of successful spores on each sample type. This would, according to collaborating advisors on the
project, be a good predictor of general ecological performance if the species chosen is good for the
targeted environment. The initial success of spores, and how many are kept over time could therefore
be a performance metric for ecology. The controlled environment of the lab delivers more easy access
to samples and reduces the risks of samples being unusable or incomparable for whatever reason. It
also accommodates precise measurement by counting spores on a given area for each sample type.
However only using a single species, even if a good model species is chosen, would narrow the useôÙ
fulness of the conclusion drawn as multiple species and animals are the actual targets for AR. Direct
cultivation of seaweed on concrete, is a fairly novel approach, and would therefore also draw a new
conclusion for such an experiment. If the cultivation is successful and provides newly established reefs
a kick start, to a more stable ecosystem with more perennial plants, then this would be a good finding
on its own.
A close collaboration with the seaweed industry, also provided opportunities to exploring inôÙdirect seedôÙ

in large scale hatcheries, and then transplanted to ropes in the oceans. This would further facilitate
comparisons between a direct and inôÙdirect method, in a cost benefit analysis.

Seaweeds or macroalgae, a subtype of which is kelp, are marine algae that grow in the ocean and
are classified as either brown, red, or green. Seaweeds are an important part of marine ecosystems

forests [85].
Seaweeds have a simple structure, consisting of a holdfast, stipe, and blade. The holdfast is a rootôÙ

supports the blade. The blade is the photosynthetic portion of the seaweed, and it is where most of
the growth occurs. See fig. 2.10, where the center of the blade is marked as the sorus tissue. This
darker center of the blade is where new spores spring from. Seaweeds are a valuable resource for

2.9
Digestion test
ion test}
This test is made to evaluate the worstôÙcase leaching scenario in an effective way. Looking mostly to
determine the contents of heavy metals harmful to organic life. Results are gathered in accordance
with the American standard EPA30551A but are tested against danish legal documentation for toxic
soils and building waste [101].
Test type
Digestion of inorganic samples refers to the process of breaking down an inorganic material, such as
a mineral or metal, into its constituent components. This is typically done using chemical reactions or
mechanical processes, and the resulting mixture is then analyzed to determine the composition of the
sample.
There are several methods that can be used for the digestion of inorganic samples, including wet
digestion and dry digestion. Wet digestion involves the use of aqueous solutions to dissolve the sample,
while dry digestion involves the use of dry reagents or heat to break down the sample. The choice of
method depends on the nature of the sample and the type of analysis being performed.
In addition to chemical methods, physical methods such as grinding or crushing can also be used to
prepare inorganic samples for analysis. These methods are generally used to reduce the sample size
or to homogenize the sample, making it easier to analyze.
For testing the sourced materials, a grinding wet process was utilized to determine to contents of the
samples. The author did most of the preparation, but not the actual analysis. The preparation will be
described in brief, with the generated report deliverable on request.

2.11.1
EPD
Environmental Product Declarations are documents that provide transparent and comparable informaôÙ
tion about the environmental impact of products. They are based on life cycle assessment principles
and include information about the inputs, outputs, and environmental impacts of a product over its entire
life cycle. EPDs are developed according to ISO 14025 and include requirements for content, format,
and verification processes. They are used to improve the environmental performance of products,
support sustainable procurement decisions, and inform consumer purchasing decisions. Key environôÙ
mental indicators in EPDs include greenhouse gas emissions, energy and water consumption, and
resource and environmental impacts. EPDs may not reflect the environmental impacts of all products
in a particular category and should be considered in the context of their use.
Section A1ôÙA3
EPD are divided into sections A, B and C, each with multiple stages. Section A covers manufacturing
of the product. B covers the use case and C is the endôÙofôÙlife section. As the EPD for building materials
Optimization of artificial concrete reef elements

are targeting traditional uses as constructional elements, the B section is disregarded. Furthermore the
C section might be irrelevant, as reefôÙbuilding could be an endôÙofôÙlife use case, or quasiôÙpermanent.
So only the A section is examined to deduce the emission footprint of the materials, but only the first
three stages as stage A4 describes transport to building site. This selection is further beneficial in two
parts. Firstly only the stages A1ôÙA3 are required by law to be described by the manufacturer, the rest
is voluntary reporting. The second relates to the first, as it would signify a greater care and precision
in developing these estimates, especially when the manufacturer is not necessarily responsible for
later stages. So within the stages A1ôÙA3 some relevant values can be highlighted and drawn for
comparisons.
GWP
The global warming potential (GWP) is a measure of the warming effect of greenhouse gases. It is the
first value listed in an EPD an often viewed as the most important characteristic due to the direct effect
of the gasses. One reason for focusing on GWP is that it provides a consistent and comparable way
to quantify the warming effect of different greenhouse gases. Noting that GWP is only one aspect of
the overall environmental impact, other factors, such as their effects on air/water quality and human
health exist.

Reports also contain information of the embedded energy, used to create the product. These are

3.1.9
Results and considerations
There seems to be considerable difference between the materials, with good homogeneity between
specimen of the same type. This indicate that the unique traits offered by each material does make
a difference in ecological performance. There is some uncertainty to this statement due to the strong
grouping of types, except for graniteôÙslabs that saw very different results in each group. This suggest
that the samples should have been mixed, to increase independence between specimen of the same
type. On independence, some could argue that the single tank and its seawater profile over time, is the
only tested parameter. Others that no independence within the types exist, again due to lack of water
separation. The taller cobblestones were also grouped, but seemed slightly less affected by the pump,
except for the very closest specimen. It is unfortunate that an unaccounted and unevenly distributed
factor affects the results so much, but water currents plays a major role in natural settlement.
The idea behind surface roughness was exactly to catch more in the current, both with a larger surface,
but also more a complex one. If speculating in hydrodynamics, increased surface roughness would
also increase turbulence, that perhaps could further increase the catch rate. Similarly for porosity, as
current could be absorbed/slowed in the material rather than deflected at speed. This was hoped to
74
Optimization of artificial concrete reef elements
==PAGE:86==
increase the retention and penetration of organisms. Porosity could be argued somewhat effective at
this, since the ACC were also close to the pump but largely unaffected. It is inconclusive if this change
can be attributed to the porous and rougher surface of ACC, or to differences in experienced current.
If the former was proved, it would signify less vulnerability for the type.
While algal cover of unspecified species is judged to be greater on the ACC and the construction
concrete, this doesnãt directly mean better performance, as the experiment set out to judge based on
members of the Saccharina Latissima species. A generalized ecological performance is deemed benôÙ
eficial in regards to the project. However, specifically to the endeavours of this test, perennial taxa
must be established for it to be worthwhile. Therefore the pursuit of counting juvenile sporophytes
continues, as the main unobtained goal for the test.
The idea of indirectly seeding through the use of spore lines was also not validated within the experiôÙ
mental time. It is possible that the thread purchased is unsuited for the application, it could have needed
the washing or a better coiling. Generally the threads did seem as covered as the granite, but even
under the microscope it was hard to verify any of the wanted species. All these uncertainties stem from
simply preparing the thread for transplantation, something that is already done commercially at scale.
So even without proving the concept of indirect seeding, the reasoning is validated. By outsourcing the
germination to existing hatcheries, the variable of cotton thread is also avoided. This is emphasized
as this complex and expensive operation, is made even more so with voluminous and heavy concrete.
If indirect seeding is even half as effective in full transplantation, that approach would likely be many
times cheaper normalized area.
It is noteworthy how much the final lowerôÙlimit concentration differs from the recommendations. Still
the tank and the samples in it, has undergone significant transformation within the project period, inôÙ
dicating the first and last seeding attempts somewhat successful. It is understandable that sacrificing
quantification of seedlings is undesirable, but perhaps necessary for larger water volumes. The propôÙ
agation method of adding the sori directly to the water should be closer to the natural conditionsm
and eases longer seeding periods. Perhaps the dryôÙstressing in between breakerôÙbatches, can make
up the differences in release time. Multiple different organism are confirmed in the tank, denying an
exclusivity argument. However, as two independent sources of SL have been used to seed the tank,
a good chance of finding SL should be acknowledged. If the desired seaweed contains even a small
subset of the total growth, there are good possibilities for successful transplantation.
Apart from starting the experiment late, the only speculated errors are transport of the first seedlings,
and then covering them in darkness for days. 72 hour periods of complete darkness was not found
be justified by the literature after all. In covering the samples and absence of movement, unwanted
bacteria could have dominated the water, which led to slow to no growth.
Outcome
The experiment has so far failed to meet its own objective of quantifying perennial SL growth, and
inferring the best suited material. It has failed to test if preôÙseeding of reef units, can lead to a more
rapidly developing stable ecosystem. It has failed to compare direct, and inôÙdirect, seeding methods in
a transplanted environment. On the RPC this would be a subscore of zero, but the experiment has not
been terminated yet. The laboratory setup is so far planned to run throughout February, in the hopes of
finding juvenile sporophytes. If confirmed SL is found, that could answer the first point. Transplantation
would and should then likely be considered an experiment in itself.

3.2.8
Results and considerations
As the the test is also still ongoing, an effort to provide more effective monitoring is essential to better
future data. It is unfortunate that issues relating to waves action was not better accounted and preôÙ
pared for. These issues are indicative of the design coming first, then fitted to the location later. Had
the testing grounds been inspected beforehand, with more time iterating on the design based on the
specifics, better data should have been retrieved.
While durability of the porous concretes are inferior in this scenario, the results seems more reflecting
of the method used, than the intended use. To clarifyëƒ the rack holding structures are not a part of a
potential reef, and would therefore not be present in an actual AR setup. The racks are an artifact, of
trying to even out differences, and keeping samples for easier retrieval.
Ease of testing, at the cost of realism, is again affecting the placement depth. As SL normally grows at
depths of 2ôÙ5m, the racks should have been place further out. This would also help reduce the duraôÙ
bility issues as orbital wave effects decrease with increased depth. If stormy weather and powerful
waves/current is responsible for washing away growth, this should also have been lessened with inôÙ
creased depth. The inôÙdetermination of causes for algal decline, either external or seasonal, obscures
the most important traits.
The ACC was the first material to show signs of colonization and retained growth the best, attributed to
more surface roughness and complexity providing increase shelter. This would question why the LECA
with similar characteristics did not perform as well. The answer might lie in the chemical composition,
as ACC is chemically more similar to the construction concrete that performed the second best. Under
this interpretation chemical composition is necessary for attraction, with retention being linked more to
physical characteristics. This would not explain why the ACC colonized more rapidly, but it could be
speculated to relate to pH regulation of the surface.
An ignored indicator of microôÙalgae could be the snails observed since the first check. As these animals
eat algae, their presence could be used to indicate it. In eating the potential algae, the snails would of
course cover their tracks, leaving a small paradox. As an example, if the granite was the best material
for algal growth it should also attract more algaeôÙeaters. This attraction would in turn lead to less algae
hidden within the snails. Such an argument favoring snail cover, in which the granite would exceed,
could have been determined by effective monitoring. By checking for sign of early growth before the
attraction of snails. An alternate explanation could see the snails preferring the ãsmell/compositionã
of granite, due to instinctual habit. As AR seeks biodiversity of not just algae, a counting of benthic
species could have provided a source quantifiable data, that the project has lacked. Such counting
should preferably be done directly in the water, before moving the racks. This missed opportunity is
however somewhat justified by the available equipment, as prolonged exposure to winter waters in
a wetsuit without gloves is ill advised. Including a more diverse set of parameters in the evaluation
should be sought out, but requires determination to do so beforehand.
Outcome
It has been observed how different materials attract bioôÙfilm and smaller benthic animals, even when
placed late in the season. In this the ACC was observed to colonize faster and retain algal cover better
than the other material types. Ranking the remaining materials is less clear and is dependent on the
inclusion of benthic animals. A decline in said algalôÙgrowth was observed for all materials, but the
cause is inconclusive. As the test is ongoing, the next milestone is observing how the next seasonal
bloom affects the materials. Focus will be put on widening the evaluation to include benthic attraction,
and if retained growth provides a benefit in the new season. As such this experiment also points to
a RPC score of zero, with management issues and inconclusive data. To iterate on the methodology,
specimen should to be reconfigured. Either by moving the test racks further out at increased depth, or
the racks disassembled and the specimen piled more akin to natural reef.

Discussion
4.1
Experiences from test
Running two simultaneous tests with the same premise under very different circumstances proved
difficult. Assumptions and oversights made in the planning phase, resulted in subôÙoptimal and delayed
execution. These primarily stemmed from inexperience with the tests and equipment normally used
for them. It highlights how the scientific method can be interpreted as inflexible, but more so for large
projects. Coordination and allocation of resources in this instance, took away from the actual testing.
As many different bodies was sought out for help, it increased coordination efforts. After the project plan
was accepted, the budget was invested quickly to give time for preparation. As this thesis report comes
to an end, it represents an opportunity to redesign the project to take advantage of the lessons learned,
see section 4.1.1. These suggestions should seek to unite the two test into a new transplantation test.
Missing macrostructure
As this project sought to test materials specifically, specimen were analysed as individual units. This
approach is based on an assumption that traits of the type would materialize at all sizeôÙscales and
ignores nonôÙlinear reefôÙeffect. This assumption is untested, and as such would require later experiôÙ
mentation to determine. Example if a large porous reef would extrapolate to reducing current speeds
by allowing passôÙthrough rather than only redirection, it could outperform other reefs in channels, or
were undertow is dominant.
At the medium scale, small openings and caves structures have by themselves acted as habitat. The
cylindrical outwards facing surface of the tested specimen provides no medium scale effects. The only
incorporation of medium scale was the rack structure. The small towers provided part shelter to each
other, and the steel frame added opportunity for catch drifting seaweed.
In the effort to simplify fitting with the stressed timeframe, these consideration were cut. It is now
valuable to ask how the results would be affected by incorporating a larger and more common reef
shape. This could be done by discontinuing the racks, as in the lab test. The specimen should still be
configured in similar ways for comparisons, but seek closer resemblance to natural reef.
Something about the lack of time
As seen from previous experiments, time is needed to properly evaluate a multiôÙannual process. As
this experiment sought to speed up the establishment, it was somewhat fitting the be stressed for time.
It is again worth remembering that the experiment was always meant to continue beyond this report,
but the conclusion would likely have benefited from longer experimentation.

Conclusion
The project hypothesised that porous concretes would perform equivalent or better in ecological test
against relevant controls. By being cheaper and/or less emission heavy per unit volume, an equivalent
ecological value would satisfy the criteria set of being an optimized candidate. The Two different porous
concretes were chosen for testing, against two controls of construction concrete and granite. The first
test material was an ACC sourced from a waste source. The second was a generic LECA concrete
purchasable at many locations. None of the tested materials were found less emission heavy than
granite, but both less than construction concrete. The ACC was found the most expensive per unit
volume, while the LECA the cheapest.
In two separate ecological experiments, still ongoing per 22/01/23, the types were tested against each
other to asses ecological value. These two experiments are meant to combine into one, to test a
separate hypothesis about the effectiveness of preôÙseeding and transplantation. As per writing, the first
two experiments have not completely concluded, and have so far not reached the maturity decisively
rank to the ecological value. So far the ACC seems to slightly outperform the other types in early
colonisation and retainment. The construction control comes in second, with delayed settlement and
worse retention. The LECA and granite both failed to acquire the same visible algal growth found on
the first two, but the granite did suggest an increase in benthic activity. As one porous concrete came
out ahead, and one dead last, their common trait of high porosity is not suggested to be a decisive
boon in itself. This would negate the hypothesis, but a potential material has emerged from the chosen
ones. Assuming less regard for price or utilization of a found wasteôÙsource, ACC seems to be good
substitution for traditional concrete in artifical reefs. One remark to this would be a durabilityôÙissue
discovered in the one of the two experiments. The issue is deemed to stem from an inôÙproper setup,
but confirmation of this is needed before large scale projects with the material.
More decisive and numerical data is still deemed necessary, to fully conclude the best of four AR
candidates. According to the Reef Performance Scale [21] the project so far has scored zero points,
awarding a neutral score. While this thesis is now concluded, the project still has potential for further
optimization and future ideas to be tested. In the very near future, the planned next steps have the
potential to lift the score by +2 points.
