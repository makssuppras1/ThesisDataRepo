Abstract
The optimization of the Advanced Combinational Encoder (ACE) speech coding strategy in
a cochlear implant (CI) for Danish language is investigated. Compared to the English language
for instance, Danish contains many more vowels. As the similarity of vowel CI output patterns is
assumed to be the key factor for decreased speech intelligibility, the focus of the optimization is put
on decreasing this similarity by adjusting the parameters of the ACE strategy according to ﬁndings
of a CI output pattern analysis. For this purpose, the "ACEMapCalculator" toolbox, based on the
Nucleus MATLAB Toolbox by Cochlear, has been developed. The optimization approach involves
increasing the low and mid frequency resolution of the CI. This emphasizes the differences of the
vowels in their ﬁrst and second formants. The assessment of setting adjustments is based on the
change in correlation of the vowel output patterns to each other. A setting that yields objective
improvements in the decorrelation of these patterns and provides a balanced electrode output was
found.
The validity of these objective improvements has been tested in a normal hearing listeners
vocoding experiment. To achieve a simple test paradigm, the perceptional difference of the vowels
processed with the reference ACE strategy setting to the optimized setting has been assessed.
Signiﬁcant preference for the optimized setting could be concluded from the results of this test.
However, the application of these changes to CI patients may cause issues that go beyond the scope
of this study. Effects on the perceived sound quality and sound convenience have to be investigated
in clinical tests, since vocoding is not perfectly resembling the perception of CI users. Furthermore,
more realistic tests, that directly assess the resulting speech intelligibility of the strategy change,
have to be considered. However, an overall positive impact on speech intelligibility and therefore
quality of communication for Danish CI users is expected. Moreover, the tools and approaches to
customize speech coding strategies provided by this thesis are seen as generally applicable to other
languages as well.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Many people around the world suffer from profound hearing loss. Where traditional hearing
aids were ineffective, people were left without hearing for a long time and established other ways
of communication. In the early 1960s, scientists came up with ways to electrically stimulate the
auditory nerve and skipping the mechano-acoustical side of hearing. First steps were made by
simply implanting a single electrode into the cochlea and feeding this electrode with electrical
impulses [1]. The high potential and chance of this new way of aided hearing fascinated researchers
all around the world and by the beginning of the 1980s ﬁrst commercial cochlear implants (CI)
with multiple electrodes were brought to the market. Different ways of transforming acoustical
perception into electrical sequences at the electrodes were established and are given the name
"speech coding strategies". Today’s implants, like the Cochlear Nucleus, mostly utilize "N-of-M"
strategies, which so far showed the best performance in scientiﬁc studies [2].
The motivation of this work titled "Teaching a Cochlear Implant How to Speak Danish" is to
optimize the Advanced Combinational Encoder strategy (ACE), a "N-of-M" strategy implemented
in Cochlear Nucleus implants, for the Danish language content. Compared to the English language
for instance, Danish contains many more vowels [3]. This leads to the assumption, that the simi-
larity of vowels, when processed through a CI, is decreasing speech intelligibility for Danish CI
users. To achieve a strategy improvement, the "ACEMapCalculator" toolbox has been developed,
incorporating the implant emulation functions of the Nucleus MATLAB Toolbox provided by
Cochlear [4]. It is used for the analysis of Danish vowel CI output patterns and the assessment
of effects on the discriminability of these patterns, caused by changes in the ACE strategy. The
general approach is to emphasize vowel differences in their ﬁrst and second formants by changing
the ﬁlterbank settings of the strategy and compensating for changes in energy distribution with
further parameters of the strategy. The similarity of vowel output patterns was determined by their
linear correlation with each other. Respectively, improvements of strategy changes were quantiﬁed
by differences in this correlation. An ACE strategy setting, that gives the desired optimization for a
set of Copenhagen Danish vowels by Nina Grønnum [3] has been developed.

CHAPTER 2
Background
This chapter provides background information about important principles and tools necessary
for the realization of this project. Firstly, cochlear implants are discussed and its main processing
stages explained. Secondly, a more detailed insight is given into the signal processing within
cochlear implants, that gives rise to the term "speech coding strategies". In the third part of this
chapter, the Danish language is introduced by means of its vowels and a comparison to English
vowels is given. Moreover, the problems coming along with processing these vowels in a cochlear
implant are introduced. Lastly, the Nucleus MATLAB Toolbox by Cochlear Ltd. is presented and
its relevance for the project outlined. It embodies the computational foundation for the simulation
of cochlear implant outputs.
2.1
Cochlear Implants
Cochlear implants are today’s state of the art devices for restoring auditory perception in
people with profound hearing loss. Unlike normal hearing aids, they do not just amplify acoustical
stimulation, but convert sound waves into electrical sequences, that directly excite the auditory
nerve ﬁbers through an electrode array inserted in the cochlea. Developed since the 1960s, they
have been steadily improved by incorporating the latest signal processing methods and hardware to
compensate malfunctions in the ear in a most natural way [5].
2.1.1
The Cochlea
The cochlea is a snail shaped organ making around 2.5 turns on a length of approximately
30 mm placed close to the temporal bone right behind the middle ear. It is responsible for the
conversion of mechano-acoustic vibrations into neural excitation. Sound waves arriving at the
outer ear are picked up by the tympanic membrane as air pressure ﬂuctuations and transformed
into mechanical vibrations in the middle ear. These vibrations are then transmitted to the cochlea,
where they excite different regions of the basilar membrane, depending on their frequency content
(tonotopic organization) [6]. Low frequencies are known to excite the more apical parts of the
basilar membrane, so the end of the snail, whereas high frequencies rather stimulate the basal part,
hence areas close to the oval window, the connection to the middle ear. The inner hair cells in the

organ of Corti along the basilar membrane then convert these vibrations into neural ﬁring patterns,
that are send to the higher stages of auditory perception in the brain. Furthermore, the outer hair
cells of the cochlea work as an ampliﬁer for weak incoming sounds and increase the dynamic range
of perception signiﬁcantly.
Hearing impairment can involve different stages of auditory processing from the outer ear over
the middle ear to the inner ear with the cochlea and ﬁnally the brain. The loss of outer or inner
hair cells in the cochlea is one of the most frequent reasons for reduced auditory sensation and
also called sensorineural hearing loss. In cases where no satisfactory treatment with hearing aids
can be achieved, cochlear implants can restore hearing signiﬁcantly by replacing the mechanical
stimulation of the basilar membrane with direct electrical excitation of nerve ﬁbers connected to
the inner hair cells.
2.1.2
"Electrical Hearing"
The transformation of acoustic signals at the ear of the CI recipient to electrical stimuli at the
electrode array in the cochlea inheres a wide range of processing stages, as shown in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Typical cochlear implant system with a behind-the-ear external processor (2), that
sends the processed signal from the outer ear (1) to a headpiece transmitter (3), that conveys the
signal to an internal receiver (4) placed under the skin. A sealed stimulator unit (5) then transforms
the signal into an electrical sequence, that is transmitted through a wire (6) to the electrode array
(7), that directly stimulates the auditory nerve ﬁbers (8), image from [1].
At the ﬁrst stage of processing, sound waves are picked up by a microphone placed in the speech
processor hooked up behind the ear. They are processed into digital sequences using so-called
"speech coding strategy" algorithms. Those sequences are then conveyed through a radio frequency
transmitter to a receiver placed underneath the skin, where they are converted into electrical current

pulses and ﬁnally send to the electrode array inserted in the cochlea.
Among the different manufacturers of cochlear implants, the number of implanted electrodes
varies between 12 and 22. The Cochlear Nucleus implant is used as a reference for this project and

The frequency to electrode allocation in the cochlea depends on the number of successfully in-

relevant frequencies (200-1200 Hz) [1].
2.2
Speech Coding Strategies
The CI’s ability to restore hearing is very limited due to the complexity of the hearing organ.
Nevertheless, the performance of these devices can be optimized for distinct signals as for instance
speech, that can be considered as being most important in the daily communication. The transfor-
mation process from acoustic signals outside of the ear to electrical sequences inside the cochlea is
based on speech coding strategies. They are designed to optimize the imitation of the cochlea’s nat-
ural ﬁring, when exposed to speech signals. Different approaches have been developed to maximize
the spectral and temporal resolution feasible with a CI. In general, speech coding strategies can be
separated in two main categories, the coarse features and ﬁne features representation. An overview
about a wide range of strategies associated with either of these categories is given in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Cochlear implant speech coding strategies [1].
Several studies showed, that waveform representation strategies are most adequate for a natural
reproduction of speech signals [5]. All waveform strategies have a common processing procedure.
In the front end, the microphone signal is emphasized to a decent level to be further processed
in a ﬁlterbank, with sub bands corresponding to the number of electrodes available. It resembles
the ﬁltering of the basilar membrane. In a further stage, envelopes are extracted from this sub

band signals and mapped to the electrode array that stimulates the cochlea. Strategies that differ in
their processing properties like the number of stimulated electrodes and the way the envelopes are
extracted have been developed. The Continuous Interleaved Sampling (CIS) strategy and "N-of-M"
strategies, in particular the ACE strategy shall be more thoroughly explained here. Studies [7] and
[2] have shown that the ACE strategy in general provides better speech intelligibility than the CIS
approach.
2.2.1
Continuous Interleaved Sampling (CIS)
The CIS approach uses all available electrodes in one stimulation cycle. First, the acoustic
signal at the microphone is pre-emphasized and then processed in a set of band pass ﬁlters. They
match the number of electrodes used in the electrode array. From this band pass signals, envelopes
are extracted by means of full-wave rectiﬁcation and low-pass ﬁltering with a typical cut-off
frequency of 200-400 Hz. Those envelopes are then used to modulate balanced biphasic pulses. In
order to avoid disruptive interaction between the electric ﬁelds induced by the electrodes, these
pulses are mapped to the corresponding electrodes in sequence. In the next step, the signal is
logarithmically compressed to ﬁt to the recipient’s electrical hearing dynamic range. The constant
stimulation rate and the non overlapping characteristic make this strategy interleaved [5]. Figure
2.3 illustrates the different processing steps in the CIS strategy.
Figure 2.3: Block diagram of the CIS strategy [5].
2.2.2
"N-of-M" Strategies
Due to the limited processing ability of the speech processor, either a high spectral or high
temporal resolution can be achieved. Contrary to the CIS strategy, the spectral load is reduced in
the "N-of-M" strategies by selecting just the highest N of M peaks for the further processing [5].

Advanced Combinational Encoder (ACE)
The ACE approach is a clinical implementation of the SPEAK strategy as described by [5]. Its
main processing stages are illustrated in Figure 2.5 by [1]. As in the CIS approach, the acoustic
signal at the microphone of the cochlear implant is pre-emphasized and level adjusted by an
adaptive gain control. It is then decomposed into its frequency content by a ﬁlterbank with the
same number of ﬁlters as electrodes. The ﬁlterbank’s crossover frequencies are linearly spaced
below 1000 Hz and logarithmically spaced above 1000 Hz to increase the low frequency resolution.
The envelopes in the M sub bands are estimated for every time frame, but only the N highest
(typically 8-12 maxima) of them are used for sequential stimulation. The number of selected
maxima determines the spectral resolution. The overall stimulation rate in one frame limits the
available stimulation rate per channel, so the possible temporal resolution, proportional to the
number of maxima selected. More selected maxima result in less available stimulation rate for
each stimulated channel. Hence, a trade off between temporal and spectral resolution is made. In
the last processing stage, the selected amplitudes are mapped to the corresponding electrodes and
compressed to ﬁt the recipients electrical dynamic range. This range is measured and deﬁned as
the difference between the electrical hearing threshold and the maximum electrical comfortable
loudness level. The difference between the ACE approach and its predecessor SPEAK is its ability
to choose a wider range of stimulation rates and number of selected envelopes.

3.2
Effect of the Main ACE Map Parameters
The different parameters of the ACE strategy affect the output of the CI in different ways.
To understand these effects and the interaction between them, four main parameters are more
thoroughly analyzed here. Based on this investigations, conclusions can be made for ﬁnding a new
parameter set for the ACE map that fulﬁlls the requirements as set in the Chapter 4. The Frequency
Table, the Channel Stimulation Rate, the Number of Maxima Selected and the Gains are more
closely investigated.
3.2.1
Frequency Table
The frequency table deﬁnes the crossover frequencies of the ﬁlterbank, that extracts the
envelopes from incoming microphone signals as described in Chapter 2. In fact, the crossover
frequencies deﬁne the channels of the CI and the frequency regions, that stimulate the different
electrodes. Therefore, this parameter is also referred to the so called frequency-to-electrode
allocation. The reference or default frequency table as implemented in the Cochlear Nucleus
implants has a linear frequency distribution below 1000 Hz and logarithmic above. The reference
crossover frequencies are shown in Table 3.2.
Different frequency-to-electrode allocations and their inﬂuence on the CI output have been
tested. The closer the crossover frequencies, the narrower are the channels of the CI at that
frequency area. Since the ACE strategy is a N-of-M strategy, the channels with the highest energy
are chosen for electrode stimulation.
Hence, a higher resolution at low frequencies due to a closer frequency-to-electrode allocation,
causes a larger amount of those frequencies in the CI output patterns. Since the Cochlear CIs work
with a ﬁxed FFT window length of 128 samples and a sampling rate of 16 kHz, a maximum FFT
bin spacing of 125 Hz can be achieved. This is to some extent limiting the ability to freely adjust the
crossover frequencies of the ﬁlterbank, nevertheless a minimum channel bandwidth of 62.5 Hz can
be achieved by weighted averaging and is also available in the Cochlear ﬁtting software "Custom
Sound". For unlimited simulations, the free allocation of crossover frequencies can be enabled in
the "ACEMapCaluclator" by choosing "vector sum" envelope extraction in the NMT settings.

3.2.2
Channel Stimulation Rate
The channel stimulation rate deﬁnes the number of current pulses per second (pps) sent to the
electrode in one channel of the CI. The default value for this parameter is 900 pps. Since the total
stimulation rate of the Cochlear Nucleus implants is in the normal processing mode limited to
14400 pps, the maximum channel stimulation rate is limited by the number of maxima selected,
maxchannel_stim_rate = 14400 pps/num_selected.
(3.2)
Earlier studies [16] showed that this parameter is heavily dependent on the patient’s sentiment.
However, it can be concluded that a higher channel stimulation rate results in a better temporal
resolution. This does not by all means result in a better speech intelligibility though. Investigations
in the course of this project showed, that a channel stimulation rate below 900 pps results in a
deeper more crumbled sound, especially for rates below 300 pps, when processed through the
vocoder section of this toolbox. Channel stimulation rates above 900 pps (up to 1800 pps) did
not show any audible changes in the vocoding experiment. This leads to the assumption that the
channel stimulation rate is a parameter which effects cannot be studied properly by vocoding and is
very dependent on the patient’s preference when used in real CIs.
3.2.3
Number of Maxima Selected
The number of maxima selected is an essential parameter of the ACE strategy - only N out of
M channels with the highest envelope energy are chosen for stimulation of the electrodes. The
default value in the Nucleus implants is N = 8 maxima and ranges in between 6 and 12 in clinical
applications. A higher number of selected maxima results in a broader frequency range and comes
along with more high frequency sounds in the stimulation spectrum, nevertheless it can result in a
lower temporal resolution.

Lowering the number of maxima usually emphasizes the low frequency content of the vowels,
since it contains more energy than the high frequencies and is therefore more likely to be chosen
for stimulation by the N-of-M selection process.
3.2.4
Gains
The gains parameter acts like a global equalizer on the microphone signal of the speech
processor and is directly applied to the output of the envelope calculation of the ﬁlterbank output.
Every channel of the CI can be boosted or attenuated individually, depending on the need and
preference of the patient. This can be used to emphasize different characteristics of a language,
that alleviate the discrimination of the processed vowels. Boosting high frequencies results in more
maxima in that region and therefore a more brilliant sound, respectively a deeper sound, when
applied on low frequencies.
3.2.5
Interaction of Parameters
The different parameters of the ACE map have different effects on the output of the CI and how
the patient perceives incoming sounds. The effects of the four main parameters by its own have
been discussed above. In this section, their interaction is discussed.
As shown above, a higher frequency resolution at low frequencies results in more maxima
located at these frequencies, hence in a CI output that emphasizes low frequencies. To preserve
some high frequency content several ways have been found. One way is to increase the number
of maxima, that are selected in the N-of-M procedure, to also capture some peaks in the high
frequencies. Another more effective way is to apply a gain to the high frequency channels and
therefore force the N-of-M algorithm to also pick them up for stimulation. The interaction of the
parameters with the channel stimulation rate is mostly concerning the number of maxima selected.
A decrease in channel stimulation rate allows the selection of more maxima for stimulation.
However, this interaction is not of further interest here, due to its very patient dependent property.

4.2
Optimization Process
The optimization of the ACE strategy for the Danish language is based on the assumption, that
the vowel output pattern similarity is a reason for decreased speech intelligibility. The goal of the
optimization process is to make the Danish vowel CI output patterns as dissimilar as possible, while
preserving the ability to perceive other sounds than speech in a most natural way.
The strategy to satisfy this goal is to ﬁt the frequency table of the ﬁlterbank to the ﬁrst and
second formant regions of the vowel space and compensate the increased energy at this areas by
means of the Gains and the Number of Maxima Selected in the ACE strategy. This approach was
formulated after trying several parameter changes and assessing them with structured analysis and
more random approaches like Monte Carlo simulation. Figure 4.3 shows the different processing
stages of the ACE strategy as already described in Chapter 2.2.2 and highlights the stages that are
included in the optimization process in red.
The optimization process is divided into three steps:
1. Fitting of the frequency table to the vowel space.
2. Adjustment of the channel gains.
3. Reduction of the maxima selected.
Their effects on the vowel output patterns are elaborated in the following.

on the array. Indeed, studies [17] and [18] have shown that increased low frequency resolution is
positively affecting speech perception by CI users. Furthermore, the importance of the most apical
electrodes for vowel recognition was outlined in another study [19]. The ﬁtting was achieved by
sizing the channels of the ﬁlterbank equally among the vowels’ formants. The full frequency table

CHAPTER 5
Listening Experiment
5.1
Introduction
The objective optimization of the ACE strategy for Danish vowels is validated in a listening
experiment with normal hearing listeners. The following two hypotheses shall be examined on the
basis of the results of this study.
• The optimized ACE setting makes Danish vowels sound more differentiable.
• Danish subjects perform better than international ones, due to language knowledge.
To make the strategy changes audible, the vowel recordings by Nina Grønnum were processed
through the NMT vocoding section. As stated in the beginning of the report, this is a limitation and
the results of such an experiment are not directly applicable to CI patients. However, an experiment
with CI patients could not be conducted by reason of time constraint and was also not planned
within this thesis. It is assumed, that ﬁndings in a normal hearing listeners experiment that support
the objective improvements, can be seen as a conﬁrmation of the optimization process and motivate
studies with hearing impaired CI users.
5.2
Methods
5.2.1
Subjects
To make conclusions about inﬂuences of language knowledge possible, ten normal hearing
Danish test subjects, as well as ten normal hearing international subjects, all within the age of 20
to 40, were asked to participate in the study. An auditory screening was achieved for every test
subject, to ensure a hearing threshold below 20 dB HL, that can be considered as normal hearing.
5.2.2
Stimuli
Three subgroups of vowels according to the clusters as deﬁned in Chapter 4.1 were chosen
for the processing through the vocoder of the NMT. The vowels produced in the front, center and
back of the mouth, uttered by a female talker in Copenhagen Danish dialect. To create a most

adequate listening environment, the experiment was conducted in psychoacoustic listening booths.
The sounds were played back through calibrated headphones at a reference level of 68 dB SPL.
5.2.3
Paradigm
The performance of the optimized ACE setting is assessed by comparing it with the reference
setting in a vocoding experiment. This is done by means of a two alternative forced choice task
(2-AFC). Several other tests, as for example the Danish Hearing in Noise Test (DAHINT) [21], have
been considered. Indeed, this kind of experiments would represent a more realistic way of testing
and directly assess the performance of the optimized strategy by means of speech intelligibility.
However, this task was found to be too difﬁcult with vocoded speech and a more simple task was
deﬁned. It was decided to compare the vowels directly to each other, similarly to the objective
similarity matrices in Chapter 4.1.
Three clusters of each 5 different vowels, resulting in 20 different vowel pairs per cluster were
tested. The 2-AFC paradigm is built to force the subjects to choose the pair out of two vowel
pairs processed with the reference map and the optimized map, that contains the most different
sounding vowels. To foster determined decisions, the pairs can be played back as often as needed.
Furthermore, the playback order of the different vowel pairs, as well as the order of processing
settings, so optimized or reference setting ﬁrst, is randomized. Figure 5.1 shows the user interface
of the paradigm in Matlab, based on a 2-AFC framework, implemented in the "ACEMapCalculator"

5.3
Results
This section elaborates the outcome of the experiment and how it is processed to achieve
conclusions about the stated hypotheses. The data is structured and different performance scores
are calculated. First, with focus on overall cluster scores and then with respect to individual vowel
pair scores. The outcome of the "Training" section is not further discussed, since of no relevance

very different sounding vowel pair, thus no bias could be observed.
5.3.1
Cluster Scores
For every test subject all three vowel clusters have been tested three times as described
previously. Every cluster consists of 20 vowel pairs that had to be rated in terms of their perceived
acoustical difference, when either processed with the reference ACE setting or the optimized
setting.
Preference Score over Subjects
To give an overview on how the subjects rated a certain cluster, a percentage number of vowel
pairs that were preferred with the optimized setting is calculated and called "Preference Score" in
the following. It is determined by dividing the number of vowel pairs preferred with the optimized
setting Nopt by the total number of vowel pairs in a cluster Ntot and multiplying it with 100. This
score is calculated for every subject, cluster and trial and used for an analysis of variance.

Analysis of Variance
Based on the preference scores over subjects, a three-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) [15]
was conducted to ﬁnd possible effects of clusters, trials and origin. The analysis for cluster effects is
performed to ﬁnd out, whether some of the clusters are more affected by the strategy changes than
other clusters. The analysis for trials was conducted to investigate learning effects. The origin so
the mother tongue of the test subjects was included, in order to reveal, whether language knowledge
is inﬂuencing the perception of the strategy changes. The analysis was run within the MATLAB
statistical toolbox. Figure 5.2 pictures the results of the ANOVA with the analysis of interactions
between the subgroups clusters, trials and origin.

5.4
Discussion
The two research hypotheses imposed to the experiment at the beginning of this chapter are
further discussed here. Conclusions about the validity of the objective improvements in this normal
hearing listeners experiment are made.
5.4.1
"The optimized ACE setting makes Danish vowels sound more
differentiable."
The ﬁrst hypotheses aims at the general effect of the optimized ACE strategy setting on the
perception of vowels in a vocoding experiment. The preference scores for the three vowel clusters
were analyzed. On one hand, a signiﬁcant preference of both subject subgroups, Danes and
Internationals, for the optimized setting is found for clusters 1 and 2, where cluster 2 shows higher
preference scores than cluster 1. This is in accordance to the objective improvements calculated in
Chapter 4.3. On the other hand, the preference scores for cluster 3 did not indicate any signiﬁcant
preference for the optimized strategy setting for Danish subjects and just a very small preference
for the optimized setting for the international subjects. Hence, no signiﬁcant improvements for
this cluster are concluded. This coincides with the smallest objective improvements found for
this cluster in Chapter 4.3. This might be due to the fact that the vowels in cluster 3 are the ones
produced in the back of the mouth with the most similar formants in natural processing. Overall,
an improvement introduced by the optimized map is noticeable. Accordingly, the ﬁrst hypothesis
imposed on this experiment is conﬁrmed.
5.4.2
"Danish subjects perform better then International, due to language
knowledge."
The second hypotheses in this experiment is referring to a different perception of the optimized
setting between Danish and International test subjects. The analysis of variance on the performance
scores showed a trend for cluster-origin interaction, therefore a chi-square analysis was conducted
to further investigate this relation. A signiﬁcant difference between the perception of vowels by
Danes and Internationals was determined for the clusters 1 and 2, where the second cluster was less
distinct than the ﬁrst. However, no difference due to origin was found for the third vowel cluster.
This might be due to a general difﬁcult discrimination of vowels in this cluster. It is concluded that
a difference in the responses of the Danish and international subjects exists. Hence, also the second
hypothesis is conﬁrmed to some extent.
In conclusion, both experiment hypotheses could be conﬁrmed by means of the result of this
experiment. Beyond that, a good agreement between the simulated improvements and the measured
vowel pair preference scores was found. This supports the validity of the chosen paradigm. The
optimized setting has promising impact on the perception of vowels processed through a CI for
normal hearing listeners. Consequently, tests with real CI patients are recommended.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
The analysis and simulation of Danish vowel CI output patterns led to the development of
an optimized ACE speech coding strategy setting. The validity of the objective improvements
introduced by this strategy setting were then proved in a normal hearing listeners experiment. In
this chapter, the applicability of this optimization to CI patients and its limitations afﬁliated with
that, as well as some shortcomings of this approach, are outlined. Furthermore, recommendations
for improvement and further investigations are given.
6.1
Optimization approach
First of all, the tools for an optimization of the ACE strategy to the Danish language were
determined. It was decided, to base the analysis on the Danish vowels. This is limiting the scope
of optimization to this languages’ feature. Anyway, it is a common feature used for specifying
languages. For practical reasons, one Danish reference vowel set was chosen for the processing. It
is based on Copenhagen Danish uttered by a female talker and is likely to differ from other dialects
in Denmark. Accordingly, effects of other dialects on the optimization strategy have not been
assessed and have to be further investigated.
The computational foundation of all CI output patterns simulations is given by the NMT by
Cochlear. This implies, that the undertaken strategy adjustments are based on their implementation
in cochlear implants of the brand Cochlear. Nevertheless, the general ﬁndings of the optimization
process should be applicable to CIs of all other manufacturers utilizing the ACE strategy or
strategies similar to this. Furthermore, only ACE strategy settings, that are editable in the Cochlear
ﬁtting software "Custom Sound" have been taken into consideration, to ensure direct applicability
of the optimized settings in clinical environments. However, these are also the main parameters that
have the biggest inﬂuence on the processed signal. Due to the limitation to normal hearing listeners
in the validation process, implant parameters that are not perceivable in a vocoding experiment
were not considered for the optimization. It is assumed that those parameters are very patient
speciﬁc and not adequate for a general ﬁtting approach.
The analysis of vowel output patterns and the assumption that their similarity is detrimentally
affecting speech intelligibility were the initial concepts for the development of an optimization
39
==PAGE:58==
40
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
process. After having considered different metrics for calculating the similarity of these patterns,
Pearson’s linear correlation was chosen as an appropriate measure for the further processing. It
showed the best agreement to experimental data from the PhD thesis of David Jackson [14] and
beyond that used as a correlation measure in many other applications [15]. Yet, it is not generally
proven to represent the perception of sound similarities perfectly.
The optimization process is based on the ﬁtting of the frequency table to the ﬁrst and second
formants vowel space and the compensation of the shifted energy distribution over the electrodes
with the gains of the ﬁlterbank and the number of maxima selected in the N-of-M process. Instead
of implementing this approach by hand, based on ﬁndings of Monte Carlo simulations and analysis
of the effect of different parameters as it is done in this thesis, a more complex computation
could be developed. An algorithm that automatically allocates the channels of the ﬁlterbank to
the vowel space in the most appropriate way could be investigated. The adaptive changes for the
gain parameter and the number of maxima selected could be calculated based on deﬁned energy
distributions over the electrodes. Even though this requires more research, the development could
aim at a toolbox that just needs to be fed with recordings of the vowel space and information about
the cochlear implant and then returns recommendations of how to adjust the speech coding strategy
settings.
The chosen optimization approach focuses on the improvement of vowel discriminability
achieved through higher frequency resolution at the vowels’ ﬁrst and second formants. This is
accompanied by a shift of higher frequency components in the vowels towards electrodes closer to
the base of the basilar membrane. No studies were made about the convenience of this shift for the
CI patients’ perception. Common clinical experience has shown that patients are most sensitive
to high frequencies and a risk of annoyance can be assumed and has to be further investigated in
clinical studies.
Nevertheless, the optimization process resulted in promising changes, and improvements for
Danish CI patients are expected. However, some time will be needed to observe improvements for
real patients. This is due to their need for time, up to 6 months [1], to adapt to changes in speech
coding strategy settings. Moreover, it is very likely that ﬁndings of CI patients experiments lead
to an improvement of the here developed ACE strategy setting. The here discussed setting was
chosen on basis of objective measures and showed that improvements in vowel discrimination are
achievable when tested with normal hearing listeners.
6.2
Listening experiment
The validity of the developed optimization has been proven in a normal hearing listener
vocoding experiment. The Danish vowels were directly compared to each other, to assess the effect
of the optimized strategy setting on the discrimination of vowels. Noise was chosen as the carrier
signal for the vocoding in the experiment, it is assumed to give the most realistic perception of
CI processed vowels. Anyway, it can not be seen as absolutely identical to the perception that CI
users have. Since only vowel pairs and their discriminability was tested, no direct conclusions
about the change in speech intelligibility introduced by the optimized setting can be made. Only
==PAGE:59==
6.3. CONCLUSIONS
41
the assumption that a more different perception of vowels increases speech intelligibility, suggests
a success of this process in CI patients. As expected, Danish subjects performed better than
international subjects in the experiment and an overall preference for the optimized setting could be
observed, even though no improvement was found for one of the three vowel clusters. For testing
the performance of the optimized strategy setting with CI patients, the same paradigm as in the
listening experiment within this thesis is recommended. Thereby, conclusions about the validity of
the testing in this thesis are made possible. Moreover, realistic tests that can be easily implemented
in a clinical study and give insights into effects on speech intelligibility, like the "Danish Hearing
in Noise Test" (DAHINT), are recommended.
6.3
Conclusions
The aim of this project was to optimize the ACE speech coding strategy in a Cochlear Nucleus
implant to improve the speech intelligibility for Danish cochlear implant users. A toolbox, the
"ACEMapCalculator", to achieve this optimization has been developed. It is based on the analysis
of Danish vowels. The obtained ACE strategy setting was tested in a listening experiment with
normal hearing listeners. The objective improvements measured in the "ACEMapCalculator"
toolbox could be validated to a signiﬁcant extent. Considering the limitations coming along with
a normal hearing listeners experiment, clinical studies with CI patients are recommended, and
effects on speech intelligibility and listening convenience should be investigated. In conclusion, the
developed approach gives basis for optimizing the ACE strategy to any language content, based on
the analysis of its vowels.
