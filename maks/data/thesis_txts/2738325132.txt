Abstract 
This thesis covers an investigation of innovative methods for double-sided joining by 
forming of open structures, specifically focusing on an implementation of the self-pierce 
riveting (SPR) principle and a hole-tab riveting concept. The tested open structure’s 
geometry is related to that of centrifugal pump impellers found in Grundfos’ pumps, and 
experimental variants were inspired by these. The implemented SPR variant pierces a 
chamfered blade through opposing plates without pre-drilling or pre-forming holes in the 
plates. This forms a secure joint by deforming the chamfered blade within the material, 
creating an interlock. High axial forces on the blades, may cause buckling, therefore 
requiring an anti-buckling fixture during the assembly process. Hole-tab riveting involves 
creating tabs on the blades that fit into corresponding holes in the plates. The tabs are 
then deformed into the holes to lock them together and create a joint with no protrusions 
due to the hole's design. Correct design of the open structure and tab deformation allows 
fixture-free assembly using the hole-tab concept. As both concepts’ joints are formed 
cold, they present opportunities in comparison to welded joints which can degrade 
material properties or create adverse geometric factors, thereby weakening the joints. 
Experimental results of destructive load testing indicate high axial strength of the hole-
tab riveting concept and that using certain layouts of the SPR concept’s blades, 
increased the concept’s strength significantly. While SPR reached higher blade-radial 
strength than the hole-tab concept in one direction, it was highly directional and 
significantly weaker in the opposite direction. Hole-tab riveting showed good blade-radial 
strength, likely limited by material properties which could be assumed independent of 
the loading direction. The hole-tab concept benefitted from higher strength plates, 
whereas the SPR concept was unable to be joined into “as is” condition steel plates. 
Based on test findings, SPR concept improvement suggestions were made mainly 
focusing on the back-to-back layout of blades, providing greater resistance to blades 
escaping the interlock. Suggestions were also made for the hole-tab concept, focusing 
on increasing the hole-fill ratio by minimizing unwanted plastic deformation. Although 
more testing is required before both concepts can be used in commercial applications 
such as pump impellers, their results show good potential. In their current forms, SPR 
joints may be the better choice for automated high-volume production but lack 
compatibility with full steel structures. In contrast, the hole-tab riveting concept may 
require tabs to be slotted into holes by hand, making it better suited for lower-volume 
production or prototyping. Solving either problem and doing comparative testing with 
currently used welding techniques, as well as testing corrosive and fatigue-life properties, 
may be recommended based on the findings in this report.

1 Introduction 
In advanced manufacturing and mechanical engineering, the search for innovative 
methods for joining components is important for enhancing the assembly process and 
performance of products. At Grundfos, a leading manufacturer of pumps, this pursuit is 
important for improving the assembly of pump impellers. Current joining techniques such 
as laser welding and resistance projection welding have served well, yet they present 
certain limitations. Issues such as fatigue due to adverse geometric factors and 
metallurgical effects are well-known problems induced by the welding process, 
motivating the search for alternative joining methods. 
A promising alternative is the use of metal forming techniques for joining, which involves 
plastically deforming materials to achieve the desired shape. This method can avoid 
weaknesses introduced by welding and enhances some material properties through 
strain hardening. Among various metal forming techniques, self-pierce riveting (SPR) 
stands out due to its ability to join different materials without needing pre-drilled holes, 
reducing the number of operations and improving efficiency. The automotive industry 
has successfully utilised SPR for 25 years, highlighting its potential. 
Impellers, at their most basic level, consist of a front and back disc separated by blades, 
which form an open structure. This structure is sensitive to axial loading, which may 
cause unstable buckling of the blades during assembly operations requiring such forces. 
Metal forming techniques such as SPR and hole-tab riveting were explored in this 
project, investigating the effects of different geometrical factors. Both joining concepts 
were based on recent literature but applied in adjusted or new ways to fit the open 
structure morphology. The two concepts and their variants were comparatively tested in 
an axial and blade-radial destructive load test. Furthermore, a replication of each SPR 
concept variant was cut through to investigate the interlock morphology itself. This was 
done to reveal or disprove connections between interlock morphology and strength, as 
some strength improvements may not only stem from the interlock morphology itself but 
from larger geometric factors.

2 Background and theoretical framework 
This chapter provides general background information regarding centrifugal pumps 
produced by Grundfos and how they relate to open structures. Articles relevant to the 
joining technology tested in this project are highlighted, with an emphasis on findings 
that were used as inspiration during this project. 
2.1 Pump impellers, open structures and joining 
Grundfos produces radial pumps (also known as centrifugal pumps). A vital component 
of a centrifugal pump is the impeller, which boosts fluid pressure through rotational 
motion. The impeller primarily consists of a front and back disc, separated by blades. 
The impeller assembly in Figure 2.1A can be put in series using multiple impellers and 
guide vane assemblies, to improve a pump's performance. At Grundfos multistage 
pumps are common, and many stages are typically used as seen in Figure 2.1.  
 
The impeller assembly is an open structure, with fluid travelling inside the open space 
separated by blades, being contained by the front and back discs. The blades and discs 
are primarily joined using laser welding for larger impellers and resistance projection 
welding for smaller impeller assemblies. Both processes involve heating blade and disc 
material to a melt, creating a bond after solidification. Due to adverse geometric (such 
as weld toe notch factor) and metallurgical effects induced by welding, welded structures 
are particularly prone to fatigue [1]. When designing parts in a pump, that experience 
changing loads due to their rotating motion or varying fluid conditions, fatigue is of great

Some alloys have higher pitting resistance equivalent numbers (PREN) due to their

are also reliant on keeping the ferrite and austenite phase mix and microstructure intact. 
As their microstructure is susceptible to change after welding due to phase precipitation 
at elevated temperatures, this should be done carefully [2]. This increases the interest in 
other processes, which do not introduce as much heat, avoiding precipitation. 
Metal forming is a process where a desired shape is achieved by plastically deforming 
the material. Process examples include bending, rolling, extrusion, upsetting, deep 
drawing, ironing etc. In metal forming the material is often subjected to high strain. When 
deforming, the material will be strain-hardened by new dislocations forming in the metal’s 
crystal structure. As dislocations’ strain interactions are repulsive, this equates to higher 
stresses needed for yielding to occur, at the cost of less ductility before failure [3]. Many 
of Grundfos’ components in Figure 2.1 are produced using metal forming methods. 
2.2 Self-pierce riveting (SPR) technology 
Self-pierce riveting (SPR) has made significant progress in the last 25 years, due to the 
need for joining different lightweight materials such as aluminium or a mix of materials 
such as steel and aluminium [4]. A major advantage of SPR rivets over traditional rivets 
is that no pre-drilled hole is required, allowing fewer operations during assembly. These 
characteristics have been of great interest in the automotive manufacturing industry. An 
application example is Audi’s second-generation space frame of the A2 model, where 
about 1800 self-piercing rivets were used, totally replacing spot welds [4]. Audi AG was 
granted a patent in 2001, detailing the structure of a semi-hollow aluminium blunt-shaped 
SPR to avoid “... different electrochemical potentials of steel on the one hand and 
aluminium on the other.” [5], which could cause increased galvanic corrosion if left 
unprotected from the environment.

The self-pierce riveting process can be divided into the four stages seen in Figure 2.2. 
During the clamping stage, the blank holder and die press the workpiece sheets together. 
In the piercing stage, the SPR is driven through the sheets by the punch, with force being 
transferred directly through the rivet. In the 3rd flaring stage, the cylindrical sides bend 
outwards to form an interlock between the lower sheet and the SPR. In the 4th stage, the 
punch retracts and the blank holder releases from the now joined sheets. 
A similar process to SPR is mechanical clinching, which similarly produces an undercut 
or interlocked area between two plates. This process does however not need a 3rd part 
to do so, as illustrated in Figure 2.3.  
 
While it may use fewer parts, mechanical clinching does not perform as well as spot-
welding in terms of static, shear and peel strength, but does however perform better in 
terms of fatigue attributed to more favourable stress distributions [6]. SPRs have 
performed better in fatigue, and static strength, and shown promising results in peel and 
shear testing compared to spot welds [6]. This, together with no need for hole alignment 
as with traditional rivets, has made SPRs a solid choice for load-bearing components 
joined in the highly automized automotive industry. 
In applications where aesthetic joint protrusions and avoiding the need for screw isolation 
related to galvanic corrosion, a new method of double-sided self-pierce riveting (DSSPR) 
has been suggested [7]. As shown in Figure 2.4, a cylinder chamfered on the inside is

2.3 Formed rivets 
A common method of joining sheets is by riveting. In its most simple form seen in Figure 
2.8, a domed pin is deformed on opposite sides of sheets with holes creating an overlap 
between itself and the sheets. The rivet assembly is held together vertically by this 
overlap and laterally by the pin-hole contact. A similar overlap is formed when using 
SPR, as an interlock is formed during the flaring stage. Applying such overlaps to 
perpendicular joints may not be as easy, but was shown for pipes in [8]. 
 
The formed rivet-like joints seen in Figure 2.9 are examples of methods used to join 
plates perpendicularly using metal forming. Discussing their applicability to be used in 
open structures, one may find it difficult to use method (c), as the use of rolling elements 
and their reach may be limited by the open structure’s shape, especially if the vertical 
plates are curved. Option (a), (b), and (d) are interesting compared to (c), as they do not 
require the same level of tooling on the inner side of the joint. Unique amongst them is 
(d), in that it has no protrusions in its final assembled form. Mitigating such protrusions 
may be key in pump impeller design, as protrusions could increase the level of 
turbulence, and drag or create cavitation in the flowing fluid. The first of two stages seen 
in Figure 2.10 forms the final hole geometry, by plastic deformation. This strain hardens 
the material, creating a higher strength geometry, compared to machining a similar 
geometry. The authors investigated the joint failure modes and strength. With the proper 
design of their tab, they reached approximately 240MPa of tensile force in the aluminium 
tab before breaking off at its base after necking. Having a joint be stronger than the base

3 Methods 
3.1 Sample production 
Sample parts were produced at DTU following drawings specified by the author. Blades 
were bent and measured by the author, to finally be assembled in a 155kN servo press 
at 1mm/s, writing force-displacement data for later analysis. 
3.2 Destructive load-testing 
Destructive load-testing was performed in the earlier mentioned servo press at 0.1mm/s. 
Test results will be used to compare factor effects within concepts, but also to compare 
across different concepts. 
3.3 Microscopy and SPR cross-sectioning 
A Zeiss stereo microscope, fitted with a DeltaPix camera on one monocle, was used to 
take close-up pictures throughout the project. The DeltaPix Insight software was 
calibrated using a Mitutoyo calibration grid and used to capture images. Microscopy was 
mostly used to take images of wire EDM cut samples of the SPR concept samples, a 
machining process chosen for its low force and therefore influence on the parts’ 
condition. 
3.4 Data analysis 
In addition to a more subjective analysis of directly plotted data and graphs, statistical 
analysis may be used where found relevant. Mainly the use of analysis of variance 
(ANOVA) and determination of P-factor to give a sense of how strong effects are 
compared to error, and thereby how well the null hypothesis can be rejected, i.e. how 
statistically significant factor effects are. Analysis was based on formulas in [10] and 
cross-checked using JMP Pro 16.

5 Destructive load testing 
5.1 Axial load test 
Instead of only relying on the cross-section analysis of SPR joints to describe the joint 
quality and to compare between concepts, destructive load testing was used to better 
determine the actual joint quality. For the SPR concept, this would also give an insight 
into what a good SPR joint looks like in this application (i.e. more/less interlock or depth).  
The axial load test fixture in seen Figure 5.1, was designed and used during the project. 
Using groves in the lower plate (white), the blue and orange “grippers” were located 
accurately and consistently relative to the samples and the orange was fixed to the load 
cell above (indicated by the black arrow). As the degrees of freedom (DOFs) were fixed 
separately for blue and orange parts, any angular misalignment was shimmed out in the 
interface between the top orange bar and the load cell. The grippers were designed to 
transfer force through a large area on the sample plate while minimizing bending. The 
fixture was calculated to withstand 8.5kN of load (limited by bending loads in grippers), 
based on analytical calculations and finite element analysis (FEA), using UHB 11 steel 
for the parts. Achieving high enough strength came at the cost of the fixture not being 
fully symmetric, and some bending loads being created between the two sides. Grippes 
are straight on one side to be able to fit blades with a bend radius higher than R25 and 
curved on the other to leave a small but consistent distance between the blade and 
gripper. Production drawings are found in Appendix 2.

5.2 Blade-radial load test 
The blade-radial load test fixture seen in Figure 5.2 is mounted in a pre-made die set 
(sub-frame) to limit any lateral and angular movement. Intermediate plates (grey) are 
interfaced with the frame (white) using dowel pins. The intermediate plates have 
counterbored holes wherein modified grade 12.9 ISO 7379 shoulder screws fit. The 
screws are modified to have a length tolerance in addition to their shaft diameter 
tolerance. This aligns the fixture components when tensioned and was done as internal 
space in the samples was limited. The fixture was evaluated analytically to have a 17.3kN 
load limit, limited by bending stresses in the ISO 7379 screws, and using UHB 11 steel 
for the remaining parts. This was found by superposition of linear beam bending cases, 
prescribing no bend slope in joints, and using moment diagrams to find worst-case 
bending moments [9]. It was also analysed using FEA showing no signs of critical 
stresses in the blue and orange contact parts or the ISO 7379 screws up to 15kN load. 
Mechanical drawings are found in Appendix 3.

6 Self-pierce riveting implementation 
The SPR principle is applied to the reference sample geometry by chamfering the blade 
edges. The conceptual implementation will in this report be referred to as “SPR”, minding 
that it differs quite a lot from commercial self-pierce-riveting. For experimental reasons, 
the blade heights were adjusted, so the experimental variants could be produced and 
still fit inside the available anti-buckling fixture during assembly. Example drawings of 
the components can be found in Appendix 4. 
6.1 Experiments and parameters for SPR 
Experiments were created using variants of select parameters in Figure 6.1.

easy-to-understand insight into factor (parameter) effects by adjusting factors to different 
levels. Axial load test samples of SPR were made in 3 replicates; 1 to be used for cross-
section analysis and 2 for axial load testing to give a brief insight into factor variance.

analysed by viewing plotted data and comparing the effects. All plotted error bars will be 
based on population standard deviation (SD) rather than sample SD. This was done, as 
with only two replications in most tests, the error bars will represent the exact sample 
values and average deviation in case of more than two samples.

6.2 SPR blade manufacturing and experimental impact 
To limit blade-to-blade variations within samples, all blades' height was measured edge 
to edge using callipers. They were then numbered and sorted by height. Blade sets with 
similar heights were put into samples. The two parameters that are influenced by this are 
final assembly displacement (FAD) and free column span (FCS). To eliminate the effect 
on FAD, samples will not be assembled using their nominal specification, but 
compensated using the mean of the blade heights going into the sample. As an example: 
4 blades going into a sample, with nominal heights of 15.5 mm, measure 15.48 mm, then 
the press final displacement is set to move 0.02 mm more to compensate. 
The effect of FCS will be considered a nuisance in analysis. The worst-case tolerance 
will change the worst-case FCS by 7%, but typically lower. This can be seen in Figure 
6.2 where most samples are distributed closer to the nominal value. By comparison, the 
effect of FCS was tested in Chapter 6.4.1 to have little effect using a variation of +-22%.

6.4.1 Free column span 
As illustrated in Figure 6.4 the free column span is the entire length of the unsupported 
blade on one side. The column may experience imperfect column buckling due to the 
chamfered edge, which induces a bending moment into the blade from the offset force. 
Increasing the length of the column will lower its stiffness. The effect is investigated for 
impact on axial strength of the assembly and cross-section analysis.  
 
Due to an experimental error when conducting the experiment, one replication of the 
lower free column span (FCS) variant was invalid and therefore removed. As such, no 
error lines are present for it in Figure 6.5. With the central test in Figure 6.5 having the 
reference value, the results show slightly increasing load performance when increasing 
FCS.  
Comparing the load performance in Figure 6.5 with the cross-section parameters in 
Figure 6.6, there seems to be no good connection between both depth and interlock and 
the destructive load performance. The tall FCS test falls between other tests for both 
depth hi and interlock i, i.e. a tendency could be observed if either one or both had been 
larger or smaller. An explanation could be attributed to only taking measurements of one 
blade in the sectioned sample. The blade-to-blade variance within a sample may hide 
the true relationship, as the true average in the entire sample is unknown. This is, 
however, not the case with load testing, as all blades are loaded simultaneously. ANOVA 
results show an effect significance P-value of 0.79, which is very high (79% risk of 
accepting the null hypothesis). The effect observed is therefore not statistically significant 
as there is too much random error compared to the factor effect.

6.4.3 Blade bend radius 
The blade bend radius was tested at 3 levels, with the lowest at the reference samples’ 
of R25 mm as seen in Figure 6.14. Plotted in Figure 6.15, the straight or infinite bend 
radius blade, is represented by lines to give a sense of the relation between the levels.  
 
Increasing the blade bend radius has a large negative effect on the strength of the joints. 
Blades with R50 mm and straight blades have 82% and 48% strength compared to the 
R25 mm reference, respectively. Increasing the bend radius results in a loss of strength, 
and with a P-value of 0.0177, the factor is statistically significant.  
The cross-section parameters in Figure 6.16 do not suggest any correlation between the 
observed effect and the interlocking. The smaller bend radius increases the bending 
stiffness of the blades, forming a stiffer structure compared to a straight beam. Another 
factor is that the smaller bend radius blade locks the in-plane degrees of freedom better, 
limiting any rotation between the upper and lower sample plates, which may lead to 
failure.

6.4.5 Blade-radial load test 
By changing the chamfer location on the blade, the two variants of blade-radial loading 
directions, as seen in Figure 6.26, can be tested in the fixture of Figure 5.2.  
The test results in Figure 6.27 show a very high dependency on loading direction, with 
test #20 having a mean strength of 11.50kN, 12.9 times as high as #19 with 0.89kN. One 
of the aluminium plates used in the highest loaded sample of test #20, was torn open at 
the middle of the blade seen in Figure 6.28, indicating that the strength is nearing the 
plate material limit. 
When viewing Figure 6.26, the performance difference makes sense, as test #20 forces 
the tip into a deeper interlock, acting like a barb on a fishhook being pulled backwards. 
The opposite relationship exists for #19, which not only pushes the tip away from the 
interlock, but because the backside of the blade is angled, a force component will also 
push blades axially out of the interlock. Close to the point of peak force of test #20, the 
interlock failed, leaving the edges dragging on the inside of the sample, which in Figure 
6.27 can be seen as the gradually lowering force, as blades were dragged to the outside 
of the structure (decreasing the friction by decreased normal force on the blades as more 
moment arm was created between the two blades).

6.5 Open structure SPR use, improvement, and future work 
It was possible to significantly increase the strength of the SPR joined structures by 
improving the blade morphology, assembly parameters, and by creating an alternative 
back-to-back layout of the blades. Combining these factors could create joints with 
satisfactory strength depending on the application. Due to their high load-direction 
strength dependency, they may have limited applicability. Back-to-back blade layouts 
may also solve this issue, as it will grip the interlock in both loading directions. 
Suggestions for future work could be investigating ways of implementing the back-to-
back blade design better, possibly by using two half-thickness blades against each other 
instead of a single blade, or as illustrated in Figure 6.31, ways of making multiple chamfer 
directions on a single blade. Like current laser welded assemblies, the SPR joints must 
be located using a fixture but do not require strict placement of the blades relative to the 
plates, as the interface is formed during assembly. This makes SPR joints attractive for 
upscaling using automated assembly, which can lack the finesse and robustness 
provided by humans during assembly. 
Improvements must be made if using steel plates, as these failed to be assembled. Ideas 
could be to strain harden the chamfered edges or soften the plates using heat or 
ultrasonic vibration. If the technology is not able to produce a satisfactory joint, it could 
be used to join parts temporarily before welding, thereby working as “green” parts in the 
production process.

7 Hole-tab riveting 
As this joint type in the form seen in [12] was found less described in general literature, 
a design first had to be established. The following subchapters will present the most 
important decisions made during the design process, the final implementation, and the 
test results. 
7.1 Geometry design 
7.1.1 Buckling of blades 
Besides achieving high strength, the three design goals for the hole-tab rivet concept 
were: 
1. Design a hole-tab geometry that requires less force to join, than the critical 
buckling force of the blades below the joints. 
2. The top of the rivet head should not protrude from the structure. 
3. Forming the rivet should not introduce stresses high enough to risk cracking. 
It was also decided to design hole geometries which could be machined easily, instead 
of forming them, as the project timeframe limited the design and production of forming 
tools required to make formed geometries. 
Some Grundfos impellers are projection welded, with 2-3 projections along a blade. This 
sparked the idea that it would be possible to achieve reasonable strength with only some 
formed tabs and not necessarily a welded or formed joint along the full length of the 
blades. Addressing the first design goal, the critical buckling load of the blades was 
calculated using Euler buckling case 1 (worst case) in Equation (7.1) to be 11.4kN. This 
load assumes thin, long, non-curved blades which the blades are not, and the actual 
elastic buckling strength is therefore likely higher.

7.3.6 Plate material 
The PACF plate variant’s upsetting displacement varied between samples, most likely 
caused by deviations in the printed plate thickness and general consistency issues. This, 
along with the aluminium plate variant, had less assembly stiffness. This was most likely 
caused by the hole deforming, thereby providing less support for the rivet. As such, it 
was expected that these samples did not perform as well as the Reference. 
The aluminium plate variant reached 2.7kN, which was 6% higher than the best SPR 
variant at 2.54kN, but only 43% compared to the Reference. While it was nice to compare 
using the same material, the true strength of the hole-tab rivet concept was its ability to 
work with higher-strength plates. The difference in doing so showed how the strength of 
the hole matters. Increasing the strength of the hole is therefore of great importance and 
can be done by strain hardening it using metal forming. The PACF plate performed very 
poorly at 129N. The material was not strong enough for the proposed geometry and 
would need a completely different design to increase strength. 
Effect conclusion 
The strength of the hole geometry is important, as the aluminium variant was both less 
stiff during assembly and lost 57% strength compared to the reference using EN 1.4404 
plates.

7.4 Open structure hole-tab rivet use, improvements and future 
work 
The interfacing holes and tabs were both one of the concept's strengths, but also a 
weakness. A strength of the interface was that the holes and tabs aligned blades and 
plates to each other which could make an assembly fixture redundant. This could 
potentially mean less lead-time when prototyping new geometries to e.g. improve 
hydrodynamics or simplify assembly in production. The weakness of this may be found 
when upscaling production, as automated insertion of the tabs into the holes may require 
too high precision and accuracy. This task is well suited for assembly by hand, making 
lower volume production the immediate candidate for this concept, at least in most 
developed countries where repetitive task labour is more strictly regulated, and more 
costly than elsewhere. This is however not explained to rule out possible concept 
changes which may improve automizing it, but as is, it may be more difficult than the 
“place anywhere” nature of SPR joined or laser welded concepts (which require fixturing). 
It is clear from the findings that improvements can be made to the proposed design. Two 
areas should be in focus: 
1. Hole strength 
2. Hole-fill ratio 
7.4.1 Hole strength 
As both the hole and tab make up the joint, the hole should also be focused on. As 
described earlier, the holes in this project were milled, making the “low-hanging-fruit” 
opportunity to increase the hole’s strength, forming it as authors in [12] did. This will 
strain-harden the hole and increase its strength. This will undoubtedly increase the 
strength of the joints, which is needed, as there subjectively seems to be more damage 
to the hole than to the tab when compared in Figure 7.19. Improving the strength could 
also improve the blade-radial strength, by preventing the tabs from pulling out as easily, 
possibly making the tab material the limiting factor despite how much bending is present 
in the loading scenario. This can be rephrased into a goal of making the material strength 
the limiting factor when axially loaded as well, resulting in a tab fracture at higher loads. 
If an axial, fully tensile stress in the tabs is assumed along with a fracture stress of 
850MPa, the highest axial destructive load expected would be at least 13.6kN.

7.4.2 Hole-fill ratio 
From assembly and testing, it was clear that the geometry functioned sub-optimally, as 
the hole was far from filled with material e.g. in Figure 7.16. This was especially true 
when not supporting the blades, leading to more plastic deformation below the base of 
the tab as seen in Figure 7.15. Some ideas have briefly been created on how this may 
be improved and are seen in Figure 7.20. The primary goal of improving the hole-fill ratio 
is increasing the overlapping area between the tab rivet head and plate hole, with a 
secondary goal of doing so without a supporting fixture. It would be a great option to test 
many geometries using FEA, optimizing for the overlapping area. It is possible that the 
flatter hole geometry from [12] helps hole-fill, as the tab cannot slide down against the 
sidewalls of the hole.

8 Future work 
Besides the concept improvement suggestions made in chapters 6.5 and 7.4, both 
concepts should, especially in a Grundfos application context, undergo further 
comparative testing between them and currently used solutions such as laser welding 
and projection welding. 
Both joining concepts' corrosion resistance properties should be tested, with a special 
focus on: 
• 
Crevice corrosion, as both concepts create small gaps and crevices when 
assembled. 
• 
Stress corrosion cracking, as stresses from forming may contribute to this, but 
the impact compared to welding should be investigated. 
• 
Corrosion fatigue, as the varying loads on the impeller could reduce the fatigue 
life properties. 
Testing the SPR variants' corrosive properties may only be relevant if it can be 
assembled using steel plates, as galvanic corrosion will be high between aluminium and 
steel. The hole-tab rivets may also be investigated for austenite to martensite conversion 
due to high strain, with a transformed martensitic structure providing lower corrosion 
protection. 
The joining methods' fatigue life should be investigated. Comparative testing of the 
concepts using standard [14] could be suggested, as it is tailored towards welded 
components which Grundfos currently use. 
Destructive load limits were used in this project to compare concepts, but with ultimate 
strength now proven, each concept's yielding limits should be found, as one should not 
design viable structures based on ultimate strength alone. 
It would be interesting to see how well the concepts work when joining impeller 
geometries identical to Grundfos products as seen in Figure 2.1 A. This may be 
especially relevant for hole-tab rivets, as they currently work in full EN 1.4404 steel as 
Grundfos use, but also because the angles between plates and blades present a new 
uncertainty to the joining concept. While tabs and holes could still align parts, the load-
carrying capability of the straight “columns” which blades form may be impaired. If 
Grundfos impellers can be successfully assembled, their hydrodynamic performance 
should be tested and compared to current solutions, as protrusions or other “artefacts” 
may increase losses.

Conclusion 
SPR technology and hole-tab riveting were both successfully used to join open structure 
samples.  
The reference SPR concept managed to sustain a mean peak axial destructive load of 
0.87kN, improved to 2.18kN by varying the blade geometry and final assembly 
displacement. It was improved even further, to the highest tested variant of 2.54kN, by 
placing blades with opposing chamfers in a back-to-back configuration, with themselves 
providing resistance to escaping their interlocks. Free column span was found to have a 
low and statistically insignificant influence on axial strength. Blade bend radius was 
impactful, nearly halving the axial strength using straight blades compared to blades bent 
at R25mm. Increasing final assembly displacement increased strength significantly for 
both 35° and 45° chamfered blades, but also increased the physical distortion observed. 
An optimal chamfer angle exists near 45°, as it is sharp enough to pierce, compared to 
55°, and has more strength than lower angles such as 35°. The reference SPR concept’s 
blade-radial strength was found to be highly directional in a destructive load test, 
resulting in a strength of 11.5kN in the strong direction, but only 0.89kN in the weak 
direction. Concept improvement suggestions propose geometries that mimic the back-
to-back blade layout, which could be advantageous in both load cases. The SPR concept 
failed to produce joints in steel plates in its tested form. 
The hole-tab concept performed best overall in the axial destructive load test, reaching 
6.26kN using blade supports and 3.72kN without. No buckling of the blades was 
observed. The higher strengths were in part achieved due to the concept's ability to work 
with stainless steel outer plates, as an aluminium variant reached a lower strength of 
2.7kN. While increasing the tab height did result in a higher strength of 8.44kN, the tabs 
were slightly plastically buckled, resulting in a visually less pleasing joint, which could 
reduce the hydrodynamic performance of an impeller due to small protrusions. While the 
hole-tab concept’s blade-radial destructive load test did reach lower values than the best 
SPR variant, it still managed 6.31kN for the variant with supported blades and 5.69kN 
with unsupported blades. The strength may well be assumed to be independent of the 
tested loading directions, and close to the material limit of the tabs. Like with the SPR 
concept, improvement suggestions to the hole-tab concept were made, such as forming 
the hole to increase strength (as opposed to milling it), altering the tab and hole geometry 
to increase the hole-fill ratio and increase the area of overlap between the rivet head and 
plate. 
Of the two concepts, SPR was assessed to be the most attractive concept for automated 
high-volume production but was not able to form a joint using steel plates in its tested 
form. While alignment of holes and tabs may be difficult to automate, they do potentially 
remove the need for fixturing, making the hole-tab concept ideal for low-volume 
production in its current form. 
Before commercial application of the joining concepts, more testing should be 
undertaken. In the case of Grundfos’ applications, they should test with special attention 
to the corrosive and fatigue life properties compared to their currently used joining 
technologies and test implementation on more advanced commercial product 
geometries.
