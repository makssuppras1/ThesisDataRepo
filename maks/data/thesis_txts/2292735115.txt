5	  
Abstract	  
This	   thesis	   considers	   the	   impact	   of	   inventory	   management	   decisions	   of	   retailers	   on	   the	  
sustainability	   of	   last	   mile	   delivery	   operations.	   Retailers	   in	   urban	   areas	   are	   often	   facing	   a	  
dilemma	  when	  it	  comes	  to	  replenishing	  stocks	  because	  they	  need	  to	  find	  a	  balance	  between	  the	  
costs	  of	  carrying	  inventory	  and	  the	  cost	  of	  ordering	  products.	  Urbanization	  and	  the	  increase	  of	  
traffic	  in	  urban	  areas	  have	  added	  a	  new	  component	  to	  inventory	  management	  decisions	  with	  
several	   challenges	   occurring	   on	   the	   last	   mile	   products	   travel	   to	   the	   retailer:	   increase	   in	  
distribution	   costs,	   frequent	   delays,	   less	   and	   less	   inventory	   space	   and	   other	   obstacles	   in	   the	  
delivery	   process.	   How	   retailers	   choose	   to	   control	   and	   replenish	   their	   inventory	   will	   directly	  
affect	  the	  number	  of	  freight	  vehicles	  present	  in	  the	  area,	  impacting	  their	  profit,	  the	  environment	  
but	  also	  the	  social	  welfare	  of	  the	  community.	  This	  thesis	  evaluates	  this	  problem	  and	  the	  possible	  
solutions	  to	  make	  operations	  more	  sustainable	  in	  the	  case	  of	  Santiago	  de	  Chile’s	  congested	  micro	  
centre.	   The	   business	   landscape	   and	   its	   inventory	   practices	   of	   the	   micro	   centre	   have	   been	  
determined	  thanks	  to	  interviews	  and	  an	  establishment	  based	  survey	  of	  businesses	  in	  the	  area.	  
The	  first	  finding	  that	  emerged	  from	  the	  data	  collection	  is	  that	  the	  notion	  of	  sustainability	  or	  the	  
responsibility	  of	  retailers’	  decisions	  are	  far	  from	  being	  understood.	  Even	  in	  large	  chain	  stores,	  
very	  few	  attempts	  to	  reduce	  freight	  traffic	  are	  made	  and	  sustainability	  is	  not	  yet	  integrated	  to	  
the	  firms’	  strategic	  plans.	  The	  first	  step	  towards	  sustainability	  could	  be	  the	  implementation	  of	  
inventory	  control	  software	  in	  small	  and	  medium	  sized	  stores	  to	  better	  capture	  the	  demand	  and	  
track	   what	   is	   in	   stocks.	  Furthermore,	  five	  alternative	  inventory	  and	  transport	  strategies	   have	  
been	  presented	  to	  the	  retailers	  with	  the	  goal	  to	  reduce	  the	  number	  of	  freight	  vehicles	  in	  the	  zone	  
or	   lessen	   their	   impact.	   Those	   are:	   increasing	   the	   order	   size,	   using	   “greener”	   trucks,	   sharing	  
deliveries	  with	  neighbours,	  storing	  products	  in	  a	  municipal	  centre	  and	  finally	  off-­‐hour	  deliveries.	  
Even	   though,	   only	   29%	   of	   retailers	   surveyed	   would	   willingly	   commit	   to	   change	   their	  
replenishment	  process,	  some	  of	  these	  alternatives	  are	  suitable	  and	  plausible	  depending	  on	  the	  
type	  of	  retail	  activity.	  Given	  the	  low	  acceptance	  level	  for	  change,	  the	  analysis	  focussed	  more	  on	  
the	  inventory	  and	  distribution	  characteristics	  of	  each	  retail	  category	  to	  determine	  the	  relevance	  
of	  each	  alternative.	  Changing	  the	  status	  quo	  often	  implies	  an	  increase	  in	  distribution	  costs	  and	  
thus	  retailers	  are	  reluctant	  to	  commit	  to	  these	  changes.	  This	  observation	  stresses	  out	  the	  need	  of	  
assessing	   how	   public	   intervention	   could	   help	   retailers	   to	   make	   their	   operations	   more	  
sustainable.	   Finally,	   this	   also	   implies	   that	   every	   stakeholder	   needs	   to	   be	   involved	   in	   the	  
implementation	   of	   these	   solutions:	   retailers,	   suppliers,	   carriers,	   customers	   and	   the	   public	  
authorities.

6	  
2 Introduction	  
Supply	   chain	   management	   has	   emerged	   to	   be	   a	   relatively	   recent	   discipline,	   extending	   the	  
boundaries	  of	  what	  was	  defined	  by	  logistics	  management	  (Christopher,	  2011).	  Concerned	  with	  
the	   linkage	   between	   every	   nodes	   of	   the	   value	   chain	   (designer,	   retailer,	   supplier,	   logistics	  
provider,	  customer	  etc.)	  it	  has	  the	  goal	  to	  maximize	  these	  interactions	  to	  allow	  businesses	  to	  
gain	   competitive	   advantage	   (Mehijerdi,	   2009).	   The	   shift	   between	   a	   “mass-­‐market”	   towards	   a	  
“market-­‐of-­‐one”	  stresses	  the	  complexity	  that	  supply	  chains	  have	  gained	  over	  the	  last	  decades	  
(Christopher,	  2011).	  Indeed,	  in	  a	  now	  demand-­‐driven	  market,	  customers	  require	  more	  and	  more	  
tailored	   solutions,	   customized	   products	   giving	   a	   certain	   volatility	   and	   unpredictability	   to	   the	  
market	   (Li,	   2009).	   That	   obviously	   impacts	   the	   management	   of	   supply	   chains	   and	   all	   the	  
stakeholders	   involved	   in	   it.	   Large	   batch	   manufacturing	   of	   standards	   products	   to	   achieve	  
economies	   of	   scale	   was	   a	   winning	   formula	   but	   no	   longer	   fits	   the	   requirement	   of	   modular	  
products	  and	  fast	  changing	  trends	  (Mikkola	  &	  Skjøtt-­‐Larsen,	  2004).	  	  
The	   scope	   of	   supply	   chain	   is	   wide,	   its	   success	   depends	   on	   many	   variables	   acting	   at	   various	  
levels:	   demand	   forecast,	   inventory	   control,	   transport,	   quality	   management,	   design	   and	   many	  
more	   (Coyle	   et	   al.,	   2003).	   However,	   it	   has	   been	   a	   rising	   concern	   since	   the	   1990s	   that	   supply	  
chain	  must	  head	  towards	  sustainability	  (Linton	  et	  al.,	  2007).	  By	  sustainability,	  it	  is	  meant	  that	  
supply	   chains	   should	   fulfil	   their	   purpose	   without	   compromising	   their	   activity	   and	   the	  
community	  in	  the	  future	  (Kleindorfer	  et	  al.,	  2005).	  
Globalization	  has	  extended	  the	  network	  of	  supply	  chains,	  manufacturing	  and	  point	  of	  sales	  can	  
sometimes	   be	   separated	   by	   thousands	   of	   miles.	   All	   of	   that	   has	   a	   tremendous	   cost	   for	   the	  
environment	  and	  controversial	  social	  implications	  (Gonzalez-­‐Feliu	  et	  al.,2014).	  The	  environment	  
is	  not	  the	  only	  component	  of	  sustainability,	  the	  larger	  objective	  that	  should	  be	  considered	  is	  the	  
triple	  bottom	  line:	  people,	  profit	  and	  planet	  (Kleindorfer	  et	  al.,	  2005).	  Many	  links	  of	  the	  supply	  
chain	   can	   leverage	   an	   improvement	   of	   this	   triple	   bottom	   line,	   the	   intertwinement	   between	  
inventory	  and	  distribution	  is	  going	  to	  be	  explored	  as	  they	  both	  play	  a	  vital	  role	  in	  supply	  chain	  
management	   (Tyworth,	   Zeng,	   1997).	   Freight	   transport	   consuming	   now	   about	   35%	   of	   all	  
transport	  energy	  (Ribeiro	  et	  al.,	  2007)	  used	  worldwide.	  
Inventory	  has	  been	  and	  still	  is	  a	  burning	  question	  of	  supply	  chain	  management	  in	  terms	  of	  what	  
and	  where	  to	  store	  and	  when	  to	  replenish.	  All	  of	  these	  questions	  do	  not	  find	  a	  universal	  answer	  
as	  it	  depends	  on	  several	  parameters	  such	  as	  the	  type	  of	  product,	  type	  of	  customers,	  geographical	  
area,	  the	  demand	  pattern	  and	  more	  (Muller,	  2011).

There	  has	  been	  a	  growing	  trend	  of	  demand	  for	  low	  inventories	  (Abernathy	  et	  al.,	  2000),	  just	  in	  
time	  systems	  combined	  with	  denser	  and	  denser	  areas,	  less	  and	  less	  parking	  spaces	  (Gonzalez-­‐
Feliu	   et	   al.,	   2014).	   An	   ever	   more	   volatile	   market	   makes	   it	   even	   more	   difficult	   to	   forecast	   for	  
inventory	   and	   thus	   for	   transport	   (Petri	   et	   al.,	   2014).	   Often	   seen	   as	   a	   potential	   loss	   of	   profit	  
because	  of	  its	  nature	  of	  non-­‐working	  capital,	  it	  is	  though	  necessary	  in	  order	  to	  avoid	  shortage	  
and	  cope	  with	  surges	  in	  demand	  (Muller,	  2011).	  
	  	  
Similarly	  to	  inventory,	  transport	  is	  a	  key	  element	  leading	  to	  success	  in	  supply	  chain	  (Mason	  et	  
al.,	  2006)	  and	  is	  in	  a	  constant	  trade-­‐off	  with	  the	  latter.	  It	  is	  rather	  straightforward	  to	  see	  than	  
since	  goods	  are	  usually	  not	  produced	  next	  to	  their	  location	  of	  sale,	  they	  need	  to	  be	  moved.	  From	  
manufacturer	  to	  distributors,	  from	  distributors	  to	  retailers	  and	  sometimes	  with	  intermediaries	  
steps.	  Eventually	  goods	  will	  arrive	  to	  the	  retailer	  after	  having	  travelled	  the	  “last-­‐mile”	  in	  urban	  
areas	  (Gevaers	  et	  al.,	  2011).	  Inventory	  and	  transport	  cannot	  be	  dissociated	  since	  one	  influences	  
the	  other	  and	  vice-­‐versa,	  this	  association	  has	  different	  consequences	  over	  operations	  in	  urban	  
zones	   and	   the	   community.	   Thus,	   this	   thesis	   intends	   to	   evaluate	   the	   impact	   that	   different	  
inventory	   management	   strategies	   of	   retailers	   have	   on	   urban	   freight	   and	   sustainability	   in	   the	  
light	  of	  a	  case	  study	  of	  the	  city	  of	  Santiago	  de	  Chile.	  This	  issue	  seems	  to	  be	  ever	  more	  urging	  as	  
logistics	   spaces,	   warehouses	   and	   stocking	   areas	   are	   progressively	   disappearing	   or	   moved	  
further	  away	  from	  city	  centres	  (Dablanc,	  2007).	  Urban	  areas	  are	  a	  special	  issue	  because	  they	  are	  
the	  location	  of	  a	  great	  density	  at	  various	  levels:	  economical,	  residential,	  pollution	  and	  nuisance.	  
The	  negative	  externalities	  have	  much	  more	  impact	  because	  they	  tend	  to	  pile	  up	  in	  a	  small	  and	  
narrow	  space.	  Decision-­‐makers	  are	  multiple	  and	  all	  have	  their	  own	  welfare	  function	  they	  want

with	  the	  case	  of	  “mega	  cities”	  in	  emerging	  countries	  such	  as	  Chile	  because	  challenges	  are	  various

subdivided	  into	  two	  sides.

The	  following	  section	  intends	  to	  provide	  the	  reader	  with	  a	  background	  on	  the	  topic	  of	  inventory

Inventory	   is	   a	   critical	   element	   of	   logistics	   often	   regarded	   as	   a	   curse	   for	   companies’	   financial	  
balance	  (Bonney,	  1994).	  Indeed	  one	  can	  argue	  it	  is	  non-­‐working	  capital	  and	  a	  risky	  investment	  
since	   stored	   products	   can	   be	   under	   the	   threat	   of	   obsolescence	   or	   simply	   be	   irrelevant	   to	  
customer’s	   demand	   (Bonney,	   1994).	   Investors	   are	   also	   monitoring	   closely	   the	   ability	   of	  
companies	  to	  sell	  their	  inventory	  fast,	  thus	  it	  is	  an	  important	  indicator	  in	  the	  stock	  exchange.	  
However	  it	  is	  necessary	  because	  replenishment	  is	  not	  instantaneous	  and	  certainly	  not	  without	  
costs.	  An	  order	  for	  more	  products	  has	  to	  be	  made,	  be	  processed	  by	  the	  supplier,	  prepared	  for	  
shipping,	   shipped,	   received	   and	   put	   in	   the	   shelves.	   Every	   step	   has	   a	   certain	   lead-­‐time	  
(sometimes	  months)	  and	  thus	  it	  is	  critical	  for	  businesses	  to	  carefully	  control	  their	  inventory	  and	  
anticipate	  stock-­‐outs	  (Chopra	  &	  Sodhi,	  2012).

retailer	  and	  must	  be	  prevented	  as	  best	  as	  it	  can	  be.

retailers	  to	  match	  supply	  and	  demand,	  therefore	  risk	  related	  to	  carrying	  inventory	  have	  become	  
a	  great	  deal	  of	  concern	  (Chopra	  &	  Sodhi,	  2012).

3.1 Inventory	  management	  
Inventory	  management	  emerged	  as	  a	  singular	  discipline	  within	  logistics.	  Retailers	  anticipate	  the	  
demand	  and	  order	  products	  to	  meet	  it	  before	  it	  actually	  happens,	  to	  achieve	  economies	  of	  scale	  
and	  cope	  with	  uncertainty	  in	  the	  supply	  chain	  (Gaur	  et	  al.,	  2014).	  Managing	  inventory	  can	  be	  
seen	  as	  managing	  a	  trade-­‐off	  between	  the	  inventory	  holding	  costs	  (h)	  and	  the	  ordering	  costs	  (K),	  
among	  other	  considerations	  (Ansari	  &	  Heckel,	  1987).	  
Carrying	  costs	  are	  typically:	  	  
• 
The	  cost	  of	  insurance	  
• 
The	  cost	  of	  stagnant	  products	  that	  do	  not	  give	  returns	  for	  the	  time	  they	  are	  stored	  
• 
Space	  utilization	  costs	  
	  
In	   September	   2012	   inventory	   of	   US	   retailers	   was	   accounting	   for	   503	   billion	   dollars	   and	  
represented	  21%	  of	  firms’	  assets	  in	  average	  (Gaur	  et	  al.,	  2014).	  
Replenishment	  costs	  represent:	  
• 
Cost	  of	  sending	  the	  order	  to	  the	  supplier	  
• 
Cost	  of	  transporting	  the	  goods	  
• 
Cost	  of	  the	  purchase	  staff	  salary	  
	  
A	   strategy	   of	   frequent	   re-­‐ordering	   will	   result	   in	   a	   low	   h	   factor	   since	   stocks	   will	   be	   kept	  
permanently	  at	  a	  low	  level	  but	  also	  a	  high	  replenishment	  cost	  K.	  The	  other	  way	  round	  is	  to	  build	  
high	  levels	  of	  inventory	  with	  more	  time	  between	  two	  replenishments	  meaning	  a	  high	  h	  and	  low	  
K.	  It	  is	  clear	  that	  neither	  of	  these	  situations	  is	  optimal	  cost-­‐wise	  or	  “freight-­‐wise”.	  Too	  frequent	  
re-­‐ordering	  is	  obviously	  generating	  an	  important	  number	  of	  deliveries.	  A	  trade	  off	  in	  inventory	  
strategy	  must	  thus	  be	  found	  in	  order	  to	  limit	  expenses	  and	  freight	  generation,	  even	  though	  the	  
actual	  motivation	  for	  supply	  chains	  might	  be	  more	  related	  to	  financial	  indicators	  (Wu	  &	  Pagell,	  
2011).	  Ways	  of	  determining	  optimal	  quantities	  of	  replenishment	  exist;	  the	  most	  famous	  might	  
be	   the	   Economic	   Order	   Quantity	   (EOQ)	   brought	   in	   1913	   by	   Ford	   W.	   Harris	   and	   extended	   by	  
Wilson.

3.1.1 Just-­‐in-­‐Time	  systems	  
JIT	   (Just	   In	   Time)	   systems,	   popularized	   in	   the	   1970s	   by	   Japan,	   have	   also	   been	   a	   response	   in	  
urban	  areas	  where	  space	  is	  scarce	  (Bhusiri	  et	  al.,	  2014).	  The	  basic	  concept	  is	  to	  have	  the	  product	  
or	  an	  element	  in	  a	  production	  process	  ready	  for	  the	  next	  operation	  exactly	  when	  it	  is	  needed.	  By	  
doing	  so	  there	  is	  a	  minimum	  inventory	  and	  the	  operations	  are	  conducted	  in	  a	  lean	  way.	  Initially	  
applied	   to	   manufacturing	   environments	   to	   minimize	   inventory,	   it	   has	   however	   disadvantages	  
when	  applied	  to	  a	  retail	  setting	  (Aghazadeh,	  2001).	  In	  a	  manufacturing	  there	  is	  a	  flow	  of	  items	  
whereas	   in	   retail	   the	   product	   arrives	   and	   waits	   to	   be	   sold.	   Retailers	   cannot	   necessarily	   carry	  
weeks	   of	   inventory	   in	   shops	   but	   have	   products	   ready	   to	   meet	   current	   demand.	   Although	   it	  
increases	   the	   level	   of	   service,	   it	   also	   requires	   more	   frequent	   deliveries	   of	   products	   and	   thus	  
generates	   additional	   costs	   (Das	   &	   Handfield,	   1997).	   In	   that	   sense	   lean	   system	   of	   inventories	  
aren’t	  exactly	  environmentally	  friendly.	  Furthermore,	  JIT	  inventory	  is	  a	  risky	  strategy	  since	  the	  
inventory	   is	   always	   at	   the	   minimum	   to	   satisfy	   demand:	   a	   delay	   in	   delivery	   (a	   particularly	  
relevant	  issue	  in	  the	  context	  of	  a	  congested	  urban	  area)	  or	  a	  surge	  in	  demand	  could	  end	  up	  in	  
shortages.	  A	  great	  visibility	  on	  the	  demand	  and	  a	  great	  trust	  in	  suppliers	  are	  needed	  to	  achieve	  
JIT	  inventory,	  in	  most	  cases	  this	  a	  difficult	  task	  (Das	  &	  Handfield,	  1997).	  
Stores	   might	   prefer	   to	   buy	   in	   large	   batch	   of	   products	   to	   beneficiate	   of	   discounts	   (quantity	  
discount	  model).	  JIT	  inventory	  is	  a	  risk-­‐averse	  strategy	  to	  inventory	  risks	  (cost,	  deterioration	  
and	   space	   utilization)	   while	   it	   embraces	   the	   risk	   of	   not	   meeting	   the	   demand	   (Wong	   and	  
Johansen,	  2006).	  	  
==PAGE:12==
	  
	  
	  
	  
12	  
3.1.2 Vendor	  Managed	  Inventory	  
Instead	  of	  managing	  inventories	  directly	  at	  the	  retail	  level,	  this	  task	  can	  be	  delegated	  on	  step	  
further	  up	  in	  the	  supply	  chain:	  at	  the	  supplier	  level.	  This	  type	  of	  type	  of	  strategy	  is	  referred	  as	  
Vendor-­‐Managed	  Inventory	  or	  VMI	  first	  used	  by	  Procter&Gamble	  and	  Campbell	  Soup	  (Waller	  et	  
al.,	  1999).	  It	  is	  indeed	  interesting	  in	  the	  sense	  that	  usually	  actors	  of	  a	  supply	  chain	  take	  their	  
decisions	   without	   necessarily	   taking	   into	   account	   up-­‐and-­‐downstream	   repercussions	   of	   their	  
choices	   (Lee	   et	   al.,	   1997).	   Via	   an	   agreement,	   the	   supplier	   takes	   over	   the	   role	   of	   managing	  
replenishment	  with	  the	  intention	  to	  keep	  the	  inventory	  level	  within	  the	  boundaries	  set	  by	  the	  
agreement	   with	   the	   retailer.	   It	   necessitates	   a	   great	   degree	   of	   collaboration	   between	   both	  
players;	  the	  retailer	  needs	  to	  share	  sales	  data	  and	  forecasts	  to	  the	  supplier	  in	  order	  for	  the	  latter	  
to	  manage	  correctly	  the	  inventory	  needs.	  Drawbacks	  related	  to	  that	  type	  of	  policies	  lie	  in	  the	  fact	  
that	  it	  benefits	  more	  to	  the	  retailer	  than	  the	  supplier	  (Mishra	  &	  Raghunathan,	  2004).	  In	  fact	  the	  
supplier	  carries	  the	  responsibility	  to	  replenish	  and	  could	  suffer	  from	  the	  costs	  of	  shortage	  or	  
excess	  of	  inventory,	  making	  this	  type	  of	  deal	  difficult	  to	  do.	  The	  benefits	  for	  retailers	  are	  the	  
transfer	  of	  inventory	  costs	  to	  the	  supplier	  and	  additionally	  the	  transfer	  of	  shortage’s	  costs	  risks	  
to	  the	  supplier,	  achieving	  at	  the	  same	  time	  higher	  profits	  (Mishra	  &	  Raghunathan,	  2004).	  
3.1.3 Enterprise	  Resource	  Planning	  
More	  commonly	  found	  in	  stores	  part	  of	  a	  chain	  with	  a	  central	  office	  rather	  than	  in	  independently	  
owned	  premises,	  Enterprise	  Resource	  Planning	  systems	  are	  also	  used	  to	  monitor	  inventory	  and	  
streamline	   business	   processes	   (Cannonne	   &	   Damret,	   2002).	   The	   reason	   it	   is	   found	   mostly	   in	  
chain	  stores	  is	  first	  the	  great	  investment	  that	  it	  represents	  (implementation	  and	  coordination	  
with	  already	  existing	  IT	  systems	  and	  purchasing	  of	  the	  software	  license	  from	  SAP,	  Oracle	  etc.).	  
These	  are	  computer-­‐based	  systems	  used	  to	  track	  inventory,	  sales,	  production	  planning,	  financial	  
data	  and	  much	  more	  key	  indicators	  of	  a	  firm,	  all	  reunited	  in	  one	  platform.	  Through	  Electronic	  
Data	  Interchange	  (EDI)	  with	  suppliers,	  stores	  can	  communicate	  efficiently	  and	  in	  real-­‐time	  the	  
evolution	  of	  their	  stocks	  and	  thus	  re-­‐order	  quicker	  than	  with	  a	  manual	  system.	  	  
A	  study	  commanded	  by	  Canonne	  and	  Damret	  (2002)	  on	  the	  implementation	  of	  ERPs	  in	  French	  
companies	   showed	   that	   actually	   the	   satisfaction	   rate	   of	   users	   regarding	   managing	   inventory	  
levels	  and	  purchasing	  was	  in	  effect	  quite	  low	  (33%).	  It	  does	  underline	  the	  complexity	  of	  keeping	  
track	  of	  inventory	  (especially	  with	  a	  great	  number	  of	  Stock	  Keeping	  Units)	  because	  ERPs	  are	  
usually	  difficult	  to	  implement	  and	  adjust	  to	  a	  firm’s	  supply	  chain	  (Huang	  et	  al.,	  2004).	  
	  
	  
==PAGE:13==
	  
	  
	  
	  
13	  
The	  consequences	  of	  a	  poorly	  managed	  inventory	  are	  multiple:	  stock-­‐outs	  may	  occur	  and	  lead	  to	  
a	  loss	  in	  profit	  or	  products	  not	  meeting	  demand	  are	  left	  in	  stock	  and	  thus	  markdown	  have	  to	  be	  
made	   to	   get	   rid	   of	   them.	   Peter	   Lynch,	   famous	   stock	   investor,	   phrased	   the	   danger	   of	   not	  
controlling	  inventory	  carefully:	  “When	  inventories	  grow	  faster	  than	  sales,	  it	  is	  a	  red	  flag”.	  Indeed,	  
as	   mentioned	   previously,	   keeping	   high	   level	   of	   inventory	   is	   exposing	   the	   products	   to	  
obsolescence,	  deterioration,	  perishing	  (time-­‐sensitive	  products)	  and	  markdowns.	  An	  increase	  of	  
inventories	  can	  also	  be	  a	  sign	  that	  the	  business	  fails	  to	  interpret	  the	  actual	  demand	  (Gaur	  et	  al.,	  
2014).	   In	   addition	   to	   the	   loss	   in	   cash	   flow,	   retailers	   also	   have	   to	   figure	   out	   what	   to	   do	   with	  
unwanted	  products.	  	  
3.2 Sustainable	  urban	  supply	  chains	  
	  
Before	  introducing	  what	  sustainable	  supply	  chains	  are,	  it	  is	  essential	  to	  trace	  back	  the	  roots	  of	  
the	   concept	   of	   sustainability	   itself.	   The	   term	   of	   “sustainable	   development”	   has	   probably	   been	  
coined	   for	   the	   first	   time	   in	   1987	   by	   the	   WCED	   (World	   Commission	   on	   Environment	   and	  
Development)	  defining	  it	  as:	  
“a	   development	   that	   meets	   the	   needs	   of	   the	   present	   without	   compromising	   the	   ability	   of	   future	  
generations	  to	  meet	  their	  own	  needs”	  
The	  acceptance	  of	  the	  notion	  spreads	  even	  more	  then	  with	  the	  1992	  Earth	  Summit	  that	  took	  
place	   in	   Rio	   de	   Janeiro	   (Brazil).	   It	   would	   then	   not	   only	   be	   a	   problem	   considered	   by	   public	  
authorities	   but	   also	   an	   integrative	   part	   of	   private	   businesses.	   Dyllick	   and	   Hockerts	   (2002)	  
described	   how	   sustainability	   should	   be	   applied	   to	   businesses	   and	   broke	   down	   the	   term	   into	  
three	   different	   aspects:	   economic	   capital,	   natural	   capital	   and	   social	   capital.	   This	   was	   first	  
referred	  by	  Elkington	  (1998)	  as	  the	  triple	  bottom	  line.	  
Economic	  capital:	  It	   is	   rather	   straightforward	   to	   see	   that	   a	   company	   that	   is	   not	   economically	  
profitable	   will	   not	   survive.	   In	   a	   market	   driven	   by	   financial	   stocks	   it	   has	   long	   been	   a	   primary	  
focus	  for	  companies	  (Bonney,	  1994).	  
Natural	   capital:	   In	   this	   category	   falls	   the	   scarcity	   of	   natural	   resources	   consumed	   by	   human	  
activity	   (raw	   materials,	   energy	   consumption)	   and	   the	   impact	   of	   this	   activity	   on	   the	   natural	  
environment.	  The	  aim	  here	  is	  to	  consume	  less	  resource	  than	  the	  Earth’s	  capacity	  of	  recovering	  
these	  resources	  and	  to	  produce	  less	  waste/pollution	  than	  the	  maximum	  the	  Earth	  can	  absorb	  
(McKinnon	  et	  al.,	  2012).	  
Social	   capital:	   This	   category	   concerns	   a	   good	   conservation	   of	   human	   capital	   (well	   being	   of	  
employees,	  motivation	  and	  skills)	  and	  the	  respect	  of	  society’s	  well	  being	  (impact	  of	  activities	  on	  
the	  community	  and	  the	  individual)	  (Dyllick	  &	  Hockerts,	  2002).	  
==PAGE:14==
	  
	  
	  
	  
14	  
It	  can	  be	  summarised	  as	  the	  intersection	  of	  three	  spheres	  (Figure	  2)	  comprising:	  people	  (social),	  
planet	  (environment)	  and	  profit	  (economic),	  more	  widely	  known	  as	  the	  3Ps.	  
	  
	  
Figure	  2	  The	  three	  spheres	  of	  sustainability	  	  (newsustainabilityinc.com)	  
Sustainability	   of	   supply	   chains	   has	   arisen	   as	   a	   key	   concept,	   as	   supply	   chains	   were	   becoming	  
more	  and	  more	  international	  pushed	  by	  globalization	  (Linton	  et	  al.,	  2007).	  It	  can	  be	  defined	  as	  
the	   management	   of	   the	   supply	   chain	   and	   the	   whole	   supply	   network	   with	   respect	   to	   all	   three	  
areas	   described	   previously.	   Porter	   and	   Vanderlinde	   (1995)	   early	   stressed	   the	   fact	   that	  
environmental	   regulations	   had	   an	   impact	   over	   firms’	   competitiveness.	   They	   argued	   that	  
innovation-­‐based	   solutions	   were	   required	   to	   incorporate	   both	   environmental	   and	   industrial	  
stakes.	  That	  was	  in	  opposition	  with	  the	  trend	  at	  the	  time,	  which	  was	  the	  adoption	  of	  a	  static	  
model	  where	  firms	  are	  fixed	  into	  stone	  processes,	  customers’	  requirements	  and	  just	  bore	  the	  
cost	  of	  environmental	  regulations	  (Porter	  &	  Vanderlinde,	  1995).	  	  Besides	  environmental,	  social	  
impact	  of	  human	  activities	  have	  also	  gained	  importance	  in	  the	  debate	  on	  how	  to	  improve	  supply	  
chains	  operations	  even	  though	  sustainability	  is	  too	  often	  only	  associated	  with	  environment.	  194	  
studies	  were	  done	  on	  this	  matter	  between	  1994	  and	  2007	  (Seuring	  and	  Müller,	  2008),	  showing	  
the	  growing	  interest	  in	  research	  (mostly	  concentrated	  in	  the	  past	  decade).	  	  
==PAGE:15==
	  
	  
	  
	  
15	  
However,	  16%	  of	  them	  were	  digging	  into	  sustainability	  as	  a	  whole	  and	  only	  10%	  were	  bringing	  
social	  concerns	  to	  the	  table.	  The	  social	  aspect	  seems	  the	  one	  that	  the	  literature	  keeps	  omitting,	  
preferring	  to	  dig	  into	  green	  supply	  chains.	  
Furthermore,	  it	  is	  important	  to	  bear	  in	  mind	  that	  the	  three	  areas	  of	  the	  sustainability	  are	  neither	  
in	  opposition	  nor	  in	  competition.	  More	  and	  more	  firms	  acknowledge	  that	  doing	  well	  in	  regards	  
to	   social	   and	   environmental	   criteria	   could	   result	   in	   increased	   profits	   as	   Corbett	   and	   Klassen	  
(2006)	  showed	  that	  supply	  chains	  that	  would	  include	  environmental	  concerns	  in	  their	  operation	  
structure	  were	  likely	  to	  outperform	  others.	  For	  instance,	  the	  redesigning	  of	  production	  process	  
to	  comply	  with	  environmental	  regulations	  can	  lead	  to	  an	  increase	  in	  productivity	  and	  overall	  
performance	  (Corbett	  &	  Klassen,	  2006).	  
Pagell	  and	  Wu	  (2009)	  suggested	  that	  the	  one	  of	  the	  key	  drivers	  of	  a	  sustainable	  supply	  chain	  was	  
the	  degree	  of	  collaboration	  in	  every	  process	  of	  the	  chain	  and	  the	  capacity	  for	  innovation	  (The	  
latter	   was	   also	   argued	   by	   Porter).	   Indeed,	   it	   is	   not	   relevant	   to	   think	   that	   firms	   can	   achieve	  
sustainability	  only	  by	  themselves	  since	  risks	  lie	  in	  the	  wider	  supply	  chain.	  If	  policies	  regarding	  
sustainability	  are	  to	  be	  implemented,	  they	  must	  be	  applied	  beyond	  the	  firm	  (the	  wider	  supply	  
chain),	   meaning	   the	   suppliers	   (and	   eventually	   2nd	   and	   3rd	   tier	   suppliers).	   For	   instance	   it	   is	  
certainly	  not	  sustainable	  to	  choose	  a	  supplier	  that	  is	  itself	  supplied	  by	  a	  company	  using	  child	  
labour	  or	  with	  high	  carbon	  emission	  levels.	  
The	  goal	  of	  sustainability	  is	  not	  only	  to	  find	  solutions	  that	  improve	  the	  profits	  of	  businesses	  by	  
optimizing	  transport	  costs	  but	  also	  to	  act	  on	  the	  environmental	  level	  and	  social	  level.	  Freight	  
generation	   is	   obviously	   a	   direct	   consequence	   of	   inventory	   management	   since	   goods	   have	   to	  
travel	   somehow	   to	   their	   location	   of	   sale	   (Crainic	   et	   al.,	   2004).	   Eventually,	   a	   poor	   freight	  
management	  (meaning	  poor	  inventory	  and	  replenishment	  control)	  will	  lead	  to	  penalties	  (over-­‐
polluting	  vehicles,	  illegal	  parking	  etc.)	  that	  will	  impact	  the	  profit	  zone	  (Taniguchi	  et	  al.,	  2002).	  
This	  shows	  that	  change	  in	  one	  sphere	  is	  likely	  to	  incur	  knock-­‐on	  effects	  on	  the	  other	  two.	  
On	  the	  social	  side,	  businesses	  have	  every	  interest	  to	  keep	  local	  customers	  and	  the	  community	  
unaffected	   (in	   terms	   of	   nuisance)	   by	   their	   activities.	   Evaluating	   the	   impact	   of	   supply	   chains	  
becomes	  even	  more	  complicated	  on	  the	  last	  mile	  since	  that	  is	  the	  moment	  when	  the	  product	  
enters	  the	  most	  complex	  environment	  with	  the	  most	  stakeholders	  affected	  (Gevaers	  et	  al.,	  2011).	  
As	  said	  previously,	  it	  is	  the	  density	  in	  population,	  traffic	  and	  buildings	  that	  makes	  the	  last-­‐mile	  
delivery	  so	  important	  and	  subject	  to	  such	  regulations.	  
	  
	  
==PAGE:16==
	  
	  
	  
	  
16	  
3.3 The	  last-­‐mile	  challenge	  
	  
The	   following	   section	   describes	   the	   different	   impacts	   that	   last-­‐mile	   delivery	   operations	   may	  
have	  on	  supply	  chains.	  However	  it	  is	  difficult	  to	  dissociate	  the	  supply	  chain	  from	  society.	  One	  
consequence	  of	  urban	  freight	  transport	  might	  affect	  directly	  the	  inhabitants	  and	  not	  necessarily	  
the	  business	  itself	  as	  an	  entity.	  On	  the	  other	  hand,	  negative	  externalities	  for	  city-­‐dwellers	  might	  
very	  well	  mean	  negative	  externalities	  for	  the	  supply	  chain.	  This	  is	  why	  the	  externalities	  falling	  
upon	  supply	  chains	  cannot	  be	  isolated	  from	  the	  one’s	  striking	  the	  community.	  
3.3.1 Profit	  
The	  last	  mile	  of	  the	  delivery	  is	  obviously	  a	  particular	  section	  of	  the	  entire	  route	  since	  it	  is	  the	  
area	  where	  risks	  are	  the	  highest	  and	  take	  various	  form	  (Bretzke,	  2013).	  The	  density	  of	  traffic	  
leads	   to	   a	   high	   level	   of	   congestion	   during	   peak-­‐hours.	   In	   Chile,	   42%	   of	   the	   entire	   country’s	  
vehicles	  are	  in	  the	  urban	  area	  of	  the	  capital	  Santiago	  (mercopress.com).	  
First	  of	  all,	  this	  congestion	  puts	  higher	  risks	  on	  the	  freight	  of	  merchandise	  carried	  by	  vehicles	  
(Muñuzuri	  et	  al.,	  2012):	  
• 
Damaging	  of	  the	  merchandise	  while	  unloading	  the	  trucks	  (operation	  often	  done	  not	  in	  
dedicated	  area	  and	  thus	  dangerous)	  
• 
Risk	  of	  theft	  when	  delivering	  in	  exposed	  areas	  
• 
Risk	  of	  accidents	  and	  thus	  deterioration	  of	  the	  merchandise	  
• 
Risk	  of	  delay	  due	  to	  congestion	  
	  
Congestion	  is	  a	  burden	  on	  the	  last	  mile	  delivery	  since	  it	  increases	  the	  chances	  of	  casualties.	  It	  is	  
then	  clearer	  that	  supply	  chains	  should	  optimize	  their	  inventory	  in	  a	  way	  that	  either	  requires	  less	  
delivery	  or	  bypass	  the	  above	  risks.	  Besides,	  in	  a	  congested	  area,	  it	  takes	  more	  time	  to	  get	  from	  
point	  A	  to	  point	  B.	  Therefore,	  this	  part	  of	  the	  driver’s	  journey	  is	  more	  costly	  and	  subject	  to	  much	  
more	  fluctuation	  when	  it	  comes	  to	  evaluating	  delivery	  time.	  	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
==PAGE:17==
	  
	  
	  
	  
17	  
As	   it	   can	   be	   seen	   in	   Figure	   3,	   congestion	   is	   relatively	   the	   highest	   part	   of	   the	   transport	   cost	  
because	  more	  fuel	  and	  time	  are	  consumed	  while	  they	  do	  not	  add	  any	  value	  to	  the	  product.	  
	  
Figure	  3	  Relative	  freight	  transport	  cost	  (Boughton,	  2003)	  
The	  profit	  area	  of	  a	  supply	  chain	  is	  consequently	  greatly	  affected	  by	  last	  mile	  delivery	  operations	  
since	   they	   often	   represent	   “extra	   costs”	   whether	   the	   products	   were	   delivered	   out	   of	   the	  
schedule,	  not	  at	  all	  or	  damaged.	  One	  can	  think	  that	  a	  frequent-­‐replenishment	  strategy	  (such	  as	  
suggested	  by	  the	  lean	  philosophy)	  is	  rather	  risky	  since	  it	  is	  costly	  and	  expose	  more	  frequently	  
the	  merchandise	  to	  the	  above	  risk.	  One	  failed	  delivery	  might	  not	  be	  fatal	  for	  large	  structures	  that	  
might	  have	  back-­‐up	  suppliers	  but	  for	  smaller	  businesses	  the	  impact	  is	  of	  a	  greater	  importance	  
(Chopra	  &	  Sodhi,	  2012).	  
3.3.2 People	  
Similarly	  to	  the	  environmental	  aspect,	  last	  mile	  delivery	  operations	  mainly	  affect	  customers	  and	  
the	  urban	  community	  (whether	  they	  are	  customers	  or	  not)	  (Dyllick	  &	  Hockerts,	  2002).	  There	  is	  
little	  evidence	  that	  this	  particular	  type	  of	  operations	  generally	  affects	  employees.	  On	  the	  other	  
side,	  effects	  on	  the	  community	  and	  the	  standard	  of	  living	  of	  city	  dwellers	  are	  much	  more	  various.	  
Some	   can	   be	   related	   to	   the	   impact	   on	   the	   environment	   since	   the	   emission	   of	   gases	   has	   been	  
proven	  to	  increase	  the	  number	  of	  lung	  and	  cardiovascular	  diseases	  (IFEU,	  2008).	  
Noise	  pollution	  affects	  the	  quality	  of	  life	  of	  inhabitants	  living	  nearby	  delivery	  zones	  because	  of	  
the	   noise	   generated	   by	   engine	   of	   heavy	   vehicles	   at	   low	   speed	   (McKinnon	   et	   al.,	   2012).	  
Operations	   related	   to	   the	   unloading	   of	   the	   trucks	   are	   also	   responsible	   for	   an	   important	  
generation	  of	  noise.	  The	  direct	  effect	  on	  the	  population	  can	  be:	  loss	  of	  concentration,	  sleeping	  
disturbance	  and	  lost	  of	  work	  productivity	  (downtown	  areas	  also	  have	  a	  high	  concentration	  of	  
offices)	  (Moudon,	  2009).	  
==PAGE:18==
	  
	  
	  
	  
18	  
Secondly,	  a	  high	  concentration	  of	  freight	  traffic	  leads	  to	  an	  increase	  numbers	  of	  accidents	  and	  
increased	  violence	  of	  those.	  A	  study	  by	  the	  Department	  for	  Transport	  of	  Great	  Britain	  (2010)	  
showed	   that	   heavy	   vehicles	   were	   killing	   3	   times	   more	   than	   cars	   while	   “killing	   or	   seriously	  
injuring”	   1,3	   times	   more.	   Although	   more	   car	   accidents	   are	   reported,	   accidents	   with	   heavy	  
vehicles	   have	   more	   severe	   consequences.	   The	   literature	   also	   reports	   the	   “visual	   aspect”	  
(McKinnon	  et	  al.,	  2012)	  of	  urban	  freight	  as	  a	  minor	  social	  impact	  that	  would	  make	  downtown	  
areas	  less	  appealing.	  
	  
Again,	   supply	   chains	   should	   have	   a	   great	   interest	   in	   preserving	   the	   social	   capital	   of	   city	  
residents.	  These	  are	  all	  potential	  customers	  and	  the	  objective	  goes	  way	  beyond	  consolidating	  a	  
brand	   image.	   Indeed,	   lessening	   the	   impact	   of	   transport	   makes	   the	   city	   more	   attractive	   for	  
potential	   new	   customers	   and	   contributes	   to	   reducing	   inequalities	   between	   residential	   areas	  
with	  minimal	  nuisance	  and	  commercial	  areas.	  (McKinnon	  et	  al.,	  2012).	  
3.3.3 Planet	  
The	   impact	   of	   last	   mile	   operations	   on	   the	   environment	   is	   perhaps	   the	   most	   obvious	   since	  
awareness	  on	  transport	  pollution	  is	  widespread.	  Difficult	  to	  say	  that	  freight	  transport	  is	  the	  sole	  
root	   of	   congestion	   in	   urban	   areas	   since	   it	   is	   a	   mix	   of	   personal	   vehicles,	   public	   transport	   and	  
delivery	   vehicles.	   Pollution	   of	   vehicles	   is	   one	   major	   concern	   related	   to	   the	   growth	   of	   freight	  
transport,	   congestion	   is	   worsening	   the	   phenomenon	   because	   the	   slower	   large	   vehicles	   are	  
driving	  the	  more	  gas	  particle	  they	  emit	  (Figure	  4).	  
	  
	  
Figure	  4	  𝑪𝑶𝟐	  emissions	  and	  speed	  for	  vehicle	  over	  40	  tonnes	  (McKinnon,	  2012)	  
In	  urban	  areas	  with	  restricted	  speed	  and	  a	  high	  volume	  of	  cars,	  delivery	  vehicles	  are	  more	  likely	  
to	  be	  driving	  between	  5	  and	  50	  km/h	  and	  thus	  release	  a	  bigger	  amount	  of	  CO!.	  
==PAGE:19==
	  
	  
	  
	  
19	  
Freight	  transport	  also	  releases	  an	  important	  number	  of	  non-­‐environmental	  friendly	  gases	  such	  
as	  (IFEU,	  2008):	  
• 
Nitrogen	  oxides	  (NOx):	  affect	  the	  lungs	  on	  the	  long	  term.	  
• 
Sulphur	  dioxide	  (SO2):	  found	  especially	  in	  diesel	  fuels	  (most	  used	  fuel	  by	  trucks).	  Causes	  
irritation	  to	  the	  eyes	  and	  throat.	  
• 
Hydrocarbons	  (HCs):	  at	  a	  certain	  level	  can	  be	  carcinogenic.	  	  
• 
Fine	  particles:	  carcinogenic	  elements,	  are	  to	  said	  to	  have	  caused	  the	  premature	  death	  of	  
12,000-­‐24,000	  persons	  in	  the	  UK	  (Rogers,	  2007)	  
	  
The	   above	   list	   accounts	   for	   the	   consequences	   on	   human	   beings	   but	   emission	   of	   gases	   is	   also	  
harming	  the	  environment	  via	  its	  contribution	  to	  global	  warming.	  In	  addition,	  the	  preservation	  of	  
natural	  resources	  is	  a	  critical	  point	  of	  sustainability	  and	  urban	  freight	  via	  its	  fuel	  consumption	  is	  
not	  exactly	  achieving	  that	  goal.	  	  
Moreover,	  if	  goods	  are	  transported	  to	  retailers	  and	  businesses,	  it	  is	  because	  they	  are	  supposed	  
to	   find	   customers.	   Shopping	   trips	   made	   by	   car	   then	   generate	   again	   more	   pollution	   and	  
congestion	  and	  are	  an	  indirect	  consequence	  of	  urban	  freight.	  
These	  aftermaths	  affect	  society	  as	  a	  whole,	  but	  also	  businesses	  in	  a	  way	  that	  has	  not	  always	  been	  
acknowledged	   in	   the	   past.	   Environmental	   concerns	   have	   grown	   a	   lot	   recently	   in	   customer’s	  
purchases	  decisions,	  a	  study	  in	  the	  US	  concluded	  that	  close	  to	  50%	  of	  American	  customers	  were	  
caring	   about	   green	   efforts	   from	   private	   companies	   (marketingcharts.com).	   The	   culture	   of	   a	  
green	  image	  and	  green	  products	  is	  now	  strong	  for	  a	  lot	  of	  brands,	  similarly	  to	  organic	  products	  
in	  the	  food	  industry.	  Technologies	  or	  strategies	  (electric	  vehicles	  for	  instance)	  that	  were	  judged	  
too	  expensive	  to	  be	  profitable	  could	  be	  reconsidered	  with	  customers	  adding	  the	  “environment”	  
variable	  to	  their	  decision.	  However,	  not	  every	  customer	  segment	  is	  ready	  to	  bear	  the	  costs	  of	  the	  
process	  of	  “greening”	  and	  thus	  there	  are	  still	  obstacles	  of	  implementing	  solutions.	  
Plus,	   environmental	   regulations	   due	   to	   pollution	   peaks	   are	   more	   and	   more	   frequent.	  
Extraordinary	   measures	   are	   likely	   to	   impose	   financial	   penalties	   to	   supply	   chains	   that	   do	   not	  
manage	  their	  transport	  (and	  by	  extension	  their	  inventory)	  in	  a	  “green”	  way.	  During	  the	  month	  of	  
March	  2015	  in	  Paris,	  unusual	  pollution	  peaks	  led	  the	  city	  of	  Paris	  to	  force	  alternate	  traffic	  in	  the	  
city	  centre.	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
==PAGE:20==
	  
	  
	  
	  
20	  
Common	  regulations	  are	  (Comi	  et	  al.,	  2008):	  
• 
Freight	  taxes	  
• 
Prohibition	  of	  trucks	  above	  a	  certain	  weight	  and	  at	  certain	  times	  
• 
Prohibition	  of	  trucks	  to	  drive	  in	  certain	  areas	  in	  city	  centres	  
	  
These	  measures	  are	  all	  consequences	  of	  the	  environmental	  impact	  transport	  (not	  only	  freight)	  
has,	  in	  the	  end	  they	  impact	  the	  profit	  zone	  of	  supply	  chains.	  As	  Porter	  argued,	  these	  measures	  
are	  usually	  seen	  as	  extra	  costs	  whereas	  they	  could	  be	  seen	  as	  incentives	  for	  improving	  delivery	  
operations.	  
3.4 Urban	  freight	  studies	  and	  city	  logistics	  
Urbanisation	  is	  an	  accelerating	  trend	  that	  has	  given	  many	  challenges	  to	  overcome	  in	  the	  field	  of	  
sustainability.	  Latin	  America	  counts	  in	  as	  one	  of	  the	  most	  urbanized	  region	  of	  the	  world	  with	  a	  
percentage	   of	   80%	   (United	   Nations,	   2014).	   The	   city	   of	   Santiago	   considered	   in	   this	   thesis	  
concentrates	   more	   than	   1/3	   of	   Chile’s	   entire	   population	   with	   a	   little	   less	   than	   7	   million	  
inhabitants.	  That	  is	  too	  little	  to	  label	  Santiago	  as	  a	  “mega	  city”	  (cities	  with	  10	  million	  or	  more	  
inhabitants,	  28	  in	  the	  world)	  but	  it	  has	  still	  all	  the	  characteristics	  of	  it:	  congestion	  and	  high	  levels	  
of	   pollution.	   More	   globally,	   the	   phenomenon	   of	   urbanization	   (somehow	   related	   to	   the	  
population’s	   growth)	   combined	   to	   a	   consumer	   goods	   market	   becoming	   more	   volatile	   and	  
gaining	   more	   volume,	   has	   led	   researchers	   to	   dig	   into	   how	   logistics	   was	   going	   to	   face	   these	  
phenomenon	  in	  a	  sustainable	  way	  (Taniguchi	  et	  al.,	  2001).	  It	  is	  an	  ever	  more	  challenging	  issue	  to	  
tackle	   in	   Latin	   America	   and	   Asia	   since	   people	   mostly	   live	   in	   these	   urban	   areas.	   The	   growing	  
recognition	  of	  these	  upcoming	  challenges	  and	  already	  existing	  issues	  showed	  the	  need	  for	  a	  new	  
concept	  and	  vision	  for	  logistics	  in	  urban	  areas:	  city	  logistics.	  This	  very	  recent	  concept	  has	  been	  
defined	  by	  Taniguchi	  et	  al.	  in	  2001:	  
	  
«	  The	  process	  of	  totally	  optimizing	  the	  logistics	  and	  transport	  activities	  by	  private	  companies	  with	  
support	  of	  advanced	  information	  systems	  in	  urban	  areas	  considering	  the	  traffic	  environment,	  the	  
traffic	   congestion,	   the	   traffic	   safety	   and	   the	   energy	   savings	   within	   the	   framework	   of	   a	   market	  
economy	  »	  	  	  
	  
The	  spirit	  of	  this	  concept	  is	  not	  only	  that	  traffic	  is	  worsening	  the	  environmental	  capital	  of	  urban	  
areas	   but	   also	   takes	   into	   account	   other	   aspects	   that	   relate	   to	   sustainability	   such	   as	   social	  
matters.	   All	   of	   these	   concerns	   encompass	   the	   constraints	   of	   a	   market	   economy	   because	  
regulations	  should	  not	  disrupt	  or	  unbalance	  the	  commercial	  activity.

3.5 Previous	  work	  on	  inventory	  and	  transport	  
Urban	   freight	   has	   been	   large	   area	   of	   study	   since	   the	   1960s	   as	   urbanization	   was	   gaining	  
momentum	  and	  thus	  urban	  challenges	  arose,	  even	  if	  traffic	  and	  transit	  concentrate	  the	  most	  of	  
the	  studies	  and	  models.	  	  
Most	  studies	  focused	  on	  the	  transport	  issues	  themselves	  regarding	  urban	  policies	  and	  much	  has	  
been	  written	  on	  how	  to	  improve	  long	  deliveries	  but	  less	  on	  the	  last	  mile	  delivery	  challenge.	  
The	  literature	  is	  rather	  well	  furnished	  when	  it	  comes	  to	  integrate	  inventory	  and	  transport	  in	  
mathematical	   models.	   However	   managerial	   implications	   of	   these	   findings	   are	   seldom	   as	   well	  
detailed	  as	  the	  theoretical	  model	  is.	  	  
Most	  studies	  are	  found	  to	  be	  recent	  as	  they	  comprise	  computer-­‐based	  models	  and	  thus	  show	  
very	  quantitative	  results,	  having	  cost	  as	  the	  sole	  parameter	  of	  the	  objective	  function.	  
One	  of	  the	  very	  first	  study	  linking	  inventory	  and	  freight	  is	  the	  one	  of	  Baumol	  and	  Vinod	  (1970).	  
Indeed,	   very	   early	   they	   linked	   inventory	   theory	   with	   freight	   transport	   demand	   by	   describing	  
how	   transit	   time	   (of	   goods)	   was	   affecting	   inventory	   levels.	   The	   greatest	   the	   lead-­‐time	   of	  
transport,	   obviously	   the	   greatest	   safety	   stock	   is	   going	   to	   be	   needed.	   Besides,	   this	   work	   also	  
argued	   that	   demand	   functions	   should	   be	   incorporated	   into	   mathematical	   models	   that	   aim	   to	  
reduce	  shipping	  costs.	  This	  very	  first	  approach	  took	  the	  aim	  of	  reducing	  total	  logistic	  costs	  but	  
did	  not	  incorporate	  sustainability	  issues	  such	  as	  reducing	  urban	  distances	  for	  instance.	  However	  
this	  work	  might	  have	  been	  the	  first	  approach	  to	  sustainability	  before	  it	  was	  properly	  defined	  in	  
1987.	  
Allen	  et	  al.	  (2000)	  studied	  the	  urban	  movement	  of	  goods	  in	  the	  areas	  of	  London	  and	  Norwich	  
(UK),	  it	  points	  out	  the	  fact	  that	  is	  not	  only	  important	  to	  quantify	  how	  many	  goods	  are	  attracted	  
by	  premises	  but	  also	  where	  do	  they	  come	  from.	  Premises,	  depending	  on	  if	  they	  are	  part	  of	  a	  
large	   corporation/network	   or	   operating	   independently,	   will	   have	   different	   type	   of	   supply	  
systems.	   The	   study	   in	   the	   UK	   shows	   that	   most	   premises	   of	   the	   sample	   are	   using	   either	   a	  
decentralised	  system	  (they	  get	  goods	  from	  several	  points	  of	  despatch)	  or	  a	  hybrid	  (most	  goods	  
come	  from	  one	  supplier	  and	  the	  rest	  from	  several	  point	  of	  dispatches).	  An	  interesting	  result	  is	  
also	  that	  decentralised	  systems	  lead	  to	  the	  most	  freight	  generation	  since	  there	  is	  not	  necessarily	  
a	  consolidation	  of	  deliveries	  of	  goods	  between	  the	  different	  suppliers.	  
In	   terms	   of	   reordering	   methods,	   orders	   to	   replenish	   stocks	   are	   mainly	   processed	   through	  
computers,	  EDI	  (Electronic	  Data	  Interchange)	  and	  EPOS	  (Electronic	  Point	  of	  Sale)	  meaning	  they	  
can	   be	   sometimes	   automatic.	   The	   rest	   is	   done	   manually	   by	   phone	   or	   fax	   for	   most	   premises.	  
Computer-­‐based	  methods	  (usually	  used	  at	  head	  offices	  rather	  than	  at	  the	  store	  level)	  tend	  to	  be	  
more	   widespread	   in	   retail	   part	   of	   large	   companies	   than	   in	   independent	   stores	   (Allen	   et	   al.,	  
2000).

Inventory	   control	   will	   eventually	   impact	   transportation	   choices,	   even	   though	   in	   last	   mile	  
deliveries	  motor	  carriers	  are	  unavoidable.	  The	  choice	  is	  located	  at	  the	  level	  of:	  vehicle	  type	  and	  
vehicle	  load.	  	  Schematically,	  delivery	  types	  can	  be	  seen	  two	  different	  ways	  explained	  in	  Figures	  5	  
and	  6.	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
Single-­‐drop	  delivery	  (Figure	  5)	  implies	  that	  the	  vehicle	  collects	  only	  one	  load	  and	  then	  delivers	  it	  
to	  one	  store.	  It	  also	  entails	  that	  the	  trip	  back	  is	  made	  with	  an	  empty	  cargo	  making	  it	  appear	  not	  
optimal	  (Allen	  et	  al.,	  2000).	  On	  the	  other	  hand,	  Figure	  6	  presents	  multi-­‐drop	  delivery	  where	  the	  
carrier	  collects	  goods	  from	  the	  supplier	  and	  then	  deliver	  several	  shops,	  making	  a	  “round”.	  
Opting	   for	   multi-­‐delivery	   systems	   implies	   that	   full-­‐truck	   loads	   (FTL)	   for	   one	   store	   cannot	   be	  
achieved	   since	   the	   truck	   is	   catering	   multiple	   locations.	   It	   is	   then	   more	   commonly	   used	   for	  
smaller	  quantities	  and	  by	  stores	  with	  low	  volume	  items.	  Allen	  et	  al.’s	  study	  in	  the	  UK	  shows	  that	  
86%	  of	  the	  shops	  surveyed	  are	  served	  via	  multi-­‐drop	  delivery	  systems,	  the	  one	  served	  through	  
single-­‐drop	  delivery	  are	  usually	  part	  of	  large	  companies.	  It	  also	  argued	  that	  single-­‐drop	  delivery	  
with	  full	  load	  appears	  to	  be	  the	  most	  sustainable	  system	  since	  less	  distance	  is	  travelled	  with	  
more	  utilization	  of	  the	  capacity	  (except	  for	  the	  return	  trip	  when	  the	  truck	  is	  empty).

Several	  studies	  also	  focussed	  more	  on	  the	  implications	  on	  transport	  of	  the	  different	  inventory	  
strategy	  present	  earlier.	  Wilson	  (2007)	  studied	  the	  impact	  of	  transport	  disruptions	  over	  supply	  
chains	  by	  comparing	  a	  traditional	  supply	  chain	  and	  a	  vendor-­‐managed	  inventory	  (VMI).	  
	  
Figure	  7	  Average	  inventory	  levels	  with	  different	  transport	  disruptions	  scenario	  (Wilson,	  2007)	  
Different	   types	   of	   disruptions	   are	   tested	   on	   the	   inventory	   level;	   those	   types	   are	   whether	   the	  
disruption	   occurs	   between	   the	   retailer	   and	   the	   warehouse	   or	   in-­‐between	   other	   nodes	   of	   the	  
supply	  chain.	  VMI	  and	  traditional	  supply	  chains	  react	  approximately	  evenly	  at	  the	  retail	  level	  
(Figure	   7),	   except	   for	   one	   disruption:	   between	   the	   warehouse	   and	   the	   manufacturer.	   This	  
finding	   is	   definitely	   not	   trivial	   since	   one	   would	   expect	   retail	   would	   be	   more	   affected	   by	   a	  
transport	  disruption	  between	  retail	  and	  warehouse.	  The	  above	  study	  has	  also	  the	  merit	  to	  show	  
that	  disruptions	  in-­‐between	  two	  consecutive	  actors	  of	  the	  supply	  chain	  may	  have	  ripple	  effects	  
both	  upstream	  and	  downstream.	  Mitigation	  of	  theses	  risks	  can	  be	  achieved	  through	  additional	  
inventory	   and	   buffer	   stock	   (providing	   that	   goods	   are	   not	   perishable	   or	   subject	   to	   be	   quickly	  
obsolete).	   Another	   solution	   presented	   is	   having	   a	   redundant	   supplier	   to	   cope	   with	   eventual	  
stock-­‐outs.	   Still,	   if	   the	   disruption	   stems	   from	   a	   natural	   disaster,	   closing	   of	   a	   route	   etc.	   the	  
redundant	   supplier	   would	   have	   to	   be	   located	   where	   it	   is	   not	   affected	   by	   the	   cause	   of	   the	  
disruption.	  This	  fact	  might	  incur	  higher	  cost	  of	  deliveries.	  	  
Venkat	  (2007)	  studied	  transportation	  and	  storage	  along	  supply	  chains	  from	  different	  industries	  
and	   their	   relation	   with	   carbon	   emissions.	   It	   is	   noted	   that	   account	   storage	   costs	   are	   rarely	  
considered	  whereas	  it	  is	  quite	  important	  for	  frozen	  or	  cold	  products	  for	  instance.	  	  
Trying	  to	  achieve	  a	  Full-­‐Truck-­‐Load	  quantity	  might	  then	  not	  be	  relevant	  if	  cost	  of	  storing	  the	  
extra	  products	  is	  higher	  than	  the	  savings	  achieved	  in	  transportation.	  
	  Venkat	   stresses	   out	   the	   fact	   that	   larger	   inventories	   (pushed	   by	   transportation	   with	   larger	  
capacities)	  are	  more	  costly	  to	  maintain	  but	  also	  cheaper	  to	  build.	  Therefore	  there	  is	  a	  trade	  off	  
between	   storage	   and	   transportation,	   even	   more	   important	   in	   the	   case	   of	   cold	   and	   frozen	  
products.

Shipment	  sizes	  are	  defined	  only	  regarding	  inventory	  carrying	  costs	  in	  the	  case	  of	  products	  that	  
do	  not	  generate	  high	  levels	  of	  energy	  to	  store.	  Finally	  it	  is	  argued	  that	  intermediate	  shipment	  
size	   work	   well	   in	   many	   cases	   where	   the	   trade-­‐off	   between	   storage	   and	   transportation	   is	  
important.	  
Hua	  et	  al.	  (2011)	  examined	  the	  impact	  of	  different	  carbon	  related	  regulations	  (carbon	  tax,	  cap	  
and	  trade	  etc.)	  on	  total	  logistics	  costs,	  basing	  the	  analysis	  on	  an	  adaptation	  of	  the	  EOQ	  model.	  
The	  interesting	  finding	  of	  this	  study	  is	  that	  the	  optimal	  order	  size	  is	  found	  between	  the	  order	  
size	  given	  by	  the	  EOQ	  and	  the	  order	  size	  that	  minimizes	  carbon	  emission.	  This	  is	  rather	  intuitive	  
since	   the	   model	   is	   aiming	   to	   optimize	   logistics	   costs	   and	   carbon	   emissions	   at	   the	   same	   time.	  
Most	   of	   the	   other	   findings	   are	   related	   to	   economic	   schemes	   of	   carbon	   taxation	   and	   trade,	  
depending	  on	  the	  scenario	  retailers	  might	  see	  their	  cost	  rise	  or	  find	  a	  compromise.	  
In	   2012,	   Bouchery	   et	   al.	   also	   took	   it	   upon	   to	   integrate	   sustainability	   concerns	   into	   inventory	  
models.	  Acknowledging	  that	  firms	  were	  getting	  more	  and	  more	  aware	  of	  sustainability	  of	  supply	  
chains,	  the	  authors	  adapted	  the	  original	  EOQ	  to	  make	  it	  the	  SOQ:	  Sustainable	  Order	  Quantity.	  
This	   adaptation	   claims	   to	   provide	   decision	   makers	   solution	   to	   encompass	   economic,	  
environmental	   and	   social	   trade-­‐offs.	   The	   starting	   point	   of	   view	   adopted	   is	   the	   one	   reducing	  
carbon	  emissions.	  While	  it	  primarily	  seems	  to	  only	  focus	  on	  the	  environmental	  aspect,	  as	  seen	  
previously,	  carbon	  emissions	  are	  also	  affecting	  the	  social	  sustainability	  of	  society	  hence	  it	  is	  a	  
relevant	  parameter	  to	  use.	  According	  to	  the	  authors,	  decision	  makers	  have	  two	  possibilities	  to	  
optimize	  supply	  chain	  operations.	  The	  first	  is	  to	  make	  operational	  adjustments	  in	  the	  inventory	  
management.	  In	  Figure	  8,	  it	  can	  be	  seen	  that	  neither	  of	  the	  two	  extreme	  solutions	  (Q1*	  and	  Q2*)	  of	  
batch	  size	  are	  optimal.	  However	  it	  can	  be	  noticed	  that	  a	  decrease	  in	  carbon	  emissions	  can	  be	  
achieved	  with	  only	  a	  small	  increase	  in	  costs	  (logistics	  costs).	  For	  instance	  a	  22%	  decrease	  in	  
emissions	   would	   only	   mean	   a	   5%	   costs	   increase.	   These	   5%	   of	   increase	   could	   easily	   be	  
compensated	  by	  the	  gain	  in	  brand	  reputation	  and	  potential	  increase	  in	  market	  share.	  However	  it	  
is	  unclear	  if	  the	  increase	  of	  batch	  size	  needed	  is	  fitting	  the	  demand	  pattern	  and	  a	  given	  type	  of	  
commodity.	   The	   second	   option	   is	   to	   invest	   in	   technologies	   to	   reduce	   carbon	   emissions,	   this	  
echoes	  to	  Porter’s	  claims	  that	  sustainability	  would	  be	  achieved	  through	  innovation.	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
	  
==PAGE:25==
	  
	  
	  
	  
25	  
The	  point	  of	  view	  taken	  here	  aims	  to	  make	  supply	  chain	  more	  sustainable	  but	  is	  not	  specifically	  
focussed	  on	  last-­‐mile	  operations	  meaning	  that	  congestion	  is	  not	  evaluated	  here	  for	  instance.	  
	  
	  
Figure	  8	  Cost	  and	  carbon	  emissions	  in	  function	  of	  batch	  size	  (Bouchery	  et	  al.,	  2012)	  
The	  problem	  evaluated	  in	  this	  thesis	  is	  two-­‐sided,	  inventory	  decisions	  impact	  freight	  generation	  
but	   freight	   also	   impacts	   inventory.	   Spiegler	   and	   Naim	   (2014)	   showed	   how	   freight	   transport	  
capacity	   was	   impacting	   supply	   chain	   dynamics.	   Constraints	   on	   capacity	   result	   in	   a	   certain	  
number	  of	  backlogs	  and	  inventory	  costs	  rise	  but	  also	  show	  a	  decrease	  of	  the	  bullwhip	  effect.	  
They	  advocate	  here	  in	  favour	  of	  collaborative	  transportation	  in	  order	  to	  reduce	  transport	  and	  
inventory	  cost,	  and	  to	  increase	  transport	  utilization.	  
Some	  of	  these	  studies	  integrate	  inventory	  and	  transport	  issues	  in	  their	  model	  but	  most	  focus	  on	  
optimizing	  an	  objective	  function	  related	  somehow	  to	  financial	  matters.	  Only	  very	  recently	  can	  be	  
found	   a	   study	   (Alho	   and	   de	   Abreu	   e	   Silva,	   2015)	   that	   is	   relying	   on	   a	   set	   of	   data	   and	   a	  
methodology	  similar	  to	  the	  one	  used	  here.	  This	  study	  is	  based	  on	  an	  establishment-­‐based	  survey	  
of	  retailer’s	  freight	  generation	  in	  the	  city	  of	  Lisbon,	  Portugal.	  The	  survey	  based	  on	  Allen	  et	  al’s	  
prior	  work,	  actually	  presupposes	  that	  the	  type	  of	  inventory	  control	  has	  an	  impact	  on	  the	  rate	  of	  
deliveries	  of	  an	  establishment.	  However	  this	  study	  differs	  from	  this	  thesis	  in	  the	  way	  that	  its	  
purpose	  is	  not	  axed	  on	  sustainability	  but	  on	  the	  method	  of	  Establishment	  Based	  Freight	  Survey	  
(EBFS)	  and	  how	  to	  show	  correlations	  between	  different	  set	  of	  data.	  The	  bottom	  line	  being	  to	  be	  
able	  to	  predict	  freight	  flow	  depending	  on	  certain	  establishment	  characteristics,	  which	  is	  not	  the	  
aim	   of	   this	   study.	   It	   is	   still	   a	   good	   point	   of	   comparison,	   within	   limits,	   to	   demonstrate	   the	  
coherence	  of	  the	  data	  collected	  in	  this	  thesis.

3.6 Why	  is	  the	  study	  new?	  
Freight	  generation	  have	  been	  studied	  in	  numerous	  cities	  across	  the	  world,	  even	  though	  some	  
parts	  of	  the	  world	  remain	  uncovered.	  	  The	  subject	  covered	  by	  this	  thesis	  is	  new	  for	  two	  reasons.	  
First,	  even	  though	  studies	  have	  examined	  the	  impact	  of	  supply	  chain	  decisions	  on	  sustainability	  
(mostly	   through	   carbon	   emissions)	   in	   a	   quantitative,	   very	   few	   have	   taken	   into	   account	  
managerial	  decisions	  and	  most	  of	  all	  very	  few	  in	  the	  light	  of	  last	  mile	  deliveries.	  Secondly,	  as	  it	  
will	  be	  covered	  later	  on,	  urban	  freight	  studies	  in	  mega	  cities	  in	  Latin	  America	  are	  quite	  rare,	  and	  
most	  importantly	  no	  such	  studies	  of	  the	  city	  of	  Santiago	  de	  Chile	  has	  been	  found.	  Solutions	  to	  
enhance	   operations	   in	   congested	   urban	   areas	   almost	   exclusively	   focussed	   on	   freight	   (routing	  
problems,	  public	  policies,	  taxes	  etc.)	  itself	  while	  widely	  ignoring	  the	  origins	  of	  freight,	  inventory	  
management	  being	  one	  for	  instance.	  
Allen	  et	  al.	  (2012)	  did	  a	  comprehensive	  review	  of	  many	  of	  the	  urban	  freight	  studies	  of	  the	  past	  
fifty	  years	  comprising	  the	  main	  data	  collection	  methods	  used.	  The	  numbers	  shown	  in	  Figure	  9	  
are	  to	  be	  considered	  as	  minimums	  since	  some	  studies	  are	  confidential	  or	  haven’t	  been	  publicly	  
published.	  
	  
Figure	  9	  Surveys	  on	  urban	  freight	  between	  1960	  and	  2008	  (Allen	  et	  al.,	  2012)

The	  prevalence	  of	  studies	  in	  the	  UK	  is	  explained	  by	  the	  fact	  that	  the	  authors	  being	  British,	  they	  
have	  a	  better	  knowledge	  of	  what	  has	  been	  done	  in	  the	  UK,	  especially	  for	  studies	  that	  haven’t	  
been	  made	  public.	  Most	  studies	  are	  located	  in	  Europe	  and	  North	  America;	  only	  7	  have	  been	  done	  
outside	  these	  two	  zones.	  As	  sustainability	  issues	  have	  taken	  more	  and	  more	  importance,	  half	  of	  
the	  studies	  have	  been	  conducted	  between	  2000	  and	  2008.	  
While	  economies	  and	  urban	  planning	  are	  much	  more	  different	  in	  South	  America	  and	  especially	  
in	  mega-­‐cities	  such	  as	  Santiago,	  it	  becomes	  obvious	  separate	  studies	  are	  needed	  to	  have	  a	  good	  
understanding	  on	  local	  issues	  and	  solutions.	  Munuzuri	  et	  al.	  (2012)	  studied	  freight	  deliveries	  in	  
the	  city	  of	  Sevilla	  (Spain)	  under	  strong	  assumptions	  that	  led	  to	  think	  that	  much	  more	  data	  on	  
supply	  chain	  policies	  and	  fleet	  management	  would	  be	  needed	  to	  be	  more	  accurate	  on	  the	  real	  
pattern	  of	  freight	  generation.	  Another	  motivation	  for	  this	  study	  relies	  on	  the	  fact	  that	  population	  
in	  urban	  areas	  is	  expected	  to	  rise	  from	  54%	  (in	  2014)	  to	  66%	  by	  2050	  according	  to	  a	  report	  
from	   the	   UN.	   The	   report	   also	   advocates	   for	   the	   need	   of	   a	   sustainable	   urbanization	   that	   will	  
inevitably	  be	  achieved	  by	  sustainable	  urban	  logistics.

4 Study	  case:	  Santiago	  de	  Chile	  	  
The	  following	  section	  intends	  to	  provide	  the	  reader	  with	  information	  about	  the	  study	  case	  that	  
has	  been	  chosen,	  the	  capital	  of	  Chile:	  Santiago	  de	  Chile.	  
	  
Chile	   is	   often	   presented	   as	   the	   success	   story	   of	   Latin	   America	   for	   its	   economic	   growth	   and	  
stability	   when	   most	   of	   their	   neighbours	   show	   disappointing	   performance.	   Part	   of	   the	  
explanation	   for	   this	   success	   lies	   in	   the	   strength	   of	   exportation	   of	   copper	   (world	   leader)	   of	  
salmon	   (second	   behind	   Norway)	   and	   wine	   (5th	   position	   in	   2010).	   Exports	   accounted	   for	   76.5	  
billion	  $	  in	  2013	  (74.7	  billion	  $	  of	  imports).	  Its	  GDP	  per	  capita	  reaches	  22	  333.1	  $,	  41%	  of	  the	  
United	   States’	   (World	   Bank	   data).	   Of	   the	   17	   million	   inhabitants,	   only	   6%	   of	   them	   remain	  
unemployed,	  partly	  because	  of	  the	  existence	  of	  low	  paid	  jobs	  that	  have	  basically	  disappeared	  in	  
most	  developed	  countries.	  With	  an	  annual	  growth	  approximating	  4%	  during	  the	  past	  5	  years	  
(even	  if	  it	  experiences	  a	  slight	  slowdown	  since	  2014),	  Chile	  has	  seen	  urbanization	  developing	  at	  
a	  fast	  pace,	  especially	  in	  the	  capital	  Santiago	  de	  Chile	  where	  the	  CBD	  (Central	  Business	  District)	  
is	  comparable	  to	  North	  American	  cities’	  CBDs.	  	  
Still,	  the	  Chilean’s	  growth	  is	  marked	  by	  inequality.	  Indeed,	  in	  addition	  of	  concentrating	  36%	  of	  
the	   total	   population	   (6,3	   million	   inhabitants	   in	   2012),	   Santiago	   also	   accounts	   for	   48%	   of	   the	  
country’s	  GDP	  (Dockemdorff	  et	  al.,	  2000).	  This	  stresses	  out	  the	  intensity	  of	  the	  economic	  activity	  
that	   takes	   place	   in	   the	   capital	   while	   this	   economy	   has	   slowly	   opened	   to	   the	   world	   market.	  
Inequalities	  are	  also	  found	  at	  the	  local	  level,	  richer	  neighbourhoods	  are	  located	  at	  the	  Northeast	  
part	  of	  the	  city	  shaped	  like	  a	  circle.	  As	  most	  offices	  and	  business	  activity	  is	  found	  in	  the	  northern	  
part,	   most	   trips	   are	   done	   from	   the	   suburbs	   to	   the	   centre	   in	   the	   morning	   and	   the	   other	   way	  
around	  in	  the	  evening.	  
The	   population	   gained	   an	   important	   power	   of	   consumption	   for	   mass-­‐market	   products	   giving	  
retail	  a	  predominant	  position	  in	  Santiago’s	  economical	  landscape.	  In	  2005,	  retail	  was	  26%	  of	  the	  
nation’s	  GDP	  (Bianchi	  et	  al.,	  2006),	  and	  it	  has	  been	  growing	  since.	  The	  most	  prevalent	  types	  of	  
retails	  are:	  
• 
Grocery	   store:	   24%	   of	   all	   retail	   sales.	   The	   market	   of	   groceries	   is	   dominated	   by	   D&S	  
(Originally	   local	   business	   acquired	   by	   Walmart)	   and	   Cencosud	   (Owner	   of	   the	   chain	  
Jumbo,	  Paris	  and	  Easy).	  Both	  of	  them	  account	  for	  63%	  of	  total	  market	  share.	  
• 
Department	  store:	  10%	  of	  retail	  sales.	  Three	  firms	  dominate	  the	  market:	  Ripley,	  Paris	  
and	  Falabella	  operating	  in	  other	  South	  American	  countries	  as	  well.	  Smaller	  size	  stores	  
such	  as	  Hites	  or	  Corona	  constitute	  the	  rest	  of	  the	  market.

Drug	  store	  (pharmacies):	  4%	  of	  retail	  sales.	  The	  top	  three	  players	  in	  this	  industry	  are	  
FASA,	  SalcoBrand	  (SB)	  and	  Cruz	  Verde.	  
	  
The	  intensity	  of	  the	  economic	  activity	  spread	  heterogeneously	  in	  the	  urban	  area	  has	  led	  to	  high	  
levels	  of	  pollution	  and	  congestion.	  During	  a	  working	  day,	  in	  average	  10	  million	  motorized	  trips	  
are	  done	  inside	  the	  urban	  area	  (Dictuc,	  2003),	  encompassing	  both	  freight	  transport	  and	  personal	  
trips.	  The	  special	  atmosphere	  conditions	  and	  the	  unfavourable	  geographic	  location	  of	  Santiago	  
regularly	  cause	  pollution	  levels	  to	  rise	  above	  limits.	  The	  situation	  worsens	  in	  the	  winter	  with	  
low	   temperatures	   preventing	   gases	   to	   rise	   up	   in	   the	   atmosphere.	   In	   June	   2015,	   the	   Chilean	  
government	   declared	   the	   state	   of	   emergency	   concerning	   pollution,	   a	   first	   since	   1999.	  
Extraordinary	  measures	  forced	  several	  manufacturing	  sites	  to	  stop	  their	  activity	  and	  circulation	  
to	  be	  alternated.	  92%	  of	  Carbon	  Monoxide	  emissions	  (CO)	  are	  the	  result	  of	  vehicle	  emissions,	  
although	  medium-­‐heavy	  vehicles	  account	  for	  only	  18%	  of	  this	  total	  (Sanhueza	  et	  al.,	  2010).	  Of

impact	   on	   congestion	   and	   pollution	   levels.	   Indeed	   vehicles	   are	   larger	   than	   cars,	   slower	   and	  
hence	   have	   a	   tendency	   to	   slow	   down	   vehicle	   flow.	   Other	   contaminants	   are	   also	   a	   source	   for	  
concern;	  several	  of	  them	  are	  largely	  superior	  to	  government’s	  norms	  (Figure	  10):

5 Problem	  definition	  
It	  has	  been	  clearly	  defined	  now	  that	  urban	  freight	  transport	  has	  a	  severe	  impact	  on	  the	  triple	  
bottom	  line	  of	  both	  businesses	  and	  the	  local	  community.	  Effects	  of	  the	  last	  mile	  delivery	  have	  
been	   quantified	   in	   a	   general	   way	   and	   it	   has	   been	   demonstrated	   that	   these	   effects	   were	  
particularly	  important	  in	  the	  study	  zone	  Santiago	  Centro.	  This	  problem	  can	  be	  seen	  from	  two	  
points	  of	  view:	  the	  public	  side	  and	  the	  private	  (business)	  side.	  Public	  institutions	  have	  the	  power	  
to	  influence	  logistics	  operations	  of	  businesses,	  and	  are	  already	  taking	  measures	  in	  that	  sense.	  
Private	  parties	  have	  also	  their	  own	  interest,	  often	  centred	  on	  maintaining	  profitability,	  and	  take	  
action	   to	   preserve	   these	   interests	   without	   necessarily	   aligning	   their	   strategy	   with	   public	  
measures.	  It	  is	  believed	  that	  no	  improvement	  will	  ever	  be	  achieved	  without	  a	  commitment	  of	  
private	   parties	   to	   make	   the	   last	   mile	   delivery	   of	   products	   have	   a	   lesser	   impact	   on	   the	   triple	  
bottom	   line.	   Therefore,	   the	   focus	   will	   be	   put	   on	   how	   sustainability	   can	   be	   achieved	   by	   these	  
private	  parties.	  The	  research	  question	  of	  this	  thesis	  can	  be	  defined	  into	  several	  sub-­‐problems:	  
	  
How	  sustainable	  is	  inventory	  management	  in	  Downtown	  Santiago?	  
• 
What	  are	  the	  characteristics	  of	  retailers’	  operations	  depending	  on	  the	  type	  of	  business?	  	  
• 
What	  can	  be	  done	  in	  order	  to	  mitigate	  the	  negative	  externalities	  associated	  with	  logistics	  
operations?	  
	  
The	   objective	   of	   the	   study	   is,	   after	   an	   assessment	   of	   the	   inventory	   management	   and	   freight	  
transport	  practices,	  to	  determine	  relevant	  alternatives	  strategies	  that	  could	  be	  applied	  to	  make	  
operations	   more	   sustainable.	   Both	   qualitative	   and	   quantitative	   data	   are	   used	   to	   create	   these	  
alternatives	  following	  the	  methodology	  detailed	  in	  the	  next	  chapter.

6 Methodology	  
The	  following	  section	  provides	  the	  methodology	  behind	  the	  data	  collection	  and	  the	  research.	  It	  is	  
explained	   how	   the	   data	   collection	   has	   been	   built	   and	   conducted	   with	   the	   support	   of	   the	  
literature.	  The	  method	  used	  consists	  in	  two	  steps:	  first	  face-­‐to-­‐face	  interviews	  with	  businesses	  
that	  operate	  in	  Santiago	  Centro	  and	  then	  a	  larger-­‐scale	  establishment-­‐based	  survey.	  
6.1 Interviews	  
It	   has	   been	   judged	   important	   to	   conduct	   face-­‐to-­‐face	   interviews	   with	   businesses	   in	   the	  
downtown	  area	  because	  very	  little	  literature	  if	  none	  exists	  on	  cities	  similar	  to	  Santiago.	  Indeed,	  
to	  build	  a	  survey	  that	  will	  be	  able	  to	  collect	  the	  data	  that	  matters,	  a	  first	  approach	  of	  supply	  
chains	  practices	  is	  needed.	  The	  aim	  is	  to	  be	  able	  to	  nudge	  the	  survey	  in	  the	  right	  direction	  in	  
terms	  of	  the	  elements	  to	  ask	  and	  their	  interpretation.	  
The	   businesses	   interviewed	   do	   not	   necessarily	   reflect	   the	   nature	   of	   the	   majority	   of	   shops	   in	  
downtown	  Santiago,	  most	  of	  them	  being	  small	  or	  medium	  sized	  shops.	  The	  reasons	  behind	  this	  
choice	  are	  multiple:	  
• 
Chain-­‐stores	  are	  easier	  to	  approach	  and	  more	  structured.	  
• 
More	   awareness	   about	   transportation	   problems	   (their	   operations	   deal	   with	   a	   greater	  
volume	  of	  goods).	  
• 
Possibility	  to	  interact	  with	  logistics	  managers	  and	  upper-­‐management	  positions.	  
• 
More	  advanced	  technologies	  of	  inventory	  control.	  
	  
Having	   an	   approach	   on	   how	   larger	   firms	   deal	   with	   the	   control	   of	   inventory	   and	   freight	  
transportation	  allows	  the	  possibility	  to	  transpose	  certain	  practices	  on	  other	  type	  of	  retail	  and	  
also	   on	   smaller	   operations.	   Besides,	   only	   stores	   that	   have	   operations	   in	   Downtown	   Santiago	  
have	  been	  selected	  in	  order	  to	  be	  consistent	  with	  the	  establishment	  targeted	  by	  the	  survey.	  
6.2 Survey

is	  done.	  Indeed,	  there	  is	  a	  gap	  between	  questions	  that	  seem	  relevant	  in	  theory	  and	  the	  reality	  on

6.3 Categorization	  and	  scope	  of	  study	  
When	   observing	   previous	   studies	   on	   urban	   freight,	   it	   is	   striking	   to	   see	   that	   very	   few	   studies	  
categorize	   supply	   chains	   to	   see	   which	   one	   is	   emitting	   the	   most	   freight.	   Comparatives	   studies	  
between	   different	   cities	   or	   different	   countries	   exist	   (Woudsma,	   2001)	   but	   lack	   greatly	   of	  
differentiation	  of	  different	  types	  of	  businesses	  that	  obviously	  operate	  differently.	  
The	   difficulty	   lies	   on	   the	   fact	   that	   many	   studies	   use	   broad	   categories	   such	   as:	   services,	   food,	  
wholesale	  trade,	  retail	  trade	  or	  manufacturing.	  	  The	  main	  categories	  that	  can	  be	  found	  in	  the	  
literature	  are:	  
• 
Wholesale	  trade	  
• 
Retail	  trade	  
• 
Accommodation	  and	  food	  services	  
• 
Manufacturing	  
	  
The	  above	  categories	  have	  been	  defined	  in	  regard	  of	  their	  predominance	  in	  urban	  freight	  studies	  
that	  have	  been	  done	  in	  the	  past.	  Among	  other	  studies,	  Masson	  (1971)	  collected	  data	  on	  urban	  
good	  movements	  in	  the	  cities	  of	  Metz	  and	  Aix-­‐en-­‐Provence	  (France).	  Turner	  and	  Blundell	  (1976)	  
examined	  the	  urban	  goods	  movement	  of	  the	  city	  of	  Chichester	  (England).	  	  
These	  studies	  and	  the	  context	  of	  the	  study	  zone	  allow	  finding	  out	  how	  the	  data	  to	  be	  collected	  
should	  be	  categorized.	  As	  mentioned	  in	  Chapter	  4,	  given	  the	  zone	  of	  study,	  it	  is	  more	  relevant	  to	  
focus	  on	  retailing	  activities.	  Indeed	  manufacturing	  sites	  are	  located	  outside	  in	  the	  wider	  urban	  
area	  and	  thus	  retail	  is	  the	  dominant	  activity.	  Another	  reason	  behind	  breaking	  down	  the	  zone	  
into	  business	  categories	  is	  to	  be	  able	  to	  do	  an	  analysis	  by	  type	  of	  freight	  generator	  and	  having	  
the	  possibility	  to	  find	  different	  solutions	  for	  different	  contexts.	  Furthermore,	  in	  order	  to	  keep	  the	  
study	  realistic,	  the	  number	  of	  categories	  must	  be	  kept	  to	  a	  reasonable	  number.	  The	  pilot	  survey

volume	  (Nestlé,	  Unilever,	  P&G	  etc.).	  
The	   DC	   commands	   the	   replenishment	   of	   every	   store	   through	   an	   integrated	   resupply	   system	  
(similar	  to	  an	  ERP)	  developed	  by	  Walmart	  in	  the	  US	  and	  then	  implemented	  in	  2013	  in	  Chile.	  
Thanks	   to	   that	   system	   connected	   to	   every	   Ekono	   store,	   most	   of	   the	   restocking	   has	   been	  
automated:	  80%	  of	  products	  are	  replenished	  automatically	  and	  the	  rest	  is	  “pushed”.	  Contrary	  to	  
Hites,	  Ekono	  sells	  perishable	  products,	  which	  brings	  a	  different	  problem	  to	  restocking.	  Indeed,	  
these	  products	  like	  meat,	  bread	  and	  fruits	  cannot	  be	  stored	  for	  a	  long	  time	  and	  thus	  no	  safety	  
stock	   can	   be	   built.	   That	   is	   the	   reason	   why	   the	   decision	   to	   replenish	   these	   products	   is	   made	  
manually	  rather	  than	  commanded	  by	  an	  ERP.	  	  
Besides,	  stores	  have	  in	  average	  only	  10%	  of	  their	  total	  area	  dedicated	  to	  storage,	  which	  means	  
that	  the	  inventory	  is	  mostly	  what	  is	  on	  the	  shelves.	  In	  contrast	  Hites	  had	  18%	  of	  storage	  area	  but	  
also	  much	  larger	  stores.	  
	  
==PAGE:46==
	  
	  
	  
	  
46	  
Having	  small	  stores	  with	  very	  little	  storage	  implies	  that	  the	  inventory	  must	  be	  replenished	  more	  
often.	  Level	  of	  service	  and	  low	  prices	  are	  two	  pillars	  of	  Walmart’s	  strategy,	  only	  3%	  of	  the	  time	  
shortages	  occur	  at	  Ekonos.	  This	  inventory	  strategy	  implies	  making	  concessions	  on	  the	  freight	  
generation	  to	  maintain	  products	  available	  at	  all	  time.	  
On	   average	   one	   store	   receives	   either	   6	   or	   12	   deliveries	   per	   week,	   from	   Monday	   to	   Saturday	  
(deliveries	  are	  not	  made	  on	  Sunday)	  with	  the	  following	  composition:	  
• 
3	  or	  6	  deliveries	  for	  “dry”	  products	  (i.e.	  not	  cold)	  
• 
3	  or	  6	  deliveries	  for	  cold	  products	  
The	  number	  of	  trucks	  depends	  on	  the	  location	  of	  the	  store;	  some	  have	  a	  larger	  base	  of	  customer.	  
Contrary	  to	  Hites	  where	  one	  truck	  was	  only	  visiting	  one	  store,	  the	  logistics	  provider	  contracted	  
by	  Walmart	  is	  performing	  multi-­‐drop	  delivery	  visiting	  more	  than	  one	  store	  per	  route.	  Combined	  
with	   the	   fact	   that	   Walmart	   prioritizes	   the	   optimization	   of	   transport	   costs,	   the	   utilization	   of	  
trucks	  is	  almost	  every	  time	  100%.	  
Night-­‐delivery	  is	  only	  made	  for	  stores	  located	  in	  an	  area	  where	  transport	  restrictions	  have	  been	  
enforced	   or	   with	   a	   particular	   structure	   (Escuela	   Militar	   for	   instance	   where	   the	   store	   is	   in	   a	  
subway	  station).	  
Finally,	  the	  inventory	  strategy	  of	  Ekono	  is	  dictated	  by	  the	  format	  of	  the	  store:	  small	  selling	  space,	  
almost	  no	  storage	  area	  and	  a	  mix	  between	  perishable	  and	  non-­‐perishable	  products.	  This	  directly	  
implies	  a	  great	  turnover	  of	  inventory	  and	  consequently	  frequent	  deliveries	  with	  a	  certain	  impact	  
on	  sustainability	  since	  these	  deliveries	  are	  done	  around	  8am	  and	  1pm.	  Congestion	  in	  the	  centre	  
certainly	  has	  an	  impact	  over	  logistics	  operations,	  causing	  delays	  up	  to	  24	  hours.	  Indeed,	  if	  an	  
order	  is	  not	  ready	  to	  leave	  the	  DC	  at	  the	  time	  it	  should	  then	  it	  will	  stand	  by	  waiting	  for	  the	  next	  
time	   window.	   Because	   the	   delivery	   area	   is	   subject	   to	   high	   level	   of	   congestions	   (and	   entrance	  
restrictions	  for	  freight),	  the	  truck	  will	  not	  be	  able	  to	  make	  its	  full	  route	  thus	  cost-­‐wise	  it	  is	  better	  
to	  wait	  for	  the	  next	  time	  period.	  
7.1.2.2 Sustainability	  concerns	  
Ever	  since	  the	  acquisition	  of	  D&S	  by	  Walmart,	  sustainability	  has	  been	  added	  as	  new	  focus	  to	  the	  
firm’s	  business	  strategy	  by	  implementing	  a	  set	  of	  good	  practices	  that	  showed	  results	  in	  other	  
countries.	  It	  seems	  the	  notion	  of	  sustainability	  is	  well	  understood	  and	  put	  into	  practice,	  with	  
several	  programs	  for	  gender	  equality,	  energy	  efficiency,	  improvement	  of	  working	  conditions	  and	  
package	  recycling.	  Walmart	  acknowledges	  that	  “a	  business	  can	  be	  efficient	  and	  profitable	  while	  
also	   being	   responsible	   with	   regard	   to	   people	   and	   the	   environment”,	   however	   this	   does	   not	  
precisely	  include	  transport	  so	  far.	  
==PAGE:47==
	  
	  
	  
	  
47	  
A	  first	  step	  has	  been	  made	  in	  term	  of	  inventory	  management	  with	  the	  introduction	  of	  Walmart’s	  
own	  ERP	  system	  automating	  the	  resupply	  of	  stores.	  Not	  only	  the	  automation	  of	  the	  resupply	  is	  
being	   enhanced	   but	   also	   the	   forecast	   of	   the	   demand	   (17	   weeks	   ahead	   based	   on	   a	   2	   months	  
mobile	  time	  window).	  These	  major	  improvements	  have	  been	  launched	  in	  2013	  and	  are	  still	  on	  
going	   with	   the	   final	   objective	   of	   having	   fewer	   products	   sent	   to	   the	   wrong	   stores.	   This	   means	  
reducing	  the	  replenishment	  errors	  but	  also	  reducing	  the	  error	  due	  to	  misinterpretation	  of	  the	  
actual	  demand.	  However,	  these	  technological	  decisions	  are	  first	  motivated	  by	  cost	  optimization	  
rather	  than	  being	  more	  sustainable,	  or	  at	  least	  Walmart	  does	  not	  communicate	  it	  as	  one	  of	  its	  
missions	  or	  CSR	  (Corportate	  Social	  Responsibility).	  Indeed	  the	  mission	  “save	  money	  for	  people	  so	  
they	  can	  live	  better”	  implies	  that	  Walmart	  needs	  itself	  to	  save	  money	  first	  in	  order	  to	  offer	  more	  
attractive	  prices.	  
At	  the	  level	  of	  Ekono,	  more	  sustainable	  inventory	  and	  distribution	  practices	  are	  unfortunately	  
difficult	  to	  consider	  for	  several	  reasons.	  First	  of	  all,	  the	  nature	  and	  format	  of	  the	  store	  as	  said	  
previously	   imposes	   the	   resupply	   strategy:	   very	   few	   in-­‐store	   inventory	   and	   high	   frequency	   of	  
deliveries.	  Furthermore,	  in	  opposition	  to	  Hites,	  Ekono	  sells	  perishable	  products	  meaning	  that	  
there	   is	   a	   whole	   different	   supply	   chain	   involved:	   the	   cold	   chain	   (different	   storage	   devices,	  
different	  transportation,	  constraints	  to	  respect).	  The	  priority	  is,	  as	  for	  a	  lot	  of	  businesses,	  to	  find	  
the	  equilibrium	  between	  minimizing	  the	  inventory	  (scarcity	  of	  space)	  and	  avoid	  shortages.	  And	  
that	  is	  achieved	  by	  frequent	  deliveries	  that	  replenish	  what	  is	  sold	  in	  stores.	  This	  matter	  implies	  
that	  it	  is	  almost	  impossible	  to	  reduce	  deliveries	  and	  gives	  little	  space	  to	  be	  more	  sustainable	  
without	  sacrificing	  the	  level	  of	  service	  and	  profits.	  Fewer	  deliveries	  would	  necessarily	  mean	  less	  
in-­‐store	  stock	  because	  storage	  is	  already	  utilized	  at	  its	  maximum,	  and	  consequently	  more	  stock-­‐
outs.	  
Because	   of	   the	   automation	   of	   the	   resupply	   process,	   most	   of	   what	   is	   replenished	   is	   what	   is	  
actually	  being	  sold	  in	  stores.	  If	  the	  process	  were	  only	  a	  “push”	  strategy	  then	  most	  probably	  the	  
demand	  would	  not	  be	  fulfilled	  at	  the	  same	  level	  as	  it	  is	  today	  (only	  3%	  of	  stock-­‐out)	  and	  thus	  
more	   freight	   would	   be	   generated	   to	   keep	   the	   same	   profit.	   In	   that	   sense,	   Walmart’s	   resupply	  
operations	  are	  sustainable	  even	  if	  it	  was	  not	  the	  primer	  aim	  of	  all	  the	  initiatives	  launched.	  
All	  of	  that	  being	  said,	  operations	  can	  always	  be	  improved	  and	  be	  more	  sustainable.	  However,	  
from	  all	  the	  solutions	  evoked	  for	  Hites,	  none	  of	  them	  seem	  to	  be	  relevant	  in	  that	  case:	  
• 
Night	  deliveries	  are	  not	  viable	  because	  Ekono	  is	  a	  cheap	  format	  of	  store	  employing	  very	  
little	  staff	  hence	  it	  would	  be	  too	  costly	  to	  receive	  products	  at	  night.	  
• 
Collaborative	   logistics:	   trucks	   are	   already	   performing	   multi-­‐drop	   deliveries	   and	   are	  
almost	  every	  time	  100%	  fully	  loaded.

To	  conclude,	  the	  margin	  for	  improvement	  might	  be	  thinner	  for	  Ekono	  than	  it	  could	  be	  for	  other	  
businesses.	   A	   high	   inventory	   and	   sales	   turnover	   imply	   that	   deliveries	   are	   made	   at	   a	   high	  
frequency	  to	  meet	  the	  demand.	  There	  is	  no	  actual	  reverse	  logistics	  process	  meaning	  that	  what’s	  
in	  stock	  only	  waits	  very	  little	  time	  in	  store	  before	  being	  sold,	  expired	  merchandises	  are	  disposed	  
as	  it	  is	  common	  for	  grocery	  retailers.	  
The	   margin	   for	   improvement	   might	   be	   more	   important	   for	   other	   format	   of	   Walmart	   Chile,	  
especially	  Lider	  that	  operates	  with	  stores	  16	  times	  bigger	  in	  area	  than	  Ekono.	  These	  stores	  have	  
consequently	   greater	   areas	   of	   storage	   and	   more	   opportunity	   to	   reduce	   deliveries	   by	  
replenishing	  stocks	  closer	  to	  the	  actual	  demand.	  
7.1.3 Conclusion	  
Several	  elements	  emerged	  from	  the	  above	  interviews	  that	  helped	  to	  shape	  a	  more	  appropriate	  
survey.	   First	   of	   all,	   there	   is	   a	   critical	   distinction	   to	   make	   between	   different	   categories	   of	  
businesses	  in	  term	  of	  freight	  generation	  and	  needs.	  The	  sale	  of	  perishable	  products	  implies	  a	  
total	   different	   strategy	   in	   terms	   of	   replenishment	   since	   products	   cannot	   be	   stored	   for	   an	  
indefinite	  period	  of	  time.	  Therefore,	  the	  fulfilment	  of	  demand	  can	  apparently	  only	  be	  made	  with	  
frequent	  deliveries.	  	  
Secondly	   the	   pattern	   of	   the	   demand	   is	   essential	   to	   evaluate	   freight	   transportation	   needs.	  
Demand	  with	  very	  low	  fluctuations	  will	  make	  it	  relatively	  to	  forecast	  transport	  needs	  and	  then	  
there	   will	   be	   little	   room	   for	   reducing	   deliveries	   since	   what	   is	   replenished	   is	   directly	   needed.	  
Other	  solutions	  have	  to	  be	  found	  to	  mitigate	  the	  impact	  of	  deliveries	  on	  the	  area.	  However,	  when	  
the	  demand	  shows	  higher	  variations	  and	  when	  it	  is	  a	  more	  complicated	  task	  to	  capture	  what	  
needs	  to	  be	  replenished,	  error	  on	  distribution	  are	  more	  likely	  to	  be	  made	  and	  then	  there	  is	  a	  
higher	  margin	  for	  improvement.	  
This	  also	  stresses	  out	  the	  importance	  of	  reverse	  logistics,	  what	  happens	  to	  products	  moved	  to	  
stores	   that	   do	   not	   find	   any	   customers	   (mismatch	   with	   demand,	   out	   of	   consumption	   date,	  
damaged	  products	  etc.)?	  They	  also	  generate	  freight	  in	  the	  sense	  that	  they	  are	  going	  to	  be	  moved	  
somewhere	  else	  to	  be	  disposed,	  placed	  back	  in	  a	  distribution	  centre,	  or	  moved	  to	  another	  sales	  
location.	  	  
Finally,	   there	   is	   a	   strong	   belief	   within	   the	   interviewees	   that	   part	   of	   the	   solution	   to	   reduce	  
congestion	  and	  improve	  logistics	  operations	  in	  the	  centre	  would	  rely	  on	  technology.	  Softwares	  
to	  manage	  inventory	  for	  SMEs	  (even	  the	  smaller	  ones)	  are	  believed	  to	  allow	  small	  and	  medium	  
businesses	  to	  automate	  their	  replenishment	  orders	  and	  synchronize	  better	  with	  their	  numerous	  
suppliers.	  	  
==PAGE:49==
	  
	  
	  
	  
49	  
That	   is	   the	   reason	   why	   it	   is	   important	   in	   the	   survey	   to	   assess	   the	   proportion	   of	   businesses	  
operating	   with	   softwares	   and	   assess	   if	   there	   is	   a	   will	   to	   change	   their	   current	   practices.	   The	  
margin	  for	  improvement	  is	  assumed	  to	  be	  found	  in	  SMEs	  because	  of	  two	  important	  factors:	  their	  
great	   density	   in	   the	   area	   and	   their	   disorganized	   practices	   of	   inventory	   control	   and

Although	  it	  has	  been	  stated	  different	  inventory	  strategies	  in	  Chapter	  3,	  the	  extent	  to	  which	  those

the	   inventory	   according	   to	   what	   is	   being	   sold.	   The	   quantity	   per	   order	   is	   constrained	   by	   the

7.2.2.3 Freight	  data	  and	  decisions	  
The	  variety	  of	  the	  different	  businesses	  in	  the	  study	  zone	  leads	  to	  a	  great	  differentiation	  in	  how

7.2.3.3 Drugstores	  
Drugstores	   operate	   in	   a	   very	   singular	   environment	   that	   needs	   to	   be	   isolated	   because	   of	   the

seem	   like	   an	   option	   that	   could	   be	   imagined.	   There	   is	   a	   great	   need	   of	   trust	   of	   every	   partner

several	   type	   of	   drugs,	   forecasting	   the	   demand	   is	   complicated,	   those	   being	   often	   ordered	  
following	  a	  customer’s	  demand.	  Therefore,	  increasing	  the	  order	  size	  (A)	  in	  order	  to	  reduce	  the	  
frequency	  of	  replenishments	  might	  not	  be	  relevant	  since	  the	  margin	  of	  extra	  products	  that	  can	  
be	  stored	  is	  thin.	  The	  increase	  in	  order	  size	  has	  to	  be	  sufficient	  in	  order	  to	  reduce	  the	  frequency	  
of	   replenishment	   but	   also	   needs	   to	   be	   supported	   by	   enough	   storage	   space.	   Premises	   usually	  
being	   rather	   small,	   this	   alternative	   has	   very	   little	   chance	   to	   be	   effective	   although	   managers	  
consent	  to	  it.	  Additionally,	  drugstores	  often	  provide	  customers	  with	  specialized	  products	  with	  a

7.3 Perspectives	  
Whatever	  alternative	  is	  considered,	  the	  percentage	  of	  establishments	  willing	  to	  implement	  them	  
is	  usually	  very	  low	  but	  not	  negligible	  (29%	  average	  approval	  for	  any	  alternative,	  35%	  without	  
the	  municipal	  centre	  alternative).	  
This	   observation	   fits	   with	   the	   fact	   that	   the	   overwhelming	   majority	   of	   retailers	   do	   not	  
acknowledge	   their	   participation	   and	   negative	   impact	   on	   last-­‐mile	   deliveries	   operations.	   They	  
also	   do	   not	   often	   acknowledge	   the	   benefits	   that	   could	   be	   achieved	   by	   improving	   the	  
sustainability	  of	  last-­‐mile	  operations.	  It	  is	  straightforward	  to	  see	  that	  every	  alternative	  results	  in	  
either	  a	  decrease	  of	  traffic,	  less	  emissions	  or	  both	  and	  thus	  lessens	  the	  impact	  on	  the	  People	  and	  
Planet	   sides	   of	   the	   triple	   bottom	   line.	   However,	   seeing	   an	   improvement	   in	   profit	   is	   not	   so	  
obvious	  and	  might	  be	  the	  reason	  why	  establishments	  are	  so	  reluctant	  to	  take	  action.	  The	  focus	  of	  
this	  study	  has	  been	  to	  find	  solutions	  that	  improve	  every	  three	  areas	  of	  sustainability	  or	  where	  
one	  is	  better	  off	  without	  any	  other	  worse	  off.	  Indeed	  several	  measures	  (Night	  delivery,	  greener	  
trucks,	   municipal	   centre)	   imply	   an	   increase	   in	   distribution	   costs.	   The	   argument	   that	   more	  
sustainable	  operations	  of	  businesses	  are	  a	  substantial	  benefit	  for	  brands	  and	  stores	  is	  valid	  but	  
the	  gain	  of	  such	  a	  Public	  Relations	  strategy	  might	  only	  be	  seen	  on	  the	  long	  term	  and	  could	  not	  be	  
sufficient	   to	   offset	   extra	   costs.	   Besides,	   Santiago	   Centro	   is	   a	   zone	   with	   customers	   that	   have	   a	  
relatively	  low	  economic	  power	  meaning	  that	  there	  is	  very	  little	  evidence	  that	  business	  owners	  
would	   be	   able	   to	   pass	   the	   cost	   increase	   in	   the	   price	   offered	   to	   the	   customer.	   The	   fact	   that	  
customers	  are	  unlikely	  to	  choose	  sustainable	  products	  over	  affordable	  prices	  advocates	  for	  the	  
necessity	  of	  a	  market	  intervention.	  	  
	  
There	  is	  certainly	  a	  need	  for	  public	  intervention	  to	  sponsor	  initiatives	  with	  financial	  incentives	  
such	   as	   night	   delivery	   as	   it	   has	   been	   the	   case	   in	   New	   York	   City.	   Public	   policies	   might	   also	  
encourage	   establishments	   to	   resort	   to	   measures	   like	   night	   delivery	   because	   these	   measures	  
require	   a	   minimum	   degree	   of	   participation.	   Indeed	   suppliers	   mostly	   performing	   multi-­‐drop	  
delivery	  will	  not	  switch	  their	  operations	  to	  night	  delivery	  for	  a	  minority	  of	  customers	  on	  their	  
delivery	  route.	  Under	  the	  right	  incentives,	  a	  significant	  part	  of	  the	  traffic	  could	  be	  shifted	  outside	  
of	  the	  peak	  hours.

with	  a	  small	  participation.

prices	  will	  decrease	  over	  time	  and	  their	  use	  will	  become	  mainstream.	  	  
For	   now,	   the	   easiest	   solution	   to	   decrease	   traffic	   would	   be	   the	   implementation	   of	   inventory	  
control	  software	  to	  ease	  the	  replenishment	  decision	  of	  store	  managers.	  It	  is	  deemed	  to	  be	  easiest	  
since	   this	   only	   represents	   a	   small	   upfront	   (or	   licensing	   fees)	   investment	   compared	   to	   other	  
measures	  that	  have	  a	  continuous	  effect	  on	  variable	  costs.	  The	  other	  measures	  are	  more	  likely	  to	  
work	   best	   for	   certain	   categories	   as	   it	   has	   been	   discussed	   earlier,	   but	   could	   theoretically	   be	  
implemented	  in	  every	  case.	  A	  case-­‐by-­‐case	  analysis	  of	  every	  business	  would	  be	  needed	  to	  assess	  
the	  possibility	  of	  implementation	  of	  each	  solution,	  but	  considering	  the	  number	  of	  businesses	  in	  
the	  area	  it	  would	  be	  not	  be	  doable.	  
Even	   if	   alternatives	   are	   theoretically	   applicable	   for	   every	   business	   type,	   they	   do	   come	   with	  
constraints	   that	   are	   difficult	   to	   overcome.	   Increasing	   order	   size	   (A)	   can	   only	   be	   done	   within	  
certain	  conditions:	  
• 
The	  new	  number	  of	  trucks	  needed	  is	  inferior	  to	  the	  number	  of	  trucks	  currently	  needed.	  
• 
There	  is	  sufficient	  storage	  space	  to	  carry	  the	  increase	  in	  inventory.	  
• 
Savings	  
achieved	  
by	  
batch	  
ordering	  
are	  
not	  
offset	  
by	  
inventory	  
costs.	  
	  
Collaborative	  logistics	  (C)	  necessitates	  a	  high	  degree	  of	  synchronization	  in	  between	  businesses	  
that	   are	   often	   competing	   with	   each	   other.	   In	   that	   matter,	   it	   does	   require	   a	   great	   degree	   of	  
participation	   to	   convince	   carriers	   to	   accommodate	   their	   delivery	   route.	   Moreover,	   logistics	  
providers	  with	  such	  services	  are	  not	  yet	  to	  be	  found	  in	  Chile.	  
The	  use	  of	  less	  polluting	  vehicles	  (B)	  is	  difficult	  to	  enforce	  by	  retailers	  that	  are	  neither	  owning	  
their	  vehicles	  nor	  using	  a	  single	  carrier.	  In	  that	  case,	  it	  is	  the	  responsibility	  of	  the	  supplier	  to	  
make	  adjustments	  to	  their	  fleet,	  usually	  coming	  with	  an	  important	  cost.	  It	  is	  then	  the	  extent	  to	  
which	   carriers	   will	   be	   able	   to	   pass	   these	   extra	   costs	   to	   retailers	   that	   will	   determine	   the	  
profitability	   of	   this	   initiative.	   Most	   likely,	   a	   public	   incentive	   will	   be	   needed	   to	   entice	   the	  
implementation	  of	  this	  measure.	  
Storing	  extra	  goods	  in	  a	  municipal	  centre	  (D)	  would	  also	  remove	  the	  constraints	  of	  space	  and	  
inventory	  cost	  of	  alternative	  A.	  
==PAGE:73==
	  
	  
	  
	  
73	  
However	   its	   implementation	   depends	   on	   the	   distance	   from	   the	   establishments	   to	   the	   centre.	  
Indeed,	  this	  distance	  will	  determine	  the	  type	  of	  vehicle	  needed	  to	  transfer	  goods	  from	  the	  centre	  
to	  the	  store.	  	  
Very	  small	  vehicles	  such	  as	  the	  ones	  used	  by	  Coca-­‐Cola	  are	  a	  plausible	  option.	  Moreover,	  the	  use	  
of	  this	  centre	  is	  constrained	  by	  the	  type	  of	  goods:	  non-­‐perishable	  and	  common	  goods	  (no	  drugs	  
nor	  clothes).	  
Finally,	  night	  delivery	  is	  in	  theory	  applicable	  for	  all	  types	  of	  business	  but	  differs	  in	  the	  easiness	  
of	  its	  implementation.	  When	  dealing	  with	  establishments	  using	  their	  own	  vehicles	  or	  a	  single	  
carrier,	  assisted	  night	  delivery	  is	  achievable	  with	  the	  right	  public	  incentives	  in	  order	  to	  offset	  the	  
cost	  of	  receivers.	  Unassisted	  night	  delivery	  is	  in	  practice	  doable	  but	  requires	  a	  great	  level	  of	  trust	  
between	  the	  shipper	  and	  the	  receiver	  and	  therefore	  plenty	  of	  businesses	  will	  not	  commit	  to	  it.	  In	  
the	   case	   of	   establishments	   receiving	   from	   various	   suppliers,	   it	   would	   be	   needed	   that	   every	  
supplier	   comply	   to	   night	   delivery	   and	   that	   makes	   it	   difficult	   for	   retailers	   dealing	   with	   30	  
different	   suppliers	   in	   one	   week.	   Only	   unassisted	   deliveries	   would	   remove	   this	   constraint	   of	  
having	   staff	   present	   outside	   of	   business	   hours,	   but	   is	   very	   unlikely	   to	   be	   adopted	   by	   enough	  
establishments.	   On	   the	   supplier	   side,	   it	   is	   also	   needed	   that	   every	   business	   on	   a	   particular	  
delivery	   route	   switches	   to	   night	   delivery	   to	   be	   cost	   efficient.	   The	   positive	   side	   is	   that	   night	  
delivery	  is	  already	  implemented	  for	  several	  businesses	  that	  cannot	  receive	  goods	  during	  the	  day	  
because	  of	  public	  regulations.	  One	  solution	  for	  small	  independent	  businesses	  could	  also	  be	  to	  
explore	  the	  possibility	  of	  VMI	  systems	  as	  seen	  in	  Chapter	  2.1.2.	  By	  doing	  so,	  small	  retailers	  could	  
benefit	  from	  the	  technical	  expertise	  in	  demand	  forecasting	  and	  inventory	  control	  of	  suppliers.	  
However,	  like	  these	  types	  of	  agreements	  tend	  to	  be	  complex,	  there	  is	  no	  certainty	  that	  suppliers	  
would	  be	  considering	  this	  possibility	  and	  that	  it	  would	  indeed	  decrease	  deliveries.

The	  first	  observation	  that	  can	  be	  made	  has	  to	  do	  with	  the	  size	  of	  the	  sample.	  At	  first	  glance	  it	  
appears	  to	  be	  very	  small	  and	  with	  a	  high	  variability	  in	  the	  results.	  However	  this	  is	  not	  cause	  for

the	  surveyor	  has	  the	  objectives	  in	  mind	  and	  knows	  what	  the	  data	  will	  be	  used	  for.

Allen	   et	   al.	   (2012).	   When	   possible,	   most	   respondents	   were	   responsible	   for	   managing	   the

information	  on	  sustainability	  matters	  when	  stores	  were	  not	  part	  of	  chain	  because	  the	  decision	  to

these	  are	  more	  likely	  to	  respond	  positively	  to	  the	  survey.	  Indeed,	  these	  stores	  usually	  have	  more

to

a

could	  not	  be	  increased	  to	  a	  larger	  number.	  
8.2 
Transferability	  of	  the	  data	  
The	  sample	  size	  being	  relatively	  low,	  it	  implies	  that	  the	  conclusions	  cannot	  be	  extrapolated	  to	  
another	  case	  than	  the	  one	  of	  Santiago	  Centro.	  There	  are	  significant	  differences	  between	  cities	  
(even	  in	  the	  same	  continent),	  advocating	  for	  the	  fact	  that	  policies	  should	  be	  evaluated	  looking	  at	  
data	  from	  the	  city	  itself	  rather	  than	  using	  comparative	  studies.	  Besides,	  the	  results	  apply	  only	  to	  
the	  zone	  of	  study	  (Santiago’s	  micro	  centre)	  and	  might	  differ	  for	  different	  part	  of	  the	  same	  city.

every	   element	   except	   the	   percentage	   of	   chain	   store.	   This	   difference	   has	   actually	   a	   lot	   of	  
consequences	  in	  the	  evaluation	  of	  alternatives	  since	  it	  changes	  their	  likeliness	  to	  be	  suitable	  for	  
implementation.

9 Conclusion	  
9.1 Contributions	  and	  findings	  
	  
Sustainability	   of	   last-­‐mile	   delivery	   operations	   seems	   to	   be	   still	   far	   away	   to	   be	   reached	   in	   the	  
micro	  centre	  of	  Santiago	  de	  Chile.	  The	  study	  zone	  is	  marked	  by	  high	  level	  of	  congestion	  and	  a	  
great	   density	   of	   businesses	   whose	   main	   inventory	   strategy	   is	   to	   replenish	   what	   is	   sold,	  
disregarding	  the	  evolution	  of	  the	  demand.	  As	  suggested	  by	  logistics	  managers	  of	  large	  retailers,	  
the	   use	   of	   more	   elaborate	   inventory	   control	   software	   could	   be	   key	   in	   order	   to	   reduce	   the	  
number	  of	  deliveries.	  If	  Hites	  estimates	  the	  potential	  decrease	  achievable	  by	  a	  better	  inventory	  
control	  in	  weekly	  deliveries	  of	  20%,	  the	  survey	  actually	  showed	  that	  companies	  using	  software	  
were	  generating	  in	  average	  36%	  less	  deliveries	  than	  those	  who	  did	  not.	  This	  solution	  may	  be	  the	  
cheapest	  solution	  available	  to	  the	  greatest	  number	  of	  businesses	  and	  the	  easiest	  to	  implement.	  
However,	  the	  upcoming	  public	  measures	  will	  force	  both	  retailers	  and	  suppliers	  to	  review	  and	  
adapt	  their	  inventory	  strategy	  and	  distribution	  process	  anyway.	  If	  establishments	  might	  mainly	  
be	  reluctant	  to	  change	  the	  status	  quo,	  the	  restriction	  of	  access	  to	  the	  main	  streets	  in	  the	  micro	  
centre	  might	  disrupt	  their	  operations.	  Deliveries	  will	  be	  made	  either	  more	  difficult	  because	  the	  
vehicle	  would	  park	  further	  away	  from	  the	  establishment	  or	  at	  a	  different	  time	  when	  access	  is	  
permitted.	   This	   regulation	   advocates	   for	   a	   more	   frequent	   use	   of	   night	   delivery,	   especially	   for	  
food	  retailers	  that	  have	  to	  deal	  with	  strict	  time	  windows	  where	  they	  sell	  most	  of	  their	  products.	  
Although	   it	   is	   rather	   understandable	   that	   small	   independent	   stores	   do	   not	   necessarily	   place	  
sustainability	  as	  their	  top	  priority,	  it	  is	  less	  for	  larger	  corporations.	  Indeed,	  the	  largest	  retailers	  
in	   Chile	   have	   not	   incorporated	   yet	   any	   plan	   to	   reduce	   the	   number	   of	   deliveries	   or	   lessen	   the	  
impact	  of	  those	  on	  sustainability.	  With	  their	  financial	  power	  and	  the	  importance	  they	  give	  to	  
public	   relations	   strategy,	   they	   could	   lead	   the	   way	   for	   smaller	   businesses	   towards	   sustainable	  
operations.	   With	   29%	   of	   acceptance	   to	   the	   alternatives	   presented,	   it	   becomes	   clear	   that	   the	  
analysis	   cannot	   only	   be	   based	   on	   whether	   establishments	   are	   willing	   or	   not	   to	   implement	   a	  
given	  solution.	  Indeed	  the	  feasibility	  of	  each	  solution	  relies	  more	  on	  the	  characteristics	  of	  each	  
retail	  category,	  the	  type	  of	  products	  sold	  and	  the	  structure	  of	  the	  distribution	  process.	  	  
	  
The	   analysis	   also	   showed	   that,	   for	   each	   alternative	   there	   are	   critical	   parameters	   such	   as	   the	  
number	  of	  distinct	  suppliers,	  the	  type	  of	  distribution	  system	  and	  more	  that	  directly	  influence	  the	  
feasibility	  of	  the	  alternative.	  Even	  when	  feasible,	  this	  is	  often	  not	  sufficient	  to	  really	  consider	  the	  
actual	  implementation	  of	  these	  alternatives.	  	  
==PAGE:78==
	  
	  
	  
	  
78	  
Indeed,	  even	  if	  they	  result	  in	  an	  improvement	  in	  the	  respect	  of	  the	  environment	  and	  the	  social	  
welfare,	  they	  might	  result	  in	  a	  sharp	  increase	  of	  inventory	  and	  distribution	  costs,	  which	  is	  not	  by	  
definition	  fully	  sustainable.	  	  
It	  is	  difficult	  to	  imagine	  a	  scenario	  where	  the	  increase	  in	  distribution	  does	  not	  result	  directly	  in	  a	  
price	  increase,	  posing	  the	  question	  of	  who	  is	  to	  bear	  the	  cost	  of	  sustainability.	  Customers	  with	  
very	   low	   economic	   power	   are	   very	   unlikely	   to	   switch	   their	   purchasing	   preferences	   to	   more	  
sustainable	  but	  more	  costly	  products.	  The	  same	  goes	  for	  small	  and	  medium	  sized	  establishments	  
that	   might	   not	   have	   the	   financial	   power	   to	   absorb	   a	   cost	   increase.	   Therefore	   a	   market	  
intervention	  seems	  necessary	  to	  incentivize	  the	  use	  of	  more	  sustainable	  strategies.	  	  The	  extent	  
to	   which	   public	   institution	   will	   be	   able	   to	   provide	   financial	   incentives,	   rather	   than	   putting	  
restrictions	  on	  freight,	  will	  determine	  the	  level	  of	  participation	  for	  each	  alternative.	  As	  shown	  in	  
the	  analysis,	  retailers	  have	  to	  be	  differentiated	  depending	  on	  their	  type	  of	  activity	  to	  propose	  
relevant	   alternatives.	   Consequently,	   priority	   of	   action	   should	   be	   given	   to	   the	   categories	  
responsible	  for	  the	  most	  part	  of	  freight	  traffic	  such	  as	  grocery	  and	  food	  retailers.	  Even	  a	  small	  
decrease	  in	  freight	  traffic	  could	  result	  in	  significant	  improvements	  for	  the	  environment	  and	  the	  
life	  quality	  of	  inhabitants	  opening	  the	  way	  for	  supply	  chains	  to	  make	  sustainability	  their	  first	  
concern.	  
	  
9.2 Future	  research	  and	  extensions	  
	  
Last-­‐mile	  delivery	  operations	  are	  a	  vast	  subject	  for	  which	  many	  parameters	  have	  to	  be	  taken	  
into	  account	  as	  well	  as	  many	  stakeholders.	  The	  aim	  for	  sustainability	  is	  constantly	  evolving	  as	  
new	  challenges	  and	  new	  forms	  of	  businesses	  arise.	  The	  boom	  of	  e-­‐commerce	  has	  added	  up	  a	  
new	   challenge:	   Internet	   sales	   rose	   by	   56%	   between	   2011	   and	   2013	   (Santiago	   Chamber	   of	  
Commerce)	   thus	   trucks	   performing	   home-­‐delivery	   operations	   have	   also	   to	   be	   considered	   in	  
freight	  analysis.	  The	  development	  of	  3D-­‐printing	  technologies	  could	  also	  disrupt	  supply	  chains,	  
products	   could	   be	   manufactured	   in	   the	   same	   place	   as	   they	   are	   sold	   eliminating	   the	   need	   for	  
transport.	  	  	  
The	  analysis	  has	  been	  carried	  out	  dividing	  retailers	  between	  business	  categories	  but	  there	  are	  
also	  other	  angles	  of	  analysis.	  As	  it	  has	  been	  used	  in	  several	  urban	  freight	  studies,	  it	  would	  be	  
interesting	  to	  classify	  businesses	  depending	  on	  the	  ratio	  number	  of	  weekly	  deliveries/number	  of	  
employees.	   By	   doing	   so,	   the	   analysis	   could	   focus	   on	   small	   businesses	   that	   are	   generating	   the	  
most	  freight	  and	  for	  which	  the	  margin	  for	  improvement	  is	  believed	  to	  be	  greater.	  
	  Moreover,	  as	  it	  has	  been	  stated	  following	  the	  analysis,	  there	  is	  a	  critical	  need	  to	  determine	  what	  
model	  of	  incentives	  could	  fit	  the	  situation	  in	  Santiago	  de	  Chile.
