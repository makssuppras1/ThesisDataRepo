Abstract
Checking compliance with fire regulations and Universal Design (UD) principles can be a time­
consuming process, particularly when it requires multiple rounds of coordination among experts.
Previous research has shown that Automated Compliance Checking (ACC) methods can signi­
ficantly improve efficiency in regulatory verification. However, the full translation of Danish fire
regulations into computable rules remains incomplete, and the automation of UD compliance
checks is still underexplored.
This study analysed the feasibility and effectiveness of using the RASE methodology to convert
texts written in natural language into computable rules, and develop a tool for checking com­
pliance with regulatory requirements and inclusive design goals. The main focus was on fire
safety requirements from BR18 and principles from Universal Design guidelines. The RASE
methodology (Requirement, Applicability, Selection, and Exception) proved effective for trans­
lating fire regulations into computable rules, although proper training is essential for correct
application. In contrast, applying this method to UD principles presented greater challenges as
these guidelines are often abstract and not expressed as verifiable rules. The findings indicate
that automating UD compliance checks requires formalising these principles into more concrete,
measurable requirements.
The outcome of this study supports the existing research on ACC using the RASE methodology
and the development of a tool that automatically verifies compliance with fire safety and UD
principles, contributing to the design of safer and more inclusive buildings.

Introduction
The Danish building regulation, known as Bygningsreglement (BR18), includes several rules
related to fire safety. It states that buildings in the event of a fire ”must have satisfactory safety
for people” (§82) and that design should ensure ”people can be safely evacuated and rescued”
(§91). Since individuals of different ages, sizes, abilities, and disabilities require various means
to evacuate in the event of a fire, it is essential to incorporate inclusivity aspects during the
design process. [1]
Universal Design (UD) is a design approach that emphasises the importance of inclusivity, aim­
ing to help designers ensure that the widest range of users can access and utilise built environ­
ments. By integrating UD principles with fire safety regulations, it becomes possible to create
buildings that are safe for all users. [2]
The current method of checking compliance with fire regulations is time­consuming and requires
multiple discussions between architects and fire engineers. While it is not possible to eliminate
fire engineers from the process, implementing automated compliance checks could significantly
enhance productivity. [3]
There has been a growing interest in automating regulatory compliance checks within the Ar­
chitecture, Engineering and Construction (AEC) industry. The development of such tools has
become feasible primarily due to the widespread adoption of Building Information Modelling
(BIM). This technology enables engineers to enrich digital building models with the necessary
information for compliance checks, utilising Industry Foundation Classes (IFC) standards.
A notable initiative is an ongoing project in Denmark called FireBIM, which aims to apply the
RASE methodology to Danish fire regulations. The RASE methodology facilitates the translation
of regulatory text written in natural language into computable rules. It involves tagging the text
with four operators: Requirement, Applicability, Selection, and Exception, and connecting them
with basic logic operators (AND, OR, NOT AND, NOT OR). This forms the basis for coding
automated compliance checks. [4]
As part of the FireBIM project, fire experts are utilising this approach to tag Danish fire regula­
tions. This project has informed the approach taken in this thesis, which also adopts the RASE
methodology to formalise regulatory and inclusive design requirements.
Previous research has demonstrated that Automated Compliance Checking (ACC) can be an
effective method for conducting regulatory compliance assessments in areas such as fire safety
and accessibility. Hjelseth and Nisbet [5] investigated the use of the RASE methodology to
represent specification rules. Similarly, Ismail et al. [6] conducted a study focused on translating
fire safety clauses in Malaysian regulations using the RASE methodology. De Mendonça et al.
[7] examined automated checks for accessibility rules, concentrating on Brazilian accessibility
standards that encompass a small portion of UD principles. In another study, Haque et al. [8]
addressed accessibility requirements in the design of healthcare facilities.
However, the Danish fire regulations have not yet been fully translated into computable rules.
Additionally, the feasibility of creating automated checks for UD principles requires further in­
vestigation.
To address these gaps, the following research questions have been formulated:
1. Is it feasible to utilise the RASE methodology to formalise and implement a compli­
ance check for fire escape corridor width?

2. Can the RASE methodology be used to formalise and implement a compliance
check for Universal Design principles?
3. Can automated compliance checks enhance the effectiveness of the design pro­
cess?
To answer these questions, various methodologies were employed in this research. The RASE
methodology was used to translate text written in natural language into computable rules. The
texts examined include the fire regulations from the BR18 regarding the width of escape routes,
specifically corridors, as well as the connected suggestions for inclusive corridor designs under
the UD framework. To assess the success of this translation, a tool was developed to perform
automated checks on IFC models. To ensure that the process and outcomes are realistic, inter­
views were conducted with experts in fire safety, UD, and architecture.
The results of this study can contribute to the existing research on ACC and the RASE method­
ology. Ultimately, the hope is that this work will advance the development of digital tools that
assist designers in creating safer and more accessible buildings.

Background
This chapter provides the necessary background information to support the understanding of
the thesis. It also presents the problem definition that forms the foundation of the research.
2.1
Automated Compliance Checking
Traditionally, compliance checks for building regulations have been conducted manually. This
process is labour­intensive, time­consuming, and susceptible to human error. Despite the po­
tential benefits, the development of ACC methods has progressed slowly. This delay is largely
due to a lack of trust among professionals in the AEC sectors toward automated processes. [9]
Recent research shows an increasing interest in adopting automation within the AEC sectors.
Professionals in these fields recognise automation as both feasible and desirable. Although
continued human oversight is still considered essential, automated processes have the potential
to enhance productivity in both design and construction. [3]
The ongoing need for human involvement arises from the complex regulations that may lead to
misunderstandings or oversight in ACC systems. Many building regulations include qualitative
aspects that automated systems find difficult to interpret accurately. There is also intentional
and unintentional ambiguity in the rules, which can prevent the use of ACC systems. [10]
The invention and growing spread of BIM are key aspects in the development of any ACC tool.
The use of BIM enables the creation of a detailed digital model equipped with the necessary
information to perform automated compliance checks. The most commonly used standard in
the BIM environment is the IFC, which is ”a set of standardised, digital descriptions of the built
asset industry. It is an open, global standard published under a Creative Commons license,
and as ISO 16739.” [11] Previous research explored methods to develop a general­purpose,
open­source checking framework for analysing IFC­based BIM models. [12]
Denmark was one of the first countries in Europe to mandate the use of BIM in 2007. [13,
14] As a result, it is reasonable to assume that BIM is now widespread in Denmark and that a
tool compatible with IFC formats will have the potential to be commonly used across the AEC
sectors.
2.2
Industry Foundation Classes (IFC)
Arayici et al. [15] highlight the importance of performance­based design in the AEC industry
through integrated BIM use. A key challenge is integrating information from various domains,
which are documented with different tools. There are two approaches to design integration:
1. Standalone Approach: All stakeholders collaborate on a common platform while using
different software to create data readable by others. However, this approach is limited, as no
single platform can support all the data generated throughout a project’s lifecycle.
2. Integrated Approach: This uses translator tools to convert proprietary formats into open
data, promoting collaboration among stakeholders using various software.
Interoperability is crucial, especially in the AEC sector, where the shift towards BIM has in­
creased the need for collaboration among diverse stakeholders throughout the building lifecycle.
To address interoperability challenges, BuildingSMART has created two key initiatives: Informa­
tion Delivery Manual (IDM) and Model View Definition (MVD). The IDM outlines interoperability

2.3
RASE Methodology
One of the biggest challenges in creating ACC systems is translating written regulations into a
format that can be utilised within a programming language.
Regulations can be categorised into five fundamental concepts that are familiar to regulatory
experts and designers. The most general of these is the ”check”, which refers to a specific sec­
tion of the regulation. A design can only comply with the regulation if every ”check” is satisfied.
[4] The four other categories are all parts of the ”check”:
• ”Requirement”: These are the actual statements or rules in the regulation that must be
followed. They are often associated with ”shall” or ”must” in the sentence. At least one is
needed in every check.
• ”Applicability”: This is the condition determining when and where the rule applies or what
building item the rule applies to. There are usually one or more in every check.
• ”Selection”: These are alternative options that the rule can apply to. If there is a selection
in a check, there are always at least two options. If the checked case satisfies either
selection, the rule applies to it.
• ”Exception”: This outlines specific conditions or cases where the check does not apply. It
describes scenarios in which compliance with a general rule is not necessary or is altered.
These are contrary to applicabilities.
The name of the method comes from the first letters of these four terms: RASE.
Actual regulations usually contain multiple requirements, applicabilities, selections and excep­
tions. Understanding how these combine is an important part of using the RASE methodology.

To utilise this method as a mark­up language, users should highlight the phrases in the regulation
that can be classified as requirements, applicability, selections, or exceptions. The mark­up is
visually represented through a colour system, with each category assigned a specific colour as

The colours make it easier for the user to see how the rule is structured.
The next step is to assign attributes to the marked phrases. These can be:
• object: ideally coming from a restricted dictionary of defined terms (e.g.: space, ramp)
• property: ideally coming from a restricted dictionary of defined terms (e.g.: type, width)
• comparison (e.g.: greater, lesser, equal, includes)

2.4
Fire Regulations
Fire regulations are essential in the building industry for reducing the risks associated with fire
incidents and for safeguarding human lives and property. As Yilmaz states, ”Most of the fire
safety measures first intend to prevent fire from occurring and if it occurs, the goal is to prevent
it from spreading and to intervene place. If a fire uncontrollably spreads over a wide area, it can
be a structural threat to the overall safety of the building and the occupants.” [19]
Fire safety encompasses a complex set of measures, including the arrangement of combustible
materials, the design and materials used for facades, temperature distribution, heat release,
smoke composition, air movement and diffusion, as well as interior ventilation. As a result,
fire regulations form a comprehensive system of rules, and it is essential to involve fire safety
experts in the building design process to ensure compliance with all regulations.
Residential fires can cause death, and several factors contribute to this risk. One major issue
is the extended detection time, often because individuals are asleep or under the influence of
substances, which makes them unaware of the danger. Moreover, individuals with visual or
hearing difficulties face a higher risk of not detecting a fire. In addition, people with mobility
impairments may struggle to evacuate their apartments, increasing their probability of suffering
fatal injuries in a fire. Therefore, implementing Universal Design principles in fire regulations
would benefit people living with disabilities in the event of a fire. [20]
In Denmark, the Danish building regulations, BR18, specify the requirements of the Building Act
and contain the detailed requirements that all construction work must meet. The rules for fire
regulation can be found in Chapter 5 of BR18. [1]
2.5
Universal Design
In the second half of the 20th century, a movement began with the goal of making the built en­
vironment more accessible. In the United States, various accessibility laws have been created
to ensure that buildings are designed in a way that does not discriminate against people with
disabilities.
Ron Mace, Ruth Hall Lusher, and others recognised that merely complying with the new legal re­
quirements was not enough. They proposed a different approach, which they named ”universal
design”. They argued that if designers aimed to enhance functionality for a broad range of peo­
ple, the design of products and environments usable by individuals with or without disabilities
would become the norm.
The definition of UD is:
”The design of products and environments to be usable by all people, to the greatest extent
possible, without the need for adaptation or specialised design.” [21]
UD is not a set of strict rules; rather, it embodies a philosophy and a mindset for approaching
design. [22] Instead of adhering to specific regulations, such as fire safety codes, UD focuses
on principles and goals that guide the design process. There are various ways to create a more

2.5.1
Principles of Universal Design
The seven principles of UD were established by a group of architects, product designers, en­
gineers and environmental design researchers in 1997. They were developed to ”evaluate
existing designs, guide the design process and educate both designers and consumers about
the characteristics of more usable products and environments.” [2] The principles are defined
as follows [2]:
1. Equitable Use: The design is useful and marketable to people with diverse abilities.
2. Flexibility in Use: The design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and
abilities.
3. Simple and Intuitive Use: The use of the design is easy to understand, regardless of the
user’s experience, knowledge, language skills, or current concentration level.
4. Perceptible Information: The design communicates necessary information effectively to
the user, regardless of ambient conditions or the user’s sensory abilities.
5. Tolerance for Error: The design minimises hazards and the adverse consequences of
accidental or unintended actions.
6. Low Physical Effort: The design can be used efficiently, comfortably, and with a minimum
of fatigue.
7. Appropriate Size and Space for Approach and Use: Appropriate size and space are
allotted for approach, reach, and manipulation regardless of physical characteristics such
as size or mobility.
2.5.2
Eight Goals of Universal Design
The eight goals were created to evaluate and implement UD across all design fields. The goals
are defined as follows [21]:
1. Body fit: Accommodating a wide range of body sizes and abilities.
2. Comfort: Keeping demands within desirable limits of body function and perception.
3. Awareness: Ensuring that critical information for use is easily perceived.
4. Understanding: Making methods of operation and use intuitive, clear, and unambiguous.
5. Wellness: Contributing to health promotion, avoidance of disease, and protection from
hazards.
6. Social integration: Treating all groups with dignity and respect.
7. Personalisation: Incorporating opportunities for choice and the expression of individual
preferences.
8. Cultural appropriateness: Respecting and reinforcing cultural values, and the social and
environmental contexts of any design project.
There are some examples in the AEC sectors where during the design process UD was an
important focus, but it is far from being used everywhere.

2.6. Problem Definition
2.6
Problem Definition
As described, several attempts have been made to create ACC methods and tools for the AEC
sectors. However, compliance with regulations is often still checked manually. The fire safety
sector is no exception to this.
The process of verifying fire regulation compliance can be time­consuming and consequently
expensive. It would be beneficial for architects to perform some preliminary checks themselves.
Later, the fire expert could also benefit from performing checks more quickly than the current
method, using new tools.
However, the complexity of regulations often requires human interpretation. The RASE method­
ology offers a promising solution by translating these regulations into computable text, enabling
automated checks to be coded and performed.
Although IFC models contain a lot of information about buildings, they do not always include all
the necessary data for automated checks. Some information needs to be calculated or collected
from users. The more calculations the program can perform independently, the easier it will be
for users.
If it is possible to formalise UD principles in a way that the RASE methodology can be applied to
them, these principles can also be turned into automated checks. This would be advantageous
because potential solutions for UD are often overlooked during the design phase. By highlight­
ing the options available to make buildings more universally designed, individuals may be more
inclined to implement these solutions.
This thesis addresses the following key questions:
1. Is it feasible to utilise the RASE methodology to formalise and implement a compli­
ance check for fire escape corridor width?
2. Can the RASE methodology be used to formalise and implement a compliance
check for Universal Design principles?
3. Can automated compliance checks enhance the effectiveness of the design pro­
cess?

3.1. Automated Compliance Checking in Fire Regulations
3.1.2
Tx3 Methodology
As described by Hjelseth [27] and de Mendonça et al. [7], the Tx3 methodology classifies reg­
ulatory statements into three categories based on their ability to be translated into computable
rules. The three categories:
• Transcribe (T1): applicable to instructions that can be directly converted into computable
rules.
• Transform (T2): applicable to instructions that can be rewritten or restructured while main­
taining their scope.
• Transfer (T3): requirements are poorly defined, lacking any connection between the reg­
ulation’s objectives and the identified indicators. As a result, automated verification is not
feasible and must be handled by a field expert (like a fire expert).
Categorising written regulations in this way can assist in determining the necessary actions for
translating the rules into computable formats.
3.1.3
Rule Formalisation
The first attempts, such as mvdXML [28] and IfcDoc [11], were limited to simple rules with re­
stricted usage. Further research proposed combining IFC with logical semantics to create a
semantic rule­checking environment. [29] Hjelseth and Nisbet [5] utilised the RASE methodol­
ogy to represent specification rules.
Kincelova et al. [30] conducted a comparison of existing tools and found that the integration of
fire protection in BIM is limited. Their study indicated that further development is necessary to
fully utilise the semantics of building models within the context of fire protection.
Wang et al. [31] developed a structured representation of Chinese fire codes using Natural Lan­
guage Processing (NLP), translating them into logical expressions for automated compliance
checking, thereby enabling computers to effectively evaluate the codes. This system has some
limitations. For example, not every fire regulation was translated due to their large number and
variety. This study mainly focused on the spatial geometric relationships in buildings; therefore,
fire regulations that focus on other aspects were not tested.
The NLP framework can be very useful and beneficial; however, it requires a level of expertise
that non­IT specialists may lack. In contrast, the RASE methodology was developed with the
goal to ensure that experts from other fields, such as AEC professionals, can easily use it.
Ismail et al. [6] translated 256 fire safety clauses in Malaysian regulations. The methods used
were classification, decomposition through the semantic mark­up RASE, and conducting inter­
views. One of the main goals of this study was to develop a user­friendly method so that rel­
evant regulatory experts could contribute effectively. They found the semantic mark­up RASE
methodology to be the most promising method in the rule translation process.
In a study conducted by Fischer et al. [32], a semi­automated analysis for escape route plan­
ning in accordance with the Vienna Building Code was presented. The researchers highlighted
the importance of allowing designers to determine the course of the escape routes. They noted
that not all regulations within the building code can be fully checked through automation. One
challenge is that some requirements are qualitative and require interpretation. In contrast, the
code­checking process proved to be efficient for quantitative values. The proposed solution
involves two automated steps: escape route generation and escape route validation, with man­
ual intervention required between these steps. This developed method significantly reduced
the time needed for rule checking.

Accessibility Regulations
De Mendonça et al. [7] conducted a study on the practical application of automated accessi­

In a study conducted by Haque et al. [8], a semi­automated framework was introduced for
evaluating compliance with the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) in healthcare facilities.
The design of healthcare facilities can be quite complex, especially for large­scale projects, due

The framework consists of several key stages: Parametric BIM Preparation, ADA Rule Map­

demonstrated the framework’s reliability, scalability, and efficiency. It was found that the frame­
work offers several advantages over manual compliance checks, such as increased automation

standards. Overall, the framework supports a proactive approach to inclusive architecture.

3.3. Research Gap
3.3
Research Gap
Based on the literature review for this project, it is evident that there is a growing trend of digital­
isation in the construction industry, along with an increasing availability of BIM and automated
compliance checks. However, most compliance checks for fire safety and Universal Design
still rely heavily on manual processes. Although some research projects and tools have aimed
at automating fire regulation checks, they often do not incorporate broader accessibility or UD
considerations.
Moreover, UD is frequently regarded as a qualitative or conceptual goal rather than a measur­
able design parameter. This perspective raises uncertainty about how – and whether – it can
be effectively evaluated using computational tools.
The main research gaps found:
Translation of Danish fire regulations:
While some research has developed advanced methods to convert building codes into computer­
readable formats, many existing tools remain confined to basic rules. To the best of this author’s
knowledge, the entirety of the Danish fire regulations has yet to be translated. There is an ongo­
ing project called FireBIM, where fire experts are working to translate these regulations, which
are written in natural language, into computable rules. The RASE methodology has demon­
strated a user­friendly and effective approach for efficiently translating regulatory clauses.
Integration of fire protection in BIM:
The findings of Kincelova et al. [30] indicate that the integration of fire protection within BIM is
still limited.
Qualitative requirements:
Fischer et al. [32] have identified significant challenges in automating the evaluation of qualita­
tive requirements within building codes. The expertise of fire safety professionals is essential
to ensure accurate translation and compliance with these regulations.
Universal Design:
No studies were found during this research that focus on using ACC in Universal Design. How­
ever, this does not necessarily mean that research in this area does not exist; it simply was
not identified in this study. It can be assumed that further research is needed to explore the
automation of Universal Design compliance checks, particularly to investigate the feasibility of
this approach.
The purpose of this study is to address the questions outlined in the problem statement and to
explore the research gaps identified during the literature review.

Universal Design Integration
According to Anne Kathrine Frandsen2 from Aalborg University in Denmark, the approach to
Universal Design should not be limited to concrete numbers and standards, as it is done in the
isUD documentation. While the numerical specifications used for automated checks can be
useful, they do not guarantee that the design will truly be universal.
One effective way to assist UD experts and architects is to develop the tool in a way that makes
existing knowledge more accessible. The tool could provide design suggestions during fire
regulation checks, informing architects about potential solutions to enhance the usability of their
designs for a diverse range of people.
In regulations, UD is often narrowed down to accessibility, focusing primarily on wheelchair
users. However, this perspective encompasses only a small portion of individuals living with
disabilities. In reality, UD has a much broader scope. In Denmark, research is focusing on
gaining a deeper understanding of how different users experience the built environment and
identifying the various barriers they encounter.
Frandsen emphasises that further research is needed to determine how we can ensure UD
principles are included in design briefs. While there are effective examples of UD that can
serve as guidelines, there are currently no established rules, unlike the explicit fire regulations,

sign choices, which can result in unintentional deviations from UD principles during the building
process. Educating individuals in this area will be a long process.
Frandsen also emphasised that the lack of complete answers should not discourage us from
continuing our efforts. Establishing minimum space requirements for different individuals, in­

RASE Methodology Application
The RASE methodology, in short, defines the scope of the decision through the Application,
Selection, and Exception, while the Requirements define the decision itself. [26]
This methodology allows AEC domain experts to create computable rules without relying on
programmers. It utilises a predefined semantic system that can be translated into automated
rule checks using the logical connectives of formal logic (AND, OR, NOT AND, NOT OR). [36]
This methodology was applied to both fire regulations in BR18 and UD guidelines as part of this
study. The results can be found in section 5.2 and section 5.3.
The RASE tagging was performed on the entire fire regulation rule, 2.3.3.2 Width of escape
routes; however, the implementation scope was limited to corridor width for depth and feasibility
testing. This focused approach allowed for a more detailed examination of the results from the
RASE methodology and the implementation of the proposed tool.

4.5. Definitions
4.5
Definitions
4.5.1
Fire Escape Corridors
According to Lene Pingel3 from DBI, a fire safety corridor is defined as a closed room that
contains a fire subcompartment, which can protect the people escaping in case of a fire. It must
not be connected to any open spaces and needs to be separated by fire­resistant walls, doors,
and floors. Additionally, there should be no furniture in the corridor that could burn or produce
smoke in the event of a fire. This differs from the architectural term ”corridor”, which refers to a
passage in a building that connects rooms.
There are several methods to determine which corridors can be classified as fire escape corri­
dors in a building. The most reliable approach is to consult a fire safety expert to select these
corridors. While this method is certainly effective, it may not always be possible if the architect
wants to perform a self­check. In such cases, the architect can define which corridors should be
assessed as fire escape corridors. They can mark these by utilising the “FireExit” Boolean vari­
able, which is part of the SpaceFireSafetyRequirements IFC PropertySet. It indicates whether
the IfcSpace is designed to function as a fire escape corridor or not.
If no expert is available, another option is to rely on the room’s naming conventions. This
involves checking every room where the LongName IFC Attribute is set to ”Corridor” in the
IFC model. However, this method is prone to errors due to potential spelling mistakes, so it is
advisable to use it cautiously.

4.5. Definitions
4.5.2
Minimum Width
According to the Danish fire regulations, ”corridors in common access routes must have a clear
width of at least 1.3 m”. [35]
This regulation requires that the minimum width of the corridor be at least 1.3 meters. To verify
compliance with this requirement, the minimum width of the corridor needs to be defined. Typi­
cally, this information is not explicitly stated in the IFC model, so it must be calculated from the
available geometric data. The minimum width of the corridor can be determined by the length
of the shortest line segment connecting the non­adjacent walls. Various methods can be used
to calculate this, and the specific definition will depend on the selected method.
Perpendicular lines method
The minimum width of a corridor can be defined as the length of the shortest perpendicular line
segment drawn from one wall of the corridor to the first wall it intersects.
This method is illustrated in Figure 4.2. First, points are marked along the walls at equal intervals.
For long walls, there are many points, while for shorter walls, there are fewer points. Next, line
segments are drawn starting from each point, extending perpendicularly away from the wall.
Each segment continues until it meets another wall, not including the one it started from. The
lengths of these segments are measured, and the shortest one is chosen as the minimum width
of the corridor.

4.5. Definitions
Ray-casting method
The minimum width of a corridor can be defined as the length of the shortest line segment drawn
from one wall of the corridor to the first wall it intersects.
This method is illustrated in Figure 4.3. Not all lines are displayed for clarity. First, points are
marked along the walls at equal intervals. For longer walls, there are more points, while shorter
walls have fewer points. Next, line segments are drawn from each point, extending in several
directions away from the wall. The angle between the lines is kept relatively small to ensure that
all directions are covered. Each segment continues until it meets another wall, excluding the
one from which it started. The lengths of these segments are then measured, and the shortest
segment is selected as the minimum width of the corridor.

4.5. Definitions
Connecting points method
The minimum width of a corridor can be defined as the length of the shortest line segment drawn
between two points on the walls of the corridor that are not adjacent.
This method is illustrated in Figure 4.4. Not all lines are displayed for clarity. First, points are
marked along the walls at equal intervals. For longer walls, there are more points, while shorter
walls have fewer points. Next, points on walls that are not adjacent to each other are connected
with line segments. Only the line segments that are inside the room are taken into account.
Points on walls that are adjacent to each other are not connected with a line segment because
the minimum width of the corridor can be determined by the walls facing each other, rather than
those next to each other. Connecting points on adjacent walls would result in much shorter line
segments, leading to an inaccurate measurement of the minimum width. The lengths of the
segments are then measured, and the shortest segment is selected as the minimum width of
the corridor.

Logical Operators
After completing the RASE tagging of the text, the next step is to link the different phrases
and sections using logical operators. This connection helps clarify how the logic of the rule
functions, ensuring that the tagging was done correctly. This step is important because the
code that checks the regulations will only operate effectively if the tagging is accurate.
The graph (Figure 5.2) illustrating the logical connections among the terms of the RASE method­
ology (applicability, selection, exceptions, and requirements) can be used as a schema for con­
necting the phrases.
Figure 5.2: Applications use a logical expansion of the mark­up hierarchy. (Figure is copyrighted to AEC3
and ICC showing model provided to the US Coast Guard by AECInforSystems.) [4]
Check: Each Check corresponds to a rule within the regulation. For the building to comply with
all fire regulations, every Check must be True. If a Check is False, it indicates a failure, and
this needs to be reported to the user for further investigation and resolution. The following four
conditions are structured so that if any one of them is True, the Check is passed.
Applicability:
If one Applicability is False, the whole Check is True because it does not apply.
If every Applicability is True, the Check needs to be further investigated because it applies to
the analysed situation.
Selection:
If every Selection is False, the whole Check is True because it does not apply.
If one Selection is True, the Check needs to be further investigated because it applies to the
analysed situation.
Exception:
If at least one Exception is True, the entire Check is True since it qualifies as an exception.
If all Exceptions are False, the Check needs to be further investigated because it is not an
exception.
Requirement:
If the Check applies and it is not an exception, then every Requirement must be True for the

If one Requirement is False, the Check fails.

5.3.1
Guidelines
One effective way to utilise the RASE methodology on UD principles is by using written guide­
lines, such as the ”Building for Everyone: A Universal Design Approach” guidelines. The second
document in this series, titled ”Entrances and Horizontal Circulation” [34], includes several chap­
ters that discuss various components of buildings, including corridors. Each chapter provides
different recommendations, explained through text and illustrated with images, on solutions that
enhance the universal design of buildings. At the end of each chapter, these recommendations
are compiled into a checklist. The checklist for corridors is as follows:
1. Ensure recommended 2000 mm clear width for corridors in public buildings.

5.3.3
Feasibility
Anne Kathrine Frandsen8 from Aalborg University in Denmark, expressed concerns about treat­
ing UD as something that measurable requirements can define. This concern is partly cultural,
reflecting a broader Danish perspective that UD should be more inclusive and holistic than
merely focusing on physical accessibility.
Frandsen also stated that the chosen regulation regarding corridor width is more closely related
to accessibility standards than to the broader concept of UD. Accessibility standards typically
focus primarily on the needs of wheelchair users, while UD principles take into account a wider
range of user needs, including those with visual or hearing impairments, mental health condi­
tions, and cognitive challenges.
Janet Cohen Muntz9 from Christensen & Co. also emphasised that there are several other
aspects of a corridor, beyond just its width, that should be considered when discussing UD.
Overall, the opinions of the interviewed UD experts indicate that concentrating solely on the
width of a corridor is insufficient. Therefore, in the process of RASE tagging UD principles, sev­
eral additional suggestions related to corridors were analysed. These suggestions originated
from both the interviewed experts and the guidelines outlined in Building for Everyone: A Uni­
versal Design Approach. [34, 40] It is a valid critique that questions why this particular guideline
was chosen over others, as different guidelines propose varied solutions, making it challenging
to determine the most appropriate one.

5.3.4
Summary
An attempt was made to apply the RASE methodology to Universal Design recommendations
for corridors. The primary challenge is that UD is not a rigid system of documented rules; rather,
it is a philosophy focused on designing for a broader group of users. Various solutions can be
applied to different scenarios, meaning there isn’t a one­size­fits­all approach. This makes UD
less concrete and comprehensive compared to the fire regulations outlined in BR18.
This issue was approached in two ways. The first method involved utilising an existing document
containing UD guidelines that experts in the field had created. This document was particularly
useful because it included checklists at the end of each chapter, which facilitated the RASE
tagging process.
The second method involved creating a list based on interviews conducted during the project
and existing UD guidelines. This list was specifically designed to streamline the RASE tagging
process by defining objects, representing Applicability or Selection, and solutions, representing
Requirements.
Both approaches were mapped to IFC Properties, using the custom PropertySet defined for
instances where no suitable property could be found in the official IFC documentation.

Corridor width from door
To determine the width of the corridor the following steps are needed:
1. Draw a line from the centre of the door, making it perpendicular to the door.
2. Create several points along this line.
3. From each point, draw lines perpendicular to the first line in both directions.
4. Extend these lines until they intersect with the corridor’s boundaries.
5. Identify the longest line and draw perpendicular lines starting from it to cover corridors
with multiple segments, such as L or U­shaped corridors.
6. Repeat until all parts of the corridor are covered.
7. Measure the length of each line segment by adding the distances from both directions.
8. The shortest total length measured will indicate the minimum width of the corridor.
Figure 5.22 shows an example using this method in an L­shaped corridor.
Figure 5.22: Visualisation of determining the width of a corridor starting from a door

door is challenging because both the door and every other element in the IFC model consist
of many small surfaces. When these surfaces are represented on the XY plane, they appear

requires less calculation time, provided that the points defined on each wall are the same. In
this approach, only one line segment is drawn from each point, whereas other methods use
multiple line segments originating from each point. Therefore, the perpendicular lines method
was chosen for implementation.

5.4.4
Width Calculation Method Analysis
In subsection 4.5.2, various methods for determining the minimum width of a corridor were
discussed. It was assumed that in standard buildings, the corridor walls are typically parallel to
each other. As discussed there, the minimum width of the corridor is determined by the shortest
line segment connecting the non­adjacent walls.
Perpendicular lines method
This method can measure the width of each corridor segment as long as the opposite walls are
parallel, there are enough points determined on each wall to cover every different part of the
corridor, and there are no walls enclosing an acute angle.
In a built environment, physical elements such as walls, columns, and doors have a minimum
thickness due to construction constraints. By selecting points along the enclosing elements of
a corridor at small intervals, such as 1 cm, it can be ensured that all these elements are fully
represented. Since no component is thinner than 1 cm, this resolution guarantees that no part
of the corridor is overlooked.
When acute angles are present, the issue arises because the shortest line segment measured
could be close to 0 meters long. As illustrated in Figure 5.25, the lines at the corner of the acute

Ray-casting method
The accuracy of this method’s results depends on the angle between the lines cast from the
same point. This angle must be small enough to adequately cover the direction that leads to
the actual minimum width of the corridor. Since walls, columns, and doors have a minimum
thickness due to construction constraints, the minimum angle can be calculated based on the
dimensions of these elements and the general width of corridors. By carefully selecting this
angle, the method can identify the minimum corridor width in cases of both parallel and non­
parallel walls. Acute angles are still an issue with this method, although this can be avoided by
disregarding the line segments between two adjacent walls. An example of this method, applied
to a corridor with non­parallel walls and an acute angle, is illustrated in Figure 5.27.

This method proves to be more accurate in a broader range of scenarios compared to the per­

will still be the one that is perpendicular to the walls, as a perpendicular line is the shortest be­
tween two parallel lines. Therefore, the perpendicular method would lead to the same result in
this case.
In the ray­casting method, multiple line segments are generated from each point along the
walls. Depending on the chosen angle, this can result in significantly more line segments and,
consequently, longer calculation times compared to the perpendicular lines method.
In conclusion, while this method is effective in more situations than the perpendicular lines
method, it does come with longer calculation times. Therefore, in cases of parallel walls, the
perpendicular lines method is the preferred approach.

5.4.6
Code Structure
To support the evaluation of fire safety compliance in architectural models, a prototype of a
desktop application was developed using Python and the IfcOpenShell library. The tool en­
ables users to load an IFC file, visualise rooms, select fire escape corridors, and assess their
compliance with specific fire safety regulations and UD principles.
The application integrates several custom modules and classes. The Application class defines
the UI and coordinates user interaction using the Tinkter Python library. The Room class rep­
resents individual room elements, including their geometry and metadata. User­provided data
related to the rooms is also stored within this class. The get_required_min_width_fire function
calculates the required minimum width in accordance with the fire safety regulations specified
in BR18.
The minimum width of the corridors is determined using specific functions defined in the geom­
etry file. The function perpendicular_lines_from_vector generates multiple perpendicular lines
extending to the left of a given vector, which represents the boundary of the rooms. This func­
tion creates a line segment between the starting point and the first boundary vector it intersects,
which is determined using the intersect_ray_with_segment function. The created line segments
are collected in a list. The find_shortest_line function identifies the shortest line from the list of
line segments. The length of this line equals the minimum width of the corridor.
The check_fire_regulation function utilises the calculated minimum width of the corridor along
with the required minimum width obtained from the get_required_min_width_fire function. Ad­
ditionally, it uses the get_room_compliance function to verify whether the measured corridor
width in a room adheres to fire safety standards and UD guidelines. This process results in
three possible outcomes:
• If the measured width is less than required in the fire regulation, it returns 0 (non­compliant).
• If it’s compliant with the fire regulations, but not wide enough based on the UD guidelines,
it returns 1 (semi­compliant).
• If it’s compliant with the fire regulations and the UD guidelines, it returns 2 (fully compliant).
Finally, the get_result_message function formats the final result messages for each room after
the compliance check is complete. The user receives visual feedback on the results through
colours on the floor plan and a list of the calculated minimum widths.
The goal of this tool was to minimise the necessity for manual checks by ensuring compliance
with fire regulations and UD guidelines during the early design phases. The implementation
code can be found in Appendix D.

5.5
Final Solution
In this chapter, various approaches to completing the same tasks are explored, demonstrating
the progress made throughout the project. Ultimately, these methods are integrated into the
final solution, resulting in the development of the AFU tool, which serves as a prototype for an
automated Fire Safety and Universal Design checking tool.
The first step in this process is the RASE tagging of the texts. For the fire regulations examined
in this study, the most effective RASE tagging approach involves tagging different sections of
the text as requirements that must be fulfilled simultaneously for the entire rule to be considered
satisfied. This method is detailed in subsection 5.2.3.
For RASE tagging of the UD guidelines, the most effective method discovered was to compile
the suggested UD solutions into a checklist that includes the main recommendations. This
checklist should ideally be created beforehand by UD experts. The Building for Everyone: A
Universal Design Approach [24] guidelines already include checklists, which provide a solid
foundation for this process.
The next step is to define and calculate the minimum width of the fire escape corridors. The
chosen method allows the user of the tool to select which rooms should be considered as fire
escape corridors. Once the rooms are selected, the AFU tool uses the room boundaries and
the perpendicular lines method (subsubsection 4.5.2) to determine the minimum width.
After the calculation, the tool compares the calculated minimum width, the required minimum
width based on fire regulations, and the suggested minimum width from UD guidelines. The
AFU tool then provides the results of this comparison for each selected corridor.
After obtaining the results, the user can discuss the problematic corridors with the client or
other engineers involved in the project. Once the original plan is modified to address the non­
compliant corridors, the user can export a new IFC file and conduct a new check.

6
Discussion
The main goal of this thesis was to examine the feasibility and effectiveness of employing the
RASE methodology to convert texts written in natural language into computable rules, particu­
larly focusing on fire regulations from BR18 and Universal Design guidelines.
This chapter outlines the key findings of the study and addresses the research questions. It
evaluates the feasibility of the methodologies employed, identifies their limitations, and suggests
areas for future research and development.
6.1
Key Findings and Feasibility
One of the key insights from the research was the need to develop a custom IFC PropertySet.
Although the official IFC documentation covers a wide range of elements and properties used
in building design, certain terms found in fire safety regulations and UD guidelines lack direct
equivalents in the standard IFC schema. Accurately mapping these critical terms to IFC termi­
nology is essential for enabling automated compliance checks that can reliably identify relevant
objects and properties in the IFC models.
The introduction of a custom PropertySet facilitated the accurate mapping of expressions found
in regulatory texts to IFC terms, even when these were not explicitly represented in the of­
ficial documentation.
In the case of fire safety regulations, for instance, the missing prop­
erty was the minimum width requirement, which was addressed through the introduction of
the custom property AFU_MinWidth.
Similarly, the examined UD guidelines led to the pro­
posal of several custom properties focused on accessibility and inclusivity. Examples include
AFU_SeatingAvailable, AFU_ContrastingColours, AFU_FireSafeArea, and AFU_Braille.
It was confirmed, consistent with findings from previous research, that the RASE methodology
can be used to create computable rules for fire safety regulations. Additionally, it was demon­
strated that this same methodology can be applied to certain UD guidelines, provided that these
resemble regulatory texts by including specific rules. A prototype tool for performing automated
compliance checks was successfully developed as a proof of concept.
6.1.1
RASE Methodology - Fire Regulations
The following question was posed in the problem statement: Is it feasible to utilise the RASE
methodology to formalise and implement a compliance check for fire escape corridor
width?
The results suggest that RASE is feasible for formalising fire regulations, but not without chal­
lenges.
According to Hjelseth and Nisbet [36], using the RASE methodology ”can and should be done by
AEC professionals, and not by programming experts.” They also state that ”a person skilled in
the AEC domain” should be able to ”develop applicable rules without support of programmers”.
These imply that AEC domain experts do not require further training or additional knowledge
beyond what they already possess.
However, the findings of this thesis indicate that using the RASE methodology to analyse the
fire regulations in BR18 presents certain challenges. In the initial attempt to apply RASE to the
chosen regulations, the results seemed logical. However, testing it across several scenarios
revealed that the outcomes were incorrect under certain circumstances. As a result, the RASE

tagging was not entirely successful on the first try; part of the regulation was mistakenly marked
as Exceptions instead of Requirements.
Even Nicholas Nisbet, a key contributor to the development of the RASE methodology, ac­
knowledged that it was understandable why specific paragraphs in the text were marked as
Exceptions. However, by leveraging his expertise, he was able to correct these markings, indi­

This experience shows that while RASE is a promising framework, achieving accurate results

somewhat contradicts the claim that AEC professionals can use RASE “without support of pro­

essential for effective use, especially for more complex or ambiguous rules.
Despite these challenges, the RASE­tagged text has been successfully converted into com­
putable code, resulting in a prototype tool that automatically checks the width of corridors in
buildings. This confirms that automated compliance checks using RASE are feasible, provided
the tagging is accurate and thoroughly tested.
6.1.2
RASE Methodology - Universal Design
The following question was posed in the problem statement: Can the RASE methodology be
used to formalise and implement a compliance check for Universal Design principles?
According to the experts interviewed in this study1, Universal Design cannot be easily expressed
as a fixed set of concrete rules. This is because it embodies a philosophy that goes beyond
strict guidelines.
Mansoori et al. [42] emphasise the importance of inclusivity in architectural interventions, stating
that ”Universal design is not a “one­size­fits­all” approach to design; rather it is a user­centred
approach that considers the functionality of the widest possible variety of users.”
As a result, translating the principles or goals of UD into rules that can be automatically verified
is more difficult than doing so for fire regulations. The RASE methodology can only be applied if
the text contains specific rules and expectations. While there are several UD guidelines that can
be used as a foundation, these guidelines often include ambiguities and qualitative expectations
that are difficult to translate into strict rules. The RASE methodology is most effective when the
text clearly outlines the requirements.
This thesis demonstrated that it is possible to construct simple RASE­tagged statements from
UD guidelines when the source material contains clear design recommendations. For instance,
the width of a corridor, the inclusion of resting areas, or the use of contrasting colours can often
be framed as Requirements linked to a specific Applicability condition. The approach was also
successful in mapping to IFC Objects, IFC Properties, or custom IFC Properties.
These findings suggest that, although UD guidelines are more interpretive than regulatory texts,
there is still value in formalising certain recommendations using RASE. This enables automated
checks of basic inclusive design features early in the design phase, thereby supporting inclusive
decision­making. By automating the evaluation of more straightforward aspects of UD, experts
can dedicate greater attention to complex, context­specific design challenges.

6.1.3
Effectiveness
The following question was posed in the problem statement: Can automated compliance
checks enhance the effectiveness of the design process?
Effectiveness in this context may be improved if compliance checks require less time and effort
from professionals, thereby increasing efficiency and reducing costs. One hypothesis is that
enabling architects to perform preliminary self­checks before consulting fire safety experts could
streamline the design process and reduce the need for rework. While this assumption appears
logical, it was not formally validated within the scope of this project. Instead, it is based on
insights gathered from interviews with professionals in architecture and fire safety.
Another assumption is that the current manual process of verifying corridor widths is less efficient
than automated methods, such as those performed by the AFU tool. However, for the automated
process to be considered truly effective, the tool must be reliable and trusted by its users. This
reliability can be achieved by further development and testing across a wider variety of IFC
models. Involving practitioners in testing the tool would help verify its accuracy and usability in
real­world scenarios.
Until these further steps are taken, it cannot be definitively concluded that the AFU tool or sim­
ilar tools enhance the effectiveness of the design process. Nonetheless, the findings indicate
promising potential, especially if the tools are robust and integrated early in the design phase.
6.2
Limitations
This chapter discusses the limitations during the project, which impact the completeness, gen­
eralizability, and applicability of the findings. These limitations are associated with the scope of
the study, the methodology used, and the technical performance of the developed tool.
6.2.1
Scope
This study focused on a single rule from the Danish fire regulations and a limited subset of
UD principles. This narrow scope may limit the generalizability and scalability of the findings.
Broader investigations that encompass additional rules or more comprehensive, inclusive de­
sign strategies could potentially yield different results. Fire regulations are often complex and
difficult to interpret without input from fire safety experts. In contrast, UD principles and re­
lated guidelines tend to be less prescriptive and more open to interpretation. Despite these
differences, the overall method of translating clear design recommendations into structured,
machine­readable formats is likely to remain applicable. Therefore, while specific outcomes
may vary, the approach used in this study can reasonably be expected to apply across a wider
range of texts.
The regulation 2.3.3.2 Width of escape routes was fully tagged using the RASE methodology;
however, only the parts concerning corridor width were tested. This approach aligns with the
RASE methodology, as inapplicable rule parts (based on context) would not be evaluated. For
instance, other elements in the regulation that do not apply to corridors would have an Applica­
bility of False.
Nevertheless, testing only parts of a rule can result in a loss of contextual meaning. This in­
troduces a risk of misinterpretation, particularly when automated tools are applied in real­life

6.2.2
Application of RASE
Accurate use of the RASE methodology is critical. Improper tagging of text can lead to incor­
rect compliance results, which reduces the reliability of automated checking. As discussed in
subsection 5.2.1, the initial attempt to apply the RASE methodology to fire regulations during
this project did not yield adequate results in all scenarios. This poses a significant risk for any
project that seeks to implement automated checks using the RASE methodology.
A key challenge lies in interpreting ambiguous natural language within regulations. For example,
the expression “large number of people” in the selected rule is not sufficiently defined to be
computable. In such cases, either a fire engineer must define the thresholds or the broader
regulation must be consulted for clarification. This ambiguity makes consistent tagging difficult
and increases the risk of human error.
The RASE methodology has potential, but its implementation depends heavily on training. Ex­
perts involved in tagging should be thoroughly trained, and their interpretations should be tested
in various scenarios. When properly applied, RASE can support the creation of clear, com­
putable rule sets – but only with a rigorous quality assurance process.
6.2.3
AFU Tool
It is important to acknowledge that the AFU tool was tested exclusively with IFC files exported
from Revit, following the method described in Appendix C. Since it relies on accurate geometry
from IfcSpace objects to calculate corridor widths, it might give incorrect results if the IFC file is
exported from other software or if the space boundaries are not well defined. This introduces a
compatibility issue that requires further investigation, particularly with other BIM tools, such as
Archicad.
Only two IFC models were used for testing, both of which included multiple corridors. While
these models demonstrated the tool’s potential, the small sample size limits the conclusions that
can be drawn from them. Testing on a wider range of projects and building types is essential
for validating the tool’s robustness.
The tool currently verifies only one specific fire regulation. Although the examined UD princi­
ples address broader corridor design elements, only the suggestions about the corridor width
were implemented in the tool. As such, the tool represents a proof­of­concept rather than a
comprehensive compliance solution.

Conclusion
This thesis aimed to investigate the feasibility and effectiveness of using the RASE methodology
to convert texts written in natural language into computable rules, with a particular focus on fire
safety requirements from BR18 and principles from Universal Design guidelines. As part of
this research, a digital tool was developed to automatically verify compliance with both sets of
regulations. The tool is intended to support architects during the design process and to enable
fire safety and UD experts to perform compliance checks more efficiently and cost­effectively.
The main contributions of this thesis are:
1. A prototype tool (AFU) designed for automated compliance checks of fire safety and UD
working with IFC models.
2. A custom PropertySet that bridges the gaps between regulatory terminology and the IFC
schema.
3. A method for calculating minimum corridor widths using perpendicular line segments.
4. Recommendations for reformulating universal design principles to allow for computable
evaluation.
The project demonstrated that the RASE methodology is an effective approach for converting
fire regulations into computable rules. Tagging regulatory text using the four RASE compo­
nents – Requirement, Applicability, Selection, and Exception – makes it possible to structure
the content logically. These tagged elements can then be connected using basic logic operators,
forming a clear foundation for implementing automated compliance checks.
Initial testing of a RASE­tagged fire rule across different scenarios revealed inaccuracies, lead­
ing to some false results. A second tagging attempt was performed after consulting Nicholas
Nisbet, a main contributor to the RASE methodology. This experience highlighted the impor­
tance of expertise in employing the RASE methodology, indicating that AEC domain experts
responsible for tagging regulations should receive adequate training.
While the RASE methodology was effective in addressing fire regulations, applying it to UD prin­
ciples posed a greater challenge due to the abstract nature of many of these principles. While
the method can be implemented, UD solutions must first be articulated as concrete guidelines
that can be tagged using RASE. This challenge arises because UD is often viewed more as a
philosophy or mindset during the design process, rather than as a definitive set of rules. Experts
in UD emphasised this point during interviews, suggesting that it may not be feasible to express
UD principles as concrete rules. To enable automated checks of UD in IFC models, UD experts
need to develop more concrete, rule­like texts, similar to those used for fire regulation checks.
To address this challenge, two complementary approaches were employed. The first involved
using an existing UD guideline document that included concrete design recommendations re­
lated to corridors in buildings. The second approach entailed developing a tailored set of sug­
gestions for effective RASE tagging, informed by interviews with UD experts and a review of
current guidelines. Collaborating with these experts to formalise such suggestions – mirroring
the structure of existing regulations like BR18 – could support the integration of inclusive design
principles into automated compliance checking tools.
To enable the developed tool to work with IFC models, the RASE­tagged expressions needed
to be mapped to corresponding IFC terminology. However, both the fire regulations and the

==PAGE:87==
UD suggestions include terms and expressions that are not fully represented in the official IFC
documentation. To address this gap, a custom PropertySet was developed specifically for this
project. While this set has proven effective for the current scope, it requires further expansion
and, ideally, formal standardisation to ensure broader applicability and long­term usability.
A prototype of the Automated Compliance Checks for Fire Regulations and Universal Design
(AFU) tool was created. This prototype currently evaluates compliance with fire regulations and
UD recommendations regarding the minimum width of corridors.
As the minimum corridor width is not explicitly available in the official IFC documentation, it
had to be calculated during the project. Several methods were evaluated, with the perpendic­
ular lines method finally selected for its efficiency and suitability, particularly for corridors with
parallel walls, which represent the most common case. This method identifies the shortest per­
pendicular line segment between opposing room boundaries, including elements such as walls,
doors, windows, and columns.
The scope of this project was limited to a single regulatory aspect and one building element:
corridors. Future work could expand the AFU tool to cover a broader range of fire safety regula­
tions and incorporate additional UD guidelines alongside them. This would enhance the tool’s
applicability across diverse regulatory contexts and design challenges.
To further validate its practical value, the AFU tool should be tested on real­life case studies
involving various IFC models. Such testing could establish the tool as a reliable resource for
architects, fire safety experts, and UD specialists, supporting them in the design of safer and
more inclusive buildings.
This work contributes to the growing body of research on ACC using the RASE methodology
by focusing on Danish fire regulations and extending its application to UD principles. By bridg­
ing the gap between regulatory compliance and inclusive design, the thesis demonstrates how
digital tools can support the creation of built environments that are both safe and accessible.

AFU - IFC Export
To ensure the program functions correctly, it is crucial to pay close attention to the specific
settings and export options used during the IFC export process. One significant factor that
affects interoperability is the mapping between the internal data structures of the BIM software
and the IFC schema, which can differ between various platforms. Incomplete mapping can lead
to several problems, including data loss or misrepresentation. [43]
This mapping must follow the IFC Types and IFC Properties described in Table 5.2. This involves
ensuring the rooms are exported properly and mapped to the IfcSpace category.
In the case of Autodesk Revit, this can be achieved in the following way:
1. Set up the Revit Rooms properly: check in a Schedule if every room is placed correctly
and the Area property is correct.
2. Go to IFC export settings. (File →Export →IFC)
3. In the Export window choose ”Modify setup”.
4. IFC version should be the most recent Official version, which is currently IFC 4x3. If that
is not an option, any IFC 4x version is suitable.
5. On the ”General” tab set ”Space boundaries” to ”2nd Level”.
6. Go to the Property Sets tab. Make sure the following are selected (Figure C.1):
• Export Revit property sets
• Export IFC common property sets
• Export base quantities
7. Export IFC file.
