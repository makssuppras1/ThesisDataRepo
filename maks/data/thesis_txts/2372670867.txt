Abstract
Nowadays, water scarcity is becoming an increasingly urgent issue for food and beverage industry,
a major freshwater consumer. Food and beverage industry is facing growing pressure from local
water shortage, public concern, political regulation, and market competition, to reduce their water
usage. Water reuse is considered promising as it not only upgrades production in a sense that less
freshwater is required to produce same amount of food products, but also exerts positive inﬂuence
on both upstream water supply and downstream wastewater treatment processes. However, water
reuse may lead to rebound eﬀects, which may counteract the anticipated reductions in freshwater
use and associated environmental impact. Therefore, it is necessary to shift the focus from water-
eﬃciency alone towards absolute assessment of entire water system’s performance to support a
move towards sustainable industrial practice.
This thesis project is based on a case study of one poultry slaughterhouse owned by HKScan in
Vinderup, Denmark, which is facing challenges from freshwater uptake and wastewater treatment.
In response to those challenges, DRIP proposed a solution of reusing rinsing water in the chicken
feet processing line. The objective of this thesis project is to carry out an absolute assessment
of the environmental and economic consequences for the water system in HKScan Vinderup after
implementing the reuse practice in the chicken feet processing line. Entire water system is con-
sidered in this study, from groundwater abstraction, freshwater supply, water use in the chicken
feet processing line, wastewater treatment, to ﬁnally disposal.
To that end, four scenarios are developed: one baseline scenario depicting current situation; one
reuse scenario representing the studied water system after adopting the reuse practice proposed by
DRIP; and two scenarios, which are based on the reuse practice, but with the addition of indirect
rebound eﬀect of using up saved freshwater for optimizing one clean-in-place (CIP) process, and
direct rebound eﬀect of using a fraction of saved water for extra chicken feet production. The four
scenarios have one common functional unit, namely, available 7.36E+07 kg freshwater that can be
supplied to the chicken feet processing line in HKScan Vinderup, who can achieve a production
of 4.98E+06 kg chicken feet under normal operation conditions for one year for the baseline year
2017. Functional unit deﬁned in this way allows the assessment to include rebound eﬀects thus
provide an absolute picture.
These four scenarios are tested by means of comparative attributional assessment, in order to un-
fold the diﬀerences in the life-cycle performance of the water system in question. The performance
of the studied water system is evaluated from the perspective of environment-protection by life
cycle assessment (LCA), from the perspective of water-saving by freshwater withdrawal impact
(FWI) and virtual water footprint (VWF), and from the perspective of cost-avoidance by conven-
tional life cycle costing (CLCC). In the end, environmental and economic assessment results are
combined by means of eco-care matrix (ECM), which follows the concept of eco-eﬃciency, in order
to provide a holistic view.
This study concludes that implementing reuse practice in the chicken feet processing line is pos-
itive all round. It helps optimize freshwater use for the chicken feet processing line and reduce
wastewater generation. It brings beneﬁts for every process along the water-service value chain

in many senses, namely, resource saving, environmental protection and cost avoidance. Based on
current capacity of the chicken feet processing line, annual water saving is expected to be 38,000
m3, and potential cost saving is expected to be 1,050,000 DKK. If compared with its beneﬁcial
eﬀects, life-cycle environmental and economic burdens caused by the reuse practice itself are neg-
ligible. Since this reuse practice is quite simple to be implemented, and there is a great amount
of similar processes in meat processing industry, it is expected that this reuse practice can be
replicated from HKScan Vinderup to the others, leading to signiﬁcant positive eﬀects.
Further, this study reveals that assessing water-eﬃciency improvement measure without consid-
ering rebound eﬀects may lead to very optimistic and misleading ﬁndings. In the scenario which
models the indirect rebound eﬀect of using up saved freshwater for optimizing one clean-in-place
(CIP) process, the indirect rebound eﬀect causes heavier consumption of chemicals and electricity
in wastewater treatment process, while on the other hand, it helps save electricity and manpower
in the cleaning process. In this case, rebound eﬀect counteracts the anticipated water savings and
results in some shifts of environmental impacts (from electricity-related impacts such as climate
change (CC) and depletion of abiotic resources, fossil (RD-fossil), to chemical-related impacts
for instance freshwater ecotoxicity (ETOX) and depletion of abiotic resources, reverse base (RD-
reserve)). In another scenario which models the direct rebound eﬀect of increasing chicken feet
production, the rebound eﬀect leads to extra electricity demand for operating machines in the
chicken processing line and intensiﬁes electricity consumption in freshwater heating and cooling
processes. In this case, rebound eﬀect fully neutralizes the positive inﬂuence of the reuse practice
on environment, while it generates high proﬁts from extra chicken feet production.
This study concludes that, in addition to the question of how could the most economical use of
freshwater be organized in food processing factories, it is of great signiﬁcance for us to consider the
following questions: whether the saved water is kept in nature or allocated to other water-using
processes? what speciﬁc water quality is required by this water-using process? if the saved water
is fully utilized? A better understanding of the trade-oﬀs between actors along the water-service
chain (i.e., water-supplier, water user, and wastewater treatment provider) can help assess the
feasibility and impacts of reuse practice without creating backﬁre.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Freshwater in suﬃcient quantities and adequate quality is a prerequisite for human societies and
natural ecosystems (Costanza and Daly, 1992). However, the concept of water being a never-
ending resource with a limitless renewable capacity belongs to the past (Beekman, 1998). Demand
for freshwater is substantially growing due to rapid economic growth, expanding populations and
increasing prosperity (Ferroukhi et al., 2015). On the other hand, the ability of water system to
meet the growing freshwater demand is constrained by physical freshwater shortage, deterioration
in water quality, and regulations (Nielsen, 2016). The challenge of meeting the growing demand
is further compounded by climate change impacts (Ferroukhi et al., 2015).
Vulnerabilities in water supply poses critical risks for food and beverage industry, for which water
is an essential input. Water is involved in many food processing methods and unit operations, e.g.,
rinsing, scalding, pasteurizing, cooling, as an ingredient, and for general cleaning, sanitation and
disinfection purposes (Casani et al., 2005). Water consumption by food and beverage industry
constitutes approximately 1.8% of Europe’s total water use (Valta et al., 2013). Besides physical
risk of freshwater shortage, food and beverage industry is facing other types of water risks including
reputation risk, risk of governmental interference and regulation, and ﬁnancial risk (Nielsen,
2016). Such risks are compelling food and beverage industry to explore eﬃcient use of limited
water resources.
Over recent years, there has been great eﬀort world-wide in researching the potential of reusing
water in food and beverage industry in order to lower freshwater demand. Many studies explored
the potential of reclaiming freshwater resource from on-site centralized wastewater treatment sys-
tem and using the reclaimed water in closed circuits for dairy industry (Mittal, 2006; Sarkar
et al., 2006), poultry slaughterhouse (De Nardi et al., 2011; Matsumura and Mierzwa, 2008),
and sugar cane industry (Ingaramo et al., 2009), and brewery (Nielsen, 2016). Besides, there
are as well alternatives to reuse water without complex treatment. For example, reuse eﬄuent
from the desensitization tank to wash the cages used for transporting live poultry (Amorim et al.,
2007), reuse condensate from water evaporation during power production (Levidow et al., 2016),
reuse water from pre-chilling and chilling for carcass pre-washing (Kist et al., 2009), and reuse
ﬁnal ﬂush water as makeup water in a cooling tower (Nielsen, 2016).
Simple water-eﬃcient measures, such as above-mentioned water reuse without complex treatment
in centralized treatment system, not only can upgrade the production chain (producing same
amount of product while lowering freshwater resource input), but also can upgrade the entire
water-service chain. Being a major consumer of freshwater makes food and beverage industry also
a signiﬁcant producer of wastewater. Vice versa, a reduction in freshwater use results in a drop in
the amount of wastewater that needs to be treated and discharged, leading to lower consumption
of chemicals and energy, which are deeply involved in freshwater supply and wastewater treatment
processes. Take energy for an example, it is needed for heating water for washing to guarantee
safe and aseptic operations, for cooling water to reduce microbial growth of food products, for

However, an improvement in eﬃciency may lead to rebound eﬀects3. As can be seen from concrete
examples of development in fuel (Saunders, 1992), transportation (Jalas, 2002) and etc, dra-

from water reuse may counteract the anticipated reductions in freshwater use and associated en-
vironmental impact. Thus, there is a need to shift the focus from water-eﬃciency alone towards
absolute assessment of entire water system’s performance to support a move towards sustainable
industrial practice (Hauschild, 2015). Til now, there have been studies assessing environmental
and economic consequences of implementing water reuse practice in food production from a life-
cycle perspective. For instance, Levidow et al. (2016) investigated the potential of water reuse
in dairy. However, rebound eﬀects derived from water reuse are seldom researched in literature.

• What are the implications of this study in terms of rinsing water management?

2.1

2.1.1
Water scarcity for the globe, Denmark, and food production
Water scarcity for the globe
Freshwater in suﬃcient quantities and adequate quality is a prerequisite for human societies and
natural ecosystems (Costanza and Daly, 1992). However, the concept of water being a never-
ending resource with a limitless renewable capacity belongs to the past (Beekman, 1998).
Nowadays, both supply-side threats and demand-side threats to water necessary to meet human
needs are increasing.
One supply-side threat arises from instances in which freshwater withdrawal rate is faster than
the rate of replenishment or recharge. Moreover, even if in some places where there is suﬃcient
amount of water, but the deterioration in the quality makes it impossible for human to use. Except
for physical water scarcity, there is as well indirect lack of water brought by economic reasons or
political decisions (Rygaard et al., 2011).
On the demand side, one concern arises from the growing population.
Another demand-side
problem is that high-demand users sometimes are geographically concentrated in regions that
cannot sustain demand levels. Still another demand-side problem arises from technologies that
waste more water than alternative technologies. Moreover, water demand is often insuﬃciently
restrained because of inadequate price mechanisms and outdated legal rules that set few limits on
excessive use.
Water scarcity for Denmark
Freshwater is actually a physically scarce resource for Denmark, though Denmark is surrounded
by water.
To reduce the pressure on surface water, Denmark had gradually transferred water use from surface
water to ground water, and in recent years, Danish drinking water supply has been based entirely
on groundwater (Stockmarr, 2013). To guard against over-extraction from the groundwater
wells, Denmark has been levying water taxes and conducting water-saving campaigns during the
past 20 years, which successfully helped to reduce groundwater abstraction by more than a third
(Stockmarr, 2013).
However, Henriksen and Sonnenborg (2003) reported that the water balance in Denmark is still
over-exploited, especially in the eastern part of Denmark, where population density is the highest
and precipitation is the lowest. Besides, many shallow aquifers are suﬀering from groundwater
pollution, especially nitrate and pesticides. In the period from 1991 to 2005, 1306 wells were closed
due to high content of pesticides or metabolites. Even now, approximately 100 wells are closed
every year due to pesticide content (Stockmarr, 2013).
Denmark’s water scarcity is also resulting from political reasons. In response to the environmental
consequences of groundwater abstraction, the European Unions Water Framework Directive (EU-
WFD) sets the environmental objectives for Europes water bodies, including both groundwater
and surface water (WFD, 2000). As a member state of the European Union, Denmark is obliged
to meet legislative requirements that ’...prevents further deterioration and protects and enhances
the status of aquatic ecosystems and, with regard to their water needs, terrestrial ecosystems and
wetlands directly depending on the aquatic ecosystems...’ (Article 1a) (WFD, 2000).
Water scarcity for food production
By 2030, the world is expected to require at least 50% more food to sustain the global population
growing in numbers and aﬄuence (GSP, 2012). Feeding a growing and aﬄuent population will
increase food demand and further intensify the scramble for water (Molden et al., 2007).
Rondinelli and Berry (2000) and WWF (2007) summarized that food and beverage industry

2.1.2
Nexus of water, food and energy in food production
Food and beverage industry requires considerable water and energy resources, and produces a
large amount of wastewater. Freshwater is mainly utilized as an ingredient, as a key processing
element, and as a cooling agent. Wastewater is the most common waste in the food and drink
industry, often containing high content of nutrients. There is as well a great number of energy
involved, as water-service system and energy are closely interrelated.
Freshwater consumption
Water is an essential input for the food and beverage industry. Its consumption in food and drink
industry constitutes approximately 1.8% of Europe’s total water use (Valta et al., 2013). Water
is involved in many food processing methods and unit operations, e.g., soaking, washing, rinsing,
blanching, scalding, heating, pasteurizing, chilling, cooling, steam production, as an ingredient,
and for general cleaning, sanitation and disinfection purposes (Casani et al., 2005). Besides, in
the plant, water is also used for purposes such as cafeteria, personal care and garden watering.
In sectors such as soft drinks and brewing, water is required as a primary ingredient in the
ﬁnal product. Water consumption is reported to range from 4 - 11 m3 water/m3 beer product
in breweries, 3.5 m3 water/m3 product for fruit juices and 2.3 m3 water/m3 for soft drinks
(Fillaudeau et al., 2006). In dairy processing, water is mostly consumed for cleaning rather than
as an ingredient (Rad and Lewis, 2014). Meat processing industry consumes water dominantly
for equipment cleaning and meat rinsing. Typical water consumption expressed as m3 per ton of
product is for pigs 1.5 - 10 m3 water/t, for cattle 2.5 - 40 m3 water/t, and for poultry 6 - 30 m3
water/t (Valta et al., 2013). Among all food industries, meat processing industry ranks ﬁrst in
consuming freshwater. Its consumption accounting for 24% of the total freshwater consumed by
the food and beverage industry worldwide (Bustillo-Lecompte and Mehrvar, 2015).
Freshwater demand is high for the food and beverage industry in terms of not only quantity but
also quality. The principal water quality parameters are of sensory, chemical and microbiological
nature. In most cases, the source water for the food and beverage industry is the potable water
from waterworks. This water is further adjusted to suit diﬀerent needs of processing steps, such
as removal of colour, softening, addition of chlorine to minimize the count of potential spoilage
microorganisms, use of ultraviolet (UV) radiation to disinfect stored water directly before use as
an ingredient, and so forth.
In addition to physical water used directly food plant, food production indirectly draws on water
resources that are mainly located in agricultural and industrial regions outside the plant. The
term, virtual water, was initially applied only to food crops that are traded internationally (Allan,
1997), but later extended to include all commodities, such as electricity and chemicals.
However, the focus of existing virtual water studies regarding meat production is generally put
on virtual water of livestock and livestock products, and there is very little literature concerning
virtual water embedded in commodities and energy consumed in the water-service chain that is
related to meat processing.

in emissions, food companies can reduce their environmental impacts, creating a green market
image, being compliant with regulations, and being competitive with opponents.
2.1.3
Water-eﬃcient practices in meat processing industry
Water management program
Masanet (2008) proposed a strategic and organization-wide water management program as a
cost-eﬀective way to bring about sustainable water eﬃciency improvements. Its key steps are:
(1) Establish commitment and goals;
(2) Line up support and resources;
(3) Conduct a water audit;
(4) Identify water management opportunities;
(5) Prepare an action plan and implementation schedule;
(6) Track results and publicize successes.
The diagnostic and prognostic phases in the ﬁrst three steps of monitoring industrial activities
allow for identiﬁcation of sites and sectors with higher water consumption and wastewater pro-
duction. In the fourth and ﬁfth phase, critical points are found and dealt with. The ﬁnal phase is
to ensure that water eﬃciency improvements do not just happen on a one-time basis, but rather
are continuously identiﬁed and implemented in an ongoing process of continuous improvement
(Masanet, 2008).
Critical points
Major water consumers observed in the case study of Amorim et al. (2007) at a poultry slaughter-
house are industrial slaughtering and cleaning/sanitizing processes, using up to 62% and 9% of the
total water consumed respectively. Matsumura and Mierzwa (2008) identiﬁed scalding, cooling
system, evisceration, receiving area, chillers and logistic sector, these six sectors contribute to 80%
total water consumption in the poultry processing plant. Poultry processing plant analysis in the
study of Kist et al. (2009) has highest water use in the scalding and pre-chilling stages.
Exact composition of the wastewater varies from plant to plant, depending on industrial process
and water consumed per slaughtered bird (Del Nery et al., 2001), but in general the wastewater
mixture is characterized by high concentrations of organic matter, nitrogen and phosphorous.

organic nitrogen) comes from blood, urine and feces, and phosphorous (as organic and inorganic

Water-eﬃciency improvement measures related to freshwater supply
It is found that a regular slaughterhouse generates vast amounts of wastewater and is commonly not
an eﬃcient user of freshwater, though typical water consumption varies considerably in the meat
processing business (Bustillo-Lecompte and Mehrvar, 2015). Amorim et al. (2007) observed
several situations of inadequate water use practices, such as the use of greater ﬂows and longer
washing times than necessary, thus he suggested providing training to raise worker’s awareness.
Nielsen (2016) also highlighted the water-saving possibilities associated with the behaviour of
the utility and production staﬀ. In addition, Nielsen (2016) proposed some technical solutions
for reducing water consumption, including optimize clean-in-place (CIP) routines, minimize water
consumption for vacuum pumps, and minimize blow down from boilers. In the slaughterhouse
studied by Kist et al. (2009), standardization of cleaning procedures with rational water use in
all work shifts and implementation of water meters on a sector-by-sector basis is estimated to
lead to a 13% percent decrease in water consumption. Similarly, rational water use evaluation
conducted by Matsumura and Mierzwa (2008) indicates a potential reduction of 14.9% in total
water demand.
Decrease in water consumption in poultry slaughterhouses can also be achieved by adopting
more water-eﬃcient equipment or technology. Suggestions from literature include: installation
of pressure-activated automatically closing water taps, which showed 40% savings in comparison
with conventional taps; adoption of dry cleaning procedures (Kist et al., 2009); adoption of high-
pressure washer to operate at the same operation existing time (Matsumura and Mierzwa,
2008); replacement of main chiller by air chilling with no water consumption (Matsumura and
Mierzwa, 2008).
Water-eﬃciency improvement measures related to wastewater treatment
Generally, waste streams in slaughterhouses are transported separately within the site and then
combined for bulk treatment. Treatment methods of slaughterhouse wastewater mixture are sim-
ilar to current technologies used in municipal wastewater, i.e., commonly pre-treatment by screen-
ing, settling, blood collection and fat separation, then physio-chemical treatment, followed by
secondary biological treatment, and/or further treatment (Bustillo-Lecompte and Mehrvar,
2015).
There has been great eﬀort in researching the probability to reclaim wastewater at centralized
treatment system by advanced treatment technologies and return to the plant. Development in
diverse treatment technologies with signiﬁcant improvements in eﬃciency and cost-eﬀectiveness
has increased this possibility to a greater extent in recent times. Mittal (2006) suggested using
membrane ﬁltration to treat slaughterhouse wastewater to allow for its reuse and non-disposal
into the WWTP. De Nardi et al. (2011) explored the potential of zero discharge by applying a
combination of sequencing batch reactor, chemical-dissolved-air ﬂotation system, and ultraviolet
(UV) disinfection as a post-treatment of the poultry slaughterhouse anaerobic eﬄuents. Results
showed that the eﬄuent met the quality standard for both potable water and eﬄuent discharge to
the receiving waters (De Nardi et al., 2011). De Nardi et al. (2011)’s case study showed that
if using all the reclaimed water in closed circuits (zero eﬄuent discharge), the well water input
could be reduced by 60%, and if applying semi-closed circuit (24% eﬄuent discharge), well water
input would experience a drop of 46%.
However, it is found that separating the slaughterhouse wastewater streams from diﬀerent pro-
cesses enables optimal treatment for each type of wastewater (Jensen et al., 2014). Concentrated
waste streams may be more suitable for recovery of valuable by-products including high-quality
eﬄuents, biogas, fertilizers, and nutrients, while wastewater with comparatively low strength can
be treated with simple technologies and used as a substitution for freshwater from well.
Many studies have been carried out to investigate the potential of reusing wastewater without
complex treatment at centralized treatment system. For example, Amorim et al. (2007) identiﬁed

Constraints on water-eﬃciency improvement measures
Above-discussed water-eﬃciency improvement measures related to freshwater supply are accepted
and implemented well, however, unfortunately, implementation of water-eﬃcient measures related
to wastewater reuse is likely to be limited. Currently, reuse practices are in general limited to
non-portable water use.
This is mainly due to hygiene concerns and strict regulations. The public are concerned about
being exposed to infectious agents or toxic chemicals through the ingestion of contaminated water
into foods; the ingestion of foods irrigated or harvested from contaminated water; and the ingestion
of foods that have come into contact with contaminated water during processing (Rad and Lewis,
2014).
According to Casani et al. (2006), in Denmark, food industries may be granted an exemption
for a speciﬁc water reuse practice if either the water to be reused is of drinking water quality
or if it can be demonstrated that the treatment process does not pose a risk for the safety of
the food product. Though legal space for use of water of qualities other than drinking water has
been opened with the current legislation, there are great challenges for both companies and public
health authorities regarding knowledge, technical expertise and documentation (Casani et al.,
2005).
There are other factors responsible for hampering successful implementation of wastewater reuse
practices, such as lack of full knowledge on the hazards associated with reuse practices, diﬃculty in
assessing real quality of reused water in real time, poor management of social aspects, no consensus
on the acceptable level of the standards to be applied, and so forth (Salgot, 2008).

Rebound eﬀects added in this study
An improvement in the technical eﬃciency of rinsing water management in the chicken feet pro-
cessing line does not guarantee a lower overall freshwater use of the plant in the end, as it is not
likely that HKScan will simply decrease its groundwater withdrawal.

processes’ performance. For example, it is mentioned in pre-assessment report that a new building
for trucks waiting to unload chicken is about to be taken into use, and consequently extra water is
required for cleaning the room (DMRI, 2015). The saved freshwater could also be used in existing
processes to improve their performance, for instance, clean-in-place (CIP) processes.
Story behind Scenario R1

production facilities is modelled in Scenario R1. It is assumed that using the saved water for

3.2.1
Goal and scope deﬁnition
Goal
The objective of this thesis project is to carry out an absolute assessment of the environmental
and economic consequences for the water-service chain in HKScan Vinderup after the adoption
of rinsing water reuse practice in the chicken feet processing line. It is hoped that this study can
assist HKScan Vinderup make an eco-eﬃcient decision, which can relieve its water stresses, and
simultaneously reduce environmental and economic burden.
Decision context
Decision context of this study is situation A ’micro-level decision support’, according to the clas-
siﬁcation of archetypal goal situations by ISO (2006). Changes in the studied water system are
expected to lead to only small-scale consequences beyond the foreground system, which are not
strong enough to trigger large-scale consequences in the market. The small-scale consequences may
aﬀect the extent to which existing equipment/capacity of the water system is used, but without
resulting in additionally installed or decommissioned equipment/capacity.
Scope
Focus of this study is put on the water system that is related with the chicken feet processing line
during normal operation (i.e., excluding accidents, spills or similar). The water system in question
covers the entire life of water, from abstraction in groundwater well, treatment in waterworks to
tap water quality, heating or cooling in the supply center, use as rinsing water in the chicken feet
processing line, pretreatment at drum sieve, ﬁnal treatment at on-site WWTP, to ﬁnal discharge
to recipient water.
The water system will be analyzed attributionally in isolation, without considering interactions
with market and stakeholders beyond the gate of HKScan Vinderup. Special attention will be paid
to the diﬀerence in the water system’s performance brought about by the changes made to current
system (i.e., practice of reusing rinsing water) and consequences derived from the changes (i.e.,
rebound eﬀects of using saved water for cleaning other production facilities in HKScan Vinderup,
or for increasing chicken feet production).
Function, functional unit, and reference ﬂow
Simply put, the main function of the water system is to supply water for processing steps in
the chicken feet processing line for rinsing purpose. However, each chicken feet processing step
demands freshwater of diﬀerent temperature and in diﬀerent amounts. Due to the diverse use
of freshwater in the chicken feet processing line, a general functional unit (FU) cannot be given.
Thus reference ﬂow as ’declared unit’ is used in this study for deﬁning functional unit (EU-JRC,
2010).
To include the rebound eﬀects and provide an absolute picture, and as suggested by Weidema
and Thrane (2007) that ’instead of product functional outputs, functional units should be deﬁned
in terms of broader functions that can be fulﬁlled by substitutable product alternatives in order
to fully account for the rebound eﬀect’, this study deﬁnes the ’declared unit’ as follows:
Available 7.36E+07 kg freshwater that can be supplied to the chicken feet processing line in HKS-

distribution via existing pipeline network was initially included, while later its contribution turned
out to be lower than 1% of any impact category thus it was excluded from the studied system in
the end.
For to-be-built water-service infrastructure for implementing reuse practice, including pipelines
and inclined ﬁlter, their entire life cycle is taken into consideration, from material extraction and
production in the construction phase, use in the operation phase, to recycling and landﬁll in the
end-of-life phase.
For machinery in the chicken feet processing line under normal operation, such as
plucker and chiller, none of their life phases is not considered.
For background processes, whose foreground process is in construction or operation phase,
for example, chemical use for treating wastewater in the WWTP, these background processes
take upstream processes into consideration (i.e., cradle-to-gate phase). On the other hand, for
background processes, whose foreground process is modelling the end-of-life phase, such as landﬁll
of plastics, they cover only gate-to-cradle phase.

Allocation approach
This study adopts the so-called ’avoided burden’ approach (Frischknecht and Stucki, 2010).
The concept of the approach is that recycling a material from one product system can avoid
extraction and manufacturing of primary material used in product system, and all avoided fu-
ture expenses and emissions are completely attributed to the present product, which delivers the
secondary material after its service life.
In this study, in foreground processes ’Reuse Pipeline End-of-life’ and ’Reuse Inclined Filter End-
of-life’, HDPE and steel is recycled with a ratio of 90% and 84% respectively (description of
material transfer see Table 3.2). In LCA, the recycled material is modelled as a replace for virgin
material, therefore it has a negative environmental impact, i.e., savings from avoided production
of primary material. In CLCC, the recycled material, e.g., scrap, has same market value as virgin
steel, thus is modelled as cost avoidance.

Background system is constructed based on the background inventory compiled in former Subsec-

For each input (e.g., material or energy) of a foreground process, its corresponding dataset of

The next step is to assign values to each input of the foreground process. Value for each input

Above-mentioned web-based database provide well-documented data of average unit processes that
are valid for European countries. However, there is no suitable dataset for recent electricity mix in
Denmark. Therefore, this study develops an external process in EASETECH program to represent
the average electricity mix in Denmark in 2016. This ’Electricity mix in Denmark in 2016’ is

energy), and available datasets for electricity generation from single source in Denmark (e.g.,
electricity generated from wind energy in Denmark) in EASETECH program.
Selection of impact assessment method
This study adopts the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) method ’ILCD recommended - 2013
Prosuit Global NR - DTU resources w/o LT’ (referred as ILCD 2011 in this study), which has
been implemented in EASETECH program. ILCD 2011 is an LCIA method at mid-point level.
It follows the International Reference Life Cycle Data System Handbook (EC-JRC, 2011), but
with some modiﬁcations, e.g., the split between fossil and elements depletion, exclusion of impact
categories of freshwater resource depletion and land use, and etc.
Characterization and Normalization
The ﬁrst step of potential environmental impact assessment is the calculation of impact categories
indicators by using characterization factors. Second step is the calculation of impact category
indicator results relative to reference values. The ﬁnal results are presented as normalized impacts
in person equivalents (PE). 1 PE represents an average European person’s annual impacts.
Table 3.6 outlines the 14 impact categories included in ILCD 2011, and normalization reference
(NR) of each impact category. Detailed description of these impact categories can be found in
the study of Hauschild et al. (2013). NR is said to be obtained from Prosuite project which
was developed speciﬁcally for the recommended ILCD method, according to the documentation
of EASETECH program (Clavreul et al., 2014).
There is another optional step in LCA, i.e., grouping of mid-points to represent potential environ-
mental impacts at the end of cause-eﬀect chain, on human health, ecosystem quality and resource
depletion. However, this weighting step often comes with high level of subjectivity and thus is
statistically uncertain. In this study, since no preference is indicated by decision maker, and for
better representing environmental concerns and identifying trade-oﬀs, mid-points expressed in PE
are used directly in later Results and Discussion chapter.

Assessment of water use in this study focuses on impact of oﬀ-stream water consumption in both
foreground and background system. Water use in foreground and background system is assessed
by freshwater withdrawal impact (FWI) and virtual water footprint (VWF) respectively.
Oﬀ-stream freshwater use is deﬁned as use of water that requires human removal from a natural
body of water or ground aquifer (e.g., pumping or diversion of water for industrial purposes),
while in-stream use is use of water in situ (e.g., navigational transport on a river or water use
for generating hydro-power) (Bayart et al., 2010). The in-stream use is out of the scope of this
study.
In general, there are two impacts that are directly related with water use, with one being eﬀect
of water consumption and the other is water quality impairment. The latter is not investigated in
this study as the nutrient load of WWTP eﬄuent entering natural water bodies is not expected
to change between scenarios. The eﬀect of water quality impairment brought about by the back-
ground processes is quantiﬁed by more specialized indicators such as terrestrial eutrophication or
marine eutrophication in LCA.
Freshwater withdrawal impact
This study adopts FWI method used in the study of Godskesen et al. (2013). FWI only looks at
physical freshwater withdrawn by HKScan Vindeurp from its on-site wells, and assesses potential
impact of freshwater withdrawal on local catchment.

Virtual water footprint
VWF adopts the concept of volumetric water footprint (WF) developed by Hoekstra and Hung
(2002) and virtual water proposed by Allan (1998). It looks at the water used to create the goods
and services that the foreground system processes consume and use. Therefore, VWF represents
water consumption in the background system.
VWF is obtained directly from the life cycle inventory (LCI) compiled by EASETECH program,
without any further treatment. As suggested by Hoekstra (2016), volumetric measure of water
itself can represent water use, therefore there is no extra step needed to transform water footprint
to another metric. In addition, low spatial resolution of the water inventory makes it not possible
to be used to assess the impact of water use in the background system on speciﬁc local catchments,
as impacts of water use vary greatly as a function of location. Low spatial resolution of the water
inventory (or say strong geological uncertainty) is also one of the reasons why impact category of
freshwater resource depletion is excluded from ILCD 2011 in EASETECH program (Clavreul
et al., 2014).

a description of the processes they are used in,. They are not really oﬀ-stream water use, thus
should be avoided (Pfister et al., 2016). In this study, VWF takes into account freshwater from

Economic assessment: CLCC
This study draws on classiﬁcation of cost types proposed by Martinez-Sanchez et al. (2015)
(i.e., budget costs, transfers and externality costs), and carries out a conventional life cycle costing
(CLCC) assessment of the studied water system by following unit cost method (UCM) approach
(Parthan et al., 2012). Key steps for CLCC are presented below.
Cost item categorization

Firstly, cost items (i.e., background processes) are classiﬁed based on their cost type (i.e., budget
costs, transfers or externality costs).
Budget costs are internal costs, incurred by the agent. Martinez-Sanchez et al. (2015) dif-
ferentiated between ’one-oﬀ’ budget costs occurring once in the lifetime of a technology (e..g.,
capital investment or back-end cost) and ’recurring’ budget costs, for example operational and
maintenance costs.
Transfers are monetary ﬂows that only represent income distribution between stakeholders while
not leading to re-allocation of resources such as land and labour, or welfare change in society.
For example, taxes and subsidies, or general taxes such as Value Added Tax (VAT) (Martinez-
Sanchez et al., 2015).
Externality costs occur outside the economic system. They represent eﬀects on the welfare of
individuals caused by activities and are not compensated. They could be environmental, e.g.,
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, or non-environmental in the form of noise and etc. Any extern-
ality priced by an authority and covered by a stakeholder within the system becomes a transfer
(Martinez-Sanchez et al., 2015).
In this study, there are some special cases where several background processes have to be repres-
ented by one cost item because their costs are aggregated together. For example, acquisition cost
of groundwater includes electricity used for abstracting groundwater as well as costs of related
upstream processes; revenue of chicken feet is already the diﬀerence between cost of unprocessed
chicken feet and price of processed chicken feet product; recycling fee of HDPE covers costs for
process stream, process water, electricity and probably labour work needed in the recycling pro-
cess.
Monetary appraisal
The next step is to assign monetary values to each cost item. For each cost item, two characteristic
parameters are needed: physical parameter that represents the quantity of the cost item (e.g., 1
kWh electricity); and economic parameter that describes the unit cost of this cost item (e.g.,
1 DKK for 1 kWh electricity). Cost for each item is calculated by simply multiplying the two
parameters (e.g., 1 kWh × 1 DKK/kWh) together.
Note that, in this study, null discount rate is applied when converting the lump-sum amounts
of ’one-oﬀ’ cost items into annuities during inventory compilation stage. In other words, equal
importance is assigned to all costs regardless of the time of occurrence.

Thirdly, cost for each unit foreground process is calculated by summing up costs for all individual

Life-cycle oriented evaluation often faces challenges of high uncertainty and variability. For com-
parative life-cycle evaluation studies, which are focusing on the changes between scenarios, it is
important to distinguish between actual diﬀerences and those due to uncertainty and variability.

Contribution analysis
Contribution analysis is carried out to identify signiﬁcant issues, and signiﬁcant issues can be
inventory items, impact categories, modelling choices and method assumptions, and etc. (EU-
JRC, 2010).
Attention of the contribution analysis in this study is paid to foreground processes that contribute
to more than 5% of any impact category, and inventory items that collectively contribute to 90%
of their foreground processes’ overall impacts.

It is noticeable that this study does not use any discount rate to diﬀerentiate between money
values in the diﬀerent times. This is mainly due to the intention of aligning CLCC with LCA. As
both economic and environmental assessments carried out in this study are based on the common
inventory compiled in Section 3.2.3, both environmental assessment model and economic assess-
ment model express inventory processes as ﬂows per the same reference ﬂow. Therefore, cost in
this study is calculated as equal annual cost, which means that no inﬂation, investment opportun-
ities, personal consumption preferences and so forth are accounted for (see discussion in Section
A.1.3 in Appendix). The second reason for adopting null discount rate is the intention of following
’strong sustainability’ principle. However, cost calculation as applied in this study may fail to
reﬂect real economic performance of the studied water system and overestimate avoided costs in
R Scenarios.
Sub-conclusions
• In view of economic beneﬁts, implementation of rinsing water reuse practice provides huge
advantages as it helps to reduce operation costs of upstream and downstream processes.
In comparison to the avoided costs (1.05E+06 DKK in Scenario R0), CLCC of the reuse
practice itself is insigniﬁcant (1.62E+03 DKK in Scenario R0).
• Electricity mix is found to be a dominant cost item. Total cost for electricity mix accounts
for the largest share in CLCC of every scenario, due to its relatively high unit cost and heavy
electricity consumption in the studied water system. It as well plays an important role in
terms of cost avoidance. Another important cost item is chicken feet, due to whose revenue,
Scenario R2 turns out to be the most favourable alternative, with a lowest CLCC.

A completeness check is carried out for the operation inventory of ’Wastewater Treatment’ pro-
cess. Operation inventory of ’Wastewater Treatment’ process is selected because ﬁrstly this process
is a signiﬁcant contributor to overall assessment results. Secondly, this process’s two background

based on the ﬁndings of the ﬁrst two steps, judge the achieved completeness, and then discuss

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This study evaluates the environmental and economic consequences of implementing rinsing water
reuse practice in the chicken feet processing line in HKScan Vinderup for processes involved in
the water system. In total, four scenarios are studied, including Baseline representing current
situation of the water system, Scenario R0 depicting the water system after the adoption of
reuse practice, and Scenario R1 and R2, both of which are developed based on Scenario R0 but
with the addition of rebound eﬀect (R1: using up the saved freshwater in ’Quicker Cleaning’
process; R2: using a fraction of the saved freshwater in ’Extra Chicken Feet Production’ process).
Comparative assessments are carried out to evaluate these four scenarios by means of life cycle
assessment (LCA), freshwater withdrawal impact (FWI), virtual water footprint (VWF), and
conventional life cycle costing (CLCC). Results of diﬀerent assessments provide speciﬁc information
that complements each other, leading to conclusions as follows.
Implementing reuse practice in the chicken feet processing line is positive all round. Environmental
and economic burdens caused by the reuse practice itself are negligible, if compared with its
beneﬁcial eﬀects on all processes along the studied water chain, from water-supplying, water-
using, to wastewater-treating processes.
LCA results of Scenario R0 show that adopting reuse practice can reduce approximately 30% of the
potential environmental impacts caused by the studied water system in Baseline. Among all impact
categories, depletion of abiotic resources, reverse base (RD-reserve) experiences the greatest drop
of 70 personal equivalent (PE). Ecotoxicity freshwater (ETOX) and depletion of abiotic resources,
fossil (RD-fossil) come next with a similar decline of roughly 50 PE. Human toxicity, cancer
eﬀect (HTOX-C) and climate change (CC) decreases by 33 and 28 PE respectively, and the rest
categories all see a smaller decrease of around 10 PE. Decrease in electricity consumption for
heating water is the greatest contributor to the reduced impacts in most categories, except for
RD-reserve, ETOX, HTOX-C, whose changes are owing to lower energy and chemical consumption
at WWTP for treating wastewater.
Indicated by FWI and VWF results, reuse practice not only helps improve water-eﬃciency of the
chicken feet processing line inside the factory, thus lowering freshwater withdrawal impacts on
local catchment, but also has a positive impact on water use beyond the gate on a global scale.
When shifting from Baseline to Scenario R0, amount of groundwater abstracted from on-site wells
decreases by 62%, leading to a lower FWI on the Limfjorden catchment (from 500 PE to 242 PE).
Consumption of virtual water, which is embedded in background processes (all materials or energy
used in water-service processes), is lowered by 31%.
Seeing from the results of CLCC, reuse practice is economically viable. It helps the studied water
system achieve a net saving of around 1.05 mDKK. Comparatively, CLCC of the reuse practice
itself is insigniﬁcant (1.62 tDKK). Among all cost items, electricity is found to be most important
ﬁrstly because it is deeply involved in almost every water-service process, and secondly due to

its relatively high unit cost. Implementation of reuse practice results in lower volumes of water
that need to be heated, cooled, and treated, thus leading to electricity cost savings along the
water-service chain.
In conclusion, reuse practice implemented in the chicken feet processing line is able to upgrade
the production line and more importantly the water-service chain. All processes along the water-
service chain beneﬁt from the reuse practice in many senses, namely, resource saving, environ-
mental protection and cost avoidance. Since this reuse practice is quite simple to be implemented
and there is a great amount of similar processes in meat processing industry, it is expected that
this reuse practice can be replicated from HKScan Vinderup to the others, leading to signiﬁcant
positive eﬀects.
However, when reuse practice liberates water, a scarce factor for HKScan Vinderup at present, it is
very likely that HKScan Vinderup will use the saved water in pursuit of proﬁt or cost avoidance.
From a product focus, Scenario R1 and R2 perform as well as Scenario R0 as they have the
same water-eﬃciency (if calculated as chicken feet product divided by the water used for chicken
feet production). From the perspective of freshwater use at the factory site and FWI on local
catchment, Scenario R1 is the worst among all R scenarios, causing the highest FWI on Limfjorden
catchment (500 PE), which is as same as in Baseline. Then comes Scenario R2 (302 PE), followed
by Scenario R0 (242 PE).
The rebound eﬀects are revealed more comprehensively by assessments from a life cycle perspective.
As shown by the comparison between LCA and CLCC results of Scenario R0 and those of Scenario
R1, rebound eﬀect can be a double-edged sword. From environmental perspective, using up saved
water for ’Quicker Cleaning’ in Scenario R1 results in a one-fold increase in impacts caused by
WWTP operation (RD-reserve, ETOX and HTOX-C rises by 35, 24, and 17 PE respectively
compared with Scenario R0). On the other hand, ’Quicker Cleaning’ contributes to lower electricity
consumption, which leads to reductions in impacts such as RD-fossil (-13 PE), RD-reserve (-10
PE), and ETOX (-8 PE). In addition, from economic point of view, Scenario R1 is clearly better
oﬀas it achieves an extra cost saving of 0.5 mDKK.
By comparison with Scenario R0, Scenario R2 is obviously worse oﬀin terms of all environmental
impact categories. Rebound eﬀect of increasing chicken feet production leads to extra electricity
demand for operating machines in the chicken processing line and intensiﬁes electricity consump-
tion in freshwater heating and cooling processes. Contributions from those processes oﬀset the
savings achieved by reuse practice, and the net impacts of RD-fossil, CC and etc. in Scenario R2
even surpass those in Baseline. In contrast, Scenario R2 turns out to be most favorable regarding
cost avoidance (2.21 mDKK compared with Baseline). Processing chicken feet for extra four hours
every day in one year generates an extra revenue, as high as 1.83 mDKK, though the rebound ef-
fect balances a fraction of cost savings in upstream and downstream water-service processes owing
to reuse practice.
In conclusion, as illustrated by Scenario R1 and R2, assessing water-eﬃciency improvement meas-
ures not from an absolute and holistic view may lead to very optimistic and misleading ﬁndings.
Therefore, in addition to the question of how could the most economical use of freshwater be
organized in food processing factories, it is of great signiﬁcance for us to consider the following
questions: whether the saved water is kept in nature or allocated to other water-using processes?
what speciﬁc water quality is required by this water-using process? if the saved water is fully
utilized?

Limitations & Future perspectives
Here presents limitations of this study, some of which could also be considered as future work.
Addressing those limitations would provide a more complete picture of the studied water system.
However, it is noticeable that, in addition to those discussed below, there are some other aspects
that cannot be covered by eco-eﬃciency assessment as applied in this study, e.g., social well-being
and political acceptance.
Incorporation of expert judgment
Table 6.1 summarizes diﬀerent types of uncertainties and variabilities identiﬁed in diﬀerent phases
of this study. Some have been discussed in former sections, while the rest due to limited time and
more importantly lack of data cannot be discussed.
The most problematic issues of this study are subjectivity and qualitative gaps including lack
of awareness of the occurrence of a ﬂow, incomplete knowledge of impact mechanisms, and so
forth. Expert judgment based on process understanding would be a reliable means of dealing with
above-mentioned issues. Uncertainty calculation could be conducted to support expert judgment,
while given the limitations of uncertainty calculation to reﬂect the true uncertainty, uncertainty
calculation is not suggested to be used as a substitute for expert judgment.
Incorporation of stakeholders’ preference
In this study, environmental assessment results are kept separate at mid-point level in order to
represent environmental concerns and to allow comparison with other relevant studies. Another
reason for no weighting step conducted in this study is the lack of information on stakeholders’
preference.
Multi-criteria analysis (MCA) could be adopted for choosing the optimal alternative, if it is possible
to get deep involvement of stakeholders or at least their clearly-stated preference.
Endpoint
metrics based on MCA would be very helpful for decision making. For one thing, they are easily
comprehensible. For another, they have the advantage of incorporating both modeling and expert
knowledge and stakeholders’ preference (Ness et al., 2007).
Investigating the role of rinsing water in wastewater treatment
Future research is needed for investigating the eﬀect of reuse practice on downstream wastewa-
ter system, including wastewater composition and treatment performance, for the model better
representing reality.
This study only provides a simpliﬁed representation of the water-service chain. All water ﬂows
modelled in this study have only one component, i.e., water, though they actually diﬀer from each
other in temperature, content of COD, TN, TP, pathogen and so forth. COD, TN and TP are
important for many LCA impact categories, e.g., freshwater eutrophication. Their concentrations
in the eﬄuent are of signiﬁcance to determine the eﬄuent tax, which is accounted for in CLCC.

It is estimated that with much lower strength, rinsing water, which is collected and sent to WWTP
together with other process wastewater streams, has a ’dilution’ eﬀect. In this study, the reuse
practice leads to a reduction in volume while not aﬀecting nutrient loading, thus the iﬄuent enter-
ing WWTP is more concentrated. But how this concentrated inﬀuent would aﬀect the performance
of wastewater treatment processes is unknown. Some studies in literature report that wastewater
treatment eﬃciency can be improved if treating wastewater from diﬀerent process areas separately
instead of treating the mixture. However, there are also studies saying that the dilution eﬀect (i.e.,
mixing rinsing water with high-strength process wastewater) is necessary, as otherwise there would
be inhibition eﬀect on biological treatment due to too high nutrient concentrations (Jensen et al.,
2014).
Investigating the possibility of upgrading WWTP conﬁguration
Wastewater system is one place where abundant opportunities exist to address water scarcity
through reclamation and reuse, and there do exist technologies that can complement existing
WWTP in terms of not only nutrient removal but also resource recovery, without making tre-
mendous changes to existing facilities.
There is already discussion on reclaiming and reusing eﬄuent from WWTP in the pre-assessment
report (DMRI, 2015). As discussed, the treated wastewater could be delivered to other factories
in industrial symbiosis, e.g., nearby mink factory or recycling station. If treated to drinking water
quality, the water could also be reused inside the slaughterhouse. While the latter is likely to
be too challenging and expensive to be implemented, the former is a promising alternative. In
addition to freshwater resource, there are as well potential valuable resources of nitrogen (N) and
phosphorous (P) in the process wastewater stream. High levels of N and P cause heavy burden
for the WWTP on the one hand, but on the other hand provide great opportunity for resource
recovery.
It would be interesting to add another scenario of upgrading current WWTP by adding a side-
stream unit, which treats a fraction of inﬂuent and recovers resources, and compare it with reuse
scenarios in this study to investigate how the position of reuse practice in the water-service chain
would aﬀect the performance of entire water system environmentally and economically.
Modelling water value along the water-service chain
Cost is used as the focus of the economic assessment in this study instead of ’value’. This is
due to the intention of aligning economic assessment with LCA. For example, LCA excludes the
machinery for chicken feet processing during normal operation hours, thus accordingly price of
chicken feet product should not be taken into account. For another instance, in Scenario R2,
machinery for the extra hours’ chicken feet processing is accounted for in LCA to model the
rebound eﬀect, thus corresponding revenue of chicken feet should be taken into account.
It would be interesting to carry out a separate economic assessment about water value along the
water chain, i.e., how the value of water is added during water supply processes, and exchanged
with chicken feet during water-using processes, and lost when mixing with other process wastewa-
ter, and made up by wastewater treatment processes (illustrated in Figure 6.1). However, this
requires site-speciﬁc data with good quality.

Appendix A
Additional Background
A.1
Eco-eﬃciency assessment
The selection of an appropriate water-eﬃciency improvement technology, which is sustainable from
both environmental and economic aspects, requires a structured and integrated evaluation.
Life cycle assessment (LCA) and Life cycle costing (LCC) are two major life-cycle oriented ap-
proaches provided in literature to solve speciﬁc partial problems of technology assessment and
decision-making. LCA focuses on revealing life cycle-related environmental burdens and beneﬁts
of a product or a system, including all involved processes and resources. It supports the develop-
ment of environmentally intended improvement measures and design of eco-friendly products and
production processes (ISO, 2006). In contrast, LCC is a cost management method for the evalu-
ation of all economic consequences (e.g., costs, revenues, and cash ﬂows) and monetary trade-oﬀs
occurring in an object’s life cycle. It can help to identify cost drivers, assess proﬁtability of a
product or production technology, and compare strategy, etc.
Though holistic instruments for aligning environmental dimension with economic dimension are not
well developed, there are already theory-based integrated framework proposed to close the gaps,
such as the studies of G¨otze et al. (2014) and Bierer et al. (2015). The following section explores
existing applications of LCA and LCC in assessing water-eﬃciency improvement technologies, and
ways to couple LCA and LCC for a structured and comprehensive evaluation.
A.1.1
Environmental assessment
Water footprint
Water footprint was proposed initially by Hoekstra and Hung (2002), as a measure of con-
sumptive and degradative freshwater use. The consumptive water footprint is composed of green
water (i.e., rainwater) and blue water (i.e., surface water or groundwater). Degradative water
footprint, referred as grey water footprint, represents the volume of water required to assimilate
pollutants entering freshwater bodies (Aldaya et al., 2012). The green, blue and grey water foot-
prints provide useful information on how water resources are allocated to diﬀerent purposes and
use of waste assimilation capacity related to the activities.
Water footprint has been widely used in existing studies as a key indicator for improving water
resource management.
Volumetric measures of green and blue water are not only able to ac-
complish the purpose of visualizing global impact of freshwater withdrawal, but also introduces
supply-chain thinking in the ﬁeld of water management and is thus helpful in analyzing the link
between human consumption and withdrawal of freshwater (Hoekstra, 2016).

The water footprint of one single process step (a unit process) is the basic building block of all
water footprint accounts. The water footprint of a product is the sum of the water footprints of
all process steps taken to produce the product. The water footprint of a business is the sum of the
water footprints of the ﬁnal products produced by the business, which includes the operational
water footprint of the business as well as its supply-chain water footprint (Aldaya et al., 2012).
There are two main limitations regarding using water footprint as an indicator. First limitation
of water footprint is being unsuitable as a stand-alone assessment tool for consumptive freshwater
use. Exclusive focus on water resource may lead to burden-shifting to other environmental impacts.
Take reclaiming water resource at WWTP as an example, construction and operation of advanced
equipment incur not only signiﬁcant capital and operation and maintenance (O&M) cost, but also
consumption of resources such as energy, coal for chemicals, and so forth (Theregowda et al.,
2015).
Another limitation of water footprint is being not comprehensive enough to account for subsequent
eﬀects of degradative freshwater use. For instance, even though grey water footprint has taken
into consideration the reduction in water quality, further consequences resulting from the water
of lower quality, such as eutrophication or ecotoxicity to aquatic organisms, are not included.
Therefore, for reﬂecting overall environmental performance of a technology, LCA is necessary.
Life cycle assessment
LCA is used to evaluate the environmental performance of goods, processes and services (collect-
ively termed products). It is a compilation and comparative analysis of the inputs, outputs and
potential environmental impacts along the life cycle of products: from production of raw materials
to ﬁnal disposal of waste generated.
There are four principle stages of an LCA study: (1) goal and scope deﬁnition; (2) inventory
analysis; (3) impact assessment; and ﬁnally (4) interpretation (ISO, 2006). LCA starts with the
deﬁnition of goal and scope, which is followed by life cycle inventory (LCI), which compiles re-
sources use and emissions for each process. At the third stage of life cycle impact assessment
(LCIA), environmental impact categories of interest are chosen, and characterization models are
applied to quantitatively model the impacts of each emission or resource use according to environ-
mental mechanism. Potential impacts are characterized to be represented by indicators of certain
impact category, then normalized to be represented in the unit of person equivalent (PE), and
optionally weighted to show the overall impact on one speciﬁc protection area.
LCA has been applied in existing studies for evaluating water system and proved its strengths. For
example, LCA is used for evaluating current situation of a WWTP and identifying improvement
alternatives (Pasqualino et al., 2009), for supporting early stage research and development of
novel wastewater resource technology (Fang et al., 2016), and etc. There are as well LCI studies
to quantitatively model and evaluate the life cycle inventories of a range of wastewater treatment
scenarios (Foley et al., 2010).
However, in current common LCA practice, impact on freshwater resources is not included due
to the challenge of regionalization and dynamization.
Both LCI and LCIA face the challenge
of high uncertainties due to spatial and temporal variability (Berger and Finkbeiner, 2010;
Pfister et al., 2009). For LCI, background inventory data may change as a function of site (e.g.,
diﬀerent technologies) or as a function of time (e.g., inter-annual variability of consumption or
trade). In LCIA, using water of 1 m3 at two locations do not necessarily have the same impact,
because one could occur in a water-scarce catchment and the other one may be located in a water-
rich catchment. To evaluate whether one activity contributes to an immediate problem of water
scarcity within the catchment where this activity takes place, one needs to put the water use of this
activity in the context of blue/green water scarcity in the catchment where the water abstraction
occurs. Similarly, the grey water needs to be regarded in the context of the water pollution level in
the catchment. In addition to the need of aggregation of more site-speciﬁc information, weighting
factor in LCIA need to take into account time-related information such as climate.

A.1.2
Economic assessment
Environmentally-intended approaches like LCA support the reduction of environmental damages
but they do not make clear contributions to cost savings or corporate proﬁts (Kokubu et al.,
2013). ISO (2006) clearly states that ’LCA typically does not address the economic or social
aspects of a product’, and it is also written that ’but the life cycle approach and methodologies
described in this International Standard can be applied to these other aspects’.
Among various economic tools provided by economics, life cycle costing (LCC) is considered
suitable to be aligned to LCA study. LCC uses cost as unit of account to represent economic
burden, which is consistent with LCA studies which normally use environmental burden as unit
of account (Reich, 2005).
LCC is an application of life cycle thinking in determining the most cost-eﬀective and thus most
competitive solution of a product or service (Woodward, 1997). It takes to into account all
the actual costs and revenues associated with an asset or investment over its economic life-cycle,
including raw material extraction, production, distribution, operation, maintenance, and ﬁnal
disposal. LCC can provide a comprehensive assessment to detect the potential cost drivers and
cost savings, compare total costs and trade-oﬀs, plan technology developments for new product
oﬀerings, inform a decision to upgrade or replace capital equipment (Hunkeler et al., 2008).
Budget costs, transfers and externality costs
There is no standard or certiﬁcation for an LCC. A variety of methods and approaches has been
developed under the umbrella of LCC, due to heterogeneity and various application scenarios of the
cases being analyzed. The focus of this section is put on the LCC tools that are LCA-compatible
and are considered helpful in providing an additional dimension for evaluating the ﬁnancial aspect
of the system studied in LCA.
There are numerous deﬁnitions of what should and should not signify (Reich, 2005). This study
adopts naming principles of Hunkeler et al. (2008) and Swarr et al. (2011), and Martinez-
Sanchez et al. (2015) ’s classiﬁcation, for clearly explaining purposes of a variety of economic
tools developed under the umbrella of LCC, and without discarding any other deﬁnition.
Overall, costs can be distinguished between ’internal’ and ’external’ (Hunkeler et al., 2008;
Swarr et al., 2011). Internal costs (also termed ’private costs’) are monetary costs occurring
both inside and outside the system.
Internal costs can be measured either in market prices
or in factor prices, the latter are market prices excluding transfers (e.g., taxes, subsidies, fees
and duties used to distribute income between diﬀerent agents in society but do not represent
any resource reallocation). External costs occur outside the economic system (also called ’non-
marketed goods/services as they have no direct monetary value in the market).
Martinez-Sanchez et al. (2015) diﬀerentiates between three types of costs, i.e., budget costs,
transfers and externality costs (as shown in Table A.1) when developing their cost model. Budget
costs and transfers belong to the ’internal’ group, while externality costs are characterized as
’external’.

Conventional, environmental and societal LCC
Conventional LCC represents traditional ﬁnancial assessment. It is also termed as ’ﬁnancial LCC’
in the study of Reich (2005), and the cost is termed ’ﬁnancial costs’. Financial cost is deﬁned
as the present value of all monetary costs for the system studied, negative or positive (Reich,
2005). For example, if a conventional LCC is done for a system under LCA, all costs for fulﬁlling
the functional unit should be included, including ’one-oﬀ’ costs such as investment costs and
decommissioning costs, and ’recurring’ costs like operative costs and sales revenues (a negative
cost) (Martinez-Sanchez et al., 2015). In conventional LCC, there is no environmental eﬀects,
other than those that have an economic impact on the system (e.g., through taxation of emissions).
Conventional LCC is commonly used in cases where environmental aspects are not in focus,
therefore ﬁnancial costs only include budget costs and transfers.
It is applied commonly for
assessing economic feasibility/viability of treatment solutions, identifying the economically best-
performing solution, and evaluating the economic consequences of speciﬁc solution’s implementa-
tion (Martinez-Sanchez et al., 2015).
Externalities are deﬁned as positive or negative impacts that one economic agent causes on another
agent and not compensated within the economic system. For example, direct and indirect damages
caused by pollutants emitted during the manufacture of a product or rendering of services, which
are not paid by the producers or the consumers nor considered in production or consumption
decisions (research Council et al., 2010).
Environmental LCC, as deﬁned by Martinez-Sanchez et al. (2015), extends conventional LCC
by adding transfers anticipated to be established in the near future, i.e., externalities expected to
be internalized in monetary terms in a time perspective relevant for the decision being assessed.
Environmental LCC is typically intended to supplement LCA study. Its results represent not only
net costs/savings but also the allocation between stakeholders, therefore could be used to identify
compensation between stakeholders.
Societal LCC includes budget costs and externality costs, both accounted for in shadow prices.
It is intended to show the economic eﬃciency of speciﬁc scenarios on a societal level, in order to
estimate welfare losses and gains related to resource reallocation.
Monetary valuation is the attempt to include externalities into LCC by translating environmental
problems into a one-dimensional monetary unit. It is also often referred as ’non-market valuation’,
’shadow pricing’ (Gluch and Baumann, 2004), or ’weighting’ in studies where LCC is taken as
the subsequent tool after LCA. The valuation weighting factors are used as other weighting factors
in classic LCIA practice at midpoint or endpoint level. There are diﬀerent ways to assign values,
for example: contingent valuation method, which uses surveys to estimate people’s willingness-
to-pay for the commons, applied in the study of Ahlroth and Finnveden (2011); market price
method, whose principle is that resource depletion and shifts in material demand will have an
impact on market prices, applied in ReCiPe (Huijbregts et al., 2016); abatement cost method,
which is carried out by determination of the marginal unit reduction cost based on abatement
technology (Davidson et al., 2005); averting behaviour method, where diﬀerent eco-taxes are used
to estimate the monetary value of speciﬁc emissions on the hypothesis that the taxes represent
the willingness-to-pay of the society (Eldh and Johansson, 2006); and so forth.
A.1.3
Integrating environmental and economic dimensions
With increasing pressure to achieve higher productivity with reduced environmental impacts,
business organizations require assessment tools that enable them to account for all inputs and
outputs to their operations with a view to support eco-eﬃcient decisions that simultaneously
improve economic and environmental performance (Kokubu et al., 2013).
Parallel application, extended LCA and LCC
Bierer et al. (2015) categorized existing approaches and studies for integrating life-cycle environ-

mental and economic assessments into three groups, which is taken as the basis for the following
discussion.
The ﬁrst category refers to parallel application of LCA and LCC. LCA and LCC can be carried
out either entirely separately (subgroup 1) or based on common goal and scope deﬁnition, life
cycle concept, and database, but speciﬁc data collection and independent modelling (subgroup
2). Keeping LCA and LCC entirely separate (subgroup 1) can limit the signiﬁcance of results
and hardly realize synergy eﬀects concerning data acquisition (Bierer et al., 2015). Apart from
leaving the trade-oﬀs and relationship between LCC and LCA uncharacterized, methods belonging
to subgroup 1 limit the inﬂuence and relevance of LCA as a decision making tool and miss the
economic relevance when cost analysis neglect LCA scope and ﬁndings (Norris, 2001). Com-
paratively, carrying out LCA and LCC in parallel but with integration on the basis of common
scopes, scenarios, life cycle inventory and database (subgroup 2) is more favorable as it is able to
cover both dimensions of eco-eﬃciency in a comparable manner and deliver valuable insights.
In the second category, LCA is taken as the foundation and extended with the addition of a separate
economic impact category.
Life cycle cost assessment for example, is a means for integrating
economic and environmental cost information into the LCA framework (Warren and Weitz,
1994). Warren and Weitz (1994) proposed a simple but useful breakdown of life cycle costs
that takes into account the following three types of costs: conventional costs, liability costs,
and environmental costs. The materials and energy ﬂows identiﬁed in the LCI stage provide a
detailed template for assigning individual costs to speciﬁc items or activities. However, studies
in this category are generally environmentally-intended. Monetary consequences without direct
connection to the environmental impacts (e.g., personnel cost) usually remain unconsidered, and
the results are not suﬃcient for economic decision making (Bierer et al., 2015). In most existing
studies, applied methods for cost calculation at the stage of LCIA are not speciﬁed, making it
diﬃcult for further research to replicate ﬁndings of these studies (Bierer et al., 2015). Besides,
there is a big challenge in selecting appropriate characterization factors and normalization factors
to make economic impact comparable with other impact categories in LCA.
The third kind of integration approach takes LCC as the leading concept, for instance, envir-
onmental LCC. This approach aims at converting the multi-dimensional environmental problems
into a one-dimensional monetary unit by assigning values to environmental impacts. The cost
in environmental LCC is expressed with the same functional unit, system boundary and actor
perspective as in LCA (Hunkeler et al., 2008). The environmental externality costs are cal-
culated based on impact pathway analysis, which consists of four principal steps, i.e., emission,
dispersion, impact and cost. ExternE project of European Commission formalizes an impact path-
way approach for determining externality costs of health impacts associated with anthropogenic
emissions via the Impact Pathway Approach (Bickel and Friedrich, 2004). There has also
been a signiﬁcant research eﬀort in measuring the external costs caused by electricity production
(Longo et al., 2008), or evaluating the impact of air emissions released (Theregowda et al.,
2016). The common limitation of approaches in this category is that only monetary target values
are calculated while environmental eﬀects are simply taken as cost components but not as stand-
alone targets (Bierer et al., 2015). In addition, there are both environmental externalities and
non-environmental externalities that are diﬃcult to be accounted for in monetary values and thus
excluded. In Hunkeler et al. (2008)’s study of environmental LCC, he mentioned the option
of including non-monetized environmental impacts and emphasized the limitation on monetary
targets.
Summarizing from discussion above on approaches to integrating LCA and LCC, it seems that an
evaluation of both dimensions in detail based on a common foundation (Bierer et al., 2015), and
presentation of two tools’ results separately and combined (Reich, 2005), would realize synergy
eﬀect to the largest degree without lowering level of details of both sides. However, there are two
main challenges on the way: alignment (i.e., construction of common evaluation foundation) and
combination ( i.e., identiﬁcation of trade-oﬀs from results).

Challenge of aligning models
Bierer et al. (2015) recommended using common foundation with concerted goal, scope, life cycle
deﬁnitions, system models, and widely shared database for ensuring consistency between the two
methods. The major diﬃculty in constructing the common foundation comes from the divergence
regarding treatment of time in LCA and LCC, which is derived from inherent characteristics of
LCA and LCC.
The ﬁrst time-related divergence lies in the scope and life phases taken into account in LCA and
LCC. The scope of LCC usually covers a lot of phase-speciﬁc monetary eﬀects directly or indirectly
caused by the product system. Inputs from upstream processes which are essential for producing
one product, are included and reﬂected in the price of the product, which is the biggest part of the
acquisition costs for the next actor. The costs of upstream processes (including the costs of other
related objects in their life cycle) are aggregated and passed from one actor to the next. Similarly,
economic eﬀects of downstream processes could be reﬂected by residual values (Bierer et al.,
2015). In contrast, such nearly complete inclusion of relevant eﬀects seems to be rather unusual
in LCA (Stazi et al., 2012). In response to this, the scope of LCA might need to be extended
accordingly in order to evaluate the relevant environmental eﬀects with high signiﬁcance.
The second time-related divergence lies in dealing of the time dimension in assessment models of
LCC and LCA (Norris, 2001). While LCA studies are usually based on steady-state models where
the timing of processes and their environmental burdens typically remain disregarded (Norris,
2001), in LCC time is a decisive factor (Bierer et al., 2015).
There are two dimensions for
alignment, i.e., make LCA as quasi-dynamic model or make LCC as a steady-state model. If
applying the former solution, functional unit of LCA may change per year, life-cycle processes
would spread out in time, and LCIA would partially spread out in time. If using the latter solution
of using LCC as a steady-state model, all net costs have to be speciﬁed on a yearly average basis
and time horizon needs to be set as inﬁnite.
Common practice at present is using the latter
solution by means of discounting (Gluch and Baumann, 2004). The most common technique of
making costs from diﬀerent times comparable thus possible to add together is discounting future
costs to a net present value. The time value of money expressed as a discount rate, depends on
inﬂation, cost of capital, investment opportunities and personal consumption preferences (Gluch
and Baumann, 2004). The equation for net present value is shown below, where n is the number
of estimated future cost F over time t discounted with the interest rate i.
