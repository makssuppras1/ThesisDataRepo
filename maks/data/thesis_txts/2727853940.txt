Abstract
The Copenhagen Municipality has undertaken ambitious measures to address climate adaptation and
urban development challenges, making substantial investments in comprehensive solutions. A pivotal
component of this initiative involves augmenting the city’s nature through a city nature strategy,
aiming to plant 100.000 new trees, including 10.000 street trees, by 2025. This study explores the
impact of adding 10.000 extra street trees on the NOx removal capacity of the current street trees
in Copenhagen, employing the MyTree model to assess ecosystem services provided by urban trees.
Drawing data from 180 street trees across nine different species in the city, the research revealed
that the existing street trees of these species are projected to remove 23,1 kg of NOx over 20 years.
Notably, the tree species, Sorbus sp., demonstrated the highest NOx absorption capacity per square
meter, while the tree species, Prunus sp., exhibited the lowest removal capacity. Adding 10.000 street
trees led to a significant (23[%]) improvement in NOx removal capacity over 20 years, resulting in a
22,6% increase in economic benefits through air pollution removal.
The study also examined the influence of sun exposure on NOx removal capacity among the nine
species, considering different crown conditions.
Local tree effects were found to be pivotal in
determining NOx absorption, and the impact of leaf area on NOx removal capacity was found
to fluctuate over time due to factors such as tree growth, mortality, new plantings, and natural
regeneration.
The findings contribute to the understanding of the role of street trees in climate adaptation and
urban planning. Integrating the air pollution removal effects of street trees into future urban planning
endeavors will assist climate adaptation professionals, urban planners, architects, and politicians in
securing economic funding for the municipality. Moreover, the study suggests the potential extension
of mapping tree characteristics and environmental factors to assess other ecosystem services, thus
fostering a comprehensive evaluation of the city’s existing nature for future climate adaptation
projects.

1. Introduction
Trees play a crucial role in urban areas, providing numerous essential ecosystem services that can be
categorized into utility and amenity benefits. The utility benefits of trees encompass their measurable
environmental functions. These include hydrological effects such as evaporation from intercepted
rain and evapotranspiration, that help regulate water balance (Wang et al., 2008), improved water
quality by facilitating water infiltration into the root zone and reducing soil contamination through
phytoremediation (Pulford and Watson, 2003). Furthermore, trees have a positive impact on the
urban climate by influencing hydrological processes, providing shade, and acting as wind breaks.
These effects mitigate the Urban Heat Island Effect (UHI) and lower energy consumption for cooling
purposes (Bolund and Hunhammar, 1999; Akbari et al., 2001). Additionally, trees enhance air
quality by capturing airborne particulates on their leaves and absorbing gaseous pollutants (Nowak
and Dwyer, 2007). Utility benefits also include trees acting as a carbon sink, sequestering carbon
dioxide (CO2) from the atmosphere (Nowak et al., 2013). Amenity benefits of trees encompass
their social effects that contribute to well-being and enhance quality of life. Psychological studies
indicate that trees reduce fear of crime and alleviate stress (Mooney et al., 2005). In fact, research
has demonstrated that hospital patients with views of trees tend to recover faster (Ulrich, 1984).
Trees also enhance amenity benefits by providing recreational value, encouraging outdoor activities,
and fostering community engagement (Nowak and Dwyer, 2007).
In an urban environment, trees face significant challenges due to intense human influence and limited
space, resulting in hindered tree growth (Bühler et al., 2017). The vitality, stability, and growth of
trees are influenced by numerous factors, with soil conditions and the availability of soil space for
root growth being particularly crucial. This presents a major challenge for urban trees as urban soils
differ from natural soils due to compaction, the presence of deicing salts, and other human-induced
contaminants. Soil compaction resulting from pavement installation, although necessary for urban
development, diminishes the presence of macropores in the soil profile, consequently restricting root
extension capacities of trees (Day et al., 1995). Furthermore, pavements contribute to reduced soil
aeration and drainage capabilities, further constraining optimal growth conditions. Addressing the
inherent challenges of urban trees requires careful planning and design to maximize benefits. This
entails selecting tree species that are well-suited to both natural and urban contexts, considering
tree diversity, and choosing species with a proven track record of successful growth in urban areas
(Nowak and Dwyer, 2007). Once planted, trees require regular maintenance, including watering and
pruning, to ensure their strength and health. Integrating trees into urban areas presents a complex
challenge in achieving sustainable urbanization (McPherson et al., 2005). However, research has
consistently demonstrated that the benefits derived from urban trees outweigh the costs associated
with their maintenance.
Trees play a vital role in the Copenhagen Municipality’s city nature strategy, which seeks to enhance
the environment for all living organisms within the city (Københavns Kommune, 2015). Enhancing
the green infrastructure of the city can not only benefit the natural ecosystem but also enhances the
quality of life for residents. As a result, a specific proposal was made to plant 100.000 new trees in

the Copenhagen Municipality between 2015-2025, reflecting the Municipality’s commitment to this
green initiative (Københavns Kommune, 2016). 10.000 of the trees are allocated as street trees, while
the remaining 90.000 are planted as part of the urban forest. Almost 62.000 trees have been and/or
are already financed to be planted, most of which are forest trees (Københavns Kommune, 2021a). A
central target in the plan is to reduce air pollution by implementing green infrastructure. With this
approach, the City of Copenhagen strives to develop holistic solutions that align climate adaptation
measures with existing urban enhancements, all in the pursuit of creating a more environmentally
friendly city.
Project Description
This thesis focuses on simulating the capacity of urban trees to remove nitrogen oxides (NOx) in
the Copenhagen Municipality, Denmark using the model MyTree. The objective is to quantify this
capacity by examining the tree characteristics that are relevant to air pollution removal. Additionally,
the fieldwork data collected on the existing urban trees in Copenhagen is incorporated into the
MyTree model. This allows for the evaluation of the environmental services and economic benefits
provided by individual trees. The thesis further explores various scenarios, considering both the
present and future number of urban trees and tree species. Ultimately, the aim is to gain insights
into the role of urban trees as valuable assets in addressing air pollution and promoting a sustainable
urban environment.
This study include the following learning objectives:
• Describe and understand the sources of NOx that contribute to the formation of ground-level
ozone.
• Explain the role of urban trees as an ecosystem service that can mitigate air pollution.
• A MyTree model that quantifies the magnitude of air pollution removal by urban trees, including
the input variables used to estimate NOx removal capacity.
• Evaluate MyTree results for different scenarios in Copenhagen and apply them to an ecosystem
service context.
1.1
Problem statement
What is the impact of planting 10.000 additional street trees upon the NOx removal capacity of the
Copenhagen Municipality’s current street tree stock?
Along with the main research question several hypotheses are subject to discussion:
• Increasing the number of street trees in Copenhagen will increase the NOx removal
capacity by more than 5%. Studies have demonstrated the role of trees in capturing and
absorbing NOx through dry deposition and leaf surface interactions (Nowak et al., 2014; Selmi
et al., 2016; Abhijith et al., 2017). However, the specific percentage increase in NOx removal
capacity resulting from an increase in street trees may vary depending on factors such as tree
species, location, and tree characteristics
• Different tree species will exhibit varying levels of NOx removal capacity in
Copenhagen. Previous studies have shown that tree species differ in their ability to remove
air pollutants, including NOx (Abhijith et al., 2017). Understanding the specific NOx removal
capacities of different tree species in Copenhagen is crucial for effective urban greening strategies
tailored to local environmental conditions.
Page 2 of 46
==PAGE:12==
• The impact of planting 10.000 street trees on NOx concentration in Copenhagen
will be more noticeable in the long term rather than immediately after planting.
The time required for newly planted trees to establish and grow significantly impacts their
pollutant removal capacity (Zhang et al., 2022). Therefore, the long-term effects of planting
10.000 street trees are expected to yield more substantial reductions in NOx concentrations
compared to immediate effects after planting.
• The sun exposure of the trees influence the NOx removal capacity by more than 5%
within each tree species. Sunlight exposure affects tree physiology, including photosynthesis
and transpiration, which in turn can influence their NOx removal capacity (Ramakrishnan and
Ray-Mukherjee, 2022). Further investigation is needed to determine the specific percentage
variation in NOx removal capacity within each tree species due to sun exposure.
• The economic benefits associated with the pollution removal provided by individual
trees in Copenhagen will increase with the planting of 10.000 extra street trees.
Assessing the specific economic benefits resulting from planting 10.000 additional street trees
in Copenhagen can provide insights into the cost-effectiveness and return on investment of
urban greening initiatives.

2. Approach
The specific effect of urban trees on the removal of NOx gasses has not previously been publicly
studied in Denmark. In Copenhagen, implementation of ecological infrastructure in urban planning
projects has focused more on climate adaptation related to either urban water management or heat
waves (Københavns Kommune, 2011). It has been demonstrated that ecological infrastructure has
the potential to reduce air pollution (Nowak and Dwyer, 2007), however, its impact is relatively minor
on a systemic level. Hence, the major challenges of this study is to investigate the NOx removal
capacity by urban trees, different tree species’ removal capacity, and how urban trees function as an
ecosystem service.
Initially the focus of this study was to determine air pollution removal by urban vegetation. This
discovery prompted a search for ecological infrastructure within the fields of urban planning and
management. In these domains, Copenhagen’s ambitious objective of planting 100.000 trees caught
attention, shifting the study’s focus exclusively to urban trees as a form of urban vegetation
(Københavns Kommune, 2016).
At the beginning of the project, a literature review was conducted, revealing that while there is
an abundance of literature examining urban vegetation’s air pollution removal, urban trees’ NOx
absorption capacity has received limited attention. This discovery prompted a significant shift
in the study’s objective, which now aims to fill this research gap by simulating the existing NOx
removal capacity of Copenhagen’s urban trees. This research aligns with the city’s tree planting
goal and serves a dual purpose: first, to investigate the importance of planting different tree species
and integrating them into an ecosystem service context; and second, to compare the current NOx
removal status with the potential influence of an increased number of urban trees.
The decision only to investigate the NOx removal capacity was taken due to the urban perspective
of this study, urban environments typically display many local NOx sources such as vehicles and
industries (Ellermann et al., 2022).
There are multiple models accessible for evaluating the effectiveness of green infrastructure practices
in urban settings. Initially, the plan was to use the model i-Tree Eco from the online available
software package i-Tree developed by United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) Forest
Service (Nowak, 2021). This proved to be difficult due to the complexity of the model and the
required data. Instead, a simplification of the i-Tree Eco model, MyTree, which was also developed
by i-Tree, was chosen for this project (Nowak, 2021). MyTree is a simplified model, requiring a small
amount of data but is still able to meet the objective of the thesis. MyTree uses the tree species,
crown condition and sun exposure to estimate the air pollution removal capacity and economic
benefits gained. MyTree is based on the same theory as i-Tree Eco with some deviations. Due to
the simplification and the limited theory about the model, the level of detail in the output data is
limited. However, the required theory and output data fulfill the objective in this study and will be
described further in the thesis.

3. Background
This chapter is divided into four sections that provide background information relevant to this study.
The first part, "Copenhagen Plans and Tree Policy", details the plans for the implementation of
100.000 new trees in Copenhagen and the economic costs. The second section, "Air Pollution and
Pollutants", introduces the phenomenon of air pollution and the different air pollutants. The third
section, "Ecosystem Services", provides an introduction to the concept of ecosystem services, urban
trees in cities and how trees are able to provide ecosystem services in urban environments. The
fourth section, "MyTree Application", provides an introduction to the MyTree application, and the
model processes that have been used to assess the air pollution removal of urban trees, to prepare
the most realistic simulation of Copenhagen.
3.1
Copenhagen Plans and Tree Policy
In 2015 the Municipality of Copenhagen developed a climate plan with the goal of being 100%
CO2-neutral by 2025. One of the targets within the plan was to plant 100.000 new trees from
2015-2025 (Teknik og Miljøforvaltningen, 2020). Around 90.000 of the trees were to be planted in
pocket parks, existing parks, and natural areas in Copenhagen, while the remaining 10.000 trees were
to be planted as street trees, with a focus on streets without existing trees (Danmarks Træportal,
2011). To accommodate the goal a tree policy for existing and new trees was developed by the
Municipality with four main principles (Teknik og Miljøforvaltningen, 2018):
1. Preserve existing trees
2. Existing trees that are felled must be replaced if possible
3. Secure the best growing conditions for existing and new trees
4. Secure a varied selection of trees species
The urban trees in Copenhagen includes trees along streets, individual trees in the cityscape and
trees in parks, cemeteries and school yards (Center for Park & Natur, 2022). The urban trees are
distributed at both private, local and governmental areas. The Municipality of Copenhagen are
continually monitoring the risks associated with both new and future tree diseases to determine which
species will prove most resilient and hence select the appropriate species to continue to plant. The
increased incidence of diseases and the concentration of tree species, have prompted the Municipality
to establish guidelines for selecting tree species. A notable guideline highlights that one species of
tree should not comprise more than 10% of the total street tree stock, corresponding to around
2.500 street trees (existing and new). This guideline applies to the replanting of street trees in larger
quantities, the planting of new street trees, and new landscaping in general. By using a wider variety
of species, they aim to reduce the concentration of certain species and thus reduce the vulnerability
to diseases.

Economic costs
The financial costs associated with tree planting encompass: expenses for acquiring trees, creating
planting holes, and restoring pavement. Maintenance costs involve activities such as watering and
pruning the trees (Københavns Kommune, 2016). The cost of planting a tree in impervious areas
(street trees), ranges from 25.000 DKK to 125.000 DKK. Additionally, the maintenance cost for each
tree per year falls within the range of 100 DKK to 2.000 DKK. The large range is due to the fact
that it involves removing existing pavement, creating a planting hole, reinstating solid pavement,
and in some cases, relocating cables. It is the variation between these expenses that accounts for the
significant range. Thus, planting a total of 10.000 trees, is projected to range between 250 million
DKK and 1.25 billion DKK, with an average maintenance cost of 1.050 DKK per tree per year.
3.2
Air Pollution and Pollutants
The worldwide increase in emissions and concentrations has made air pollution a global threat with
significant impacts on both human health and ecosystems (European Environmetal Agency, 2019).
The phenomenon of air pollution arises from the accumulation of specific gases and particulate
matter in the atmosphere to concentrations that can be detrimental to human health, causing
respiratory and breathing difficulties, and even resulting in premature mortality, in addition to
environmental degradation. These pollutants, generated mainly from anthropogenic activities (such
as the combustion of fossil fuels like coal, oil, petrol, or diesel), are composed of a complex mixture
of chemical species. However, they can also emanate from natural sources, such as wildfires and
volcanic eruptions.
Although efforts have been made to reduce emissions and ambient concentrations, many areas
in Europe still have poor air quality (European Environmetal Agency, 2019). Air pollution is
currently the most significant environmental risk to human health and is considered the second
biggest environmental concern, following climate change. As a result, air quality issues have garnered
growing political, media, and public interest, with more support for actions to address this problem.
Meteorology has a significant impact on air pollution levels through its influence on atmospheric
dispersion and transportation processes (Finlayson-Pitts and Pitts, 2000). Factors such as wind
speed and direction, atmospheric stability, and temperature can affect the rate of pollutant dispersion
and dilution in the atmosphere. Low wind speeds and stable atmospheric conditions can lead to
the buildup of pollutants in urban areas, resulting in higher pollution levels. Conversely, high wind
speeds and unstable atmospheric conditions can facilitate the dispersion of pollutants, reducing
their concentration in the air. Temperature inversions, which occur when a layer of warm air traps
cooler air near the surface, can also exacerbate air pollution by preventing pollutants from dispersing
vertically. This can lead to the buildup of pollutants near the ground, resulting in higher levels of
pollution.
Other important factors that can influence the source of pollution relate to local and regional air
pollution. Local air pollution refers to pollution generated from a specific local source that affects
the immediate surrounding area (Jiang et al., 2021). Regional air pollution refers to pollution that
is transported over longer distances and affects a broader geographic area. The distinction between
local and regional air pollution is important because it affects the strategies used to mitigate and
control pollution. Local air pollution can often be addressed through targeted interventions that
focus on reducing emissions from specific sources, while addressing regional air pollution requires
more comprehensive and coordinated efforts across a larger geographic area. Local and regional air
pollution are not mutually exclusive, and many sources of pollution can contribute to both types of
pollution. For example, emissions from a large industrial facility can contribute to both local air
pollution in the immediate vicinity of the facility and regional air pollution through transport of
pollutants over longer distances.

Air pollutants can be categorised as primary pollutants or secondary pollutants (European Environ-
metal Agency, 2019). Primary pollutants are those that are directly released into the atmosphere
from various sources such as transportation and industrial activities. Secondary pollutants are
formed by the transformation of primary pollutants through chemical reactions and microphysical
processes in the atmosphere. Key primary pollutants are carbon monoxide (CO), nitrogen dioxide
(NO2), sulfur dioxide (SO2), particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10) and ammonia (NH3). Key
secondary air pollutants are PM2.5 and PM10, ozone (O3), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs).
These gaseous air pollutants all contribute to the overall air pollution threat countries are facing.
However, the relevant pollutants in this study include NO2, O3 and VOCs due to their interaction
in the atmosphere.
NO2
NO2 is contained within the reactive gases known as NOx, which are formed as byproducts of fossil
fuel combustion and naturally by lightning strikes (United States EPA, 1999).
NO2 typically occurs in conjunction with nitrogen monoxide, NO, as both substances are emitted
during combustion processes. The two compounds are collectively referred to as NOx, and the
amount of NOx is understood as the sum of NO and NO2. When the exhaust gas disperses, a rapid
reaction occurs where O3 reacts with NO, forming NO2:
NO + O3 →NO2
(3.1)
This reaction relies on the presence of O3. Therefore, the concentration of O3 in the surroundings is
crucial in determining the amount of NO2 that can be formed (Milijøstyrelsen, 2009).
In the presence of both air and ultraviolet light from sunlight, NO2 undergoes a reaction to produce
NO and the secondary pollutant O3 via photolysis:
NO2 + O2 →NO + O3
(3.2)
The NO produced in this process further reacts with free radicals that are formed by UV acting on
VOC in the atmosphere. NO is recycled to NO2 by the free radicals, enabling each NO molecule
to generate O3 multiple times through this process. NO2 has a direct impact on health, while NO
itself does not have health effects (Ellermann et al., 2022).
In the absence of shortwave radiation NO2 can react with O3 forming the radical NO3:
NO2 + O3 →NO3 + O2
(3.3)
NO3 can react with NO2 and form dinitrogen pentoxide (N2O5) which acts as a night reservoir for
NOx. These secondary pollutants can contribute to environmental issues, such as acidification and
nitrogen enrichment (United States EPA, 1999).
O3
O3 is one of the most widespread global air pollution issues today in the lower troposphere (World
Health Organization, 2006). In the troposphere (ground-level) O3 is a secondary pollutant formed in
the atmosphere from the precursors NOx and VOC (Aarhus University, 2023).
O3 concentration gradients are relatively large in and around urban areas, with low levels in busy
urban centers and higher levels in suburban and rural areas due to the "scavenging" of O3 by NO
near strong NOx emission sources. When NO reacts with O3, it forms NO2 and O2:
Page 7 of 46
==PAGE:17==
NO + O3 →NO2 + O2
(3.4)
O3 and its precursors can be transported over long distances in the atmosphere, making it a
transboundary issue. Elevated levels of O3 at ground-level is known to have adverse effects on human
health and vegetation (Jenkin and Clemitshaw, 2000).
VOC
VOCs refer to a large class of chemical compounds with high vapor pressure and low water solubility,
which are classified based on their molecular structure or functional groups such as chlorinated, oxy-
genated, nitrogenous, aromatic hydrocarbons, alcohols, ethers, esters, aldehydes, sulfur compounds,
and so on. VOCs include solvents such as acetone, toluene, benzene, formaldehyde, and chloroform
(Aarhus University, 2023). VOC can react with sunlight, NO2 and O2 and form O3
NO2 + sunlight + V OCs + O2 →O3 + otherproducts
(3.5)
The atmosphere acts as the primary sink for VOCs, which are environmentally concerning because
they react with NOx, leading to the formation of ground-level O3 and secondary organic aerosols in
airborne particulate matter (Anand et al., 2014). Although VOCs are emitted in the environment
by anthropogenic sources like industrial waste, motor vehicles, and gas stations, the majority of
atmospheric VOCs come from biogenic emissions such as those from vegetation, soil, and forest fires.
3.3
Ecosystem Services
Cities are composed of various interconnected ecosystems, both internal and external, that can
provide numerous beneficial attributes to the urban environment (Sukhdev et al., 2013). The concept
of ecosystem services offers a multi-faceted approach to addressing environmental, societal, and
economic challenges. Ecosystem services refer to the benefits that nature provides to society and the
economy and can be classified into four categories: 1) Provisioning, 2) Regulating, 3) Cultural, and
4) Supporting. These categories provide different types of goods and can be acquired directly or
indirectly by humans (Matyssek et al., 2013). Provisioning services include food, water, and raw
materials; regulating services include climate regulation, pest control, and water purification; cultural
services include recreation, aesthetics, and spiritual enrichment; and supporting services include soil
formation, nutrient cycling, and biodiversity conservation. The importance of each service depends
on the socio-economic and environmental conditions of the specific region Sukhdev et al. (2013).
Page 8 of 46
==PAGE:18==
Figure 3.1: Illustration of ecosystem services.
Urban ecosystem services are provided by various habitats such as urban forests, parks, cemeteries,
and other types of ecological infrastructure that are known as nature-based solutions (Costanza et al.,
1997). Estimating the economic and social value of the benefits that nature provides to humans, such
as clean water, air, soil, pollination, carbon storage, and recreation is difficult. However, valuation
can help to raise awareness of the importance of these services, guide policy and decision-making,
and inform management practices. Valuation methods can be quantitative or qualitative, and can
include market-based, cost-based, and stated preference approaches. However, valuing ecosystem
services is a complex and often contested process, because it can be difficult to valuing especially
the cultural services.
3.3.1
Trees in Urban Environments
Urban trees, whether located on public or private property, contribute to the urban forest by growing
in close proximity to areas heavily influenced by human activities (Nowak and Dwyer, 2007). This
presents challenges due to limited space, which generally hampers tree growth. The health, stability,
and growth of trees are influenced by various factors, but the most crucial ones are soil conditions
and the availability of soil space for root expansion. This is a significant challenge for urban trees
because urban soils differ from natural soils due to compaction, exposure to deicing salts, and
other contaminants caused by human activities (Ow and Ghosh, 2017a; Bartens et al., 2010). Soil
compaction resulting from pavement construction is a typical consequence of urban development. It
eliminates macropores in the soil profile, reducing the elongation and penetration capabilities of tree
roots. Furthermore, pavements limit soil aeration and drainage, thereby restricting optimal growth
conditions (Day et al., 1995).
A crucial factor that also restricts root growth and nutrient uptake in urban trees is that the soil
typically exhibits relatively high pH levels because of the accumulation of alkaline particles stemming
from construction operations, pavement, and asphalt (Bühler et al., 2017). This condition can
Page 9 of 46
==PAGE:19==
compromise the accessibility of nutrients to plants, potentially leading to nutrient insufficiency and
imbalances. If the soil condition, water availability and sun exposure is insufficient, trees may exhibit
signs of stress such as wilted leaves, reduced growth, and a weakened root system clearly seen in the
crown condition.
Gaseous pollutants can enter the leaves of trees stomata (gas exchange) (Ramakrishnan and Ray-
Mukherjee, 2022). Stomata, which are tiny pores surrounded by guard cells in the outer layers of
leaves and stems, play a crucial role in plant respiration. Stomata facilitate the uptake of CO2 for
photosynthesis and the release of oxygen (O2) and water vapor. However, it can also allow the entry
of gaseous pollutants. When there is a higher concentration of gaseous pollutants in the surrounding
air, the pollutants tend to diffuse into the leaf where the concentration is lower. This diffusion occurs
until an equilibrium is reached. The same principle of concentration gradients and diffusion applies
to various pollutants present in the atmosphere.
3.3.2
Urban Trees as an Ecosystem Service
Urban trees are able to provide provisioning, regulating, cultural, and supporting services to the
urban environment. Urban trees can contribute to cooling and reduce economic costs associated with
cooling systems, sequester and store carbon, filter airborne pollutants to improve air quality and
reduce associated health costs, intercept surface water runoff, provide habitats for pollinators and
seed dispersers, and enhance the attractiveness of urban areas while providing diverse physiological,
social, and aesthetic benefits (Bolund and Hunhammar, 1999). The following bullet points give
examples of some of the different services urban trees are able to provide:
• Urban trees can provide products such as fruits and nuts which can be harvested for human use.
While the extent to which the provisioning services are provided may be limited compared to
rural areas, urban trees can still contribute to the provision of food through urban agriculture
or community gardens.
• Roadside and sidewalk trees have the potential to regulate local air and surface temperatures
through various mechanisms such as reflecting solar radiation, providing shade to nearby areas,
and reducing heat storage. This can help mitigate the urban heat island effect (Sukhdev et al.,
2013; Armson et al., 2012).
• By decreasing the surface temperature, urban trees can also lower the photo-chemical reactions
that lead to the creation of air pollutants, such as O3 (Milliken, 2018).
• The ability of urban trees to reduce air pollution in urban areas varies locally. Urban trees
are able to filter out air pollutants such as CO, NO2, SO2, O3 and PM10 from the air. The
effectiveness of this filtering method depends on the amount of leaf area available (Bolund and
Hunhammar, 1999). The trees can also absorb gaseous pollutants through stomata, capture
particulate matter on leaf surfaces, and break down various organic compounds present in the
soil or plant tissue (Matyssek et al., 2013; Milliken, 2018).
• Urban trees can reduce noise pollution by absorbing, deflecting, reflecting, and refracting sound
waves (Sukhdev et al., 2013; Ow and Ghosh, 2017b). Planting trees in rows has been found
to reflect and refract sound waves, dispersing the sound energy through trees. Different tree
species have varying levels of effectiveness in reducing noise (Pathak et al., 2008).
• Urban trees have proven to be effective in regulating the urban water cycle and intercepting
water through leaves and stems (Milliken, 2018). Urban trees are capable of intercepting and
storing water until it eventually evaporates, although the rate of interception varies depending
on the tree species and size (Armson et al., 2013). Additionally, the soil below the surface can
store water within its pore space and reduce infiltration rates (Sukhdev et al., 2013).
Page 10 of 46
==PAGE:20==
• The largest part of carbon sequestered in urban environments is primarily stored in trees
(Milliken, 2018). Through the process of photosynthesis, trees act as carbon sinks by storing
atmospheric carbon dioxide within their biomass (Armson et al., 2013). The sequestration
rate depends on the size and health of the tree, and the amount of carbon dioxide stored is
proportional to the tree biomass. Increasing the number of trees in urban areas is expected to
decelerate the accumulation of atmospheric carbon (Sukhdev et al., 2013).
• The urban environment can affect organisms, including humans, influencing their health and
social cohesion (Douglas, 2012). Green urban spaces have been found to improve mental health,
with research indicating that views of parks, trees, and other ecological infrastructure can
enhance emotional state, concentration, and reduce stress (Milliken, 2018).
• Urban green spaces promote social cohesion and provide opportunities for interaction, resulting
in various benefits for individuals and groups (Gotham and Brumley, 2002).
• Urban trees have aesthetic value, which has been linked to reduced stress and improved mental
health (Ulrich et al., 1991).
• Urban trees can provide economic benefits by reducing costs associated with managing urban
environments. For example, reduce the need for cooling systems by providing shade and cooling
through transpiration, thereby lowering energy consumption costs (Donovan and Butry, 2009).
• Urban trees can help mitigate the economic costs associated with air pollution and water
runoff, which can have negative impacts on human health and infrastructure.
• Urban trees can also contribute to the economic development of urban areas by enhancing
their aesthetic value and attracting tourism (Tyrväinen et al., 2005).
• Urban green spaces and parks can provide opportunities for recreation and outdoor activities,
which can boost local economies through increased spending on restaurants, cafes, and other
businesses. Furthermore, the provision of fruits and nuts by urban trees can also provide
economic benefits through urban agriculture or community gardens (Tyrväinen et al., 2005).
It is worth noting that the economic benefits of urban trees as an ecosystem service may vary
depending on the specific context and location. For example, the economic benefits of urban trees
may be more pronounced in densely populated urban areas with high levels of air pollution and a
greater need for cooling, as compared to rural areas with more abundant natural resources.
However, there are also recognized negative impacts of urban ecosystem services on human well-being,
such as the emission of VOCs by some tree species, and some pollinated trees or plants that can
cause allergic reactions (Bolund and Hunhammar, 1999; Pataki et al., 2011; Milliken, 2018). Urban
ecosystems may also generate negative impacts, which are functions of ecosystems perceived as
negative for human well-being (Sukhdev et al., 2013). Urban wildlife can also cause infrastructure
damage, such as the decomposition of wood structures from microbial activity or corrosion of concrete
from bird excrement. Tree roots can also damage pipes and wires underground, as well as break
pavements (Milliken, 2018).
3.4
MyTree Application
To produce a simulation to assess the effectiveness of street trees in removing air pollution in
Copenhagen, MyTree, which is an online software tool, was utilized (USDA Forest Service, 2023).
MyTree is a tool for assessing individual trees environmental services of carbon sequestration, air
pollution removal, and rainfall interception and is developed by i-Tree, which is a cooperative between
public and private partnerships. MyTree use data derived from the core tool i-Tree Eco. The exact
Page 11 of 46
==PAGE:21==
theory behind MyTree is very limited, but the theory behind the model is based on the theory from
i-Tree Eco (Nowak, 2021). The theory explained in this section will therefore be based on i-Tree Eco
with some deviations.
MyTree combines various tree data, such as tree species, diameter at breast height (DBH), tree
condition and sun exposure (amount of sun that reaches the leaves of the tree based on its sur-
roundings) with local environmental data; meteorological and air pollution concentration. The
application is only able to investigate one tree at a time. MyTree uses the location of the tree to
estimate the carbon sequestration, storm water mitigation and air pollution removal provided by
the tree. By using the location, MyTree can estimate the local pollutant concentrations and other
environmental variables that affect tree growth and function, such as temperature and precipitation.
This information is used to calculate the potential benefits provided by the tree over a 20-years
period, in this study focusing on air pollution removal. The following paragraphs describe the theory
and the calculation procedures being used to determine air pollution removal. The theory follows
the document "i-Tree Methods 2021" (Nowak, 2021).
Trees in MyTree
According to i-Tree, a tree is considered any plant with a DBH that measures greater than or equal
to 2,54 cm. The information required about the DBH must be measured at 1,37 m. The DBH is used
to estimate the tree size (height and crown-width) and associated leaf surface area. The leaf surface
area is then used in the air pollution removal model. The estimated growth of trees in MyTree over
a 20-year period is determined based on the tree’s species, condition, and crown light exposure.
The annual tree diameter growth is estimated for this study based on species growth rates. The
average standardized diameter growth for open-grown trees are set to 0,58 cm/year for slow growing
species, 0,84 cm/year for moderate growing species, and 1,09 cm/year for fast growing species.
Leaf Area
The leaf area within the canopy of a tree is a crucial factor that affects the estimation of biomass,
air pollution removal, carbon storage and sequestration, and other ecosystem services. Leaf area,
which refers to the total surface area of leaves on a tree, is a key parameter used to measure the
extent of foliage coverage.
The total amount of leaf area per unit of projected ground surface area is referred to as the leaf area
index (LAI), which is calculated as the ratio of leaf area (m2) to ground area (m2). For individual
open-grown, deciduous trees, the leaf area is estimated using the following regression equation:
lnY = −4.3309 + 0.2942H + 0.7312D + 5.7217S + −0.0148C
(3.6)
Where Y is leaf area (m2), H is crown height (m), D is average crown diameter (m), S is the average
shading factor for a given species refers to the percentage of light intensity that is blocked by the
tree crowns with leaves, and C is based on the outer surface area of the tree crown (πD·(H+D)
2
).
The shading coefficient is an estimation of the percentage of sunlight that strikes a tree crown but
does not reach the ground through gaps. Trees with dense crowns have a higher shading coefficient,
as they allow less light to pass through to the ground. In cases where there is no shading coefficient
available for a specific species, the average of the shading coefficients from other species in the same
genus is used. If there is no genus coefficient available, then the average of family, order, subclass, or
class coefficients is used.

Air Pollution Removal
MyTree is able to estimate air pollution removal (dry deposition) from individual trees.
The
estimates are based on modeling of gas exchange and particulate matter (PM) interception by trees
for CO, NO2, SO2 and PM2.5. MyTree estimates the hourly dry deposition of CO, NO2, SO2 and
PM2.5 throughout the year based on tree cover data with weather data and pollution concentration
monitoring data.
Pollution removal (F; in g/m2/s) is calculated as:
F = Vd · C
(3.7)
Where Vd = deposition velocity (m/s), and C = pollutant concentration (g/m3).
Deposition velocity is calculated as:
Vd =
1
Ra + Rb + Rc
(3.8)
Where Ra = sum of the aerodynamic boundary layer, Rb = quasi-laminar boundary layer, and Rc =
canopy resistances.
Ra and Rb are calculated as hourly estimates using standard resistance formulas and hourly weather
data.
Hourly Rc values for O3, SO2, and NO2 are calculated based on a modified hybrid of big-leaf and
multilayer canopy deposition models. Rc has three components: stomatal resistance (rs), mesophyll
resistance (rm), and cuticular resistance (rt):

For SO2, rm is assigned a value of 0 seconds/meter (s/m), whereas for O3 it is set to 10 s/m.
However, rm is set to 100 s/m for NO2 to accommodate the difference in water transport and NO2
within the leaf interior and to ensure that the calculated deposition velocities fall within the typical
range of NO2 deposition velocities. Furthermore, rt is set at 8.000 s/m for SO2, 10.000 s/m for
O3, and 20.000 s/m for NO2 to consider the variations in rt that are observed among the different
pollutants.
Deposition velocities are adjusted to 0 when it is raining, to limit deposition estimates to periods
of dry deposition. The model is executed at the population level to assess the impact of pollution.
The daily leaf area is calculated using the following factors: the percentage of evergreen tree species,
leaf-on and leaf-off dates determined from the first and last frost dates from local weather stations
for international locations. The total daily leaf surface area is estimated by considering a 4-week
transition period around the leaf-on and leaf-off dates for spring and autumn, respectively.

Where Ix = pollution removal by individual tree x (kg), Rt = total pollution removed for all trees

To calculate the amount of pollution within the mixing layer (g/m2), hourly mixing heights (in

to total pollution within the boundary layer assumes a well-mixed boundary layer, which is typically
observed during the day under unstable conditions.
Economic Valuation of Air Pollution Removal
The economic valuation of air pollution removal in i-Tree takes into account several factors (Nowak,
2021). It considers the health benefits derived from improved air quality, such as reduced healthcare
costs and increased productivity.
It also considers the value of avoided damages to property,
infrastructure, and crops resulting from reduced air pollution, and last it consider external values. It
then assigns a monetary value to the air pollution removal by estimating the costs associated with
avoiding the negative impacts of that pollution.
Nowak et al. (2014) have developed regression equations to estimate dollars per tonne (y) based on
population density (people per km2, x):
NO2 : y = 0.7298 + 0.6264x (r2=0.91)
O3 : y = 9.4667 + 3.5089x (r2=0.86)
PM2.5 : y = 428.0011 + 121.7864x (r2=0.83)
SO2 : y = 0.1442 + 0.1493x (r2=0.86)
The monetary values linked to the reduction of adverse health effects rise in correlation with county
population density.
The regression equations provide approximate values based on the density of the population and can
provide an estimate of the economic valuation (in dollars per ton of pollution removal) for countries
other than the United States. Although the specific economic valuation method used in MyTree is
not known, it is anticipated that the regression equations are applicable since Denmark is not within
the United States.

6. Discussion
The results of this simulation study have shown that the street trees in the Municipality of Copenhagen
are able to absorb NOx gasses from the air. However, the absorption effectiveness of trees are found
to vary due to tree species, crown condition and sun exposure since each tree has different functional
traits. The simulation show that the initiative of planting 10.000 extra street trees within the borders
of Copenhagen will increase the NOx removal capacity with 22,6% and result in a financial benefit
of 1.407.652 DKK per year.
6.1
Air Pollution in Copenhagen
Air pollution in Copenhagen mainly originates from traffic, idling vehicles, heat and power plants,
and smoke emitted from wood-burning stoves (Sundheds- og Omsorgsforvaltningen, 2021). The
annual average concentrations of NO2 remain below the limit of EU air quality guideline (40 µg/m3)
(Ellermann et al., 2022). Since 2005, there has been a remarkable reduction in air concentrations of
NO2, with levels decreasing by up to 50% in Copenhagen.
The latest available data showing the sources of NOx emissions in the Municipality of Copenhagen
is from 2018 (Marlene Schmidt Plejdrup, 2023). A total of 2294 tons NOx was emitted in 2018
where 53% came from road transport, 27% from heat and power plants, 14% from non-road traffic,
3% from wood-burning stoves, and 2% from construction industries (Solvang Jensen et al., 2021).
A comparison between the amount of NOx removed by street trees in Baseline per year (1,115
kg NOx) and the amount of NOx emitted in Copenhagen in 2018 show that street trees removed
approximately 0,05% of the emitted NOx. It is important to note that the comparison is based
on the 13.915 street trees (constitute the nine tree species in Baseline) in Copenhagen. However,
trees in parks, cemeteries, and urban forest is expected to increase the removed percentage of the
total emitted NOx emissions in a year in Copenhagen. Moreover, is the amount of NOx emitted in
Copenhagen in 2018 expected to be lower today, mainly due to the ongoing replacement of vehicles
with lower emissions (Sundheds- og Omsorgsforvaltningen, 2021).
Despite the contribution from local sources it is also important to mention that air pollution is
transboundary. This means that it can travel across national or regional boundaries, affecting
areas beyond the source of pollution. Transboundary air pollution highlights the interconnection of
environmental issues and emphasizes the need for international cooperation and coordinated efforts
to address air pollution. Since the effects of transboundary air pollution extend beyond political
boundaries, addressing it requires collaboration between countries to develop and implement effective
pollution control measures, emission reduction strategies, and monitoring systems.

==PAGE:37==
6.2
Effect of Leaf Area
The study findings reveal significant variability in the absorption of NOx among different tree
species, which can be attributed to various tree characteristics, such as leaf surface area and
stomata conductance (Kimm and Ryu, 2015). The total surface area of leaves plays a crucial role in
determining the trees’ capacity to remove air pollutants, as the absorption primarily occurs through
the stomata located on the leaves (Ramakrishnan and Ray-Mukherjee, 2022). The distribution of
stomatal pores, with some plants having them on both leaf surfaces and others only on the underside,
further contributes to the observed differences (Ramakrishnan and Ray-Mukherjee, 2022).
Stomatal behavior and regulation are influenced by several environmental factors (Ramakrishnan
and Ray-Mukherjee, 2022). One factor is natural light, which triggers different responses in plants
based on their location, shading changes, and the angle of the sun. Stomata tend to open in
response to increased light intensity and close when light intensity decreases. This divergence in
stomatal response to sunlight explains the variations in NOx absorption among different tree species,
particularly those with varying crown conditions (Scenarios 2, 3, and 4). The results suggest that
higher natural light intensity leads to varying degrees of NOx absorption for each tree species.
Additionally, shaded areas with street trees exhibit lower NOx absorption, possibly due to stomatal
closure. However, it is important to note that stomatal responses can vary among plant species,
with shade-tolerant plants potentially keeping their stomata open even at lower light levels.
Stomatal behavior is also influenced by elevated temperatures in urban environments. As tempera-
tures rise, stomata tend to open to aid in leaf temperature regulation, but this can increase water
stress (Wachendorf and Küppers, 2017). Conversely, stomata promptly close to reduce transpiration
when plants sense a decrease in water availability (Ramakrishnan and Ray-Mukherjee, 2022). The
current and projected temperature increases in cities limit gas exchange due to these stomatal
responses, exacerbating challenges faced by urban plants. If Copenhagen experiences temperature
rise, the NOx removal capacity is not expected to increase further. However, successfully avoiding
temperature increases in the city can enhance the potential of ecological vegetation as a valuable
asset for reducing NOx levels.
Elevated concentrations of NOx can have detrimental effects on plants by interfering with the electron
transport between photosystems, thereby reducing overall photosynthesis rates (Chaparro-Suarez
et al., 2011). The impact of NOx on plants depends on the duration of exposure. Short-term
exposure causes stomatal opening, while prolonged exposure can lead to complete stomatal closure.
It is worth noting that real-time results might slightly differ as the MyTree model used in this study
does not account for short-term or prolonged exposure.
The leaf surface area is not constant due to seasonal variations and vegetation growth (Zhang et al.,
2022). During the spring and summer seasons when trees have a full canopy with abundant leaves,
the NOx removal capacity is expected to be generally higher due to the larger leaf surface area. In
contrast, during the dormant season (fall and winter) when trees have fewer leaves or are leafless,
the NOx removal capacity is expected to be generally reduced. It is worth noting that the data
used in this study was obtained in late spring when the observed trees exhibited vitality, indicating
readiness for a new season. However, if the data collection had taken place during the winter period,
the outcomes might have diverged due to potential tree mortality resulting from the preceding dry
period. As a result, a greater number of trees could have been classified as having a worse crown
condition. These discrepancies could have influenced the incorporation of leaf area within the model
and consequently affected the estimation of each tree species’ NOx removal capacity.
Diurnal variations also influence the NOx removal capacity during the day. The rate of gas diffusion
was calculated to be 0,0012 µg NOx/day/m2. MyTree incorporates diurnal variations in air pollution
levels and the corresponding tree activity during the day and night. The flux is therefore an indication
of the gas diffusion during 24 hours.
Page 28 of 46
==PAGE:38==
The impact of planting 10.000 street trees on the NOx removal capacity in Copenhagen will become
more apparent in the long term, as the trees develop and the leaf surface area increases. When street
trees are newly planted, they are typically smaller in size and have less developed root systems. As
a result, their immediate impact on NOx concentrations may be limited. However, over time, as the
trees grow and mature, their root systems expand, and their canopies provide greater coverage, they
become more effective in mitigating air pollution. The noticeable impact on NOx concentrations
is further accentuated by the increased leaf surface area of mature trees, allowing for greater NOx
capture.
In addition, more mature urban trees have already acclimatised to the urban environment, whereas
newly planted trees require time to develop their root systems within the challenging urban conditions.
Factors such as soil conditions and the availability of soil space can limit the initial growth of newly
planted trees. However, as they establish stronger root systems and adapt to the urban environment,
their ability to contribute to NOx removal increases.
In conclusion, this study demonstrates the significant variability in NOx absorption among different
tree species, influenced by factors such as leaf surface area and stomata conductance. The findings
highlight the importance of environmental factors like light intensity and temperature in shaping
stomatal behavior and ultimately impacting NOx absorption. The results also emphasize the
seasonal variations in leaf surface area and its implications for the overall removal capacity of trees.
Understanding these factors and their effects can contribute to more effective urban planning and
pollution mitigation strategies, particularly in optimizing the use of ecological vegetation for reducing
NOx levels in cities.
6.3
Trade-offs of Street Trees
In relation to the background information presented in Section 3 on urban trees, it is evident that
urban trees, have direct and indirect impacts on urban air quality (Nowak et al., 1998). Tree
influence: (a) meteorological factors such as air temperature, radiation absorption, heat storage,
wind speed, relative humidity, turbulence, surface roughness, and the evolution of the mixing-layer
height; (b) the dry deposition of gases to the Earth’s surface, including deposition velocity; (c) the
emission of VOCs that contribute to the formation of O3 and CO; and (d) anthropogenic emissions
by reducing energy consumption through lower air temperatures and shading of buildings.
Due to the temperature dependency of VOC emissions and the general cooling effect of trees on
air temperatures, increased tree coverage can lead to an overall reduction in VOC emissions and
subsequently lower O3 levels in urban areas (Nowak et al., 2000). However, in certain situations,
increased tree cover may have negative effects on air quality, particularly with respect to O3.
The release of VOCs by trees can lead to the formation of O3 when they react with other pollutants
and sunlight. Therefore, it is essential to consider the potential impact of increased tree cover on O3
levels, particularly in areas where VOC emissions from trees are already significant and O3 formation
is high.
The VOC emission characteristics of different tree species play a crucial role in this context. Some
tree species are low VOC emitters, and strategically incorporating them into urban planning and
management can offer significant benefits. For instance, Nowak et al. (2000) observed that an area
consisting exclusively of low VOC-emitting species (Prunus sp.) resulted in 60% reduction in VOC
emissions from urban trees.
While decreased pollutant emissions generally enhance air quality, reduced NOx emissions, particu-
larly at ground level, may result in a local increase in O3 concentrations under specific conditions
due to diminished NOx scavenging of O3.
The impacts of increased urban tree cover on air quality, particularly O3, can vary locally. This
Page 29 of 46
==PAGE:39==
means that the effect of trees on O3 levels may differ from one city or region to another. Some
factors that can influence this variation include climate, weather patterns, pollutant emissions, and
the specific characteristics of the urban environment.
6.3.1
Street Trees in Street Canyons
The presence of trees in a street canyon has been found to significantly influence air exchange and
pollutant accumulation (Buccolieri et al., 2015, 2009; Gromke et al., 2008). Trees play a crucial role
in reducing wind speed within the canyon, which in turn leads to decreased air exchange between
the air above the roofs and within the canyon itself. This reduced air exchange can result in the
accumulation of pollutants inside the street canyon, leading to higher pollutant concentrations
compared to street canyons without trees.
The impact of tree proximity to roads on air exchange and pollutant dispersion is influenced by
various factors. Wind direction and speed play a significant role in determining how trees affect
pollutant exposure in street canyons (Abhijith and Gokhale, 2015; Amorim et al., 2013; Buccolieri
et al., 2011). Studies have shown that the presence of trees in street canyons can lead to reduced
pollutant concentrations with an increase in wind velocity under different wind directions (Hofman
and Samson, 2014).
Other factors related to street canyons and trees themselves also influence pollutant exposure. Tree
spacing, and the distance between pollutant sources and trees are crucial considerations (Abhijith
and Gokhale, 2015; Buccolieri et al., 2009). Research suggests that increasing the spacing between
tree crowns can contribute to a decrease in pollutant concentrations in street canyons.
Furthermore, the configuration and arrangement of trees in relation to street canyons and nearby
buildings are important. Gromke and Ruck (2007) highlighted the need for sufficient free space
between tree crowns and adjacent walls to prevent hindrance to air exchange, as this can lead to
higher pollutant concentrations at the pedestrian level. Moreover, is it crucial to ensure that the
total height of trees does not exceed the rooftop level, as this can result in a substantial reduction of
entrained air.
Additionally, different types of trees, such as deciduous and evergreen species, play a role in the
seasonal fluctuations of pollutant exposure within street canyons. During the summer months,
street canyons with deciduous trees have been observed to trap pollutants, leading to elevated
concentrations (Jin et al., 2014; Salmond et al., 2013). Conversely, in the winter season, street
canyons with evergreen trees show increased pollutant levels. The selection of tree species and
awareness of the different impact related to the species, should be a part of the selection process for
the future street tree allocation in the Copenhagen Municipality.
It is worth noting that the impact of tree proximity to roads on air exchange and pollutant
concentrations can vary depending on the specific characteristics of the urban environment and the
emission sources in question (Grylls and van Reeuwijk, 2022). Therefore, it is important for the
Municipality of Copenhagen to consider the specific location of the 10.000 street trees in order to
avoid a deterioration of air quality in the city.

The economic benefits of air pollution removal in the Baseline and the enhanced economic benefit in

a limited selection of ecosystem services, specifically air pollution removal, and the values provided

One such uncertainty is the economic valuation of air pollution removal, which is based on estimates

The external values are likely to vary between Denmark and the United States due to differences in

legislation and regulation can influence how external values are managed and quantified in different
countries. The extent of environmental protection measures, investments in sustainability, and
awareness of environmental issues can also differ between countries. Furthermore, the societal
consequences of pollution can vary depending on factors such as the composition of the population,
the strength of the healthcare system, and other country-specific factors. Assessing external values
can be complex and subjective, and there may be different approaches and methods to quantify and
address these values in different countries and contexts.
Additionally, the valuation of external values is connected to the concept of ’willingness to pay.’
Estimating the willingness to pay for ecosystem services in Denmark versus the United States is
complex due to factors such as cultural differences, economic conditions, and the specific valuation
methodologies employed in each country. Similarly to external values, the willingness to pay can
also differ between Denmark and the United States. When individuals are willing to pay more for a
particular ecosystem service, it implies that they perceive a higher value in its provision. Therefore,
the economic benefits calculated using MyTree could be higher or lower if the economic valuation of
air pollution by urban trees were based on Denmark rather than the United States.
Furthermore, future external values and willingness to pay for ecosystem services may also change
due to shifting priorities and needs in future societies.
6.5
Robustness of Results
Models are simplified representations of reality that allow to analyze, simulate, and predict outcomes.
Models enable to formulate hypotheses, test theories, and uncover patterns and relationships that
may not be immediately apparent in the real world. This also counts for MyTree. MyTree have the
advantages to simplify, predict and forecast the NOx removal capacity of urban trees by simulating
scenarios with different assumptions. However, MyTree is also limited and the statistic data does
not reflect the Municipality of Copenhagen 1:1.
The data input about the current tree condition, tree size, and location of the street trees to MyTree
is based on 180 trees and not the 19.892 street trees in Copenhagen. The data output from MyTree is
therefore a simulation of the NOx removal capacity and can not provide exact value but can give an
estimation. Moreover, is the data input based on personal observation from the conducted fieldwork,
which also involve uncertainties. The observed crown condition may vary between individuals and
can affect the estimated NOx removal capacity for each tree species. However, as discussed in
Section 6.2 is the data obtained in late spring with leafs on, where the crown condition can be
determined with less uncertainty compared to off-leaf seasons.
When considering the robustness of the results, it is worth noting that two independent studies
have reported similar findings. Nowak et al. (2006) found that trees removed <1% of the emitted
NOx, while Selmi et al. (2016) found an annual pollutant removal rate of 0,92 g NOx per m2.
Page 31 of 46
==PAGE:41==
Although these two studies differ from the present study in their focus and methodology, they
provide valuable insights into the magnitude of air pollution removal by urban trees. Nowak et al.
(2006) investigated the air pollution removal capacity of urban trees in 55 US cities, considering
meteorological conditions, pollution concentrations, and urban tree cover data. On the other hand,
Selmi et al. (2016) used i-Tree Eco to estimate air pollution removal by urban trees specifically in
Strasbourg, France, based on urban tree cover data. Nonetheless, these comparisons offer a reliable
estimation of the order of magnitude of air pollution absorption by urban trees, supporting the
findings of this study.
MyTree capability to estimate air pollution removal is apparent. However, it solely focuses on
estimating air pollution removal and its impact on local pollutant concentrations, overlooking
tree-related influences in the following areas:
• Trees have the potential to modify wind patterns, leading to concentration changes at the local
site scale. These alterations can either increase or decrease pollutant concentrations.
• The model does not consider the emission of VOCs by trees.
• The model does not account for the potential effects of tree pollen on human health.
• During drought periods, the model can overestimate the removal of pollutants such as NO2,
SO2, and O3. It is important to consider when urban trees actually experience drought, human
watering practices, in many cases, prevent urban trees from undergoing drought effects.
In conclusion, MyTree serves as a valuable tool in simplifying, predicting, and forecasting the NOx
removal capacity of urban trees through scenario simulations. While it has certain limitations, such
as the use of data from a limited sample size and the exclusion of certain tree-related influences,
MyTree provides estimations that contribute to the understanding of air pollution removal. By
acknowledging its limitations and considering the broader context, MyTree remains a valuable asset
in assessing the potential benefits of urban trees in mitigating air pollution.
6.6
Modelling Perspectives
MyTree Modelling Potentials
MyTree, powered by the i-Tree Engine, offers extensive modeling potentials to support future urban
planning efforts focused on street trees (Jason Hanning - i-Tree Technical Services, 2023). Regular
updates to the i-Tree Engine include the addition of new species, improved modeling in various
locations, species-specific carbon equations, and updates to local precipitation and pollution data.
These ongoing updates ensure that MyTree remains up-to-date and provides valuable information
for decision-making. Moreover, the development work on the MyTree mapping site, currently in a
beta version, aims to enhance project tracking and exporting capabilities. This will enable users to
extract data contributed by multiple individuals for a single related project, promoting collaboration
and efficient data management.
The developments are interesting as it will make it possible to extract more real-time data about
the local effects of the trees. This tool empowers planners to make informed decisions, assess the
benefits of street trees, in order to create sustainable and resilient urban environments.
Storm Water Mitigation
The ecosystem services and benefits in MyTree does also include storm water mitigation (Nowak,
2021). Urban trees have an effect on the urban hydrology and contribute to the removal of water by
Page 32 of 46
==PAGE:42==
interception and evapotranspiration. Storm water mitigation refers to the ability of urban trees and
vegetation to capture and retain rainwater, thereby reducing the volume and velocity of storm water
runoff. By intercepting rainfall and allowing it to infiltrate into the soil or evaporate back into the
atmosphere, trees can help mitigate the impacts of storm water runoff, such as flooding, erosion,
and water pollution. The evaporation and evapotranspiration of trees do also have positive effects
on the UHI, because the evaporation and transpiration contribute to the cooling effect (Donovan
and Butry, 2009). These relations show the multi functionality and benefits of trees which MyTree
is able to help estimate.
Carbon Dioxide Uptake
Becoming the first carbon neutral capital in the world by 2025 is the main goal in Copenhagen’s
climate plan (Københavns Kommune, 2020). One of the key concepts in the climate plan is the
implementation of activities that reduce carbon emissions, including the planting of trees. Trees
play a crucial role in carbon sequestration and contribute to the city’s carbon neutrality efforts.
During photosynthesis, trees absorb CO2 from the atmosphere, storing carbon and releasing oxygen
as a byproduct (Kallarackal and Roby, 2012). As described in Section 3.3.2, trees have the ability to
remove carbon from the atmosphere and store it, thereby aiding in carbon sequestration. MyTree,
in addition to estimating air pollution removal, also provides estimates of carbon storage in trees
and annual carbon sequestration, allowing for an understanding of the financial benefits associated
with these ecosystem services.
By planting 10.000 street trees in Copenhagen, not only will there be a noticeable impact on NOx
concentrations in the long term, but these trees will also contribute to the city’s efforts in achieving
carbon neutrality.
6.7
Management Perspectives
The urban environment presents a complex landscape that requires careful consideration of various
interests. In the realm of urban climate adaptation, it becomes imperative to develop comprehensive
solutions that recognize the multifunctionality of urban spaces. The management of trees in urban
areas involves multiple stakeholders, each with their own unique perspectives. Climate adaptation
professionals prioritize the logical and economic aspects of trees, emphasizing their crucial role in
regulating ecosystem services like run-off mitigation, carbon storage, and climate regulation (Figure
3.1). On the other hand, urban planners and architects place emphasis on cultural perspectives,
taking into account the aesthetic and social aspects associated with trees (Figure 3.1). While the
amenity aspects are widely acknowledged, their economic value is often overlooked. Ultimately,
politicians who shape future strategies must prioritize the economic aspect while also considering
the social and aesthetic factors to maintain public support. Effective communication among relevant
stakeholders is vital for identifying and integrating the necessary priorities in climate adaptation
measures, ensuring a holistic perspective. Incorporating an ecosystem approach that equally values
amenity and utility aspects in the urban planning process in Copenhagen will be instrumental in
securing economic funding for the Municipality.
Another factor to consider is the projected population increase of 62.000 in the Copenhagen
Municipality between 2022 and 2032 (Københavns kommune, 2021b). This growth will likely result
in the expansion of residential areas, leading to increased coverage of impervious surfaces in the
city. This global trend is expected to continue (UN Habitat, 2022). The rise in impervious surfaces
poses a potential challenge in terms of creating sufficient space for trees in Copenhagen. The space
requirements pose a significant hurdle in realizing the goal of planting 10.000 new street trees in the
city. As previously discussed, the availability of space and suitable soil conditions are critical factors
for the health and growth of trees.
Page 33 of 46
==PAGE:43==
The expanding impervious surfaces present challenges to these conditions and the availability of space.
Additionally, planting additional trees in parks, cemeteries, or other open spaces may be limited by
their existing uses, such as recreational activities or an already established tree canopy. Identifying
the locations where trees can provide the greatest benefits becomes another critical management
concern. For example, planting trees in street canyons is a complex endeavor that necessitates
consideration of meteorological conditions, tree species, stand density, and their arrangement in
relation to the street canyons.
The findings of this study can be valuable for the Municipality of Copenhagen in their efforts to
enhance air pollution removal through the planting of 10.000 new street trees. By incorporating the
insights gained from this study, the municipality can strategically select tree species that demonstrate
a high capacity for removing air pollutants, specifically targeting the goal of air pollution mitigation.
This approach not only helps in achieving the objective of increasing street tree coverage but also
optimizes the outcome of air pollution removal.
The objective of this study is to enhance the logical understanding of trees and gain recognition among
professionals, laying the foundation for a more comprehensive evaluation of the economic aspect. By
recognizing street trees as providers of ecosystem services and ensuring that all stakeholders receive
equal consideration, optimal decision-making processes concerning the role of trees in urban areas
and climate adaptation projects can be achieved.

7. Conclusions
The Copenhagen Municipality is committed to investing billions of DKK in its climate adaptation
plan, focusing on comprehensive solutions that integrate climate adaptation and urban development.
To further support the enhancement of city nature, the municipality has initiated a city nature
strategy, an urban tree policy, and a strategic goal of planting 100.000 new trees, including 10.000
street trees between 2015 and 2025. The research question addressed in this study focuses on the
impact of planting an additional 10.000 street trees on the NOx removal capacity of street trees in
Copenhagen. To investigate this question, the study employed the MyTree model, an online tool for
assessing the ecosystem services provided by individual urban trees. The estimation of air pollution
removal was based on an analysis of 180 street trees from 9 different species in Copenhagen, taking
into account their DBH, sun exposure, and crown condition, which were assessed during fieldwork.
The analysis results revealed that the existing nine species of street trees (Gleditsia sp., Malus
sp., Platanus sp., Prunus sp., Pyrus sp., Quercus sp., Robinia sp., Sorbus sp., and Tilia sp.) in
Copenhagen are expected to remove 23,1 kg of NOx over a span of 20 years. Among these species,
Sorbus sp. demonstrated the highest NOx absorption capacity per square meter, while Prunus sp.
exhibited the lowest removal capacity per square meter. By increasing the street tree population by
an additional 10.000 trees, the NOx removal capacity improved by 23% over 20 years. Furthermore,
the analysis indicated that the addition of a further 10.000 street trees would result in a 22,6%
increase to the current economic benefits associated with the removal of air pollutants.
The study’s scenarios also indicated that sun exposure influenced the NOx removal capacity of all
nine species across three different crown conditions (fair, good, excellent). Therefore, it can be
concluded that local tree effects significantly impact NOx absorption. Additionally, the influence of
leaf area on NOx removal capacity will vary over time due to tree growth, mortality, new plantings,
and natural regeneration, meaning that the results from one year are not necessarily applicable to
another year in the past or future.
In summary, this study enhances the understanding of the role of street trees in improving localised
air quality conditions, enabling climate adaptation professionals, urban planners, architects, and
politicians to recognize and incorporate the air pollution removal effects of trees in future urban
planning endeavours. Integrating an ecosystem approach that values both amenity and utility aspects
in the Copenhagen urban planning process will play a crucial role in securing economic funding for
climate adaptation solutions for the municipality. Moreover, the mapping of tree characteristics and
environmental factors can be extended to quantify the effects of trees on other ecosystem services,
facilitating a comprehensive assessment of the city’s existing nature in climate adaptation projects.

8. Recommendations
One of the limitations in this study is that not all street trees in the Municipality of Copenhagen
were measured. Future research should aim to include all street trees in fieldwork. By including
more street trees and species, a more comprehensive understanding and realistic estimate of the
NOx removal capacity of street trees can be obtained. If future research decides to measure all
street trees in Copenhagen, the i-Tree Eco model could be used, which can include canopy area
easily and other information about ecosystem services and their value to communities.
In continuation of this a future study could also investigate other ecological infrastructure such
as green walls, green roofs, vegetation along roadsides etc.
again to provide a extended and
comprehensive analysis of the NOx removal capacity by urban vegetation.
In order to overcome the limitation of a relatively small sample size within each tree species in future
research, it is crucial to strive for a more extensive and diverse sample of trees within each species.
This can be accomplished by measuring a significant proportion of street trees, including those with
which the Municipality of Copenhagen may not have had extensive prior experience. By incorporating
a wider range of tree species, the study findings can gain greater comprehensiveness, robustness, and
representativeness of the various ecosystem services provided by different tree species. Furthermore,
conducting interviews with multiple individuals within the planning department of the Copenhagen
Municipality enables a thorough exploration of the challenges, opportunities, and potential solutions
associated with expanding the number of tree species as street trees. This approach facilitates
a more holistic understanding of the complexities and dynamics involved in urban development.
By expanding the sample size and ensuring diverse representation within each tree species, future
research can transcend this limitation and provide a more nuanced and comprehensive analysis of
the NOx removal capacity and the factors influencing the success of street trees.
Incorporating seasonal variations in fieldwork is another important perspective to consider. By
investigating trees during both summer and winter, researchers can assess the crown condition and
performance of street trees under different environmental conditions. This approach will provide
insights into the resilience and adaptability of tree species throughout the year, helping to refine
recommendations for species selection and management practices. Additionally, studying seasonal
variations can help identify specific challenges or opportunities related to air pollution removal,
carbon sequestration, or other ecosystem services provided by street trees during different seasons.
One of the important perspectives to consider in future research is engaging in dialogue with the
Municipality to understand the challenges they face when budgeting for street trees. By conducting
interviews or surveys with key stakeholders in the planning department or relevant decision-makers,
researchers can gain insights into the constraints and considerations that influence the allocation
of resources for urban greening initiatives. Understanding these challenges will provide valuable
context for future studies and allow for the development of realistic and actionable recommendations
that align with the municipality’s goals and limitations.
Page 36 of 46
==PAGE:46==
Another crucial perspective to explore is the challenges associated with future urban planning. As
cities continue to grow and evolve, it is essential to investigate how street trees can be effectively
integrated into urban planning strategies. Research should aim to identify potential barriers, such as
limited space, conflicting infrastructure needs, or competing priorities, that may hinder the successful
implementation of street tree initiatives. By addressing these challenges, researchers can provide
valuable guidance on how to incorporate street trees into future urban development plans, creating
greener and more sustainable cities.
In order to advance the research, it is important to proceed beyond the initial step of quantifying
the NOx removal capacity and explore further stages. This could involve a cost benefit analysis, in
order to assess the financial viability and potential benefits of the Municipality of Copenhagen’s
initiative of increasing the number of street trees with 10.000. To increase awareness of the benefits
provided by trees and promote their inclusion in urban planning decisions, it is important to conduct
a cost-benefit analysis based on the ecosystem services they offer. By quantifying the economic value
of services such as air pollution mitigation, temperature regulation, stormwater management, and
aesthetic improvements, researchers can create a compelling business case for investing in street trees.
This analysis should consider not only the direct financial costs of tree planting and maintenance
but also the long-term economic benefits and savings associated with improved public health, energy
efficiency, and property values. Presenting the findings in a robust cost-benefit framework can
help decision-makers recognize the potential return on investment and prioritize tree planting as a
strategic action.
Further could the Municipality of Copenhagen collect all data about the street trees in an open
source platform, so no data is lost and more data is easily available.
By incorporating these different perspectives into future research, the study can provide a more
comprehensive understanding of the challenges, opportunities, and potential solutions related to street
trees in urban environments. This multidimensional approach will contribute to the development of
evidence-based recommendations that can inform policy decisions, urban planning practices, and
community engagement efforts, ultimately leading to greener, healthier, and more sustainable cities.
